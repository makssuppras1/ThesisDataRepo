Abstract 
This master´s thesis investigates valorization of the dry matter content of stickwater, a side 
stream from a production of black soldier fly larvae (BSFL, Hermetia illucens) The project 
concerns characterization and fermentation of the stickwater and application of the produced 
ferment, with the purpose of inclusion for fish feed. Characterization of the stickwater 
showed a content of 48.22% proteins and 34.6% N-free substances on a dry matter basis. The 
content of total amino acids was 52.92 g/100g crude protein and a content of 28.32% 
essential amino acids of the total amino acids. The N-free substances were determined to 
consist of 20.61% available carbohydrates and 4.41% dietary fiber of the total dry matter. 
The stickwater did not contain any measurable chitin, however 3.65% hexosamines on dry 
matter basis, was found and speculated to be glucosamine, the subunit of chitin. Bacillus 
licheniformis isolated from the stickwater and 20 strains of lactic acid bacteria was tested in 
stickwater, first for the ability to grow and then a few strains were selected for further 
fermentation. A strain of Lactococcus lactis showed the greatest potential for reaching the 
goal of pH 4.3-4.5. Small and pilot-scale fermentations reached pH of 4.56 and 4.71, 
respectively. 
Solids from the pilot-scale fermentation was recovered by centrifugation. The centrifugation 
recovered 11.91% of the total dry matter, 14.66% of the total proteins and 10.47% of the total 
other solids in a slurry. This slurry contained 16.82% dry matter, which consisted of 59.33% 
protein, 6.46% ash and 34.21% other solids, which is mainly N-free substances.  
The dry matter in both the obtained slurry and stickwater are high in protein and low in lipids 
and chitin, which is an advantage as these parameters for some fish species is a limiting 
factor for the inclusion of BSFL meal. The high water content, has the potential to substitute 
water that should have been added either way. However, the water content is believed to 
become the limiting factor for inclusion at the moment. 
Extrusion trials with inclusion of 4.00 and 17.11% of dry matter from stickwater and slurry, 
respectively, yielded pellets that were significantly different regarding water stability index, 
sinking and floating behavior. However, the extrusion trials demonstrated the ability to 
include stickwater and the slurry for extrusion purposes.

Resume 
Denne kandidatafhandling undersøger udnyttelse af tørstoffet i limvand, en side-strøm fra 
produktionen af sorte soldaterfluelarver (BSFL, Hermetia illucens), fra karakterisering til 
fermentering og anvendelse af det producerede ferment, med det formål at inkludere det i 
fiskefoder. Karakteriseringen af limvand viste et indhold af 48,22% protein og 34,6% N-frie 
forbindelser på tørstofbasis. Indholdet af totale aminosyrer var 52,92 g/100 g rå protein, med 
et indhold af 28,32% essentielle aminosyrer (EAA) af det samlede antal aminosyrer. De N-
frie forbindelser blev bestemt til at bestå af 20,61% tilgængelige kulhydrater og 4,41% 
kostfibre af det samlede tørstof. Der blev ikke fundet chitin i limvandet, men der blev fundet 
3,65% hexosaminer på tørstofbasis, som formodes at være glucosaminer, en bestanddel af 
chitin-molekylet. Bacillus licheniformis, isoleret fra limvandet, og 20 stammer af 
mælkesyrebakterier blev testet. Først for deres evne til at vokse, og derefter blev nogle få 
udvalgt til videre fermentering. En stamme af Lactococcus lactis viste det største potentiale 
for at nå målet om en pH på 4,3-4,5. Fermenteringer i lille og pilot-skala opnåede pH-værdier 
på henholdsvis 4,56 og 4,71 uden optimal temperaturkontrol. 
Tørstoffet fra fermenteringen i pilotskala blev genvundet ved centrifugering. Centrifugerigen 
udvandt 11,91% af det samlede tørstof, 14,66% af det samlede protein og 10,47% af resten af 
tørstoffet, i en slurry. Den opnåede slurry indeholdt 16,82% tørstof, som bestod af 59,33% 
protein, 6,46% aske og 34,21% andre faste stoffer, hovedsageligt N-frie forbindelser. 
Tørstoffet i både slurry og stickwater er højt i protein, lavt i fedt og lavt i chitin, hvilket er en 
fordel, da disse parametre for nogle fiskearter er den begrænsende faktor for inklusion af 
BSFL-mel. Det høje vandindhold har potentiale til at erstatte vand, som under alle 
omstændigheder skulle tilføjes. Dog vurderes vandindholdet på nuværende tidspunkt at være 
den begrænsende faktor for inklusion i fiskefoder. 
Ekstruderingsforsøg med inklusion af henholdsvis 4,00% og 17,11% tørstof fra limvand og 
slurry resulterede i pellets, der var signifikant forskellige med hensyn til vandstabilitet, 
synke- og flydeegenskaber. Dog demonstrerede ekstruderingsforsøgene evnen til at inkludere 
limvand og slurry til ekstruderingsformål.

2. Introduction 
The rapid growth of the world population has significantly impacted food consumption, 
leading to an increased demand for protein from agriculture and livestock. This surge in 
demand carries risks, including deforestation, higher water consumption, and increased 
greenhouse gas emissions (FAO, 2017). In response to the challenges posed by traditional 
agricultural and livestock practices, the search for novel, sustainable protein sources has 
intensified. One such source gaining considerable attention is insects (van Huis, 2013), owing 
to their high protein content and provision of all essential amino acids (EAA) necessary for 
human consumption (Bukkens, 1997). Among insects, one of the most promising species is 
the black soldier fly (BSF, Hermetia illucens), whose larvae can convert various side streams 
into biomass rich in crude protein and lipids, approximately 40% and 30% on a dry basis, 
respectively (Diener et al., 2009; Oonincx et al., 2015). Additionally, BSF is a source of 
chitin, comprising about 6.9% of its dry matter (Eggink, 2023).   
Using BSF larvae for the valorization of side streams addresses two key challenges. Firstly, it 
serves as an effective tool for bioconversion of side streams into higher-value products of 
commercial interest, ensuring the retention of nutrients in the food chain that would otherwise 
be lostduring waste processing. This contributes to a more circular food system (Jucker et al., 
2020). Secondly, black soldier fly larvae (BSFL) offer a promising alternative ingredient for 
feed production, potentially replacing conventional sources such as soybean and fishmeal  
(Guo et al., 2021). Several studies have already demonstrated the feasibility of including 
BSFL meals in fish diets without negatively affecting growth characteristics across various 
species (Abd El-Hack et al., 2020; Bessa et al., 2020; Gasco et al., 2019; Ravi et al., 2020). 
An additional advantage of BSFL is their suitability for large-scale production, due to their 
short life cycle, rapid growth and high feed conversion ratio (Diener et al., 2011; 
Weththasinghe et al., 2022). These traits contribute to BSFL being the most farmed insect 
species for feed in Europe (IPIFF, 2021). Nonetheless, several challenges must be addressed 
before production volumes can match those of traditional feed ingredients.  
 
After the larvae are reared, they undergo processing for inclusion in various feed 
applications. Different methods can be employed to fractionate the larvae into proteins and 
lipids, including wet dry fractionation, as well as variations using enzymes or solvents 
(Azzollini et al., 2020; Caligiani et al., 2018). In the dry fractionation method, the whole 
larvae are dried pressed to produce crude oil and pellets rich in protein. Both drying and

pressing involve high temperatures to maximize drying and oil yield, but this process can 
denature the proteins, potentially reducing their functional properties – a significant challenge 
in BSFL processing (Caligiani et al., 2018). In wet fractionation, mechanical processes 
separate the protein and lipids directly from the fresh larvae (Azzollini et al., 2020; Ravi et 
al., 2021). However, this method generates a liquid side stream containing proteins and other 
nutrients, referred to as stickwater, named after its counterpart in the fishmeal industry 
(Kousoulaki et al., 2009). Stickwater derived from insects is not well described in literature, 
likely due to the focuson other challenges within the insect industry.  
 
2.1 Aim of the study 
The main objective of this study is to comprehensively characterize the dry matter of BSFL 
stickwater by analyzing its amino acid composition and carbohydrate profile. Based on this 
characterization,the study aims toidentify a suitable microorganism for biorefinery, focusing 
on lowering the pH to the isoelectric point (pI) of BSFL proteins. The fermentation process 
will then be scaled up to pilot-scale, subsequent evaluation and use of downstream processing 
for obtaining a product will be considered. Finally, extrusion trials will be conducted to 
assess the potential of including the derived product in fish feed applications. 
 
2.2 Research Questions 
This research attempts to answer the following questions: 
- 
What are the primary components of the dry matter in stickwater, specifically 
focusing on amino acids and carbohydrates? 
- 
Which microorganisms are most suitable for biorefinery of stickwater to reach the pI 
of BSFL proteins? 
- 
What are the key considerations for designing and scaling up the fermentation 
process? 
- 
What are the most effective downstream processing methods for recovering a 
valuable product from the fermented stickwater? 
- 
What is the potential for including the obtained product in fish feed applications?

3. Theory 
This section will in detail present the theory and literature regarding the topics relevant for 
understanding and working with the project aim. 
3.1 Black soldier fly larvae 
3.1.1 Life cycle 
The BSF species is native to the subtropical and tropical climates of South America but is 
currently widespread in all regions with these climates, due to its tolerance to a wide variety 
of environmental conditions. Furthermore, the BSF are non-pest, wasp-like, adaptable, and 
saprophagous insects, that lays a lot of eggs and has fast growing larvae, which gives 
opportunities for mass production (Smet et al., 2018). Black soldier flies are characterized by 
a short life cycle ranging from 40-45 days (Tomberlin et al., 2002). In this cycle the BSF 
undergoes five distinct stages shown in Figure 1, egg, larvae, prepupae, pupae and adult/fly 
stage.  
 
Figure 1 - Life cycle of black soldier flies (Hermetia illucens) (Lievens et al., 2021). 
The eggs hatch after approximately 3-4 days and a small larva emerges from each egg, called 
a neonate. The larvae then undergo 5 instars as a larva for 13-18 days, followed by a sixth 
instar prepupal stage, where the larvae stop consuming and turns into a pupae, this stage is 
approximately 7 days. The next stage is the pupal stage, which can last from 10 days to 
several months before the fly emerges. The adult fly only consumes water and lives for 
around 5-8 days, with the sole purpose of mating. The female fly then lays 500-900 eggs to

complete the cycle (Smet et al., 2018). During the larvae stage, the BSFL can consume a 
wide range of organic substrates and convert it into biomass which mainly consist of protein, 
fat and chitin (Čičková et al., 2015). 
 
3.1.2 Digestive system and nutritional requirements 
The digestive system of the BSFL is very extensive with an alimentary canal divided into 
three parts, foregut, midgut and hindgut, where only parts of these has been studied 
intensively (Tettamanti et al., 2022). Each part of. the alimentary canal has different functions 
which will not be elaborated, but the main differences is the working pH which ranges from 
pH 2-9 and the wide ranges of enzymes the gut can excrete. Some of the known enzymes 
includes, amylases, lipases, trypsin, chymotrypsin, endo- and exopeptidases (Bruno et al., 
2019; Seyedalmoosavi et al., 2022).  
Even though, the BSFL has a digestive system available to live on a wide range of substrates 
it has some nutritional requirements to maintain its biological functions and grow optimal. An 
interesting feature about some insect species, is their ability to self-regulate their intake of 
nutrients, when substrates is deficient in specific nutrients. They have some metabolic 
mechanisms to deal with imbalanced nutrient sources, which then leads to prolonging of the 
larval stage and thus amount or rate of ingested feed (Green et al., 2003; K. P. Lee et al., 
2008). 
However, in an industrial setting it is unfavorable if the larvae lack certain nutrients and start 
to self-regulate their intake, while this affects certain growth parameters and increases rearing 
time. This underlines the importance of optimizing rearing substrates to meet the 
requirements of the larvae. 
 
3.1.2.1 Protein and amino acids 
The main source of nitrogen in the diet is proteins, which are essential for growth and 
maintenance of the insect. Although protein is very important, the optimal dietary content for 
BSFL has not been established yet. Results suggest that the level of dietary protein for BSFL 
is in the range of 10-30% on dry matter basis, mainly depending on the larval stage and 
protein source. Below this range decreased survival and growth has been observed, probably 
due to the requirements for maintenance or a specific amino acid is not met (Eggink, 2023). 
Above the range only decreased survival was observed, which could be due to increased

production of toxic metabolites from protein metabolism among other things – as seen with 
other insect species (K. P. Lee et al., 2008). 
Of the twenty commonly found amino acids, ten are considered essential for insects, which 
are arginine, histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, 
tryptophan and valine (House, 1961). For the non-essential amino acids, the insects can either 
synthesize or derive them from the essential amino acids (EAA). For the moment data on the 
amino acid requirements for BSFL are lacking, but it can be expected that an imbalanced 
dietary amino acid composition will affect the larvae negatively, which has been shown for 
other insect species (Horie & Watanabe, 1983). 
 
3.1.2.2 Lipids 
Lipids are of great importance for the larvae metabolism as lipids has the role of acting as 
electron carriers, substrate carriers in enzymatic reactions and building blocks in cell 
membranes, however it is not usually included in high ratios in commercial productions 
(Allen Carson Cohen, 2016). Lipids are a very efficient energy source compared to protein 
and carbohydrates, due to lipids lack of water binding capacity (Jobling, 1994). However, 
lipid requirements for BSFL are currently not established, but one research suggest that 
dietary lipid levels above 36% dry matter have negative effects (Nguyen et al., 2015). Most 
insect species are not able to synthesize polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) and other highly 
unsaturated fatty acids (HUFA). For some insect species PUFA and HUFA has been shown 
to be essential, however in some species of the order Diptera, PUFA and HUFA has shown to 
be non-essential, this remain to be shown for BSFL (Capinera et al., 2008). 
 
3.1.2.3 Carbohydrates 
The main source of energy for BSFL is carbohydrates, they are used for synthesizing the 
backbone of most amino acids and Chitin (Pimentel et al., 2017). The carbohydrates are 
divided into sugars, with monosaccharides (glucose and fructose) and disaccharides (sucrose 
and maltose) and polysaccharides, consisting of storage polysaccharides (glycogen and 
starch) and structural polysaccharides (cellulose and chitin). The BSF larvae are quite 
efficient in utilizing sugars and the storage polysaccharides, but have limited ability to digest 
the structural polysaccharides, likely due to lack of or limited number of digestive enzymes 
that are required (Gold et al., 2018).

Studies therefore suggest rearing BSF on substrates with low amounts of structural 
polysaccharides or alternatively pre-treat the substrate either using specific microbes or 
enzymes to assist the BSFL in the digestion of fibrous material, to increase the harvest yield 
of BSFL (Wong et al., 2020).  
Furthermore, the dietary macronutrient ratio can also influence insect performance. Mostly 
the ratio between proteins and carbohydrates are important for growth, reproduction and 
survival (Aguila et al., 2013). Therefore, the ratio between protein and carbohydrate (P:C) 
has been studied in for a wide range of insects (Le Gall & Behmer, 2014; K. P. Lee et al., 
2008; Roeder & Behmer, 2014). In nature the BSFL consume substrates with P:C ratios of 
1:1.5 – 1:2 (Barragan-Fonseca et al., 2017). These findings are comparable to 1:2 - 1:3 found 
for rearing of BSFL, outside this range lower survival, growth and development was 
observed. However, the results are dependent on larval stage, source and quality of 
macronutrients (Eggink, 2023).  
 
3.1.3 BSFL composition 
As the whole larvae is used for processing, the nutritional value of the products depends on 
the body composition of the larvae. Several studies have been focused on manipulation of the 
composition of the insect by changing factors such as rearing substrate and/or time of harvest. 
The studies also demonstrated the importance of quality and quantity of the rearing substrate, 
additionally the larval stage is also of great importance (Eggink, 2023; Gold et al., 2018; 
Nguyen et al., 2015; Oonincx et al., 2015). Focus will remain on macronutrients, despite the 
importance of some micronutrient, such as vitamins and minerals. 
 
3.1.3.1 Proteins and amino acids 
BSFL are known for a high protein content, with 29.7-63.1% on a dry matter basis, 
depending on rearing substrate, larval life stage and the method of protein quantification 
(Eggink, 2023).  
The standard methods for crude protein measurement are either Kjeldahl or Dumas. Both 
methods measure total nitrogen from the samples, and thus a nitrogen-to-protein conversion 
factor (KP) is needed. The standard KP is 6.25, which assume that that all protein contains 
16% nitrogen, and that all nitrogen is derived from protein. The standard KP is used by the 
industry and some studies. However, research has shown this value to greatly overestimate 
the protein content in insects, due to its content of non-protein nitrogen, which consist of

Master’s thesis, 30 ETCS

chitin, nucleic acids and excretion product from the larvae (Janssen et al., 2017). As an 
example, pure chitin only contains approximately 6.9% nitrogen, which leads to this 
overestimation (S. Liu et al., 2012). A KP factor can be calculated based on the sum of 
(anhydrous) amino acids, which for BSFL results in a much lower KP value of 3.35-4.67 
(Eggink, 2023; Janssen et al., 2017; Smets et al., 2021). As for the total protein content, the 
KP is also dependent on rearing substrate and life stage of the larvae.  
Several studies have examined different functional properties of the proteins derived from 
insects and BSFL. An important property used in this study is the isoelectric point (pI), which 
is reported to be in the range of 4.3-4.5 (Queiroz et al., 2021; Zielińska et al., 2018).  
BSFL contains all amino acids except for taurine. The most abundant amino acids are 
glutamine (+glutamic acid), asparagine (+aspartic acid) and leucine. The amino acids in 
lowest concentration are cysteine, tryptophan and taurine. Additionally, the contents of amino 
acids remain relatively similar between life stages and rearing substrates (Eggink, 2023). 
 
3.1.3.2 Lipids 
The lipid content of BSFL range from 8.5-58.4% of the dry matter and is mainly dependent 
on the life stage and rearing substrate but can be affected by other factors (Eggink, 2023). 
The fat content is highest in the end of the larval stage, as it conserves energy for a non-
feeding adult stage, where they depend on stored energy. The metabolism of BSFL converts 
excess protein and carbohydrates into lipids, which are stored as body fat, this is observed for 
other insects aswell, to regulate their energy balance (Arrese & Soulages, 2010). The fatty 
acid profile of BSFL is to some extent modifiable by different rearing substrates. However, in 
general the BSFL mainly consist of saturated fatty acids (SFA) with a high content of lauric 
acid (Ushakova et al., 2016). The content of both unsaturated fatty acids and n-3 fatty acids in 
the larvae can be increased by providing substrate with high contents (Eggink, 2023). In 
addition, the BSFL has a physiological limit of accumulation of these highly unsaturated fatty 
acids, as they will eventually convert them into SFA (Hoc et al., 2020).

The contents of carbohydrates in BSFL are not well described in literature. Often, the 
carbohydrates are not measured, but calculated as nitrogen-free (N-free) extract, which is the 
rest after protein, lipids, water and ash has been subtracted. This N-free extract ranges 
between 15-35.45% of the dry matter (Nugroho, 2022; Zulkifli et al., 2022). Some articles

3.2.1 Fractionation methods 
Some industries sell the whole larvae, but recently additionally processing of the larvae into 
different product has shown great potential and can be beneficial for improving product 
quality and the economic value (Weththasinghe, Hansen, Nokland, et al., 2021). In general, 
the fractionation of the larvae can be done using a dry or wet fractionation method. The 
common goal is to separate the protein and the lipids. Figure 3 shows flow diagrams for the 
unit operations used in the two methods.

The dry fractionation starts with drying of the whole insect followed by a pressing step to 
yield a lipid fraction and a grinding step to obtain a grinded fraction high in protein (Purschke 
et al., 2018). The wet fractionation starts with a grinding step to crush the larvae followed by 
2 centrifugation steps, first to obtain a liquid and solid fraction. Then the liquid fraction is

centrifuged to obtain a lipid and aqueous fraction, this process is sometimes done by using 
decanters or tricanters. The aqueous and solid fraction can be dried to obtain a fraction high 
in protein (Ravi et al., 2021). 
Both methods provide a fraction high in protein and a lipid fraction that can find its use in 
several animal feed purposes. For which fractionation method that is most optimal, a set of 
things needs to be considered, such as, raw materials, processing set-up, requirements of the 
final product and scale. In general, the dry fractionation is built on simpler technology and is 
therefore more suitable for small scale production, whereas the wet fractionation is more 
suitable for larger scale productions (Eggink, 2023). 
An important aspect when choosing the fractionation method is the quality of the products 
and most often the quality of the proteins. The dry fractionation method generally produces a 
protein fraction, where the native functionality of the proteins is better preserved, but also 
produces a fraction of lower purity, which has been established and applied for cereal 
processing (Schutyser & van der Goot, 2011). However, drying of the larvae and pressing at 
high temperatures to obtain the oil can result in denaturation of the proteins and reduce their 
functional properties (Caligiani et al., 2018). 
For the wet fractionation method high temperature, harsh pH conditions and mechanical 
separations can result in detrimental effects on native protein functionality and additionally 
energy and water consumption (Schutyser & van der Goot, 2011). Furthermore, different 
variations of the wet fractionation method have been investigated. Several studies have used 
enzymatic treatments to optimize the protein yield and others examined the possibility of 
using homogenization and sonication to increase the yield (Azzollini et al., 2020; Leni, 
Soetemans, et al., 2020; Ravi et al., 2021).  
Additional process steps can be performed to separate chitin from the protein rich fraction, 
this step can also increase the protein purity. One method used for chitin extraction from 
crustacean exoskeletons, is a chemical method including demineralization and 
deproteinization, using acids and bases. This method is however not very suitable as it 
produces high quantities of chemical waste, even though higher chitin purities can be 
obtained (Bajaj et al., 2011). Others have investigated sieving into different fractions, which 
yielded a fraction higher in chitin, however the content of chitin only changed from 7.9% 
initially to 15.4% chitin in the fraction with highest chitin (Eggink, 2023). Electrostatic 
separation of the chitin from the protein fraction has been suggested but until now it has not 
been tested (Ravi et al., 2020).

3.2.4 Special course about valorization of stickwater 
Prior to writing this master thesis, a special course was conducted regarding valorization and 
concentration of the proteins in the stickwater. The purpose of the special course was to test 
acid precipitation and membrane filtration for concentration of the proteins. In the special 
course 16.6-25.1% of the proteins were recovered by acid precipitation, where the pH was 
lowered to the pI of 4.3-4.5. The obtained slurry made up 5.7% of the total mass, had a 
moisture content of 86.1% and a protein content of 47.0% on a dry matter basis. 1 kDa and 5 
kDa membranes was tested to recover the proteins from the slurry and thus allowing the other 
solids to pass through the membrane. Protein rejections of 73.16-73.35% and 46.4-60.16% 
were achieved for 1 kDa and 5 kDa membranes, respectively. The challenges with membrane 
filtration were that the obtained permeated still had a high content of protein and that the 
membranes rejected part of the N-free substances, thus not reaching the desired separation. 
Additionally, the water content of the permeate was higher than the obtained slurry.  
Furthermore, spray drying was conducted on the different treatments of the stickwater with 
yields from 43.5-73.1%, which was acceptable for the amount of product fed to the dryer and 
the scale of the dryer. However, the dried powder was observed to be sticky which could 
complicate the spray drying process. Lastly, the report considered the potential of including 
the obtained products for different applications. 
 
3.3 Biorefinery using fermentation with LAB 
The aim of a biorefinery is to create a sustainable process that turns biomass into a spectrum 
of products to fit different purposes. This could be food, feed or different chemicals for 
specific purposes (Lübeck & Lübeck, 2019). The aim for this study is to provide a biorefinery 
process targeting the yield of proteins for the feed industry. A similar process has been 
proposed for green crops, where green juice is obtained from screw pressing. This green juice 
is then fermented using lactic acid bacteria (LAB), which triggers a decrease of pH to the pI 
of the proteins, which makes the proteins precipitate so they can be obtained in a protein rich 
fraction (Santamaría-Fernández et al., 2017).

3.3.1 Lactic Acid Bacteria  
LAB are widely used in the production of fermented products and among the best studied 
microorganisms. They belong to a group of gram-positive anaerobic bacteria, which main 
fermentation product is lactic acid (Konings et al., 2000). LAB consist of both round 
(Streptococcus, Pediococcus and Leuconostoc) and rod-shaped (Lactobacillus and 
Bifidobacterium) (Sedó Molina et al., 2022). LAB is furthermore found in a great variety of 
environments, such as fermented foods, plant surfaces, soil or the gastrointestinal tract and 
oral cavities of both human and animals. As a result of the unique compositions of these 
different environments, LAB has been forced to adapt to specific characteristics to thrive 
(Endo et al., 2018). These evolutionary changes have led to enormous variations between 
species, which is then specialized in thriving in specific niches, which could be either scarce 
or rich in nutrients. The metabolism has then adapted to the nutrients that are available in that 
specific environment by losing genes, acquiring new or upregulate pathways for synthesizing 
or degrading products (Matthews et al., 2017).  
LAB can be divided into hetero- and homofermentative species. Homofermentative species 
produce LAB as the main product from fermentation of glucose, whereas the 
heterofermentative species produces a range of product, such as carbon dioxide, lactic acid, 
ethanol and acetic acid (Altieri et al., 2017). 
LAB are in general aerotolerant anaerobes, which means they do not require oxygen for 
growth and primarily produce their energy through anaerobic fermentation, even if oxygen is 
available. While LAB can grow under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions, they do not use 
the oxygen due to lack of cytochromes and porphyrins, which are essential in the respiratory 
chain. However, it has been found that some LAB grow faster under aerobic conditions 
(Bryukhanov et al., 2022).  
An example of a LAB used in this study is Lactococcus lactis, which is a facultative 
anaerobe. It grows in environments with and without oxygen. When no oxygen is present, it 
produces lactic acid with the help of lactate-dehydrogenase. When oxygen is present, 
conversion of sugar into energy is however easier and faster (Da Silva et al., 2019).  
When developing new fermented products for either food or feed safety requirements needs 
to be considered and numerous approvals from different authorities are required. In Europe, 
the European Food Safety Authorities (EFSA) approves microorganisms that can be used for 
food production by a system called Qualified Presumption of Safety (QPS) (EFSA, 2024;

phase the bacteria try to adapt by up-regulation of nucleotide metabolism, repair damaged 
cells and RNA transcription, so it is ready for protein and enzyme production, which is 
involved in energy metabolism. When the bacteria have adapted to the environment it is 
ready to allow more rapid growth (Rolfe et al., 2012).

3.4 Downstream processes 
After fermentation it is important to consider recovery of the cells and protein in the best way 
possible regarding both yield and quality. When the product is recovered, the next 
considerations is about how to treat the product for specific purposes, should it be dried or 
used as a wet product. 
3.4.1 Recovery methods 
The recovery of cells and proteins from the media can be defined as a primary recovery step, 
which usually consists of either centrifugation or filtration. Centrifugation separates based on 
differences in the density between the solids and the liquid. This difference is then amplified 
by applying centrifugal force by rotating the mixture at very high speed. Whereas filtration 
separates components according to their size. For both methods there exist variations and 
==PAGE:25==
Morten Rojek Marvig 
 
Master’s thesis, 30 ETCS  
S223157 
 
12th of August, 2024 
 
 
25
solutions to overcome some of the challenges associated with their principles (S.-M. Lee, 
1989).  
The main advantage of the centrifugation is the ease of use when a high throughput is needed, 
less dry matter is lost in the centrifuge, less maintenance and less exchange of working parts 
and centrifuges are easy to scale for the specific purpose. Furthermore, the slurry obtained 
from the centrifugation can often reach higher contents of dry matter than the retentate from a 
crossflow filtration. Disadvantages of the centrifugation process is buildup of heat, which 
could lead to additional cooling requirements and power requirements. Centrifuges does not 
distinguish based on particle size, thus not recovering lighter particles (S.-M. Lee, 1989). 
When considering filtration for removal of solids in larger scale plants, crossflow filtration is 
the most used as it allows continuous operation. Another advantage is the selectivity of 
filtration, as it separates based on particle size and thus has the opportunity to exclude 
particles of less interest such as simple carbohydrates or ash. Furthermore, filtration is known 
to inflict less shear stress and generate less heat than centrifugation, which is an advantage as 
these effects can have adverse effect on product quality. On the other hand, filtration comes 
with several challenges as well, the main challenge to consider is fouling of the membranes, 
which is clogging of the filters which results in loss of product and need for extensive 
cleaning and replacement of membranes. Furthermore, the use of filtration can lead to loss of 
cells or proteins that are small enough to permeate the membrane. Membrane filtration can 
usually not reach the same levels of dry matter as in other operations. Lastly, filtration 
requires a lot of experimental work to be able to select the best filter material, pore size and 
operational settings (Kofod Nielsen, 2000). 
When choosing suitable downstream operations for a material, several factors regarding the 
material need to be considered. First, the nature of the solution should be considered. Is it 
high density particles, large cells or uniform proteins, usually the operation which exploits 
the biggest difference in the desired separation is used. Secondly, the desired purity be 
considered, if all dry matter should be recovered or is the target a specific molecule in the 
material. Thirdly, the scale of the operation, higher scale favors centrifugation. Lastly, 
process time and maintenance should be considered (Turner Richard and Joseph, 2018). 
Depending on content of the streams created and the separation of the components of interest, 
the streams could either be used directly as a product or further operations could be included 
to concentrate even further with the use of operations such as evaporation or drying.

3.4.2 Drying 
An important consideration when a product is obtained from the downstream processes is the 
need of drying, or if the product could be used as a wet product. This depends on which 
purpose the product should be used for. If the aim is inclusion in a formula for fish feed, a 
product with a higher moisture content could be desired, as the moisture content before 
extrusion of fish pellets is generally around 22-32% but can be as high as 45-75% for some 
applications (Cheng et al., 2022; K. Liu et al., 2021). For production of fish feed, mainly dry 
products are used, this leads to addition of water, which will be removed later in the process. 
Using a wet product for the formulation could therefore reduce the need for water addition. In 
this way energy would be saved for drying of the earlier obtained stickwater product and 
water would be saved in the formulation. However, the use and production of a wet product 
comes with some challenges that needs to be considered. A wet product in general has a 
lower shelf life due to the higher water content. Wet products are more difficult to store and 
transport, as they require more space and sometimes cooling. Additionally, they are difficult 
to handle, which results in extra loss of product, cleaning or special packaging(Srinivasan & 
Kirk, 2017). 
Many of these challenges is overcome by including a drying step and having a dry powder as 
the final product. In general, powders have longer shelf life, easier to handle, store and are 
more stable if stored correctly. However, dry products have some drawbacks as well, 
sometimes rehydration is needed before use and sometimes the texture and functionalities of 
the product is affected (Srinivasan & Kirk, 2017). 
If drying is to be considered, different methods can be used depending on the nature of the 
material. For a fluid with relatively high water content, spray drying could be an option, 
which has earlier been used on stickwater from the fish industry with success and small scale 
trials on stickwater from BSFL (Teknologisk Institut, 2014 and section 1.2.4). If the product 
is more viscous or obtained as a slurry, it will not be possible to feed to a spray dryer. For 
slurries methods such as tunnel, oven or drum drying would be solutions worth to consider.

3.5.1 Proteins 
The most expensive macronutrient when producing fish feed is protein and is therefore 
important not to add in excess, as the extra protein will be used for energy rather than for 
growth of the fish (Stone, 2003). Dietary protein in fish feed ranges from 24-62% on a dry 
matter basis, dependent on external parameters, amino acid composition and target species 
(Teles et al., 2020; Wilson, 2003). An ideal source of protein for fish feed generally has a 
balanced amino acid profile with 50:50 of essential and non-essential amino acids, with the 
purpose of maximizing the protein retention in the fish (Mai et al., 2022; Wilson, 2003).  The 
EAA for fish and insects are the same – valine, tryptophan, threonine, phenylalanine, 
methionine, lysine, leucine, isoleucine, histidine and arginine. If the protein source is 
deficient in one of the EAA, protein synthesis in the fish will be affected and limited to the 
rate which the limiting amino acid is available. Terrestrial protein sources are limited in 
several EAA, which is often lysine and/or methionine. This is a downside compared to 
fishmeal, which are more balanced in its amino acid profile (Nunes et al., 2014). This 
imbalanced can be counteracted by adding synthetic amino acids to the fish feed, however 
this is a costly solution and addition of synthetic amino acids can result in negative side 
effects in fish growth and nutritional uptake (Bodin et al., 2012; Davies & Morris, 1997). To 
compare the potential of BSFL meal to fishmeal and soybean meal, the EAA profile can be 
compared. Overall, the three different meals are very similar, BSFL meal is found to be 
higher in valine but comparatively lower in arginine, leucine and lysine (Eggink, 2023). 
 
3.5.2 Lipids 
Lipids is another macromolecule with great importance in fish feed, as the support many 
important biological processes in the fish. In general, most of the lipids is consumed through 
the feed, even though they can produce some lipids through lipogenesis (Henderson & 
Sargent, 1981). Most fish species have a requirement of 5-20% lipid on dry matter basis in 
the feed, however these requirements are very dependent on several factors (Jobling, 2012). 
Fish needs high quality lipids sources including enough omega-3 and omega-6 PUFA and 
HUFA, as they are important for many of the biological processes (Tocher, 2010). Sources of

Comparing the fatty acid profile of the lipids from BSFL with fish oils and terrestrial oils. 
The BSFL lipids contains very low amounts of n-3 fatty acids, less than 2% of the total fatty 
acids. On the other hand, the fish oils contain 16-40% and 7-11% n-3 fatty acids for the 
terrestrial oils. The BSFL lipid fraction contains a high amount of saturated lipids and a low 
proportion of HUFA, which makes is less fit for inclusion in a fish feed application. 
However, studies have shown that the fatty acid profile of BSFL can be modified 
considerably by including n-3 sources in the rearing substrate (Eggink, 2023). This could 
increase the potential of inclusion for fish feed applications. Another study has shown that the 
retention of HUFA from rearing substrates has a physiological limit in BSFL, which could 
mean that the lipids fraction is not able to meet the requirements even though the rearing 
substrate is enriched with HUFA (Hoc et al., 2020; Rodrigues et al., 2022). It would likely be 
more efficient to extract the HUFA directly from side streams instead of using it directly as a 
substrate for rearing. This also means when using BSFL meal for fish feed applications the 
lipid content is wanted to be as low as possible to not be a limiting factor for the inclusion. 
 
3.5.3 Carbohydrates 
Dietary carbohydrates are not essential for the fish to grow and evolve, as it can convert 
lipids and proteins to glucose by gluconeogenesis. The reason why carbohydrates are 
included in fish feed, is because they are a cheap energy source and are easy available. 
Additionally, the carbohydrates can offer functionality to the pellets by improving structure, 
floating and stability of the pellet. Here especially sources including gluten has to be 
considered, as gluten is known to have a great impact on several pellet parameters and 
especially the final structure of the pellet (Draganovic et al., 2013). 
The carbohydrate intake in nature is almost solely limited to chitin found in algae, 
crustaceans and insects (Kaushik et al., 2022). Chitin has contradictive effects when included 
in fish feed, showing both positive and negative results. It seems the results are mostly 
dependent on level of inclusion, fish species and chitin derivative. The positive effect of 
chitin is on the gut microbiome, where it acts as a prebiotic and have positive effect on the 
immune system (Esteban et al., 2001; Gopalakannan & Arul, 2006; Terova et al., 2019). The 
negative effects of chitin and its derivatives has been observed when chitin is present in too 
high concentrations, then nutrient digestibility and growth is affected (Eggink, 2023). Some 
fish species can digest chitin to some extent and thus it will act as an energy source for the

3.5.4 Extrusion and fish feed production 
Extrusion has become an important technology in many kinds of industries and especially 
important when producing fish feed. The extruder is a complex technology, while its products 
are affected by several physical and mechanical factors, small changes can yield very 
different products. A lot of effort is therefore put into the design of new products (Kaddour, 
2018). Some of the operation that can be performed by extrusion is expansion, gelatinization, 
mixing dehydration, degassing, grinding, homogenization, thermal cooking, shearing, 
shaping and protein denaturation. This showcases that the extrusion process has almost 
unlimited possibilities of application.  
In this section focus will be on the use for fish feed applications. There are two types of 
extruders, a singe screw extruder and a twin-screw extruder. The single screw is simpler 
mechanically, requires less maintenance, good for products containing more oil and that are 
cheaper. The twin screw extruders a more commonly used in larger scale applications. The 
extrusion process greatly relies on provided heat and thermal generation from shearing, when 
the formulated product moves through the extruder barrel zones from the feeding zone to the 
die zone. Here many factors influence the extruder performance and product quality, it could 
be moisture content, temperature, viscosity, particle size, screw speed, retention time, length 
and diameter of the screw. The different zones of the extruder can be controlled to have 
different temperature profiles to reach the desired product. After extrusion, drying and 
cooling profiles is also important to consider (Riaz, 2000).  
Expanded products like floating aquatic feed and pet food pellets are produced by using high 
shear stress extruding profiles, which usually has a small length to diameter ratio. The die in 
the end of the extruder is very important to reach the desired pellet and is sometimes applied 
with a cutter making pellets directly after extrusion (Kaddour, 2018).  
Several trials have been made to include new sources of sustainable protein in fish feed 
formulations, with the purpose of replacing fish meal or soybean meal. Some of these has 
been testing meal from BSFL with partial success. Eggink, 2023 provides an extensive 
overview of the findings of different studies, which shows that the inclusion ratio has very

4. Materials and methods  
This section in details describe the methods and materials used over the course of the project, 
as well as describing how different materials or samples are prepared. All analysis included 
in the section was conducted in triplicate, unless otherwise is stated. 
 
4.1 Stickwater Sampling and Handling 
The stickwater was sampled from the pilot plant of Enorm Biofactory. The sampling was 
done directly after the centrifuge where oil is separated from the liquid fraction, as seen in  
Figure 4. A pipe was connected to the outlet of the centrifuge and containers of 5 liters were 
filled with stickwater and sealed immediately to avoid evaporation. Containers were freezed 
at -18C on a pallet with space between each box. After 24 hours the containers were 
transported to Technical University of Denmark and freezed again at -40C within the same 
day. Prior to experiments the water was completely thawed at 5C before use. 
 
4.2 Freeze drying  
Freeze drying was conducted on the stickwater, by pre-freezing approximately 3 liters of 
stickwater divided on two plates to -40°C. The plates were placed in the freeze-dryer 
(Thermo Scientific Heto Drywinner DW8, Waltham, MA, USA) and the pressure was 
lowered. The plates were left in the dryer for 4 days with gradually increasing temperature, 
with an end temperature of 35C. The freeze-drying was stopped when the weight was stable 
for 1 hour. The freeze-dried material was grinded with a mortar and pestle until the material 
was a less coarse powder. The freeze-dried powder was stored in centrifuge tubes at -2C 
until analysis.

muffle furnace (Heraus D-6450 Hanau, Germany) at 600C. Dry matter and ash were

The composition of the total amino acid (TAA) was analyzed by Liquid Chromatography 
Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) and the freeze-dried sample. Proteins and peptides were

amino acids but estimates the content from the corresponding acids.

M KOH and further diluted to a dilution factor of 32, with a buffer solution. For identification 
and quantification of the amino acids, the samples and different volumes of standards were 
analyzed by LC-MS. The amino acids were separated using a BioZen 2.6m Glycan

(B). For elution a gradient of 100% buffer A was set from 0 to 2 min. Then buffer A was 
reduced to 95% and buffer B increased to 5% from minute 2 to minute 7. Buffer A was then 
decreased to 80% and buffer B increased to 20% until minute 8. From minute 8-12 Buffer A 
was reduced to 20% and buffer B increased to 80%. Lastly, buffer A was increase to 100%

4.9 Selection of organisms for fermentation 
4.9.1 Screening of microbial activity and isolation of bacterial cultures 
Screening of the microbial activity was performed with a dilution series of the stickwater 
containing dilutions of 1, 1/10, 1/100, 1/1000 and 1/10000, each dilution was mixed using a 
vortex-genie 2 (Scientific industries inc., New York, USA), before transferring 1 mL for the 
next dilution. 
Plates with different medium was prepared for the screening. M17 with 1% glucose, malt 
extract, 5% calf blood and De Man- Rogosa-Sharpe (MRS) agar were used as media. A set of 
5 agar plates with each medium was prepared and an additional set of M17 with 1% glucose 
for each dilution. The extra plates containing M17 were treated with either 1 mL of 0.1 g/L 
cycloheximide or 1 mL of 0.1 g/L of chloramficanol. The plates were left to dry before 
adding 1 mL of the designated dilution of stickwater to each plate. The plates were incubated 
for 48 h at 30 C. Growth of the different plates were evaluated and the colony forming units 
(CFU) were determined by manual counting.

4.9.2 Species identification 
Several single colonies from the screening experiment were selected and re-streaked on new 
plates (M17 and 1% glucose) and grown overnight at 30C. Biomass from each of the re-
streaked colonies were identified using a matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-

biomass from each colony was inoculated and grown in 5-6 mL MLS with 2% glucose and 
1% fructose overnight at 30C.   
Biomass from each of the colonies were added to the target plate in triplicate and left to dry. 
Formic acid was added to each spot on the target plate and then left to dry once again, before 
analyzing them in the MALDI-TOF Biotyper Sirius (Bruker Daltonics, Bremen, Germany). 
For each identification of the samples, the accuracy is specified by the software with a score 
between 1-3, for all successful protein extractions. A value between 1-1.69 is considered 
unreliable, scores between 1.70-1.99 means results can be trusted on genus level only. A 
score above 2, is a high-confidence identification and the best match can be trusted on both 
genus and species level. All identifications were made in technical triplicate, whereas only 
the highest score from each triplicate will be presented.

4.12 
Pilot-scale fermentation 
Before performing the pilot-scale fermentation fresh plates with stickwater and 1.2-1.5% w/v 
of agar were streaked with L. lactis and incubated overnight at 33C. Pre-fermentations were 
then performed in 4x50 mL bluecaps inoculated with single colonies from the plates and 
incubated overnight at 33C. Another pre-fermentation using 4x250 mL bluecaps were 
initiated by adding the content of the 4x50 mL bluecaps, then they were incubated overnight 
at 33C. 
The pilot-scale fermentation was performed using a 100 L stainless steel body reactor 
(Appendix B) equipped with a small stirrer, air inlet at the bottom, the air was let through a 
sterile filter with a pressure of approximately 0.5 bar. A sterile filter was put on top of the 
fermenter where air was led out. 
Prior to fermentation the fermenter was heated with steam in the heating cap. The 
temperature reached between 100-105C and this temperature was held for 30 minutes, to 
reduce the chance of contamination. 
Then 40 L of frozen stickwater was put in the fermenter. It was then heated up to 90C for 30 
minutes to pasteurize the stickwater. It was then cooled to 35C using cooling water in the 
heating cap. 
The stickwater was inoculated to an increase in OD600 of 0.1 using Equation 7 with a volume 
of 40 L ferment. Inoculation was done by opening the lid and pouring the fermented media 
from the pre-fermentation. 
The fermenter was equipped with a small stirrer and together with air flowing from the 
bottom, mixing and an aerobic fermentation was ensured, as oxygen should be available in 
surplus. 
The fermenter did not allow control of the temperature, so the fermentation was allowed to 
run without heating or cooling applied. Inoculation was done when the temperature of the 
stickwater reached 35C. Samples was taken from a valve in the bottom of the fermenter. A 
sample was taken before inoculation, after inoculation, the next day after 18 hours, after 24 
hours and after 42 hours, where the fermentation was stopped.  
For every sample the temperature was monitored, pH was measured, and a sample was 
filtered with sterile filter to be prepared for HPLC. The ferment coming from the bottom was 
too dense in particles to perform meaningful measurements of OD600.

Recovery of proteins by centrifugation 
When the fermentation was finished the content was run through a disc-stack centrifuge 
(LAPX 404 clarifier centrifuge, Alfa Laval, Lund, Sweden) with 9600 rpm. The Fermenter 
was connected to the centrifuge to allow continuous centrifugation of the fermented 
stickwater. The discharge was collected in a bucket and the rest was led back to the 
fermenter. To discharge the sludge the centrifuge flushes with water, adding an unknown 
amount of water. An operation error resulted in incomplete centrifugation. Therefore, both 
fractions were centrifuged at 2500×g for 15 min using a Thermo Scientific Multifuge X3R 
(Waltham, MA, USA), to obtain a slurry fraction and a supernatant. Both fractions were 
weighed. Then moisture, ash and protein content were measured according to section 4.3.1 
and 4.3.2, to conduct a mass balance.

5.1.1 Proteins and amino acids 
As the protein content is measured by Dumas, which measures total nitrogen, the protein 
content greatly relies on the protein conversion factor (KP) that is used. At the moment there 
is no consensus of a KP for BSFL in the industry, even though literature has suggested 
otherwise (Janssen et al., 2017). This is the reason a KP of 6.25 is used in this report. Dumas 
does not distinguish between protein and nonprotein nitrogen compounds, such as chitin, 
however the content of chitin in stickwater should be limited, while it is not soluble in water 
(Roland et al., 2023). Another measurement of protein is the Bradford method, which 
measures the soluble protein in solutions. Soluble proteins are of great importance for quality 
of the stickwater, as soluble proteins are easier absorbed by the fish and often possess 
functionalities in different applications in food, feed and sometimes pharmaceuticals (Pattan 
et al., 2019). The stickwater originating from BSFL is however measured to contain 1.85 ± 
0.01 g/L of soluble proteins, which is relatively low compared to stickwater originating from 
fishmeal productions, which can reach up to 4% soluble proteins on wet basis (Jao & Ko, 
2002). For fish feed applications there are some drawbacks including high contents of soluble 
proteins, as the feed comes in contact with water and can diffuse into the water and will be 
lost to the surroundings. A measurement of this can be made and will be discussed in later 
sections. 
A visualization of the protein size distribution was made by SDS-PAGE, with the raw 
stickwater, chitin spiked stickwater and chitin “solubilized” in water, this can be found in 
Figure 6. Several bands seem to be present with the most abundant proteins in the lower 
region around 15-20 kDa, however bands around 75 kDa and 37 kDa are also visible, which 
corresponds with the findings of the literature, that finds visible bands in the same area (Ravi 
et al., 2021). Comparing the results to SDS-PAGE ran on other fractions of the BSFL, it 
becomes clear that that most of the soluble proteins are found in the aqueous fraction, which 
is an important sign that there could be an increased quality of the proteins in stickwater 
(Ravi et al., 2021). Additionally, Leni, et al., 2020 found that 58% of the protein belong to 
muscle protein, 19% responds to cuticular proteins and 5% belongs to enzyme proteins.

5.1.2 N-free substances 
The N-free substances make up a great portion of the dry matter both for the whole larvae 
and the stickwater with 34.6% of the dry matter being N-free substances. To get a better 
understanding of the content the ACH and dietary fiber content of the stickwater can be 
found in Table 4. As seen in the table, no results are available from the literature as little 
focus has been given to the carbohydrate content of BSFL and their fractions. 
ACH makes up 20.61% of the dry matter, which is either present as simple carbohydrates or 
polysaccharides, such as starch that could be degraded by α-amylase and amyloglycosidase, 
to hydrolyze into simple sugars. On a dry matter basis 20.28% and 0.33% consist of D-
glucose and D-fructose, respectively. The relatively high content of D-glucose could be due 
to the presence of starches or other polysaccharides that contains glucose. The content of 
glycogen has been found to be around 4-5% of the dry matter, this could partly be a source of 
D-glycose and partly in the dietary fiber as no enzymes in the analysis kit debranches the α-
1,6-bonds (Romano et al., 2022). 
A HPLC analysis of the stickwater was desired to check which simple sugars would have 
been present and in which concentrations. Several attempts were made, but however 
unsuccessful, due to different circumstances. However, the content is expected to be low, as 
the BSFL is efficient in utilizing simple sugars as energy or converting them (Gold et al., 
2018).  
The dietary fiber content of 4.41% of the dry matter is relatively low, which was expected as 
the stickwater is liquid and most insoluble and heavy particles will have been separated by

5.2 Screening of present organisms 
A suitable organism for fermentation of the BSFL stickwater should have the following traits, 
safe to use (QPS), able to ferment filamentous, produce acid or lower the pH in other ways. 
Alternatively, the organism could be one that produce other products of interest or where the 
organism itself is of interest. The most obvious candidate for the purpose is LAB, which is 
used for other applications, where the goal is to lower the pH (Santamaría-Fernández et al., 
2017). 
As a first, it makes sense to explore which microorganisms are already present in the 
stickwater, as these should have a great chance of growing in the stickwater. However, it is 
worth noting that the stickwater was received at 80-90°C, where most organisms do not 
survive, so the present organisms have likely “contaminated” the stickwater later in the 
handling process or been present as spores that could withstand the temperature.  
A screening of present organisms was made used M17 with 1% glucose and agar as media, a 
media chosen to support growth of most organisms. Some plates were treated with either 
chloramphenicol or cycloheximide. The results from the 1:100 dilution can be found in 
Figure 8. The plates 7A and 7B with only media and stickwater, had 9000 and 8700 
CFU/mL, respectively. Plate 7C was treated with chloramphenicol, which inhibits growth of 
organisms other than yeasts and molds, thus excluding bacteria. This suggests that no yeast 
and mold are present in the stickwater and that only bacteria are present. Plate 7D was treated 
with cycloheximide, which is a preservative against fungi. No visible colonies are excluded 
when comparing with plate 7A and 7D, which suggest no fungi is present, however the 
content of CFU is lower when treated with chloramphenicol, with 7600 CFU/mL.

5.3 Selection of suitable organisms 
As the goal is to lower the pH to the pI of 4.3-4.5, 20 LAB strains, who secrete acid were 
selected as candidates for the fermentation together with A. sojae and the isolated B. 
licheniformis. A complete list of all the candidates can be found in Appendix A. 
As it is difficult to choose a suitable organism only based on the characterization of the 
stickwater. The way of selecting an organism was to test many different species, with 
different metabolisms, such as homo- and heterofermentative LABs. With the hope of finding 
a few or at least one good candidate. 
Bromthymol blue was used as pH indicator, which changes to green when pH is below 7.6 
and changes to yellow below 6.0. Figure 10, shows the four organisms that showed visible 
growth on the plates with BSFL stickwater.   
To verify that the present growth was the inoculated bacteria and not contamination, another 
MALDI-TOF was made. The scores were 2.22-2.29, 1.71-1.74 and 1.85-1.87 for C. 
divergens, L. lactis and B. licheniformis, respectively. The scores for L. lactis and B. 
licheniformis was in the low end, however accepted as it was the inoculated bacteria that was 
the best fit.

is however difficult to use a liquid media such as BSFL stickwater and as the aim is to reduce

Licheniformis has several applications in several industries, where it can provide functional 
enzymes or have probiotic functions etc. (He et al., 2023). B. licheniformis was therefore 
chosen to be used in further trials, together with the two LAB strains to check their potential

5.5 Pilot-scale fermentation 
The pilot-scale fermentation was set up with the parameters found in earlier experiments. The 
volume of stickwater was 40 L in a 100 L fermenter. The purpose of the pilot-scale 
fermentation was to show that it is possible to ferment in a larger scale, which is more 
relevant in an industrial setting and to recover enough product to use for extrusion trials. The 
fermenter was setup with stirring and air flowing from the bottom which secured that oxygen 
was available in surplus and a pressure of 0.5 bar was maintained inside the fermenter, to 
avoid contamination from outside the fermenter.  
As the fermenter did not allow control of temperature, the fermentation did not run at the 
temperature used in the small-scale fermentations. However, the optimal temperature for this 
strain of L.lactis has not been determined for fermentation in BSFL stickwater. As samples 
was taken from the bottom of the fermenter, the sample was very dense in particles, it did not 
make sense to measure the OD600, as the initial OD600 was already very high due to particles.

5.6 Recovery of proteins by centrifugation 
Centrifugation was chosen to recover the proteins and the rest of the dry matter, while earlier 
results had shown centrifugation to be more efficient in recovering proteins compared to 
membrane filtration for BSFL stickwater (Section 3.2.4). Furthermore, centrifugation 
recovers the solids in a slurry with lower water content the membrane filtration. The slurry is 
more useful in later applications. Additionally, a centrifuge would be easier to implement in 
Enorm Biofactorys current facilities compared to membrane filtration, which requires a lot of 
experimental work. 
To mimic a solution that would be relevant for the industry, a disc-stack centrifuge was used 
for the recovery of the dry matter. The centrifuge was connected to the fermenter, to make a 
continuous setup, with recovery of the slurry and recycling of the supernatant to the 
fermenter. This was done to recover as much dry matter as possible. However, the setup did 
not work as intended due to differences in the pressure needed to feed the ferment and 
pressure which the supernatant returned to the fermenter. This resulted in a separation that 
was not optimal. As much as possible was recovered from the disc-stack centrifuge and the 
fermenter, to be centrifuged in smaller amounts in another centrifuge. Due to the failed setup, 
some dry matter may have been lost in the disc-stack centrifuge and some water may have 
been added from the discharge.

5.8 Improvements and future perspectives 
An even deeper understanding of the content could be desired, especially about the 
carbohydrate content as the protein content of BSFL is in general well explored. A successful 
HPLC run could have helped cover this better, both to clarify the initial content of simple 
sugars and to verify that lactic acid was produced and if other metabolites were present. This 
could lead to a better selection of organism, even though the current strain of L. lactis has 
shown potential. To verify the goal and thus the pI of the proteins present in the stickwater, 
experiments with different pH value could have been conducted. This would have highlighted 
if it is important to reach a specific pH with addition of acid. But to reach the current goal of 
a pH in the range of 4.3-4.5, improvements in the fermentation process will be needed. This 
could possibly be achieved by scaling up the fermentation process in smaller steps, including 
e.g. a 1- and 5-liter fermentation with the possibility of controlling temperature and 
monitoring pH and/or oxygen throughout the fermentation. This would have given more 
information about the fermentation and how to scale it in the best way to achieve a better 
product after centrifugation.  
To further investigate the potential of inclusion, trials where stickwater or slurry replaces 
ingredients such as soybean meal or fish meal would be interesting. Together with a more 
depth analysis of the dry matter obtained in the slurry, eventhough it is expected to be similar 
to the dry matter of the initial stickwater, some interesting differences could possibly be 
found. Then finally check how the pellets are affected by the inclusion and find a commercial 
recipe that is suitable to include the slurry, probably a recipe with high moisture content, and 
test how the inclusion would affects different parameters regarding the fish and its growth.

6. Conclusion 
The project somewhat successfully demonstrated a potential process to valorize the dry 
matter in BSFL stickwater. The characterization of the stickwater confirmed a high protein 
content of 48.22% on a dry matter basis, with a significant part of the solids (34.6%) being N-
free substances.  
Amino acid analysis showed a content of total amino acids of 52.92 g/100g crude protein, 
with essential amino acids comprising 28.32% of the total amino acid content. Notably, 
methionine and lysine levels were low, aligning with the typical composition of BSFL meal. 
This is a critical consideration for fish feed applications, as these amino acids are often 
limiting for fish growth. 
The relative high content of N-free substances in the stickwater included 20.61% ACH on dry 
matter basis, likely consisting of simple carbohydrate or polysaccharides that can be degraded 
by α-amylase and amyloglycosidase. Additionally, dietary fibers constituted 4.41% of the 
drymatter. Chitin analysis showed levels below 0.3% of the dry matter, while 3.65% 
hexoamines were detected, probably as glucosamine, indicating the presence of the more 
soluble subunit of chitin. 
Microbial isolation from the stickwater identified B. licheniformis as the only organism 
considered QPS, highlighting the need for pretreatment before fermentation. A short trial 
demonstrated that pasteurization at 90°C for 30 minutes was sufficient to prevent microbial 
growth for 72 hours, consistent with treatments used in industrial settings. 
Among the tested organisms, Lactococcus lactis showed the greatest potential to reach the 
target pH of 4.3-4.5, with aerobic fermentation being the most effective. Small-scale 
fermentations indicated that a duration exceeding 24 hours influenced pH, although the pilot-
scale fermentation could have ended after 24 hours despite not reaching the target pH. 
Improved temperature control might have helped achieve the desired isoelectric point (pI). 
Centrifugation was selected to recover solids due to its ability to produce a lower moisture 
product and recover a higher fraction of solids. Initial attempts with a disc-stack centrifuge 
were unsuccessful, necessitating the use of a smaller 4-L centrifuge. This process yielded 
3.28% of the total mass as slurry, containing 11.91% of the total dry matter, 14.66% of total 
proteins, 6.00% of total ash, and 10.47% of other solids. The unsuccessful centrifugation led 
to dry matter losses, affecting the mass balance. 
The obtained slurry had a dry matter content of 16.82%, composed of 59.33% protein, 6.46% 
ash, and 34.21% other solids, primarily nitrogen-free substances. This slurry, along with the 
stickwater, possesses unique traits that could enhance BSFL meal. The low lipid content 
==PAGE:70==
Morten Rojek Marvig 
 
Master’s thesis, 30 ETCS  
S223157 
 
12th of August, 2024 
 
 
70
(1.33% in the stickwater) is advantageous, as BSFL lipids generally do not meet the fatty acid 
composition required for fish feed. The absence of chitin in both the slurry and stickwater is 
another benefit, as chitin can limit BSFL meal inclusion in some fish species. Furthermore, 
the high protein content in the slurry enhances its potential for inclusion in fish feed. 
While the slurry’s high water content may be a limiting factor for inclusion as-is, it could still 
be beneficial by substituting the water normally added during fish feed extrusion. Extrusion 
trials with maize meal formulations and either stickwater or slurry, both at 50% moisture 
content, showed that dry matter replacement with 4.00% and 17.11% from stickwater and 
slurry, respectively, had varying effects. No significant impact on WAI or pellet hardness was 
observed, but both formulation (P<0.05) and screw speed (P<0.001) significantly influenced 
WSI, with lower screw speeds and the slurry formulation leading to higher WSI. 
Additionally, pellets containing slurry sank more significantly over time compared to those 
with stickwater. The absence of gluten in the formulations likely explains differences 
observed with the literature. Nonetheless, this study demonstrates the feasibility of including 
stickwater and slurry in extrusion processes for fish feed.
