Abstract
Earth’s magnetic field arises from various sources, both internal and external. Internally, it includes
the core field from outer core convection, the lithospheric field from magnetized crustal minerals, and
the oceanic field from the movement of conducting seawater. Externally, it comprises the ionospheric
field from ionized upper atmosphere particles and the magnetospheric field from solar wind interactions.
Among these, the Ocean-Induced Magnetic Field (OIMF) is the least documented.
This project aims to construct realistic forward models of Earth’s magnetic field sources, to use these
to generate synthetic data at satellite altitude, and to test inversion and filtering strategies designed
to retrieve the OIMF as best as possible. New diagnostic tools and field modelling schemes have been
developed and used to assess the feasibility of extracting the OIMF. Ten years of real locations from the
Swarm satellites have been used to construct synthetic datasets based on the best available observation-
based models and simulations of the core, ocean, magnetospheric and ionospheric sources. These datasets
have been inverted for time-dependent internal field models with a knot spacing for the 4th order B-splines
of 60 days and a maximum spherical harmonic degree of 30 and subsequently processed to highlight the
ocean signal.
Our analysis indicates that the ocean signal can be reliably retrieved from satellite data, provided external
field signals can be sufficiently well corrected for. Non-regularized inversions showed successful retrieval
of the ocean signal, which can be separated from the core signal, for example by considering spherical
harmonic degrees greater than 15 in the annual period band. However, the presence of strong external
field signals significantly complicates the extraction process, even after applying spatial and temporal
filtering.
Regularization techniques minimizing norms of the radial field and radial field acceleration
calculated at Earth’s surface proved beneficial, enhancing the model’s reliability by suppressing strong
spurious features (zonal in quasi-dipole coordinates) from ionospheric sources and isolating the ocean
signal in specific spherical harmonic bands and geographic regions.
Initial tests with more advanced spatial regularization using an a-priori model covariance matrix based
on the ocean simulation are in theory promising but revealed instabilities. Future work should focus
on stabilizing these covariance matrices and optimizing regularization parameters.
Looking towards
applications with real satellite data the results obtained in this thesis emphasize the need to pre-correct
satellite data as best as possible for lithospheric and external field signals that will be the crucial sources of
leakage and noise. Appropriate model regularization and pushing model parametrization towards shorter
time and length scales may also help to reliably retrieve the ocean signal.

Introduction
Earth’s magnetic field originates in natural electrical current sources.
These are typically split into
categories of external and internal origin, with respect to the Earth’s surface, as seen in figure 1.
Figure 1: Sketch of the various sources of the Earth’s magnetic field (Olsen et al. 2015). Not to scale.
The internal field consists of: the core field, generated by convection in the outer core, the lithospheric
field, generated by magnetized minerals buried in the Earth’s crust and upper mantle, and the motionally
induced oceanic field, generated by the movement of electrically conducting saltwater in the Earth’s
oceans. The external field consists of: the ionospheric field, generated by ionized particles in the E and
F layers of the upper atmosphere, which are driven into motion by thermal gradients and gravitational
forces, and the magnetospheric field, generated by transfer of energy from the solar wind to charged
particles that are trapped within the region dominated by Earth’s magnetic field, giving rise to ring
currents in the radiation belts, and magnetopause and magnetotail currents on the sunward and anti-
sunward sides of the Earth (Lowrie 2007).
The least documented magnetic field source is the Ocean-Induced Magnetic Field (OIMF). Simulations
based on estimated ocean flow models and electrical conductivities (ˇSachl et al. 2019) suggest that the
OIMF mainly comes from the upper 2000m of the ocean. Here, the movements are larger, and tempera-
tures, and thus electrical conductivity, are higher (Irrgang et al. 2018). At Earth’s surface and satellite
altitude, the Antarctic Circumpolar current’s signature appears to be the largest time-averaged signal
(Stephenson & Bryan 1992, Vivier et al. 2004, Glazman & Golubev 2005, Manoj et al. 2006). However,
when it comes to the magnetic signal caused by ocean variability over months to decades, the situation
is less clear.
In the context of OIMF observations, significant progress over the past two decades has been achieved in
extracting ocean tidal signals from satellite magnetic measurements. The M2 tide is the most prominent
and straightforward to extract (Tyler et al. 2003, Sabaka et al. 2016, 2020), and it is currently provided
as an L2 Data Product by ESA’s Swarm satellite mission.
Additionally, the N2 and O1 tides have
been successfully extracted (Grayver & Olsen 2019, Saynisch-Wagner et al. 2021), and more recently,
the weaker Q1 tide has been effectively isolated (Grayver et al. 2024). In the latest research by Grayver
et al. (2024), spherical harmonic models of the internal magnetic field, tuned to a specific period, are
matched to observations from the CHAMP and Swarm satellites. This process uses only night-side data
and includes corrections for core and magnetospheric fields. The detected OIMF signals correspond well
with predictions from numerical simulations that incorporate established tidal flows. These findings are
5
==PAGE:7==
crucial as they demonstrate the reliability of retrieving the OIMF, even with its small signal amplitude,
from satellite magnetic measurements, through meticulous processing and modeling.
Considering the broader OIMF signal generated by ocean circulation, there has been only one published
claim of its detection, derived from CHAMP satellite observations (Golubev 2012). This finding has
yet to be independently verified. Recently, Hornschild et al. (2022) attempted to advance this research
by assuming the correctness of the OIMF signal from a simulation and adjusting for a spatially-varying
rescaling factor that they estimated. In summary, substantial work remains in the observational study
of the general circulation-driven OIMF.
Current observations of the OIMF primarily focus on the ocean surface.
To fully understand ocean
dynamics and changes, it is essential to have information on subsurface processes. Float programs, par-
ticularly ARGO, have provided significant insights in recent years (Roemmich & Gilson 2009). However,
our understanding of ocean currents, temperature, and salinity at various depths remains incomplete,
especially below 2000m. If we can reliably derive geomagnetic field models of the OIMF from satellite
measurements, it may be possible to obtain global information about the ocean at any depth.
This
capability could greatly enhance our ability to monitor the ocean and, consequently, the climate.
The aim of this project is to construct realistic forward models of the various sources of Earth’s magnetic
field, to use these to construct synthetic data at satellite altitude, and to test various inversion and
filtering strategies for retrieving the ocean-induced magnetic field. The goal is to assess the feasibility
of retrieving the OIMF from 10 years of synthetic Swarm satellite data and to recommend possible
strategies for extracting the OIMF from real data. This synthetic data will be based on the best available
forward models of the different magnetic field sources evaluated at real Swarm satellite locations. By
testing different inversion methods, as well as spatial and temporal filtering, in a case where we know the
truth, we hope to gain insights into how best to retrieve the global OIMF signal from modern satellite
measurements. In section 2 we present the methods used in this thesis, including spherical harmonic
field modelling and various useful diagnostics. In section 3 we present a survey of the spatio-temporal
characteristics of the field sources considered, with a focus on the properties of the expected OIMF, based
on a recent advanced simulation. In section 4 we define our synthetic data and chosen data selection, as
well as present selected statistics concerning our data distribution. In section 5 we present results of our
field modelling experiments. In section 6 we discuss various perspectives for future attempts to extract
the OIMF from satellite data and, finally, in section 7, we offer some conclusions.

2.1
Geomagnetic field modelling
In this thesis we will construct geomagnetic field models from satellite data with the aim of extracting
the ocean-induced magnetic field. We begin by setting out the method we use to construct geomagnetic
field models, with a focus on the time-varying internal field that includes the ocean induced magnetic
field.
2.1.1
Potential field modelling
In geomagnetic field modelling an initial assumption is usually that we are working in a region that is
current-free. This means that the current density is zero, J = 0, where magnetic field measurements are
collected and where an estimate of the magnetic field is to be made. Under these assumptions, according
to Maxwell’s equations, and making the quasi-static approximation so that displacement currents are
neglected, which is reasonable for timescales of minutes and longer considered here, the curl of the vector
magnetic field, B, is zero:
µ0J = ∇× B = 0
(1)

of a scalar magnetic potential, V , such that:

2.1.4
Field model estimation: Solving the inverse problem for the Gauss coefficients
The relationship between the predicted magnetic field data, dmod, and the model parameters, m, takes
the form:
dmod = g(m)
(13)
where g(m) represents the possibly non-linear dependence of the predicted field components at the time
and locations of interest on the model parameters, m. The determination of the model parameters from
the magnetic field measurements, dobs (which include the vector field components (Br, Bθ, Bϕ) and the
scalar field intensity F), takes the form of a non-linear inverse problem. This can also be an ill-conditioned
problem, since it involves downward continuation of measurements from satellite altitudes. Furthermore,
since there are field sources, particularly the magnetospheric, ionospheric and magnetosphere-ionosphere
coupling currents, that vary rapidly in space and time that cannot be captured by the model, the model
is incomplete, so the residuals between the model predictions and the data are often long-tailed and not
simply Gaussian distributed. A well-proven technique for solving this non-linear inverse problem is the
regularized, robust, iteratively reweighted least squares algorithm (IRLS) (Olsen 2002). This involves
iteratively minimizing a cost function that takes the form:

2.3
Spatial and temporal filtering
2.3.1
Spatial filtering
Spatial filtering is straightforward when working in spherical harmonic space. As described in section
2.1, we are already spatially filtering the model by picking a maximum spherical harmonic degree, and
thus a similar process can be used to select a range of spherical harmonic degrees and orders that are of
interest for a specific signal.
For example, the core is usually dominating the low spherical harmonic degrees. So we can truncate our
models to only look at them above a specific spherical harmonic degree (e.g. 15) if we wish to focus
on lithospheric or oceanic signals originating close to Earth’s surface. Similarly, we can do this for the
spherical harmonic order. For example, the external sources are usually dominating the low spherical
harmonic orders. So we can truncate our models at that minimum spherical harmonic order when seeking
to isolate the ocean signal.
2.3.2
Temporal filtering
The filtering of a digital time series is one of the most widely used signal processing operations. Temporal
filtering involves passing a certain range of frequencies in the signal, or equivalently a certain period band,
while removing others. The range of frequencies that are passed is called the pass band and the range
of frequencies that are removed is called the stop band. Signals that are corrupted by other interfering
signals or noise can in this way be retrieved if the frequencies of the interfering signals or noise are different
from those in the desired signal. In practical applications there is usually some overlap, resulting in only
a partial recovery of the original signal.
In order to filter based on frequencies we must first convert our digital signal from the time domain to
the frequency domain. We first remove the mean of the signal and again use the DFT based on equation
(22), which in practice is done through an FFT. A transfer function is then used in order to filter the
signal in the frequency domain, such that:
Y (f) = H(f) · X(f)
(31)
where Y is the filtered signal in the frequency domain, H is the transfer function for the chosen filter,
and f are the frequencies in the signal. Once filtered we can use the inverse FFT to return to the time
domain (Alexander & Williams 2016).

2.3.4
Strategy to minimize filter warm-up
Digital and linear filters will have some warm-up or phase distortion. This means that the filter will
not effectively attenuate the desired frequencies for some length of time in the beginning of the filtered
signal. In order to minimize this problem, in this thesis, we carry out the filtering via a specific zero-phase
forward-backward filtering, more commonly called the Gustafsson method (Gustafsson 1996).

in order to eliminate phase distortion. The Gustafsson method further improves this by implementing
specific initial conditions for the filter. Linear filters can always be expressed as:

Source models
In order to create synthetic data which is as realistic as possible for our experiments, we will be using

3.1

3.1.2
Numerical simulation of the OIMF
The OIMF is numerically simulated based on a time-domain, spherical harmonic-finite, element solver of
equations (39)-(44). The magnetic field in G and the scalar magnetic potential in A are expanded into
truncated series of vector and scalar spherical harmonics, respectively. The magnetic field is then further
parameterized in the radial direction using piecewise-linear finite elements discretized over the radial
coordinate. An integral formulation of (39), a Galerkin scheme applied over the spatial coordinates, and
a Crank-Nicolson time-integration scheme are then used to construct a linear system of equations. The
16

initial conditions are chosen to be the solution of the stationary limit of equation (39). The linear system
is then assembled and solved using MPI parallelization in the time coordinate, referred to as the ElmgTD
time-domain numerical scheme (Vel´ımsk´y & Martinec 2005, Vel´ımsk´y et al. 2019).
For the ocean source model we employ results from a numerical simulation of the ocean-induced magnetic
field based on the ocean state reanalysis ECCOv4r4 (Fukumori et al. 2021) by Prof. Jakub Vel´ımsk´y
from Charles University Prague (jakub.velimsky@mff.cuni.cz). The model has been derived by using
WOA13 monthly mean ocean conductivities (Tyler et al. 2017), sediment and lithosphere conductivities
(Everett et al. 2003), and mantle and core conductivities (Grayver et al. 2017) and solving the induction
equation using the ElmgTD time-domain numerical scheme (Vel´ımsk´y et al. 2019).
ECCOv4r4 uses
satellite observations of sea surface heights, sea surface temperatures and time-variable gravity, as well as
in-situ data from floats (including the ARGO program) and hydrological stations which are assimilated
into the MITgcm numerical ocean model. It is believed to represent well the large scale ocean circulation
at least in the upper 2000m of the ocean. The electrical conductivity model is based on 50 ocean layers
following the ECCO discretization, 40 sediment and lithospheric layers, and 116 1-D core and mantle
layers. The resulting magnetic field model has a maximum spherical harmonic degree of 120, but here
we only use it up to maximum spherical harmonic degree 60, since this covers the signals likely to be
detectable at satellite altitude.
The simulated OIMF is provided in daily values, but unfortunately only from 01/01/2013 to 31/12/2017.
Since we want to study the oceanic signal in 10 years of Swarm data from 01/01/2014 to 31/12/2023 it
was necessary to extend the simulated time series such that it repeats in an appropriate manner for the
desired 10 year time range. This has been done by repeating the time series twice with a 30 day window
of smooth transition using linear weights between the merges. We then remove the first year and the last
three years resulting in a time series from 01/01/2014 to 31/12/2024.

3.2
Core
For the core source model we will make use of a geodynamo simulation derived by Prof. Julien Aubert
(Aubert 2023) that has been pushed into an Earth-like configuration by assimilating data from the
CHAOS geomagnetic field model (Finlay et al. 2020) and includes physically realistic patterns of secular
variation (Finlay et al. 2023). The spherical harmonic field model extracted from the outer edge of the
dynamo model was provided to a maximum spherical harmonic degree of 30 and is available in a time-step
of 18.2625 days from 01/07/2001 to 30/06/2040.
The dynamo model includes high frequency variations due to inertial waves in the core that are expected
to be filtered out by Earth’s electrically conducting mantle. We therefore removed these high frequency
waves by using an order 8 Butterworth low-pass filter with a cut-off period of 100 days.

3.6.4
Adopted strategies
For this project we will be focusing on the annual period band. The strategy to deal with the core for
this project will consist of spatial and temporal filtering in order to focus on short length scales and
relatively rapid time scales (above spherical harmonic degree 15 for the annual period band). Similarly,
the strategy to deal with the magnetosphere for this project will consist of spatial and temporal filtering,
since the magnetosphere should have less power than the ocean signal above degree 15 in the annual
period band.
However, simple filtering by spherical harmonic degree and period band will not be sufficient, since the
ionosphere still dominates here. In this thesis we will in the following explore more sophisticated strategies
to isolate the ocean signal, for example, model inversion strategies including regularization appropriate
for oceanic sources, and focusing on specific regions where the ocean signal is strong.

Synthetic data
4.1
Data setup
In this project we have built new synthetic satellite vector magnetic field datasets, including a superposi-
tion of the predicted signals from the various source models described in section 3. This was constructed
using real satellite locations taken from the Swarm mission. The Swarm satellite mission is a minisatel-
lite low-Earth-orbit constellation designed to survey Earth’s magnetic field from space (Friis-Christensen
et al. 2006, Olsen & Floberghagen 2018). Swarm consists of three satellites, two of which are flying
at lower altitudes, measuring the east-west gradient of the magnetic field, and one satellite flying at a
higher altitude, typically about 40km higher, in a different local time sector. All three satellites carry a
vector field fluxgate magnetometer and an absolute scalar magnetometer in order to produce well cali-
brated, absolute measurements of the Earth’s vector magnetic field. The Swarm mission was launched
on November 22, 2013, and is still active, at the time of this projects completion (ESA 2012).
(a)
(b)
Figure 24: (a) artist’s rendition (ESA 2012) and (b) orbital configuration (ESA 2011) of the Swarm constellation.
In this project we make use of vector and scalar data as well as North-South vector and scalar gradients,
and East-West vector and scalar gradients. The synthetic data is produced by evaluating the source
models at the real satellite locations and times and adding the vector components together.
4.2
Data selection
In this section we describe down-sampling and selection of the Swarm satellite data that is typically done
to produce a dataset suitable for geomagnetic field modelling. The original Swarm data files used for
this project were down-sampled from 1 sec. to 15 sec. intervals, all data for which the Kp index, a global
range index measuring geomagnetic disturbances at mid-latitudes, was above 30 and the change of the
Dst index, which measures the disturbances associated with the magnetospheric ring current, was above
3nT/hr has been removed, data residuals to the field model CHAOS-7.16 with values above 500nT and
data flagged as poor quality have been removed, and sunlit data within 10 deg. QD-latitude (Laundal &
Richmond 2017) of the magnetic equator has been removed.
From there we further convert all data pole-ward of 55 deg. QD-latitude to scalar intensity data, which
are less influenced by field aligned currents, remove all dayside data pole-ward of 55 deg. QD-latitude
later than 4:00 and earlier than 20:00 local time, remove all sunlit data equator-ward of 55 deg. QD-
latitude with a smaller zenith angle than 100 deg. We further remove all data for which the Kp index is
above 20 and the change of the RC index is above 2nT/hr. We further down-sample to 1 min. intervals
for Swarm B and 2 min. intervals for Swarm A and C, except for Swarm A and C East-West gradients
which are sampled at 1 min. intervals, and then finally, for gradient data, we remove all pairs of data
that are separated by more than 50 sec. The Swarm A, B, and C gradients are computed as differences
in the along-track direction of measurements and the Swarm A and C East-West gradients are computed
as differences at the closest latitude. These selection criteria have previously proved useful in modelling
core, crustal and ocean tidal fields (Olsen et al. 2017, Finlay et al. 2020, Grayver et al. 2024). The only

novel choice here is to include nightside times at high latitudes, even in polar summer. We do this as we
wish to have global coverage on timescales as short as 2 months, if we exclude all sunlit data there are
long gaps at high latitudes during the polar summer.

5
Results
In this chapter we collect the main results of the thesis which are inversions of synthetic satellite data
build from various source combinations. The aim here is to document which parts of the ocean magnetic
signal can be retrieved, and to show what field model parameterization, inversion and post-processing
filtering techniques are useful for retrieving the ocean signal. We will also document the challenges found
in trying to retrieve the ocean signal in the case when it is known.
5.1
Basic modelling setup
All models are derived in the time period 2014-2024 with a knot spacing for the 4th order B-splines of
around 60 days. It includes both a parameterization of the internal field, to spherical harmonic degree 30,
and the external field, using the same parameterization as for the CHAOS model. The starting conditions
for the internal field is set to be CHAOS-7.17 from degree 1 to 14 and the mean of the ocean source model
from degree 15 to 30. Data selection, as described in section 4.2, was employed for all models. The choice
of the truncation at maximum spherical harmonic degree 30 was first set in order to have room above
spherical harmonic degree 15 to isolate the ocean. The choice of 4th order B-splines and 60 day knot
spacing, in a 10 year period, where chosen based on initial tests and limited based on the memory on our
high performance computer cluster.

5.6
Experiments with regularization
Finally, in a further effort to suppress the power from the external sources we now present a final inversion
result, based on the same dataset SYN SWARM 10y OCIM containing ocean, core, ionospheric, and
magnetospheric signals, but implementing spatial and temporal regularization based on norms of the
radial magnetic field and radial secular acceleration averaged over the Earth’s surface and over time, as
described in section 2.1. We do not use robust weighting for the results presented here; we carried out
test inversions including Huber weighting but found this has little impact for the synthetic data tested
here, which we are still able to fit better than the nominal data errors allocated to the synthetic data,
based on expectations from real data.
During the project we carried out a large number of experiments exploring different choices of the regular-
ization parameters λ0 and λ2, including attempts to construct L-curves. However, based on comparisons
with the synthetic truth, we finally decided to choose the regularization parameters so that we obtained
a similar level of power as the expected ocean signal, considering both the unfiltered and annual period
band signals. We found this was possibly by setting λ2 = 2.5e2 (nT days−2)−2 and λ0 = 4e4 (nT)−2.
We applied the regularization only at high spherical harmonic degree where we expect the ocean signal
to be present. The degree-dependent regularization was implemented using a cosine window. For the
spatial regularization, this cosine window is 0 for all degrees below degree 15, then gradually increases to
1 between degree 15 and 20, and is equal to 1 above degree 20. The same cosine window was also used
for the temporal regularization, but it is implemented instead between degree 10 and 15. This enables us
to regularize the degrees where we expect to find the ocean signal and ensure that there are no sudden
jumps in power.

6
Discussion
6.1
Retrieval of ocean signal from synthetic data
During this thesis we have demonstrated, using a series of tests involving different combinations of field
sources, the feasibility of retrieving the ocean signal from satellite magnetic data in different scenarios.
Test model 04B, see section 5.3, where we used synthetic satellite data derived from the ocean source
model only, showed that the amplitude of the expected ocean signal from data at satellite altitudes
was sufficient for the ocean signal to be retrieved at all the length scales (spherical harmonic degrees
1 to 30) and time scales (5 years down to 140 days) captured by our chosen model parameterization.
This definitively answers the question as to whether the ocean signal is large enough to be measured
by satellite, in the (admittedly unrealistic) case that signals from all other sources could be eliminated.
We retrieve similar amplitudes, global and regional features, as well as temporal variation, within the
bandlimited representation of our field model, when compared to the ocean source model.
Test model 04E, see section 5.4, where we used synthetic satellite data derived from the ocean and core
source models, demonstrated that by considering spherical harmonic degrees greater than 15 it is possible
to reliably separate the ocean signal from the core field signal when considering the annual period band.
Following this combination of spatial and temporal filtering, we are once more able to successfully retrieve
the amplitudes, global and regional features, as well as temporal variation present in the source model.
This result is expected to hold for other period bands as well, with the spherical harmonic threshold
becoming higher for longer periods and lower for shorter periods, as suggested by figure 21b.
Test model 05K, see section 5.5, where we used synthetic satellite data derived from ocean, core, iono-
spheric and magnetospheric sources, demonstrated that without any correction or removal of external
sources it becomes extremely difficult to reliably retrieve the ocean signal. Even after extensive spatial
and temporal filtering, we are able to retrieve very little of the ocean signal. There seems to be pollu-
tion of the estimated internal field signal, for example in the annual period band at spherical harmonic
degrees 15-30 where a strong ocean signal is expected, by two spurious signals. One consists of quasi-
dipole zonal features and is of ionospheric origin, and the other involves high amplitude ringing near
the ocean-continent boundaries, and is due to currents induced in the conducting Earth and ocean by
magnetospheric variations.
Test model 06B, see section 5.6, where we again used synthetic satellite data derived from ocean, core,
ionospheric and magnetospheric sources but apply temporal and spatial regularization during the model
construction, shows us that using suitable regularization can help to retrieve some aspects of the ocean
signal, in particular by ensuring a sensible level of power in the spatial and temporal bands where we
expect to see the ocean signal. Furthermore, spatially filtering at a minimum spherical harmonic order
of 6, seems to suppress the strong pollution by quasi-dipole zonal features, which is thought to be due
to signals of ionospheric origin. The regularized model 06B, considered in the annual period band, at
spherical harmonic degrees 15 to 30 and considering orders great than 5, shows that we can, in specific
regions where the annual signal is large, retrieve similar amplitudes, regional features, and temporal
variations as seen in the ocean source model. On the other hand it was not possible to retrieve the ocean
signal in all locations, in particular there was remaining contamination from the magnetospheric induced
field near the continent-ocean boundaries.
Overall our tests suggest that retrieving the ocean signal from satellite magnetic data is difficult when
strong external fields are present in the data. High quality corrections for external and related induced
fields, and more sophisticated regularization methods are needed.
6.2
Limitations of the conducted tests
The main limitations of this project stem from the currently available simulations of the OIMF and from
computational constraints.
As mentioned in section 3.1, the currently available version of the ECCO ocean reanalysis model, see
section 3.1, only spans the time period from 01/01/2013 to 31/12/2017. Since we want to test inversions
derived from 10 years of Swarm data, we have had to make do with duplicating the time series. A longer
simulation is currently in the works by Prof. Jakub Vel´ımsk´y, however, it was unfortunately not ready in
time for this project. Dr. Vel´ımsk´y is also currently carrying out other simulations based on alternative
63
==PAGE:65==
ocean and Earth conductivity models, these will be important for assessing possible error bars on the
expected ocean signal.
In this project, we have chosen to focus primarily on the annual bandpass, however, other period bands
exist in the signal. Other period bands, that can still be captured by the knot spacing in the B-splines,
might be less contaminated by the external signals.
For example the intraseasonal period band, 2-5
months, might prove to be worthwhile.
Arguably the largest limitation of the models we have constructed stems from the relatively large knot
spacing of 60 days and the restriction of the maximum spherical harmonic degree to 30. The ocean
is expected to have considerable variability on monthly and shorter timescales, and on length scales of
hundreds of kilometers and less, for example in the form of mesoscale eddies (Chelton et al. 2011), so
having a shorter knot spacing and a higher truncation degree might allow us to retrieve more of the ocean
signal in the presence of other sources. The reason our knot spacing is 60 days and we were restricted to
a maximum spherical harmonic degree of 30, is because that is how many model coefficients, in a 10 year
time period, the memory available on nodes of our high performance computer cluster could allow. Two
approaches to fix this issue could be considered.
The first is to better parallelize the building of the normal equations matrices and their solution. Currently
in Multifit, when computing the matrices GT
i WGi and GT
i W (dobs −g (mi)) from equation (17), the
computation is parallelized only in terms of chunks of data and not in terms of parts of the model (i.e.
subsections of the matrices are not built in parallel, the entire matrices are built in parallel and then
accumulated). This means that the full matrices still need to be stored on one machine, where the inverse
problem is solved. If we could parallelize the solve in such a way that these matrices can be stored across
several machines and only the model parameters, after the solve has completed, is stored on one machine,
we could bypass the memory issues.
The second approach was to solve for several shorter inversions, a few years at a time, and then combine
them afterwards using some kind of smooth transition. This can be done formally through a Kalman
filter and smoother scheme applied sequentially to short time periods of data, rather than treating all
the data at once.
6.3
Future work and recommendations
Looking forward, I suggest approaches to some of the issues encountered during this project.
Some
approaches that we did not attempt have already been mentioned in section 6.2.
6.3.1
Convolution window for magnetospheric induction
Our tests in section 5 suggest that one of the major problems when trying to isolate the OIMF in the
annual period band are the magnetospheric induced signals. We currently make use of a relatively short
convolution window of 120 days to compute the 3D induction based on the magnetospheric currents. For
future work, it is suggested to redo this with a longer convolution window, for example 720 days, in order
to check that the induced signal in the annual band has been accurately computed. However, matrices
with longer convolution windows were unfortunately not available at the time of this thesis.

6.3.3
Recommendations for working with real satellite data
For this project, we have, as a first step towards isolating the ocean signal in satellite magnetic data,
focused on synthetic tests where we know the properties of the ocean signal and confound signals from
other sources beforehand. Unfortunately, we do not have this luxury in the real world. We here summarize
our recommendations for how best to retrieve the ocean signal from real satellite data, based on the tests
performed during this project.
Based on the synthetic tests presented in section 5, we highly recommend pre-correcting the satellite
data to remove as best as possible the signals due to external currents and associated Earth-induction,
especially the ionospheric fields whose signals overlap with the length scales and timescales of the expected
ocean signal, or solve for these sources during the inversion. In addition, the best available model of the
global lithospheric field should also be removed as a pre-processing step. This should remove leakage
from the static field and decrease the noise in both the annual and semi-annual period bands due to the
external sources. We also recommend using dark and quiet data selection, in order to minimize the power
from the ionosphere and magnetosphere, however, the level of strictness in the criteria should be tested.
Furthermore, we recommend using robust Huber weighting in the real data case, as this should suppress
the high frequency noise from the external sources that can’t be fit by the model. In order to suppress
the power from the core, we recommend temporally filtering to focus on period bands from interannual
to monthly, depending on what can be captured by the knot-spacing in the B-splines, and then spatially
filtering the model at a minimum spherical harmonic degree of 15-20 depending on the chosen period
band. Afterwards, further spatial filtering using a minimum spherical harmonic order, for example 6 in
the annual period band, might prove useful, depending on how well the external sources are corrected
for. Provided the external field can be adequately corrected for, it should be relatively straightforward
to isolate the ocean signal from the core.
In terms of the field modelling, the choice of a shorter knot spacing will allow for shorter periods to be
captured while a higher maximum spherical harmonic degree will allow for shorter length scales to be
captured. At shorter periods we expect the ocean signal to be relatively larger while at shorter length
scales the external signals (although, not necessarily the Earth-induced signals) are expected to be less
prominent.

7
Conclusions
The aim of this project was to test inversion and filtering strategies designed to retrieve the ocean-induced
magnetic field (OIMF) using realistic forward models of various sources of the Earth’s magnetic field at
satellite altitude. During the project we have developed new diagnostic tools and field modelling setups
and regularization schemes that have been implemented into DTU’s Multifit field modelling software.
These have been used in a series of experiments to assess the feasibility of retrieving the OIMF from 10
years of synthetic Swarm satellite data and to recommend strategies for extracting the OIMF from real
data.
The following source models have been implemented. For the ocean magnetic signal, we used an advanced
numerical simulation based on the ECCOv4r ocean state reanalysis (Fukumori et al. 2021) that was carried
out by Prof. Jakub Vel´ımsk´y. For the core, we utilized an Earth-like dynamo simulation (Aubert 2023),
which incorporated observations from the CHAOS geomagnetic field model (Finlay et al. 2020). For the
lithosphere, we considered the LCS-1 model (Olsen et al. 2017), but excluded it to avoid leakage. For the
ionosphere, we used the Comprehensive Inversion Swarm model (Sabaka et al. 2002, 2015, 2018), and for
the magnetosphere, we used the CHAOS model series (Olsen et al. 2014, Finlay et al. 2020) with internal
3D induction data from Dr. Alexander Grayver (Grayver et al. 2017, 2021).
Using various combinations of these source models, we constructed synthetic datasets based on 10 years
of real Swarm satellite locations. Our tests involved a realistic set of vector, vector gradient, scalar, and
scalar gradient data.
Inverting synthetic satellite data containing the ocean signal alone showed an excellent retrieval of the
ocean signal within the spatio-temporal band covered by the model parameterization. Including the core
source we found that restricting attention to spherical harmonic degrees above 15 effectively isolates the
ocean signal in the annual period band. However, including all sources confirmed that the presence of
strong external field signals indeed complicates the retrieval of the ocean signal. Implementing spatial and
temporal regularization of the model at the Earth’s surface, with spatial filtering at spherical harmonic
order 6, helped isolate the ocean signal, demonstrating that ocean signals can be extracted in some
regions even in the presence of external fields. The regions in question had a correlation of 0.715 and
0.687, respectively, when compared to the input ocean simulation, indicating partial retrievals.
An initial test model of a new type of spatial regularization based on the expected statistics of the
ocean signal, showed some promise but revealed instabilities in the a-priori model covariance matrix.
Future work in this direction should focus on stabilizing covariance matrices and optimizing regularization
parameters. When using real data, we recommend pre-correcting the satellite data as best as possible
using existing models for external, and related induced, fields and for the lithospheric field. It is especially
important to correct for the ionospheric fields, in order to minimize leakage and noise in the annual and
semi-annual bandpass. Care should also be taken when interpreting estimated ocean signals close to
the ocean-continent boundaries where signatures of currents induced in the Earth by magnetospheric
variations can be significant.

9.5
List of functions written
The following is a short description of each function in the diagnostic toolbox written for this project.
Several other miscellaneous scripts were written for this project, which will not be included here, since
the main output is the toolbox.
• filter signal: Filters a digital signal based on filter type, filter method, and period band or
cut-off period.
• temporal spectrum: Computes the temporal spectrum of a digital signal based on the spectrum
type.
• get colors: Defines a list of colors to use when plotting for consistency.
• plot frequency response function: Plots frequency response for a filter based on the function
for a digital signal with variable frequency.
• plot warm up signal: Plots the warm-up of a filter for a digital signal.
• Gauss coef: Outputs the list of Gauss coefficient names based on the maximum spherical harmonic
degree.
• plot B temporal spectrum: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a component of the
magnetic field based on a set of Gauss coefficients.
83
==PAGE:85==
• plot Gauss temporal spectrum: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a Gauss coefficient
based on a set of Gauss coefficients.
• compare B temporal spectrum: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a component of the
magnetic field based on a set of Gauss coefficients before and after being filtered.
• compare Gauss temporal spectrum: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a Gauss coeffi-
cient based on a set of Gauss coefficients before and after being filtered.
• correlation: Computes the correlation between two sets of Gauss coefficients.
• plot B timeseries models: Plots the time series of a component of the magnetic field based on
two sets of Gauss coefficients after being filtered.
• plot B timeseries: Plots the time series of a component of the magnetic field based on a set of
Gauss coefficients before and after being filtered.
• plot Gauss timeseries models: Plots the time series of a Gauss coefficient based on two sets of
Gauss coefficients after being filtered.
• plot Gauss timeseries: Plots the time series of a Gauss coefficient based on a set of Gauss
coefficients before and after being filtered.
• qd to geo: Converts a set of Gauss coefficients from the QD domain to the geographic domain.
• geo to qd: Converts a set of Gauss coefficients from the geographic domain to the QD domain.
• zero m: Zeros all the Gauss coefficients below a set minimum spherical harmonic order.
• movie global field: Produces a global movie of a component of the magnetic field after being
filtered based on a set of Gauss coefficients and a time range.
• compare time longitude fields: Computes a table of the RMS difference between two time-
longitude plots based on two models, the RMS of the true model, the variance explained, and the
correlation.
• plot time longitude field: Plots a time-longitude plot of a component of the magnetic field
after being filtered based on a set of Gauss coefficients and a longitude and time range.
• decimal year to datetime: Converts a decimal year to a datetime object.
• get season: Outputs the season based on a datetime object.
• plot regional field seasons: Plots a latitude-longitude plot for the four different seasons in
a given year of a component of the magnetic field after being filtered based on a set of Gauss
coefficients and a longitude and latitude range.
• plot global mean and rms diff: Plots two global maps of the mean and RMS difference of a
component of the magnetic field between two models after being filtered based on two sets of Gauss
coefficients and a time range.
• plot global field diff: Plots a global map of the difference of a component of the magnetic field
between two models after being filtered based on two sets of Gauss coefficients and an epoch.
• plot global mean and std field: Plots a global map of the mean and standard deviation of a
component of the magnetic field after being filtered based on a set of Gauss coefficients and a time
range.
• plot global field: Plots a global map of a component of the magnetic field after being filtered
based on a set of Gauss coefficients and an epoch.
• ocean mask from topo: Outputs ocean mask with the Black and Caspian Sea and the Hudson bay
set as continents based on topography data.
• save temporal spectra SH: Saves the frequencies and power of the temporal power spectral density
as .dat files based on a set of Gauss coefficients, the spectral density type, and chosen spherical
harmonic bands.
84
==PAGE:86==
• IRLS grid: Computes the robust mean or median using iteratively reweighted least-squares based
on a data set, latitudes, and a chosen weight distribution.
• plot temporal power distribution: Plots a histogram of the temporal power spectral density
distribution of a component of the magnetic field with different methods for computing the mean
based on a set of Gauss coefficients, a target period, and the spectral density type.
• plot global temporal power: Plots a global map of the temporal power spectral density of a
component of the magnetic field based on a set of Gauss coefficients, a target period, and the
spectral density type.
• plot temporal spectrum models: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a component of
the magnetic field for several different models based on several sets of Gauss coefficients, a chosen
spectral density and mean type, and chosen spherical harmonic bands.
• plot all temporal spectra: Plots all the temporal spectral densities of a component of the mag-
netic field based on a set of Gauss coefficients and a chosen spectral density type.
• plot temporal spectra SH: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a component of the mag-

• plot temporal spectrum: Plots the temporal power spectral density of a component of the mag-
netic field based on a set of Gauss coefficients and a chosen spectral density and mean type.
• table of amplitudes: Computes a table of typical amplitudes for a model before and after being

• plot mean and rms diff: Plots the mean and root mean square difference between two models

• plot sensitivity: Plots the time-averaged sensitivity between two models after being filtered

• plot correlation: Plots the time-averaged correlation between two models after being filtered

• plot degree correlation: Plots the time-averaged degree correlation between two models after
being filtered based on two sets of Gauss coefficients.
• plot spatial spectrum models: Plots the time-averaged spatial power spectra for several different
models before and after being filtered based on several sets of Gauss coefficients and chosen period
