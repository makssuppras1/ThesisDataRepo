Abstract
Growing concerns about the effect of climate change have played a major part in changing
electric energy production and consumption. The changes can be seen everywhere but
are noticeably present in the transportation sector, where a majority of newly registered
cars for personal transportation in Denmark are EVs. This fact both presents challenges
and opportunities for the collective grid and EV owners. This thesis will primarily investi­
gate the potential cost benefit individual EV owners could achieve, by utilizing a range of
different charging scenarios.
The scenarios will base their findings on mixed­integer linear programming models, mini­
mizing the cost of charging for 50 real­world household charging stations. The models will
be expanded sequentially, adding to the objective function and constraints, while always
keeping the same energy goal of the original data so the optimization poses no hindrance
to the EV owners’ usage of their car.
It was found from the case study that the Baseline scenario already exhibits average
relative savings of 23% when compared to an uncontrolled benchmark. The optimized
scenarios are V1G, V2G, V2G with SOC, and V2H, all operating with perfect foresight.
• The average relative savings of V1G is 37% compared to the uncontrolled scenario.
• Average savings from ordinary V2G is less than 1% larger.
• V2G with initial SOC optimization raises the relative savings slightly to 39% com­
pared to the uncontrolled.
• V2H results in a lower relative savings of 34%, due to additional costs factored into
the benchmark. However, this scenario yields the highest absolute savings overall.
The additional equipment cost for making the V2G and V2H scenario physically possible
is not included in this thesis but could prove detrimental to any additional savings found
by these models.

Introduction
The electricity market has been ever­evolving. From the first generators powering light­
bulbs to today’s world where electricity is all around us and powers our lives [1]. The
evolution continues, especially in these past few decades where the effects of climate
change have become more apparent. Awareness about climate change has, in general,
shifted the electric energy production field, from large power plants that rely on burning
fossil fuels, to a more diverse inclusion of many smaller renewable sources to such a
degree that investments in green energy solutions are twice as high as investments into
fossil fuels [2]. This is a drastic but needed change to reduce emissions, in the opera­
tion of power grids, as 24% of Danish carbon emissions originated from electricity and
heat production in 2022 [3]. This is a change that will require solutions to combat the un­
predictable nature of renewable energy and maintain the balance and reliability that our
modern everyday life is built upon.
Large­scale sentiments towards transitioning into a greener future have been the topic
of political discussion all around the world [4]. Denmark is no exception, with some of
the highest levels of climate awareness in the European Union (EU) among its citizens
[5]. Approximately 55% of Danes believe they care more about climate change than the
government does, driving public demand for action [6]. This momentum has contributed
to the rise of low­emission technologies and an increased understanding of how individual
choices impact the environment [7]. Electrification of everyday life is part of this transition,
be that from changing old oil heating to electric alternatives or replacing one’s method of
transportation to electric vehicles (EVs) that can be fuelled by renewable sources. This
all changes the lives of many people and in turn, affects many households’ electricity
consumption.
The EV market has expanded significantly in the past decade. Going for a drive, or being
in traffic in general, shows this expansion quite clearly. EVs are taking a larger part of
newly registered cars on Danish roads, making up 51.5% of all new cars in 2024 [8], which
is a result of both the general consumer awareness of personal transportation impact on
climate change and also from the rapid development of longer reaching cars [9]. Now
EVs are not only practical for people with small commutes, or people who have time to
charge on the way, but also for people travelling further every day who now have enough
energy capacity to get home and plug in [10].
Getting home and charging the EV so it is ready for the next day presents both opportuni­
ties and challenges. The ease of not having to wait in line at a public charging point and
the many offers currently on the market for home charging, makes it lucrative for many
Danish EV owners to install their own integrated EV charger [11]. Home charging adds an
additional and significant energy consumption, something that can be seen on the elec­
tricity bill. Many consumers in Denmark are becoming more and more aware of the price
of electricity, both its size and how it fluctuates, and are actively taking steps towards re­
ducing their bill. With 17% saying that they use energy saving modes on electric devices
and 30% saying that they mainly use electricity during the cheapest hours of the day [12].
Shifting the time period for when an electric car is charged is, therefore, an opportunity
to lower your electricity bill. This is a tedious task if performed manually, which is why
automatic corrections are needed. However, electric cars offer several technologies that
could potentially increase the savings even further. A Vehicle­to­grid (V2G) integration is

an option that has been on the drawing board for many years. Frameworks for implemen­
tation are being developed in several countries [13], while car manufacturers are working
on EVs’ internal equipment so that discharging via the charging socket is possible [14].
The result of such equipment will also open up more possibilities, the primary one being
charging directly to the household (V2H). Here, the consumption from, for example, a
washing machine or a stove will be covered by the energy in the electric car’s battery,
which could potentially contribute to greater savings by avoiding grid consumption in the

This thesis will follow the trend of lowering the overall cost of electricity for a household.
The focus will be on the opportunities created for the individual EV owner, mainly the cost
savings from bidirectional smart charging via the V2G approach, where energy will be
bought cheaply to be sold at a later point, and offsetting the load profile of the home with
a V2H scheme. The thesis will not go into details about the physical implementation, but
some of the effects smart charging and discharging to the net could have will briefly be
discussed. Optimization based on real­world data will simulate realistic scenarios for the
chosen case study, while also being the basis of a cost comparison that the results and
conclusion are built upon.
This all leads to the following research questions:
• What is the potential of optimized charging compared to the baseline based
on real­world data?
• How can optimized charging be mathematically represented?
• How much cost savings will individual households experience with different
optimization schemes?
The report will answer these questions by going through the current state­of­the­art re­
garding V2G, V2H, and energy arbitrage in Chapter 2. The methodology of the thesis
will then be explained. This thesis handles several optimization models which differ in
complexity. Chapter 3 will therefore dive into the mathematical expressions behind the
models while highlighting significant variations. This chapter will also explain the equa­
tions used for validating the results obtained from the optimization. Chapter 4 introduces
the case study on which the later analysis will take place. It will present an overview of
the data used and the final implementation of the models. This case will introduce the
V1G scenario, optimized smart charging based on electricity prices, the V2G scenarios
both with and without an initial state of charge (SOC), and lastly the V2H scenario with
an added load profile. These scenarios will have their own assumptions, but the general
cost­saving goal will be the same. This is followed by the results section Chapter 5 going
through the impact of V2H and the other models, concerning their ability to reduce the
overall cost of the individual households. Plots, graphs, and excerpts will be commented
on in this results section, while key takeaways will be discussed more in­depth in Chap­
ter 6, alongside any relevant limitations and assumptions. The discussion is followed by
chapter Chapter 7 concluding the current business case for energy arbitrage by EVs and
V2H.

2
State­of­the­art overview of V2X/V2H
This chapter examines existing research on the technologies relevant to V2G and V2H
systems. It explores the necessary equipment for enabling bidirectional charging, along
with various control strategies used to optimize energy flows. Additionally, the general
concept of energy arbitrage—where EV owners can leverage price fluctuations to reduce
costs—will be discussed.
Towards the end of the chapter, this thesis’ contributions will be outlined in comparison to
existing research, highlighting key differences and advancements. Finally, the expected
outcomes of the study will be presented.
Engaging in an investment market to profit from price fluctuations is known as an arbitrage
strategy [15]. This concept also holds true for investment into the energy market by buying
and selling energy, where [16] explores different energy storage technologies in the form
of pumped hydro storage (PHS), compressed air energy storage (CAES), and Lithium­Ion
(Li­ion) batteries with regard to energy arbitrage. This study also underlines the impor­
tance of visibility in the grid price differences that allow energy arbitrage, as fixed price
schemes would render time­dependent arbitrage useless. Likewise has [17] explored the
feasibility of Li­ion batteries to partake in energy arbitrage and finds them to be econom­
ically viable in the current EU market. Grimaldi et al. [18] contrast this profitability of
battery energy storage systems (BESS) with a case study based in America, factoring in
battery degradation, thereby finding energy arbitrage to be economically unviable.
The potential carbon emission reduction from electrifying the transportation sector has
been identified by [19], estimating a net reduction of 1.8 GtCO2−eq for the world. Similarly,
[20] has found potential reductions of up to 47% when using real data to analyse the
electrification of heating and transportation. Research focusing on EVs as a utility will
have to keep the underlying functionality of the EV in question in mind. This is what [21]
is doing while looking at the possibility of electric delivery fleets’ ability to partake in the
energy arbitrage market. The study finds that energy arbitrage is feasible, but is heavily
influenced by the fluctuation of prices. Feasibility is also shown by [22] who in addition to
having a total charge requirement at the end of a charging session also mathematically
models the degradation that the EV’s battery will be subject to.
A hard requirement for EVs to offer additional energy arbitrage service is the ability of the
EV’s charging equipment to be bidirectional, as explained by [23]. Discharging to the grid
will be referred to as vehicle­to­grid (V2G) and allows for the selling of electricity enabling
energy arbitrage. The bidirectional flow of electricity also enables vehicle­to­home (V2H),
as [24] explains. This study further notes that supplying the load of a household will usually
happen at lower power levels than V2G, something the charging equipment will have to
support. This lower power level could course some efficiency problems as demonstrated
by [25]. [26] has investigated the critical elements needed for V2G and their associated
cost, finding that the additional complexity of bidirectional charging equipment is currently
an important price factor. This price will have to drop to make V2G more viable as identified
by [27]. The current need and, therefore, investment cost of bidirectional DC­chargers
have also been identified by [28], who created an overview of other challenges like legal
framework and consumer doubts. Companies are working towards overcoming these
challenges like [29] who have been legally certified to perform V2G in the United Kingdom.

2.1
Contribution from this thesis
This study builds upon previous contributions by developing several optimization models
and applying them to newly acquired data on both electricity consumption and EV charging
patterns. The models will be implemented in Python using the Gurobi library to conduct
the optimization. Additional data processing and analysis have also been carried out
in Python, leveraging libraries such as Pandas for data manipulation and Matplotlib for
visualization.
The new EV charging data consists of real­world recorded measurements from chargers
across Zealand, spanning the entire year of 2023 with a 15­minute resolution. In other
words, this dataset links charger IDs to time series data detailing all AC power levels
experienced by each charger. The consumption data used, on the other hand, originates
from UK households in 2012. These datasets provide a realistic foundation for a case
study on a Danish household, enabling further exploration of cost­saving opportunities for
individuals participating in V2G and V2H scenarios.
The case study is expected to show cost savings when smart charging is utilized, with
further savings occurring with each additional expansion of the optimization model. The
results will enhance the need for continued development regarding the implementation
cost and functionality of V2G and V2H.

3
Methodology
This thesis utilizes a Mixed­Integer Linear Programming problem (MILP) to facilitate the
optimization of charging and discharging, based on data from existing charging profiles
and energy prices. This chapter starts by going through the general structure of the mathe­
matical formulation and then goes into the specifics of the different constructed scenarios.
3.1
The general optimization problem
The optimization is performed from the perspective of individual EV owners. The opti­
mization will therefore run once for each session of each charger over a whole year. A
session is defined as the time frame from the vehicle’s plug­in time, to its plug­out time.
We denote the plug­in time as t = 1 and plug­out time as t = T where 1, . . . , T is the whole
time frame in which the EV is available to the charger. These intervals can be different
lengths for all the sessions. The time­series data is in a 15­minute resolution that gives a
total plug­in time of 1 hour the size T = 4.
The objective will for all the chargers being investigated be a minimization of the over­
all cost. As mentioned, the sessions will be optimized one at a time. This results in no
session having any influence upon any other session and thereby no charger having any
influence on other chargers.
3.1.1
Overall constraints
Each optimization problem aims to minimize the overall cost for each session while ful­
filling some charging constraints. It is important not to neglect the fact that an EV is a
vehicle and the primary function of a vehicle is transportation.
This means that the sum of charged energy in the optimization session should equate the
total recorded energy for that session in the original dataset. This manner of constraint
contributes to the transportation functionality of the EV not being disturbed by the addition
of optimized charging behaviour. The charged energy at the end of a session is the same
as in the real data. This will ensure that the optimization follows any physical limits that the
EV might have regarding total effective battery capacity so that the charged energy never
exceeds the concrete limit. The constraint is placed on the energy added to the battery
but the optimization changes the power level of the charging. A conversion is therefore
made, using the fact that each time­step in the interval has a fixed length.
Another general limitation is placed on the maximum/minimum charging power levels.
These are limited to the largest recorded power in the session and a predefined lower
boundary defined in the IEC standard [31] respectively. This is again to maintain the
EV’s actual physical limits. Likewise, there are physical conditions that apply to the car’s
charging and discharging stages. The connection technology does not make it possible to
be in more than one stage at a time. The internal logic that controls whether the car should
receive or release energy will be implemented with binary variables. This translates into
two binary variables for the more complex models, capturing the states that an EV can be
in while plugged into a charging station. The variables are Ych for charging and Ydch for
discharging. They will each be multiplied with a corresponding part in the other different
constraints. As they can only take the values of 0 or 1 and their sum should be 1, the
decision of, for example, charging the car (Ych = 1) results in the other binary variable
being 0. This entails that each term relevant to either Ydch will be 0 and not contribute to
that time­step.

We first have the defined objective function (3.1). As mentioned, the goal of the optimiza­
tion is to minimize the cost associated with the charging. Finding the total cost of charging
for a session is done by taking the sum of each time­step’s charging power multiplied by
the size of the time­step, again multiplied by the cost at that specific time­step. The billed
cost is for the energy used, so the transformation from power to energy with the ∆t is
needed.
The first constraint is (3.2), describing the desired energy to charge in the vehicle. The
charging power is again translated into energy, before being summed over the whole
session. The constraint then states that this sum should be equal to the desired energy to
charge, ∆E. This again, as mentioned, stems from the desire to ensure that the charged
energy at the end of the session is the same as in the baseline.
There are limits imposed on the charging capabilities. We describe these limits with (3.4)
and (3.3). These are both grounded in reality, with Pmax being the largest recorded power
level in the corresponding session and Pmin being a pre­defined value determined by the
EV’s on­board charger’s technical capabilities, as described in [31].
Binary variables are also needed for the logic of the equations. Ych is a decision variable
restricted to 0 or 1. It stipulates if the EV should be charging or not. Its effect is seen in
the two previous constraints, where taking the value of 1 or 0 directly influences the power
level decision variable Pch,t.

V2G | Vehicle­to­Grid
The first expansion to the algorithm is the addition of the ability to discharge the EV’s

market spot­price. This all means additional decision variables in the form of Pdch and
a corresponding binary variable Ydch. The goal remains the same and the optimizations
are, therefore, still designed to minimize the charging cost of each session.

4.1
Input data structure
This section will explain all the input data used in the optimization models, regarding

assumptions will be tied to their corresponding dataset as they occur.
4.1.1
EV charging data structure
All the optimizations are based on real­world data. This is a key feature of the thesis.
The EV charging data is real recorded power levels observed by wall plug­in charging
stations situated at homes all around Zealand. The data is gathered during the entire
span of 2023, with data entry every 15 minutes. This results in 35040 recorded power
levels for each charger, in the unit kW. Before this thesis started, the dataset was filtered
and reduced to 50 chosen chargers, that all exhibit high levels of data quality and range
from low to high consumption. Each charger is identified by an ID of 4 numbers, which
will remain fixed throughout the subsequent chapters.
The raw power level data is accompanied by two additional datasets, both expanding
on the charging data of all 50 chargers. The first is a time­series data structure like the
power levels but displays the charging state from the outlets’ point of view. The states
are represented by either 0 for no EV connected, 1 for when an EV is charging, and 2
when an EV is plugged in but no charge is taking place, accounting for all the states an
EV charger can be in. The second is a summarised dataset of all the ”transactions” that
have taken place in the charging data, again for each charger. These transactions will
be referred to as a session, with an associated time of plug­in, plug­out, and total energy
charged in that session.
4.1.2
Electricity prices
Another dataset that is used for all scenarios, contains prices corresponding with each
time­step. There are two entries; one for the price of buying energy and one for the
selling price gained when discharging energy to the grid, with 35040 values each. The
selling price is the recorded spot price of the DSO Radius for the year 2023 in the unit
DKK
kWh. The buying price is constructed from the spot price by adding relevant tariffs, making
up the bulk of the buying price. Tariffs are time­dependent and will add a different fee in
expended high­consumption time intervals compared to expected low­consumption time
intervals. They also differentiate between the winter and summer half of the year making
it more expensive to use electricity when it is expected to be cold.

Construction of the smart charging algorithm is the first part where optimization is imple­

scenario. The charging will for every time­step in the session decide the optimal power
level, and create a modified data frame with the new values. This data is extracted for all
the chargers, and saved to be used for the later analysis.
The smart charging algorithm was given in the beginning of the thesis. It has been im­
plemented with the Gurobipy python library and is the natural stepping­stone towards the
more advanced algorithm for controlled charging behaviour, as the comparatively reduced
complexity allows for easier validation when the model is expanded.

4.2.3
Bidirectional (V2G) Smart Charging
The first major expansion from the single­directional smart charging algorithm is the addi­
tion of bidirectionality with grid discharge capability. The modification will, as mentioned,
take place on the smart charging model while utilizing the exact same data. This includes
that the model has the same energy demand goal and the same limiting factors on the
power level. The injection of AC power to the grid is assumed to happen flawlessly, with
no wind up or down necessary when going from charging to discharging and vice versa.
There will, however, as can be seen from the formulation in Section 3.3, be added effi­
ciency to the power level in the constraints. This will be an interpretation of the losses
associated with converting the grid AC power to the battery DC power and the other way
around too. It is assumed that this efficiency is 90% for both AC­DC and DC­AC, resulting
in a 10% loss [32]. The internal energy goal is likewise translated to a DC measure using
an efficiency, that is assumed to be the same AC­DC efficiency of 90%. The revenue from
selling is determined by the day ahead market spot price for each time­step, as defined
by the Danish regulation.
Every part of the algorithm that previously only consisted of a decision value for charging,
will now have an additional discharging decision variable value that will also be chosen
by the optimization. As the amount of energy in the plugged in EV’s battery is unknown
during the entire available session, a few assumptions will be attached to the SOC of the
car, so any physical limits are kept in check at all times. These are mentioned in Sec­
tion 3.3 when discussing the limits of the discharge.
An expectation from this model is larger cost reductions for most chargers but nothing too
significant. This happens because, as explained in Section 4.1.2, there is a noticeable
size difference between the buying and selling prices. This fact should make the model
prioritize charging cheaply, as V1G, in a time­step instead of selling. There is also the
fact that any amount discharged from the EV, will have to be charged at a later point in
the session for the EV to reach its energy goal. If a session is able to enter V2G and
discharge to the grid, this will happen with the aforementioned efficiency. An efficiency
that in a sense is applied twofold, as it will already have been applied when adding the
energy. This all effectively limits the economic profitability of V2G.
4.2.4
Vehicle­to­grid with state­of­charge
As mentioned in Section 4.1.4, some data regarding SOC and battery capacity was ob­
tained. This allows for a new model, where an initial SOC and a battery size can be given
to the model as input parameters, from which the optimization makes its decisions. The
additional energy at the start of the charging session, will, as mentioned in Section 3.4,
allow the model to discharge immediately, potentially boosting the cost reductions. The
SOCmin and SOCmax were chosen to be 10% and 90% respectively as those are the same
values used in the creation of the SOC profiles. It is again expected that this expansion
should increase savings, as a larger part of the charging interval is available for discharg­
ing, due to not having to wait for energy to be added to the battery. The SOC profiles

4.2.5
Vehicle­to­Home
Finally, data for home appliance power profiles will be included in the optimization, with
the goal of optimally supplying the load profiles and the EV’s energy demand, all with re­
gard to minimizing the overall cost of the household.
The household should be understood as the combination of appliances and EV demand.
This new goal is not a complete mimic of the other scenarios’ objective functions but in­
cludes some of the same characteristics. A load profile has been added to the model,
and, therefore, an additional cost. This cost is absent in the other scenarios, something
to keep in mind when the results are being compared.
The choice that the optimization algorithm will have to make for each time­step, is now, as
it was previously, if the EV should charge or discharge into the system, with the addition
of being able to dedicate some of the discharge towards serving the appliance load. Any
time­step in which the load is not being completely provided for by the EV, is forced to
buy energy from the grid to serve the load. The same conversion factor between alter­
nating current and direct current, used in the V2G scenario, is used when discharging to
the home appliances with this model. For this model to be applicable in an actual home
with an EV, the internal wiring and protective equipment of the household would have
to comply with not only general regulations but also the additional complexity of multiple
electricity supply lines.
The model can not change the load demand at any point during the charging session and
the price of buying energy from the grid to service the load is chosen to be the same used
for purchasing the energy going to the EV.
This model will firstly be used on the real data described in the section above, and then
it will be used to do a sensitivity analysis of constant load profiles. These profiles will for
all time­steps be the same value. The different chosen values are 1kW, 3kW, 4kW, 5kW,
7kW, and 9kW.
The model is still expected to experience cost savings, for many of the same reasons as
the other models. Direct comparison to the other models would however be unfair, since
the added cost from the appliance load will skew the basis of comparison.

Discussion
Optimized charging significantly reduces costs for most investigated chargers. The ini­
tial implementation of standard smart charging provides the largest reduction in costs
compared to uncontrolled charging, while further model expansions yield only marginal
additional savings. This is evident in the summarized box plots in Figure 5.15, where the
statistical differences between optimized models remain minor
The diminishing benefits of increased model complexity become even more apparent
when compared to baseline charging behaviour. While all optimized models yield greater
savings, the difference between current real­world charging patterns and the optimized
models is relatively small. Both V2G cases result in slightly higher savings over the year,
but the improvement is not particularly significant compared to unidirectional smart charg­
ing. Another layer of inconvenience for the discharging is the price difference in the equip­
ment required. Most current available AC chargers for homes are incompatible with V2G
and those that can accept discharge are around twice as expensive. The EV itself also
needs internal hardware that can facilitate the DC­AC conversion again reducing poten­
tial profitability. Various companies are working on cheaper solutions to mitigate these
limiting factors for V2G, so the 100­500DKK extra yearly savings when compared to V1G
become more favourable.
Reliable data is crucial for data­driven decision­making, and this study’s algorithms are
no exception. The datasets span an entire year with recorded values for all 15­minute
intervals creating potential for accuracy problems. Scale that with 50 chargers and the
likelihood of no drop in quality occurring practically disappears. Some examples from the
recorded data are the charging session lasting up to 18 days while sporadically charging
at 2.7kW. This could be true and reflect some internal loss compensation with a mostly
stationary vehicle, or it could show a loss of accuracy in the recorded data.
Data for SOC values marked the first instance of expanding the models to include mea­
sures not directly recorded. These are derived from an independent optimization process
based on the same charging data but are still not directly linked to the real­world EVs from
which the data is recorded. Any results that rely on SOC data will subsequently be limited
to following the inherited assumptions like each charger always charging the same EV.
Data for appliance load profiles are likewise limited as they do not correspond to the actual
households from where the EVs are present. They are assumed to represent a normal
Danish household, on the same level as the charging data represent normal charging, so
coupling them together should therefore not pose a significant problem.
Any savings the V2H model has found will have to be held up against the additional cost
associated with an electrical installation capable of accepting energy directly from the EV,
alongside the aforementioned investment in bidirectional charging equipment as with the
V2G scenarios. The absolute savings are potentially higher for this scenario since the
additional price of buying electricity for appliances can be offset. Results from constant
appliance load profiles suggest that larger loads yield greater absolute savings, strength­
ening the case for V2H in these manufactured households. Since the upgrade to V2H
compatibility is a one­time expense independent of household consumption, the long­term
financial viability of V2H could improve. However, assuming a constant load is unrealistic
since most large appliances will follow some specific duty cycle and generally be limited

in which hours of the day they are active. The results could differ when using real­world
appliance data with large loads.
Determining optimal charging based on prices places a significant weight on said prices.
The buying price is determined from the Danish tariff model of 2023 where the added
cost per kWh is different throughout the day and year. These tariffs are added to the
spot prices of Eastern Denmark. This thesis assumes that the models can sell at the
spot price with no added tariffs, resulting in the buying price of any given hour being
larger than the same hour’s selling price. The results could be influenced by adding tariffs
and other negative factors to the selling price, diminishing the potential revenues from
selling. Furthermore, the spot price could change with the more widespread adoption of
smart charging schemes, since the spot price is roughly determined by the expected grid
consumption. So accumulating all EV charging to the same hours would present a reason
for regulatory action regarding grid prices. This complexity is disregarded in this thesis.
The relative significant savings achieved beyond the baseline data are inherently ideal­
ized. The models exhibit perfect foresight concerning all relevant variables and uncertain­
ties, so any results are optimal. Real­world variations in the size of the charging interval
or price fluctuations are disregarded leading to less robustness towards uncertainty. Any
comparisons between the baseline and the smart charging scenarios will have to account
for this fact. The results are based on real­world data making them valid as a possible
outcome, but factoring in more uncertainties would further solidify the determined sav­
ings. The impact magnitude of changes to the deterministic nature of the models is left
untested in this thesis.

Conclusion
This thesis has worked with several comprehensive datasets measuring time series data
for EV charging, cost of electricity, and household appliance load profiles. These datasets
were used to construct several charging scenarios, each aiming to minimize their respec­
tive overall costs through mixed­integer linear programming. The results demonstrated
the ability of existing households to individually save money, by utilizing either flexibility
in the charging interval or from diverting into becoming a prosumer, selling energy back
to the common grid.
The analysis revealed that the vast majority of the charging stations in the originally
recorded data are already using control schemes, delaying the charging time to more
cost­effective time slots. Summarized costs of the baseline chargers were compared to
their counterpart in an uncontrolled reconstruction and major savings were observed. The
first smart charging algorithm was used on the same chargers achieving even higher sav­
ings. The same pattern, as in the baseline, of delaying the time interval for when charging
takes place emerged, just more explicit. This underlines a significant potential for opti­
mized charging compared to real­world baseline and the significant savings an EV owner
could achieve.
It can also be concluded that V2G improves the savings but that initial knowledge about
SOC and battery capacity is needed to increase the savings noticeably from the V1G sce­
nario. The high savings will be reduced when factoring in additional equipment costs for
V2G compatibility; a relatively substantial investment. Diminishing long­term profitability
from battery degradation as a consequence of the larger energy throughput associated
with V2G’s energy arbitrage would also play a key factor in reducing the savings ob­
served. As a result, the viability of energy arbitrage for individual households using their
EVs remains questionable but leans towards infeasibility.
On the other hand, discharging energy directly toward household loads (V2H) achieved
the highest absolute savings. However, like V2G, it also requires consideration of the
added costs associated with bidirectional charging infrastructure. The potential for fi­
nancial returns increases with larger household loads, enabling a faster payback period.
Nevertheless, further research with real­world household data is needed to accurately
assess the current potential of V2H.
Several areas could benefit from further investigation. An aspect that will need more re­
search is price sensitivity for individual households. Tariffs are prone to change and have a
huge impact on the decisions made by the models. Analysing how large these impacts are
and prospects for future changes would help solidify the investment opportunity of V2G
and V2H. Additionally, future studies should consider installation costs for bidirectional
charging infrastructure in comparison to unidirectional systems. The cost associated with
the degradation of the battery cannot go undetermined as this alone could remove any
economic feasibility found with discharging the EVs. Without a thorough assessment of
this factor, the long­term economic feasibility of V2G and V2H remains uncertain.
