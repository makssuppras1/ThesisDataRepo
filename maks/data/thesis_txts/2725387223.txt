Abstract
This thesis shows a study of the feasibility of modifying a three-bladed 15MW turbine
to use partial pitch (PP) blades and individual pitch control (IPC). A model for tuning a
Coleman transform-based individual pitch controller is developed and first tested with a
full pitch turbine. The 15MW reference wind turbine (RWT) from the International Energy
Agency (IEA) is modified into three different cases of partial pitch, with the pitch bearing
placed at varying distances along the blade-span. Turbulent simulations are then run
in the aeroelastic simulation software HAWC2 over the entire operational range of the
turbine, and the resulting loads of the PP turbines and the full-pitch turbine are compared
in a parametric study to determine the ideal position of the pitch bearing. The model for
tuning IPC is modified to to account for PP, and IPC is then added to the PP turbine.
Turbulent simulations are run again for the IPC case, and the loads and fatigue loads are
compared with the ones from the original full-pitch case and the PP case without IPC.
The use of PP resulted in some load alleviation for out of plane bending moments and
yaw-bearing pitch, but higher loads for in-plane blade bending moment and tower fore-aft
moment. IPC proved as reliable with a PP turbine as with a full-pitch turbine, and resulted
in great alleviation of out of plane bending moment fatigue, as well as some decrease in
yaw-bearing fatigue, but at the cost of much greater pitch activation and higher torsional
fatigue at the pitch bearing.

1
Introduction
As of 2018, electricity generation accounted for 32% of human-caused greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions [1]. In order to lower emissions and thus the increase of climate alter-
ing radiative forcing in the atmosphere, renewable sources of energy generation are of
interest, as by their nature they do not add to carbon cycle. Wind energy, both onshore
and offshore, has been shown to be the form electricity generation with the least GHG
emissions per unit of energy produced [2], at a similar level to nuclear energy, and prices
have been steadily falling, recently reaching competitive levels in terms of levelised costs
of electricity (LCOE) [3].
The recent invasion of Ukraine by Russia and the following threats by president Vladmir
Putin of ceasing the distribution of natural gas to Europe in response to EU sanctions
against Russia have heightened the calls for energy sovereignty in the EU. The produc-
tion of energy without the need of importing fossil fuels from politically unstable regions
is seen increasingly as a matter of national security, and thus so is the transition towards
renewable energy. For example, Germany has hastened its targeted to fulfil all its elec-
tricity needs with renewable sources by 2040, now aiming to do so by 2035, in an effort
to become less dependant on Russian gas [4].
As rotor size continues to increase for wind turbines [5], specially with the current push for
offshore turbines where economic factors of the construction of platforms and of mainte-
nance of turbine components make it advantageous to increase power production per tur-
bine, alternatives in turbine design are sought to address challenges such as the greater
relevancy of gravity loads, greater variation of wind speed along the rotor disc due to tur-
bulence and wind shear, extreme loads due to storms, and difficulties in transporting the
blades [6]. For the latter two of these, a promising solution is the use of partial pitch, where
the pitch bearing is located somewhere along the blade rather than at the root, which has
been shown effective in decreasing extreme loads during turbine shutdown and allows
the blade to be transported as two separate pieces and then assembled at the turbine’s
location. For the variation of wind speed along the rotor, the use of individual pitch control
(IPC) is can be added to the traditional collective pitch control (CPC) and can significantly
reduce fatigue on the blade and other components.
1.1
Partial Pitch
Partial pitch turbines have the pitch bearing installed partway through the the blade’s
length instead of at the root. Thus, the blade is divided in two sections: one with fixed
pitch from the root until the pitch bearing, and one with variable pitch extending from the
pitch bearing to the blade’s tip.
Partial pitch is a fairly recent concept in wind turbine blade design, and thus few articles
have been written on the subject, and all of them on two bladed turbines. A series of
studies was conducted at the Risø National Laboratory of Denmark on the use partial
pitch for load alleviation under standard conditions in two-bladed turbines [7] [8] [9]; this
research program included detailed CFD analyses of the effects of the introduction of PP
such as the presence of a gap between the inner and outer blade, and through it the
aeroelastic software HAWC2 was modified to account for the partial pitch concept. The
use of partial pitch for a two-bladed turbine was also shown to lead to significant load
alleviation in cases of extreme winds by avoiding cases where the entire blade is subject
to full lift or full drag [10].

In 2014, Taeseong et. al conducted a study comparing simulation results from HAWC2 of
a two-bladed partial pitch turbine with experimental data collected on-site, showing great
agreement between the two.
The potential for load reduction makes the introduction of partial pitch to the large turbines
of the offshore market, today around the scale and power output of the 15MW Reference
Wind Turbine (RWT) from the International Energy Agency (IEA) [11], to be worth evalu-
ating. The consistency of HAWC2 results with experimental data on the operation of PP
turbines opens the possibility for studies on partial pitch turbines to be conducted through
aeroelastic simulations with the same tool.
1.2
Individual Pitch Control
Individual pitch control refers to the individual pitching of each turbine blade. As rotors
become larger, discrepancies between the loads of each blade increase due to wind-
shear, both vertical and horizontal, and turbulence.
IPC schemes typically take as input the out of plane (OoP) blade root bending moments
(BRM) for each blade and output pitch increments that are added to the collective pitch an-
gle that is requested from the pitch actuator [12]. Differing architectures for the individual
pitch controller exist, such as
IPC, if implemented correctly, can completely remove the peak in loads at the frequency
of the rotor rotation (called the 1P frequency) [12], leading to a great decrease in fatigue
loads in the blade. Reductions at the yaw bearing loads are also seen, though much
lower than the reductions in blade loads, as yaw bearing loads mainly come from 3P
excitations, which are not targeted by common IPC schemes [13]. At the same time, IPC
greatly increases pitch action, increasing fatigue in the pitch bearing and torsional moment
fatigue in the blade, presenting a hurdle to commercial implementation of IPC.
1.3
Combining PP with IPC
Though studies exist both for IPC and for partial pitch rotors, there is a lack of literature
on the intersection of these two topics, looking at the effect of IPC in a partial pitch tur-
bine. With this in mind, it is proposed to assess the advantages of IPC and partial pitch
for a 15MW turbine, around the power of the largest existing turbines, by conducting sim-
ulations with the HAWC2 software comparing a standard wind turbine with CPC with the
same turbine modified to use partial pitch, with and without IPC.

Theory
This chapter describes the theoretical background used in the present study. This mainly
encompasses the aerodynamic models, structural models, and control theory.
2.1
Wind Models
An ideal description of the turbulent wind flow around the turbine would be given by direct
solution of the Navier-Stokes equation by direct numerical simulation, DNS [6]. This would
require a discretization of the domain with elements around the size of the surface rough-
ness of the turbine, and thus would be completely unfeasible computationally. A less
taxing method is Large Eddy Simulation (LES), where the solution to the Navier-Stokes
equation is approximated by only directly solving turbulence for large scale vortexes and
the turbulent behaviour at the smallest scales is not solved directly, but rather described
by empirical models. Still, LES is too computationally expensive and not suited for run-
ning the large number of simulations required to assess the turbine’s behaviour at many
different conditions and with varying parameters.
Instead, it is more practical to use models based on Blade Element Momentum theory,
which can be modified to include dynamic inflow, dynamic stall, and turbulent wind.

2.1.2
Dynamic inflow
BEM assumes steady conditions for the application of momentum theory. To deal with the
reality of unsteady winds and non-instant response of the flow to changes in the turbine
such as pitching of the blades, models of dynamic inflow and dynamic stall are added.
Dynamic inflow models are based on calculations of thrust performed throughout the rotor
disk.
Other than the standard radial subdivisions used with BEM, HAWC2 divides the rotor disc
in azimuthal sections [14], as seen in Figure 2.4. The thrust in a given grid point is found
by calculating the thrust with the local wind speed at that grid point and the local induced
velocity at the same radial distance for the two nearest blades. Linear interpolation of
Ct to the two nearest blades is then applied using the azimuth distance between the grid
point and the blade .
Figure 2.4: Illustration of the polar greed used by HAWC2.
2.1.3
Dynamic Stall
The MHH Beddoes dynamic stall method is used. It accounts for the Theodorsen effect,
torsion rate terms and added mass terms, as well as trailing edge stall [14].

In partial pitch turbines, the pitch bearing is located not at the root, but further along the
blade, as shown in Figure 2.5. This means that from the root to pitch bearing, the blade
is fixed, maintaining a constant pitch angle. Tuning the pitch controller for a partial pitch

3.1
Evaluating advantages of Partial Pitch
The introduction of PP changes the entire load profile on the turbine, altering the loads
along the whole blade-span and with it also the loads perceived by the hub, shaft, and
tower. This, of course, is highly dependent on the positioning of the pitch bearing along
the blade.
A study is conducted to evaluate possible advantages and disadvantages from using a
PP turbine in place of a full-pitch turbine. Turbulent simulations over the whole operational
range of the turbine are ran for the base full-pitch case and for three different cases of
partial pitch. In Case 1, the pitch bearing is positioned at 33% of the blade length, in Case
2 it is positioned at half the blade length, and in Case 3 it is positioned at 66% of the blade
length, as illustrated in Figure 3.1:
Figure 3.1: Different cases of pitch bearing positioning that were tested.
For Cases 2 and 3, the collective pitch controller is tuned again so that the power output
of the turbine remains the same. Tuning of the CPC controller would normally be done
through HAWCStab2, but HAWCStab2 does not allow for the simulation of more than
one aerodynamic rotor at a time, which is necessary to model the inner and outer rotors
simultaneously for a PP turbine. The tuning is then done manually through trial and error.
3.2
Scaling of the pitch system mass
In the full-pitch turbine, the mass of the pitch system is included in the mechanical proper-
ties of the hub. For the PP turbines, the mass of the pitch system needs to be accounted
for separately as it is placed in the new position of the pitch bearing along the blade, and
it significantly changes the dynamic behaviour of the blade.
As only a part of the blade needs to be pitched, the capacity of the pitch system can be
decreased, and thus a lighter pitch system can be used. The report for the 15MW RWT
does not contain detailed information on the pitch system. In the present study, the mass
of the pitch system is assumed to account for 40% of the mass of the hub, therefore
76,000 kg among the three blades. Once the mass of the pitch system is calculated, it is
subtracted from hub’s mass. To calculate the mass of the new pitch system, linear scaling
in reference to the blade length is used. The mass of the outer blade (blade section that is
pitched) is assumed to be proportional to its volume, which is assumed to be proportional
to the cube of the blade length. A scaling factor SF is defined as the length of the outer
blade divided by the length of the full blade:

3.3.1
Determining the parameters for IPC tuning
Tuning of the IPC controller by the previously described method requires determining
the constants τ, Dθ and C0. This is done by analysing data from HAWC2 simulations.
Using a MATLAB-HAWC2 interface, a simulation at constant wind speed is run for 300
seconds of in-simulation time, with a 0.005s time-step. At the 250s mark, after the turbine
dynamics have reached stability, a step in pitch of 1° is applied to one of the quadrature
axis via the Coleman Transformation. From the results, MATLAB’s system identification
is used to determine the time constant τ by fitting a first-order model into the actual pitch
vs prescribed pitch signal.
Figure 3.3: Prescribed pitch by the controller and pitch delivered by the pitch actuator.
By fitting a first-order model using the pitch at the quadrature axis as input and the out-of-
plane bending moment at the location of the pitch bearing as output, the constants Dθ and
C0 are determined. As only the perturbation in moment caused by the step in pitch is what
is analysed, the mean value of the moment before the pitching is subtracted. An issue with
this process are the periodic oscillations in moments seen in the graph (Figure 3.2), which
can drastically taint the speed of the step in moment due to the step in pitch, resulting
in unreliable results for C0. Looking at the bending moment signal in frequency space
through a Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT), it can be seen that the oscillations are of
frequencies that are multiples of the 3P frequency, as shown in Figure 3.4:

3.3.3
Other parameters
Other than the PID controller, the standard HAWC2 cyclic pitch controller includes a low-
pass filter and a lead angle to be added to the Coleman transformation so that pitch action
can be anticipated, countering the delayed response of the pitch actuator. The low-pass
filter frequency is set at higher than the range that the 1P excitation is active at, and its
damping ratio is increased as needed to remove load increases from the individual pitch
controller at higher frequencies from. The lead angle is chosen through trial and error to
minimize the 1P loads, but generally a value between 10 to 30 degrees is appropriate.
3.4
IPC tuning model performance.
The IPC tuning model was tested with the DTU 10MW reference turbine, using different
frequencies and damping ratios. As the Coleman transformation turns a constant signal
into a 1P signal, to correctly target the 1P loads the variance of the moment disturbance
in the quadrature axis should be of significantly lower frequency (slower response) than
the 1P frequency, ensuring a stable signal. To dampen the moment disturbances, a high
damping ratio is chosen. A frequency of 0.01P and a damping ratio of 4.5 seemed optimal,
as shown in a wind-step case in Figure 3.8:

5
Conclusions
The introduction of partial pitch leads to a significant change in the loads throughout the
whole turbine. Overall, out of plane bending moment sees a decrease in its extremes
towards the root, but can lead to an increase around the middle of the blade for cases
where the pitch is placed after the middle length of the blade. In-plane bending moment
is increased both in average and extremes, though notably the case with the highest
extremes had the pitch bearing at half the blade length; this increase is likely due to the
added mass in the blade from the pitching mechanism, as the added mass increases in-
plane loads due to gravity but this mass is reduced the farther the pitch bearing is placed
along the blade, probably explaining why a reduction is seen from Case 2 to Case 3. Yaw-
bearing pitch is significantly reduced both in average and in extreme loads with the use of
partial pitch, while yaw-bearing roll undergoes little to no change. Partial pitch leads to a
significant increase in tower-base fore-aft loads, but no significant change in side-to-side
loads.
The partial-pitch turbine proved as susceptible to the use of IPC as a full-pitch turbine,
the same method for tuning IPC for a full-pitch turbine being directly applicable to a partial
pitch one. For future investigations, it is suggested to use with partial-pitch turbines the
same tools used for tuning IPC for full-pitch turbines, merely changing the position of
measurement of the bending moment to the position of the pitch bearing instead of the
blade root.
A significant decrease in fatigue loads was found for the out of plane bending moment on
the blade with the use of IPC. This is true both at the blade root and at the location the
pitch bearing is installed at, and also at intermediate points. This is consistent with the
behaviour in full-pitch turbines using IPC. As is expected, the introduction of IPC greatly
increases the amount of pitch action; this results in extra fatigue on the pitch bearing and
on the blade from torsional moment. The introduction of IPC did not result in significant
changes in the fatigue loads of in-plane blade bending moment, of the tower base (either
fore-aft or side-to-side).
While IPC did prove directly applicable to PP turbines, and even able to tackle some of the
challenges specific to the use of partial-pitch, it still suffers from the same issues seen in its
use in full-pitch turbines, those being the great increase in pitch action and in the torsional
moment induced fatigue both in the blade itself and in the pitch bearing. The concept of a
three-bladed partial pitch turbine with IPC with a power of 15MW has demonstrated initial
feasibility, but to conclude that it is preferable to standard full-pitch collective pitch solution
for the targeted offshore market would necessitate further studies.
To determine if this or a similar turbine design could prove commercially viable, further
studies analysing the effects of these changes in the costs of production (for example
from structural changes necessary to account for the altered loads, such as higher tower
base loads), maintenance (mainly to lifetime differences of various turbine parts due to
altered fatigue loads) and assembly (mainly from possible cost reduction in the blade
transportation by using a split blade) would need to be conducted. Here also exists the
possibility of further optimizing the positioning of the pitch bearing and tuning of the indi-
vidual pitch control in order to strike an optimal balance between its pros and cons, such
as reduced bending moment fatigue loads from IPC coming at the cost of increased pitch
activation and pitch bearing fatigue.
