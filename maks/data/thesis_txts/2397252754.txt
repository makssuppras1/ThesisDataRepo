Abstract 
Renewable energy has been subsidized the past years in order to increase the investment po-
tential. The number of biomass power plants has therefore increased. On biomass incinerating 
power plants, approximately 40% of the fuel is converted to electricity. By collecting and utiliz-
ing the wasted, the efficiency and sustainability of biomass could be increased. 
This master thesis will focus on increasing the efficiency of biomass power plants to improve 
the sustainable electricity production. A techno-economic investigation is completed to analyze 
the feasibility of waste heat recovery from biomass power plants with an organic Rankine cycle 
(ORC). The investigation includes a brief technical description of ORC, a detailed analysis with 
thermodynamic modelling and economical assessment to determine investment potential. 
These results are made into a generalized tool meant for investors to use when assessing feasi-
ble circumstances for ORC. This is important because in order for the ORC to become reality and 
thereby increasing sustainability of biomass power plants, economic feasibility has to be proven 
to the investor. 
A main focus of this thesis is to determine the power consumption and thermodynamic proper-
ties of an air-cooled condenser and a dry-cooler. Methods are therefore developed from existing 
fans and components. 
Two implementation options are suggested for a biomass power plant, where both utilize flue 
gas as heat source. One case study analyses pure waste heat recovery at 125˚C. Another analyz-
es the removal of the steam preheater to perform waste heat recovery at 150˚C. Each case study 
was analyzed using optimization models for two different ORC layouts:  
1. A simple reference system using direct heat recovery from flue gas to ORC and heat re-
jection from ORC to ambient air with an air-cooled condenser. 
2. An industrialized system taking into account safety measures, practical aspects, regula-
tions, commercial design and availability of equipment. 
Replacing the steam preheater was found to be the optimal scenario for implementing the ORC. 
The reference ORC (layout 1) yielded a thermal efficiency of approx. 11% and increased the 
power plant production with 385 kW net. The cost of capacity was found to be approx. 2,800 
€/kW with 5 year payback-time (PBT). The industrialized ORC (layout 2) yielded a thermal effi-
ciency of approx. 9% and a total increment of 230 kW net. The cost of capacity was approx. 
4,500 €/kW with 8 year PBT. As expected, the more complex industrial ORC performed poorly 
compared to the simple reference ORC, nevertheless it was still profitable. 
For this thesis it can be concluded that it is technically possible to implement waste heat recov-
ery with ORC and thereby increase the combined efficiency of the biomass power plants to ap-
prox. 45%, but with high investment costs and relatively long PBT.

1 Introduction 
Although the energy sector is known to be the main source of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions 
[1], it is still driven by economy and regulated by political decisions. Despite the known devas-
tating effects GHG have on the environment and climate changes, no changes are made before 
the solutions are economically feasible and politicians can agree. Even after a sustainable and 
economical feasible solution has been found, investors demand unreasonable short payback-
time, before carrying out the initiative. This means, compromises must be made on efficiency, 
quality and thereby sustainability in order to maximize profits. Unfortunately, the result is inef-
ficient energy production and wasted resources. 
A political initiative implemented to increase the share of renewable energy sources (RES) on 
the energy market, have been to subsidize certain RES. This has caused a shift in the merit order 
of energy supply, allowing renewables to a front position on the electricity market. As a result, 
United Kingdom (UK) experienced approx. 28% increase in bioenergy from 2014-2015 [2]. 
Burmeister & Wain Scandinavian Contractor A/S (BWSC) [3], has since early 2000 developed 
and constructed 9 turnkey biomass power plants [4] in UK.  
However, although biomass power plants have entered the market, there are still improvements 
to be made. Danish power plants improve fuel efficiency by collecting waste heat and distrib-
uting it on a district heating network, but this infrastructure does not yet exist in UK. Heat will 
therefore remain waste.  
1.1 Master thesis objective 
The objective of this Master thesis is to determine the potential of utilizing the waste heat from 
biomass power plants to generate electricity with an organic Rankine cycle. The fuel efficiency 
of the biomass power plant will thereby increase. The purpose is to increase sustainability, thus 
utilizing renewable resources more efficiently. Since the success and implementation of energy 
projects highly depend on economical parameters, the technical solution will be accompanying 
with an economic analysis. Emerging power conversion technologies, such as ORC, are still un-
der development and are currently especially uncertain in terms of economy  because the com-
ponents cost data is limited [5] and the field of application is very wide. 
The thesis will be completed in cooperation with BWSC. By doing so, information and data from 
power plants will be readily available. Working with BWSC both challenges the theoretically 
optimal solutions which are found throughout literature [6]–[12], and also the company’s fixed 
procedures and economical decision making. The result should provide BWSC with a decision 
tool on whether to further pursue the WHR with an ORC by taking into account the current 
technology, electricity market and costs.

1.2 Scope 
The thesis will be concentrated on the waste heat recovery (WHR) from flue gas at biomass 
power plants and electricity generation with ORC-technology. The ORC will be implemented as a 
bottoming cycle utilizing the flue gas as heat source without interfering with the biomass power 
plant significantly. The flue gas parameters are therefore constants which the ORC must be de-
signed to complement.  
In order to ensure real-world applicability, a case study is conducted on a typical BWSC biomass 
power plant. Current UK regulations and legislation will be practiced. The ORC will be designed 
as a stand-alone unit, completely independent from the original power plant, with exception of 
the heat exchange interaction with the flue gas. 
The case study will be carried out for two implementation scenarios:  
1. ORC WHR from the furthest point in the flue gas duct before it enters the stack. Here the 
temperature will be 125˚C. 
2. Replacing an existing flue gas heat exchanger used for preheating condensate. Here the 
temperature will be 150˚C. 
The purpose of the first scenario is to generate electricity from pure waste heat. The second 
scenario is to determine whether an ORC can generate more electricity than what is gained from 
preheating condensate from the steam power plant. 
The solution will be a product of a thermodynamic model optimization and an economical eval-
uation. The thermodynamic model will consist of an equation system, describing the ORC and 
related equipment. The net power production is to be optimized. Impacts on external compo-
nents, e.g. increased consumption, will be found to assess all consequences of the ORC imple-
mentation. For the purpose of assessing the system, certain components of the ORC will be de-
signed in detail: 
1. An air-cooled heat exchanger model, incl. fans. 
2. A flue gas cooler. 
3. Two intermediate heat transfer circuits. 
An existing thermodynamic ORC model will be revised and altered for the purpose of this thesis 
in order to meet the specific circumstances. The model is originate from Andreasen et al. [10], 
Department of Mechanical Engineering, Technical University of Denmark (DTU [13]).  
The air-cooled heat exchanger model will be constructed for two situations: 
1. Desuperheating and condensation of organic fluid – air-cooled condenser (ACC). 
2. Cooling of liquid cooling water – dry-cooler.

2 The organic Rankine cycle 
This section will provide an overview of the basic principles and components necessary for the 
ORC. The ORC basically consists of a boiler, expander, recuperator, condenser and feed pump. 
Electric power is generated from the turbine through a gearbox to a generator. The heat input to 
the ORC is flue gas from the biomass power plant. A description for each component are provid-
ed in this section 
The main difference between the ORC, sketched in Figure 1, and the steam Rankine cycle (SRC), 
sketched in section 3.2, Figure 7, is the type of working fluid. The organic fluids have different 
properties than water, especially in terms of expansion and cooling. However, this will be fur-
ther described in section 2.3. 
Commercially, ORC has been available on the market since the late 1970’s as a result of the oil 
crisis which encouraged research in power production from sources alternative to oil [20]. 
ORC’s was especially used for WHR from e.g. industries or engines. An example is one of the 
largest ORC’s (15MW) which was installed in Japan at Mitsui Engineering & Shipbuilding [20], 
who is now the owner of BWSC.

2.1 Thermodynamic configuration 
The working fluid used in the ORC is an organic compound often characterized by having a low-
er ebullition temperature than water [5], thus allowing power generation from relatively low 
temperatures, such as the flue gas in this thesis. The thermodynamic ORC path varies with the 
temperature, pressure and individual working fluid. Figure 2 depicts an example of five differ-
ent cycle paths, illustrated on an isentropic fluid saturation curve.  
 
 
Figure 2. Examples of different Rankine cycles with an isentropic fluid: 1) Subcritical, 2) Trilateral, 3) Superheated, 4) 
Saturated and 5) Supercritical. Inspired by literature from Yamada et al. [25]. 
Common for all cycle types are the initial pumping and heating of saturated liquid and the final 
condensing, depicted as black solid lines in Figure 2. The different types are identified as follow-
ing according to the legend found in Figure 2: 
1) Subcritical: The fluid is heated to just below the critical point where it evaporates at 
constant temperature (pressure losses neglected) and is further superheated. The fluid 
is then expanded and desuperheated.   
2) Trilateral: The fluid is heated to a point below the critical point and expanded through 
the two-phase region. 
3) Superheated: The fluid is evaporated below the critical point and hereafter superheat-
ed, expanded and desuperheated.  
4) Saturated: The fluid is evaporated and expanded along the saturated vapor line. 
5) Supercritical: The fluid is heated until it reaches the critical point where it is further 
superheated, i.e. going directly from saturated liquid to superheated vapor. The fluid is 
then expanded and desuperheated. 
This thesis will be concentrated on developing a thermodynamic model which handles the su-
perheated and the saturated cycle both with a pressure limit up to 80% of the critical point.

2.2.1 Boiler  
The purpose of the boiler is to achieve a phase change of organic fluid, thus going from liquid to 
vapor. For calculation purposes, the heat exchanger is divided into three sections: economizer, 
evaporator and superheater. The economizer preheats the working fluid until evaporation tem-
perature is reached. The evaporator ensures a phase change of the working fluid so that satu-
rated vapor is achieved. The superheater increases the working fluid temperature further to 
avoid droplet formation in case of expansion to the two-phase area. Superheating is not strictly 
necessary in an ORC [30] when using a dry or isentropic fluid. This is evident from the slope of 
the saturated vapor curve which tend to be positive or near infinite for these fluid types. Com-

Compared with the SRC is the boiler for the ORC simpler. The density difference between vapor

The efficiency of the boiler is defined by the PPTD and pressure drop [5]. The most common 
boiler type for exhaust/flue gasses are the shell & tube design. The pressure loss influence the 
biomass power plant process since it increases the load on the ID fan; it should therefore be lim-
ited while still conserving space [5].  
Figure 3 by MAN Diesel & Turbo [33], is an illustration of the influence of PPTD on steam pro-

change in PPTD will result in a significantly larger heat exchanger area and only a small change 
in steam temperature [33]. The PPTD is therefore mainly estimated from an economical and 
size perspective.

2.2.2 Expander 
The expander converts chemical energy into mechanical power. This is accomplished by ex-
panding the pressurized hot fluid which in return moves the internal parts of the machinery. 
For calculation purposes can the expansion process be assumed isentropic, i.e. adiabatic and 
reversible, thus no entropy change. In reality, losses will occur due to friction and other re-
sistances; therefore an isentropic efficiency is applied to calculate the actual performance. Gen-
erally, for dry- and superheated isentropic fluids, the fluid will remain superheated even after 
expansion (see section 2.3.2, Figure 4). Opposite to this, wet fluids can be expanded into the 
two-phase region; to a minimum of 85% vapor quality to avoid damage to the expander [30]. 
Several expander technologies are available for ORC purposes; the most suitable types for 
small-scale ORC applications [30] are listed in appendix A.2, Table 49. The system size and con-
ditions determine the preferred technology. Generally, two types of expansion technologies ex-
ist: turbomachines and positive displacement type machines [5]. Turbomachines include axial 
and radial flow turbines which are excellent for high flowrates, rotational speeds and high ca-
pacity, but are also expensive. The axial turbines are currently mostly used in large-scale ORC. A 
single pressure stage is usually sufficient organic fluids. In comparison to SRC, this is due to the 
relatively low enthalpy drop across the ORC turbine which also reduce rotational speed and 
centrifugal stress [21]. The lifetime of an ORC turbine is therefore above 30 years compared 
with that of SRC with an estimated lifetime of 15-20 years [34].  
Positive displacement type machines include many types of expanders which generally are suit-
able for small-scale ORC due to their tolerance towards low flow rates, high pressure ratios and 
low rotational speeds [5]. Most of them are still only prototypes [5]. Examples of commercial 
positive displacement machines, used for specifically for ORC, are listed in appendix A.2, Table 
50. 
Because the turbine is a well-documented technology, this will be used in the further models 
and economical assessment. The efficiency, listed in Table 1 used for the thermodynamic model, 
is found in a study Angelino et al. [29] for turbines with similar power range to what was found 
during this thesis.  
2.2.3 Gearbox 
The gearbox converts the variating rotational speed of the expander shaft to constant speed by 
adjusting the gear ratio between input and output. Losses occur in the gearbox due to friction.  
A gearbox with several ratios might not be necessary for the ORCs in cases where the heat 
source is constant at all times. In this case would fluctuation in shaft rotational speed ne-
glectable and a single gear or none at all would be sufficient to obtain the desired generator 
speed. 
The efficiency, listed in Table 1, is included in the thermodynamic model to adjust for any addi-
tional losses.

2.2.5 Recuperator 
The recuperator utilizes the remaining heat after the turbine expansion. As previously explained 
and seen in Figure 2, the working fluid most likely to remain superheated after expansion. The 
remaining energy can therefore be used for preheating before the boiler, thus saving fuel and 
increasing cycle efficiency. The recuperator cold side is liquid pressurized working fluid and the 
hot is superheated working fluid. 
The recuperator is optional in because all heating can be performed in the boiler. The recupera-
tor contribute with higher boiler inlet temperatures which is convenient in systems where the 
heat source outlet temperature is restricted to a certain level (e.g. to avoid acid formation in 
WHR from exhausts gasses from internal combustion engine [21]). It is therefore possible to 
control the heat source temperature with the amount of heating performed in the recuperator. 
Economically, the recuperator might increase the capital cost of the ORC. It can be argued that 
WHR from “clean” sources (i.e. no acid, hence no lower temperature limit) will not benefit from 
regenerative preheating. In terms of WHR the power output is prioritized [30] because the heat 
source will be wasted if it is not consumed. Fuel efficiency therefore is less important since fuel 
can be considered “free”.  
The heat transfer coefficient in the recuperator is low due to the superheated hot side. The as-
sumed heat transfer coefficient is listed in Table 1. Section 4.5 further elaborates the origin of 
this assumption. The pressure ratio and the PPTD are estimated values. 
2.2.6 Condenser 
In the condenser the working fluid is desuperheated to saturated vapor and condensed to satu-
rated liquid. This is done by dissipating the energy from the working fluid to a cooling media. 
Having two phase changes makes the condensing process different from that of traditional 
steam condensers. In a cycle without recuperation, the desuperheating will be performed in the 
condenser. However, this will increase the load on condenser and it has to be designed accord-
ingly [30]. 
The condensation can occur near atmospheric pressure due to the low boiling point of organic 
fluid. Although large cycle pressure ratio increase cycle efficiency, the condensation pressure 
should always be kept higher than atmospheric pressure to avoid air leaks [30], [10]. Introduc-
ing air to the cycle would decrease efficiency especially because air decrease heat transfer abili-
ties. 
Various types of condensers can be utilized, e.g. air-cooled condenser (ACC), water-cooled con-
denser etc., depending on the available space and cooling media accessibility. The fan and pump

2.2.7 Feed pump and auxiliary pumps 
The feed pump pressurizes the liquid working fluid to overcome the system pressure losses and 
to ensure the desired evaporation/turbine inlet pressure. Besides adding pressure, the pump 
also provides the desired working fluid flowrate. The entropy difference between saturated liq-
uid and saturated vapor is much lower for the organic fluid compared to water, as presented in 
section 2.3.2, Figure 4. This means higher flowrate is necessary to transfer the same amount of 
heat to an organic fluid [5]. The high flowrate will increase pump power consumption, thus 
making the ORC less efficient compared to the SRC. Therefore, it is important for the feed pump 
to be as efficient as possible to minimize the power consumption. The pump can be electrically- 
or mechanically drive from the generator or turbine shaft respectively. For this thesis all pumps 
are assumed to be electric with the efficiency listed in Table 1. Other small auxiliary pumps, e.g. 
for intermediate circuits, are assumed to have slightly lower efficiency than the larger feed 
pump.  
2.3 Working fluid 
The working fluid separates the ORC from the traditional SRC. In theory, hundreds of fluids, 
both organic and inorganics are available to use as working fluid in an ORC. These include hy-
drocarbons, aromatic hydrocarbons, ethers, perfluorocarbons, CFCs, alcohols, siloxanes, inor-
ganics etc. [30]. However, this thesis will only focus on fluid candidates matching the conditions 
and regulation constraints of the case study which will be presented in chapter 3.  
2.3.1 Pure vs. mixed working fluids 
Both pure fluids and mixed fluids can be used as working fluid in an ORC. However, to limit the 
scope of this thesis, only pure fluids are considered. 
2.3.2 Fluid characterization 
Organic fluids generally have a lower boiling temperature than water, hence enabling evapora-
tion from low temperature sources. However, in contrast to water most organic fluids will 
chemically deteriorate and decompose at high temperatures and pressures [35]. As known from 
water, must organic fluids freezing point also be respected [30]. It is therefore important to en-
sure the chosen fluid is able to operate within the temperature and pressure demands of the 
ORC. The fluid maximum temperature can be found individually for each fluid, e.g. with the 
CoolProps software [18] or in their web catalogue [36]. 
==PAGE:24==
2 | Working fluid 
14 / 116 
Working fluids are generally categorized according to the saturation vapor curve since the 
shape of this is essential to the ORC performance and configuration [30]. Working fluid type can 
be either dry, wet or isentropic. Figure 4 illustrate the difference between the three types in a 
temperature-entropy (T-s) diagram. The slope of the saturated vapor curve for a dry fluid is pos-
itive, negative for a wet fluid and near infinitive for an isentropic fluid. This means that without 
superheating, a dry fluid will expand isentropically to become superheated, a wet fluid will ex-
pand to the two-phase region and an isentropic fluid will expand along the saturated vapor 
curve. This is illustrated in Figure 4 with an imagined simple Rankine cycle with isentropic 
pumping (1-2), heating (point 2-3), evaporation (3-4), isentropic expansion (4-5) and desuper-
heating/condensation (5-1).

2.4 Waste heat recovery (WHR) 
As previously stated, this thesis will focus on using WHR to fuel the ORC. Many studies have 
been carried out on WHR from various industries as heat input for an ORC [35],[40]. An im-
portant factor in WHR is that the energy source can be considered free because the energy 
==PAGE:26==
2 | Economy 
16 / 116 
would otherwise have been lost. This means, when analyzing the operations and maintenance 
(O&M) cost of producing electricity, fuel costs are neglected. Typically, environmental regula-
tions already require flue gas to be cooled before being released to the atmosphere [12]. In 
terms of thermal power plants, the flue gas cooler (FGC) is often used for WHR for either pre-
heating the intake air or process feed water.  
2.5 Economy 
Economy of scale is important to consider for this type of application since both capital and 
O&M cost increase with decrease in equipment size or capacity [12]. A recent study by Zhang et 
al. [12] on the WHR market potential in Asia, demonstrated this by comparing the costs of dif-
ferent WHR technologies based on electric capacities. As anticipated, it was found that the lev-
elized cost of electricity (LCOE) and cost of capacity for low efficient ORC (low temperature and 
thermal efficiency of 5-13%) was depending on the electric output. ORCs with low electric out-
put (50-500 kW) yielded a LCOE of approx. 0.07 €/kWh with 5 year pay-back time (PBT) and a 
capital cost of approx. 2,100 €/kW. ORCs with high electric output (1-20 MW) yielded lower 
LCOE of approx. 0.04 €/kWh, 5 year PBT and a capital cost of approx. 1,300 €/kW.  
This is lower than cost of WHR with a steam Rankine cycle (SRC), where for small applications 
(0.05-0.5 MW) the LCOE is approx. 0.08 €/kWh and capital cost is approx. 2,500 €/MW. For 
larger SRC applications (1-20 MW) is the LCOE approx. 0.04 €/kWh, thus lower than what was 
found for large ORC and the capital cost of SRC is approx. 1,400 €/MW which is higher than 
what was found for ORC. This indicates that ORC is only an advantage for the low power range 
applications. It should also be noted that the SRC requires higher temperatures in order to pro-
duce the same amount of electricity [41], as depicted in Figure 5. It is therefore difficult to com-
pare the costs only based on electricity output; the available temperature would be a more ade-
quate cost measure as suggested by Sternlicht et al. [42], shown in Figure 52 in appendix A.1.

3.2.1 BWSC biomass power plant configuration 
BWSC’s biomass power plants are based on the traditional steam Rankine cycle (SRC). A graph-
ical overview of the biomass power plant is presented in Figure 6. Figure 7 illustrate the process 
simplified as a pipe & flow diagram (P&FD). The heat from incineration of biomass is used for 
generating steam in the boiler. The steam is expanded through the turbine to generate electrici-
ty and subsequently condensed in an air-cooled condenser (ACC).  
3.2.1.1 Flue gas quality 
The flue gas is cleaned through series of processes. A bag filter catches fly ash and lime is inject-
ed to reduce acids such as Sulphur dioxides (SO2) and hydrogen chloride (HCl) [43]. In a selec-
tive catalytic reduction (SCR) ammonia (NH3) is added to reduce nitrogen oxides (NOx) by con-
verting it to nitrogen (N2) and water (H2O). A flue gas cooler (FGC) reduces the flue gas temper-
ature by preheating some of the condensate feed water prior to the boiler. The induced draft 
(ID) fan provides sufficient pressure to overcome the flue gas filters, silencer and stack pressure 
losses. In case the stack temperature is too high, some of the flue gas is extracted before the si-
lencer and recirculated to the boiler air intake to reduce NOx. According to BWSC will the flue 
gas cleaning process eliminate the acid dew-point and WHR can be performed without a lower 
temperature limit.

3.3 ORC requirements 
BWSC has following requirements to the introduction of an ORC to their power plants: 
1. The process must under no circumstances be able to interrupt the main power plant 
production. 
2. The ORC and related components must be space conserving and the arrangement must 
be flexible. 
3. Organic fluids must be environmentally responsible and non-hazardous to humans in 
case of leaks. 
4. The ORC must not increase power consumption on the power plant without replacing 
the power.  
5. The components, machinery, cycle configurations etc. must be commercially available. 
As seen in Table 4 for the Widnes project, the flue gas temperature is 125˚C as it enters the 
stack. As previously mentioned the flue gas cooler (FGC), seen on Figure 7, are currently used 
for preheating SRC condensate. BWSC informs they are willing to remove it in case a better al-
ternative is found. The flue gas temperature is 150˚C at the FGC inlet. Two ORC implementation 
options will therefore be considered:

1. Implementation of WHR and ORC just before the stack to only utilize heat otherwise 
wasted.  
2. Replacement of the SRC flue gas cooler with the WHR and ORC. 
Option 1 will have minimal impact on the SRC and will only affect the biomass power plant by 
decreasing the stack inlet temperature. The available energy will however be higher by imple-
menting in option 2 since the temperature is higher. This will however interfere with the bio-
mass power plant since the steam generation capacity would decrease in favor of the ORC. Re-

what is lost from the SRC.   
The commercial availability of the ORC components is an important factor for BWSC. Possible 
commercial ORC design options have therefore been investigated. The primary objective of this 
is to model a realistic ORC configuration and account of potential losses. The differences in cycle 
configurations from theory to practice are identified as:  
1. WHR – The interface between heat source and ORC. 
2. ORC cooling – The interface from ORC to cooling source. 
3. ORC configuration. 
3.3.1 WHR to ORC interface 
Although direct conversion from flue gas to organic fluid would yield highest conversion effi-
ciency, BWSC requirements could be compromised. The organic fluid could introduce a risk to 
the primary biomass plant in case it leaks to the flue gas. A leak of organic fluid to the flue gas 
duct would expose the flue gas quality measuring instruments to the fluid, thus potentially com-
promising emission, health and safety requirements. The purpose of these instruments is to sig-
nal the power plant demanding an alteration to the process in attempt to decrease the meas-
ured pollution. Ultimately, this would risk the entire power plant production to shut down since 
the source was external from the process. Even if the risk was accepted, another problem would 
be finding an ORC supplier capable of supplying a boiler suitable for flue gas ducts, which often 
vary in size, flow rate, surrounding space etc. A solution could be to purchase the boiler sepa-
rately at a boiler manufacture for the specific case, hence assuming the ORC supplier accepts the 
organic fluid to be transferred to another manufactures product before returning to the ORC 
expander. One solution could be to implement an intermediate heat transfer circuit between the 
flue gas and ORC, thus separating the plants with a save heat transfer fluid. Many organic fluids 
are flammable and should therefore also be contained as much as possible [45]. Safety will be 
further elaborated in section 3.3.5.3. 
3.3.2 ORC cooling interface 
The existing BWSC biomass power plants do not have access to natural cooling water such as 
rivers or sea. Air cooling is therefore required. Cooling or working fluid can either performed in 
an air-cooled condenser or indirectly with an intermediate circuit of cooling water. An interme-
diate circuit between a dry-cooler and the cycle process is commonly used in refrigeration sys-
==PAGE:34==
3 | ORC requirements 
24 / 116 
tems. The dry-cooler consists of a number of radiator coils containing cooling water and fans for 
motivating air across the radiator coils.  
An advantage of the intermediate cooling circuit is the ORC condenser can be kept compact. The 
quantity of organic fluid is therefore decreased. The dry-cooler can be positioned elsewhere and 
cooling distribution pipes will only contain non-hazardous cooling water. Figure 8 illustrate the 
air-cooled condenser and dry-cooler type proposed.  
In contrast to direct condensation in an air-cooled condenser, the intermediate circuit layout is 
the safest method because the organic fluid would be contained in the ORC-unit. A disadvantage 
is the increased cooling temperature due to the conversion efficiencies which is likely to impact 
electricity production. The total investment cost would also increase due to the additional com-
ponents.

Common for considerations so far are the practical aspect of space requirements for the equip-

[5], it is favorable to decrease the volume needed.

can be reduced by designing a cycle without it.

3.3.5.2 Environment 
Environmental factors are important to consider when designing a process with potentially 
harmful substances. From a company’s point of view, the motivation for producing electricity 
from renewable biomass is mainly to generate a profit, but also knowing this is achieved with 
only small impacts to the environment. Hence, extending the biomass power plant with an ORC 
should not induce a potential risk to the environment. Fluids used in low temperature ORCs are 
often refrigerants traditionally known for their ozone depletion potential (ODP) and global 
warming potential (GWP). However, after starting regulating and phasing down these fluids, 
more environmental friendly fluids have been developed as replacements. The 10 fluids listed in 
Table 6, are environmental responsible, available on the market and in the case specific temper-
ature range as illustrated in Figure 10. All fluids on the list have a GWP<6 and a ODP = 0 [37], 
[54], [10]. The fluids potentially harmful to the environment have therefore been eliminated.

3.3.5.4 Corrosiveness 
The fluid corrosiveness influences the material requirements in ORC components. This should 
therefore be investigated with the component manufactures during the design process. 
3.3.5.5 Thermal conductivity/viscosity 
Thermal conductivity and viscosity influence the performance of the heat exchangers. Through 
heat exchangers low viscosity will, in both liquid and vapor phase, contribute to low friction 
losses, while high thermal conductivity ensure high heat transfer coefficient [30].  
3.3.5.6 Volume flow rate/ Enthalpy drop/ Mach number 
The choice of expander is influenced by the designed volume flow rate and enthalpy change 
across it. The Mach number is a limitation in a radial inflow turbine design. To avoid choking in 
the inflow should the Mach number be kept under 0.85 [30]. This thesis will not go into further 
detail regarding expander design. 
3.3.5.7 Component design 
The optimal fluid may very well cause the heat exchanger design to be infeasible due a large sur-
face requirement [10]. However, due to the scope of this thesis, only a limited amount of the 
heat exchangers be modelled in detail.

4.1.1 Optimization and model tools 
The optimization process is performed in MATLAB with two solution search functions: parti-
cleswarm and patternsearch. An equation system, which will be described in detail in section 
4.2, are arranged to imitate the ORC system. Component specific constants from Table 1 and a 
number of function variables, described in section 4.2 are given to the equation system to initi-
ate the calculation. Each function variable is given a set of upper- and lower bounds imitating 
the physical constraints to limit the solution area.  
Optimization is performed with mathematical functions: particleswarm and patternsearch. The 
particleswarm solver is constructed to search for the global minimum in the equation system 
and patternsearch search for a local minimum within the area of the global minimum; hence to 
maximize the net power production, the net production value must be calculated as negative.  
Particleswarm 
The particleswarm optimization algorithm [17] is developed to work as if a population of parti-
cles were cooperating to find the best position to settle, in other words the deepest valley in the 
solution space. The particles are released in a multidimensional space, each with a known veloc-
ity/direction and position. The property of each particle is known and they are all aware of the 
other particles position after each step, meaning that they can change direction and velocity to 
obtain the best known position. If another particle has a better position than their own, they will 
change direction and accelerate towards that position. The position where the particles settle 
will be the global minimum, i.e. the optimal solution (given that sufficient boundary conditions 
are given). The particleswarm optimization will stop when the certain requirements are fulfilled, 
e.g. overall particle movement has stopped. The position will yield the optimal value of the func-
tion variables and this yield the solution to the equation system, i.e. the ORC net power produc-
tion.

The patternsearch optimization continues where the particlesearch terminates. The optimiza-
tion searches for a local minimum around the solution previously found. Hence this method is 
used for optimizing the solution.

4.2.1 Reference ORC model 
A simple ORC system is made as a baseline reference to systems further expansion. The simple 
system assumes that the ORC boiler can be directly incorporated in the flue gas pipe. This 
means that the only energy conversion limit will be the boiler PPTD. Figure 16 illustrates this 
system in a pipe and flow diagram (P&FD). Organic fluid is directly transferred through the 
condenser which is air-cooled (ACC). The condensed organic fluid is pressurized and pumped 
through the recuperator to the boiler. The organic fluid is heated, evaporated and superheated 
through a series of steps in the boiler before it enters the turbine as saturated organic steam. 
The work generated in the turbine is converted to electricity through a gearbox and generator. 
The organic steam remains saturated through the turbine to avoid droplets which can cause 
damage to the turbine blades. After the turbine is some of the remaining energy in the organic 
fluid used as preheating in the recuperator before it is completely condensed in the condenser 
again. For this reference system, the ORC stages are numbered with nodes: “ORC#”, for identifi-

4.2.3 Industrial ORC model 
The industrial ORC model has intermediate circuits in order to separate the organic fluid from 
the flue gas and air. This introduces two new pumps, fluids and heat exchangers to enable heat 
transfer with the intermediate circuits.  
To absorb heat from the flue gas, a flue gas cooler (FGC) is implemented. The intermediate cir-
cuit between the FGC and ORC boiler will be referred to as boiler circuit. The fluid used for heat 
transport in the boiler circuit is thermal oil with boiling temperature above the flue gas temper-
ature. Practically, this ensures the intermediate fluid remains liquid throughout the circuit and 
the pressure can be kept at a minimum, thus decreasing the components needed (e.g. expansion 
vessel). 
To avoid filling an air-cooled condenser with organic fluid, an intermediate circuit is imple-
mented between the ORC condenser and dry-cooler. Water is then being cooled in a dry-cooler 
in order to cool the organic fluid in the condenser. The intermediate circuit between the con-
denser and dry-cooler will be referred to as the condenser circuit. Due to the risk of freezing 
(ref. Table 4), the water is mixed with glycol. Practically, this configuration enables the conden-
ser to be more compact and the organic fluid can be kept within the ORC-unit. As a consequence 
the heat exchange from air → water → organic fluid will make the organic working fluid con-
dense at higher temperature due to the conversion efficiencies, e.g. pinch point temperature dif-
ferences. 
The industrial system introduces more stages due to the intermediate boiler circuit. These 
points in the system are identified with “F#” on the flue gas side and “B#” on the boiler/oil side 
and listed in Table 13.

4.3 Air-cooler model 
Both an air-cooled condenser (ACC) and a dry-cooler will be introduced in this section. The ACC 
is used for the reference model and the dry-cooler is used for the intermediate cooling circuit in 
the industrial model. The purpose of the condenser is to ensure a phase change of the organic 
fluid i.e. from vapor to liquid so it can be pumped back to the boiler. By doing so, heat is trans-
ferred to another media with lower temperature which in this case is the air.

condenser was originally water-cooled with a pump. Two different cooling approaches were 
developed and tested, however one was found to be become unstable when the system com-
plexity increased in the industrial ORC model. Only one approach is therefore presented here, 
while the description of the unstable one can be found in appendix C.2.

The ACC model approach is based on a concept where each fan coil is cooling an equal share of 
the hot fluid flow. The inlet and outlet temperature of each fan coil is therefore the same for all 
fan coils, i.e. only one fan coil need to be modelled.  
The organic fluid is transferred twice through the same air stream in order to cool it. The air 
temperature increases as the air is moving across the first part of the heat exchanger coil, i.e. the 
part closest to the fluid outlet. Next, the same air will move across the second part of the heat 
exchanger coil, i.e. the part closest to the fluid inlet. The second heat exchanger part will there-
fore be cooled by air with a higher temperature than it was at the first coil. This is illustrated in 
Figure 22 where the fluid hot inlet first passes through the intermediate temperature air; the 
fluid is hereafter returned to pass through the cold air inlet.

The economical calculations will only be included in the industrial ORC model. Economical eval-

4.6.1 Cost estimations 
The cost of each component is to be estimated in order to estimate the feasibility of the ORC ex-
tension to the biomass power plant. In addition to the cost of each component, is a cost for on-
site related work included. The component cost estimations are based on the methods de-
scribed by Turton et al. [16] for a chemical plant.  
The cost estimation correlations are included in the MATLAB model as individual functions for 
each equipment type. Most equipment functions were prepared by DTU, however modifications 
were made on all functions to convert cost from dollar ($) to euro (€) with exchange rates from 
[70] listed in appendix C.1, Table 52. 
The cost estimations are based on scaling known prices with a component specific scaling pa-
rameter, A , such as the capacity or size. Combining the scaling parameter with a material-, 
pressure- and time (inflation) factor yield a correlation for the cost of a component. The cost 
information can come from previous purchases in similar projects. This is generally known as 
an Order-of-Magnitude Estimate. The ORC capital cost estimate is based on the components 
shown in the system P&FD in Figure 18.

4.6.5 Economical investment evaluation 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the feasibility of implementing an ORC for WHR on a 
biomass power plant. The thermodynamic analysis were made to determine the electric power 
output from the ORC and from the cost analysis give an indication of the capital cost. Combining 
this information can be utilized to estimate the project economy in terms of pay-back-time 
(PBT), net present value (NPV) and internal rate of return (IRR).  
The objective of this evaluation is to determine whether the ORC will generate high enough rev-
enue to cover the cost of acquiring the equipment and maintaining it. This is a crucial decision 
parameter since the outcome determines the future of the project. 
The average electricity market price from 2016-2017 is estimated to be 0.05 €/kWh4 based on 
the monthly average day-ahead baseload contracts in Great Britain [72]. However, the levelized 
cost of electricity (LCOE) generated in 2018 is estimated to be approx. 0.11 €/kWh4 for dedicat-
ed biomass conversion <50 MW [73]. This price correspond to the electricity market price from 
the Widnes biomass power plant contract, see section 3.2.1.2, Table 4. For combined heat and 
power (CHP) biomass plants are the LCOE estimated to be 0.19 €/kWh4 [73]. The cost of gener-
ating the electricity is therefore higher than the baseload market price and it is therefore as-
sumed that biomass power plants receive subsidies to cover the difference. The obtainable elec-
tricity price is therefore assumed to be equal to the LCOE of dedicated biomass conversion <50 
MW.

5.4 Air-cooling models 
5.4.1 Number of fans 
The number of fans used in the ACC and dry-cooler are an optimization parameter which allows 
the model to find the optimal balance between cooling demand and fan power consumption. 
However, in the ORC model, component cost was only calculated as an output and not a decision 
parameter, hence choosing large quantity of fans turned out to be an advantage thermodynami-
cally. The total power consumption decreases as the number of fans increase as seen in Figure 
30. This is until a certain point where the minimum power consumption possible is reached. 
The optimization model would therefore choose this number of fans if no constraints were set.

6 Results and discussion 
The results derived from the ORC model result is highly dependent on the input parameters, 
choice of fluid and component design as explained throughout this thesis. Two major influences 
on the final result is the heat source temperature and conversion method. To highlight the dif-
ferences, two cases were formulated: the 125˚C heat source and the 150˚C heat source. These 
cases will be solved with the reference ORC model using direct conversion from flue gas to or-
ganic fluid and with the industrial ORC model using an intermediate heat transfer fluids. The 
heat source temperature depends on the position in the flue gas system, and in order to obtain 
the high temperature, a current FGC must be replaced. It will therefore be investigated whether 
the ORC can generate higher net power from the FGC than the SRC is currently able to.  
The results presented will be divided into two case studies solved with the two ORC models:  
1. Case study: 125˚C heat source, heat exchanger after the FGC. 
 
Reference model / Industrial model 
2. Case study: 150˚C heat source, replace the FGC. 
 
Reference model / Industrial model 
The model results are collected by running the models 15 times. Especially the industrial ORC 
model experienced deviations in the results, which might be expected since the model is more 
complex and has more optimization variables, i.e. a larger solution space. To evaluate the stabil-
ity of the model, the relative standard deviation is utilized. This enables comparison between 
each case in terms of optimization stability and likelihood of obtaining the optimal result with 
each optimization. 
Because the net power is used, the calculated thermal efficiency accounts for all consumptions, 
i.e. both the internal ORC (feed pump) and the external consumption (fans, circulation pumps 
etc.). The thermal efficiency is therefore expected to be lower than seen from commercial ORCs 
(Table 5) since the external consumption is disregarded.

will be estimated from methods described in section 4.6 and used for the further case analysis. 
A quick impact analysis of the seasonal changes will also be presented.

In the industrial model the temperature in the turbine outlet and condenser outlet will be de-
pending on the mass flow of the intermediate heat transfer media. As previously mentioned the 
fans (ID-fan and dry-cooler fans) will consume more power than pumps. Therefore, if the mass 
flow across the dry-cooler is decreased, less air is needed and the fan power will be decreased. 
However, the low mass flow means less heat can be transferred in the condenser and the cycle 
performance will be decreased. Therefore, the optimal solution is a balance between fan power 
consumption and providing sufficient cooling to the condenser.

7 Conclusions 
The work performed and methods developed during this thesis were done in order to deter-
mine the feasibility of implementing an organic Rankine cycle (ORC) utilizing waste heat from 
biomass power plants. The purpose and motivation behind identifying feasible situations is to 
increase the efficiency of biomass power plants and thereby enhance the benefits of implement-
ing and improving thermal renewable technologies on the energy market.  
To evaluate the potential of recovering heat from biomass flue gas, two main scenarios was con-
sidered: recovering heat just before the flue gas is released at a temperature of 125˚C or replac-
ing an existing steam preheater (flue gas cooler) to achieve higher temperature of 150˚C. Hence, 
the first scenario recovers pure waste heat while the second scenario replaces an existing com-
ponent in attempt to produce more power with the advantages of organic fluid.  
Above mentioned scenarios were formulated as two case studies with conditions from an exist-
ing biomass power plant situated in Widnes, United Kingdom. Each case study was completed 
with two different ORC layouts: A simple reference ORC with direct conversion between heat 
source and cooling and an industrialized ORC considering safety measures, regulations, practi-
cal aspects and commercial availability of components. Both ORCs were thermodynamically 
modelled and economy was assessed for a 20 year lifetime. 
Climate and seasonal impacts on the ORC performance were assessed with weather data from 
nearby Liverpool city. The seasonal temperature changes were found to impact the hourly pow-
er production throughout the year. However, average power production during a year corre-
sponded with power production at an assumed average temperature, thus yearly accumulated 
power production matched expectation. 
Different fluid candidates were found to compliment the flue gas temperature profiles for each 
case study. For both scenarios isentropic fluids was found preferable: R1234yf was preferred 
for the 125˚C heat source and R1234zd(E) was preferred for the 150˚C heat source. The selec-
tion of fluids was based on maximizing net power production, i.e. the two selected fluid is not 
the optimal choice in terms of thermal efficiency. 
Recovering waste heat just before releasing the flue gas was found to increase the total power 
plant production with 289 kW with the reference ORC and 194 kW with the industrial ORC. The 
total power plant efficiency increased from 40.0% to respectively 45.3% and 44.6% by imple-
menting the reference- and industrial ORC. 
Replacing the flue gas cooler and deprive the biomass power plant from steam preheating, in-
creased the total power plant production with 385 kW with the reference ORC and 232 kW with 
the industrial ORC. In this case the total power plant efficiency was increased to respectively 
==PAGE:118==
7 | Conclusions 
108 / 116 
46.5% and 45.6% by implementing the reference- and industrial ORC. Therefore, both case 
studies proved to increase the performance of the biomass power plant.  
As expected, the economical assessment showed that the less complex reference ORCs were 
best both case studies. The cost of capacity varied from approx. 2,700 to 6,700 €/kW between 
the four scenarios. Replacing the flue gas cooler turned out to be the most favorable investment, 
yielding respectively 5 and 8 year payback-time (PBT) for the reference ORC with a cost of ca-
pacity of 2,772 €/kW and the industrial ORC with cost of capacity of 4,545 €/kW.  
Ultimately, a general tool was created to assess ORC feasibility under different circumstances. 
This was done by visualizing the relationship between electricity market price, investment cost 
and payback-time in a graph. From this, it can be concluded that in order to obtain a favorable 
investment scenario with 3 year PBT, investment cost should be below 1,500 €/kWh and the 
electricity price should be minimum 0.09 €/kWh.

7.1 Recommendation for further work 
A list of recommended subjects for further analysis has been collected. 
1. The industrial ORC model optimization was found to be unstable in terms of consisten-
cy in results. Several attempts were made to increase the accuracy and the problem was 
suspected to be related with the FGC model. More attention should therefore be given to 
this part of the model in a further study. 
2. The air-cooler models made for this thesis worked in terms of thermodynamic results. 
The model needs to be expanded for an actual design proposal to be made. The further 
work should be to model the pressure losses and heat exchanger area with different ra-
diator fin layouts. 
3. The industrial ORC optimization made during this thesis was missing detailed model-
ling for several components. It was seen that the FGC model had significant impacts on 
results, thus further detail should in the future be incorporated to achieve more accu-
rate results. Detail designing internal ORC components could also be used for determin-
ing fluid impact on for example heat transfer area. Some fluids might turn out to be 
proven infeasible because of high component costs. 
4. The recuperator was found to have very small or no impact at all on ORC performance. 
An investigation is therefore proposed for a future study to determine when the cost of 
the recuperator outweighs the benefits of implementing it in the ORC.   
5. Incoherence was found between the component costs estimated from methods de-
scribed by Turton et al. [16] and the known costs from previous purchased components. 
A better understanding of the method or an updated version of it might therefore be 
needed. Expectations of high costs and low returns are one of the main reasons ORC is 
not common. The uncertainty in cost estimation should therefore be limited to increase 
the interest towards the technology. This is also in regards to ORC O&M costs which 
have not been addressed in any studies to the authors’ knowledge. 
6. An assumption made during this thesis was that decreased the stack temperature 
would have no further consequences. This is a simplified version of the truth and might 
not be correct for flue gas from all biomass incineration types. The concequences should 
therefore be investigated and the acid dew points should be found for the flue gas to set 
a minimum flue gas cooler outlet temperature.
