Abstract
Parkinson’s disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that results from the progressive degen-
eration of dopaminergic neurons in the midbrain.
This results in a reduction of dopamine
levels in the striatum resulting in motor dysfunctions. While there are therapies to alleviate
the symptoms, there is no method to alter the course of the disease. Cell-based regenerative
medicine using human neural stem cells could be a potential strategy to replace the degenerated
dopaminergic neurons. However, monitoring the eﬃcacy of transplanted cells in real time is
still a challenge. Moreover, it is not possible to stimulate dopamine release on demand. Thus,
there is a need for an eﬃcient cell scaﬀold to monitor and to selectively stimulate these trans-
planted neurons. Electrochemical techniques enable real time detection of dopamine exocytosis
due to the electroactive nature of dopamine. Pylolytic carbon has been used as an electrode
and a substrate for the diﬀerentiation of human neural stem cells into dopaminergic neurons.
Optogenetic modiﬁcation of stem cells to make them sensitive to light permits stimulation on
demand. However, implanting an optical ﬁber for light delivery together with a cell-scaﬀold
increase the invasiveness. Moreover, the light from the ﬁber tip allows stimulation of a limited
neural population. This master thesis describes a novel method to reproducibly fabricate 3D
side-emitting optical ﬁbers by patterning micro-optical windows on the entire surface of a py-
rolysed optical ﬁber using laser ablation. This was achieved through the optimization of a 3D
printed platform, which permits the ﬁxation and rotation of an optical ﬁber at very precise an-
gles. The optimal settings to ablate micro-holes on the surface of a pyrolysed optical ﬁber were
investigated by varying the laser power, pulse repetitions and focus of laser, ensuring suﬃcient
light leak while maintaining the mechanical integrity of the pyrolysed ﬁber. The electrochemi-
cal properties of the laser ablated ﬁber were characterized by acquiring cyclic voltammograms
in ferri-ferro cyanide redox probe. A good behavior was observed despide a decrease in the
current with increasing the number of holes ablated on the ﬁber. Oxygen plasma treatment
rendered the ﬁber hydrophilic and was found to improve signiﬁcantly the conductivity of the
ﬁber due to the presence of oxygen function groups. The 3D side-emitting optical ﬁbers were
used as a substrate for the in-vitro diﬀerentiation of optogenetically modiﬁed neural stem cells
into dopaminergic neurons. Two diﬀerent types of coating (PLL 10µg/mL and Geltrex) and
two diﬀerent cells densities (100000 cells/mL and 200000 cells/mL) were analyzed. Finally, the
ability of the ﬁber to detect dopamine release due to the optical stimulation of dopaminergic
neurons from the light leaking through the micro-holes was veriﬁed using chronoamperometry.

Introduction
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disease.
It aﬀects
approximately 100000 persons each year and currently an eﬀective diagnostic method and
treatment does not exist[1]. PD results from the progressive neuronal degradation of dopamin-
ergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta leading to a decrease in the physiological
dopamine levels. The reduction of dopamine is the cause of the motor and non-motor mani-
festation of PD, such as tremors at rest, rigidity of limbs and slowness of motion[2]. The few
medications available are only symptomatic, they do not contrast the worsening of the disease
and tend to lose their eﬃcacy in the advanced-stage of the disease[3]. Consequently, cell-based
regenerative medicine is gaining lots of attentions as a new potential solution. Among the
diﬀerent types of stem cells that have been tested, human fetal ventral mesenchepalic stem
cells, collected from 5 to 10 weeks old aborted fetuses, provide the most promising results in
terms of diﬀerentiation into dopaminergic neurons[4]. However, it is not possible to control the
spreading of the cells once implanted and the release of dopamine from the transplanted tissue.
With optogenetic modiﬁcation, stem cells express light sensitive transmembrane proteins,
called opsins. Opsins act as ion channel inside the cell membrane, permitting a control of cell
activation or inhibition using light stimulation. Several types of opsins with diﬀerent properties
exist. Channelrhodopsins-2 act as cation-permeable ion channel, favors the inﬂux of calcium
ions and the cascade of events that lead to the release of dopamine. To stimulate optogenetically
modiﬁed neuronal population, a light delivery scaﬀold is required. Pyrolytic carbon has been
shown to enhance stem cell diﬀerentiation into dopaminergic neurons. However, it is opaque
and cannot delivery light to the cells. On the other hand, the light emitted from the tip of an
optical ﬁber has a smaller activation volume. Moreover, using an optical ﬁber together with
a large cell scaﬀold as implant results in increased invasiveness. Thus there is a need for a
device that can act as a scaﬀold and illuminate a large population of neurons without causing
excessive heating of the surrounding tissue.
The primary goal of this project is the creation of a novel approach for the production of
3D side-emitting pyrolysed optical ﬁber. This enhances the functionality of an optical ﬁber
as an implant for Parkinson’s disease therapy. First, the procedure for the fabrication of 3D
side-emitting pyrolysed optical ﬁber and the associated challenges are described. Then, the
results from electrochemical characterization to verify their conductivity and ability to perform
as an electrode are presented. Optogenetically modiﬁed human neural stem cells were seeded
and diﬀerentiated on the 3D side emitting ﬁbers in-vitro. Immunocytochemistry was used to
characterize and verify the presence of dopaminergic neurons on the ﬁber after 10 days of
diﬀerentiation. Finally, light was coupled into the ﬁber and the ability of the ﬁber to stimulate
the diﬀerentiated dopaminergic neurons through light leaking from the micro-optical windows
and subsequently detect dopamine release was veriﬁed using chronoamperometry.

This project addresses a minor part of the work of a PhD student, Shashank Vasudevan, an

Theoretical Background
2.1
Parkinson’s disease
Parkinson’s disease is the second most common neurodegenerative condition after Alzheimer’s.
It was ﬁrst described clinically by the British physician James Parkinson’s in “An Essay of the
Shaking Palsy”, a monograph published 1817[1]. It involves motor and cognitive abnormalities
as a results of the progressive degeneration of midbrain dopaminergic neurons in the substantia
nigra pars compacta (SNc)[6].
The main hallmarks of PD are the loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra and
the presence of intracellular inclusion called Lewy bodies, formed by aggragation of a particular
presynaptic protein called α-Synuclein (α-Syn)[6; 7]. Parkinson’s Disease is characterized by
motor and non-motor symptoms. The three cardinal clinical symptoms of this disease are motor
dysfunctionalities and are well know since the last part of the nineteenth century[2]. They are:
resting tremor,muscle rigidity and akinesia (loss and impairment of the movement). Non-motor
problems include depression, psychosis, constipation, dementia and cognitive dysfunction[1].
2.1.1
Diagnosis
Diﬀerent discoveries have helped in the process of understanding PD. Investigations post
mortem have permitted the observation of damages of the substantia nigra, the discovery of
neuronal loss and the presence of Lewy bodies[2]. Although is known that all of this symptoms
result from striatal dysfunctions due to the death of neurons[6], currently, it’s not possible to
diagnose PD with any laboratory test or imaging technique[1]. The rate of PD misdiagnosis is
still high, approximately 10-25% and the diagnostic time point corresponds to a loss of 70% of
all the dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta[2; 3].
The diagnosis of PD is currently based on patient’s medical history, neurological and physical
evaluation[1; 2]. A further conﬁrmation of the disease can be obtained with a patient’s response
to dopaminergic therapy[2].
2.1.2
Incidence, Prevalence and Mortality
Parkinson’s disease aﬀects approximately 100000 persons each year[1].
The number of PD
patients is expected to double by 2030 due to the increased aging of population[1]. PD is an
age dependant disease. It is rare before age of 50 and its incidence and prevalence increase
after the age of 60[1]. It is usually more common in men. The male-to-female ratio is 1.5[1]. In
the literature, a possible explanation to this phenomena is given by the diﬀerent occupational
exposure and the natural protection of the estrogens in women[1]. Literature studies did not
found any consistent variation caused by geographical diﬀerences[1].
Evidences have proved that the mortality is higher in young patients than older ones and
the mortality risk increases with the worsening of motor deterioration and dementia[1].

2.1.3
Potential Treatments
An eﬀective treatment against PD does not exist. The current therapies are only partially
eﬀective and aim to restore normal dopamine level in the brain[1; 8].
The main treatment currently used is pharmacological.
Diﬀerent types of drugs have
been tested, a dopamine precursor called levadopa was found to be the most eﬃcient, due
to its ability to cross the blood brain barrier[9]. Other strategies involved the prescription of
dopamine agonists, whose aim is to directly stimulate the postsynaptic receptors, and non-
dopaminergic agents, mainly antidepressants[2; 9; 10]. Ropinerole, pramipexole, catechol-o-
methyltransferase(COMT) and rotigotine, which is also approved by the Food and Drug Ad-
ministration, are the most used dopamine agonists[2; 10].
The eﬃcacy of levadopa decreases in many later-stage of the disease and it is often ac-
companied by other side-eﬀects such as dyskinesia and motor ﬂuctuation, known as on-oﬀ
phenomenon[8; 9; 10]. Dopamine agonists are also not absent of drawbacks. Studies registered
less eﬃcacy than levadopa and the presence of side eﬀects as behavioral alterations[2; 9].
The problems found with the use pharmacological drugs and the new technological discov-
eries led to the development of new therapeutic-surgical approaches. One of them is deep brain
stimulation (DBS). This approach is based on high frequency electrical stimulation of the in-
ternal segment of globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus or ventral intermediate thalamus[8; 2].
In this procedure, as it is described in Fig.1, the permanent implantation of electrodes inside
the brain to modulate the activity of neurons is required[11].
Figure 1: Deep Brain Stimulation - most used brain location [adapted from [12]].
The electrodes are placed with the patient fully awake and connected to an implanted pulse
generator, whose functionality is similar to a standard cardiac pacemaker, through wires buried
under the skin[11; 2]. The generator is programmed to deliver stimulation for several years and
15
==PAGE:16==
the batteries can last for four to ﬁve years[11]. The stimulation is adjusted in order to optimize
the outcome and minimize the unwanted side eﬀects[11]. The choice of the best site to place the
electrodes is controversial. It is often the subthalamic nucleus or the globus pallidus[11]. Both
present diﬀerent advantages, the former is chosen for its ability to control symptoms and low
energy requirements, while the latter presents similar clinical beneﬁts and reduced psychiatric
adverse eﬀects[11].
Researches have shown the potential risks related with the use of DBS and are mainly
based on intraoperative events such as cerebral haemorrhage or stroke. Adverse events related
to the stimulation, such as paraesthesias(itching), dysarthria (diﬃcult or unclear articulation
of speech) and motor contraction[11], have also been observed. Finally, long term complica-
tion caused by hardware problems such as breakage, malfunctions and infections have been
recorded[11].
The presented strategies are only symptomatic therapies with no signiﬁcant disease modi-
fying eﬀects[2].
For this reason, cell replacement therapy has recently been tested in-vitro and in-vivo as
potential alternative. The aim is to overcome the loss of eﬃcacy and problems found with the
use of pharmacological drugs and DBS. The aim is to alter the course of the disease. This
strategy is based on the transplantation of cells which can acquire dapaminergic phenotype[9;
13].
In order to achieve this diﬀerentiation, diﬀerent types of stem cells had been isolated and
tested. Nowadays, Fetal neuronal stem cells (NSCs), derived from fetal mesencephalon, are
considered the best options in terms of dopaminergic release and lower tumorigenicity[13].
Cell therapy could provide some advantages respect the previous treatments in terms of
its long-lasting suppression of the symptom[14]. However, there are some concerns due to the
possibility of tumor formation if the cells are non properly diﬀerentiated and ethical problems
due the use of fetal tissue specimen[9]. Furthermore, the hazard of graft rejection is still a
concrete possible drawback[2].
Hence, diﬀerent strategies have been tested to overcome these obstacles, as the use of dif-
ferent types of cell lines or the reprogramming of other cell type. The state of the art of stem
cell application and Parkinson’s disease will be presented in the next chapter.

2.3.1
Action Potential
Action potential is essential for neurons communication.
Nerves are characterized by a cell membrane which contains voltage dependent ion channels,
responsible for the passive and active mechanism of transports of the ions across the membrane.
A mechanism of transport is classiﬁed as active or passive if, for its activity, it requires or not the
consumption of adenosine triphosphate (ATP). These channels are particular proteins, which
are speciﬁc for each ion presented in the extra-cellular liquid or cytoplasm of the cell[10].
Every ion exists in diﬀerent concentration in the internal or external environment of a cell.
Therefore they have a characteristic membrane potential, whose variation can modify the status
of a channel, opening or closing it.
In a resting state, the resting membrane potential of a nerve is negative and equal to
approximately -70mV[10]. It is the result of the activity of two diﬀerent forces known as electric
drive and concentration gradient, which exist for every ion, plus the activity of a particular
pump known as sodium/potassium-adenoshine triphosphatase (Na+/K+ −ATPase) pump.
Na+/K+ −ATPase pumps out 3 positive sodium ions for every 2 positive potassium ions into
the cell with a consumption of 1 ATP. This unbalanced charge transfer results in a more negative
charged cytoplasm and contributes to the separation of charge across the cell membrane creating
a concentration gradient for each of two ions. Moreover, in a resting state, the cell membrane
is permeable to potassium but not to sodium. Therefore, the value of -70mV is given by the
balance of two diﬀerent forces that acts on the potassium ion: the electrochemical drive and the
concentration gradient. As a matter of fact, since every ion exists in a diﬀerent concentration
in the outside or inside of a cell, it tends to be attracted for concentration gradient from where
it is higher concentrated to where it is lower concentrated. In addiction, every ion is positively
or negatively charged, therefore it is driven by an electrochemical force.
The nerve continues to maintain its resting value until an event change the membrane
18
==PAGE:19==
permeability to sodium[10] and reached the cell threshold potential.
When the threshold potential ( which is equal to -55mV for a nerve) is reached, a phe-
nomenon, essential for cell communication, called action potential starts[10].
Figure 4: Phases involved in the action potential of a neuron[17].
As it is possible to see if Fig.4, when this threshold is reached, a change in the membrane
permeability to sodium ions occurs. The voltage dependant sodium channels start to open,
resulting in the depolarization of the membrane. The suddenly entrance of ions of sodium inside
the membrane, during the depolarization phase of the action potential, causes an inversion in
the membrane potential, from negative to positive. The entrance of sodium also inﬂuences
the adjacent cell membrane and permits the propagation of the stimulus. Indeed, the negative
charge of the adjacent membrane attracts the new ﬂow of positive ions, resulting to a new
opening of sodium channels[10].
Once the spike of 40mV has been reached, another phase called repolarization begins. The
aim of the repolarization is to reestablish the resting potential, decreasing the membrane poten-
tial. It is possible thanks to the closure of the sodium channel, the opening of potassium channel
and the activity of the Na+/K+−ATPase pump[10]. During the repolarization, the membrane
is refractory. There are two diﬀerent refractory periods, absolute and relative[10]. During the
absolute refractory period, which lasts for less than half of a millisecond and corresponds to
time between the opening of sodium channels to just after their closure, no stimulus can lead to
the rise of a new action potential. While during the relative refractory period, only a stronger
stimulus than the normal can lead to a new action potential, due to the hyperpolarizion, which
corresponds to a more negative membrane potential state[10].
2.3.2
Synaptic Transmission
The communication among neurons is possible thanks to synapses and neurotransmitters.
19
==PAGE:20==
Inside the nervous system there are two diﬀerent synapses: electrical and chemical[10]. The
former is faster but less common in the nervous system. It is based on the passage of ions
through opening gaps inside the cell membrane called gap junctions[10]. While the latter is
slower and permits only one way communication to the next neuron.
A chemical synapse
consists of three main components: a presynaptic neuron, a synaptic cleft and a postsynaptic
neuron.
There are two diﬀerent types of chemical synapse: excitatory and inhibitory. An excita-
tory synapse will lead to a depolarization of the postsynaptic neuron membrane opening ions
channels, while an inhibitory will decrease the postsynaptic membrane potential decreasing the
chances to generate an action potential in the postsynaptic neuron[10]. As a matter of fact,
opening ion channels leads to an increase of positive charge inside the postsynaptic neuron
moving the cell membrane potential closer to the ﬁring threshold.
The functionality of a synapse depends on impulse generation.
As it is possible to see
in Fig.5, if an impulse stimulates a presynaptic neuron, an action potential will propagate
through the presynaptic neuron membrane up to the axon terminal. There, the depolarization
induced by the action potential will stimulate the opening of voltage-dependent calcium chan-
nels, causing an inﬂux of calcium. The inﬂux of calcium triggers a cascade of events resulting
in the movement of the synaptic vescicles to the cell membrane border and their fusion with
the membrane. This, in turn, will result in the release of neurotransmitters into the synaptic
cleft. Once released, neurotransmitters bind to receptors, which are speciﬁc protein embedded
in the postsynaptic membrane, on the postsynaptic neuron[10], causing the opening or closing
of sodium channels.
For a correct and controlled synapsis connection a rapid removal of the neurotransmitters
is required. Neurotransmitters removal can be done in three diﬀerent ways: it can diﬀuse away
from the synaptic cleft, it can be broken down by enzymes and ﬁnally there can be a process
called reuptake, where it is taken back up into the presynaptic neuron[3].

2.3.3
Neurotrasmitters
Neurotransmitters are endogenous chemicals essential for chemical synapses and central nervous
system communications[10]. According to their chemical structures they can be categorized as:
amino acids, peptides and monoamines[10].
Gamma-Aminobutyric acid (GABA), one of the most important inhibitory neurotransmitter
in the central nervous system, belongs to the ﬁrst category[10]. Among peptides, molecules con-
stituted by the union of two or more amino acids, there are somatostatin and endorphins. Pep-
tides are involved mainly in perception and pain sensation[10]. Monoamines are characterized
by one amine group (-NH2) and examples are catecholamine such as dopamine, norepinephrine
and epinephrine[10].
Neurotransmitters are synthesized inside the axon cytoplasm thanks to the activity of special
enzymes and then stored inside synaptic vescicles[10]. Generally, each neuron is able to produce
only one type of neurotransmitter[10].
Neutransmitters are classiﬁed as excitatory or inhibitory according to the fact that they can
inhibit or activates a receptor[10].
It is worth noticing that another class of messenger molecules can be released by axon term,
neuromodulators. Neuromodulators activity leads to long-lasting changes, suppressing or en-
hancing receptor activity[10]. Dopamine and Achetylcoline are the two main neuromodulators
in the central nervous system[3].
2.3.4
Dopamine and its Receptors
Dopamine is the most abundant catecholamine and one of the most important neurotransmitter
inside the central nervous system[13]. Its function is not restricted inside the nervous system
but it plays an important role inside the hormonal and renal system[18].
21
==PAGE:22==
Dopamine is released by dopaminergic neurons[9]. Dopaminergic neurons are arranged in
three diﬀerent nuclei: the substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc), the ventral tegmental area
(VTA), the retrorubal ﬁeld (RrF). All the diﬀerent neurons populations project to diﬀerent
areas and with their activity modulate speciﬁc functions[9]. VTA and RrF control the emotional
behavior and all the cognitive functions with their projection to parts of the limbic system and
prefrontal cortex. For this reason, problems in these two areas cause psychiatric disorders[9].
On the other hand, SNc projects to the dorsolateral part of the striatum, which is called
nigrostriatal pathway, and it is responsible of all the motor functionalities. The degeneration
of neurons in this area is responsible to motor impairment and, consequently, Parkinson’s
disease[9].
Dopaminergic receptors are G-protein-coupled receptors and their activation can trigger two
opposite cascades of event according to which G-protein has been activated[3]. There are two
families of dopaminergic receptors: D1-like family and D2-like family.
D1-like family activation starts a cascade of event which ends in the increase accumula-
tion of cyclic adenosine monophospate messenger (cAMP). D2-like family activation stops the
production of cAMP[3]. Increased cAMP levels allow an increase inﬂux of calcium ions and
hence higher neuronal excitability[3]. On the other hand, lower levels of cAMP lead to a re-
duce sodium ions currents, enhanced potassium ions current which in turn lead to a decreased
neuronal ﬁring rate[3].
2.4
Optogenetics
The term optogeneticts was described ﬁrstly by Deisseroth as “the combination of optics and
genetics methods to achieve gain or loss of function of well-deﬁned cellular events in speciﬁc
cells or living tissue”[19]. The advent of optogenetics has permitted to address an unmet need,
the control of particular events in diﬀerent cell types at a particular time[19].
Optogenetics allows the control of speciﬁc neuronal population using light[6]. As a conse-
quence, brain cells can be stimualted or ihibited by the use of light. All of this is achieved
through an engineering modiﬁcation of neurons in order to express light sensitive transmem-
brane protein called opsins, that could act as ion channel pump or G-protein-coupled receptors[20].
As a matter of fact, this protein can respond to light by generating a ﬂow of ions across the
cell membrane[21]. Opsins represent the most powerful optogenetic actuators[20]. Indeed, light
can be used to illuminate neurons and trigger a conformational change of the opsins, leading
to a cell depolarization, hyperpolarizatrion or maintaining the resting potential[20]. For this
reason, opsins can be used to induce single or multiple action potentials and consequently to
inﬂuence neuronal activities[20].
During the last years, a large number of opsins have been found permitting the stimulation
or inhibition of neurons using diﬀerent types of light with diﬀerent wavelengths[20].
As a
matter of fact, blue light can be used to immediately depolarize a neuron that expresses the
non-selective cation channel Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2). This will trigger an action potential
which results in calcium ions inﬂux and a cascade of events which ends with the release of

==PAGE:23==
dopamine[6].
As described by Marie T. Alt et all[6], there are diﬀerent types of opsins. Among the most
important there are:
• Microbial opsins(type 1 opsins): which induce a transmembrane ions ﬂux if activated
with light.
• Bacteriorhodopsins: which act as proton pumps.
• Halorhodopsins (NpHR): which act as chloride pumps
• Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2): which act as cation-permeable ion channels
The actual discovery of these opsins has occured in diﬀerent times. While bacteriorhodopsins
were discovered in 1971 by Stoeckenius and oesterhelt, the halorhodopsins were discovered in
1977 by Matsuno-Yagi and the channelrhodopsins only in 2002 by Hegemann and Nagel[19].
According to which opsin is stimulated, the optical activation can lead to a change in the cell
resting potential, working as an excitatory or inhibitory input. All of this is possible because
each opsin possesses a particular activation spectrum.
For instance, NpHR, which are the
most used inhibitory opsins, pumps chloride ions into the cells upon green, yellow or red light
activation, causing cell hyperpolarization. They have an activation peak using light with a
wavelength equal to 590nm[20].
It is worth to notice that this same wavelength can not be used to activate other opsins[22].
The possibility to choose the desired light source is one of the most important feature in the
use of opsins. As a matter of fact, the choice of the illuminating devices and the light intensity
should be selected according to the desidered eﬀect, the depth, the location and the quantity
of cells.[22]
A particular discussion can be done for channelrhodopsin. Channelrhodopsins (ChRs) were
found in two diﬀerent unicellular green algal species, channelrhodopsin-2 from Chlamydomonas
reinhardtii and channelrhodopsin-1 from Volvox carteri (VChR1). Both work as light depen-
dent cations channels and can cause cell depolarization if stimualted by blue light but they
are activated at diﬀerent wavelengths and they need diﬀerent deactivation times[23]. ChR2
are maximally activated at 470nm and their deactivation time is constant and equal to 12ns,
while VChR1 remain sensitive for wavelengths up to 589nm and possess a longer, constant,
deactivation time of 120ns[23]. The lower deactivation time and the possibility to combine in
a same neuron ChR2 with other opsin such as NpHR3, due to the suﬃcient spacial separation,
explain the reason why ChR2 is more commonly used[20; 23]. They can enhance the neural
control. Moreover, the use of ChR2 can overcome the limitation of the pour blue-light pene-
tration thanks to their low excitation level necessary for their photoactivation equal to 1mW
mm-2[24].
Diﬀerent techniques can be used to engineered a neuron with opsins. Among the most used
there are: viral transfection, utero electroporation and transgenic crossing[21; 22]. The use of
viral vectors is one of the most common[22].

Opsins could be easily found in nature, in organisms like microbes and primates[20]. Gen-
erally speaking, naturally disposable opsins can be divided in two main categories:
• Type 1 or microbial opsins.
• Type 2 or vertebrate opsins.
While the former are found in in prokaryotic and eukaryotic microbial organisms, the latter are
found in animal cells. The main physiological diﬀerence among these two types is their velocity
of action. Indeed, type 2 opsins produce slower changes due to the fact that their stimulation
induce a cascade of events[20].
Nowadays, optogenetics is largely used in neuroscience due to its large experimental poten-
tial.
Unlike electrical stimulation, optogenetics can be very speciﬁc. It permits a modulation and
stimualtion of only neurons engineered with opsins preserving all the nearby neurons, which lack
of opsins[25]. Furthermore, optogenetics presents almost no side eﬀect for the host tissues[6].
As previously described, the key element that permit the use of optogenetics is the light.
Light delivery is very challenging due to the possible tissue damage and the possible light
scattering and absorption[25]. Diﬀerent sources of light could be used in the attempt to deliver
light to neurons for optogenetic purposes such as micro light emitting diodes (LEDs) and optical
waveguide such as optical ﬁbers[26; 25]. While the former presents drawbacks in the long term
use because of the too much heating produced, the latter presents the advantage to keep the
source of light outside the body, avoiding a direct and excessive heating[20; 25].
Diﬀerent
source of light could be use to deliver light to optical waveguides but lasers are one of the best
possibilities[20; 26]. Among the reasons behind the use of laser as light sources there are their
narrow bandwidth and their easy coupling with optical ﬁbers[20].

Optical Fibers
Optical ﬁbers are one of the most common and eﬀective method to guide light from a source to
distant sites. There are diﬀerent advantages in the use of optical ﬁbers instead of other methods:
insigniﬁcant light loss, faster speed, less signal attenuation and no sign of electromagnetic
interference are among the most important[27]. As it is illustrated in Fig.7, an optical ﬁber its
composed of a central core and a surrounding cladding. This characteristic is essential in order
to permit light propagation. As a matter of fact, the cladding provides support to the ﬁber in
order to avoid ﬁber distortion and light leaking[28].
24
==PAGE:25==
Figure 6: Optical ﬁber[28].
Light propagation is based on the phenomenon of total internal reﬂection[22; 28]. In order
to achieve this property, the refractive index (n1) of the core has to be higher than the refractive
index (n2) of the cladding and the incident angle greater than the critical angle[22]. The critical
angles is given by the Snell’s law (n1sinθ1 = n2sinθ2, where θ1 and θ2 are angles of incidence
and refraction, respectively) when θ2 is equal to 90◦.
The diﬀerence among the refractive indexes inﬂuences the numerical aperture(NA), deﬁned
as the angle of the light cone coupled into the waveguide[22]. It is a measure of the light-
gathering power of the ﬁber[28].
Figure 7: Optical ﬁber - total internal reﬂection[29].
The use of optical ﬁbers for light propagation is not absent of many criticizes. First of all,
the coupling of the source into the waveguide of the ﬁber is not simple and, secondly, light
scattering and absorption can lead to loss of light intensity[22].
According to core size, an optical ﬁber can be classiﬁed as single-mode and multimode.The
former presents a small core diameter, usually between 2 micrometer to 10 micrometer and
a similar refractive index between core and cladding, while the latter presents large core di-
mensions (10micrometer to 1500micrometer) and bigger NA [22]. Consequently, the use of
multimode optical ﬁbers lead to an easier light coupling. It is worth to notice that a mode is
deﬁned as a transverse ﬁeld distribution that propagates along the ﬁber without any change
except for a change in the phase[28]. In long term application, the cladding is essential in
order to avoid the degradation of the core and protect it from the external environment and to
maintain the ratio between the two diﬀerent refractive indexes[22]. Theoretically, in implanted
25
==PAGE:26==
optical ﬁbers, this process could be avoided, due to the external watery environment which
presents a lower refractive index[22].
Literature studies have demonstrated the use of optical ﬁbers in many biomedical appli-
cations. Among the most common there are endoscopy, phototheraphy and optogenetics[27].
The reasons behind the large use of optical ﬁbers must be placed in the simplicity to cut
them and obtain the desired measure to reach the desired location inside the body and their
biocompatibility if the diameter is up to 200 micrometer[20; 27].
Nevertheless, reaching large areas inside the body is impossible using the same ﬁber. Its
illumination is limited only in the areas around the tip[26]. An illumination of wider area
inside the body, requires the combination of more ﬁbers and therefore complicated surgical
procedure[27; 26]. On the other hand, in the past year, eﬀorts have been made in order to
obtain more practical designs and resolve this issue. The use of multi-point, side-ﬁring optical
ﬁbers as described by Nguye et all [27] seems to be an important strategy. Thanks to this new
improved design, in optogenetics, diﬀerent neuronal target regions, can be stimulated by light
at the same time. In the past years, diﬀerent techniques have been used in the attempt to create
side-emitting ﬁbers. Methods as surface-emitting ﬁber lasers, ﬁber Bragg grating, focused ion
beam milling and side-glowing ﬁbers haven’t found success due to problems in terms of eﬃcacy,
cost and velocity of execution[27; 30].
On the other hand, the use of laser ablation techniques was found to be the most powerful,
cost-eﬀective and faster[27; 30].

3.1
Electrochemistry and Electrochemical Methods
Electrochemical methods are based on charge-transfer processes occurring on electrods. The
transfer of charge gives rise to potentials and/or current. If the measurement involves current
ﬂow this process is known as amperometry or voltammetry or polarography. They diﬀer for
details of the experimental design[31].
3.1.1
Cyclic Voltammetry
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) is one of the most common technique used to examine the reduction
and oxidation processes of diﬀerent molecular species in order to characterize their electrochem-
ical behaviour[32; 33]. The graph produced by CV is called voltammograms or cyclic voltam-
mograms and it is described in Fig.8. It is illustrated in the two convention commonly used
[32]. The diﬀerence among the two conventions is a diﬀerent beginning and sweep direction[32].
In a voltammogram, the x-axis is represented by the applied potential, while in the y-axis is
expressed the resulting current[32]. Scan rate is another meaningful parameter to be considered
in this technique. It describes the speed selected each second to vary linearly the potential and
control how fast the applied potential is scanned[32].
Figure 8: The two convention used for CV [adapted from:[32]].
In order to obtain CV, the aim is to construct an electrochemical cell. An electrochemical
cell is mainly characterized by 3 electrode: a working electrode, a reference electrode and a
counter electrode.
The working electrode is composed of redox-inert material which can become a stronger
oxidant or reductive electrode according to the applied potential. Consequently, it is the main
responsible of the electrochemical event of interest, reduction or oxidation[32]. Possible good
materials that can be used as working electrode is glassy carbon or gold[32].
The reference electrode presents a deﬁne and stable equilibrium potential and it is used as
reference point in order to measure the potential of the other electrode[32]. A silver wire coated
27
==PAGE:28==
with silver chloride (AgCl) inserted in a specif electrolyte solution such as saturated potassium
chloride is commonly used as reference electrode.
The counter electrode is fundamental in order to complete the electrical circuit. As a matter
of fact, the current is generated by the movement of electrons from the working to the counter
electrode through the electrical circuit created inside the device[32]. A platinum wire, gold or
glassy carbon are frequently the main choice for the realization of counter electrodes[32].
In order to obtain a well functional three-electrode setup for CV, it is necessary that the
surface area of the counter electrode is greater than the surface area of the working electrode[32].
These three electrodes need to be inserted in a liquid and connected to a potentiostat which
sets the parameter of the experiments and controls the diﬀerence in the potential between the
reference and working electrode[33].
The potential is swept from a starting value of potential to a ﬁnal value and then returned
back to the initial one (Fig.9a). As it is possible to see in Fig.9b, the current is continuously
measured and plotted against the applied potential[31].
(a) Potential sweep[34].
(b) CV normal shape[35].
Figure 9: Cyclic Voltammetry - Potential sweep and standard cyclic voltammetry shape.
In Fig.9b, Ep,a and Ep,c represent the oxidation (anodic process) and reduction peak (ca-
thodic process), respectively, while Ep,2 is the midpoint calculated as 1/2(Ep,c + Ep,a)[35].
Eswitch represents the switching potential. In a voltammogram signiﬁcant are also the the peak
current, which represents the value of the current during the oxidation and reduction peak[33].
As described in Fig.9b, tha anodic and cathodic peak currents can be found considering the
distance between the values of the current in the peak and the respective value of the current
considering the tangent to the graph at the beginning of each switch.
The eﬀects of the scan rate on the peak current are described by the Randles-Sevcik equation:
ip = 0.4463nFAC(nFvD/RT)1/2
where, ip is the peak current, n the number of electrons that are transferred in the redox
event, A the electrode area, F the faray constant, D the diﬀusion coeﬃcient, C the concentration,
v the scan rate, R the Gas constant and T the temperature in Kelvin. The peak current ip can

==PAGE:29==
be plotted versus the square rate of the scan rate in order to provide evidence for a chemically
reversible redox process[33].
As it is possible to see in Fig.9a, the potential scanning has a waveform as isosceles triangles.
From point a to c, the potential is reversed so that from point a, where no reaction occurs,
the potential is increased up to point b, where according to the tested material an oxidation
or a reduction can be seen: At this point, the potential is inverted until the initial value of the
potential is reached again.
Redox couple as ferro-/ferricyanide ( [Fe(CN)6]4- , [Fe(CN)6]3-) can be used to create re-
versed scanning. In this case, the current ﬂow is from the electrode to the solution species during
the forward scan, while from the solution species to the electrode during the reverse one. The
cyclic voltammetry of a reversible process should be characterized of this three aspects:
• the distance between the peaks Ep,a - Ep,c=59mV at all scan rates[33].
• The peak current ratio should be equal to 1[33].
• The peak potential should be independent of the scan rate[33].
One of the main advantages of CV and the reason why it is so largely used is because it
allows the characterization of electrodes.
3.1.2
Amperometry
Amperometry diﬀers from voltammetry because the electrode potential is controlled and kept
constant throughout the experiment[33]. The beneﬁt of this procedure is how fast data can
be collected and the only limit is the fact that it should be used only when it is known the
substance that is going to be released[31]. Consequently, it can be used for the detection of
electroactive analytes from cells, such as dopamine. Once the baseline is reached after the
initial decay in the current level, caused by the initial applied potential, the release of the the
electrolyte compound appear as a peak in the amperometry trace.
3.2
Laser Ablation
Laser ablation consists in the removal of molecular structures from a solid because of the
activity of a pulsed laser beam or an intense continuous wave[36]. Laser ablation can have
diﬀerent application because of its ability to perform deep cavities in very hard material like
diamonds[36].
Laser ablation technique can be used to perform cavities alongside the ﬁber in order to
modify light propagation. Diﬀerent geometries and design can be created and patterned on
optical ﬁbers to fabricate multi-point, side-ﬁring, three dimensional optical ﬁbers[27].
The
power of the laser can be critical. At the same time, it can allow the exit of more light, forming
deeper and larger holes, and create larger heat-aﬀected zone[27]. The latter can result in a very
brittle section and formation of debris[27]. It’s worth to notice that literature studies have also
demonstrated how the depth of the holes in the ﬁber can inﬂuence its behaviour. As a matter
29
==PAGE:30==
of fact, holes deeper than half of the diameter of the optical ﬁber can reduce signiﬁcantly the
strength of the ﬁber causing problems or rupture during the insertion[27].
3.3
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of materials at elevated temperatures in an atmosphere
characterized by the absence of oxygen. As a matter of fact, the release of the gases as oxygen
or hydrogen lead to an isometric shrinkage of the structure, which can be seen as a general
mass loss of the polymer[37]. At high temperatures, the decomposition of some polymers can
be used in order to obtain carbon on the substrate. All the commercial optical ﬁbers contain
thin polymer layer that protects them from the environment. The aim behind the pyrolysis is
to obtain a conductive carbon layer. This carbon layer can then act as an electrode for electrical
measurements.
Diﬀerent evidences have demonstrated that the pyrolytic conditions were found to aﬀect
carbon properties[37].
As a matter of fact, high temperature, approximately 1000◦C, can
provide a better electrochemical behaviour, improving the conductivity, without an excessive
shrinkage or distortion of the carbon[37].
3.4
Oxygen Plasma Treatment
Oxygen plasma treatment allows the modiﬁcation of the surface of a substance in a low pressure
environment. It can be used for cleaning the surface of a material and to turn a hydrophobic
material into a hydrophilic one due to an increase in the oxygen functionalization[38]. Moreover,
the increased wettability obtained with oxygen plasma treatment is demonstrated to enhance
the adhesion of cell adhesion factors[13]. The wettability can be measured investigating the
contact angle (CA), the angle between the liquid and the surface. An increase in the CA will
result in an increase in the hydrophilicity, while lower CA will results in a hydrophilic behaviour.
3.5
Scanning Electron Microscopy - SEM
Scanning electron microscopy uses a narrow electron beam, which scans across the sample
surface, to collect high resolution and magniﬁcation images of backscattered electrons which
are emitted from the surface of the sample[39]. It is the movement of the electron beam and its
interaction with the sample that results in diﬀerent types of emitted electron signals which are
then collected, processed and analyzed to create an image that appears three dimensional(3D)
from the surface topograpy of the sample[40]. It is the tool of choice for a detail investigation
of the surface of a specimen[40].
3.6
X-Ray Microtomography
This is a non-destructive technique that permits the creation of a 3D image with its external
and internal structure[41]. It was originally developed after 1970 and it has become one of
30
==PAGE:31==
the most used technique to create 3D images in the industry. It is based on the irradiation
of a material with X-ray, while the material sample rotates 360◦[41]. Then, according to the
material ability to absorb X-rays, thousand of images are captures by an inner detector[41].
Using a 3D analysis software, the images can then be used to reconstruct the 3D image of the
material.The possibility to slice the 3D image and visualize it layer by layer, is one of the most
powerful tool of this technique.
3.7
Confocal Microscopy
In confocal microscopy the light is focused from a pinhole aperture onto a small single point
on the specimen at a speciﬁc depth. The ﬂourescent light is then transmitted from the sample
through a second diﬀerent pinhole aperture before hitting a detector. The particular of these
two aperture is that they are confocal to each other. The detector pinhole is important in
order to avoid a possible out-of-focus[40]. The detected light is digitized in order to create an
image from it on a computer. It can produce images of specimen collecting light from very thin
region, smaller than 1µm. Confocal microscopes are the tool of choice for imaging fourescently
stained neurons.

State Of The Art
In this chapter a presentation of the most important discoveries, drawbacks and limitation
related to stem cell therapy, optogenetics and optical ﬁber in the attempt to treat PD will be

4.1
Stem Cells in the treatment of Parkinson’s Disease
Diﬀerent articles have been written regarding the use of human mesencephalic tissue and stem
cells transplantation in order to treat Parkinson’s disease. The path that has led to the use
of human fetal stem cell in clinical trial was long due to logistic, safety and ethical problems,
especially in humans. As a matter of fact, the transplantation of human fetal tissue in humans
is regulated by diﬀerent requirements.
Pre-clinical data and ﬁnancial-regulatory approvals
from the country where it is conducted are necessary[42]. Ethical problems have risen due
to the vast amount of foetal tissue needed. According to some research, the foetus that can
lead to better results for in vivo application are among 6.5 and 9 weeks of age and from
six to seven human foetuses are required in order to obtain suﬃcient dopaminergic neurons
to treat one singular patient[43].
Consequently, some groups have tried to use less ethical
contentious cells[42]. Examples are fetal ventral mesencephalon stem cells derived from pigs,
cells derived from the adrenal medulla or retinal pigmentary epithelial cells, mesenchymal or
parthenogenetic (which are derived from unfertilised oocytes) stem cells. Pour results were
found. There were no evidences of long survival time, reinnervation and signiﬁcant reduction
of motor symptoms[42]. Hence, new strategies have been pursued in order to minimize the
problems related to the use of human fetal stem cells. These new strategies are based on a
more accurate analysis and planiﬁcation. Only patients that can have the maximum advantages
from the surgery can be treated. The choice was based according to the age and time since
the onset of the motor symptom[42].
The potential eﬃcacy of human fetal mesencephalic
tissue transplantation due to its high content of dopaminergic neurons is shown to be eﬀective
by researchers since 1970 using animal model[14].
Exciting survival time, up to 10 years,
not negligible dopamine released, a connection with host brain and an improvement in motor
behaviour were found in animal model graft[42]. Due to the promising results found, studies
have tested and compared human fetal stem cell lines belonging to diﬀerent brain region. Stem
cell lines from the ventral mesencephalon were found to have the most promising results in terms
of dopaminergic neurons formation and ability to generate action potentials[4]. Furthermore,
a diﬀerent study found that human ventral mesencephalic cell line (hVM1) cell line are able to
survive in diﬀerentiation medium for more than 30 days and are able to sustain their molecular
identity acquiring neuronal properties[44]. One of the biggest advantages in the use of hVM1
is their ability to maintain their regional identity, which can lead to dopaminergic neurons
generation[43]. First attempts to treat PD with stem cell therapy in humans were based on the
transplantation of human foetal ventral mesencephalic tissue directly inside the brain of the
patients[43; 45]. Controversial results were found due to graft induced dyskinesias, unregulated
32
==PAGE:33==
dopamine release and an excessive amount of cell proliferation and dispersion[43]. On the other
hand, this attempts were fundamental as proof-of-principle of the possibility to transplant
dopaminergic neurons and reinnervate the striatum[44; 45; 46].
In order to overcame all these problems, other researchers based their studies in the at-
tempt to treat PD, using scaﬀold to contrast and regulate the cell dispersion and improving
the characteristics of human fetal neural stem cells, using genetic modiﬁcation or epigenetic
expansion[43]. As it previoulsy mentioned NSCs are multipotent stem cells, derived from the
central nervous system, able to diﬀerentiate in neurons, astrocytes and oligodendrocytes. Dif-
ferent strategies were used in the attempt to improve the use of these cells. Firstly, several
growth factors as ﬁbroblast growth factors (FGF) and epidermal growth factor (EGF) were
added to the growth medium in order to promote their proliferation[43]. Later, this epigenetic
propagation was used in combination with immortalization using an oncogene (oncoprotein)
called v-myc. Immortalization, made by infection with a retroviral vector coding for v-myc, has
permitted the possibility to increase the life span of human fetal derived stem cells in culture
and, at the same time, to grow these cells maintaining a stable phenotype and genotype[4; 44].
On the other hand, v-myc immortalization can compromise and limit the potential use of this
cells in clinical applications. Evidences have found how their pathways are involved in tumors
insurgent and proliferation[47]. One last amelioration was used by some research groups. An
anti-apoptotic B-cell lymphoma 2 (Bcl-2) family protein called basal cell lymphoma-extra large
(Bcl-XL)[45; 46].
The use of this protein has permitted to resolve the problems of limited
propagation and dopaminergic phenotypes generation[46]. Bcl-XL was found to be very im-
portant during central nervous system development for its anti-apoptotic role, its ability to
modulate and promote neuronal and dopaminergic diﬀerentiation and ﬁnally for its ability to
protect cellular diﬀerentiation from cytotoxic insults[46]. In some studies using hVM1 from
rodent and humans, it has been found that Bcl-XL can improve not only neural human stem
cell neurogenic capacity, but also, it can generate more dopaminergic neurons[45]. This eﬀect
is obtained because of its antiapoptotic and proneural eﬀect as well as its enhanced expression
of genes involved in dopaminergic process(diﬀerentiation and maturation)[46]. Furthermore, it
preserves the dopaminergic behaviour of neurons in expanded cultures[46]. In-vitro and in-vivo
experiments, the latter in hemiparkisonian rats, have conﬁrmed the previous statements[46].

to deactivate the trypsin. Then, an Eppendorf Easypet was used to rinse the bottom of the
ﬂask, where the cells were atteched, to obtain an eﬀective cell detachment. At this point, the
BM with the detached cell, was transferred into a 15mL Falcon conical centrifuge tube and
centrifuged at 1000rps for 5 minutes. Once the centrifuge was concluded, the supernatant, the
liquid above the solid residue of cells, was removed and the cells were suspended in 3mL GM.
200µL of the new suspension were then taken and the number of cells per milliliter was counted.
The desired number of cells were then added to the new ﬂask and GM was inserted in order to
reach a ﬁnal volume of 6mL.

6
Results and Discussion
This chapter discusses the obtained results of this project. Firstly, a novel method of real-
ization of multi-point, side-emitting optical ﬁber with the use of laser ablation technique is
investigated and presented. Secondly, the possible impact, of the created micro-cavitites, in
the electrochemical behaviour of the ﬁber is examined. Lastly, the results of an in-vitro study
using the produced side-emitting ﬁbers in combination with optogenetic modiﬁed stem cell are
presented. The aim was to detect dopaminergic neurons diﬀerentiation and dopamine release.
6.1
Laser Ablation
Improving the amount of light leaking out from an optical ﬁber in order to stimulate more
neuronal population adherent to the ﬁber was one of the main goals of this project. The limited
illumination potential of an optical ﬁber in the area around the tip is the main drawback in
their biological application for the stimulation of optogenetic modiﬁed stem cell.
The creation of patterns of micro-holes on optical ﬁber (Fig.23) permits an enhancement in
light leaking, but the stimulation is still limited in the areas around the holes.
Figure 23: Model of laser ablated micro holes on the surface of a ﬁber.
The introduction of a novel approach to permit the rotation of the optical ﬁber sample aims
to resolve this problems, creating three-dimensional side ﬁring conﬁguration.
According to recent literature studies, laser ablation techniques are considered one of the
best method to create cavities in hard materials, especially on optical ﬁber[27; 36].
The process of holes creation was a progressive study. It involved an analysis of the laser
power, repetition and focal points in the attempt to investigate the best parameters of the laser
micromachining tool.
The optimal paramenters were investigated focusing on the characteristics of the holes, in
terms of shape, dimension and depth. The appearance of the holes can aﬀect the electrochemical
properties of the ﬁber. As a matter of fact, stronger power values can cause damages to the
51
==PAGE:52==
external carbon layer and cause a reduction in the conductive capacities of a pyrolytic optical
ﬁber. The depth of the holes is another crucial aspect. Deeper holes can increase the amount of
light leaking and obtain an higher stimulation of optogenetics modiﬁed cells but can interfere
with the ﬁber mechanical strength. The deeper the holes, the more brittle is the ﬁber and more
diﬃcult is their potential surgical insertion.
Once found, the ideal parameters were then exploited for the creation of a three-dimensional
side emitting conﬁguration. An innovative approach based on the realization of a 3D printed
platform which matches the optical ﬁber and laser micromaching tool requirements, was se-
lected.

6.1.4.1
Creation of a platform able to rotate an optical ﬁber
In order to achieve the creation of holes around an optical ﬁber, the available moving
platform of the laser micromachining tool and the old sample described in Fig.24, could not
be used. The range of motion of the platform is limited to horizontal and vertical movements
making impossible the realization of holes on the bottom of the ﬁber.
A new sample and method able to permit the placing and ﬁxation of the ﬁber as well as its
rotation was then considered.
Diﬀerent options were examined, the creation of a 3D printed structure was considered the
optimal one. This choise was the result of consideration of the inner structure of the laser
micromachining tool, the limited movements of the platform, and all the steps required to
select the right focal point and the reference points for the initial position and rotation.
For the creation of a structure able to achieve the aims, diﬀerent features were considered:
• The creation of a stable structure.
• The presence of a groove required to place and ﬁx an optical ﬁber during the procedure.
• The right dimension in order to permits not only the adhesion of the structure to the
sample vacuum chuck ( moving platform ) of the Laser Micromachining Tool but also to
permit any movement inside the device without damage the inner component.
62
==PAGE:63==
• The ability to rotate and ﬁx the ﬁber at the desired angles.
• The presence of references in order to select the focus of the laser on the bottom of the
ﬁber.

6.2
Electrochemical characterization of optical ﬁbers
Cyclic voltammetry was used to determine the conductivity of the pyrolyzed optical ﬁber and
to detect if they can be used as electrodes even if laser ablation was performed. As a matter of
fact, in the last years, carbon electrodes have been used as surface of working electrodes. The
reason behind this large employment of carbon is that carbon is among the most important
materials presented in nature with good chemical, electrical and electrochemical properties.
The optical ﬁber used for this project presents were preventively pyrolyzed at 900◦C which
has led to the formation of an external carbon layer covering completely the ﬁber. Consequently,
it was interesting to investigate the conductivity of the ﬁbers and whether their behaviour is
inﬂuenced by the presence of few or many holes. The reason behind this possible changes lays
on a reduction of the working electrode area a consequence of the ablation of holes on the
surface of the ﬁber.

7
Conclusion
The aim of this study was the realization of a novel method to create 3D side-emitting pyrolyzed
optical ﬁber which can act both as a substrate for stem cell diﬀerentiation and stimulation
of optogenetically modiﬁed neural stem cells. Pyrolysed optical ﬁbers were laser ablated to
produce micro-holes on the surface. The inﬂuence of laser power, number of repetitions and focal
points on the diameter, depth and shape of the holes were analyzed. Deeper holes were created
by higher laser powers to increase the light leak intensity, but at the cost of distorted shape
of the hole, damaged carbon layer and reduced mechanical strength of the ﬁber. This resulted
in a extremely brittle ﬁber with deteriorated electrochemical properties.
Conversely, lower
power resulted in shallower holes with light intensities unsuitable for optogenetic stimulation.
However, the desired shape of holes as well as the integrity of the pyrolytic carbon layer were
maintained. A solution of the problem was found by a further investigation of the selection of
focal position of the laser during laser ablation. An increase in the holes depth, without an
excessive brittleness of the ﬁber, and the minimum diameter were found by placing the focus of
the laser at the bottom of the ﬁber. The diameter of the holes was observed to double when the
focal point was close to the top of the ﬁber, increasing the possibility of cell entrapment in the
ablated holes. The optimal settings were found to be at a laser power of approximately 2.75W,
the use of 600 repetitions and a focal point on the bottom of the optical ﬁber. Once the ideal
parameters were found, these settings were applied in the study of a novel method to create
holes all around the surface of a pyrolysed optical ﬁber. Two 3D printed components were
designed and assembled to allow ﬁxation and rotation of the pyrolysed optical ﬁber without
damaging the external carbon layer. The eﬀective ablation of six rows of holes at six diﬀerent
angles (45◦, 90◦, 135◦, 225◦, 270◦and 315◦) was achieved. Cyclic voltammetry was used for
the electrochemical characterization of the 3D side emitting optical ﬁbers. Voltammograms of
the same ﬁber were examined to verify a possible change in the behavior without the presence
of holes, with a single raw of holes and in the 3D side emitting conﬁguration. A decrease
in the oxidation and reduction currents was observed due to the decrease in the surface area
with inceasing number of holes. Moreover, in the case of 3D side emitting ﬁber, there was
widening of peak separation potential. However, by subjecting the ﬁbers to oxygen plasma, the
current increased followed by reduction on the peak separation on the same order as a pristine
pyrolysed ﬁber. In-vitro studies using human ventral mesencephalic stem cells seeded on the
3D side emitting ﬁber were performed. Two types of coatings (PLL 10µg/mL and Geltrex)
and two diﬀerent amount of cell densities, for each coating, were tested (100000 cells/mL and
200000 cells/mL). To verify the results, real-time dopamine exocytosis measurements using
chronoamperometry as well as immunocytochemistry were performed on the stem cells after
10 days of diﬀerentiation. An eﬀective real-time dopamine exocytosis detection was measured
with the two diﬀerent types of coating. Higher values of dopamine were measured for higher
cell densities and highest release of dopamine was found on PLL coated ﬁbers. This was due to
the higher number of dopaminergic neurons on PLL coated ﬁbers. This was further conﬁrmed
86
==PAGE:87==
by immunocytochemistry. On the other hand, Geltrex coated ﬁbers displayed excellent cell
expansion and adhesion to the ﬁber, but with poor diﬀerentiation into dopaminergic neurons.
These results demonstrate the reproducibility of using laser ablation for the fabrication of 3D
side emitting ﬁber as a substrate for diﬀerentiation of neural stem cells and an electrode for
electrochemical detection of dopamine.
7.1
Outlook
A 3D printed assembled structure was found to produce optimal results for the creation of three
dimensional side emitting pyrolysed optical ﬁbers. Further improvements of the 3D platform
and in the pattern of holes can lead to the ablation of a wider distribution of holes over larger
surface of the ﬁber. Moreover, the versatility of 3D printing could be exploited to pattern
multiple ﬁbers at the same time. Based on the preliminary results from this work, in-vivo
experiments in animal model could be the next step to assess the behavior and suitability of
these 3D side emitting pyrolysed optical ﬁbers as an implant device for Parkinson’s therapy.
