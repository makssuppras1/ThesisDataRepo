I 
 
 
ABSTRACT  
Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) have shown to be toxic and carcinogenic for the human body. 
PAHs are formed from the incomplete combustion of coal, oil, gas and organic waste and are released 
into the environment. They can adsorb onto particular matters and thereby accumulate in vegetation. 
Especially four PAHs (PAH4); benzo[a]anthrancene (B[a]A), chrysene (CHR), benzo[a]pyrene (B[a]P) 
and benzo[b]flouranthene (B[b]Fa) are considered to be carcinogenic. PAH4 can be found in amounts up 
to 25 ng/g in coffee beans and 2700 ng/g in tealeaves. Despite the lipophilic character of PAHs, they are 
found in coffee brew and tea infusion as well.  
Coffee and tea are consumed by the Danish population and due to the carcinogenic effect of PAHs, it is 
important to know the exact exposure to PAH4 when consuming coffee or tea. 
In this report, a risk assessment was conducted, based on numbers from EFSA, which showed no risk of 
getting cancer from coffee and tea. A transfer coefficient of 26% was used for the calculation which was 
obtained from other studies were the transfer differed greatly.   
To validate the potential carciogenic effects of coffee and tea, 4 types of tealeaves, 2 types of coffee beans 
and 2 types of instant coffees were analysed for their content of PAH4. This made it possible to calculate 
a transfer coefficient from beans/leaves to brew/infusion and establish a mass balance for the PAH4. 
Samples were analysed before and after being used to make coffee brew and tea infusion and the coffee 
brew and tea infusion were analysed as well. A ASE and SPE method was used for extracting PAH4 from 
beans and leaves and C18-colums where used for extraction of PAH4 from brew and infusion. Extracts 
where analysed on GC-QTOF-MS.  
The methods were validated by specificity/selectivity, linearity, recovery, LOD and LOQ. From the 
recovery of the 4 PAHs it was found that the method used on coffee beans and tealeaves was more 
imprecise than the other. This was furthermore implied in results from the 2 instant coffees where different 
results were obtained in the 2 methods, even though all the instant coffee is dissolved in the water. The 
PAH4 content from the instant coffees were between 5.62-13.42 ng/g from the coffee beans and tealeaves 
method and between 0.33-0.37 ng/g from the coffee brew and tea infusion method.  
PAH4 detected in tealeaves varied from 30.47 to 44.41 ng/g and from 5.5 to 8.17 ng/g in coffee beans. 
The coffee brews and tea infusions had a PAH4 content of 0.019-0.027 ng/g yielding a transfer coefficient 
from beans/leaves to brew/infusion of 3.37-4.78% and 4.85-6.33%, respectively. The experimentally 
found transfer coefficient is much lower than the one used by EFSA. With this lower transfer coefficient, 
the risk of getting cancer from consumption of coffee and tea is lower than stated by EFSA.  
The content of PAH4 in the used coffee beans and tealeaves were higher than before use, resulting in the 
mass balance not breaking even. This could be due to the boiling water making it easier to homogenise 
and extract the PAH4 from the samples.  
SERS was tested on 4 PAH4 extracts from coffee and tea using Ag substrate. It was found that it should 
be possible to use B[a]P as an indicator of PAH4 because B[a]P had a unique spectrum compared to the 
other 3 PAHs. It was however not possible to find B[a]P in the spectra. The extraction contained too many 
undesirable compounds which made it difficult to analyse the spectra. 
Before a clear conclusion can be made, some modifications have to be considered. The method for the 
coffee beans and tealeaves has to be optimised, hence the recoveries need to be improved. More 
experiments have to be conducted before concluding on the PAH4 content and the transfer coefficient. 
Furthermore, the extractions have to be cleaner before SERS can be used.

RESUMÉ 
Polycykliske Aromatiske Hydrocarboner (PAH’er) har vist sig at være toksiske og kræftfremkaldende i 
menneskekroppen. PAH’er dannes ud fra en ufuldstændig forbrænding af kul, olie, gas og organisk 
materiale og frigives til omgivelserne. De kan adsorbere på partikler og derved akkumuleres i planter. 
Især 4 PAH’er (PAH4): benzo[a]anthrancene (B[a]A), chrysene (CHR), benzo[a]pyrene (B[a]P) og 
benzo[b]flouranthene (B[b]Fa) betragtes som kræftfremkaldende. PAH4 findes i kaffe i koncentrationer 
på op til 25 ng/g og i te, i koncentrationer op til 2700 ng/g. Selvom PAH’er er lipofile, findes de også i 
kaffebryggen og te drikken.   
Kaffe og te drikkes der meget af i Danmark og på grund af den kræftfremkaldende effekt af PAH’er, er 
det meget vigtig at kende den præcise eksponering fra kaffe og te. 
Ud fra en risikovurdering med tal fra EFSA blev det fundet frem til at kaffe og te ikke udgør en kræft 
risiko. Der blev dog brugt en overførselskoefficient på 26% hvilket var fundet ud fra tidligere studier som 
havde fundet meget varierende overførselskoefficienter.  
For at validere den potentielle kræftfremkaldende effekt, blev der i dette studie analyseret 4 typer teblade, 
2 typer kaffebønner og 2 typer instant kaffe for deres indhold af PAH4, således at det var muligt at udregne 
overførselskoefficienten fra bønnerne/bladene til bryggen/drikken og opstille en massebalance for PAH4. 
Prøverne blev analyseret før og efter de blev brugt the at brygge kaffe og lave te. Kaffebryggen og te 
drikken blev ligeledes analyseret.  
Der blev brugt to forskellige metoder, en til kaffebønnerne og tebladene som inkluderede ekstraktion med 
ASE and SPE og en metode til kaffebryggen og te drikken som inkluderede ekstraktion med C18-kolonner. 
Alle ekstraktionerne blev analyseret på GC-QTOF-MS.  
Metoderne blev evaluret ved specificitet/selektivitet, linearitet, genfindelse samt LOD og LOQ. Ud fra 
genfindelsen af de 4 PAH’er blev det set at metoden til kaffebønnerne og tebladene var lidt upræcis. Det 
samme kunne ses ud fra resultaterne fra de 2 instant kaffer, da resultaterne varierede meget afhængigt af 
hvilken metode der blev brugt, selvom alt instant kaffen blev opløst i vandet. Der blev fundet en 
koncentration af PAH4 på 5,62-13,42 ng/g fra kaffebønne og teblade metoden og 0,33-0,37 ng/g fra 
kaffebryg og te drik metoden.  
Den detekterede koncentrationen af PAH4 i tebladene varierede fra 30,47 ng/g til 44.41 ng/g mens 
kaffebryggen og te drikken indeholdte fra 0,019 til 0,027 ng/ml. Dette resulterede i en 
overførelseskoefficient fra bønner/blade til bryg/drik på 3,37-4,78% for kaffe og 4,85-6,33% for te. Den 
fundne overførelseskoefficient er meget lavere end den EFSA brugte til deres vurdering. Med den nye 
overførelseskoefficient bliver risikoen for kræft endnu lavere.  
Indholdet af PAH4 i de brugte kaffebønner og teblade var meget højere end før de blev brugt, hvilket 
resulterede i at massebalancen ikke gik op. Dette kunne være på grund af det kogende vand som kan have 
gjort det nemmere at homogenisere og ekstrahere PAH4 fra prøverne. 
SERS blev testet på 4 PAH4 ekstraktioner fra kaffe of te på Ag substrat. B[a]P blev valgt som indikator 
for PAH4, fordi B[a]P havde et unik spektrum i forhold til de andre 3 PAH’er. Dog var det ikke muligt at 
finde B[a]P i spektrene fra kaffe og te. Ekstraktioner indeholdte for mange andre stoffer som gjorde det 
svært at analysere spektrene.  
Før der kan blive lavet en klar konklusion, skal der ske nogle ændringer. Kaffebønne og teblade metoden 
skal optimeres så genfindelsen bliver større. Der skal laves flere eksperimenter før der kan konkluderes 
på PAH4 indholdet og overførelseskoefficienten. Ydermere skal ekstraktionerne være renere før de kan 
anvendes med SERS.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) have shown to be toxic and carcinogenic for the human body 
and are classified as group 1 “carcinogenic to human”, 2A “probable carcinogenic” and 2B “possible 
carcinogenic” by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) [1]. PAHs are formed from 
incomplete combustion of coal, oil, gas and organic waste. The PAHs are released into the environment 
where they can be adsorbed onto particles such as soil [2]. PAHs can accumulate in vegetation, so they 
can be found in a broad spectrum of food. In addition to carryover of PAHs from contamination of the 
environment into food, they can also come from food processing such as roasting and drying. 
 
One type of food in particular where PAHs have been detected, is in coffee and tea. The PAHs in the 
coffee beans and tealeaves can come from both the environment by contamination and from the 
production, which include a roasting or a drying step. Even though coffee has several possible beneficial 
effects, it does contain unhealthy components and is classified as 2B by IARC in regards to urinary bladder 
cancer [3], [4]. Especially four PAHs (PAH4): benzo[a]anthrancene (B[a]A), chrysene (CHR), 
benzo[a]pyrene (B[a]P) and benzo[b]flouranthene (B[b]Fa) are considered carcinogenic and legislation 
for the use of these compounds have been conducted [5]. Previous studies have analysed the PAH content 
in in both coffee beans and coffee brew as well as tealeaves and tea infusion [6]–[13]. The content of 
PAH4 in the coffee beans and tealeaves can be as high as  25 ng/g for coffee beans [7] and 2700 ng/g for 
tealeaves (mate tea) [11]. Likewise the content of PAH4 in coffee brew and tea infusion have been found 
to be around 3-5 ng/g for coffee brew [13] and up to 1400 ng/g for mate tea infusion [12]. 
 
European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) has collected European food data for the content of PAHs and 
conducted an exposure assessment, which included the amount of PAH4 gotten from different groups of 
food. Coffee and tea was one of those groups of food, however, the calculation of the exposure of PAHs 
in coffee brew and tea infusion were questionable. The transfer coefficient of PAH4 from the coffee beans 
to the coffee brew they used was 26% which EFSA has obtained from for example one study where the 
transfer coefficient varied from 0.6-30.9% for B[a]A, CHR and B[b]Fa with no B[a]P transferred. There 
is a big difference in the content of PAH4 if 0.6% is transferred compared to 30.9%. This can have a big 
impact on the exposure assessment and it is therefore important to know the correct transfer coefficient.   
 
In Denmark, coffee and tea are a normal part of the daily life. People often go out to drink coffee or tea 
with friends and it is often associated with having a good time. In fact, Denmark is one of the countries 
in Europe, where the population drink the most coffee, tea and cocoa [14]. It is therefore particularly 
important to know the correct content of PAH4 in coffee brew and tea infusion to be able to better estimate 
the risk of getting cancer from drinking it. This fact together with the lack of information concerning the 
transfer, makes it interesting to investigate.  
 
The path to finding the PAH content is very long and takes a lot of time [15]. The tested sample have to 
be prepared before the PAHs can be extracted which often involve several processes and equipment. After 
the extraction the sample then have to be analysed on a detector and mass analyser.

1.1 Aim 
The aim of the study is to estimate the transfer of PAH4 from dried coffee beans and tealeaves into coffee 
brew and tea infusion. Four tea samples and four coffee samples will be analysed for their content of 
PAH4 before and after they have been used for coffee brew and tea infusion. The coffee brew and tea 
infusion will likewise be analysed for their content of PAH4. By analysing those, it will be attempted to 
calculate a transfer coefficient and establish a mass balance.   
Two different methods (one for coffee beans and tealeaves and one for coffee brew and tea infusion) will 
be adapted to extract the content of PAH4. The content will be detected and quantified by using Gas 
Chromatography - Quadrupole time of flight - Mass Spectrometry (GS-QTOF-MS). The samples will also 
be tested on Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering (SERS) in collaboration with DTU Nanotechnology to 
investigate whether a new and faster way to detect PAH in a food matrix can be found.  
 
In addition, a risk assessment will be conducted, to see whether PAHs in coffee and/or tea are a health 
concern for the population. The numbers from EFSA will be compared to the obtained result in the study, 
to see how big a difference there is.

2 POLYCYCLIC AROMATIC HYDROCARBONS  
Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) are most commonly defined as organic compounds that 
contain two or more fused aromatic rings made up of only carbon and hydrogen atoms and does not 
contain heteroatoms [2]. Some PAHs are found to be toxic. In 2002 the Scientific Committee on Food 
(SCF) evaluated the risk of PAHs in food to human health [16]. SCF evaluated 33 compounds because 
these were included in the International Programme on Chemical Safety (IPCS) Environmental Health 
Criteria document on PAHs [2]. It was found that 15 out of the 33 evaluated PAHs showed clear evidence 
of mutagenicity/genotoxicity and 14 of these also showed carcinogenic effects on experimental animals. 
In total SCF found 15 PAHs to have a potentially genotoxic and carcinogenic effect on humans [16]. 
In 2005 the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food additives (JECFA) re-evaluated the PAHs. They 
concluded that 13 out of the 15 PAHs SCF found were clearly genotoxic and carcinogenic. They further 
recommended another PAH should be included as carcinogenic because it might contribute to the 
formation of lung tumours after oral exposure [17]. The 16 PAHs are listed in table 1 and their structure 
can be seen in appendix I.

The most toxic and well-studied PAH is B[a]P. Until recently B[a]P was the only PAH with legislative

the occurrence and toxicity of the 16 PAHs. Focus was on 8 carcinogenic and genotoxic PAHs that were

The CONTAM Panel concluded that PAH4 and PAH8 were the best indicators of PAHs in food. PAH8

this limit will be lowered to 30 ng/g fat on April 1st 2015 [5]. Because of the conclusion made by the 
CONTAM panel, PAH4 will be focus of this study and unless otherwise is stated.  
 
2.1 Physical and Chemical properties 
The physical and chemical properties of PAHs are mainly determined by the conjugated α-electron system 
which varies according to the number of rings and molecular mass. At room temperature all PAHs are 
solids. PAHs in general have a high melting point and a high boiling point, in addition they have a low 
vapour pressure. Their solubility in water is very low but they are soluble in many organic solvents and 
they are highly lipophilic [2]. In table 3 the boiling point and solubility in water for the 4 PAHs are listed. 
All the PAHs in PAH4 have a high boiling point (400 - 495ºC) and the solubility in water differs from 
1,200 ng/L to 14,000 ng/L.

Due to the lipophilic characteristic, PAHs in general are adsorbed by particulates in aqueous 
environments. They can also dissolve in oily contaminants which can be present in water and soil. The 
solubility of PAHs is inversely proportional to the number of rings the molecule contains [14]. 
 
PAHs are chemically inert compounds. They can undergo two types of reactions: electrophilic 
substitutions and additions. In the electrophilic substitution reaction, a hydrogen atom in the PAH is 
replaced by an electrophile and thereby forms derivatives. In the addition reaction, the aromatic character 
==PAGE:13==
 
6 
 
 
of the affected benzene ring is destroyed. After an addition reaction an elimination reaction can occur 
often resulting in net substitution [2]. PAHs have shown to react with nitrogen oxides, nitric acid, sulfur 
oxides and sulphuric acid to form nitro derivatives of PAH and sulfinic/sulfonic acid, respectively [2].  
In the presence of air and sunlight PAHs can undergo photodecomposition which leads to formation of 
oxidative products, including quinones and endoperoxides [2].  
 
2.2 Formation and Sources 
About 500 different PAHs have so far been detected in ambient air [14]. PAHs are created when 
substances such as coal, oil, gas and organic waste are burned incompletely in a process termed pyrolysis, 
which occurs at high temperatures when no oxygen is present [14]. 
Experiments have been performed to investigate exactly how PAHs are formed. Richter and Howard 
wrote a review in 2000 which combined all the experiments made on the formation of PAHs [19]. A lot 
of different chemical pathways have been suggested, which both depend on the fuels but also on the 
temperature and pressure. Overall, the formation of the first aromatic ring happens through a series of 
reactions involving C4H3, C4H5 and C3H3 with benzene (C6H6) being end product. The two-ring aromatic 
naphthalene can be formed by two cyclopentadienyl reacting with each other. The cyclopentadienyl comes 
from a benzene ring which has been oxidized. Another way to fuse two rings together is with free radicals, 
like phenyl and benzyl, which can react with acetylene and thereby form the two-ring aromatic 
naphthalene. After the formation of naphthalene, further growth can occur by hydrogen-
abstraction/acetylene-addition steps where the rings are formed by adding acetylene to the rings [19].  
As mentioned, PAHs are formed from incomplete combustion, which means that they are ubiquitous. 
They are present in the atmosphere, surface water, sediments, soil and food. There are both anthropogenic 
sources and natural sources [2], [14], [16]. Approximately 80% of the annual amount of the formed PAHs 
are caused by anthropogenic sources [14]. Many of the PAHs are released into the atmosphere by these 
incomplete combustions. PAHs with three or fewer aromatic rings occur mostly in the atmosphere while 
PAHs with more aromatic rings enter the environment adsorbed onto particular matter like soil [2]. This 
means that the PAHs will also be present in plants and are able to accumulate in vegetation [20]. PAHs 
can be found in at broad spectrum of food, for example cereals, chocolate, fats, vegetable, nuts, meat, fish, 
coffee and tea. Besides environmental source, PAHs can also come from processing, including industrial 
food processing and some home cooking [14], as roasting, drying, barbecuing and smoking.  
 
2.2.1 Coffee and Tea 
The PAHs in coffee comes either from a contamination of the green beans or from an endogenous 
formation in the coffee beans during the roasting process [6]. There are different factors that controls the 
formation of PAHs in coffee. Houessou et al. studied in 2007 the effect of temperature and roasting time 
[6]. Arabica green coffee beans were roasted at temperatures between 180 - 260 ˚C for 5 or 20 min. The 
different temperatures changed the content of PAH4 from 1.06 to 6.07 ng/ml (180 ᵒC–260 ᵒC and 5 min.) 
while the different roasting times changed the content of PAH4 from 1.06 to 1.6 ng/g at 180 ᵒC and from 
6.07 ng/g to 25.49 ng/g at 260 ᵒC [6]. They concluded that with an increased temperature or a longer 
roasting time, the content of PAHs increased as well. A combination of long roasting times and high 
temperatures also yielded a higher content of PAHs.  The group tested 11 different PAHs where PAH4 
were among them and found, that the major contribution of PAH4 was B[a]A while B[a]P were either 
==PAGE:14==
 
7 
 
 
not detected or found only at very low levels under strong roasting (20 min at 240-250 °C) [6]. Coffee 
brew prepared from roasted coffee beans was also tested. The content of PAH4 in the coffee brew was 
very low (<0.3 ng/g) which was expected due to the hydrophobic character of PAHs. The transfer 
coefficient of the infusion was under 30.9% for the 4 PAHs in PAH4 independently of the roasting time 
[6]. In another study by Houessou et al. in 2008  it was suggested that several compounds in the coffee 
beans could lead to the formation of PAHs [7]. Trigonelline by mimicking coffee roasting [21], lipids by 
heating 2 hours at 220 ˚C [22] and steorids by pyrolysis [23]–[25] all showed sign of formation of PAHs.  
 
The amount of PAHs in tealeaves before processing, depends on the surface of the leaves as well as the 
thickness and the plants composition. The processing of tea also has an impact on the PAH content due 
to the different combustion sources used to dry the leaves. Both wood, oil and coal are used as heating 
sources when drying the leaves [26].The content of PAH in tea varies a lot depending on which type of 
tea examined, which can likely be explained by different manufacturing processes [26]. As an example, 
green tea consists of young tealeaves that are withered, steamed or pan fried, then dried, graded and 
packaged. Black tea is made by fermenting the slightly wilted leaves and then smoked firing, flamed firing 
or steaming. Mate tea, which has the highest content of PAH4 (around 2700 ng/g [11]), is first slightly 
fermented and then blanched at high temperatures to deactivate enzymes before drying it slowly, often 
using wood smoke [8], [27]. 
Both tealeaves and tea infusion have been tested for PAH content and will be discussed in the following.  
In 1997 Schlemitz and Pfannhauser tested different types of tea [11]. It was found that mate tea has the 
highest content of PAH4 (around 2700 ng/g for roasted mate tea and around 1500 ng/g for green mate 
tea). The other tea had a much lower content of PAH4, for example Earl Grey had a content of PAH4 of 
82.1 ng/g and some instant fruit tea had a content of PAH4 of 2.18 ng/g [11]. In 2011 Singh et al. tested 
different brands of tea (Red Label, Super Cup, Tata Agni, Dunken, Harman) for the 16 toxic PAHs [9]. 
The content of PAHs ranged from 101.9 ng/ml (Dunken tea) to 464.0 ng/ ml (Tata agni tea). However the 
content of PAH4 was almost non existing, only two samples had a content of CHR (14 ng/g in Super Cup 
tea and 16.6 ng/g in Dunken tea) while B[a]P, B[a]A and B[b]Fa were not detectable in any of the samples 
[9].  
In 2014 Schulz et al. determined the content of the 16 toxic PAHs set by SCF and JECFA in various types 
of teas [12]. It was discovered that by just preparing various types of herbal tea under normal house 
preparation (20 g in 2 L boiling tap water for 10 min) no PAHs was detected [12]. By adding a 
homogenising step of the tea before adding water, PAHs was found in most of the samples. The mate tea 
infusion had the highest content of PAH reaching 2500 ng/ml where PAH4 counted for 1400 ng/ml. The 
tea infusion with the next highest content of PAH4 was peppermint tea which had a content of PAH4 up 
to 100 ng/ml or else was the content of PAH4 around 1-40 ng/ml [12]. Some tealeaves were also tested. 
For example black tealeaves had a content of PAH4 up to 1200 ng/g, while the content of PAH4 in green 
tea was lower (up to 430 ng/g) [12].    
  
One of the newest studies from 2015 by Duedahl-Olesen et al. investigated both coffee and tea in dried 
form and ready-to-drink solutions (coffee brew and tea infusion) [13]. The results showed that tealeaves 
in general have the highest content of PAH4 (5.8-57.5 ng/g). They tested three types of tea (black, herbal, 
fruit) and determined that the content of PAH4 was highest in the black tea compared to the others.  
Dark roasted coffee beans had a higher content of PAH4 than medium roasted (3.1 ng/g and 1.0 ng/g, 
respectively). Two samples of instant coffee were tested and the content was 2.2 ng/g and 5.1 ng/g  giving 
==PAGE:15==

an average higher content of PAH4 than dark roasted coffee [13]. It was suggested that the reason could 
be that instant coffee is produced by first preparing a coffee brew followed by freeze drying and that this 
process increases the concentration [13]. Duedahl-Olesen et al. also tested the ready-to-drink infusion and 
found that the tea infusion had 0-2.3 % of the amount of PAH4 compared to the levels found in the 
tealeaves, but 45 % of the tea infusion did not contain PAH4. All of the ready-to-drink coffee solutions 
had a content of PAH4 except for one of the medium roasted coffee brews. However the transfer was only 
found to be between 5-14 % of the original content of PAH4 [13].

2.3 Analytics methods 
There are many ways to analyse PAHs in a given sample, depending on which matrix the PAHs are located 
in. It is possible to test the PAH content in different types of air (ambient air or vehicle exhaust) and in 
different matrices such as food, oils, waters and soils [2]. This section will provide information about the 
equipment used in this study. Figure 1 shows an overview of the steps involved in the detection of PAHs. 
 
 
The preparation of the sample is basically a homogenisation step to ensure the uniformity of the samples. 
To extract the sample in this study, two principals are applied:  Accelerated Solvent Extraction (ASE) and 
Solid Phase Extraction (SPE). In ASE a combination of liquid solvent at elevated temperature and 
pressure, extracts the PAHs. SPE is a way to concentrate and purify samples by separate the compounds 
in the mixture according to their physical and chemical properties.     
 
For separation and detection of the PAHs in this study, Gas Chromatography - Quadrupole time of flight 
- Mass Spectrometry (GC-QTOF-MS) and Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering (SERS) are used. The 
mechanism behind GC-QTOF-MS and the principle in SERS are described below.

2.3.2 Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering 
Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering (SERS) is an adaptation of Raman scattering. In Raman scattering 
or Raman spectroscopy the sample is excited with a laser. The laser light is scattered by the molecules. 
Most of the scattered radiation will have the same energy as the original light and cannot be seen. A very 
small part of the laser light (around 1 photon out of 1,000,000) undergoes a slight energy change. The 
light is shifted from its original frequency which is called a Raman shift. The difference in energy between 
the incident laser excitation and the Raman scattered light is equal to vibrational transition energy of the 
molecule. The vibration of the molecules depends on the orientation between the atoms and bonds, the 
mass of the molecules, the bond order and hydrogen bonding. The scattered photons yields a spectrum 
containing several Raman peaks which can be identified due to their unique molecular fingerprint [29]. 
 
SERS is a technique where a surface is used to enhance the Raman signal scattering of a sample. It happens 
when an analyte comes close to or adsorbs onto a metal surface. Metals such as silver (Ag) or gold (Au) 
are commonly used. The excitation laser hits the metal surface and it excites surface plasmons, which are 
coherent electron oscillations on the surface of the metals [30], [31]. If the frequency of the laser is in 
==PAGE:17==
 
10 
 
 
resonance with the surface plasmons the greatest enhancement can occur. Analytes that are on or close to 
the metal surface will therefore get an enhanced Raman signal. The principle behind SERS is sketched in 
figure 3.

The identification, detection and quantification of PAHs have been tested with SERS in seawater [32], 
[33]. PAHs were found in the studies, but some problems occurred. Schmith et al. from 2004 found out 
that it is possible to detect PAHs in seawater using SERS, however, the SERS signal decreased when they 
tested the PAHs in sediment instead of just seawater. The PAHs adsorbed to the sediment particles, which 
resulted in the amount of PAHs adsorbed to the sensor surface decreasing [32]. The same was seen in 
Phannkuche et al. in 2012 [33]. 
When conducting this study no information of PAHs tested in food matrix was found. SERS, however, 
has been used on food, where melamine was detected in gluten, chicken feed and some processed foods 
(cake and noodles) by Lin et al. in 2009 [34]. Here it was found that SERS was able to detect the melamine 
in the mentioned foods. They evaluated the method by using HPLC and found that SERS had a lower 
limit of detection (LOD) than the HPLC (0.033 µg/ml versus 1 µg/ml). Furthermore, the detecting time 
was much shorter for SERS than for HPLC (30 min for 8 samples using SERS versus 3 h for 8 samples 
using HPLC). The only drawback with using SERS was that the accurate quantification was lower for 
SERS than for HPLC shown by the R values (R = 0.90 using SERS and R = 0.99 using HPLC) [34].

3 RISK ASSESMENT  
To assess the health risk PAHs pose to the humans from drinking coffee and tea, a toxicological risk 
assessment can be performed. The risk assessment consists of four sections: Hazard identification, hazard 
characterization, exposure assessment, and risk characterization. 
 
3.1 Hazard identification 
Hazard identification focuses on the body and what happens to it, after ingesting PAHs through food and 
the environment (toxicokinetics). Furthermore hazard identification also includes what harm PAHs can 
cause the body (toxicodynamics). In two words hazard identification is toxicokinetics and 
toxicodynamics. 
 
3.1.1 Toxicokinetics 
In toxicokinetics the absorption, distribution, metabolism and excretion of a given compound is 
considered.  Some of the PAHs described in published studies are not included in PAH4, which are the 
focus of this study. Some of these data are, however, still taken into account in this section because they 
give a better understanding of the toxicokinetics of PAHs in general. 
  
3.1.1.1 Absorption  
PAHs can be absorbed through the lungs (inhalation of PAH-containing aerosols), the gastrointestinal 
tract (GIT) (ingestion of contaminated food or water) or the skin (contact with PAH-bearing materials) 
[2]. Due to the fact that coffee and tea is ingested, this section will limit its focus to absorption through 
the GIT.  
There are several factors which determine the absorption of PAHs; the size of the molecule, the 
lipohilicity, the dose, the content of lipid in the diet and the presence of the bile in the GIT. The more 
lipophilic the compound is or the more fat present in the GIT the better PAHs are absorbed through oral 
ingestion [35].  
 
The absorption of  PAHs has been confirmed in vitro where it was found that PAHs are dissolved and 
transported through the external and internal lipoprotein membranes of mammalian cells [2]. Studies on 
humans have been conducted, where absorption of B[a]P have seen to occur [36], [37]. 
The presence of bile was found to be significant in rats for the absorption of B[a]P and 7,12-
dimethylbenz[a]anthracene found in a study by Rahman et al. in 1986 [38]. However the absorption of 
tricyclic PAH (phenanthrene, anthracene and 2,6-dimethylnaphthalene) was found to be independent of 
the presence [38]. This is consistent with EFSA which state that in mammals the oral absorption of 3-4 
ring PAH are higher compared with the lipophilic higher-molecular mass PAHs [14].  
 
Human faeces has been investigated after consumption of charcoal-broiled meat containing B[a]P [36]. 
Even though each person consumed around 9 µg B[a]P, no B[a]P was detected in the faeces (<0.1 µg). 
This could indicate that most of the B[a]P is absorbed in the human body.  In the same study rats which 
were gavage exposed 14C-labelled B[a]P were tested. Between 74-79 % of the dose was excreted in the 
faeces within 48 hours as B[a]P and several metabolites. This suggest that some of the B[a]P should have 
==PAGE:19==
 
12 
 
 
been excreted in the human faeces. The authors of the study implies therefore that more experiments have 
to been made before any conclusion can be drawn [36].    
The Agency of Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) states that the absorption in rats are 
incomplete and may be influenced by the presence of fat in the GIT, as also stated by Rahman et al. [35], 
[38]. 
In another study were humans consumed a diet containing small amounts of B[a]P, high levels of the 
metabolite 1-hydroxypyrene was detected in the urine. This is indirect evidence that a absorption of B[a]P 
occurs [37].  
 
To sum up, it is difficult to say how much of the PAHs are absorbed in the GIT. Not many human trials 
have been conducted so a concrete amount of absorption cannot be estimated but data suggests that an 
absorption of PAHs does occur. 
 
3.1.1.2 Distribution 
Three things were discovered when studying the distribution of PAHs in the tissues of a body [2]:  
 
1. PAHs and their metabolites can be found in all internal organs. 
2. Organs rich in adipose tissue can serve as storage from where the PAHs can be released.  
3. The GIT contains high amounts of PAHs and their metabolites.  
 
Concentration of B[a]P, phenanthene and pyrene were determined in the muscle and adipose tissue of 
rats. The rats were treated with B[a]P with a concentration of 15 µg/kg b.w. alone or with phenanthene 
and pyrene with a concentration of 4300 µg/kg b.w. and 2700 µg/kg b.w., respectively. The highest 
concentration of B[a]P (23.6 ng/g) was found in the muscle tissues after 20 days. Phenanthene and pyrene 
were found in both the adipose and muscle tissue.  
 
To test whether PAHs reaches the brain, rats were given 0, 25, 50, 100 and 200 mg/kg b.w. of B[a]P in 
peanut oil by oral gavage [39]. After 0, 2, 4, 6, 12, 24, 48, 72. 96 hours samples of plasma and brain tissue 
(celebellum and cortex) were collected. A significant difference was detected in the unmetabolised B[a]P 
in plasma and brain tissue when the rats were treated with 100 and 200 mg/kg b.w. compared to the basis 
levels of plasma and tissue. The metabolites of B[a]P in all the studied groups were found in the plasma, 
cerebellum and cortex.  The concentration peaked after 2 hours and remained the same for up to 6 hours 
but then decreased to 1% of the dose after 72 hours and only trace levels of the metabolites were detectable 
after 96 hours [39]. This has also been documented earlier with B[a]A, CHR and triphenylene, showing 
that PAHs can be transported to the brain tissue [40]. 
 
In addition to the fact that PAHs can be distributed to the brain, muscle and adipose tissue, it can also be 
transferred to the fetus. Pregnant mice and rats were treated with B[a]P, 7,12-dimethylbenz[a]anthracene 
and 3-methylcholanthrene. The PAHs were widely distributed in the maternal tissue and also detected in 
the fetus which shows that PAHs can cross the placenta [2].

3.1.1.3 Metabolism  
The PAHs start to metabolise after they are distributed throughout the body. There are different processes 
and pathways which the PAHs can undergo. There are currently three know major pathways which are 
proposed for the metabolic activation of PAHs: the pathway via bay region dihydrodiol epoxide by 
cytochrome P450 enzymes, the pathway via radical cation by one-electron oxidation and the ortho-
quinone pathway by dihydrodiol dehydrogenase [41].    
 
The bay region dihydrodiol epoxide pathway has three enzyme-mediated reactions. The first step is an 
oxidation of a double bond yielding an unstable arene oxides (epoxide) catalysed by P450 enzymes. The 
second step is a hydrolysis of the arene oxides by microsomal epoxide hydrolase which yields a trans 
dihydrodiols. The third and last step is another oxidation of a double bond catalysed by P450. The second 
double bond is oxidised next to the first one which leads to a vicinal diol epoxide, see figure 4(1). These 
bay region or fjord region diol epoxides are electrophiles and thereby able to bind to DNA and some of 
the diol epoxide stereoisomers of PAHs have been shown to be carcinogens [14], [41]. Many PAHs have 
been shown to be metabolised through this pathway. Among others B[a]P, CHR, MCP, B[a]A and 
DB[al]P can be metabolised through bay region dihydrodiol epoxide pathway [41].The binding to the 
DNA occurs between the benzylic carbon of the epoxide and the exocyclic amino groups of 
deoxyguanosine (dGuo) and deoxyadenosine (dAdo) residues in the DNA. 
 
The second pathway is the one-electron oxidation pathway where PAHs form radical cations by removal 
of one electron from the π electron system. This is done by an oxidation process catalysed by P450 
peroxidase, where one electron is transferred to an oxidant, see figure 4(2). The radical cation binds to the 
amino groups of dGuo and dAdo like in the bay region dihydrodiol epoxide pathway and is catalysed by 
P450 [42].  
 
The last pathway of the three main pathways is the ortho-quinone pathway. The diols formed from the 
PAHs are further oxidised by dihydrodiol dehydrogenase forming a ketol which are tautomerises to 
catechol. The catechol is first oxidised to an ortho-semiquinone anion radical. The ortho-semiquinone 
anion radical is further oxidised to ortho-quinone, see figure 4(3). These reactions generate reactive 
oxygen spieces (ROS) which can be very toxic [41], [43], [44]. 
The ortho-quinones are reactive 1,4-Michael addition acceptors (addition of a nucleophile to an 1,4-
unsaturated carbonyl compound). This gives the potential to form various DNA adducts. Both stable and 
unstable, depurinating DNA adducts can be formed and bind to dGuo and dAdo like the other two 
pathways [14], [43].  
 
To sum up, all of the three major pathways lead to DNA adducts that can react with dGou and dAdo in 
the DNA chain. The PAH-DNA adducts can be stable in the DNA meaning that they remain intact in the 
DNA during normal conditions of purification. However the PAH-DNA adduct can also depurinate where 
the PAH-DNA adduct are released from the DNA by cleavage of the glycosidic bond yielding a apurinic 
site and leads to alteration of the DNA sequence and cause harm [42].

3.1.1.4 Excretion  
PAHs can be excreted as intact PAHs or metabolites. The metabolites are excreted into bile and further 
eliminated in the faeces and a smaller portions in the urine. The intact PAHs are excreted unmetabolised 
in the faeces [45]. A study in 1997 by van Schooten et al. [46] concluded that the excretion of unchanged 
PAHs are higher for pyrene and B[a]P when rats are exposed to contaminated soil compared to if they are 
exposed to pure PAHs. This suggest that a soil matrix can lower the absorption [46]. 
As mentioned in section 3.1.1.1 an increase of 1-hydroxypyrene in the urine of humans have been detected 
when an increase in dietary B[a]P occurred [37]. Also 3-hydroxy - and 9-hydroxybenzo[a]pyrene, two 
major metabolites of B[a]P, can be detected in urine while 4,5-diol and 7,8-diol can be detected in faeces 
up to 8 hours post exposure [45].  
 
3.1.2 Toxicodynamics 
Toxicodynamics covers the adverse effects of PAHs. It has been established what happens to the PAHs 
when they metabolise to DNA-adducts and binds to the DNA, however, many of the effects like 
immunosuppression, teratogenicity and tumour promotion are believed to be mediated by activation of 
arylhydrocarbon receptors (AhR) [16].  The AhR receptor which is located in the cytoplasm will enter the 
nucleus after binding a ligand (e.g. PAH). In the nucleus the ligand-AhR complex will bind to specific 
DNA sequences which normally are regulated by AhR. These sequences are located in the promotor and 
enhancer region, so binding will lead to an up-regulation of the transcription and thereby increase mRNA 
and genes. Many of these genes are involved in metabolic and physiological events, like proteins involved 
in signal transduction and growth factors [16]. Overall when PAHs activate AhR by binding, it leads to 
endocrine disruption, which can result in a number of toxicities in organs. 
 
Toxicity can occur after one single exposure to PAHs or after short term exposure. A few studies have 
investigated the acute and short term toxicity of some PAHs. Among others, Haddow et al. in 1937 
investigated the acute toxicity of B[a]P, CHR, DB[ah]A, fluranthene and pyrene in rats [47]. No reduction 
in growth was detected in the rats after exposure to 30 mg of CHR, DB[ah]A or 10 mg of pyrene. However 
10 mg of B[a]P caused reduction in the growth rate. Furthermore one or two intraperitoneal injections of 
3-90 mg of DB[a,h]A did lead to a reduction in growth that persisted for at least 15 weeks [47].  
Short term effects are liver toxicity, nephropathy, haematology, and cataract which are caused by 
naphtalene, flourene, flouranthene, B[a]P and acenaphthene [16]. 
 
Long term effects are almost always associated with carcinogenic effects. There are some non-
carcinogenic effects of PAHs like growth inhibition, liver damage and irritation, but these effects occur 
often at concentrations which also cause carcinogenic effects [2].   
From animal studies, it is known that PAHs cause cancer and the carcinogenesis of PAHs is well-studied 
in animals. Most studies regarding carcinogenesis of PAHs have been carried out dermal, subcutaneous 
or by inhalation, only few have dealt with oral administration. B[a]P has shown to give tumours in the 
GIT, liver, lungs and mammary glands of mice and rats after oral administration. DB[ah]A and B[a]A 
have produced tumours in the GIT, lungs and liver in mice but not rats. Skin carcinogenicity has been 
seen after dermal application of PAHs. Carcinogenicity followed by inhalation of B[a]P was tested in 
golden hamsters. Cancer was developed in the respiratory tract in 35 % of the Golden hamsters after long-
term inhalation of 10 mg B[a]P/m3 [16]. 
==PAGE:23==
 
16 
 
 
Not a lot of carcinogenic experiments with PAHs have been conducted on humans due to ethical reasons. 
However case-control studies on human workers exposed to PAHs through their occupation and also 
through dietary exposure have been described [1], [48], [49]. Cancer associated with occupational 
exposure was seen in many sites of the human body; respiratory system, larynx, bladder, stomach, skin 
and kidney. The most dominant and frequent form of cancer from occupational exposure to complex 
mixtures of PAHs were found in the lungs [1], [48], [49]. 
 
In most studies tumour development is depending on the route of administration. Oral administration 
induces gastric tumours, dermal application induces skin tumours and inhalation results in lung tumours 
[2].  
 
Besides cancer PAHs can lead to a lower birth weight among humans (developmental toxicity) [50]. A 
study conducted by Duarte-Salles et al. in 2010 suggested that  pregnant women, both smokers and non-
smokers, with elevated dietary B[a]P during the first trimester is associated with significant lower birth 
weight of their babies [50]. In 2014, 10 human skeletal muscle biopsies were obtained to test how a higher 
level of B[a]P and its metabolite benzo[a]pyrene-7,8-dihydrodiol-9,10-epoxide (BPDE) can lead to a 
lower birth weight. The result showed that both B[a]P and BPDE have the ability to inhibit the myogenic 
differentiation in the human skeletal muscle-derived progenitor cells. This suggest a mechanism that could 
cause the lower birth weight seen in mothers with elevated B[a]P intakes [51].  
Studies in mice and rats have shown clear developmental toxicity in form of embryonic and fetal death, 
reduced fetal weight and malformations. The occurrence and extent of the developmental toxicity is 
dependent partially on maternal and fetal genotypes [16]. 
 
Most of the toxic PAHs have showed to be mutagenic/genotoxic in somatic cells in vivo but in general 
the evidence overlaps with carcinogenicity regarding the mechanism between DNA adducts formation, 
mutations and cancer outcome followed by exposure of PAHs [16], [52]. When the metabolised PAHs 
binds to DNA it can alter the DNA sequence and thereby cause genotoxicity.    
 
There is also a link between PAHs and immunotoxicity. The most common effects followed by exposure 
to PAHs is immunosuppression, which is associated with an increased susceptibility to the development 
of cancer or infectious diseases. Immunopotentiation has also been seen after exposure to PAHs, which 
results in an increased secretion of cytokines by immune cells. This can lead to inflammation which under 
specific circumstances can facilitate tumour development or expression of hypersensitivity or auto 
immunity [53]. 
 
3.2 Hazard Characterisation 
Hazard characterisation consists of the critical effect of PAHs and a health-base intake threshold. The 
critical effect is the first observed adverse effect which occurs with the intake of PAHs while health-base 
intake threshold is the amount of PAHs it is possible to be exposed to without any adverse effects 
occurring. 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
17 
 
 
3.2.1 Critical Effect and Health-base Intake Threshold 
As stated in section 3.1.2, PAHs can cause many different types of adverse effects, however, almost all 
of the effects can lead to carcinogenicity. Normally the critical effect is the first adverse effect, but due to 
all the effects leading to carcinogenicity, carcinogenicity is chosen as the critical effect. This is supported 
by the fact that the health-base intake threshold, which is stated by EFSA, uses carcinogenicity as the 
indicator for how much PAH the body can tolerate without any affects. The CONTAM panel and JECFA 
used a 2-year carcinogenicity study on coal tar mixtures in B6C3F1 female mice to set a limit for PAHs 
[14]. It was decided to use the benchmark dose lower confidence limit for a 10% increase in the number 
of tumour bearing animals compared to the control animals (BMDL10) as a health-base intake threshold. 
BMDL10 was calculated using different mathematical models, where a criterion of the p-value was set up. 
The p-value had to be significant larger than 0.1 before the model was accepted [14].  
The BMDL10 for PAH4 for those model which were accepted ranges from 0.34-0.93 mg/kg b.w./day, 
where it is estimated that a body weighs 60 kg. To be most caution, the CONTAM panel chose the lower 
limit for the BMDL10 as the marker for the carcinogenesis. This gives a BMDL10 for PAH4 of 0.34 mg/kg 
b.w./day [14].  
 
3.3 Exposure Assessment  
As described in section 2.2 there are different ways of being exposed to PAHs. For non-smokers the major 
contributing factor to exposure of PAHs, is the consumption of food, but for smokers the contribution 
from smoking can also be significant [14]. 
 
EFSA has collected data from 16 countries in Europe (Belgium, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Denmark, 
Finland, France, Germany, Hungary, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Slovakia, Sweden, 
United Kingdom) to evaluate the intake of PAHs through food. They collected data from different types 
of food including: cereals and cereal products, sugar and sugar products such as chocolate, fats (vegetable 
and animal), vegetables, fruits, alcoholic beverages, meat and meat products, seafood and seafood 
products, fish and fish food products and finally cheese. Denmark is one of the countries where the most 
coffee and tea is consumed, therefore in the following, Denmark will be compared to Europe (the average 
of all the 16 countries) in the exposure assessment instead of simply looking at Europe as whole. The 
amount of PAH4 in food will be compared to what is gotten from coffee and tea in figure 5. The exposure 
is calculated by the author, however, all the data to the calculations are from EFSAs report [14]. The 
calculations are included in appendix III.

4.3 Fat determination  
It was necessary to determine the fat content of the coffee beans before extraction to be sure no fat was 
extracted. Fat content was determined in duplicates. In a mortar, 5.0 g of ground coffee was mixed together 
with 5.0 g of Ottawa sand, 2.0 g of polyacrylic acid and added to a 66 mL ASE cell and extracted using 
an ASE 300 System (Dionex, Sunnyvale, CA). The instrument settings for the extraction was 100 ᵒC, 2 
static cycles of 5 min., 70 % flush volume, 100 s of purge with nitrogen, and an extraction solvent of 
hexane:acetone (50:50). Extracts were evaporated to around 5 mL using vacuum evaporator (Büchi 
Rotavapor, V-800, Switzerland) at 40 ᵒC. The 5 mL extracts were transferred to fat determination glasses 
with pentane and the total fat were detected using gravimetric fat determination [55].  
 
4.4 Extraction of PAH4 from coffee beans and tealeaves 
For the coffee beans and tealeaves the in-house method was applied with some alterations [55].  
In a mortar, 3.0 g of ground coffee or instant coffee or ~2.0g of tea (one tea bag per trail were used) was 
mixed together with 5.0 g of Ottawa sand (to destroy the structure) and 2.0 g of polyacrylic acid (to remove 
water). 
In a 100 ml ASE cell a filter was placed in the bottom. A spoonful of polyacrylic acid was added in the 
ASE cell to remove water if deemed necessary. Before another spoon of polyacrylic acid was placed, 
20.0g of cleaned silica gel was added into the ASE cell. Next, the mix of the homogenised coffee 
beans/tealeaves, Ottawa sand and polyacrylic acid was added. The internal standard was added to the cell 
with a concentration of 50 ng/ml. For recovery, a concentration of 100 ng PAHs/ml was added as a spike. 
The rest of the ASE cell was filled with Ottawa sand and the lid was screwed on. A sketch of the ASE 
cell with the different layers can be seen in figure 8.   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
After filling the ASE cell, the sample was extracted by Dionex ASE 300 using the same settings as 
described in section 4.3.

The extract was transferred to a round-bottom flash where it was evaporated to almost dryness on a rotary-
evaporator with a 40 ̊ C water bath. To make sure all the PAHs were transferred, the glass material was 
cleaned with pentane two times and transferred to the round-bottom flash. After the evaporation the extract 
was transferred with pentane to a brown round conical flash for light protection and dried by blowing 
Nitrogen (N2, AGA Nitrogen plus ( 99,996 %), (g)).  A clean-up step and another extraction step was 
performed on Gilson ASPEC XL (Gilson, France) with SPE. The dried extract was dissolved in 1 ml of 
cyclohexane and placed in the Gilson ASPEC XL. The SPE column with SI sorbent was conditioned with 
cyclohexane followed by the sample. The PAHs was eluted with cyclohexane and then dried with N2.  
For samples with residues of fat (the fat do not disappear by blowing with N2), 1 ml of acetonitrile was 
added, mixed and the sample was frozen for 20 min at -23 ᵒC. This resulted in the fat freezing and the 
acetonitrile was transferred to a clean glass, dried with N2 and dissolved in 300 µL acetonitrile. It was 
then transferred to GC-vials and thereby ready for GC-QTOF-MS. If no fat was present, the dried sample 
was dissolved in 300 µL acetonitrile and transferred to GC-vials excluding the freezing step.   
    
4.5 Extraction of coffee brew and tea infusion 
The method for extraction of the coffee brew and tea infusion is based on García-Falocón et al. who 
developed a method to detect PAHs in drinking water [56].  
The instant coffee was made by weighing 3 teaspoons (3.6 g) of coffee into 200 ml of water. The coffee 
beans were used to make piston coffee to ensure uniformity. It was made by adding 22,0 g of coffee to 
400 ml of water in the cafetiére. The coffee set for 5 min. before the coffee brew were poured into a cup. 
The tea infusion was made by pouring 200 ml of boiled water into a cup, then putting the tea bag into the 
water for 3 min.  To see whether time had an impact on how many PAHs defused from the tealeaves into 
the water, a test with Earl Grey was made. A tea infusion was made where the tea bag had been in the 
water for either 2, 4, 6, 10 or 15 min.   
The used tea bags and coffee beans was air dried, frozen and kept for analysis. These were analysed by 
the same extraction method for coffee beans and tealeaves (see section 4.4).   
The coffee and tea was filtrated before use. In a glass, 150 ml of filtrated coffee or tea was added together 
with 45 ml of acetonitrile, internal standard and PAHs to the spiked samples, resulting in concentration 
of d-PAHs and PAHs of 50 ng/ml and 100 ng/ml, respectively. The samples were passed through a 500 
mg C18-column, which was first conditioned with 5 ml of acetonitrile followed by 10 ml MilliQ water 
without letting the column dry. The glass material which was used to contain the coffee and acetonitrile 
was washed with 20 ml of acetonitrile/water (30/100) and passed through the column as well. Next the 
column was dried by blowing with N2 for 20 min. The absorbed PAHs were eluted with 7 ml of hexane 
and dried again by blowing with N2. The residue was dissolved in 0.5 ml acetonitrile and transferred to 
GC-vials.

5 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  
In total 4 types of tea and 4 types of coffee were tested for the content of PAHs by preparing the samples 
and then extracting the PAHs before detecting the PAHs by GC-QTOF-MS.  In total 95 samples were run 
on the GC-QTOF-MS in two different sequences which ran consecutively. The samples included 2 
calibration series, 9 samples of the calibration standard with 100 ng/ml, 16 samples used for LOD,  4 
blank samples, 4 spiked samples and the remaining were all either coffee or tea extractions. From the 
coffee and tea extractions, 4 were chosen to be tested on SERS. The raw data from GC-QTOF-MS can be 
found in Appendix V. 
 
The results and discussion will be divided into several sections. First, the development of the methods and 
the validation of the methods is discussed. Second, the results of the coffee and tea samples is discussed 
and compared to results from other studies. Third, the risk assessment will be re-evaluated. Finally the 
results from SERS will be investigated and it will be discussed whether it is a viable method for detection 
of PAHs.  
 
5.1 Quality assurance  
To ensure that the chosen methods were optimal, preliminary trials were conducted to investigate and 
modify the methods to achieve the best results. Different aspects were applied to analyse the accuracy of 
the methods and validate them. Criteria for method validation have been set by the Food Chemistry 
department at DTU Food [58] and some of these will be used to validate the methods used in this study.  
 
5.1.1 Preliminary trails 
Both methods in this study were analysed before use by conducting preliminary trials. The trials and the 
alterations are described below. 
 
5.1.1.1 Development of method for coffee beans and tealeaves 
To find the best way to analyse the content of PAHs in coffee beans and tealeaves, the in-house method 
was investigated [55]. To adjust the method, 3 x 2 samples were prepared for extraction in the Dionex 
ASE 300 to test the effect of silica and the extraction solvents. The samples were 2 blanks and 4 samples 
with ground coffee. Internal standard was added to all the samples and PAHs with a concentration of 100 
ng/ml was added to 2 of the ground coffee samples. To 3 of the samples, 15 g of silica was added together 
with 15 g of coffee in the ASE cell as described in section 4.4, while the other 3 was without silica. The 
samples were eluted with dichloromethane:hexane (15:85) as the in-house method recommends [55]. 
However the dichloromethane was impossible to evaporate on the rotated-evaporator or by blowing with 
N2, so hexane:acetone (50:50) was used instead. 
By visual inspection it was clear which 2 samples silica gel was used. The extract from the 2 coffee 
samples without silica were very dark and unclear compared to the coffee samples with silica gel, see 
figure 9. It was therefore decided to use silica gel in all the samples.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:35==
 
28 
 
 
Another problem was encountered after analysing the 15 g of ground coffee with 15 g of silica gel. There 
was too much fat it the extraction, so the samples were not able to run on the GS-QTOF-MS since it would 
risk clogging the column in the GC. It was concluded that the amount of fat in the coffee beans was too 
high so the silica gel could not retain all the fat. An earlier study by Lund et al. in 2008 calculated a fat-
to-fat retainer ratio (FFR) which tells how many grams of fat per gram of silica the silica can retain (g 
fat/g silica) [59]. In the study it was concluded that a FFR ratio of 0.025 gave a fat free extraction.  
The fat content was investigated in the two coffee beans samples and it was found to be around 9 % for 
the Ethiopian Sidamo coffee beans and around 10 % for the Organic Ethiopian Espresso coffee beans, 
appendix VI. Based on this, as a precaution it was decided to use 3 g of coffee beans which gives an FFR 
of 0.014 and should therefore give a fat free extraction.   
 
5.1.1.2 Development of the method to coffee brew and tea infusion 
The method from García-Falcon et al. from 2005 was chosen for analysis of the coffee brew and the tea 
infusion due to the simplicity of the method [56]. To adjust the method, 2 samples consisting of 250 ml 
of coffee and 75 ml of acetonitrile and 1 sample with 250 ml of MilliQ water and 75 ml acetonitrile were 
prepared. To all 3 samples internal standard was added with a concentration of 50 ng/ml. To one of the 
coffee samples extra PAHs were added until the sample had a concentration of 100 ng/ml which were 
used to test the accuracy of the method by looking at the recoveries of the PAH4. The 325 ml of sample 
was passed through a 500 mg C18 column, as described in section 4.5 with the exception that the absorbed 
PAHs were eluted with 5 ml of hexane as García-Falcon et al. had used [56].   
After running the samples on the GC-QTOF-MS it was discovered that a lot of different components also 
eluted which made it difficult to identify the PAHs. The amount of coffee and acetonitrile was therefore 
lowered to 150 ml and 45 ml, respectively so the ratio between the coffee and acetonitrile remained the 
same. García-Falocón et al. used a 360 mg C18-column instead of a 500 mg C18-column as in this study, 
so to make sure that all the PAHs was eluted the amount of hexane was increased to insure that the ratio 
between the column and hexane was the same [56]. The results of these samples were cleaner and the 
peaks from the PAHs were also higher. The recovery of the PAH4 was calculated giving a recovery 
between 89-110% which indicated that the method was good enough according to the standards set by the 
European Commission (EC) [60]. 
It was also discovered that the coffee brew contained too large particles, so the C18-colunm clogged. All 
the samples in subsequent tests were therefore filtered before passing it through the C18-column.

5.1.1.3 Testing of SERS 
Two things were investigated before deciding on how to obtain the best results with SERS. The first

In general, there would be another step in the in-house method, a clean-up step where the fat and 
undesirable compounds are removed. This step is very time consuming and it was therefore decided to 
remove the step by applying the FFR (fat-to-fat retainer ratio). Therefore, a lot of compounds present in 
the sample can interfere with the GC-QTOF-MS and maybe retain some of the PAHs. The chromatograms 
from the coffee beans and tealeaves samples also look quite different from the coffee brew and tea infusion 
chromatograms. An example of the chromatogram from a coffee bean extraction, coffee brew extraction 
as well as a tealeaves extraction and tea infusion extraction are shown in appendix XI. The coffee beans 
and tealeaves contain a lot more compounds, which can be seen in the chromatogram as a high bump in 
the first 28-30 min of the run. This can influence the eluting of PAH4 which elutes from 21 min (B[a]A) 
to 30.6 min (B[a]P), see table 8. This means that most of the 4 PAHs elutes during the bump and could 
have consequences for the results. It is possible that some of the PAHs does not elute or even that the 
eluting of the other compounds interferes with the responses from the PAHs and the RT. Another 
explanation of the low recoveries is that the method involves many steps where the extraction is moved 
from one glass to another. Every time the extraction is moved, there is risk of losing PAHs.   
 
Removing the clean-up step was probably not the best way to lower the time of the experiments and some 
investigation has to be made to find a way to lower the time and still get rid of the undesirable compounds 
and increase the recoveries. Overall, it seem like the methods for coffee brew and tea infusion are precise 
enough, while the method for coffee beans and tealeaves is too inaccurate.  
 
5.1.5 LOD and LOQ 
The LOD and LOQ was determined by extracting two samples (150 ml coffee and 45 ml acetonitrile) 
using the same method as for extracting coffee brew and tea infusion, but without adding the PAHs. After 
eluting with hexane, the extract was divided into 16 samples where PAHs were added to 8 of them, in a 
concentration of 2 ng/ml. The other 8 were spiked with PAHs to a concentration of 10 ng/ml. All the 
samples were run on GC-QTOF-MS with the same program as described in section 4.6. 
By applying a small concentration in a coffee matrix, it is possible to get an idea of how low a 
concentration that can be detected and the influence of the coffee matrix. From the samples, it was seen 
that 2 ng/ml was detectable (see figure 13) and this concentration was therefore chosen for calculating the 
LOD and LOQ.

According to the EC the LOD and LOQ must be ≤ 0.3 ng/g and ≤ 0.9 ng/g, respectively [60].  All the 
LOD and LOQ fall within that limit, concluding that the GC-QTOF-MS is precise enough and that the 
coffee matrix does not interfere to such a degree that the LOD and LOQ becomes too high.  
Normally LOQ is used to be sure that the amount present in a sample can be quantified. The LOQ and 
LOD are, however, normally calculated from a blank sample with or without an added concentration. In 
this case the LOD and LOQ are calculated from a matrix, so it is assumed that LOD can be used as LOQ 
in this study.

ruined because the internal standard was missing or to low (Green tea). One plausible explanation for why 
the internal standard was missing could be that the 2H in the d-PAHs (internal standard) are exchanged 
with H from others compounds. In that way the d-PAHs would transform to PAH and thereby lower the 
response of the internal standard. This can happen in acidic and catalytic matrices [61]. In this study there 
was no acidic or catalytic matrices, but it could indicate that the d-PAHs are not stable.

Looking at the results obtained from the coffee samples, the 2 types of coffee bean samples were selected 
for testing the impact of the roasting time while the 2 instant coffees were selected to test the impact of

EC accepts the results if the HARROTr is less than 2.0 [60]. Several of the HARROTr values are missing 
for the coffees due to the complications described above. Two HARROTr values are higher than the 
permitted  which are the Organic Instant decaffeinated Coffee with a HARROTr value of  9.75 for B[a]A 
and the Organic Instant Coffee with a HARROTr value of 2.59 for CHR. The reason for the high 
HARROTr value for the Organic Instant decaffeinated Coffee is that one of the samples was negative for 
B[a]A while the other sample had a high content of B[a]A. This either means that the PAHs are not 
equally distributed in the coffee or the method was imprecise. In either case, more experiments have to 
be performed before any conclusion can be made.  
Even though some of the result are questionable, the obtained results show that the Organic Ethiopian 
Espresso coffee has a higher content of PAH4 than the Ethiopian Sidamo coffee. Since the coffee beans 
are the same type (Arabica) and both comes from Ethiopian it is possible to conclude that the roasting 
have an impact on the PAH4 content, as it was also concluded in Houessou et al. [6]. The content of PAH4 
in the two coffee beans sample is higher than the results obtained from Duedahl-Olesen et al. in 2015 
[13]. This is probably because the coffee beans comes from different countries and are different types, 
thus contamination can differ.  
Duedahl-Olesen et al. results from the instant coffee had a large span (2.2-5.1 ng/g) which also is seen in 
this study (5.62-13.42 ng/g). Some have a higher content of PAH4 than the dark roasted coffee and some 
have a lower content [13]. This implies that more studies would be preferable to get a better impression 
on the content of PAH4. However the large span in this study can also be because one of the instant coffee 
is decaffeinated while the other is with caffeine. The Organic Instant decaffeinated Coffee is the one with 
the highest content of PAH4. The process where the caffeine is removed could have an impact on the 
content of the PAHs. This might be due to the caffeine is removed by CO2 and water [62]. The extra PAHs 
can come from contaminated water and the drying, if it takes longer to dry the beans after removing the 
caffeine.    
In the 1980’s, a method including a caffeine-formic acid complexation was often used to extract PAHs  
[63], [64]. The hypothesis was that caffeine binds to the PAHs, so by using that method the PAHs content 
would be expected to be lower, so when removing the caffeine, the caffeine should remove some PAHs. 
The results in this study, however, shows the opposite that the Organic Instant decaffeinated Coffee have 
a higher content of PAH4 than Organic Instant Coffee. So according to the result the hypothesis is not 
true. The results obtained from the other studies also showed that the recovery of PAHs after the caffeine-
formic acid complexation was high (66-95%) [63] and (94-140% except for anthracene) [64], which 
implies that the caffeine most likely do not bind to PAHs. If any PAHs are removed together with the 
caffeine it is a smaller amount than the PAHs there are added from the contamination and drying.   
 
The tea brand Pickwick was chosen because it is a well know tea brand in Denmark and they have a lot 
of different types of tea [65]. The Spicy Chai and the Forrest Fruit tea were chosen to see if spices or fruit 
might have an impact of the PAH4 content. The Earl Grey and the Green tea was chosen to see if different 
tea processing methods would have an impact of the PAH content.  
All the HARROTr values for the tealeaves are within the limits set by EC [60]. The values varies between 
0.28-1.41. A way to lower the HARROTr values could be by mixing the content of several teabags, making 
the content more similar instead of assuming that the content is the same in every teabag. The coffee, 
==PAGE:46==
 
39 
 
 
which is in the same bag, however, also show a variation in their result, so it is more plausible that it is 
the method that could be optimised the most.  
The content of PAH4 in the tealeaves ranges from 30.47 ng/g (Earl Grey) to 44.41 ng/g (Forrest Fruit).  
By conducting ANOVA, it was seen that there was no significant difference between the content of PAH4 
in the tealeaves (p< 0.05), so it is impossible to say whether the spices in the Spicy Chai have an impact 
on the PAH4 content. Actually the Spicy Chai has one of the lowest contents of PAH4. One explanation 
for this, could be that it contain a lot of different spices (see table 5), so the content of tealeaves is lower 
than for the other types. This indicates that the spices could actually have a lower content of PAH4 than 
tealeaves. The Forrest Fruit tea, however, has the highest content of PAH4 compared to the other tealeaves 
which gives reason to believe that the fruit have a higher content of PAHs than tealeaves. This is mainly 
speculation though, because the difference was not significant using an ANOVA analysis, more 
experiments would have to be conducted before any conclusion can be establish.   
The Green tea have a slighter higher content of PAH4 than Earl Grey. Since there are no significant 
difference between them either, it is not possible to say whether the production has an impact on the 
formation of these two types. Pickwick define Earl Grey as a black tea [65] and in other literature there 
are conflicting results from green tea and black tea. One study has found that green tea contains more 
PAHs than black tea [8] while others have found a higher content in black tea [11], [12]. This could mean 
that the difference in the production of black and green tea does not contribute greatly to the content of 
PAHs. It has, however, been concluded that the production does contributes a lot to the content of PAHs 
in Lin and Zhu from 2004 [26]. They concluded that the drying of the tealeaves in the drying houses, 
resulted in the high content of PAHs [26]. Lin and Zhu only look at the production of black tea and not 
green tea. The production of both green tea and black tea include a drying step, so while it is not known 
precisely which production type contributes most to the content, it is reasonable to assume that it does so 
in both the production of both green and black tea. The similar results obtained in this study, are therefore 
more likely to be because the two production types contribute an equal amount. 
 
It is difficult to compare the results obtained in this study with results obtained from earlier studies because 
the PAH4 content varies a lot from brand to brand, as can be seen by looking at the earlier studies included 
in appendix II. All earlier studies have found lower PAH4 content for some types of tea but they have also 
found teas with a higher content of PAH4. The teas most likely come from different countries where the 
contamination from the environment and water differs which can lead to the different results.  
 
In general the HARROTr values varied a lot for both the coffee beans and tealeaves. Some of them were 
found to be too high and some had a high HARROTr value but were still acceptable. The high HARROTr 
value could be caused by the preparation step where the samples were homogenised/mixed in a mortar 
with Ottawa sand. This step was done manually and even though the Ottawa sand helped to destroy the 
structure of the coffee beans and tealeaves, it was very hard to ensure the same level of homogenisation 
every time. An optimisation of this step would therefore be preferable.

The content of PAH4 for the tea infusions is very similar so there are no large difference to drinking one 
type of tea instead of another, even though the content of PAH4 in the tealeaves themselves varied. 
Comparing the content to other studies, it is seen that most of them have found a higher content in their 
infusion. Schulz et al. from 2014 found up to 30 ng/g in the infusions except for mate tea where the content 
of PAH4 were of up to 1400 ng/g and peppermint tea which had a content of PAH4 of up to 100 ng/g [12] 
(Appendix II). However Schulz et al. also concluded that tea infusion did not contain any PAHs if the tea 
was prepared as in this study, so they homogenised the tealeaves first, which then resulted in the high 
content in their infusion. This could mean that more PAHs are released into the water when homogenising 
the tealeaves first and confirms that it is important the samples are homogenised properly and identically. 
PAHs are highly lipophilic so only a small amount enters the water except if they are forced to, by for 
example homogenising.  
The PAH4 content of the coffee brew from the 2 types of coffee beans also have approximately the same 
content of PAH4 as the tea infusion even though the coffee beans were in the water for 5 min whereas the 
tea bags only were in for only 3 min. So there are also no difference between drinking tea or coffee 
according to these results. The 2 instant coffees does only have a slightly higher content than the other 
samples (see table 15) even though all the instat coffee is dissolved in the water. However, it seems like 
it does not matter which types of coffee of tea there are ingested.  
 
In general the results obtained from the coffee brew and tea infusion are lower than the results obtained 
from other studies [6], [10], [12], [13]. A reason could be, that the tea and coffee used are different types 
than the ones chosen in this study. Another reason could be that the ratio of tealeaves/coffee beans to 
water is lower, which could result in a higher PAH4 content. As an example, Houessou et al. in 2007 
tested the coffee brew and they found a slightly higher PAH4 content in their coffee brew (see appendix 
II). They used 50 g of ground coffee to 300 ml Milli-Q water, so ratio between their coffee beans and 
water was lower and thereby increase the transfer. 
In this study, the tea infusion and coffee brew have been prepared according to EFSA guidelines and the 
instructions on the container. EFSA defines a cup to be 125 ml and that 7 g of ground coffee beans is used 
for 125 ml.  According to the instructions on the tea container, one tea bag is used per cup and on the 
instant coffee container it says that 1-2 teaspoon is used per cup of water. These instructions have been 
applied in this study to insure as realistic as possible exposure from coffee and tea was found.   
 
There were no difference in the content of PAH4 detected, between the tea infusion and the two coffee 
brews even though the coffee beans had been in the water for a longer period. To test whether time has

5.5 Risk assessment   
After the results have been considered and discussed, it is interesting to look at the risk assessment and 
reevaluating it in the light of the obtained results.  
A worth mentioning about the risk assessment in section 3, is that all the data is taken from EFSA, and 
the results obtained from EFSA differ from those in this study. It was impossible to find information on 
how EFSA obtained their results. The results in this study are based on the average, while EFSA 
sometimes use the average and sometimes the median. EFSA calculated a MOE for PAH4 on the median 
of the EU mean estimates of the dietary exposure to be 17500 and the EU 97.5th percentile to 9900 which 
indicates low concern for the consumer health [14]. The MOE was calculated to be lower in this study 
(8829 for the average and 2750 for the 97.5th percentile). The difference lies in the calculations and it is 
difficult to conclude which results are the most precise.  
The risk assessment showed that coffee brew and tea infusion were not a health concern for the population 
when a transfer coefficient of 26% was used. The transfer coefficient has been estimated from earlier 
studies, for example Houessou et al. where the transfer coefficient was found to vary from 0.6 to 30.9% 
for B[a]A, CHR and B[b]Fa. B[a]P was not found in any of the brews [6]. Due to the large difference in 
the transfer coefficients and the fact no B[a]P was found, it is easy to question the validity. Houessou et 
al. uses just under 21 g of coffee per 125 ml of water where EFSA use 7 g to 125 ml water. It is likely 
that the transfer of PAHs will be higher when more coffee is used. EFSA also use other studies where the 
transfer coefficient have been found to be around 1% [68].  Another study mentioned by EFSA have found 
up to 26% with a mean value of 5% [14]. Only the study of Houessou et al. from 2007 have found a 
transfer coefficient over 26%. This means that the transfer coefficient is most likely lower than the ones 
used in EFSA.

Even with a transfer of 26% it was shown that the risk of getting cancer from drinking coffee and tea is 
very low. In general there were no big risk of getting cancer of PAHs from dietary exposure. With the 
new results from this study the estimated risk will be even lower.  
One thing that can be questioned about the transfer coefficient found in this study is that the method for 
the coffee beans and tealeaves are most likely not precise enough. This could mean two things; the results 
are either too high or too low. If the PAH4 content is estimated too high, the transfer coefficient will be 
higher. However the estimated content of PAH4 would need to be a lot higher than it is, before getting 
the same transfer coefficient as the one EFSA use. EFSA have estimated the lower and upper bound for 
PAHs in coffee powder and the upper bound for B[a]P is 2.46 ng/g for B[a]A it is 3.22 ng/g, for CHR it 
is 3.87 ng/g and for B[b]Fa it is 2.69 ng/g [14]. None of the content of the 4 PAHs in the 2 types of coffee 
beans exceed these limits, meaning that the content is most likely not estimated too high and thereby the 
transfer coefficient will likely not be bigger.   
In the end, there are no health concern when drinking coffee and tea regarding PAH4.

6 CONCLUSION 
The risk assessment showed that coffee and tea alone did not provide a health threat in regards to 
carcinogenicity. The MOE was calculated for the total dietary exposure of PAH4 including coffee and tea 
and was found to be approximately 8800 for the average dietary exposure and approximately 2700 for the 
97.5th percentile. So even though the dietary exposure does not exceed the BMDL10 (340000 ng/kg 
b.w/day), there is a risk of getting cancer from the total dietary exposure based on these calculations.  
 
The PAH4 content from the 4 tealeaves samples and 2 coffee beans samples varied between 30.47-
44.41ng/g for the tealeaves and between 5.5-8.17 ng/g for the coffee beans. The coffee brew and tea 
infusion had a content of 0.010-0.013 ng/g yielding a transfer coefficient of 4.85-6.33% for the teas and 
3.37-4.78% for the coffees. This transfer coefficient is smaller than the 26% used by EFSA. This could 
mean that the PAH4 amount obtained by drinking coffee and tea is a lot smaller than first estimated and 
thereby lowers the dietary exposure. Coffee and tea are, however, not main contributors in the total dietary 
exposure to PAH4 so even when applying the new transfer coefficient found in this study, it will not 
change the dietary exposure significantly. 
 
Analysis shows that there is no difference in the PAH4 content in the types of leaves, which leads to 
believe that the spices, fruit or the production of the different types of tea have no impact on the PAH4 
content. Furthermore, many of the duplicates where not alike, especially for the coffee beans results, so it 
is hard to conclude if the results are correct. Likewise, the HARROTr values was found to be too high 
(>2.0) for CHR in the coffee brews and tea infusions, which is due to TRP and CHR not being separated 
enough on the GC chromatograms which is a result of TRP and CHR being eluted too close to each other. 
 
When the methods used in the study were validated from different aspects, it was found that the GC-
QTOF-MS can be used to detect PAHs in a given sample. The extraction method for the coffee beans and 
tealeaves, however, are questionable. Some of the recoveries was outside the limits set by EC and 
furthermore the results from the 2 instant coffees samples varied a lot from method to method (5.62-13.42 
ng/g for the coffee beans and tealeaves method and 0.33-0.37 ng/g from the coffee brew and tea infusion 
method). This suggest that one of the methods is not precise enough. Due to a clean-up step in the coffee 
beans and tealeaves method being removed and thereby giving a chromatogram with many different 
compounds that possibly influenced the eluting of PAHs, it is suggested that it is this method, that is not 
precise enough and some optimisation will have to be performed for this method. 
 
From the mass balance, it was seen that it did not break even. The concentration of PAH4 in the used 
coffee beans and tealeaves was a lot higher than before they were used for brewing and infusion. The 
reason for this could be that either the method was not precise enough or due to the boiling water in the 
production of coffee brew and tea infusion making it easier to homogenise and extract the PAH4 from the 
samples.  
 
The spectrum of B[a]P was successfully obtained from SERS, but the spectra of the 3 other PAHs was 
not possible to distinguish from acetonitrile. It was not possible to use SERS to detect B[a]P in the 
extractions from the coffee and tea. The reason for this was that the extraction contained a lot of 
undesirable compounds which made it impossible to see the B[a]P peaks.

7 PROSPECTIVE 
Before any clear conclusions can be made, some addition and alterations to this study have to be 
performed.  
 
First of all, the experiments have to be conducted again. Too many of the duplicates failed in one way or 
another, so it is not entirely certain that the results are reliable or if the results are random. More different 
experiments have to be conducted as well to validate the method properly. The trueness, uncertainty, 
ruggedness and reproducibility, which all are validation methods in department K at DTU Food, have not 
been able to be considered, due to the lack of experiments. Before these could have been considered, more 
spiked samples had to be conducted or certified reference material need to be analysed. Furthermore, the 
samples had to be analysed at least three times.    
 
The method for the coffee beans and the tealeaves has to be optimised. The preparation has to be 
investigated more to ensure uniformity every time. Instead of removing the additionally step where the 
fat and undesirable compounds are removed, it would be an idea to find a better technique to remove 
undesirable compounds from the extraction. Furthermore, there are many steps in the method where the 
extract is moved from one glass to another. Every time the extracts are moved, there is a risk of losing 
some PAHs. For example the recoveries are very low for some of the PAHs which could be due to the 
moving of extracts from glass to glass.   
 
The GC-QTOF-MS was found to be very precise. The only optimisation in regards to the GC-QTOF-MS 
could be changing of the oven program so CHR and TRP elutes more separately.
