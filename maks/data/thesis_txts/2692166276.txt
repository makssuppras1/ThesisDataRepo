Abstract
The planning of the fuel supply at a thermal power plant is key in order to keep the plant
running. If the planning fails, it could result in either fuel shortage or lack of fuel stor-
age. This project investigates whether or not the fuel supply at Køge Kraftvarmeværk
(KKV) can be optimized by an optimization algorithm and is made in collaboration
with the engineering company ABB A/S. The current baseline at KKV, regarding the
fuel supply, is based on the experience of each operator and on spreadsheet calculations
planning the fuel distribution for the upcoming week. After the optimization algorithm
is developed, it will be tested on test data given by KKV and later the performance
of the optimization algorithm will be evaluated by comparing with the performance of
KKV’s current baseline. Furthermore, the optimization algorithm will be evaluated on
its compatibility with the control system used at KKV. The test results shows that the
optimization algorithm can produce outputs that in theory would be at least as good
as the current baseline at KKV but in practice it would be more questionable whether
or not the optimization algorithm could work, since the output given every hour must
be manually typed into the system by the operator. Despite the fact that the optimiza-
tion algorithm can generate outputs that seems applicable, it is inconclusive whether or
not the optimization algorithm is better than the spreadsheet approach currently used
for fuel distribution planning since the algorithm has not been tested on the plant at
KKV. However, if the optimization algorithm is tested to perform suﬃciently, the future
goal is then to automate the fuel supply system by letting the algorithm determine the
set-points for the fuel distribution.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Technology is evolving rapidly and gives the industries lots of possibilities to optimize
their processes. One of the industries that wants to optimize their processes is the heat-
ing plant industry and one of the procedures that can be optimized in a heating plant
is the fuel supply system. The planning of the fuel distribution at a heating plant is
crucial in order to avoid fuel shortage or lack of fuel storage.
Køge Kraftvarmeværk (KKV) is a thermal power plant that delivers district heating
in the metropolitan area of Copenhagen and is owned by Vestegnens Kraftvarmeselskab
(VEKS). The fuel supply to the plant consists of wood chips, sawdust and ﬁne wood dust.
The wood chips are supplied from VEKS’ wood chips stock and the sawdust and ﬁne
wood dust are delivered by the wood ﬂooring company Junckers Industrier A/S (Junck-
ers)[1]. Junckers supplies the fuel to the plant throughout the weekdays and in order
to keep up with the fuel deliveries, KKV has to plan how the fuel should be distributed
during the upcoming week. This is done manually by the operator with calculations
on a spreadsheet. This approach is very time consuming and therefore KKV wants to
optimize this procedure such that the spreadsheet calculations are no longer needed.
1.1
Problem
KKV is interested in an algorithm that can help the operator of the plant to optimize
the distribution between the fuel types on a weekly basis, such that in the future the
algorithm can replace the spreadsheet calculations planning the distribution of the fuel
supply.
This project is done in cooperation with ABB A/S, which is an engineering company
specializing in power and automation technology. We would like to investigate how to
optimize the fuel supply to the thermal power plant of KKV by developing an optimiza-
tion algorithm that uses a mathematical model describing an optimal solution to the fuel
distribution. Furthermore, the algorithm must be compatible with ABB’s 800xA con-
trol system, which is used at KKV. We expect our solution to be found by the following
procedure:
• Investigate the current plant setup and the current operating baseline of the fuel
supply system
• Investigate how to develop mathematical models for the power production and the
silo levels by analyzing given data from KKV
• Investigate how linear programming can be applied to develop a mathematical
model of the optimization of the fuel distribution
• Develop an optimization algorithm for the fuel distribution using linear program-
ming and test on data
• Evaluate the result by comparing the current baseline of the fuel supply system
with the optimized solution
• Evaluate the compatibility of the developed algorithm with the 800xA control
system

THE FUEL SUPPLY SYSTEM
2
The Fuel Supply System
The fuel supply system at KKV is illustrated in the block diagram as seen in Figure 1.
It consists of two boilers, Boiler 7 and Boiler 8, which will be referred to as K7 and K8,
respectively. The boilers are fueled with three diﬀerent types of bio fuel; sawdust, wood
chips and ﬁne wood dust. The dust gets ﬁred directly into the boilers, while the sawdust
and wood chips are mixed on a conveyor belt before they are fueled.
Figure 1: A block diagram showing an overview of the fuel supply system at KKV. It consists og
two boilers, K7 and K8, two dust silos, two sawdust silos and two wood chip silos. Furthermore,
it consists of two dust day silos and two sawdust/wood chip silos which is where the fuel being
consumed within the approximately next 30 minutes gets stored. The sawdust and wood chips
gets mixed on a conveyor belt before going into the day silos. In case of overﬁlling in the sawdust
silos, wood chips gets separated from the sawdust and transported back to the wood chips stock.
In case of needing a buﬀer to the sawdust silos to produce more power, the ﬁlling plant is ﬁlled
with wood chips from the wood chips stock.

THE FUEL SUPPLY SYSTEM
2.1
Sawdust
The sawdust at KKV is actually a mixture of sawdust and wood chips and throughout
this report this mixture will be referred to as sawdust. The sawdust is being transported
from Junckers to the two sawdust silos at KKV. Junckers is delivering sawdust Monday-
Friday. If the sawdust silos are low during the weekends, the ﬁlling plant must be used
to ﬁll the sawdust silos with wood chips. The ﬁlling plant is located next to the sawdust
silos and can be seen on Figure 1. This task is done manually by a small excavator.
However, if the sawdust silos are too full, the excess sawdust must be removed. This is
done by separating the wood chips from the sawdust and then transporting the separated
wood chips to the wood chip stock.
2.1.1
Filling conﬁguration
The sawdust is being ﬁlled into the two sawdust silos with a two-way conveyor belt. This
means that only one silo can be ﬁlled at a time depending on which way the conveyor
belt is going. When using the sawdust as fuel, it is fed into the fuel transportation by
rod feeders in the bottom of the silos. Each sawdust silo can hold approximately 300
tons of sawdust.
2.1.2
Level measurement
Each sawdust silo contains a radar sensor used as a measurement device for measuring
the levels in the silos. This also means that the sensor can only measure the level in one
spot in each silo. Each radar is located above the spot where the two-way conveyor belt
is delivering sawdust to the silos.
2.2
Wood chips
The wood chips are stored in large piles on the wood chip stock and gets transported to
the two wood chip silos manually with a large excavator. The wood chips are fed into
the fuel transportation by rod feeders situated in the bottom of the silos and are used
as a buﬀer for the sawdust. This means that if the sawdust silos can’t deliver enough
sawdust to the boilers, the wood chips are used to fulﬁll the fuel demand. Each wood
chip silo can hold approximately 80 tons.
2.3
Dust
Junckers has made an agreement with KKV in order to get rid of the ﬁne wood dust
that remains from their sawmill. Therefore, the dust from Junckers gets stored in two
dust silos at KKV, which then gets distributed into two dust day silos, that each can
ﬁre dust directly into K7 and K8, respectively. The dust inside the dust day silos will
be consumed within approximately 30 minutes. Only one boiler can receive dust at a
time and each boiler has two dust burners which, depending on the amount of dust that
needs to be consumed, can be controlled by setting dust burner steps from 0 to 5. The
maximum level capacity for the dust silos are 75 tons in total, i.e. 37.5 tons per silo.
2.4
Fuel transportation
The fuel transportation consists of conveyor belts going from the sawdust silos and from
the wood chip silos into the sawdust/wood chips day silos, which then will be consumed

2.5
Assumptions
When developing an optimization algorithm for the fuel supply system, assumptions has
to be made. These assumptions are the following:
• KKV has announced that K7 is soon to be taken out of order. Therefore, when
developing the algorithm it will be assumed that KKV only runs with K8.
• By assuming that only K8 is in use, the ﬁlling plant will not be considered when
developing the algorithm. This is because it will not be likely to use the ﬁlling
plant when only the fuel capacity for one boiler is needed instead of two boilers.
This is also mentioned by KKV.
• Silos containing the same type of fuel are identical, i.e. the two dust silos are
identical, the two sawdust silos are identical and the two wood chips silos are
identical.
• The maximum level capacities are 37.5 tons for one dust silo, 300 tons for one
sawdust silo and 80 tons for one wood chip silo.
• The maximum fuel output rate from the sawdust silos is 25 tons/h and that the
maximum fuel output rate from the wood chips silos is 30 tons/h.
• The wood chips silos are full whenever an employee hired to ﬁll up the wood chips
silos is at work.
• Only non-critical faults of the plant can occur. This means that no faults leading
to the plant not being able to run can occur.

CURRENT BASELINE OF THE FUEL SUPPLY SYSTEM
3
Current Baseline of the Fuel Supply System
The current baseline for running the plant is based on the experience of each operator and
on spreadsheet calculations. The spreadsheet calculations involve calculating predicted
silo levels and wood chips consumption based on the estimated energy production during
the week. In this way the operator can make sure that the silos won’t be overﬁlled. Thus,
by taking the fuel consumption and silo level strategies into account, each operator will
determine and calculate suitable set points for each type of fuel.
3.1
Power production
The amount of heat that KKV produces is decided by Varmelast[2].
Varmelast dis-
tributes the heat produced by the diﬀerent heating plants to the Copenhagen metropoli-
tan area. This is done by asking the diﬀerent heating plants how much heat they can
produce and what the cost is for producing the heat. The plants with the lowest cost
will produce the majority of the heat. For KKV, this means that the heat consumption
is restricted by the heat distribution given by Varmelast. Thus, based on the restricted
heat consumption from Varmelast, a power production goal for the boilers are set.
3.2
Sawdust silo strategy
KKV has two strategies for the sawdust silos; a saving strategy and an emptying strategy.
The goal of the saving strategy is to save up as much sawdust as possible before a given
deadline whereas the goal of the emptying strategy is to use as much sawdust as possible
before a given deadline. Both strategies are used all year around but depending on the
season, the usage of the strategies diﬀer.
3.2.1
Winter
Usually, during the winter period the heat consumption is high in the district due to
the cold weather. In order to keep up with the demand of heat in the weekends where
Junckers do not deliver wood, KKV uses the saving strategy during the weekdays such
that the levels in the sawdust silos are as close to their maximum before the weekend
begins. When the weekend begins, KKV uses the emptying strategy in order to produce
the power required by Varmelast. Wood with high caloriﬁc value is prioritized for the
weekends.
3.2.2
Summer
Usually, during the summer period the heat consumption is low in the district due to
the warm weather. Since Junckers supplies the same amount of fuel to KKV during the
weekdays as they do during the winter period, KKV needs to consume as much of the
supply as possible before a new week starts (before every Monday). This means that
KKV uses the emptying strategy throughout the week since the sawdust silos need to
be as close to empty before Monday. If the level in the sawdust silos are too high when
a new week starts, an external worker must separate the wood chips from the sawdust
and transport the excess wood chips to the wood chip stock in order to avoid overﬁlling
of the sawdust silos.

3.3
Dust silo strategy
As mentioned in subsection 2.3, KKV has an agreement with Junckers where Junckers
leaves the leftover ﬁne wood dust from the sawmill to KKV. Since the dust does not
contribute signiﬁcantly to the overall energy production, KKV’s overall strategy for the
dust silos is to prevent the silos from overﬁlling by using the dust when it is possible.
Each boiler has two dust burners and once one of these burners has started it must
remain on for as long as possible since it is costly to start and stop a burner frequently.
3.4
Maintenance
When maintenance on one of the silos or boilers results in one of the components being
taken out of service for a given period, KKV’s strategy must be altered such that the
plant still runs as intended. It is currently the operator’s task to alter the strategy.
3.5
Caloriﬁc value
There is currently no set-point for the caloriﬁc value of the wood used for fuelling. Thus,
it is the operator’s task to alter the set-points of the distribution of the fuel supply in
accordance to the wood types that are currently in use.
3.6
Spreadsheet calculations
KKV has a spreadsheet template in which they use to calculate the predicted wood
chips consumption during the week by determining suitable set-points for the sawdust
and dust consumption and the total energy consumption.
The operator determines
the energy consumption throughout the hours of the week based on the restrictions
from Varmelast. Furthermore, the sawdust and dust consumptions are set based on
the strategy of the silo levels. Thus, by knowing the caloriﬁc values of the fuels, the
estimated amounts of dust and sawdust consumed and the total energy consumption
every hour throughout the week, the operator can calculate the amount of estimated
wood chips needed to consume every hour as follows:

An example of the spreadsheet used for calculations at KKV for week 50, 2020 can

3.7
Primary goals for the fuel supply system
From the investigated current baseline of the fuel supply at KKV, it can be seen that
the operator has two primary goals to fulﬁll. The ﬁrst one is to satisfy the power goal
restricted by Varmelast, and the second one is to fulﬁll the sawdust and dust silo level
strategies.

DATA ANALYSIS AND MODELLING OF POWER PRODUCTION
4
Data Analysis and Modelling of Power Production
When developing the optimization algorithm for the fuel supply system it is necessary
to know the relation between the thermal input power and the produced output power
of the plant. However, the power loss of the heating plant at KKV is unknown. Thus, in
order to determine the relation between the thermal input power from the fuel and the
produced output power, a power model must be developed. This will require relevant
data from the plant, which is provided by KKV.
4.1
Power production data set
The data set provided by KKV consists of data from the month of February 2021 and
contains the calculated produced power in MW by the two boilers and the steam tem-
perature in ◦C and pressure in bar inside each of the boilers.
It also contains the
temperature in ◦C in the mixing tank which is the tank that contains the water that
gets heated in the boilers. Furthermore, it contains the steam rates in kg/s coming
out of the two boilers and the input rate of the fuel in tons/h. The fuel going into the
boilers are dust, sawdust and wood chips. All the given measurements in the data set
are averaged over each hour of February 2021 and the complete data set can be found
in Appendix E subsection E.2.

power produced over time is shown.

f(x) = −0.0100958425x2 + 2.57087940x −67.3369642
(2)
The polynomial regression is of second order since it ﬁts quite well and higher order

where there are many data points and less reliable in the regions with few data points.

MW in total. Therefore, a solution to the diﬀerentiated 2nd order polynomial regression

f′(x) = 0 →x = 127.3236

127.3236 MW of thermal power will in theory give the same amount of produced power,
namely 96.33 MW, which agrees with the fact that the maximum produced power ca-
pacity by the boilers is around 100 MW in total. In practice the boilers could possibly

4.2.1
Thermal power
The thermal power is the power that is put into the system. This can be calculated
by multiplying the caloriﬁc value with the rates of the fuels. The caloriﬁc value is the
amount of energy that is released by burning a speciﬁc amount of the fuel[3]. At KKV,
the caloriﬁc value of all the fuels are being sampled randomly during a month. These
samples are measured in GJ/tons. The rate of sawdust and wood chips are measured
by conveyor belt scales. The rate of the ﬁne wood dust is measured by a scale after the
wood dust has been ground to ﬁne wood dust. All the rates are measured in tons/h.
Multiplying the caloriﬁc value of a fuel with the rate of the same fuel would result in
the power being given in GJ/h. In order to get the power in MJ/s (or simply MW), a
conversion factor of 0.28 has been multiplied with the rate and caloriﬁc value[4]. Thus,
the thermal power for the three types of fuel are:
Pchipsin = Bchips · mchips · 0.28 [MW]
(4)
Psawin = Bsaw · msaw · 0.28 [MW]
(5)
Pdustin = Bdust · mdust · 0.28 [MW]
(6)
where, Pchipsin, Psawin and Pdustin are the thermal power of wood chips, sawdust and
dust measured in MW,respectively, Bchips, Bsaw and Bdust are the caloriﬁc values of
wood chips, sawdust and dust measured in GJ/tons, respectively, and mchips, msaw
and mdust are the consumption of wood chips, sawdust and dust measured in tons/h,
respectively. Thus, the total thermal power put into the system is:
Ptotin = Pchipsin + Psawin + Pdustin [MW]
(7)
4.2.2
Produced power
The 800xA control system used at KKV is already calculating the power generated by
the steam turbine in the boiler. This is calculated by multiplying the mass ﬂow rate of
the steam going into the turbine with the enthalpy drop in the boiler[5]. The enthalpy
is a property of a thermodynamic system and it is deﬁned by the sum of the system’s
internal energy and the product of its volume and pressure[6]. The enthalpy drop in
the boiler can be found by calculating the diﬀerence between the enthalpy of the steam
going into the turbine and the enthalpy of the water inside the mixing tank, which is a
tank at the bottom of the boiler where the water being heated is stored. When knowing
the temperature and pressure inside the boiler, which are measured, the enthalpy of the
steam going into the turbine can be found by looking up in the thermodynamics steam
table[7]. The enthalpy of the water in the mix tank can be found in the same manner.
When the enthalpy drop in the boiler has been determined, the produced power can be
==PAGE:18==
Page 10 of 42
4

found by multiplying the ﬂow rate of the steam going into the turbine with the enthalpy
drop. Thus, the calculation for the produced power is:
Pprod = htp · steamout [kW]
(8)
where Pprod is the total produced power in kW, steamout is the steam ﬂow rate in kg/s
and htp is the enthalpy drop in the boiler in kJ/kg, which is the diﬀerence between the
enthalpy of the steam going into the turbine, hturbine, and the enthalpy of the water in
the mix tank, hmixtank:
htp = hturbine −hmixtank
(9)

Data Analysis and Modeling of Sawdust Silo Levels
The sawdust silo level measurements from the radar sensors are known to be very unsta-
ble. This is due to the fact that each silo only has one radar sensor, which only captures
the level of the sawdust silos beneath that speciﬁc spot, where the sensor is positioned.
As the sawdust can form peeks inside the silos, the radar sensor can wrongly register an
increase in level. Furthermore, these peeks will eventually collapse and cause the radar
sensor to register a decrease in level.
Due to the unreliable radar measurements it is impossible for the optimization algo-
rithm of the fuel supply system to be able to keep track of the correct sawdust silo
levels. Therefore, KKV has managed to perform a level test of the east and west saw-
dust silos in order to determine the relation between the sensor measurements and the
actual levels in tons inside the silos. The test was performed by ﬁlling the two sawdust
silos from empty to maximum level capacity while logging the radar level measurements
and the in-going weight measurements. The data results for this level test can be found
in Appendix E subsection E.2.

are shown. It can be seen that the maximum level recorded in the east silo is about
300 tons and the maximum level recorded in the west silo is about 285 tons. However,
it is assumed that both silos are identical, which is why it is then assumed that both

6.1.1
Time and prediction
The fuel supply system is a slow system due to the time it takes for the diﬀerent fuel
types to be transported from the silos into the boilers. Therefore, the main program will
only run once every hour to update the set-points. Furthermore, the main program also
predicts how the levels in the dust and sawdust silos will behave a week ahead in order
to be able to warn the operator if the silos are close to or will get overﬁlled. This is done
by running the predict() -function in the main program 168 times (since there are 168
hours in a week) in a loop, where the ﬁrst iteration uses the inputs from 800xA, i.e. the
actual behavior of the fuel supply system at that time, as a baseline to how the levels
will behave the following 167 hours, assuming the operator will adjust the set-points
according to the program’s suggestions every hour. This is also illustrated in Figure 8.
As a way to smoothen the set-point outputs for the distribution between the sawdust
and wood chips, the sawdust program and wood chips program looks X hours ahead
in time in each iteration of the prediction to account for upcoming fuel deliveries from
Junckers in the near future. By doing so, the set-points does not change rapidly at
every update and thereby gives smoother set-points. Obviously, the prognoses for the
fuel deliveries from Junckers are not necessarily accurate, so if X is set to be very high,
the suggested set-points will be based on the fact that most of the prognoses are true,
which might end up giving a failed attempt to fulﬁll the strategies for dust and sawdust.
Therefore, a reasonable X is chosen to be X = 6 hours.

6.8
Optimization Program
The optimization program determines the optimal amount of sawdust and wood chips
that should be fueled during the next six hours in order to fulﬁll the power goal and
the the sawdust silo strategy goal. The sawdust silo strategy goal will be fulﬁlled if the
amount of sawdust determined in the sawdust program (see subsection 6.6) is consumed
within the next hour. Furthermore, the power goal will be fulﬁlled if the thermal input
power calculated with the suggested amount of sawdust and wood chips that should be
fueled the next hour is equal to the desired thermal input, which is calculated from the
power model in subsection 4.2.
6.8.1
The optimization problem
In order for the optimization program to ﬁnd a solution to the problem, a mathematical
model of the optimization problem has to be set. If the power goal for the next hour
is denoted g1 and the desired amount of sawdust to be consumed within the next hour
is denoted g2, then by using equations (4), (5) and (7), we can write the following two
objective functions:
obj1 : 0.28Bchipsx1 + 0.28Bsawx2 + Pdustin = g1
obj2 : x1 = g2,
where x1 and x2 are variables that describe the suggested amounts of sawdust and wood
chips that should be consumed within the next hour given in tons/h, respectively. Pdustin
is the thermal input power from the suggested amount of dust being consumed during
the next hour given in MW and is a ﬁxed value determined from the output of the dust
program and the caloriﬁc value of the ﬁne wood dust.
It can be seen that the two objective functions can be conﬂicting, since the desired
amount of sawdust might be too high or won’t necessarily be suﬃcient in order for the
power goal to be fulﬁlled. As an approach to solve for the optimal values of x1 and x2,
goal programming is applied. Goal programming is a generalization of linear program-
ming to handle multi-objective measures, which typically are conﬂicting each other[8].
Each of these measures are given a goal or a target value to be achieved, which in this
case are g1 and g2. When using goal programming as an approach, the two conﬂicting
objectives will be seen as soft constraints, meaning that they can be violated by the
feasible solutions to the multi-objective model. An optimal solution to the problem is
found when both of the goals have been satisﬁed as much as possible to their desired
levels, while satisfying a set of constraints. In this case, due to the fact that the fuel has
limited supply, the amount of sawdust and wood chips being solved for is bounded by
some hard constraints. These constraints are described as follows:
• c1 : x1 ≤Msaw
• c2 : x2 ≤Mchips

• c3 : Bchipsx1 · 0.28 + Bsawx2 · 0.28 + Pdustin ≤g1
• c4 : x1 ≥bl
• c5 : x2 ≥bl
• c6 : x1 ≤bu,saw
• c7 : x2 ≤bu,chips
where c1 sets a constraint on the solution to x1 saying that the suggested amount of
sawdust being consumed within the next hour cannot be larger than the total amount
of sawdust in tons inside the sawdust silos, Msaw. In the same way for wood chips, c2
is a constraint on x2 saying that the suggested amount of wood chips being consumed
within the next hour cannot be larger than the total amount of wood chips in tons in-
side the wood chips silos, Mchips. Constraint c3 indicates that the thermal input power
calculated using the suggested amounts of sawdust and wood chips to consume, x1 and
x2, cannot exceed the power goal, g1, since the fuel supply system won’t consume more
fuel than the load of the boiler will allow.
Furthermore, there are upper and lower bounds for each of the two variables. Both
x1 and x2 have a lower bound of bl = 0 tons/h, since it is not possible to have a negative
fuel consumption. These lower bounds are described by the constraints c4 and c5. The
maximum amount of sawdust that can be transported from the sawdust silos into the
boiler in one hour is known to be around 25 tons, which is why the upper bound for x1
is set to 25 tons/h and is described in constraint c6. Similarly, the maximum amount of
wood chips found to be available and able to fuel during the next hour, bu,chips, is set to
be an upper bound for x2. This is also shown in constraint c7 and the value of bu,chips
is found from the wood chips program (see subsection 6.7).
Now that the two objectives and their constraints has been set, the next step is to
minimize the deviation between the two objectives and their goals. The deviation will
be measured as the squared error and the minimization can be written as
min obj1 = min((0.28Bchipsx1 + 0.28Bsawx2 · +Pdustin −g1)2)
(13)
and
min obj2 = min((x1 −g2)2)
(14)

tion problem using a weighted sum, where the importance of each objective function is
determined by inserting appropriate weights[9]. Since the power production is KKV’s
ﬁnal product, the importance of obj1 is set as a higher priority than for obj2. This means
that the program would prioritize to reach the desired power goal even if it would cost
not reaching the desired sawdust strategy goal. If the weight of obj1 is denoted ω1 and
the weight of obj2 is denoted ω2, the minimization problem combining the two objectives
can be written as
min(ω1obj1,ω2obj2)

plants[11]. The KKS-number is used to to identify components in a plant and the KKS-
number even contains information about the location of a component.
A list of the
KKS-numbers used by the optimization algorithm can be found in Appendix B and the
KKS-numbers assigned to the outputs of the algorithm are only suggested.
The optimization algorithm has inputs and outputs which are in datetime format. For
example, the operator has to inform the program when the sawdust silos must be full by
giving the algorithm a date and a time. However, 800xA can’t handle data which are in
datetime format since 800xA gives dates in seconds since 01/01-0001 00:00. Therefore
the datetime must be converted to Unix time in order for 800xA to handle the datetime
format. Unix time is the number of seconds that have elapsed since 01/01-1970 00:00:00
which is the Unix epoch[12]. The optimization algorithm then converts the Unix time
back to datetime format in order for the algorithm to handle the dates easier. Before
the optimization algorithm has to send its outputs to the database, it must be converted
back to Unix time. This might seem like a cumbersome method but nonetheless it is
the best workaround in order for 800xA to handle datetime data. The alternative would
have been to break up each datetime to a year, month, day and hour meaning that
each datetime would need four integers in order to be deﬁned. This would result in the
operator having 60 inputs only for the datetime inputs.
Since the optimization algorithm can fetch and send data to the 800xA system and
the data sent by the optimization algorithm can be handled by 800xA and vice versa, it
can be concluded that the optimization algorithm is compatible with the 800xA system.

operator does not necessarily have to change the set points every hour in order to fulﬁll

fully test the performance of the algorithm. If the algorithm performs suﬃciently, the
algorithm could then be automated and thereby relieve some of the demand of attention
from the operator on the fuel distribution set points.

FUTURE WORKS
9
Future Works
Many diﬀerent tests, improvements and experiments have been left for the future due
to the restricted timeline for this project. First and foremost, the algorithm’s perfor-
mance need to be tested at KKV by having the operator set the suggested set-points of
the fuels from the algorithm every hour for a period of time. The performance of the
algorithm can then be evaluated on how the strategy of the dust and sawdust silos and
how the power production goal are upholding. If the algorithm performs suﬃciently,
the next step would be to automate the system. This would mean that the algorithm
should be able to set the suggested set-points instead of the operator. This is also what
KKV desires to achieve with the algorithm in the long term, since it would replace their
time spent on spreadsheets to calculate and plan how the plant should run week by week.
Furthermore, maintenance for the database could be implemented such that the database
won’t run out of storage. This is of course a minor problem since the database can han-
dle many years of data without overloading, but eventually old data should be removed.
This leads to the idea to a system moving the algorithm’s old input/output data from
the database into external ﬁles instead of deleting them. The old data could then be
used for analysis of the system’s overall performance over a greater period of time and
it could be incorporated into the algorithm such that it could learn from this data. The
idea of the algorithm learning and gaining experience from data is also suggested as a
future improvement from KKV’s evaluation in subsubsection 8.1.1.
As mentioned earlier in this project, the power model in subsection 4.2 is not perfect
and is only reliable in the regions where the data has been recorded. A large deviation
between the produced power goal and the actual power produced is also mentioned as a
concern from KKV in their evaluation. Therefore, if the power model is not suﬃcient for
the algorithm to determine the correct amount of thermal input power needed to reach
the produced power goal, an approach to minimize the error between the actual power
produced and the power production goal could be to implement a closed-loop conﬁgu-
ration where the error is corrected for by regulating the thermal input power. Another
approach would be to create a better and improved power model; The current power
model is based on one month of data where the plant was running with both boilers,
K7 and K8. However, since K7 is taken out of service in the near future, a new power
model based on data from the fuel supply system only running with K8 over a longer
period of time could improve the reliability of the power calculations.

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
The purpose of this project was to investigate how to develop an optimization algo-
rithm which could distribute the diﬀerent types of fuel at KKV and thereby replace the
spreadsheet calculations used by the operators to plan appropriate set points during the
upcoming week.
By investigating the plant setup and current baseline at KKV, deriving a power model
and a sawdust silo level model and developing an optimization algorithm using linear
programming it was found that the optimization algorithm can generate outputs that
seems to be applicable. The tests mentioned in section 7 shows that the sawdust silos
are following the given strategies and so does the dust silos. Furthermore the power
produced seems to match the power goal more or less.
The optimization algorithm has not been tested on the plant at KKV and therefore it
can not be concluded whether or not the optimization algorithm works better than the
spreadsheet approach which is currently used.
The optimization algorithm is currently only generating suggestions for the operator
in which the operator then has to type in manually. This is of course not very conve-
nient for the operator in practice. However, if the optimization algorithm performs well,
the next step is to automate the system by setting the suggested set points automati-
cally and thereby relieving some of the demand of attention from the operator on the fuel
distribution set points. At this stage the spreadsheet would then become redundant.

Introduction and Problem
Technology is evolving rapidly and gives the industries lots of possibilities to optimize
their processes. One of the industries that wants to optimize their processes is the heat-
ing plant industry and one of the procedures that can be optimized in a heating plant
is the fuel supply system.
Køge Kraftvarmeværk (KKV) is a thermal power plant that delivers district heating
in the metropolitan area of Copenhagen and is owned by Vestegnens Kraftvarmeselskab
(VEKS). The fuel supply to the plant consists of wood chips and saw dust which is
delivered by the wood ﬂooring company Junckers.
Junckers supplies the fuel to the
plant throughout the weekdays. However, the plant does not receive any fuel during
the weekends. In case of fuel shortage during the weekends, an external employee from
Junckers must be hired to be available to transport woodchips to the plant. This is a
large expense since KKV pays the external employee to be available the whole weekend
and is not necessarily transporting woodchips the whole time. Therefore KKV wants
to optimize the fuel delivery and consumption of the plant in order to determine work
hours for the external worker and thereby save money.
KKV is interested in an algorithm that can help the operator of the plant to optimize
the fuel supply and its distribution between the fuel types on a weekly basis, such that
the external employees for fuel transport are only used when necessary.
This project is done in cooperation with ABB, which is an engineering company special-
izing in power and automation technology. We would like to investigate how to optimize
the fuel supply to the thermal power plant of KKV by deriving a mathematical model
describing an optimal solution to the problem. Furthermore, the algorithm must be
compatible with ABB’s 800xA system, which is used at KKV. We expect our solution
to be found by the following procedure:
• Investigate the given data from KKV and the their current fuel supply system by
establishing the system parameters
• Investigate the current baseline of the the fuel supply system
• Investigate how to set up a mathematical description of the optimization problem
• Develop an optimization algorithm for the fuel consumption and test on data
• Evaluate the result by comparing the current baseline of the fuel supply system
with the optimized solution
• Evaluate the compatibility of the solution with the 800xA system

Solution Method
In order to optimize the fuel supply to the thermal power plant of KKV, the following
steps will be performed:
• Establish the system parameters (inputs/outputs) of the fuel supply system of the
thermal power plant. This is done by analyzing given data from KKV and by
drawing a block diagram of the system.
• Establish the current baseline of the fuel supply system by interviewing operators
at the plant. The result of the interview should clear how and why the operator
sets the diﬀerent set points in the fuel supply system.
• Set up a mathematical description of the optimization problem. The problem is a
minimization problem and the system constraints need to be established.
• From the mathematical description and the constraints an optimization algorithm
for the fuel supply system is developed. The algorithm is then tested on the given
test data.
• Evaluate the test results by comparing the previously established baseline of the
system with the found optimal solution to the minimization problem.
• Evaluate whether the algorithm can be implemented in the 800xA system. This
is done by looking at the documentation for 800xA and compare with the pseudo
code of the algorithm.

Evaluering af projekt brændselsstyring på KKV: 
 
Projektet skal håndtere data i form af manuelle inputs, automatiske inputs, prognoser, faktisk forbrug og 
outputs. 
Der er mange ting som kan ændre output, eksempelvis hvis en vægt vejer forkert, stor forskel mellem 
prognose og den faktiske last på kedlen samt andre variabler. 
Projektet er endnu ikke testet på KKV, men de forskellige tests som programmet har kørt, ser ud til at 
stemme godt overens med hvad KKVs regnearksmodel også havde forudsagt. 
For at kunne vurdere om programmet virker efter hensigten, skal det testes på KKV da der er mange 
faktorer der påvirker det. 
Det er godt at programmet er tænkt til at genberegne hver time, for at opdatere set punkterne. 
Det er ligeledes godt at programmet kan resettes hvis operatøren vurderer det, er nødvendigt.  
• 
Er der lavet en indikator for hvornår programmet selv anbefaler at det skal resettes? Måske skal 
programmet selv kunne gå i fejl hvis der er behov for det? 
• 
Kan programmet selv håndtere at komme tilbage på sporet hvis noget skrider. Eksempelvis i form 
af machine learning?    
• 
Hvad er mulighederne for at få programmet til at kigge længere ud i fremtiden, i mere end én uge?  
• 
Bliver programmet klogere af erfaringer jo længere tid programmet kører. 
 
Effektmodellen: 
Programmet bruger en effektmodel til at beregne hvor meget brændsel der skal bruges, grundet tab fra 
den indfyret effekt til den produceret effekt. 
• 
Det er godt at programmet tager højde for tab, da outputs burde blive mere præcist. 
• 
Kan det skabe udfordringer hvis effektmodelen afviger for meget?  
 
Mængder af brændsel i savsmuldssiloer: 
Det er ikke muligt at aflæse præcis hvor mange tons savsmuld der er i de to siloer, da det er en radermåling 
som måler niveauet. Savsmuldet falder ned og står i en pyramide, og derfor er radermålingen ikke 
retvisende.   
Derfor er det en udfordring at programmet ikke kender den præcise mængde i savsmuldssiloen. 
• 
Det er godt at programmet kigger på vægtene ind og ud af siloen for at få den mest præcise måling 
af mængden i siloen 
• 
Kan det være en udfordring hvis der er fejl på vægtene eller de måler forkert?
