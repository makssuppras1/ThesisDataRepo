Abstract
Nowdays, artificial intelligence platforms are increasingly integrated into workplace envi┬¡
ronments, promising to strengthen efficiency, streamline workflows, and optimize decision┬¡
making. However, quantifying their actual impact remains a challenge, particularly when
assessing their effectiveness in supporting employeesÔÇÖ daily tasks. This study evaluates
a generative AI platform during a defined period, from February 17 to August 17, 2025,
following its introduction in The Company in September 2023. I choose a mixed┬¡methods
approach combining qualitative interviews and quantitative surveys to investigates the
platformÔÇÖs real┬¡world value and usability within this specific context at this specific mo┬¡
ment in time.
At the outset of this study, internal discussions at The Company revealed a strong enthu┬¡
siasm for the AI platform, largely driven by the belief that it would make everyoneÔÇÖs work
easier. However, there was little clarity on how exactly it should be used or for which
specific tasks, its adoption appeared to be mostly exploratory. This reflects a broader
industry trend, where companies feel pressure to integrate generative AI without a clear
understanding of its return on investment (ROI) or long┬¡term value.
My findings show that while employees are generally eager to use the tool, and perceive
improvements, its use still lacks strategic directions and organizational alignment. For AI
to be truly effective, it needs to be adapted not only to The CompanyÔÇÖs broader goals but
also to the specific workflows of individual teams and employees.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
Artificial intelligence is becoming more common in workplaces, with the promise of com┬¡
pleting tasks faster and automating routine work. However, as AI technologies rapidly
evolve, their impact extends beyond technological advancement, encompassing strate┬¡
gic, ethical, and regulatory dimensions. Organizations must ensure that AI tools contribute
to real productivity improvements rather than simply following industry trends.
Although my thesis focuses on understanding the impact of an internal AI platform, it is
part of a broader research agenda that aim to estimate the return on investment (ROI) of
AI in enterprise environments. While AI adoption promise great things, it also presents
notable challenges, particularly in demonstrating tangible ROI [1]. Quantifying AIÔÇÖs actual
value in workplace settings remains difficult, as organizations often lack clear efficiency
metrics to measure its impact, and it is difficult to have baseline metrics to compare pre┬¡
and post┬¡ AI performance. This is further complicated by the growing discussions on
Human┬¡Centered Artificial Intelligence (HCAI), an approach that emphasizes the role of
humans in AI development and use [2]. The introduction of AI regulations, such as the
European UnionÔÇÖs AI Act (AIA), represents a pivotal step in addressing ethical concerns,
but also introduces challenges for businesses in balancing compliance with innovation
[3].
The idea for this project first came to me back in 2023, when I started my MasterÔÇÖs degree
and began working at this particular industrial company. Around that time, they had just
launched their AI platform, so I pitched the idea for this project to my manager. Initially,
I planned to keep it as an internal initiative, but since the platform had only just been
released, it was not yet mature enough for the small group of testers. Fast forward two
years, I proposed it again, this time as my thesis, and it was met with much more support.
Other companies had started talking about similar challenges, and internally, after two
years we had reached much more maturity with the tool.
This study represents a crucial first step in a larger evaluation. By investigating how
employees interact with the AI platform, what tasks it supports, and how it fits into their
workflows, the research uncovers where value is currently created, and where friction
remains. Understanding which occupational skills are most engaged versus those that
are less supported offers early insight into the nature of human┬¡AI collaboration. This lays
the groundwork for future efforts to quantify economic gains and calculate ROI.
1.2
Defining the Problem
The key contribution of this thesis is identifying which occupational skills are mostly in┬¡
volved in human┬¡AI interactions. My research question is:
Does AI actually make work more efficient, or does it create new problems that outweigh
its advantages?
In this context, an advantage refers to any improvement in workplace efficiency, produc┬¡
tivity, or user satisfaction that employees of The Company attribute to the AI platform. A
problem, refers to any negative impact introduced by the AI platform. Many businesses
1
==PAGE:10==
assume that AI┬¡driven solutions will lead to better performance, yet evidence supporting
these claims is limited.
To explore this, I have to ask how employees actually interact with the AI platform and
how useful do they perceive it to be? What specific efficiency gains emerge, if any, from
the use of the AI platform? Are tasks completed faster, do error rates decline, does it
really automate routine activities? Does the AI platform create new obstacles or complex┬¡
ities, does it impose additional cognitive demands, usability challenges, or unforeseen
inefficiencies?

1.3
Dissertation Outline
This section is an overview of the structure and logical flow of my thesis. The aim is
to guide the reader from the broadest contextual considerations down to the specific re┬¡
search problem and methods I have applied.
In Section 2, I present a literature review to explore the current state of research on the
use of AI in workplace settings. The review is organized using a funnel structure to pro┬¡
gressively narrow the focus and identify the specific research gap this dissertation wants
to address.
The first layer examines broader debates around the timing and control of digital technol┬¡
ogy regulation. Technologies evolve rapidly but regulatory strategies are not fast enough,
and it is difficult to strike a balance between encourage innovation and protecting societal
interests. This tension is illustrated by well┬¡known challenges such as the Collingridge
Dilemma [4] and the Pacing Problem [5].
The second layer focuses on the context in which this research takes place, starting from
The Company and then, narrowing it down to the specific department of interest, and
finally addressing the specific AI platform studied.
The third and final layer reviews existing literature on AI implementation in workplaces,
highlighting what has already been explored and where there are still gaps or open ques┬¡
tions.
Figure 1.1: Funnel of Layers for Research Domain
Section 3 outlines the methodological choices that shaped this research, grounded in a
constructivist perspective [6] , where knowledge is co┬¡constructed through interpretation.
I followed an abductive approach [7], I allowed insights from the data to guide the research
direction. The whole process was structured using the Double Diamond framework [8],
that supported a clear, iterative flow from exploration to delivery. I narrowed the scope
to a specific department of The Company, called Domain 10, which was the first to adopt
the AI platform and thus the most experienced with its use. I applied a non┬¡probability
sampling strategy [9], selecting interview participants purposively, aiming for one person
per seniority level across the 12 teams and conducted eight interviews in total. In parallel,
I distributed a survey to all 108 members of the domain, achieving a total of 61 responses.
Interview data were analysed using a multi┬¡phase thematic analysis, supported by Python
tools for coding, clustering, and affinity mapping followed by a structured AI┬¡assisted anal┬¡
3
==PAGE:12==
ysis [10]. Survey results were interpreted using standard usability and satisfaction metrics
(SUS and NPS).
Section 4 presents the findings from both qualitative and quantitative analysis. The results
reflect employee experiences and how they use the AI platform across different levels of
seniority.
After conducting the eight interviews, I could see common patterns emerge around coding
help, saving time, and creating content. A separate review of the transcripts using text
analysis tools also highlighted similar themes, including ease of use, how well the tool fits
into daily work, and levels of trust. These were further validated through an AI┬¡assisted
thematic analysis based on Braun and ClarkeÔÇÖs framework [11], adapted by Goyanes [10].
Quantitative survey results (n=61, 59% response rate) showed an average System Us┬¡
ability Scale (SUS) score of 68.01 meeting the usability benchmark but with notable vari┬¡
ation. Segmenting results by seniority revealed differing perceptions: students reported
lower NPS and mixed enthusiasm, while engineers and senior staff valued the platformÔÇÖs
usefulness despite usability challenges. The findings suggest that the platform supports
productivity but it requires refinement in areas such as trust, integration, and user experi┬¡
ence.
I, then, use the discussion in Section 5 to reflect on the findings through the lens of the
research funnel introduced earlier. Starting from a broad organizational perspective and
narrowing down to individual experiences and tool┬¡specific outcomes. By layering the
discussion in this way, I wanted to make sense of the data while highlighting both the
opportunities and the challenges observed throughout the study.
In addition to reflecting on the results, I also acknowledges the studyÔÇÖs limitations, dis┬¡
cussing how these may influence the interpretation of the findings. Finally, I highlight the
broader relevance of the research and suggest directions for future studies that could
build on these insights.
I conclude the study in Section 6 synthesizing the insights from the results and discussion
sections to answer my main research question. This thesis wants to contributes to the field
of human┬¡AI collaboration by showing when and how AI adoption can support, or hinder,
productivity in the workplace. It also points to areas where future research is needed, such
as measuring return on investment (ROI), understanding how adoption evolves over time,
and exploring how AI tools can be better designed to fit the needs of different employee
profiles.

Background
This section introduces the first layer of the research funnel 1.1 by looking at the rise of
internal AI tools in workplaces. Each part of the funnel will be unpacked throughout the
thesis, starting here with the general motivations and assumptions behind AI integration,
before narrowing down to the specific organizational and user┬¡level perspectives.
Technological innovation has consistently shaped the evolution of societies, industries,
and economies throughout history. From the steam engine to the internet, each wave of
advancement has brought profound changes to how people work, communicate, and live.
Today, Artificial Intelligence represents the latest technological frontier, offering immense
potential. However, the rapid pace at which AI is evolving presents unique challenges,
particularly when it comes to managing its integration and assessing its true impact within
organizational settings [1] .
Unlike previous technological revolutions, that unfolded over decades, AIÔÇÖs development
is characterized by its unprecedented speed, making it difficult for organizations to keep
pace, implement appropriate oversight, and measure effectiveness. This rapid pace of
change makes it even more important to take a close look at whatÔÇÖs being adopted, espe┬¡
cially when big investments are made with the hope that it will genuinely make work run
more smoothly.
The challenge of controlling and evaluating an AI platform is not new to the discussions
on technological governance. David CollingridgeÔÇÖs Dilemma of Control [4] highlights the
paradox that arises when managing emerging technologies: in the early stages of de┬¡
velopment, it is difficult to predict a technologyÔÇÖs full impact, making timely regulation
challenging. However, by the time the consequences of the technology become clear,
it is often too entrenched in societal and economic structures to be easily controlled or
modified. This dilemma is very relevant for AI, as its integration into workplaces precedes
a full understanding of its long┬¡term implications, leaving organizations to struggle with its
benefits and limitations after adoption has already occurred.
Another big challenge is the Pacing Problem, the gap between how quickly technology
advances and how slowly rules, ethics, and everyday practices catch up. One reason
this gap has grown is MooreÔÇÖs Law [12], which predicts that computing power will roughly
double every two years while becoming cheaper. The current pace of AI development is
surpassing even this long┬¡standing trend, with advancements in machine learning models,
neural networks, and data processing occurring at unprecedented speeds. This acceler┬¡
ating innovation intensifies the challenge for regulatory frameworks, which are traditionally
very slow to evolve. Many companies are bringing in this new technology faster than they
can set clear rules for how to use it. This can raise concerns about efficiency, data se┬¡
curity, and ethics. Without clear measures, strong oversight, and good governance, they
risk running into inefficiencies, pushback from users, and unexpected problems.
As the idea of Human┬¡Centered AI (HCAI) becomes more widely accepted, thereÔÇÖs a grow┬¡
ing understanding that these systems should be built with people, not just technology, at
the center. HCAI calls for tools that boost productivity while supporting decision┬¡making,
reducing unnecessary strain, and staying aligned with ethical and organizational values
[2]. Evaluating AI platforms, then, isnÔÇÖt just about measuring technical performance, itÔÇÖs
also about seeing how they fit into daily work, affect employee satisfaction, and con┬¡
tribute to wider company goals. This thesis fills an important gap by providing a grounded,
5
==PAGE:14==
employee┬¡focused look at an internal AI platform in real use. Rather than assuming that
adoption automatically means higher productivity, it examines how employees actually
experience the platform, offering a clearer picture of its value, its limits, and where it could
go next.

Literature Search and Selection Process
To build the literature foundation for this thesis, I conducted a structured search using
DTU Findit in February and March 2025. For example, I used the Boolean search strings:
Impact AND of AND AI AND on AND the AND Workplace
The initial search on DTU Findit gave back 1,068 results. Due to the volume I sorted
the results by the databaseÔÇÖs default ranking, which presumably balances factors such as
keyword relevance, citation frequency, though its internal algorithm is not publicly docu┬¡
mented. From this sorted list, I screened the titles and abstracts of the top 50 entries.
Any papers that appeared directly relevant, particularly empirical studies on humanÔÇôAI
interaction in workplace settings were selected for full┬¡text review. Studies that were too
theoretical, outdated, or minimally aligned with the research focus were excluded.
Figure 2.2: Literature Search
Beyond database searches, some papers were recommended by colleagues or class┬¡

2.3
Related work
In this section, I examine the third layer of the research funnel 1.1 by exploring how the AI
platform is actually used in practice in workplaces. Moving beyond adoption trends and
organizational context, this part of the thesis looks closely at existing literature on how
employees use AI tools, and what benefits and barriers are observed in practice, how
they perceive its usefulness, and what challenges arise in day┬¡to┬¡day interaction.
Previous research on the implementation of AI in workplaces has provided important in┬¡
sights into both its benefits and challenges. These studies often utilize a mix of quantitative
and qualitative approaches, including case studies and surveys, to evaluate the practical
impact of AI technologies on employees and organizational workflows.
One prominent example of this approach is the OECDÔÇÖs extensive case studies [20] and
surveys conducted across various sectors, including finance and manufacturing. Their
research involved structured interviews and questionnaires send to stakeholders such
as workers, managers, and union representatives in multiple countries. These studies
highlighted the diverse effects of AI, results shows that it frequently improves job quality
by automating tedious and repetitive tasks, which reduces workloads, improve employee
engagement and workplace safety. However, Milanez [21] also uncovered increased work
intensity, stress, and anxiety about job loss, reflecting the complexity of AIÔÇÖs impact in real┬¡
world settings.
In terms of employee behavior and interaction with AI, Qin, Qiu, Li, and Jiang [22] have
used quantitative analyses and structural equation modeling to explore how employee
perceptions of AI influence their proactive work behaviors. These studies demonstrate
that when employees see AI as integral to their roles and view interactions with AI posi┬¡
tively, it significantly increases their motivation to engage proactively at work.
Additionally, Marguerita and Morgan [23] have emphasized the importance of clearly
defining and categorizing AI systems within workplace contexts, highlighting that orga┬¡
nizations benefit significantly from understanding precisely what kind of AI technology
they adopt and how it fits into their workflows. OECD research stresses that categoriz┬¡
ing AI tools based on their functionality and intended impact helps companies effectively
select and integrate AI into their operations. They argue that the successful integration
of AI in workplaces requires transparency, employee involvement, and targeted training.
Their findings suggest that when these factors are prioritized, employees show trust in AI
systems and report improvements in job satisfaction and productivity. Neglecting these
aspects often leads to confusion, resistance, and reduced adoption, undermining AIÔÇÖs
potential benefits.
While these studies provide valuable high┬¡level insights into how AI is reshaping work,
they often treat employees as a single, uniform group. What remains less explored are
the domain┬¡specific and skill┬¡based differences in how AI tools are adopted, trusted, or
resisted. This gap is especially relevant in diverse technical teams, where employees vary
widely in responsibilities, expertise, and expectations. By focusing on only one domain,
this study aims to contribute a more nuanced understanding of how internal AI tools are
experienced across different roles in The Company.
2.4
Final Problem Statement
The key contribution of this thesis is identifying which occupational skills are most com┬¡
monly involved in human┬¡AI interactions in a real┬¡world digital work environment. This
research is guided by a central question:
10
==PAGE:19==
Does AI actually make work more efficient, or does it introduce new challenges
that outweigh its benefits?
Because this study takes a qualitative approach, efficiency and challenges are explored
through participantsÔÇÖ own accounts rather than through direct performance metrics. In┬¡
sights are based on interviews and open┬¡text survey responses, which were systemati┬¡
cally reviewed and grouped into themes to highlight recurring patterns in how employees
weigh the benefits and drawbacks.
While AI platforms are becoming increasingly common in workplace tools, they are of┬¡
ten adopted with the assumption that they will improve productivity and streamline tasks.
However, these assumptions are rarely examined from the perspective of everyday users
within specific work settings.
To explore this, the research is structured around this guiding sub┬¡questions:
1. The Rise of Internal AI Tools in Workplaces: Why is the AI platform being adopted
so rapidly within the Company, and what underlying assumptions drive expectations
about its impact on productivity and the employee experience?
2. Research Context: How do employees within the domain of interest interact with
the AI platform, and what are their patterns of use, perceptions of usefulness, and
skill┬¡based challenges?
3. AI in the Workplace: What specific benefits or inefficiencies arise from the platformÔÇÖs
use? Are tasks completed faster, with fewer errors or do usability issues, cognitive
overload, or lack of integration undermine its potential?
Figure 2.3: The funnel outlines the main research question and its three sub┬¡questions.
Together, these sub┬¡questions look at different angles of the main research question, mak┬¡
ing it possible to compare how employees see the benefits and drawbacks of the platform.
I aim to show how the platform influences everyday work, understand how people and
technology work together, and provide guidance for developing an AI platform that fit with
employeesÔÇÖ experiences and skills at The Company.

Methodology
In this chapter, I describe the choices and steps that shaped the research process. The
aim is to give readers a clear understanding of how the study was carried out, making it
possible to follow the reasoning behind the findings and replicate the approach. [9].
The chapter begins with the foundations of the study, including the epistemological and
paradigmatic assumptions that frame the research. It then, outlines the logic of inquiry
and the overall research strategy. Following this, the design and sampling approach are
explained, describing how participants were selected and how data was collected. Finally,
the chapter introduces the qualitative and quantitative methods used, which are later dis┬¡

3.1
Epistemology
In this thesis, I adopted a constructivist epistemological perspective, meaning that I ap┬¡
proached the research with the understanding that knowledge about sentiment towards
the AI platform is not simply discovered but actively constructed by individuals based on
their personal experiences, context, and interactions [6].
While effectiveness, defined as the degree to which intended goals are met, can in prin┬¡
ciple be measured objectively, in this study it was explored through the subjective lens
of participantsÔÇÖ experiences. Similarly, efficiency, defined as how quickly and with how
few resources tasks are completed, and satisfaction were understood not through quanti┬¡
tative performance metrics, but through employeesÔÇÖ perceptions and accounts. Different
employees perceived and interacted with the technology differently depending on their
roles, prior experience, and personal expectations. Recognizing this subjectivity allowed
me to investigate how individual perceptions, interactions, and experiences shaped the
perceived impact of the AI platform in The Company. Through interviews and surveys,
I engaged participants in a process of co┬¡constructing knowledge, aiming to capture the
diverse perspectives and meanings they attributed to their experiences with the AI plat┬¡
form.
3.2
Research Paradigm
In alignment with constructivist philosophy, this research adopts an interpretivist paradigm
to guide both the methodological choices and the interpretation of findings. As empha┬¡
sized by Crotty [6], interpretivism recognizes that reality is socially constructed through
interactions and shaped by contextual understanding. This perspective allows for a nu┬¡
anced exploration of human experiences and subjective meanings, particularly relevant
in the study of emerging technologies.
Interpretivist research acknowledges the active role of the researcher in the co┬¡construction
of knowledge. Rather than remaining detached observers, I engaged with participants,
interpreting their words through the lens of our own experiences, values, and situated
context [9]. Reflexivity played a key role throughout the process: I consistently reflected
on how my presence, background, and assumptions may have influenced both the data
collection and its interpretation.

==PAGE:21==
This approach was particularly fitting given the exploratory nature of my inquiry. I did not
aim to generalize findings universally, but rather to gain rich, in┬¡depth insights into how
employees experience, evaluate, and adapt to the AI platform in The Company.
Ultimately, adopting an interpretivist stance allowed me to immerse myself in the phe┬¡
nomenon under study, prioritizing meaning┬¡making over measurement, and embracing
the complexity of human┬¡AI interaction in a real┬¡world organizational setting.
3.3
Logic of Inquiry
To guide the design, implementation, and interpretation of my study, I adopted an abduc┬¡
tive logic of inquiry, aligned with a constructivist epistemological stance. This approach
enabled me to explore the nuanced, context┬¡dependent experiences of employees using
AI tools within their daily workflows [7].
Unlike deductive reasoning, which tests predefined hypotheses, or inductive reasoning,
which builds theory from raw observations, abductive reasoning allows for a dynamic in┬¡
terplay between theory and data. My process began with an initial interest in AIÔÇÖs role in
enhancing efficiency, which was shaped and refined as I engaged with interviews, survey
responses, and related literature. This circular approach helped me continuously revisit
and refine my assumptions in light of emerging insights. It was particularly valuable in
capturing the complex and sometimes contradictory experiences of users, ensuring my
findings reflect both the benefits and the challenges of AI adoption in The Company set┬¡
ting.
3.4
Research Strategy
The research strategy in this thesis is based on a mixed┬¡methods case study. This ap┬¡
proach supports my exploratory goals and helps me work abductively, moving between
data and theory. Because the aim is to understand the real┬¡world value and usability of
the AI platform at The Company in domain 10, combining user research with data anal┬¡
ysis, this single case study was the best fit to explore the topic in this specific setting.
As defined by Yin [24], case studies are particularly valuable when studying contempo┬¡
rary events and when the boundaries between the phenomenon and the context are not
clearly evident. In my case, the dynamics between perceived efficiency gains and newly
introduced complexities were highly context┬¡dependent, necessitating a deep and situ┬¡
ated understanding.
Instead of seeking statistical generalizability, this case study aims for analytical general┬¡
ization, offering conceptual insights that may be useful in similar organizational contexts.
Case studies are sometimes seen as limited in scope or lacking rigor, but I addressed this
by following systematic steps in the research design, data collection, and analysis [24].
Given that the AI platform under study was introduced relatively recently at The Company,
September 2023, this research also captures the specific needs of the employees in a
specific snapshot in time. As such, user perceptions may reflect both present utility and
expectations shaped by broader technological trends.
3.5
Research Design
By focusing on a single company I was able to study the phenomenon in depth and
within its natural environment. This decision also enabled me to allocate resources to┬¡
ward achieving data saturation and ensuring analytical depth, rather than distributing effort
across multiple, potentially disconnected, contexts.

Although a multiple case study might have offered comparative perspectives across de┬¡
partments or companies, constraints such as access, time, and analytical scope led me to
prioritize rich insight generation within this single case. My focus on triangulating findings
through mixed methods, quantitative surveys and qualitative interviews, ensured a robust

The research design of this exploratory case study follows the five components proposed
by Yin [24] :
1. Final Problem Statement: The study investigates whether AI improves workplace
efficiency or introduces complexity. This overarching question is unpacked into sub┬¡
questions concerning perceived usefulness, task performance, and user experience

2. Propositions: Given the limited academic focus on internal enterprise AI tools, my

integration in daily tasks.
3. Unit of Analysis: The use of the internal AI assistant by employees in The Company
in their everyday work.
4. Linking Data to Propositions: Through AI assisted thematic analysis, TF┬¡IDF┬¡based
clustering, and structured survey analysis (SUS, NPS and 5┬¡point Likert scales),

enabling a comprehensive view of how AI affects knowledge work (see Section 4.2).
5. Criteria for Interpreting Findings: Analytical generalization is pursued by comparing
and integrating qualitative and quantitative results, evaluating convergence across
data sources, and reflecting critically on internal validity (see Section 3.7.2).
The single case study allowed me to dig deep into one context while keeping the findings
relevant to The Company, which fits with the interpretivist approach of this thesis.

To deepen my understanding of the interview data, I deliberately applied two different ana┬¡

analysis offered an alternative perspective by highlighting patterns and connections that

seemed less prominent. Using these two methods in parallel was not intended to validate
one against the other, but rather to expand my analytical lens and ensure a more com┬¡

findings in human┬¡centered analysis and remain open to unexpected patterns emerging
from automated techniques.
Therefore, I have also adopted a thematic analysis approach supported by a structured
protocol for working with generative AI using the AI platform on which this research is
based on, as proposed by Goyanes et al. [10]. This method builds on the classic phases
of thematic analysis by Braun and Clarke [11], adapting them to incorporate the strengths
of Large Language Models (LLMs) while maintaining methodological rigour.
The steps of this AI┬¡assisted thematic analysis were as follows:
1. Data Familiarization: The interview transcripts were manually cleaned and anonymized,
with interviewer utterances, filler expressions, and identifiable information removed.
This ensured a consistent, clean input for AI processing.
2. Preparing the Chatbot: The cleaned transcripts were integrated into a structured
prompt design [10].
3. Defining the Analysis Process: Clear analytical goals were established before in┬¡
teraction with the AI, including identifying recurring patterns, contrasting viewpoints,
and how these relate to the main research question about AIÔÇÖs impact on efficiency
and usability.
4. Chatbot Interaction: Iterative prompts were used to extract meaningful themes, rep┬¡
resentative quotes, and relevant interpretations.

==PAGE:27==
5. Iterative Refinement: The AIÔÇÖs initial outputs were reviewed, and further refined
through successive rounds of prompting and synthesis, ensuring the themes re┬¡
mained grounded in the data and relevant to the research objectives.
6. Review and Validation: Final themes were manually cross┬¡checked against the raw
transcripts to ensure validity and to mitigate the risk of overgeneralization or hallu┬¡
cination by the AI model.
This mixed approach made it easier to identify themes while still keeping researcher con┬¡
trol. The automated tools helped speed up the coding process and highlight patterns,
which I then reviewed, interpreted, and validated within the context of the study.

Quantitative data
To complement the qualitative insights from interviews, I conducted a survey to measure
user perceptions and experiences using standardized scales. The survey incorporated
the System Usability Scale (SUS) [30], a widely recognized and reliable 10┬¡item question┬¡
naire measuring usersÔÇÖ perceptions of the platformÔÇÖs ease of use and learnability. Each
20
==PAGE:29==
SUS item was rated on a 5┬¡point scale from strongly disagree to strongly agree, and the
total score was calculated by summing these ratings and multiplying by 2.5, resulting in
scores ranging from 0 to 100. Higher SUS scores indicate greater usability, with scores
above 68 considered above average and those above 80 excellent.
Additionally, the survey included the Net Promoter Score (NPS) [31], a single┬¡item metric
assessing user advocacy through the question, How likely are you to recommend this AI
platform to others? Participants responded on an 11┬¡point scale from 0, not at all likely to
10, extremely likely. Users were categorized based on their scores into detractors (0ÔÇô6),
passives (7ÔÇô8), and promoters (9ÔÇô10). The final NPS was calculated by subtracting the
percentage of detractors from the percentage of promoters, with scores ranging from ┬¡100
to +100. Typically, an NPS above 0 is considered good, above 50 excellent, and above
70 world┬¡class.
Further quantitative measures asked participants to rate their experience with the assis┬¡
tant in terms of time savings, error reduction, trust in the outputs, and overall productivity.
These items were structured on a 5┬¡point Likert scale. To complement this, open┬¡ended
questions invited participants to compare the assistant with other digital tools they use,
describe which tasks they find it most helpful for, and suggest improvements to better
support their daily work.
The survey was created using Microsoft Forms and initially distributed via email. To in┬¡

To calculate the System Usability Scale (SUS) score, I, first, mapped qualitative survey
responses (e.g., ÔÇ£Strongly disagreeÔÇØ to ÔÇ£Strongly agreeÔÇØ) to numerical values from 1 to 5.
Then applied this mapping to the ten SUS┬¡related questions in the dataset. Following the
standard SUS scoring method, I treated odd┬¡numbered questions (1, 3, 5, 7, 9) as posi┬¡
tively worded and subtracted 1 from each corresponding response, while even┬¡numbered
questions (2, 4, 6, 8, 10) were considered negatively worded, so I subtracted each re┬¡
sponse from 5. The SUS score for each participant was computed by summing these
adjusted values and multiplying the total by 2.5. This result was stored in a new column
called SUS_score, and we printed both the mean score and a descriptive summary of the
score distribution across all respondents.

5
Discussion
In this section, I use the findings and theoretical framework to discuss the sub┬¡questions
defined in this dissertation. Each sub┬¡question corresponds to layers within the research
showcasing the direct contribution to the research domain 2.3. This will subsequently
lead to answering the main research question:
Does AI actually make work more efficient, or does it introduce new challenges that out┬¡
weigh its benefits?
First, I asked: Why is the AI platform being adopted so rapidly within the Company, and
what underlying assumptions drive expectations about its impact on productivity and the
employee experience?
As outlined in 2.1 , the rollout of the internal AI assistant was largely driven by the or┬¡
ganizationÔÇÖs strategic goal to empower all employees to use it for everyday tasks. The
adoption was rapid, with minimal resistance, in part due to its ease of access and visible
organizational support. However, as the results in show in 4.13, adoption is not always
synonymous with meaningful engagement. Many users were introduced to the tool out of
curiosity or through nudges from leadership, not necessarily because they had identified
a task┬¡specific need.
The assumption underlying this rollout is that access to AI tools will inherently lead to
increased productivity and innovation. However, as discussed in 4.4, this assumption of┬¡
ten overlooks the complexity of user contexts, particularly differences in digital fluency,
role┬¡specific workflows, and trust in AI┬¡generated outputs. This gap between organiza┬¡
tional intent and user reality reflects a broader tension identified in existing literature by
Binns [33], which warns against the dangers of techno┬¡solutionism, the belief that complex
social or organizational problems can be effectively addressed through purely technical
fixes. Binns and colleagues emphasize that such approaches often overlook the nuanced,
situated nature of real┬¡world contexts in which AI systems are deployed. When organiza┬¡
tions introduce AI tools based on abstract ideals of efficiency or innovation, without fully
considering how these systems will be interpreted, adopted, or resisted by actual users,
a disconnect emerges. This misalignment can lead to unintended consequences such
as reduced trust, suboptimal usage, or even active workarounds. Therefore, their work
underscores the importance of contextual alignment, ensuring that AI tools are not only
technically sound but also meaningfully integrated into the workflows, values, and lived
experiences of the people who use them.
In addition to internal strategic goals, the rapid adoption of AI can also be understood as
a response to broader institutional pressures. According to institutional theory explained
by DiMaggio and Powell [34], organizations often adopt emerging technologies to main┬¡
tain legitimacy within their industry, not necessarily because of proven effectiveness. This
process, known as institutional isomorphism, suggests that companies may imitate per┬¡
ceived best practices or follow external trends to appear innovative and competitive. In
this light, the introduction of AI tools may reflect a desire to align with industry norms and
expectations, even when their value and role within the organization are still unclear.
In summary AI tools are being adopted so quickly within The Company due to strong insti┬¡
tutional assumptions that AI inherently leads to greater productivity, enhanced decision┬¡
making, and reduced workload across roles. However, this research highlights that such
47
==PAGE:56==
assumptions oversimplify the real┬¡world experience of employees. While some users in┬¡
deed report efficiency gains, particularly for repetitive or communicative tasks, others face
usability issues, contextual mismatches, or a lack of integration with existing workflows.
The enthusiasm for adoption appears to be based on the belief that AI tools are universally
beneficial and can be deployed without significant onboarding, adaptation, or long┬¡term
support. Yet, my findings suggest that the impact of AI is highly dependent on role, ex┬¡
perience level, and the quality of internal implementation. Ultimately, while AI tools hold
promise, their success depends less on their technical capabilities alone and more on
how well they align with organizational needs, workflows, and employee expectations.
Then also asked: How do employees within the domain of interest interact with the AI
platform, and what are their patterns of use, perceptions of usefulness, and skill┬¡based
challenges?
In 2.1.1 of my thesis I presents three user personas within the company context: Explor┬¡
ers, Builders, and Operators, based on definition given by the company itself. Explorers
are early┬¡stage users who often rely on templates and onboarding guidance. Builders,
more confident and technically fluent, use the assistant for ideation and prototyping. Op┬¡
erators, the most advanced group, focus on strategic oversight and require trust, trace┬¡
ability, and integration into secure systems.
Usage patterns differ significantly across these personas. As described in 4.4, most users
engaged with the assistant intermittently and for narrowly scoped tasks, such as rephras┬¡
ing emails, debugging code, or summarizing documents. However, even among more
active users, low trust in output accuracy remained a consistent theme. Also, even when
the assistant was helpful, it often lacked reliability, memory, or contextual awareness,
leading to workarounds or parallel use of external tools like ChatGPT or Copilot.
These findings echo and reinforce insights from the literature on trust calibration and men┬¡
tal models in humanÔÇôAI interaction [35]. Effective interaction with AI systems depends not
only on technical performance but also on usersÔÇÖ ability to develop accurate mental mod┬¡
els, internal representations of how the system works, what it can and cannot do, and
how it is likely to behave in different situations. When systems fail to provide consistent
or transparent behavior, especially in the absence of meaningful feedback mechanisms,
users struggle to adjust their trust appropriately, either over┬¡trusting the system and rely┬¡
ing on it too much, or under┬¡trusting it and avoiding its use altogether. In this case, the
assistantÔÇÖs limited contextual retention, its inability to remember previous queries or user
goals, and lack of responsive feedback loops led to moments of friction and unmet ex┬¡
pectations. This was particularly evident among more advanced users, who anticipated
a higher degree of contextual awareness and adaptive behavior from enterprise┬¡grade
AI tools. As one participant noted, I recommend the company prioritize internal context
solutions over replicating external tools. This statement highlights the practical implica┬¡
tions of broken trust calibration: rather than valuing superficial parity with popular external
assistants, users called for deeper contextual integration, signaling a desire for tools that
align with their specific workflows and organizational knowledge rather than generic AI
features.
In summary, employees within the domain of interest interact with the AI platform in highly
varied ways, reflecting differences in role, seniority, and digital fluency. The data reveals
three dominant user patterns: some rely on the assistant for communication tasks (e.g.,
rewriting, summarizing), others engage with it as a coding partner for debugging or gen┬¡
erating code, while a third group, typically less frequent users, approach it sporadically
for inspiration or clarification. Perceptions of usefulness are similarly diverse: while many
48
==PAGE:57==
users report time┬¡saving benefits and appreciate the AIÔÇÖs ability to reduce reliance on col┬¡
leagues, others express frustration with inconsistencies in output, slow response times,
and limited integration with internal resources. Skill┬¡based challenges also emerge, par┬¡
ticularly among less experienced users (e.g., students), who may lack the confidence or
contextual understanding to frame effective prompts or evaluate AI┬¡generated outputs.
Conversely, senior users are more likely to adapt the assistant strategically to their work┬¡
flows, using it as a trusted co┬¡pilot. These differences suggest that a one┬¡size┬¡fits┬¡all
deployment does not suffice, tailored support and onboarding are essential to maximize
value across the employee spectrum.
Finally, I considered: What specific benefits or inefficiencies arise from the platformÔÇÖs use?
Are tasks completed faster, with fewer errors or do usability issues, cognitive overload, or
lack of integration undermine its potential?
The AI assistant does help users complete certain tasks faster, particularly content gener┬¡
ation and debugging, by reducing the time spent searching for information or drafting initial
versions. However, these benefits are unevenly distributed. Many users reported usabil┬¡
ity issues like delay, formatting problems, and poor memory between prompts. These
inefficiencies not only slow down work but also require users to invest cognitive effort in
rephrasing or reconstructing queries. Moreover, the platformÔÇÖs inability to access inter┬¡
nal documentation or provide consistent context across sessions further limits its utility.
Thus, while there are clear productivity gains for some use cases, they are frequently
undermined by friction in the user experience and system limitations.
As shown in the SUS and NPS analysis 4.2.2, the platform achieved a mean SUS score of
68.01, just above the acceptability threshold, yet user feedback was highly polarized. For
example, students reported a neutral NPS of 0, indicating skepticism or a lack of perceived
value, whereas engineers, despite slightly lower SUS scores, reported the highest NPS,
suggesting they found the assistant effective for their specific workflows.
The section 4.2.1 and the open┬¡ended qualitative responses in A.4 reveal that users ap┬¡
preciated the assistantÔÇÖs ability to help with writing, debugging, and brainstorming, but
at the same time several inefficiencies were cited. These include slow response times,
unreliable formatting, and poor handling of internal data or attachments. Such usability
issues often interrupted flow states and required users to manually reformat or re┬¡input
context.
Cognitive overload emerged most acutely among Builders and Operators, user groups
who typically interact with complex workflows and rely on quick, seamless access to in┬¡
terconnected information. These users entered the system with high expectations for re┬¡
sponsiveness and integration, anticipating that the assistant would not only retrieve data
but intelligently interface with commonly used platforms. However, the assistantÔÇÖs siloed
interface, operating in isolation rather than embedded within existing digital ecosystems,
led to fragmentation and increased cognitive burden. Users often had to manually switch
contexts, rephrase queries, or duplicate effort to bridge gaps between tools, diminishing
the assistantÔÇÖs perceived value.
These findings reflect what Moore [12] describes as the pacing problem in AI adoption, a
structural mismatch where technological innovation advances more rapidly than the de┬¡
velopment of the institutional, infrastructural, or cognitive frameworks needed to support
its effective use. In this case, while the assistant may offer technically advanced fea┬¡
tures, the surrounding ecosystem (e.g., lack of deep integration, limited interoperability,

Limitations
While this study provides meaningful insights into the adoption and impact of an internal
AI assistant, several limitations must be acknowledged to contextualize the findings and
guide future research.
Importantly, these findings must be interpreted within the specific context of the research
2.1. This study was conducted in a technology┬¡focused domain, where digital fluency
among employees may be higher than average. The AI platform has been accessible for
over a year, meaning most participants were not encountering it for the first time. How┬¡
ever, results may not generalize to other domains, departments, or less tech┬¡savvy envi┬¡
ronments. Also, this research captures a specific moment in time during the early adoption
phase of the AI assistant. As discussed in section 2.1, the platform is still evolving, with
ongoing updates and feature rollouts. Consequently, user perceptions and interaction
patterns shown in sections 4.1 and 4.2 may shift over time as the technology matures or
as employees gain experience. A longitudinal study could have provided a richer under┬¡
standing of how efficiency, trust, and adoption evolve in response to organizational and
technological changes.
Furthermore, out of 61 survey responses, only 54 were complete enough for SUS anal┬¡
ysis, and qualitative feedback came from a subset of participants who self┬¡selected into
interviews 4.1.1. This may have introduced self┬¡selection bias, with responses skewed
toward individuals more engaged with or opinionated about the tool. Additionally, the sam┬¡
ple was uneven across roles and seniority levels since I received more responses from
junior profiles, which may not fully represent organizational diversity in AI usage patterns.
While interviews offered rich, contextual insights, their limited number restricted the explo┬¡
ration of role┬¡specific needs beyond the three identified personas. As section 4.2.1 notes,
Builders and Operators were particularly under┬¡represented in one┬¡on┬¡one sessions, po┬¡
tentially overlooking distinct friction points relevant to advanced users.
The thematic analysis 4.2.1 employed AI┬¡based support to cluster and synthesize qual┬¡
itative data. While this approach increased efficiency and traceability, it may have con┬¡
strained the depth of interpretation. AI┬¡assisted coding can miss subtle cues, latent emo┬¡

introduces a potential interpretative bias, especially when mapping user sentiment or com┬¡
plex perceptions of trust.
Another limitation is the lack of objective usage or performance metrics from system logs,
meaning that efficiency and productivity gains are based on self┬¡reported data and may
be influenced by personal bias or recall errors. The combination of surveys and interviews

Conclusion
The objective of this dissertation was to investigate and answer the research question:
ÔÇ£Does AI actually make work more efficient, or does it introduce new challenges that out┬¡
weigh its benefits?ÔÇØ To address this question, I examined the use, and perceived impact of
an internal AI platform within a specific domain from The Company. By combining survey
data, interviews, and AI┬¡assisted thematic analysis, this research provided a multi┬¡layered
understanding of how employees interact with the AI platform, the benefits they experi┬¡
ence, and the inefficiencies that may hinder their productivity.
Our findings suggest that while the AI platform hold considerable promise for improving
workplace efficiency, particularly for tasks such as drafting text, debugging code, and
brainstorming ideas, this potential is not fully realized in practice. Adoption of the tool was
rapid, driven largely by organizational enthusiasm and top┬¡down encouragement. How┬¡
ever, assumptions that deployment alone would increase productivity were only partially
supported. Many employees reported inconsistent usability, slow response times, lack
of memory across prompts, and poor integration with internal data sources as shown in
section 4. These friction points often offset the expected efficiency gains and, in some
cases, discouraged frequent use of the platform.
To address my research question, I extend the conclusion by offering an outline of the
answers to the individual sub┬¡questions already addressed in section 5.
In the first sub┬¡question, we asked: Why is the AI platform being adopted so rapidly within
the Company, and what underlying assumptions drive expectations about its impact on
productivity and the employee experience? The findings in Section 4.1 indicate that adop┬¡
tion was primarily driven by top┬¡down initiative and organizational enthusiasm, with AI
positioned as an inherently productivity┬¡enhancing tool. Many users engaged with the
assistant due to leadership encouragement or curiosity rather than a clear, self┬¡identified
need. This demonstrates a misalignment between institutional assumptions of efficiency
gains and employeesÔÇÖ real┬¡world experiences, where limited onboarding, poor contextual
integration, and varying digital fluency prevented uniform benefits. These findings echo
literature on techno┬¡solutionism [33], showing that technological deployment alone rarely
guarantees improved performance if contextual barriers are left unaddressed.
In addressing the second question: How do employees interact with the AI platform, and
what are their patterns of use, perceptions of usefulness, and skill┬¡based challenges?
Section 2.1.1 illustrates that interaction patterns varied significantly across the three iden┬¡
tified personas: Explorers, Builders, and Operators. Explorers needed structured guid┬¡
ance and templates to engage effectively. Builders used the assistant for coding and
ideation but demanded speed and better integration with existing tools. Operators val┬¡
ued reliability, trust, and compliance features, which were only partially delivered. Across
groups, inconsistent accuracy, lack of prompt memory, and poor handling of context led
to frequent double┬¡checking and parallel use of external tools like ChatGPT or Copilot.
These findings align with research on trust calibration in AI, Gunning [35] emphasize that
mental models and confidence strongly affect adoption and value realization.
For the third question: What specific benefits or inefficiencies arise from the platformÔÇÖs
use? Are tasks completed faster, with fewer errors, or do usability issues, cognitive over┬¡
load, or lack of integration undermine its potential? As shown in Sections 4.2.2 and 4.2.1,
from interviews employee perceive that the assistant offered measurable time savings
54
==PAGE:63==
for some tasks, particularly repetitive communication and debugging. Yet many users
reported friction that negated these benefits, including slow response times, clunky for┬¡
matting, cognitive overload from repetitive rephrasing, and lack of access to internal doc┬¡
umentation. The gap between expected and actual efficiency highlights a pacing problem
[12], where organizational processes, infrastructure, and support mechanisms have not
evolved quickly enough to match the toolÔÇÖs introduction, leading to frustration and incon┬¡
sistent value across teams.
Ultimately, these three sub┬¡questions answer the main research question: Does AI make
work more efficient, or does it introduce new challenges that outweigh its benefits? The
evidence suggests that AI can enhance efficiency in specific, well┬¡defined use cases, but
this effect is highly dependent on user experience, organizational context, and system
design. Where technical limitations, poor integration, or skill gaps exist, AI introduces
new challenges that undermine its potential gains. Thus, deployment alone is insufficient═¥
efficiency benefits depend on alignment between tool capabilities, workflows, and human
factors.
Lastly, when thinking about future research in Section 5 relevance and the limitations
of this study in Section 5, this dissertation should be seen as an exploratory step to┬¡
wards understanding workplace AI adoption. The study is bounded by its single orga┬¡
nizational context, self┬¡selected participants, and AI┬¡assisted thematic analysis, which
may have missed subtle nuances. Future research should conduct longitudinal studies
to capture how adoption and efficiency evolve over time, extend analysis to diverse orga┬¡
nizational environments, and develop structured ROI frameworks that combine technical
performance with human┬¡centered outcomes like trust, cognitive load, and employee sat┬¡
isfaction. These next steps are crucial for building a more complete understanding of how
AI tools can truly enhance, rather than complicate, modern work.
From a theoretical perspective, this study contributes to understanding AI adoption in
workplace settings by extending the discussion beyond adoption rates to actual effective┬¡
ness and perceived value. The results challenge techno┬¡solutionist assumptions that AI
inherently drives efficiency and instead suggest that context, usability, and organizational
readiness are critical mediators of value creation. The persona┬¡based framework intro┬¡
duced here provides a novel lens to understand variability in adoption and to guide design
strategies that support different user journeys. This echoes calls from human┬¡centered
AI research to design systems that are understandable, trustworthy, and adaptive to user
needs [40].
Practically, the study highlights that organizations cannot assume that introducing AI tools
will automatically lead to efficiency gains. Effective adoption requires:
1. Robust onboarding and support mechanisms to help new users (Explorers) engage
meaningfully with the tool.
2. Technical improvements in speed, reliability, memory retention, and internal system
integration to support advanced users (Builders and Operators).
3. Transparency and governance features to foster trust and ensure responsible use
at scale.
4. Feedback loops and continuous iteration to improve usability and output quality over
time.
These measures are essential to bridge the gap between AIÔÇÖs potential and its realized
benefits in everyday work.

Ultimately, this dissertation concludes that AI can improve workplace efficiency, but its
benefits are neither guaranteed nor evenly distributed. The internal AI assistant studied
here delivered tangible value in specific use cases but introduced friction, inconsistencies,
and unmet expectations in others. Without improved usability, better integration, and tai┬¡
lored support for diverse users, AI risks becoming a net┬¡neutral or even counterproductive
tool despite its promise. This research contributes to a more nuanced understanding of
AI adoption in organizations, emphasizing that true efficiency gains require not just tech┬¡
nological availability, but thoughtful design, continuous alignment with user needs, and
organizational adaptation to harness AIÔÇÖs potential effectively.
In the end, AI is not a silver bullet for workplace productivity, it is a mirror reflecting the
strengths and gaps of our existing workflows. For AI to truly make work more efficient,
organizations must invest as much in thoughtful integration and user support as they do
in the technology itself. Only then can the promise of AI move from potential to practice.
