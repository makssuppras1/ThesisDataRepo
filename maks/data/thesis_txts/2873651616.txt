Abstract
The pharmaceutical industry plays a critical role in global health but faces increasing scrutiny
for its environmental footprint, particularly regarding raw material inefficiencies and waste
generation in biomanufacturing. In upstream production, overfeeding and conservative input
planning in large-scale bioreactors leads to substantial raw material waste, elevated oper-
ational costs, and avoidable environmental burdens. Addressing these challenges requires
analytical approaches capable of capturing complex process flows while accounting for vari-
ability and uncertainty in demand forecasts.
This study addresses this problem within a Contract Development and Manufacturing Or-
ganization (CDMO), Fujifilm Biotechnologies, using a 20,000-liter bioreactor campaign as a
case study. A Material Flow Analysis (MFA) model was developed to quantify nutrient and
glucose feed usage, identify inefficiencies, and evaluate the impact of alternative operational
strategies. Real-world production data were integrated into the STAN software environment,
combined with statistical demand distributions, to simulate multiple scenarios varying feed
tank safety factors and forecast confidence levels.
The proposed solution addresses this problem and reduces the volume initially prepared into
the feed tank by approximately 5% for nutrient feed and 60% for glucose feed. These volume
reductions translate into waste reductions of around 83% for nutrient feed and 91% for gluc-
ose feed, alongside CO2 footprint reductions of 5.4% and 59.1% respectively. Economically,
the improvements correspond to significant raw material cost savings, amounting to mil-
lions of Danish Kroner annually. The findings demonstrate the effectiveness of combining
high-confidence forecasting with moderate safety margins to optimize resource use, minim-
ize waste, and maintain operational reliability in pharmaceutical bioprocessing. Beyond the
case study, this work contributes a replicable, data-driven MFA framework applicable to
other biopharmaceutical production settings, supporting industry-wide sustainability and
efficiency goals.

Introduction
The pharmaceutical industry plays a vital role in global health by researching, developing,
and manufacturing essential medicines that treat and prevent a wide range of diseases. As
a fundamental pillar of modern healthcare systems, it contributes significantly to improving
quality of life and life expectancy. However, this critical sector also faces increasing scrutiny
for its environmental footprint—particularly regarding inefficient raw material usage and the
generation of excessive waste during production processes [1, 2].
Despite producing lower volumes of toxic waste than other chemical sectors, the pharma-
ceutical industry generates an estimated 18 million tons of waste annually—up to 125 times
the mass of finished drug products [2]. For every kilogram of pharmaceutical product manu-
factured, significantly larger quantities of raw materials—including solvents, excipients, and
active pharmaceutical ingredients—are discarded. These losses stem from overproduction,
quality buffer requirements, limited shelf lives, and planning inefficiencies. This waste con-
tributes to both environmental and economic burdens, while also straining broader systems
such as energy consumption, food and water safety, and the depletion of non-renewable
feedstocks. Organic chemicals—many of which are petroleum-derived—form the basis of
approximately 90% of all pharmaceutical products [3, 4].
These sustainability challenges are becoming increasingly urgent as the pharmaceutical in-
dustry continues to expand. Globally, the market has grown from USD 390 billion in 2001
to USD 1.6 trillion by the end of 2023, driven by macroeconomic factors such as aging pop-
ulations, rising prevalence of chronic diseases, and growing access to healthcare in emerging
economies [5]. This anticipated growth brings with it a heightened responsibility to manage
resources more efficiently and minimize environmental impact [6].
Sustainability is therefore not only an environmental imperative but also a strategic priority.
It contributes to long-term operational viability, regulatory compliance, and public trust.
However, academic research into sustainability within the pharmaceutical sector remains
limited, creating a gap in evidence-based approaches to improving resource use and redu-
cing waste. This is especially important given the industry’s complex position—balancing
profitability with its obligation to ensure equitable drug access and patient well-being. As
one of the most tightly regulated sectors in the EU and globally, the industry faces increas-
ing pressure to align its practices with environmental, social, and governance (ESG) goals [7].
In addition to material waste, the pharmaceutical sector is also a notable contributor to
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The industry’s reliance on carbon-intensive chemicals and
energy-demanding production, storage, transportation, and retail operations further ampli-
fies its environmental impact. As global life expectancy rises and access to pharmaceuticals
expands, these challenges are expected to intensify. Without proactive measures, the sector’s
contribution to climate change and resource depletion will likely worsen [3]. Improving raw
material efficiency is thus not merely a cost-saving tactic-it is a crucial step toward sustain-
able pharmaceutical manufacturing. Reducing waste and optimizing production processes
can support regulatory goals, minimize environmental impacts, and enhance the industry’s
long-term resilience in an increasingly sustainability-conscious global landscape [3].

1.1
Problem Statement
2
While sustainability and cleaner production have become increasingly prominent topics in
industrial research, the pharmaceutical sector remains underrepresented in this area of re-
search.
Several studies, such as those by Veleva and Cue (2017) [8] and Belkhir and
Elmeligi (2019) [9], highlight the environmental implications of pharmaceutical produc-
tion, particularly regarding carbon emissions and waste generation. Despite the sector’s
substantial environmental footprint—reportedly greater than that of the automotive in-
dustry—pharmaceutical manufacturing has attracted only limited academic attention. A
key reason for this is the pervasive lack of transparency; pharmaceutical companies often
treat production data as confidential, making it difficult for researchers to obtain detailed
insights into material use and waste patterns [10]. This lack of visibility creates a signific-
ant gap in the literature, especially on operational-level inefficiencies such as the systematic
overestimation of raw material requirements. Overestimation contributes directly to unne-
cessary waste, especially in biologics manufacturing, where materials are costly, sensitive,
and subject to strict shelf-life and quality constraints [7].
Surprisingly, waste management itself remains a largely neglected topic in pharmaceutical
sustainability research. While it is a major focus in other industrial sectors, only a few
studies address it in the pharmaceutical context [7] [3]. The limited literature that does
exist tends to focus on waste at the user level—such as the disposal of expired drugs and
packaging—or broader water usage concerns. However, at the production level, the manage-
ment of raw material waste, including that caused by excessive material provisioning, has
received insufficient attention. Although some studies highlight supply chain sustainability
(Singh et al., 2016 [11]; Kumar et al., 2019 [12]) and the need to assess environmental impact
across all stages of the product lifecycle, few provide detailed or quantitative insights into
how operational decisions contribute to overuse and waste. Even studies that explore lean or
green manufacturing in pharmaceutical settings tend to focus on general process efficiency or
conceptual frameworks, very few examine material estimation or quantify operational waste
using empirical production data.
This highlights a persistent gap in understanding how
internal provisioning decisions directly contribute to inefficiency and environmental burden
[13].
This thesis directly addresses the identified gap by examining the issue of raw material over-
estimation in pharmaceutical manufacturing using empirical production data. By focusing
on a specific operational practice—material provisioning—within a real-world production
environment, the study moves beyond conceptual models to provide data-driven insights
into the scale and consequences of overestimation. The research quantifies the extent of
excess material use, analyzes its variability across production cycles, and explores its envir-
onmental and economic implications. In doing so, the thesis contributes to the limited body
of empirical work in pharmaceutical sustainability and supports the development of more
transparent, efficient, and environmentally responsible manufacturing practices.
1.1
Problem Statement
Fujifilm Biotechnologies, a pharmaceutical CDMO (Contract Development and Manufactur-
ing Organization), faces recurring challenges related to inefficient raw material utilization
and excessive waste generation during manufacturing campaigns. A manufacturing cam-
paign refers to a series of consecutive production batches executed under the same process

Research questions and objectives
3
conditions and using the same raw materials, typically aimed at producing a specific biologic
drug substance. Despite operating in a highly regulated and quality-controlled environment,
material losses frequently occur due to factors such as overproduction, suboptimal planning,
and operational inefficiencies. These issues lead to financial strain and increased environ-
mental burden, highlighting the urgent need for more effective and sustainable resource
management strategies.
This thesis assesses raw material inefficiencies in a specific pharmaceutical campaign at Fuji-
film Biotechnologies, a Contract Development and Manufacturing Organization (CDMO),
using a Material Flow Analysis (MFA) approach to identify waste patterns and evaluate
opportunities for improved resource use. By mapping material flows and identifying ineffi-
ciencies, the study aims to propose actionable strategies to reduce waste, minimize environ-
mental impact, and improve overall operational sustainability.
Given the complexity of pharmaceutical production at Fujifilm Biotechnologies—including
both upstream and downstream processes—it is essential to clearly define the scope of this
project. This study focuses on a single 20,000-liter production bioreactor located at the
company’s manufacturing facility in Hillerød, Denmark, which plays a central role in the up-
stream processing of a monoclonal antibody used in therapeutic treatment. The bioreactor
was chosen as the unit of analysis because it represents the core step of upstream biologics
manufacturing and is among the most resource-intensive stages in the process. It consumes
large volumes of raw materials such as cell culture media and several feed solutions. In-
efficiencies at this stage—such as overfeeding, failed batches, or underutilized inputs—can
lead to significant material waste, environmental burden, and financial loss. The scope is
limited to one biopharmaceutical production campaign, focusing specifically on the use and
disposal of two key inputs: glucose and nutrient solution, selected due to their high con-
sumption volumes, cost relevance, and environmental impact. Furthermore, the bioreactor
process is well-documented and supported by reliable batch-level data, making it a suitable
candidate for Material Flow Analysis (MFA) to identify inefficiencies and evaluate potential
improvements.
1.2
Research questions and objectives
The main research question of this thesis is as follows:
How can effective waste reduction scenarios be designed and assessed to enhance raw material
efficiency in biologics manufacturing within the pharmaceutical industry?
To answer this question, the following sub-questions will be addressed:
• How can a Material Flow Analysis (MFA) model be designed and applied to support
the development of waste reduction strategies?
• What data, assumptions, and data collection approaches are required to construct a
robust (MFA) model for pharmaceutical campaigns, and how can data limitations be
effectively managed?
• How can hypothetical or optimized process scenarios be formulated to reduce raw
material waste in a CDMO setting?

1.3
Structure of the Thesis
This thesis is structured to progressively develop an understanding of pharmaceutical manu-
facturing practices, identify the root causes of raw material inefficiencies, and explore the ap-
plication of Material Flow Analysis (MFA) as a strategic tool for waste reduction—particularly
within Contract Development and Manufacturing Organizations (CDMOs).
Chapter 1 introduces the background and motivation of the study. It outlines the problem

2.1
Overview of Pharmaceutical manufacturing
The present section provides an overview of pharmaceutical manufacturing, outlining its key
stages and operational modes to establish the necessary context for defining the thesis scope
and addressing the research questions.
Pharmaceutical manufacturing is the process of producing compounds and medicines inten-
ded for human or veterinary use, following a series of well-defined stages to ensure safety,
efficacy, and consistency in production[14]. The nature of the therapeutic product plays a
crucial role in shaping both the manufacturing and distribution processes. Broadly, pharma-
ceutical products are classified into two main categories: small molecules and biologics. Small
molecules, such as paracetamol or acetylsalicylic acid, are chemically synthesized through
established, large-scale chemical processes. In contrast, biologics—which are the focus of
this study—include products such as monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) and vaccines. These
are derived from living organisms and require complex, cell-based production systems along
with intricate downstream processing. Biologics are typically produced in batch mode to
maintain control over biological variability and ensure consistent product quality[15].
Pharmaceutical manufacturing is generally divided into two main stages. The first is primary
manufacturing, which involves the production of the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API),
also referred to as the drug substance. The second stage, secondary manufacturing, focuses
on converting the API into a final drug product suitable for administration—such as tablets,
capsules, or injectables. These steps vary depending on the drug type [15].
2.1.1
Primary Pharmaceutical Manufacturing
For small molecules, primary manufacturing typically involves chemical synthesis followed
by purification steps.
Secondary manufacturing begins with mixing the API with other
components known as excipients, non-active substances that aid in stability, absorption, or
taste, followed by unit operations such as granulation, compression, coating, and packaging.
In contrast, biologic manufacturing involves the production of the API—or components of

the final drug product—using living organisms. For example, as it is the case of this study,
monoclonal antibodies are produced in mammalian cell cultures within bioreactors, a pro-
cess known as upstream processing (USP). This is followed by downstream processing (DSP),
which includes separation and purification techniques like filtration and chromatography to
remove impurities and ensure product quality [15].
Most primary pharmaceutical processes use batch or semi-batch operations, which are well-
suited for low-volume, high-value products. These methods offer flexibility, allowing manu-
facturers to switch between different products within the same facility in response to client
or market demand. Production is typically organized in campaigns, during which a single
product is manufactured for a specified period—ranging from a few days to several months.
At the end of each campaign, equipment undergoes thorough cleaning before beginning
production of a new product or intermediate using different raw materials and processing
conditions [14] [16].
2.1.2
Upstream and Downstream Processing
In the context of biologics manufacturing, which is a subset of primary pharmaceutical manu-
facturing, production is broadly divided into two critical phases: upstream processing (USP)
and downstream processing (DSP) [17]. Upstream processing encompasses all steps involved
in the growth of the biological system that will produce the desired product—commonly
recombinant proteins such as monoclonal antibodies. This typically involves [17]:
• Media preparation, where nutrient-rich media is formulated and sterilized to support
cell growth
• Inoculum development, which scales up the initial cell culture across vessels while
maintaining viability
• Fermentation and seed culture, which occurs in bioreactors where cells are grown
under controlled conditions to maximize product formation
• Harvesting, in which the cultured product is separated from cells via filtration or
centrifugation.
Following this, downstream processing focuses on isolating and purifying the biologic product
to meet stringent regulatory quality standards [17].The output of the purification becomes
the input to secondary manufacturing, where the drug is formulated and packaged for pa-
tient use.

trated in Figure 1, which presents a simplified representative bioprocess flow diagram high-

can occur at multiple points across both upstream and downstream processing—whether
through inefficient feeding strategies, overproduction, failed batches, contaminations or dis-

phase, specifically the production bioreactor, where resource inefficiencies can lead to signi-
ficant environmental and financial burdens[18] [19] [20].

2.1.3
Secondary pharmaceutical manufacturing
Secondary manufacturing, or drug product manufacturing, involves combining the active
pharmaceutical ingredient (API) with excipients to produce the final medication. This in-
cludes formulation development, dosage form creation (e.g., tablets, injectables), and pack-
aging.Throughout the process, rigorous quality control and assurance measures are imple-
mented to ensure each batch meets required regulatory standards for safety, efficacy, and
product consistency. Manufacturing requirements vary by drug type, with biologics posing
greater challenges due to their complexity and sensitivity, requiring stringent environmental
controls and quality systems [15] [16].
2.1.4
Batch processing
Batch processing has long been the standard in pharmaceutical manufacturing. It involves
a series of steps where starting materials are charged into a reaction vessel, processed under
predefined conditions, and then discharged for downstream operations. Each batch follows
validated process recipes with set operating variables to ensure high reproducibility and
consistent product quality [14].
While this approach offers flexibility and relatively low
setup costs, it also leads to longer lead times and poses risks related to contamination,
downtime, and supply chain continuity. As a result, there is growing interest in continuous
manufacturing as a more efficient alternative [22].
2.1.5
Batch versus Continuous Manufacturing
Continuous manufacturing (CM) is an advanced pharmaceutical production method where
ingredients are transformed into final products through uninterrupted, continuous processes.
Compared to traditional batch processing, CM offers higher efficiency, reduced material
waste, lower variability, and a smaller equipment footprint due to its high degree of auto-
mation [22, 16, 15]. This continuous flow enables greater scalability and faster response
to market demand while minimizing human error.
However, its adoption—especially in
biologics manufacturing—remains limited due to the complexity of biologic products and
challenges in integrating upstream and downstream operations. Maintaining product qual-
ity requires advanced monitoring to prevent costly disruptions. Ongoing research focuses on

2.2
Organizational Landscape in the Pharmaceutical Industry
The pharmaceutical industry comprises a wide range of organizations involved in drug devel-
opment, manufacturing, and distribution. Among these, Contract Development and Manu-
facturing Organizations (CDMOs) have emerged as key players, offering specialized capabilit-
ies and flexible capacity to companies seeking to outsource parts of the product lifecycle [23].
This section provides an overview of some main categories of pharmaceutical manufacturers,
brand-name companies, generic producers, and CDMOs,followed by a more detailed explor-
ation of the strategic importance and operational challenges of CDMOs.
The emphasis
on CDMOs reflects their central role in the case study analyzed in this thesis, where the
organization under investigation operates as a CDMO within the biopharmaceutical sector.
2.2.1
Pharmaceutical manufacturers
The pharmaceutical supply chain is a complex and highly regulated system involving nu-
merous stakeholders responsible for bringing a medication from concept to patient. Key
participants include raw material suppliers, drug manufacturers, regulatory agencies, dis-
tributors, pharmacies, and healthcare providers, among others. Each plays a critical role in
ensuring the safety, efficacy, and availability of pharmaceutical products [24].
At the core of this network are pharmaceutical manufacturers, whose primary role is to
transform raw materials and active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) into finished medic-
ations that can improve and even save lives. Pharmaceutical manufacturers can be broadly
categorized into groups based on their operational models and ownership of the product
lifecycle [25] [24]:
• Brand name pharmaceutical companies: These are the stakeholders that own the
whole process.These companies typically oversee the entire drug development life cycle,
including discovery, preclinical and clinical trials, regulatory approval, manufacturing,
and commercialization. They often maintain in-house facilities for both research and
production. Additionally, generic pharmaceutical companies are the generic manufac-
turers, who produce out-of-patent and over-the-counter products [24].
• Contract Development and Manufacturing Organizations (CDMOs): CD-
MOs are third-party service providers that specialize in the development and manu-
facturing of pharmaceutical products on behalf of other companies. They offer flexible
and scalable production capabilities, allowing sponsor companies to outsource part or
all of their manufacturing activities. CDMOs support formulation development, clin-
ical trial material production, commercial-scale manufacturing, and even packaging
and distribution in some cases [24].

Pharmaceutical Outsourcing and the Strategic Role of CDMOs
Outsourcing has emerged as a critical strategic approach within the pharmaceutical in-
dustry, driven by the need to reduce costs, increase flexibility, and accelerate development

timelines. This model involves delegating specific processes—or entire stages of the product
lifecycle—to external partners with the necessary technological capabilities, regulatory com-

focus internal resources on core competencies such as research, innovation, and commercializ-

2.3
Resource Efficiency Challenges in Biologics Manufacturing
Pharmaceutical manufacturing faces significant challenges in managing resource efficiency
due to the complexity of its processes and the strict regulatory environments under which
it operates. Pharmaceutical plants generate diverse and complex waste streams, originat-
ing from core production activities, cleaning and maintenance, and general housekeeping.
While some practices, such as cleaning protocols, are standardized, the proprietary nature
of pharmaceutical processes results in large variability in waste types and volumes across
sites and campaigns. Common waste includes spent fermentation broths, solvents, process
liquors, equipment wash waters, off-spec products, and raw material spills. A further chal-
lenge lies in the industry’s largely non-circular nature, where most waste cannot be reused or
repurposed and instead requires energy-intensive and often hazardous disposal processes [30].
The following subsections examine the environmental and operational inefficiencies that con-
tribute to this resource burden, with particular focus on biologics manufacturing.
They
explore challenges related to water, energy, and material usage, and highlight upstream
processing,especially feed material handling,as a critical area for sustainability-driven in-
novation. Lean Manufacturing principles are also introduced as a framework for improving
efficiency and reducing waste in these contexts.
2.3.1
Environmental and Operational Inefficiencies in Biologics Manufacturing
Environmental sustainability has become an increasingly important focus in the pharmaceut-
ical industry. Historically, environmental concerns have primarily centered on small-molecule
drug manufacturing, due to its reliance on hazardous organic solvents, exotic reagents, and
heavy metal catalysts—factors that contribute significantly to environmental and occupa-
tional health risks [19]. On the other hand, the production of large-molecule therapeutics
(biologics) has traditionally been perceived as less environmentally harmful. This perception
arises from the predominance of aqueous-based processes involving food-grade nutrients and
the minimal use of flammable or toxic solvents. However, as biologics manufacturing has
matured and scaled, this assumption has been increasingly questioned [19].
Although biologics manufacturing generally involves fewer hazardous reagents than small-
molecule synthesis, it is markedly more resource-intensive, particularly in terms of water con-
sumption, energy demand, and solid waste generation. Process Mass Intensity (PMI)—defined
as the total mass of raw materials used per kilogram of product—serves as a key sustainab-
ility metric in evaluating these impacts. Notably, PMI calculations typically exclude water
used for cleaning and facility operations, meaning actual resource use may be even higher
than reported values [31].
Biologics production, especially for monoclonal antibodies (mAbs), consistently shows PMI
values that are 10 to 100 times greater than those observed in small-molecule API processes.
While the latter typically range from 100 to 200 kg of input per kg of product [32], biologics
manufacturing can require an average of 7,700 kg of materials per kg of mAb [33, 34].A bench-
marking study across multiple biopharma facilities reported PMI values for biologics ranging
between 2,000 and 25,000 kg/kg product [31]. These elevated figures are largely driven by
extensive water use in upstream cell culture and downstream chromatography operations.
Taken together, these findings highlight the urgent need to reassess the environmental impact

of biologics manufacturing and to prioritize sustainability-focused innovations, particularly
in high-burden areas such as media preparation, feed strategy, and purification.[32]
Water usage is by far the most significant contributor to PMI in biologics manufactur-
ing.It is fundamental across all stages of biologics production—not just in upstream and
downstream operations, but also in cleaning, sterilization, cooling, and facility maintenance.
Biomanufacturing facilities often rely on highly purified water types, such as Water for In-
jection (WFI), which require energy-intensive production. Despite this, much of the water
is discharged as waste without reuse or recovery, raising major sustainability concerns, es-
pecially in water-scarce regions. The E factor, known as the ratio of the mass of waste per
mass of product, for process water has emerged as a critical environmental index, especially
in biomanufacturing, where water is used in virtually every step of the process.As such,
water consumption represents one of the most critical levers for improving environmental
performance in biologics production [19] [35].
In terms of solid waste, downstream purification of biologics involves complex, multistep
processes to remove cellular debris, host cell proteins, and closely related product variants.
These operations generate significant quantities of solid waste, including disposable filters,
chromatography resins, or single-use components. Proper management of this waste stream,
typically known as consumables, is critical to minimizing environmental impact and ensuring
regulatory compliance [19]. Additionally, energy consumption is another major contrib-
utor to the environmental footprint of biologics manufacturing. Biomanufacturing facilities
are inherently energy-intensive and in addition to powering production equipment, substan-
tial energy is required to maintain cleanroom environments and produce clean utilities such
as steam, compressed air, and chilled water [19]. Among the most energy-intensive oper-
ations are the seed train and production bioreactor stages, which have long runtimes and
high demands for environmental control. These units are critical not only from a process
perspective but also from a sustainability standpoint, as they represent some of the largest
contributors to cleanroom energy consumption [36]. Enhancing productivity in these stages
or reducing their operational burden could therefore yield significant environmental and eco-
nomic benefits [19].
Resource inefficiencies are particularly pronounced in raw material management.
Over-
preparation driven by batch uncertainty, conservative safety margins, and poor scheduling
can lead to premature expiration or disposal of viable materials. In many cases, materials
are discarded not due to quality issues, but due to misaligned timing or cautious inventory
strategies. It is estimated that for every kilogram of active pharmaceutical ingredient (API)
produced, approximately 100 kilograms of waste are generated [37]—a statistic that under-
scores the scale of this issue.
In sum, while biologics manufacturing avoids many of the overtly hazardous materials of
small-molecule synthesis, it introduces unique and resource-intensive challenges—particularly
around water, solid waste, and energy. Addressing these effectively requires a proactive,
data-driven, and cross-functional approach that embeds sustainability into the core of bio-
pharmaceutical process design and operations.These strategies offer a foundation for broader
sustainability efforts in the industry and can inform the development of green engineering
projects across biopharmaceutical production environments [19].

2.3.2
Upstream Processing and Feed Material Waste
Upstream biologics manufacturing, encompassing seed train expansion and production-scale
bioreactors, is a significant contributor to overall resource consumption and waste generation.
Benchmarking studies reveal that upstream operations account for approximately 32%–47%
of total process mass intensity (PMI) in biologics production [38]. This is primarily due to
the extensive use of media and feed solutions, which are water-based formulations enriched
with nutrients, such as sugars, amino acids, peptides, and growth factors, essential for cell
growth and protein expression [39].
The first step in upstream processing is the determination and formulation of media tailored
to the cell line, desired product, and production stage. Media for the seed train, adaptation,
and cell banking typically consists of enriched basal media optimized for rapid cell prolifer-
ation. In contrast, production media is formulated to support both cell growth and protein
production. In fed-batch cultures, which is used in 90% of commercial operation of biolo-
gics, the production medium is periodically supplemented with feed media—nutrient-dense
solutions designed to prolong high productivity and maintain cell viability over extended
culture periods [39].
Due to uncertainty in batch demands and conservative risk-averse strategies, these nutrient
feeds are often over-prepared to avoid shortages or batch-batch variability. However, this
leads to systematic non-utilization of prepared feed volumes, resulting in the disposal of vi-
able but unused media. This inefficiency leads to waste of both the nutrient inputs and the
water that constitutes the majority of the solution—often over 90% of the total mass [36].
Water thus becomes the primary contributor to the high PMI of biologics manufacturing.
In addition to the environmental burden, these inefficiencies create operational and financial
costs related to the procurement, storage, preparation, and disposal of unused feed mater-
ials. Moreover, feed expiration due to limited stability further exacerbates the waste issue,
as feed solutions not used within a certain timeframe must be discarded—even if uncontam-
inated. The media preparation strategy itself adds complexity, since when feed solutions
are discarded, the corresponding mix/hold and feed tanks must also undergo cleaning and
sterilization, generating additional water consumption, cleaning agent use, and wastewa-
ter—further amplifying the resource and sustainability burden of upstream operations.
Reducing upstream waste, especially in feed solution management, is therefore critical. In-
tegrating advanced process control systems, predictive models, and real-time monitoring
can enable more accurate feed delivery aligned with cellular demand. This shift to dynamic,
data-driven nutrient feeds management can significantly reduce raw material waste, decrease
water usage, and lower the overall environmental footprint of biologics manufacturing.
The most effective time to implement such changes is during early process design or facility
development, before product commercialization. At this stage, adjustments can be made
with minimal disruption, and high-throughput characterization technologies can accelerate
process optimization for new molecules. However, even in mature commercial operations,
there remains significant potential for improvement. Facilities that produce a high volume of
batches accumulate historical performance data and operational insights, which can be used
to identify inefficiencies and opportunities for optimization. To this end, many companies

2.3.3
Lean manufacturing in pharma
To address operational waste and inefficiency, the pharmaceutical industry is increasingly
turning to Lean Manufacturing principles. Originally developed in the automotive sector,
Lean focuses on eliminating non-value-adding activities, optimizing resource use, and im-
proving process flow [40] [41].
In the pharmaceutical context, Lean can reduce material losses, shorten lead times, and
improve quality without compromising regulatory compliance. It targets seven main types
of waste—transportation, inventory, motion, waiting, overproduction, overprocessing, and
defects—each of which is especially relevant in GMP environments [13].
For example [13]:
• Overprocessing: Redundant testing, excessive documentation, or overly complex
validation steps that consume time and resources without adding product value.
• Defects: Errors arising from equipment failures, procedural inconsistencies, or inac-
curate measurements, often leading to discarded batches or rework.
• Overproduction: Producing more than is immediately needed, resulting in surplus
stock that may expire before it reaches the market.
• Inventory: Excessive raw materials or intermediates held in storage, increasing the
risk of expiration and tying up capital.
• Transportation and Motion: Unnecessary movement of materials or personnel,
often caused by poor facility layout or disconnected process steps.
• Waiting: Idle time due to unbalanced workflows, delayed approvals, or bottlenecks in
the production process.
Raw material waste is particularly problematic under GMP constraints, where short shelf
lives and limited flexibility in storage often lead to the premature disposal of otherwise us-
able inputs. Additional losses occur during cleaning, changeovers, or purging operations
to prevent cross-contamination in multi-product facilities [42]. These issues are especially
pronounced in upstream biologics processes, where nutrient-rich media and feed solutions
must be carefully prepared, stored, and discarded under strict quality and sterility controls.
Implementing Lean tools such as Value Stream Mapping (VSM)-a method for visualizing and
analyzing the flow of materials and information throughout a process to address inefficiencies-
can improve synchronization between preparation and consumption, minimize raw material
losses, and enhance operational efficiency. Several studies demonstrate the effectiveness of
Lean in pharmaceutical settings. For instance, Chowdary and George (2012) reported a 38%
reduction in storage space and a 50% decrease in staffing requirements while maintaining
product quality [40]. Karam et al. (2018) applied Lean principles in a Romanian pharma-
ceutical business and achieved a 30% reduction in changeover time by reducing packaging

machine downtime and increasing output [42]. Nagaich (2022) highlighted the pharmaceut-
ical industry’s growing use of Lean methodologies to eliminate non-value-added activities
and streamline operations [43].
Several recent studies reinforce the relevance of Lean principles in biologics manufacturing,
particularly in the context of media and feed preparation. For example, Ismail et al. (2014)

2.4.1
Sustainability Drivers and Evolving Industry Practices
While the pharmaceutical industry plays an essential role in global health, its environmental
impact has raised increasing concern. Historically, pharmaceutical manufacturers prioritized
compliance, safety, and efficacy over environmental performance. Unlike other sectors where
sustainability is embedded into operational strategies, this industry has often lagged behind.
However, growing public scrutiny, investor expectations, and regulatory pressures are forcing
a shift in mindset. Despite being 28% smaller in output than the automotive industry, the
pharmaceutical sector was found to emit 13% more carbon dioxide as of 2019, highlighting
a disproportionately large environmental footprint [48, 9]. This is largely due to high en-
ergy use, inefficient material flows, and excessive waste generation. As global demand for
medicines continues to rise—alongside the industry’s 5–6% annual growth—pharmaceutical
companies face mounting pressure to adopt sustainable practices [49].
In response, companies are increasingly integrating principles of green chemistry and green
engineering into early-stage design and manufacturing, aiming to reduce hazardous inputs,
optimize energy and raw material use, and minimize waste. Regulatory frameworks play

2.5
Material Flow Analysis
Material Flow Analysis (MFA) is a systematic method for assessing the flows and stocks of
materials within a system defined by spatial and temporal boundaries. Based on the principle
of mass conservation, MFA enables the construction of material balances by comparing all
inputs, outputs, and internal stocks, ensuring consistency and reliability of the analysis [57].
It plays a central role in environmental management, industrial ecology, waste reduction,
and resource efficiency [58, 59]. MFA is often used alongside complementary methods such
as Material Flow Cost Accounting (MFCA), which incorporates financial performance, and
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), which extends analysis to environmental impacts. Collect-
ively, these tools provide a powerful framework for diagnosing inefficiencies and informing
sustainable production practices [60].
Numerous studies illustrate MFA’s versatility across sectors. For instance, it has been ap-
plied in cassava processing [61], meat production [62], textiles [63] and wood manufacturing
[64], among others. These applications consistently demonstrate MFA’s value in identifying
inefficiencies, reducing waste, and improving cost structures. By highlighting bottlenecks
and excesses, MFA supports operational optimization and often leads to enhanced profitab-
ility and resource efficiency. In addition to these applications across different sectors, MFA
has contributed to policy-level insights and environmental governance. For example, na-
tional MFA frameworks have helped improve waste management strategies, evaluate urban
resource flows [65], and track the fate of pharmaceuticals in ecosystems ([66]). Its potential
as a strategic planning tool has been recognized by institutions like the European Commis-
sion and the Ellen MacArthur Foundation, who advocate MFA for assessing circularity and
supporting sustainable innovation [60].
Despite its broad application, a key methodological limitation remains: most MFA studies
treat organizations as black boxes, focusing only on aggregated inflows and outflows without
analyzing internal processes. This limitation is particularly problematic in complex produc-
tion systems, where inefficiencies often stem from internal provisioning decisions, process
sequencing, or wasteful practices. While some recent studies have attempted to establish
frameworks for applying organizational-level MFA ([60, 67]), few provide process-level in-
==PAGE:28==

sight or address challenges such as the processes behind resource consumption and related
issues. Moreover, empirical applications in the pharmaceutical sector are rare, particularly
those linking MFA to measurable environmental or economic outcomes.
This thesis addresses that gap by applying MFA within the internal production environ-
ment of a pharmaceutical manufacturer. Drawing from detailed operational data, the study
maps material flows at multiple stages of the production process, identifies inefficiencies and
quantifies potential improvements in both environmental and cost terms. By focusing on

The 13-day production cycle in the 20,000-liter bioreactor follows a structured and carefully

and extend the productive phase of the cell culture.

3.2.3
Feeding System Practices and Process Constraints
A key operational challenge in the current setup involves the preparation and utilization of
feed solutions. While feed tanks may or may not be filled to full capacity, they are typically
filled to a target volume specified by the respective recipe of the campaign, which tends
to overestimate the actual nutrient and supplement requirements of the cell culture within
the bioreactor. These volumes are generally based on conservative estimates intended to
accommodate process variability and ensure adequate nutrient availability throughout the
production cycle. However, this setup results in systematic over-preparation of feed materi-
als for several reasons.
First, a certain level of material loss is inherent to the process, primarily resulting from
sterile filtration and liquid transfer operations between processing units. In particular, hol-
dup volumes-liquid that remains trapped within piping and equipment after a transfer is
complete-constitute an unavoidable form of process waste. Once the designated volume of
solution has been delivered to its destination vessel, the transfer is stopped, and any residual
liquid within the lines is discarded. The magnitude of these holdup volumes is influenced
by factors such as pipe diameter, length of transfer lines, and overall system design; longer
distances and larger piping diameters tend to result in greater losses.
Then, an even more substantial source of material loss occurs at the end of the production
cycle, when unused feed solutions remaining in the stationary feed vessels are drained and
discarded. This outcome is typically the result of input volumes being conservatively over-
estimated to ensure that feed availability does not become a limiting factor during critical
growth phases. Although this practice is common in biomanufacturing and contributes to
process robustness, it also leads to a portion of the prepared feed not being consumed. Part
of the challenge lies in the prevailing risk-averse approach to process design in biomanu-
facturing. The potential consequences of running out of feed solutions during a batch-a
scenario that could jeopardize the entire production run-has led to consistently conservat-
ive overestimations. In both economic and operational terms, the perceived risk of batch
failure outweighs the cost of raw material overpreparation. As a result, the true extent of
over-preparation has remained largely unexamined and accepted as a necessary safeguard.

Methodology
This thesis adopts a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative, simulation-based
modeling with qualitative process insights to analyze and optimize raw material usage and
waste generation in a pharmaceutical manufacturing campaign conducted at a Contract De-
velopment and Manufacturing Organization (CDMO). The core of the methodology is a
Material Flow Analysis (MFA) implemented using the software STAN, based on the frame-
works developed by Brunner and Rechberger (2004, revised in 2016) [57]. The simulation
approach enables the evaluation of multiple hypothetical scenarios to explore material effi-
ciency improvements and assess their environmental and economic implications.
The simulation is grounded in quantitative data but also incorporates qualitative knowledge
of the biomanufacturing process such as operational practices, process constraints, and risk-
averse design strategies,which inform system boundary definitions, modeling assumptions,
and scenario development. This integrated approach enables a comprehensive evaluation of
feed-related material use within the production bioreactor, balancing a data-driven analysis
with practical relevance. In this project, the quantitative and qualitative phases were con-
ducted in close temporal proximity and used to validate, enrich, and explain key results.
This mixed-methods structure is maintained throughout the methodology: while quantitat-
ive data forms the basis for the model and simulations, qualitative process knowledge is used
to inform assumptions, guide scenario design, and contextualize the results.
The methodology follows a structured and rigorous process, divided into several key phases.
Each phase is briefly introduced below and developed in greater detail in Chapters 5, 6, and 7.
Phase 1: Material Flow Analysis Model Building
This initial phase establishes the foundation for the simulation study by constructing a robust
Material Flow Analysis (MFA) model. It begins with system definition, where key materials
and substances of interest are identified, along with the spatial and temporal boundaries of
the analysis. Relevant processes and flows are also selected to clearly define the modeling
scope and ensure transparency. Next, modeling assumptions and system boundaries are
summarized to frame the conditions under which the model operates and to prepare for the
quantification of material flows.
In parallel with the system definition, data collection and processing are conducted. This
involves gathering historical data, filtering it for relevance and quality, and transforming it
into usable inputs for the model.Then, a data analysis step evaluates the statistical char-
acteristics of the inputs—such as checking for normal distribution and calculating averages
and standard deviations—to characterize typical material behavior.
Finally, using the principle of mass conservation, the model quantifies and balances all in-
flows, outflows, and stock changes across each process and for the overall system. The result
is a quantitative simulation model that reflects the real-world material dynamics of the case
study system.
The simulation model is developed using STAN 2.7 (short for subSTance Flow ANalysis), a

widely used open-source MFA tool developed at TU Wien [57]. STAN supports graphical
modeling that is translated into a mathematical model. It offers a structured interface for
inputting data—including sources and uncertainties—and performs automatic data recon-

are visualized using Sankey-style diagrams, which clearly represent flow magnitudes within
the system [70].

System Definition
The system investigated in this Material Flow Analysis (MFA) is focused on a 20,000-liter
bioreactor operation, commonly refered to as 20K production bioreactor. The primary ob-

resource efficiency and minimizing waste, particularly within the upstream production phase.
The system is composed of a series of interconnected processes, each corresponding to a spe-
cific material transformation or transfer occurring during the production campaign. These
processes encompass inputs such as seed train feed, media, and the addition of base, gluc-

between process steps, and outputs related to raw material usage throughout the production

5.1.1
Selection of goods
In Material Flow Analysis (MFA), “goods” are physical substances or materials that are
quantitatively tracked through the system. For this study, the selection of goods focused
on the bulk solutions—complex mixtures of multiple components—that enter the process.
MFA tracks these goods as whole entities to quantify overall material flows relevant to pro-
cess management [60].
In contrast, Substance Flow Analysis (SFA) balances substances
composed of uniform entities, which are defined as chemical elements (atoms) or chemical
compounds (molecules). Performing an SFA requires selecting specific substances that are
relevant to particular goals, such as waste management or environmental assessment. Al-
though MFA and SFA differ in focus, they are linked: all substances analyzed in SFA must
be contained within the goods tracked by MFA [71].This study employs MFA to capture the
total material flows of feed solutions, rather than tracking individual chemical substances,

chosen due to the availability of complete and up-to-date data. Analyzing a full year of

the project’s goal of identifying concrete strategies to optimize raw material use and reduce
waste.
5.2
MFA Model Assumptions
To represent the production process in a form suitable for simulation using STAN, a series
of assumptions and simplifications were made to manage the inherent complexity of phar-
maceutical manufacturing. These adjustments were necessary due to the intricate nature of
biopharmaceutical operations and the need to focus the analysis on specific material flows.
The model runs separate simulations for the two primary raw materials of interest, glucose
and nutrient solution, in order to independently assess their respective contributions to ineffi-
ciency and waste. Furthermore, the logistics and detailed flow behavior of the inoculum feed
was not explicitly modeled. Instead, their usage was represented through average consump-
tion values to further reduce model complexity. To ensure data integrity, a small number of
production batches were excluded from the analysis due to missing or unreliable data. In
particular, some batch records showed inconsistencies in material quantities—for example,
cases where the volume of product reported after a filtration step exceeded the volume recor-
ded before filtration, despite the expectation of material losses. Such discrepancies indicated
likely documentation or measurement errors, and these batches were therefore excluded to
maintain consistency and reliability in the dataset.
The scope of the simulation was deliberately restricted to the 20,000-liter bioreactor and its
associated feed systems. Upstream processes—such as seed train bioreactors—and down-
stream stages, including harvest, purification, and formulation, were excluded from the
system boundary to preserve analytical focus. Additionally, it was assumed that glucose
and nutrient feed rates remained approximately constant throughout the 13-day production
period. This simplification was based on calculating an average feed rate using the total
amount added over the entire campaign, despite this not accurately reflecting the dynamic
feeding profile typically observed in real-life operations.
Together, these assumptions form the conceptual basis of the model and support the evalu-

ing inefficiencies and exploring targeted scenarios for waste reduction and improved resource
use.

5.3.2
Data Quality Assessment using Pedigree Matrix
To characterize the uncertainty associated with the input data used in this Material Flow
Analysis (MFA), a data quality assessment was conducted using a Pedigree matrix.
A
pedigree matrix is a qualitative tool used to systematically rate data inputs across multiple
dimensions of data quality. This approach builds on methodologies developed by Weidema
and Wesnæs (1996) for life cycle inventory (LCI) data [72], Hedbrant and S¨orme (2001)
for MFA [73], and further developments by Laner et al. (2014) to systematically address
uncertainty characterization [74, 75]. The pedigree matrix evaluates five independent data
quality indicators [74]:
• Reliability: Refers to the credibility of the data source, including how well the data
collection methods (such as sampling and verification procedures) are documented.
==PAGE:47==
5.3
Data collection and Processing
37
• Completeness: Assesses the extent to which the data accounts for all relevant mass
flows. It considers potential gaps or partial flows that could lead to under- or overes-
timation.
• Temporal correlation: Evaluates how closely the data aligns with the time period
relevant to the system being analyzed.
• Geographical correlation: Measures the spatial relevance of the data by comparing
it to the actual location or region of interest.
• Other correlation: Accounts for any additional mismatches, such as differences in
technology, product, or system setup between the available data and the case study
under consideration.
Each indicator is scored on a scale from 1 (high quality) to 4 (low quality), based on pre-
defined qualitative criteria. Table 2 shows the detailed descriptions used to assign scores.For
example, as a reliability score of 1 corresponds to directly measured and documented data,
while a score of 4 may represent assumed values without formal documentation. Similarly, a
temporal correlation score of 1 indicates that the data originates from the same year as the
case study, while a score of 4 would represent data older than ten years [74].

Unit Transformations and Harmonization
To ensure consistency and comparability across datasets, the following transformations were
applied:
• Nutrient Feed: SIMCA reported values in kilograms, which were converted to liters
using the density values previously obtained from the PCD. Since SAP and the Data
Entry Set Excel Sheet already reported in liters, all nutrient feed values were sub-
sequently converted to cm3/day to analyze feed behavior over the 13-day production
process.
• Glucose Feed: SAP data were in kilograms and converted to liters using the density
value from the PCD, while SIMCA and Data Entry Set Excel Sheet reported in liters.
All glucose values—including quantities fed, consumed, and disposed—were then trans-
formed into cm3/day to allow for time-series analysis and facilitate integration in the
MFA model.
These unit conversions and harmonizations were essential for accurate modeling of flows
and identification of inefficiencies in the upstream process.
Also, this normalization fa-
cilitated batch-to-batch variability analysis and highlighted patterns in material efficiency.
Subsequently, the same approach was extended to the remaining bioreactor feeds to establish
average input values for each material. For the inoculum (seed train feed), antifoam, and
tyrosine, data were obtained from the Excel sheet titled “Entry Data Set.” Antifoam and
tyrosine are added manually—from a bag and a can, respectively—so the recorded values
directly reflect the actual quantities used. In contrast, the inoculum volume depends on
the culture propagated in the final seed bioreactor, which can involve either a partial or a
complete transfer. Accordingly, the values reported in the Entry Data Set correspond to the
actual amounts introduced into the production bioreactor for the batches studied.
For the culture media, the entire prepared volume is transferred directly into the bioreactor
without intermediate holding; thus, the data in the Entry Data Set also reflects the final
consumption amount. Finally, for base addition—used for pH control during cultivation—no
discrete measurement exists in the dataset. The consumed volume was therefore estimated
in SIMCA as the difference in feed tank volume between Day 0 and Day 12.5 of the process.
These processing steps enabled the data to be structured in a way that allowed for the
analysis of average waste values for each feed, as well as a historical review to identify the
minimum recorded disposal amounts. These minimum values will later serve as reference
points for developing various waste reduction scenarios.

for defining the input conditions of the material flow analysis model, ensuring it realistically

consumed.

To evaluate the performance and efficiency of the modeled pharmaceutical manufacturing
process, several key performance indicators (KPIs) have been selected. These indicators

are grounded in both process-specific operational needs and relevant industry practices in
environmental and material efficiency assessment.
Within the pharmaceutical industry, process-level metrics such as the E-Factor, Process Mass
Intensity (PMI), and Green Aspiration Level (iGal) are widely used to evaluate the overall
efficiency, material intensity, and environmental footprint of production systems [32]. Al-
though these metrics provide useful conceptual guidance for assessing mass efficiency, waste
generation, and sustainability more broadly, it is emphasised, that mass-based metrics such
as E-Factor or PMI must be supplemented by broader environmental and economic assess-
ments to capture a complete sustainability profile. In particular, other metrics are needed
to evaluate the environmental impact of waste generation, as well as economic indicators,
that are essential for assessing the financial viability of process changes [76].
While the above metrics offer valuable conceptual frameworks, this study proposes KPIs that
can be applied specifically to individual raw materials rather than the full process. Taking
inspiration from principles behind PMI, E-Factor, and related indicators, the KPIs developed
here focus on evaluating raw material usage, material loss, and associated costs at the feed
level, as well as CO2 impacts. This allows for a more targeted assessment of how specific in-
puts contribute to overall efficiency and sustainability. Together, these raw-material-specific
KPIs offer a practical framework for analyzing the impact of process optimization scenarios
on material efficiency, waste reduction, and cost-effectiveness within the scope of feed pre-
paration and utilization.

6.1

To enhance system performance, this experimental phase systematically investigated various
configurations by modifying key input parameters. By adjusting these parameters, multiple
scenarios were defined and analyzed to assess their potential impact on operational perform-
ance.
The input factors selected for scenario testing were:
• Volume of raw material feed in the respective feed tank: Current operations
show that feed tanks are often filled beyond actual demand, leading to significant waste.
Reducing feed tank fill volumes aims to optimize raw material usage and minimize
unnecessary losses.
• Volumetric flow rate of the raw material entering the bioreactor (demand):
Adjusting the flow rate directly influences the amount of material consumed during the
process and helps identify realistic reduction thresholds without compromising process
reliability.

ments to key input parameters.

obtained through the historical volume consumed by the bioreactor during the 2024 produc-

Discussion
This chapter interprets the results of the scenario modeling and KPI analysis, linking them to
the study’s broader objectives of improving material efficiency, reducing costs, and support-
ing environmental sustainability in feed preparation. The discussion evaluates key findings
from the simulations, assesses their implications in real-world operational contexts, and con-
siders both environmental and practical implementation aspects. Limitations of the current
model are also acknowledged, along with suggestions for how the insights can be translated
into actionable improvements within a pharmaceutical manufacturing environment.
By taking a broader perspective, this chapter bridges the gap between theoretical optim-
ization and real-world implementation, thereby supporting a more informed and nuanced
interpretation of the study’s findings.
8.1
Reflections on key findings
The implementation of Scenario 8 demonstrates clear and meaningful reductions in raw ma-
terial waste and associated costs, underscoring the value of an optimized feeding strategy
that combines high-confidence demand forecasting with a modest safety margin. The results
confirm that aligning feed volumes more precisely with process requirements, rather than re-
lying on overly conservative safety buffers, leads to substantial economic and environmental
gains. Economically, the decrease in waste generation translates into significant cost sav-
ings per batch and annually, highlighting the potential for considerable financial benefits
alongside sustainability improvements. Environmentally, the reduction in unnecessary raw
material usage contributes to a notable decrease in CO emissions, reinforcing the importance
of process-level optimization in reducing the pharmaceutical industry’s carbon footprint.
The comparative scenario analysis emphasizes that improvements are driven primarily by
enhancing the accuracy of demand forecasting, which allows for confident volume reductions
in feed preparation without compromising operational security. This approach mitigates the
risks associated with underfeeding while avoiding the inefficiencies of excessive overfilling.
Overall, the findings demonstrate that data-driven volume planning is a powerful tool for
balancing operational reliability with resource efficiency. By minimizing over-preparation,
pharmaceutical manufacturers can achieve better material utilization, cost efficiency, and
environmental performance—all key components of sustainable bioprocessing.

8.2
Environmental Considerations of Feed Volume Reductions
As part of the scenario analysis, the environmental impacts associated with the feed solutions
were incorporated into the KPI evaluation to capture broader sustainability considerations.
The total CO2 footprint per feed tank volume filled was estimated by combining the emis-
sion contributions from the feed ingredients—based on emission factors provided by raw
material suppliers—and the impact associated with Water for Injection (WFI), produced
in-house. It should be noted that the reported CO2 values are approximations, as confiden-
tiality agreements limit the disclosure of specific ingredient compositions and exact emission
factors composing the feed solutions.
The CO2 impact values represent the total emissions associated with the actual volume of
feed solution prepared and filled into the tank for each specific scenario. For the baseline
scenario, the estimated CO2 footprint for the volume filled was approximately 1248 kg CO2
for the nutrient feed and 689 kg CO2 for the glucose feed.
The scenario modeling demonstrated that these environmental impacts could be significantly
reduced by minimizing the volume of feed unnecessarily prepared or overfilled into the tanks.
This reinforces the value of accurate demand forecasting, not only from an operational effi-
ciency perspective but also from an environmental standpoint. Therefore, for the suggested
Scenario 8—characterized by a 1.5% safety factor and a high-confidence demand prediction
at the 99.9% confidence level—the CO2 impact was reduced to approximately 1181 kg CO2
for the nutrient feed and 282 kg CO2 for the glucose feed. Compared to the baseline es-
timates, this represents a reduction of about 5.4% for the nutrient feed and a significant
59.1% for the glucose feed.
These reductions highlight the tangible environmental benefits of optimized feed prepar-
ation strategies.
In large-scale operations, such savings could compound across multiple
campaigns, contributing meaningfully to corporate sustainability targets and resource effi-
ciency goals.
Beyond greenhouse gas emissions, reducing feed waste also leads to significant local en-
vironmental benefits. Discarded feed solutions contribute directly to the site’s wastewater
discharge profile, which is strictly regulated under Fujifilm’s environmental permit. The
local wastewater treatment facility (Hillerød Forsyning) operates under the national imple-
mentation of the EU Water Framework Directive which sets stringent limits on pollutants
such as nitrogen and phosphorus in discharges to natural water bodies—including the lake
systems that flow toward Roskilde Fjord [78].
Feeds typically contain nitrogen-rich components essential for cell growth. As nitrogen is
one of the regulated parameters under the site permit, reducing the waste of nutrient and
glucose feeds directly lowers nitrogen loading to the treatment plant. Additionally, feeds
often contain small amounts of trace metals, used for vitamin enhancement or glycation
control. These metals are particularly problematic in wastewater due to their persistence
and difficulty of removal during treatment. National discharge thresholds for metals are
typically very low—often in the microgram-per-liter range—so even marginal reductions in
feed volumes can have an outsized impact on environmental performance.

8.3
Model Limitations and Real-World Feasibility
The model developed in this study offers a simplified representation of the actual feeding
system used in the production process. While it captures key dynamics necessary for scen-
ario analysis and comparative evaluation, several assumptions and simplifications—outlined
throughout this report—were required to keep the analysis tractable. In reality, the sys-
tem includes a broader range of operational constraints, process interactions, and variability
sources not explicitly modeled here.
Translating the proposed optimizations into practice requires careful consideration of their
feasibility. First, implementing the proposed feed volume optimizations would require prac-
tical and coordinated adjustments across multiple departments and stakeholders. On the raw
material management side, the dispensary team would need to adapt to changes in ingredient
quantities, as smaller feed volumes would potentially translate to different purchase sizes.
Solution Preparation would also be directly impacted, as all existing recipes would need to
be redefined to reflect the new volumes, compositions, and potentially different preparation
sequences. This change would extend to digital platforms such as RDO and Syncade, which
currently provide automated guidance on ingredient quantities—these systems would need
to be updated to align with the revised feed requirements.
From a manufacturing perspective, coordination with all Manufacturing Associates is essen-
tial to ensure understanding and proper handling of the new procedures on the production
floor. Engineering teams would play a critical role in assessing whether equipment modifica-
tions are necessary—such as the potential need for different agitators or changes to agitation
setpoints—requiring testing and equipment validation. Quality Assurance and Validation
teams would also be heavily involved to ensure all modifications comply with regulatory
standards, including re-validation of any process parameters that are affected. In addition,
Automation and Process Control teams would need to revise all related control strategies, up-
date system logic, reconfigure alarms, and adjust setpoints to accommodate the new volumes
and operating conditions. Every recipe programmed into the control systems would need to
be revised and validated to avoid process deviations and ensure reliable execution.
Overall, implementing the proposed changes would affect nearly all operational areas and
require a structured cross-functional effort to assess, test, update, and validate every para-
meter influenced by the optimization. These broad implications reinforce the importance of
comprehensive stakeholder engagement and impact analysis before any changes are executed.
As an alternative or complementary strategy, the use of smaller, portable feed tanks could

Building on the findings and insights derived from the scenario analysis and KPI evaluation,
this section offers a broader reflection on the use of Material Flow Analysis (MFA) as a
strategic tool for process improvement. A SWOT analysis is presented to critically assess
the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats associated with applying MFA in a
pharmaceutical manufacturing context. Based on this assessment and the study’s results,
practical recommendations are proposed for the company to improve raw material efficiency,
data integration, and operational decision-making. Finally, the chapter revisits the research
questions outlined at the beginning of the thesis, addressing how the methodology and results
have contributed to answering them.
9.1
SWOT Analysis
The application of Material Flow Analysis (MFA) in this context provided a structured
and quantitative framework to evaluate the use and disposal of both nutrient and glucose
feeds across production batches. By systematically mapping the inputs, outputs, and losses
within the feed tank system, MFA enhanced the transparency of material usage and en-
abled the identification of critical inefficiencies. Specifically, it facilitated the quantification
of significant waste patterns—ranging from 10–25% for nutrient solutions and up to 75% for
glucose—thereby offering a data-driven basis for optimizing safety margins and refining pre-
dictive demand models. This level of insight supports informed decision-making by clearly
illustrating the trade-offs between operational safety and material efficiency.
Furthermore, the outputs of the MFA directly contributed to assess key performance indic-
ators such as the kg waste disposed of the raw materials per kg of product or the losses
associated with those disposals, aligning with broader goals of process optimization and sus-
tainability. The methodology also complements continuous improvement initiatives, offering
a reproducible baseline against which future process changes can be assessed, and can be
integrated with predictive analytics to further enhance forecasting accuracy and resource
planning.

9.2
Recommendations to the Company
The findings of this thesis highlight the need for a more consistent and accessible data-
set that consolidates all relevant information across the various process feeds. At present,
data is dispersed across multiple systems—each offering different levels of detail, traceability,
and structure. This fragmentation makes it challenging to conduct comprehensive analyses,
identify inefficiencies, or support system-wide optimization efforts. Establishing a unified
and standardized data system would not only streamline future material flow analyses but
also enable faster, more reliable decision-making.
Beyond data fragmentation, a significant challenge encountered during this study was identi-
fying personnel with the specific operational knowledge required to understand and contex-
tualize feed management practices. Because the process involved many different departments
that tended to operate quite independently, it was difficult to get a comprehensive overview.
Initially, finding individuals with clear insights into raw material handling and safety margin
rationales was challenging, which delayed data collection and model development. This ex-
perience underscores the need for better knowledge management and clearer communication
channels across departments to ensure that critical expertise is readily accessible for con-
tinuous improvement initiatives. Furthermore, establishing a dedicated document focused
specifically on raw material additions for each campaign would help consolidate knowledge
such as typical preparation targets, historical consumption patterns, and observed variab-
ilities. Such documentation would support operators and planners in optimizing resource
management by helping to set realistic feed volumes, reducing reliance on conservative over-
estimations, and minimizing unnecessary overpreparation—without compromising process
reliability.
A further area for potential improvement lies in optimizing feed tank sizing and utilization
strategies. By analyzing historical data from this and other campaigns, the feasibility of
using smaller tanks—or reconfiguring the usage of existing tanks—can be evaluated. This
would enable better alignment between tank capacities and actual material requirements,
helping to reduce residual volumes, especially in processes characterized by low consumption
or high variability.

9.3
Addressing the research questions
The primary research question of this project is: ”How can effective waste reduction scen-
arios be developed and evaluated for pharmaceutical manufacturing processes to improve
raw material efficiency?”.To address this, several sub-questions were explored:
How can a Material Flow Analysis (MFA) model be designed and applied to
support the development of waste reduction strategies?
The application of a Material Flow Analysis (MFA) model in this study has demonstrated its
utility as a systematic tool for quantifying and visualizing the flows and stocks of materials
throughout the biopharmaceutical production process. The design of the MFA model was
centered on capturing the key inputs and outputs at one key process stage, with particular
emphasis on feed tank operations where considerable waste was observed. By structuring the
material flows in a consistent and transparent manner, the model enabled a comprehensive
assessment of inefficiencies associated with nutrient and glucose feed management.
To support the development of waste reduction strategies, the MFA model was populated
with empirical batch data collected from 2024, allowing for the identification of baseline waste
profiles and the simulation of alternative scenarios. Through this scenario-based approach,
the model facilitated the evaluation of operational changes, such as improved prediction
of solution demands and adjusted safety margins. These insights were critical in selecting
realistic and effective reduction measures that align with both operational feasibility and
regulatory compliance.
Moreover, the application of MFA enabled a shift from reactive to proactive waste man-
agement, allowing decision-makers to visualize where and how resource losses occur. This
supports targeted interventions, such as minimization of overfilling practices. Additionally,
by quantifying the environmental and economic implications of current waste levels, the MFA
model provided a data-driven foundation for motivating sustainable process improvements.
In summary, the design and application of the MFA model served as both an analytical and
decision-support tool. It not only illuminated hidden inefficiencies in material use but also
guided the formulation of tailored strategies for waste minimization—thereby contributing
meaningfully to the broader sustainability goals of the production system.
What data, assumptions, and data collection approaches are required to con-
struct a robust (MFA) model for pharmaceutical campaigns, and how can data
limitations be effectively managed?
The development of the MFA model was based on a combination of quantitative and qualit-
ative data sources. Quantitative data—including batch-level records from several company
databases-served as the foundation for modeling input volumes of nutrient and glucose feeds,
production outputs, and waste volumes. These datasets allowed for the establishment of ma-
terial balances. Simultaneously, qualitative insights were gathered through site observations,
process documentation, and discussions with various stakeholders and operators. These in-
puts provided practical understanding of feed addition strategies, production constraints,

and the reasoning behind safety margins commonly used in pharmaceutical manufacturing.
While the qualitative information was crucial for interpreting the data and shaping the
model’s structure, it was not always directly applicable to the MFA framework. To develop
a workable and generalizable model, certain simplifying assumptions were necessary. One key
assumption was that feed additions were uniformly distributed over the 13-day production
campaign. Although this does not reflect actual operations—where feed additions depend
on cell growth demands—this assumption simplified the process and addressed the lack of
detailed daily feeding data.
Furthermore, some process aspects, such as transfer losses prior to the feed tank, were ex-
cluded from the model since they fell outside the study’s scope. Nonetheless, the availability
of a full year’s data allowed the incorporation of standard deviations to characterize uncer-
tainty within the model, improving its robustness despite these necessary simplifications.
How can hypothetical or optimized process scenarios be formulated to reduce
raw material waste in a CDMO setting?
To formulate hypothetical or optimized process scenarios aimed at reducing raw material
waste in a CDMO setting, historical production data was first analyzed. This enabled the
identification and quantification of inefficiencies in the usage and disposal of nutrient and
glucose feeds. Based on this data-driven insight, predictive demand models were developed,
incorporating high-confidence statistical intervals to forecast material requirements for fu-
ture batches. To account for operational variability and potential losses during transfers, an
additional safety margin was applied- not arbitrarily, but aligned with the maximum vessel
volume capacity and process limitations. This combined approach allows for the formulation
of optimized supply strategies that minimize over-preparation and reduce discard volumes,
while still maintaining production robustness and compliance.
How can the model be used to evaluate the environmental and economic impacts
of the proposed waste reduction scenarios?
The MFA-based model supports the evaluation of environmental and economic impacts by
quantifying material losses across process scenarios. By calculating the volume of raw ma-
terials discarded per batch under both current and optimized conditions, the model provides
a direct estimate of material savings, which can then be translated into economic costs us-
ing unit prices of nutrient and glucose feeds. From an environmental standpoint, reducing
waste volumes leads to decreased raw material consumption, lower wastewater generation,
and reduced disposal requirements—all of which are critical environmental performance in-
dicators in pharmaceutical manufacturing. Additionally, the model integrates CO2 impact
conversion factors, enabling a direct calculation of the environmental benefits associated with
waste reduction. By applying these values to the quantities of material discarded, the model
quantifies the potential decrease in carbon emissions under optimized process scenarios. This
combined environmental and economic evaluation allows decision-makers to assess the cost-
benefit trade-offs of proposed improvements, considering both financial savings and tangible
sustainability gains in the form of reduced greenhouse gas emissions.

Future work
While this study provided a focused analysis of nutrient and glucose feed flows into a 20,000
L bioreactor, there remain several promising directions for extending and deepening the re-
search. Further development of the modeling framework—both in terms of process scope
and methodological sophistication—could significantly enhance the decision-making tools
available for raw material optimization. This chapter outlines potential avenues for future
work, ranging from technical improvements in the simulation model to broader applications
across other client campaigns and production stages.
First, further development of the simulation model could significantly expand its scope and
increase its level of detail. In particular, the model could be extended to include the analysis
of all feed streams entering the bioreactor. Subsequently, it could be adapted to explore addi-
tional stages of the production process, thereby offering a more holistic representation of the
manufacturing system. This would also allow for a more complete impact analysis, highlight-
ing how resource optimization strategies can generate benefits both economically—through
cost reductions—and environmentally—by minimizing waste and resource consumption.
A key opportunity for enhancing the model lies in the application of dynamic Material Flow
Analysis (dynamic MFA). Unlike static MFA, which is based on aggregate or average flows
over a defined time period, dynamic MFA incorporates time-dependent variations and pro-
cess kinetics. Applying this approach would allow the model to reflect the actual temporal
behavior of feed additions, nutrient uptake, and biomass growth across the 13-day produc-
tion campaign. This would significantly improve the model’s accuracy and predictive power,
especially for processes like fed-batch fermentation where material flows are not constant
but evolve in response to biological and operational dynamics. Dynamic MFA would also
support scenario modeling under more realistic operating conditions. For example, it could
simulate the effects of adaptive feeding based on real-time process data. This would make
it possible to test the impact of process changes on material efficiency and waste generation
with greater fidelity, thereby providing more actionable insights for process optimization and
sustainability initiatives [79].
Another promising direction for future work involves replicating this methodology across
other client campaigns. By adapting the simulation framework—potentially incorporating
dynamic MFA—to different production processes, the company can reinforce its commitment
to environmental responsibility and operational efficiency. This not only supports internal
sustainability goals but also presents a strategic advantage as a Contract Development and
Manufacturing Organization (CDMO).
Demonstrating a structured and data-driven approach to reducing resource waste and im-
proving process performance can serve as a point of differentiation in a highly competitive
market.
Offering clients clear evidence of efforts to minimize environmental impact and
production inefficiencies may enhance the organization’s value proposition and strengthen
long-term partnerships.
Moreover, future research could explore the integration of Substance Flow Analysis (SFA)
alongside Material Flow Analysis (MFA). While MFA tracks the overall material quantities
==PAGE:96==
86
flowing through the process, SFA focuses specifically on the flows and transformations of
individual chemical substances or elements that the feeds are composed of. Combining these
methods would provide a more detailed understanding of both bulk material movements and
the behavior of critical substances, enabling targeted improvements in raw material use and
waste management.
Additionally, coupling MFA and SFA with Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) offers a promising
pathway to deepen environmental assessments. While MFA and SFA provide granular in-
sight into process-level material and substance flows, LCA broadens the scope to encompass
upstream and downstream impacts—such as raw material extraction, energy consumption,
and waste treatment. This integrated approach can yield a holistic sustainability profile, sup-
porting strategic decisions that consider both process efficiencies and their broader environ-
mental footprints. The integration of MFA, SFA, and LCA could thus form a comprehensive
framework for sustainable process design and continuous improvement in pharmaceutical
manufacturing, aligning economic, operational, and environmental objectives.
In summary, these avenues for future work provide a roadmap to evolve the current frame-
work into a comprehensive, dynamic, and multi-dimensional tool for sustainable pharmaceut-
ical manufacturing. Addressing challenges such as data integration and interdepartmental
coordination will be essential to realize the full benefits of these advancements. Ultimately,
this continued development promises to support not only improved operational efficiency
but also meaningful progress towards environmental and corporate sustainability goals.”

Conclusion
The primary objective of this thesis was to address the challenges of material inefficiency
and avoidable resource loss in pharmaceutical manufacturing, with a particular focus on
upstream biologics production. Centered on a 20,000-liter bioreactor at Fujifilm Biotechno-
logies, the study investigated issues such as overfeeding and poor input planning that lead to
significant material waste and environmental impact. Given the complexity of pharmaceut-
ical processes—especially within GMP-compliant environments where variability, regulatory
constraints, and operational silos often limit optimization—there was a clear need for a
systematic approach to analyze material flows and identify opportunities for improvement.
Material Flow Analysis (MFA) was chosen as the modeling framework due to its capability
to represent inputs, stocks, and outputs in a structured and transparent manner.
Throughout the study, several challenges were encountered, including limited data availab-
ility, inconsistent operational records, and the necessity to harmonize technical process data
with modeling assumptions. Handling outliers and managing variability in campaign beha-
vior through robust statistical analysis were essential to developing a realistic and reliable
model. Despite these challenges, the thesis successfully developed and validated a simula-
tion model using the STAN software, quantifying baseline glucose and nutrient feed usage
while identifying inefficiencies related to overpreparation and resource planning. Scenario
testing revealed that even modest adjustments to feeding strategies could yield measurable
improvements in environmental performance—particularly in CO2 footprint reductions—and
operational costs.
Among the evaluated scenarios, Scenario 8—featuring a 99.9% demand forecast confidence
combined with a 1.5% feed tank safety factor—was identified as the optimal solution. The
volume of nutrient feed solution prepared and filled into the feed tank was optimized to ap-
proximately 6,240,553 cm3 (6,241 L), representing a decrease of about 5.3% from the baseline
volume filled. Similarly, the glucose feed volume prepared and filled was significantly reduced
from 660 L to 270,000 cm3 (270 L), a reduction of approximately 59.1%. These reductions
in prepared feed volumes directly reduce overfilling, minimize waste, and improve overall
process efficiency. Consequently, this scenario achieves as well a reduction of approximately
5.4% in CO2 impact for the nutrient feed and a substantial 59.1% reduction for the glucose
feed. Economically, these improvements translate into significant raw material cost savings
by lowering waste generation, resulting in millions of Danish Kroner saved annually. This
combination of environmental benefit, cost efficiency, and operational security demonstrates
the practical value of integrating high-confidence forecasting with a moderate safety margin
in pharmaceutical feed management.
In conclusion, this thesis not only presents a validated MFA model and actionable insights
for optimizing feed preparation in biologics manufacturing but also establishes a replicable
methodology that can support future digitalization and sustainability initiatives across the
pharmaceutical industry. While further work is needed to extend the model to other process
stages and deepen environmental and economic analyses, the findings confirm that MFA is a
powerful decision-support tool for reducing waste, enhancing sustainability, and maintaining
robust production operations in complex pharmaceutical environments.
