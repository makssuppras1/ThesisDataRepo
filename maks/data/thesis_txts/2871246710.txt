Abstract
Heart failure poses a significant global health challenge, with accurate and timely assessment of car-
diac function being crucial for effective management. Left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) is a
key indicator used in this assessment; however, current clinical methods for its measurement is prone
to intra- and inter-observer variability, and require substantial expertise.
While AI offers potential
solutions, many deep learning models lack interpretability, hindering clinical trust and adoption. This
thesis addresses these limitations by exploring and developing prototype learning methodologies for
LVEF estimation from echocardiography videos.
The first chapter presents a proof-of-concept analysis that simplifies the LVEF estimation task into a bi-
nary classification problem focused on heart failure prediction. The model learns a set of representative
examples called ”prototypes”, each emphasizing clinically relevant regions of interest. Using a similarity
metric, the model associates data points with these prototypes and generates a score sheet that quantifies
the membership association of each data point with the respective class. Results demonstrate that the
prototypes are semantically meaningful, accurately representing the critical spatio-temporal features,
such as left ventricular wall movement, which are essential for reliable LVEF prediction. Compared
to a baseline convolutional neural network (CNN), the proposed model achieves a 5% improvement in
balanced accuracy and a 2% increase in the F1 score. This confirms the potential of prototype learning
to provide accurate and interpretable heart failure predictions.
Building on this, the second chapter introduces ProtoEFNet, a novel prototype learning framework
tailored for LVEF estimation in the video domain.
ProtoEFNet extends prototype learning to the
regression task.The framework enhances transparency by employing prototype similarity metrics and
activation maps, which emphasize clinically significant spatio-temporal regions. It ensures reliable ex-
planations by clearly illustrating each prototype’s contribution to the prediction. ProtoEFNet achieves
comparable LVEF estimation accuracy to state-of-the-art black-box models, specifically attaining a
Coeﬀicient of Determination (R2) of 0.80, a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 4.07, and a Root Mean
Squared Error (RMSE) of 5.49, while providing clinically meaningful explanations.
This research makes several key contributions. First, ProtoEFNet is introduced as an inherently inter-
pretable case-based model for LVEF estimation, a novel application of prototype learning to this task.
Second, this work pioneers the investigation of prototype learning for regression in video understanding,
extending its applicability to sequential data. Finally, an angular-based prototype separation loss is
proposed to enhance prototype diversity, improving model robustness and predictive performance by
encouraging the learning of distinct and representative prototypes.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates the effectiveness of prototype learning for LVEF estimation,
offering a promising avenue for developing accurate and interpretable AI tools in cardiology.

4.1
An Overview of the architecture of ProtoEFNet. The feature extractor f(.) is similar to
a classification task, inspired by XProtoNet [18], the prototype layer gp(.) is the cosine
similarity vector s = {s1, ..., sm} between the feature vectors {f1(x), ..., fm(x)} and the
respective prototype vectors {p1, ..., pm}. The weights of the regression layer represent the
prototype importance of each prototype θ = {θ1, ..., θm}. CS denotes cosine similarity, and
the final prediction is the weighted sum of the prototype labels l = l1, ..., lm, where the
contribution of prototype pi to the final prediction is denoted as βi.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Heart failure remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide, requiring accurate and
timely assessment for effective management [52]. A key indicator of cardiac function is the left ventric-
ular ejection fraction (LVEF), which quantifies the percentage of blood ejected from the left ventricle
during each heartbeat. LVEF is a cornerstone in the clinical assessment and management of cardiovas-
cular disease, providing crucial insights into systolic function and guiding treatment decisions [49].
Traditionally, LVEF is measured using echocardiography, where the left ventricle is manually delineated
at the end-systolic (ES) and end-diastolic (ED) frames. However, this process is operator-dependent,
prone to intra- and inter-observer variability, and requires significant expertise. Inter-observer varia-
tions for LVEF estimation range from 7.6% to 13.9% [38]. Artificial intelligence (AI)-driven approaches
offer the potential to automate LVEF estimation, improving reproducibility, reducing clinician work-
load, and enabling large-scale screening.
1.1
Challenges in Automated LVEF Estimation
Despite the promise of AI, existing methods face several challenges.
Most rely on black-box deep
learning models [36, 39, 31, 27, 30, 29], which provide limited interpretability. Explainability in medical
AI is crucial, as clinicians require transparent insights into how predictions are made to ensure trust
and safe integration into clinical workflows. Current explainable AI (XAI) methods often rely on post-
hoc explanations, which attempt to rationalize predictions after they are made.
While techniques
such as EchoGNN [29] apply Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to interpret model decisions, post-hoc
explanations are inherently limited and may not reliably reflect a model’s reasoning.
1.2
Problem Statement and Research Question
To address these challenges, ante-hoc XAI methods have emerged, incorporating explainability directly
into model architectures. Prototype-based learning is one such approach, where models identify class-
specific prototypes representing key patterns in the data. These models have shown success in classifi-
cation tasks by providing intuitive and interpretable decision boundaries [6, 32, 20, 18, 50]. However,
applying prototype learning to LVEF estimation presents unique challenges:
• LVEF is a continuous value, requiring a regression-based adaptation of prototype learning.
• Echocardiographic videos exhibit spatio-temporal dependencies, unlike the static images used in
most prototype-based models.
While some works have extended prototype models to regression tasks [13, 12] and video-based classi-
fication [50], no prior work has developed a video-based regression prototype neural network for LVEF
estimation.
This thesis explores how prototype learning can be adapted and applied to LVEF estimation in echocar-
diographic videos. The primary research question is:

Clinical Background
2.1.1
Echocardiography
Echocardiography is a type of ultrasound (US) imaging that is used to examine the heart structure
and function. Due to its non-invasive, effective and cost eﬀicient nature it is one of the most widely
used diagnostic imaging modalities in cardiology [46]. US imaging can be performed in different modes:
A-mode, B-mode and M-mode. A-mode is one-dimensional US usually in the form of Doppler measure-
ments. In B-mode, which is used in this project, a two-dimensional plane is formed by sweeping the
transducer (probe) to capture images that vary with angle and depth. M-mode is similar to A-mode
but incorporates a temporal dimension to illustrate movement over time.
There are different types of echocardiography. A standard echocardiogram is known as a transthoracic
echocardiogram (TTE), where the echocardiography transducer (or probe) is placed on the chest wall
(or thorax) of the patient to capture images of the heart. The images obtained pertain directly to the
heart itself. There are three main cardiac planes: the short-axis, the long-axis, and the apical plane
(or four-chamber plane). Because bones reflect ultrasound waves, structures located directly behind
them are not visible. Consequently, echocardiography relies on obtaining images from various ”acoustic
windows” of the heart. The most common windows include the Parasternal, Apical, Subcostal, and
Suprasternal Windows, as illustrated in Figure 2.1. Although around 14 standard views can be ob-
tained from the combination of acoustic windows and cardiac planes, this project focuses solely on the
Apical Four-Chamber (A4C) view. This view is acquired from the apex of the heart toward the base
and includes the Mitral Valve (MV), Tricuspid Valve (TV), and the four chambers of the heart: Left
Ventricle (LV), Right Ventricle (RV), Aortic Valve (AV), Left Atrium (LA), and Right Atrium (RA).
The A4C view is used to measure the volume of the LV, which is then used to calculate the ejection
fraction. The A4C view is shown in Figure 2.2.

2.1.2
Left Ventricle and Ejection Fraction
The cardiac cycle encompasses two main periods: diastole, when the heart muscle relaxes and refills
with blood, and systole, when the heart muscle contracts and pumps blood.[41] Specifically, concerning
the left ventricle (LV), the contraction phase is known as end-systole (ES) where the LV volume is
maximised, and the expansion phase is known as end-diastole (ED)- LV volume minimized.
Different methodologies have been used to quantify cardiac function and diagnose dysfunction. Left
ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) is a critical metric, representing the proportion of blood pumped
out of the left ventricle with each contraction. LVEF is a potential indicator of heart failure [14]. LVEF
is calculated as the ratio of the difference between left ventricular end-diastolic volume (EDV) and
end-systolic volume (ESV) to the EDV [24]:
LVEF = EDV −ESV
EDV
(2.1)
The difference between EDV and ESV is known as stroke volume (SV). In clinical settings, EDV and
ESV are often estimated using apical four-chamber (A4C) or apical two-chamber (A2C) views. The
pipeline of acquiring ultrasound (US) imaging, tracing the left ventricle (LV) volume, and detecting
the end-diastolic (ED) and end-systolic (ES) frames is prone to human error. Consequently, LVEF
measurement exhibits inter-observer variability ranging from 7.6% to 13.9% [38]. The EF values below
50% indicate heart failure, and according to [28] the heart failure can be categorised into 3 categories of
Reduced EF with EF range below 40% (HFrEF), Mildly Reduced EF with EF range between 40% and
50% (HFmrEF), and Preserved EF (HFpEF) with EF range above 50%. Due to the overrepresentation
of data from normal individuals, the model exhibits a bias favoring the HFpEF category, resulting in
higher prediction errors for HFrEF and HFmrEF.
The values above 70% could indicate hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. A key visual feature is the ex-
aggerated ventricular contraction, where the left ventricle’s walls contract with exceptional vigor
during systole, leading to a significant reduction in the ventricular cavity’s size [34]. This is accom-
panied by increased wall motion, where the left ventricular walls demonstrate greater than normal
force in contraction [9]. In some instances, this forceful contraction can result in near-obliteration of
the ventricular cavity during systole [22]. Furthermore, rapid valve movement of both the mitral
and aortic valves can be observed [33].
The visual characteristics of reduced heart failure with low ejection fraction (HFrEF), are indicative
of impaired left ventricular function. A primary visual finding is diminished left ventricular con-
traction, where the ventricular walls exhibit reduced inward motion during systole, resulting in a less
than normal decrease in ventricular cavity size [34]. This is accompanied by decreased wall motion,
reflecting the weakened contractile force of the myocardium [9]. The left ventricular cavity may appear
enlarged or dilated due to the ineffective emptying of blood, and this dilation can contribute to a
globular or spherical appearance of the ventricle [22]. Furthermore, there may be reduced or slug-
gish movement of the mitral and aortic valves, indicative of the compromised hemodynamic state [33].
Echocardiography, particularly 2D and 3D imaging, is crucial for assessing these visual characteristics,
allowing clinicians to quantify the severity of left ventricular dysfunction. Doppler echocardiography
can further demonstrate the presence and severity of mitral regurgitation, which is a common finding
in HFrEF.

Frame Sampling and Augmentation:
To standardize the number of frames for each video, we em-
ployed a down-sampling technique consistent with previous literature [36, 29]. The model was trained
using random clips, each containing a fixed number of frames, denoted as T = 64. This value was

chosen based on pilot experiments, and a sampling period of 1 was used, meaning no down-sampling of
frames occurred. This ensured that we did not lose temporal information, which is crucial for under-
standing the dynamic changes in the heart cycle. For videos with more frames than T (Toriginal > T),
we uniformly selected the first frame of the clip from the range [1, Toriginal −T]. This temporal augmen-

random rotations around the center of the video. The advantage of this frame sampling approach over
traditional clinical methods, which rely on segmentation masks of end-diastolic (ED) and end-systolic
(ES) phases, is that ejection fraction (EF) prediction becomes independent of the annotations of ED
and ES frames.
During the evaluation, each video is split into clips of T frames, and the EF value is estimated for each
clip independently. The final EF estimation of the video is the mean of the clip-wise predictions. This
is to reduce randomness in prediction.
2.1.4
Related Work on Ejection Fraction
Various machine learning approaches have been developed to automate EF estimation. Early methods
relied heavily on segmentation masks derived from ED and ES frames [47], but the inherent noise in these
labels has consistently undermined prediction accuracy.. More advanced techniques have emerged using
CNN-based architectures—particularly those employing ResNet2+1D models [36]—to directly predict
EF from 2D echocardiogram videos. Moreover, the integration of an ensemble method [21] into these
models has improved prediction accuracy under data imbalance, although their reliance on black-box
models renders the decision-making process opaque, thus limiting clinical trust.
To address interpretability challenges, recent research in explainable AI (XAI) for EF estimation has
predominantly focused on post-hoc techniques. For example, methods utilizing Graph Neural Networks
like EchoGNN [29] examine attention mechanisms and gradient information to pinpoint the cardiac
structures and temporal frames most influential to the prediction. Despite these advancements, post-
hoc explanations may not faithfully represent the model’s true reasoning because they rationalize the
decision-making process only after the prediction has been made [42].
Transformer-based approaches such as GEMTrans [30], EchoCoTr [31], CoReEcho [27], and Reynaud et
al. [39] have been developed to improve prediction accuracy. Attention-based mechanisms offer inher-
ent explainability by indicating which input regions the model prioritizes for its predictions. However,
they do not reveal prototypical cases that share similarities with the focused input regions. Moreover,
techniques like EchoCoTr [31] often focus on irrelevant features, such as the black background of medical
images, which does not contribute to diagnosis. Additionally, methods like CoReEcho [27] frequently
produce Gradient-weighted Class Activation Map (GradCAM) visualizations, a visual explanation tech-
nique, that lack proper localization, obscuring relevant anatomical structures.
In parallel, some studies have introduced uncertainty estimation methods to complement EF prediction
models. For instance, Bayesian Neural Networks (BNN) using ResNet2+1D encoders [16] and Delta
Ensemble Uncertainty [17] approaches have been proposed to quantify epistemic uncertainty i.e. the
model uncertainty. While these methods enhance the reliability of the models by providing confidence
metrics, they do not explain why a model is uncertain.

2.2
Explainability Methods in Medical Imaging for Regression Tasks
The increasing adoption of deep learning in medical imaging has underscored the need for explainable
artificial intelligence (XAI) to foster trust and facilitate clinical translation.
In the domains where
decisions have significant consequences, such as medical diagnosis in healthcare, using opaque ”black
box” machine learning models do not provide insight into how the decisions are made, and their lack of
transparency hinders accountability in critical applications. In the context of regression tasks, such as
predicting ejection fraction (EF) from echocardiography videos, various methods have been employed
to provide insights into model predictions.
2.2.1
Gradient-Based Methods
Gradient-based methods, such as Grad-CAM [43] and SmoothGrad [45], highlight salient regions
in input images by computing gradients of the predicted output with respect to the input.
While
effective for classification, their applicability to regression tasks like EF prediction is limited. Gradients
often produce noisy and less interpretable heatmaps in video sequences, especially when dealing with
temporal dynamics inherent in echocardiography. Furthermore, they are primarily post-hoc methods,
offering explanations after the model has made a prediction, which may not faithfully represent the
model’s true reasoning [42].
2.2.2
Saliency Maps and Feature Visualization
Saliency maps, which highlight the most important pixels or features for a given prediction, and feature
visualization techniques, which aim to visualize what features a model has learned, are also used [44].
These visualisation techniques could be accompanied by an inherently interprable models which provide
clinical context.
2.2.3
Attention Mechanisms
Attention mechanisms, commonly used in the transformer models, can provide insights into which
frames or regions are most influential in predicting EF [2]. However, attention weights alone may not
directly correspond to clinically meaningful features. They often require further processing to generate
interpretable visualizations. Additionally, attention mechanisms lack case-based reasoning. Case-based
reasoning could be beneficial in medical domain since it mimics physicians’ diagnostic processes by
comparing the new cases with the previous examples, and by clearly laying out the contributing features
(e.g., imaging features in echocardiography) that led to a prediction [42].
2.2.4
Cluster-Based Regression Techniques
Cluster-based regression techniques extend the concept of similarity-based reasoning to regression tasks,
providing explanations by relating predictions to representative instances or clusters. These methods
are inherently explainable.
K-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) Regression
K-nearest neighbors (k-NN) regression [1] extends the k-NN algorithm to regression by predicting
the output based on the average of the k nearest neighbors’ target values. In medical imaging, k-NN
regression can provide explanations by identifying similar cases from the training set and their associated
EF values. However, similar to k-NN classification, its reliance on raw pixel similarity might not always
capture clinically relevant features.

Bayesian Case Model (BCM) Regression
The Bayesian Case Model (BCM) regression [11] is a probabilistic framework that clusters data points
into cases and predicts the output by relating new data points to these learned cases. It offers a more
principled approach to case-based reasoning for regression, but its computational complexity can be a
limitation for large-scale medical imaging datasets.
Prototype Learning
The traditional prototype learning methods rely on unsupervised clustering techniques, such as k-means,
to compute prototypes as centroids in the latent space, which serve as cluster representatives. Prototypes
derived from unsupervised clustering are optimized for capturing average group characteristics, not
necessarily for discriminative classification. Prototype learning, such as ProtoPNet [6] and its variants
adapted for regression, offers a compelling approach for explainability in medical imaging regression
tasks. Unlike traditional methods, prototypes are learned representations of clinically relevant patterns
in the input data, directly linked to the predicted output. In the context of EF prediction, prototypes
can represent specific cardiac phases or anatomical features, such as the left ventricular (LV) wall during
systole, and their corresponding EF values.
2.3
Key Characteristics and Quantitative Metrics for Explainable
Models in Medical Imaging
The explainability extends beyond mere prediction accuracy, requiring models to provide insights into
their decision-making process. We outline the critical characteristics of explainable models, particularly
within computer vision and medical imaging, and connect these characteristics to quantitative metrics
relevant to prototype learning.
2.3.1
Key Characteristics of Explainable Models
Transparency
Transparency refers to the model’s ability to be understood by humans.
In medical imaging, this
translates to models that provide clear, intuitive explanations that align with clinical knowledge [7].
Interpretability
Interpretability focuses on the degree to which a human can consistently predict the model’s results
[23]. In medical imaging, this involves relating model outputs to clinically relevant features, such as
anatomical structures or pathological patterns.
Faithfulness
Faithfulness measures how accurately the explanation reflects the model’s actual reasoning process [40].
In medical imaging, faithful explanations ensure that clinical interpretations are grounded in the model’s
internal logic.
Robustness
Robustness assesses the stability of explanations under perturbations of the input data or model pa-
rameters. In medical imaging, robust explanations ensure reliable and consistent clinical insights across
diverse patient populations and imaging protocols. In the context of prototype learning, this means
that the prototypes and their contributions to the final prediction should not change drastically under
==PAGE:20==

small, reasonable perturbations. This is crucial in medical imaging, where data can be noisy or contain
artifacts, and where models might be updated or fine-tuned. A robust explainable model should still
highlight the clinically relevant features (e.g., the tumor) as the reason for the (incorrect) classification.
Clinical Relevance
Clinical relevance ensures that the explanations provided by the model align with medical knowledge
and contribute to clinical decision-making. In medical imaging, this requires models to highlight features
that are diagnostically or prognostically significant.
Sparsity of Explanation
Sparsity of explanation refers to the extent to which an explanation focuses on a small number of the
most relevant input features, rather than providing a dense explanation that considers all features. In
medical imaging, sparse explanations are often preferred because they can highlight the key areas that
are most important for diagnosis or prognosis, making it easier for clinicians to understand and trust
the model’s output. In prototype learning, model sparsity measures the number of prototypes that are
activated for a given input. Lower activation means higher sparsity.
2.3.2
Quantitative Metrics in Prototype Learning for Explainability
Prototype learning, particularly methods like ProtoPNet [6], aligns well with the aforementioned ex-
plainability characteristics. The following metrics are used to quantify these characteristics:
Transparency and Interpretability
Prototype Similarity Metrics: Measures the similarity between input samples and learned proto-
types, providing insights into which prototypes are most influential for a given prediction. Metrics like
cosine similarity or Euclidean distance are used.[6]
Prototype Activation Maps: Visualizes the regions in the input that activate the prototypes, offering
a direct spatial interpretation of the model’s focus. This visually shows which portions of the image
the prototype is most similar to.[6]
Prototype Clinical Relevance Scores: Measures the alignment of prototypes with clinically defined
features, based on expert annotations or medical knowledge. This is a qualitative metric, often using
expert review. This could for instance be the comparison of prototypical parts with the segmentation
mask of LV wall. However, in this project we measure this qualitatively.[18]
Faithfulness
Prototype Contribution Scores: Quantifies the contribution of each prototype to the final predic-
tion, ensuring that the explanation reflects the model’s actual reasoning.[6]
Sensitivity Analysis: Assesses the impact of perturbing prototypes on the model’s prediction, ver-
ifying that the explanation is consistent with the model’s behavior. For example, one could slightly
alter the feature vector of a prototype and observe how the predicted output changes. A faithful model
should show changes in the output that are consistent with the change in the prototype.
Robustness
Adding Noise: Introduce Gaussian noise to the input images and measure how much the prototype
activation maps change. If the important regions highlighted by the activation maps remain largely the
same, the model is considered more stable.
Image Transformations: Apply common image transformations such as rotations, translations, scal-
ing, and measure the change in prototype activations and contributions and model prediction.[12]

Bootstrapping: Resample the training data and retrain the model multiple times. Measure
the variability in the learned prototypes across these different training sets.[19]
Clinical Relevance
Clinical Evaluation Metrics: Assesses the clinical utility of the prototype-based explanations through
expert reviews and clinical studies.
Correlation with Clinical Outcomes: Measures the correlation between prototype-based features

Sparsity of Explanation
Methods can be designed to explicitly select a sparse set of prototypes during the learning process.
For example, INSightR-Net [13] encourages competition among prototypes, so that each data point is
primarily represented by only a few prototypes. This is achieved through a combination of:
Similarity-based Representation: INSightR-Net represents each input sample as a weighted com-
bination of prototypes, where the weights are determined by the similarity between the input and the
prototypes. The latent representation is learned using a sparse similarity metric.
Sparsity in Prototype Assignments: Encourages each input to be similar to only a small subset of
prototypes, it is quantified using a model sparsity metric.
Diversity Among Prototypes: Encourages the prototypes to be dissimilar from each other, pre-
venting redundancy and ensuring that they cover different aspects of the data. It is quantified using a
model diversity metric.

2.4
Prototype Learning
2.4.1
Overview
Prototype learning is a technique used in machine learning, particularly in computer vision, to enhance
model interpretability. Instead of learning a complex function to map inputs to outputs, the model
learns a set of representative examples called ”prototypes”. Each prototype represents a distinct concept
or class within the data, capturing the typical patterns or clusters. The model then learns to associate
data points with these typical patterns.
In the traditional approach, prototypes are chosen directly from the training data. However, a key
advancement is the concept of learnable prototypes. In this approach, the model includes a ”prototype
layer” where the prototypes are initialized, often randomly. During the training process, these proto-
types are optimized along with the other model parameters, such as the weights of a neural network.
This allows the model to learn a transformation of the input data into a space where the distance to
these learned prototypes is meaningful.
A prominent example of a model that employs learnable prototypes is ProtoPNet [6].
ProtoPNet
employs a supervised end-to-end learning process, enabling the learning of prototypes that represent
meaningful parts of images, which are then used directly in the classification process. Instead of using
the whole image as a prototype, ProtoPNet uses more localized ”prototypical parts” of the image. Pro-
toPNet leverages part-level attention and also provides relevant prototypical examples for comparison.
ProtoPNet determines attention based on similarities with the learned prototypes. This approach does
not require part annotations during training and offers a natural explanation for its focus: it attends
to a particular region of the input because of its similarity to a prototypical instance, similar to how
humans explain their reasoning.
==PAGE:23==
2.4 Prototype Learning
13
Specifically, given an input image x, the convolutional layers of ProtoPNet extract a set of features de-
noted by f(x) ∈RH×W ×D. The network then learns a set of m prototypes, represented as P = {pj}m
j=1,
where pj ∈RH1×W1×D. Since each prototype has the same depth as the convolutional output but a
smaller height and width, it effectively captures a prototypical activation pattern within a patch of the
convolutional output. This patch corresponds to a prototypical image patch in the original pixel space.
Consequently, each prototype can be interpreted as a latent representation of a prototypical part of an
image. For example, a prototype might represent the head of a bird, or the wing of a bird.
The process results in an activation map calculated from similarity scores. The values in this map
indicate the degree to which a prototypical part is present in the image. The activation map retains the
spatial relationships of the convolutional output and can be upsampled to the size of the input image.
This upsampling yields a heat map that highlights the portions of the input image that are most similar
to the learned prototype. For example, the network might find a strong resemblance between a bird’s
head in the input image and a prototypical head, assigning it a high similarity score. These individual
similarity scores are then weighted and summed together to produce a final score that represents the
likelihood of the bird belonging to a particular class. Using a weighted combination of distances is
more robust than choosing only the closest prototype, and it regularizes overfitting to noisy patterns,
meaning that a single noisy sample will have less influence on the overall prediction.
2.4.2
Advantages of Prototype Learning for Ejection Fraction Prediction
In the medical domain, where trust and interpretability are paramount, prototype learning stands out
as a superior method for explainability in regression tasks like EF prediction. Prototype learning offers
a unique combination of clinical relevance, part-level and video-level explainability, and direct link to
prediction. They provide case-based reasoning mimicking the physician’s diagnosis process. Moreover,
the visualisation of the part-level attention map of the input image gives further insight to the clinician
on why this new case looks like that case, and it allows the clinician to evaluate the correctness of the
features. The list of the advantages of the prototype learning model in ejection fraction is summarised
below.
• Part-Level and Video-Level Explainability: Prototypes provide both part-level (e.g., specific
anatomical regions) and video-level (e.g., temporal dynamics) explanations, crucial for understand-
ing EF prediction from echocardiography videos.
• Sparsity and Interpretability: Prototype learning can introduce sparsity, highlighting the
most relevant spatio-temporal features and reducing redundancy, which is beneficial for clinical
interpretability.
• Direct Link to Prediction: Prototypes are directly linked to the predicted EF value, providing
a clear understanding of how specific part-level prototypical features contribute to the prediction.
• Learning Clinically Relevant Features: Unlike post-hoc methods and cluster-based methods,
prototype learning guides the model to learn clinically meaningful features, such as specific cardiac
phases or anatomical structures.
2.4.3
Challenges of Prototype Learning for Ejection Fraction Prediction
Prototype learning offers a promising avenue for enhancing the interpretability of machine learning
models, particularly in medical imaging. However, its application to the estimation of ejection fraction
(EF) from echocardiographic videos presents several unique challenges.
==PAGE:24==
2.4 Prototype Learning
14
Skewed Data Label Distribution
A common characteristic of EF datasets is the skewed distribution of EF values.
There is often a
higher prevalence of samples from healthy patients with EF values in the normal range (e.g., 50-70%),
compared to patients with cardiac dysfunction (EF < 40%). In prototype learning, this imbalance
can lead to challenges in effectively representing the underrepresented range of EF values. The model
may learn prototypes that predominantly capture the characteristics of the majority class (normal EF),
while struggling to accurately map latent features to the distinct EF values in the lower ranges. This
can result in lower EF prototypes being poorly represented or treated as outliers, with their closest
samples belonging to the more common, higher EF regions. To mitigate this effect, we oversample the
underrepresented region and add constraints on the label range of the prototypes, as detailed in Section
4.1.2.
Challenges in Adapting Prototype Learning to Regression Tasks
Applying prototype learning, which is inherently designed for classification, to a regression task like EF
estimation introduces several challenges:
Continuous Latent Space
Unlike class-based prototype learning, EF values are continuous, requir-
ing a continuous latent representation space, making it challenging to separate the clusters representing
different EF regions. However, for accurate estimation, distances in this latent space need to be propor-
tional to differences in EF values. This can lead to prototypes from different EF regions being located
very close to each other in the latent space, making it diﬀicult to distinguish between them. To address
this, we introduce the Prototype Angular Separation (PAS) loss, which increases the latent distance be-
tween the prototypes, representing more distinct and relevant characteristics of each EF region without
affecting the continuity of the latent space. More details on PAS can be found in Section 4.1.2.
Non-Sparse Predictions
Unlike prototype learning for classification tasks, where sparsity in the
fully connected layer encourages sparse contributions from the prototypes of the other clusters, in
regression, all prototypes contribute to the final prediction. This can reduce the model’s interpretability,
as it becomes less clear which prototypes are most relevant for a given EF value. To address this, we
introduce a softmax function with a low temperature in the regression head to promote sparsity, giving
more weight to the most relevant prototypes. More details on this approach are provided in Section
4.1.2.
Large Continuous Space
The continuous EF space is inherently large, making it challenging to
ground it effectively with a limited number of learnable prototypes. It is diﬀicult to determine the
optimal number and placement of prototypes that accurately represent the data distribution while
avoiding overfitting to noisy cases and outliers.
Lack of Attention to Disease-Specific Locations
Unlike natural images, medical images often exhibit subtle variations, with pixel values being very
similar across different regions. In the context of EF estimation, this poses a challenge for prototype
learning. The model needs to focus on specific anatomical structures and their motion patterns, but this
is diﬀicult because the pixel-level differences between relevant and irrelevant regions are less pronounced.
As a result, the model may struggle to learn localized attention, attending to less meaningful locations.
To tackle this, we use L1 loss regularization to penalize activations outside of clinically meaningful
locations, encouraging the model to focus on relevant regions.

2.4.4
Literature Review
Recent advances in prototype-based deep learning have led to the development of models that inherently
provide interpretable predictions by comparing input features to learned prototypical representations.
ProtoPNet [6] pioneered this approach by integrating case-based reasoning into convolutional neural
networks, where prototypes represent discriminative parts of an image and serve as a basis for clas-
sification decisions. This methodology achieves competitive accuracy and offers transparent, human-
understandable explanations by revealing the image regions that contribute most to a given prediction.
However, initial implementations such as ProtoPNet sometimes suffer from overlapping or ambiguous
prototypes, which can undermine the clarity of the explanations.
Subsequent work has focused on refining prototype quality and expanding the approach to diverse appli-
cation domains. SPARROW [20], for instance, introduces constraints that promote sparsity, narrowness,
and uniqueness, ensuring that each prototype encapsulates a distinct semantic concept and reduces re-
dundancy. Leveraging self-supervised learning, PIP-Net [32] narrows the semantic gap between latent
and pixel spaces, producing a compact, scoring-sheet style explanation where the presence of specific
prototypical parts directly adds evidence for a class. Additionally, its ability to abstain from decision-
making on out-of-distribution data enhances the model’s reliability in practical applications. In medical
imaging, XProtoNet [18] extends the prototype-based paradigm to chest radiography by dynamically
adapting prototype representations to capture disease-specific features. Meanwhile, ProtoASNet [50]
extends the prototype learning to the video domain, and further enhances the model’s reliability in a
clinical setting by incorporating aleatoric uncertainty estimation into the prototype framework, which
is particularly useful for diagnosing aortic stenosis from echocardiography videos.
Recent efforts have sought to further advance the interpretability of prototype‐based models by broaden-
ing their scope beyond traditional CNN-based classification. For example, ProtoPnetVIT [26] integrates
Vision Transformer backbones into the prototype paradigm, introducing deformable, adaptive proto-
types that adjust to geometric variations in objects. This innovation not only enhances performance
by leveraging the strengths of ViTs but also produces more coherent and flexible explanations. The
adaptive nature of the learned prototypes allows the model to capture complex, variable features in
images while maintaining an inherently interpretable decision process.
In parallel, the prototype-based approach has been extended to tackle regression tasks in medical
imaging. INSightR-Net [13] addresses diabetic retinopathy grading by incorporating a prototype layer
within a CNN architecture, where the final prediction is modeled as a weighted mean of prototype
labels. By emphasizing sparsity and specificity in its explanations, INSightR-Net ensures that each
prediction is closely tied to clinically relevant features. Similarly, ExPeRT [12] designed for brain age
prediction, exemplifies this trend by learning prototypes that represent whole training images and decom-
posing image-level distances into patch-level similarities using Optimal Transport. This yields detailed,
example-based explanations that reveal subtle structural changes in the brain. Together, these inno-
vations represent a significant evolution in prototype-based methods, balancing the trade-off between
high diagnostic performance and the need for inherently interpretable, transparent decision-making.

3.1
Motivation
This chapter serves as a proof of concept (POC) to explore the capability of prototype learning methods
in the context of Ejection Fraction (EF) estimation. Given the inherent complexity of accurately predict-
ing EF, especially in the presence of noisy annotated labels, we simplify the problem by transforming it
into a binary classification task. This simplification is well-aligned with the design of prototype learning
techniques, which are fundamentally tailored for classification.
Prototype learning leverages case-based reasoning by comparing unseen samples to a bank of prototypi-
cal cases. Based on the computed similarities, a membership score is assigned for each class, effectively
enabling the model to reason in a case-based manner. Case-based reasoning could improve performance
compared to simpler benchmark methods, particularly in scenarios where label noise and data variabil-
ity pose challenges.
By grounding predictions on the similarities between new inputs and learned prototypes, our approach
provides insight into model prediction. This chapter, therefore, aims to validate the effectiveness of
prototype learning for heart failure estimation and to lay the groundwork for EF estimation in the
more complex setting of the regression task.

3.3.1
Experimental Settings
Similar to ProtoPNet [6], the prototype vectors are initialized using uniform distribution, and there are
10 prototypes per each class. We tested with receptive fields of 7 × 7 and 14 × 14, and the occurrence
maps were more meaningful for 14 × 14. This is because the features used for EF prediction are in the
larger localities, such as the LV walls. We tested with the prototype feature dimension of size 512 and
256, no performance difference, so we chose 256 due to lower computation cost.
A larger batch-size of 32 resulted in a more stable prototype learning, and using the pretrained back-
bone on Kinetics400 [48] improved the performance. We trained both of the models for 40 epochs, and
XProtoNet had a warmup of 5 epochs, and the prototypes were projected to the closest train sample
after each 10 epochs starting first after warmup. We use Adam optimizer with ReduceLROnPlateau
learning rate scheduler. The prototype learning and feature extractor are trained end-to-end using the
different initial learning rates for different parts of the architecture. The values can be seen in A.1.1
and are found from pilot experiments.

Conclusion
In this chapter, we explored the potential of prototype learning methods for Ejection Fraction (EF)
estimation through a proof-of-concept study that simplified the problem into a binary classification
task. By leveraging case-based reasoning, our approach compares unseen samples to a bank of learned
prototypes and assigns class membership scores based on the computed similarities. This methodology
not only enhances interpretability by grounding predictions in representative cases but also improves
robustness in the face of noisy, variable labels.
Our analysis shows that the model is capable of aligning with critical spatio-temporal features—such
as focusing on the LV wall during the systolic phase— which is essential for accurate EF prediction.
Moreover, the aggregation of evidence across multiple prototypes contributes to a more stable and regu-
larized latent space. However, several limitations were also identified: activations sometimes extend to
background regions or noisy parts, certain prototypes converge to redundant or spurious features, and
duplicate prototypes lead to potential overfitting. These issues suggest that further refinement—such
as fine-tuning regularization parameters, pruning low-quality or duplicate prototypes, and enhancing
the weighting of occurrence maps—is necessary.
Overall, our findings confirm that prototype learning is a promising strategy for EF estimation, offering
significant advantages over traditional CNN-based benchmarks.
However, the observed limitations
indicate that further refinement is necessary. Building on the insights from this proof-of-concept study,
we transition to the next section, where we explore prototype learning applied to the more complex
task of EF estimation as a regression problem.

Method
4.1.1
Problem Setting
We define a fixed number of m learnable prototypes P = {p1, p2, p3, ..., pm}, each associated with a
continuous EF label l = {l1, ..., lm} and an importance score θ = {θ1, ..., θm} which is learned during
training. Intuitively, these prototype vectors are designed to encode representative video segments cor-
responding to a specific EF value. In contrast to class-based prototype learning models [6, 18], where
each class is allocated a fixed number of prototypes, ProtoEFNet employs prototypes with continuous
labels spanning the entire EF range. Although the theoretical EF range is [0, 100%] we limit the label
range to [10%, 90%], since values below 10% and above 90% are clinically uncommon and not present
in our dataset. The initial labels for the prototypes are uniformly distributed across the ground truth
range.
Based on the similarity scores and the learned importance of each prototype, we generate a score sheet
that is normalized using Softmax. This score sheet yields predicted probabilities for a video to belong
to various overlapping EF regions, with each region grounded by a prototypical example. Unlike con-
ventional classification tasks where the probabilities correspond to discrete classes, here the predicted
probabilities are associated with each prototype, reflecting the continuous nature of the latent space.
Conceptually, this mechanism can be interpreted as a membership association, where the contribution
of each prototype indicates the likelihood that a sample belongs to a specific cluster. This approach not
only offers deeper insight into the model’s prediction process but also potentially improves regression
performance through a regularization effect, as aggregating contributions across multiple EF regions
may mitigate the impact of noisy human annotations.

4.2.1
Experimental Settings
Similar to the classification experiments, we sampled each video into 64 frames with a sampling period
of 1. This corresponds to on average 2-3 heart cycles. We applied the same temporal and regional
augmentations as in the classification experiments. The model was trained end-to-end for 30 epochs
using a similar schedule of varying learning rates. In the final epoch, prototypes were projected to their
nearest training samples, and then the last layer was fine-tuned for 3 iterations.
To address the issue of data imbalance, we discretized the ejection fraction (EF) labels into two equal
bins and upsampled the minority class, which consisted of samples with EF < 50%. We selected two

using 4, 8, 12, or 16 bins in our experiments. Additionally, inspired by [19], we explored reweighting the

on the long-tail, it concurrently increased errors in the majority region. Consequently, we addressed the

For the clustering loss, we evaluated k values of 1, 3, and 5 and selected k = 3, as it resulted in visually

a more uniform distribution of prototype labels across the EF range, with prototypes corresponding to
distinct EF regions being well separated in an ordinal latent space. Consequently, samples with similar
EF values clustered around their respective prototypes, forming semantically coherent groups.

4.2.3
Explanability Analysis
We validate ProtoEFNet’s explainability and its ability to make case-based predictions using prototypes.
For each test video, we display the top three prototypes alongside the most similar frame to the pro-
totypical feature. These prototypes effectively capture relevant spatio-temporal characteristics of the
heart cycle, with most highlighting key frames such as end-diastolic (ED) and end-systolic (ES). Given
that each prototype spans 16 frames, it is rare for a single prototype to encompass features from both
ED and ES simultaneously.
The model further demonstrates its strength in capturing data periodicity. In sample clips that include
about two heart cycles, high activations occur consistently at the same phase within each cycle—this is
clearly observed in the aligned activation maps corresponding to similar phases of the heart cycle, seen
in the first row of Figure 4.5.
A key advancement of ProtoEFNet over ProtoPNet lies in its implementation of flexible attention. This
architectural design facilitates the capture of expansive spatio-temporal regions, notably the left ven-
tricular (LV) wall, which is crucial for accurate cardiac phase analysis. For instance, when a prototype
encapsulates the systolic phase, ProtoEFNet demonstrates the ability to precisely align with this phase,
irrespective of the test clip’s starting point within systole. This selective matching mechanism effectively
isolates the most pertinent segments of the clip, thereby eliminating redundant temporal information.
As depicted in Figure 4.6, a subtle phase discrepancy exists between the cardiac cycle of an unseen
sample (top) and its corresponding prototypical case (bottom). Despite this variance, the occurrence
map, visualized as a heatmap overlaid on the sample, accurately highlights the LV wall features that
correspond to the systolic phase, while concurrently disregarding irrelevant portions of the cardiac
cycle. This process not only minimizes informational redundancy but also introduces temporal sparsity,
a feature that significantly enhances interpretability for clinicians. Furthermore, the model’s ability to
discern and activate features associated with two distinct systolic phases within the sample underscores
its robustness. The observed elongation of the prototype’s systolic phase is attributed to the patient’s
slower heart rate, illustrating ProtoEFNet’s capacity to adapt to physiological variations.

performance by acting as an implicit regularizer. When predictions are influenced by several prototypes,
the model avoids overfitting to specific prototypes or noisy patterns, thereby preventing the formation
of overly sharp decision boundaries.

Figure 4.10 (first row) illustrates the impact of the different loss components on the latent space vi-
sualized as 2D PCA plot. The embedding space is learned using cosine distance, thus the underlying
data manifold exhibits high-dimensional curvature, with sample distances computed as geodesic dis-
tances along this manifold. This curvature is observable as a continuous curve in the low-dimensional
PCA projection. Without the LClst term, the samples are widely dispersed in the embedding space,
and the prototypes are scattered without clear clustering. In contrast, the exclusion of LP SD causes
one prototype to become an outlier, while removing LP AS results in prototypes clustering too tightly
together—evident from the cosine similarity matrix, where even the furthest prototypes exhibit a sim-
ilarity of approximately 0.75, seen in Figure 4.10 (last row). Moreover, prototypes corresponding to
similar EF values appear nearly identical in the PCA plot as overlapping circles, and their high cosine
similarities (close to 1).
In contrast, when the LClst loss is applied, samples cluster around prototypes that share similar EF
labels. This clustering encourages each prototype to capture representative features, thereby enhancing
their discriminative power for the EF regression task. Additionally, the soft ”class” constraint defined
by the ∆l parameter promotes ordinality in the embedding space; that is, the geodesic distances be-
tween samples correlate with the differences in EF labels, ensuring that samples with closer EF values
also exhibit closer features in the high-dimensional latent space. This structured ordinality not only
tailors our model to the regression task but also demonstrates that our representation is semantically
meaningful.
The second row of Figure 4.10-showing the histogram of probability density of each prototype appearing
as top-10 contributing in model prediction- further highlights the effects on model diversity and sparsity.
The observed improvements in these metrics when LP SD is removed are a direct result of a broader array
of prototypes contributing to model predictions. Although incorporating LP SD might improve some
aspects of performance, its presence can cause certain prototypes—particularly in low-density regions
corresponding to lower EF values—to become outliers. These outliers, lacking nearby samples, fail to

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary
This thesis explored the application of prototype learning for estimating Ejection Fraction (EF) from
echocardiography videos.
In Chapter 3, we presented a proof-of-concept study where we simplified the EF estimation task into a
binary classification problem to predict heart failure. The results from Section 3.3.2 demonstrated that
XProtoNet achieves a consistently higher performance compared to the CNN baseline (ResNet2 + 1D)
across different evaluation metrics. The Balanced accuracy is increased by 5%, while F1 score is in-
creased by 2%.
This improved performance is attributed to the region of interest (ROI) module’s
attention mechanism, which directs the model to focus on important localized features, and the use of
multiple prototypes, which allows the model to base its prediction on multiple sources of evidence.
Chapter 4 introduced ProtoEFNet, a novel model designed for direct EF estimation from echocardio-
graphy videos. ProtoEFNet integrates prototype learning for regression tasks, providing interpretable
predictions by comparing unseen samples to learned prototypical cases. ProtoEFNet achieves competi-
tive performance while offering valuable insights into its decision-making process.
The quantitative results in Section 4.2.2 demonstrate that ProtoEFNet achieves comparable perfor-
mance to state-of-the-art (SOTA) black-box models in EF estimation. Specifically, ProtoEFNet attains
a Coeﬀicient of Determination (R2) of 0.80, a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 4.07, and a Root Mean
Squared Error (RMSE) of 5.49. Furthermore, ProtoEFNet shows strong performance in heart failure
prediction, with an F1 score of 0.78, while providing clinically meaningful and localised explanations.
The explainability analysis in Section 4.2.3 demonstrates ProtoEFNet’s ability to focus on clinically
relevant spatio-temporal regions, such as the movement of the left ventricle wall and mitral valve, in
the systolic phase. The model provides a comprehensive regional and temporal focus and generates
clinically relevant prototypes, enhancing the interpretability of its predictions. Qualitative analysis of
model predictions further illustrates ProtoEFNet’s capacity to provide valuable insights, even in chal-
lenging cases with low-quality samples.
Ablation studies in section 4.2.4.1 underscore the importance of the proposed Prototype Angular Sepa-
ration (PAS) loss. Removing the PAS loss degrades regression performance seen by the reduction in R2,
and increase in MAE and RMSE. It also reduces sparsity and model diversity, highlighting its critical
role in the model’s effectiveness. The effect of this loss can also be seen qualitatively by improving the
semantically more meaningful representation learning.
Considering the explainability metrics defined in section 2.3.2, ProtoEFNet exhibits key characteristics
of an explainable model:
• Transparency and Interpretability: ProtoEFNet provides transparency through prototype
similarity metrics and prototype activation maps, visualizing influential input regions. The learned
prototypes represent clinically relevant patterns, such as specific cardiac phases or anatomical
features.

• Faithfulness: Prototype contribution scores quantify the influence of each prototype on the final

5.2
Future Work
Continuous Representation.
Currently, the representation of video patches is not fully continuous.
Given three latent patches z1, z2, z3, with EF labels l1, l2, l3 where |l1−l2| < |l1−l3|, it is not guaranteed
that the distance between latent vectors z1 and z2 will be smaller than that between z1 and z3. Future
work could focus on developing a more continuous representation that better reflects the relationships
between latent patches and their corresponding EF labels.
Sparse Similarity Metrics.
The current approach results in multiple prototypes having a cosine
similarity of 1.0 with a given sample. This can lead to the activation of numerous prototypes, including
those with EF values that deviate significantly from the actual annotated EF label. Implementing an
activation function, such as an exponential function, to refine the similarity score may improve the
model’s sparsity and predictive accuracy.
Finetuning.
Future work could involve fine-tuning various model components, such as the transformer-
based encoder, pruning low quality prototypes, and other similarity metrics, while also exploring alter-
native initialization strategies for prototype vectors, prototype labels, and regression layer weights (or
prototype importance scores). For example, instead of uniformly initializing the prototype importance
scores to 1, smarter, data-driven values that better reflect the underlying distribution could be adopted
to enhance model performance, particularly for long-tail prediction.
Probabilistic Prototype Learning.
Transitioning to a probabilistic framework—for example, mod-
eling the prototype layer with a Gaussian mixture model [10]— may enhance prediction trustworthiness
by reporting confidence measures and stabilizing the learning process. This approach could also yield
greater robustness to low-quality samples by defining prototypes as clusters rather than fixed points.
Abstention Mechanism.
Currently, the model makes predictions even for out-of-distribution (OOD)
samples, which is not ideal for trustworthy decision-making. Future work should incorporate an absten-
tion mechanism, enabling the model to indicate uncertainty or refrain from making a prediction when
faced with OOD inputs.
Prototype Learning for Long-Tail Prediction.
Instead of uniformly initializing prototype labels,
a dynamic initialization method that considers label distribution could improve performance on long-tail
predictions. Inspired by prior work on Deep Imbalanced Regression [53] and Deep Feature Reweighting
[19], future research could explore adaptive prototype learning and regression layer adjustments to
maintain good performance across both majority and minority EF categories.
Concept-based Prototype Learning.
Future research could explore the integration of explicit con-
cept learning into prototype-based models. This would move beyond learning prototypes that represent
general visual patterns (e.g., ”LV wall”) and towards learning prototypes that correspond to more ab-
stract, human-understandable concepts (e.g., ”mitral valve regurgitation”, ”dilated LV”, ”exaggerated
ventricular contraction”, ”foreshortened”).
==PAGE:53==
5.3 Concluding Remarks
43
Robustness and Clinical Analysis.
Future work could incorporate robustness analysis, such as
Adversarial Robustness Testing to ensure that explanations remain reliable even in the presence of noise
or perturbations. Furthermore, clinical case study or clinical evaluation metrics could be incorporated
to validate the clinical utility of ProtoEFNet.
5.3
Concluding Remarks
This thesis delivers a significant contribution in the field of cardiac function assessment through the
development of ProtoEFNet, a novel prototype-based model designed for inherently interpretable left
ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) estimation. This work pioneers the application of prototype learn-
ing to the LVEF estimation task, achieving a strong Coeﬀicient of Determination (R2) of 0.80 and a
Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 4.07, demonstrating its competitive accuracy. Beyond quantitative
performance, ProtoEFNet surpasses the SOTA models in providing quality. To the best of our knowl-
edge, this work is the first to investigate prototype learning in the context of a regression task in video
understanding. This extension is non-trivial and opens new avenues for applying prototypical learning
in continuous value prediction scenarios involving sequential data.
Lastly, the introduction of the prototype angular separation (PAS) loss represents a significant method-
ological innovation. This loss mechanism demonstrably enhances the diversity and reduces redundancy
among learned prototypes, which is crucial for achieving robust and reliable LVEF estimations. Abla-
tion studies confirm the importance of this contribution, revealing that removing the PAS loss leads to
a degradation in regression performance, underscoring its role in optimizing prototype distribution and
overall model effectiveness.
