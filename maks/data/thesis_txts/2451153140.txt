Abstract
Drinking water will become a scarce resource, therefore the interest of using recycled water
in the food industry has increased.
Ultraviolet (UV) has the potential to treat recycled
water. However, UV-doses are usually not adjusted to the quality of the water, which can
be problematic as the characteristics of recycled water varies. Therefore, knowledge about
which quality parameters that aﬀect the UV inactivation of microorganisms is of great value
in order to apply correct UV doses. In this thesis, the eﬀect of ultraviolet light (UVC) on
the inactivation of E. coli and L. monocytogenes in water of diﬀerent water quality was
investigated. The examined UV-doses were 2, 5, 7, 9, 12, 20 and 40 mJ/cm2.
The inorganic material kaolin was not found to aﬀect the inactivation rate of E. coli, whereas
the organic materials peptone and horse blood decreased the inactivation rate due to their
UVC-light absorption. When the absorbance of the diﬀerent water types was considered in
the UV-dose calculations, E. coli was inactivated at the same rate regardless of the absorbance
or turbidity. The inactivation rates of E. coli and L. monocytogenes in UV treated water from
three unit operations in a slaughterhouse was also found to be identical when the absorbance
of the water was considered in the UV-dose calculations. Even a high turbidity level (299
NTU) did not inﬂuence the inactivation rate. When the absorbance was considered, a log
reduction of 4 was obtained at UV-doses between 5 and 7 mJ/cm2 in the three waters from
the slaughterhouse.
It was found that long-amplicon qPCR was not able to detect the same 2 log reduction as
quantiﬁed by total viable counts at UV doses of ≤40 mJ/cm2. In addition, the long-amplicon
qPCR detected more copies of DNA in the untreated slaughterhouse water compared to the
viable cell counts.
Overall, a UV system should be designed to account for the absorbance of the water matrix
to be UV treated in order to deliver correct UV-doses.

Background
Drinking water is becoming a scarce resource and as a result of a growing population the
demand for drinking water is growing. Water is widely used in the food industry, either as
an ingredient or in a unit operation e.g. washing, rinsing, cooling, blanching and for cleaning
and sanitation [19]. This intensive water consumption is prompting food processors to want
to optimise their use of water.
Recycling or reuse of water is useful to reduce water use and thus reduce wastewater pro-
duction [18].
Due to available technology and ongoing development of better treatment
technologies, implementation of water recycle practices in the food industry has become eas-
ier. Ultraviolet (UV) irradiation is a useful technology for water disinfection. It is eﬀective
against a wide range of pathogenic microorganisms and, in contrast to chlorine disinfection,
it does not lead to formation of potentially hazardous disinfection by-products [53; 21]. For
many years, limited recycling has taken place in the food industry due to strict regulations,
which stipulates that potable water must be used in food production. Current legislation
allows food producers to use non-potable water in processing steps, if they can document that
this is not jeopardizing food safety. However, the current quality parameters are based on
potable water. Therefore, it is desirable to have quality parameters based on recycled water.
Since the water has been through diﬀerent process steps and in contact with food products,
it may contain a complex mixture of constituents. UV-doses are usually not adjusted to the
quality of the water which can be problematic when UV is used for disinfection of recycled
water. It is therefore important to know which water quality parameters that aﬀect the UV
inactivation of microorganisms, so food producers use correct UV doses. The turbidity of
the water is a well-studied parameter regarding inactivation, however, the absorbance is an
overlooked parameter in the ﬁeld of research. Therefore, it is interesting to study if measure-
ment of the absorbance or the turbidity can be used to determine UV-doses that ﬁt to the
quality of the water.
Microbial water quality is often determined by using the time-consuming technique plate
count. It takes at least 24 hours to get the results from this method which mean that the
recycled water already are recirculating in the food production before the results are obtained.

Aim and objectives
The objective of this project is to investigate if treatment with UV-light can kill microorgan-
isms in water acquired from diﬀerent unit operations in a slaughterhouse for the purpose of
(re)use in the same and/or diﬀerent unit operations.
The objectives are stated as research questions in the following:
• How will diﬀerent water quality parameters inﬂuence the inactivation kinetics of Es-
cherichia coli and Listeria monocytogenes in water from a slaughterhouse following UV
irradiation?
• Can measurement of turbidity or absorbance in the recycled water be used to determine
the necessary UV dose to achieve the desired log reduction?
• Can long amplicon qPCR be used to detect the same log reduction after UV treatment
as obtained with plate counts?
• Is UV treated water from diﬀerent unit operations in a slaughterhouse microbiological
safe to reuse?
Research study design
The methods used to obtain the purpose and the aims of the project were performed by
literature reviews as well as experimental studies in the laboratory.
The analysed water
samples were acquired from Danish Crown, Horsens.
The thesis consisted of four main
experiments:
1. Preliminary experiments were performed to investigate how diﬀerent UV doses (0-100
mJ/cm2) from the bench scale apparatus worked on inactivation of E. coli and L.
monocytogenes in diﬀerent types of water. The water types included seawater (from
Øresund), salt water and sterile physiological saline with peptone (FKP).
2. The water quality parameters turbidity and absorbance were analysed in kaolin-water,
peptone-water and horse blood-peptone-water in order to determine how they inﬂu-
enced the eﬃcacy of UV treatment on inactivation of E. coli. The survival populations
were enumerated by plate counts. Survivor curves after UV treatment were obtained

by plotting the logarithm of the survival fraction versus treatment doses. To ﬁt the
survival curves the model-ﬁtting tool GInaFiT was used.
3. An experiment investigated how absorbance and turbidity inﬂuenced the eﬃcacy of UV

conductivity, pH, total nitrogen, total phosphate, and chemical oxygen demand.

Introduction
3.1
Water use in food processing plants
The food industry has a high consumption of water since it is a key processing element
and a major ingredient in food processing. Water is involved in unit operations such as
soaking, washing, rinsing, scalding, blanching, heating, cooling and used for general cleaning,
sanitation and disinfection purposes [19]. Water used in food companies shall as a rule be
of the same quality as potable water and the food company must make sure that there are
access to suﬃcient quantities of potable water [28].
Process water, which is used potable water than can be reclaimed from food processing plants,
is diﬃcult to characterise as it varies according to the processes, season and related products.
The main contaminants in process water are microorganisms, biodegradable organic material,
sanitising products, fertilisers, pesticides, metals, nutrients, organic, and inorganic material.
The water is thought to be contaminated with microorganisms as a consequence of contact
with food, equipment, employees, or the food processing environment [41].
Availability of high quality water will become a scare commodity in many areas which enforce
the food industry to put eﬀort into reducing the water consumption. In addition, signiﬁcant
savings can be made for the food industry by reducing their water consumption [41]. Accord-
ing to Kirby et al. [41] there are ﬁve ways to reduce water consumption in food processing
plants:
1. Reduction in uncontrolled use
2. Improved planning and control
3. Water reuse
4. Water recycling after treatment
5. Layout design improvements
In this thesis the focus is on water recycling after treatment as described in section "aim and
objectives".

Slaughter houses are estimated to use 0.9% of the total water consumption in Denmark [59].
The slaughtering process of pigs involves many steps and the water consumption is huge.
The ﬁrst steps take place in an unclean area and involve stunning of pigs followed by sticking
and debleeding. Afterwards, diﬀerent "outer" treatments are performed (scalding, dehairing
and ﬂaming) in order to remove bristles, clean the skin, obtain smooth surfaces and kill
possible microorganisms present on the surface. The whipping line (WL) is the last step in
the unclean area and it consists of a brushing part and a whipping step. Water is sprayed
on carcasses and rotating brushes make sure that dirt is removed from the surfaces of the
pigs. Here after, the carcasses enter a clean area where most of the steps are performed by
robots. A bung loosening robot cuts a hole around the rectum to free them followed by a
robot making an incision in the sternum to open the carcasses so the organs can be removed.
Before the splitting takes place, a robot measures the position of the spine and do a shallow
cut before the carcasses are divided into two pieces by the splitting robot. The last steps of
the slaughtering process involve chilling, deboning and cutting. A detailed ﬂowchart of the
slaughtering procedure can be found in ﬁgure 1.
Figure 1. Flowchart of the production line in a slaughterhouse.
Between every carcass the knives, saws and other tools are cleaned and disinfected with steam
to prevent the transfer of disease between carcasses [26]. Due to food safety regulations, ster-
ilisation of utensils between every carcass is sustained until each carcass has been veterinary
approved for consumption.
3.2
Regulations regarding recycled water
According to directive 852/2004/EU on the hygiene of foodstuﬀs it is permitted to use recy-
cled water in a process or as an ingredient if it does not pose any risk of contamination [2].
The recycled water has to either be of the same quality as potable water or of ﬁt-for-purpose

quality that enables the company to prove to the competent authority that the safety of
their products is unaﬀected by the recycled water. In the directive it is stated that water
of another quality than potable water has to be circulated through separate pipelines. This
water may not be in connection with potable water systems or have the possibility to reﬂux
into those systems [2].
The assessment of recycled water quality is grounded on the quality parameters stated in
directive 98/83/EU on the quality of water intended for human consumption [1].
These
quality requirements cover physical, chemical and microbiological parameters. It shall be
noted that the requirements for these parameters are based on potable water. Based on
European Union legislation, Denmark has implemented Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen where
all quality requirements for potable water are listed. Table 1 summarises the microbiological

The presence of E. coli in a sample indicates possible fecal contamination and it implies a risk
of other enteric pathogens present in the sample. E. coli is a reliable indicator for Salmonella
spp., Shigella spp., and Campylobacter spp. in drinking water supplies due to presence in
faeces and their similar sensitivity to ultra violet irradiation (UV) [81]. C. perfringes spores is
also an indicator of faecal pollution. In addition, C. perfringes spores have been proposed as
an indicator of protozoa in treated drinking water supplies due to its high resistance against
disinfection processes [81].
However, some bacteria do not originate from faeces such as L. monocytogenes and Legionella
therefore other indicators than E. coli are preferable [19]. In addition, Yersinia enterocolit-
ica has a longer persistence in water compared to E. coli and the result could therefore be
false-negative in situations where E. coli is used as indicator microorganism.
Microorganism’ resistance to UV disinfection diﬀer and viruses and protozoa are more resis-
tant to disinfection than E. coli. E. coli is therefore not considered a reliable indicator for
viruses and protozao. Bacteriophages are a better indicator for the presence of viruses due
to their easy and fast detection and their great persistence in water [19; 81].

The Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (i.e. the competent authority in Denmark)
allows food producers to use water of other qualities than that of potable water. In such sit-
uations, the food producers have to write an application wherein they describe the recycling
system and document that the water quality does not aﬀect the food safety. Since recycled
water may contain substances not listed in Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen the examination of
the water quality shall also reﬂect chemical and/or microbiological analysis that are consid-
ered relevant in the speciﬁc situation [2]. However, according to Casani et al. [19], it can be
challenging for the food producers to convince authorities that recycled water is safe to use
and that it does not pose a threat to the product’s safety.
The Codex Alimentarius Commission (CAC) has published draft guidelines on hygienic reuse
of processing water in food plants. The guidelines from CAC emphasise that water for reuse
should meet the quality requirements for potable water but they also consider the use of
alternative qualities if the water does not aﬀect the quality or safety of a food product [23].
In order to prevent safety issues associated with reuse of water in food companies the micro-
biological quality of the water must be monitored all the time. CAC therefore suggests to use
the systematic safety management tool Hazard analysis and critical control point (HACCP)
to ensure the safety and quality of a food product [23]. HACCP consists of seven principles
but before these principles can be implemented the food industry has to take some prelimi-
nary steps. The preliminary steps include information on the "history" of the previously used
water and its intended use. A good tool to evaluate the potential for reuse is by making a
microbiological mapping of the process water at the diﬀerent unit operations of the food pro-
duction chain. Such a mapping also facilitates the identiﬁcation of microbiological hazards
[17].

3.3
Recycled water
According to CAC, recycled water is deﬁned as water obtained from a food manufacturing
operation which has been treated, when necessary, such that it may be reused in a subsequent
food manufacturing operation [23].
Implementation of recycled water in a food processing plant is demanding since it requires
continuous monitoring of quality parameters to ensure that the treatment system perform as
intended. When selecting a treatment system it is important to make sure it removes unde-
sirable physical, chemical and microbiological components and prevent growth of pathogenic
and spoilage microorganisms. Depending on the type of water to be treated, pre-treatment
can be necessary in order to remove large particles which otherwise can decrease the eﬃciency
of the treatment [19].
If water has low microbial and chemical contamination or the need for treatment is minimal
the water is more likely to be recycled [58]. CAC exempliﬁes diﬀerent places to reuse re-
conditioned water in the meat industry as long as the water does not contain human waste,
is from a single source within the food production and is free of pathogens after treatment.
Such water could for instance be used as water to wash of beef carcasses, to remove heavy
solids, to ﬂush the bottom of open evisceration troughs or to wash antemortem pens [23].
Most of the available publications on treated process water is based on recycling within the
same unit operation [18]. Examples of this include washing water treated with sedimenta-
tion from a potato production, chiller spraying water treated with nanoﬁltration, UV and
chlorination from a sausage production, and chiller shower water treated with preﬁltration,
nanoﬁltration, reverse osmosis, and UV from a meat production [19]. A study by Mavrov et
al. [57] shows that vapour condensate from the dairy industry is able to be reused in another
unit operation namely as boiler make-up water if the condensate is treated with pre-ﬁltration,
nanoﬁltration and UV.

3.4
Ultraviolet disinfection
Inactivation of microorganisms with UV light was reported for the ﬁrst time in 1892. A
couple decades later certain wavelengths of UV light were reported to be the underlying
mechanism for the germicidal eﬀect and the ﬁrst use of UV to disinfect drinking water is said
to have taken place in 1906 [46].
In the electromagnetic spectrum UV light lies between X-rays and visible light and the
spectrum of UV light range from wavelengths of 100 to 400 nm. The UV spectrum can be
divided into four groups based on the interactions of the wavelength’s with the molecules:
Vacuum ultraviolet (VUV), ultraviolet ’C’ (UVC), ultraviolet ’B’ (UVB), and ultraviolet ’A’
(UVA), as shown in ﬁgure 2. The VUV spectrum covers the most energetic wavelength within
the UV spectrum and includes wavelengths smaller than 200 nm. The wavelength’s of UVC
are 200-280 nm. Since the maximum absorption spectrum of bacterial DNA is 260-265 nm
and the optimum microbial killing eﬃciency ranges from 254 to 260 nm, the UVC spectrum
is called the germicidal spectrum. The spectrum of UVB ranges from 280-315 nm and UVA
ranges from 315 nm to 400 nm. The latter spectra produce less damage to the DNA because
of DNA’s low absorbability in this range [42; 31].
Figure 2. Spectral ranges of UV, visible and infrared light. The UV light ranges from 100-400 nm where
UVA ranges from 315-400 nm, UVB ranges from 280-315 nm and UVC cover the wavelengths from 200-280
nm [11].
UVC light as a disinfection treatment has various advantages, especially because it has been
found to be eﬀective against spoilage microorganisms and the two waterborne pathogens
Giardia and Cryptosporidium contrary to disinfection with chlorine which is not eﬀective
against these pathogens [35; 31].

3.4.1
Eﬀect of UV light on microorganisms
Both Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and RNA are aﬀected by UVC light treatment. DNA is
composed of nucleotides which consist of deoxyribose, phosphate and one of the four nucleic
acid bases: Thymine (T), adenine (A), cytosine (C), or guanine (G). In RNA, thymine is
replaced by uracil (U). Thymine (uracil) and cytosine are pyrimidines and have a single-ring
structure whereas the purines (adenine and guanine) have a double-ring structure. Pyrim-
idines, purines and aromatic amino acids such as tryptophan and phenylalanine have conju-
gated ring structures. Those structures have large, non-localized φ orbitals which make them
more stable compared to σ orbitals. When a φ orbital is hit by a photon from UVC light
with a wavelength around 254 nm, the energy from the photon is converted into vibrational
energy and if this energy is high enough, an electron is excited to an orbital of higher energy.
Such a disruption of stable electrons can move through the entire organic structure and raise
a bonded electron out of a bonding pair resulting in an unstable conformation [27; 47]. Struc-
tures containing conjugated bonds share the φ orbital throughout the ring structure and the
photon’s energy from UVC light is therefore absorbed by the entire structure. The weakest
bond in the structure obtains the extra energy from the photon which leads to conformational
changes [36].
When microorganisms are exposed to UVC light the nucleotides within the DNA absorb the
photons from the light resulting in formation of dimers in the DNA chain (ﬁgure 3a). UVC
light causes formation of DNA photoproducts, in particular cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers
and pyrimidine 6-4 pyrimidines. As shown in ﬁgure 3b, such ring structures can be formed
when the number ﬁve and six-carbon atoms of adjacent pyrimidines are saturated due to UV
absorption. This results from a covalent bond which is formed when one of the nucleic acid
bases absorb a photon [31; 50].

bases have an impact on the probability of dimerization.

have found that UVC light is able to aﬀect cytoplasmic and membrane structures. UVC can
directly aﬀect cytoplasmatically located transcription factors, kinases closely located to the
cellular membrane and even membrane receptors [86; 70; 72].

3.4.4.1
The inﬂuence of process parameters on UV disinfection
The main process parameters aﬀecting the eﬀectiveness of UV light are the UV dose and
wavelength emission. The inactivation caused by UV irradiation is dependent on the amount
of UV energy absorbed by the microorganisms (dose) [31]. The ultraviolet dose (D) is deﬁned

as the product of exposure time (T) and the UV intensity (I):
D = I × T

where D is expressed as mJ/cm2, I as mW/cm2 and T as seconds.
The UV lamp is an important component in UV irradiation since the wavelength emission
depends on the UV lamp. The two most common types of lamps are LP and MP mercury
vapor lamps. MP lamps emit a polychromatic spectrum covering wavelengths from 200-600
nm where 15-23% is emitted at 253.7 nm. LP lamps emit a monochromatic spectrum (UVC
wavelengths) and radiate about 95 % of the energy at a wavelength of 254 nm which corre-
sponds to the maximum absorption of DNA. Therefore, LP lamps have a higher production
of germicidal wavelengths compared to MP lamps. Since UV lamps use the toxic heavy metal
mercury, alternative lamps are of interest. UV light emitting diodes (LEDs) are a beneﬁcial
alternative because these lamps do not require an electronic ballast for starting, are smaller
in size and do not use mercury. UV LEDs are also able to produce wavelength within the
germicidal range [48].
Flow pattern and geometry of the UV equipment may also aﬀect the eﬃciency. However,
these parameters do not have a directly impact on the microorganisms, only on the UV dose
received by them [31].
3.4.4.2
The inﬂuence of water quality factors on UV disinfection
Various studies have shown that the eﬃciency of UV disinfection is inﬂuenced by water
quality parameters [29; 73; 71]. According to Bolton and Cotton [14], UV transmittance
(UVT) is the most important quality parameter because it can aﬀect the UV facility size
and cost. UVT is a measure of the amount of light absorbed by the water and its diﬀerent
compounds. It is deﬁned as a function of the absorption coeﬃcient (α) of the treated water
(UVT = 100 x 10−α) [43]. The parameters related to the UVT in water are of primary
concern since a decrease in UVT indicates that much of the light has been absorbed by the
water matrix meaning that the microorganisms in the water received a lesser dose. Common
and measurable parameters aﬀecting UVT are turbidity, TSS, COD, and particle size [44].
COD is an indicator that represents the level of organic pollution in water. It is deﬁned as
25 of 103
==PAGE:27==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology, DTU
DTU
"the amount of oxygen equivalents consumed in oxidising the organic compounds of samples
by strong oxidising agents such as dichromate or permanganate" [84]. Most of the organic
pollutants have absorption peaks in the UV region because of their molecular characteristics
[84]. Therefore, COD is expected to have an impact on the UVT of the water.
Turbidity in water is the reduction of transparency due to the presence of inorganic and
organic matter or biological particles such as bacteria [81; 29]. Turbidity causing materials
(TCMs) can obstruct light transmission through the water and thereby impact the eﬀect of
UV inactivation. The particles might scatter and absorb incident UV light or shield bacteria,
protozoa and viruses thus preventing suﬃcient UV dose for inactivation of pathogens. The
potential for microorganisms to attach the surface of a TCM or to be bound together by large
organic molecules are other parameters that inﬂuence the inactivation eﬃciency. Microbial
attachment to a particle’s surface is due to electronic interactions, hydrophobicity, surface
tension and surface roughness [39].
Farrel et al. [29] have studied the relationship between turbidity composition and the eﬀect
of UV inactivation. In order to achieve diﬀerent turbidities, diﬀerent TCMs were suspended
in ﬁltered mineral water. The required UV dose for a 3 log reduction of E. coli were approx-
imately double for Fe (III) hydroxide precipitate and humic acids at 2 NTU compared to 0.2
NTU. The observed diﬀerence was due to decreasing UVT with increasing concentration of
the two materials. However, these results were not seen for chalk. They found that the par-
ticle concentrations were diﬀerent between the TCMs for a given turbidity. This illustrates
that the turbidity is dependent on diﬀerent parameters such as the physical and chemical
composition, size distribution and particle shape.
Walters et al. [79] have studied the inﬂuence of particle association and particle size as well as
TSS on UV inactivation. The study shows that when E. coli and enterococci were associated
with particles smaller than or equal to 12 µm, they respectively were inactivated on average
2 and 1.7 times faster than when they were associated with particles of the size between
12 and 63 µM. In addition, they found that an increased TSS concentration resulted in a
decreased UV inactivation for both particle sizes. This is probably because TSS act to shield
microorganisms from UV irradiation.

low growth rates but not at medium or high growth rates [31].

3.4.4.3.1 DNA repair mechanisms
Microorganisms have mechanisms that are capable of protecting them from UV exposure.
When microorganisms have been exposed to UV light they have diﬀerent DNA repair mech-
anisms to repair biological damage which could otherwise induce cell death.
The repair
mechanisms include photoreactivation, dark repair, recombination repair, and SOS repair.
In the following, photoreactivation and dark repair are described. These repair mechanisms
might help bacteria regain their growth potential. This is important to consider, because the
water otherwise could be judged to be pathogen free without this being the case [19].
Photoreactivation is the microorganisms’ main defence against UV damage.
Photoreac-
tivation is the mechanism where inactivated microorganisms recover activity because the
pyrimidine dimers in the DNA are monomerized by the enzyme photolyase. The process is
dependent on light and occurs in conditions of prolonged exposure to UVA and visible light
(310-480 nm) [65; 87]. The repair ability of microorganisms diﬀers for diﬀerent species and
strains. It has been shown that bacteria have the enzymes necessary for photorepair while
viruses lack those enzymes. However, publications have shown that viruses can use the host
cell’s repair mechanism [69].

Dark repair is a mechanism by which microorganisms can repair damaged DNA without
light. However, dark repair can also occur in the presence of light [78]. A form of dark
repair is called excision repair which is enzyme dependent.
Here, dimers from damaged
DNA are excised and regenerated using the existing complementary DNA strand. Excision
repair can therefore only occur with double stranded DNA and RNA. Dark repair has been
demonstrated in almost all bacteria with exception of few due to their lack of enzymes needed
for dark repair [35; 78]. Spores do not have an active metabolism but repair instead upon
germination [68].
3.4.5
Advantages and disadvantages of UV disinfection
UV irradiation is an eﬀective disinfection technology for inactivation of microorganisms.
It has been shown to be eﬀective for inactivation of the two chlorine resistant protozoan
pathogens Giardia and Cryptosporidium [10; 80]. UV disinfection doses not consume chemi-
cals and it does not cause a signiﬁcant change in the water quality neither with regard to pH,
corrosivity, turbidity, total organic carbons (TOC), or disinfection by-products (DBP). By-
products are not formed which is a typical problem with chlorine disinfection. In addition,
the required contact times to inactivate bacteria are short [82].
The disadvantage of UV treatment is the lack of residual disinfection. However, the missing
residual eﬀect after UV irradiation is beneﬁcial because no chemicals are left in the disin-
fected water which otherwise can interfere with the water quality and lead to harmful DBP
formation. Other disadvantages include diﬀerence in the output depending on the lamp and
factors which reduce the killing eﬃciency of UV light such as aggregation or clumping of
microorganisms, interference with turbidity or UVT and formation of bioﬁlm [10].
3.5
The Viable But Nonculturable state in bacteria
For quantiﬁcation of bacteria in a water sample, plate counting methods have been used for
many years. The hypothesis behind the plate counts methods is that one bacterium forms one
colony if it is viable. Therefore it is a bacterium’s culturability that represents its viability.
However, in 1982 Xu et al. [86] found that bacteria could enter a viable but nonculturable
(VBNC) state under diﬀerent stress conditions. Cells entering the VBNC state are alive, but
32 of 103
==PAGE:34==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology, DTU
DTU
typically demonstrate low levels of metabolic activity and are not able to form colonies in
usual media. By giving the cells a temperature upshift they can be resuscitated and thereby
become culturable again [9; 67].
VBNC bacteria often show dwarﬁng and go through metabolic changes including reductions
in nutrient transport, respiration rates, and macromolecular synthesis [67]. The VBNC state
has been detected in pathogens such as Salmonella spp., Campylobacter spp., Escherichia
Coli, and Listeria monocytogenes [9].
Diﬀerent chemical and environmental stress factors have been found to induce bacteria into a
VBNC state: Adverse nutrition levels, incubation outside the temperature range of growth,
exposure to white light, and osmotic pressures [67; 86]. Ozone and UV disinfection are also
reported to induce bacteria into a VBNC state. Xu et al. found that VBNC states in both
E. coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa were induced following UV radiation [86].
3.6
Polymerase Chain Reaction
Commonly used methods to detect if bacteria have survived UV treatment is the pour plate
method and the spread plate method. Both methods involve counting CFU per ml of a sam-
ple. However, the methods are labour-intensive, time-consuming and ineﬃcient, which is not
favourable from a food producer’s perspective. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is a more
sensitive and rapid method and it has the potential to overcome the mentioned diﬃculties
[16]. However, qPCR detect both DNA from live and dead cells which is unfavourable if the
goal is to test for viability. The length of the amplicon used during qPCR has been shown to
be an important factor when diﬀerentiating between live and dead cells. The use of a longer
amplicon is expected to be more sensitive in detecting damaged DNA compared to a shorter
amplicon length because it is more likely that the DNA polymerase in the qPCR will detect
damaged DNA. This will lead to disruption in the ampliﬁcation process [22].
PCR involves repeated cycles of heating and cooling of a reaction mixture containing DNA
template, DNA polymerase, primers, and nucleotides. DNA template is the DNA containing
the target sequence.
Primers are short chains of nucleotides locating the speciﬁc target
sequence of interest and bind to DNA upon cooling through complementary base pairing.
33 of 103
==PAGE:35==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology, DTU
DTU
DNA polymerase is the enzyme responsible for synthesizing new strands of DNA which
are complementary to the target sequence. The primers act as a starting point for DNA
polymerase to create the new complementary strand. Each cycle of PCR consists of three
steps:
1. In the denaturation step, the reaction mixture is heated to over 90 °C to separate the
two strands of DNA by breaking apart the hydrogen bonds.
2. In the annealing step, the reaction mixture is cooled to 45-65 °C which enables for-
ward and reverse primers to hybridise through complementary base pairing to opposite
strands of the DNA.
3. In the extension step, the mixture is heated to the optimal temperature for DNA
polymerase. The enzyme binds to the primers and assembles then a new complementary
strand by using the original DNA as template. The new strand is made by using the
free nucleotides in the reaction mixture.
Following the extension step, the reaction is returned to the denaturation step and PCR
continues. The amount of DNA is doubled after each cycle as a new strand acts as a template
for replication in the following cycle resulting in an exponential increase of DNA. The number
of PCR cycles is between 25-60 depending on the expected yield of PCR product.
The qPCR product, also known as the amplicon, can be analysed by diﬀerent methods.
When using agarose gel electrophoresis, the amplicons are separated on the basis of size
and charge [37].
Another method is quantitative PCR (qPCR). The method allows for
detection and quantiﬁcation of the target DNA as the reaction runs. The amount of produced
amplicons per cycles is measured by the intensity of a ﬂuorescent molecule. A commonly
used ﬂuorescent DNA binding dye is EveGreen®.
When it is bound to double-stranded
DNA it emits ﬂuorescence and the intensity of ﬂuorescence increases proportionally to the
concentration of amplicons. However, the binding dye has some disadvantages as it can bind
to any double-stranded DNA including primer dimers. It is therefore important do design
primers that only bind to the speciﬁc target sequence. Melt curve analysis is used to ensure
that only one type of product is ampliﬁed [37].
The point in PCR cycle when the target ampliﬁcation ﬁrst is detected is referred to as

==PAGE:36==

DTU
cycle threshold (Ct). This value indicates at which time or cycle the ﬂuorescence intensity is
greater than the background level (known as the baseline). The Ct value is usually calculated
automatically and corresponds to the ﬂuorescence signal which is three standard deviations
above the mean baseline values within the exponential phase. Thus, the greater the amount
of produced target DNA is in the starting material the faster the ﬂuorescence crosses the
threshold and thereby yielding a lower Ct [83]. Figure 5 shows a qPCR plot with the relevant
parameters.

The Petri factor is used to give an estimate of the average incident ﬂuence rate over the

4.10
Modelling of UV inactivation kinetics
UV inactivation kinetics of each bacterium in the diﬀerent water types was mathematically
determined from dose-response curves using the ﬁrst-order inactivation model (equation 2),
the Geeraerd’s log-linear plus tail model or the Weibull plus tail model. The latter models
considered eﬀects of shoulder and tailing in contrast to the ﬁrst-order model. To evaluate
the eﬃciency of UV inactivation, the inactivation rate constants were calculated - using the
linear part of the survivor plots - from the log-linear model. Geeraerd’s log-linear model [32]
was used with equation 8:
N = (N0 −Nres) × e−kmax×t + Nres
(8)
where N0 is the initial bacteria concentration (log CFU/ml), t is the applied dose for UV
(mJ/cm2), Nres is the residual population density (log CFU/ml), and kmax is the maximum
inactivation rate, given as the slope of the linear part (cm2/mJ).
The Weibull with tail model [4] was used with equation 9:
N = (N0 −Nres) × 10(−( 1
δ )p) + Nres
(9)
where δ is the UV dose of the ﬁrst decimal reduction (mJ/cm2) and p is the shape parameter
(dimensionless) describing concavity or convexity of the curve.
4.11
Statistical analysis
Graphs were constructed by using Microsoft Oﬃce Excel 2016.
A one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to analyse diﬀerences between experi-
ments by the data analysis add-in of Microsoft Oﬃce Excel 2016. Diﬀerences at p<0.05 were
considered signiﬁcant. The values of root mean square error (RMSE) and correlation coef-
ﬁcient R2 were calculated by the GInaFiT programme. An RMSE less than 1.0 indicated a
good ﬁt. The model with the lowest RMSE was considered to be the best ﬁt for inactivation.
An R2 > 0.9 indicated a good ﬁt. 1 CFU was used in the calculation of the limit of detection
(LOD) resulting in a LOD of 0.7 log CFU/ml: (1 ml/0.2 ml) × 100 × 1 log CFU/ml.

Discussion
6.1
Eﬀect of turbidity and absorbance
The results in this thesis show that kaolin do not signiﬁcantly (p>0.05) aﬀect the inactivation
rates compared to the salt water control at the three studied turbidity levels (3.4, 8.2 and
20.4 NTU). In all experiments, a more than a 6 log reduction was observed at a UV-doseABS
of 20 mJ/cm2. Kaolin typically scatter rather than absorb incident light [29], which the
absorbance scan of kaolin in ﬁgure 18b conﬁrms. It indicates that a higher turbidity caused
by the presence of an inorganic chemical such as kaolin does not aﬀect the UV inactivation
kinetics. This is in line with a study by Farrell et. al [29], which found kaolin unable to
protect microorganisms against UV irradiation at a turbidity of 5 NTU.
The kaolin data showed that with no diﬀerences in absorbance, but with diﬀerences in the
turbidity, there were no diﬀerences in the inactivation rates. Therefore, the diﬀerent inac-
tivation rates found in peptone-water were probably due to diﬀerent absorbance levels and
not because of diﬀerent turbidity levels. Figure 18a illustrates that peptone has strong ab-
sorption at a wavelength of 225 nm and a smaller peak in the 240-270 nm region. The peaks
at the two wavelengths are due to the presence of peptide bonds and aromatic amino acids,
respectively [3]. The same absorption spectrum for peptone has also been observed in a study
by Jeon et al. [38].
The absorption spectra gave rise to study how the absorbance aﬀects the inactivation of E.
coli. The study with inactivation of E. coli in peptone-water revealed that as the absorption
coeﬃcient increased, so did the UV-dosesalt required to reduce E. coli. When the absorbance
of peptone was considered in the UV-doseABS calculations it was possible to obtain inac-
tivation rates which was signiﬁcantly (p<0.05) diﬀerent from the inactivation rates based
on UV-dosesalt. From the results, peptone appears to be a good absorbing material with
poor scattering properties. In addition, peptone seems to protect microorganisms against
UV light. The protective eﬀect against UV could be ascribed to the potential of peptone to
attach to bacteria, due to its particle size or as a result of the peptide bonds absorbing the
photons from the UV light [85].

==PAGE:83==

DTU
When the doses were based on the absorbance of peptone-water, a 7 log reduction was
obtained.
However, this was only obtained if the absorbance of peptone was below 0.62
cm−1. In addition, the corrected inactivation rates did not diﬀer from the control. All this
show that it is possible to adjust the UV dose if the absorbance of the water is considered.
Therefore, it would be preferable to continually measure the absorbance of recycled water in
a slaughterhouse in order to deliver the correct UV-doses.
From the results it is clear that the absorbance aﬀects the inactivation rate and that the UV
dose can be adjusted based on the actual absorbance of the water. When the absorbances
were considered for both horse blood and peptone in the UV-doseABS calculations, it was
possible to obtain inactivation rates which were not signiﬁcantly (p>0.05) diﬀerent from the
inactivation rate of the control. Since it was possible to obtain a similar inactivation rate
at diﬀerent turbidity levels, it indicates that the absorbance is more important than the
turbidity when considering the inactivation rate. It is expected that the same results will be
obtained with blood from pigs as it probably absorb UV light in the same regions as horse
blood.
6.2
UV inactivation of bacteria in used water from a slaughter-
house
The ﬁndings that it was possible to adjust the UV dose so it ﬁtted to the quality of the water
by considering the absorbance of the water, water samples from the slaughterhouse Danish
Crown were analysed to study if the same could be obtained.
As UV treatment does not provide a residual disinfection eﬀect the content of nutrients in the
water is crucial for the potential for microbial growth if the water is recontaminated after UV-
treatment [52]. Phosphate and nitrogen are essential nutrients for survival of microorganisms
and the content of the these nutrients was high in all three waters from the slaughterhouse.
Therefore, it is important to consider the content of nutrients when using UV treatment as
a method for disinfection of water.
The COD, which is a measure of organic material in the sample, was lowest in water from the
SL and highest in water from the WL and BL. Water from the SL had the highest absorption
82 of 103
==PAGE:84==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology, DTU
DTU
coeﬃcient, which may be correlated to a high COD [61]. One of the major contributors to
organic load in used water streams from slaughterhouses is likely blood [6]. But since the
organs have been removed when the carcass enters the SL, the blood content in the water is
lower in this unit operation. This indicates that water from the SL probably contains other
compounds capable of absorbing UV light.
The pH of all three waters was around neutral, and a neutral pH is a requirement for potable
water according to Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen [28]. The high conductivity in the water
samples is due to the high salt content in the samples, but neither the conductivity or the
chloride content exceeded the maximum limits stated in Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen. The
turbidity is an important quality parameter of potable water according to Drikkevandsbek-
endtgørelsen and may not exceed 1 NTU. The turbidity of the three slaughterhouse waters
signiﬁcantly exceeded this limit but regardless of the turbidity, it was possible to obtain in-
activation rates insigniﬁcant diﬀerent from the control (p>0.05). Actually, water from the
SL had the highest turbidity and the highest inactivation rate.
The absorbance is not a quality parameter in Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen, however the re-
sults of this thesis indicate that the absorbance of the water is much more important for the
eﬀect of the UV treatment than the turbidity.
The inactivation of E. coli based on UV-dosesABS occurred faster compared to the inacti-
vation rates based on UV-dosessalt. It indicates that even if the absorbance is much higher
(1.254 cm−1 than assumed (0.018 cm−1), it does not have a negative consequence in relation
to the inactivation rate. However, it was shown that the applied UV-dosessalt were much
lower than assumed if the absorbance of the water was not considered. This can be a risk if
more resistant microorganisms are present in the water, since they require a higher UV dose.
The fact that none of the inactivation rates were signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from the control
(p>0.05) showed that, by considering the absorbance of the water, it is possible to adjust
the UV dose so it ﬁts to the quality of the water. It is recommended that the food industry
uses a set-up where the exposure time is adjusted based on the absorbance of the incoming
water.
The inactivation rates of E. coli in this study ranged from 1.30 to 1.97 cm2/mJ which

are higher compared to inactivation rates found in other publications (0.44-0.91 cm2/mJ)
[75; 68; 35; 29]. The diﬀerences can be due to diﬀerent disinfection lamps since an inactivation
rate of only 0.31 cm2/mJ was obtained with UV-LED lamps in a study by Farrell at al. [29].
The experiment was performed with the same E. coli strain as used in this thesis.
The
variation in inactivation rates can also be due to strain diﬀerences as Sommer et al. [75]
reported that more resistant strains of E. coli required higher UV doses do reach a certain
log reduction compared to more sensitive strains.
L. monocytogenes was found to be inactivated as fast as the control when using UV-dosesABS.
Though, it was only seen in water from the WL, because the inactivation rate of L. mono-
cytogenes in the BL was much lower (0.81 cm2/mJ). The inactivation rate in the WL was
based on more data compared to the BL. This might explain the diﬀerence.
Since the inactivation rates of E. coli was the same in the three waters from the slaughter-
house, the inactivation rates of L. monocytogenes is also expected to not to vary among the
diﬀerent water types. In this study, a 4 log reduction of L. monocytogenes was achieved after
a UV-doseABS of 7.6 mJ/cm2, which is in line with the results of other studies. Matak et al.
[55] reported >5 log reduction of L. monocytogenes in goat milk at a UV dose of 15.8±1.6
mJ/cm2 and McKinney et al. [40] found that a >5-log reduction of L. monocytogenes in
sterile distilled water was achieved at a UV dose of 8.1 mJ/cm2.
The inactivation rate of the Gram-positive L. monocytogenes did not signiﬁcantly diﬀer
from the inactivation rate of the Gram-negative E. coli in water from the BL. However, the
inactivation rate of L. monocytogenes in salt water (control) was signiﬁcantly lower compared
to the inactivation rate of E. coli in salt water (control). This greater resistance to UV light
may be attributed to the thick peptidoglycan wall that surrounds the cytoplasmic membrane
in Gram-positive bacteria, whereas Gram-negative bacteria only have an external membrane
[38]. The greater UV sensitivity of E. coli compared to L. monocytogenes could also be due to
diﬀerences in repair eﬃciency. An analysis of DNA damage has showed that the cyclobutane
pyrimidine dimer formation is slightly greater in E. coli than in L. monocytogenes [31].
Comparing the absorbance scan of horse blood with the absorbance scan of waters from
the slaughterhouse, the peak seen in the 380-420 nm region for the slaughterhouse water is

undesirable since it might reduce the eﬃcacy of UV light.
6.3
Inactivation kinetics
In the present study, the inactivation kinetics of E. coli in peptone and kaolin as well as E.
coli and L. monocytogenes in water from the slaughterhouse cannot be explained with the
ﬁrst-order kinetics model because of the sigmoidal shape of the survival curves. Therefore,
the ﬁt of the log-linear plus tail and the Weibull plus tail models to the experimental data
were compared statistically. Both models ﬁtted the experimental data of kaolin-water and
water containing horse blood with RMSE. Survival curves similar to the ﬁndings of this thesis
have been reported before [7; 68; 35]. The inactivation data of water with peptone could also
ﬁt both models, but when the absorbance >0.29 cm2/mJ none of the models were able to
ﬁt the inactivation data. Water from the slaughterhouse ﬁtted also both models, though the
best RMSE values were seen for the log-linear plus tail model.
When the water was exposed to a UV-doseABS of 40 mJ/cm2, the results from the total viable
cell counts showed that more resistant microorganisms than E. coli and L. monocytogenes
were present in the water from the slaughterhouse, as the survivor counts belonging to the
tail were not below the LOD. It indicates that a UV-dose higher than 40 mJ/cm2 is required
if the recycled water has to be free from microorganisms.
The tailing phases in the experiments with kaolin-water and peptone-water was likely to
survivor numbers falling to below the LOD and the same was observed in water from the
WL and SL. However, the tailing phase observed in water from the BL was not below the
LOD. The tailing phase started after 99% (2 log reduction) of the initial microorganisms were
inactivated which is reported to be normal [35]. If the end of the survival curve corresponds
to a tailing phase, it means that the UV treatment is less eﬃcient with the applied UV
dose. Tailing eﬀects may be due to associations with particles in the disinfection medium,
nonhomogeneous treatments, aggregation of microorganisms or the presence of resistant sub-
85 of 103
==PAGE:87==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology, DTU
DTU
populations [7]. The tailing phase in the BL water can be due to the presence of organic
particles as microbes can be bound together by organic molecules, creating complexes of
which can aggregate [29].
When the Weibull plus tail model was applied to the data based on the UV-doseABS a
shoulder phase was observed (p>1) for peptone-water and kaolin-water and water from the
BL and SL. However, water from the WL did not show a shoulder. The curve for the WL
was based on more data compared to the other two waters which might explain the shape
diﬀerences. In addition, this may also explain the signiﬁcant lower inactivation rate observed
in the WL. Shoulder phases are thought to represent the period in which the microorganisms
receive a sub-lethal dose and little or no inactivation occurs because various photon events
have to aﬀect the bacterial chromosome until the number of lesions exceeds the capability of
DNA repair mechanisms [31].
The presence of repair mechanisms is another factor which can aﬀect the inactivation rate.
Dark repair has been found to be a less important repair mechanism than photoreactivation
[75] and therefore photoreactivation was considered during the experiments of this thesis,
where care was taken to store the samples in the dark. Sommer et al. [75] found that dark
repair by liquid-holding recovery did not play an important role in reactivation of E. coli
after UV irradiation compared to photoreactivation. The risk that dark repair has occurred
during the experiment is therefore considered to be low.
A study by Moﬁdi et al. [60] found that when E. coli was in a late-stationary phase, a 5.5
log reduction was obtained at a UV dose of 3 mJ/cm2 whereas a log reduction of 1.5 log
at a UV dose of 3 mJ/cm2 was obtained when E. coli was in the mid-exponential phase,
demonstrating how the growth phase can inﬂuence the inactivation rate. This is in line with
the results of this thesis where a reduction of 3-4 log was found with UV-dosesABS of 5
mJ/cm2.

6.4
Long-amplicon qPCR as a method for quantifying UV inacti-
vation of bacteria
TVC showed an immediate reduction of E. coli in salt water at a UV-dosesalt of 7 mJ/cm2,
showing that E. coli is sensitive to UV treatment at low doses. However, the qPCR analysis
did not show the same log reduction at this dose. Furthermore, no correlation between the
Ct-values and the TVC was seen. As long-amplicon qPCR detects damaged DNA and TVC
is the total number of colonies the same log reduction is not expected. The reason for the
low reduction seen by qPCR can be due to too few modiﬁed nucleotides (thymine dimers) in
the ampliﬁed region which did not inhibit the ampliﬁcation [64]. The results indicate that
qPCR analysis is not reliable to detect DNA damage at low UV doses.
By giving the water from the slaughterhouse treatment of UV-dosesABS up to 40 mJ/cm2, the
qPCR signal increased as the UV dose increased, suggesting that the 16S rRNA genes had
undergone more modiﬁcations as the UV dose increased. However, the reductions observed
with qPCR was not near the reductions observed for TVC where the initial cell concentration
was reduced below the LOD in the SL at a UV-doseABS of 40 mJ/cm2. This indicates that
a complete loss of culturability was obtained at a UV dose of 40 mJ/cm2, which also has
been observed by Nocker et al. [64]. The results indicate also that long-amplicon qPCR has
detected both dead and live cells.
In general, qPCR detected higher cell loads than the TVC, which is in agreement with other
studies [25; 24]. Since UV light has shown to induce a VBNC state in E. coli this might
explain the higher cell loads found in the qPCR results [86]. In addition, it is important to
consider the growth phase of the overnight culture so the cells are not in the death phase
when running qPCR [49]. As the long-amplicon qPCR method was not able to detect DNA-
damage in the 16S rRNA gene of E. coli after treatment with low UV doses the technique
has to be optimized.

Conclusion
The presence of the inorganic material kaolin did not aﬀect the inactivation of E. coli at a
high (20.4 NTU) turbidity level. The reason was postulated to be as a result of the kaolin
particles scattering the UVC light instead of absorbing it.
However, the presence of the
organic materials, peptone and horse blood, signiﬁcantly (p<0.05) inﬂuenced the inactivation
of E. coli if the absorbance of the water was not considered when calculating the eﬀective
UV-doses. If on the other hand, the absorbance of the diﬀerent water types was considered
in the UV dose calculations, E. coli was inactivated at the same rate even in water with
diﬀerent absorbance and diﬀerent turbidity characteristics.
The results from this thesis show that UVC light is an eﬀective treatment for inactivation
of E. coli and L. monocytogenes in used water from a slaughterhouse. It was found that
the absorbance at 254 nm is of much more importance than the turbidity regarding UV
inactivation, as the same inactivation rates were obtained at a turbidity of 19 NTU and 299
NTU. The thesis found that a UV system should be designed to account for the absorbance of
the matrix to be treated in order to deliver the correct UV-doses. One possibility is to design
the system (lamp output and exposure time) based on the highest expected absorbance of
the recycled water. Otherwise there may be a risk of using too low UV doses and thereby
not reaching the intended log reductions.
When the absorbance of the slaughterhouse water was considered in the UV dose calculation,
a log reduction of 4 was obtained at UV doses between 5 and 7 mJ/cm2. Therefore, the UV
treated water appears to be safe to reuse from a microbiological perspective.
The thesis found that log-linear plus tail and the Weibull plus tail model could be used to ﬁt
the inactivation data of water from the WL, BL and SL. Since conﬂicting results were found
regarding the inactivation rates between E. coli and L. monocytogenes no distinct conclusions
could be made. If further experiments ﬁnd a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the inactivation rates
between E. coli and L. monocytogenes, it is recommendable to design the UV process based
on the most resistant bacteria.

Future perspectives and experiments
The project has showed that it is important to calculate the UV dose based on the absorbance
at 254 nm of the water to be irradiated. In order to implement UV treatment of recycled
water in the food industry, more research is needed. Future studies might, for example, look
into:
• Test how a mixture of the three water types from the slaughterhouse inﬂuence the
inactivation kinetics.
• Do challenge experiments with a bacterial cocktail.
• Characterise the water in order to study which compounds that attribute to the ab-
sorbance.
• Test if it is possible to adjust the UV dose based on the absorbance in full-scale.
• Test how diﬀerent temperatures aﬀect UV treated recycled water.
• Study if high contents of nitrogen and phosphate aﬀect the water following UV treat-
ment.
• Investigate the correlation between qPCR and total viable counts when the standard
curve is based on UV treated cells or the change in Ct values.
• Investigate how likely it is that photoreactivation takes place.
