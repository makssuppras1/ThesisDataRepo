ABSTRACT  
The first aim of this thesis is to determine the impact of the client and the role of their decisions in projects. 
Part of determining the impact is through determining if there is a difference in how experienced and less 
experienced clients make their decisions and looking at how clients vary in terms of their time and timing of 
decisions. The second aim is to determine have the client consultants and project managers support the 
clients’ decision processes.  
Through interviews with client consultants and project managers from two separate project management 
companies, case examples of the private construction industry in Denmark have been examined. Among the 
findings were differences in how experienced and less experienced clients understand and work with 
purpose, own organisation, phases, tools and frames and specific knowledge. The similarities found in 
experienced and less experienced clients were network, conflicts and optimism bias when suggesting time 
frames.  
There turned out to be a significant difference in timing of experienced and less experienced clients’ decisions 
in the projects, but no clear indication of a difference in the time of the decisions. For all of the above, the 
client consultants’ and project managers’ methods for support has been established along with their 
methods to avoid or deal with conflicts in projects. The main finding in the support method is the distribution 
of information, as the client consultants and project managers are both delivering expert knowledge to the 
client and further trying to get the relevant information from the client, to fill in the informational gaps of 
the project.

 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 CONTEXT, IMPORTANCE, BACKGROUND 
Since the establishment of the Project Management Institutei and the International Management Systems 
Associationii in the 1960’s there has been an increased interest in the subject of Project Management.  
One field that often deliver themes for study and research is the construction industry. Within this field there 
is a growing body of literature that recognises the importance of the people involved and the contributions 
they add or peril they pose to projects. Likewise, the contribution of the client to projects run by project 
management companies has gained some interest in the later years (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006; Kometa, 
Olomolaiye, & Harris, 1996; Turner & Müller, 2004; Wilkinson, 2001; Winch, 2002).  
This thesis concerns projects in the construction industry where the role as Client Representative is taken on 
by a project management company. The client is therefore also the project sponsor in this particular setting. 
The habit of hiring project management companies has become common practice worldwide, and some have 
already pointed to the client-generated risks for project consultants (Kometa et al., 1996; Wilkinson, 2001).  
Project management companies need to be able to interpret the clients’ wishes and transform it into 
something workable. If the project management company does not succeed in understanding the wishes of 
the client, they will be poorly equipped to guide the clients throughout the project. This may lead to the client 
being less content with the service provided by the project management company and possibly also less 
happy with the result of the project.  
1.2 CONTROVERSY IN THIS FIELD 
Consequently, from the project management companies point of view it is relevant to consider which clients 
to engage with, how to engage with clients and how to pick the best suited project manager and project 
management methodology for that particular client. So far, however, very little attention has been paid to 
how the project management companies should approach the above mentioned challenges.  
Winch suggests the client should not only be seen as a ‘purchaser of services’, but also as a ‘strategic actor’ 
(Winch, 2014). However, not all clients will be quite ready for this role. To some clients, construction projects 
are their main business. To some it is a project they do every 5 years. No two clients are exactly the same 
and they all come with different experience and expectations (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006).  
One way to look at clients is in terms of their experience. The experience of the client can be part of 
determining if they are ready for the construction project. With more experience, the client might become 
better prepared for taking on the role as a strategic actor in the project.  
Previous studies have suggested that there would be reason to consider how best to match the project 
manager’s leadership style with the type of project at hand (Müller & Turner, 2007). This thesis attempts to 
show that there would be reason to match the project manager’s leadership style with the client at hand. To 
do this it is necessary to have a look at different ‘types’ of clients.

1.3 OBJECTIVE 
The objective of this study is to determine how the clients impact the projects. As clients have many ways of 
impacting a project, the framework of this thesis will be looking at the clients impacts on projects through 
their decisions. To further be able to determine if there is a significant difference in clients, this thesis will be 
looking at experienced and less experienced clients with the expectation that it will allow for patterns of 
differences and similarities to emerge. As time and timing is an essential part of most projects, this thesis will 
further be looking towards potential variations in clients’ time and timing of decisions.  
As the focus of this study is from the project management companies point of view, the thesis will also dig in 
to the support of the clients’ decisions processes delivered by the client consultant or project manager.  
This leads to the following research questions;  
RQ1 How does the client impact the project? What is the role of their decisions in the project? 
RQ1.1 How do experienced and less experienced clients differ in terms of how they make decisions? 
RQ1.2 How do clients vary in terms of time and timing of decisions? 
RQ2 How does the project manager/client consultant support the client in their decision processes?  
Project management companies with a good understanding of the above factors are likely to better deliver 
the consulting style most suitable for their particular client. This will enable the project management 
companies to pair the clients with the most suitable project managers and train those project managers to 
know, how best to guide their clients. 
The overall theoretical approach of the thesis can be seen in Figure 1. The gap or research questions that 
are mentioned above leads to the investigation with its choices within philosophy, methodology, method 
and data analysis. These will be following the ethics of the author and the scientific culture the writer is 
affected by. The credibility of the investigation depends, to a great extent, on the interaction between 
those parts. If the investigation is successful, then there is the option of a new contribution to the theory. If 
the new theory is of interest to others, it might eventually lead to a contribution to practice.

 LITERATURE REVIEW 
3.1 THE CLIENTS’ IMPACT AND RESPONSIBILITIES 
Considering the client as an important stakeholder is not a new trend within project management, but in 
recent years there has been an increasing interest in the client as a more active participant in the 
construction projects (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). There are, however, very different takes on this theme as 
some suggest for the client to pursue a more active role in the project (Aritua, Male, and Bower, 2009; 
Boyd and Chinyio, 2006; Constructing Excellence, 2013; Winch, 2002). While others mainly focus on the 
responsibility of the project management company to better prepare and involve the client in the project 
(Bettencourt, Ostrom, Brown, and Roundtree, 2002; Lindahl and Ryd, 2007; Schein, 2006; Turner and 
Müller, 2004).  
Especially, for project management companies the importance of the impact of clients should not be 
neglected as it is vital to the success of projects and thereby also the company’s income and reputation. For 
these companies it will make sense to better prepare and involve the clients in projects, but it may also be 
smart to look out for the clients who are already aware of participating actively as these clients are likely to 
lead to more successful projects.   
Most do agree that the client, no matter the level of collaboration, has certain responsibilities. One take on 
these responsibilities is the one mentioned by the British Construction Clients’ Group who claims it is vital 
that the clients take leadership for the sake of individual project success as well as to enable the 
construction industry to excel (Constructing Excellence, 2013).  
The Construction Clients’ Group include the following points in client leadership (Constructing Excellence, 
2013);  
- 
Clear client vision 
- 
Developing a procurement strategy that supports the delivery of your core business 
objectives 
- 
Adequate client resource throughout the project  
- 
Detailed brief  
- 
Clear financial objectives, programme and definition of success 
- 
Promoting best practice  
- 
Clear and collaborative procurement policy  
- 
Clear, decisive decision making  
- 
Working within the project team  
- 
Continually seeking improvements in performance. Be demanding (increasing standards, 
stretched targets, etc.) 
- 
Creating a working environment of TRUST between all the parties 
3.1.1 The clients’ impact on the working environment 
The client will be an active part of shaping the working environment, even if they are not aware of it. 
Bettencourt et al. claims that many problematic relationships are the effect of clients who assume their 
only role is to pay the fee in exchange for a service and do not undertake a shared role in the problem 
solving and solution development (Bettencourt et al., 2002). 
==PAGE:11==
3 Literature review 
 
 
 
Page 11 
Turner and Müller identified that on the best performing projects, the clients participated in establishing a 
good, trusting working relationship by developing a medium level of structure with high collaboration with 
the project management company (Turner and Müller, 2004). Furthermore, the best results for project 
performance was obtained when the involved where working together in partnership and the project 
manager was empowered (Turner & Müller, 2004).   
An American survey of owners from 2005 (FMI/CMAA, 2005) found that the owners who experienced the 
best results for time taken in each phase of the construction where the private/public owner group that 
ranked trust/integrity as the most important and ability to deliver projects on time as the second most 
important characteristics of the Construction Manager and Contractor (FMI/CMAA, 2005). Thus, it seems 
that trust has been pointed out as a relevant factor for better projects also by client organisations.   
Bettencourt et al. claims that effective co-producing clients see the “big picture” and maintain their focus on 
the primary project goals, therefore they are willing to compromise on minor points and defer to expert 
judgement of the service provider in order to reach their primary project goals and maintain a functional 
working relationship with the service provider (Bettencourt et al., 2002). The client can actively promote an 
effective and open working environment and concurrently contribute to functional conflict resolution, by 
showing tolerance when small hitches and hurdles occur (Bettencourt et al., 2002).   
Some clients seem to be aware of their own impact. When asked about the best practices owners applied 
to improve project collaboration the top three were as follows (FMI/CMAA, 2005) (The number in 
parenthesis indicate the percentage of clients in the survey who use this practice);  
- 
Provide a clear contact for decisions and approval (85%) 
- 
Openly share project information (83%) 
- 
Assemble the project team early and meet frequently (80%)   
The structure of the client organisation will furthermore be shaping the project through determining the 
project organisation, selecting the participants of the project and choosing the criteria for feasibility 
(Kometa et al., 1996).   
3.1.2 Perceptual gap 
Another phenomena that may cause problems in the relationship between the customer and the project 
management company is the perceptual gap (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). The perceptual gap occurs when the 
client and the project management company are on different ‘wavelengths’. The client might mainly see 
the construction project in the light of its own business whereas the project management company is 
concerned with all the details of the design and construction, but might not be aware of the clients 
business or business environment (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). Furthermore, for the client this project may even 
be part of a bigger organisational change, while for the project management company it is simply a 
construction project (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006; Lindahl & Ryd, 2007).   
This is further enhanced if the client lacks the ability to clearly articulate what they want in business 
process terms (Winch, 2002). The client is likely to see the purchase in terms of a facility, rather than a 
service - an artefact, rather than an asset (Winch, 2002). Unfortunately, the industry is rarely challenging 
this way of thinking (Winch, 2002) and the client may therefore not perceive the full influence that this 
project may have on the clients own company.  
In addition, the perceptual gap can appear during a project. Almost all projects will change somewhat from 
they are conceived until they are accomplished and some changes will appear due to the client becoming 
==PAGE:12==
3 Literature review 
 
 
 
Page 12 
more clear and precise on the project mission (Winch, 2002). Therefore, it is essential for the clients’ 
perceived success of the project, to ensure that the client has understood the reason for changes and 
approved these during the development of the project (Winch, 2002).  
Winch (Winch, 2002) suggest five different gaps to be aware of when managing perceptions in the delivery 
of consumer services in general (Winch, 2002); 
- 
Gap 1 between consumer expectations and management’s perception of consumer 
expectation; 
- 
Gap 2 between management’s perception of consumer expectation and management’s 
translation of those perceptions into service quality specifications; 
- 
Gap 3 between service quality specifications and actual service delivery;  
- 
Gap 4 between actual service delivery and information provided to the consumer about the 
service;  
- 
Gap 5 between the actual service delivery and consumers’ perception of the service 
delivered.  
3.1.3 The client organisation in a change process 
When considering the behaviour of the client, it is important to understand that the construction client 
organisation is in a change process. As construction projects are a rather large scale project for many 
organisations, it is often a strategic management change that is occurring, which will inevitably affect the 
whole client organisation  (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). Boyd and Chinyio assert that many clients underestimate 
the organisational change that is happening. When the whole client organisation is affected there is bound 
to be some emerges of emotions, because the change is so substantive and involves major resources and 
significant effects (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). Boyd and Chinyio mentions two types of emotions, but clarifies 
that both of them will occur in mixed form at any one time (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006); 
- 
Aspirational emotions deal with the feelings individuals have about the future, i.e. those 
positive views that people desire and those negative outcomes that people fear. 
- 
Consequential emotions are the feelings people have for events that have happened or are 
happening, and thus have a connection to immediate cause. 
Boyd and Chinyio further suggests that the illegitimate nature of feelings in business, results in a slight 
taboo and thereby adds to the difficulty of understanding emotions caused by construction projects in 
client organisations (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006).   
3.1.4 Sharing of information 
It is important, for the project manager to be aware, that there is a risk that the client is not sharing all the 
information needed for the project. There can be many reasons for why the client might exhibit this 
behaviour, Winch mentions the following (Winch, 2002); 
- 
The client did not appreciate that the design team needed the information;  
- 
Internal disagreements within the client body mean that it cannot arrive at a clear position, 
and restricts disclosure to hide this state of affairs;  
- 
The client does not give its representative…the authority to make decisions, and so the 
disclosure process is slow and insecure and decisions taken are overridden later by senior 
management; 
- 
The client does not have the organisational capabilities to communicate its needs clearly to 
the design team; 
==PAGE:13==
3 Literature review 
 
 
 
Page 13 
- 
The client does not devote enough resources to being a client; 
- 
The client is behaving opportunistically towards the design team, i.e. information is 
impacted because it does not trust its designers  
As can be seen from the examples, it may not be on purpose that the client exerts this behaviour. Schein 
further adds that the client might be insufficiently perceptive to see the reality (Schein, 2006) and Winch 
mentions that the client organisation may not have the capabilities to communicate its needs clearly 
enough (Winch, 2002).  
One way to look at the information available to and about the client is to use the Johari Window, see Figure 
2 below. The Johari window is a tool that categorises information as (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006);  
- 
Known to self 
- 
Unknown to self 
- 
Known to others 
- 
Unknown to others 
Winch describes the information of the four different arenas as (Winch, 2002);  
 
- 
Public information is both available and commonly understood by both parties, i.e. it is 
shared and not opportunistically manipulated; 
- 
Private information is known by the client but is not communicated to, or understood by, 
the design team; 
- 
Blind information is known by the design team but not communicated to, or understood by 
the client; 
- 
Unknown information is not available to any of the parties – this is the zone of uncertainty.

The Johari Window acts as useful tool to explain how it might for example be difficult for some clients to 
prepare an adequate brief. Furthermore, it helps explain how the scope of the project might change as the 
project management company can enlighten the client on the blind spot and explore the Unknown 
together with the client (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006).

In addition, the Johari window sheds light on how the phenomena espoused theory and theory-in-use can 
become relevant terms when working with the client. The espoused theory (what the client says it does) 
may not completely match the theory-in-use (what the client actually does), as these differences might be 
in the blind spot to the client (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). 
As sharing of information goes both ways it is important that the project manager shares relevant 
information with the client. This makes it important for the client not to have a negative impact on the 
amount and type of information they are getting from the project manager. Some clients implement very 
tight control and  exerts their power in a coercive way (Perkins, 2014; Turner & Müller, 2004). Among 
others, this can lead to defensive silence from the project manager, who might restrict herself to politically 
satisfying information rather than the right information (Perkins, 2014). Perkins also mentions the MUM 
effect, i.e. keeping mum about unfavourable messages (Perkins, 2014). The client exerting coercive 
behaviour will therefore risk getting less accurate information and not being informed about e.g. changes in 
project processes, new ideas and honest opinions (Perkins, 2014).  
3.1.5 Conflicts 
When taking decisions in a communicatively healthy environment, there is bound to be some debate and 
possibly even conflict. Conflicts can be constructive, but not all conflicts are, so it is important to be aware 
of the type of conflict in the group. If it is a cognitive conflict it is task oriented and the debate will be about 
issues and ideas (Roberto, 2009). This type of conflict may be part of clarifying the goal structure by 
challenging existing goals (Vaaland, 2004). An affective conflict, however, is about personality clashes, 
anger and personal friction and will typically be of an emotional and personal nature (Roberto, 2009).  
Many of the less constructive conflicts may be avoided if both the client and the project management 
company are being aware of keeping a healthy communicative environment, where it is possible to ask 
potentially unpopular questions and where the conflicts are kept constructive in order to achieve the best 
working relationship. Vaaland (Vaaland, 2004) suggest that the parties adopt a more relaxed view about the 
role of conflict, recognizing it as a natural and even valuable ingredient in any well-developed interaction. 
This view implies not limiting conflicts in general, but being aware of how to use conflicts in a constructive 
way.  
In a “well-developed” relationship, thanks to open communication, the parties acknowledge that they have 
different perceptions of conflict events and design the project to take these differences into account, having 
a positive effect on the relationship (Vaaland, 2004).  
It is also important to remember that the situation and the subjective experience of the situation can 
change very quickly. To demonstrate this, Karpman’s drama triangle can be seen in Figure 3 below.

As seen in the figure there is three different roles, the Persecutor, the Victim and the Rescuer. It is likely for 
both the client and the project management company to feel like they are going through more of these

on the project affects the reputation of the company and thereby their long-term commercial viability as 
other clients will be reluctant to employ the project management company (Kometa et al., 1996).  
To handle these differences in perception Vaaland suggest that open communication, due to “well-
developed” relationships will help the parties acknowledge the differences in their perception of the 
conflict events and thereby enable a project design that accommodates these differences (Vaaland, 2004).   
3.2 CLARIFICATION OF TERMINOLOGY 
As can be seen from the above, the client will have a major impact on the collaboration and thereby impact 
on the project. One aspect that can influence the way in which the relationship between clients and project 
managers develop is the experience of the client. This thesis will examine different case examples of 
experienced and less experienced clients, to explore if there is a difference in how these clients impact the 
project. First it will be necessary to define some basic project definitions and then move on to an 
explanation of the experienced and less experienced client.  
A project is defined by PMI (Project Management Institute (PMI), 2000) as a temporary endeavour 
undertaken to create a unique product or service.   
It has become common practice to employ a project manager through a separate project management 
company (Wilkinson, 2001). The project manager is an external to the client organisation. The project 
manager [PM] is the person assigned by the performing organization to achieve the project objectives. 
(Project Management Institute, 2008) 
It is often seen that clients employ a client consultant to help them administrate the project. A client 
consultant [CC] is the client’s independent representative and adviser (FRI, 2012). The client consultant 
safeguards the interests of the client in matters involving the designers [engineers] and the contractors (FRI, 
2012).  
As this thesis concerns the projects in the construction industry, the most frequently used term client will 
be applied, to the project sponsor role. The project client will be defined as the person or group that 
provides the financial resources, in cash or in kind, for the project. (Project Management Institute, 2008) 
Experienced clients know what they want and have taken decisive measures to make sure they achieve it 
(Latham, 1994). The experienced client would be able to undertake the construction project itself already, 
build on a regular basis or is from the industry, but has chosen to outsource the labour and, to some 
extend, the risk (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). The benefit of working with an experienced client can be the 
clients’ familiarity with the process and the clients’ ability to engage actively for the benefit of the process 
(Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). On the other hand, experienced clients are at the risk of developing negative 
expectations and behaviours towards the industry, limiting the advance of the relationship with the 
industry (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006).  
Furthermore, Boyd and Chinyio (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006), mention that it is only an assumption that 
experienced clients should be more capable of dealing with the emotional process of change, due to the 
high stakes in the investment in the building, and the changes induced in the organisation.  
==PAGE:16==

At the other end of the scale is the less experienced client. Boyd and Chinyio (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006) 
question whether there is such a thing as an ‘completely inexperienced client’ as no client will enter the 
project without any knowledge. They go on to mention the ‘partially informed’ clients, who have procured a 
few projects, often spaced apart and the ‘uninformed clients’ who tend to procure projects on either a one-
off or very infrequent basis. In this thesis the term ‘less experienced’ client will be employed to cover both 
the partially informed and the uninformed client, as opposed to the ‘experienced client’.  
The less experienced client may not be prepared for the organisational development that is required when 
entering construction processes (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006).  The client may not be aware of, or geared for, the 
emotional side of building as it will include some significant ups and downs for the client due to the 
noticeable size and importance of building projects. Furthermore, the client may simply not be aware of the 
more common problems of building.   
Unfortunately, there is a chance that the less experienced client will have most of its knowledge of the 
industry from reports of projects gone wrong and others negative experiences as these are the most likely 
ones to be passed on. This could negatively influence the collaboration from the beginning.  
The Construction Clients’ Group acknowledge that not all clients are capable of leading and some will 
therefore have to rely heavily on their suppliers (Constructing Excellence, 2013). This is in agreement with 
Turner and Müller who mentions that there is a big need to cooperate as buildings are technically complex, 
the organisations involved are numerous and fragmented, the people involved have difficulty expressing 
what they mean, other people do not know what they mean, and people do not know what they do not 
know. (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006) 
When considering a subject like this, it is important to remember that just like humans and organisations, 
clients are not unitary (Boyd & Chinyio, 2006). Furthermore, it is important to remember that the client will 
be represented by one or few people. These representatives will therefore not be able to reflect the full 
diversity of the organisation and will subjectively, and possibly unknowingly, influence the opinion of the 
company as passed on to the project management company.

Rather few have focused on the level of experience of the client, and the impact it has on the projects the

4.1 DECISION MAKING  
Roberto talks about decision making happening at three different levels; the individual, the group and the 
organisational level (Roberto, 2009). All of these three levels are relevant, as all of the mentioned levels will 
be employed during a project. Figure 4 below shows some of the possible levels where decisions can 
happen at and across.  
 
Figure 4 – Different decision levels that may be seen in a project organisation and its surroundings 
It is worth mentioning that most individuals do not continue to look for solutions until they have found the 
very best solution possible, but will stop searching for alternative solutions as soon an acceptable solution 
has been found (Roberto, 2009). Most individuals do not go through mountains of data or examine every 
single possible solution. Rather, most individuals will draw on experience, rules of thumb and other 
heuristics when making decisions (Roberto, 2009). This is called satisficing and is part of what makes most 
individuals susceptible to cognitive biases and decision traps when making decisions (Roberto, 2009). This 
allows for repetition of systematic mistakes when making decisions (Roberto, 2009). Some of the biases 
relevant to decision making are discussed in the following part.  
==PAGE:18==
4 Theory 
 
 
 
Page 18 
4.1.1 Overconfidence bias 
Roberto mentions that; Psychologists have shown that human beings are systematically overconfident in 
our judgments (Roberto, 2009). This is further supported by Kahnemann (Kahneman, 2011), who mentions 
the illusion of validity. He illustrates this with a personal story where he, even though proven wrong 
multiple times, keeps his confidence in his own ability to evaluate other people’s capabilities of 
accomplishing specific managerial roles (Kahneman, 2011). Overconfidence bias is sometimes referred to as 
optimism bias (Geraldi & Stingl, 2016; Kahneman, 2011). Geraldi and Stingl explains how this can lead to 
the illusion of control, where individuals perceive the risks as far more insignificant if they feel in control of 
the situation (endogenous risk) (Geraldi & Stingl, 2016). Likewise individuals are likely to exhibit a greater 
dread of situations they conceive to be out of the own sphere of control (exogenous risks) (Geraldi & Stingl, 
2016).   
4.1.2 Availability bias  
The availability bias is the bias that leads us to place more emphasis on the information that is more 
available to us. Another effect mentioned by Roberto is the recency effect, which is a particular form of the 
availability bias (Roberto, 2009). The recency effect is when we place too much emphasis on recent events, 
which of course are quite salient to us (Roberto, 2009). 
Availability bias can feed in to the overconfidence bias, e.g. if the company or person has experienced more 
success than failures within a recent time period, they will not find any memories of failures easily available 
in their minds and will therefore be more likely to predict a high chance of success with their current 
project (Kahneman, 2011).  
This further leads to the self-efficacy theory. This hypothesis suggests that high self-efficacy might lead 
decision makers to think themselves better and quicker at a job and hence likely to be successful with 
projects, that others would not manage (Geraldi & Stingl, 2016). This is especially relevant as it might lead 
clients to believe they would be able to finish a project exceptionally fast. It might also lead the client to 
underestimate the chance of themselves being the reason for delays in the project. As high levels of self-
efficacy might further lead to an optimistic distortion of risk perception (Geraldi & Stingl, 2016), this could 
lead the client to underestimate the risk of both internal and external disruptions of the project.  
4.1.3 Confirmation bias 
Another common bias that individuals have, often without any knowledge of it happening, is the 
confirmation bias. The confirmation bias refers to the individual’s tendency to seek out and rely on the 
information that confirms the image of reality that the individual holds, and effectively avoid the 
information that proves deficiencies in this image (Roberto, 2009). This bias is also sometimes referred to 
colloquially as Cherry picking. This implies that it might be very difficult for a client consultant or project 
manager to explain a client why a particular solution might not be feasible or possible in their professional 
opinion.  
4.1.4 Framing effect 
Kahneman introduces the saying What You See Is All There Is (WYSIATI) to explain how the confidence that 
individuals have in their beliefs depends mostly on the quality of the story they can tell about what they see, 
even if they see little. We often fail to allow for the possibility that evidence that should be critical to our 
judgment is missing—what we see is all there is (Kahneman, 2011). This implies that, to the individual’s 
subjective confidence, neither the quantity nor the quality of the information is credited much importance 
(Kahneman, 2011).  From this we can conclude that depending on how information is framed, the 
individual will perceive it differently. Kahnemann presents a great example: The statement that “the odds 
==PAGE:19==
4 Theory 
 
 
 
Page 19 
of survival one month after surgery are 90%” is more reassuring than the equivalent statement that 
“mortality within one month of surgery is 10%.” … The equivalence of the alternative formulations is 
transparent, but an individual normally sees only one formulation, and what she sees is all there is. 
(Kahneman, 2011) 
Roberto explains the importance of this when considering decision making, as a situation framed as an 
opportunity will be likely to make the individual or group react in a more flexible and adaptive way. On the 
other hand, framing the same situation as a threat or potential loss will be likely to cause a more rigid 
response. This might lead to just doing the same well-known routine in a greater degree, rather than 
developing a new routine. However, when framing a situation as a threat, the team/individual are likely to 
work harder to avoid the consequences. Framing a situation as a threat is therefore likely to cause a 
noticeable response, as more resources might be allocated to the problem. These resources, however, 
might not be used as effectively, as they would have been, had the situation been framed as an opportunity 
(Roberto, 2009).  
As a client consultant or project manager it might be worth to be aware of this effect, as framing a situation 
as a potential loss can make the client react faster, but it might also make the client follow old, known 
solutions and be less willing to trust new solutions suggested by the CC/PM.  
4.2 PROJECT LIFE CYCLE  
Another common flaw is that many individuals and groups tend to make decisions without taking the time 
to figure out how best to frame the problem and deciding the decision process, before making the decision 
(Roberto, 2009). To be able to make decisions within the project it is therefore favourable to start with an 
overall understanding of the life cycle of projects.  
Riding the project life cycle is a term Winch (Winch, 2002) uses when describing the process of describing 
the project mission in greater detail as the project moves along. As it is described by the ‘rolling wave’ 
concept (see Figure 5 below), it is only possible to describe things in the near future of the project in great 
detail (Winch, 2002). The further out in the future of the project, the fuzzier the details.

is possible to move from the greater project mission towards a more detailed description of the

4.3 TIME AND TIMING 
As expressed by the iron triangle of project management (Atkinson, 1999) (see Figure 8 below) time is an 
essential part of the project along with cost and quality. Keeping the time schedule is often an essential 
part of the project being a success for the client. The iron triangle illustrates that all of the corners are 
connected and that one cannot be influenced without affecting the others. If the client wants to spend less 
time on a project, it is likely to cost more money and/or affect the resulting quality.  
 
Figure 8 – The 'iron triangle' 
Collaboration difficulties might arise if the organisations involved in the project have conflicting time norms 
(Dille & Söderlund, 2011). The organisations ‘local time’ will affect when each organisation is starting and 
ending activities and the speed of these activities. As it is essential to match the timing of activities within a 
shared project time schedule, it is necessary to adapt the local times of the organisations with the ‘global 
time’ of the project (Söderlund, 2002). 
When working closely together for months or even years, individuals and organisations are likely to 
naturally start displaying some isopraxism; homogenous practices (Dille & Söderlund, 2011). Likewise, this 
effect can also occur with the timing. The process of adopting “the same time” is called isochronism (Perez-
Nordtvedt, Payne, Short, & Kedia, 2008) and it is seen when the organisations involved start to display 
similar tempos and phases of their activity cycles (Dille & Söderlund, 2011).  
Not all project organisations will experience a state of isochronism, but it may give value to work actively 
towards it from the beginning of the project when aligning expectations of work processes. This can result 
in an awareness of the organisational differences and the various time constraints and time related 
demands and risks each organisation is subject to.

 METHODOLOGY 
Case study research was chosen as the preferred methodology, because this thesis is concerned with 
developing theory over a contemporary phenomenon. The case study research allows for theoretical 
sampling of cases suited to reveal how clients make decisions in a real-life environment within the 
contemporary phenomena of projects. As no clients, project management companies or projects are alike, 
this thesis faces a research problem with far more variables of interest than data points (Yin, 1994). 
Furthermore, it will not always be possible to distinguish the lines between the phenomena (the project) 
and the context (the whole project management company and the whole client company) (Yin, 1994).  
As a general rule, the case study research is used when the research question is a “how” or “why” question, 
that tries to illuminate decisions; why was a particular decision made, how was it made and what was the 
effect (Yin, 1994). The case study research is also a preferred strategy when researching contemporary 
phenomena, within a real-life context. Case study research is done from a variety of different sources, e.g. 
documents, systematic interviews and direct observations (Yin, 1994).  
Because of the short timeframe of a master thesis, the method chosen for data collection has been 
explorative research through qualitative interviews, using case examples to steer the conversations. The 
case examples have been limited to two: a case with a less experienced client and a case with an 
experienced client, both within the private industry. As these cases can be seen as extremes, they are 
classified as polar type cases (Eisenhardt & Graebner, 2007).  
The polar type cases are expected to produce contrasting results for predictable reasons, meaning that 
they are a theoretical replication (Yin, 1994). This is done to expose and thereby better recognise the 
contrasting patterns in the data from the cases (Eisenhardt & Graebner, 2007).

7.1 DIFFERENCES RELATED TO THE CLIENTS LEVEL OF EXPERIENCE 
The first question in the study sought to determine how the clients impact the project and what their role 
of their decisions are in the project. The first sub-question to this question is how the experienced and less 
experienced clients differ in terms of how they make decisions.  
The differences found by this study regarding how experienced and less experienced clients impacted the 
project can be divided in to five main categories as seen in Table 7, below. Along with the differences, the 
table gives an overview of some of the main impacts on the project and the support provided by the CCs 
and PMs. Each of the differences, along with the impact and support will be discussed in the following. 
Table 7 – Differences between experienced and less experienced clients 
 
7.1.1 Purpose 
The more experienced clients seem to have a clearer idea of their purpose and goals concerning the 
projects. When considering the Johari window (3.1.4), it would seem like the experienced client has more 
of its project-relevant information in the Known-to-self-part of the window. This will make it easier to 
express to the CC/PM what they need and want as it is only possible to actively express to others what you 
know yourself. 
This implies that when dealing with less experienced clients, the CCs and PMs will have to spend more time 
in the beginning of the project trying to clarify both for themselves and the client what the purpose and 
goals of the project are. In other words, making certain that this knowledge is not only moved from the 
unknown part to the known-to-self-part, but specifically to the Public area of the window. If this is done 
successfully it is likely that it will lead to a smaller perceptual gap (3.1.3) as there should be a greater 
understanding from both the CCs/PMs and the clients side of what the clients’ needs are.  
Keeping the goals and success criteria in mind during the project would help in guiding the client to make 
the decisions best suited for obtaining the desired project outcome. Likewise, having a clear priority of the 
objects in the iron triangle (4.3) will help the client decide e.g. if it is more important for the project to be 
done quickly or to be cheap.

7.1.2 Own organisation 
When considering their own organisation, the experienced clients seem to have a far better understanding 
of how to use and navigate within their own organisation during projects. This is both when considering 
who to involve in the project to find the suitable goals and success criteria and it is in knowing who to ask 
for permission and when to do that. This leads to a higher level of effectiveness in the project as there will 
be less unforeseen stops due to stakeholders within the client organisation slowing down or temporarily 
stopping the project.  
For the more experienced clients, the awareness of their own organisation seems to lead to faster decision 
processes within the organisational frame they have. Even though some decisions have to go past a 
steering committee that only has meetings every three months, there is the possibility of preparing them 
for the decision before they have to take it. This may lead to a higher certainty of getting a positive 
outcome when going through the official process of getting permission at the steering committee meeting, 
thus avoiding delays in the project.  
For the CCs and PMs, it is important to help the less experienced client find out who it might be relevant to 
ask or talk to in their own organisation during the project. Here the CCs and PMs will have to draw on their 
experience with other projects and client organisations to find the relevant individuals in the client 
organisation they are working with. It might therefore take a bit more experience to help a less 
experienced client perform well.  
When considering who has which decision responsibilities in the client organisation, it might also be a good 
time to consider responsibilities within the project team and clarify which responsibilities the CCs and PMs 
are willing and obliged to take within the current contract. This is to avoid having a gap between the actual 
service delivery and information provided about the service (Gap 4, 3.1.3). 
7.1.3 Phases 
The less experienced clients seem to have a more limited understanding of the different project phases and 
the purpose of these within the project. This impacts the timing of their decisions.  
First of all, the client might not know when specific decisions are supposed to be taken. Secondly, some 
clients do not seem to have the understanding of why it is important to lock the decisions, once taken.   
This seems to suggest a general lack of knowledge of the standard structure of projects among the less 
experienced clients of the construction industry.  
To raise the standards of projects it would therefore be relevant for these clients to have their personal 
level of knowledge raised before entering construction projects. One could also consider if a basic 
introduction to project management and the role of the client in the construction industry should be 
offered to less experienced clients. This would allow for a better coordination of efforts, give less wasted 
time while waiting for the other organisation to deliver their part and lower the amount of double work 
performed by the CCs and PMs. All together this could help deliver timelier and cheaper projects for the 
client.   
7.1.4 Tools and frames 
Just like a good understanding of the different phases of a project will help clients take timely decisions, so 
it is also practical if they are accustomed with at least some of the more basic tools of project management. 
This could be stakeholder management tools, the iron triangle, a risk log, Gantt charts, etc.  
==PAGE:40==
7 Discussion 
 
 
 
Page 40 
Knowledge of the tools and an understanding of the purpose may lead the client to consider more different 
aspects of the project on their own and thus move knowledge from Unknown to the Private arena, with the 
possibility of moving it into the Public arena. This makes it altogether easier for the client to lead the 
project in the desired direction and easier to avoid perceptual gaps.  
As the surroundings of the project will have an impact on the possibilities of the project, it is relevant to 
consider the impact they have on the project. The clients own organisation, the project organisation and 
the market are all framing the space the project is happening in. This is why the CCs and PMs are expected 
to know the market and the legislations that will affect the project. It will be the responsibility of the 
CCs/PMs to help set the frame of the service provided by them to the client. It will however be the 
responsibility of the client, guided by the CCs/PMs, to provide the information of the frame of the client 
organisation. 
7.1.5 Specific knowledge 
It would seem like the less the client knows about the way of projects and the industry, the more unrealistic 
they become in their time scheduling. This might be due to overconfidence bias (4.1.1) and the client 
having the illusion of control (4.1.1). This bias and illusion might be further enhanced by the less 
experienced client due to the availability bias (4.1.2). As they have little experience with projects, they may 
also not have any recent experiences of unsuccessful projects. This might further feed the high self-efficacy 
(4.1.2) of the client, making them certain that they can overcome the hurdles and therefore perform with 
no time- or budget overruns.  
The experienced client might still be somewhat unrealistic in their time scheduling, but they do seem to be 
closer linked to reality then the less experienced clients. This could be due to them having their 
confirmation bias (4.1.3) challenged by the recency effect (4.1.2). If operating in a client organisation that 
runs many projects, it will be likely for the individual to have information of recent projects with time 
overruns in the organisation more easily available in their memories. This will not keep them from being 
overconfident, but it might moderate their amount of overconfidence.   
To fill in the necessary gaps, it seems it might be necessary for the CCs and PMs to involve third parties to 
challenge the clients’ perception of the world. An example was given of using a third party to challenge the 
clients’ confirmation bias.  The example was given by a PM who explained the solution used for a client that 
did not seem to grasp the limits of what can be done. The client was given an unrealistically short time 
frame, and was then sent to the construction company, assuming that they as well, would let the client 
know that this could not be done.  
Some CCs and PMs mentioned that the experienced clients ‘spoke the same language’, meaning that it was 
easier to communicate about the project. Besides the benefit of it leading to smaller perceptual gaps it also 
saves time and consequently money. When the client needs everything to be explained it will either take 
more hours for the CCs and PMs, or alternately the same number of hours will be used, but less hours will 
be used on improving the project and more hours on simply informing the client. With less experienced 
clients it will also be necessary to be better at informing the client of expected consequences of their 
decisions and non-decisions.  
As it is mentioned both by some of the CCs and by the Danish Association of Consulting Engineers (FRI), it 
seems reasonable that the projects would have less errors and better outcomes if the time frame was not 
squeezed so much by the clients. Furthermore, it was mentioned by one CC that spending longer time on 
the planning phase, makes the execution phase faster. This seems plausible as it makes it easier to 
coordinate among both CCs, PMs, contractors and even within the client organisation.

7.2 CONFLICT HANDLING BY THE PROJECT MANAGEMENT COMPANY 
As the CCs are the advisors of the client and the PMs have the interest of delivering a project the client is 
happy about, it would be expected that they would be found in the Rescuing part of the Drama Triangle 
(Figure 3, 3.1.5). However, this does not seem to be how the client perceives the CCs and PMS at all times. 
Instead, smaller controversies and sometimes even fully grown conflicts can occur.  
When looking at the conflicts mentioned in the interviews they seemed to revolve around the following 
subjects: 
- 
Human relations 
- 
Economy 
- 
Time pressure 
- 
Felt manipulation 
The conflicts due to human relations may be prevented to some extend if the focus from the beginning of 
the project, from both the client and the CC/PMs side, is on building trust and working as a team.  
Furthermore, the project management company can, in the beginning of the project, focus on matching the 
CC/PM with the client, assuming that the resources allow for the organisation to pick and choose. Later in 
the project, it may be too expensive in time and resources to change the CC/PM, and only severe personal 
conflict with the clients’ representative render such a change necessary.  
The economy and time pressure conflicts seem to be very common issues in the industry. These conflicts 
are, however, likely to be seen as a bigger threat from the clients’ point of view. Smaller clients might not 
be able to afford much more than they had allocated to the project in the first place. For bigger clients, the 
budget might very well be one of the main factors the clients’ project manager will be measured on.  
The time pressure can be related to economy as it might cost the client money if the project is not done on 
time either due to e.g. lost production or rent.  
The felt manipulation, as described in the interviews with CCs and PMs, becomes less of a conflict between 
the client and project management company, but more of a problem for the project management 
companies. They generally consider it unfair behaviour from the clients’ side, because it often seems to 
involve a financial risk to the project management company. It might therefore limit the trust in the client 
and make the project management company reluctant to work with the client. This author would consider 
it likely that the client, if asked, would also describe some felt manipulation from the part of the CC/PM.   
There has not been a clear difference in the type and size of conflicts depending on the type of client. It is 
however reasonable to expect that experienced clients may be more willing to pick fights over the 
contracts as they know the loopholes of the industry. The less experienced clients might, however, be more 
likely to make big controversies over smaller issues as they may conclude a conflict to be of bigger 
importance to the project than it actually is. 
7.3 SOURCES OF DATA UNCERTAINTY  
Only CCs and PMs have been interviewed about the impact of clients. It is important to bear in mind the 
possible bias in these responses. To get a more accurate picture of the clients’ impact, it would be relevant 
to interview clients as well. There might also be a point in making a more stringent division between the 
information given by CCs and the information given by PMs. It is possible that there would be a difference 
in the impact perceived by the two different groups.  
==PAGE:42==
7 Discussion 
 
 
 
Page 42 
With a small sample size, caution must be applied as it might not be possible to extrapolate the results to 
the majority of project management companies or client organisations.  
Even though the author has strived towards objectivity, there is still a possibility of bias in the observational 
studies. To counter this issue, the better solution for the interviews might have been transcription. There is 
a possibility that some of the meaning could have gotten lost in the summaries or translations done from 
the summaries. The interview summaries were not sent to the CCs and PMs for approval. Moreover, it 
would had been very useful to be able to have an extra interview with each of the interviewees, both for 
the sake of further clarification of opinions and to be able to dig deeper in to each of the cases.  
Furthermore, it is worth noting that there could be other reasonable ways to code and categorise the data. 
This could affect the patterns that have emerged leading to different patterns and therefore other possible 
conclusions to be drawn from the same raw data.

 CONCLUSION 
8.1 SUMMARY  
The current study found that there is a significant difference in the decision processes of experienced and 
less experienced clients as the basic knowledge they have to base their decisions on differs widely, and 
consequently so do their decision processes. This will furthermore result in a difference of the impact on 
the projects. The role of the clients’ decisions in the project can be to work towards the result they 
envision, but this will only happen if the client understands the meaning and consequence of their own 
decisions. This also demands that the client has a clear goal to steer towards.  
When considering time and timing in projects, a significant variation in timing was found between 
experienced and less experienced clients. The experienced clients potentially leave a positive impact on the 
project as having timely decisions and information deliveries will help the project follow the time schedule. 
In contrast, the less experienced client might have a negative impact on the project as not being able to 
understand the timeliness of making decisions and delivering information may lead to delays in the time 
schedule.  
When considering the time of decisions, it is more uncertain if there is any difference among experienced 
and less experienced clients. It would seem the experienced clients with a more developed setup for 
handling projects might have longer decision processes, but have found ways to counter this challenge and 
there might therefore not be a significant difference in the impact due to the type of client in this aspect.  
Less variation was found when considering the realism concerning time frames, where the majority of 
clients seem to be unrealistic when it comes to making time frames for the projects. The data does suggest 
that there might be a tendency of less experienced clients being less realistic and more optimistic when 
developing their project time frames. This can potentially have a negative impact, as it leads to rushed 
through designs. 
When looking at characteristics that cut across the level of experience, the use of network seems to be the 
most constant one. Both experienced and less experienced clients seem to use their network to find client 
consultants and project managers they trust. Experienced clients are, however, likely to have a bigger 
network within the industry to choose from. The use of network may potentially be a positive impact as it 
may lead to a greater amount of trust in the projects.  
Likewise, conflicts occur with both experienced and less experienced clients. It has not been possible to 
positively confirm a difference in the size or severity of conflicts with the different types of clients. The 
cognitive conflicts (3.1.5) may lead to an improvement of the projects, but the affective conflicts (3.1.5), 
which seems to appear with both types of clients, potentially has a negative impact on the trust and 
consequently the projects.  
The overall difference is the experienced clients’ ability to help the projects run more smoothly by having 
their own goals and success criteria clear from an early stage in the project and being aware of and clear 
about the frame the projects are happening in. This has a profoundly positive effect on the projects.  
The less experienced clients will need more guidance on the part of the CCs and PMs. As this is a ‘lower’ 
starting point and more time will have to be spent on explaining on a more basic level, this is likely to lead 
to either a non-positive or possibly even a negative effect on the project.  
==PAGE:44==
8 Conclusion 
 
 
 
Page 44 
Naturally, it is important to remember that not two clients are alike and this goes for two experienced or 
two less experienced clients as well. As noted, experience is a variable thing. The clients might very well be 
experienced in one aspect of projects, but not in another. Therefore, even though there will be more 
characteristics of high experience when dealing with an experienced client, they may still be mixed with 
some characteristics that match what is typically seen in a less experienced client. Likewise, for the less 
experienced client.   
The support of the CCs and PMs towards both types of clients can be summarised as distribution of 
information. The CC/PM obtains information about the project from the client in order to help the client set 
her goals. Information about the client organisation and the role of CC/PM is shared to be able to define 
their responsibilities. The CC/PM also delivers information about project processes and the industry in 
order to define impact and consequences, clarify benefits of tools and frames and fill in the necessary gaps.  
For the project management company, the implications of this study may be further consideration given to 
the clients when making a risk log for the project. If the client has little experience, there is a potentially 
greater risk for perceptual gaps if this is not handled well from the beginning of the project. It may thus be 
suggested that the level of experience of the client is an important factor for the client being able to get a 
project they are content with. The other important factor would be the ability of the CC/PM in limiting 
perceptual gaps. 
None of the project management companies seem to want to lose a paying client, even if they seem 
challenging to handle for the CCs/PMs involved. As Denmark is a rather small market, there seems to be a 
big concern in avoiding a bad name. However, the question should be raised if it would be possible to turn 
down clients prone to affective conflicts and still keep a good name in the business. This might force some 
clients to reconsider their project-related behaviours and their impact.  
An issue that this study rises for client organisations is, as the Construction Clients’ Group suggested 
(Constructing Excellence, 2013), that clients need to take leadership in their own projects, in the sense that 
they need to know where they want to go with the project and why. To further improve the collaboration 
with the project management companies and thereby the outcome of the project it would be 
recommendable for the client organisation to prepare their own project managers by acquiring some basic 
knowledge on the process of construction projects.  
8.2 LIMITATIONS 
A limitation of this study has been the very limited time for the ‘field research’. Due to this factor, there is 
only a very small sample size. This has also left out the possibility of performing test-interviews before 
performing the actual interviews, to be able to test the form and effect of the questions and figures in the 
interview guides. Moreover, if there had been more time for data collection it might have been possible to 
have more interviewees and more interviews with the current CCs and PMs to explore deeper into the case 
examples. Furthermore, the interviews have been semi-structured qualitative interviews, to allow for the 
freedom of following relevant clues. This allows for solely qualitative conclusions and does not provide the 
option of making quantitative conclusions on the results. Having quantitative results along with the 
qualitative results, would be useful in building a stronger thesis with more generalizable results.  
This study was limited by the absence of one or more projects with both clients and project management 
company to perform an actual case study on. This would have allowed for more than one perspective on 
the impact of the clients and their decisions. Furthermore, it would have allowed for a more in-depth study 
of the decisions processes of the clients. 
==PAGE:45==
8 Conclusion 
 
 
 
Page 45 
The main weakness of the study was the paucity of specific case examples of decision processes of clients. 
Especially, when concerning the less experienced clients, it has been difficult to get much information on 
how specific decision processes were executed as the CCs and PMs were often not involved until the 
decisions had been made. Regarding the experienced clients there were mainly more general information 
to be had on the decision processes. The official decision process structure was sometimes available to the 
CCs/PMs, but this was the case only in some of the experienced client case examples. 
8.3 SUGGESTIONS FOR FUTURE WORK 
It would be very relevant to make a case study research concerning the decision processes of clients 
involved in construction projects to learn how the decisions are taken in these projects. As these projects 
are often big and expensive, it is very much within the clients’ interest to succeed. It would seem that the 
client itself has much of the responsibility, but might not always be able to steer via their decisions towards 
the goal they are wishing for.  
In future research it might also be relevant to explore what expectations the project management 
companies could or should have for clients. If the client is demanding professional project handling, is it 
likewise fair for the project management company to expect professional dedication of the client to the 
project? Within this line of research, it might also be interesting to look at the possibility for a project 
management company to turn down a client, based on the clients lacking performance, without the project 
management company suffering loss of other clients. 
It was suggested by some of the Project managers to further study the overlap between the Initiation and 
the Design and Planning phases, as it is a point where a lot of changes are happening to the project 
organisation. There will often be a change in personal from the project management company, as the 
project is passed from the sales team to the project management team. In this correlation, the salesmen 
were caricatured as professional optimists, whereas the project managers were caricatured as professional 
pessimists. Sometimes changes in personal are also seen on the clients’ side in these phases. There is 
therefore potential for a loss of knowledge and a clash of expectations during this phase change. 
As time and timing has been a rather essential theme in the interviews this seems to be a relevant subject 
for future study. This may be split in to three different lines of study: 
One concerning the unrealistic time frames expected by most of the clients. Some questions relating to this 
could be: 
- 
What would the impact of a longer time frame be on the economy of projects and client 
satisfaction?  
- 
If it turns out to be more feasible, then how to make clients accept longer time frames?  
The other potential line of study concerning time could revolve around the timing of decisions in projects. 
Here a suggestion of focus would be on how best to help the client understand the project process well 
enough to deliver information and take decisions in a timely manner.   
The third line of study could be the actual time of decisions in projects;  
- 
What is a normal amount of time for specific project decisions and what impact does this 
have on the project?  
- 
Could the time spend on decisions be shortened and would that be favourable?

8 Conclusion 
 
 
 
Page 46 
Last but not least – the author of this thesis has strived to add to the body of knowledge concerning 
decision making, project management and the impact of clients. Studies should also be conducted in order 
to disconfirm the findings of this thesis, for testing the accuracy of its conclusion.
