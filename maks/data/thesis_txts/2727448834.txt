Abstract
The immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) is hostile for immunotherapy.
Extensive efforts have been undertaken to modulate the TME with various chemokines,
angiogenesis inhibitors, or checkpoint blockade antibodies. However, systematic injecôÙ
tion of these drugs causes severe clinical problems related to both efficacy and safety.
Due to the short halfôÙlife of the drugs, high doses are often required. Moreover, when
given systemically, these drugs can potentially induce uncontrolled modulation of the enôÙ
tire immune system, leading to severe autoimmunity. Novel approaches to administer
immunomodulatory drugs to the tumor site in a safer, more controlled manner could enôÙ
hance their therapeutic potential and reduce toxicity.
It has been demonstrated that nanoparticles can deliver drugs to the tumor site with reôÙ
duced toxicity. To circumvent the disadvantages associated with systematic injection of
immuneôÙregulatory drugs, we will develop tumorôÙtargeting nanoparticles to deliver immuneôÙ
modulatory substances to the TME. We postulate that the TMEôÙmodulating nanoparticles
can normalize the cellular and vascular microenvironments in solid tumors, which will
greatly augment the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy.
In this study, we introduce a stimuliôÙresponsive nanoparticle that contains two sensor
points, one pHôÙresponsive and one enzyme responsive. Through this structural design,
we are taking advantage of the acidic tumor environment as well as the metalloproteinase
MMPôÙ2 that is functioning an enzyme and is abundant in many tumor types. In addition
to that, this nanoôÙbased delivery system will be easier transferred to the site of the tumor
due to the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, sparing healthy tissues from
doseôÙlimiting side effects.

Introduction
Cancer is a leading cause of many annual deaths and has a great impact on prolonged
life expectancy. According to recent statistics, in 2020 there were 19.3 million new cancer
cases and 10.0 million of those were deadly, indicating that cancer is becoming one of the
biggest threats to public health. The most prominent characteristics of cancer are its irôÙ
regular proliferation, the local invasion, and metastatic spread [2]. Tumors, based on their
response rate towards immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI), are categorized as either cold
or hot. Cold tumors are characterized by low immune response rate and the presence
of natural killer (NK), and cytotoxic (CD8+) T cells in the surrounding area rarely trigger
an immune response. Few examples of such cancer types include glioblastoma, ovarian
and pancreatic cancer. On the other hand, hot tumors accumulate a high mutation load
and respond faster to ICIs. Consequently, the number of NK and CD8+ cells trigger cell
apoptosis though three signaling sequences, as seen in Figure1.1, and become maligôÙ
nant. Some common hot tumors are melanoma, kidney, and liver cancer.
Figure 1.1: Cold and hot tumor environment.
Few of todayãs most common treatment strategies are chemotherapy, surgery, radiation,
targeted therapy, and immunotherapy. However, a recent therapy strategy that seems
promising is the chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy, in which patientsã T cells
are collected, and genetically modified so as to encode a specific tumor antigen receptor
and infused them back to the patient[3]. Primary T cell activation requires three signalôÙ
ing sequences and they include the antigen recognition, coôÙstimulation, and cytokineôÙ
mediated differentiation and expansion of T cells.
1.1
TME physicochemical barriers
Although great advances have been achieved in cancer therapies, especially through
targeted therapy and immunotherapy, there are still many challenges to overcome mainly
due to the tumor microenvironment (TME) and its physical barriers.
The immunosuppressive TME is a strongly hostile environment for immunotherapy and
delivery of immunomodulatory drugs since it is characterized by abnormal cell proliferation
and vascularization, resulting in hypoxia and nutrient deficiency. In addition to that, the
immunosuppressive and stroma cells along with some ECM components and the acidic

Oxygen deficiency, also known as hypoxia, is a prominent feature of solid tumors that
originates from abnormal vascularization and rapid tumor cell proliferation. It is also asôÙ
sociated with tumor metastasis and obstruction of antitumor immune responses [4, 5, 6].
Oxygen is affecting cellular responses through the hypoxia pathway, which is mediated by
the hypoxiaôÙinducible factors (HIF). In oxygen presence, the propyl hydroxylate domain
proteins (PHDs) are active and break down the HIF in the proteasome, while in oxygen
insufficiency those proteins are inactivated and allow HIF translocation in the nucleus,
where they bind in specific regions called hypoxia response elements (HREs). Those
elements are part of genes that encode proteins, whose role is to control adaptation to
hypoxia, some of which are the vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGFôÙA) and glycolytic
enzymes. Those factors are important since they are expressed by activated T cells that
promote antitumor immunity [7]. In addition to that, when T cells are activated the gluôÙ
cose and glycolytic levels rise so as to ensure their proliferation and effector function.
This mechanism is aided by HIFs upon TCR interaction under hypoxia [8, 9].
In general, T cell metabolic adaptation to oxygen alterations, especially under hypoxia
conditions, exhaust T cell function as well as the constant change of immunosuppressive
pathway and competition for nutrients [9]. For example, hypoxic T cells increase the proôÙ
duction of a specific oncometabolite, the 2ôÙHG, resulting in the alteration of their terminal
differentiation and promoting a specific phenotype[10, 11].
The oxygen levels are also affecting therapies that use T cell strategies in order to deal
with tumor cells, one of which is the CARôÙT cell therapy. Here Hypoxia is limiting the
CARôÙT cell expansion level, by stopping their differentiation into effector memory cells
and expanding the culture of T cells containing a specific antigen memory[12].

plays multiple roles as a mechanical support provider, TME modulator, and signaling proôÙ
moter. The ECM is a compartment of tumor stroma and is composed of fibroblasts, tumorôÙ
associated fibroblasts (TAFs), endothelial cells, such as pericytes, as well as immune cells
like tumorôÙassociated macrophages (TAMs) and lymphocytes. It also contains some nonôÙ
cellular components, such as collagen, fibronectin, hyaluronan, and others. Collagen is
one of the most important and abundant components of ECM since it makes up 90% of the
ECM and its concentration directly affects ECM density and stiffness[13]. In addition to
that, the density of a collagen mesh is guided by the posttranscriptional collagen maturaôÙ
tion. Furthermore, collagen formation comes after intramolecular crossôÙlinking, creating
macromolecules. The degree of this covalent crossôÙlinking determines the mechanical
properties of collagen that form the fibrins.

Challenges in CAR T cell therapy
As mentioned above, CARôÙT cell therapy has shown remarkable efficacy for hematoôÙ
logical malignancies but has shown insufficient evidence in patients with solid tumors.
Understanding of the mechanisms that limit CARôÙT cell effectiveness in solid tumors will
open the field of advanced CARôÙT cell therapy strategies. One of the main obstacles
that limits CARôÙT cellsã applicability is the immunosuppressive TME and its physical barôÙ
riers. Regardless of the physical barriers that inhibit T cell penetration and infiltration in
tumor sites, there are also a variety of different tumorôÙinfiltrating immune cells that funcôÙ
tion as barriers towards effective immunotherapy. Those cells are the T regulatory cells
(Tregs), the tumorôÙassociated macrophages (TAMs), and the myeloidôÙderived suppressor
cells (MDSCs).
Tregs are a subcategory of T cells, responsible for themaintenance of selfôÙtolerance but
also inhibition of antitumor immunity. It is known that they can inhibit T cell proliferation
and cytokine production while playing a critical role in autoimmunity prevention. Their
high presence in cancer patientsã peripheral blood indicates a poor prognosis. SpecifiôÙ
cally, Tregsã role is to suppress antigenôÙspecific cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) in many waysãÎ
However, in cancer, their population is increased, and they cleave off the tumorôÙtargeted
T cells. Thus, elimination of Tregs is one strategy that can be followed so as to enhance T
cellsã infiltration. Another category of immunosuppressive cells are TAMs, which are the
most abundant and are responsible for the infiltration of cancer cells and also associated
with poor prognosis in many tumor types. The way TAMs obtain cancer progression is
achieved by the secretion of specific growth factors responsible for tumor cell proliferaôÙ
tion, while at the same time, they secrete enzymes that assist matrix reconstruction and

1.3
Exploring TME with Nanoparticles
As seen from the above mentioned, tumor microenvironment has been proven challengôÙ
ing for CAR T cell therapies, inducing multiple risks of complications in cancer patients.
However, recent developments in nanotechnology have brought new approaches and
strategies for cancer diagnosis and therapy. NanoôÙbased carriers are advantageous over
other technologies since they are favored by the EPR effect, excluding healthy tissues
from toxic effects. In addition to that, their morphological characteristics can be tailored
according to the needs as well as the nanomedicine bioavailability and pharmacokinetôÙ
ics release profile. Furthermore, regulation of dosage according to patientsã compliance
should not be avoided. There are also many design potentials when it comes to combiôÙ
nation with therapeutic agents along with surface modification with specific ligands for a
tumorôÙtarget strategy.
The pathway NPs follow through TME is still challenging and emerges the need for exôÙ
tensive research regarding the immunosuppressive characteristics that could hinder onôÙ
target drug release. NPs are systemically delivered intravenously and through blood cirôÙ
culation, they extravasate from microvessels and penetrate the ECM so as to reach the
tumor site. The first barrier they meet is the extravasation from tumor vasculature. NP
extravasation might be benefited from the leaky tumor vessels due to the EPR effect,
yet the pericytes that surround the capillary walls and basement membrane (BM), limit
their penetration. Convective transport of NPs is further hindered by the IFP brought on
by arterial hyperpermeability and lymphatic dysfunction. The thick stroma is the second
barrier that impedes NP diffusion. The stroma is comprised of nonôÙtumor cells, like fibrobôÙ
lasts, epithelium, and endothelium as well as muscle and immune cells. Additionally, the
stroma includes the dense ECM with the fibrilôÙlike structure, which prevents NP passive
diffusion. Considering all those barriers that NPs must go through in order to reach their
tumor target, it is essential to further analyze them and seek for strategies that overcome
the heterogenous TME.
As mentioned above, IFP is one of the first barriers NPs must deal with and is one of
the key factors that limit their convection. Normally in tissues, the IFP is averagely 0 mm
Hg, but in tumor sites, the microvascular pressure is increased at a range of 10ôÙ40 mm
Hg. However, there is the paradox that IFP reduces NP convection but favors passive
diffusion. This can be explained by the fact that diffusion is a much slower process than
convection, especially for bigôÙsized NPs. Additionally, intratumoral blood and lymphatic
arteries are compressed by stroma cells, which reduce blood flow and further inhibit NP
penetration.
Pericytes, on the other hand, are important cells that surround the capillary walls and
promote angiogenesis, releasing the VEGF agent. Moreover, they regulate immune cell
entry to Central Nervous System (CNS) and control the brain blood flow. They also comôÙ
municate with other cells, like astrocytes and neurons. Thus, the perivascular coverage,
provided by pericytes, affects NP extravasation and is defined by the pericyte coverage
percentage in various tumor types. Kano et al. have classified malignant tumors accordôÙ
ing to their pericyte coverage into high and lowôÙ pericyte coverage subtypes, ranging from
10ôÙ70%. To be more specific, low pericyte coverage tumors, such as colon and ovarian
cancer have a coverage at the range of 10ôÙ20%, while high coverage tumors, like glioblasôÙ
toma and pancreatic cancer display 60ôÙ70% of pericyte coverage. Another factor that also
affects pericyte coverage is the addition of inhibitors, like TGFôÙöý, that increase vessel leakôÙ
iness and thus enhance intratumoral NP penetration on a 100nm scale. Therefore, the
relationship between pericyte coverage and NP extravasation varies for each tumor type
and also relies on blood vessel stabilization, which can be achieved by normalization of

The BM is one of the ECM compartments, containing collagen type IV as a main compoôÙ

1.4.3
Remodeling of NP design
Apart from the remodeling of both vasculature and TME, the natural characteristics of
an NP also affect their transportation into the tumor sites. One of the most important
factors is the particle size and the correlation to the transportation speed. It is well known
that smaller particles diffuse faster than NPs but cannot be easily entrapped in the tumor
tissue. This decreases the efficiency of the drugs and can even cause side effects by
diffusing in another site, like healthy tissue. Thus, the size of the NP should be tailored
regarding the tumor type and site of effect using different molecular weights of dextran in
tumor models.
The shape and the surface charge of a NP are of great importance regarding extravasation
and interstitial transport. In terms of surface charge, cationic particles target endothelial
cells better than anionic or neutral ones and perform better vascular permeability. After exôÙ
travasation from the interstitial space, the cationic particles will cluster with the negatively
charged hyaluronan, while anionic ones will cluster with the positively charged collagen.
As a result, neutral particles diffuse more quickly and uniformly in the tumor interstitial
space. Regarding how particle form affects diffusion, it is said that linear, semiôÙflexible
macromolecules can diffuse in the ECM more quickly than spherical particles of the same
size.
The ECM is a significant barrier for NP diffusion, but at the same time, its physicochemical
characteristics can be used in order to enhance the permeability and efficacy of the drug.

Dextran, on the other hand, is a natural polysaccharide, consisting of linear öÝôÙ1,6 linked

candidate for multiple therapeutic applications. Furthermore, AcôÙDex has multiple sigôÙ

techniques and also contains domains that are pHôÙresponsive.
The size of the particle should also be taken into considered since NPs over 300 nm face
many challenges in terms of their diffusion and transportation through the TME barriers.
Thus, the size of the overall nanoparticle should be withing the limit of 200 nm and so the
polymer and peptide size should also be tailored according to those parameters.
OnôÙtarget drug release is a key parameter for the NP design. Therefore, the synthesis

Conclusions and Future perspectives
The challenges of TME are strongly influencing cancer therapies as well as NP strategies.
The differentiations in cancer types and their phenotype lead to alterations of strateges for
NP construction in terms of sensor linkers, size, coating and surface charge. However,
NP formulation is a versitile tecnhique that can be easily tailored according to each tumor
characteristics. After all those considerations, an srNP model was constructed with dual
sensitivity in acidic environment and in MMPôÙ2 enzyme, which is abundand in most tumor
sites.
The double emulsion technique has been used successfully for the hydrophilic drug enôÙ
capsulation. AcôÙDex based NPs produced by double emulsion result into particles charôÙ
acterized by a mean size of 200 nm, spherical morphology. In addition to that, together
with NMR analysis and GPC analysis, showed that the produced DexôÙpeptideôÙDex has
around 11kDa mass in total, confirming the successful stnthesis of final MMPôÙ2 responôÙ
sive polymer substrate. Elisa test performed good ILôÙ2 releasing and the standard curve
displayed accuracy of the test. Furthermore, ILôÙ2 was faster released in presence of douôÙ
ble MMPôÙ2 concentration, reaching the ultimate in 25h. The NPsã surface charge in both
MMPôÙ2 and acid pH presence, showed relevant zeta potential ranging from ôÙ5 to ôÙ10, inôÙ
dicating good transportation through the neagatively charged interstitial tissue. Finally,
the synthesized NPs, when tested in Jurkat cells, showed good cell viability, regardless
of the NP concentration.
Despite the advantageous properties of AcôÙDex NPs, this technology is still in the early
stage of development, considering the limited performance in vivo data. First, the polyôÙ
mer synthesis and characterization must be standardised, to reduce batchôÙtoôÙbatch difôÙ
ferences. To avoid undesired NP degradation due to acetal hydrolysis, it is advisable to
monitor water and especially acidic exposure in every fabrication step. Second, reproôÙ
ducibility and wellôÙcontrolled NP production should be achieved, to verify the homogeneôÙ
ity of their physicochemical characteristics. Regarding the payload loading, a variety of
anticancer drugs should be tested, so as to record their encapsulation efficacy, release
profile and mode of action. Sterilization and longôÙterm storage should also be taken into
consideration for clinical purposes. There are few recommended sterilization techniques,
including aseptic processes and ö°ôÙradiation that can be utilized. Moreover, biosafety reôÙ
sults should be established for the nanoparticle system, its distribution pathway, and the
desired application. Finally, the current NP system can be also combined with other techôÙ
nologies such as ultrasounds, inhibitors etc., to improve penetration and transportation
through the barriers.
Apart from the establishment of in vitro tests and reproducibility of the process, this new
drug delivery system should be tested in mice models to test drug efficacy and the release
profile. One approach is to test the in vivo biodistribution by labeling ILôÙ2 with a specific
dye, like cyanine 7 (CYôÙ7), which is considered more stable than other stains, and monitor
its distribution in mice model. Another future prospective for this NP design, is to attach
