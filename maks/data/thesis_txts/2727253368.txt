Summary
Today, with the development of the Internet of Things (IoT), many IoT devices
have become an integral part of people’s daily lives, playing significant roles.
Among them, IP cameras hold a crucial position. Due to their popularity and
remote monitoring capabilities, IP cameras are highly attractive to hackers and
can pose serious security issues once compromised.
To enhance the security of IP cameras, using the honeypot can be a valuable
approach. Honeypots protect genuine devices by creating deceptive yet highly
similar ones.
They divert attackers’ attention away from the actual targets
and gather their attack data for analysis, enabling the further study of defence
mechanisms.
There have been several IP camera honeypots, however, they either require the
usage of real IP cameras, which increase the setting cost and complexity; or
use ordinary recorded video, which can not provide the interaction abilities for
attackers, making it hard to trap human attackers.
Facing these drawbacks, this thesis is centred on how to build IP camera honey-
pots using 360-degree video/images, to provide interaction abilities for attackers
without using real devices. In addition to presenting the design and implemen-
tation details of the honeypot, sample honeypots are also deployed on the cloud
for ten days and gathered some attack data.
These data are analysed from
different aspects to show the attack scenario.
Finally, the honeypot is com-
pared with other IP camera honeypots from various aspects to demonstrate its
exceptional features.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Recently, with the development of the Internet of Things (IoT), many IoT de-
vices have been widely adopted in people’s daily life like smart TV, smart lock,
smart bulbs and IP cameras etc. Among them, the IP camera is a very vital
type of IoT device since it is very close to people’s daily life, and the break of the
IP cameras can cause severe security issues like privacy leaking and trespass.
According to [10], IP camera systems face numerous threats stemming from
their inherent vulnerabilities.
These vulnerabilities may arise from outdated
or faulty security modules, inadequate patch management, insufficient access
control measures, inadequate physical security etc. Addressing the security and
privacy challenges posed by IP camera systems is particularly challenging due
to their limited resources. Additionally, many of these systems are constructed
upon outdated infrastructure, lacking the latest software updates. Consequently,
many recent attacks have exploited vulnerable devices, exemplified by the Mirai
botnet 1 and its variants which facilitated the denial-of-service (DoS) attacks
[1].
To enhance the security of IoT camera systems, one valuable approach is to use
honeypots. They are widely employed to identify active threats, gather evidence
of attacks, engage and mislead attackers by emulating specific functionalities of
systems or devices and record attacker traces.

This paper investigates how to design a functional honeypot that can help to

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter introduces the related theoretical background of this project. It
explains what is an IP camera, including its features and possible security issues,
as well as the protocol widely used in IP cameras: the Real-Time Streaming
Protocol (RTSP). Then, it moves on to the honeypot, discussing the related
theories and different types of honeypots. Finally, the terms 360-degree video
and image are explained and their attributes are presented.
2.1
IP Camera
An IP camera, also known as a network camera, is a type of digital video
camera that is used to send and receive data over the internet or a computer
network. Unlike traditional analogue cameras, IP cameras use digital signals to
send video and audio, and they are connected to a network via the Ethernet
cable or wirelessly through Wi-Fi.
IP cameras can be accessed and controlled remotely through a web browser,
a mobile app, or specialized software, which allows users to view live video
feeds and recordings from anywhere in the world. Some IP cameras also come
equipped with advanced features such as motion detection, night vision, and
==PAGE:12==

pan-tilt-zoom (PTZ) capabilities, making them useful for surveillance and secu-
rity applications in both residential and commercial settings.
However, an IP camera can be suffered from security issues and make it possible
for attackers to break in. These kinds of security issues may include using default
passwords, firmware vulnerabilities and physical security flaws etc. To mitigate
these risks, it is important to follow best practices for IP camera security, such
as using strong passwords and keeping up-to-date firmware. Also, techniques
like honeypot can help to trap the attackers, divert them from real IP camera
devices and gather the attack traces for further investigation.
2.2
Real Time Streaming Protocol
The Real-Time Streaming Protocol (RTSP) is a network control protocol used
for streaming multimedia data over a network, such as the Internet.
It was
developed by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) [15] as a standardized
way of controlling streaming media delivery.
RTSP provides a way for the client, such as a media player, to control the media
server, like a video streamer, and request specific media streams to be played.
It allows for real-time control of the media stream, such as starting, stopping,
pausing, and seeking, and also supports the delivery of live and on-demand
media.
RTSP uses a client-server architecture, where the client sends RTSP commands
to the server, and the server responds with RTSP messages. The media data
is typically delivered over another protocol, such as RTP (Real-time Transport
Protocol) or HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol).
RTSP is widely used in applications that require real-time delivery of stream-
ing media, like video conferencing, online gaming, and video surveillance sys-
tems. This protocol is supported by many media players, including VLC player1,
QuickTime2, and Windows Media Player3. In this SweetCam honeypot, a sim-
ple RTSP server is set up for attackers to view the videos through the RTSP
protocol.

2.3
Honeypot
Honeypot is the central topic of this thesis. Typically, the honeypot is a security
mechanism that creates a trap to lure attackers. A honeypot can appear to be
a real computer system that contains applications and data, deceiving cyber
criminals into thinking it is a valid target. For example, a honeypot can mimic
a company’s customer billing system, which is a common target for criminals
looking for credit card numbers. Once the hacker gains access, they will be
tracked and their actions are evaluated for ideas on how to improve the security
of real systems. Besides tracking the traces of the attackers, the honeypot can
also help to protect the real system by behaving to be appealing to the attackers
to divert their attention from the real application.
Honeypots can attract attackers by constructing some security vulnerabilities
on purpose. For example, a honeypot may use weak passwords or be designed
to suffer from some common vulnerabilities and exposures (CVE).
There are different types of honeypots, as listed below:
• High-interaction honeypot.
A high-interaction honeypot is specifically
created to engage attackers and encourage them to spend a significant
amount of time within the honeypot environment. The primary objective
is to provide the security team with ample opportunities to closely mon-
itor the attacker’s targets, intentions, and actions, while also increasing
the chances of identifying vulnerabilities within the system. In addition
to simulating the main system, a high-interaction honeypot may include
supplementary components such as databases that attract the attention of
attackers, who attempt to infiltrate them. This enables researchers to ob-
serve the attacker’s methods of searching for information, their preferences
regarding specific data, and their tactics for escalating access privileges.
• Medium-interaction honeypots are a type of honeypot that focuses on sim-
ulating elements at the application layer rather than incorporating a full
operating system. Their primary purpose is to perplex attackers or de-
lay their progress, giving the organization additional time to analyze and
respond to the specific type of attack at hand. Medium-interaction hon-
eypots provide attackers with greater interaction capabilities compared
to low-interaction honeypots, while offering less functionality compared
to high-interaction solutions. These honeypots anticipate specific activ-
ities and are designed to generate responses that go beyond the limited
responses provided by low-interaction honeypots [18].
• Low-interaction honeypots: Low-interaction honeypots offer attackers ex-
==PAGE:14==
2.4 The 360-degree Video and Image
6
tremely limited access to the underlying operating system. They provide a
static environment that restricts the adversary’s ability to interact deeply
with the decoy system. Typically, a low-interaction honeypot emulates a
minimal set of internet protocols and network services, solely aiming to
deceive the attacker without offering any additional functionality. Com-
monly, businesses simulate protocols like TCP and IP, creating an illusion
for the attacker that they are engaging with a genuine system rather than a
honeypot environment. Deploying a low-interaction honeypot is straight-
forward, as it doesn’t grant access to a real root shell and doesn’t require

a low-interaction honeypot may be limited, as it merely provides a rudi-

In this project, the built honeypot SweetCam is considered to be a medium-

2.4
The 360-degree Video and Image
The 360-degree video, also known as surround video[11] or spherical video, is a
type of video recording that captures a complete 360-degree view of a scene in
all directions. This means that the viewer can look around the video as if they
were physically located in the environment that was recorded.
This kind of video is typically captured using specialized cameras that have
multiple lenses or a single lens with a wide field of view. The captured footage
is then stitched together using specialized software to create a seamless 360-
degree view4.
This technology has become increasingly popular in recent years, particularly
for use in virtual reality and augmented reality experiences[16]. With 360-degree
videos, viewers can be fully immersed in a virtual environment, giving them a
sense of presence and interactivity that traditional videos cannot match. The
360-degree videos can also be used in a variety of applications, including enter-
tainment, education, tourism, real estate, and more[14]. They can be viewed
on a variety of devices, including smartphones, tablets, computers, and virtual
reality headsets.
With regard to the 360-degree image, it has similar features to 360-degree videos
with the only difference being that the former is a static image while the latter

Chapter 3
Related Work
Honeypot is a technology used to allure attackers by providing them with fake
targets and gathering the attack traces of the intruders for defence study. The
earliest honeypot techniques are described by Clifford Stoll in his book The
Cuckoo’s Egg [19], in which Clifford recorded how he set up honeypots to trace
and arrest the intruders at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory.
With years of development, many honeypots targeting various applications and
products have emerged. For example, Cowrie [2] is a medium-interaction secure
shell (SSH) and Telnet honeypot intending to record possible attacks launched
using SSH and Telnet. Cowrie often works as a component of a bigger honey-
pot, which pattern is also adopted in this project. The Glastopf honeypot 1 is
designed for web applications, it is considered to be a low-interaction honeypot
that emulates a vulnerable web server hosting many web pages and web appli-
cations with thousands of vulnerabilities. Clastopf can reply to the attack using
the attacker’s expected response [9].
In recent years, with the fast development of the Internet of Things (IoT)[13],
the related IoT honeypots also gained increasing attention. These honeypots
are developed to protect IoT devices or study possible attacks. For example, in
[21], Wang et al. proposed IoTCMAL, a hybrid IoT honeypot framework for
capturing comprehensive malicious samples aimed at IoT devices, based on the
1https://www.honeynet.org/projects/old/glastopf/
==PAGE:17==
9
observations that there are two types of services in IoT devices that are easily
exploited by attackers, namely the weak authentication services (e.g., SSH/Tel-
net) and the exploited services using command injection. And in [8], Luo et al.
proposed an approach to build IoT honeypots automatically and intelligently.
The honeypot named IotCandyJar uses machine learning technology and pub-
licly available IoT devices on the internet to generate potential responses for
requests received by the honeypot. Furthermore, along with the growth of IoT
devices, the used IoT protocols also see corresponding growth. , Wang et al.
[22] proposed TingPot, to test these protocols and study possible attacks.
Within all IoT devices, the IoT camera (IP camera) is a vital class and is widely
used. Targeting IoT cameras, several honeypots have been developed to enhance
the overall security of IoT camera systems. U-POT, an IoT honeypot framework
for UPnP (Universal Plug and Play) protocol, was introduced by Hakim et al.
[5]. It is a honeypot targeting the Universal Plug and Play (UPnP) protocol,
which many IoT devices use, including smart switches, bulbs, and IP cameras.
U-POT can utilise device description files to automate honeypots and generate
fake responses. In [20], Vishwakarma et al. managed to combine the IoT hon-
eypots with machine learning technologies. They obtained the attack data from
IoT honeypots and used it as training data to train the machine learning model
to study the newer emerging variants of IoT malware and combat the Zero-Day
attacks. However, these kinds of honeypots can only handle attacks launched
by scripts, they are typically unable to engage human attackers for a prolonged
period due to the lack of video streaming functionality.
Facing this challenge, SIPHON, a high-interaction IoT camera honeypot archi-
tecture, was proposed by Guarnizo et al. in their work [4]. This architecture
deploys honeypots on the cloud and uses traffic forwarding techniques to redi-
rect attacker commands to real IoT cameras in their laboratory to give attackers
the feel of interacting with real IP cameras. Similarly, IoTCMAL, a hybrid IoT
honeypot framework proposed by Wang et al. [21] also uses real IP cameras
to back up the deployment of honeypots using traffic forwarding. Nevertheless,
this approach has limited scalability due to the requirement of actual physical
devices and extra network bandwidth, and could also be too costly.
Besides using real IP cameras, recorded video can also be used, Tabari et al.
[23] proposed a multi-phased approach to build a honeypot ecosystem and built
a low-interaction honeypot for IoT cameras named POSTER which mimics the
D-Link IP camera and can continuously present recorded videos to attackers to
engage them. In this way, the authors gathered many attacking traces exploiting
existing D-Link vulnerabilities. Though recorded videos are easier to obtain and
use, they are not very convincing for human attackers since the attacker can not
interact with the provided video image.
==PAGE:18==
10
To allow the honeypot that uses the recorded video to be able to interact with
the attacker, Guan et al. proposed a way in [3] to make the video image to be
able to rotate and zoom in/out based on user control by using 360-degree video
and build a honeypot named HoneyCam. However, their solution requires an
extra video processing unit that presents different video images based on the
control signal sent by the attacker. And the used 360-degree video is not easy
to make for ordinary users.
Based on the drawback of Guan’s solution, we
investigate how to provide the user interaction function without using an extra
processing unit and use more easily obtained 360-degree images. The detailed
functionality comparison between IP camera honeypots can be viewed in Section
6.3.

Chapter 4
Functionality Analysis
In this chapter, we analyse the functionalities that the honeypot application
should have. The functionalities are analysed from two aspects, one is from the
attackers’ aspect and the other one is from the administrators’ aspect, namely
those deploying the honeypot application.
It is worth noticing that, in this chapter and the following chapter, the term
attacker(s) will be used to refer to those who are expected to interact with the
honeypot and try to break in, and the term administrator(s) will be used to
refer to those who deploy the honeypots and expect to obtain attack traces.
4.1
Analysis from Attackers’ Perspective
Attackers’ are those who will interact with the honeypot and will treat it as
a real IP camera. Therefore, to be able to spoof the trackers, the honeypot
application should be authentic enough and possess all the essential features
that a real IP camera should have.
To fulfil this goal, we will use the website Shodan1 which works as a search

Figure 4.1: The search result of Shodan
Among all the provided information, two parts ought to be put the most atten-
tion: the service a typical IP camera should provide and the user interface of
the IP camera webpage.
To investigate the offered service, we first check the opened ports. Since the
ports are used by the processes on a certain machine to communicate with
other machines, they can reflect the services that a certain application provides.
For the opened ports, different IP cameras opened different ones. However, there
are some ports that are widely used and are opened by most IP cameras, making
them to be significant flags of an IP camera application. After investigating the
IP cameras through Shodan, we can argue that the following ports are essential
to an IP camera application:
• Port 80. The port used by Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP).
• Port 554. The port used by the Real Time Streaming Protocol (RTSP).
According to the open ports, we can conclude that the services of the honeypot
application should at least include the web services and the RTSP services.
Namely, the attackers ought to be able to access the webpage of the honeypot

To investigate the user interface of the IP camera webpage, we can first check

• The brand image: The webpage should display the brand of the IP camera

• Username: When an attacker is logged in and redirected to the camera

• The control buttons: The video should be displayed together with several
buttons to manipulate the video. Typically, there should be four buttons
to rotate the video horizontally and vertically, and two buttons to zoom
in and zoom out the video.
The control buttons should be the most important features to spoof the attackers
since it is the most obvious difference between a real IP camera and a prerecorded
video.
The honeypot needs to provide the attackers with the experience of
interacting with a real IP camera by enabling them to control the view of the
camera image through buttons on the webpage.
The RTSP service is also vital for our application, which is a typical function
for IP cameras. Attackers should be able to view the prerecorded video using

4.2
Analysis from Administrators’ Perspective
Standing at the perspective of the administrator, there are some other func-
tionalities that the application should provide. As discussed in the following
sections.
4.2.1
Lure the Attackers and Record Attack Activities
Honeypots should be attractive enough to gain attackers’ interest, therefore,
besides the service related to IP cameras, we can provide some other services
that attackers value. IP cameras can typically be regarded as microcomputers,
and attackers may try to connect them using the SSH protocol to control the
IP camera for vicious usages, like being part of the botnet [17]. For example,
a botnet named RapperBot [12] targeting Linux devices will try to hack these
devices by launching brute-forcing attacks on weak or default credentials. Based
on this consideration, we can try to integrate an SSH honeypot within our
honeypot application.
One key function of a honeypot is to record the traces of attackers for further
study, thus, there should be proper logging systems to record attackers’ activities
when they manage to use the web service, RTSP service and SSH service.
==PAGE:23==
4.2 Analysis from Administrators’ Perspective
15
4.2.2
Administration
The application should also provide the administrators with some functionalities
to configure its behaviours. Since the goal of the project is to provide a cus-
tomisable honeypot that enables the user to adjust based on their own needs to
simulate multiple types/brands of IP cameras, therefore, the application should
provide the following functionalities for administrators to configure:
• Configure the brand image. The administrator should be able to upload
the brand image and configure the brand image to be displayed. There-
fore, the honeypot can better mimic the target IP camera and be more
convincing to the attackers.
• Configure the rotation angle and zoom ratio of the camera view.
The
administrator should be able to control the rotation angle and zoom ra-
tio when attackers press the corresponding button, since different types
of IP cameras may have different behaviours on this aspect due to the
manufacture and design differences.
• Configure the size of the displayed view. The user should be able to adjust
the size of the displayed video view.
• Change the displayed video/image. The user should be able to change
the displayed video or image based on their own needs to pretend the
honeypot is in a certain location.
• Configure the reaction timeout. Generally, a typical IP camera will not
respond to the user’s instructions immediately. Certain timeouts will occur
due to the transformation of the signal and the time the IP camera is
required to process. Thus, there should be an intentional timeout provided
by the application. Since different types of IP cameras will have different
timeouts, this parameter should be configurable.
• Configure the displayed medium. This application supports two types of
medium: 360-degree video and 360-degree picture, the administrator can
configure which one to use.
• Add fake credentials. Most IP cameras will have default credentials when
produced, and many people use them directly. Trying the default creden-
tials is a common approach for attackers to hack in, therefore, when users
try to mimic another type of IP camera, besides changing the displayed
brand image, they also need to add corresponding default credentials to
lure attackers.

5.1
The Design of SweetCam Honeypot
Based on the before functionality analysis, the core components of the SweetCam
honeypot are composed of three parts:
• The web services. The web services are responsible for serving the fron-
tend web pages for the attackers to log in and view the camera images.
And also works to provide backend services that support the front-end
service together with a set of APIs for the administrator to manage the
application. A proper logging system should be integrated into the web
services for recording the attackers’ traces.
==PAGE:25==
5.1 The Design of SweetCam Honeypot
17
• The RTSP services enable the attackers to view the camera video through
the RTSP protocol, a corresponding logging system should also be added
to record attackers’ trace of using the RTSP service.
• The SSH honeypot together with the corresponding logging system records
attackers’ trace of using SSH honeypot.
The web services and RTSP services should be built manually in this project,
yet the SSH honeypot can directly use an existing one: the Cowrie honeypot.1.
Cowrie is an open-source honeypot software that can be used to emulate an
SSH server and monitor malicious activities by attackers attempting to gain
unauthorized access [2]. It is designed to be easy to deploy and can run on
various Unix-based systems, including Linux, BSD, and macOS.
Cowrie works by creating a fake SSH server that mimics the behaviour of a
real server. When attackers attempt to connect to the server using SSH, they

For admin authentication, the JWT technique is utilized. JWT stands for JSON
Web Token. It is a standard method of securely transmitting information be-
tween parties as a JSON object. JWTs are typically used for authentication and
authorization purposes and are commonly used in web applications and APIs.
They are lightweight and easy to use, making them a popular choice for modern
applications.
A JWT is made up of three parts: the header, the payload, and the signature.
The header contains information about the type of token and the algorithm used
to sign it, while the payload contains the actual data being transmitted. The
signature is used to verify the authenticity of the token.
When the admin’s identity is verified, a JWT token will be returned. In the
following access, the admin should put the token into the Authentication header
to denote his/her identity. The JWT token is valid for an hour and the user
needs to authenticate again when it expired.

Containerization
To make the application to be easily launched and deployed, Docker and Docker
Compose are used together to provide and manage services. For the web services
and RTSP services, corresponding images are built using customized Dockerfile,
and for the Cowrie and MySQL services, the available images in the Docker hub
are used. The architecture of the containerized application is shown in Figure
5.11.
Dockerfile is a script file used to build Docker images. It contains a series of
instructions that guide the Docker engine on how to build the image. Using a
Dockerfile, developers can package their application and all dependencies into a
single image, making it portable and repeatable. Dockerfiles make it easier to de-
ploy and run applications in different environments without having to manually
configure each environment. Through the Dockerfile, we can specify the base
image, install packages, copy files, set environment variables, run commands,
and more. Ultimately, Docker will build an image based on these instructions,
which can be used to create Docker containers and run the application. In sum-
mary, Dockerfile provides a convenient, repeatable way to build Docker images,
making it easier and more controlled to deploy and run applications.

essary for every scenario. For static scenes, the 360-degree picture is a better
choice since it is much easier and cheaper to make. In addition, HoneyCam
doesn’t have the proper function to record SSH attack traces. These two draw-
backs are properly solved in the SweetCam honeypot, the administrator can
choose to use the 360-degree video or 360-degree image based on specific needs.
And the Cowrie honeypot is integrated to work as an SSH honeypot.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This paper is written according to the central topic: design and implement a
honeypot for IP cameras named SweetCam.
The paper first introduces what are IP cameras and honeypots together with
other vital conceptions, thereafter, some related research and outcome are briefly
discussed. Then it analyses the functionality of the SweetCam application from
the perspective of both attackers and administrators. Based on the outcome
of functionality analysis, the paper then shows how the SweetCam honeypot is
implemented in detail.
Thereafter, the paper evaluates the SweetCam honeypot, at this phase, it first
shows how the honeypot should be deployed in production, and based on the
gathered attack logs from deployed honeypots, the attack targeting the Sweet-
Cam honeypot is analysed. Finally, it compared the feature of the SweetCam
honeypot and other IP camera honeypots to demonstrate its outstanding func-
tionalities.
However, SweetCam only has a primary simulation of the web page of real IP
cameras, the real ones are much more complicated than the ones provided by
SweetCam. In further work, more authentic web pages can be built to provide a
more authentic experience for attackers. Besides, for the deployment part, now
it requires a manual process on the cloud platform. To simplify this procedure,

a continuous integration and continuous deployment pipeline (CI/CD) can be
built to enable the automatic deployment of SweetCam.
