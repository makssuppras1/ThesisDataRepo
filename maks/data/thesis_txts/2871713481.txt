Abstract
NASA with collaboration of ESA has plans of constructing a lunar base in the near future. The
harsh environmental conditions and the financial burden of transporting resources to the Moon, in­
centivizes the possibility of conducting lunar construction operations remote with in situ resource
utilization (ISUR). The prioritized method of ISUR to this day is opting for additive manufactur­
ing. For the purpose of automatizing the remote construction operations, this paper investigates the
accuracy of a constructed CFD model to simulate the additive manufacturing of geopolymers using
replicated samples from Apollo missions. A wide variation of Apollo mission measurements were
chosen to asses the CFD model’s compatibility with the variation of lunar regolith. The compati­
bility was assessed based on the variation of rheological parameters based on the Herschel­Bulkley
model. A definitive conclusion of the model’s accuracy could not be determined .

Introduction
NASA have planned missions to return humans to the moon to establish the foundation for long­
term scientific exploration at the Moon, land the first woman and first person of color on the lunar
surface. The plans were originally set to be conducted by 2024, which has been delayed with the
plans, at the date of this paper, then set to 2026 [1]. NASA and ESA has planned the concept
of constructing lunar bases to achieve sustained lunar development and exploration. The plans
from NASA aims to develop a sustained, strategic presence at the lunar South Pole called the
Artemis Base Camp[2]. This plan is set to be executed as a following of the Artemis mission,
returning humans to the moon. The missions of habitation on the moon is meant to pave the way
for long­term scientific activity at the Moon, as well as for the first human mission to Mars in the
2030s. In 2022 ESA completed a study of the concept of developing inflatable lunar habitats[3].
This concept includes transporting large inflatable membranes from Earth to the moon and further
protect the membranes with a protective barrier. According to NASA‘s report, the cost per kilogram
of material sent to the moon‘s orbit is more than USD 20,000[4]. Transporting material from Earth
to the moon in order of construction of protective barriers would become a large financial obstacle
for the projects. Instead utilizing the loose layer of regolith for In­Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)
would be a more realistic solution for lunar constructions[5].
One of the main issues of lunar construction is the harsh lunar environment. The days on the
moon equals 27 Earth days, and due to a thin atmosphere on the moon, the energy is not contained,
which causes significant thermal cycling[6]. The long days with low atmosphere results in the
temperature ranging from ­244◦C at the permanent shadow areas in the craters at the poles to
122◦C at the equator[7]. These temperature cycles can cause significant thermal stress, strain
and fatigue to to the operative machines used to construct lunar bases. This also means that the
constructed material must be able to withstand freeze­thaw conditions of this magnitude. The thin
atmosphere also results in a poor protective barrier against cosmic rays. Based on the density of
the constructed protective barrier, this could be required to have a thickness up to 5 m, in order to
reduce the radiation to a habitable level[4]. The thickness of the protective barrier should also be
required to protect against meteoroid and micrometeorites, as they cannot be burned up by the lunar
atmosphere. The protective thickness against this should be adequate at 2 m[8]. The gravity on the
moon is approximately 1/6 g, which results in an increased bearing capacity and great conditions
for construction of complex structures[9].
1.1
State­of­the­art
Researchers are investigating various construction methods, to render the loose regolith usable
for construction. These methods include sintering lunar regolith—fusing it into solid forms us­
ing focused sunlight, lasers, or microwaves. While this can produce strong building components,
these methods can be energy­intensive and too slow for constructing large structures[10]. Another
option, sulfur concrete, which uses molten sulfur to bind regolith, has been explored; however,
it faces challenges such as sulfur turning directly into gas in the lunar vacuum and its proper­
ties changing significantly with temperature[11, 12]. Polymer concrete, while performing well,
typically requires polymers to be shipped from Earth, which undermines the main advantage of
utilizing local resources and is financially inconvenient.
In this context, geopolymer technology emerges as a particularly attractive solution for building
on the Moon, especially when combined with additive manufacturing, also known as 3D print­
ing. Geopolymers are inorganic, concrete­like materials formed when aluminosilicate­rich sub­
stances, like the lunar regolith, are activated by alkaline solutions. A significant advantage is that

this geopolymerization can take place at lower temperatures and use less energy compared to the
production of conventional cement[13]. Geopolymers created in this manner often exhibit impres­

printing provides the flexibility to construct complex structures layer by layer, reducing waste and

1.2
Project objectives
The aim of this project is to evaluate the accuracy of simulating the printing process of geopolymers
based on lunar regolith. This evaluation was conducted by the following steps:
• Analyze the composition and collection sites of Apollo 11–17 lunar samples.
• Prepare these samples using relevant mineral constituents by milling and mixing the con­
stituents with the simulants.
• Recreate representative samples from each Apollo mission that reflect the highest and lowest
aluminum content observed in the collected materials.
• Conduct rheological characterization of the recreated Apollo sample analogues.
• Evaluate the suitability of the samples for 3D printing applications.
• Perform computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations of multilayer regolith structures
using OpenFOAM
• Use simulation results to analyze how composition­dependent rheological parameters affect
the stability of printed multilayer structures.

Background
2.1
Lunar regolith
Lunar regolith is the unconsolidated layer of dust, soil, broken rock, and glassy particles cover­
ing the Moon’s surface. It has been formed over billions of years primarily through continuous
bombardment by meteoroids and micrometeoroids of all sizes, as well as exposure to solar wind
and cosmic radiation[16]. Its composition and properties vary significantly depending on location,
primarily distinguished by two main terrains:
Mare Regolith
Found in the large, dark plains (maria) formed by ancient volcanic eruptions. Mare regolith is
derived primarily from basaltic rocks and is consequently richer in iron (FeO), magnesium (MgO),
and titanium (TiO2) compared to the highlands. Key minerals include pyroxene, olivine, ilmenite,
and plagioclase feldspar[17].
Highland Regolith
Covers the older, brighter, heavily cratered lunar highlands. This regolith is derived from the pri­
mordial lunar crust, which is predominantly composed of anorthositic rocks. As a result, highland
regolith is significantly richer in aluminum (Al2O3) and calcium (CaO) due to the abundance of
calcium­rich plagioclase feldspar (anorthite), and generally lower in Fe, Mg, and Ti compared to
mare regions[17].
A unique characteristic of lunar regolith is the significant presence of glassy components formed
by impact melting and complex particles called agglutinates, which are aggregates of mineral and
rock fragments bonded together by impact melting, often containing nanometer­sized iron particles
formed by space weathering processes[18].
2.2
Geopolymers
Geopolymers are inorganic materials formed by the alkali activation of aluminosilicate source ma­
terials, resulting in the formation of three­dimensional networks of mineral molecules connected
by covalent bonds[19].
The geopolymerization process is understood to proceed through multiple stages of the reaction.
The process initiates with the highly alkaline activator solution reacting with the solid alumi­
nosilicate precursor. This breaks down the covalent Si­O­Si, Si­O­Al, and Al­O­Al bonds within
the precursor’s structure in the more reactive amorphous phases[20]. The broken bonds releases
monomers of silicate and aluminate, that may form smaller and more stable aluminosilicates.
The dissolved or reorganized aluminosilicate species begin to polymerize through polycondensa­
tion reactions. This involves the formation of Si­O­Al or Si­O­Si bonds and leads to the formation
of a continuous, three­dimensional gel network. The activator provides readily available reactive
species, that accelerates the gel formation. The gel networks are often referred to as N­A­S­H,
K­A­S­H, C­A­S­H or C­S­H depending on the reactants in the gel.
2.3
Lunar regolith simulants
Numerous lunar regolith simulants have been developed over the years, targeting different lunar
regions and specific applications[21]. Examples relevant to the scope of this report (mare and
highland types) include:
JSC­1/1A/1AF/1AC: Developed by NASA JSC, these are low­Ti mare simulants based on glassy
basaltic ash from Arizona[21]. Known for its relatively high glass content, making it reactive
for geopolymerization[22]. Often used as a benchmark mare simulant, though availability is now
limited.

3.2.4
Apollo sample simulants
To represent the compositional variability of lunar materials, two samples were selected for repli­
cation from each Apollo mission. The selection was guided by identifying compositional extrema
in aluminum oxide (Al2O3) and silicon dioxide (SiO2) content. These extrema were determined
from a plot of lunar sample compositions available at the Lunar Meteorite site hosted by Washing­
ton University in St. Louis [27]. The target samples were identified by the content of these oxides,
since the content of both Si and Al have a major role in the reactivity of the geopolymerization
process. Therefore by choosing extremities based on this content, the targeted samples are also
extremities of formation of geopolymers of lunar samples. A total of 13 samples were selected as
targeted compositions for the project: two samples each from Apollo 11, 12, 14, 15, and 16, and
three samples from Apollo 17. The Apollo 17 selections included two extremities and one outlier,
the latter noted for its distinct composition compared to other lunar samples.
For the project, the 13 samples were targeted to be reproduced with a mix design of regolith and
5 lunar constituents. For the remainder of this paper, the compositions designed to replicate lunar
samples are referred to by the sample number of the corresponding targeted lunar sample.
Sample identification numbers follow a classification pattern based on their initial digit(s): num­
bers beginning with 10 correspond to Apollo 11, 12 to Apollo 12, 14 to Apollo 14, 15 to Apollo
15, 6 to Apollo 16, and 7 to Apollo 17. The two extremities identified for samples from Apollo 11
are both from the same bulk soil sample, 10084. As the design compositions were named after the
corresponding lunar sample, in order to differentiate, the two replicating compositions of sample
10084 were named 10084a and 10084b.
The design compositions were determined by comparing simulated mixtures to the published stud­
ies of bulk chemistry of the target lunar samples from the compiled compendium[28]. The bulk
chemistry of the regolith and the constituents, shown in table 3.1 and table 3.2, were used as inputs.
A custom program was developed to simulate compositions with a fixed regolith mass of 10 g. The
mass of each of the five constituents was individually varied from 0 to 20 g in 0.1 g increments.
Simulations were performed using both LHS and LMS as regolith.
Each simulated composition was compared to the target sample on the basis of the contents of six
main oxides that occur for the material: SiO2, TiO2, Al2O3, FeO, MgO, and CaO. The discrepancy
between the simulated and target compositions was quantified as the sum of absolute errors in
percentage points between the six oxides.
The composition with the lowest total error was selected as the design that best replicated the
target lunar sample. Visual representation on the relative oxide­content of each lunar sample and
respective mix design is presented in appendix A.2.

3.2.5
Preparation of geopolymer mixture
Preparation of the design mixture was conducted by individually weighing using an analytical
balance, with a maximum allowable tolerance of ±1% of the target mass for each component.
Following weighing, the dry constituents were combined in a clean container and manually mixed
using a laboratory­grade spoon. Mixing continued until the powder blend was visually homoge­
neous, ensuring uniform distribution.
The activator solution was added to the homogeneously mixed dry material using a pipette. The
activator was dispensed dropwise, with a tolerance of ± 0.04 g, which corresponds to the ap­
proximate mass of a single drop. The mixture was then stirred carefully by hand using a spoon,
promoting uniform wetting and the initiation of the geopolymerization reaction. To further en­
hance dispersion and eliminate any unmixed agglomerates, the mixture was subjected to vibration
using an IKA MS 3 digital shaker to ensure complete homogenization[29].
3.3
Milling
The properties of the geopolymer is affected by the particle size distribution (PSD) of the dry
aggregates. This parameter is however not a main topic of this paper, and the aggregates are
therefore aimed to be of similar PSD.
The acquired LRS in this project was already milled, while the constituents were unmilled. Due
to the milled LRS having smaller particle sizes in the distribution, the constituents are milled to
match the PSD of the LRS.
The milling was performed using a planetary ball mill (PULVERISETTE 6 classic line, Fritsch
GmbH, Idar­Oberstein, Germany). For the milling grinding bowl and Ø10 mm grinding balls
made of zirconium oxide were used with a ball­to­powder ratio of 20:1 and a rotational speed of
600 rpm [30]. The milling duration for each constituent is stated in table 3.5.

3.6
Rheology
Rheological measurements were performed using an Anton Paar MCR 502 rotational rheometer
equipped with a 25 mm parallel plate geometry. All tests were conducted at a controlled tempera­
ture of 25◦C, maintained using PTD 200 temperature control system.
Sample Loading and Gap Setting The sample was initially positioned on the lower plate, and the
gap was set to 2.05 mm to allow for trimming of excess material around the edges. After trimming,
the gap was reduced to 2.00 mm, intentionally producing a slight peripheral bulge. This technique
is widely adopted to ensure uniform contact between sample and plates, reduce slippage, and im­
prove measurement reproducibility—especially for soft or complex fluids [33, 34, 35, 36]
The rheological testing protocol consisted of two sequential phases:
Pre­shearing Phase: A pre­conditioning sequence was applied to break structuration and forma­
tion of inter­particle connections [37, 38]. The sample underwent oscillatory shear strain with a
linear ramp from 1% to 100% strain at a constant frequency of 1 Hz, across 10 intervals of 10 sec­
onds each. This protocol aligns with prior studies emphasizing strain ramping to assess nonlinear
viscoelastic transitions and precondition structured fluids [39, 40].
Flow Sweep Phase (Steady Shear Mode): Immediately after pre­shearing, a steady shear test was
conducted to characterize flow behavior:
Additive manufacturing with recreated Apollo mission regolith materials
11
==PAGE:17==
Ramp­Up: Shear rate increased linearly from 2 to 40 s−1, over 15 data points, each held for 30
seconds.
Ramp­Down: Shear rate decreased linearly from 40 to 2 s−1, again over 15 data points at 30­second
intervals.
The duration from mixing the dry material with the activator to beginning pre­shearing was kept
in the interval 6­8 minutes, to prevent variation of time as being a factor [25]. The duration was
estimated to approximately match the duration of geopolymerization until the printing has started,
when 3D printing the structures.
All measurements and instrument operations were managed using Anton Paar RheoCompass soft­
ware.

3.7.2
CFD model setup
The structure of modeling in OpenFOAM is based on customizing the content of the three main
folders:constant, system, and 0
The constant directory
This directory contains information that remains constant throughout the entire simulation. The
contents and function is presented below:
• PolyMesh: The collected mesh generated for the simulation. This contains information of
points of the mesh vertices including their coordinates, the faces constructed of the points
and their neighbours.
• 6DoF: Data file which the defines the movement path of the overset mesh
• dynamicMeshDict: Defines the solver to control the motion and deformation of the mesh
• g: Defines the gravity vector
• transportProperties: Specifies rheological properties for printed material and surround­
ing air. The properties are described as Herschel­Bulkley fluid for the printed material and
Newtonian fluid for the air.
• turbulenceProperties: Sets the turbulence model to run as laminar.
The system directory
This directory controls the solution procedure, including solver settings, discretization schemes,
and run­time controls. The contents and function is presented below:
• blockMeshDict: Defines geometry of domain and blocks for meshing including cell count
of each block for blockMesh utilization.
• controlDict: Manages general simulation parameters like start/end time, time step, and
data writing.
• decomposeParDict: Configures domain decomposition for parallel processing.

3.7.3
Project­specific modeling
Replicating the printing process of the multi­layered bars, required implementing the same nozzle
as used for the experimental printing.
The 3D file used to print the nozzle (see fig. 3.4), was utilized by applying cfmesh to generate
the nozzle mesh for the simulation (see fig. 3.5) by following a user guide[41] . The nozzle was
meshed using cartesianMesh. The cells in the bounding box for the mesh was scaled such that
the spacing from the geometry to the outer bounds of the bounding box was at least 6 cells. This
spacing was needed to prevent errors such as snapping errors and failed meshing. This guideline
is based on best practices communicated in forums and OpenFOAM training materials.
For the simulations to replicate the printing process, the nozzle in the simulation was given the
movement in 6DoF based on Listing 3.1. The domain dimensions are defined to ensure the bound­
ing box with the nozzle remains within bounds throughout its translation. The nozzle outlet was
located at the initial Z­coordinate of 0. To ensure space for first printed layer, the domain bottom
plane acting as a surface was defined at Z­coordinate of ­0.0036, given that the layer height was
3.6 mm. The domain was split into two blocks in blockMeshDict, with the lower block ranging
from ­0.0036 to 0.018 in the Z­direction while the upper block ranging from 0.018 to 0.0858 in the
Z­direction. The range of the lower block was set up to the printing height of the final layer of the
simulation. The blocks were split up in 121 cells in the X­direction and 51 cells in the y­direction.
The lower block was split in 40 cells in Z­direction while the upper block was split in 45 cells in Z­
direction. The lower block had higher concentration of cells due to the simulation printing only in
this block, and the simulated results in this area being the priority. The grading in the lower block
was uniform whereas the upper block was stretched in the z­direction with simpleGrading(1 1
3).

switch to the height of the next layer, was defined with the same velocity as the extrusion velocity.
This was defined, as other values would result in either the material would accumulate and bulge
or the space would not be filled which would cause gaps in the extrusion. The motion was defined
as printing 50 mm in x, then raise 3.6 mm in z. This was repeated to move back and forth in x until
6 layers had been simulated. In total the duration of the simulated printing was 70.6 s. In order to
extract results at every step a new layer had been simulated an interval to note the results was set
to 7 s.

3.7.5

The diversity of simulations reached in this project was based solely on the variation of rheological
properties of the simulated geopolymer. Thus, the only changes of simulated cases was found
by changes in the single file transportProperties. To simulate the viscous behavior of the

the non­Newtonian fluid . This model is a generalized model, that is described by three parameters:

consistency index, k, to describe the viscosity when flowing. The flow behavior index, n, which
describes the change of viscosity when changing the shear rate, ˙γ. The fluid could be described as
shear­thinning for n < 1 and shear­thickening for n > 1. If n = 1 the model would be reduced
to describe the behavior of a Bingham plastic. The viscosity, ν, of the fluid with this model was
described by eq. (3.3) [44].

Conclusion
Throughout the project, a total of 13 lunar samples were replicated by the procured simulants. The
largest deviation of the design content compared to the content of their respective lunar sample,
was the content of titanium­ and iron oxides. The constituents were milled to accurately repli­
cate the PSD of the procured lunar regolith dust simulants, as the median particle size was only
ranging from 8­10 µm. The 3D printing of the sample geopolymers were conducted with the
same activator­to­binder ratio except for three samples with higher content of aluminum­ and cal­
cium oxide as these were the reactive partition of the binder. The three samples depicted highland
regolith, while the remaining 10 samples depicted mare regolith. All mare geopolymers were
printable with the same activator­to­binder ratio, although some samples were close to be either
over­saturated or non­extrudable. Rheological measurements were conducted for all geopolymer
samples with the same activator­to­binder ratio as used during 3D printing. The majority of the
samples experienced sample spill during the measurements which rendered the measurements in­
accurate. Although many rheological measurements were fitted with a high accuracy based on
the R2­value, the fitted parameters were significantly lower than what was expected, based on the
printed geopolymers. This was also observed for the simulated samples. It was determined, that
simulating with a critical shear rate of 0.01 limited the deformation rate compared to using a value
of 0.1. This was however not enough to limit the deformation of the simulated geopolymers, since
the deformation caused layers merging for 8 out of 13 simulated samples. The deformation of the
simulations were significantly larger than the observed case of the printed geopolymers. This was
not completely unexpected, since the rheological parameters, used as input for the simulations,
were already suspected of being too low.
The final assessment of the accuracy of the model used to simulate could not be concluded. It
was deemed, that the input parameters were faulty, and so the accuracy of the output from the
simulations were corrupted by the rheological measurements.

Future work
Even though this paper sought to asses the simulation accuracy of the modeling; as this aim was
considered inconclusive a similar study should be conducted again. The inconclusive results were
identified to be rooted at inaccurate rheological measurements. Thus, a similar study with method­
ology that would yield reliable rheological properties should provide a conclusive assessment of
the simulations.
This project conducted its investigation based on several replicated lunar samples, however the
replications did show deviation in the metallic content. A notable source of iron for lunar regolith is
meteorites. Impact of micrometeorites on the regolith creates nanophase iron (npFe0) and melting
of regolith which forms the agglutinates[51]. The meteorite contribution on the lunar regolith was
not applied for the replicates in this project, however this should be investigated to accurately
replicate the behavior of lunar regolith. While the iron content was not expected to affect the
geopolymerization, it might affect the mechanical properties, which should also be investigated.
