Abstract
The Methanol-to-Jet process is a promising method of producing climate neutral fuels for
the aviation industry. However, the process is currently limited by the low technological
maturity of the intermediate olefin oligomerization step. In this thesis, the oligomerization
of propene on five different aluminosilicate catalysts was studied. Three of the considered
catalysts were nanometer crystal H-ZSM-5 zeolites with different Si/Al ratios of 13.5, 26.4
and 36.1 respectively. In addition, two mesoporous amorphous silica-alumina catalysts
(ASA and MSA) were studied with ASA being a commercial strictly mesoporous catalyst
while MSA had been synthesized in-house and had both mesopores and micropores. The
kinetics and product distributions for propene oligomerization on these catalysts were in-
vestigated through low conversion experimental studies using a high pressure gas-phase
fixed bed reactor setup with on-line GC analysis of the formed products. In addition,
regeneration experiments were carried out where partially deactivated catalysts were re-
generated by applying temperature ramps under inert Ar atmosphere or air respectively.
Based on these experiments, it was shown that the main products of the oligomeriza-
tion process at 200◦C were propene dimers, trimers and tetramers. The kinetics of the
oligomerization process at 200◦C and low propene conversion was shown to be of first
order with respect to propene, with the conversion increasing approximately inversely
proportional to the space velocity, proportional to the pressure and exponentially with
temperature. It was also shown that MSA had the highest activity at 200◦C whereas the
H-ZSM-5 catalyst were far more active at 300◦C with higher apparent activation energies
for the oligomerization process of 85-111 kJ/mol for these catalysts compared to only 47-
61 kJ/mol for the amorphous catalysts. At similar levels of conversion, ASA and MSA
showed higher selectivity toward propene dimers and lower selectivity toward trimers and
tetramers compared to the H-ZSM-5 catalysts. Further, the H-ZSM-5 catalysts showed
higher cracking selectivities. The observed catalyst activity was highly affected by time on
stream with the fastest deactivation occuring during the first 20 hours. After this period,
the H-ZSM-5 catalysts achieved a mostly stable level of activity around 33-46% of their
initial activity while the ASA and MSA catalyst seemed to deactivate somewhat further.
Based on the catalyst regeneration studies, it was indicated that the fast initial deactiva-
tion was mainly caused by accumulation of heavy hydrocarbons and not non-volatile coke
species for both the H-ZSM-5 and the amorphous catalysts.
Finally, a kinetic model was developed and fitted to the experimental data. This model
gave a reasonable description of the system at low conversion. However, it is unlikely
that the current model can be used to predict the behavior of the process at industrial
conversion levels due to simplifying assumptions regarding adsorbate coverages and since
the model neglects cracking.

Introduction
The aviation industry is currently responsible for 2-3% of the global GHG emissions world-
wide. However, the current lack of feasible long-range aviation technologies that do not
rely on hydrocarbon fuels makes the sector diﬀicult to decarbonize through direct electri-
fication or similar. Therefore, sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) produced from non-fossil
feedstocks are expected to play a key role in reducing the emissions from the aviation
sector in the medium and long term [1, 2].
Several technologies for producing SAF exist, but as of yet only the so-called HEFA process
is technologically mature and is therefore the only major source of SAF in the current
market. However, the lipid feedstocks used in the HEFA process are quite limited, and
therefore the process is unsuited for producing SAF in the quantities needed globally.
In this regard, so-called e-SAF produced using renewable hydrogen and captured CO2
eliminates the feedstock limitations and could therefore be a scalable option to fill the
demand as renewable electricity is more widely adopted [3].
Currently, the two major routes considered for e-SAF production are the Fischer-Tropsch
pathway and the methanol-to-jet (MtJ) pathway.
In this regard, the MtJ process is
promising since methanol can be produced directly from CO2 and hydrogen without the
need for a reverse water-gas shift step. In addition, methanol can also be produced from
gasification-derived syngas. Methanol production is therefore flexible with regards to feed-
stock, with the option of using non-lipid biomass feedstocks. Lastly, the MtJ process may
be more energy eﬀicient when applied specifically to-wards jet-fuel range hydrocarbons
than the Fischer-Tropsch process [4, 5, 6].
The MtJ pathway consists of three steps. First, methanol is converted to short- to medium-
length olefins.
Secondly, these olefins are oligomerized to larger olefins in the jet-fuel
range of C8-C16 on a solid acid catalyst. Lastly, the olefins are hydrogenated to produce
paraﬀins. However, the MtJ pathway is currently limited by the technological maturity
of the oligomerization step. Specifically, although oligomerization of olefins on solid acid
catalysts have been applied for many years in the fossil industry, its use towards jet fuel
range products is currently poorly optimized and has issues regarding selectivity, activity,
and deactivation of the catalysts. In addition, the impact of catalyst structure on the
selectivity of the process is currently poorly understood. Therefore, more work is needed
to understand and optimize the catalysts and process conditions of the acid-catalyzed
oligomerization process [7, 4].
This thesis will specifically focus on improving the understanding of the olefin oligomer-
ization step in the methanol to jet pathway using aluminosilicate catalysts. This is done
through laboratory studies with propene as the feedstock focusing on the effects of pro-

1.1
Decarbonizing the aviation industry
The global consumption of aviation fuels was about 280 Mt in 2023, and is expected to rise
to 330 Mt/year by 2025 [8]. Furthermore, the aviation industry was responsible for about
2% of anthropogenic CO2-emissions worldwide and about 4% of direct GHG emissions in
the EU in 2022. Consequently, decarbonizing the aviation sector is an important hurdle
for the EU target of reaching climate neutrality by 2050 [1]. In addition, the International
Air Transport Association (IATA) have, as of 2021, committed to a goal of reaching net-
zero CO2-emissions within the aviation industry by 2050 [9].
Figure 1.1: The global annual consumption of aviation fuels in the period 2019-2025. Data
shown in red are estimates of the expected growth. The plot was made based on data pub-
lished by IATA from [8].
Aviation, and especially long distance aviation, is considered a hard-to-abate sector due to
the lack of currently feasible solutions that do not rely on hydrocarbon fuels [10]. In this
regard, technologies for the aviation industry such as hydrogen-fueled or battery-powered
electric planes have been considered since these completely eliminate the need for fossil
hydrocarbon fuels.
However, the low volumetric and/or gravimetric energy density of
hydrogen and lithium batteries compared to hydrocarbon fuels will likely limit the usage
of these technologies to short or medium distance flights within the foreseable future [10, 6].
In addition, a wide deployment of these new types of planes will likely be associated with a
high cost, high investment risk and will likely take a long time, given the long investment
life time (∼30 years) for commercial jet planes [10]. Consequently, IATA estimates that

these technologies will only contribute with around 13% of the overall emission reductions
by 2050 [2].
An alternative approach to decarbonizing the aviation industry is through the use of so-
called sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) which are not derived from fossil feedstocks, but
instead from renewable sources such as biomass, waste and/or renewable electricity. In
the case where biomass is used as feedstock, the resulting fuel is often known as biojet
fuel, and in the case where the energy input is derived from electricity, the resulting fuel is
sometimes referred to as e-SAF [6, 4]. Generally, SAF products are designed to be used as
a direct substitute for the fossil-derived kerosene currently in use by the aviation industry.
Therefore, the use of SAF avoids the need for replacing the existing aviation fleet and is
suitable for long distances flights [10]. For these reasons, the deployment SAF is expected
to be provide the majority (65%) of the emission reductions in the aviation industry [2].
However, the wide deployment of SAF is currently limited by the feedstock availability for
existing SAF production methods and the low technological maturity or cost of alternative
production methods as outlined below.
1.2
Aviation fuels
Several types of aviation fuels currently exist on the market, but the types of fuels most
commonly used for civilian flights are the jet fuels Jet A and Jet A-1 [11]. The exact
composition of these fuels can vary between manufacturers and over time due to varia-
tions in processing, feedstock and similar. However, fuels with these designations must
adhere to a number of industry specifications regarding their physicochemical properties
and composition. In general, the specifications for Jet A and Jet A-1 are nearly identical
with the only major difference being a lower maximum freezing point of -47◦C for Jet A-1
compared to -40◦C for Jet A. For this reason, Jet A-1 is better suited for cold climates and
is commonly used in Europe while Jet A is more commonly used in the USA [11]. While
the composition of jet fuels may vary, they are mainly composed of C8 −C16 hydrocar-
bons (i.e. the kerosene range) with the majority being n- and isoparaﬀins, cycloparaﬀins
and aromatic hydrocarbons [11, 12]. The composition of a representative Jet-A fuel is
illustrated in Figure 1.2.
In recent years, the aromatics content in jet fuel has been subject to significant interest
since it has been shown to be responsible for increased soot formation and associated non-
CO2 climate impacts [4]. However, in the present aviation industry, a certain aromatics
content is needed in jet fuels since aromatics function as swelling agents for nitrile O-
rings currently used in many commercial aircrafts. Therefore, the aromatics content in
commercial jet A and A-1 must be in the range of 8-25 wt% [4, 12]. However, this may
be subject to change in the future as new types of O-rings not requiring aromatics are

1.3
Production of sustainable aviation fuels
In 2022, the global SAF production was estimated to be between 240 and 380 thousand
tonnes which amounted to about 0.1-0.15% of the total fuel demand in the aviation in-
dustry [9]. In 2024, this number is expected to grow to about 0.53% [13]. Consequently,
although the current fractional growth in the production and adoption of SAF is large, the
overall market share of SAF in the aviation industry is still extremely limited. Although
a number of technologies for the production of SAF have been studied, only 11 pathways
have been ASTM-approved and only the so-called HEFA SPK process is currently consid-
ered technologically mature with a TRL of 9 [14]. The approved pathways along with their
feedstocks and maximally allowed blend ratios are shown in Table 1.1. This section will
attempt to briefly outline the most important methods for SAF production with a special
emphasis on their potential advantages and limitations compared to the MtJ process.
1.3.1
Lipid-based methods
As previously mentioned, the vast majority of the SAF produced in the current market is
produced using the HEFA (Hydrotreated Esters and Fatty Acids) process. As the name
would suggest, this method uses lipid feedstocks such as vegetable oil or animal fats. In
the HEFA process, these feedstocks are typically pretreated to remove impurities, and
hydrotreated. Typically, this produces a paraﬀinic hydrocarbon mixture with a fraction
in the jet fuel range (∼15%) with the remaining being a diesel fraction that is separated
using distillation. Although this diesel fraction can be cracked to produce more kerosene,
this typically comes with a loss of yield. In recent years however, new legislation has
made SAF production more attractive, with many biorefineries intending to shift their
production towards up to 70% SAF [14, 16]. One challenge with HEFA derived fuels is
that they do not contain aromatics. Therefore, HEFA fuels must currently be blended
with other fuels to obtain the appropriate aromatics content [4], with blend limits up to
50% being allowed [14].
In addition to HEFA, so-called catalytic hydrothermolysis (CH) is another ASTM-approved
method based on a completely different route which can be used to convert lipids into
SAF. In this process, pretreated lipid feed is combined with supercritical water at very
high temperature and pressure which causes reactions such as cracking, isomerization and
hydrolysis to occur. The resulting mixture is subsequently hydrotreated and fractionated
[4]. The main advantages of CH over HEFA is that it uses less hydrogen, and also that the
resulting kerosene contains aromatics as well as paraﬀins and could therefore potentially
be used as a fully drop-in fuel [4]. However, the technological maturity of CH is currently
lower than HEFA due to aggressive reaction conditions presently required [14].

Alcohol-to-Jet

work be considered separately since the MtJ process specifically uses methanol as feed-

is currently hindered by large production costs [17, 4].
1.3.3
Fischer-Tropsch method
The Fischer-Tropsch (FT) process is a well known and mature industrial process which
is used to convert a syngas into a mixture of mostly linear paraﬀinic hydrocarbons [4].
Industrially, the process uses Co or Fe catalysts and the resulting product mixture (also
known as a syn-crude) is a distribution of hydrocarbons of different lengths following the
Anderson-Schultz-Flory distribution [4, 5].
This means that, the maximum attainable
weight fraction of jet fuel range hydrocarbons (C8-C16) produced with these catalysts is
about 41%, with the remaining being a light fraction (C7–) and a heavier wax fraction
(C17+). The wax-fraction can subsequently be cracked to yield a larger fraction of jet fuel,
but this adds complexity to the production process [4]. In addition, the linear product
mixture can be an issue e.g. due to the lower cloud point of linear paraﬀins compared
to their branched counterparts. However, this issue can be overcome by introducing an
isomerization step, although this also introduces additional complexity [5].
The FT process is ASTM approved for producing SAF, and since it uses a syngas as feed,
it can be used with a variety of feedstocks. For example, gasified biomass can be used
as feedstock although this can pose challenges with regards to the impurities often found
in biomass compared to coal. For this reason, complicated pretreatment is often required
when using gasified biomass in order to avoid excessive catalyst deactivation in the FT step
[14, 4]. In addition, the FT-process is one of the main candidates considered (alongside the
MtJ process) as a method to produce so-called e-SAF. This is because the syngas may also
be produced from reaction between CO2 and H2 via the reverse water-gas shift reaction [6].
Since the H2 may be produced from water electrolysis using renewable energy, and since
the CO2 may be sourced from CO2-capture, this approach is highly scalable and avoids

2.1
Mechanism and reactions in oligomerization
This section aims to give an brief overview of the most common reaction types occurring
for olefins on solid acid catalysts with a special emphasis on the reactions that are relevant
under the conditions commonly employed in olefin oligomerization. Wherever relevant, a
short discussion is provided on the mechanistic understanding of the given reactions.
2.1.1
Oligomerization
Oligomerization is a process where two or more molecules combine to form larger molecules.
Oligomerization of olefins on solid acid catalysts occurs through the so-called carbenium
ion mechanism as depicted in Figure 2.1 [19].
Figure 2.1: The oligomerization mechanism for olefins on solid acid catalysts including initia-
tion (1), chain growth (2-3) and termination (4). In addition, H-shift (5) and alkyl shift (6) is
also illustrated. The figure was copied from [21] with permission from the publisher.
In the initiation/protonation step (1), a Brønsted acid site on the catalyst reacts with an
olefin to form a carbenium ion. It should be noted that tertiary carbenium ions are most
stable, while secondary carbenium ions are less stable and primary carbenium ions are
the least stable. For this reason, the formation of primary carbenium ions is rare and for
e.g. propene, the carbenium ion will the almost exclusively form on the secondary carbon
atom [21, 19]. For the same reason, the reactivity of ethene on solid acid catalysts is very
low compared to higher olefins, since oligomerization of ethene on solid acid sites must
occur through the formation of a primary carbenium ion [22]. In step (2), the carbenium

ion is added to an additional olefin to form a dimer. This chain growth step can then
repeat a number of times to form the corresponding higher oligomers (trimers, tetramers
etc.) as shown in step (3) [21]. Here, it should be noted that due to secondary and tertiary
carbenium ions being favored, each olefin addition will usually introduce a new branch in
the growing chain [21]. Finally, at some point the reaction may terminate to release the
olefin and regenerate the acid sites as shown in step (4) [21].
Concurrently with the oligomerization reactions, a number of side reactions may occur
such as isomerization (double bond or skeletal) through e.g. H- and alkyl-shifts as well as
cracking to produce lighter olefins, cyclization, and hydride transfer reactions as described
in the following sections [21].
2.1.2
Cracking
Cracking is essentially the reverse of oligomerization and thus leads to the formation of
smaller olefins from larger olefins. Acid-catalyzed cracking has classically been thought
to occur through a carbenium ion mechanism as shown in Figure 2.2 [19]. It should be
noted, that the acid sites on the catalyst have been omitted for simplicity, but that the
reactant and product carbenium ions are still associated with the acid site in the form of
an ion pair as in the oligomerization reaction.
Figure 2.2: The classic β-scission mechanism shown for an arbitrary iso-olefin reactant. The
curved red arrows indicate the relocation of an electron pair, and certain explicit H-atoms have
been included for clarity.
The C-C bond scission occurs on the carbon atom located β to the reactant carbenium
ion. For this reason, the actual acid-catalyzed cracking step (2) is also commonly known
as β-scission. Because the formation of primary carbenium ions is unfavorable, cracking
generally does not lead to the formation methane or ethene but only C3+ olefins under
the conditions usually used for oligomerization [19]. An alternative mechanism for the
cracking reaction has been proposed by Sie [23]. In this article, it is proposed that the
cracking reaction occurs through a protonated cyclopropane (PCP) intermediate rather
than the classic mechanism shown above. This alternative mechanism explains some gen-
eral observations about cracking which the classic β-scission mechanism seemingly does
not. Firstly, it explains the observation that n-alkenes also crack readily whereas that
would require a primary carbenium ion in the classical mechanism and should therefore
be unlikely. Secondly, it explains the observation that a drastic increase in the cracking
activity has been observed for C7+ olefins whereas smaller olefins crack more slowly [23].
On the other hand, some of these observations may also be explained from skeletal iso-

merization prior to β-scission [19]. However, a detailed discussion on the mechanism of
catalytic cracking is beyond the scope of this thesis.
Because cracking occurs concurrently along with H-shift (which is facile at most relevant
temperatures), the product olefins often have C-numbers which are not integer multiples
of the feedstock olefins. Cracking along with oligomerization therefore leads to a product
distribution which is spread out over a range of C-numbers rather than distinct integer
multiples of the C-number of the feedstock olefins [24]. However, the relative rates of
oligomerization and cracking, and by extension also the product distribution, are highly
governed by the reaction conditions (especially temperature) as discussed in section 2.4.
2.1.3
Isomerization
Double-bond isomerization happens due to H-shift as shown in reaction (5) in Figure 2.1.
This reaction can lead to a shift in the double bond location when the olefin is released from
the acid site. Generally H-shift strongly favors the formation of more stable carbenium
ions and occurs readily for C4+ olefins under most conditions used for oligomerization [24,
7]. In addition, so-called skeletal isomerization may also occur which changes the skeletal
carbon structure of the olefins such as the reaction (6) shown in Figure 2.1. These reac-
tions can occur through a standard carbenium ion mechanism [19]. However, it has also
been proposed that the reactions may occur through a PCP-intermediate. This theory
also explains certain experimental observations, such as the slow skeletal isomerization
of butane to iso-butane. Similarly, skeletal isomerization occurs much more readily for
C5+ olefins, whereas high temperatures (>300◦C) are required for skeletal isomerization
of butenes [19]. Generally, skeletal isomerization favors the formation of more branched
products since these reactions involve tertiary carbenium ions which are very stable [7].
2.1.4
Hydride transfer
Hydride transfer reactions are of importance since they lead to the simultaneous forma-
tion of paraﬀins which are inactive towards oligomerization and aromatics which are coke
precursers [21, 19, 25]. Hydride transfer reactions occur by the transfer of protons from
one olefin molecule to another to produce polyunsaturated olefins. These olefins may then
react in a series of steps to form aromatic compounds as illustrated in Figure 2.3. Finally,
the aromatics may subsequently react in further steps to result in polycyclic coke, which
is generally undesired as it results in catalyst deactivation by blocking pores and/or active
sites on the solid acid catalysts [21, 19].

2.2
Types of catalysts
Many different types of solid acid catalysts have been developed and studied in literature.
However, for olefin oligomerization, silica-alumina materials such as mesoporous silica-
alumina and zeolites have been particularly well studied due to their low cost, and high
thermal and mechanical stability, which make them easy to handle and regenerate [21, 19].
This section aims to provide a brief overview of the most common silica-alumina catalysts
used for olefin oligomerization in fuel applications. In addition, a brief mention of other
less commonly used catalysts will also be given.
2.2.1
Silica-alumina
Silica-Alumina (SiO2 Al2O3) catalysts were discovered from natural clays and used com-
mercially as cracking catalysts more than 80 years ago. Since then, a number of synthetic
types of amorphous and structured silica-alumina catalysts have been developed and seen
wide industrial application for many industrial processes such as cracking, isomerization
and similar refinery processes [19]. The Brønsted acidity of silica-alumina materials was
initially a surprise to researchers since neither silica or alumina are acidic on their own,
and in fact its explanation remains a contentious issue [26]. It has proposed that the
Brønsted acidity of silica-alumina materials comes from isomorphic replacement of Si4+
ions in the SiO2 matrix with relatively small amounts of Al3+ ions. This was believed to
cause a charge imbalance which allowed the crystal to bind protons [19]. However, more
recently the Brønsted acidity of amorphous silica-alumina and zeolite materials has been
ascribed to bridging or terminal silanol groups on the surface of the material [26, 27]. In
this overview, the term silica-alumina will generally be used to refer to amorphous materi-
14
Oligomerization of Medium Length Olefins
==PAGE:29==
als without a well defined crystal structure. Conversely, zeolites which are a special class
of silica-alumina materials will be treated separately owing to their different properties
(well defined crystal structure and stronger acid properties) compared to other types of
amorphous or mesoporous silica-alumina materials [19].
Silica-alumina catalysts can be produced using a variety of methods. One of the most
important ones is the so-called co-gelling method. Here, aqueous silica and alumina pre-
cursors are treated at low pH which results in a gel being formed. At this step so-called
structure-directing agents such as rod-shaped micelle forming surfactants can be included
as a kind to template to introduce structure (e.g. mesoporosity) into final catalyst mate-
rial. The two materials MCM-41 and SBA-15 are well known examples of such mesoporous
catalysts. Subsequently, the pH is neutralized and the gel is washed, dried and calcined
thereby yielding the final catalyst [26]. It should be noted, that the amount and strength
of acid sites in amorphous silica-alumina materials are often measured using ammonia
temperature-programmed desorption (TPD) and is highly dependent on the method of
preparation and the Si/Al ratio [27]. However, as a general rule, silica-alumina catalysts
typically have acid sites of lower strength than zeolites. This means that they are generally
less catalytically active. However, since they are relatively inexpensive and tend to form
more branched products (with corresponding high octane and low cetane numbers) they
are preferred for some applications [26, 21].
2.2.2
Zeolites
Zeolites, as previously mentioned, are a special class of silica-alumina materials with well
defined crystal structure and uniform 3-dimensional micropores with diameters in the
range 0.3-1.2 nm [19].
Generally, the acid sites are distributed both on the external
surface and inside the micropores [19]. Over 230 types of zeolites are known today, and
they are among the most commonly used solid acid catalysts in industry [21]. Similar to
amorphous silica-alumina materials, zeolites are typically synthesized by mixing silica and
alumina precursors (e.g. sodium silicate and sodium aluminate). Often, this is done in
alkaline solution at elevated temperatures and with the use of structure directing agents
(e.g. alkylammonium species) which stabilize the microporous structure of the zeolite. The
operating conditions and method of synthesis are extremely important for determining the
nucleation and crystallization behavior of the zeolite particles and therefore also the final
structure, acidity and morphology of the material [28]. However, a full review on the topic
of zeolite synthesis is beyond the scope of this thesis.
One of the most important types of zeolites in industrial catalysis are the MFI zeolites char-
acterized by 10-membered ring pore openings with H-ZSM-5 being particularly well studied
and used commercially for the Mobil Olefin to Gas and Distillates (MOGD) oligomeriza-
tion process [29]. The pore structure of the MFI framework found in H-ZSM-5 is shown
in Figure 2.4, and consists of straight elliptical channels 5.3×5.6 Å in size interconnected
by slightly smaller 5.1×5.5 Å zig-zag channels.

2.2.3
Multifunctional metal and acid catalysts
As mentioned in section 2.1, oligomerization of ethene on pure solid acid catalysts is slow
since the oligomerization needs to occur through a primary carbenium ion intermediate.
This can be an issue for the MtJ process as ethene is a common product from the MtO
process [19, 18]. However, it has been found that the rate of ethene oligomerization can be
vastly increased if certain transition metal ions are deposited on the solid acid catalysts
such that the catalyst material has both metal ion and acid sites. Particularly, nickel has
been extensively studied for combined metal/acid catalysis since it shows good activity
towards oligomerization and is relatively cheap and abundant [22].
Figure 2.5: The Cossee-Arlman mechanism for ethene oligomerization on Ni(II) sites. The
figure was adapted from [22] with permission from the publisher.
The way these multifunctional catalysts work is by allowing ethene to react through the
Cossee-Arlman mechanism on nickel(II) sites to produce higher linear olefins of varying
lengths as shown in figure 2.5. The exact nature of the active site has been debated in lit-
erature, but most commonly, Ni2+ ions in exchange positions have been reported to be the
active site. The product mixture arising from this mechanism follows the Schultz-Flory
distribution, and is governed by the so-called chain growth probability α. Specifically,
lower values of α lead to lighter products (such as butene) while higher values tend to
favor higher products [22]. The value of α on these multifunctional catalysts are affected
by the operating conditions and the Ni-loading. For example, Heveling et al. [32] found
that the value of α for ethene oligomerization on Ni-exchanged Y-zeolite at 473 K in-
creased from 0.1 to 0.5 with the Ni loading (1-4 wt%). The higher olefins formed on the
nickel sites may then subsequently oligomerize, crack and/or isomerize on acid sites by
the carbenium ion mechanism as previously described. Consequently, the final product
distribution generally does not follow a Schultz-Flory distribution [22, 21]. Just as for
non Ni-exchanged solid acid catalysts, the selectivity, activity and deactivation of these

and meso-porosity) as well as the number and strength of acid sites of the catalyst. Ad-
ditionally, the activity of ethene oligomerization is strongly dependent on the Ni-loading

2.2.4
Other catalyst materials
In addition to the aforementioned silica-alumina catalysts, a number of other solid acid
catalysts have been developed and studied for olefin oligomerization. One notable type
are the so-called solid phosphoric acid (SPA) catalysts, which are made up of phosphoric
acid groups supported on SiO2. These catalysts have been used commercially for olefin
oligomerization in the so-called CatPoly process to convert propene and butene into gaso-
line range hydrocarbons.
However, one of the main limitations of SPA is the limited
lifetime and problematic disposal compared to silica-alumina materials [21]. In addition,
polymeric acid resins have also been used to perform olefin oligomerization at low tem-
peratures <100◦C and often have significantly higher selectivity to specific products [21].
However, silica-alumina and zeolites have major advantages over these catalysts due to
their higher temperature stability. This allows them to be regenerated by burning off
the deposited coke at high temparatures [21]. Several other types of catalyst have also
been proposed for olefin oligomerization but their current use is relatively limited and will
therefore be beyond the scope of this thesis.

2.4
Effect of reaction conditions
The quantitative effects of the reaction conditions on silica-alumina and zeolite acid cat-
alyzed olefin oligomerization depends on the catalyst composition and structure as well as
the feedstock. However, the qualitative trends remains the same for most types of feeds
and catalyst materials. This section aims to give a brief overview of how the reaction
conditions impact the performance of olefin oligomerization processes.
2.4.1
Temperature
Since the activation energies vary between different reactions (oligomerization, cracking
etc.), the temperature is one of the most important process variables for controlling the rate
and selectivity of acid catalyzed oligomerization on micro- and mesoporous silica-alumina
catalysts. Generally, the activity of the catalysts increases with temperature. However,
oligomerization is thermodynamically favored at low temperatures whereas cracking be-
comes favored at higher temperatures. In addition, cracking reactions have a higher activa-
tion energy than oligomerization [35]. Consequently, the selectivity towards oligomeriza-
tion is generally highest at relatively low temperatures of ∼200
◦C [25] whereas cracking
starts to dominate at higher temperatures [21, 35, 24]. This effect is quite dependent on
the reactant olefin, with e.g. 1-octene being prone to cracking at temperatures as low as
180◦C on H-ZSM-5 whereas 1-hexene requires temperatures of 275-300◦C for cracking to
be significant [24]. However, as found in the article [35] which studied gas-phase 1-butene
oligomerization on H-ZSM-5, very low temperatures (∼150◦C) could also increase the
selectivity towards cracking and negatively impact the oligomerization selectivity. Conse-
quently, the selectivity towards higher olefins (C8+) had a maximum at a temperature of
approximately 200◦C. In the article, this is explained by the fact that the lowest temper-
atures studied were below the boiling point of the formed oligomers. This effectively traps
the oligomers on the catalyst where they have a long time to participate in cracking re-
actions [35]. Consequently, choosing the operating temperature for olefin oligomerization
towards higher olefins in gas-phase processes requires careful consideration of the boiling
points of the formed oligomers, accounting for possible capillary condensation effects and
similar. In addition, choosing the temperature also becomes a compromise between the
activity and selectivity of the catalyst towards oligomerization [35].
Due to the limited cracking at low temperatures, the main products of the oligomerization
process will be integer values of the original olefin (i.e. dimers, trimers etc.) [36, 35].
However, when the temperature is increased, the higher cracking rate causes the product
distribution to spread out such that more non-integer multiples of the original olefins are
observed [35, 24]. In addition, increasing the temperature also shifts the product distribu-
tion towards lighter compounds with less trimers and heavier oligomers, and significantly
more light cracked compounds being produced [35].

Temperature is also very important for the rate of isomerization as shown by Dubray et
al. [7] for 1-pentene and 1-hexene on Al-SBA-15. In this article they show that at 200◦C
double-bond shift occurs rapidly for the unreacted feedstock olefins whereas skeletal iso-
merization only occurs to a lesser extent (<10%). Conversely, when the temperature is
increased to 250◦C, both double bond and skeletal isomerization are very significant for
the unreacted feed. This effect was also observed for the product olefins, where a larger
number of tertiary olefins were observed at high temperatures which corresponds to a
larger degree of branching [7]. The rate of hydride transfer, and by extension the amount
of aromatics and paraﬀins produced also depends on temperature. In this regard, de Klerk
[37] showed that the yield of saturated paraﬀins increased significantly with temperature
in the range 140-265◦C for oligomerization of mixed C3-C6 olefins on amorphous silica-
alumina at 68 bar. A similar effect was observed by Coelho et al. [35] who observed that
the selectivity towards aromatics for 1-butene oligomerization on H-ZSM-5 also increased
with temperature.
The effect of temperature on deactivation is a little bit more complicated since deacti-
vation can occur by several mechanisms. Firstly, formation of heavy primary products
olefins unable to desorb from the catalysts can lead to blockage of the catalyst pores.
These products are sometimes referred to as ”soft coke”. Secondly, formation of poly-
cyclic aromatics (secondary products) formed due to hydride transfer can block active
sites and/or pores [35, 38]. This type of coke is sometimes referred to as ”hard coke”.
The rate of the first mode of deactivation generally decreases with temperature for two
reasons. Firstly, elevated temperatures increase the rate of desorption of the heavy com-
pounds. Secondly, the increased cracking rate at higher temperatures also help remove
the heavy oligomers from the catalyst surface [35, 24, 38]. However, the second mode of
deactivation is much more diﬀicult to characterize generally since the amount of hydride
transfer, and per extension the rate of formation of polycyclic aromatics, depends highly
on the catalyst material. For example, polycyclic compounds have been shown to require
temperatures of more than 240◦C to form for H-ZSM-5 whereas they start forming at only
180◦C for ultrastable Y-zeolite [24].
2.4.2
Pressure
Pressure generally has a positive effect on the rate of gas-phase olefin oligomerization, with
the conversion increasing with pressure for a fixed WHSV and temperature as shown by
Fuchs et al. [36] and Coelho et al. [35]. On the other hand, Dubray et al. [7] observed no
effect of pressure on the rate or selectivity of the oligomerization of 1-pentene and 1-hexene
on Al-SBA-15 in a pressure range of 35-65 bar. However, in this article the authors point
out that the pressure is above the critical pressure of the reactants. Consequently, their
results indicate that a pressure increase beyond the critical pressure does not significantly
impact the rate of oligomerization or product distribution [7].

Below the critical pressure, the pressure does seem to have an effect on the product dis-
tribution although it is not as pronounced as temperature [35]. In this regard, Coelho et
al. [35] showed that increasing the pressure in the range of 12.5-50 bar led to fewer light
cracked products and a larger selectivity towards C8+ products for 1-Butene oligomer-
ization on H-ZSM-5. This is understandable since oligomerization is thermodynamically
favored at high pressures whereas cracking is favored at low pressures. However, increas-
ing the pressure also led to a slightly decreased selectivity towards trimers (C12) which
may be somewhat unexpected [35]. A similar effect was observed by Fuchs et al. [36]
for oligomerization of mixed C2-C4 olefins on Ni-loaded SIRALOX 40. Here, the selec-
tivity towards C13+ products decreased when pressure was increased from 16 to 32 bar.
One possible explanation for this phenomenon is that at increased pressures, more liquid
products are formed on the catalyst. This could block the pores and introduce diffusion
limitations for light educts, thereby limiting the exent of oligomerization. Alternatively
the liquid products could function as a solvent and therefore decrease the residence time
of the oligomers on the active sites which could have a similar effect [36].
2.4.3
Space velocity and time on Stream
For hydrocarbon processing on solid acid catalysts, the amount of time that the reacting
mixture is in contact with the catalyst is often characterized by the so-called weight-hourly
space velocity (WHSV). This parameter is defined as the mass flow of reactant divided
by the mass of catalyst in the reactor, and can thus be thought of as a kind of inverse
residence time [35]. Coelho et al. [35] studied the effect of residence time on the conversion
and selectivity of 1-butene oligomerization on ZSM-5. Here it was found that increasing the
residence time (i.e. decreasing the WHSV) increased the olefin conversion as expected.
In this article, it was also found that cracking was favored at longer residence times,
leading to lower selectivity toward trimers C12 and higher selectivity toward light cracked
C3-C7 species as the residence time was increased [35].
In another article by Dubray
et al. [7] the exact opposite effect was observed for oligomerization of 1-pentene and 1-
hexene on Al-SBA-15 where the selectivity toward trimers and tetramers increased with
the residence time. However, this difference may be possibly be attributed to differences
in the reaction conditions and catalyst material leading to very limited cracking in the
experiments performed by Dubray et al.
The so-called time on stream (TOS) denotes the time that a catalytic process has been
operating. This parameter is also a very important for olefin oligomerization due the the
relatively rapid deactivation of silica-alumina and zeolite catalysts. This deactivation oc-
curs due to the formation of heavy oligomers and/or polyaromatic coke on the active sites
and pores of the catalyst [35, 24]. Generally, it has been reported in literature that the
activity of the solid acid catalyst decreases rapidly in the beginning due to rapid coking

of the strongest acid sites [35, 7, 24]. However, for longer TOS, the deactivation generally
seems to slow down significantly [35, 7]. It should be noted that the rate of deactivation is
very dependent on the reaction conditions. For example, the rate of deactivation changes
significantly with temperature [24]. In addition, it was observed by Van Grieken et al.
[25] that deactivation was negligible when the oligomerization was performed in organic
solvent (n-octane), which is likely due to continuous dissolution and removal of heavy
oligomers and coke precursors.
2.4.4
Conversion
In an article by Dubray et al. [7], the authors studied the effect of reaction conditions
(temperature, WHSV, feed-composition and TOS) on the oligomerization of mixtures of
1-pentene and 1-hexene diluted in n-heptane over mesoporous Al-SBA-15 catalyst in a
fixed bed reactor. Further, these experiments were conducted under conditions not lim-
ited by mass transfer and at temperatures in the range 200-250◦C. As expected, they
showed that the feed conversion decreases with TOS due to deactivation caused by coke
formation in pores and on active sites. In addition, the conversion increases with tempera-
ture due to enhanced kinetics, and also increases with contact time. Lastly, the conversion
was mostly unaffected by pressure due to operation in the supercritical regime. Interest-
ingly however, when they plotted the dimer/trimer/heavy oligomer selectivities from all
experiments against the conversion, the authors found that these selectivities all seemed
to follow the same trendlines as seen in Figure 2.7. This was true even for the experiments
performed under different conditions (pressure, temperature, fractional deactivation and
different feedstock). Based on this, they argue that only the conversion is important for
determining the selectivity towards dimers, trimers etc. and not the reaction conditions,
catalyst deactivation or feedstock [7]. Consequently, the only way to obtain improved
conversion/selectivity characteristics would be to change the catalyst material [7].
This may seem to contradict the previously mentioned effect of temperature and pressure
on the cracking/oligomerization selectivity. However, it should also be mentioned that
the article [7] does not explicitly report the presence of any light cracked compounds (C3-
C4), even at 250◦C although they might be expected. Conversely, a significant amount of
cracking was reported by Coelho et al. at 250◦C for 1-butene oligomerization on ZSM-5
[35]. This difference may be due to differences in feedstock, catalyst or other parameters
which could make cracking less significant under the conditions studied in [7]. An impor-
tant finding in the article by Dubray et al. is that the dimer selectivity is nearly 100% at
low conversions. However, as the conversion is increased, the selectivity towards dimers
decrease and the selectivity towards trimers and heavier oligomers increase. For example,
at 90% conversion, the selectivity towards C18+ products is about 23% [7]. Therefore, the
conversion is extremely important in determining the product distribution.

Selectivity toward jet fuel
Although a large number of studies have been done on olefin oligomerization toward gaso-
line range hydrocarbons (C6-C10) and some studies have been done on oligomerization
towards middle distillate and lubricating oils [24], fewer articles have explicitly studied
the application of olefin oligomerization toward jet fuel range hydrocarbons. Therefore,
this section aims to summarize and compare the most significant findings regarding the
selectivity of olefin oligomerization toward jet fuel.
The articles [40, 41, 42, 39] all studied oligomerization of pure ethene feedstocks on mul-
tifunctional Ni-loaded solid acid catalysts with the aim of producing jet fuel. Generally,
these articles report selectivities toward jet fuel (here defined as C8+) in the range of
40-60% with the remaining products mainly being C5-C7 olefins which could be useful for
gasoline production [36]. In addition, Fuchs et al. [36] studied oligomerization of 1-butene
and a mixture of C2-C4 olefins resembling those from the MtO process toward gasoline
and jet-fuel on Ni SIRALOX 40. In this study, the selectivity toward C8+ was about 99%
at 91% conversion of pure 1-butene and about 85% for a 76% conversion of the mixed
C2-C4 feed. However, it should be noted, that in these articles, the selectivity is given
without an upper bound on the acceptable C-number. Therefore, the actual selectivity
towards the kerosine range C8-C16 may be lower since C17+ oligomers may be included in
the reported selectivity. In this regard, Fuchs et al. [36] report that the selectivity toward
C17+ is less than 5% for the 1-butene experiment.
In the article by Dubray et al. [7], oligomerization of 1-pentene and 1-hexene toward jet-
fuel is studied. In this article, they report >99% selectivity to jet-fuel (in the article defined
as C10-C18) at <10% conversion. Conversely, at 50% and 90% conversion, the selectivity
toward jet fuel is about 98% and 75% respectively due to the selectivity toward heavier
oligomers increasing with conversion [7]. However, it should be noted that the jet fuel
obtained at low conversion is almost exclusively composed of dimers in the C10-C12 range
which the authors point out is very light. Consequently, a higher quality jet fuel is likely
obtained at higher conversions where a larger fraction of trimers are formed [7]. Lastly,
although production of jet fuel is not the explicit aim of the article by Van Grieken et al.
[25], the selectivity towards C10-C18 was extracted from the reported product selectivities.

2.7
Kinetic modeling of olefin oligomerization
The simultaneous occurrence of oligomerization, cracking and isomerization in acid cat-
alyzed olefin processing leads to a large number of different reactions and chemical species.
This is especially true when higher olefins are considered, since the number of possible
structural isomers increases exponentially with the C-number [44]. This makes compre-
hensive kinetic modeling extremely challenging when applied towards oligomerization to
higher olefins since the size of a reaction network describing all possible interconversions
and intermediates becomes too large to be computationally feasible. Therefore certain sim-
plifications are usually required to limit the computational effort. The following sections
will attempt to briefly outline some of the most important approaches and simplifications
described in literature.
2.7.1
Reaction networks and termination criteria
One of the key challenges in rigorous modeling of olefin conversions is the establishment
of a comprehensive reaction network. In this regard, it is important that the network
contains all the most important species and reactions. However, when modeling solid-acid
catalyzed reactions involving higher hydrocarbons it can often be necessary to employ
certain simplifications based on previous knowledge of the reaction system of interest in
order keep the size of the reaction network within feasible limits. One common way of
doing this is based on specifying the maximum C-number and species rank of species
allowed to react in the network [44]. In that case the termination criterion is often stated
as ”ci-rj” where i is the maximum C-number and j is the maximum rank of reacting
species. Here, the concept of species rank describes the order at which the species appear
from the primary reactants in the network [45]. Suppose for example that we have the
following reaction with initial reactants arbitrary reactants A and B:
A + B →C →D
Then the rank of A and B would be 0, the rank of C would be 1 and the rank of D
would be 2.
For more complex reactions a set of formalisms are used as described in
[45]. For example, if a reaction involves reactants with different rank, the rank of the
resulting species is then always the minimum rank of either of the reactant molecules plus
one. Further, if two reactions leads to the same product but with different rank, then the
lowest rank is chosen for the product. Finally, radicals are prescribed a rank 1 lower than
otherwise, since they have yet to be realized as proper molecules [45].
When modeling acid catalyzed reactions, a rank as low as j = 0 is often chosen as the ter-
mination criterion to limit the amount of reactions and species in the network as done in
[46, 47] which models propene oligomerization on ZSM-5 and β-zeolite at low conversion.
Specifically, the termination criterion was chosen as c9-r0 which resulted in 269 gas-phase
species, 640 adsorbed species and 4243 elementary reactions. In this regard, it can be
noted that inclusion of species up to C12 would have resulted in >60.000 reactions [47]

==PAGE:45==
which further highlights the need for model simplifications and termination criteria. Fi-
nally, additional restrictions can be imposed on the reaction network based on knowledge
of the reacting system. If, for example, a family of reactions is known in advance to be
very slow, or certain intermediates are known to be very unstable (e.g. primary carbenium
ions [48]) they can sometimes be omitted [44]. In addition, pore size limitations have also
been used to impose additional constraints as described in [49]. Finally, an approach is
described in [48] where a stochastic simulation is used to only include the most probable
reaction routes and species in the network.
2.7.2
Single event models
Due to the aforementioned large number of reactions in acid-catalyzed hydrocarbon pro-
cessing, it is usually impractical to determine the kinetic parameters for all possible el-
ementary reactions individually from experiments. To overcome this issue, the so-called
”single events” theory was developed which reduces the full number of kinetic parameters
for reactions involving carbenium ions into a much smaller set of kinetic parameters [48].
This is usually done by decomposing all the relevant elementary reactions into a limited
set of types characterized only by the reaction family (e.g. oligomerization, β-scission
etc.) and nature of the reactant and product carbenium ions (i.e. primary, secondary,
tertiary). The rate constants for a given elementary reaction is then calculated as the
product of a so-called ”number of single events” ne which depends on the structure of
the reactant and activated complex and a ”single event rate constant” ˜k which is only
related to the type of reaction [48]. This concept is illustrated in figure 2.9. As indicated,
molecule A may react in two different ways to form molecule B through methyl shift,
whereas molecule B may only react in one way to form A. Both forwards and backward
reactions involve only secondary carbenium ions.
If the single-event rate constant for
an arbitrary s; s (secondary-secondary) methyl-shift is denoted ˜kms(s; s), then it follows
that kA = 2˜kms(s; s) and that kB = ˜kms(s; s). Consequently, the rate constants of both
the forwards and backwards reactions may be easily determined if only ˜kms(s; s) is known.

is from [48].
The number of single events for any reactant participating in any reaction may be deter-
mined in advance based on the symmetry numbers for each reactant in each reaction if
the reaction network has been established [48, 50]. Therefore, the only kinetic parameters

ditional simplifications may also be used to further reduce the number of rate constants
that have to be determined. For example, if primary carbenium ions are neglected due to
their relative instability, then all cracking reaction steps in a network of an arbitrary size
may be described using only 4 kinetic rate constants1 ˜kcr(s; s), ˜kcr(s; t), ˜kcr(t; s), ˜kcr(t; t)

of 1-heptene and 1-hexene in articles by Von Aretin et al. [50, 49]. However, the main
challenge when applying such models towards oligomerization is that a comprehensive
reaction network is needed since the rates of reaction for each individual species depends
on its structure. Consequently it will likely be very challenging to apply a comprehensive
single-event model to oligomerization toward jet fuel range products due to the infeasible
size of a reaction network describing all relevant compounds.
2.7.3
Lumped models
The term ”lumping” in kinetic modeling of olefins is used in literature to describe two sep-
arate types of modeling approaches. The first type is based on lumping of species within
rigorous kinetic models to describe the full hydrocarbon interconversion [49]. The second
type refers to non-rigorous models based on lumped pseudo-kinetics [51].
Rigorous lumping has been used successfully to describe the kinetics of cracking processes,
which contain simultaneous cracking and oligomerization reactions, and is generally based
on assuming quasi-equilibrium within ”lumps” of chemically related species [50, 49, 44].
Often these lumps are defined based on the C-number of the different compounds. How-
ever, conversely to the non-rigorous types of models, the species dependent kinetics are
still accounted for based on e.g. single-event kinetics and equilibrium calculations within
each lump. Consequently, the rigorous approach still requires a comprehensive reaction
network [44, 46]. However, this approach remains a significant simplification compared to
non-lumped models since it eliminates the need for considering isomerizations when mod-
eling the olefin interconversion. In addition, it also simplifies the ODE system required
to describe the development of the reaction mixture over time since only one ODE is re-
quired for each ”lump” instead of one for every species [49]. However, the applicability of
rigorous lumped models is limited by the assumption of quasi-equilibrium, which is often
not satisfied at the relatively low temperatures used for olefin oligomerization [44].
The other type of lumped models are the non-rigorous models or lumped kinetic mod-
els. In this type of model, chemically related species are lumped together into ”pseudo-
components” and the reaction network then only models the interconversion between these
pseudo-components using pseudo rate-laws [51]. This type of model therefore assumes that

In order to characterize the acidity of the catalysts, ammonia temperature programmed

is done to remove water and other impurities from the catalyst surface or active sites.
Secondly, the sample is cooled to 100◦C and a flow of 18 sccm of a gas mixture consisting
of 0.5 mol% NH3 in He is fed through the sample for 3 hours. This saturates the acid

3.3.2
GC configuration
During the experiments, the on-line gas chromatograph measures the concentration of
different species in the gas based on a principle which will be described in the follow-
ing.
For every sample, a small amount of gas is injected into the column of the GC.
For the experimental work described here, the GC was fitted with a 60 meter RESTEK
Rtx®-5 column placed in a temperature controlled oven. This column contains a station-
ary phase (diphenyl dimethyl polysiloxane) which interacts differently with different gas
species thereby splitting the different components into distinct fractions which exit the
column at distinct times. At the exit of the column, the effluent gas is combusted in a
so-called flame ionization detector (FID). This FID measures the amount of ions formed
during the combustion of each distinct fraction. The amount of ions is proportional to the
concentration of the species. By comparing the retention times and FID signal against a
known library of calibration curves, it is therefore possible to determine the concentration
of different species in the gas. Each GC run starts at an oven temperature of 50◦C. How-

==PAGE:56==
ever, since some species in the gas phase go through the column quickly, while others take
a long time, a temperature ramp is applied to the GC column for each sample to allow
adequate separation of the different fractions while still eluting all species within the elu-
tion time (∼23 minutes). This relatively short elution time was chosen to allow for more
frequent sampling of the outlet stream during the experiments. The temperature ramp is
started after 1 minute of elution time and proceeds at 20 ◦C/min until a temperature of

For hydrocarbons, the response of the FID is proportional to the number of carbon atoms
in the combusted species [55]. This means that the area of the FID peak for each olefin
species is directly proportional to the mass concentration of the given olefin in the effluent
gas. Further, it means that the total peak area (i.e. the FID signal integrated over time) is
approximately constant between GC samples as long as no hydrocarbons are accumulated
(e.g. in the condenser) and no leaks are present in the system. This principle was used
to determine the mass fractions of each hydrocarbon in the product mixture. Conversely,
if the total peak area for a sample is lower than the peak area at zero conversion, then
it indicates that some fraction of the hydrocarbons produced on the catalyst have been
condensed.

3.4.1
Low conversion experiments
In the first experimental campaigns it was decided to study the oligomerization of propene
on the five different catalyst materials described in section 3.1.1 at low conversion. Firstly,
this was done to study the effect of operating conditions on the oligomerization process,
and secondly to compare the activity and selectivities of the different catalysts.
The
reason why the conversion was intentionally kept low was to avoid condensation of heavy
hydrocarbons in the condensation flask. Condensation of hydrocarbons is an issue for
two reasons.
Firstly, the condensed products cannot be measured on-line by the GC.
This means that some fraction of the products are lost from the analysis which makes it
more diﬀicult to accurately determine the propene conversion and product selectivities (cf.
section 3.5.1). Secondly, condensed products produced at higher conversion tend to slowly
evaporate from the condensation flask when the conversion is lowered which consequently
skews the analysis regarding product selectivity and conversion. Further, this means that
the system takes a long time (several hours) to reach a pseudo steady state and is thus
desirable to avoid. Finally, the low conversion is beneficial since it limits temperature
gradients within the bed and secondary reactions which makes it more diﬀicult to extract
kinetic information from the experimental data.

3.5
Data analysis
In the results chapter, a number of derived quantities are presented based on the data
recorded from the experiments described above. This section aims to describe the methods
and equations used to calculate these quantities. In this regard, two types of data are
presented. The first type refers to data that are given as a function of time on stream.
This type could for example be the measured reactor conditions (temperature, pressure
etc.) or derived quantities calculated from the GC measurements such as the conversion or
apparent first order rate constant. The second type of data refers to data that is averaged
for a given experimental step. This was done in order to minimize uncertainty with regards
to the GC sampling and to more easily summarize the data from a single experimental
step (cf. tables 3.2 and 3.3). The data analysis carried in both of these cases will be
described in the following sections.

3.5.4
Averaged Results
In order to minimize measurement uncertainties and more easily visualize data recorded
at pseudo-steady state, averages were taken for the experimental steps in tables 3.2 and
3.3. These averages are used whenever product distributions and other quantities (e.g.
conversion) at different experimental conditions are compared. In this regard, product
distributions and conversion often showed an initial transient behavior whenever process
variables were changed.
Because of this, it was decided to only use the last 3 hours
of each experimental step when calculating the average product distributions to allow
for the system to reach a pseudo-steady state. Because the measured properties varied
somewhat during these three hours (likely due to random fluctuations in process variables,
catalyst deactivation and/or measurement uncertainty) it was decided to include error bars
displaying the observed standard deviation when averaged measurements are presented.
3.6
Development of kinetic model
In this section, it is desired to construct a kinetic model which is able to adequately
describe the kinetics of the propene oligomerization process. To simplify the modeling
procedure, a number of simplifying assumptions were made based on the observed results
from the initial experimental campaign. These assumptions are described in the following:
1. Cracking is neglected.
This assumption is somewhat reasonable at temperatures
close to 200◦C since cracking products amounted to less than 10% of the overall
product selectivity for most of the experimental data at 200◦C.
In addition, it
greatly simplifies the resulting kinetic model since the oligomerization may then be
modeled as a series of consecutive reactions involving only C3n species rather than a
more complicated network involving a large number of co-oligomerization reactions.
2. All products of the reactions are assumed to be olefins (i.e. hydrogen transfer and
cyclization reactions are neglected).
Oligomerization of Medium Length Olefins
57
==PAGE:72==
3. Olefin isomers are lumped into groups based only on their C-number. Consequently,
the rate of a given reaction involving an olefin of C-number i really represents a sum
of all reactions involving different Ci isomers. This assumption is necessary since the
different isomers could not be reliably distinguished in the GC-FID chromatograms.
4. Only products up to C12 are included in the model.
This assumption is some-
what unfortunate for applications toward jet-fuel (C8-C16) but was chosen since
products with higher C-numers could not be detected reliably be the GC. Further,
C≤12-products accounted for most of the product mixture for the low-conversion
experiments in section 3.4.1.
5. Adsorption and protonation of olefins are assumed to be at pseudo-equilibrium, and
the C-C bond formation (i.e. steps 2-3 in figure 2.1) is assumed to be rate limiting.
This assumption is supported by DFT studies from Sarazen et al. [57] which show
that the activation energy is significantly higher for the C-C bond formation than
for the initiation and termination steps.
6. The C-C bond formation is assumed irreversible. This is also a corollary of assump-
tion 1, since cracking is the reverse of C-C bond formation.
7. It is assumed that oligomer formation always occurs through consecutive reactions
involving at least one propene molecule. This assumption is reasonable since the
molar concentration of propene at low conversion is significantly higher than the
concentration of products. Consequently, it is much more likely that e.g. C6 reacts
with propene to form a trimer than with another C6 to form a tetramer.

the amorphous catalysts become inactive at higher temperatures due to desorption of the
olefins. Conversely, more sites may remain active at higher temperatures due to the higher
acid strength of zeolites thereby leading to increased activity compared to the amorphous
catalysts. However, further studies are likely needed to determine the actual cause of this
phenomenon.

5
Discussion
In this section, a number of reflections and perspectives regarding the errors and chal-
lenges of the experimental and modeling work in this thesis will be presented. Following
that, a number of industrial perspectives of the work will be discussed along with recom-
mendations for future work. For a more detailed discussion regarding individual results,
the reader is referred to the results chapter (chapter 4).
5.1
Mass transfer limitations
When determining kinetic data such as rate constants, activation energies etc. it is gen-
erally desirable to operate under experimental conditions that are not limited by mass
transfer. This is because mass transfer effects influence the observed apparent kinetics in
a way which makes it diﬀicult to discern the true kinetic behavior of the catalyst. How-
ever, it is often diﬀicult to know in advance whether or not experiments are done in a
mass transfer limited or kinetically limited regime since the kinetic behavior of the cata-
lyst is unknown. Therefore, the influence of mass transfer limitations are often evaluated
post-hoc after kinetic data have been obtained. Such an evaluation will be presented here.
For systems with first order kinetics, the influence of both external mass transfer and
mass transfer in the catalyst mesopores can be quantified based on the so-called ”overall
effectiveness factor” (Ω) once kinetic data has been obtained. This effectiveness factor
represents the ratio of the apparent reaction rate to the rate if no mass transfer limitations
had been present. Consequently, a value of Ωclose to 100% indicates that experiments
are kinetically limited rather than limited by mass transfer.
This effectiveness factor Ωwas calculated for each catalyst as a function of the reaction
temperature based on structural properties of the catalysts and the measured Arrhenius
fits from section 4.1.4. A full description of this analysis may be found in Appendix S.
It should be noted, that the calculated effectiveness factors are highly dependent on the
catalyst particle size. However, for the work presented in this thesis, the particle size is not
uniform but instead just known to be in a range of 150-300 µm. Therefore, it was decided
to calculate the effectiveness factor for both 150 µm and 300 µm particles to obtain an
upper and lower bound on Ω. In addition, a total gas flow rate of 200 sccm was assumed.
However, the calculated effectiveness factors were not significantly affected by the assumed
flow rate in the range between 45-200 sccm so this assumption should not influence the
generality of the presented results. The results are shown in Figure 5.1.
As it can be seen from Figure 5.1, the calculated effectiveness factors were > 99% for all
catalysts at 200◦C for both particle sizes. Consequently, this indicates that all experiments
performed at lower temperatures were free of mass transfer limitations. As the temper-

5.2
Experimental challenges
For the experiments described in this thesis, a major limitation has been related to the
gas chromatograph. Firstly, the number of isomers and the substantially overlapping peak
groups in the FID chromatogram makes a quantitative determination of the selectivity to-
ward products with C-number greater than 11-12 impractical. In addition, the sampling
loop of the GC is currently operating at a constant temperature of 150◦C. This means that
without a condensation step, larger isomers would tend to get stuck in the GC sampling
loop. Therefore a condensation step is used. However, this condensation combined with
92
Oligomerization of Medium Length Olefins
==PAGE:107==
the uncertain detection of higher products makes C12 the practical limit for detection by
the current GC setup. This upper limit would be reasonable when studying e.g. gasoline
production but is a significant issue when studying oligomerization towards jet fuel for
two reasons. Firstly, this is because it does not allow a meaningful assessment of the se-
lectivity towards jet fuel range products (C8-C16), which makes the setup poorly suited for
optimization of process parameters for the applied industrial process. Secondly, due to the
experimental challenges related to the condensation of hydrocarbons in the condensation
flask of the setup, it was required to operate at low conversions in order to obtain detailed
on-line data regarding the kinetics and product selectivities of the oligomerization process.
However, the real industrial process would likely run at much higher levels of feedstock
conversion. This means that oligomers would be present in larger quantities than propene
and would therefore change the surface coverage of different adsorbates on the catalytic
sites. This may change the kinetic behavior of the process, and means that the kinetic data
obtained in this work (i.e. rate constants for propene conversion, deactivation behavior
etc.) may not be accurately extended to the industrial process.
Because of these factors, the experimental setup used in this thesis is likely better suited
for fundamental studies which improve the qualitative understanding of phenomena such
as mass transfer, kinetics, shape selectivity, relative catalyst activity and similar rather
than a quantitative estimation of parameters for the industrial process.
5.3
Modeling challenges and perspectives
In this work, a simple kinetic model was constructed, which seems to describe the behavior
of the real system with reasonable accuracy. However, due to the modeling assumptions,
it is unlikely that the model can be accurately extended to the high conversion conditions
of the real industrial process.
One of the reasons for this is that increasing the conversion would change the surface
coverage of the different adsorbates (i.e. more oligomers would be present compared to
propene) and thus likely impact the kinetics of the primary oligomerization reactions in a
way which is not accounted for in the simple model. This could potentially be accounted
for by applying more sophisticated kinetic expressions like equations (3.9)-(3.11) cf. section
3.6. However, as previously mentioned, it was not possible to fit this more sophisticated
model accounting for the surface equilibria of adsorbed and protonated species, since this
systematically resulted in overfitting.
In addition, the real catalysts do not simply have acid sites of a single acid strength but
rather a distribution of sites with different acid strengths making the assumption of a
single value of Ki for each olefin inaccurate. In this regard, a different modeling approach
has been successfully applied by Oliveira et al. [62] where the value of the equilibrium
constants Ki are calculated for each strength of acid sites in the acid site distribution
based on results from NH3-TPD experiments. This is done by first calculating the acti-

since the surface seems to be mostly covered with propene (i.e. K3C3 ≫1) under most
conditions studied. This means that the equilibrium constants Ki likely do not have a
strong influence on the observed kinetics and would therefore be diﬀicult to fit.
Another limiting assumption of the presented model is that cracking and other secondary
reactions are neglected.
However, since the cracking selectivity in the oligomerization
process increases with the conversion cf. section 4.1.5, it is a poor assumption to neglect
cracking for the industrial process. Consequently, a significantly more complex reaction
network would need to be implemented which accounts for cracking and subsequent co-
oligomerization of the cracked products in order to extend the model to higher conversions.
In order to do this, it would require a better understanding of the kinetics of these co-
oligomerization reactions. However, since cracking products amounted to only a small
fraction of the product distribution for most the experiments described in this thesis,
it was deemed unlikely that kinetic information of suﬀicient quality could be extracted
from the data.
Consequently, implementation of this kind of reaction network in the
model would likely require that additional experiments were conducted to further study
the influence of the structure of the reactant olefins on the kinetics of the oligomerization
process.
5.4
Industrial perspectives
The majority of the experiments presented in this thesis work were conducted at low
conversion conditions which are most likely far from the conditions desired in an industrial
olefin oligomerization process. However, there are still a number of observations from the
experiments which could be relevant for the design of an industrial process. Firstly, it was
previously argued, that the values of the effective rate constants for the different catalysts
may not be accurately extended to industrial feedstock conversion levels due to different
adsorbate coverages etc. which may impact the kinetics. However, it is still expected that
94
Oligomerization of Medium Length Olefins
==PAGE:109==
the determined values of the effective first order rate constant k at low conversions give a
reasonable relative indication of which catalysts are most active even as the conversion is
increased.
Secondly, while zeolite catalysts are often stated in literature to be more active than
amorphous silica-alumina catalysts owing to their higher acid strength [19], the results
from this thesis seems to indicate that this is highly dependent on the temperature. For
example (cf. section 4.1.4), the MSA catalyst was by far the most active catalyst at
200◦C as reflected by the high value of k which was more than double compared to the
most acidic H-ZSM-5 catalyst. However, at 300◦C the effective rate constant for the H-
ZSM-5 (Si/Al=13.5) catalyst was roughly 4 times higher compared to the MSA catalyst.
Consequently, which catalyst is most active seems to be highly dependent on the reaction
temperature. Therefore, strictly considering the catalyst activity, it may be more practical
to use an H-ZSM-5 catalyst if high temperature operation is required. Conversely, at lower
temperatures amorphous silica-alumina catalysts may be favoured.
However, the relative activities are not the only factors which dictate the industrial feasi-
bility of the catalyst. For example, the product distributions obtained from the catalysts
may also be noted.
In this regard it has been shown in this work in agreement with
previous studies by Van Grieken et al. [25], that the amorphous MSA (likely a form of
Al-SBA-15) and ASA catalysts show somewhat higher dimer selectivities compared to
H-ZSM-5 which have comparatively higher selectivity towards trimers and tetramers. No-
tably, the article [25] found that this apparently higher dimer selectivity of Al-SBA-15 also
applies for 1-hexene at industrially relevant conversion levels of 75-90%. Consequently,
using Al-SBA-15 (and probably also ASA) may result in a somewhat lighter jet fuel mix-
ture compared to H-ZSM-5 catalysts. Whether a high dimer or trimer selectivity is desired
is most likely quite dependent on the feedstock olefin. For example, if propene or butene
is used as feed, then a high dimer selectivity is likely not that desirable since the dimers
(C6 C8) fall outside or on the very edge of the desired C-number range of jet fuel for
these feeds. Consequently, for these feeds a higher trimer selectivity is likely preferred.
However, if pentene to octene range feedstocks are used, then a high dimer selectivity
would likely be desired since the trimers fall on the edge or outside the desired C-number
range. Consequently, the optimal choice of catalyst for producing a high quality jet fuel
may be dependent on length of the olefins produced in the previous MtO step.
The long term performance and regenerability of the catalysts is also crucial for the com-
mercial viability of the catalysts. In this regard, it was shown that all investigated catalysts
regain the majority of their activity by regeneration under inert atmosphere at elevated
temperatures ∼400◦C. When regeneration in an oxidative atmosphere was used instead,
this often led to a decreased catalyst performance cf. section 4.2 for the experiments per-
formed in this thesis. However, for the industrial process it is likely necessary to use a
somewhat oxidating atmosphere for regeneration since non-volatile coke formation likely
Oligomerization of Medium Length Olefins
95
==PAGE:110==
becomes significant for longer time on stream or through repeated regeneration in inert
atmosphere [38]. In this regard, it was observed by De Klerk [37] that a controlled slow
oxidation in atmospheres dilute in oxygen was crucial to avoid rapid temperature increase
in the catalyst bed and resulting catalyst damage. In addition, the results of this thesis
indicate that H-ZSM-5 catalysts seem to retain a higher fraction of their initial catalyst
activity than the amorphous ASA and MSA. However, longer studies are likely needed to
fully evaluate the long-term activity of the catalysts.
Last but not least, the price of the catalysts may also be crucial for determining the com-
mercial viability of the catalysts. In this regard, it should be noted that aluminosilicate
catalysts are generally relatively cheap [21]. However, more detailed technoeconomic as-
sessments accounting for the regenerability and total lifetime of the catalysts are needed
to determine which catalysts are most feasible for the process from an economic point of
view.
5.5
Future work
As mentioned in a previous section, one of the main challenges involved with describing
the cracking reactions in the oligomerization process was the lack of suﬀicient data on
the kinetics of oligomerization of cracked species. In the beginning of the thesis work
presented here, it was planned to study oligomerization of different chain-length feedstocks
to characterize the influence of the feedstock chain length and structure on the kinetics
of the process. In addition, it was also desired to perform co-oligomerization studies with
mixed feedstocks in order to investigate the kinetics when mixtures of feed olefins are used.
Unfortunately, this turned out not to be possible due to equipment failure preventing the
use of liquid feeds. However, it would be interesting to pursue this topic further once the
equipment has been made operational. This may allow the construction of a more realistic
reaction network also including cracking and co-oligomerization in the kinetic model. In
November 2024 (i.e. during this thesis work), an article by Dubray et al. was published
applying a very similar approach for olefin oligomerization of mixed C3-C8 feeds diluted in
n-heptane solvent to successfully construct a kinetic model of the process. In this regard,
it would be interesting to investigate if, and to what extent, a similar approach may be
applied to oligomerization in the gas phase.
It would also be relevant to perform oligomerization studies under conditions with higher
conversion in order to characterize the kinetics and product distributions under more
industrially relevant conditions. In the current setup, high conversion studies present a
trade-off where results obtained at high conversion may be more industrially relevant,
but limit the continuous analysis of the product distribution due to condensation effects.
However, it is possible that these issues could be solved by modification of the valve oven
in the GC (which is currently operating at 150 ◦C) to operate at a higher temperature.
Alternatively, a method of on-line liquid sampling could also be implemented in the current
setup such that the condensed liquid fraction could be included in the product distribution.

In this regard, an accurate quantification of the relative amounts of different C-number
products would likely be diﬀicult for the condensed liquid fraction due to the previously
mentioned large numbers of structural isomers and overlapping detection windows for
higher olefins.
However, while it may be diﬀicult to strictly separate olefins based on
C-number, it would likely still be possible to roughly estimate the selectivity towards
products of based on their size. Alternatively, other GC-methods are available such as
simulated distillation curves which can be used to estimate the fuel selectivity and evaluate
the fuel quality (even for substantially overlapping GC-peaks). This method is based on
correlating the GC retention time with the approximate boiling points of the detected
products and thus avoids the need for separation of products based on their C-number
[63, 25].
In addition, as discussed in section 4.1.5, the conversion-selectivity relationship seems to be
affected by the temperature but not pressure, WHSV or deactivation in the range of 200-
300◦C for the low conversion gas-phase oligomerization studied in this report. However,
more work is likely needed to fully characterize and confirm the effect of temperature on
the product distribution. One way of investigating this would be to compare experiments
at different temperatures under iso-conversion conditions achieved by adjusting the WHSV
or similar to achieve a specified conversion. Alternatively, experiments could be conducted
at different temperatures but with a wide flow variation at each temperature to establish
the full conversion-selectivity relationship at each temperature. Last but not least, it is
presently unclear whether and how this temperature effect impacts the fuel quality under
industrially relevant conversion. Another notable results from these experiments was the
observation that the activity of H-ZSM-5 was much more affected by temperature than the
amorphous catalysts. It is presently unclear why this is the case, and therefore it would
be interesting to study this phenomenon further.
For the deactivation and regeneration studies presented in section 4.2, it would likely be
interesting to further characterize the spent catalysts after each regeneration step (using
FTIR, NH3-TPD or other methods) to better understand why the observed deactivation
occurs (e.g. loss in the number of active sites, loss in catalyst surface area or similar), and
to more confidently confirm the nature of the deactivating species (occluded oligomers or
coke). Finally, it would be interesting to perform a more thorough study to investigate
the long-time performance of the different catalysts and the quantitative effect of reaction
conditions on the catalyst deactivation.

Conclusion
In this thesis, oligomerization of propene using different aluminosilicate catalysts was stud-
ied in a high pressure gas-phase fixed bed reactor setup. The effect of reaction conditions
(WHSV, pressure, temperature and time on stream) were studied for three n-H-ZSM-5
catalysts with Si/Al ratios of 13.5, 26.4 and 36.1 respectively as well as two amorphous
mesoporous silica-alumina catalysts which in this work were referred to as ASA and MSA
respectively. The ASA catalyst was a commercial catalyst and was strictly mesoporous
whereas the MSA catalyst was synthesized in-house and had both micropores and meso-
pores. These experiments were done under mostly low conversion conditions (<10%) to
limit issues related to gradients within the catalyst bed and condensation. Based on these
experiments, it was observed that the main products of the reaction were propene dimers
(C6), trimers (C9) and tetramers (C12) with a smaller fraction of non-integer multiples of
propene also being formed due to cracking. Here, it was found that the propene conversion
was approximately inversely proportional to WHSV in the range 10-45 h−1, proportional
to the pressure in the range 16-36 bar and increased exponentially with temperature. The
catalyst activity decreased with time on stream, with the most significant effect being ob-
served within the first 20 hours. It was also observed that oligomerization kinetics are first
order in propene at 200◦C and low conversion since the variation in propene conversion
with WHSV and pressure was well described by a first order reaction PFR equation. Con-
versely, the corresponding second order reaction PFR equation gave a poor description of
the variation in the propene conversion with pressure.
Apparent first order rate constants fitted to the experimental data for all catalysts from
data collected at 200◦C between 23 and 48 hours on stream showed that MSA was
by far the most active catalyst at 200◦C with an apparent first order rate constant of
k = 0.199 m3/kg/h being observed. Conversely, the ASA catalyst in the same period
had a rate constant of k = 0.0549 m3/kg/h. However, it was later discovered that this
catalyst had not been properly calcined, so the true value is likely somewhat higher. For
the H-ZSM-5 catalysts, the activity decreased with increasing Si/Al ratio with the high-
est value of k = 0.0994 m3/kg/h being observed for the catalyst with an Si/Al ratio of
13.5 at 200◦C. However, as the temperature was increased, the activity of the H-ZSM-5
catalysts showed a stronger increase compared to the amorphous catalysts as reflected
by the higher apparent activation energies of 84.7-110.6 kJ/mol for the oligomerization
process on H-ZSM-5 catalysts compared to only 46.6-60.6 kJ/mol for the amorphous cata-
lysts. Therefore, all H-ZSM-5 catalysts were significantly more active than the amorphous
catalysts at 300◦C.
Finally, ASA and MSA produced similar product distributions at
the same levels of conversion. ASA and MSA generally showed higher selectivity toward
dimers and lower selectivity toward trimers and tetreamers compared to the H-ZSM-5 cat-

99
==PAGE:114==
alysts. Conversely, the H-ZSM-5 catalysts showed higher cracking selectivities, especially
at increased temperatures. The selectivity of each catalyst was mainly dependent on the
propene conversion and not significantly affected by WHSV or pressure at similar conver-
sion levels. However, temperature seemed to have some effect on the selectivity-conversion
relationship.
Experiments were also conducted for the ASA and H-ZSM-5 (Si/Al=26.4) catalysts to
study regeneration of the catalysts in inert argon atmosphere or air respectively. From
these experiments, it was observed for both catalysts at 200◦C as well as for the ASA
catalyst at 280◦C that the initial catalyst activity could be regained by applying a tem-
perature ramp up to a temperature of 400◦C under inert argon flow. This indicates that
accumulation of large oligomers on the catalyst surface and in catalyst pores is the main
cause of deactivation for both catalysts and not non-volatile coke species. Conversely, a
decrease in catalyst activity was often observed when air was used during the temperature
ramp. It was hypothesized, that this occurred due to hot spots or similar as hydrocarbons
are combusted within the catalyst bed.
Last but not least, a simple kinetic model was constructed and fitted to the experimental
data from the H-ZSM-5 (Si/Al=26.4) and ASA catalysts at 200◦C. This model seemed
to give a reasonable description of the conversion and product yields at low conversion.
However, it is unlikely that the model can be extended to industrial conversion levels due
to simplifying assumptions regarding the adsorbate coverages and since the model neglects
cracking.
