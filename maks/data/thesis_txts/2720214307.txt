Abstract 
Background: Musculoskeletal injuries and chronic degenerative diseases commonly affect both 
athletic and sedentary horses. Equine athletes are at risk of suffering musculoskeletal injuries after 
intense exercise. In humans muscle biopsies are a common method for monitoring muscle health 
and injuries. However, due to the invasiveness of the procedure muscle biopsies are not applicable 
in equine athletes.   For this reason, innovative non-invasive methods are needed for monitoring 
muscle health, injuries and recovery in equine athletes.  Recently, myotonometry proved its validity 
and reliability in assessing mechanical properties of skeletal muscles.  
Aim: The aim of the present study was to evaluate the validity and variability of MyotonPro as a tool 
for skeletal muscle assessments in equine athletes. 
Methods: Myotonometry was applied on the extensor carpi radialis muscle of equines (n = 53). Four 
different protocols were applied: 1. Different standing positions (n=11); 2. Cooling and heating of the 
muscle tissue (n=17); 3. Laser therapy (n=18); 4. Exercise (n=20). Furthermore, differences in 
muscle stiffness were investigated between healthy (n=36) and injured (n=30) horses and between 
horses that lived on the paddock more than 8 hours a day (n=24) and horses spending less than 
eight hours on the paddock (n=42). 
Results: A horse, standing in a squared position, resulted the most optimal position for 
myotonometry measurements to ensure the relaxation of muscles without resorting to sedation. 
Muscle stiffness obtained was 361.5 ±28.29 N/m on the right side and 363.3 ±28.29 N/m for the left 
limb (p=0.9494). After cooling the muscle tissue, muscle stiffness and frequency significantly 
increased (p=0.0028, p=0.021), while relaxation time and creep significantly decreased (p=0.0144, 
p=0.141). Muscle heating resulted in a significant decrease in muscle stiffness and frequency 
(p=0.0013, p=0.002), and a significant increase in relaxation time and creep (p=0.0005, p=0.0004). 
Myotonometry applied one hour after low-level laser therapy showed a significant decrease in 
decrement, relaxation time and creep (p=0.0071, p=0.0282, p=0.028). Regardless of the intensity of 
exercise, muscle stiffness and frequency increased significantly (p=0.0273, p=0.0137), while creep 
and relaxation time decreased (p=0.0149, p=0.0131). Injured horses exhibited significantly 
increased muscle decrement compared to healthy horses (p=0.0134). No significant changes were 
observed among horses that had access to roam in the paddock.  
Conclusion: This study demonstrated that the MyotonPro is a valid and reliable non-invasive device 
that can assess mechanical properties of muscle tissue in equine athletes. The MyotonPro device 
proofed to be sensitive enough to detect changes in muscles tissue in different conditions. The 
present data suggest that MyotonPro device can be effectively used in the veterinary field. The 
results are highly reliable when conducted properly, in a calm environment for the horse.

Introduction 
Equestrian sport is growing in popularity around the world and includes a wide range of different 
disciplines including racing, steeple-chasing, show-jumping, cross-country, dressage, polo, 
polocrosse, endurance, carriage driving, military, western, vaulting and hunting [1]. In general, 
equestrian sport is associated with a high risk for musculoskeletal injuries especially at high 
competitive levels [2]. However, musculoskeletal injuries and chronic degenerative diseases 
commonly affect both athletic and sedentary horses and can cause the end of an athletic career and 
for some horses even death [3]. Horses can suffer injuries, strains, tears, or bumps, especially in 
case of strenuous activities. A study by Cullen et al. showed that muscle tears were a primary cause 
of lameness in horses [4]. Lameness is the most common diagnosis in equine veterinary practice [5, 
6]. It can be challenging to make an accurate and precise diagnosis of horses’ muscle health, which 
is why the development of new and reliable techniques is essential. In addition, to prevent 
musculoskeletal injuries, including lameness in equine athletes, it is important to understand and 
monitor changes in muscle and connective tissues during rest, exercise, and recovery [7, 8].  
Skeletal muscle tissue is the most prevalent type of tissue and is responsible for generating muscle 
force through contractions, which enables movement. In humans, skeletal muscle tissue can account 
for up to 50% of a person's body mass, depending on their level of physical fitness and training [9]. 
For horses, this percentage is similar, but may vary depending on the breed [10]. The force 
generated by the skeletal muscle tissue is transmitted through tendons and bones, which then 
causes movement in the joints. Skeletal muscle tissue is also responsible for maintaining posture, 
providing support for internal organs, and producing heat to regulate body temperature. Overall, 
skeletal muscle tissue is vital for maintaining physical function and mobility, and plays a crucial role 
in overall health and well-being [11].   
The architecture of skeletal muscle is an important aspect, which determines muscle function and 
their capability to generate force. Skeletal muscle tissues consist of muscle fibres that are wrapped 
together by connective tissue, where each bundle of muscle fibres is called a fasciculo. The muscle 
fibres are composed by a number of myofibrils which are comprised of myofilaments [12, 13]. The 
arrangement of myofibrils in a striated pattern contains the contractile units of the skeletal muscle, 
called sarcomeres [14]. The sarcomeres consist of thick and thin filaments disposed longitudinally. 
The thick filament is composed mainly by the myosin, a large protein which comprises two pairs of 
light chains and one pair of heavy chains. The two heavy chains form a helical tail of the myosin, 
twisting around each other, while the light chains have the role of being the two heads of the myosin 
at the other end. The thin filament is composed of three proteins: actin, tropomyosin, and troponin 
[15]. To perform muscle contraction, an electrical impulse from a motor neuron stimulates the release 
of calcium into the sarcoplasm. This activates myosin heads which bind to exposed actin-binding 
==PAGE:13==
Page | 4  
 
sites, change their conformation and slide along the thin filament. After releasing their grip on actin 
and returning to their original position, the myosin heads are ready for a new cycle. [15, 16]. At a 
macroscopic level, the architecture of a muscle can influence its stiffness and elasticity, which in turn 
affects its performance [17]. Any changes in  the mechanical behaviour of muscle tissue is influenced 
by changes in muscle mass or intensity of muscle activity [8]. The mechanical behaviour of a skeletal 
muscle is viscoelastic and anisotropic. The displacement of the musculoskeletal tissue shows a 
strain-rate dependency on the applied loading rate. Thus, muscle stiffness represents the rigidity of 
muscle tissue, and is dependent on forces applied, intrinsic material composition of muscle and 
muscle structure, especially the length and the cross-sectional area of the tissue [18]. Stiffness is a 
mechanical parameter that describes the resistance to deformation in response to stress. It can 
reveal the presence of muscle injury and muscle disorders including strains, lacerations, contusions  
and degenerative diseases (e.g. muscular dystrophies) [19]. Under pathological conditions muscle 
stiffness can play an important role, individuals with cerebral palsy (CP) may suffer from an increase 
in muscle stiffness, also known as muscle contractures. This includes a decrease in mobility and 
range of motion, resulting in a decrease in quality of life [20].  During active tension, the stiffness 
depends on the properties of contractile units. When the muscle is relaxed the stiffness is dependent 
on the structural components [21]. Muscle damage and injuries lead to altered stiffness values. 
Palpation is the standard clinical method of assessing stiffness in soft tissues.  
Creep is the time dependency of skeletal muscles under constant load, while the stress-relaxation 
represents the reduction of stress under constant deformation [22]. The biomechanical properties of 
musculoskeletal tissue can be drastically altered by pathologies and diseases. These conditions can 
have a profound impact on the ability of the tissue to function properly, leading to a wide range of 
physical impairments. Muscle fibrosis or atrophy are conditions that result from abnormalities in the 
skeletal muscles’ functionality. Atrophy is caused by a decrease in mechanical load of the muscle, 
and results in a reduction of muscle mass, density and mitochondria. On the other hand, hypertrophy 
coincides with an increase in muscle mass and with a better aerobic capacity of the muscle [20].  
Muscular fatigue is a complex phenomenon caused by biochemical alterations that modify the 
mechanics of muscle contraction, resulting in a decrease in the capacity of muscle to produce force 
in response to contractile activity [23]. This usually occurs after an increase in the effort of sustaining 
a physical task [24, 25]. While the exact cause of muscle fatigue is not fully understood, several 
theories exist. Different studies suggest that a fatiguing condition leads to an accumulation of 
potassium in extracellular fluid, causing a decrease in the release of Ca2+, which is necessary to 
initiate muscle contraction [25-27]. Extreme muscle fatigue increases the risk of muscle damage and 
can lead to an increase in muscle stiffness, which can be caused by various factors including ambient 
temperature or exercise intensity [8, 28].

Page | 5  
 
Laser therapy is often used to loosen the muscle after intense exercise or injuries. Low level laser 
irradiation stimulates cellular activity and their chemical processes in order to promote tissue repair, 
decrease inflammation and improve blood flow [23]. Aghamohammadi et al. have demonstrated that 
laser therapy is effective in reducing tonsillectomy post-surgical muscle pain [29]. However, further 
scientific evidence is required to gain a better understanding on its application. Many studies have 
tried to verify the benefits of laser therapy in case of muscle fatigue, but without finding significant 
results regarding the efficacy of the treatment [30, 31]. 
In order to monitor and evaluate muscle health, invasive and non-invasive techniques can be 
adopted. Muscle biopsies provide insight into the internal structures of the body, allowing for accurate 
information about muscle stiffness when using in situ techniques and tests to evaluate mechanical 
properties. Furthermore, muscle biopsies are incredibly useful for detailed muscle histology and 
other analyses [32]. However, due to their invasive approach and the potential for adverse effects 
(e.g. pain, infection, bleeding, discomfort, nerve damage), biopsies are unfortunately not a viable 
option for equine athletes [33]. Thus, non-invasive techniques are needed in order to monitor the 
health and wellbeing of equine athletes during every level of their training. One non-invasive method 
that can be used to monitor muscle stiffness in human athletes is myotonometry.  
Myotonometry is non-invasive and entails digital palpation of skeletal muscle tissue. In humans the 
MyotonPro device has been proven to be a valid and reliable method that provides information about 
biomechanical and viscoelastic properties in superficial soft tissues [8, 34-38]. Davidson et al. (2017) 
assessed the validity and reliability of this device, when used on smaller muscles of the body (thenar 
and perineal muscles in women). Good interrater and intrarater reliability for muscle stiffness was 
observed, indicating that the MyotonPro device facilitates an accurate diagnosis of muscle tone [35, 
39]. Garcia-Bernal et al. (2022) validated the measurements of muscle elasticity and stiffness 
performed by the MyotonPro. The device was able to detect differences between healthy muscles 
and muscles affected by spasticity. This approach provided a valid and easy method to quantify 
muscle mechanical properties in patients post-stroke [38]. 
The MyotonPro device performs digital palpation, facilitating accurate diagnosis of muscle tone [35]. 
During the measurements the MyotonPro device is placed perpendicular to the skin over the muscle, 
where a small plastic probe applies brief (15ms), gentle mechanical impulses to elicit muscle 
oscillations. A pre-pressure load of 0.18N is applied initially to maintain the skin contact and 
compress the subcutaneous tissue. Then other brief impulses of 0.40N, lasting 15µs, are impressed, 
resulting in a total applied force of 0.58N [35, 38]. An in-built accelerometer records the oscillations 
and determines five biomechanical and viscoelastic parameters that characterize the stiffness of 
myofascial tissue [40]. Three parameters are already shown on the display of the device at the time 
of the measurement: frequency (F), dynamic stiffness (S) and logarithmic decrement (D). The last 
==PAGE:15==
Page | 6  
 
two values, intrinsically registered by the device, are the mechanical stress relaxation time (R) and 
creep (C). In this study the feasibility of the MyotonPro (Myoton AS, Tallin, Estonia) device, will be 
investigated. 
MyotonPro is an invaluable tool for diagnosing pathologies and detecting early signs of injury by 
measuring changes in stiffness after exercise. It is well-known that muscle injuries are a common 
occurrence among athletes, resulting in pain, dysfunction, and reduced capacity in performance. 
Muscle damage in athletes leads to an increase in muscle stiffness. This device can help with an 
accurate diagnosis, optimizing the treatment process and expediting recovery, thus reducing the 
time needed to gain full recovery [41]. 
Furthermore, several other non-invasive techniques to assess muscle health have been developed, 
such as, magnetic resonance elastography, shear-wave elastography (SWE) and acoustic 
myography (AMG) [42]. Kelly et al. (2018) compared ultrasound shear wave elastography and 
superficial mechanical deformation using myotonometry. Magnetic resonance elastography utilizes 
ultrasound radiation forces to generate a wave that propagates through the tissue to evaluate its 
material properties. Myotonometry technique has been applied through the MyotonPro device, which 
assesses muscle stiffness after a mechanical compression to the surface next to the muscle tissue. 
Both methods have demonstrated good reliability when using single or mean of three measurements. 
However, there are substantial differences in the costs per unit and availability of each approach. 
The authors concluded that the MyotonPro was easier to use, portable and with lower costs than the 
SWE [18].  
Acoustic Myography is an alternative technique to electromyography to evaluate muscle activity and 
fatigue [26, 43]. This approach records signals of muscle contraction being independent of electrical 
signals between nerve and muscle [44]. AMG can be particularly useful in studies involving athletes 
or subjects who have to perform complex movements or in equine athletes that cannot be shaved 
since the sensors are insensitive to sweat, fur and any kind of frenetic motion. Muscle activity and 
fatigue is shown through the amplitude and frequency of the recorded signal [26, 43]. Pingel et al. 
(2019) used this method to evaluate differences in muscle activity during treadmill exercise between 
healthy subjects and subjects affected by CP. In this study AMG (MyoDynamik ApS, Copenhagen 
Denmark – www.myodynamik.com – referred to as a CURO-Diagnostic) was used to record the 
activity of the medial gastrocnemius muscle, from both the legs in hemiplegic individuals with CP. 
The authors observed that during treadmill exercise, individuals with CP recruited more fibres in 
order to maintain the same speed compared to healthy control subjects. Thus, the authors concluded 
that the acoustic myography is a valid method for the assessment of muscle health and activity [44].  
The aim of this study was to evaluate the validity and variability of MyotonPro as a tool for skeletal 
muscle assessments in equine athletes. In addition, it was aimed to assess the outcome of

myotonometry in different conditions including different standing positions, cooling- and heating of 
the muscle tissue, laser therapy, and exercise. Furthermore, a sub-aim of this study was to 
investigate differences in muscle stiffness in healthy horses compared with horses with a current 
injury, and in horses that lived on the paddock most of their daily time compared to horses spending 
less than eight hours on the paddock. 
Methods 
Subjects 
In total, 53 horses were included in this study. 32 males and 21 females, age mean 10 ± 6.2 years. 
An information sheet has been compiled, for each horse measured, where all the personal data, 
clinical history, training status and daily activity were recorded. In the Appendix, subject 
characteristics, clinical history and daily activities are shown in Table A 1, Table A 2 and Table A 3 
respectively. In this study four different protocols were applied: 1) Standing protocol 2) Cooling 
protocol and Heating protocol 3) Laser therapy protocol and 4) Exercise protocol. Table A 4, in the 
Appendix, shows which protocol each subject performed. 
For the pilot study, 11 horses were included for the measurements (12.7 ± 4.6 years, 8 males and 3 
females) and measured in three different standing positions. Additionally, on four of these horses 
(11.3 ± 5 years, 2 males and 2 females), muscle activity data have been collected using acoustic 
myography.  
For the cooling and heating protocol, 17 horses were included (11.7 ± 5.8 years, 12 males and 5 
females) and measured pre and post heating/cooling. While 18 horses were subjected to laser 
therapy and measured pre, post and post_1h (10.36 ± 1.5 years, 11 males and 7 females). Finally, 
for the exercise protocol, 20 horses have been measured pre and post exercise (7.55 ± 5.53 years, 
10 males, 9 females). One subject has been measured twice, after two exercise sessions in two 
different days.  
MyotonPro device 
In this study, the main method that was used was myotonometry. The MyotonPro (Myoton AS, 
Tallinn, Estonia) is a device that measures the mechanical properties of skeletal muscle in vivo. The 
settings of the used MyotonPro device were as follows: a custom-made protocol was added and Pre 
and Post measurements for all four protocols were recorded and saved. The MyotonPro was set to 
do multiple scans with 3 repetitions at each timepoint.  
The biomechanical parameters obtained from the device are the frequency, muscle stiffness, 
decrement, relaxation time and creep. The frequency, measured in Hz, indicates the tone of the

In order to minimize variation, all measurements have been made by the same experienced operator. 
The muscle of interest was palpated, and the thickest part of the muscle belly was marked in order 
to secure that all measurements were made at the exact same spot. All measurements were made 
holding the device perpendicular to the skin and as still as possible. The MyotonPro device indicated 
the variation by showing the variation coefficient (CV) between the three repetitions right away. If the 
CV was higher than 5% the measurement was repeated. 
Acoustic myography equipment 
CURO diagnostic sensors have been used in the present study. The equipment consists of two 
platinum sensors placed on the extensor carpi radialis muscles of the animal. A big quantity of gel 
was placed between the fur and the sensors, to minimize the interference created by the fur. 
Connected to these sensors two Bluetooth devices have been connected and synchronised with the 
mobile device, where its particular app was installed. From CURO software three parameters are 
obtained: E-score, S-score and T-score, all numbers from 0-10 [43]. E-score represents the activity 
periods within the total activity time of the muscle, it gives an overview of efficiency and coordination 
==PAGE:18==
Page | 9  
 
of muscle contractions. For example, for low E-score values the muscle results in low coordination 
and efficiency and the muscle fibres are constantly activated. S-score indicates the spatial 
summation of the signal, and it is inversely proportional to the number of muscle fibres recruited. 
Finally, T-score is calculated as the temporal summation of the signal and represents the frequency 
at which the muscle fibres are activated [26, 44].  
Pilot Study - Standing 
A pilot study has been conducted using n=11 horses, where myotonometry measurements have 
been made from extensor carpi radialis muscles, to determine the most optimal standing position for 
the myotonometry measurements. The analysis was conducted by measuring muscle stiffness from 
the extensor carpi radialis muscles of both forelimbs. An assistant was instructed to raise the left 
forelimb to an angle of approximately 45° while the operator held the device, conducting 
myotonometry on both sides. Subsequently, the right limb has been lifted and the operator has 
repeated both the measurements at the marked points.  
In addition, the activity of the extensor carpi radialis muscle was measured from four horses with the 
acoustic myography (MyoDynamik ApS, Copenhagen Denmark – www.myodynamik.com – referred 
to as a CURO-Diagnostic). While the horses were standing on hard ground two platinum sensors 
have been applied on the muscle belly of both the forelimbs with a big amount of gel underneath, to 
avoid that the air between the fur could interfere with the signal. Connected to the piezoelectric 
sensors, two AMG units have been connected. A smartphone was connected to the units, and the 
muscle activity was recorded. During the measurement the horses were standing appropriately in a 
squared position for 15 seconds. In order to track the change of position during the analysis, the left 
sensor was gently touched, before the left leg was raised. After 15 seconds with the left limb raised 
to approximately 45 degrees, it was placed on the ground and the same procedure was repeated on 
the right side, lifting the right limb after touching the left sensor. Following the pilot study, it was 
decided that all subsequent measurements would be made while the horses were standing in a 
squared position, with all the limbs aligned, with special attention on full alignment of the forelimbs. 
Cooling and heating 
For the cooling and heating n=17 horses were included, and the muscle of interest was the left 
extensor carpi radialis muscle. Measurements were performed with the MyotonPro device, as 
described previously, and in order to check the temperature of the considered body area, a 
temperature gun (Helect, H1020, China) was used. For the cooling phase, two bags of instant ice 
were placed on each horse’s muscle (Matas sport care engangs ispose' (350gr) distributed by 
Tranberg & Dahl A/S (Sorø, DK)). While, two cherry bags (INatura, Belgium), with dimensions of 
13cmx55cm, were used for the muscle heating phase. The extensor carpi radialis muscle on the left 
forelimb was measured for all the horses. First, the operator measured and recorded the temperature 
==PAGE:19==
Page | 10  
 
of the area considered with the temperature gun, and then proceeded with the myotonometry 
measurements. For all the measurements, the operator checked carefully that the horse was 
standing in a squared position. Subsequently, two bags of ice were placed on the muscle for 25 
minutes and kept in place by the operator. After 25 min the operator measured and recorded the 
local skin temperature again using the temperature gun and proceeded with a post cooling 
myotonometry measurement. After a 20 min break one heated cherry bag was, placed on the same 
site as the cooling bag and held by the operator. To heat the cherry bags, they were placed in a 
microwave (Kenwood, K20MSS21E, China) for 3 min 30 seconds.  The operator was holding the 
cherry bags by hand in order to check that the bags were not too hot and caused burn injuries on 
the horse. After 12 min the bag was replaced with a new heated bag to ensure hot temperature 
during the entire protocol. After 25 min in total the temperature and myotonometry measurements 
were repeated and done exactly as previously.  
Laser treatment 
For this protocol n=18 horses were included, and the muscle of interest was the extensor carpi 
radialis muscle of the left forelimb. A K-Laser Vet 12 (Eltech K-Laser s.r.l., Italy) was the device used 
for the laser therapy. The measured horses were placed in the correct standing squared position 
and myotonometry was measured pre, post and post 1h using the MyotonPro device as previously 
described. The laser device has been set up for therapy of muscle fatigue for horses with dark coat 
and it lasted 7.30 minutes in total. The constant parameters were an average power of 5W duty cycle 
set to 50%, wavelength of 800-970 nm and a maximum power of 10W. Once the device has been 
set, the therapy is presented in 6 phases where the variable parameter is the frequency and the type 
of light emission. The first three phases consist of continuous emission at 100Hz, 500Hz and 
1000Hz, while the other three use the intense super pulse (ISP) mode using the same three 
frequencies. The therapy was carried out on all the horses by the same operator. For safety reasons 
the operator wore protective goggles and moved the probe continuously to avoid burn injuries on the 
animal. After lower-lever laser irradiation of the muscle, myotonometry was measured both directly 
post the treatment and one hour post the treatment. During the hour after the laser treatment the 
horses were allowed to move freely around in their box.  
Exercise 
For the exercise protocol, n=20 horses were included and the extensor carpi radialis muscle on the 
left forelimb was measured using myotonometry pre exercise and post exercise.  The included 
subjects were horses from different breeds and were used for different types of activity (see Table A 
3, in the Appendix). The workouts performed by the horses were all different from each other, so the 
type and intensity of the exercise was recorded by asking the rider to classify the performance based 
on how much exercise was done that day and how that exercise session related to the usual daily 
==PAGE:20==
Page | 11  
 
activity levels of their horse. The horses were divided into two groups, those that performed light 
exercise (n=10) and those that performed medium-intense exercise (n=10). In addition, two elite 
trotter horses were included in the study. Trotter horses possess a greater physical performance, as 
they are used to very high-intense training, in comparison to the other horses examined (dressage 
horses, jumping horses, and riding horses). For the present study, the two trotter horses underwent 
medium-intensity exercise. In the Appendix, Table A 4 listed the type and intensity of the exercises 
performed by the horses. 
Statistics 
All statistics were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.5.0 and R Statistical Software. To evaluate 
differences between standing position between horses, a two-way ANOVA analysis for the muscle 
stiffness was performed. When comparing the cooling and heating state of the muscle, a one-way 
ANOVA analysis was performed for each biomechanical parameter and paired t-tests were 
conducted between ‘Pre’ and ‘Post Cooling’, ‘Pre’ and ‘Post Heating’, ‘Post Cooling’ and ‘Post 
Heating’ phases. The results collected from the laser protocol have been analysed in the same way 
as the cooling and heating protocol. For the comparison between pre- and post- exercise, paired t-
tests were performed for each parameter. Finally, two additional analyses were carried out between 
healthy (n=36) and injured (n=30) horses and between horses that were spending most of their time 
in a paddock (n=24) or in a box (n=42). For these analyses unpaired t-tests were performed for all 
the muscle parameters. Results were considered significant when P<0.05. Means and standard 
deviations (SD) were obtained for all the parameters. All graphs were made in GraphPad Prism 
9.5.0, data are presented as means ± standard error of mean (SEM). Acoustic myography results 
are shown as mean ± SD.

Discussion 
The main finding of this study is that the MyotonPro device can be used as a clinical tool in the 
veterinary field for the evaluation of skeletal muscle of horses. The present study indicates that 
MyotonPro measurements on horses are both valid and reliable when conducted with care. Since 
muscle injuries are common in equestrian sports, this device might help to detect inappropriate 
changes in the mechanical properties of muscle and thereby help to prevent and/or diagnose muscle 
injuries in equine athletes. 
The pilot study revealed that MyotonPro can be effectively used on equines. The device accurately 
measured muscle stiffness from the extensor carpi radialis muscle while the horses were standing 
in a squared position. This finding demonstrates the potential of MyotonPro to provide valuable 
insights into the muscular health of equines. However, the initial measurements taken in this study 
were conducted on the thoracic trapezius muscle (data not shown). When measuring the thoracic 
trapezius muscle of 11 horses, a considerable intra-individual variation in the results was observed. 
It was evident that the results changed significantly when the horse moved its neck or head during 
the measurement. Even small distractions like the owner padding the head, excitement due to the 
presence of food, or loud noises would disturb the measurements. This observation was in line with 
a study by Maija Talvikki Vesterien (2018), who investigated the effect of finish sauna treatment on 
the  post- exercise recovery of muscle tissue of several muscles including the thoracic trapezius 
muscle in equine athletes [48]. The authors used myotonometry on nine horses, five horses were 
tested before, between and after two finnish sauna sessions (15 min and 27 min), and four horses 
were used as controls. The authors reported a large intra-individual variation in the measurements 
and attributed this to the horse's neck and head movements [48]. On the other hand, other studies 
have applied myotonometry on sedated and immobilised animals. The aim of the present study was 
to evaluate MyotonPro as a non-invasive device, this was why sedation was not taken into 
consideration during measurements. A study by Sonja Kneckt utilized myotonometry to detect 
myopathy on sedated blesboks. Twelve blesboks were sedated with two different drugs and a 
decrease in muscle stiffness and frequency was observed over time after both protocols. It was 
concluded that MyotonPro can effectively detect changes in the muscle tone of immobilized animals 
[49]. These results indicates that myotonometry is dependent on external factors including 
movement of the muscle of interest, its tension and relaxation. Thus, any operator using 
myotonometry has to create a silent and relaxed environment to ensure consistent measurements, 
especially in muscles that are prone to constant movements like the thoracic trapezius muscle or the 
masseter muscle. However, in the present study, it was observed that measurements on the 
extensor carpi radialis muscle were more consistent, since the movement of the limbs was limited 
once the horses were placed in a squared position. 
==PAGE:28==
Page | 19  
 
One of the sub-aims of the current study was to identify the optimal way to perform myotonometry 
in horses. For this reason, the effects of different standing positions of the horses were tested. The 
results of the study concluded that the optimal position for myotonometry was with the horse standing 
in a squared position. When one of the limbs was lifted, the data showed that the related extensor 
carpi radialis muscle was stiffer compared to the muscle on the other side and to the measurements 
obtained with a normally standing position. This might indicate that, when a forelimb is lifted, the 
extensor carpi radialis muscle is compressed and its muscle fibres squeezed and contract. On the 
other hand, the limb that remains on the ground showed an increase in muscle stiffness, but not as 
much as in the lifted limb. This can be explained by the fact that the limb has to contract to hold more 
weight and maintain the horse's balance. Acoustic myography analysis revealed that, in a standing 
position, horses exerted a greater force on both their forelimbs compared to when they lifted one. 
For the four horses subjected to AMG, the left extensor carpi radialis muscle was more active 
compared to the one on the right, which might be a consequence of the balancing movements of the 
horses. Therefore, the optimal way to collect data from the extensor carpi radialis muscle was 
observed to be with the horse standing in a squared position, with limbs aligned and on a level 
ground.  A study conducted on 27 humans revealed how postural position can affect myotonometry 
measurements. Arved Vain et al. (2015) used a Myoton-3, a precursor model of MyotonPro, to apply 
myotonometry on the medial gastrocnemius muscle of men lying down in a supine position, when 
standing on a stable ground, and when standing on an unstable ground. The results showed that 
there was no significant change in decrement, while frequency and muscle stiffness increased 
significantly. Compared to the lying position, the coefficients of variation for frequency were 162% 
(stable ground) and 166% (unstable ground), and for muscle stiffness, 199% (stable ground) and 
203% (unstable ground). The authors concluded that the muscle tone and stiffness of the medial 
gastrocnemius muscle were significantly higher when standing compared to the lying position [50]. 
These findings underline the importance of the muscle being relaxed and at rest when measuring 
muscle stiffness using myotonometry. However, in the present study, it was not possible to do the 
measurement in horses that were lying down. Therefore, the standing position producing the least 
variation was chosen as the optimal position for the measurements. It is important to keep in mind, 
that most measurements that are undertaken on horses by vets or other health care giver will be on 
horses that are standing. Thus, it is an important criteria for a medical device for veterinary athletes 
that it is applicable in horses that are standing, otherwise the use is not compliant for veterinarians. 
To verify the validity and variability of MyotonPro, myotonometry was applied pre and post 
cooling/heating protocol. When the muscle was cooled down (for 25 min), its stiffness and frequency 
increased significantly, while the relaxation time and creep decreased significantly. These results 
indicated that cooling caused the muscle fibres to contract, stiffness, muscle tone and intrinsic 
tension increased, while the gradual elongation over time decreased. After heating up the muscle 
==PAGE:29==
Page | 20  
 
(for 25 min), the results showed that muscle stiffness and frequency decreased significantly, and the 
relaxation time and creep increased significantly. In this case, the muscle fibres were more relaxed 
and looser, taking longer to return to their initial shape after deformation. Muscle decrement resulted 
not significantly affected by cooling or heating, meaning that there was no loss of energy and 
elasticity due to changes of temperatures of the muscle. This physiological behaviour is in line with 
what was expected and indicates valid results. The current findings show that in horses, as in 
humans, cooling down a muscle results in contraction, tension and stiffness. Cooled muscles are 
forced to work harder to complete tasks, causing damage to the muscle tissue and increasing 
soreness [51]. These findings are in agreement with a study conducted by Sirpa Mustalampi et al. 
(2012), who used Myoton-3 to evaluate the mechanical properties of muscles after an ice pack 
session (for 20min). The quadriceps of 39 human participants were tested in a lying position. The 
authors observed a significant increase in muscle stiffness (p = 0.013) and oscillation frequency (p 
= 0.0019) immediately after the cooling phase. In contrast to the present study, humans showed a 
significant increase in the decrement (p<0.001), meaning less muscle elasticity [52]. On the other 
hand, when heat is applied to a muscle, the blood vessels widen and the blood flow in the tissue 
increases leading to more elasticity and lower stiffness of the muscle [51]. In a previous study by our 
laboratory (Ósk Þórðardóttir E. et al. unpublished results) the same pattern using the MyotonPro 
device in humans was observed. The cooling of medial gastrocnemius and vastus lateralis muscles 
caused increased stiffness, while heating of the same muscles caused a decrease of muscle 
stiffness [8]. The only deviation that was observed when comparing the present results with the 
findings from Ósk Þórðardóttir was that, after the heating phase, human muscle stiffness decreased 
below the pre- measurement baseline. This might be attributed to the fact that the humans were 
lying down during the measurements, thus having fully relaxed muscles, while the horses were 
standing for the measurements [8]. Furthermore, the humans were lying down inside in a warm 
laboratory covered with a blanket, while the horses were standing in a cold stable without wearing 
their usual coats. Nevertheless, these findings indicate that myotonometry might be a valid method 
for investigating the mechanical properties of muscles on equine athletes. 
Laser therapy is often used on athletes for muscle fatigue treatments [23]. In order to evaluate the 
clinical relevance of the MyotonPro, the present study investigated whether the MyotonPro device 
was sensitive enough to detect changes after a laser treatment in equine athletes. Decrement, 
relaxation time and creep decreased significantly one hour after the laser therapy. However, muscle 
stiffness and frequency, even if not significant, showed a small increase one hour after the treatment. 
These results might indicate that the muscle had gained energy and increased its elasticity one hour 
after the laser therapy. Therefore, these findings showed that the laser irradiation caused a 
physiological response of the muscle after the treatment. However, the present muscle stiffness 
results to not agree with a study by Takahisa Yonezu et al. (2013), which measured muscle stiffness 
==PAGE:30==
Page | 21  
 
on the gastrocnemius muscle of anesthetized adult rats [53]. Muscle stiffness was analysed with a 
hardness transducer 30 minutes after a low-level laser irradiation, with a wavelength of 808nm. Thirty 
minutes after the laser therapy, muscle stiffness was observed to decreased significantly [53]. One 
possible explanation for these contradictive results might be that the wavelengths used were similar 
despite of the big difference in size of the two species of animals. It is plausible that the treatment 
was more effective on smaller animals, such as rats, due to the bigger size, thicker skin and thicker 
fur which is present in horses. Another reason might be that the horses were not anesthetized and 
therefore they experienced an unknown instrument in contact with their body, which might have 
scared them and caused muscle tensioning. Significant results may not have been obtained due to 
the short time (1h) elapsed between the end of therapy and the last measurement. A measurement 
taken after more than one hour might have led to different results. Another study by Mauricio J. 
Falcai et al. (2015) investigated the mechanical properties and recovery of skeletal muscle subjected 
to a muscle lesion and low-level laser treatment [54]. The authors divided 45 female rats into three 
groups, a control group, a lesion group and a lesion + laser therapy group. No significant difference 
in muscle stiffness was observed between the groups. However, the histology findings indicated 
significant improvement in muscle recovery in the group that received laser treatment, thus the 
authors concluded that laser treatment positively influences the regeneration process of an injured 
muscle [54]. Finally, MyotonPro succeeded in detecting significant changes for most of the 
parameters obtained, meaning it is a fairly sensitive instrument. Nevertheless, further research and 
analysis are needed to monitor changes over longer time (e.g. Post, Post 1h, Post 12h, Post 24h). 
In the present study we used myotonometry measurements pre and post exercise in order to capture 
changes of the mechanical properties of the muscle. All horses that were measured pre and post 
exercise were sub-divided into either a light exercise or a medium to intense exercise group. This 
subdivision was based on the horse owners’ judgement of intensity level.  No significant differences 
in oscillation frequency, muscle stiffness, relaxation time and creep were observed between light 
and medium-intense exercise. Decrement increased significantly only for horses that performed 
medium-intense exercise, this might indicate that the extensor carpi radialis lost more energy and 
elasticity post medium-intense exercise when compared to light exercise. Muscle stiffness slightly 
increased by 4.41% for the horses that performed medium-intense exercise and by 11.6% for the 
other group, however these increases were not significant. The present results show that there was 
no clear distinction between the light exercise group and the medium-intense exercise group. The 
muscle stiffness increased slightly more after light exercise, this might be explained by the level of 
training of the two groups. These results are contradictive to a previous human study from our 
laboratory. In humans it has been shown that light exercise caused a decrease in muscle stiffness, 
while high intensity exercise resulted in significantly increased levels of muscle stiffness. In humans 
these high levels of stiffness in response to intensive exercise reflected muscle damage and muscle 
==PAGE:31==
Page | 22  
 
edema (Ósk Þórðardóttir, results unpublished). Equine measurement might have to be interpretated 
in a slightly different way. When horses exercise, the local vascularization in the muscles increases 
and consequently the stiffness increases. How the mechanical properties of muscle tissue of equine 
athletes change after muscle damage is still unknown. Horses that are used for competitions and 
intense exercise were not particularly affected by the intensity of exercise they conducted for the 
present measurements. Consequently, the medium-intensity level of exercise might not have been 
intense enough for the subjects to create muscle damage. The horses were mostly well trained, 
especially two elite trotter horses, that were included in this study.  When all horses were considered, 
without any distinction in terms of exercise intensity, significant increase in muscle stiffness and 
frequency was observed. Furthermore, relaxation time and creep decreased significantly post 
exercise. These results indicate that physical activity leads to the contraction of muscle fibres and a 
subsequent resistance to elongation. On the other hand, a study by Kisilewicz A. et al. (2020) used 
myotonometry to assess changes in the upper trapezius muscle before and after 24h an eccentric 
exercise program. Fourteen humans were included in the study, and they performed eccentric 
exercises using a dynamic shoulder dynamometer. Five bouts of 10 contractions at 100% level of 
maximum voluntary contraction were completed, separated by a 2-min rest. Myotonometry showed 
a significantly decrease in muscle stiffness 24h after exercise. Shear wave elastography was used 
to check muscle stiffness results. The SWE approach confirmed the significant decrease in muscle 
stiffness [55]. Compared to the present study, the type of exercise is different. Eccentric exercise is 
a contraction while the muscle is lengthened. However, in the present study the horses conducted 
both concentric and eccentric contractions at different levels of intensity.  Furthermore, a study by 
Farias L. F. et al. (2019) observed that high-intensity interval and moderate-intensity continuous 
exercise led to muscle soreness, damage, and inflammation, especially for overweight men [56]. 
When the intensity of the performance is too high, it can lead to muscle fatigue, soreness, exhaustion 
and muscle damage [57]. The following muscle soreness is caused by edema, accumulation of fluid 
in muscle tissue, which triggers an inflammatory response after muscle damage [58]. In a study by 
Howell et al. (1993), muscle soreness was induced in the forearms of 13 humans after an eccentric 
exercise protocol. Three bouts of eccentric exercise were performed by applying a load equal to 90% 
of the maximum isometric force capability of the elbow flexors for each subject, until the subject was 
exhausted. Muscle force was measured through a strain gauge apparatus at different elbow angles 
and converted into torque.  Muscle stiffness was obtained by plotting torque against elbow angle, it 
resulted to double immediately after muscle injury induced by the first eccentric exercise. Muscle 
activation levels remained elevated for approximately four days, which may be due to low-level 
myofibrillar activation induced by muscle stretching [59]. For future studies, the biomechanical 
properties of the muscles from horses should be analysed during the same high-intensity and light-
intensity exercise, in order to obtain a clearer picture of the effects of exercise. Finally, the MyotonPro 
is a promising, valid tool for monitoring muscle stiffness and muscle health in equine athletes during 
==PAGE:32==
Page | 23  
 
a training season or even individual exercise sessions. This device might represent a propitious tool 
for injury prevention and training optimization of equine athletes. 
Musculoskeletal injuries, especially at competitive levels, are commonly associated with equestrian 
sports [60]. Since the MyotonPro is intended as a clinical device, an additional analysis was carried 
out to verify any changes between injured and healthy subjects. The distinction between healthy and 
injured horses was established by only including horses with limb injuries and excluding horses with 
injuries in other parts of the body. All the resting values collected at the beginning of each protocol 
were used for this analysis. The extensor carpi radialis showed a slight increase in muscle stiffness 
and a significant increase in decrement for injured horses. Relaxation time, creep, and frequency 
remained largely unchanged between injured and healthy subjects. Injured muscles become stiffer 
and lose energy and elasticity, leading to a possible decrease in mobility and performance. These 
results, even if not consistent, are in line with existing literature, Pickering-Rodriquez et al. (2017) 
used myotonometry to assess the relationship between muscle stiffness and injuries in netball 
players. The study demonstrated that myotonometry of musculotendinous stiffness may be a 
valuable predictor of injury risk. The authors observed that players with higher stiffness levels in their 
soleus muscles and Achilles tendons were more likely to become injured during the season. 
Furthermore, the subjects were divided into "stiff" and "compliant" groups based on median split, the 
stiff group resulted having a higher incidence of injury than the compliant group [61]. Another study 
by Kawai T. et al. (2021) used MyotonPro device to identify previous hamstring strain injuries in 
soccer players. A group of 16 players was included, half of whom had experienced injuries. It was 
observed that football players with a history of hamstring strain injury exhibited significantly higher 
tissue stiffness [62]. These applications of myotonometry demonstrate its efficacy in preventing and 
detecting athletic injuries, making it an invaluable tool for athletes. This analysis suggests that 
MyotonPro might be used to detect any musculoskeletal abnormalities. It is worth noting that in the 
present study, the horses had lesions on one of the four limbs, not necessarily on the one measured. 
This means that the increased stiffness that is measured here might reflect a secondary response 
to the immobilization of the horse caused by the current injury in one of the limbs. To further validate 
MyotonPro, future investigations should be conducted between a control group and subjects with 
the same lesion on the same muscle, using MyotonPro directly on the injured muscle.  
Another essential factor for the wellbeing of a horse is the ability to move freely. Horse owners often 
have different opinions on the matter, some believe that a horse should be allowed to roam freely in 
a paddock for the majority of the time, while others argue that this could lead to potential injuries 
caused by slipping, uncoordinated movements or kicking and biting from other horses. In the present 
study a comparison was made for horses that daily spend more than 8 hours in the paddock and 
those that spend less than 8 hours, the latter are the once forced to stand in a box for the majority 
of their day. The present results showed no significant changes in any of the measured parameters 
==PAGE:33==
Page | 24  
 
(decrement, relaxation time, creep, and frequency) when the two groups were compared. Muscle 
stiffness presented a decrease of 1.61% for horses used to stand outside in the paddock, however 
this result was not significant. It is important to note that horses of all ages and with different lifestyles 
were taken into consideration for this analysis and that the included horses were measured at 
different times during the day. Some were measured early in the morning and others in the evening. 
Furthermore, the temperature during the measurements was not controlled. In a recent study 
conducted by Abigail Reilly et al. (2021), the correlation between the amount of time spent in the 
paddock and the prevalence of soft tissue injuries in non-elite performance horses was examined. A 
retrospective study was conducted on 146 riding horses. Soft tissue injuries were diagnosed by 
veterinarian, using ultrasounds machine and MRI. The horses were divided into two groups: those 
that spent more than 12 hours in the paddock and those that spent less than 12 hours. The incidence 
of soft-tissue injuries resulted 24.6% for horses that turned out for 12 or more hours per day, and 
50.6% for those horses that spent less than 12 hours outdoors per day. This suggests that horses 
that are given more time to roam and graze in the paddock are less likely to suffer from soft-tissue 
injuries. These results revealed a significant relationship between the two variables, suggesting that 
extended periods of time spent in the paddock may decrease the risk of soft tissue injuries in horses. 
This finding has important implications for the management of equine, as it suggests that extending 
the amount of time spent in the paddock may help to reduce the risk of soft tissue injuries [63]. 
Therefore, further studies, where the circumstances are controlled, are needed to gain a more 
accurate understanding of the effects of standing in a box. 
Limitations 
The present study had several limitations. The MyotonPro was tested as a non-invasive device, thus 
it was not possible to make measurements on sedated and immobilized equines. Therefore, 
measurements were made on standing horses, where the extensor carpi radialis muscle was not 
completely relaxed. It was also observed that the animals' movements can significantly affect the 
variation of the results. To address this issue, one possible solution is to train horses to be 
comfortable with the device and impulses given by it. Additionally, the surrounding environment is 
particularly important. In the present study, measurements during feeding time were difficult to 
perform, since all the horses in the stable were excited and nervous. This made it impossible to 
proceed with the measurements. Thus, a horse needs to be in a relaxed and serene state in order 
to be measured with the MyotonPro device. A challenging issue to address is the selection of 
muscles. Muscles, such as the trapezius thoracic muscle, have been proven to be susceptible to any 
type of movement of the horse, which led to a change of muscle selection for this study. 
Nevertheless, for clinical and monitoring purposes, if the coefficient of variation is too high due to the 
animal's movements, the measurement has to be repeated to avoid variation.  
==PAGE:34==
Page | 25  
 
Each protocol of the present study had specific limitations. For the cooling and heating protocol, ice 
packs or cherry bags might not have changed the temperature of the area of interest sufficiently due 
to the thickness of the skin and the presence of the fur. In addition, the temperature gun, used to 
record the temperature indicated the temperature of the surface, not of the temperature of the actual 
muscle underneath the skin. The measurements that were made pre, post and post_1h from the 
laser treatment only showed a snapshot of the effect of the treatment one hour post. It would have 
been more optimal to monitor the muscle not only one hour after the therapy, but also 12 and 24 
hours later. Furthermore, in order to investigate the effects of laser treatment, it would be beneficial 
to subject the horses to a series of three consecutive laser treatments rather than just a single 
treatment, in order to observe tangible changes. For the exercise protocol, the most significant 
limitation was that the horses conducted different types of exercise and were trained for different 
riding disciplines. The horse owners were responsible for determination of the intensity level of the 
exercise that was performed, which caused a very subjective classification. To ensure more accurate 
results in future studies, it would be beneficial to have all animals performing the same type of 
exercise with the same level of intensity. The analysis conducted on healthy and injured horses 
entailed a huge variation between subjects due to the great variation in the type and site of injuries. 
The horses considered injured had different injuries including tendon injuries, muscle injuries or 
abscesses in the hoof and the injuries were located on different limbs. This disparity in the injuries 
might explain the non-significant results. To ensure more accurate measurements, it would be 
beneficial to consider a group of equine athletes with the same type of injury on the same limb. The 
final analysis conducted was based on the duration of time spent in the paddock by horses. The 
most significant limitation that impacted this analysis was the time of the day at which the 
measurements were taken. Some data was collected in the morning, prior to the animals being 
released into the paddock, while others were collected in the afternoon after the horses had spent 
the day roaming freely in the paddock. This inconsistency in the data collection might explain the 
inconclusive results. 
Conclusion 
In conclusion, this study demonstrated that the MyotonPro is a valid and reliable non-invasive device 
that can be used to assess the mechanical properties of muscle tissue in equine athletes The present 
results show that the MyotonPro device is sensitive enough to pick up local changes after the 
exposure to cooling and heating of the muscle, laser therapy, injury and exercise. When 
myotonometry is measured on horses, the optimal way to perform measurements is for the animal 
to stand in a squared position on flat, hard surface. This is the position in which the muscle is the 
most relaxed despite of the standing position of the horse. We suggest that the MyotonPro device 
==PAGE:35==
Page | 26  
 
can be used in the veterinary field, and the results are reliable when conducted correctly, in a calm 
environment and with care.  
Perspectives 
The present study demonstrates the possibility of using MyotonPro on animals such as equines. It 
might be introduced into injury diagnosis as a clinical tool to replace manual and therefore subjective 
palpation. Furthermore, MyotonPro could be commonly used by horse owners in stables to monitor 
the state of the muscle after intense exercise   or during recovery from injuries, to better understand 
when the horse is ready for further physical exercise or to restart activity. Equine athletes, who are 
transported for competitions, often experience severe stress that can lead to higher sympathetic 
activity and increased muscle stiffness, which can affect the performance of the animal. The 
MyotonPro would be the ideal tool to easily examine the muscle tension after transportation of the 
animal. 
Further studies are needed to ascertain the reliability of MyotonPro. Previous studies have already 
exposed interesting improvements on the footprint of this study. It would be interesting to verify any 
linear dependency between the temperature change of the muscle and its stiffness. To further 
evaluate the standing condition of the horse and how much this influences the state of the muscle, 
it would be useful to compare measurements made on the same horses in an awake state and a 
sedated state and analyse how that influences the resting state of the muscle. This could provide 
valuable insight into the efficacy of MyotonPro as a reliable and accurate tool for assessing muscle 
health in equines.
