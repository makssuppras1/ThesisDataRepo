Abstract
Renewable energy represents a valid alternative to fossil fuel, being clean and charac-
terized by recent declining cost trends. Hybrid Power Plants combining renewable assets
attenuate the unpredictable nature of renewable sources, therefore enhancing their ben-
eﬁts. A Hybrid Power Plant combining wind and solar generators with battery energy
storage system is analyzed in this thesis. The objective is to determine the rated capaci-
ties of the technologies which yield the maximum revenue stream while ensuring a feasible
investment. Therefore, a mathematical model to maximize the Net Present Value (NPV)
is implemented. Electricity price proﬁles, power time series and costs are the parameters
aﬀecting the energy arbitrage of the battery, the anti-correlation between wind and so-
lar resources and the installation ratio between the capacities. The sizing optimization is
applied to an indian case study, considering speciﬁc wind and solar resources and Power
Purchase Agreement conditions.
Technical and economical parameters are analyzed to
assess the synergies of the technologies involved. Cost decrease, reduction of renewable
sources intermittency, capacity factor and revenue increase are the main beneﬁts observed.
Diﬀerent scenarios are considered in order to compare these aspects. Finally, a sensitivity
analysis is carried out to estimate the uncertainties involved in the model.

Introduction
1.1
Context
The global path towards a sustainable energy system entails a shift from conventional fossil
fuel plants to renewable energy sources (RES) deployment. The transition is favoured by
renewable energy cost decline, environmental policies and market reforms [3]. Furthermore,
research and development of Hybrid Power Plants is advancing, in order to enhance the
ability of renewable plants to operate as a conventional unit, with the additional beneﬁts
of avoiding CO2 emissions and fuel costs. Hybrid Power Plants are on grid utility scale
plants, combining multiple technologies connected to the grid through a common point of
connection and operated as a single asset [4]. Speciﬁcally, wind, solar and battery coupling
is examined in this study.
The complementary nature of wind and solar resources and the controllable operation
of the battery potentially lead to reduction of Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) and to
increase of proﬁtability. Indeed, power curtailment is reduced, exploitation of electrical in-
frastructures is more eﬃcient, energy dispatch is more schedulable and power ﬂuctuations
are smoothed ensuring greater grid stability [5]. The presence of a single point of common
coupling implies the installation of only one substation to connect to the grid, resulting in
cost reduction. The possibility to allow over-planting of wind and solar assets, meaning
that the installed rated capacity is greater than the power that can be evacuated to the
grid, leads to a more constant use of the electrical infrastructure compared to single renew-
able plants. The potential level of eﬃciency and reliability of HPPs is comparable to that
of a conventional plant, hence more suitable to the current market structures and grid code
requirements, which were developed for a fossil fuel dominant power sector [6]. However,
speciﬁc policy schemes and revision of market structures are required to incentivize HPP
integration [4][5]. The desirable outcome is the future growing participation of HPP to
capacity markets, energy markets and ancillary services markets. Currently, energy mar-
kets are the most relevant source of revenue, based on feed-in tariﬀs incentives or Power
Purchase Agreements (PPA) [5]. Diﬀerent sizing and operation optimization approaches
for wind-solar-battery plants [7] [8], [9], [10], [11], only solar-battery or wind-battery [12],
[13] [14], [15], [16], [17] have been implemented. Each proposed design focuses on diﬀerent
objectives, such as cost minimization in case of a more detailed physical design or revenue
maximization when the main concern is market operation. A more detailed description
of advantages and disadvantages of HPP, applications and sizing methods is given in the
literature review in Chapter 2. It can be noted that the sizing and operational limits of
wind-solar-battery HPPs have not been widely considered, especially their simultaneous
assessment included in a unique optimization problem. Furthermore, at this early stage
of HPP development, a major focus on the business model might be required. Hence,
the technologies cost, sizes, power availability, type of market structure addressed and
potential revenues, are primary factors to evaluate the viability of these type of plants.
India is pursuing the target of installing 175 GW of RES capacity by 2022 and ensuring
eﬃcient transmission grid utilisation [18]. A National Wind-Solar Hybrid Policy was es-
tablished to contribute to this goals and HPP auctions are being set up [4]. For instance,
the Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI), issued a tender for setting up 1.2 GW of
HPP projects connected with the interstate transmission system (ISTS). For each project

a PPA is stipulated, based on a peak/oﬀ-peak tariﬀsystem. A case study on one of this
projects, deﬁned by 300 MW of contracted capacity, is analyzed in this thesis [19].
1.2
Problem deﬁnition, Limitations and Assumptions
The design of a Hybrid Power Plant consists of sizing and operational optimization of the
technologies composing the plant. The two processes can reach high degrees of complexity
depending on the factors considered. The factors are based on the speciﬁc objective of
the optimization. The detailed physical characteristics of the single technologies and their
interaction are typically considered for cost minimization. In case of revenue maximiza-
tion, technical operation limits, market structure and prices are considered. In case of
proﬁt maximization (i.e. NPV), both technical operation limits and sizing constraints are
considered.
This report focuses on the optimal sizing of a solar-wind-battery HPP. The objective of the
optimization is the maximization of the NPV while determining the solar and wind rated
capacities, battery energy capacity and power rating. A mathematical optimization model
is built considering battery operation constraints, power curtailment and grid connection
capacity limit. The purpose is to develop a simple sizing optimization tool to determine
the conditions that ensure a viable project, to evaluate the trade oﬀs between the rated
capacities costs and the additional market value obtained by their combination. Succes-
sively, the outputs of the optimization model are used to calculate the most important
economic and technical performance metrics, in order to analyze the HPP beneﬁts and
to compare it to other possible scenarios, i.e.diﬀerent electricity price conditions, or plant
conﬁgurations, i.e. single wind or solar plant.
The optimization model is applied to a case study that involves the development of a HPP
located in India. Favourable policies for the investment on HPP are being developed in the
country and PPA are used as the main form of investment security. The case study is based
on a real tender where PPA peak and oﬀ-peak tariﬀs are assigned. Particularly, this thesis
aims at answering the following questions: How to optimally size a solar, wind and battery
HPP in order to yield the highest revenue while determining a feasible investment? How
are the indian solar and wind resources and PPA conditions inﬂuencing the sizing of the
HPP? How is the revenue enhanced by the combination of the technologies, particularly
by battery energy arbitrage?
Which are the technical operation limits of the battery?
Which are the most inﬂuential parameters to the optimal sizing? Which are the beneﬁts
of hybridisation?
The optimization model is based on deterministic information. One year time series with
hourly values for prices, wind and solar power are used and then extrapolated for the
entire lifetime of the project. This limits the realistic aspect of the model, considering that
resources and prices, along with costs and discount rate, are characterized by variations
and uncertainties. Therefore, a sensitivity analysis is carried out to assess the robustness
of the model. The HPP developer is assumed to be a price taker, as it can be deﬁned
as a small generator within a large power system, therefore its inﬂuence on electricity
price and load is insigniﬁcant. The battery is only charged by the HPP generators and its
degradation is disregarded, in order to ﬁrstly validate the model in its simple version.
The wind and solar capacity installed is allowed to be higher than the contracted capacity

Literature review
2.1
Background
Deﬁnition and objectives
Hybrid Power Plants are on grid utility scale plants, with an
order of magnitude of hundreds of Megawatts. These plants are formed by the combination
of multiple electricity power generating assets, which can include both conventional and non
conventional generators, with or without storage. The distinctive characteristic of these
plants is the connection to the grid through a single common point for all the technologies
[4]. The HPP serves the needs of the bulk power system and its potential in diﬀerent
market context is being assessed. Particularly, this analysis focuses on plants composed by
wind, solar assets and energy storage. Two main objectives can be pursued from the plant
owner point of view: develop economies of scope, namely reduce LCOE by a more eﬃcient
exploitation of resources and infrastructures, and increase proﬁtability [5]. The former is
based on the anti-correlation character of wind and solar sources and sizing optimization
of the plant, the latter is highly enhanced by the controllable storage operation. In the
majority of the locations, solar and wind result being two complementary resources. Solar
and wind proﬁles can be inverse on a daily and seasonal base [5]. It was demonstrated by
means of diﬀerent statistical methods, that in several regions of the world, the combination
of solar and wind mitigates their intermittency, generating a more predictable power output
[20]. Currently, poor information is available regarding the impact of climate change on
the complementarity between the two sources [20]. It is interesting to point out that there
are some examples of areas where the inverse-correlation does not subsist [5]. Nonetheless,
the coupling of solar and wind technologies is still potentially convenient in terms of power
delivery strategy. For instance, with high wind penetration in the market, prices peaks
occurrence can be inverse to high wind production occurrence, but revenues can still be
obtained through the sell of solar production [4].
Market participation
The revenue stream opportunities of these type of plants depend
on the market structure: capacity market, energy market and ancillary services. Although
all the mentioned markets are potentially suitable for HPP, they require great reliabil-
ity, while the energy markets, both day-ahead and real time, are currently the dominant
source of revenue. Revenues are either based on price ﬂuctuations over time, feed-in tariﬀs
incentives or power purchase agreement [5], as in the case study analysed in this work.
Challenges
Despite the facilitation of having to arrange only a single grid connection,
there are a number of challenges related to the development of this type of plants. All
strongly related to the lack of a clear policy line and well deﬁned standards, the coor-
dination in the permitting process for each technology, which have diﬀerent lengths and
requirements and the need of various expertise [4].
Existing projects
In [4] examples of existing and under construction hybrid power
plants are indicated.
The wind-solar ratio is fairly variable, it depends mainly on the
location resources.
At present, the minority of HPP presents a storage facility, but it
is included in several under licensing HPP projects. One existing large scale solar-wind-
storage HPP project is located in the Netherlands (60 MW with 12 MWh of storage with
5
==PAGE:15==
main function of enhancing and ﬂattering output and participate to balancing market),
another one is the Kennedy energy park in Australia (60 MW, 4 MWh of storage with main
function of enhancing and ﬂattering power output, ease fulﬁllment of grid requirements) [4].
Some other examples are located in the United States, such as Hardin Wind Energy Center
(620 MW power capacity, 60 MW battery capacity), Skeleton Creek Energy Center (500
MW capacity, 200 MW battery power capacity and 800 MWh battery energy capacity) and
Wheatridge Wind Energy Facility (600 MW total capacity, 30 MW battery capacity)[21].
There are also several HPP commissions in the micro-grids [4].
2.2
Advantages
HPPs present a number of beneﬁts which deﬁne them as more convenient than the single
technology use. Additionally, their operation is more similar to that of conventional plants.
The complementary nature of wind and solar, increases the daily and seasonal power
availability. It reduces the variability of the joint wind and solar power output, making
it more predictable. The storage properties allow to avoid curtailment, consequently, the
capacity factor is higher. The presence of the battery reduces the RES ﬂuctuations, the
power output is smoothed, ensuring fulﬁlment of grid stability requirements. Moreover,
the power dispatch schedule is more controllable through battery charge/discharge, which
eases the energy arbitrage.
Another striking beneﬁt is the reduction in cost, both CAPEX and OPEX. One reason for
cost reduction is due to the possibility of sharing Balance of system costs (BOS) such as
hardware, interconnection, permitting, engineering, customer acquisition, labor, and land
costs [22] [23]. Hardware costs sharing, such as transmission lines, controllers and power
electronics cost, highly depends on the conﬁguration of the system. The grid connection
cost is reduced by the presence of a single point of connection.
Moreover, the greater
capacity factor, allows a more eﬃcient exploitation of the electrical infrastructure compared
to independent solar or wind plant. Furthermore, a higher capacity factor compared to
the single asset renewable plant, determines a higher business case certainty, which leads
to lower ﬁnancing cost. Further support in the investment is given by granted subsidies.
For instance, in the United States, policy incentives are given, such as the investment tax
credit [23]. Moreover, maintenance often requires shut down of plant elements for a certain
period, during which it is still possible to deliver the necessary power with BESS optimized
schedule [24].
The enhanced predictability lowers the risks of bidding in the day ahead market and
eventual forecast errors can be compensated in the balancing market by discharging the
battery. The latter, in addition, increases the possibility to provide ancillary services [4].
It is worth mentioning very valuable beneﬁts for society: more eﬃcient land use, as the
installed capacity and energy output per square meter of used land increases; the greater
installed capacity does not aﬀect the grid connection capacity but enhances grid utiliza-
tion since more power is transmitted. Considering that the current status of the grid is
negatively aﬀected by penetration of intermittent sources, this solution defers further in-
vestments on the renovation of the grid. In some cases, this often translates in reduced
tariﬀs for customer [4].
Cost reduction and increase in market value are the main beneﬁts to eventually lead to a
6
==PAGE:16==
more attractive investment than separate technologies projects [21]. A number of applica-
tions based on the synergy between battery and generators are identiﬁed:
• Energy arbitrage
It consists of storing energy during low price times and discharge it during high price
periods with the purpose of increasing the revenue. It is particularly advantageous
when there is a substantial spread between high and low electricity prices which
guarantees return on initial investment.
• Frequency regulation
Frequency variations are caused by imbalances between load and generation. An
automatic response in power regulation is required to restore the frequency. Batteries
are characterized by a fast response in power supply which is ideal for this service.
• Ramp control
Storage contributes to power output control which allows to attenuate fast power
ﬂuctuations of renewable generators which aﬀect power quality and grid stability.
For this reason, grid operators often impose ramp rate limits for wind or solar plants,
which limits the volume of electricity sold aﬀecting the proﬁtability of these plants.
• Capacity
Since storage contributes to enhance the capacity factor of the plant, it makes HPP
more suitable for earning revenues from capacity markets. The battery can ﬁrm total
output while the generator increases power availability.
• Resilience and reliability
Resilience is provided when power is guaranteed during severe but rare transmission
system disruptions such as natural disasters and reliability during more frequent but
shorter power outages. Hybrid systems could potentially substitute Diesel generators
in oﬀering these services.
• Flexibility
High RES penetration requires a higher integration of ﬂexible components which
can compensate for their intermittent nature. Moreover, the value generated during
peak hours, may decrease for PV or wind plants working individually, since their
higher generation is often displaced in respect to peak demand. Their value is thus
enhanced by the presence of the battery. HPP combining batteries and renewable
plants fulﬁll the requirements for participation to diﬀerent market structures and
ensure grid stability.
[4],[23],[25]

Disadvantages
The drawbacks of this type of plants are related to the uncertainties of eﬀectively enhanc-
ing their value in the market. Especially considering that market rules are continuously
evolving and subsidies are often needed. Market participation models are not maturely

==PAGE:17==
developed, speciﬁc features of the battery and constraints, solely related to the coupling of
multiple technologies, inﬂuence the dispatching strategies, which are not easily formulated
as conventional dispatching models [21]. For instance, the physical conﬁguration and limits
can aﬀect the operation strategy, particularly for what concerns the battery. Inﬂuencing
factors are the coupling of battery with generator, whether it is AC or DC or the capacity
of the inter-connectors [21]. Independent storage might be located closer to the load site

2.4
Storage
The value of storage for renewable energy systems has been assessed both in terms of its
technical characteristics and cost trends. In [26] an overview of the utility-scale storage
development trends is presented, from the share of each technology installed globally to cost
and services addressed. Both [26] and [27] indicate Lithium-ion batteries as the dominant
storage technology installed, only preceded by Pumped Hydro storage.
In [27] typical
values of the main technical characteristics of diﬀerent types of storage can be found,
pointing out how their duration, namely discharging time supported, is a crucial factor
in deciding which type of storage is suitable for each service.
For instance, Lithium-
ion battery is an electro-chemical type of storage with a duration time that ranges from
minutes to several hours.
In [2] the main characteristics of Lithium-Ion batteries are
thoroughly illustrated, along with their integration in the power grids, both independently
and coupled with variable generators. It describes the electro-chemical principle of the
battery, the BESS structure and control strategies, its degradation over the lifetime, sizing
and dispatch strategies. Furthermore, optimal sizing and operation of storage coupled with
wind or solar is carried out in [7], with a detailed analysis on its added value to renewable
energy revenues. The main ﬁndings on the storage value are related to its cost, provided
subsidies and type of application. According to [25], storage independently exploited for
energy arbitrage is not suﬃciently proﬁtable, while it is considerably more convenient when
coupled with renewable generators for the same application. Moreover, value stacking,
namely the combination of more value streams can further increase proﬁtability. However,
the renewable generation cost drop needs to be followed by a decrease in storage cost,
otherwise the installation of the single renewable technologies would still be preferred.
In the analysis carried out in [7], turns out that with the current cost in the United
States, subsidies are required for the case studied in order to earn suﬃcient proﬁt after
storage integration. For the same reason, the EU is trying to eliminate market speciﬁc
obstacle related to storage technologies, in order to promote the investment on it [27].
Overall, the most inﬂuencing factor remains the storage cost, although it has signiﬁcantly
declined in the latest years. For instance, Lithium-ion battery costs dropped by more than
65% between 2010 and 2015 [25]. Cost projections also foresee a continuous decrease of
battery storage cost up to year 2030. The main reasons behind this trend are the global
demand increase, soft costs fall as manufacturers gained more expertise and streamlined
their processes. Power electronics are also slightly cheaper. As previously stated, despite
the cost fall, investment on storage could still be massive and government subsidies or low
cost ﬁnancing is needed. Additionally, the storage cost varies over time due to degradation

==PAGE:18==
which depends on the type of utilization [27]. Particularly, in [2], the most important cost
factors and the storage components contributing to them are described for Lithium-Ion
batteries. For some applications the power capacity needs to be higher than the energy
capacity or viceversa. Each performance characteristic determines a diﬀerent battery cost.
These two cost can be distinct because they are generally modular: BOS cost usually
scales with maximum power output (kw) while battery pack cost scales with power output
duration, thus energy (kwh). Both soft costs and hardware costs which compose the total
BOS, could decline with Hybridization. Hardware cost savings depend on the conﬁguration
while soft costs such as permitting, decrease when all the technologies are built at the same

2.5
Sizing
The main objectives and approaches adopted for sizing of HPP plants are presented.
2.5.1
Objectives
The sizing optimization considers the various factors characterizing each single technology
and additionally the interaction between them. As observed in the literature, the main
objective is generally the minimization of the annualized capita the minimization of the
LCOE (levelized cost of electricity) or maximization of NPV [28].
2.5.2
Approaches
The sizing methodologies usually applied present diﬀerent levels of complexity, according
to which they are listed as follows: probabilistic, graphic, analytical, iterative, artiﬁcial
intelligence and hybrid [28].
Very few examples of sizing and operation of utility-scale Wind-Solar-Battery HPP have
been found in the literature.
One is described in [8], where an Iterative and genetic
algorithm is used to ﬁnd optimal combination of wind solar and battery at large scale.
The ratio among technologies capacity is determined, but the total HPP rated capacity
is previously set. Battery power rating is determined but battery rated energy is ﬁxed.
Another example of sizing scheme of a solar-wind-battery plant can be found in [9]: after
determining a certain wind and PV capacity, battery capacity is obtained considering
load and expected wind and solar production in the minimization cost problem. The cost
function includes system CAPEX, OPEX and battery replacement cost. It is an iterative
scheme, the sizes of the three technologies are not simultaneously determined and revenue
streams are not considered. In [10] a wind-solar-battery stand-alone system is considered.
The capacity of all the technologies in terms of number of components is simultaneously
determined with objective of minimizing the total annualized cost. In [11], a linear program
is solved for wind-solar-lead-acid battery HPP. The proﬁt is maximized while determining
BESS power and energy capacities, the other rated capacities are deﬁned in advance.
Instead of meteorological data, their corresponding solar and wind power output forecast
is given as input.
The majority of the hybrid plant analyzed regards the coupling of wind and solar, wind
and battery or PV and battery, especially for residential application. It is striking to note
9
==PAGE:19==
that in [12], one of the few studies taking into account both sizing and optimal dispatch
is presented. A small scale grid connected PV- battery system and rule-based operation
strategies are considered. The NPV is maximized while determining lithium-ion battery
rated capacity and operation variables. However, the PV size is not a decision variable,
but diﬀerent values of its capacity are given as input in the optimization problem. In [13],
a wind-battery plant is analyzed, battery capacity in terms of both power and energy is
obtained but it is not simultaneously deﬁned with wind rated capacity. Firstly, a power
dispatch strategy based on the maximum or minimum value of the wind output is deﬁned.
Secondly, battery capacity is adjusted according to the power dispatch and the capacity
level which ensures the minimum life cycle cost is chosen as optimal.
Other examples
battery sizing for small scale wind-battery or solar-battery systems are found in [14], [15],
[29].
The operational optimization of hybrid plant has also been analyzed in order to
compare the battery model and operational constraints needed.
Examples on how to
deﬁne the battery SOC variation and the battery power and energy bounds are shown in
[16], [30], [17]. In [16] a daily optimized dispatch schedule is obtained in order to maximize
the proﬁt. It demonstrates how the battery operation adds value to the revenue streams.
The system is composed only by a PV and battery, thus the eﬀect of the complementary
nature of wind and solar cannot be analyzed.
In [17] a robust based optimization of

in each cycles is typically assessed according to the power dispatch schedule or load proﬁle
that has to be guaranteed. Most of the cases cited are constrained by the satisfaction of
load requirements[12], [13], [14], [29]. Furthermore, battery degradation is often integrated
in the optimization models and its inﬂuence in cost and performance is highlighted [12],

Physical design
The following methods are diﬀused alternatives applied when the main purpose is to op-
timize speciﬁc physical characteristics of the technologies such as type of PV and WT,
position, spacing between technologies, possible interactions such as shadowing of WT
over the PV, wake eﬀect and power losses due to layout characteristics: short distances or
land size limitations. More speciﬁc features are PV panel inclination angle, PV panel area,
wind turbines sweep area and wind turbine installation height [31]. Artiﬁcial intelligence
methods are widely applied lately. These algorithms can be applied to non linear and
complex problems [31] [32]. The hybrid methodology is claimed to be the most accurate
because it involves more than one method [28]. This methodology has been applied to
single technology plants, revealing a higher level of complexity of the issue whenever the
level of accuracy is improved. For instance, a multi- ﬁdelity optimization of wind farms
was developed in [33].

Topology
Frequently, prior to the optimization problem, is the choice of the plant topology based on
the electrical infrastructure. Generally, all the HPP components share the same substation

Methodology
3.1
Research design
This section describes the main steps and approaches adopted to answer the research
questions.
• Identify a suitable metric to address the main objective
The objective is to determine the wind and solar rated capacities, battery energy
capacity and battery power rating which lead to the maximum revenue stream while
ensuring a feasible investment. These two aspects are both included in the maxi-
mization of the Net Present Value.
• Problem formulation
The optimization tasks are identiﬁed and the linear mathematical problem, with its
objective function and constraints, is formulated. The NPV is the objective function
and the wind and solar rated capacities, battery energy capacity and battery power
rating, power curtailment, battery SOC limit and battery power charge/discharge
are decision variables used to deﬁne battery and HPP total power output operational
limits.
• Identify data needed as input
Electricity prices, Power time series, Technologies parameters, Capital and Operation
and Maintenance cost are all inﬂuential parameters in the sizing optimization process.
• Data analysis and assumptions
Costs, power time series, price proﬁle building, Power Purchase Agreement prices
and Market prices are speciﬁcally analyzed for the indian case study.
• Calculate economic and performance metrics
Net Present Value, Levelized cost of energy, Internal rate of return, Capacity factor,
Full load hours, Capacity factor during peak hours, Energy to power ratio and total
curtailment are deﬁned and calculated after the solving of the linear optimization.
The outputs of the linear optimization are the inputs for these calculations.
3.2
Data collection
This section outlines the input data general characteristics and the reasons for introducing
them in the model. The use is clearer in the mathematical model formulation. The analysis
and assumptions of the input data for the indian case study are carried out in Chapter 4.
3.2.1
Electricity price
The HPP developer is assumed to be a price-taker. A price-taker is unable to aﬀect the
market price with its own production volume, it has to accept the prevailing prices in the
electricity market or deﬁned by long term contracts. The sizing model is valid both for a
price system based on peak/oﬀ-peak tariﬀs or a ﬂuctuating price proﬁle over each day. The
sizing process is generally based on deﬁning the rated capacity that ensures load demand
requirements satisfaction. In this case, the sizing process is based on deﬁning the rated
12
==PAGE:22==
capacity that ensures maximum production for revenue increase. The production volume is
driven by the price trend and does not inﬂuence load and market prices. Electricity prices
usually reﬂect the electricity demand trend, cost of power plant, fuels and transmission
and distribution systems and regulations of the country. Hence, peak prices are expected
when load demand is high. Consequently, the production volume during peak price hours
is required to increase, maximizing the revenue without having to meet a speciﬁc load
demand.
Power Purchase Agreement
It is a long-term contract stipulated between two parties. Price and amount of energy
to be sold are settled prior to the renewable plant construction and operation, in order
to guarantee remuneration for the plant owner over a long period. Although there are
diﬀerent types of PPA in terms of conditions and parties involved, it is usually stipulated
between a state-owned company and an independent power plant developer. It is a mean to
provide ﬁnancial incentives in renewable energy project investment. These were originally
provided by government subsidy schemes.
The PPA is an alternative form of security
for future revenue streams, which makes the investment in the project more attractive.
Indeed, lenders assess project future cashﬂows in order to decide weather those are eligible
for ﬁnancing. They conclude that the sell of electricity at ﬁxed price as per PPA, lowers
the risk of investment. While, market price volatility determines greater uncertainty on
the future cashﬂows prediction [34]. The price proﬁle considered in the case study is based
on a PPA tariﬀsystem and its impact on the sizing optimization of the HPP is assessed.
Market prices
The market price proﬁle is analyzed and used as input parameter in one of the case study
scenarios as opposed to PPA to analyse the value of PPA. The zonal Day-ahead market is
considered, which consists in short term auctions that take place in the electricity pool. The
latter is an electronic platform that allows producers to bid a speciﬁc quantity of production
at the price they are willing to sell it and at the same time consumers express the price they
are willing to pay to obtain the amount of electricity needed. In turn, the market operator,
through a market-clearing tool sets scheduled production and consumption levels and the
market clearing price.
The market clearing price sets the balance between supply and
demand for each time frame considered. The analysis of historical market price ﬂuctuations
over one year gives information on the peak price periods occurrence. Moreover, the input
of market price proﬁle in the optimization model, gives an example on how the decision
making process on the type of technology and the related size is inﬂuenced by the signiﬁcant
variation in price. It could possibly highlight the advantages of stipulating a power purchase
agreement to create a beneﬁcial business case on hybrid power plants.

3.2.3
Technologies characteristics
Lifetime
The technical lifetime is the number of years of operation during which a tech-
nology can maintain high performance standards, assuming maintenance provision. The
number of hours of usage, the operation strategy and the reinvestments made over the years
inﬂuence the lifetime duration. When the plant reaches a poor performance level, it under-
goes decommissioning or lifetime extension. The research and development improvements
in Wind and Photovoltaic systems manufacturing led to an increase in performance level
and lifetime of this technologies. Wind turbines maximum lifetime is generally assumed
to be 25-27 years for recently constructed large wind turbines on land and it is expected
to reach 30 years around 2030 [37] [38]. Large scale PV utility systems have a technical
lifetime that ranges between 30-35 years and it expected to reach 40 years around 2030.
It is relevant to compare the technical lifetime to the lifetime of the project, in order to
assess whether the technology could potentially withstand operation for the entire period
required.
Overplanting
Over-planting is the over dimension of the HPP plant, which means that
its installed capacity is higher than the grid connection capacity with the aim of exploiting
the wind and solar resources more eﬃciently. Indeed, since the global average CF are
30-40% for wind and 20% for solar, a higher number of assets and a wider space is needed
to reduce the intermittency of these sources and take advantage of their anti correlation
[39]. Indeed, in the indian case study, clear distinction between the two terms is stated:
the contracted capacity corresponds to the maximum AC power that can be delivered to
the grid through the connection point. Hence, it corresponds to the maximum capacity
that can be scheduled, but not necessarily to the actual installed capacity.
Battery storage
The storage technology chosen is the Lithium-ion battery. Besides the downward trend of
Lithium-ion batteries cost, they cover a wide range of both power and energy capacities
that can be traded oﬀ. Their duration, indeed, ranges from seconds to up to 8 hours [27],
which ﬁts the need of daily energy shifting. Indeed, battery cannot be considered as a power
generator but it is a mean for energy shifting and other applications described in Chapter
2. The main characteristics of batteries were considered in the problem formulation phase
15
==PAGE:25==
in order to understand which parameters need to be included in the equations. The Depth
of discharge indicates the maximum percentage of discharge allowed from the battery. It is
the ratio of discharged energy to usable battery energy capacity. For Lithium-ion batteries
usually ranges between 80-100% [40]. It is used to assess the minimum State of charge of
the battery.
Charging and discharging eﬃciencies are introduced in order to take into account the
amount of energy that is lost to heat every time charging or discharging occurs.
For Lithium-ion batteries the same eﬃciency for charging and discharging is used and
it is equal to 99%. These parameters also aﬀect the state of charge of the battery and
the maximum instantaneous power ﬂow, but in case the same eﬃciency is assumed for
both charging and discharging, they can be disregarded.
The life cycle is the number
of equivalent full cycles that can be delivered by electricity storage before it becomes
inoperable. An equivalent full cycle is the ratio of overall energy throughput to the usable
capacity. There are diﬀerent ranges of equivalent full cycles depending on the chemistry
of the lithium ion battery. For instance, the lithium iron phosphate battery can bear up
to 10000 cycles. In general, the number of cycles during the lifetime ranges between 500
and 20000 cycles [40].
The Calendar lifetime is the shelf life of a battery system under given conditions. It reﬂects
the chemical deterioration of the battery. It ranges from 5 to 20 years [40] and it is expected
to reach 25 years by 2030 [38]. In this work, the battery degradation due to the number of
cycles is disregarded, hence only its Calendar lifetime is compared to the assumed lifetime
of the project.

3.3
Mathematical model
The aim is to establish the rated power capacity of wind power and solar power to be
installed and the relative adequate battery rated energy capacity and power rating in or-
der to obtain the maximum Net Present Value. Revenue and cost depend on the size of
technologies. In the NPV, cashﬂow is considered, which is the diﬀerence between revenue
and yearly operational costs, as described in Section 3.4.1. The optimal sizes are the ones
determining a sum of the discounted cashﬂows greater than the total investment cost. Pre-
cisely, revenue depends on the total power output of the HPP, adjusted according to the
grid connection capacity limit and the price value in each hour of the time frame. The
adjustment occurs through battery charging or discharging of the power generated by the
solar and wind system or power curtailment when necessary. Battery charging/discharg-
ing is based on energy arbitrage strategy and curtailment avoidance and depends on its
technical operation characteristics. Energy arbitrage consists of shifting power from hours
of lowest prices to hours of highest prices. Part of available solar and wind generation is
charged into the battery during low price hours and discharged during high price hours.
19
==PAGE:29==
The battery is able to charge/discharge instantaneously only a limited amount of power
according to its power rating. Simultaneously, the power ﬂowing over a certain time in-
terval, namely the energy amount, has to be within the maximum and minimum energy
level allowed in the battery. The role of the optimization is to ﬁnd the trade-oﬀbetween
wind and solar rated capacities required to exploit resources, the battery power and energy
rating needed to enhance their generation value and all the associated costs. The rated
capacities are all interdependent in the energy arbitrage process: the possibility to enhance
power output during periods of high price is limited by the availability of suﬃcient wind or
solar generation to charge the storage system in advance. However, the availability might
be suﬃcient but the battery size is limited by its cost, leading to the shifting of only a
fraction of the available power from solar and wind.

The levelised cost of electricity is the average cost of generating one unit of energy over the
lifetime of a power plant. It is the ratio of the total life cycle cost of the plant to the total
lifetime energy production [EUR/kWh]. In renewable energy systems analysis, it takes
into account capital expenditures and operation and maintenance cost, discounted over the
lifetime by the interest rate of the investment. Therefore, it considers the time value of
money indicating which would be the long-run marginal cost of the electricity generated.
Furthermore, it depends on the site conditions since the energy production variates with
wind and solar irradiance of the location. It can be considered as the minimum selling
price of the energy output in order to make the project attractive. Hence, it can serve
as support in decision-making on the development of the project but it is not a suﬃcient
measure for assessing its economic viability. Moreover, it is a signiﬁcant cost metric for a
quick comparison between technologies and alternatives (diﬀerent costs, lifetime, interest

capacity and energy output per square meter of occupied land could potentially increase.

grid limit, instead of curtailing it. The high capacity factor enhances the reliability of the
base case HPP. For instance, in the indian context, characterized by a large developing grid

operating conditions. The battery guarantees ﬁrm capacity while the generators available

Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis is carried out in order to assess the robustness of the results. Indeed,
several risks are involved in this project. Technical risks such as the reliability of generation
due to dependence on weather, eﬃciency and maturity of the technologies, unforeseen costs
of operation, due to battery degradation for instance. Economic risks such as obtaining
access to ﬁnancing sources as equity and debt and reasonable interest rates, market risks
due to uncertain and partially controllable volume production and market prices volatility
in case of direct participation to spot or balancing market. The sensitivity analysis is based
on the variation of one parameter while the others are kept as in the base case, in order
to assess the impact that it has on the output value that is being analyzed. The most
uncertain input parameters are chosen, since they would probably be the most inﬂuential.
Subsequently, a range of variation is determined for each parameter by identifying a high
and low scenario. These are based on information retrieved from the literature.
6.1
Base case
The tornado diagram in Figure 6.1 proves how the NPV is particularly responsive to a
change in the discount rate. The lower bound of the range deviate by 29%, corresponding
to a discount rate of 5%. Since renewable energy projects are typically characterized by a
considerably elevated risk of investment, it would be quite optimistic to reckon a further
lower value. The chosen upper deviation corresponds to a discount rate of 8%, since a
higher value would be equal to the IRR. Its impact is quite signiﬁcant, but still allowing a
total NPV of 44 mEUR. Since the discount rate really depends on the type of investors and
its trust in the reliability and feasibility of the project, it is reasonable to believe that there
could be an improvement of 134.66 million euros in the NPV, leading to a total estimate
of the NPV approximately equal to 217 mEUR. Hence, from an economic point of view,
this single parameter decrease would probably lead to one of the best project models. As
mentioned in Section 4.1.3, all the costs except the battery maintenance cost are assumed
equal to the value on the lower end of the possible ranges found in [44]. Therefore, only
a 10-20 % decrease is taken into account. While for the upper value of battery energy
cost and battery power cost a threshold analysis was ﬁrstly calculated: the maximum
possible deviation that would still ensure a positive NPV including battery installation.
For the battery energy cost, it corresponds to an increase up to 40%, beyond it, the

the diﬀerence between the value of the energy capacity installed and the battery power

adequate battery energy capacity, is critical in supporting energy shifting. Regarding the

6.2
Global sensitivity analysis
The sensitivity analysis carried out above is based on changing one input parameter at
a time and assessing the impact on the output without considering the interaction with
the other parameters. In the global sensitivity analysis, the eﬀect of variations in all input
44
==PAGE:54==
parameters simultaneously and over the entire range of values of interest, can be examined.
As a result, it indicates which parameter is more responsible for changes in the output and
which ones have an insigniﬁcant impact, therefore it would be reasonable to consider them
as ﬁxed values. Two methods that are widely used for this type of analysis are polyno-
mial chaos expansions (PCE) and Monte Carlo simulations. The former is implemented
in Python using the package Chaospy. The mathematical derivation of the model and the
Chaospy description can be found in [51]. PCE is a probabilistic method consisting of a
polynomial approximation of a function. The function considered is the one computing
the output on which the uncertainty needs to be quantiﬁed. A surrogate model simulating
the response of the output function in chosen points is obtained. The main steps for the
construction of the model in Python are:
• Calculation of a random joint probability distribution of the input parameters.
• Calculation of nodes and weights: a Gaussian quadrature rule is used to identify
the points of the probability distribution space where the output function will be
evaluated.
• Calculation of samples, namely the function outputs calculated on the nodes.
• Determination of orthogonal polynomials used to build the approximation model.
• The surrogate model is obtained from the use of a mathematical method called spec-
tral projection. This method is based on polynomials, nodes, weights and samples
to calculate the approximation model over the distribution space.
• The Sobol indices are deduced from the surrogate model and used to quantify the
uncertainty on the output.
The Sobol indices are fractions of the output variance related to each input in order to
quantify their impact. There are three types of Sobol indices: ﬁrst order, second order and
total order.
First order index measures the main impact of each parameter. It is the contribution of
one parameter to the output variance.
Second order index indicates the fractional impact to the output variance of each pa-
rameter due to interaction of each pair of parameters .
Total order index indicates the relative importance of one input parameter with respect
to all the inputs.
Figure 6.3 shows the ﬁrst and total order Sobol indices for four uncertain parameters of
this model and their impact on NPV: WT cost, PV cost, Battery energy capacity cost and
Discount rate. The Discount rate Sobol ﬁrst order index, conﬁrms the observation related
to the tornado diagram. The great eﬀect of the Discount rate was already noticeable, but it
is dependent on the deviation ranges chosen. The Sobol total index, instead, conﬁrms that
the discount rate is the most impacting parameter in absolute terms and it quantiﬁes its
fractional contribution to the NPV variation (0.844). As stated in Section 6.1, this result
highlights the importance of improving HPP plants performances that lower the risks on the
45
==PAGE:55==
investment. The global sensitivity analysis supports the decision making process on which
approach to adopt on setting the value of the most aﬀecting parameters, especially when a
linear program with several uncertain inputs is implemented. For instance, a conservative
approach could be adopted in this case, by adopting a higher discount rate in order to
base the analysis on the worst case scenario. The most insigniﬁcant parameters, instead,
could be considered ﬁxed. successively, the complexity and uncertainty of the model can
be increased by introducing more realistic inputs. For instance, random schedule variations
of peak/oﬀ-peak prices occurrence could be included, which in this thesis are assumed to
be constant every day. Moreover, when the number of uncertain parameters is signiﬁcant
(more than 20 inputs) [51], the global sensitivity analysis could be performed with Monte
Carlo simulations as alternative to Polynomial Chaos Expansion.

Conclusion
7.1
Discussion
Chapter 5 and 6.1 provide a detailed description, interpretation and comparison of the
results. In this section the key ﬁndings are reiterated.
A simple sizing optimization taking into account the basic operation of the battery, allows
to assess the key economic and technical performance metrics related to the project. The
amount of power dispatch mainly depends on the size of the technologies, battery energy
arbitrage and grid constraint, but it is not required to meet the load demand.
The solar capacity installed is greater than the wind capacity in most of the cases analyzed,
reﬂecting the low cost of the technology and the elevated solar resources in the Indian
region considered. Indeed, the downward cost trends and upward capacity factor trends
observed in the literature, supported the considerably low investment costs assumed for all
the technologies. For the speciﬁc conditions of the Indian case study, based on the PPA,
hybridization proves to be a favourable solution both in case of a wind-solar plant and even
more in the base case with wind-solar-battery capacity. The common grid connection point
for all the HPP assets, results in cost reduction, higher capacity factor and therefore more
eﬃcient exploitation of the electrical infrastructure. In the base case scenario, the battery
reduces power curtailment and increases the revenue streams through energy arbitrage.
The capacity factor and the NPV are higher than the ones obtained from the optimization
of a solar-wind plant.
The inﬂuence of the input price proﬁle is particularly noticeable in the comparison between
the base case and the market price scenario. The market price scenario is the only case
in which a hybrid conﬁguration of the plant is not feasible.
Nonetheless, the highest
NPV is obtained in this case. However, the base case represents the type of plant with
the most positive balance between economic parameters and technical performance. The
comparison between real market prices and PPA tariﬀs for the analyzed Indian case, allow
to reasonably assess the main obstacles to a HPP participation in the Day-ahead market,
which are mainly related to the cost of the technologies and the uncertainty on revenue
streams based on price ﬂuctuation. Considering battery value stacking obtained from HPP
participation in diﬀerent markets could be a useful investigation. It could enhance the
possibility to create an attractive business case of the HPP for direct market participation.
The comparison between base case and evening peaks scenario demonstrates the results
dependence on the schedule of the peak hours, not only on elevated peak price values.
However, in this work, the peak/oﬀ-peak schedule is not varied through the days. Moreover,
one year of data for prices and power and solar time series is adopted and then extrapolated
for the 25 years of lifetime of the project. These aspects should be further taken into
account for a more accurate analysis. The potential hardware cost reductions based on the
conﬁguration of the HPP and the sharing of soft costs due to simultaneous and co-located
installation of the assets, are not evaluated in depth. There are great uncertainties on
the impact of costs and discount rate given as input to the sizing model. The discount
rate appears to be one of the most aﬀecting parameters on NPV and battery energy
capacity installed. However, the analysis of the technical performance metrics, such as the
increase in capacity factor, suggests the opportunity of enhancing the HPP revenue streams
from diﬀerent application. This potentially lowers the risk perceived on the investment,
48
==PAGE:58==
hopefully leading the investors to apply modest discount rate on the investment. Battery
energy cost and battery capacity maintenance cost have a relevant impact on the battery
energy capacity installed. The estimations of the ﬁrst one vary within a wide range of
values, but only an increase of its cost up to 40% leads to an optimal solution including its
installation. The second one is related to the amortized cost of battery replacement, since
the lifetime of the battery is presumably shorter of the lifetime of the project. In order to
assess its impact with more accuracy, it would be relevant to include battery degradation
in the sizing model.
7.2
Conclusion
The investment on a HPP combining wind, solar and battery technologies turns out to be
proﬁtable for the case study considered. Particularly, the PPA conditions are currently
required to guarantee the viability of the project, considering that hybridization is not an
optimal solution in the market price scenario. In the base case, the three assets synergies
enhance reliability and eﬃciency of the plant.
The anti-correlation of wind and solar,
the curtailment avoidance and energy arbitrage of the battery are crucial factors in the
maximization of the Net Present Value. Overplanting of wind and solar capacities is the
optimal outcome to leverage the complementary nature of the two sources. A common grid
connection point and the recent lower costs of renewable generators and battery, guarantee
a feasible initial investment.
The PPA peak/oﬀ-peak conditions induce the battery to
operate energy arbitrage, yielding higher revenues than the single technology plants or
the sole combination of wind and solar.
The increase in capacity factor enhances the
reliability of the plant, lowering the uncertainty and risk on the business model. It is an
important improved aspect, considering that the discount factor results being the most
inﬂuential parameter given in input and strongly depends on the investment risks. The
diﬀerent scenarios of the case study highlight the technical and economic performance
improvements obtained with hybridization, with or without battery integration. However,
the base case with battery integration under PPA conditions, is the one presenting the
greatest beneﬁts.
In general, the developed sizing optimization model serves for an initial screening of the
characteristics of the HPP that determine a viable project. It has been demonstrated that
indian conditions are favourable for the realization of the HPP development. Moreover,
the sizing optimization program can be applied to other study cases involving diﬀerent
input parameters.
The problem formulation is based on general constraints and HPP
characteristics that allow to simultaneously deﬁne the size of all the assets involved and
their operational limit. Constraints related to a particular case requirements or speciﬁc
types of technologies are not speciﬁed. The case study conditions are set by the input
parameters.
7.3
Future work
The sizing optimization model has been tested and several economic and technical perfor-
mance metrics have been evaluated. An aspect which has great impact on the results is the
uncertainty on the input parameters, both the ones analyzed in the sensitivity analysis and
49
==PAGE:59==
price and power time series. The next step to be taken should be considering the forecast
of solar and power time series for the entire lifetime of the project. The price proﬁle daily
variations based on diﬀerent peak/oﬀ-peak combinations could be estimated.
Implementing a stochastic optimization could be more appropriate for the level of com-
plexity increased by the above mentioned inputs.
It would be very relevant to include a cost function in the objective function of the model,
in order to take into account the battery degradation over the lifetime. A high number
of cycles accelerates its degradation causing higher costs. The optimal battery size should
guarantee that the additional value determined by its operation is greater than the cost of
its lifetime reduction.
It would be interesting to consider diﬀerent HPP topologies with the aim of assessing their
impact on BOS costs and land area occupied. For instance, the sharing of inverters between
technologies would reduce the hardware costs. Moreover, it could require a smaller land
area for the HPP installation.
As it can be seen in the Appendix, the area calculation for a certain HPP conﬁguration and
the physical parameters required are presented. A more detailed study should be carried
out to introduce the land area constraint in the model, in order to consider its cost and
assess its impact on the sizing optimization.
It would be extremely interesting to perform a complete global sensitivity analysis and
evaluate the consequent implications for the model.

A.4
Threshold analysis
A threshold analysis consists of ﬁnding the maximum possible deviation of the parameter
that would still ensure an optimal solution. Regarding battery related cost, the maximum
value that would still allow a positive NPV including its installation were found. The
same rationale was applied to evaluate wind and PV maximum costs. The purpose of

scenario characterized by the most extreme assumptions in terms of costs. In this case
the outcome is positive for all the costs except the battery energy capacity costs. Indeed,
their maximum deviation is well beyond the upper end of the cost ranges found in the
literature. However, battery most elevate energy cost is assumed equal to approximately
450 Eur/MWh, but the values in the range between 255 and 450 would not lead to a
feasible solution in this case. The maximum deviation for PV cost and BoS cost is not
indicated because even an extreme increase up to double or triple the base case value would
