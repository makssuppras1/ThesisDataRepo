Abstract
The Radio Frequency Identification(RFID) technology has been applied into various fields,
especially in retail sector. Nowadays, a lot of companies started implementing this tech­
nology to track their products. Due to the limitations of RFID, the creation of ecosystems
by numerous companies introduces significant challenges. In various ecosystems, it will
produce electronic waste(e­waste) when the ecosystem’s life­cycle is expired. Based on
the research on the solutions to this problem, the existing solutions will cause huge re­
sources cost and it can not improve the adaptability of various ecosystems.
To address this problem, this thesis focuses on the blockchain technology to solve it.
Based on the design of front­end and back­end, integrating with the blockchain estab­
lished on FISCO BCOS platform, the whole system presents an comprehensive and user­
friendly interface for administrators to manage the RFID and the data of RFID is stored in
blocks which is aimed to avoid the e­waste. The entire system is designed in distributed
ways and convenient to be add/remove new functional modules.
Because the limitations of lack of the RFID prototype, the entire system has been not
applied into a real RFID product but integrating with simulated RFID object. In the future,
this thesis can focus on doing further research on RFID technology and implementing a
real­type RFID tag applying into the existing system.

Introduction
In the evolving landscape of location technology, Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)
tags have become a focal point due to their broad application across diverse industries. A
example of this innovation is Apple’s AirTag system, demonstrating the widespread inter­
est in employing RFID technology for precise location tracking. As the utilization of RFID
expands, the creation of proprietary ecosystems by numerous enterprises introduces sig­
nificant challenges. Users face issues like market exclusivity and interoperability conflicts
between different RFID systems, which hinders universal application.
Moreover, the rapid growth of these RFID ecosystems raises substantial environmental
concerns, particularly in terms of electronic waste (e­waste). With the expiration of an
ecosystem’s lifecycle, all associated tags turn obsolete, exacerbating e­waste accumula­
tion and environmental degradation. At the heart of these tracking systems is the deploy­
ment of unique identifiers within the RFID tags, enabling devices to communicate location
data to a unified database. Access to this database allows users to monitor the where­
abouts of their assets. However, the dependence on proprietary databases complicates
continuity once an ecosystem ceases operation.
Blockchain technology, renowned for its decentralization and transparency, presents a
viable solution to these problems. It offers the potential to forge a sustainable and inter­
operable framework for managing RFID­tagged assets, minimizing reliance on centralized
systems and mitigating e­waste. This thesis explores the integration of blockchain with
RFID­based tracking systems, assessing both the benefits and the challenges. The goal
is to develop more sustainable, efficient solutions for tracking technologies, fostering bet­
ter compatibility and environmental stewardship.

Theoretical Background
This section presents the fundamental concepts and technical theory. A deep dive into
both blockchain technology and Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) is essential to un­
derstand their individual characteristics and the potential of their integration.
2.1
Blockchain Technology
Blockchain is a distributed ledger technology (DLT) that maintains a growing list of records,
called blocks, securely linked together using cryptography. Each block contains a crypto­
graphic hash of the previous block, a timestamp, and transaction data, thereby creating
a chronological and unalterable chain. Originally devised for the digital currency Bitcoin,
blockchain has evolved into a robust platform for decentralized consensus in various ap­
plications.
2.1.1
How Does Blockchain Technology Work?
Blockchain[1] represents a sophisticated amalgamation of pivotal technologies that un­
derpin its revolutionary impact across numerous sectors. At its core, the architecture of
blockchain is founded on the harmonious integration of cryptography keys, a peer­to­peer
(P2P) network with a shared ledger, and computational algorithms that sustain the net­
work’s transactions and records.
• Cryptographic Keys and Digital Identity:At the forefront of blockchain’s security
measures are cryptographic keys, comprising two distinct parts: a private key and
a public key. These keys form the cornerstone of a user’s digital identity, crucial for
conducting secure transactions. The private key is kept confidential and is used to
sign transactions, thereby asserting ownership and consent. Conversely, the public
key is shared with others; it can be seen as an address to which others can send
information or assets securely. Together, these keys generate a digital signature,
assuring that transactions are both authenticated and unforgeable. In the domain of
cryptocurrencies, for example, this digital signature is indispensable for controlling
and verifying transactions, serving as a robust digital counterpart to a handwritten
signature.
• Peer­to­Peer Network and Shared Ledger:The peer­to­peer network of blockchain
is an expansive tapestry of nodes, each participant acting as an individual authority.
This network leverages consensus algorithms to validate transactions and records,
eschewing the need for a central authority. The shared ledger is a synchronized
and distributed database that transparently records all transactions across the net­
work. Each validated transaction, secured by the digital signature and verified by
the network, becomes a part of the immutable ledger. The ledger not only provides
a historical record of all transactions but also ensures their visibility and permanence
• Consensus Mechanisms and Transaction Validation: Blockchain’s innovation
lies in how transactions are ratified. A consensus mechanism, driven by the col­
lective agreement of network participants, is employed to confirm each transaction.
This process involves sophisticated mathematical computations and algorithms that
furnish proof of the transaction’s validity. Once a transaction is agreed upon and
cryptographically signed, it is irreversibly inscribed onto the blockchain. This de­
centralized verification process is fundamental to the network’s trustworthiness and
is a testament to the robustness of blockchain as a secure transaction platform.

Characteristics of Blockchain:
• Decentralization: Unlike traditional centralized systems, blockchains are main­
tained by a network of nodes and that make sure no single entity has control over
the entire chain.
• Transparency: All transactions on a blockchain are visible to participants, fostering
an environment of trust and accountability.
• Immutability: Once data is entered into the blockchain, it is exceedingly difficult to
alter, making it an ideal platform for recording transactions and asset tracking.
• Security: The use of cryptographic hashing and consensus algorithms makes blockchains
secure against fraudulent activities and hacking.
2.1.2
Bitcoin
Bitcoin[2], designed by an unknown individual or group under the pseudonym Satoshi
Nakamoto, emerged in 2008 as the first decentralized cryptocurrency. It operates on a
peer­to­peer network that is powered by blockchain technology, embodying the principles
of maximum transparency, decentralization, and security. As a digital currency, Bitcoin
allows for direct transactions without the inter­mediation of traditional financial institutions.
Key Features of Bitcoin:
• Decentralized Nature: Bitcoin’s network is not controlled by any single entity or
government, distributing power across its users.
• Supply Limitation: There is a capped limit of 21 million bitcoins, which combats
inflation and simulates scarcity akin to precious metals like gold.
• Mining: Bitcoins are created through a process known as ’mining,’ where powerful
computers solve complex mathematical problems to secure the network and vali­
date transactions, in turn, generating new bitcoins.
• Blockchain­Driven: The underpinning ledger of Bitcoin is a blockchain that records
every transaction in a series of blocks. This ensures that each bitcoin is unique and
cannot be replicated or spent twice.

2.1.3
Ethereum
Ethereum[3] is an open­source, blockchain­based platform that enables developers to
build and deploy decentralized applications (dApps). Often referred to as a ”world com­
puter,” Ethereum extends the blockchain use case from simple financial transactions, like
those seen with Bitcoin, to complex applications involving programmable transactions
known as smart contracts.
How it works?
Ethereum operates on a blockchain, similar to Bitcoin, but with several key differences,
primarily its support for smart contracts. The Ethereum blockchain records all transactions
that occur on it, which are validated and secured by a consensus mechanism. Originally,
Ethereum used proof­of­work (PoW) similar to Bitcoin, but it has plans to transition to
proof­of­stake (PoS) to improve scalability and energy efficiency.
Smart Contracts
Smart contracts are self­executing contracts where the terms of the agreement between
buyer and seller are directly written into lines of code. These contracts are stored on the
blockchain and automatically execute when predetermined conditions are met, without
the need for intermediaries.
• Functionality: Smart contracts can be programmed to perform a wide range of
functions without human intervention. This can include automatically transferring
funds based on certain conditions, issuing tickets or tokens after receiving payment,
or even more complex scenarios involving multiple parties and conditions.
• Deployment and Operation: To deploy a smart contract on Ethereum, developers
write the contracts in a programming language called Solidity, and then deploy them
to the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM). The EVM executes the contract’s code
securely and effectively, ensuring that the contract operates as programmed.
• Benefits and Use Cases: There are some advantages of smart contracts, including
transparency (the code is visible and verifiable by all parties), security (transactions
are secure and immutable), and efficiency (reducing transaction times and eliminat­

various industries such as finance, real estate, healthcare, and logistics.
• Gas and Transaction Fees: Transactions on Ethereum, including interactions with

for the computational energy required to process and validate transactions on the
decentralized network.
How it smart contract works?[4]
• Identify Agreement: Multiple parties agree upon a cooperative opportunity and the

• Set Conditions: The terms of the smart contract are clearly defined. These are the

• Code Business Logic: A computer program is written based on the agreed condi­

what dictates the contract’s behavior on the blockchain.
• Encryption and Blockchain Technology: The smart contract takes advantage of

nication. Encryption ensures that messages between parties are secure and that
the contract’s integrity is maintained.
• Execution and Processing: Once the conditions are met, the smart contract is
automatically executed without the need for an intermediary. The smart contract’s
code runs on the blockchain, and the results are processed in real­time.
• Network Updates: After the contract is executed, the transaction is verified by the
network, and all nodes on the blockchain update their ledger to reflect the change.
This update is distributed across all copies of the ledger, ensuring transparency and
immutability.

2.2
RFID Technology
Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)[5] is a form of automatic identification technology
that uses electromagnetic fields to automatically identify and track tags attached to ob­
jects. An RFID system consists of a tiny radio transponder, a radio receiver, and transmit­
ter. When triggered by an electromagnetic interrogation pulse from a nearby RFID reader
device, the tag transmits digital data, such as an identifying inventory number, back to the
reader. Types of RFID Tags:
• Active RFID tags: Equipped with a battery, these tags broadcast their signal to be
read by an RFID reader.
• Passive RFID tags: These tags are powered by the electromagnetic energy trans­
mitted from an RFID reader.
• Semi­passive RFID tags: These tags use a battery to run the tag’s circuitry but
communicate by drawing power from the RFID reader.
RFID technology has numerous applications, including supply chain management, inven­
tory control, theft prevention, and much more, providing a contactless method to effec­
tively track and manage assets.
2.2.1
How RFID works
RFID technology utilizes radio waves to identify tagged items. A typical RFID system
is composed of three fundamental elements: an antenna, a transceiver (often combined
with the antenna into what is called an RFID reader), and a transponder, commonly known
as the RFID tag.
• Antenna: Facilitates the emission and reception of radio waves.
• Transceiver: Processes incoming signals to interpret the tag’s information.
• Transponder (Tag): Carries the tag’s ID and data, responding to the reader’s query.
At the heart of the RFID tag lies the RFID inlay, which is the critical layer containing
integrated circuits where data is encoded and stored. When the tag comes within proximity
of the RFID reader, the antenna receives electromagnetic waves, activating the tag. The
transponder then transmits the data back to the reader, where it is converted into a digital
format for various applications.
In the retail sector, RFID systems can be integrated with inventory management solutions
to vastly improve the accuracy and efficiency of stock tracking. This capability is essential
for meeting the demands of shopping experiences, where consumers expect consistent
and real­time inventory information across all purchasing platforms.
RFID’s tracking ability enhances inventory visibility, significantly reducing discrepancies
between physical stock and inventory records. This system ensures that retailers can
maintain optimum stock levels, minimizing overstock and out­of­stocks, and ultimately
leads to more efficient operations and an enhanced consumer experience.

2.2.2
Integration of Blockchain and RFID
The convergence of blockchain and RFID technologies has the potential to revolutionize
asset tracking and supply chain management. By integrating RFID’s efficient tracking
capabilities with blockchain’s secure and decentralized ledger, it is possible to create a
transparent of product and avoid producing e­waste in different ecosystems.
Advantages of Integration:
• Enhanced Security and Privacy: The integration ensures that sensitive data recorded
by RFID tags is securely stored and managed on a blockchain and it possibale to
reduce the e­waste.
• Increased Transparency and Traceability: Blockchain provides a verifiable and im­
mutable history of RFID­tagged asset movements, enabling stakeholders to track
the provenance and authenticity of products.
• Improved Efficiency: This integrated system can automate various supply chain
processes and it can reduce the need for manual checks and reducing the likelihood
of errors.
While the integration has several advantages, challenges such as scalability, energy con­
sumption of RFID systems, and the complexity of implementing smart contracts in diverse
scenarios must be addressed to realize the full potential of this convergence.
2.2.3

implementation of decentralized networks that can support large­scale RFID deploy­
ments.
• Sustainability in RFID­Blockchain Ecosystems: Investigates the environmental
impact of these technologies and explores sustainable practices in their deployment
and operation.

3.1.3
Comparative Conclusion of the Two Papers
Both papers explore the integration of cutting­edge technologies—blockchain with RFID
in the first paper and blockchain with IoT in the second—to address operational and en­
vironmental challenges in distinct contexts. The first paper focuses on improving cargo
transportation efficiency using RFID and blockchain and this new system increased se­
curity and interoperability across systems. The second paper discusses an IoT­enabled
system for e­waste management in smart cities, emphasizing enhanced tracking, secu­
rity, and responsible recycling practices.
The first paper utilizes blockchain technology primarily for improving logistics through bet­
ter tracking and reduced compatibility issues among different RFID systems. The second
paper extends blockchain’s application to the realm of smart cities, using IoT RFID Tag to
manage e­waste effectively. Both systems use smart contracts to automate operations,
ensuring transparency and reducing manual errors, but they apply these technologies to
different parts of the supply chain.
A notable focus of both studies is environmental sustainability. The first paper addresses
the reduction of e­waste through better lifecycle management of RFID tags, whereas the
second paper deals with the comprehensive tracking and safe disposal of e­waste, lever­
aging IoT devices to monitor and manage waste in urban settings.
Both papers focus on security and privacy, particularly through the immutable and trans­
parent nature of blockchain. However, the second paper places a heavier emphasis on
data security in relation to e­waste, ensuring that sensitive data on electronic devices is
securely destroyed.
3.1.4
Implications for Thesis Design
The integration of blockchain with RFID and IoT, as explored in these papers, provides
a robust framework for enhancing the tracking and security of systems. This approach
can be directly applied to developing a sustainable and efficient solution for RFID­based
systems in various industries, including retail and logistics.
Both papers highlight the importance of managing the environmental impact of technolog­
ical systems. This perspective will inform the design of a blockchain­based RFID system
that not only improves operational efficiencies but also incorporates mechanisms for re­
ducing e­waste and promoting recycling.
The challenges of interoperability presented in the first paper and the solutions offered
through IoT in the second can guide the development of a system that supports various
RFID tags and devices across different platforms. Also in both two papers, the use of
smart contracts for automating processes and ensuring compliance with regulatory stan­
dards is crucial.
The thesis can explore the development of smart contracts that facilitate the management
of RFID data and operations, enhancing efficiency and reliability.
This thesis will also consider extending the blockchain­based RFID system to broader
applications, such as RFID tracking system to avoid e­waste, drawing from the second
paper’s focus on smart waste management systems.
By synthesizing the insights from these papers, the thesis will aim to design a system that
not only addresses the technical and operational needs of RFID tracking systems but also
considers the broader societal and environmental impacts, such as saving device/energy
resources.

Because of data isolation between groups, each group runs its own consensus algorithm
independently, and different groups can use different consensus algorithms. Each ledger
module mainly includes three layers from bottom to top: core layer, interface layer and
scheduling layer. These three layers cooperate with each other and implements its spe­
cific tasks. FISCO BCOS can ensure that each single group operates independently and
robustly.
The core layer is responsible for recording the group’s block data, block information, sys­
tem tables, and block execution results into the underlying database.Storage is divided
into two parts: world state (State) and distributed storage (AMDB). State consists of two
types of storage states ­ MPTState and StorageState. The MPTState is designed to store
the status information of transaction executions, providing a reliable and secure means
to track changes across transactions. In contrast, StorageState offers enhanced per­
formance by focusing on current state information without maintaining a history of block
transactions, thus optimizing for quicker access and processing speeds. AMDB enhances
the system’s flexibility and efficiency with its simple yet powerful database interaction ca­
pabilities, which include basic operations like query (select), commit (submit), and update.
AMDB is particularly adept at handling operations across contract tables, system tables,
and user tables.
The interface layer is structured into three critical modules: Transaction pool (TxPool),
Blockchain and Block Executor (BlockVerifier). Transaction Pool (TxPool) interacts with
the network layer and scheduling layer, and is responsible for caching transactions broad­
cast by clients or other nodes. The scheduling layer (mainly synchronization and con­
sensus modules) takes out transactions from the transaction pool for broadcast or block
RFID Management
27
==PAGE:34==
packaging. Blockchain interacts with the core layer and the scheduling layer. It serves as
the only gateway for the scheduling layer to access the underlying storage. The schedul­
ing layer (synchronization and consensus module) can query the block height, obtain the
designated block, and submit the area through the blockchain interface. Block Execu­
tor (BlockVerifier) interacts with the scheduling layer and is responsible for executing the
blocks passed in from the scheduling layer and returning the block execution results to
the scheduling layer.
The scheduling layer is designed to manage and optimize transaction processing and
block creation. It consists of two main components: the consensus module and the syn­
chronization module. Consensus Module includes two threads, Sealer Thread and Engine
Thread. Sealer Thread is responsible for collecting transactions from the transaction pool
(TxPool) and packaging them into new blocks. It acts as the initial step in the block creation
process, ensuring that transactions are ready for validation and eventual addition to the
blockchain. The engine thread takes over to execute the consensus process on the new
blocks. This process involves validating the transactions with the blocks and achieving
agreement among all participating nodes on the state of the blockchain. Once consensus
is achieved, the block with its execution results will be committed to the blockchain. Syn­
chronization Module manages two key processes, Transaction Broadcasting and Block
Synchronization. Transaction Broadcasting module do the broadcasting when new trans­
actions are created and ensures all nodes stay updated. This is crucial during the con­
sensus process because it requires a comprehensive dissemination of transaction data
to prevent any node from lagging. Block Synchronization considers the differences in
block heights between all nodes. Synchronization module proactively sends the latest
block information to other nodes. When nodes detect they are behind, they request and
download the latest blocks to align with the network to avoid.

In this section, the transaction flow will be illustrated. The general solution is that the

After the block is generated, the consensus engine performs verification and consensus.
After verifying that the block is correct and reaching a consensus among the nodes, the

The first step is that users initiate transactions to connected nodes by operating the SDK
or directly writing curl commands. After the node receives the transaction, if the current
transaction pool is not full, it will append the transaction to the TxPool and broadcast the
transaction to the nodes it is connected to; otherwise, the transaction will be discarded
and an alarm will be output. Meanwhile, Sealer will continuously take out transactions
from the transaction pool, and immediately package the collected transactions into blocks
and send them to the consensus engine. Thes engine calls BlockVerifier to verify the
block and perform consensus in the network. BlockVerifier calls Executor to execute each
transaction in the block. When the block is verified correctly and the nodes in the network
reach consensus, the consensus engine sends the block to Blockchain. When Blockchain
receives the block and checks its information (such as block height, etc.). Finally, it will
write the block data and table data to the underlying storage, and completes the block
on­chain.

Conclusion
This purpose of project is to develop of a blockchain­based RFID Tag management sys­

standing of both blockchain and RFID technologies. On the basis of extensive research
