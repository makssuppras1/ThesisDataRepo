Summary 
 
According to statistics, approximately 9% of the global fish production is used for fish 
meal and oil production. Fish meal is an extremely valuable feed ingredient which is mainly 
used in feed for fish, poultry, pig and cattle. It is produced by a standardized wet pressing 
method by a big variety of fish species as a mixture of whole fish and fish by-products.  
This master thesis was conducted in cooperation with Vedde AS, which is a fish meal 
and fish oil producing company, located in Langevåg outside Ålesund, Norway. The aim of the 
thesis was to study how the season, the species entering into fish meal composition and the 
effect of temperature, as processing parameter, influence its quality.  
 For that purpose, eight fish meal samples with variations in composition and three 
samples from the production line (before and after dryers) were analyzed. In addition, four 
samples of frozen raw material were used only for determination of their general characteristics. 
These samples were divided into three main categories of raw material; fish meal, material from 
the company’s production line and frozen raw material. The studied raw material is mainly used 
in production of Norse-LT and Norse Nat-LT fish meal products. These samples were analyzed 
with respect to their nutritional composition (lipids, dry matter, ash content and protein) and 
quality. Concerning the lipid analysis of the samples, lipid extraction, peroxide value (PV), 
thiobarbituric acid reactive substances (TBARS) were implemented. The protein analysis 
differentiated among the three sample categories. The applied methods for the fish meal 
samples were; crude protein content (CN-analysis), determination of protein solubility (Lowry), 
size distribution of peptides (FPLC), total amino acid composition (hydrolysis) and protein 
content (ELISA). Regarding the other two categories of samples (material from the production 
line and frozen raw material), were conducted Lowry, hydrolysis and ELISA. Finally, all the 
given samples were tested for their dry matter and ash content.  
Statistical analysis such as standard deviation, principal component analysis (PCA) and 
simple and multiple linear regression analysis were carried out in order to evaluate significant 
differences among specific results and identify correlations between them. Microsoft Excel, 
Unscrambler X and SigmaPlot 14 were the main used statistical programs. The results showed 
that the different species which are used in the fish meal production, and especially their by-
products, may have an effect in the oxidative stability of fish meal and therefore in the overall 
quality of the product. Seasonality revealed some difference regarding the protein content of 
fish meal. In addition, the processing parameters were studied in relation to temperature but the 
result did not give any conclusion due to obstacles during the analysis.

1 Introduction 
Fish meal is a brown dry material in the form of powder which is characterised 
by high nutritional value and used as a protein supplement in animal feed 
manufacturing. It is utilized mainly in diets for aquaculture species but it can also be 
added in feed aimed to livestock, such as poultry, pigs, cattle, etc. (FAO, 1986; Kim et 
al., 2016) 
Through the years, the utilization of fish meal in animal diets has been 
differentiated, as demonstrated in Figure 1-1. In 1960, the fish meal production was 
equally distributed among poultry and pigs. Over the years, the increasing development 
of aquaculture resulted in the use of fish meal in farmed fish diets in the expense of 
pigs. Nowadays, the largest part of fish meal production is used in aquaculture (Miles 
& Chapman, 2006).  
 
Figure 1-1. Overview of the changes in fish meal utilization from different animal sectors 
[source: (IFFO, 2018; Shepherd & Jackson, 2013) 
The sources for fish meal production are either pelagic or demersal fish, with 
little or no demand for human consumption, called industrial fish, and fish by-products. 
Pelagic fish are small, bony, oily fish which inhabit the surface of coastal and oceanic 
waters, such as anchovies, sardines, herring and mackerel. They can be found in schools 
(groups) or in solitary (Lal & Fortune, 2000). On the other hand, demersal fish are white 
fish which live on the seafloor, such as blue whiting and Norway pout (Wikipedia, 
2018). In addition, by-products are increasingly utilized in fish meal production in order 
to reduce both overfishing and disposal of fish residuals. As fish by-products are 
considered all the secondary products derived during fish processing for human 
consumption and consist of heads, viscera, bones, skin and trimmings (cut offs from 
filleting and gutting) (Kim et al., 2016).

The process for the fish meal production includes cooking, pressing, drying and 
milling of the raw material. These processing parameters as well as the raw material 
used in fish meal can affect the quality of the final product which in its turn can 
influence the nutritional value.  
 Trends in fish meal production  
According to Shepherd and Jackson (2013), approximately 25-35% of the raw 
material used in fish meal production consist of by-products. As it is illustrated in 
Figure 1-2, OECD/FAO (2013) estimates that the use of by-products in fish meal 
production will be increased to 49% by 2022. 
 
Figure 1-2. World fish meal production from while fish and by-products [source:

Mt from inland waters) and 73.8 Mt from aquaculture (47.1 Mt inland and 26.7 Mt 
marine). An overview of global fish production is illustrated in Figure 1-3 (FAO, 2016).

 Aim of the study 
This project was conducted in cooperation with Vedde AS, which is a fish meal 
and fish oil producing company, located in Langevåg outside Ålesund, Norway (Vedde 
AS, 2018). The fish meal products of Vedde AS are Norse-LT 94, NorSeaMink, 
Standard Fish Meal, NorseEco-LT and Norse Nat-LT. In addition, the company 
produces NorSalmOil and NorsECOil which are fish oil products. The produced fish 
meal and fish oil are intended for animal consumption.  
The industry was established in 1925 and in 2013 became a member of 
TripleNine Group AS which is a Danish company with activities in more than 40 
countries including production, trading and sales within aquaculture, agriculture and 
pet food sector. According to the annual report of TripleNine Group AS, the revenue 
for 2015 was 1.7 milliard DKK (TripleNine Group, 2018). 
In this master thesis, the studied products were Norse-LT and Norse Nat-LT. 
The aim of the thesis was to study how the season, the species entering into fish meal 
composition and the effect of temperature, as processing parameter, influence its 
quality. Therefore, the research hypothesis is that all these factors have an effect in the 
final quality of the product.

2 Theoretical background 
 Raw material resources 
As mentioned, fish meal is produced from whole fish and fish by-products. 
Their composition reflects the composition and final quality of fish meal. 
2.1.1 Whole fish 
A big variety of fish species is used for the fish meal production (Table 2-1). 
The chemical composition of the fish, influences the chemical composition of the final 
products (fish meal and oil). As it will be explained later in section 2.2, one of the most 
important operations in fish meal and fish oil manufacture is the separation of lipids. 
The major part of the fish fat ends up in fish oil (approximately 60%), while the rest 
(approximately 40%) leaches into fish meal (Opstvedt, 1985). The lipid fraction into 
fish meal and oil depends on the used species. The approximate fat composition in fish 
meal is 5-10% in total. During the fish meal and oil production, the biggest part of 
triacylglycerols is transferred to the oil, while the phospholipids are transferred almost 
completely, along with the rest of triacylglycerols, in fish meal (Opstvedt, 1985).  In 
addition, the fish proteins end up in fish meal as stickwater during the processing (FAO, 
1986). The carbohydrates in fish are extremely low, leading to almost zero content in 
fish meal (Aursand et al., 2011).  
With respect to the fat content, the fish species can be divided into fatty and 
lean. The fat content of fish may differ as season changes (Hall, 2011).  
 Fatty fish. The fat content of fatty fish is > 5% and is stored as triacylglycerols 
(neutral lipids) distributed in the whole body while the membrane lipids 
(phospholipids) are placed in the cell membranes. Fatty fish used in fish meal 
are the clupeids (herrings), the scombroids (mackerels) and the salmonoids 
(capelin and salmon by-products) (Aursand et al., 2011; FAO, 1986; Sen, 2005) 
 Lean fish. The fat content in lean fish is < 5% and is stored as triacylglycerols 
in the liver. As in fatty fish, the phospholipids are placed in the cell membranes. 
The lean species used in fish meal are the gadoids (cod, haddock, blue whiting, 
Norway pout, etc.) and the final product is characterised as white fish meal 
(Aursand et al., 2011; FAO, 1986; Sen, 2005).

 The fish meal and oil production  
The production line of Vedde AS is based on the standardized wet pressing 
method of fish meal and oil (FAO, 1986). This method removes the water, oil and fat-
free solids from the raw material and consequently separates it to fish meal and oil 
(Hall, 2011). The production of these two products is therefore strongly related to each 
other. The duration of the process for the fish meal production is estimated at about 2.5 
hours (excluding the cooling time). A flowchart of the company’s production line is 
illustrated in Figure 2-10. 
 
 Figure 2-10. The LT-production process, after Vedde AS (2018), is divided into nine steps: 1) 
heating, 2) pre-straining 3) pressing, 4) centrifugation, 5) separation of press liquor, 6) oil 
polishing 7) stickwater evaporation, 8) drying and 9) cooling and milling. Each colour 
represents a different stage on the production line.  
As pre-processing stage is considered the raw material unloading from the 
fishing vessel to the raw material tank. The unloading is conducted through pumps and 
gravity flumes. Seawater, known also as blood water, is used to support the flow (Hall, 
2011). The blood water is removed during the fish meal process. The raw material is 
==PAGE:29==
16 
 
divided, with respect to their type, to three silos with 900 tons capacity each which feed 
further the cookers (Vedde AS, 2018). 
Since the storage duration can reach 30 hours, the preservation of the raw 
material is of high importance. The freshness of the fish material can be preserved either 
by physical or chemical methods. The most known physical preserving methods are the 
draining, chilling, refrigerated water systems and ice. Chemical preservatives, such as 
sodium nitrite, sodium sulphite, ascorbic acid and benzoic acid, can be used to reduce 
the bacterial load on the fish surface (FAO, 1986). In the case of Vedde AS, the raw 
material is preserved by chilling (approximately 4 °C) both on the vessel and on land 
(silos).  
The production of fish meal and fish oil at Vedde AS can be divided into nine 
main steps: 1) heating, 2) pre-straining 3) pressing, 4) centrifugation, 5) separation of 
press liquor, 6) oil polishing 7) stickwater evaporation, 8) drying and 9) cooling and 
milling (FAO, 1986; Vedde AS, 2018). 
Heating 
The first step in the fish meal/oil production is the cooking (heating) of the raw 
material. The purpose of this is to liberate the oil from the fish fat depots and at the 
same time to prevent the bacteriological problems in the process. For this reason, 
cooking is the most crucial phase and the regulator of fish meal quality and capacity in 
production (Vedde AS, 2018). 
The company is using steam cookers. Blood water, water and press liquor are 
mixed inside the cookers and are heated up to an optimum cooking temperature. The 
optimum temperature is very hard to be identified. The raw material is usually heat 
treated at temperature below 100 ˚C and atmospheric pressure. According to some 
recent studies, there is no reason to heat the raw material above the 75 ˚C, which is the 
coagulation temperature of the fish protein. In addition, the heat treatment duration is 
usually between 15-20 min. Nevertheless, the optimum heating conditions can be 
reached through practical experience in the industry. Overcooking can lead to problems 
at pressing and evaporation steps due to large amounts of suspended particles in the 
stickwater. Therefore, a heating control must be implemented on a regular basis in order 
to prevent overcooking,  while ensuring sufficient cooking (FAO, 1986). 
==PAGE:30==
17 
 
Pre-straining 
After the cooker, the liberated liquid is drained from the coagulated fish pulp in 
a strainer in order to facilitate the functioning of the press. This liquid is combined with 
the press liquor, which is derived after the pressing. The strainers that are usually used 
in fish meal production are conveyor, vibrating or rotary. The perforation size of the 
strainer depends on the fish type (FAO, 1986; Vedde AS, 2018). 
Pressing 
At the third step, the raw material, which is pre-strained in a strainer conveyor, 
is guided to the press. At this point, the liquid (press liquor) is expelled from the solids 
(presscake). The goal in this phase is to receive as less as possible content of solids and 
liquids for the press liquor and presscake respectively (Vedde AS, 2018). This is 
implemented by the use of single or twin screw presses. The screw inside the presser 
moves the material along a tapering shaft by inlet flights and squeezes the liquid.  
The regulating factors are the cooking and pressing temperatures and the 
balance between throughput and pressing time. In addition, a higher porosity of the raw 
material improves the pressing efficiency. The identification of the optimum 
parameters is again a matter of experience and skill. Use of centrifugation instead of 
pressing for solid-liquid separation is a possible option (FAO, 1986; Hall, 2011). After 
pressing, the presscake is transferred further to the dryers and the press liquor to the 
decanters. 
Centrifugation 
The fourth step in the process is the separation of the press liquor from its 
remaining solids in the decanters. The raw material in the decanters consists of about 
70% press liquor and 30% presscake. The separation can be implemented with either 
press or decanter. Vedde AS is using a decanter (centrifugation), where the raw material 
is heated up to approximately 90-95 ˚C, and then is separated (Hall, 2011; Vedde AS, 
2018).  
The advantages of centrifugation are that it is a simplified, controllable and 
quick unit operation. In addition, it provides a soft and very fluid material (FAO, 1986). 
The removing dried solid, which is called sludge or grax, is mixed with the presscake 
and sent to driers. The liquor, in its turn, is sent to separators for further separation into 
fish oil and stickwater. 
==PAGE:31==
18 
 
Separation of press liquor 
At this step, the press liquor is pumped to a separator (centrifuge) which 
separates the fat from the liquid. After separation, the residual liquid still contains about 
8% fine dry matter (sludge). This fluid, which is very viscous and sticky, is called 
stickwater and contains dissolved protein, suspended protein, residual oil, minerals, 
vitamins and amines/ammonia. At this point, the process is divided to the production 
of fish oil and fish meal. On the one hand, the fat is going further for cleaning to oil 
separators and fish oil production, and on the other hand, the stickwater is guided to 
evaporator for the fish meal production. In addition, one part of the gained sludge is 
guided either to the cooker or sent for mixing with the presscake and then to the dryers 
(Vedde AS, 2018). 
Oil polishing 
The oil polishing is carried out in separators. The storage oil stability is ensured 
by hot water, which is used to facilitate the oil polishing, and extract the impurities from 
it. The regulating parameter of this procedure, which can affect the raw material 
properties (viscosity, specific gravity), is the temperature. The recommended 
temperature in oil separators is approximately 90-95 ˚C but not less than 90 ˚C (FAO, 
1986). Finally, the polished oil is guided to the oil silos for inspection and storage 
(Vedde AS, 2018). 
Stickwater evaporation 
The amount of stickwater, about 65% of the raw material, is pumped first to a 
two level waste heat evaporator (WHE), and then to a mechanical vapor recompression 
evaporator (MVR). The purpose of that is the removal of as much as possible water by 
a multi-stage evaporation (Vedde AS, 2018). The water, after water treatment, is 
disposed to the sea via deepwater pipelines. Furthermore, there is waste off gas to the 
environment. The removing of the large quantities of water is heat demanding and thus 
non-economic. The heat economy and fuel conception are very crucial at this stage and 
must be taken into account at the planning stage.  
At this stage, it is decided what type and how many evaporating plants should 
be used, how many evaporating stages will be implemented and other important 
operating conditions. High temperatures (above 130 ˚C) can destroy vitamin B12 and 
the important amino acids; cysteine, lysine and tryptophan or can cause material 
==PAGE:32==
19 
 
discoloration. In addition, a mechanical treatment must be executed regularly in order 
to remove the deposited solids from the hot surfaces, which can hinder the heat transfer 
and block the tubes.  After the evaporation, the material, which is called now 
concentrate, still consists of 20-35% dry matter. The stickwater concentrate is possible 
to be sold under the name ‘fish soluble’ but is usually fed to the dryers for fish meal 
production (FAO, 1986). 
Drying 
Both the concentrate, presscake and sludge are collected, homogenized at a 
moisture content of less than 12%, and dried in the dryers. The dryers’ task is to remove 
the water from the solid matter by heating it up to water evaporation temperature. Vedde 
AS has a two level drying process.  
At the first level, three large steam dryers ensure that the water percentage of 
the material drops to 35-40% before goes on to the LT (low temperature) dryer. It is 
recommended that the temperature at these dryers must not exceed 90 ˚C. However, the 
implemented temperature in these dryers is 90-100 ˚C for 1 hour. Overheating can lead 
to anti-nutritional reactions (protein–protein and protein–lipid interactions). At the 
second level, a gentle drying (70 ˚C) for approximately 30 min is conducted in the LT 
dryer to maintain the biological digestibility of the fish meal (FAO, 1986; Hall, 2011). 
The LT dryer ensures that the water content in the product is down to about 8%. The 
derived vapor is guided to atomizers for purification (Vedde AS, 2018). 
Cooling and milling 
The ninth and last step in the process is the cooling and milling. The ready fish 
meal is transported by conveyor screws to a cooler where air flow ensures the cooling 
to 25 ˚C. The product remains in this condition for approximately 24 hours. Afterwards, 
it is milled to the desired fineness and is transported to the mixing silo in bulk and then 
to the storing silo. There, the product is stored in paper bags of 25-40 kg or big sacks 
of 1000 kg (Vedde AS, 2018) at a storage room where the temperature is affected by 
the environmental conditions. The shelf life of the product is estimated to be two years.

mainly used in small and large salmon, mink, halibut and small pigs (Vedde AS, 2018).  
 
Norse Nat-LT 
It is a high-quality fish meal produced under gentle drying (LT) conditions from 
wild fish raw material without synthetic preservatives and a natural antioxidant. 
According to Norwegian regulations, NorseNat-LT is accepted as an organic feed 
ingredient, which is used in food production for pets (Vedde AS, 2018).

2.3.1 Proteins 
The protein content of fish meal is approximately 60-72% and is usually 
expressed as crude protein (nitrogen content x 6.25) (Hall, 2011). The main factor that 
influences this content is the nature of raw material. In case that by-products are used 
in fish meal production (skin, bone, connective tissue), the protein content has been 
observed to be close to 60%.  (FAO, 1986).  
The nutritional value of any dietary protein depends on its amino acid profile 
and digestibility (Miles & Chapman, 2006). It is not the protein content that is required 
for animals but specific amino acids in certain quantities (Wilson & Halver, 1986). 
Consequently, fish meal of high-quality is a valuable protein supplement in feed 
formulation due to its high digestible protein (above 95%) followed by balanced amino 
acid composition (Hall, 2011).  
2.3.1.1 Amino acid requirements of fish  
The amino acids are divided into essential and non-essential (Table 2-8). The 
former cannot be synthesized by animals, in contrast with the non-essential, thus must 
be provided through the diet (Wu, 2013). The protein digestion leads to the release of 
amino acids in the animal’s circulatory system which are further distributed to different 
tissues (Wilson & Halver, 1986).  
The essential amino acids are necessary for maintenance, optimal growth and 
reproduction of animals through protein synthesis (Wilson & Halver, 1986). In cases 
of excess protein supply in the organism, after the required protein synthesis has been 
achieved, the rest of the amino acids are oxidised and the carbon skeletons are used as 
energy source. The produced nitrogen is either excreted as urea via kidneys or used for 
the synthesis of non-essential amino acids (Coultate, 2009). In addition, other non-
protein nitrogenous compounds of major importance for the organism’s physiology are 
formed from amino acids. These can be thyroid hormones (tyrosine), catecholamines 
(phenylalanine), carnitine (lysine), purines (glycine, glutamine), creatine (glycine, 
arginine), melanine (tyrosine), histamine (histidine) (Tacon, 1987). 
Even if one amino acid is totally absent, or is present in lower amounts than 
required, inhibition of protein synthesis is observed (Coultate, 2009). This amino acid 
is characterised as ‘limiting amino acid’ resulting to a poor nutritional value of the 
==PAGE:38==
25 
 
protein and further leading to reduced growth and weight loss in animals. The most 
common limiting amino acid is lysine. Losses of lysine are observed during feed 
manufacturing due to its ε-amino group which reacts with other compounds (Belitz et 
al., 1999). Other limiting amino acids are methionine and tryptophan (Craig et al., 
2017). In excessive deficiency conditions in fish, lysine has been related to increased 
mortality and fin erosion, methionine may cause cataract and tryptophan can be 
responsible for scoliosis, lordosis, fin erosion and decreased carcass lipid content 
(Tacon, 1987). Generally, amino acid deficiencies are displayed as reduced growth rate 
(Cowey, 1994). 
Animals have the ability to convert phenylalanine into tyrosine (aromatic amino 
acids) and methionine to cysteine (sulfur containing amino acids). In both cases, 
tyrosine and cysteine can partially replace phenylalanine and methionine respectively 
in diet (Belitz et al., 1999).  
The amino acid requirements for animals can be determined by trials where the 
weight gain is used as indicator of feed efficiency (dose-response curves) (Cowey, 
1994). Fish seem to have the highest requirement in dietary protein (30-50%) in 
comparison to other domestic farmed animals (Anderson & Lall, 1994). The age and 
size of fish are inversely proportional to the required amount of protein (Wilson & 
Halver, 1986). Studies have shown that the amino acid composition in the body of 
different fish species appear to have a lot of similarities (Table 2-9). Hence, significant 
differences in amino acid requirements are not expected (Kaushik, 1998; Wilson & 
Cowey, 1985). Ideally, when animal feed formulation occurs, the protein content must 
provide amino acids in levels proportional to the body’s demands (Coultate, 2009).

2.3.1.2 Stickwater concentrate 
As mentioned in section 2.2, stickwater is the derived, of high viscosity, water-
soluble fraction of fish (press liquor) during fish meal manufacturing. It contains 
various water-soluble components, such as water-soluble vitamins, minerals, water-
soluble proteins, small peptides etc. and a small amount of lipids (Kousoulaki et al., 
2009; Zarkadas et al., 1986). According to Hulan et al. (1987), improved growth was 
observed when stickwater was added into fish meal. Thus, it is crucial to incorporate 
this fraction into fish meal. 
The water-soluble proteins are relatively small sarcoplasmic proteins and their 
presence in stickwater are of high importance. Approximately, 20-30% of total protein 
content consists of water-soluble proteins (T. Samuelsen & Oterhals, 2016). According 
to Tonheim et al. (2007), water-soluble proteins due to their small size, can be absorbed 
better from the animal’s digestive system. Thus, they are characterised by higher 
digestibility compared to non-soluble proteins. In addition, they are related to the high 
palatability of fish meal (Dong et al., 2016). Furthermore, water-soluble proteins can 
==PAGE:40==
27 
 
improve the physical properties of pellets (fish feed), such as hardness and durability 
(T. A. Samuelsen et al., 2013). 
2.3.2 Lipids 
The fat content of fish meal depends on the utilized raw material. Fish meal 
produced from pelagic fish has fat content between 5-14%, but when white fish are 
used, the fat content is below 5% (Anderson & Lall, 1994). 
Animals cannot synthesize the essential fatty acids linoleic [omega-6 
polyunsaturated fatty acid (LC n-6 PUFA)] and α-linolenic acid (ALA, LC n-3 PUFA), 
therefore these must be obtained through the feed. The α-linolenic acid can be 
synthesized by plants from linoleic acid and further converted to EPA and DHA. 
Although animals cannot synthesize EPA and DHA, they can metabolize the α-linolenic 
acid, through elongation and desaturation, to other important PUFA, such as 
eicosapentaenoic acid (20:5 n-3, EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (22:6 n-3, DHA) 
(Opstvedt, 1985). Due to the limited conversion of α-linolenic acid to EPA and DHA, 
the organisms have to obtain them through the diet. High amounts of EPA and DHA in 
the fish composition are due to consumption of algae rich in these polyunsaturated fatty 
acids. (Yongmanitchai & Ward, 1991). In contrast, fish contain a small amount of LC 
n-6 PUFA, thus the produced fish meal does not provide large amounts of these. 
Consequently, fish meal, along with fish oil, are the main sources of omega-3 
long chain polyunsaturated fatty acids (LC n-3 PUFA) used in feed formulation which 
consist mostly of eicosapentaenoic acid (20:5 n-3, EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid 
(22:6 n-3, DHA). Fish meal contains approximately 1-3% LC n-3 PUFA. In general, 
marine lipids are characterised by high digestibility (Anderson & Lall, 1994; Pike, 
1999).  
EPA and DHA are very important for the function of cell membranes, providing 
structure and flexibility (Hall, 2011). In addition, they can improve the immunity 
response in animals resulting to higher resistance in diseases and especially 
cardiovascular diseases (reduced inflammation, reduced blood pressure). In addition, 
these LC n-3 PUFA have a positive effect in brain function (Cho & Kim, 2011; Efsa, 
2012), in reproduction and growth (Pike, 1999).  
The requirements of animals in marine lipids vary and can be met by adding 
fish oil in the feed during manufacturing (Opstvedt, 1985; Pike, 1999).

2.3.3 Vitamins and minerals 
Fish meal is considered a good source of vitamins and minerals. The B-group 
of vitamins (water-soluble) is present in fish meal, in contrast with the fat soluble 
vitamins (A, D, E) which are mostly removed with the fish oil during processing (Miles 
& Chapman, 2006).  
The ash fraction of fish meal consists mainly of minerals such as calcium, 
phosphorous and potassium, but it can also contain trace elements such as selenium, 
copper, zinc and iron (FAO, 1986). Fish meal contains ash at levels 10-20%. The 
highest levels are observed when either white fish or fish by-products are used for the 
production of fish meal, influencing negatively the bioavailability of trace elements 
(Anderson & Lall, 1994). In addition, Shearer et al. (1992) showed that the ash content 
and the growth rate were inversely proportional.  
 Quality of fish meal 
The quality of fish meal is influenced by the used raw material and its freshness, 
the processing parameters, the stability of lipids, storage conditions and hazards 
(microorganisms, amines) (Hall, 2011). 
2.4.1 Raw material freshness  
The time between the arrival of the raw material in the processing plant and its 
insertion in the fish meal production line has an effect in the final quality of the product. 
Prolonged storage (more than 30 hours) leads in reduced quality, due to the 
decomposition of raw material (lipolysis, proteolysis) which is a result of enzymatic 
and bacterial activity (FAO, 1986). Protein breaks down to biogenic amines, peptides, 
amino acids, volatile ammonia and trimethylamine. Lipid decomposition is slower and 
the accumulated products are free fatty acids, mono- and diacylglycerols, glycerol and 
lysophospholipids (Aursand et al., 2011). 
The presence of biogenic amines, such as histamine, cadaverine, putrescine and 
tyrosine, and the measurement of total volatile nitrogen (TVN) in raw material 
constitutes an indication of its freshness ((Ricque-Marie et al., 1998). The total volatile 
nitrogen in the raw material must be below 8% (8 g N/100 g fish) (FAO, 1986). As 
==PAGE:42==
29 
 
mentioned in section 2.2, the preservation methods during raw material storage can 
retain its freshness. 
2.4.2 Processing parameters 
The most important parameter during fish meal production is the temperature. 
Elevated temperatures (> 120°C) applied for long period must be avoided throughout 
the fish meal production (cooking, stickwater evaporation, drying) as overheating of 
fish meal can reduce the nutritional value of proteins (FAO, 1986).  
When proteins are exposed to temperatures where denaturation occurs, the 
protein molecule unfolds. Under mild heating, the availability of amino acids in fish 
meal is increased as the unfolded protein structure can facilitate the activity of digestive 
enzymes in the animal’s intestine (Opstvedt et al., 2003). On the other hand, higher 
processing temperatures result in interactions of amino acids within the protein and 
interaction with other compounds present in fish meal. Then, the protein aggregates and 
is no longer nutritionally available (Boye et al., 1997; Opstvedt et al., 2003). Lysine is 
considered as highly reactive amino acid due to its ε-amino group (Belitz et al., 1999).  
In addition, elevated temperatures can reduce the protein solubility by 
disrupting the protein-water bond. The reason is that hydrophobic amino acids are 
exposed due to interactions within proteins (Boye et al., 1997).  
However, as mentioned in section 2.2, the common applied temperatures during 
fish meal production are mild, resulting to the preservation of its nutritional value.  
2.4.3 Oxidation 
The oxidation of lipids is of major importance for the final quality of fish meal. 
During the fish meal production, the combined effect of temperature and air along with 
the presence of unsaturated fatty acids (especially LC n-3 PUFA) in the lipid fraction, 
promotes the oxidation (Anderson & Lall, 1994). The LC n-3 PUFA and especially 
EPA and DHA are highly susceptible to oxidation. In addition, the freshness of the raw 
material and the storage conditions influence the rate of oxidation (Aursand et al., 
2011). The resulting lipid oxidation products can further facilitate the oxidation of 
proteins, which is associated with losses of amino acids (Belitz et al., 1999).  
As a result, the nutritional as well as the organoleptic properties of fish meal are 
influenced negatively (Anderson & Lall, 1994).

2.4.3.1 
Lipid oxidation 
Lipid oxidation can be conducted through three mechanisms.  
1. Enzymatic oxidation. Catalyzed by enzymes present in the raw material 
(lypases, phospholipases, lipoxygenases etc.) (Aursand et al., 2011). 
2. Photooxidation. The combination of light exposure with the presence of 
photo-sensitizers, leads to the activation of oxygen (singlet oxygen) which 
reacts with the fatty acids (Aursand et al., 2011). 
3. Autooxidation. It is a spontaneous reaction between oxygen and fatty acid 
radicals (Jacobsen et al., 2013). 
According to Laohabanjong et al. (2009) the mechanism catalyzing the lipid 
oxidation in fishmeal is the autooxidation. As shown in the Figure 2-12, the oxidation 
consists of three steps: 1) initiation, 2) propagation and 3) termination (Coultate, 2009). 
 
Figure 2-12. Overview of the autooxidation chain reactions. The ( •) shows the unpaired 
electron, (R) is the remainder molecule, (X•) symbolizes the first free radicals, (R•) is the alkyl 
radical, (RO•) is the alkoxyl radical, (ROO•) is the peroxyl radical, (ROOH) is the 
hydroperoxides [source:(Coultate, 2009)]. 
Initiation.  The reaction is initiated by singlet oxygen (1O2). This constitutes a 
highly reactive form of oxygen (3O2) produced under various reactions. The singlet 
oxygen interacts with an unsaturated fatty acid (double bond). As a result, the first 
hydroperoxides (ROOH) are produced and further decomposed to hydroperoxy and 
alkoxy radicals. These constitute the first free-radicals (X•) which are able to remove a 
hydrogen atom from unsaturated fatty acids (RH) leading to the formation of alkyl 
radicals (R•) (Coultate, 2009).  
Propagation.  At this stage, the produced alkyl radicals react with atmospheric 
oxygen (O2) and form peroxyl radicals (ROO•). In their turn, the peroxyl radicals react 
with new unsaturated fatty acids and produce hydroperoxides (ROOH) and new alkyl 
radicals (R•). The accumulated hydroperoxides constitute the primary oxidation 
==PAGE:44==
31 
 
products. High temperature or presence of transition metals (iron) promote the 
decomposition of hydroperoxides to alkoxyl (RO•) and peroxyl (ROO•) radicals which 
can contribute to further formation of hydroperoxides. (Jacobsen et al., 2013). 
Termination. The oxidation terminates when two free radicals react and form 
stable products (RR, ROR, ROOR) (Jacobsen et al., 2013).  
The alkoxyl (RO•) and peroxyl (ROO•) radicals, except for the formation of 
hydroperoxides, can give rise to secondary oxidation products. Aldehydes, ketones 
and alcohols are formed by cleavage of carbon chain in every side of the alkoxyl 
radicals, through a b-scission reaction (Aursand et al., 2011; Coultate, 2009). The rancid 
odour is due to these secondary products. 
At the same time, peroxyl radicals (ROO•) can be oxidized to hydroperoxy 
epidioxides and bicycloendoperoxides. These may further lead to malonaldehyde. 
2.4.3.2 Protein oxidation 
The direct or indirect reactions of protein with reactive oxygen species (ROS) 
and secondary by-products of oxidative stress respectively result to its oxidation 
(Davies, 2016).  
In general, changes in food texture, browning, loss of essential amino acids and 
reduced solubility can occur due to protein reactions with hydroperoxides or their 
degradation products. The produced hydroperoxides from lipid oxidation, are able to 
abstract hydrogen atoms from protein (PH) and especially from the side chains of the 
amino acids tryptophan, lysine, tyrosine, arginine, histidine and cysteine leading to the 
formation of a protein network (Belitz et al., 1999).  
Malonaldehydehe, can crosslink proteins utilizing the ε-amino acid group of 
lysine. In addition, the condense of monocarbonyl compounds, which are derived from 
the autoxidation of unsaturated fatty acids, with NH2 results to Schiff bases which are 
responsible for browning. The most common indicator of protein oxidation is the 
protein carbonyl (aldehydes and ketones) content (Belitz et al., 1999) 
2.4.3.3 Prooxidants and antioxidants 
Prooxidants facilitate and accelerate the lipid oxidation in food products. Fish 
meal contains metal ions originated either from its raw material (iron and copper) or 
the production equipment (iron) which are catalysts of lipid oxidation (Jacobsen et al., 
2009).

2.4.4 Microbiological issues – Biogenic amines 
These hazards influence the final quality of fish meal. Despite the fact that their 
analysis is beyond the scope of the current master thesis, it is worth to be mentioned 
briefly. 
Salmonella 
The major part of harmful bacteria present in fish is killed during fish meal 
manufacturing. However, precautions must be taken, as poor hygienic conditions in the 
production line may lead to recontamination of the material, especially with 
Salmonella. Thus, great attention must be given in the proper cleaning and maintenance 
of the processing plant (Windsor, 1971).  
In case that Salmonella is detected in fish meal, this must be processed again 
utilizing either heat treatment with steam (90°C for 10-15 minutes) and then redrying 
or acids, such as formic and propionic acid (FAO, 1986). 
Biogenic amines 
Elevated levels of cadaverine, putrescine, histamine and tyrosine are closely 
related to low quality fish meal and can have a negative effect in animals health (Cho 
& Kim, 2011). Histamine is produced from histidine, cadaverine from lysine, 
putrescine from arginine and tyrosine from tyramine, under the decarboxylase activity 
associated with variety of bacteria belonging to Enterobacteriaceae (Halász et al., 1994; 
==PAGE:47==
34 
 
Ricque-Marie et al., 1998). High quality fish meal must contain < 500 mg/kg histamine 
(Cho & Kim, 2011).  
2.4.5 Storage conditions 
Another parameter that affects the fish meal quality and stability is its storage. 
Proper fish meal storage contributes to almost no loss in its nutritional value for very 
long period (Windsor, 1971). However, Bragadóttir et al. (2004) showed that the season 
can affect the quality of fish meal during storage, as capelin fish meal displayed faster 
lipid oxidation in autumn than in other periods. 
Fish meal is preserved in paper, hessian or plastic sacks. It is not refrigerated 
because it cannot easily be spoiled by bacteria due to its low water content. However, 
fish meal must be kept in cool and dry place in order to avoid either the reaction with 
oxygen or the animal contact (rodents and birds). Good ventilation of the stored sacks 
is also very crucial especially in hot climates. However, many manufacturers keep the 
fish meal in big silos (storage in bulk). In this case, the risk of fish meal contamination 
is higher during loading and unloading in transport and must be avoided (Windsor, 
1971).

3.3.1 Dry matter and ash content 
The samples were analyzed in two parallels as described by (AOAC, 1990). 
Approximately 2 g were weighed into crucibles and placed in the heating cupboard at 
105°C for 24 hours. After a constant sample weight had been achieved, the crucibles 
were moved to a desiccator for cooling up to room temperature and then weighed again. 
The difference between the final (crucible + sample) and the initial weight of the 
crucible gave the dry matter.  
Subsequently, according to AOAC (1990) the samples were set into the ashing 
furnace at 550°C for approximately 12 hours. As before, the crucibles were moved to a 
desiccator until cooled and then they were weighed again. The ash weight was 
computed from the difference between the total final weight (crucible + sample) and 
the initial weight of the crucible.  
In both cases, the dry matter and ash content were afterwards calculated as a 
percentage of the initial sample weight.  
3.3.2 Protein analysis 
3.3.2.1 Crude protein content – CN-analysis 
The determination of total nitrogen was used for the calculation of crude protein 
content only in fish meal samples. The analysis was performed in three parallels. 
As fish meal is considered a dried product (~92% dry matter), 1 mg was directly 
weighed out in the analytical micro-balance and placed into a tin capsule container (4 
x 6 mm). Each capsule was closed carefully by shaping it into a small ball and then was 
transferred into a 96-well tissue culture plate. Consequently, the plate was delivered to 
Inger Beate Standal, SINTEF Ocean AS, who ran the CN-analyzer. In the analyzer, the 
capsules were dropped in a tube and burned, under oxygen presence, at 1800°C. 
The principle of this process is that the derived from the combustion gases (N2, 
NOx, H2O, SO2, O2 and CO2) are carried (helium is used as a gas carrier) to a Cu-
column, through a column filled with chromium and cobalt oxide. There, oxygen is 
reduced to CuO and nitrogen to elementary nitrogen. At the same time, water is 
absorbed in another column. The rest of the gasses go through a TPD (Temperature 
Programmed Desoprtion) column and are bound while N2 passes through it. The 
separation of N2 and CO2 is achieved by a temperature raise in the column. Finally, the 
==PAGE:55==
42 
 
concentration of nitrogen and carbon is determined by a thermal conductivity detector 
(TCD) which produces an electrical signal proportional to these amounts.  
In order to compute the crude protein content, the amount of nitrogen was 
multiplied with a conversion factor of 6.25 (equivalent to 0.16 g nitrogen/g of protein).  
3.3.2.2 Determination of protein solubility – Lowry method 
Preparation of crude extracts for determination of protein solubility 
In centrifuge tubes, approximately 1 g of the fish meal samples was weighed 
out and approximately 5 g of the samples representing the frozen raw material and the 
material taken from the fish meal production line. Next, each sample was homogenized 
with 10 ml distilled water for 30 seconds. After that, the samples were left in the cold 
room for 10 min and were stirred occasionally. Then, they were centrifuged at 4°C. The 
fish meal samples were centrifuged at 6000 rpm for 10 minutes while the samples 
representing the frozen raw material and the material taken from the fish meal 
production line were centrifuged at 10400 x g for 20 minutes. The supernatants were 
filtered through glass wool and the acquired protein extracts, if not used immediately 
for the determination of protein solubility, were stored at -20°C for no more than a 
week. This process was repeated twice for the fish meal samples.  
Preparation for the determination of acid soluble peptides 
The following procedure is based on Hoyle and Merritt (1994) with 
modifications. The principal behind this method has to do with the fact that the very 
small peptides can be soluble into the acid, while the large ones aggregate as the 
hydrophobicity is increased with the increasing length (Yvon et al., 1989). 
The acid soluble peptides were determined for the fish meal samples. Three 
parallels were analyzed from each extract (derived as described above). In test tubes, 2 
ml extract were added and mixed for 30 seconds in the vortex mixer with 2 ml 20% 
trichloroacetic acid (TCA). Then, the mixtures were left for 30 minutes to rest. 
Following this, the extracts were filtered using filter paper (5891 Schwartzband 70 mm) 
into test tubes and the content of acid soluble peptides was determined by the Lowry 
method.

This process was performed with modifications as described by Lowry et al.

3.3.2.3 Hydrolysis of connective tissue – Determination of total amino acid 
composition 
The determination of the total amino acid composition was conducted as 
described by Blackburn (1968) with some modifications. This method is based on the 
hydrolysis of the proteins using acid which leads to the release of their amino acids and 
their subsequent separation via chromatography. Characteristic of this method is the 
total destruction of tryptophan during the process with the acid. Other amino acids such 
as serine, methionine, cysteine and threonine may be gradually destroyed. At the same 
time oxidation of tyrosine is observed. In addition, asparagine and glutamine can be 
transformed partially to aspartic and glutamic acid influencing the accuracy of their 
measurement (Nielsen, 1998).

samples from the fish meal production line (before dryer and after dryer) were freeze

3.3.2.4 Size distribution of peptides - Fast protein liquid chromatography

The column used in the FPLC leads to the separation of proteins, peptides and 
other biomolecules according to their size (molecular weight between 100-7000).

3.3.3 Lipid analysis 
3.3.3.1 Lipid extraction and determination of total lipid content 
The determination of total lipid content was conducted as described by Bligh 
and Dyer (1959) with modifications. The homogenization of the samples using 
chloroform, methanol and water leads to the formation of two layers; the one is the non-
polar layer consisting of chloroform and the dissolved lipids in that and the second is 
the polar layer consisting of water, methanol and the non-lipid components. Therefore, 
the lipid extract can be obtained isolating the chloroform bottom layer. 
The process was repeated twice for the fish meal samples and once for the rest. 
Both times, the total lipid content was determined. 
Lipid extraction 
Two parallels were analyzed from each sample. Approximately 10 g were 
weighed out in centrifuge bottles. Next, 16 ml of distilled water, 40 ml of methanol and 
20 ml of chloroform were added in the bottles followed by their homogenization for 2 
minutes. Further, 20 ml of chloroform were again added in the samples and 
homogenized for 40 seconds. Then, 20 ml of distilled water were added and 
homogenized for 40 seconds. Subsequently, the samples were centrifuged in order to 
create the two layers (polar and non-polar). The fish meal samples were centrifuged at 
9000 rpm for 15 minutes and the rest of the samples (representing the frozen raw 
material and the material from the production line) were centrifuged at 7000 rpm for 15 
minutes. The temperature during centrifugation was 4°C.  
Following this, the obtained bottom layers (chloroform + lipids) were pipetted 
out and placed into centrifuge tubes. In order to acquire as pure chloroform extract as 
possible, all the tubes were centrifuged again at 6000 rpm for 10 minutes and then were 
filtered (Whatman filter papers, circles with 55 mm diameter). Samples were processed 
immediately, or stored at -20°C for further analysis.  
Throughout the procedure, the samples were kept on ice.

weighed test tubes using glass pipettes. Then, they were placed in the evaporating unit 
at 60°C for one hour, with the simultaneous use of nitrogen, to evaporate the

3.3.3.3 Determination of thiobarbituric acid reactive substances (TBARS) 
The decomposition of primary oxidation products can lead to the formation of 
secondary oxidation products, especially malondialdehyde (MDA). Using the method 
of thiobarbituric acid reactive substances (TBARS), these products can be detected. The 
thiobarbituric acid (TBA) reacts with them, under the simultaneous effect of low pH 
and high temperature, leading to the formation of pink colour. Its intensity is 
proportional to the concentration of MDA in the sample and can be measured 
spectrophotometrically (Decker et al., 2010; Kilcast & Subramaniam, 2011). 
The analysis was performed in fish meal lipid extracts taken from the second 
extraction process. Hence, it was conducted only once for all the samples (fish meal, 
frozen raw material and material from the production line). 
Prior to the procedure, the following solutions were prepared: 
 TBA stock solution. 
 Sodium sulphite solution. 
 TCA solution. 
==PAGE:65==
52 
 
 TBA work solution containing: 180 ml TBA stock solution, 120 ml 
chloroform, 15 ml sodium sulphite solution and 9.45 ml butylated 
hydroxytoluene (3% BHT in ethanol). The solution had to be used 
within 30 minutes. 
 TEP stock solution. 
From each chloroform extract obtained (see section 3.3.3.1), 200 μl were added 
in kimax tubes and vortexing for 15 seconds with 5 ml TBA work solution. Next, the 
tubes were incubated in boiling bath for 45 minutes and after that, they were cooled 
down in cold water. Further, 2.5 ml TCA solution were added in every tube, followed 
by inversion and centrifugation for 7 minutes at 900 x g. After the centrifugation, the 
creation of two layers was observed. The upper butanol layer was characterized by a 
pink color and was further collected by using Pasteur glass pipettes and placed into 
glass cuvettes. Absorbance of the resultant butanol layer was then measured at 538 nm 
against the blank by using spectrophotometer. Distilled water was used as a blank.  
In addition, the same procedure was performed for the standard solutions in two 
parallels. A calibration curve was constructed using serial dilutions (0-200 μL) of 
1,1,3,3-tetramethoxypropane (TEP) in water (when dissolved in water gets hydrolyzed 
to produce MDA). The TBARS content was determined from the standard curve. The 
colour in the standard solutions became more intense as the concentration of the TEP  
increased (very light pink to intense pink). From the acquired standard curve (the 
absorbance was set in y-axis and the TBARS concentration in x-axis) the slope and 
intercept were used for the calculation of TBARS value in the samples, according to 
the Equation 3-9. The factor 1000 was used to convert the given μl/g lipid (total lipid 
content) to μmoles/g.

Additionally, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was conducted using the 
software ‘The Unscrambler X’. PCA is a multivariate statistical technique applying 
orthogonal transformation to convert a set of correlated variables into a set of 
orthogonal, uncorrelated axes called principal components (Jolliffe, 2002). It is 
convenient that, if principal components (PCs) explain a high percentage of variance, 
variables (quality parameters, composition data, etc.) that are not correlated with them 
can be disregarded in the analysis (Toepfer, 1998). In this work, PCA was applied to 
different fish meals in order to gain more information on possible interrelation among 
their physical and chemical parameters. PCA was also applied to enable efficient 
differentiation among different fish meals in regard to fish species used in their 
production. Thus, the application of PCA helped to classify and separate products (fish 
meals) on the basis of similarities in their composition. 
Finally, the software ‘SigmaPlot 14’ was used to carry out both single and 
multiple linear regression analysis. In all cases, the significance levels were set at p < 
0.05 and the confidence level at 95%. The simple regression analysis was conducted on 
fish meal samples and samples from the production line. The purpose of this was to 
reveal significant correlations between two factors (variables) from experimental data.  
The multiple regression analysis is used to predict the value of analyzed quality 
parameters (PV, TBARS, etc.) from a set of predictors - fishmeal composition 
components. 
Our assumption is that quality parameters of each of the fish meals are strongly 
dependent and influenced by its heterogeneous composition. Thus, by knowing the 
exact composition of each of the fish meals, we can estimate linear or non-linear 
associations between the fishmeal components and quality parameters. This will help 
==PAGE:67==
54 
 
to identify and conclude how the percentage of different fish species entering into the 
fishmeal composition influences its final quality attributes.  
A multivariate linear regression analysis was performed to determine the weight 
coefficients of the 11 individual factors making part of the analyzed fishmeal 
compositions. All statistical analyses were performed using SigmaPlot software v.14. 
The level of significance was set at p < 0.05, and the confidence level was set at 95%. 
Individual factors listed in Table I in the Appendix (composition parts – X1-X11) were 
used in the regression analysis. Table I lists the descriptive characteristics of all the 
compositional factors.

4 Results and discussion 
The goal of this master thesis was to investigate the final quality of fish meal, 
in relation to the used fish species and the season they are caught. In addition, the effect 
of the applied temperature during the production of fish meal had to be studied in order 
to find possible negative effects on the final quality. For that purpose, eight fish meal 
samples with different compositions and three samples from the production line (before 
and after dryers) were analyzed. Furthermore, four fish species, stored as a 
homogenized frozen raw material, were subjected to chemical analysis.  
In order to determine the quality of fish meal samples, their composition was 
analyzed followed by analysis regarding their oxidative status, the total amino acid 
composition and the size of the protein peptides. Similar analysis was conducted for the 
rest of the samples (production line, frozen raw material) (Figure 3-3). 
The results are given as [n] number of parallels and their standard deviation 
(SD). The level of significance was set at p < 0.05. The data used in the statistical 
analysis are displayed in Appendix.

5 Conclusion 
The effect of season, fish species and temperature as processing parameter to 
the quality of fish meal was the aim of this study. For this reason, eight different fish 
meal samples were obtained from Vedde AS for analysis. In addition, three fish meal 
samples were taken from different stages of the production line. 
Principal component analysis (PCA) displayed two main groups of the analyzed 
fish meal samples; group A and group B. Fish meal of group A consisted mainly of 
herring by-products while fish meal belonging to group B consisted of fatty whole fish. 
A significantly high protein content was observed in group A. Within this group, 
fish meal samples with 70% similar composition showed a notable protein content 
variation. This variation can be possibly explained by the different production date. The 
sample produced in November displayed a higher protein content than the other, which 
was produced on February. The effect of season in the quality was very hard to be 
studied, as the obtained samples did not differ much in relation to the production date. 
Furthermore, multiple regression analysis showed that both mackerel and 
herring by-products contributed to higher lipid content in fish meal, while NVG herring 
and herring by-products seemed to be responsible for the formation of primary and 
secondary oxidation products. Subsequently, the decomposition of these products 
contributed to the protein corbonylation. The protein carbonyls were correlated with 
the decrease in the amount of water-soluble proteins in fish meal samples. Thus, NVG 
herring and herring by-products appeared to be responsible for the quality reduction in 
fish meal.  
Additionally, there is no clear conclusion about the impact of processing 
parameters, especially temperature, in the fish meal quality (changes in the amino acid 
composition).  
Consequently, further research is suggested in order to validate the findings 
related with the effect of both NVG herring and herring by-products in the quality. Also, 
analysis of different samples with respect to season, and from different stages of the 
production line could be conducted. 
==PAGE:91==
78 
 
6 Suggestions for further work 
One of the most important parameters related to the availability of fish meal in 
animals is the quality which can be affected by various factors.  
Seasoning could be a parameter that may affect both the nutritional value and 
quality of fish meal. Thus, a further research could be implemented with focus only on 
season in fish species.  
Another suggestion could be the study of the effect of storage in relation to the 
oxidative stability of the product. Thus, chemical analysis could be conducted more 
often. In addition to that, further research could be done regarding the effect of fish by-
products and their use in fish meal.  
Also, it is worth to mention that a further study regarding the use of natural 
antioxidants in fish meal would be important. Finally, samples from different fish meal 
companies could be analyzed in order to reveal possible variations in the quality, related 
to different handling procedures.
