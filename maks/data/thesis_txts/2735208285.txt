Abstract
Measuring pH with a high spatial resolution down to cellular-sizes of 1-200 µm, is chal-
lenging as it requires specialized and expensive equipment. However, localized in-situ
pH measurements on cell cultures, contributes to a deeper understanding of e.g. cancer
growth mechanisms and its response to drug treatment. Multimaterial microrobots, manu-
factured using two-photon polymerization (2PP) printing and a smart hydrogel material, are
a promising, cost-effective, fast and accessible alternative to conventional methods. Until
now, manufacturing microrobots is challenging and labour-intensive. Within this report, an
optimized design of a microrobot is suggested together with general improvements of the
manufacturing processes, increasing both yields and flexibility of the process.
In multiple iterations, different microrobot designs are fabricated, with each iteration adding
a further improvement step to the manufacturing process or design. 2PP printing with the
Nanoscribe Photonic Professional GT+ is used to 4D print the microrobots, whereas a
scanning electron microscope is used to characterize the prints. Activation tests using
three buffer solutions with different pH levels are used to test the size-changing behaviour
of the microrobots respectively the smart hydrogel material incorporated in the microrobots.
An optimized microrobot design was manufactured, containing a feature for easy qualitative
detection of acidic or basic environments and showcasing a shrinking of the smart hydrogel
material of 20 % ± 3.9 % when going from a neutral to an acidic environment. The overall
manufacturing process is improved, increasing the yield of microrobots by approximately
200%, providing a more flexible process and therefore allowing more freedom. This is
setting the foundation for future improvements and optimization of the manufacturing
process.
Additionally, for the first time, the designed multimaterial microrobots are manipulated
using optical trapping, and measurements previously performed under a microscope are
repeated on the optical trapping setup, validating the concept pursued by DTU Nanolab.
These results establish the proof-of-concept for 2PP-printed, multimaterial microrobots
designed for localized in-situ pH sensing. Challenges in printing the smart hydrogel material
still require an iterative optimization approach for new designs. However, it could be
demonstrated that optimized microrobot designs can be manufactured with the newly
developed, more flexible alignment process, and that they show promising measurements
when activating them with different pH buffers.

Introduction
pH is extremely important for almost all living systems due to its role in biochemical
reactions, mainly the regulation of acid-base homeostasis. As an example, the bicarbonate
buffer system regulates the pH value of human blood within the narrow range of 7.36-7.42.
In other places in the body, pH is important for the activation of enzymes [1]. Certain
biological processes cause disruptions of the pH within organelles, that lead to dysfunctions
and diseased states. Tumor cells for example generate an acidic extracellular environment
due to their high metabolic rates. The acidity of this could be an important factor in
determining how aggressive the tumour is [2]. To understand the cancer mechanism
it becomes extremely important to monitor local pH variations during the growth and
in response to drug treatment. A high sensitivity and high spatial resolution of the pH
monitoring can be crucial to understanding the involved processes. To understand pH
variation on a cellular level, the measurement must be localized to a range in the micrometre
to the nanometer scale, as most cells are between 1-200 µm [3] and organelles within the
cell are typically ranged from 100 nm-10 µm [3].
The continuous development of two-photon polymerization (2PP) printing, an additive
manufacturing process, has enabled the manufacturing of structures in the micro- and nano-
meter range with short manufacturing times [4]. Together with the increased research on
smart materials and as more smart materials are becoming available for four dimensional
(4D) printing more applications are being developed. Especially in the medical field
where miniaturization is crucial, 2PP printing is getting increased attention. It enables the
manufacturing of complex, real-size biomimetic structures, allows for the fabrication of
microscale devices for minimally invasive procedures or benefits diagnostic by enabling
sensors with higher resolutions.
By implementing smart materials in a 2PP manufacturing process, multimaterial 4D printed
microrobots to measure changes in pH levels, are fabricated during this thesis. The herein
fabricated microrobots consist of two different polymer materials. An acrylic thermoset
polymer is used to form a rigid backbone, and a stimuli-responsive hydrogel polymer
enables sensing of the pH levels.
The microrobots are intended for localized and qualitative in-situ sensing of regions with
different pH in aqueous environments. Applications for the microrobots are for example
seen in monitoring localized drug-induced effects on cells during drug development. In-situ
monitoring changes of diseased cells to exposure to drugs also helps reduce animal
testing[5]. The microrobots are intended as a cheap and easy-to-use alternative to existing,
more complex systems for localized and real-time sensing.
1.1
Aim of the Project
This project aims to improve the fabrication process of multimaterial microrobots, used to
detect in-situ changes in pH levels.
Two-photon polymerization 2PP, is used to manufacture the multimaterial microrobots
in two sequential steps. In the first step, the rigid backbone is printed, followed by a
subsequent step to print the pH-sensing hydrogel. This stepwise multimaterial printing
approach comes along with multiple challenges regarding materials and processes. Among
other things, the re-alignment between the two printing steps, adhesion between the
multiple materials and to the substrate, or optical transparency of all previously printed
materials to avoid aberration, are difficulties that occur.

This thesis’s objective is to design multimaterial microrobots, focus on optimizing the
fabrication, as well as actuate and characterize the shape change of the stimuli-responsive

1.2
Starting Point
Previous work performed on this topic consisted of validating the anionic hydrogel polymer
as a useful material in 4D printing and characterizing its swelling response to different pH
levels, see Piekarczyk [6]. The smallest feature size when printing with the hydrogel was
defined, together with a general parameter set. An ideal parameter set for all structures
does not exist and every design needs some optimization. Further, it was shown that small
objects show larger swelling and a higher laser power increases the swelling response.
At the same time Vilella [7] designed, fabricated, and validated multimaterial microrobots,
suitable for the detection of changes in pH levels of samples in aqueous environments.
The main dimensions of the microrobots were defined and the first tryouts of multimaterial
printing were performed. The conclusion from this work is, that multimaterial printing of
microrobots is feasible and that microrobots with qualitative pH sensing features are easier
for pH detection. The following work builds up on the findings from those two projects.
The hydrogel material used in those two projects was developed by the Florea Lab team,
at Trinity College Dublin. The size-changing behaviour of the material upon exposure to dif-
ferent pH values is implemented into the microrobots to achieve a 4D printed, multimaterial
microrobot that reacts predominantly to acidic environments.

by defining and narrowing down the focus. The design phase involves the development
and testing of potential solutions to identify the most suitable for the problem at hand. This
ensures that the right ”thing” is designed and that the ”thing” is designed right.

Given the work covered in previous microrobot studies, the research phase was relatively
concise in this thesis, and more focus was put on the design phase. The design phase of
the project was structured into four main stages, each introducing an additional layer of
complexity. This incremental approach helped break down the project into manageable
segments and allowed for agility in response to changes during development. In the initial
stage, various microrobot designs were printed using only the stable backbone material.
In the two subsequent stages, refined designs were printed using both materials (stable
backbone and pH sensing hydrogel), optimized designs, and improved alignment and
multimaterial printing processes. These microrobots were then exposed to various pH
levels to measure their reactions. The final stage involved the optimization of the process
to achieve maximal yield in a batch. The most promising design was then used in the
optical trapping setup, to demonstrate the ability to steer the multimaterial microrobots
within buffer solutions and measure their size change as intended.

2.1
What is pH
The pH value is a measure of the acidic or basic characteristics of an aqueous solution
and stands for the power of hydrogen [9]. Because of its strong water connection, pH
is crucial for the environment and living organisms. In the human body, pH triggers the
activation of enzymes, plays an important role in water absorption, and digestion [1] or can
assist in the detection of cancer, as cancerous cells accumulate acidic metabolic waste.
The pH is defined as the negative decimal logarithm of the hydrogen ion activity αH+[1]:
pH = −logαH+ = log

For very diluted solutions the pH can be directly related to proton concentration[1]:
pH = −log(cH+)

The pH scale ranges from 0-14 whereas a pH value of 7 is regarded as neutral and
corresponds to water, values <7 are acidic and values >7 are basic. A low pH means a
high concentration of H+.
An aqueous solution containing a combination of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or
vice versa) is referred to as a buffer solution and can maintain its pH almost constant while
adding small amounts of strong acid or bases[1].
Other important definitions around pH are the pK values. The pK value equals the pH
value at which the protonated fraction is equal to the deprotonated fraction, and it is a
constant for each ionizable proton[10]. In other words, it describes the degree of ionization
of an acid or base and is a more ”true” value of its strength since adding water to a solution
won’t change the equilibrium [11]. With Ka being the acid dissociation constant, pKa is
simply the −log of this constant, same for Kb as the base disassociation constant[11]:
pKa = −logKa
(2.3)
Those disassociation constants are usually expressed in moles per litre, and a large value
indicates a strong acid or base as it is mostly disassociated into its ions. They are related
to each other through the ion constant for water Kw and are temperature dependent[11]:
Kw = Ka · Kb
(2.4)
2.1.1
State of the Art of Localized pH Measurement
The pH meter is the most used electronic device to measure the pH of a liquid besides
conventional pH paper. The most commonly used technique in a pH meter is based on a
glass electrode, making use of the fact that glass is an ion conductor but not an electron
conductor. The voltage between the two sides of a glass membrane is measured on the
electrodes immersed in the solution [1]. Due to fragile materials, a complex construction
and the need for frequent calibration, they are not ideal for miniaturization [9] and therefore
localized measurements. In the following two paragraphs, an overview of some available
methods on how to achieve localized pH measurements is given.

Microelectrodes and Electrical pH Sensors
Scanning probe microscopy (SPM) is a set of techniques where the pH is measured by
monitoring the electrochemical signal on miniaturized tips of electrodes. The spatial reso-
lution here is limited to the dimension and geometry of the tip[12]. Those sensors are often
potentiometric or voltammetric pH sensors, which measure the electric potential between
the working electrode and the reference electrode, respectively the current generated in a
redox process in the sample [13].
Potentiometric and voltammetric scanning electrochemical microscopy (SECM) normally
make use of an inert or metallic material with a pH-sensitive electrochemical response for
the tip, surrounded by an insulator [12]. For potentiometric SECM sensors, polymer films,
functionalized with pH-sensitive molecules have also been used. Those electrodes are
easy to manufacture but they have a relatively long response time of a few seconds which
leads to calibration issues while measuring pH maps. Voltammetric sensors for SECM are
less common because materials that involve hydrogen ions in their electrochemical reaction
are limited, however, they have a faster response time[12]. Other techniques like scanning
ion-selective electrode technique (SIET) or scanning ion-conductance microscopy (SICM)
are very similar to scanning electrochemical microscopy (SECM) but incorporate an ion-
selective membrane, respectively a quasi-reference counter electrode in the tip.
The high spatial resolution and the wide pH range that can be measured with different
tips are major advantages of this technique. However, a specific setup is needed to
perform such measurements. Song et al. [14] manufactured a potentiometric scanning
electrochemical microscopy electrode with polyaniline-modified Pt to monitor extracellular
pH. The electrode has a diameter of 25.0 µm and was able to measure pH within 10 s and
in a range from pH 4 to pH 8 of stimulated cells.
Ion sensitive field effect transistor (ISFET) pH sensors are sensors based on field effect
transistors and measure the current flow through the drain terminal of the transistor once
immersed into the solution. Advancements in manufacturing made those sensors smaller
and comparatively inexpensive to glass electrodes. When putting ISFET into an array
one can generate an image sensor, which can generate dynamic pH maps, whereas one
ISFET represents one pixel [9].
A differential ISFET based pH sensor for implantable biomedical applications has been
shown by Shawkat et al. [15]. They achieved to measure pH with satisfactory performance
for a range o4, with a total sensor size of 160×80 µm. The two sensing areas have a size
of 10×10 µm respectively 80×80 µm which shows ISFET sensors can be manufactured
in the cellular-size range.
Optical pH Sensors
Optical pH sensors are based on materials sensitive to acids and bases with a specific pKa
or absorbance and emission spectra of light at different wavelengths [13]. Fluorometric
pH sensors are one example of optical pH sensors which allow miniaturization down to
submicron scale and therefore intracellular measurements. In fluorescence microscopy,
dyes with pH-dependent optical properties are administered to the sample and monitored
with fluorescence microscopy [9]. Those dyes can be modified to be attachable to specific
proteins or molecules of a cell. The wavelength or intensity (absolute or relative) of the
fluorescence emission can then be used to determine the pH quantitatively. Marín et al. [16]
reported one example of such a nanosensor based on fluorescence for precise localized
determination of pH within organelles.
Another approach where fluorescence is used is the hydrogel microparticles by Rizzo et al.
[17]. They integrated a ratiometric fluorescent pH sensor consisting of a pH-sensing dye
4D printing of multimaterial polymer microrobots
5
==PAGE:14==
and a reference dye in a thermoresponsive hydrogel system. The synthesized pH sensors
have a diameter of 1.548 µm and are loaded together with cancer cells into a hydrogel
matrix, to observe spatiotemporal pH maps.
With such fluorometric pH sensors, pH maps can be obtained relatively quickly and with
high spatial resolution but the added fluorophore may affect the processes that are studied
[12].
Another approach for optical pH sensors is to immobilize those pH-responsive materials on
either optical fibres or planar sensors. The most common optical pH sensors consist of a
dye embedded in a polymer [13]. Those pH sensors generally have a wide working range
and can be manufactured inexpensively, however, leakage of the pH-sensitive optical
species into the sample limits the lifetime [13]. Also, fluctuations of the light source and
surrounding light can lead to decreased measurement stability [18].
Yin et al. [19] manufactured the microfiber pH sensor displayed in Figure 2.1b, by patterning
a hydrogel onto a tapered optical fibre with a diameter of 30 µm. The hydrogel swells and
shrinks depending on the surrounding pH and causes a change in the refractive index
of the cladding of the fibre. This change can be observed as a red shift of the resonant
wavelength.
A miniaturized holographic pH sensor seen in Figure 2.1a, which reaches comparable

size change affects the grating and therefore the reflected hologram. By measuring the
peak wavelength the pH can be predicted. The sensing area of the sensor has a diameter
of 0.4 mm and is placed in a microfluidic system to monitor bacterial growth.

4D Printing
Four dimensional (4D) printing is the combination of conventional three dimensional (3D)
printing, with stimuli-responsive smart materials, whereas time is referred to as the fourth
dimension. 4D printing is expected to revolutionize industrial manufacturing, with the
aerospace, automotive, clothing, artwork, military, and healthcare industries being among

the users of this technology [26]. The compound annual growth rate of the 4D printing
market in healthcare is expected to reach 32 million USD by 2026 from 9 million USD in
2021 [27].
For future applications in the healthcare industry such as tissue engineering, drug delivery,
or biosensors, 4D printing represents one of the most innovative fabrication techniques.
This is due to the additional dimension which allows for the optimization and adjustment of
bioprints over time, which enables mimicking for example tissue [26], and could lead to
implants that e.g. ”grow” with the patient.
In comparison to 3D printing, 4D printing uses smart materials (see section 2.5) and an
external stimuli, which triggers the structural change of the smart material [26]. A common
definition for 4D printing therefore is: ”4D printing is the evolution in shape, property, and
functionality of a 3D printed structure with time when exposed to an external stimuli” [28].

It is worth mentioning that 4D printing is not confined to one single additive manufacturing
method like fused deposition modeling (FDM), stereolithography (STL), selective laser
sintering (SLS), etc. but can be applied to all methods. Thus many of the advantages and
drawbacks of 4D printing also relate to the different print methods. 4D printing therefore
has the same advantage as 3D printing, which is the ability to manufacture complex 3D
structures in a single manufacturing step in short time and with low costs compared to
traditional methods such as milling [30]. Additive manufacturing allows for new design
freedom, helps save raw material by reducing waste and removes the need for expensive
tools and moulds, thus it allows for customisation and economically attractive small batch
production [31]. The addition of the 4th dimension adds the benefit, that post-processing
can be minimized[32] (e.g. self-assembly) and that the functionality can be extended with
simple stimuli which enables more potential applications.
Drawbacks of additive manufacturing are, that the print size is limited and some printers are
comparatively slow, therefore not well suited for mass production. The main challenges of

4D printing lie within technology, material and design. Depending on the printing technology

2.3.1
Two-Photon Polymerization
2PP stands for two-photon polymerization and refers to an additive manufacturing technol-
ogy to fabricate 3D structures with high resolution. The foundation of 2PP is two-photon
absorption (2PA), the excitation of a molecule to a higher energy level by simultaneously
absorbing two photons of the same or different frequencies [36]. The first use of 2PP is
documented in the 1970s after the invention of lasers. This interestingly pre-dates the
development of STL by over 10 years. It took another 20 years for 2PP printing to receive
considerable attention from researchers [29]. This attention has increased ever since the
first demonstration of 2PP as a fabrication method to manufacture structures with feature
sizes beyond the diffraction limit of the applied laser wavelength [37].
2PP printing with the right parameters can be used to print structures with resolutions
approaching sub-micrometre and up to 100 nm which can not be achieved by most other
additive manufacturing approaches [38, 30]. To reach such a high resolution an ultra-short
pulsed laser is focused into a photopolymer transparent for the laser wavelength applied.
By absorption of the two photons which happens exclusively within the focal point where
the photon density is high enough, the polymerization reaction is initiated [37]. This reaction
stays mostly within the focal point of the laser as the photon density in surrounding areas
is not high enough to initiate a polymerization [29]. This is because the spatial distribution
of the square light intensity is more precise than the linear one[30]. This can be seen in
Figure 2.4a. By scanning this focused laser through the photopolymer 3D structures are
generated.

Photopolymer
Photopolymers are materials that react with a change of properties e.g. polymerization
upon exposure to photons. There is a wide application range of such photopolymers, the
most popular are rapid prototyping, dental implants or biomedical applications e.g. for
UV-curable glues. [30]
A typical photopolymer consists of the following main components [26]:
• Monomer and Oligomer: building blocks of the polymer. The photoinitiator reacts
with the monomer or oligomers to start the polymerisation. It can be either a single
compound or multiple different compounds.[41]
• Photoinitiator: molecules that generate reactive species, in the form of free radical
anions or cations, when exposed to light.[30]
• Additives: used to dissolve the monomers and other parts of the photopolymer in
a solution to get a liquid photopolymer[29] or influence mechanical properties. A
multitude of other functions is possible depending on the added additive.
The characteristics of the cured polymers are controlled mainly by the oligomers, which
have a larger chain structure than monomers.
There are two main photopolymerisation types for photopolymers, one being free-radical
photopolymerization to polymerize acrylates and methacrylate, and cationic photopolymer-
ization to polymerize epoxy and vinyl ether[29]. In the case of 2PA two photons instead of
one need to be absorbed to activate the photoinitiator. The first mentioned free-radical
photopolymerisation has wider application due to easier availability of photoinitiators for dif-

From Idea to Print
To be able to print a structure with 2PP printing, the desired structures need to be prepared
accordingly. The design is initially modelled or conceptualized with a computer aided
design (CAD) software, followed by a translation into a STL model. The STL file describes
the surface of the body with small triangles and removes all the construction data, modelling
history etc. [29]. In the next step, the model is transferred into a language understand-
able for the printer, this is usually done by printer-specific software with corresponding
parameters. In many cases, the file will be G-code for the sliced model. In the case of the
NanoScribe 2PP printer, the DeScribe software slices the STL file and translates it into
GWL-code.
Important parameters during this step are among the following:
• Orientation
• Scaling
• Slicing distance (SD)
• Hatching distance (HD)
• Laser power
• Scanning speed
• Interface depth
The orientation of a part can be important in case there are areas with large overhanging
structures, so the part can be oriented to minimize those. Supporting structures are
generally not used for 2PP printing, as prints are ”supported” by the liquid resin during the
print process and they would be very challenging to remove. The orientation and scaling
can also be useful to print within one print field which increases accuracy and removes the
need for cross-stitching between the print fields to combine structures. The print field is
4D printing of multimaterial polymer microrobots
12
==PAGE:21==
defined as the area which can be illuminated with just the Galvo mirrors. Prints larger than
this require a movement of the stage, which is slower and less precise.
The slicing distance denotes the thickness of the individual printed slices along the Z-
axis. The hatching distance is the distance between parallel lines on the XY plane as
depicted in Figure 2.7. Those lines are formed by individual volumetric pixels also known
as voxels, that get illuminated individually by the laser. Small SD and HD lead to much
longer print times, a higher crosslink-density which affects the mechanical properties, but
also to smoother surfaces [4] and more shape accuracy [44]. Small, voxel-sized errors
are partially smoothed out by the chemistry of the resin [44]. Increased Laser power
or a decreased scanning speed results as well in a higher crosslink density and more
mechanically stable print, but also in a larger voxel size.
For an ideal print and to achieve good mechanical properties, the parameters need to
be balanced to achieve an interlinked structure that is evenly crosslinked while not being
overexposed due to overlapping voxels or too high exposure dose.

The interface depth is the distance below the substrate surface where the printing starts
and helps to ensure a connection between the printed structures and the substrate. Too
deep interface depth can lead to structures that are not fully printed and can maybe not be

necessary.

2.4
Multimaterial Fabrication
Multimaterial fabrication or printing is defined as the manufacturing of structures which
involve the combination of two or more material properties in a single structure [30].
Currently, most 3D printed structures are made out of a single material, in comparison to
nature where many structures are compromised out of multiple materials. Multimaterial
mechanisms, which could be imitated by 4D printing, are seen through natural organisms
in all sizes [45]. One example is muscle where a rigid skeleton and a soft muscle work
together to achieve a movement. Multimaterial printing enables microrobots for example
to facilitate large motions within small spaces due to increased compliance [45].
The multimaterial aspect brings along challenges to the printing process. The main
challenge is the adhesion between the different materials to have good integrity of the final
printed object. This adhesion especially becomes challenging when materials with different
properties e.g. metals and polymers, or organic and inorganic materials are supposed to
be combined [38, 30]. There can also be specific, additional requirements for the materials,
such as that they have to be transparent for a certain wavelength of light to be able to cure
through multiple materials. Postprocessing of the materials needs to be compatible and
there should be no undesired interaction between the materials [45]. All this makes the
choice of materials more challenging. Additionally, depending on the printing technology
and process, a re-alignment step is needed. Each additional step, especially if performed
manually, limits the accuracy of the print and reduces repeatability [46].
Multimaterial fabrication can be achieved with most printing technologies. In the case of
printing with photopolymers two main categories are distinguished:

2.4.2
Varying Print Parameters
Instead of varying the resin during the printing process, one can also change the printing
parameters or add a stimulus during the print to change the properties of sections of the
printed parts.
The most common print parameter that gets adjusted is the laser power. By increasing
the intensity of the laser, the photopolymer reaches higher degrees of crosslinking and
therefore its material properties are locally changed. Xin et al. [49] manufactured trumpet
shaped micro machines (TSMM) which change their size depending on the pH level of the
solution they are in. Due to the specifically designed vortex beam of the laser, the trumpet
shaped micro machines can be fabricated with a single exposure, which also leads to
the intensity decreasing along the exposure axis. This enables very fast manufacturing
and affects the shrinking capabilities locally, where the less exposed parts show larger
shrinking than stronger exposed parts.
Similarly Jin et al. [50] manufactured reconfigurable compound micromachines that change
shape upon exposure to different pH levels. A bi-layered structure with a layer of highly-
and another layer of low-crosslinked hydrogel was generated to print a micro ball which
changes its shape from spherical to a slender cylinder. The same bilayered approach is
used by Wang et al. [51] to print a pH-triggered flytrap.
This approach where the intensity of the laser is varied, is also called grey-tone or gray-
scale lithography [38].
Instead of changing the laser intensity, Hu et al. [52] change the spacing of the scanning
points (e.g. HD) and the direction of scanning. This is a different method, having the same
outcome as the crosslink density.
The wavelength is another promising parameter for achieving multimaterial structures.
Dolinski et al. [53] uses a single resin that can be cured with 530 nm and 470 nm wavelength
light, whereas both wavelengths induce another polymerization reaction that results in
different polymeric networks.
Schwartz et al. [54] did the same with photoinitiators that react to 450 nm and 390 nm
wavelength of light. This method has the advantage that strong multimaterial interfaces

groups. There are two types of pH-responsive hydrogels: cationic and anionic hydrogels.
Cationic hydrogels have side groups such as amine groups and ionization happens below
the pKb. Anionic hydrogels on the other hand have carboxylic or sulfonic acid side
groups and ionization happens above the pKa [60]. In both cases, the ionization leads to
electrostatic repulsion between the ions and other molecules, which results in an expansion
of the crosslinked polymer structure [52] as shown in Figure 2.13. It is important to note,
that the ionization and protonation according to the pH is only the stimulus, however, the
physical change is due to the uptake of water [61].
If cationic hydrogels are immersed in a solution above pKb and anionic hydrogels below
pKa, they also accept protons and are protonated, leading to swelling. However, the
ionization or deionization process generally has a much stronger influence on the size
change compared to protonation or deprotonation. The hydrogel however swells in both
cases compared to the dry size [52].

2.6.1
Optical Microscopy
Besides the eyes which are probably the most important characterization tool, optical
microscopy can be used to visualize structures that are too small to be seen with the naked
eye. Generally microscope consists of two main parts, the objective is a system of lenses
on the object side which produces a magnified picture of the objective, and an ocular
generally two lenses that function as a magnifying glass on the other side [63]. Different
illumination sources and a stage to move the sample can be used to make the sample
better visible.
The optical microscopes are limited in their magnification due to diffraction, which limits the
resolution to about half the wavelength of the used light [64]. The depth of field is rather
low when comparing for example with scanning electron microscope (SEM), especially
at higher magnifications. The main advantage of this technology is that usually no pre-
treatment of the sample is needed and it is a simple technology therefore fast and easy
assessments of samples can be done.
2.6.2
Scanning Electron Microscope
In comparison to optical microscopes, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) uses a beam
of focused electrons instead of light to generate an image of the sample. This beam is
rastered over the area that is to be observed, whereas the higher the magnification the
smaller this area is. The interaction of the beam and specimen is detected as a signal and
the intensity of this signal is converted into a grayscale image for visualization.
The main components of the SEM are [65]:
• Electron gun: source of the e-beam
• Condensor and objective lenses: control the diameter and focus of the beam
• Apertures: controls properties of the beam
• Stage: mounting for specimen position as well as area of beam-specimen interaction
• Detector: detecting the produced signal
• Vacuum system: reduces the amount particles interfering with the beam in the
specimen chamber
• Computer: to display images and control the setup
The beam-specimen interaction happens within a 3D interaction volume, where multiple
signals are generated, see Figure 2.14. Backscattered and secondary electron signals are
among the most common ones to measure. Backscattered electrons are electrons coming
from the source/electron gun, which are diverted due to electron interactions with atoms
in the specimen and scatter back out of the specimen. The amount of backscattering
depends on the specimen density (the higher the atomic number the more backscattering),
thus backscatter detection allows for observation of sample composition.
Secondary electrons (SE) can be further divided into SE1, SE2 and SE3, but they all
originate from a transfer of energy to an atom of the sample, which in turn emits a SE. SE1
originate from the beam-specimen interaction, SE2 from the backscatter electrons (BSE)
to specimen interaction, and SE3 from the BSE-chamber interaction. The SE signal is well
suited to assess the topography of a sample due to the edge effect which makes edges
appear brighter because of the the interaction volume being more exposed to the sample
surface in edges and therefore making it more likely for SE to escape. [65]

In cases where the sample is non-conductive, charging of the material due to the exposure

suited for fast sample characterization. The strengths of SEM lie in the high resolution that
can be reached at about 0.4 nm [66] and its large depth of focus.
2.7
Optical Trapping
As most microrobots don’t have internal energy storage due to their limited size, an external
force is needed to move the microrobots to the desired locations. Optical trapping is one
optical micromanipulation technology that has been broadly used in physics and biology
[67].
By focusing a laser beam with a lens of high numerical aperture, an optical trap is formed.
Dielectric spheres near this focus will experience a force from the scattering of the incident
photons which transfer the momentum to the sphere, depicted in Figure 2.15. If the sphere
is in the centre of the beam, the forces cancel each other out. If the sphere is eccentric
to the beam, it refracts more light in one direction due to the intensity distribution of the
laser (higher intensity in the centre). This refracted light can be thought of as a photon
”water hose” which pushes the sphere towards the centre. Because not all the incident
light colliding with the sphere gets scattered, and the rest gets absorbed by the sphere, it
also experiences a force that pushes it in the direction of the incident light. To avoid this, a
converging lens is introduced to the beam. This leads to a smaller incident spot size on
the sphere but a larger spot size when exiting the sphere, creating momentum opposite
the direction of the light. [68]

To achieve stable trapping in all three dimensions, where the forces along and perpendicular
to the incoming light cancel each other out, an objective with a high numerical aperture
(NA) is needed. Besides the laser, dichroic mirrors and lenses, beam-steering optics to
move the objects, and a camera or optical microscope to image the trapped objects are
part of an optical trapping setup [67].
In comparison to magnetic fields, optical trapping has a much smaller penetration depth
slower speed and lower force output. This makes it more suitable for biotechnology, and
lab/organ-on-a-chip applications where transparent environments are present and the
penetration depth does not need to be high and generally low forces are needed. As the
step of magnetising them can be omitted, it is easier to fabricate optical microrobots. [70]

3.4.2
DefocusFactor
The materials used for this step are all mentioned in subsection 3.1.2.
Snell’s Law provides theoretical values for the DefocusFactor; however, Nanoscribe
advises measuring it manually. By suspending two printed lines between two thicker walls
with a known z-position difference, and measuring the actual z-position difference after
printing, the DefocusFactor can be calculated to compensate for focal-point shifts.
A simple suspended ”bridge” structure was designed in two iterations with SolidWorks and
printed in hydrogel only. An Excel sheet has been used to calculate the true z-distance
from SEM pictures with simple trigonometry, as the SEM images and measurements were
taken under a tilt of 40°. The median of all measured and calculated DefocusFactors was
then used for the following prints with hydrogel. A total of 2×6 structures have been printed
on a single substrate, whereas six of those structures without any signs of overexposure

The alignment process was improved by calibrating the pixel size of the live view from

4.1
Design Process
4.1.1
Requirements
Most of the requirements were defined during the research phase in previous works,
however they keep evolving throughout the design process. A summary of the most
important requirements are listed below:
• Minimum feature size: Must not be smaller than 2.0 µm due to the limited print
properties of the hydrogel.
• Available materials: Designs must be printable in the available materials, considering
material strength, resolution, and small feature adaptability.
• Limited overhang: Due to 2PP limitations, structures should avoid extensive over-
hangs to prevent collapse or floating.
• Overlap of materials: Maintain overlap for mechanical stability without causing
overexposure.
• General size considerations: Structures must balance size with mechanical stability,
printing time and functionality.
• Sensing structures: Microrobot sensing features should be small, as small structures
exhibit the most significant size change with pH variations.
• Qualitative change: Design should facilitate the qualitative detection of pH levels,
allowing for visual observation of induced changes.
• Design space: The structures should stay within the predefined design space from
previous work.
• Dimension of the optical trapping sphere: Sphere size should range within 5-10 µm
to optimize the efficiency of the optical trap.
• Optical trapping spheres: Designs must incorporate at least three spheres to enable
movement in all directions.
• Lightweight design: Maintain low microrobot weight to minimize inertia and ensure
efficient optical trapping.
• Processes: Simplify alignment and other processes to enable more efficient printing
of multimaterial microrobots and to print them in larger quantities.
• Batch size: Maximize microrobot yield within one production batch for increased
production output.
Within the design space resulting from those requirements, different designs of the micro-
robots were developed.
4.1.2
Microrobot Design
The ideation resulted in the creation of eight different designs, which can be categorized
into three main groups.

Qualitative Change
This category of design focuses on the detection of pH with a qualitative sensing feature
that can be easily observed visually.
Having two opposing small sensing elements in the Caliper design enables easier visual
detection. The two opposing callipers ”double” the visual size as their overlap changes
when swelling and shrinking.
The Comb design makes use of the fact that relative changes are often easier to detect.
By having the same small structure printed in both materials close to each other, one
should be able to detect the design getting activated.
In the Puzzle design, it is again a relative change that provides the visual clue for detecting
an acidic environment. With the knob printed in hydrogel and the rest of the design in the
backbone material, the two parts overlap and fit together seamlessly in neutral solutions.
In acidic solutions, the knob shrinks relative to the socket and generates a gap where, for
example, light shines through.
The Hinge design makes use of a lever and amplifies a small movement generated by
the hydrogel sensing part, to a larger movement. Compared to most other designs, the
mechanism of the lever is rather complex which adds uncertainty.
In all those designs, especially the ones that use relative changes between the hydrogel
and backbone material as a detection method such as the Puzzle and the Comb, proper
alignment between both materials is crucial for them to work as intended. Possible
misalignment during printing could lead to the designs being wrongly interpreted and result
in false detection of the surrounding pH.
Reduced Alignment Needs
Therefore the next two designs focus on reducing the alignment need between the two
different materials.
The EasyAlign design reduces the need for precise alignment since the hydrogel and
backbone material are not combined by overlap but rather mechanically interlocked. This
4D printing of multimaterial polymer microrobots
34
==PAGE:43==
way manufacturing tolerances can be ”absorbed”. The sensing part of the EasyAlign design
is based on a previous design which showed promising results, but it can technically be
adapted to many different designs.
A variation of this is the FallThrough design. The concept for easier alignment stays
the same, but the mechanical interlock is supposed to disappear when exposed to acidic
environments, which leads to the sensing part floating away. This can then be easily
detected. However, it will be difficult to find the right dimensions for the interlock to be
resolved at the desired pH concentration and to manufacture this dimension repetitively
enough. The FallThrough design would also be single-use which is a disadvantage
compared to all other designs that can be re-activated.
Small Features
In the last category, the designs make use of the fact that small hydrogel structures tend
to show larger swelling behaviour.
The Snake design consists of a small serpentine between the backbones, which is sup-
posed to stretch and straighten out when the hydrogel shrinks in acidic environments.
For the Fishnet design, two backbones are connected with multiple thin, parallel strings
of the hydrogel. The gaps between the strings are supposed to provide the qualitative
change that can be observed.

Improvement of Processes
4.3.1
Multimaterial Printing Process
Both polymers worked within this thesis are acrylic-based, and are therefore compatible
material-wise. This has been shown in the work of Vilella [7], where no issues of attachment
between the materials were detected, and defects only showed up if the overlap between
the first and second material was too high.
It is important to be able to manufacture those microrobots in large quantities, as it is not
possible to eradicate printing defects and losses of microrobots during their manufacturing
completely. Uncontrollable effects during printing, losses during development, and actua-
tion of the microrobots diminish the number of microrobots and highlight the importance
of being able to produce them in larger quantities. This leads to the conclusion that the
process time and stability used to manufacture a microrobot are important, also to enable
others to reproduce the manufacturing of multimaterial microrobots. One time-consuming
step that can be influenced is the alignment of the structures for the second material. Due
to limited availability of the hydrogel material and generally the costs of photopolymers,
approaches with microfluidic channels for in-situ development as seen in subsection 2.4.1
which allow omitting the alignment are not considered. Those methods use comparatively
large amounts of material which is currently not available for the used hydrogel. Other
approaches that require big changes to the existing set-up are not pursued, for cost rea-
sons as well as because the printer is used for multiple projects which do not allow for
a refitting of the printer. Therefore, improving the process of the manual alignment step,

4.3.2
DefocusFactor
The DefocusFactor is a factor used to account for focal point shifts caused by refractive
index mismatches between the objective, resin, immersion oil, and substrate. Because the
refractive index of the resin is often index-matched for DiLL setups, the DefocusFactor is
more important for Oil Immersion setups, as in this case, when an index mismatch exists.
The first iteration of the structure collapsed most likely because the mechanical properties
of the hydrogel material were overestimated, also due to the relatively large structure
printed in comparison to the microrobots. Increasing the width of the walls and expanding
the supporting surface on the substrate in the second iteration resulted in structures that
could be used.

4.3.4
Increased Batch Size
The increase in batch size within the given print field of the Nanoscribe, achieved by
manually programming the array and employing pixel-calibrated alignment, functions as
intended. There are no merging effects between the individual backbones, and the yield
from one batch, or one alignment process, can be increased by almost 200 %, from 9 to 26
microrobots (see Figure 4.20), with a only slightly more time-intensive alignment process.
This increased yield might be advantageous in later stages of the project, where larger
quantities of microrobots are required for specific experiments, or when applying the
microrobots in-situ. However, in the earlier stages of development, where many steps and
experiments are conducted manually or with minimal automation, the dense packing can
pose challenges in certain experiments. The different orientations and therefore hatching
directions of the microrobots could impact the swelling behaviour of the hydrogel or have
other adverse impacts. This makes the increased batch size only beneficial in specific
situations for now.

5
Conclusion
This thesis explored the manufacturing of multimaterial microrobots, capable of localized
in-situ pH sensing with a high spatial resolution down to cellular sizes. Understanding
pH variations at the cellular scale, ranging from 1 to 200 µm, with high sensitivity and
spatial resolution is important in understanding complex biological processes, particularly
in cancer research where pH plays a crucial role in the cancer’s progress or maybe even
helps assess its severity.
The microrobots, manufactured using two-photon polymerization (2PP) printing and a
smart hydrogel material, offer a fast, accessible, and comparably low-cost alternative to
conventional localized pH monitoring. They consist of a rigid acrylic thermoset polymer
backbone, giving structural integrity to the microrobot, and a pH-responsive, size-changing
hydrogel or smart material for pH sensing. 2PP as a fast, low-cost, and accessible
technology to print precise and complex microstructures while allowing great design
flexibility, is used as a manufacturing technology.
The aim of this project was to evaluate new microrobot designs while focusing on optimiz-
ing the fabrication process. Additionally, the actuation characteristics of the microrobots
were evaluated in two different setups, one of them being an optical trapping setup.
There are many ways to measure the pH locally in different solutions, but there is no ideal
solution that fits every scenario. Many of the available sensors exhibit linear responses
within narrow pH ranges and have different drawbacks that have to be considered for
each application. Besides the pH paper, most methods require specialized equipment
for the quantitative detection of pH values with the desired spatial resolution. Qualitative
measures can usually be done more easily, for example, with fluoroscope microscopy.
The herein-developed microrobots are intended as a comparably simple, cost-efficient, and
accessible alternative to overcome some of those restrictions for localized pH measuring.
The design process for new microrobot designs was carried out in four iterations, each
contributing to a better understanding of the generated designs and used processes while
implementing additional optimizations with each iteration. In the first iteration, the superior
print properties of the IP-L backbone material were displayed, and the groundwork from
the previous thesis regarding the design space could be verified and leveraged.
The second iteration with the first multimaterial microrobots showed the challenges of
printing with the smart material, leading to the exclusion of some designs.
In the third iteration, design changes made to the microrobots highlight the importance
of iterating new designs and adjusting print properties for better results. Additionally, it
could be seen that designs with structures lying upon one another lead to more issues
during printing and also affect visibility when activating the designs in buffer solutions.
Factors such as volume-to-surface ratio and attachment points to backbone structures
were confirmed as crucial considerations for future designs. In the last iteration, the yield
of microrobots could be increased by 200%, making full use of the developed more flexible
alignment process.
The activation tests, performed in 2.4, 7.2, and 12.2 pH environments, were used to
test the pH-responsive behaviour of the hydrogel material and the different designs. The
Caliper design showed the most consistent and robust actuation, with a shrinkage of 20 %
when going from pH 7.2 to pH 2.4, making it the design of choice. Other designs, while
achieving comparable changes in size, showed more variation or worse visibility when
immersed in the buffers than the Caliper design.
For the first time, multimaterial microrobots could also be trapped and actuated in the
4D printing of multimaterial polymer microrobots
57
==PAGE:66==
optical trapping setup. This revealed actuation results comparable to the so-far used micro-
scope setup and proved that optical trapping together with the 2PP printed multimaterial
microrobots is a viable way to measure localized pH. The test however also revealed the
drawbacks of the increased batch size, leading to more variation due to position-depended
alignment accuracy between the hydrogel and the backbone structures.
The Improved process of the pixel-calibrated alignment allows for more flexibility during
the manufacturing of the multimaterial microrobots, enabling increased yield and possibly a
fully automated printing process. The yield could be increased by almost 200 %, however
with some drawbacks as mentioned above.
Generally it will not be able to eliminate all printing defects or the loss of microrobots during
subsequent application steps completely. But with the increased flexibility and yield it is
assumed that microrobots can be manufactured reproducible and in the desired quantities
for further upcoming experiments.
Further work on microrobots could focus on improving the shrinking and swelling re-
sponses of the existing designs by optimizing print parameters even more. With the
harvesting and optical trapping setup soon ready, calibration curves for microrobots with
more gradual pH steps can be recorded, characterizing the maximised size change within
the desired pH range more detailed.
Additionally, efforts could focus on exploring alternative hydrogel materials now that the
design space is outlined and multiple designs are available. Better visibility during actu-
ation, other pH ranges, or better print properties of the material could be objectives of
those explorations. Automation of certain steps in the manufacturing process could also
be pursued to increase efficiency.
In conclusion, this research marks a step forward in the development of multimaterial
microrobots for localized pH sensing. Whereas some challenges with the smart material,
adhesion of microrobots to the substrate, or visibility in some buffer solutions remain, a
proof of concept could be shown, and the insights gained during this work contribute to
the fast-evolving field of 2PP printed multimaterial microrobots.

A
Appendix: ServerMode
A.1
Materials and Methods
The Server Mode is an interface provided by Nanoscribe that enables interaction with the
2PP printer through a scripting language such as Python. This enables the automation of
certain process steps, such as alignment. The following additional software is required
compared to regular printing:
• Python 3.7
• Additional modules: watchdog, numpy, matplotlib, scipy, scikit-image (0.16.1), numpy-
stl
• PyCharm 2023.2.3, Community Edition
Example code from Nanoscribe (available in NanoGuide [44]) has been used as a starting
point. Due to a different setup of the printer here at DTU compared to the sample code
from Nanoscribe, as well as differences in the objective, the code had to be adapted.
In the first step, the sample code from Nanoscribe was read to understand exactly what
was being done. Subsequently, the code was adjusted to the task of printing microrobots.
This involved adapting the process of taking pictures of the printed structures to the DTU
setup, as external software (ZEN lite) is used for image acquisition, unlike the example
code where the Nanoscribe software is used.Two separate Python files were generated:
one containing the code to print the backbones and another to align and print the hydrogel
structures. This was followed by implementing these changes in a first version and testing
it on the printer to verify if the code runs. After debugging, a second version was tested on
the printer to collect data and adjust the parameters used during image recognition.
The code used during the second test can be found in the appendix in section A.4.
A.2
Results
The ServerMode has been successfully implemented and runs on the existing setup,
allowing multiple print jobs to be loaded and executed without the need for interactions.
However, the generated code is only partially operational, and only some backbone and
alignment markers can be printed with it until now. One issue is that the Python code does
not recognize the .log files generated by the printer after finishing a print job. This leads
to the code getting stuck and not generating the next print job. A simple workaround is
manually copy-paste of the log file, which allows the code to continue as intended.
The second and more significant issue is the image recognition of the printed alignment
structures. Unlike the example provided by Nanoscribe, our case involves structures other
than just the alignment marks. These backbone structures impede the recognition of the
alignment markers with the provided code, as illustrated in Figure A.1.

A.3
Discussion
Despite the current limitation of not being able to print multimaterial microrobots, the Server-
Mode has demonstrated its potential as a powerful tool to automate the manufacturing
process of such microrobots on the existing printer. Enabling the consecutive printing of
multiple jobs without manual intervention allows for autonomous 24/7 printer operation.
However, several challenges need to be overcome to achieve this, with the primary hurdle
being the implementation of image recognition for alignment structures. The flexibility
offered by pixel calibrated alignment helps in this context, as it allows the use of all sorts
of structures within the print field and with known distance between each other, to be
detectable as alignment markers.
Considering the right expertise in software and image recognition, along with more time, it is
expected that the ServerMode can be effectively employed to automate the manufacturing
of multimaterial microrobots on this setup. It is worth noting that different 2PP printers
more suited for this task might also be available.

B
Appendix: Actuation Measurement
Improvement
B.1
Materials and Methods
Hydrogel structures are often challenging to detect under the microscope when immersed
in liquids and are frequently washed away when dispensing or removing buffer solutions.
In this section, various approaches to improve either the visibility of hydrogel structures or
their adhesion to the substrate are explored. The following additional material compared
to the actuation tests described in section 3.3 are needed:
• Green food colorant (Tørsleffs)
One approach to enhance visibility involves adding food colorant to the buffer solutions.
For this, 100 µL of buffer solution was mixed with 10 µL of green food colorant. This is
not expected to significantly impact visibility since the swollen hydrogel structures remain
immersed in the colored buffer solution, thereby not enhancing contrast. A second visibility
approach was to adjust the acquisition parameters of the microscope through trial and
error on the same structure. To enhance adhesion to the substrate, a brim was added
to the IP-L backbone structure. The brim increases the surface area on the substrate,
thereby enhancing adhesion.
(a)
(b)
Figure B.1: Output of the NanoWrite slicer for the Caliper backbone design. a) Regular
backbone, with only the bottom part of the spheres for adhesion to the substrate. b) Brim
structures (blue) are added to the design to increase the surface area of the spheres for
better adhesion.
The brim was added in IP-L because previous work by Piekarczyk [6] suggested that the
changing size of hydrogel structures introduces stresses in the contact area, leading to
disassociation. While IP-L does not undergo size changes, stresses due to resin curing
shrinkage can still lead to similar issues. Hence, only limited improvement is expected
from this change. Additionally, modifications in the process of applying and removing
buffers are being tested. By observing how many samples remain on the substrate in their
original positions before and after buffer addition or removal, the most critical process step
can be evaluated and possibly adapted.

Most obvious is the drop in remaining microrobots on the substrate for each design with

B.3
Discussion
There is no clear pattern indicating which design exhibits the best adhesion to the substrate,
nor is there any clear effect of the added brim to be seen. The tendency of microrobots to
wash away with basic solutions may be influenced by several factors. Firstly, the basic
solution is the third applied solution, and with each solution, the likelihood of microrobots
washing away generally increases due to mechanical forces exhibit. Additionally, the basic
solution might alter surface tension, electrostatic and ionic interactions, or other factors
that could contribute to more microrobot loss than observed with acidic buffer solutions.
However, the swelling or shrinking of the hydrogel, as well as the IP-L material, appears to
have a negligible influence. This is seen from the behavior of the EasyAlign design, which
has contact points from the hydrogel to the substrate, and the Puzzle design, which has
the largest IP-L to substrate surface; both designs behave similarly. On the other hand, the
remaining two designs, FallThrough and Caliper, theoretically have identical contact points
to the substrate but behave differently. As a result, the variations between the designs are
assumed to be mostly random.
Therefore, the increase of the IP-L to substrate contact surface can be omitted. In this
case, it has been found that experience and patience are the most helpful in keeping as
many microrobots as possible on the substrate. This observation confirms the decision to
build the optical trapping setup, enabling calibration measurements on a single microrobot
and preventing it from washing away.

C.1
Materials and Methods
Since the hydrogel is mixed batch-wise without quality control, the concept of a hydrogel
test structure emerged. This test structure is intended to be printed with each hydrogel
use, using the same parameters to assess the quality of the hydrogel batch. This can be
helpful to determine the ”shelf-life” of hydrogel batches which is limited due to the presence
of volatile components. The test structure design draws inspiration from a test structure
for STL printing developed by a resin manufacturer (refer to Siraya Tech Test Model [71]). The
structure was designed in SolidWorks and translated into GWL with DeScribe. It comprises
three main sections, each serving a distinct function.
Figure C.1: Final design of the implemented test structure with three main sections. Section
one (green) tests resolution and laser dose adjustment. Section two (red) measures printed
structure shrinkage with the three points of measurement. Section three (blue) challenges
printing limits with arch structures.
Section one assesses the resolution of the printed structures. Ideally, all pins and crosses
are printed, while negative pins and crosses are open. If pins aren’t fully formed or
holes are covered, laser dose adjustments are necessary. This section also provides
insight into printer resolution for the used material. Previous work suggests the hydrogel’s
minimum feature size is around 2 µm. The second section combines two tests: the triangle
structure assesses laser dose (rounding edges suggest dose decrease), and the scale
bar measures material shrinkage after printing and development. In the third section,
varying-sized arches challenge the printer and material. It also tests how the material
reacts to larger accumulations or thin beams.
A parameter sweep of laser power (50% to 100%) and scanning speed (5000 mm/s -
10000 mm/s) was applied to the hydrogel test structure to establish new parameters, after
the replacement of the laser source. Remaining parameters align with hydrogel printing
parameters (refer to section D.1). According information from Nanoscribe similar print
parameters can be expected with the replaced laser unit.
After that, the test structure was included into the hydrogel prints of the microroboters
using an iteration loop to enhance the print length and a few print properties. The following

C.3
Discussion
Despite having the printing parameters for the replaced laser unit defined with the first
design of the test structure, which, in comparison to the microrobots, was relatively volumi-
nous, these parameters proved to work well for the microrobots. The fact that a similar
parameter set was achieved as in previous work with a different test structure suggests
that the laser source is almost identical and that the optimal parameters lie in this vicinity.
Although there was room for improvement, a refinement of the printing parameters with
the updated test structure was not performed. This decision was influenced by the DoE
carried out by Daniel Maher, which rendered further improvement unnecessary as long
as the microrobots could be printed sufficiently well for their intended purpose. It is not
expected that substantial changes to the printing parameters are required to bring out the
largest size change in the desired pH range, and this simple tweaking of the parameters is
unlikely to risk rendering the designs unprintable.
4D printing of multimaterial polymer microrobots

==PAGE:84==
The trend observed in the measured scale bar, indicating more shrinkage of the hydrogel
material with a lower exposure dose, is rather surprising. This type of shrinkage should
not be confused with the contraction that occurs when immersing the hydrogel in different
pH solutions; instead, it results from thermal contraction and a chemical loss of volume
after printing. Consequently, one would anticipate that structures exposed to lower doses
exhibit lower shrinkage due to fewer crosslinks and less heat in the structure. However,
it’s important to note that these results are based on only one scale bar printed with each
parameter combination, which was measured three times. For more compelling evidence,
the test would need to be repeated. Additionally, in this version of the test structure,
the scale bar was integrated onto a base consisting of hydrogel, which could potentially
influence the measurements if, for example, the base sinks in due to too low an exposure
dose, distorting the scale bar. Conducting a repetition of the test with the scale bar printed
directly on the substrate would yield more meaningful results.
The unexpected overexposure of the test structure, as illustrated in Figure C.5, when
incorporated into the hydrogel print jobs for microrobots was surprising, especially given
that the microrobots printed in the same print job and with the same parameters turned out
fine. Despite having used the test structure to define the print parameters, the problem was
first assumed within the design of the test structure itself. The large volume of the structure
appeared to promote the appearance of bubbles, and once bubbles formed, they acted
like a chain reaction, amplifying the effects of overexposure. To address this, reducing the
volume of the test structure seemed like a reasonable approach to minimize the problem
while also achieving faster print times. However, as depicted in Figure C.6, this adjustment
only brought slight improvement. The next assumption regarding the cause of such
variations was the hydrogel material itself, as it is known to contain volatile components
that could influence print properties. As many measures to minimize the evaporation of
these components have already been implemented and no noticeable correlation between
the age of the hydrogel batch and overexposure was seen, segregation of the compounds
was considered. Introducing a vortex step before each dispensing of the hydrogel on
the substrate provided some further improvement. It is assumed that during the storage
of the resin, the components segregate to some extent, leading to an inhomogeneous
hydrogel. The mixing that occurs during dispensing with the pipette might introduces some
partial homogenization, explaining why some structures printed well and others did not.
Additionally, the larger volumes of the structure is the more likely they are to encounter
inhomogeneous sections in the resin, whereas the smaller microrobots have a smaller risk
for that to happen.
Adding an additional vortex step however does not get rid of the issue completely, but
enough to enable consistent enough printing of the micrororobots. It however highlights
that the hydrogel material is still in its infancy and to achieve a stable printing process for
larger batches of microrobots it is important to know the material better.
Incorporating a test structure to assess the print properties of a material seems helpful
and important, especially when introducing a new or less familiar material. While many
features of the test structure were primarily utilized to determine print parameters for optimal
resolution in the first step, a modified version of the test structure could prove beneficial for
quality control in later stages. The test structure doesn’t significantly increase the workload
or print time but provides a way for better detecting irregularities and unforeseen influences
in the hydrogel printing process.

Step-by-step guide to hydrogel printing on the Nanoscribe 
 
Resin preparation - Make sure the white light in the room is off (only yellow light is on)! 
1) Select one brown bottle with photoinitiator (PI, BAPO) and check the labelled weight (notify 
Ada/Marcin if only 2 bottles remain). 
2) Calculate the necessary volume of sartomer (SR9035) and acrylic acid (AA): 
 
SR9035 (µL) = PI (mg) * 105 
 
AA (µL) = PI (mg) * 19.5  
3) Using the 1 mL Eppendorf pipette with a cut tip, add SR9035 to the PI bottle. 
4) Cut two pieces of parafilm suitable for small bottle lids. 
5) Using the 200 µL Eppendorf pipette, add AA to the PI bottle. Close both the AA bottle and 
the resin mix bottle as soon as possible in order to minimize AA evaporation, and wrap 
them with parafilm.  
6) Vortex the resin well to ensure mixing. 
7) Sonicate the resin (5 min) to ensure that the PI dissolves. 
8) Vortex the resin again after sonication, then let it rest until any bubbles inside disappear. 
 
(For hydrogel printing): 
1) Open NanoWrite software, wait for initialization and accept prompts as needed. 
2) Check laser calibration: click on the white miniscript box, type recalibrate, then click submit 
to check MPS and AOM curve. Record user, configuration and MPS in the Nanoscribe 
Logbook. 
3) Load job file. 
4) Press Exchange holder and select the correct holder and the position of your coverslip 
(corresponding to the TOP view in the holder!). 
 
5) Make sure that during this step the white light in the room is off (only yellow light is on)! 
Press OPEN on Nanoscribe table controller and lift the lid all the way up. Carefully lift piezo 
flap to fully upright position and check if the correct objective (63x) is mounted and that the 
correct position is selected from the microscope controller. If not, unmount / clean existing 
objective and mount the desired one in the correct position. 
6) Carefully lower sample stage on metal support (loading position). 
 
7) Place Nanoscribe holder BOTTOM side up 
8) Clean round substrate (if needed) and place on holder (“empty” side visible, if doing multi-
material printing); use tape to keep it in place. 
9) Place immersion oil on the bottom and flip to have the TOP side up. 
10) Add two pieces of tape on the top on the coverslip, careful not to damage existing structures 
(if any). 
11) Place a thin square coverslip on the tape supports to create the printing cell. 
12) Using the 200 µL Eppendorf pipette, add 40 μL of hydrogel resist at the edge of the printing 
cell and wait for it to infiltrate. 
13) Carefully insert holder (TOP side up) in the Nanoscribe stage and push until you hear a click. 
14) Lower stage in position and fix with metal support, then close the lid.

Pixel alignment process with a known distance
1. Prepare print: Insert the substrate with the resin added to it. On the NanoWrite
window, turn on Transmitted illumination. Open the ZEN software and turn on the
microscope camera. Approach the sample and find the interface (either automatically
or manually).
2. Locating the Print Origin.
3. Calibrate the galvo-scanning position: Move the stage away from the backbone
strucutes. On the NanoWrite window in the GWL mini script, type in ”manualcontrol”
and press Submit. On the new window that pops up, click on ”Shutter closed” to open
the laser shutter and increase set the Laser power until either the laser or printed
structures are visible (approx. around 10-15 % laser power). In the ZEN software,
right click on the Live window and select ”Draw annotation scale bar” and drag one
of the edges of the scale bar to align with the bright spot/printed structure. Close
the laser shutter and the Manual Control window, and note the pixel values of the
calibrated galvo origin in the excel sheet.
4. Rotate the print plane: Click on the Camera tab in NanoWrite and on the Marker tab
set M0 design x,y as (0,0); move the stage to align the scale bar with the alignment
marker in corresponding to the origin, use the ”FindInterfaceAt 0” command at this
location and click ”Get Position” to record the real x,y coordinates. Repeat the same
procedure for the M1 position; set M1 design x,y as (1,0); move stage to align the
second alignment marker along the X-axis with the scale bar, ”FindInterfaceAt 0” and
click ”Get Position” to record the real x,y coordinates.
5. Apply the transformation: Make sure ”Apply to: All” is selected Click on Transformation
button to activate the new coordinate system To check the transformation print a
alignment marker somewhere outside of the field. It should be aligned with the
alignment marker of the BB now.
6. Calculate the pixel calibration: Move the stage to a frame where all three crosshairs
can be seen. Record the pixel position of all three alignment markers in the excel
sheet. Make sure to assign the correct values (e.g. the alignment marker on the
origin form M(0,0), the alignment marker on the X-axis for M(1,0),..) and do NOT
move the stag during this step! The current pixel of the mouse can be seen in the
lower bar in the ZEN software. Hint: The used alignment markers contain a full arrow
along the X-axis and a ”half-arrow” along the Y-Axis for easy distinction.
7. Move the scale bar: Update the known distance between the alignment markers
if necessary (usually 120 µm). Insert the know distance from the print start of the
hydrogel structures to the alignment marker to be used from the backbone structures
(with a print start in the center and using the origin alignment marker this would be
X = -60 and Y = -60). The excel calculates the new pixel position of the scale bar.
Move the scale bar to this position with drag and drop.
