Abstract
The demand for organically farmed products is on the rise, imposing increased produc-
tivity requirements for farmers around the world.
The GALIRUMI project aims to
respond to that issue by developing an autonomous agricultural platform which will
remove weeds of the types Rumex obtusifolius and Rumex crispus through electric and
laser weeding. This thesis develops the detection algorithm needed to localize these
weeds. In preparation for that, the RumexWeeds robotic vision dataset was collected by
Güldenring et al. and is used in this work to develop the vision-based detection of the
aforementioned target weeds. A dataset analysis is carried out with the dataset man-
agement tool FiftyOne, after which several off-the-shelf, state-of-the-art object detectors
are trained on the dataset. Afterwards, some experiments analyzing the potential of new
approaches but also well-known configuration parameters are conducted and a final con-
figuration for the YOLOR-CSP detector is established. The configuration parameters
are a result of a parameter sweep facilitated by the machine learning framework Weights
and Biases. A ROS software architecture is developed and partially implemented to
pay tribute to the robotic dataset, delivering an easy-to-use development environment
for the RumexWeeds dataset. Other approaches in pursuit of a better detection perfor-
mance are lined out, which are to integrate the temporal relationships in the dataset.
The detection algorithm is implemented on an NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX embedded
GPU through TensorRT and achieves an mAP.5:.05:.95 of 0.3013 at a frame rate of 10 FPS,
making it ready for deployment on the envisioned agricultural robot of the GALIRUMI
project.
Keywords: object detection, object tracking, agricultural robotics, weed control,
YOLOR, machine vision, computer vision

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In Denmark, turnover with organically farmed products in retail shops has almost
quadrupled in the last 20 years, from about 150 000 tons in 2003 to about 600 000
tons in 2021 [Sta21]. This indicates an increased demand from customers for these prod-
ucts, especially with an increased awareness of pesticide and herbicide use in agriculture.
With the world’s population steadily increasing, thus creating a more densely populated
world, the requirements to players in the agricultural field regarding productivity and
eﬀiciency are assumed to increase. Most often, the response to this is an increased usage
of herbicides. The usage of herbicides has more than doubled in the last 30 years, with
a worldwide usage of about 900 000 tons in 1990 to around 2 220 000 tons in 2019
[FAO21].
Although effective in general, there are several risks and shortcomings that come
with herbicide usage such as accidentally applying substances to non-target weeds, de-
velopment of resistances or movement of herbicide particles to undesired areas caused
by air turbulences or improperly calibrated equipment [JVT14]. Especially when using
non-selective herbicides, manual application is often required [JVT14] and is therefore
a labor- and by that cost-intensive process. This also applies to a limited amount to
mechanical weed removal.
The GALIRUMI project is partly funded by the European Commission (EC) and the
European Agency for the Space Programme (EUSPA) and aims to tackle these problems
by developing a robot eliminating the weeds Rumex obtusifolius and Rumex crispus
autonomously. It is envisioned to use either a laser or a electric weeding mechanism,
making the use of herbicides for these weeds obsolete and heightening the attractiveness
of organic farming. The robot will operate autonomously and make use of cameras and
other sensors to recognize these plants, which then subsequently get treated with the
aforementioned tools.
This thesis develops the recognition software needed to detect the target weeds Rumex
obtusifolius and Rumex crispus. An extensive survey of the current literature regarding
object detection is carried out. After an intensive analysis of the dataset obtained by
Güldenring et al. [GEN21], numerous off-the-shelf object detectors are trained and sub-
sequently, a baseline detector is selected based on quantitative and qualitative metrics.
Different experiments are carried out in pursuit of a better detector performance. To-
gether with the result of these experiments, the detector is deployed on an embedded
device. An easy-to-setup development and simulation environment is created to promote
and boost community acceptance of the dataset. The robotic nature of the dataset is

CHAPTER 2
Problem description
This thesis tackles the problem of detecting the weed species Rumex obtusifolius and
Rumex crispus in an agricultural context, namely in dairy grasslands. The object de-
tector is to be implemented on a mobile agricultural platform with limited computing
resources.

(b) Robot used by Güldenring

An RGB camera is mounted on the platform, providing the input for the object
detector. Using that camera, the dataset RumexWeeds has been collected by Güldenring
[Gül22]. Figure 2.1 depicts a conceptual drawing of the camera view and a picture of
the robot used by Güldenring to collect the dataset which is to be used to train and
evaluate object detectors. The detector outputs bounding boxes which will serve as a
rough localization of the weeds. The localization is then further used by the platform
to eliminate these. This requires a detection algorithm that discovers most of the weeds
present, but doesn’t produce too many false alarms, as these decrease the range of the
platform and might harm other, non-target plants. Lastly, the final algorithm is to be
deployed on an embedded platform, where design choices considering trade-offs are to
be made.

CHAPTER 3
Background knowledge
This chapter will give brief brush-ups on the relevant topics for this thesis. It does not
aim to give a complete introduction, as these topics are considered to be prerequisites
for the targeted audience.
3.1
Object detection
Object detection is the process of determining the presence of one or several objects in an
image, obtaining their membership to a certain category of an object and localizing them
[Zou+19; Koš20; AFG21]. In this thesis, object detection is referred to as a function
f : X →P
where X is an input image of shape (C, H, W), C being the number of color channels,
H and W being the height and width of the input image respectively. P is an output
matrix containing predictions and is of shape (N, 6), with the six columns reflecting
the dimensions of the N predicted bounding boxes, individual confidence and class
membership respectively.
In order to measure the performance of object detectors, several metrics are com-
monly used in the field. Those include:
• Intersection over Union (IoU): An illustration is depicted in Figure 3.1. Given
two bounding boxes that overlap to some extent, it is the ratio of the overlapping
area (purple rectangle) compared to the entire area covered by both bounding
boxes (hatched area).
==PAGE:16==

• Mean Average Precision (mAP)1 The commonly used Microsoft Common
Objects in Context (COCO) dataset [Lin+15] uses mAP @ .5:.05:.95 as a metric for
detectors. In general, mAP is calculated by first calculating the IoUs between each
ground truth-prediction pair within a class in each image. Then, the predictions
are matched to a ground truth object, where more confident predictions have
priority over lower-ranking predictions. This will generate a True Positive (TP),
False Positive (FP) and False Negative (FN) classification for each prediction,
which then enables counting those and subsequently the calculation of precision
p =
tp
tp+fp and recall r =
tp
tp+fn. The former is done for a range of IoU thresholds,
meaning that only predictions with a minimum IoU of [0.5, 0.55, ..., 0.95] are
taken into account. The generated precision-recall points are then interpolated in
a monotonous, declining manner. The area under this curve is the mAP.
• Frames per Second (FPS): This metric measures for how many pictures the
detector can infer predictions in a time frame of one second.
Figure 3.1: Illustration of IoU
The topic of object detection has gained popularity in recent years, as can be seen
in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2 shows a plot of data retrieved from arXiv, depicting the number of publications
containing the substring ”object detection” on the y-axis over time on the x-axis. The
development of the numbers can partially be linked to the historical development in
object detection. While hand-engineered features were widely used in object detection
prior to 2014 and their potential has been exhausted, the rise of Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNNs) has led to a revival of interest in the field, thanks to the performance
improvement of algorithms that make use of CNNs [Zou+19].
Present-day object detectors can roughly be divided into two categories: one-stage
detectors and two-stage detectors, where the latter refers to architectures in which the
first stage generates regions of interests that are then passed to the second stage, where
classification and bounding box regression takes place [SI18]. The former describes ar-
chitectures in which there is an end-to-end approach, meaning the CNN directly predicts
bounding boxes, confidences and categories.

3.2
Deep Learning
As mentioned previously, present-day object detectors are mainly based on neural net-
works that make use of deep learning. This section will introduce the basic terminology
needed when dealing with deep-learning based object detectors.
3.2.1
Convolutional Neural Networks
First and foremost, the backbones used in object detectors are nowadays based on CNNs.
These perform matrix dot-products on the input with shape (C, H, W) with a filter of
size (D, S, S) with S being the spatial dimension of the filter (height and width) and
2Data obtained from the arXiv dataset from Kaggle, containing the metadata of arXiv publications
==PAGE:18==
3.3 Software frameworks for robots
7
D being the number of filters, which is also the number of channels of the convolution
result, also called feature map. The process of doing this operation is called a forward
pass. The spatial dimensions of the result depend on the filter spatial dimensions, the
input spatial dimensions and the so-called stride s, which denotes the distance between
two successive multiplications. The values of the filter are called weights.
3.2.2
Loss functions
In supervised deep learning, one usually has a dataset available with inputs X and targets
Y , in case of object detection these would be the bounding boxes and the corresponding
classes. The loss function then measures how much the predictions from the network ˆY
differ from the actual targets Y . This is needed to optimize the weights of the filters. A
typical loss function is the Sum Squared Error (SSE) loss.
3.2.3
Optimizers
When doing the forward pass, deep learning frameworks keep track of the arithmetic op-
erations carried out. Together with the loss function, an optimizer can adjust the weights
of the filters according to how much each operation contributed to the corresponding
loss of a target Yi. This step is called backpropagation which uses an estimate of the
gradient of the weights w.r.t. the loss function and there are several types of optimizers
that then will attempt to minimize the loss function. One commonly used optimizer is
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD): Given a batch of inputs, forward pass the batch,
compute the gradient (stochastic because it has a mean and variance caused by accumu-
lating a batch of forward passes and not just one) and go into the negative direction of
the gradient, as the gradient indicates the direction in which a function value is growing
the most. There are some hyperparameters to SGD that can be used to smooth out the
descent to prevent overshooting and resting in local minima. In the following, most of
the object detectors use this SGD algorithm together with said hyperparameters.
3.3
Software frameworks for robots
Robot Operating System (ROS) is widely used as the base for software architectures
concerning robotic platforms.3 It enables an eﬀicient way of handling data, distributing
workloads (e.g. outsourcing the detection mechanism to a remote host) and communicat-
ing with additional hardware attached to a robot such as cameras, Light Detection and
Rangings (LiDARs) or other actors / I/O. In a few words, ROS-powered platforms con-
sist of one or multiple machines executing nodes that implement algorithms and such.
These nodes communicate through topics over the network in a Publisher-Subscriber

CHAPTER 4
Related Work
This chapter will give an overview of the related work about object detectors and agri-
cultural datasets.
4.1
Object detectors
The following section gives an insight to the working principle of present-day object
detectors, including their predecessors, to integrate the technical development. The first
subsection is about object detectors from the You Only Look Once (YOLO) family, the
second subsection is about other object detectors that have been used in this thesis.
4.1.1
Traditional YOLO detectors
This subsection is divided into two parts, where the first part covers traditional YOLO ob-
ject detectors and the second covers non-traditional YOLO object detectors. Traditional
in this thesis means that the object detectors are direct successors of the original YOLO
object detector introduced by Redmon et. al [Red+16]. Those include YOLO [Red+16],
YOLOv2 [RF16], YOLOv3 [RF18], YOLOv4 [BWL20] and Scaled-YOLOv4 [WBL21].
Object detectors that have a bigger difference in their architecture like YOLOX [Ge+21]
or YOLOR [WYL21] are considered as non-traditional in this thesis.
4.1.1.1
YOLO
The original YOLO object detector was released by Redmon et al. in 2016 and was the
first deep-learning based one-stage object detector [Red+16; Zou+19].
The backbone is a modified GoogLeNet [Sze+14] image classification network without
the inception modules. Those are replaced by 1x1 convolutional layers to reduce the
channel dimension and 3x3 convolutional layers [Red+16]. The YOLO head, which is
responsible for bounding box regression and classification discretizes the input image
into a S × S grid where each grid cell gets assigned B bounding box predictions and C
class probabilities, so that the class membership is on a per-cell basis. Therefore, the
output tensor of this detector is of shape S ×S ×(5∗B +C). Redmon et al. use in their
work S = 9, B = 2, C = 20 [Red+16]. Each bounding box consists of the location of the
center x, y, the width and the height of the bounding box w, h and the confidence score

for the box, which is the estimated IoU between the ground truth and the predicted box

4.1.1.2
YOLOv2
The successor to YOLO, YOLOv2, was again developed by Redmon and Farhadi and
was too released in 2016 [RF16]. Changes compared to YOLO include the new back-
bone Darknet-19, introduction of anchor boxes, as well as multiscale detection and the
replacement of Dropout layers by batch normalization layers.
==PAGE:22==
4.1 Object detectors
11
Given an input image of size 416 × 416, the object detector returns a tensor of size
S × S × (5B + C) as in [Red+16], but with S = 13, B = 5 which corresponds to more
than a thousand bounding box predictions per image. These bounding box coordinates
are predicted as offsets from pre-determined, so-called anchor boxes. Anchor boxes are
dimension priors (w, h) and are found by running a clustering algorithm like k-means on
the training split of a dataset, e.g. on Pascal VOC or MS COCO [RF16]. The bounding
box regression is then done by the following formulas [RF16]:
bx = σ(tx) + cx
(4.1)
by = σ(ty) + cy
(4.2)
bw = pwetw
(4.3)
bh = pheth
(4.4)
In Equation 4.4, bx, by, bw, bh correspond to the center location and the width and the
height of the bounding box accordingly. pw, ph are the bounding box priors (the anchor
boxes), cx, cy is the grid-cell offset from the top-left corner of the image and tx, ty, tw, th
are the predictions from the detector, where tx, ty are constrained between 0 and 1 and
are relative to the grid cell. Redmon and Farhadi argue that by constraining these values,
the representation is easier to learn by the network [RF16] and stabilizes the learning
process.
The backbone Darknet-19 contains 19 convolutional layers and produces a 13 ×
13 × 2048 feature map. With YOLOv2, the idea of multiscale detection was introduced,
meaning that incorporating feature maps from shallower layers of the network encourages
detection across various object sizes. In Darknet-19, a pass-through layer enables the
concatenation of a 26 × 26 × 512 feature map from a shallower layer to a 13 × 13 × 2048
feature map to be integrated with a feature map of a complete forward pass [RF16].
According to Redmon and Farhadi, this particularly enables the detection of smaller-
size objects [RF16]. This can be explained by counteracting the maxpool layers, as they
will discard some information in order to lower the spatial dimensionality of the network.
The learning process for the backbone on the image classification task is almost the
same as in [Red+16], with the difference that after the initial pre-training, a fine-tuning
on 448×448 images is done for 10 epochs which is then followed by a fine-tuning for the
object detection task [RF16]. Another technique introduced in YOLOv2 is the so-called
multiscale training, where the network input is resized to multiples of 32 with 320 × 320
being the minimum input size and 608×608 the maximum [RF16]. As stated by Redmon
and Farhadi, this too increases detection accuracy across different scales [RF16]. The
hyperparameters for both tasks are listed in Table 4.2. Redmon and Farhadi report that
YOLOv2 trained on Pascal VOC 2007+2012 train split achieves between 69.0 and 78.6
mAP (Pascal VOC protocol) on VOC 2007 test at 91-40 FPS and an input size of 288 to
544 [RF16]. They report similar results on VOC 2012 test. On MS COCO test-dev2015,
they achieve 21.6 mAP (COCO protocol, mAP.5:.05:95) with notably better results with
bigger objects (APL) [RF16].

4.1.1.3
YOLOv3
After nearly two years of hiatus, Redmon and Farhadi released another version in 2018
named YOLOv3. Major changes include multi-label classification, enhanced prediction
across scales and a new backbone Darknet-53 [RF18].
While the bounding box prediction method hasn’t changed drastically besides the
introduction of NMS to YOLOv3 compared to [RF18], Redmon and Farhadi brought
multi-label classification to YOLOv3. One reason they state for that is the existence of
more hierarchical datasets like OpenImages [RF18], where for example a cat would be a
class label itself, but also be a member of the animal class. The softmax activation func-
tion, which is usually used for classification layers to obtain probabilities, is according
to Redmon and Farhadi not suitable for this kind of data as it assumes only single-class
memberships and because of that, they replaced this activation function with a logistic
activation function [RF18]. Redmon and Farhadi use cross-entropy loss instead of SSE
loss compared to [Red+16].
The amount of bounding box priors, or anchor boxes, was lifted to 9 compared
to YOLOv2, however, Redmon and Farhadi do not deliver a reason for that number
and state that this was an arbitrary choice [RF18]. The bounding box priors are pre-
determined by clustering the bounding boxes on the COCO dataset.
==PAGE:24==
4.1 Object detectors
13
Similar to YOLOv2, YOLOv3 incorporates features from different scales. However,
the mechanism of doing that changes in YOLOv3 and is much more inspired from Feature
Pyramid Networks (FPNs) [RF18]. Redmon and Farhadi use three scales, where feature
maps from deeper layers of the backbone get upsampled and concatenated with feature
maps from shallower layers to combine high-resolution features with low-resolution fea-
tures [RF18]. Each scale gets assigned a part of the bounding box priors which then in
turn are used for predictions at each scale, so bounding box predictions are done three
times for a picture and are combined at the end to output a final tensor [RF18]. The
motivations behind this are the same as in [RF16].
The backbone Darknet-53 has 53 convolutional layers and also incorporates resid-
ual connections [RF18], inspired by ResNet where He et al. hypothesize that residual
(bypass) connections in image recognition networks (backbones) are easier to optimize
[He+15].
While Darknet-53 with 18.7 billion operations in a forward pass is signifi-
cantly larger than Darknet-19 with 7.29 billion operations, it is more accurate with 77.2
% top-1-accuracy on ImageNet compared to 74.1 %. It is on par with ResNet-152, which
achieves 77.6% top-1 accuracy [RF18] respectively 80.6% top-1-accuracy as reported in
[He+15]. However, ResNet-152 needs 29.4 billion operations in a forward pass and thus
has, with 37 FPS compared to 78 FPS for Darknet-53, approximately half the frame rate
of Darknet-53 [RF18]. In conclusion, Redmon and Farhadi use the Graphics Processing
Unit (GPU) more eﬀiciently than He et al..
As [RF18] is a tech report, Redmon and Farhadi do not mention details about the
training process, but the wording lets one assume that the training process is similar to
[RF16].
Redmon et al. report results in the COCO format, so it is assumed that the testing
was done on the COCO dataset. Compared to YOLOv2, they achieve an increase of 11.4
in mAP.5:.05:.95 with YOLOv3 with an input size of 608 × 608 [RF18]. Especially with
higher IoU thresholds, YOLOv3 performs worse, which is an indicator for localization
problems as higher IoU thresholds require better localization performance. For YOLOv3-
608, they report an inference time of 51 ms which corresponds to 19 FPS [RF18], which
is lower than the 30 FPS real-time threshold commonly used in the computer vision
community. However, when decreasing the input size to 320 × 320, YOLOv3 can reach
up to 45 FPS at an mAP.5 of 51.5 on the COCO dataset [RF18].
4.1.1.4
YOLOv4
YOLOv4 marks another milestone in the YOLO family. While YOLOv3 incorporated
only minor but effective changes, YOLOv4 inaugurated major changes throughout the
network and the training process. The object detector was developed in 2020 by Bochkov-
skiy et al., marking a change of ownership, as Redmon and Farhadi weren’t involved in
the development of YOLOv4 [BWL20]. Changes include the new backbone CSPDark-
net53, scale-incorporating mechanisms like Spatial Pyramid Pooling (SPP) and Path
Aggregation Network (PAN) as well as extensive training and inference enhancement
==PAGE:25==
4.1 Object detectors
14
techniques.
Backbone
Bochkovskiy et al. present a modified version of the YOLOv3 backbone,
Darknet53 [RF18]. The modifications include using the Mish activation function [Mis20],
incorporating the Cross-stage partial connections (CSP) mechanism developed by Wang
et al. [Wan+19] and using multi-input weighted residual connections better known as
BiFPN established by Tan et al. [TPL20].
In addition to the self-regularizing behavior and the smooth loss function surface
of Mish compared to e.g.
ReLU that make networks using Mish easier to optimize
[Mis20], both Misra and Bochkovskiy et al. deliver experimentally obtained results that
show that Mish increases the accuracy of image classification and object detection tasks
[Mis20; BWL20]. By using Mish, Bochkovskiy et al. report an increase of 0.6 % in
mAP.5:.05:.95 using the CSPDarknet53-PANet-SPP backbone [BWL20].
CSP is an add-on for CNN backbones which aims to reduce the redundant compu-
tation of gradients to enhance the learning and optimization process [Wan+19]. CSP
also splits feature maps channel-wise, where the first split will be propagated through a
CNN building block and the second split will be fusioned with the output feature map
of the building block, thus reducing computation [Wan+19].
In YOLOv4, Bochkovskiy et al. report that PANet is used as a path aggregation
network in CSPDarknet53 [BWL20]. They also include so-called Multi-input weighted
residual connections (MiWRC), as a so-called Bag of Specials (BoS), which are add-ons
that significantly increase the accuracy of the detector without adding an unreason-
able amount of computation to the inference process [BWL20]. Using both PANet and
MiWRC results in implementing a Bidirectional Feature Pyramid Network (BiFPN).
BiFPN is a weighted path-aggregation mechanism proposed by Tan et al.
to incor-
porate features in a repeated top-down and bottom-up approach from several scales
[TPL20]. Tan et al. state the hypothesis that features from unequal scales have unequal
importance, thus it is imperative to introduce learnable weights to rate the importance
of the features accordingly when fusing them together [TPL20]. BiFPN can lift the
mAP.5:.05:.95 on COCO by 2.1 % compared to a repeated top-down FPN, while using
fewer parameters and Floating Point Operations (FLOPs) than FPN [TPL20].
Training and inference improvements
To enhance the training process, Bochkovskiy
et al. present various techniques that they call Bag of Freebies (BoF) as these have a
positive effect on mAP but do not increase the inference time of the network. This is
due to the techniques only being used during training (offline) and not during inference
(online) [BWL20]. Bochkovskiy et al. distinguish between BoFs used for the backbone
and the detector. The BoFs for the backbone include [BWL20]:

CutMix data augmentation developed by Yun et al. takes two images xA, xB as well
as an image patch B from xB and pastes it in xA such that a new picture ˜x is created
with an adjusted label ˜y = λyA + (1 −λyB), where yA, yB are the labels of image xA, xB
respectively and λ is sampled from the uniform distribution (0, 1) [Yun+19]. CutMix
stabilizes training and reduces overfitting [Yun+19] and improves the detection of object
outside their normal context [BWL20], e.g. detecting a plant on a sidewalk as opposed
to a plant on a field. CutMix implies multi-label classification. An example of CutMix
is illustrated in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Example of CutMix usage on the RumexWeeds dataset
Mosaic data augmentation is similar to CutMix data augmentation and was devel-
oped by Bochkovskiy et al. [BWL20]. Instead of mixing two images together, Mosaic
uses four images, as depicted in Figure 4.2. When Mosaic or CutMix is used in object
detection, the bounding boxes are transformed accordingly instead of having a ratio of
classes for the augmented image.
DropBlock is a regularization method and operates on the feature maps as opposed
to the data augmentation methods that operate on the raw images. DropBlock takes a

feature map and randomly selects cells, a so-called mask [GLL18]. For each selected cell,
DropBlock sets the feature map to zero in an area of S ×S around the selected cell, and
the remaining features are normalized [GLL18]. According to Ghiasi et al., this acts as
occluding certain parts of the image, thus removing semantic information, which in turn

Class label smoothing is mainly used to improve multi-class problems [MKH20],

• Complete IoU (CIoU) loss function

CIoU loss was proposed in 2019 by Zheng et al. to combat convergence problems and
inaccurate results of bounding box regression when using basic IoU loss or Generalized
==PAGE:28==
4.1 Object detectors
17
IoU (GIoU) loss [Zhe+19]. While IoU loss only considers the amount of overlap between
predicted bounding box and ground truth bounding box, CIoU loss also incorporates
the center point distance between the ground truth and predicted bounding box as well
as the difference in aspect ratio [Zhe+19]. Both Zheng et al. and Bochkovskiy et al.
report that CIoU loss converges faster and solves the regression problem more accurately
[Zhe+19; BWL20]. When using CIoU loss instead of Mean Square Error (MSE) loss,
Bochkovskiy et al. report an increase of 1.9% in mAP.5:.05:.95 in their ablation studies
conducted on the CSPResNeXt50-PANet-SPP backbone [BWL20].
Bochkovskiy et al. adopt their own modification of Cross-Iteration Batch Normal-
ization (CBN) proposed by Yao et al.. CBN stores the statistics (mean, variance) of
k previous iterations and uses them again when calculating the mean and the variance
of the current mini-batch, effectively enlarging the mini-batch size eﬀiciently [Yao+21].
CmBN modifies it such that the boundaries of a complete batch consisting of n mini-
batches are respected: Instead of using the last k previous iterations, the amount of
stored statistics increases with every iteration [BWL20]. This means that in the first
mini-batch of n, only the statistics of the first-mini batch are used when normalizing the
layers. When normalizing in the n-th iteration of a batch, the statistics of mini-batches
1 to n are used. Furthermore, while CBN updates the weights and biases in each it-
eration, CmBN only updates them at the end of each epoch [BWL20]. Bochkovskiy
et al. do not compare the effectiveness of CmBN compared to CBN, but demonstrate
the effectiveness of CmBN in their ablation studies, which shows an increase of 0.4% in
mAP.5:.05:.95 [BWL20].
Self-Adversarial Training is proposed by Bochkovskiy et al. as an additional data
augmentation technique. When using self-adversarial training, instead of altering the
network weights, the input image is modified such that the objects are not visible any-
more [BWL20]. Then, on the manipulated image, a normal forward pass and backward
pass is done. Unfortunately, Bochkovskiy et al. do not provide any reasoning or ex-
perimental results that document the assumed effectiveness of this data augmentation
method.
Compared to YOLOv3, ground truth boxes in YOLOv4 can get assigned to multi-
ple anchors [BWL20; NVI]. With this, YOLOv4 has a higher chance of producing a
more accurate box at the cost of having multiple predictions, but those can be filtered
out with NMS. However, the experimental results of the ablation studies conducted by
Bochkovskiy et al. on the CSPResNeXt50-PANet-SPP backbone, using this as a stan-
dalone BoF actually decreases mAP.5:.05:.95 by 1.1% [BWL20]. In combination with other
BoF mAP.5:.05:.95 is higher [BWL20], but the influence of multiple anchor boxes for a
single ground truth boxes can’t suﬀiciently be verified by that.
Bochkovskiy et al. also adopt multi-resolution training, as introduced in YOLOv3 by
Redmon and Farhadi [RF18; BWL20]. Again, the motivation behind this is to preserve
the accuracy of the detector across different scales. In their ablation studies, using this
BoF also decreases mAP.5:.05:.95 when used as a standalone BoF and there is no further
evidence that demonstrates the effectiveness of multi-resolution training combined with

While the components for the backbone have already been mentioned in a previous para-
graph, the BoS for the detector are yet to be presented. SPP in CNNs proposed by He
et al. obtains a fixed-length representation of the feature maps produced by the convo-
lutional layers of a CNN [He+14]. SPP takes a n-channel feature map of an arbitrary
spatial size as an input and lays over grids of various scales, e.g. 1×1, 2×2 or 4×4 and
performs pooling operations (maxpool in [He+14]) in the channel dimension for each
grid cell to obtain a vector of fixed length n to be fed to e.g. a fully connected layer
or a classifier [He+14]. This makes image transformations like cropping or warping ob-
solete and reduces the influence of missing contexts or geometric deformations [He+14].
There is no ablation study conducted for the SPP module by Bochkovskiy et al., how-
ever, they list results for YOLOv3 with and without SPP. YOLOv3 with SPP achieves
36.2% mAP.5:.05:.95 as opposed to 33.0 % without SPP, marking an increase of 3.2% in
mAP.5:.05:.95 while maintaining inference time [BWL20]. Thus, it is assumed that SPP is
a useful mechanism to increase mAP with almost no additional cost on inference time.
SAM is one component of the Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) pro-
posed by Woo et al. [Woo+18]. CBAM consists of a Spatial Attention Module (SAM)
and a Channel Attention Module (CAM), both learning inter-spatial and inter-channel
relationships respectively [Woo+18]. Woo et al. state that channels in a feature map can
be understood as separate feature detectors, so it can be inferred that the CAM learns
which features in a feature map are important and the SAM learns the importance of
the location of features in a feature map [Woo+18]. Experiments conducted by Woo et
al. on ImageNet and MS COCO show a significant increase in accuracy without adding
any significant computational cost, e.g. for a ResNeXt50 backbone used for image classi-
fication, the top1-accuracy on ImageNet increases from 77.15 % to 78.08 % using CBAM

ing boxes are treated as positive examples when backpropagating the gradients. Ge et
al. report an increase of 2.3 in mAP.5:.05:.95 when applied together with center sampling
and the other methods mentioned in the center sampling paragraph [Ge+21].
On the COCO 2017 test-dev dataset, Ge et al. report 47.4 % mAP.5:.05:.95 for their
YOLOX implementation with the Darknet53 backbone [Ge+21]. Compared to YOLOv4
with 43.5 % mAP.5:.05:.95, this marks an increase of 3.9 in mAP.5:.05:.95. On an NVIDIA
Tesla V100 GPU, they report an inference frame rate of 90.1 FPS compared to 62.0 FPS
for YOLOv4, an increase of 28 FPS [Ge+21]. When using the scalable modified CSP v5
backend, they report up to 51.2 % mAP.5:.05:.95 for the YOLOX-X detector at a frame
rate of 57.8 FPS.
4.1.2.2
YOLOR
Proceeding without Bochkovskiy, Wang and Liao who were also involved in the devel-
opment of YOLOv4 proposed together with Yeh a new approach to YOLO and named
it You Only Learn One Representation (YOLOR). YOLOR is not limited to object de-
tection only, as the objective stated in their paper is to learn one data representation
that can be used for multiple tasks [WYL21]. It is one approach to the recent devel-
opments in the deep learning community to establish a joint architecture that can be
used throughout different domains like computer vision and natural language processing,
with another example being Swin Transformer [Liu+21].
Wang et al. distinguish between two types of knowledge: explicit, which is directly
correlated to an observation / sample X and implicit, which is not directly linked to a
data point but rather to a task [WYL21]. In the simplest version, implicit knowledge is
a set of constant vectors that are added to an intermediate, task-shared feature map (e.g.
object detection, semantic segmentation etc.) to transfer this map from the implicit to
the explicit domain [WYL21]. Since the mechanisms behind this are more on the side of
information theory and signal processing topics, a further presentation is omitted, and
the reader is advised to [WYL21] for further reading.
Using YOLOv4-P6-light as a baseline, Wang et al. introduce implicit knowledge for
the following tasks in the detector [WYL21]:
• Feature alignment: implicit knowledge (constant tensor) is added to the feature
maps (for each scale) of the FPN
• Prediction refinement: implicit knowledge [constant vector of length (4bb +
1conf + Nclass prob)] is element-wise-multiplied with each bounding box prediction
With the introduction of these components, Wang et al. report 51.9 % mAP.5:.05:.95,
measured on the COCO validation data split [WYL21], marking an increase of 0.5
==PAGE:34==

compared to the YOLOv4-P6-light baseline. The network is trained without pre-training
on ImageNet, first for 300 epochs. Wang et al. also perform a fine-tuning for another 150
which boosts the mAP.5:.05:.95 to 52.5 %, which then results in a total increase of 1.1 in
mAP.5:.05:.95 compared to YOLOv4-P6-light [WYL21]. Wang et al. also report that their
YOLOR object detector performs on par with Scaled-YOLOv4 in terms of mAP.5:.05:.95,

4.1.3
Other detectors
CenterNet, like the YOLO-family object detectors, is a one-stage detector developed by
Duan et al. in 2019 and is based on CornerNet also developed in 2019 by Law and Deng
[Dua+19; LD19]. In CornerNet, top left and bottom right corners for bounding boxes
are detected through heatmaps and respective feature vectors for the detected corners
[LD19]. Duan et al. include a center region relative to the top-left and bottom-right
corner and look for keypoints in that region too [Dua+19]. The triplet (top-left, bottom-
right and center keypoints) then, according to Duan et al., can improve the accuracy by
producing higher-quality bounding boxes compared to CornerNet [Dua+19]. However,
as Duan et al. state an average inference time of 270 ms (∼3.7 FPS) on an NVIDIA
Tesla P100 GPU for their CenterNet511-52 backbone, this detector is not investigated
further as the frame rate is too low for the application thematized in this thesis.
Faster R-CNN developed by Ren et al.
in 2016 is, in contrast to the previously
presented object detectors, a two-stage detector. While the one-stage detectors directly
detect objects on the full image, two-stage methods first extract region proposals and
then try to classify and refine those. In case of Faster R-CNN, a CNN backbone VGG-16
extracts a feature map, which is then fed to a Region Proposal Network (RPN) [Ren+16].
Subsequently, the extracted proposals from the feature map are fed to the classifier to
obtain the final detection result [Ren+16]. While Ren et al. report an mAP.5:.05:.95 of
21.9 % on the COCO test-dev split [Ren+16], a very good result for the time, they report
frame rate on an NVIDIA K40 GPU about 5 FPS [Ren+16], unsuitable for application
in this thesis.
4.2
Object detection evaluation
While the mAP.5:.05:.95metric is mostly used to measure the accuracy of object detectors,
the computation of this metric is rather complex and does not disclose the actual error
causes that might have a negative impact on mAP.5:.05:.95. Therefore, Bolya et al. intro-
==PAGE:35==
4.3 Agricultural datasets
24
duced the Toolbox for Identifying Object Detection Errors (TIDE) to tackle this issue
and provide a more compact and effective error analysis [Bol+20].
Bolya et al. introduce the following error types [Bol+20]:
• Classification Error
• Localization Error
• Both Classification and Localization Error
• Duplicate Detection Error
• Background Error
• Missed Ground Truth Error
A detailed description of the classification criteria for the error types are further elabo-
rated in [Bol+20], but are left out here due to the intuitive naming of the error types.
The idea of considering different error types and their influence on the overall change
in mAP.5:.05:.95 is not new and has also been considered by the authors of the COCO
dataset3.
One key difference is the way the error contributions are computed.
The
COCO evaluation toolkit computes errors progressively, meaning that the influence is
measured after eliminating the influence of one error type and keeping it eliminated
when applying another fix [Bol+20]. However, this is not feasible as applying a fix for
one error type usually influences the previously applied fix and the order of application
produces different results [Bol+20]. Therefore, Bolya et al. propose to avoid progressive
computation of error type contribution and show problems concerning the explainability
of the mAP.5:.05:.95 measure [Bol+20].

Methodology

– Optimizing the inference

5.2.2
Training from scratch
It is hypothesized that the COCO dataset, on which there exist pre-trained weights for
YOLOR2 are actually not useful due to the difference in nature between the COCO
dataset and RumexWeeds. Differences include:

• Different color properties: RumexWeeds consist of primarily green images.
When comparing the histogram of hue values ranged between 0-179 (half preci-

class Person.
• Bounding box dimensions: When extracting the bounding boxes of both datasets,
one can cluster those by using the k-means algorithm. This is done from YOLOv2
onwards to determine the bounding box priors, and more recent versions (YOLOv4

good fit to the dataset. As the pre-trained weights are obtained by training on

performing the clustering on the entire datasets respectively, the results as dis-

is variable and tends to be smaller at a maximum of (640 × 640), but for better
comparability these dimensions have been normalized. In general, it can be seen
that RumexWeeds tends to have near-to-square bounding boxes and COCO tends
to have rectangular ones where one dimension is significantly larger than the other.

might be a good fit to both datasets and can be left as they are.

There is an antiproportional relationship between the NMS threshold, displayed on
the leftmost axis in Figure 5.14 and mAP.5:.05:.95, displayed on the rightmost axis in
Figure 5.14. The explanation behind that is based on the nature of the NMS algorithm.
Given a set of overlapping bounding boxes, all boxes with an IoU bigger than the
NMS threshold are considered candidate boxes. Out of all these candidate boxes, only
the box with the maximum confidence score is kept, while the other ones are being
discarded. Therefore, a lower NMS threshold tends to discard more boxes as the amount
of candidate boxes grows. This reduces the amount of duplicate detections, but also
increases the risk of discarding boxes that actually cover a different instance of an object.
Precision and recall values are directly affected by this: lower NMS thresholds achieve
lower recalls but higher precision (as they remove more boxes), high NMS thresholds
achieve higher recalls (they do not discard possible true positives) but lower precisions
(they keep duplicate detections / false positives).
It is a good approach to select an NMS threshold that balances precision and recall,
however, one can see in Figure 5.14 that the range of recall values is much smaller
([0.48; 0.56], ∆= 0.08) compared to the range of precision values ([0.07; 0.52], ∆= 0.45).
Thus, lower NMS threshold values are actually preferred in this project as they greatly
increase the precision of the detector, but only slightly decrease the recall. A balanced
precision and recall is reached at an NMS threshold at around 0.15 which results in a
precision and recall of both around 0.5 at an mAP.5:.05:.95 of 0.2414.

CHAPTER 7
Future work
This chapter presents additional ideas to improve the agricultural platform’s performance
and features that might be useful to an end-user of the dataset.
7.1
Establishing a plant tracker
Previous sections of this work have established that the detector on itself can achieve
precisions up to 1.0 and recalls up to about 0.55. Standalone object detectors do not
take into account that there are temporal relationships in most of the applications in
which they are deployed, thus these are mostly paired with object trackers to increase
the reliability of standalone object detectors. Taking these temporal relationships into
consideration, there are some common phenomenons regarding false positives and false
negatives:
• Lost detections can occur: While the detector has already identified an object in
the previous picture, there might be occasionally a frame in the sequence in which
the detector fails to detect the plant. An object tracker might still be able to
detect the plant through e.g. Kalman filtering or matching hand-crafted features
like SIFT or ORB
• False positive detections occur randomly: The detector does not have perfect
precision due to random false detections. In a sequence, these might only occur in
one out of many pictures. Together with tracking, one could employ a threshold of
n out of m subsequent detections, meaning that a detection is only declared valid
if the detector has detected the same object at least n times in the most recent m
frames.
• Missed detections are common. The detector has a limited recall and this case
is the opposite of the first case: the plant is not detected in most of the frames but
only in a few frames of the sequence. The detection can then be used to compute
features and match them in past and future frames. However, this should be done
conforming to the n out of m detection scheme mentioned previously to avoid
wasting computational resources.
The tracking approach has a few prerequisites. The following lists them, a more detailed
explanation will follow.

Classifying and handling incoming detections
The handling of an incoming detection message will be handled in up to three stages.
In a first step, the incoming detections are compared against the database, which re-
flects previous detections. On the grounds of a distance-based matching algorithm with
conflict resolution, the detections in the database are classified as:

• new: New detection previously not held in the database. This happens when an
incoming detection does not get matched to any detection in the database.
• kept: Incoming detection was matched to a detection in the database that previ-
ously held the state new or kept.
• lost: Detection in the database with the state new, kept or lost that wasn’t
matched to any incoming detection and the recovery mechanism failed.
• discard: Detection that kept the state lost in the last m frames will get removed
from the database.
• rediscovered: Detection that had the state lost but was now detected again.

7.1.4
Recovering lost detections
In addition to the second stage, if a detection in the database has the state lost, the
detected and matched keypoints will be used to attempt a recovery of the object. To-
gether with the state estimate of the Kalman filter, a recovery algorithm could work as
follows:
1. Retrieve the ellipsis of possible center locations and the current state estimate of
the bounding box dimensions to obtain an area where the plant should be
2. Check if the majority of the keypoints lie within the predicted area
3. If the previous holds true, change the state of the detection in the base to kept.
The last step does not have any influence on future actions, but rather on the proposed
n out of m detection scheme and increases the chance of the detection to be kept in the
database.

8.1 Discussion

8.1
Discussion
Given the reference performance on the GeForce RTX 3090 GPU, the PyTorch and
TensorRT models on the Jetson Xavier NX have no significant changes in precision,
recall or mAP.5:.05:.95. The only significant drop that can be seen is the drop in frame
rate due to the reduced amount of computing resources. Based on this, the TensorRT
half-precision pre-compiled model is used for deployment, which achieves 10 FPS on the
RumexWeeds dataset with the inference parameters listed in Table 8.1. As the dataset is
recorded at 5 FPS this is deemed suﬀicient for experimental purposes, however, for real-
life applications it would impose some constraints on the velocity of the robot, lowering
overall eﬀiciency of the solution.
A TIDE error analysis has been carried out again for the final detector and the re-
sult is depicted in Figure 8.1. Compared to the baseline, a lot of background and false
positive errors have successfully been suppressed, as indicated by the decrease in ∆AP
for Bkg and the extremely significant decrease in ∆AP for FN in Table 8.3. This, of
course, brings the already discussed costs of a lower recall with it, as indicated by the
increased ∆AP for Miss and FN. Although having these drawbacks, this marks an im-
provement compared to the baseline, successfully completing the objective of improving
the accuracy of the object detector.
In summary, the achieved results show a detector that is able to detect about half of
the present plants in the dataset test split. By adjusting several inference parameters,
the behavior of the object detector can be adjusted according to a chosen strategy, such
as a more protective strategy with restricted use of energy-consuming peripherals. The
solution is successfully deployed on an embedded device with a frame rate that lives up
to the requirements of the provided dataset. In addition, the model is not specific to
the camera hardware used nor to the location where the dataset was recorded. A video
taken with a mobile phone and subsequently passed through the model can be found
on YouTube. Remaining problems are the occurrence of high-confidence predictions of
non-target plants, as well as the detection of small-sized and contrast-less ground truth
examples. It is expected that the proposed measures in Chapter 7 will improve the
performance of the solution even further.

CHAPTER 9
Summary
This thesis tackled the problem of detecting weeds of the types Rumex obtusifolius and
Rumex crispus in grasslands used for dairy farming. Starting from the RumexWeeds
dataset created by Güldenring et al. [GEN21], an extensive dataset analysis was car-
ried out to get accustomed to the specific nature of the dataset, hereof the domain of
agriculture and the robotic data included in the dataset. A handful of off-the-shelf ob-
ject detection models like YOLOX, YOLOv4, YOLOv5 and YOLOR have been trained
to establish a baseline. This baseline was selected on the basis of typical object detec-
tion metrics like mean precision (mP), mean recall (mR) and mean average precision
(mAP.5:.05:.95) as well as an error analysis carried out with the TIDE toolbox.
Although the trained detectors showed similar performances and detection behaviors,
YOLOR-CSP was chosen as the baseline as the backbone is designed to extract features
useful for multiple downstream tasks, paving the way for enabling other downstream
tasks like semantic segmentation of weeds with minimized additional computational
costs due to the sharing of computed features.
Subsequently, upon gained inside knowledge of the dataset’s specific attributes, sev-
eral experiments were carried out with the objective of improving the object detector’s
accuracy and performance. Motivated by the difference in object sizes and hue color
value distribution of the RumexWeeds dataset compared to the usually utilized COCO
dataset, experiments showed that the common practice of re-training a detector from
pre-trained weights obtained from the COCO dataset yield little to no advantage on the
domain-specific dataset used. Therefore, it was decided not to use pre-trained weights,
but rather train the detector from scratch.
From the analysis of the dataset frames, it was also found out that the weeds have
a different structure which distinguishes them very well from the background. While
the background consists of an immeasurable amount of grass straws yielding a lot of
keypoints when the SIFT algorithm is employed, the weeds have a rather homogeneous,
texture-less surface and containing areas have a lower density of SIFT keypoints. It was
found out that manipulating the training images by increasing the pixel intensity values
when the density is low and decreasing them when the density is high actually highlights
the weeds and can act as an eﬀicient augmentation technique to prevent overfitting and
increase the recall of the detector by 0.07. An experiment employing said algorithm
not on the input images but on the feature map created by the backbone showed no
beneficial effect.
==PAGE:78==
9 Summary
67
As a last step to optimize the performance of the detector, a sweep of the confidence
and IOU thresholds have been carried out. Doing a random search with defined minima
and maxima for the respective parameters, it was found that using a confidence threshold
of 0.21 and an IOU threshold of 0.15 yields a good compromise between the increase
in precision and the decrease in recall, with values of 0.60, 0.46 and 0.30 for precision,
recall and mAP.5:.05:.95 respectively.
A software environment that pays tribute to the robotic nature of the dataset has
been developed. The implemented ROS nodes reflect the temporal history of GNSS, IMU
and odometry data together with the images. Subsequently, the optimized detector was
converted to a TensorRT engine and has been deployed on an embedded device without
sacrificing accuracy at the cost of a lower frame throughput at 10 FPS on an NVIDIA
Jetson Xavier NX compared to 58 FPS on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090. However,
this still represents a rate that lives up to the 5 FPS frame rate of the dataset. The
development environment has been containerized for an easy deployment, making the
RumexWeeds dataset more accessible for community development.
Additional ideas to further improve the performance of the detector have been pre-
sented, but only partially implemented. These include a tracking node that leverages the
temporal relationships between the frames to make a more robust detection algorithm.
In conclusion, the conducted experiments and implemented software structures show
a real potential for the usage of computer vision in non-chemical, autonomous weed
control, clearing the way for more sustainable, eﬀicient, economically attractive farming
practices as a response to the higher demand of organically farmed products and food
in general.
