Abstract 
In 2026 Ørsted will start the operation of three parallel carbon capture units at Asnæs power plant. It 
is a wood chip fired boiler, which will be retrofitted with carbon capture units. The Asnæs power 
plant typically operates flexible, with high and low loads each day.  
Based on the operational data provided by Ørsted, the energy requirements for different operational 
philosophies have been evaluated in this work. The dynamics and energy requirements for the parallel 
carbon capture units have been investigated in three separate models.  
 
In this work, three models have been developed. The first and simple model had the objective to 
evaluate the energy performance for steady state operation. The model did not include any absorber 
dynamics but focused on developing a desorber model. The limited absorber model resulted in a 
model with limit reliability.  
The second model was an advancement of the first model. The model for the absorber was developed 
and implemented into the existing model. The new absorber model was developed with textbook 
absorber equations and validated with experimental results. The model was found reliable when the 
carbon capture units was loaded above 60%. Two operational philosophies were evaluated and a 
potential saving of above 5 mill/year were found. However, the economical favorable operational 
philosophy reduced the amount of CO2 captured with 8700tons/year. It was further found that the 
energy requirement increased rapidly when the load on a carbon capture unit exceeded 60%. 
To understand the dynamics of a carbon capture unit during load changes, a simple time dependent 
model was developed (model 3). The model was used to investigate how long time the reboiler could 
be postponed after a start-up of the flue gas. It was found that if 50% of the solvent is stored as lean, 
the start-up of the reboiler could be postponed with 12 minutes while still achieving stable operation. 
With 60% of the solvent stored as lean it was able to postpone the start-up of the reboiler by 17 
minutes. Hereby it was shown that it is possible to operate the carbon capture units fast up/down, 
independently of the available steam for the reboiler. 
With the information found from the analysis of the models a new operational philosophy was 
developed. The philosophy reduced the time where the units is operated below 60% and eliminated 
the time where it is operated below 50%. The new philosophy captured more that 90% of the available 
CO2 required to meet the 430000 ton agreed with the Danish Government. The philosophy showed 
that it is possible to save 0.57 mill/year in fuel cost while only reducing the amount of captured CO2 
with 800 ton compared to equal flue gas distribution.

1 Introduction  
Climate change is the greatest challenge humankind faces in the 21st century. The global temperature 
is increasing, together with a rising sea level. This results in more frequently extreme weather as 
drought, flooding and wildfires[1]. Climate change is caused by increasing levels of greenhouse gases 
(GHG) primarily carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O) [2]. N2O is the most 
potent of the three greenhouse gasses one kilogram of N2O has the same impact as 265 kilogram CO2. 
CH4 is the second most potent and is 28 times more impact full than CO2. However due to the quantity 
of CO2 that is emitted, CO2 is the most impactful of the GHG [3].  
Emissions of CO2 primarily origin from combustion of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas. 
The fossil fuels are burned for production of energy, transport, and in industrial production. The 
emitted CO2 is accumulating in the atmosphere where the concentrations has increased from below 
320 ppm in 1960 to 420 in august 2023 [4]. Data of the CO2 concentration measured from the National 
Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration at Hawaii are presented in Figure 1 [4]. The red curve is 
the CO2 concentration each month, and the black curve is the yearly average. On Figure 1 there is a 
clear trend that the yearly average CO2 concentration increases each year. The monthly average is 
varying depending on the season, the CO2 concentration is larger in the winter months where there is 
less uptake from trees and plants. It can also be found on  Figure 1 that the yearly average has been 
increasing year after year since 1960 and is still increasing. 
 
Figure 1. CO2 concentrations from National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration measurements in Hawaii. The red curve is the 
monthly variations and the black is the yearly average[4]

The increased concentrations of CO2 in the atmosphere shown on Figure 1 result in increased 
temperatures. In the report “state of global climate 2022” from the World Meteorological 
Organization [1] it is reported that the global mean temperature has increased to 1.28 °C above the 
preindustrial level, due to increased level of greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere [1].  
In 2015 the 196 member countries of the United Nation (UN) agreed to reduce the emissions of 
greenhouse gasses to keep the global temperature increase below 2 °C and preferably 1.5 °C. This is 
known as the Paris agreement [5]. To reach this target a series of different actions are needed. This 
includes installation of new renewable energy sources like solar and wind in combination with 
increased electrification and reducing energy consumption [6]. This will however not reduce the 
emissions fast enough to ensure a limited temperature increase. In their report from 2023 the 
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has concluded that removal of CO2 from the 
atmosphere is needed to keep the temperature increase below 2 °C in 2100 [7]. Removal of CO2 can 
be understood as negative emissions and can help reach a net zero emission society. 
 
1.1 Negative emissions 
Even if some sectors emit CO2, a net zero emissions society can be reach by creating negative 
emissions elsewhere. Negative emissions are removal of CO2 from the atmosphere and permanently 
underground storage. Some sectors have a long path to decarbonize. These sectors are known as hard 
to abate sectors, and include aviation, heavy transport, and agricultural production [8]. 
One technology which can help creating a net zero society by creating negative emissions elsewhere 
is direct air capture. Direct air capture (DAC) is a technology that can remove CO2 directly from the 
air. The general principle for DAC is that a large amount of ambient air is sucked into contactors 
where the CO2 is absorbed/adsorbed to a fluid or material. When the contactor material is saturated 
the air fans is turned off and the CO2 is released from the contactor material typically with heat [9]. 
The status of DAC is that only few larger scale demonstration units have been applied. The 
technology is challenged with high energy consumptions resulting in large operational costs [10].  
Besides DAC, there exists other pathways to create negative emissions. Bioenergy with carbon 
capture and storage (BECCS) is such a technology , which at the same time produces green electricity. 
BECCS is the production of energy by burning of biomass such as wood, biogas and straw, while in 
the same time removing CO2 from the flue gas [11]. The principles for BECCS are presented in Figure 
2, where it is shown how CO2 is sequenced from the atmosphere by photosynthesis in plants. The 
plants are after wards used for production of energy while the CO2 is stored.

On Figure 2, it is ilustrated how the leaves on the plants convert CO2 into biomass with the energy

safely stored in the underground where it can stay for 1000 of years [12]. 
BECCS is expected to be a keystone in a future renewable energy system. IPCC estimates that 
BECCS has the potential to reduce the global emission with more than 1 GT in 2030 [7]. The 
technology is still emerging and has not been applied at large scale. There is however construction 
plans of large scale BECCS in Denmark.  
 
1.2 Carbon Capture in Denmark 
A broad majority of the Danish parliament agreed to reduce the national emissions of greenhouse 
gasses with 70% by 2030 [13]. To reach this target, reductions are needed in all sectors of the Danish 
society. From the parliament it is decided that some of the emission reductions must be found by large 
emitters such as powerplants or industry [13]. 
The government therefore initiated a tender to catch at least 400,000 tons of CO2 per year from the 
industry/power production by 2026. The tender includes governmental support for each ton of CO2 
removed with a total support of 8 bill DKK. In the spring 2024, Ørsted was awarded the first 
governmental carbon capture contract. By signing the contract Ørsted agreed to capture 430,000 tons 
CO2 pr year in 20 years starting from 2026.  
==PAGE:16==
 
4
 
The CO2 will be captured at Avedøre powerplant near Copenhagen and at Asnæs power plant in 
Kalundborg. The locations are shown on Figure 3 where Asnæs power plant is in the western part of 
Zealand, and Avedøre in the eastern part of Zealand. On Avedøre power plant a straw fired boiler 
will be retrofitted with carbon capture. The CO2 from Avedøre will be transported by truck to 
Kalundborg. Here it will be mixed with CO2 from the wood chip fired boiler at Asnæs power plant. 
From Kalundborg it will be loaded to ships and transported to Northern Lights storage facility located 
offshore near the Norwegian city Øygarden [14]. The CO2 will be stored permanently 2600 meters 
below the seabed in saline aquifers. The first phase of the Northern Lights is scheduled to start 
operation in 2024 with an annual capacity of 1.5 million tons of CO2 [15]. A map of the transport of 
CO2 from the powerplants in Denmark to the storage facility in Norway is shown in Figure 3.  
 
Figure 3. Map over the transport of CO2 from Ørsted operated power plants to Northern lights storage facility in Norway [15]. 
In this section and on Figure 3 the overview of the first carbon capture projects in Denmark has been 
explained. The boilers at Asnæs and Avedøre power plant is partly operated based on the power 
prices. Therefore, the available CO2 for capture and hereby the load of the carbon capture units is also 
dependent on the power prices.

1.3 Power production and power prices 
As part of the green transition, the Danish power production has changed from fossil-based power 
production to a power generation dominated by wind and solar. In the first 6 months of 2023 64% of 
the Danish power consumption was produced by solar panels and wind turbines [16]. The high part 
of renewable power in the Danish energy mix has resulted in larger fluctuations of power prices.  
 
The Danish power price is determined hour by hour by a marked-based principle. The power price is 
influenced by several factors, including production of cheap renewable electricity, import/export of 
power to neighboring countries, and the demand on power [17]. The demand is influenced on the 
weather and the time of the day, and is typically largest in the morning hours, and in the evening 
when people are cooking dinner. This influences the power price such that it is typically more 
expensive in the morning and in the evening.  
The power production/consumption on a typical summer day is presented on Figure 4. The two bands 
on the top are production of power from central and decentral power plants including waste 
incineration plants. The dark blue band in the bottom represents the production of power from wind 
turbines. On top of the wind production a yellow band show the production from solar cells. The 
production is shown in MW and the right y-axis is used. The red line Figure 4 is the power 
consumption in Denmark. The green line is the power price, which vary from 536 dkk/MWh from 14 
and 15 pm to 1477 dkk/MWh between 19 and 20 pm [18].

On Figure 4 it is found that the energy production in Denmark on September 4th, 2023, was only 
larger than the consumption in the hours around midday. In these hours, the energy prices were 
lowest. The rest of the day the remaining power demand has been delivered by import of power from 
our neighboring countries. Only in the middle of the day, when solar production peaked Denmark, 
there was a net exporter of power. On the figure it is also found that the power production from 
thermal power plants had the largest peak production between the hours 7-9 and 17-19.  
 
The production on Asnæs power plant (ASV) is dependent on the power prices, but also the heat, and 
steam demand. At low power prices ASV is still in operation to ensure steam supply to the nearby 
pharmaceutical industry. The boiler is then ramped up and down depending on the energy prices on 
the grid, and the need for district heating. The carbon capture units will also follow this pattern. The 
ramp up and down of the carbon capture unit will also impact the choice of technology.  
 
1.4 Carbon capture technologies 
Carbon capture technologies can be split into three categories: Pre-combustion, oxy fuel combustion, 
and post-combustion. Pre-combustion refers to the oxidation of the fuel before burning it, resulting 
in a low carbon fuel for the boiler. Oxyfuel combustion is the combustion in an oxygen rich 
environment with the lack of nitrogen making the flue gas almost pure CO2 ready for compression 
and storage. Post-combustion carbon capture is removing the CO2 from the flue gas of a point source. 
A great advantage for post-combustion is the possibility to retrofit existing sources of CO2 emissions 
with only little disturbance of the production. Post-combustion carbon capture is the most mature of 
the three concepts for power plants, and it is therefore most likely also post combustion technologies 
that will be used for BECCS [19]. 
 
There exist several post combustion technologies that can be used to capture CO2 from a biomass 
fired boiler. Most of the CO2 separation technologies are developed in the fossil industry used to 
natural gas purification, or in hydrogen and ammonia production [20]. Some of the most promising 
of the technologies are cryogenic separation, membrane separation, and chemical scrubbing with 
amine [21]. These technologies will be reviewed in the following sections focusing on the advantages 
and disadvantages together with the applicability in a BECCS system. 
 
==PAGE:19==

1.4.1 Membrane  
An emerging technology to remove CO2 from a process stream is membrane-based carbon capture. 
The technology shows exciting potential with low energy demand, and low environmental impact. 
Membrane-based carbon capture is a physical separation method, where membranes as physical 
sieves separate CO2 and from the remaining gasses [22]. These membranes are designed to allow CO2 
to permeate through them while blocking the passage of other gases, thus concentrating, and capturing 
the CO2 ready for later liquefaction, transport, and storage. In recent years, significant progress has 
been made in the development, optimization and commercialization of membrane-based carbon 
capture technologies [23]. Especially the development of new membrane types and process 
configurations has been in focus [24]. 
 
On Figure 5, a general flow chart for carbon capture using membrane is presented. The red line is the 
flue gas intake into the membrane. In the membrane CO2 permeate through the surface of the 
membrane. The remaining gasses, primarily N2, are held back by the membrane. These gasses are 
named the retentate and are further treated or let out to the atmosphere. The gas that passes through 
the membrane is named the permeate and is ether passed through another membrane or directly 
compressed depending on how high the selectivity of the membrane is [25]. 
 
Figure 5. Flow chart of membrane based carbon capture. The red line is the flue gas inlet, where it is passed to the membrane. The 
gas that pass through is the permeate, and the gas which is held back by the membrane is named the retentate[25]. 
 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
8
One of the great advantages of membrane-based carbon capture is that membranes are modular and 
therefore essay to scale-up. However, membranes made for separation of gasses are difficult to 
produce and therefore expensive [23]. Another drawback for membrane-based carbon capture is 
clogging, meaning that the pore over time is stopped with contaminations in the flue gas. Hereby the 
permeability is lowered, resulting in an increased power consumption [26].  
 
Membrane-based carbon capture uses no chemicals for operation and therefore has an advantage in 
capturing CO2 without contributing to the surroundings with new emissions. Membrane based carbon 
capture is therefore considered an environmentally friendly solution. If operational stability and 
energy requirements are improved membrane based carbon capture could be used in the future [27].  
 
1.4.2 Cryogenic 
Cryogenic carbon capture operates on the fundamental principle that gases can be efficiently 
separated based on temperature difference for phase shift. For separation of CO2 from flue gas 
primarily consisting of nitrogen, the flue gas is cooled to cryogenic temperatures, typically around 
173 K (-100 °C). At these frigid temperatures, CO2 undergoes a phase transition while nitrogen 
continues to be a gas [28]. There exist many different designs for cryogenic carbon capture, and the 
most established process is the Ryan-Holmes process [29]. The process is a cryogenic distillation 
which was developed to remove CO2 from natural gas [29].  
 
On Figure 6, an illustration for the cryogenic distillation process is shown. The flue gas enters at the 
left of the diagram where it is pre-cooled before it enters the distillation column. In the distillation 
column the CO2 undergoes a phase shift. First it sublimates to dry ice. When it is in the solid phase 
the remaining gases, mostly nitrogen, continues upwards and is vented out in the top of the distillation 
column. CO2 in the phase of dry ice falls on a melting tray. Here it is melted to liquid CO2, which is 
drawn out in the bottom and ready for storage [30].  
==PAGE:21==
 
9
 
Figure 6. Flow sheet of the cryogenic distillation. The flue gas inlet is in the left. The flue gas is passed into a column where the CO2 
is cooled down such it sublimates [30]. 
The cryogenic distillation is based on temperature changes, therefore large amount of energy 
addition/removal is needed. On Figure 6 there is shown two energy exchange steps. At Q1 the flue 
gas is cooled to sublimate CO2, and at Q2 some of the energy is sent back to melt the dry ice. The 
illustration on Figure 6, is simplified and an actual process has a more extensive heat integration 
network [30].  
 
Cryogenic distillation uses power to create the cooling needed for the separation, the process therefore 
does not need process steam. This makes it applicable for industry where steam is not available, such 
as cement production and biogas upgrading. For biomass fired power plants steam is typically 
available and therefore not an advantage [29]. Cryogenic carbon capture has only been tested in few 
pilot scale campaigns on flue gas and have first recently been scaled up to 1 ton/day [31]. The field 
testing showed high purity of CO2 and low energy demands compared to benchmark amine processes. 
The technology shows promising signs and extensive research is put into optimizing the performance 
and testing of new process designs [28].

1.4.3 Chemical absorption using amines 
The most mature technology for carbon capture is chemical scrubbing with aqueous solutions of 
amines. The technology was developed to remove CO2 and H2S in natural gas production and refined 
to capture CO2 from combustion [32]. The technology has been applied in two full scale projects both 
with coal fired boilers. The two projects are the Petro Nova project, in Texas, USA designed to capture 
1.6 mil ton/year, and the Boundary Dam power plant, Canada designed to capture 1.15 mil ton/year. 
Both projects have experienced operational problems, both technical and financially [32]. In 
September 2023 Petro Nova was revamped. Currently, both projects are in operation [33]. 
 
Chemical scrubbing of CO2 operates by the same principles regardless of the solvent used for the 
process. The benchmark solvent is 30 wt% monoethanolamine (MEA) in aqueous solution, however 
most industrial technology providers have their own property solvent. The process consists of two 
main steps. First, the absorption of CO2 from the flue gas into a solvent, and second the release of 
CO2 from the solvent and the collecting of an almost pure CO2 stream.  
 
On Figure 7, a flow chart of a simple amine scrubbing process is showed. In the left of Figure 7, the 
flue gas enters the absorber column where gas is traveling counter current with the solvent in a packed 
bed. The CO2 dissolves and reacts with the amine in the solvent as it flows down the column. When 
the solvent has reached the bottom of the absorber and has reacted with the CO2, it is named a rich 
solvent. The rich solvent is transported through a heat exchanger where it is preheated before entering 
the stripper column. On Figure 7, the blue lines represent the cold solvent streams of the process, and 
the red lines represent the hot solvent streams. In the stripper, the solvent is regenerated with heat 
provided by the reboiler. The CO2 is released from the solvent and is leaving at the top of the column 
ready for compression, liquefaction, and storage. When the CO2 is stripped of the solvent, the solvent 
is named “lean”. The lean solvent is pumped back to the absorber entering the solvent loop again 
ready to capture new CO2 [34].  
 
==PAGE:23==
 
11
 
Figure 7. Flow chart for chemical scrubbing carbon capture. The red lines are the hot solvent around the stripper, and the blue line is 
the cold solvent around the absorber. The hot and cold solvent heat integrated in the middle between the absorber and the stripper in 
the main heat exchanger.  
The thermal energy in the hot lean solvent is then recovered in a main heat exchanger placed between 
the stripper and absorber on Figure 7. The energy in the hot lean solvent origins from the reboiler, 
which is found on Figure 7 in the bottom right corner. The energy in the reboiler usually origins from 
process steam.  
 
Major challenges for amine-based carbon capture are the emissions of environmental harmful solvent 
degradation products, and large energy requirements for the regeneration of the solvent.  
In the absorber some of the amine is emitted in the clean gas as pure amine or as amine-derivatives 
[35]. Some of the derivatives have a harmful impact to the environment and can cause problems in 
getting regulatory permission. The amine-derivates are primary created by reactions with 
contaminants in the flue gas or by thermal degradation in the reboiler [36].  
==PAGE:24==
 
12
The large energy requirements for amine processes are linked to the reboiler which is operated by 
process steam. The energy requirement is around 3.6 GJ/ton CO2 for a MEA process. Producing 3.6 
GJ steam is expensive and often it creates financial challenges during business development of 
potential carbon capture projects[37]. During testing and development, the technology has been 
improved in all steps of the process. Optimization has primarily been focused on two tracks: 
Development of new solvents and process configurations. Both solvent optimization and process 
configurations have been developed to improve the efficiency and reduce emissions of harmful 
substances. Significant progress has been shown in both categories, making realization of full 
commercialization with widespread application in many different sectors closer [38]. 
 
Amine-based carbon capture is the most tested technology for post combustion carbon capture. It is 
the only technology that has full scale operational plants on record, and with the increased knowledge 
and development amine-based carbon capture is the most mature. It will probably be the technology 
chosen for most carbon capture projects within the next years, where other emerging technologies are 
not yet ready for full scale projects [38]. Amine-based carbon capture is also chosen as technology 
for the projects at Avedøre and Asnæs power plants.

1.6 Optimized load scheduling for carbon capture units integrated with a power plant 
Most literature about  operation of carbon capture units covers integration of carbon capture units 
with existing power plant, and primarily focuses on steady operation. It is not the expectation that 
ASV6 will be operated with a stable load for many hours due to variable demands for utilities. In this 
section literature on operation with variable load on amine-based carbon capture units will be revived. 
It will focus on case studies on existing biomass fired power plants. 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
14
The papers analyzed focus on a specific power plant. The studies model how the power plant could 
be operated if a carbon capture unit is applied. In an article by Wiesberg et al. [39] a dynamic model 
of a biomass fired boiler including carbon capture is developed and evaluated. They modelled a 
fluidized bed fired with bagasse, a by-product from sugar production. The developed model includes 
a 130 ton/h fluidized bed, power generation by steam turbines, interconnections in the plant and a 
standard 30 wt% MEA post-combustion capture unit. The focus in the model were on the fluidized 
bed, turbine, and interconnections. The model for the carbon capture is a standard 30 wt% MEA 
solvent taken from a process model library[39]. The authors succeeded in developing a model. 
However, they primarily present results from steady state operation, and they focus on the energy 
penalty at different capture rates. Scheduling of viable load is not included in the article [39].  
 
Zanteye et al. [40] model a carbon capture system with additional solvent tanks for rich and lean 
solvent retrofitted to a 600 MW coal-fired power plant [40]. The additional tanks add increased 
possibilities for operating the power plant with flexible load. The authors only focus on the modeling 
of the carbon capture system and neglect the system dynamics for the original power plant. The 
modeled capture system is a standard with 30 wt% MEA. No additional process configurations except 
the extra tanks are implemented in the model. The objective of the study is to maximize the profit 
from the power plant over one day with variable power prices. The model is solved using a stochastic 
simulation approach and resulted in a 40% increased profitability of the plant.  
 
In the literature review there was found no papers covering integration multiple parallel carbon 
capture units to an existing power plant which has a variable load profile. This project will focus on 
the energy consumption for multiple parallel carbon capture units. The objective of this project is to 
develop a model which can evaluate different operational philosophies and find the most optimal 
philosophy.

2 Problem Statement 
The urgency of climate action cannot be overstated. Our planet is experiencing unprecedented 
changes in weather patterns, rising temperatures, and extreme natural disasters that threaten the very 
fabric of our ecosystems and societies. The consequences of inaction are dire and far-reaching. 
Therefore, the Danish parliament has agreed to reduce the CO2 emission with 70% before 2030. 
Urgent reductions in the CO2 emission are needed to reach this target.  
One technology, which can help reduce the CO2 emissions, is carbon capture. In the spring of 2023, 
the Danish power company Ørsted won the first government tender to capture at least 400,000 tons 
of CO2 starting in 2026. Ørsted will apply capture units on two biomass fired boilers, one at Avedøre 
power plant and one at Asnæs power plant. At Asnæs power plant, the carbon capture concept that 
will be installed consists of three parallel units. Asnæs powerplant is operated with a base load to 
ensure steam to Novo Nordisk pharmaceutical production. Besides the baseload the plant produces 
power to the grid and heat to district heating. The boiler is operated to optimize profit from selling 
power to the grid and therefore has a variable load depending on production/consumption of power.  
 
This project aims to investigate how to operate the carbon capture units at Asnæs power plant such 
that the required 430,000 tons of CO2 is captured at the lowest possible cost. This includes 
investigation of the production profiles and to model how the carbon capture units will be ramped 
up/down. The project will design a model which can evaluate the energy consumption at full load and 
part load. The model will be used to find an optimal operation profile for the three carbon capture 
units at Asnæs power plant, with the possibilities of running them in parallel or individual. The 
economic benefits for a full year of operation will also be evaluated. 
Learning objectives: 
1. Collect and make an overview of literature, which discuss CO2 capture in relation to part load 
operation of biomass fired power plants 
2. Create a simple model for energy consumption in a carbon capture plant  
3. Perform simulations with actual load profile from ASV6 and test different operational 
philosophies 
4. Increase the reliability by developing a more advanced model 
5. Investigate how the solvent inventory on a carbon capture unit can be used to increase 
flexibility in the operation of the carbon capture units 
6. Based on the finding develop a new operation philosophy for the carbon capture units at ASV6 
7. Upscale the findings to a year and evaluate the economic potential 
8. Summarize and discuss the obtained results  
9. Disseminate the results in form of a report

3.2 Scenario development  
Operational data from ASV6 was analyzed to find data that represented a usual 24 h operation at 
various times at the year. Three 24-hour operation periods were chosen as they created a variety of 
different operational possibilities. The development of the three cases will be described in this section. 
 
After the days were chosen, the data was simplified so each day had maximum 7 different loadings. 
This was done by leveling periods where the load was almost even. E.g., a day where the load was 
between 36% and 38% for 6 hours the load was assumed to be 37% in each hour. This simplification 
is important to limit number of setpoint changes to a minimum. 
A parasitic load was added to count for the additional steam that would be used in the capture unit. 
The parasitic load is assumed to be 23%, based on a calculation with a specific reboiler duty on 3 
GJ/ton CO2. This only include the steam for the units. Utilities for pumps and compression are 
disregarded in this calculation. Example: a day where the load on ASV6 was 37% a parasitic load on 
23% was added, which yielded a new load on 45.5%. Hereby has the operational data been used to 
estimate future load profiles for the operation on ASV when the carbon capture units will be installed.  
 
The dry oxygen concentration was found in data provided by Ørsted. Based on the dry oxygen 
concentration, the CO2 concentration was calculated using an idealized combustion of wood chips 
with the mass composition 60% C, 29.93 % O, 4.58 % H, 0.086% N, and 3.72% ash. To simulate the 
input to the absorber, a water content equivalent to the inlet temperature of the flue gas on 35 °C was 
added to the flue gas and the CO2 concentration was diluted proportionally. During the calculations, 
all gases is assumed to be ideal, due to the low temperature and pressure.

5 The advanced model, the second model 
The simple model developed in section 4 was limited by the assumption that the absorber always 
captures 90% of the CO2 available in the flue gas. In section 4 it was further assumed that the only 
capacity limitation in the carbon capture plants was the capacity of the reboiler. The capacity 
limitations on the absorber also need to be evaluated. The absorber model is therefore further 
developed to better reflect an actual absorber column. The remaining part of the model, the desorber 
and heat exchanger, is preserved from section 4. The objective of this section is to develop a model 
which calculate the recovery of CO2 in the absorber based on the CO2 inlet concentration. The first 
step is to design an absorber column, and the CO2 recovery can then be calculated based on the 
column specifications.  
 
5.1 Design of absorber 
The development of the absorber model will be based on the methods that is described in chapter 13 
and 14 in the text book Kemiske Enhedsoperationer [46]. The design of the absorber will be 
dimensioned to capture 90% of the CO2 from a flue gas stream containing 15 % CO2 at 35 °C. This 
is equivalent to 13.3 ton/h CO2 and a gas flow on 49000 Nm3/h. It is chosen that the packing material 
is Raschig rings as there are plenty of design information available on this packing material[46].  
 
The absorber column is designed to operate with sufficient margin to the flooding point. Flooding in 
an absorber occur when the flow of gas traveling upwards and the liquid traveling down wards is too 
large such that there is not space enough in the column. This result in the liquid to be pushed out of 
the column and creates flooding. The flue gas flow for the design of the absorber column is therefore 
calculated with a safety margin 𝛽 which ensures that there is space enough for the fluids to travel in 
counter current directions.

different solvents. The first solvent which was tested was 30 wt% MEA. The CESAR campaign at 
Esbjerg power plant was performed on a pulverized coal boiler with a CO2 concentration around

In section 3 to 5 it was investigated how to optimal operate a carbon capture unit most optimal with 
different loads. Both operational philosophies discussed in the previous sections requires fast load 
changes. These load changes were not investigated in the simple and advanced model, where its 
steady state was assumed. In this section the dynamics of start-up/shutdowns be investigated. This

load changes in the carbon capture units and investigate how fast the steam supply should follow a  
start-up. This section has the objective to support the findings in section 3 to 5. This section will also 
provide new knowledge of how flexible operation of a carbon capture unit could occur. 
 
6.1 Time constants, CO2, capacity in the solvent inventory 
To further understand the dynamics of the carbon capture units. The capacity of CO2 in the solvent is 
analyzed. During start-up of a carbon capture unit, the solvent has capacity to capture some CO2 
without need of heat to recover the solvent in the reboiler. This capacity can be used during start-up 
to postpone the steam flow to the reboiler. Thereby making it possible to perform quick start-up or 
ramp up of the utility production without the steam flow to the reboiler follow immediately. The 
flexibility of the plant can hereby be increased which can increase the profitability of the whole 
project. To calculate the time constants the solvent inventory, need to be estimated.  
 
The solvent inventory is assumed to be proportional with the CESAR pilot plant and therefore is 
scaled based on the inventory and the capacity to capture CO2. The CESAR plant had a total inventory 
of 13 ton of pure solvent [50]. The CESAR plant had a capacity on up to 1.2 ton CO2/h, each unit that

capture units stable with a delayed rich solvent flow and a delayed addition of steam. This will be

is achieved. From the response and dynamics of the cases that will be investigated in this section it

7 Discussion and new operation philosophy 
In the modelling of the carbon capture units, a series of underlying assumptions was made. These 
assumptions were needed to simplify the carbon capture units, such that they were possible to model. 
These assumptions will be discussed in this section to increase the understanding and limitations of 
the finding from this project. In this section, a new operational strategy will also be developed based 
on the findings in section 5 and 6. 
 
In section 3.2, the case days was developed with the assumption of constant load during each hour 
and maximum 6 load changes during a day. This is a simplification. ASV 6 usually has more than 
one load during an hour, and the load on the plant varies up and down depending on the need for 
process steam for Novo Nordisk. A general assumption in the modeling was that the carbon capture 
units were always operated in steady state. In section 6, it was found that the steady state time for the 
solvent is around two hours. The assumption that the carbon capture units are always operated in 
steady state, is therefore not fully valid.  
 
In section 5, the advanced model is developed, and it is assumed that the flue gas only consists of two 
components: CO2 and N2. The actual flue gas consists of many different components, most 
importantly O2, which can react with the amine creating solvent degradation products. Degradation 
products are nether assumed in the model, and it must be expected that increased degradation products 
in the solvent will change the CO2 recovery rate and energy consumption. Solvent management in a 
carbon capture unit is considered an important parameter to ensure plant stability and prevent 
unexpected shutdowns.  
 
In the simple model it was assumed that the capture rate was constant on 90%. This was found 
insufficient to reflect an actual absorber column. Therefore, was the model further developed to 
include mass transport in the absorber column. In the advanced model, the capture rate is calculated 
based on a mass balance over the absorber and the mass transfer equations for an absorber column. 
The mass transfer equations assume that the packing material is fully wetted, independent of flow in 
the absorber column. This is valid when the unit is operated with full load.  
When the unit is operated in part load, the solvent flow in the absorber column is also lowered. As it 
is assumed that the loading on the solvent is always increased from 0.21 to 0.5 mol CO2/mol MEA in 
the absorber. The liquid flow is therefore linearly dependent on the mass of CO2 captured. When the 
==PAGE:88==
 
76
flue gas flow is lowered the amount of captured CO2 is reduced, and the solvent flow is therefore also 
lowered. When the solvent flow is reduced to a certain point, there will not be enough solvent to fully 
wet the packing material. Gas channels in the absorber column can then occur where the gas can pass 
untreated. It is therefore important to notice, that the absorber column included in the advanced model 
is only valid in full load or almost full load. Further, the assumption that the solvent is always loaded 
to 0.5 mol CO2/mol MEA, is also only valid in full load operation.  
 
In this section the underlying assumptions of carbon capture have been discussed, and in general it is 
found that the assumptions made are valid. The assumptions made in this project simplify the process, 
and it is therefore expected that the actual values will be a bit different. Depending on the design of 
the carbon capture plant, the solvent will also influent the CO2 recovery and energy requirement. 
However, it is expected that the overall trends will be valid and can be trusted.  
 
7.1 Optimal operational philosophy  
In section 4, 5 and 6 the energy consumption, CO2 recovery and dynamics for a 13.3 ton/h carbon 
capture have been analyzed. Findings from these sections will be used to develop a new operational 
philosophy.  
In section 5 it was found that operation with prioritized flue gas distribution had a lower SRD but 
captured less CO2 than operation with equal flue gas distribution. The objective of this new 
philosophy is that it should be able to capture more CO2 than when operating with prioritized flue 
gas. This is while at the same time having a lower average SRD than operating with equal flue gas 
distribution. Further, the average CO2 recovery should be above 90%. This is to ensure CO2 enough 
to fulfill the contract with the Danish government.  
 
In the analysis of the simple model in section 4, it was found that operating with prioritized flue gas 
reduced the cost of fuel by 1.3 mill dkk/year compared to operation with equal flue gas distribution. 
However, the simple model did not take any dynamics of an absorber model into a consideration. The 
model was therefore further developed to increase the credibility of the results. This was done by 
implementing an absorber model,  which was developed based on data from the CESAR project and 
the general textbook equations for absorber columns.  
In the analysis in section 5 of the advanced model, which included a more detailed absorber model, 
it has been found that there is a large energy penalty for operating with a load below 60%. In the 
==PAGE:89==
 
77
validation for the absorber model, the model was also found to be most accurate in loads above 60%. 
In section 6, it was found that from a process engineering perspective it is possible to operate the 
carbon capture units fast enough up and down to meet the requirements of both the suggested 
operational philosophies. However, fast ramp up/down will tear valves and equipment. It is therefore 
suggested to find an operational philosophy where the ramp up/down is limited.  
 
An operational philosophy is therefore developed such that the time where the load on a carbon 
capture unit below 60 % is limited. The new operational philosophy is: 
• If the flue gas flow is larger than 150% of the capacity of one unit, the flue gas is split equally 
between the 3 units. 
• If the flue gas flow is lower than 150% of the capacity for a single unit, then carbon capture 
unit number 3 is turned off and the flue gas is split between the remaining 2 carbon capture 
units. 
• If the flue gas flow is below 100% of the capacity of a single unit, then carbon capture unit 2 
and 3 is turned off and the full flue gas flow is treated in carbon capture unit 1.

During the validation of the advanced model, it was found in section 5.3 that the model was primarily 
valid when the load on the carbon capture units is above 60%. It was further found that the difference 
between the CESAR data and the model increased rapidly when the load decreased below 60%. At 
20% load the relative SRD for the CESAR increased up to 140%. In the model developed in this 
project it only increases to 113%. It is therefore unsure if there is an actual benefit of operating with 
all three units in the hours with low amount of available flue gas. The results from the CESAR 
campaign further indicates that the CO2 recovery rate decreases when the load on the carbon capture 
unit decreases below 60% due to decreased gas/liquid surface contact. The additional CO2 that can 
be captured when splitting a low flue gas between three units is therefore unsure. The model needs to 
be further developed to include lower absorber performance at low loads if the model shall be valid 
in low load cases.  
In section 6, a time dependent model was developed.  Here it was found that if the reboiler can be 
operated in 10% overload, it is possible to postpone start-up of the reboiler by up to 17 min. If 60% 
of the solvent inventory is stored as lean solvent in the desorber tank, the time dependent model also 
demonstrated that it is possible to operate the carbon capture units with variable load without the 
reboiler needing to follow immediately.  
 
The new operational philosophy developed in this section ensures a minimum of hours below 60 % 
load and never below 50% load. This ensures a high precision of the simulations, and the result can 
hereby be trusted. The new balanced operational philosophy developed in this section further reduces 
the energy consumption compared to operation with equal split flue gas. While the reduced CO2 
recovery using the new operational philosophy is limited compared to operating with equally split 
flue gas, it is recommended to apply the new operational philosophy, which will reduce the fuel cost 
by 0.57 million dkk/year but also the amount CO2 captured with 800/year.

8 Conclusion 
Asnæs power plant unit 6 (ASV6) is a wood chip fired boiler operated with multiple different loads 
during a day. On ASV6, three parallel carbon capture units will be installed, and they need to follow 
the load changes on ASV6. The energy consumption for operation with parallel carbon capture units 
at different loads is investigated in this project using three different models. Each model uses input 
which is found from operational data on ASV6. 
 
Based on operational data, three case days were chosen to reflect an entire year of operation on ASV6. 
The case days illustrated different operational dilemmas, which can occur when parallel carbon 
capture units are installed. The case days showed that part load occurred frequently and the transition 
from part load to full load occurs rapidly. The load profile for the case days were used as input data 
for the models. The objective of the first and simple model was to evaluate the energy performance 
for steady state operation. The model did not include any absorber dynamics but focused on 
developing a desorber model. The model increased the understanding of which processes the energy 
added in the reboiler is used. The model showed a lack of dynamics in the absorber model, and the 
results therefore had a lack of creditability. 
  
In the advanced model, an improved absorber model was developed and implemented into the 
existing simple model. The new absorber model was developed based on experimental results 
together with textbook absorber equations. The model was validated with experimental results from 
the CESAR project at the Esbjerg power plant. The validation showed that the model was reliable in 
full load operation and in part load operation down to around 60% load. At loads below 60%, the 
model showed to underestimate the energy consumption and overestimate the CO2 recovery. From 
the results of the model, it was found that there is an economical benefit of operating with prioritized 
flue gas distribution compared to splitting the flue gas equally between the three carbon capture units. 
The reduced fuel cost was on 5.25 million/year. However, the amount of CO2 captured was reduced 
by 8700 tons/year. The reduced amount of CO2 captured could be a problem, as Ørsted has committed 
to capture at least 430,000 tons/year. In the development of the simple and advanced model steady 
state was assumed. To be able to justify this assumption the dynamics of the carbon capture units 
were investigated.  
 
==PAGE:94==
 
82
To understand the dynamics of a carbon capture unit, a simple time dependent model was developed. 
The model further had the objective to investigate whether it is possible to delay the addition of steam 
to a reboiler during start up and thereby increase the flexibility of the carbon capture units. The model 
showed that it is possible to start a carbon capture unit without the reboiler following immediately. 
The reboiler can be delayed by utilizing the capacity to capture CO2 in the lean solvent. The 
simulations showed that if 50% of the solvent during a shutdown is stored as lean. It is possible to 
delay the start-up of the reboiler by 12 minutes while still achieving stable operation, however most 
of the solvent is then stored as rich solvent and the unit has limited flexibility. It was successfully 
shown that with a 10% increase in rich solvent flow it is possible to reestablish the filling in the 
desorber and thereby recreate the flexibility in the operation. To analyze the limits of the flexibility 
of the carbon capture, a start-up case with 60% of the solvent stored as lean was analyzed. The 
scenario was simulated, and it was found that the reboiler could be delayed by up to 17 minutes while 
still achieving stable operation afterwards. In the analysis of the time dependent model, it was found 
that the flexibility of the carbon capture units could be increased by moving the solvent inventory 
from lean to rich and back again. It was also shown that it is possible to achieve stable operation while 
ramping the solvent flow up and down. This finding showed that it is possible to use an operational 
philosophy which has frequent load changes while stable operation is maintained. 
 
In the validation of the second model, it was found that the time below 60% load should be limited, 
as the SRD increases rapidly at loads below 60%. Based on the results from the time dependent model 
and the advanced model, a new operational philosophy was developed. The new operational 
philosophy limited the time where a carbon capture unit is loaded below 60% and eliminated the time 
below 50% load. This operational philosophy was evaluated against equal and prioritized flue gas 
distribution. It was found that prioritized flue gas distribution resulted in a CO2 recovery rate below 
90%, which will make it difficult to reach the 430,000 tons of CO2 Ørsted has agreed with the Danish 
government to catch. Comparing equal flue gas distribution with the new philosophy showed that it 
is possible to reduce the fuel cost for carbon capture by 0.57 mill/year while only reducing the amount 
captured by 800 tons. Further operation below 60% load occurs when the flue gas is equally 
distributed, at this load the SRD could be underestimated and the capture rate over estimated. The 
benefit for the new operational philosophy could therefore be greater than the simulated results. It is 
therefore concluded that operating with the new operational philosophy is most optimal. It will reduce 
the fuel cost with 0.57 mill. dkk/year and reduce the amount of captured CO2 with 800 tons/year.

9 Future work 
This project has opened for a series of unexploited topics, which can help increase the understanding 
of the operation of parallel carbon capture units with a variable load profile. The project succeeded 
in creating three different models capable of investigating the energy consumption at different 
operational philosophies. However, there is still room for improvement of the model and other critical 
issues which need to be investigated. These topics will be explained in this section. 
 
9.1 Further development of the models 
It would increase the value and the reliability if the model was further developed to tackle some of 
the limitations which exist. This includes modeling of transient periods and additional process 
configuration. Most important for the ASV6 project is to develop a model which is based on the 
specific design of the Aker Just catch 100. Which is the units that will be installed at ASV6. 
 
The simple and advanced models developed in this project covered steady state operation. However, 
with multiple different loads each day on ASV6, and an approximate steady state time of two hours 
it is crucial to understand the energy consumption in transient period between the load setpoints. Such 
a dynamic model would increase the understanding of the carbon capture unit and how it is possible 
to operate with variable levels in the tanks below the absorber and desorber column.  
 
Another development of the model would be to adjust it such it is simulate the Aker just catch 100 
carbon capture units, instead of a generic 30 wt% MEA unit. This would require that the columns are 
adjusted to have the same dimensions and packing material as the just catch 100. The solvent should 
also be changed to have the properties of Aker’s proprietary solvent. To further increase the reliability 
of the model it is needed to include additional process equipment installed on the carbon capture units 
delivered for ASV6 but not included in the model. This includes the energy saverTM developed by 
Aker, which recover waste heat from the lean stream to lower the energy demands in the reboiler. It 
would increase the value of the model if precondition of the gas in the direct contact cooler was 
included in the model, together with the wash sections after the absorber and desorber. 
Compression and liquefaction were outside the scope of this project. It would however be interesting 
to understand the dynamics of how the flow of CO2 from the desorber would impact the compression 
and liquefaction processes. This would make it possible to investigate possible bottle necks in the 
following processing steps. 
==PAGE:96==
 
84
 
9.2 Non modeling future work 
During the modeling and analysis in this project some problems and questions were detected which 
need to be investigated by other methods than modeling. These issues need to be investigated in 
experimental campaigns either on pilot equipment or on the actual carbon capture units which will be 
installed on ASV6. Some of these topics are tear on equipment and degradation of solvent connected 
to ramp up/down. Together with energy performance for operation with load below 50%. 
 
The tear on equipment and how it is impacted by how fast and frequent the unit is ramped up/down 
are in this work not included in the economic evaluation of the operational philosophy. Research on 
how frequent and rapid load changes impact the tear on equipment would make it possible to increase 
accuracy of the economical evaluation. It would further help to increase the knowledge of how the 
key process equipment can be changed pro-actively or hold in stock for future break down. It would 
further be interesting to investigate how the plant should be operated when it treats no flue gas, if the 
desorber should be hold warm and of there should be constant flow through the heat exchanger.  
 
The tear on equipment is also connected to the degradation of the solvent. If the solvent has a 
degradation build-up, it will increase the corrosion rate of the equipment. The degradation rate of the 
solvent could be impacted by fast start up. In a fast start-up the reboiler duty will be high; this could 
create high temperatures on the surface of the reboiler. On the hot surfaces thermal degradation can 
occur. This will start degradation of the solvent, which then further can be accelerated by the 
degradation product from the thermal degradation. The degradation mechanisms need to be 
investigated in relation to ramp up speed. The solvent chemistry needs to be investigated to ensure 
that the operational philosophy do not increase the risk of degradation. Which will increase the cost 
of solvent and increase the corrosion on equipment.  
 
Based on the calculations obtained in this project the time before steady state is reached was estimated 
to around two hours. This calculation was based on the solvent flow and the estimated inventory of 
solvent. However, it is unsure what the actual inventory would be, and it is unsure how fast the carbon 
capture units will transient toward steady state. With several load changes during a day, it is a 
possibility that most of the time the carbon capture units will be operated in transient periods. The 
==PAGE:97==
 
85
time from set point change to steady state is important to investigate to evaluate if a steady state model 
can be used for future load scheduling. 
 
In this section a series of different topics of where future research would increase the knowledge on 
the issue of operating three parallel carbon capture units with variable load. The suggested research 
topics include topics which is important for Ørsted to increase the knowledge before start of 
operation. The suggested improvements also include topics which would be relevant for DTU to 
investigate, and continuously increasing the knowledge within the field of part load operation of 
carbon capture units.
