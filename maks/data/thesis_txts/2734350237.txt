Abstract
The primary objective of this thesis is to explore the potential of spatially offset optical
coherence tomography (SO-OCT). SO-OCT is an innovative imaging tool that promises
improved imaging depth and contrast, and I tested it on mouse tongues as part of my
research. Furthermore, this study also aims to delve into the field of image analysis.
Three key points define the scope of the study. First and foremost, is to assess the
penetration depth accomplished through the use of SO-OCT in comparison to the con-
ventional OCT. Secondly, the investigation of segmentation procedures, with a focus on
the AI software Ilastik, which includes a critical assessment of filtering and segmentation
algorithms, to develop an optimal strategy. Thirdly, the research is based and involved
around histopathologic data, that served as the ground truth.
The motivation behind this task originates from the need to enhance and simplify imaging
methods to detect and characterize cancer. The promising results of SO-OCT as an
innovative imaging technique indicate its potential contribution to early cancer diagnosis
and therapy planning.

Introduction
In the fast-paced environment of improving quality of life, biomedical imaging and bio-
photonics, aim to assist doctors and clinicians with their demanding tasks. Biomedical
imaging has made significant advantages over the last decade and it promises to provide
solutions to a variety of challenges, by providing better accuracy and less invasiveness,
compared to more traditional techniques like histopathology. This thesis project is primar-
ily focused on Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT), exploring the efficacy of Spatially
Offset Optical Coherence Tomography (SOOCT) with a specific focus on soft tissue char-
acterization in the context of mouse tongue tissues.
OCT has greatly contributed to the advancement of tissue imaging, allowing for high-
resolution imaging in the micro-scale. Its ability to provide real-time, cross-sectional im-
ages of tissues has been used in various medical fields, such as ophthalmology and car-
diology. Despite its popularity, OCT has limitations, mainly due to the penetration depth
and resolution trade-off. SOOCT aims to overcome it by offering deeper penetration and
improved contrast, enabling better visualization, which the integration of AI software and
segmentation algorithms can further enhance.
This project involves three objectives:
• Assess the penetration depth enhancement achieved by SOOCT compared to con-
ventional OCT.
• Explore, evaluate and develop an optimal strategy for image segmentation to en-
hance image analysis for mouse tongue.
• Correlate the findings with histopathological data to assess the accuracy of the
modality and the segmentation methodology.
The thesis is structured into several key chapters, each briefly explained as follows:
1. Introduction: Overview of the research.
2. Image Processing and Reference Methods: Background information in tissue imag-
ing, subsequently leading to a comparison with histopathology and techniques uti-
lized for medical image analysis.
3. Optical Coherence Tomography: Techniques and Applications: OCT modality is
explained along with some key information about the sensitivity roll-off and the basic
principles of SOOCT.
4. Materials and Methods: Animal model of the study, sample preparation, and the
imaging protocol followed.
5. Processing and Analysis: Development of workflow and strategy for image process-
ing.
6. Results: Results of the imaging sessions and segmentation procedures.
7. Discussion: Observations and commentary on the results.
8. Conclusion: Key remarks along with ideas for future research.
Lastly, the appendices include the filtering and processing algorithms A, imaging dataset
B, and the abbreviations C.

2.1
Tissue Imaging
Tissue imaging is a critical component of pathology, allowing us to evaluate tissue and
cell images. Clinicians utilize different imaging methods to observe structures faster and
clearer, with better detail and a larger field of view (FOV). Imaging methods, when com-
bined with advanced tools in biology and advanced computation algorithms can uncover
great details. The selection of the right imaging method depends on the application. Every
imaging modality has its strengths and weaknesses, like how deep it can look into tissue
or how clear the image quality is. In most cases, there is always a trade-off i.e. sacrific-
ing resolution to view a larger area. To select the right imaging method, it is important to
understand the question to be answered.[1]

2.2
Golden Standard
Histopathology, a core brunch of pathology, involves the examination of biological tissues
to analyze cell and tissue structures for diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. It is often
referred to as the gold standard, mainly for its contribution towards the diagnosis of neo-
plasia [2]. The process of histopathology includes various steps such as fixation, tissue
processing, sectioning, and staining. Taking a biopsy is a very time-consuming proce-
dure, that might not be applicable in a time-demanding environment, i.e. during surgery.
Additionally, it might cause discomfort and trigger inflammation in the patient.
A common reason to consider obtaining a biopsy sample is to determine if the area has
been affected by cancerous cells. Imaging evaluations are performed by comparing dis-
eased tissue with control or healthy tissue [3].

2.3
Medical Image Analysis
Medical Image Analysis is a field of study focusing on the development of methods for
processing and extracting useful information from medical images. Image processing has
allowed experts to extract quantitative data from images that can be utilized to diagnose
diseases, track progression, and evaluate treatment options [5]. Specifically, medical
image analysis or biomedical image processing techniques can be used to enhance the
contrast of an image, extract specific features, and classify structures within the image.
One of the most common techniques used is denoising algorithms [6], aiming to remove
noise and artifacts, making images easier to interpret and distinguish features.
The most important categories of image analysis and visualization methods are [7]:
• Image Enhancement: Enhancement of image contours and other essential qualities,
reduction of noise and background inhomogeneities.
• Image segmentation: Identification of the contours of an anatomical structure.
• Quantification: Calculation of geometrical or physiological properties.
• Computer-aided detection: Automatic or supervised detection of pathological struc-
tures, such as tumor lesions.
Image segmentation is the process of separating an object of interest from its background.
The goal of this process can be either a mask or an outline. The most popular segmenta-
tion methods include intensity-based, edge-based, region-based, clustering, and neural
networks. Image segmentation is a difficult task, due to issues such as spatial resolution,
poor contrast, unclear boundaries, noise, and acquisition artifacts.[8]

Techniques and Applications
In the following chapter, I will delve into the basic principles of those techniques.
3.1
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
Optical coherence tomography is a non-invasive imaging technique with resolution (<1μm)
enabling high-speed (>millions of A-scans/s) imaging with tissue penetration of up to 2mm,
a value close to that of conventional histopathology [9]. Figure 3.1 illustrates the principle
of Spectral-Domain OCT (SDOCT). Light from a broadband source is headed to the beam
splitter, where it gets divided into a reference and a sample beam. The reference beam
gets reflected by a mirror installed into the reference arm whereas the sample beam gets
through a focusing lens and then to the sample. The backscattered or reflected light from
the sample gets merged with the light reflected from the reference arm. A spectrometer
then analyzes the combined light by dispersing it into its constituent wavelengths, thus
the term spectral-domain. [10] [11].
Figure 3.1: Schematic of a SDOCT system. [10]
As described before, the SD-OCT engine creates a depth profile from the interference of
photons sent into the sample and received back with photons reflected in the reference
arm. This depth profile is referred to as A-scan. When scanning one mirror while collecting
multiple A-scans, a 2-dimensional image is created. This is referred to as a B-scan [12].
==PAGE:14==
3.2
Spatially Offset OCT (SOOCT)
Spatially Offset Optical Coherence Tomography (SOOCT) surpasses some limitations of
conventional OCT by improving the collection of scattered light from a sample. Backscat-
tered light can be categorized into [13] [14] [15]:
• Ballistic light: Does not experience scattering by tissue, carries direct imaging infor-
mation.
• Multiply scattered light: Experiences several scattering events and carries complex
imaging information.
• Diffuse light: Carries little direct imaging information; degrades image quality and
resolution.
Multiply scattered light has the advantage of probing deeper into tissues and thus, carrying
information that would not be attained by ballistic light. SO-OCT successfully utilizes
the Huygens-Fresnel principle [16] to effectively distinguish and capture light that has
undergone multiple scattering. ’Selective collection of the multiply scattered signal leads
to substantially enhanced contrast at depth and reveals mesoscale features obscured by
the limits of detection noise despite favoring multiple scattering’ [13].

3.3
Roll-off
Roll-off is the reduction of signal intensity as depth increases. Due to the spectrometer,
the limited number of sampling points and the bounded resolution of the frequency scan
leads to a decrease in the visibility of the interference fringes. This phenomenon occurs
because the spectrometer’s finite resolution restricts its ability to accurately measure high-
frequency fringes, resulting in a reduction of measurements at greater depths [17] [18]
[19].

Materials and Methods
The following chapter includes a description of the overall experimental procedure, the
sample preparation and handling as well as a description of the setup used. In this chapter,
it is also explained what the imaging protocols were and how they were formed.
4.1
Animal Model
For the imaging studies, tissue samples were prepared by Amanda, a Ph.D. student based
at the Panum, Copenhagen. Samples originated from NMRI nude mice, all female, with
lifespans of 7 weeks. The study included a main group of five healthy mice tongues.
Tongues were harvested after euthanasia (by cervical dislocation) at the 7-week mark.
After extraction, all tongues were fixated in 4% Formaldehyde for 24 hours, followed by
a cleansing process using 70% Ethanol. Finally, the samples were stored in PBS (Dul-
becco’s Phosphate Buffered Saline) and kept refrigerated until further analysis.
4.2
Sample Preparation
Sample handling was always realized in a separate room of the imaging lab, wearing
gloves and taking the necessary precautions. Samples were refrigerated in PBS and
were carefully removed from their sample vials into a culture dish using forceps. After
sample placement, forceps were cleaned using 70% ethanol.
The sample was then carried into the Imaging lab, where it was placed on the sample
stage of the OCT instrument. Additional PBS was always getting carried with me, as the
samples might get dehydrated during an imaging session.
At the end of an imaging session of a tongue, the culture dish was cleaned using 70%
ethanol, before the next sample gets placed into it. At the end of the overall session, the
sample is transferred back to its sample vial and the culture dish gets discarded.
4.3

imaging system, developed by Thorlabs. The setup includes the base unit with central
wavelength of 1310 nm, the imaging probe and the computer unit, as shown in 4.1.

Discussion
Examining Figures 6.1 to 6.5, which include the outcomes of supervised segmentation for
conventional and spatially offset OCT images, an initial observation suggests an increase
in the total area highlighted in green, indicating an enhanced visualization of muscle tis-
sue. Table 6.1 further confirms this finding by consistently showing a larger value for
the green labeled pixels in spatially offset images, thus confirming the hypothesis that
spatial offset OCT improves tissue imaging depth. Additionally, a reduction in the yellow
area (background) in all the merged spatially offset images, compared to the conven-
tional ones, further confirms it, as it indicates a greater detection area. This observation
is further verified in Figures 6.6 to 6.10.
Figure 6.9 highlights the advantages of spatial offset imaging, displaying a great detection
of the base membrane (red area) that is not present in the conventional OCT image. This
observation highlights the potential of spatial offset to improve contrast, hence clarifying
the distinction between tissue layers. Examination of the raw images from M1 to M5, which
can be found in B, shows the advantage of spatial offset in displaying sharper distinctions,
as the boundaries between layers can be recognized more easily.
A key finding of the study is the difference in the quantity of manual labeling required
throughout the supervised annotating process. In regards to Figures 6.12 to 6.16, it is
evident that three out of five images required fewer labels when acquired through spatially
offset OCT, while in one of the remaining two images (Figure 6.15), the difference in the
total pixels labeled is only 0.6%.
It is a very encouraging finding that the software has shown an ability to differentiate even
the smallest tissue layers like the base membrane, which accounts for just 2% of the total
image pixels. This capability highlights the potential of using AI software for automatic
tissue segmentation on spatially offset OCT technology.
Upon examination of the images we can make some observations:
• Figure 6.6: The M1 Merged - Unsupervised image, demonstrates accurate identifi-
cation of the lamina propria and muscle. Notably, there is base membrane detection,
on the left side. However limited recognition on the right side is likely due to image
quality, which affects the distinction of layers even for expert observers.
• Figure 6.7: M2 Merged - Unsupervised image stands out because it shows very
accurate layer identification. It highlights a pattern where the epithelial layer is mis-
labeled as muscle, probably due to pixel intensities that confuse the AI.
• Figures 6.8: Similar to the above, there is a good identification of the muscle and
lamina propria layer in the M3 Merged - Unsupervised image. Identification of the
epithelial layer is also improved, but base membrane recognition is reduced.
• Figure 6.9 & 6.10: Similar to M2 Merged, there is a very good recognition of the
base membrane, appearing slightly better for M4. Muscle and lamina propria layers
are also identified correctly.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the combined capabilities of Optical Coherence Tomography and
AI software for deep tissue imaging. My research focused on testing the hypothesis that
SOOCT has better imaging depth compared to the conventional modality, which I val-
idated by comparing the results of segmentation procedures for conventional and spa-
tially offset OCT. Additionally, a reduction in the manual labeling of pixels for supervised
annotation by Ilastik was observed. The software overall performed more accurate layer
detection on the untrained dataset of spatially offset images. This highlights the room for
improvement in terms of algorithmic and segmentation approaches to improve automatic
detection capabilities within OCT scans on soft tissues.
As mentioned in Chapter 5, ‘Processing and Analysis’ none of the images underwent any
other pre-processing but roll-off correction only, before the analysis in Ilastik. It would
be great if future work could consider advanced denoising and contrast enhancement
algorithms to improve image quality and differentiate tissue layers more distinctly. Addi-
tionally, rather than merging all the different offset images into one, examining individual
offsets may expose the best compromise between contrast enhancement and penetra-
tion depth that will aid segmentation algorithms and software to undertake more accurate
segmentation tasks.
Focusing on the vital sector of cancer therapy, which receives major attention in con-
temporary medical research, several situations for recognizing malignant tissues might
be investigated. This is especially important given our effective identification of the base
membrane—a structure that, like many malignant tissues, is tiny and has unclear borders.
Given the complexities of malignant tissues and the importance of early diagnosis, it is
crucial to further investigate the application of SOOCT in oncology.
In summary, the thesis has not only shown how SOOCT can be used for tissue imag-
ing and segmentation more effectively but also suggested ways of increasing their per-
formance. Future studies should focus on optimizing image preprocessing techniques
and extending the applicability of SOOCT to various tissues to explore new prospects for
biomedical imaging and diagnostics.
