Abstract 
Evaluating large volumes of high-dimensional, text-based ideas in crowdsourcing contests, 
internal search, or venture evaluation often overwhelms human’s bounded attention capacity, 
leading to fatigue, heuristic-induced biases or even a total neglection of potentially promising 
innovations. The promise of generative AI and especially large language models (LLMs), in 
mitigating bounded rationality, has consequentially raised high awareness in the academic 
discourse on idea evaluation. Yet whether AI-driven idea screening should rely on scoring or 
ranking remains unclear. While prior research in human-based evaluations has shown a 
dominance of scoring over ranking, this study finds that for AI-based evaluations neither of both 
approaches holds a universal advantage. Drawing on an empirical analysis of startup idea 
assessments, it is instead revealed that evaluation accuracy is context dependent. While Scoring 
tends to outperform ranking when the construct is relatively well-structured, ranking proves 
superior under more ambiguous evaluation constructs. Moreover, the analysis of a total of 64,685 
evaluations performed across 46 distinct AI evaluation variants, reveal multiple, significant 
interaction effects of additional variables that can shift the relative dominance to one approach 
or the other. The study thereby contradicts assumptions from human-based research, where 
scoring typically prevails.  Additionally, it enhances the academic discourse an the moderating 
effect of task ambiguity on AI’s evaluation accuracy by not just showing its existence, but how it 
interacts specifically with different evaluation setups. 
Managerial Summary 
In innovation contests, venture evaluation, as well as internal idea search, organisations across 
industries often face an overwhelming number of text-based proposals, where finding the best 
ideas quickly can become difficult. Generative AI, and especially large language models, offers a 
new way to manage and evaluate these submissions at scale – However, evaluation quality must 
be in line with managerial expectations. This research compares AI’s evaluation accuracy for two 
key methods – scoring (assigning a numerical score to each idea individually) and ranking 
(providing a comparative order from best to worst). These methods are tested under two types of 
task complexity. First, a broad measure of 'investment attractiveness' (covering aspects such as 
market size and team quality) versus, secondly, a narrower focus on 'idea novelty'. The study finds 
that the method’s accuracy strongly depends on the task it is used for, highlighting that a one-fits-
all solution is not available. While scoring performed better for the simpler construct of idea 
novelty, ranking different items was superior for evaluating investment attractiveness. From a 
managerial perspective, the findings offer practical and copyable guidance on how best to design 
AI-based screening processes when tasked with varying degrees of uncertainty and proposal 
volumes. By clarifying how method choice aligns with task complexity and model configuration, 
this research supports more informed adoption of AI-driven evaluations in organizational settings. 
 
Keywords: AI-Based Idea Evaluation, Evaluation Accuracy, Task Ambiguity, Idea Screening, Idea 
Novelty, Investment Attractiveness, One-Shot Scoring/Ranking, Chain-of-Thought (CoT), 
Ensemble Scoring, Bubble Sort.

Introduction 
The rising adoption of generative AI in idea screening has drawn increasing attention from both 
industry and academia (Shrestha et al., 2019). Evaluating large volumes of ill-structured, high-
dimensional textual data remains a major managerial challenge, as human reviewers can be 
prone to cognitive overload limitations and subjectivity biases (Mueller et al., 2012). In response, 
scholars in management, innovation, and information systems have begun to explore how 
generative AI – especially large language models (LLMs) – might automate or augment the initial 
screening of new ideas (Just, 2024). Literature broadly showed that LLMs can already assist in 
filtering and prioritizing thousands of idea proposals (Bell et al., 2023) and can thereby provide a 
more manageable set of promising business ideas for ongoing, human due diligence (Klein & 
Garcia, 2015). These studies collectively suggest that AI systems can deliver consistent and 
scalable evaluations, though questions persist regarding their reliability in highly uncertain and 
multifaceted contexts (Von Krogh, 2018). In the human-based literature, recent work by Cui et al. 
(2019) shows that scoring methods show a weak dominance over ranking approaches which gets 
especially pronounced when individuals evaluate large sets of ideas, due to cognitive load and 
attention constraints. However, it remains unclear whether these findings extend to AI-based 
evaluations, where cognitive fatigue does not apply in the same way (Shrestha, Krishna, et al., 
2021). Addressing this gap forms a central focus of the present study. Additionally, von Krogh 
(2018) highlights that AI methods may struggle with ill-structured tasks, for instance, where the 
relevant evaluation criteria or final outcomes are not clearly defined in the training data. This study 
extends this perspective by demonstrating how construct ambiguity shapes the accuracy of AI-
driven evaluations dynamically for different algorithmic setups. 
The methodological setting empirically tests 46 algorithmic variant’s relative approximation of a 
human judgement in the context of start-up idea evaluation based on a total number of 64,685 
evaluations conducted by these variants. Building on Tyebjee and Bruno (1984) and related 
venture-screening frameworks, specifically two constructs that differ in complexity are being 
investigated: 
(1) Investment Attractiveness: a multifaceted construct capturing market size, team quality, 
financial potential, and other screening criteria. 
(2) Idea Novelty: a more targeted measure akin to “product uniqueness” (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984), 
focusing on how novel or differentiable the proposed solution is. 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
By comparing how generative AI performs on scoring versus ranking tasks under different levels 
of construct ambiguity, it is assessed whether the accuracy dominance for scoring persists or 
fundamentally changes when the evaluator is an LLM rather than a human. The results reveal 
interesting interaction effects indicating that the same AI algorithm can perform quite differently 
in scoring versus ranking depending on how ill-structured or ambiguous the evaluation target is.  
The main research question (RQ) on how AI-based scoring compares to AI-based ranking to 
accurately predict human judgement is methodologically addressed by comparing correlation 
measures between evaluation-results gathered from a VC expert panel on the one hand, and the 
results gathered from AI’s scoring and ranking evaluations on the other hand. The sub-questions 
(SQ) examine whether adjustments, in the way how ranking and scoring evaluation algorithms are 
set up, can provide a contextualized result on what AI setup performs more accurately. First, it 
investigates whether the accuracy of AI-based ranking declines as the number of evaluated 
proposals grows (SQ1). Second, it tests different multi-agent structures and thereby the effect of 
additional breadth and depth in the evaluation framework on evaluation accuracy (SQ2). Third, it 
evaluates how AI’s behavioural parameters (e.g., temperature settings) shape AI’s tendency to 
produce creative yet potentially hallucinated assessments versus more conservative but possibly 
less innovative outputs (SQ3). 
 
Literature Review 
This literature review examines the established frameworks in venture capital screening and the 
biases that shape both human and AI-driven evaluations. As the empirical analysis is based on 
the context of start-up evaluations, the first section reviews traditional venture capital (VC) 
evaluation frameworks to highlight why early-stage screening is both crucial and complex. The 
second chapter investigates human biases that affect evaluator judgments, addressing the 
question why venture capital firms might even consider the integration of generative AI algorithms 
beyond questions of salaries. The third chapter outlines the parallel phenomenon of algorithmic 
bias, showing that AI systems can suffer from their own data and model limitations. The fourth 
chapter discusses organizational and cultural factors that shape how AI-driven evaluations are 
practically implemented in actual evaluation processes. The final chapter of the theoretical 
background ultimately describes and compares the two primary evaluation modes of scoring and 
ranking along with advanced ensemble or iterative approaches.

Idea Evaluation in Venture Capital 
This chapter provides an overview of how venture capital (VC) evaluations have traditionally been 
conducted and contrasts these with emerging practices that employ generative AI. By examining 
both historical and current approaches, the chapter lays the foundation for understanding the 
challenges and opportunities associated with the integration of AI in early idea detection. In this 
way, a conceptual basis is created for the empirical and methodological analyses in the following 
sections of this thesis. 
Investment Criteria and Idea Novelty: In VC, well-performed screening processes play a crucial 
role for identifying evolving, disruptive, and novel technologies (Baeyens et al., 2006). The 
evaluation typically relies on a variety of criteria, including market factors such as size and growth 
(Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984); the human and social capital of the team, as for instance founder’ 
experience and networks (Colombo et al., 2023; Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984); environmental 
uncertainties like regulatory ambiguity and public opinion (Baeyens et al., 2006); financial 
metrics, including fixed assets such as intellectual property and monetization potential for exit 
(Köhn, 2018; Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984) – and product differentiation (Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984). The 
relative importance of the listed factors varies across financial milestones, such as seed versus 
scale-up stages (Colombo et al., 2023). The aggregated assessment of the construct shaped by 
all these criteria combined is what this thesis calls investment attractiveness. Investment 
attractiveness’ subset – product differentiation – is referred to by the term of idea novelty in this 
study. However, adjacent terms like innovativeness or uniqueness are also common in academic 
discourse. The criticality of the construct of idea novelty for identifying game-changing 
opportunities is frequently highlighted in research on innovation and entrepreneurship 
(Magnusson et al., 2014). Novel solutions can confer first-mover advantages or open entirely new 
markets, promising higher returns if they succeed (Arts & Veugelers, 2015). At the same time, 
extreme novelty often meets scepticism, as it defies established benchmarks and its true 
usefulness is thus harder to predict (Harvey & Mueller, 2021; K. Wang et al., 2019).  Criscuolo 
(2017) characterize an inverted-U relationship: mildly novel ideas attract positive interest, 
whereas exceedingly radical or overly familiar concepts may encounter dismissal. The result is a 
potential bias against breakthrough inventions, which might otherwise yield high returns if 
adequately funded (Harvey & Mueller, 2021). Similarly, Johnson (2024) notes that evaluators' 
judgments about highly novel ideas tend to vary more compared to less novel ideas, resulting in 
reduced agreement on the worth of these new ideas, which is interpreted as a signal of risk.  
Imperfect Filter: While formal evaluation tools aim to improve objectivity they can be resource-
intensive (Eling et al., 2015, 2016). The complexity of evaluating a start-up based on multiple 
==PAGE:11==
4 
 
constructs shaped by uncertainty and information asymmetry (Colombo et al., 2023), combined 
with a high volume of inbound proposals and external searches, highlights the significant 
demands on human evaluators in VC (Baeyens et al., 2006). Furthermore, human evaluators often 
struggle to maintain consistency when faced with high volumes of proposals (Blohm et al., 2016). 
Traditional research in the domain of VC investing has proposed a staged evaluation framework 
to face these burdens – Beginning with a broad, imperfect filter, followed by more in-depth due 
diligence (Fried & Hisrich, 1994; Tyebjee & Bruno, 1984; Wright Robbie, 1998). By structuring the 
screening process, organizations reduce cognitive biases and decision fatigue, ensuring more 
consistent evaluations across later stages (Rietzschel et al., 2010). There are multiple, academic 
frameworks for imperfect idea filtering that have shown promising results for reducing the load of 
ideas. For instance, does the bag-of-lemons (BOL) approach by Klein and Garcia (2015) utilize 
external crowd feedback for eliminating obviously bad ideas. While Klein and Garcia (2015) 
showed that the BOL approach can increase the accuracy of bad idea elimination by 33% 
compared to traditional Likert-scale based evaluations, the underlying risk inherent to imperfect 
filters – namely to accidentally eliminate good ideas – persists (Bell et al., 2023).  Recent work by 
Bell et al. (2023) introduced an idea screening efficiency curve, which illustrates this trade-off. 
Their empirical findings indicate that an optimal model might discard up to 44% of incoming 
proposals correctly, however also missing 14% of viable ones. Nevertheless, in practice, staged 
evaluations with imperfect filters often perform better than exhaustive evaluations by retaining a 
diverse range of possibilities, and by avoiding the risk of focusing on local optima too soon 
(Dahlander et al., 2023; Eling et al., 2016; Hoornaert et al., 2017; Knudsen & Levinthal, 2007)). 
However, also for imperfect filters, the capacity to evaluate proposals is dependent on the firm’s 
resources in domain-specific knowledge or the availability of such expertise in its close network 
(Baeyens et al., 2006). Especially in specialized areas such as biotechnology ventures this 
expertise is scarce (ibid.).  
The Promise of AI: Artificial intelligence is of increasing academic interest in the context of idea 
evaluation as it promises to solve the constraints on human resource availability while mitigating 
subjectivity (Sharapov & Dahlander, 2022). For example, Miric et al. (2023) demonstrated that a 
machine learning (ML) filter achieved 96% accuracy in categorizing patent abstracts, while only 
including 6% of false-positives. Publicly available generative AI, and in particular large language 
models (LLMs), may extend the capabilities of traditional ML by introducing advanced methods 
for synthesizing unstructured data that can be distinct to the data the model has been trained on 
(Mesbah et al., 2023; Von Hippel & Von Krogh, 2016). The paper by Just (2024) poses how 
advanced LLMs cannot just analyse large volumes of textual data but also ensure to detect even 
weak signals of novelty. In essence, the recent academic contributions give a promising outlook 
==PAGE:12==
5 
 
on AI’s streamlining potential – However, imperfect filtering performed by generative AI is still 
typically understood as a pre-stage to a more nuanced, in-depth analysis by human experts 
(Freisinger et al., 2023).  
 
Human Biases 
Human biases can fundamentally shape which ideas are perceived to be useful and novel and 
which are prematurely dismissed (Mueller et al., 2012). Evaluators operating under uncertainty 
draw on cognitive heuristics, risk-avoidance strategies, and social cues – factors that can bias the 
objectivity of any screening process (Mueller et al., 2012; K. Wang et al., 2019). In the context of 
this thesis, understanding human biases remains important for three reasons. First, AI’s 
performance is typically benchmarked against human judgments, so recognizing the strengths 
and weaknesses of these human processes clarifies what better or equal performance might 
mean. Second, it underlines the importance of research on evaluation by AI as it is the human-
part of a bias trade-off when considering automation and automation strategies. The third 
inclusion-rationale for this chapter is, as the paper by Csaszar and Steinberger (2022) put it, the 
importance to consider analogies between literature on AI and organizational theory (OT) and to 
learn from these analogies. Especially with regards to AI’s ever-increasing adoption in 
organizational processes (Felin & Holweg, 2024), it is important to keep the perspective broad and 
to respect human biases not just for the reason to focus on AI’s bias mitigation promise, but also 
for the rationale to acknowledge biases that may be prone not just to humans, but also to AI 
evaluation. Accordingly, first this chapter discusses how human decision-makers respond to 
uncertainty and novelty, as well as the cognitive biases that systematically interfere with objective 
evaluations. Secondly, this chapter highlights the perspective on general subjectivity biases that 
are inherent to humans in evaluation processes. 
Decisions under Uncertainty: Decision-making in entrepreneurial and venture contexts often 
unfolds under conditions of significant uncertainty, where decision-makers must navigate 
complex information, limited time, and potentially high stakes. The classical notion of bounded 
rationality (Levinthal & Workiewicz, 2018; Puranam et al., 2015) stresses that individuals cannot 
process all possible data or foresee every outcome. Instead, they rely on heuristics, rules of 
thumb, or intuitive judgments to cope with these constraints.  As a result, some ideas may be 
discarded prematurely, while others receive attention primarily because they align with simpler, 
recognizable patterns (Mueller et al., 2012).

Cognitive Overload and Heuristics: While bounded rationality describes the general idea that 
human decision-making is limited by cognitive constraints (e.g., memory, attention, processing 
power) and external constraints (e.g., time, available information), cognitive overload describes a 
temporary situation in which these resources are overwhelmed (O’Reilly, 1980). As the number of 
ideas requiring evaluation or their complexity increases, humans eventually face limitations in 
their cognitive abilities, leading to a constrained attention towards certain ideas (Chen et al., 
2023). More practically, may this cognitive overload result in people experiencing fatigue, leading 
to inconsistent or poorer judgment (Hoornaert et al., 2017). As a result, evaluators faced with their 
bounded rationality and especially in environments of uncertainty, unconsciously favour familiar 
ideas over novel ones, as they struggle to process and attend to the vast array of options 
presented to them (Boudreau et al., 2016; Johnson & Proudfoot, 2024; Lu et al., 2019). 
Incremental improvements thereby often gain preference over transformative innovations, 
limiting the identification of groundbreaking outcomes (Dane, 2010). Research on cognitive load 
and bounded rationality has mostly distinguished between two cognitive systems that have 
advantages over each other depending on the type of tasked faced with. Dual-process theories 
distinguish between System 1 (fast, automatic, intuitive) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, rational) 
thinking (Magnusson et al., 2014). Under high uncertainty, System 1 often relies on immediate gut 
feelings, especially when time is short or information is overwhelming (Dahlander et al., 2023). 
While fast thinking can be adaptive – enabling swift choice in high-pressure settings – it may also 
maintain subjective, human biases or encourage the dismissal of novel ideas that do not fit 
established patterns (Mueller et al., 2012). AI in contrast, does not truly embody the intuitive and 
experience-driven qualities of human System 1 thinking, as it relies on rational, programmed ML 
algorithms, which rather parallels System 2 capabilities. However, as the rational processes an AI 
model performs are being performed way quicker than rational reasoning by humans, it has the 
potential to support with rational decision-making in tasks that traditionally required fast and 
intuitive System 1 thinking (K. Wang et al., 2019). 
Cognitive and Social Biases: Beyond the heuristic biases resulting from bounded rationality, 
further cognitive biases are less based on the limitation of resources and more strongly related to 
social cues (e.g., Denrell et al., 2004). Cognitive biases, such as anchoring, confirmation bias, and 
sequence-dependent phenomena, distort attention and lead to inconsistent assessments (Bian 
et al., 2022; Denrell et al., 2004). Anchoring describes the tendency of humans to rely on initial 
impressions and overly influence subsequent evaluations (Denrell et al., 2004). Similarly, 
confirmation bias can lead evaluators to disproportionately focus on evidence that aligns with 
preexisting beliefs of their own mental model while dismissing contradictory data (Piezunka & 
Dahlander, 2014). Sequence-dependent biases highlight how the order of presentation influences 
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
evaluation outcomes. For instance, Bian et al. (2022) found that initial pitches are often evaluated 
less favourably, while later pitches may benefit from an advantage. These dynamics can reduce 
impartiality by creating systematic advantages or disadvantages tied to pitch order (ibid.). 
Additionally, the way ideas are presented (framed) affects people's emotional responses and 
perceptions of usefulness, which in turn influences how they evaluate those ideas (Falchetti et 
al., 2022). Further social biases, including proximity effects and hierarchical norms, tend to favour 
submissions from familiar individuals or higher-status actors (Mollick & Nanda, 2016). For 
example, social proximity can lead evaluators to favour ideas proposed by individuals within their 
network, potentially increase homogeneity and reduce diversity (ibid.). Together, these cognitive, 
social, and group-level biases systematically disadvantage uncommon proposals, lower-status 
actors, and ideas that deviate from established norms (Bian et al., 2022; Denrell & Fang, 2010). 
While human evaluators are vulnerable to these cognitive and social biases, research suggests 
that AI-based algorithms holds potential for mitigating many of these biases (Hoornaert et al., 
2017). By applying standardized criteria across submissions, AI could increase fairness and 
consistency if properly implemented (Banken et al., 2019; Bell et al., 2023). Computational tools 
may reduce cognitive burdens, potentially enabling organizations to focus on top-tier candidates 
(Lane et al., 2024). For example, AI can mitigate sequence bias by randomising the order of 
evaluation. Even if AI is also susceptible to sequence bias, the fact that it does not suffer from 
mental fatigue and can therefore be run several times – each time in a randomised order – would 
virtually eliminate any sequence (dis)advantage. Additionally, AI’s ability to provide consistent 
evaluations can mitigate sequence-dependent and social biases, such as proximity or groupthink 
tendencies (Piezunka & Schilke, 2023). According to Banken (2019), algorithms can limit the 
number of ideas that reach human reviewers at any one time, filtering out low-potential or 
repetitively similar content. This filtering process may help mitigate cognitive biases that derive 
from humans relying on heuristics to cope with excessive load, such as salience bias (where 
prominent but unsubstantiated ideas attract disproportionate attention) or herding bias (the 
tendency to favour popular proposals regardless of their intrinsic value). By systematically 
classifying and grouping ideas, algorithms may present evaluators with a best-first order, ensuring 
reviewed subsets meet a threshold of relevance or quality (Banken et al., 2019; Freisinger et al., 
2023).

AI Biases 
Bias in artificial intelligence (AI) represents a persistent challenge in screening and evaluating 
ideas and innovation proposals (Lebovitz et al., 2021). Although AI promises consistency and 
scalability, the quality of its judgments hinges on several contingent factors, such as reliance on 
historical data, oversimplification of context, and the difficulty to handle moral and cultural 
considerations (Loewenstein & Mueller, 2016). This section synthesizes key insights on how 
biases emerge in AI systems and their implications for uncertain, high-stake environments. 
Bias from Data Limitations: Biases in AI systems arise from two key data-related limitations: The 
representational bias embedded in unbalanced datasets and the structural challenges posed by 
incomplete data in ill-defined scenarios. Large language models (LLMs), for example, often reflect 
predominantly English-speaking perspectives in their training data, which can skew evaluation 
outputs and, for instance, lead to misinterpreting the innovativeness of business models tailored 
to non-Western contexts (Boussioux et al., 2024). In addition to representational bias, data 
incompleteness in uncertain environments poses significant challenges (Jia et al., 2024; Von 
Krogh, 2018). AI-based processes tend to thrive in well-structured environments with consistent 
data, such as scenarios involving quantifiable success metrics like revenue growth (ibid.). 
However, in ill-structured environments that may not be represented in the training data models 
often show biased or inaccurate behaviour (ibid.). The reliance on probabilistic reasoning based 
on historic training-data can be insufficient for addressing fundamentally unknowable or 
indeterminate future states (Townsend et al., 2024). Unlike humans, who can adaptively integrate 
tacit knowledge, moral considerations, and intuition (Hagtvedt et al., 2024), AI systems struggle 
with Knightian uncertainty, where probabilities cannot be assigned to outcomes (Townsend et al., 
2024). This is particularly important because in many contexts submitted ideas can still be 
immature (Hoornaert et al., 2017). This limitation causes AI to systematically underestimate high-
uncertainty ventures, potentially abandoning disruptive innovations (ibid.). Csaszar et al. (2024) 
agreeingly observe that purely data-driven models risk homogenizing strategies, overlooking rare 
but transformative ideas that fall outside historical patterns. If disruptive ideas are systematically 
disadvantaged, that does not only pose a risk to VC firms using automated screening systems, 
but also to the innovativeness of the start-up environment (Froehlich et al., 2016).  
Systematic Biases: As intentionality and vision are core criteria in venture evaluation (Köhn, 
2018), the blind-spot on disruptive ideas can moreover be intensified as AI lacks semantic 
understanding (deep comprehension of meaning) and intentionality (goal-directed or purpose-
driven behaviour) and may therefore not be able to truly understand or value an entrepreneur’s 
vision (Townsend et al., 2024). The possibility to not understand the intention also becomes a 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
problem when it leads to misinterpretation of results, which can cause a model and subsequently 
a human to act on information that does not address the original intention (Lane et al., 2024). For 
instance, may a criterion in venture screening be that the screening algorithm is supposed to filter 
for environmentally sustainable investment strategies. An LLM might misinterpret ‘sustainable’ as 
‘financially sustainable’ and provide unrelated advice. Correspondingly to an entrepreneur’s long-
term vision, Townsend et al. (2024) raises concerns regarding AI models’ long-term sight. What 
they describe as competitive recursion means that AI models can overlook underlying market 
dynamics and the iterative nature of their recommendations (Townsend et al., 2024). 
Especially when human reliance on AI’s output is not aligned with the models’ true capabilities, 
hallucination and post-hoc rationalization are further systematic threads inherent especially to 
LLMs (Tonmoy et al., 2024). When an LLM is faced with the trade-off between its goal (to satisfy 
the human request) but on the other hand does not have factual training data directly answering 
this question, it can try to generate a coherent, plausible, yet wrong response to fill the blanks 
(ibid.). These responses can often be easily detected by simple human-lead fact-checking 
mechanisms yet remain difficult to address in automated systems without humans in the loop, or 
alternatively if human trust is uncalibrated and the human augmenting the system trusts it blindly 
(Lee & See, 2004). Blind trust is unfortunately intensified by what is called AI’s false confidence, 
which describes that LLMs might present their outputs with undesirable certainty, misleading 
users about the reliability of the information (Bai et al., 2022), as well as by sycophancy, which 
refers to the tendency of LLMs to agree with or provide overly positive responses to users, 
regardless of the validity or reasonableness of the input (Sharma et al., 2023). 
A contrasting effect on trust is derived from AI’s inconsistency. While AI models are statistical and 
probabilistic, they also have inherent randomness in order to make the output less deterministic 
and feel more human-like (Zhang et al., 2023). However, therefore, such models can also produce 
varied responses to similar or identical inputs, which can reduce the reliability of their answers 
and confuse human agents (Elazar et al., 2021). While the biases of post-hoc rationalization and 
false confidence rather support overconfidence in the model’s capabilities, inconsistencies lead 
to opposite trust dynamics (Lane et al., 2024).  
In addition to the heuristic biases often associated with human raters, large language models also 
face contextual biases that affect their evaluation accuracy (Leng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). In 
particular, Liu et al. (2024) note that these models follow a U-shaped performance curve that 
focuses predominantly on the beginning (primacy) and end (recency) of long inputs, making it 
difficult to retrieve and interpret information in the middle. Meanwhile, Leng et al. (2024) show 
that beyond a certain threshold of input length, adding more context can actually worsen 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
responses by retrieving irrelevant or low-quality content – an effect they term ‘relevance dilution’ 
(Leng et al., 2024). 
Transparency Paradox: Beyond structural limitations of AI systems, transparency challenges 
arise from the difficulty of interpreting or scrutinizing AI-generated decisions, particularly in high-
stakes contexts like venture evaluation. This opacity – sometimes called the black-box 
phenomenon – undermines accountability, as stakeholders lack sufficient insight into why certain 
results appear (Rahman, 2021). Efforts to enhance transparency, such as publishing model 
architectures or partial weighting criteria, often fail because the underlying logic remains too 
complex for users to interpret (Lebovitz et al., 2022). This is known as the transparency paradox 
and attempts to explain that complex systems can overwhelm users with technical details, 
offering no meaningful clarity (ibid.).  
These insights underscore the fragility of purely data-driven AI systems in dynamic, high-
uncertainty domains like venture evaluation. To address these challenges, researchers advocate 
for hybrid approaches that combine algorithmic efficiency with human oversight (Eapen, 2023; Jia 
et al., 2024; Just et al., 2024).  
 
Implementation of AI in Organizational Processes 
After reviewing the biases that can emerge on both the human and the algorithmic side, this thesis 
also examines academic insights on the organizational realities of adopting AI in venture-capital 
screening. Even though scholars acknowledge efficiency-gains and formality of automated, 
human-out-of-the-loop systems (Raisch & Krakowski, 2021), the tendency is to suggest that AI 
systems serve in augmented decision-making with human agency (ibid.). However, in high-stake 
decision-making, where transparency is important, automated concepts could face significant 
managerial resistance and implementation may fail due to such adoption barriers (Gama & 
Magistretti, 2023), why accuracy alone is insufficient to guarantee the success of AI-driven 
evaluations. Therefore, this chapter explores how human-AI collaboration (HAI-C) unfolds in real 
contexts, highlighting four key considerations: Explainability, the inherent tension within trust 
dynamics, the influence of organizational culture, as well as suggested hybrid models.  
Human-out-of-the-loop vs. Human-in-the-loop: This section explores three collaborative 
models of human-AI interaction: Human-in-the-loop (HITL), Human-out-of-the-loop (HOTL), and 
hybrid systems. HOTL models (also referred to as automated systems) delegate entire decision 
flows directly to AI, with limited or no human intervention (Raisch & Krakowski, 2021). Advantages 
are that automating routine tasks reduces cognitive load and mitigate fatigue-induced 
==PAGE:18==
11 
 
inconsistencies, potentially leading to more systematic outcomes (Banken et al., 2019). However, 
HOTL models face limitations in unstructured domains and complex innovation tasks require 
subjective judgment, social learning, and reflexivity where AI may not fully replicate such (Haefner 
et al., 2021). For example, AI may excel at identifying patterns but struggle with context-specific 
nuances (Choudhary et al., 2023; Puranam, 2021). In contrast, HITL collaboration (also referred 
to as augmented systems) keeps human agency while leveraging AI’s speed and pattern 
recognition capabilities (Dellermann et al., 2020; Raisch & Krakowski, 2021). HITL are often 
differentiated with regards to (a) the degree of human agency, and (b) with regards to the structure 
of how humans and artificial intelligence split tasks. The level of human agency ranges from 
exploitation – where the AI assists in processing information and managing data but operates 
under limited autonomy – to exploration and human-as-overseer mechanisms – where the AI 
automatically evaluates ideas, while humans oversee and monitor this evaluation process 
(Haefner et al., 2021). Considering the way how humans and AI split tasks, researchers mostly 
distinguish between two variants. First, sequential augmentation, which occurs when tasks are 
broken down into subtasks where the output of one subtask serves as the input for the next. This 
means that each step depends on the previous one, creating a linear flow of work. (Choudhary et 
al., 2023). In parallel augmentation (also referred to as ensemble) subtasks are performed 
simultaneously, and they contribute to a common outcome, which allows for more efficiency as 
multiple tasks can be completed at the same time without waiting for the completion of others 
(ibid.). In more advanced configurations, HITL systems enable iterative collaboration, forming a 
cycle of engaged augmentation (Lebovitz et al., 2022) or tight coupling (Raisch & Krakowski, 2021) 
where evaluators refine AI’s outputs and provide feedback for improvement. Such hybrid systems 
capitalize on AI’s speed and consistency while leveraging human intuition for evaluating 
subtleties (Wood & Williams, 2014). Additionally, retaining a measure of human agency, such as 
enabling users to adjust AI-suggested rankings, enhances trust and acceptance of these systems 
(Hammedi et al., 2011). However, the paper by Vicente & Matute (2023) adds a hurdle to that 
simple solution. Specifically, they suggest that when humans rely on biased AI recommendations 
in augmented settings, the initial assistance from AI can lead to the inheritance of biases from the 
AI to the human collaborators (Vicente & Matute, 2023). In this case humans implement biased 
views in their own mental models that they have not been prone to before (Vicente & Matute, 
2023), highlighting the importance of understanding how the sequence of AI interactions can 
affect the overall effectiveness of collaborative processes involving AI. 
Trust in AI and Adoption Barriers: Cognitive trust refers to the belief in the reliability and 
competence of an automated system and is shaped by its tangibility, transparency, reliability, and 
responsiveness (Glikson & Woolley, 2020). While perceptions such as performance expectancy – 
==PAGE:19==
12 
 
the belief that AI will improve job outcomes, streamline processes or enhance decision accuracy 
– act as strong drivers of acceptance (Cao et al., 2021), the adoption of AI tools in venture 
screening also hinges on opaque questions. First, black-box models generate managerial 
scepticism due to their lack of transparency, which hinders understanding of how decisions are 
made (Lane et al., 2024; Magni et al., 2024). Transparency paradoxes complicate this dynamic, as 
it highlights that while the use of technology is expected to increase transparency by making 
information more visible, it can paradoxically lead to reduced transparency (Lebovitz et al., 2022) 
– AI specifically deals with the conceptual issue of fundamental opacity, which describes that 
achieving true transparency in AI systems may be technically challenging or even impossible due 
to the inherent, complex structure (ibid.). Especially in high-stake decision-making this could 
raise concerns regarding accountability (Lane et al., 2024). However, research suggest that there 
are two opposing tendencies for trust in AI – algorithmic aversion and algorithmic appreciation. 
Algorithmic aversion refers to the hesitation and distrust individuals have towards using 
algorithmic outputs, even when these algorithms demonstrate better performance than human 
judgment, while humans prone to algorithmic appreciation prefer algorithmic over human advice 
(Logg et al., 2019). Both can lead to negative consequences. Misuse, on the one hand, occurs 
when individuals incorrectly use automation, often by relying on it in situations where it should 
not be trusted, leading to avoidable errors. This risk is amplified when AI systems present overly 
simplified or deterministic explanations, creating a false sense of certainty (Balasubramanian et 
al., 2022; Gama & Magistretti, 2023). The opposing idea of disuse refers to situations where 
individuals do not utilize the automation's capabilities, which can result in missed opportunities 
for efficiency or effectiveness gains (Glikson & Woolley, 2020; Lee & See, 2004). Studies have 
shown that especially humans with high knowledge in a certain domain are prone to this aversion 
(Logg et al., 2019). To avoid both – disuse and misuse – researchers emphasize the importance of 
‘calibrated’ or ‘appropriate’ trust (Lee & See, 2004). For trust to develop effectively, there needs to 
be a match between what people initially expect from an AI system and how well that system 
actually performs (Glikson & Woolley, 2020). 
Besides the perceived reliability that an AI performs a certain task matching the human 
expectation (cognitive trust), trust in automated systems needs to be addressed as a multi-
dimensional construct, also involving emotional dimensions based on perceived integrity, and 
benevolence (ibid.). The emotional tensions are especially important to acknowledge when 
considering the question of managerial redundancy and the fear of job replacement (Choudhary 
et al., 2023). In this regard, Choudhary et al. (2023) have introduced the concept of Human 
Redundancy Probability (HRP), which is defined as the likelihood that AI outperforms humans, 
making them redundant. However, paralleling with the insights from the previous chapter on 
==PAGE:20==
13 
 
human-AI collaboration, they found that certain collaborative settings can mitigate the risk of 
human redundancy. However, as indicated the issue of trust is multi-facetted and is ultimately 
moderated by several factors, such as the organizational culture (An et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2017) 
and the formality of its structures (Maher & Fisher, 2012; Sharapov & Dahlander, 2022), how fast 
AI can unfold its usefulness (Harvey & Mueller, 2021). 
To mitigate the listed challenges, researchers recommend several strategies aimed at reaching 
appropriate trust and improving adoption. First, incremental rollouts of AI systems allow 
organizations to build familiarity and address scepticism incrementally (Freisinger et al., 2023; 
Keding & Meissner, 2021). Secondly, user education initiatives that enhance understanding of AI 
capabilities and limitations can help bridge the gap between cognitive and emotional trust 
(Glikson & Woolley, 2020). Third, explainability in the model design through interpretable 
algorithms, visualizations, or simplified models (Lane et al., 2024) and the implementation of 
human-in-the-loop systems to ensure that managers retain agency and contextual judgment in 
decision-making processes (Cao et al., 2021). Ultimately, balancing interpretability, user trust, 
and technological sophistication is highlighted as a core foundation for utilizing AI’s full potential 
in large-scale decision support (ibid.). 
 
Scoring, Ranking, and advanced Evaluation Algorithms 
Having established the theoretical context of human and AI biases, this chapter examines the 
core mechanisms of idea evaluation considered in this study: Scoring, ranking, and their 
advanced algorithmic extensions. By detailing these methods and the biases these approaches 
specifically may encounter or mitigate, the chapter sets the foundation for the empirical 
assessment of how generative AI can refine or replicate human pre-screening outcomes. 
Scoring and Ranking: Selecting the most promising ideas from a potentially large pool is a 
persistent challenge in innovation and investment contexts (Bell et al., 2023). Two predominant 
methods – scoring and ranking – shape how evaluators process, compare, and ultimately identify 
top proposals. Innovation literature have explored how these methods, when utilized by humans, 
vary not only in their relative accuracy under different conditions, but also in the biases they may 
engender, particularly when evaluators face cognitive constraints, sequential decision-making, 
or attention fatigue (Cui et al., 2019). Scoring, as the first method introduced, treats each idea in 
quasi-isolation, assigning a numeric or categorical value that reflects an evaluator’s judgment 
without consideration for other objects being evaluated (Blohm et al., 2016). Because scoring 
does not mandate a full comparison of each proposal against every other, it can be less cognitively 
==PAGE:21==
14 
 
demanding – particularly as the number of alternatives expands (Cui et al., 2019). Ranking, by 
contrast, builds a complete order of submissions, emphasizing their relative standing (Blohm et 
al., 2009; Cui et al., 2019). It does not assess objects in absolute terms but rather in relation to 
one another (ibid.). This means the ranking algorithm inherently evaluates business cases with 
full awareness of the alternatives in the set. This approach can feel intuitive for smaller pools of 
proposals – evaluators quickly see how ideas stack up against one another. However, evidence 
suggests that, as the number of evaluated ideas grows, the burden of maintaining consistent 
comparisons across all pairs escalates (Criscuolo et al., 2021).  
Method-Specific Biases: Despite these differences in mental effort and accuracy, both scoring 
and ranking are prone to certain systematic biases that can affect evaluators’ perceptions. 
Scholars highlight two categories: biases especially characteristic of scoring tasks and those that 
manifest more strongly in ranking tasks. Still, neither method is immune from human cognitive 
tendencies. 
Scoring is typically associated with judgment tasks (Blohm et al., 2016). Evaluators rate each idea 
on a numeric or ordinal scale, opening the door to issues like scale usage bias – which describes 
the tendency to stay within a narrow band of scores if uncertain (Pimentel, 2019) – and extremity 
bias – in which a rater overestimates the importance of extreme, but very rare cases (Denrell & 
Fang, 2010). These phenomena, intensified when raters lack calibration guidelines, can lead to 
compressed or inflated distributions that undercut the discriminatory power of the scale (Bian et 
al., 2022). Additionally, each new submission can be subtly anchored to the scores assigned to 
prior ones (Cui et al., 2019). For instance, if the first few pitches receive moderately high scores, 
subsequent ones might be judged relative to those anchors, skewing the overall grading curve. 
While anchoring can affect ranking as well, numeric scoring can mask or normalize these shifts if 
evaluators do not regularly recalibrate (Blohm et al., 2016). 
As ranking requires pairwise or relative comparisons, it is more vulnerable to sequence-based 
biases (Elhorst & Faems, 2021; Knudsen & Levinthal, 2007) causing skewed evaluations such as 
‘last-is-best’ tendencies. Here, Bian et al. (2022) found that this recency effect, partly advantages 
later submissions over initial ones due to evaluators having a fresh memory of the most recent 
proposal. However, the researchers notice that these findings are conflicting previous research 
that found the opposite effect of primacy bias, in which the first item frames subsequent 
judgments when being evaluated in a continuous group debate (Bian et al., 2022; Jordan et al., 
2014). Both recency and primacy effects highlight that contextual or memory constraints can 
overshadow objective proposal quality. Fatigue further compounds these difficulties in ranking 
(Hoornaert et al., 2017) – with dilution of relevance as its AI-based analogy (Leng et al., 2024). As 
==PAGE:22==
15 
 
the number of options rises, keeping each new submission in mind relative to all previously ranked 
ideas can become mentally exhausting (Elhorst & Faems, 2021). By contrast, scoring tasks may 
let evaluators re-ground each idea in standardized criteria, potentially reducing the relative 
impact of how an item compares to the immediately preceding entries (Cui et al., 2019). 
Recent findings (Cui et al., 2019) indicate that scoring generally outperforms ranking, when 
humans are the evaluators. As Cui et al. (2019) have demonstrated, scoring’s relative advantage 
grows with the size of the set, providing a more stable mechanism for comparing large pools of 
ideas. Ranking might still prove viable for smaller or more homogeneous sets, especially when 
time constraints are limited and the cognitive overhead of pairwise comparisons is manageable 
(Blohm et al., 2016). These findings, however, do not necessarily translate to generative AI systems 
that, for instance, do not experience fatigue in the same way humans do. Whereas humans may 
struggle to maintain consistent comparisons as the number of proposals grows, generative AI can 
theoretically handle large pools of data using uniform computational methods, potentially 
altering the relative efficacy of scoring versus ranking in this new setting. Accordingly, the question 
of whether scoring still outperforms ranking when generative AI itself conducts the evaluation 
remains unanswered, forming a central focus of the present research. By systematically 
examining the two methods in an AI-driven context, this thesis addresses the gap in existing 
literature and explores how distinct evaluation modes operate when free from human cognitive 
limitations. Therefore, this thesis provides an empirical assessment on the following major 
research question: 
RQ: “How does scoring compare to ranking in a generative AI model for detecting novelty and 
investment attractiveness, relative to human benchmarks?”

Advanced Scoring and Ranking Algorithms: In developing AI-driven evaluations for large sets of 
ideas or proposals, researchers and practitioners have turned to more sophisticated algorithms 
that extend beyond simple scoring or ranking methods. These advanced evaluation algorithms 
can include ensemble approaches, Bubble Sort Rankings, or other specialized techniques, often 
adopted to improve overall accuracy, mitigate biases, or address the scale of contemporary 
screening tasks (Park et al., 2022; You Yang et al., 2011). Building on insights from both machine 
learning and organizational theory, scholars have proposed that combining multiple evaluative 
perspectives can yield more robust outcomes (Beretta, 2019; Girotra et al., 2010).  
A prominent trend in predictive analytics and decision support is the application of ensemble 
models, which involves aggregating outputs from multiple independent models (Abhinand & 
Poonam, 2022; Nourani et al., 2018). In straightforward screening contexts, an AI may rely on a 
single algorithm, such as a neural network, decision tree, or large language model, to judge the 
quality of a proposal – However, ensemble methods capitalize on the idea that no single model 
perfectly captures every dimension of a complex task (Panja et al., 2022; Park et al., 2022). By 
synthesizing multiple weak agents into a meta-model or stacked framework (see figure 1), 
ensembles can better handle the variance or noise inherent in data (Z. Wang & Srinivasan, 2017). 
From a management perspective, this concept parallels the wisdom of the crowd, which suggests 
that diversified or partially independent judgments often outperform lone expert opinions, 
provided each source is at least minimally competent (Csaszar, 2018). This multi-layered tree 
logic furtherly parallels organizational theories advocating for sequencing initial broad scans 
followed by deeper collaborative scrutiny (Zhou et al., 2019). In an AI-based evaluation pipeline, 
using multiple sub-models can approximate the same benefit that arises when different 
evaluators bring their own biases or heuristics but collectively arrive at more accurate best ideas 
(Girotra et al., 2010).

such setups predecessor and successor agents can designed without an individual evaluation 
focus or can be assigned a specific role such as being critical when assessing the output from the 
predecessor (Zheng et al., 2024). 
One iterative process within the sphere of ranking methods is Bubble Sort. While also iterative it 
is distinct from the CoT logic as it does not directly focus on the reassessment of predecessor 
outputs. Traditionally this method is mostly adopted for simple, rule-based rankings (e.g., sorting 
numbers by their value). However, in the context of this thesis, it will be adopted for sorting 
evaluation elements by their indicators of success (i.e., by idea novelty and investment

list iteratively, and just by considering the question of which element in a neighbouring-dual is

Behaviour Parameter: In generative-AI-based evaluations, the performance of scoring or ranking 
may also hinge on how the AI’s behaviour parameters are tuned (Christensen et al., 2017). For 
instance, large language models rely on temperature or other sampling parameters to govern how 
creative or conservative their outputs are (Zhang et al., 2023). Adjusting these parameters can 
shift the system’s tendency to produce broad or safe answers, affecting how thoroughly the AI 
discerns differences among proposals. If a model is set to produce highly exploratory responses, 
it might capture outlier, high-variance ideas better, but at the risk of generating hallucinated or 
irrelevant evaluations (Tonmoy et al., 2024) . Conversely, a conservative parameter setting might 
skew the AI toward incremental or mainstream solutions (ibid.). In an organizational analogy, tight 
processes prioritize convergence on known success factors, whereas loose processes encourage 
the detection of atypical gems (Zhou et al., 2019).  
Expanded Research Question: As conventional wisdom suggests, can additional depth (i.e., 
iterative refinement) and breadth (i.e., ensemble expansions, diverse domain signals) improve 
overall evaluation quality (Csaszar, 2018; Shrestha, He, et al., 2021). In the realm of AI-based 
evaluation this addresses the underlying evaluation framework – meaningly, the question of how 
different AI agents interact to enhance overall accuracy (Zheng et al., 2024). However, addressing 
breadth and depth can be understood as two-edged, where depth and breadth is not just defined 
by the evaluation framework. It is also defined by the inherent depth of the opaque evaluation 
structures of an individual agent, or in other words, by the parametric depth of the underlying AI 
model (Chen et al., 2023). While research and benchmarking tests suggest a notion of ‘bigger-is-
better’ meaning that, ceteris paribus, larger and deeper AI models outperform smaller models 
(Ahuja et al., 2023), they differ in their cost structure for systematic, interface-based use (OpenAI, 
2025). While this study does not aim for benchmarking different models, the question of how 
model depth unfolds under different constructs, task characteristics, or evaluation methods 
==PAGE:26==
19 
 
completes the two-edged perspective. Furthermore, it serves a managerial interest. In contrast, 
the systematic variation of AI behaviour parameters does not play a direct economic role use 
(OpenAI, 2025). However, altering behaviour parameters is found to have the availability of 
reducing different AI-inherent biases (Zhang et al., 2023), and thereby indirectly shape economic 
value. However, how these effects unfold for scoring vs. ranking remains underexplored. Hence, 
this study empirically addresses these research gaps by introducing sub-questions to the main 
research questions. Each sub-question contains a specific empirical question as well as an 
exploratory investigation of the ‘how’ that goes beyond merely analysing the existence of effects. 
To maintain clarity, this thesis adopts one main research question (RQ: “How does scoring 
compare to ranking in a generative AI model for detecting novelty and investment attractiveness, 
relative to human benchmarks?”) and subsequent sub-questions (SQ): 
SQ1: “Do ranking evaluations by generative AI suffer if the number of evaluations elements 
increases and is the organizational choice on scoring vs. ranking therefore context-dependent?” 
SQ2: “Does an increase of breadth and depth in the evaluation design (adding weak agents) or 
model choice (AI model size) increase evaluation accuracy?” 
SQ3: “Does the deviation of behavioural settings of the AI evaluator affect the evaluation’s 
accuracy under different circumstances?” 
 
Methodology 
This study develops and tests generative AI approaches for evaluating startup business cases – 
specifically measuring their novelty and investment attractiveness – in comparison to expert 
human judgments. Individual variant-based accuracy was tested with Spearman’s / Pearson’s 
test for ranked / metric data, while correlation comparisons rely on Fisher Z-tests to gather 
insights on local dominances. Interaction and potential moderation effects of environmental 
conditions on the relationship between groups and the benchmark were tested on significance by 
using multi-variable regression analyses with Microsoft Excel's Data Analysis ToolPak. In essence, 
the overarching aim of the methodological setup is to determine whether, and under what 
conditions, AI-based scoring and ranking methods can approximate upon the decisions that 
human professionals would. As high-stakes investment choices ultimately rest with human 
investors, expert VCs serve as the decisive benchmark for this study. Their evaluations, collected 
via a structured questionnaire, provide the reference standard against which AI performance is 
measured. Each expert assessed real-world business cases (selected and summarized from 
==PAGE:27==
20 
 
Shark Tank pitches) along two critical dimensions – novelty and investment attractiveness – using 
a scored (0 to 5) format, as existing evidence indicates that scores more accurately perform in 
human evaluations than pure ordinal rankings.

AI evaluations (scoring and ranking) are tested. As scores are metrically scaled (interval scale), 
while rankings only inherit an ordinal scale, they are not directly comparable with each other.

comparisons. Accordingly, correlation comparisons are based on Spearman’s rank-based

Addressing SQ2: To investigate whether increasing the depth (e.g., CoT) or breadth (e.g.,

1. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Scoring (multiple iterative passes)

Retrieving Human Benchmark 
To establish a reliable human benchmark for evaluating the performance of generative AI models, 
32 VC experts were recruited. The selection process aimed to ensure that participants possessed 
significant experience and relevant domain expertise. An online survey was developed using Wix 
Forms, which allowed for secure and efficient data collection. The survey included questions 
designed to evaluate the perceived novelty and investment attractiveness of various business 
models, on a scale from zero to five. 
Selection Criteria for Participants and Recruitment: The experts were recruited using the 
LinkedIn Sales Navigator. The process involved identifying applicable VC firms within the DACH 
region (Germany, Austria, and Switzerland) and saving them as account leads. A specific persona 
was set based on the expert criteria outlined below, allowing for targeted suggestions of suitable 
VC professionals. Potential participants were contacted directly with a request to participate in 
the questionnaire and were offered an individual post-research presentation of the findings as an 
incentive. 
Participants were required to meet specific qualifications to ensure the relevance and quality of 
their evaluations. The criteria included VC employees with at least three years of investment 
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
experience or significant involvement in the screening of business cases within their investment 
cluster. Furthermore, experts needed to have the majority of their investment expertise in the 
domain of consumer goods. 
This approach ensured that experts evaluated business models within their areas of expertise, 
enhancing the validity of their assessments. 
Ethical Considerations & Confidentiality: Ethical guidelines were followed to protect the rights 
and confidentiality of the participants. Prior to participation, experts were presented with an 
online consent statement at the beginning of the survey hosted on Wix Forms. The statement 
detailed the purpose of the study, the procedures involved, and their rights as participants, 
including the voluntary nature of their participation and the right to withdraw at any time without 
penalty. Experts indicated their informed consent by selecting an “I agree” checkbox before 
proceeding to the evaluation tasks. To maintain confidentiality, each expert was assigned a 
unique identification code not linked to any personal information. Personal identifiers, such as 
names or contact details, were stored separately from the evaluation data and were accessible 
only to the primary researcher. The survey was configured to collect IP addresses solely for the 
purpose of identifying and excluding duplicate responses. Participants were informed of this data 
collection in the consent statement at the beginning of the survey. After verifying the uniqueness 
of responses, IP addresses were securely deleted to protect participant anonymity. In reporting 
the results, data are presented in aggregate form without disclosing any identifying information.  
Preparation of Evaluation Tasks and Questionnaire: To create a relevant set of business models 
for evaluation and to minimize biases that may arise from synthetic cases, pitches from the 
television show Shark Tank were selected. 
The methodology involved the following steps to ensure randomized selection of business cases 
and eliminate selection bias: 
1. All business cases, presented on Shark Tank in the last five years (after September 2019), 
were sampled in an Excel list using episode information available online. 
2. Pitches were clustered based on business stage and industry to align with the experts' 
areas of specialization. Only early-stage ventures from ‘Shark Tank’ in the domain of 
consumer goods were included. All other cases were discarded. 
3. The list’s order was being randomized, and the first 17 cases were chosen. 
4. If a business case was not available on YouTube, this case was skipped as the complete, 
original pitch is required to maintain equality in the pitch transcription. In these cases, the 
next case from the randomized list was added additionally.  
==PAGE:31==
24 
 
Each pitch was summarized into approximately 200 words, a length determined after consulting 
with two VC experts to ensure a low (due to pre-screening applicability), but still sufficient 
information. The selected pitches were transcribed using the software Descript, and transcripts 
were manually checked for accuracy. To minimize researcher bias, OpenAI's GPT-4o language 
model was employed using a staged prompting method. For the first prompt, the model was 
instructed to identify the unique selling propositions (USPs) of the business concept, focusing 
solely on the business concept and excluding historical financial performance. For the second 
prompt, the model was guided to summarize the idea and business concept in approximately 200 
words (see Appendix A). All AI-generated summaries were reviewed to ensure accuracy and to 
verify that no incorrect information was introduced. It was also manually checked that the length 
of the descriptions did not deviate greatly from the target length of 200 words – the shortest 
description had 181 words, the longest 218 words, and the average word count was 201.5 words. 
Product or service price details were added manually to ensure completeness and correctness. 
All company and founder names were replaced with “Company X” to prevent recognition biases 
that could influence evaluations. 
Evaluation Process: All participants in one panel received the same evaluation cases. However, 
to limit potential order bias, the order in which the business cases were presented was 
randomized.  
Experts were provided with clear guidelines to ensure consistent evaluations. They were 
instructed to consider the role of a VC investor specializing in their respective domains. Experts 
were asked to evaluate each business model intuitively, relying on their professional judgment to 
simulate a pre-screening process. Evaluations were to be based solely on the information 
provided in the summaries. The experts were assigned to evaluate the business models for the 
dimension of idea novelty and investment attractiveness. Experts assigned scores to each 
business model for both novelty and investment attractiveness using the specified ordinal scale 
from 0 to 5. The scale endpoints were defined as ‘0’, representing very low novelty/investment 
attractiveness, and ‘5’ representing very high novelty/investment attractiveness. 
 
AI-based Evaluation: Overview of Variant Groups 
This study investigates the accuracy and reliability of two primary evaluation methodologies: 
scoring algorithms and ranking algorithms, focusing on their accuracy in detecting idea novelty 
and investment attractiveness compared to expert benchmarks. To address the main research 
question (RQ), this study’s methodological setup of One-Shot Scoring and One-Shot Ranking 
==PAGE:32==
25 
 
algorithms are being introduced. As outlined, both will be compared by regression analysis with 
the human benchmark to establish a comparative analysis. 
As highlighted in depth in the theoretical background, the fundamental difference between these 
algorithm types lies in their approach to evaluation. Scoring algorithms evaluate each object 
(business case) individually, assigning scores without consideration for other objects being 
evaluated (Blohm et al., 2016). This method operates with ‘blinders on’, as it assesses objects in 
isolation, independent of the quality or characteristics of other objects in the set. Ranking 
algorithms, by contrast, rely on comparative evaluations with full awareness of other alternatives 
in the set. In the context of this study, both – scoring and ranking – algorithms ultimately aim to 
arrange business cases into an ordinally scaled order (Blohm et al., 2016; Cui et al., 2019). 
However, they achieve this in different ways. Ranking algorithms directly produce an ordinal 
ranking based on pairwise or group comparisons. Scoring algorithms, on the other hand, first 
assign a cardinal or metric score to each business case (e.g., idea A = 3.3 novelty points, idea B = 
2.4 novelty points). These scores are then translated into an ordinal ranking to achieve the desired 
prioritization (e.g., Score [Idea A] > Score [Idea B]). 
To explore sub-question SQ1, it is further examined how the accuracy of ranking evaluations is 
influenced by an increasing number of evaluation elements. This includes analysing whether the 
effectiveness of ranking algorithms diminishes with growing set sizes, highlighting the potential 
context-dependence of scoring versus ranking methodologies. 
Beyond basic implementations, the study introduces advanced algorithmic structures that differ 
in their depth and breadth of evaluation, addressing the sub-question SQ3. For humans, even 
standalone ranking and scoring procedures can lead to cognitive overload (Cui et al., 2019). 
However, as the computational boundaries of artificial intelligence are less constrained than the 
cognitive boundaries of humans, AI has the potential of processing vast amounts of data (Just et 
al., 2024). While in human evaluations cognitive overload often even constrains attention to all 
elements, AI could potentially not just evaluate all elements, but also do this in more complex 
structures featuring additional levels of breadth and depth (e.g., expert panels) for evaluating a 
single object. These advanced structures include both algorithmic scoring and ranking variants 
that incorporate additional computational layers, such as ensemble methods and refinement 
techniques, to simulate multi-agent evaluations similar to expert panels. By analysing these 
sophisticated structures, it is aimed to determine whether adding layers of depth and breadth 
enhances evaluation accuracy.

One-Shot Ranking 
The One-Shot Ranking variant group uses a bag-of-elements approach, where evaluation objects 
are aggregated into a single block of text and passed to the AI model for ranking based on a 
specified criterion, such as idea novelty or investment attractiveness (Cui et al., 2019). The AI 
returns a complete ordered list in response to the prompt, without requiring further iterations or 
re-ranking steps. However, it requires the AI to process large, high-dimensional token sequences 
within each prompt, which may be an issue for the accuracy of results as indicated by multiple 
scholars (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Ratner et al., 2022). As for One-Shot Scoring, also for 
One-Shot Ranking this study distinguishes between two models (gpt-4o and gpt-4o-mini) and 
three behaviour parameter settings (high/moderate/low determinism), introducing six distinct 
variants. 
 
Bubble Sort Ranking 
Bubble Sort is a comparison-based sorting algorithm traditionally used to arrange distinct 
elements in a specified order, typically ascending or descending (You Yang et al., 2011). The 
algorithm works by repeatedly stepping through a list, comparing adjacent elements, and 
swapping them if they are in the wrong order, which is often referred to as ‘bubbling’ (Astrachan, 
2003). In other words, does a complete Bubble Sort involve multiple, individual rankings – Each 
consisting of only two evaluation elements. For the iterative process this work separates between 
==PAGE:38==
31 
 
three different terms: A run is one full execution of the Bubble Sort algorithm starting from a 
randomized initial order and proceeding until a stable ranking is reached or the maximum number 
of passes is exhausted (Astrachan, 2003). A pass refers to a single complete traversal of the entire 
list of evaluation objects, during which each adjacent pair is compared and potentially swapped 
(Astrachan, 2003). Each pass, meaning that the algorithm stepped through the whole list once, 
ensures that one element is placed in its correct position at the end of the list (Astrachan, 2003). 
An iteration can be understood as a single, lower-level step within a pass, involving the 
comparison of just one pair of elements (Astrachan, 2003). As illustrated in figure 3, the process 
continues until no more swaps are needed, indicating that the list is fully sorted (Astrachan, 2003). 
Bubble Sort has a theoretical worst-case complexity of 𝑂(𝑛2) where 𝑛 is the number of elements 
in the list, and the result for big 𝑂 represents the total number of iterations aggregated from all 
passes (You Yang et al., 2011). Unlike classical Bubble Sort, which operates on distinct numerical 
values, the adaptation in this study involves ranking business cases based on nuanced and tacit 
evaluations by the AI. Since the AI evaluates novelty and investment attractiveness dynamically, 
it may perceive the relative order of two business cases differently across passes. As a result, 
while the theoretical worst-case time complexity for Bubble Sort remains 𝑛2, the practical worst-
case scenario in this context could be 𝑖𝑛𝑓., as the algorithm may cycle endlessly when trying to 
rank cases that are perceived as nearly identical. However, the advantage of Bubble Sort in 
comparison, for instance, to a complete ranking of all elements in one single iteration lies in its 
breaking down the comparing of multiple elements into multiple pairwise comparisons. In other 
words, Bubble Sort requires the AI to compare only two business cases at a time. This is 
particularly beneficial for reducing computational strain when processing large datasets (Liu et 
al., 2024). For example, a ranking of 200 business cases with a description length of 500 words 
each, would require a full ranking to order a total description length of more than 100,000 words, 
while Bubble Sort would break it down into pairwise comparisons involving only approximately 
1,000 words per iteration. Not only with regards to a model-based context window, after which the 
context is truncated (Ratner et al., 2022), but also with regards to AI’s biases such as dilution of 
relevance (Leng et al., 2024), focus issues (Liu et al., 2024), or computational reasoning (Li et al., 
2024), this can be valuable to maintain consistency and thereby accuracy of the results.

Before issuing any requests, the system is configured with essential parameters: an API key for 
authentication, the endpoint URL, and model-specific attributes such as maximum tokens, 
temperature, top_p, frequency_penalty, and presence_penalty. These parameters control the 
style and variability of the AI’s responses (Stevenson et al., 2022). When a chosen algorithm

underlying principle is similar, the exact method for assembling the prompt differs by algorithm. 
For example, a scoring variant group may request an absolute assessment of a single object, while 
a ranking variant group may present two alternatives and ask the AI to identify which one is 
superior on a specific dimension. These prompts are partly generated by programmed VBA 
subroutines, and partly by Excel formulas that reference dynamically updated cells. A dedicated 
VBA subroutine packages the constructed prompt into a JSON request and sends it to the AI 
endpoint. The external model processes the input and returns a structured JSON response.  
The returned content is then parsed, extracting only the essential information needed for the 
algorithm’s next step (e.g., the preferred alternative in a ranking context). This information is 
written back into the Excel worksheet, ensuring that the algorithm proceeds with the most recent 
AI-informed data. The API interaction is designed to be modular. Rather than embedding prompt 
construction or parsing logic into each algorithm’s core routine, dedicated subroutines handle 
these tasks. Any algorithm can simply call these subroutines and pass appropriate parameters 
(e.g., specifying the prompt text, model parameters, or token limits) to ensure code clarity and 
maintainability. If a different AI model is introduced or parameters are adjusted, the changes 
remain confined to the API-related sections of the code, eliminating the need to modify each 
algorithm’s main logic. Although not central to this thesis, basic error handling is implemented to 
ensure reliable operation. Checks are performed to confirm that valid inputs exist and that an API 
key is provided. If an API request fails or returns an unexpected response, the system can handle 
these cases, preserving data integrity and operational stability. This study utilizes OpenAI’s GPT 
models as the back-end evaluation model. By using these widespread, globally used models, the 
resulting assessments gain practical relevance and external validity, as custom models would be 
less generalizable. Furthermore, this makes the results more relevant in a management context, 
and useable for companies with limited or no internal ML resources. By offering standardized APIs 
and adjustable parameters, the solution allows firms to integrate the AI’s judgments into their 
existing processes without substantial technical overhead.

AI Behaviour Settings 
For One-Shot Scoring and One-Shot Ranking, certain parameters are customized and 
communicated to the AI model via the API to define its behaviour during the evaluation process. 
These parameters control various aspects of the AI's decision-making process, ensuring 
consistent and replicable results. The key parameters used in this study include the following: 
Maximum tokens determines the maximum length of the AI’s response, measured in tokens 
(approximately equivalent to words or parts of words). For example, setting a limit of 20 tokens 
can ensure concise and focused outputs. In certain evaluation contexts, where only a single 
character (e.g., ‘1’ or ‘2’) may be required to indicate which business case ranks higher, and 
consecutive rule-based code requires the integer output for further operations, low token limit 
helps maintain rule adherence.  
The temperature parameter adjusts the randomness of the AI's token selection by modifying the 
probability distribution of potential outputs (Zhang et al., 2023). Mathematically, temperature 
serves as a scaling factor applied to the probability distribution before sampling. Each token’s 
original probability 𝑃(𝑥) is raised to the power of 
1
𝑇 , where 𝑇 is the temperature (ibid.). When 𝑇 is 
low (0.0 ≤𝑇< 1.0), the AI sharpens the probability distribution, making likely token, even more 
likely and thereby making the output more deterministic. At 𝑇= 1.0 , the model samples from the 
original probability distribution without modification. When the temperature is high (1.0 < 𝑇≤
2.0), the distribution is flattened, enabling less likely tokens to be chosen more frequently and 
thus increasing the randomness and diversity of the output. As an illustrative example, a token 
with an initial probability of 50% might be increased to around 90% under a very low temperature, 
thereby making the model’s choice more predictable.  
Top-p sampling (also known as nucleus sampling) determines how the AI selects its next token by 
limiting the cumulative probability distribution of the considered tokens – instead of evaluating all 
possible next tokens, the AI includes only the top tokens that collectively sum to a probability 
mass 𝑝 (Zhang et al., 2023). In other words, instead of looking at all possible next tokens, the AI 
limits the range of tokens to a mass that collectively make up a certain percentage of the 
probability mass. The less likely options are ignored. Once the cumulative total reaches 𝑡𝑜𝑝𝑝 (e.g., 
𝑡𝑜𝑝𝑝= 0.8) the AI selects the next token randomly from this reduced set (Zhang et al., 2023). Thus, 
a low top-p value (0.0 ≤𝑡𝑜𝑝𝑝< 0.5) results in more deterministic and focused outputs, as less 
likely tokens are excluded from consideration. While temperature reshapes how probabilities are 
interpreted (flattening or sharpening them), top-p sampling filters out the less likely tokens 
altogether, without altering their original probabilities (Zhang et al., 2023).

randomized identifiers, creating the initial randomized setup. This randomized order determines 
the sequence in which the evaluation objects are compared during the first iteration of the sorting 
process. For example, in Figure 10, the first comparison is between the objects with fixed IDs 10 
and 7, which correspond to randomized IDs 1 and 2, respectively. The third section (see figure 10, 
3rd from left) contains the dynamic ranking setup, where the Bubble Sort algorithm executes the 
sorting process. In this section the algorithm compares two evaluation objects at a time to

determine their relative ranking. The comparison aims to bring the evaluation objects with the 
highest levels of idea novelty or investment attractiveness to the top of the list and those with the

Algorithmic Workflow: The code implementing the Bubble Sort algorithm, including the 
initialization and execution steps described here, is provided in Appendix E. The following is a 
descriptive overview of how the user interacts with the process and how the algorithm responds 
to these inputs (see figure 12). The process begins when the user opens a custom user form in 
Excel, designed to configure and launch the Bubble Sort algorithm. Within this form, the user 
Figure 11: Bubble Sort Excel Setup (Logging of Results) 
==PAGE:47==
40 
 
specifies two key parameters Number of Runs (numberOfRuns) and Maximum Attempts per Run 
(maxAttempts). Number of Runs determines how many complete sorting processes the algorithm 
will execute. Each run begins with a randomized order of the evaluation objects, mitigating initial 
biases. Each run ultimately produces a final ranking of all the evaluation objects. Maximum 
Attempts per Run sets a stopping heuristic on the number of iterations allowed within a single run. 
Its purpose is to prevent the algorithm from becoming stuck in endless loops, which can occur 
when the AI struggles to differentiate among highly similar evaluation objects. Once these 
parameters are entered, the user clicks the ‘Start’ button. At this point, the algorithm retrieves the 
user’s inputs and initializes the Bubble Sort process with the specified settings. A separate ‘Stop’ 
button allows the user to halt the procedure at any time. If this button is clicked, a global stop flag 
is activated, and the algorithm will regularly check this flag to ensure a controlled and safe 
termination without risking data integrity.  
After the user initiates the process, the algorithm sets up its operating environment. This includes 
selecting the AI model, defining token limits, and configuring parameters such as temperature 
and top-p that influence how the model generates responses (Zhang et al., 2023). The code also 
maps specific Excel columns for data handling, ensuring that input data, prompts, AI responses, 
and intermediate results are stored in designated locations. The algorithm then identifies the data 
range that contains the evaluation objects. By locating the minimum and maximum row 
boundaries, it dynamically adapts to datasets of varying sizes. Next, a dedicated routine 
randomizes the initial order of these evaluation objects for each run. This step helps ensure that 
no particular object benefits from a fixed starting position, thereby reducing the risk of systematic 
bias in the ranking results. The randomization routine creates a list of identifiers and then assigns 
each identifier to an object in a random order. Internally, it builds an array of sequential numbers 
Figure 12: Workflow Bubble Sort 
==PAGE:48==
41 
 
and randomly selects one number at a time to assign to the next object in the list. Each selected 
number is then removed from the pool, preventing duplicates. As a result, each run starts from a 
unique configuration, ensuring that the sorting outcomes are not influenced by any preset 
arrangement. 
With all preparations in place, the Bubble Sort algorithm commences its iterative comparisons. 
The procedure repeatedly examines pairs of evaluation objects and may swap them based on the 
AI’s judgments on novelty or investment attractiveness. Before each comparison, an uncoded 
excel formula retrieves the two business case descriptions. These formulas dynamically 
assemble the full prompt string by combining the two startup descriptions into a standardized 
instruction for the AI (see Appendix F). The prompt positions the AI as an investor evaluating which 
of the two pitches has a higher level of the targeted attribute. Only the two relevant summaries 
are provided, and the AI is instructed to respond with a single character (‘1’ or ‘2’) to indicate which 
one it considers superior. The fully assembled prompt is then passed to a dedicated VBA 
subroutine, which communicates via an API call to the AI model. The indication number that is 
received from the AI via the API (‘1’ or ‘2’) is used as an indicator for the rule-based bubbling. This 
process iterates in a loop until no further swaps are needed or the maximum attempts threshold 
is reached. If the algorithm reaches its maximum attempts without achieving a stable ranking, the 
system applies predefined heuristics to finalize the results for that run. Once the process achieves 
a stable order or exhausts the allowed passes, the clearing subroutine logs the results in the 
results section, enabling accurate comparisons against the human benchmark.

Analysis 
The analysis presents the empirical findings of this study, examining the performance of 
generative AI in replicating or refining human judgment. The analysis is structured to address the 
primary research question, the sub-questions related to the effects of dataset size, algorithmic 
depth and breadth, and AI behavioural parameters, as well as certain explorative insights. The 
analysis finds significant effects and reveals an interesting ambiguous tension with the evaluation 
context. An aggregated overview on the correlation coefficients per variant is provided in Table 6

Basis Finding on Criticality 
A broad initial observation concerns mean scoring behaviour, referred to here as ‘criticality’. In the 
heatmap of average AI scores (see figure 22), deeper approaches (e.g., more refinement steps) 
systematically suggested lower, more critical overall scores. Statistical tests support this pattern 
for both idea novelty and investment attractiveness with very high statistical significance (𝑝<
0.001***). In contrast, increasing the breadth of the evaluation (i.e., switching from a single 
evaluator to a Meta Ensemble) did not systematically reduce scores. This was only tested 
between One-Shot Scoring (breadth = 1) and Meta Ensemble (breadth = 3), as the individual effect 
of its feature-based nature cannot be excluded and would skew the insight. Hence, the analysis 
forms the following insights: 
(1) Depth Induces Criticality: Additional refinement steps lower the AI’s mean scores 
(accuracy not assessed here). 
(2) Breadth Leaves Average Scores Unchanged: Broader structures do not systematically 
reduce scoring levels.

Discussion 
This study contributes by first, showing when scoring vs. ranking leads to higher accuracy for AI-
based evaluations, secondly, demonstrating how task ambiguity interacts with these methods, 
and third, uncovering new insights on cost/accuracy trade-offs, ensemble designs, and model 
parameters. While the main findings are also listed in table 5 at the end of this chapter, in the 
following these insights, their potential reasons, as well as their (dis-) alignment with current 
research are discussed in more depth. While doing so, the findings on AI-based idea evaluation 
are interpreted through a broader academic lens, building on both Information Systems (IS) and 
Organizational Theory (OT) perspectives. As Csaszar and Steinberger (2022) argue, bridging IS and 
OT literature can enrich theoretical reasoning. They specifically encourage OT scholars to learn 
from IS research on artificial intelligence, but this study inverts that logic by applying 
organizational insights to AI-based screening methods. By doing so, it goes beyond the purely 
technical explanations of why scoring and ranking might differ, instead situating these results in 
established OT concepts to illuminate how and why different AI evaluation setups succeed or fail. 
Scoring vs. Ranking and its interplay with Task Ambiguity 
A key finding in this study is that idea novelty is evaluated with substantially higher AI–human 
alignment than the more multi-dimensional concept of investment attractiveness. Although the 
research questions did not explicitly focus on the issue of construct ambiguity, the results strongly 
==PAGE:69==
62 
 
suggest that construct-dependent analysis is critical. In other words, the level of complexity or 
multi-dimensionality within each construct (e.g., novelty vs. investment attractiveness) shapes 
how accurately generative AI can align with human judgments. This finding emerged consistently 
across scoring vs. ranking, model comparisons, and parameter settings, underscoring the notion 
that evaluation performance varies not only by method but also by the nature of what is being 
evaluated. Consequently, while the principal goal is to compare AI-based scoring and ranking, the 
data reveal a broader implication: construct structure – whether it is well-defined or ill-structured 
– can significantly change the AI’s accuracy for methods differently. At one level, this result seems 
intuitive as simpler criteria reduce error variance, echoing classical bounded-rationality theories 
for humans (Levinthal & Workiewicz, 2018). Yet it also underscores that generative AI is not 
immune to ambiguity-induced variance, contradicting assumptions that large language models 
(LLMs) would seamlessly handle multi-faceted tasks (Boussioux et al., 2024). The discussion 
around construct ambiguity typically emerges in innovation literature as a human-centric bias 
phenomenon (Cui et al., 2019). Here, the analogous effect in AI outputs can be seen – even 
without cognitive fatigue, LLMs show reduced consistency when a single dimension branches into 
sub-criteria like market size, team characteristics, and regulatory hurdles. While certain 
management literature also addresses algorithmic constraints with regards to ill-structured 
problems (Von Krogh, 2018), the question of how it unfolds for different evaluation variants 
specifically remained untouched. This study enhances the academic discourse by describing the 
dynamic moderative role not just for accuracy in general, but also method-based. With regards to 
the main research question (RQ) the significant moderation of ambiguity answers it with an 
inherent tension: For idea novelty, as the construct with less ambiguity, OS-Scoring seem to 
outperform OS-Ranking, however, when investment attractiveness introduces additional 
ambiguity, ranking significantly proves to be more accurate. Reasoning based on the literature 
review may explain the observed performance gap which potentially arises from differential 
cognitive (or computational) loads. According to Blohm et al. (2016), scoring each idea 
independently can be advantageous when the dimension is clear-cut (e.g., novelty), because the 
evaluator does not need to keep multiple references in mind. In simpler constructs, ranking 
seems to anchor more reliably on an absolute scale without confusion (Blohm et al., 2016). 
Another angle of explanation can be found in scale usage bias (Pimentel, 2019). As the analysis 
shows that the MSEs differ substantially between scoring novelty and scoring investment 
attractiveness, the bias Pimentel (2019) expects for humans may also apply to AI. Hence, the 
lower variance between different evaluation elements may have caused outlier scores to have a 
more significant impact on the ultimate rank. Conversely, ranking can be beneficial in more 
ambiguous or multi-criterion contexts – such as investment attractiveness – by harnessing 
==PAGE:70==
63 
 
pairwise or groupwise comparisons (Cui et al., 2019). Scoring’s underperformance in ambiguous 
tasks might also reflect an AI-analogy to human scale usage bias, which describes that humans 
facing complexity often converge on mid-scale safe ratings (Pimentel, 2019). In this study, AI’s 
scoring variants showed very similar behaviour using a significantly smaller span of scores for 
investment attractiveness, suggesting it ‘defaulted’ to moderate values. Ranking, by contrast, is 
systematically forced to produce a definitive choice of better or worse. However, the results also 
underline, that ranking is not unaffected by task ambiguity either. Ultimately, the difference to 
human evaluation is that for AI-based evaluations, scoring advantage is with high certainty not a 
strict domination but instead requires specific contextual circumstances. Based on the analysis, 
it could be hypothesized that OS-Scoring strictly outperforms OS-Ranking in the baseline – 
meaning for tasks with very little or no ambiguity – which would align AI-based evaluation with the 
findings for human-based evaluation (Cui et al., 2019). However, as idea novelty is not a zero-
ambiguity construct, further research with adapted methodology and construct definitions would 
be required to test this. 
 
The Effect of Varying Set Sizes 
Furthermore, it is also found that accuracy declined for ranking variants, when the set size of 
evaluation elements is increased. When implementing this to the notion of overall task ambiguity, 
the negative gradient by increased set sizes, indicates that there likely is an intersection with 
scoring accuracy also for investment attractiveness as scoring is unaffected by the number of 
elements, complementing human-based literature (Criscuolo et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2019). 
However, the strong performance gap between the models for small sets, where the 4o-mini 
model shows a strong dominance, raises an analytical question. The counterintuitive 
underperformance of the 4o model can (non-exhaustively) explained by two reasons. A first 
reason stems from the methodological setup. As Spearman’s correlation ranges from -1 to 1, even 
slight deviations from the evaluation in the human benchmark can have a significant magnitude 
in when assessing only 3 items. As explained in the methodology part, for the ranking-internal 
comparison it is not possible to build averaged rankings first, which are then compared with the 
human benchmark in the second step. Instead, the correlation with the human benchmark was 
first computed for each ranking iteration individually and then the correlation coefficients have 
been averaged in the second step. Due to this necessary setup, in smaller evaluation sets, 
multiple correlation coefficients of 𝜌= −1 are included in the averaged correlation, which 
increases the amplitude of negative deflections. As already indicated, in this study the 4o model 
is mostly outperformed by the 4o-mini model, making it more likely that the 4o model included a 
==PAGE:71==
64 
 
larger number of  𝜌= −1 correlation coefficients in its mean. This can explain the strength of the 
deflections for the 4o model from a methodological perspective. However, there is also a 
theoretical indication, which is in line with the general model-based findings already made. 
Retrospectively, the earlier findings indicate that the 4o model is more robust when it comes to 
ambiguous tasks. It seems that this is not only applicable to the structuredness of the construct 
(i.e., novelty vs. investment attractiveness), but also to the structuredness of the input. For smaller 
inputs the structure is clearer and easier to grasp for an AI model. Here, the 4o-mini model plays 
out its advantage, explaining the strong deflection with the 4o model.  
However, it is also found that there is a tipping point. Precisely, the negative gradient between 
evaluating 9 and 17 cases is smaller for the 4o model. This parallels the insights found on the 
accuracy-interplay between ambiguity and AI model. As the number of elements increases the 
task becomes more nuanced. Here, the 4o model plays out its higher robustness illustrated by a 
lower negative gradient. It should also be acknowledged that this insight cannot be explained by 
context-window constraints as all prompts including the prompts for OS-Ranking with 17 
elements are within both models’ context window of 128k input tokens (OpenAI, 2025).  
In essence, the higher robustness of the 4o model for larger sets is not a surprise as it 
complements previous research and the benefits of advanced models communicated by the 
model provider (ibid.). However, the counterintuitive findings for smaller evaluation sets aligns 
with this study’s findings on a paradoxical tension shaped by multiple layers of task-ambiguity.  
It is noteworthy that the patterns observed for investment attractiveness and idea novelty show a 
high degree of parallelism. Although the two constructs differ in inherent ambiguity, the models 
show consistent reactions to set-size increases – namely, linear or inverted-U declines in 
correlation. This alignment does not mean the two constructs are equally simple or complex – 
rather, it suggests that the mechanisms by which set size influences AI accuracy (e.g., the need 
for more pairwise comparisons, potential confusion at higher volumes) operate similarly across 
both constructs, even though the baseline difficulty level differs. These new observations do not 
contradict the initial finding that idea novelty generally produces higher accuracy than investment 
attractiveness. Rather, the alignment of set-size trends for both constructs reinforces that each 
model’s performance depends substantially on how many items must be compared, in addition 
to how ambiguous each construct is. An important takeaway is that larger sets intensify the 
inherent ambiguity of a construct. For instance, the maximum set size of 𝑘= 17 under investment 
attractiveness effectively represents a double layer of complexity: (1) a more ambiguous 
construct, and (2) an increased number of items to compare. This amplified ambiguity often 
magnifies the weaknesses in the smaller model.

Evaluation Depth and Breadth 
With regards to sub-question SQ2, a notable side-result from the scoring analyses is that deeper, 
iterative methods tend to yield progressively lower (i.e., more critical) average scores – even as 
they improve overall accuracy. The observation that deeper, iterative approaches consistently 
generated lower (i.e., more critical) average scores, while broader parallel evaluations did not 
reduce scoring levels, opens a novel angle on how AI-based assessments evolve through repeated 
introspection. Prior research on human scoring typically emphasizes issues like score 
compression or extremity bias (Bian et al., 2022), but the systematic downward shift in scores 
with each additional evaluative step highlights a distinct dynamic in generative AI. One plausible 
explanation is that subsequent refinement prompts repeatedly interrogate the weaker points of 
an idea, leading the model to overweight negative factors. Although the literature on iterative 
human evaluations often addresses how repeated exposure can sharpen critical thinking 
(Knudsen & Levinthal, 2007), the finding partially aligns with human-centred theories that more 
reflection leads to more caution (ibid.). Yet, as it extends them into a new domain where reflection 
is machine-driven. However, as being more critical did not affect accuracy in a negative way, it 
may not require future research to find mitigation paths. More precisely, did greater depth (i.e., 
additional refinement layers) in fact improve accuracy, which complements existing arguments 
for iterative evaluation logics in information systems and innovation literature (Zheng et al., 2024; 
Zhou et al., 2019). By re-checking or expanding on previous outputs, chain-of-thought prompting 
appears to reduce noise and converge on a more stable or coherent appraisal (Zheng et al., 2024). 
However, the data also shows that the benefit of depth is especially pronounced for ambiguous 
constructs (investment attractiveness). These results again point towards a research gap 
regarding task ambiguity’s moderation effect. While advanced prompting strategies (e.g., chain-
of-thought) have been shown to reduce inaccuracies in general text-generation tasks (Wang et al., 
2023), depth’s varying effect under different levels of ambiguity is underexplored in management 
literature. Precisely, this could address whether there are potential upper bounds (for additional 
depth) or lower bounds (for task ambiguity) beyond which additional depth provides diminishing 
returns.  
While additional breadth does not show additional criticality as additional depth does, its positive 
effect on accuracy is mostly aligned with the effect of additional depth. However, the significant 
accuracy decline from OS-Scoring with the Feature-Based Ensemble (𝑏= 2), might be initially 
confusing and seem contradicting to the elsewise positive effect of additional breadth. However, 
it must be acknowledged that the base agents in the Feature-Based Ensemble are assigned with 
==PAGE:73==
66 
 
artificial roles. Therefore, drawing conclusions on breadth’s role based on the comparison of OS-
Scoring’s and Feature-Based Ensemble’s correlation would be skewed. Instead, the comparison 
provides a notion on the effect of artificially implemented diversity in the base-evaluations. It 
could be hypothesized that the increase in base diversity may have actually contributed to a 
higher level of ambiguity as the decision foundation for the aggregator varies more strongly, 
possibly weaking its capability to decide on a perceived truth. Additional hypothesizing on the 
practical reason could suggest that artificial roles may skew the decision basis. For instance, if an 
evaluation element is of high novelty and high-investment attractiveness, the positive evaluators 
would just speak out their actual thoughts, while the negative agents would be pushed to 
hallucinate negative findings even if not backed by the underlying data (vice versa). However, it 
must also be acknowledged that the findings and hypotheses for the Feature-Based Ensemble 
are purely indicative, as the synthetically assigned roles are one of many. Other role descriptions 
might potentially turn the findings. Thereby the insights are limited to the extent that artificially 
implied base-evaluator-diversity does not necessarily help with increasing evaluation accuracy. 
As breadth increases within the feature-based variant, all RCGs between these distinct breadth-
levels are positive indicating a positive effect of breadth on accuracy. 
Also here, the findings ultimately align with information systems research (Park et al., 2022) and 
wisdom-of-the-crowd logics in management science (Csaszar, 2018). The present findings affirm 
that even when the aggregated judgments all stem from a single LLM architecture, instructing 
different base agents or partial roles can introduce enough variance to raise the final accuracy. 
That said, the Feature-Based Ensemble variant suggests that how diversity is introduced (e.g., 
artificially assigning ‘positive’ or ‘negative’ roles) can yield mixed outcomes. While the results 
highlight the potential for improved performance under higher breadth, they also point to a 
theoretical question whether artificially forcing evaluative diversity might amplify hallucinations 
or contradictory logic. Future research could explore which types of role assignments or prompt 
engineering produce truly independent viewpoints, ensuring that diversity does not degenerate 
into random output variance or forced negativity. Furthermore, the consistent notion of task 
ambiguity’s moderative role also applies to breadth. That points toward the equivalent of the 
research gap pointed out for depth’s interplay with ambiguity. Based on the analysis on additional 
scoring breadth the main insights are summarized as: 
Bubble Sort is perceived in this thesis as the equivalent to CoT for the ranking context. However, 
the analytical results showed contrasting results to its interplay with ambiguity. Rather than 
reducing ambiguity’s moderative effect on overall accuracy, Bubble Sort seems to be strongly, 
negatively influenced by it. At first glance, these results seem paradoxical, particularly because 
==PAGE:74==
67 
 
(a) additional depth improved evaluation accuracy for idea novelty and investment attractiveness 
in the scoring comparisons, and (b) prior analyses indicated that ranking methods tend to cope 
better with ambiguity than scoring. However, a closer look at the iterative process clarifies why 
Bubble Sort struggles in the more complex investment-attractiveness context. While One-Shot 
Ranking must provide a complete ordered list in a single output, Bubble Sort compares only pairs 
of items at a time and swaps them if they appear out of order. Because the initial list is 
randomized, Bubble Sort with a perfectly structured construct (e.g., tasked to sort numbers by 
ascending value) would require an average of roughly 𝑛/2 passes for a set of 𝑛 items, as the best-
case scenario (i.e., the initial, randomized list equals the sorted list) would be 1 pass and the 
worst-case number of passes would be 𝑛−1 passes (when the randomized, initial list mirrors the 
sorted list). For idea novelty, the data show that Bubble Sort performed an average of 10.6 passes 
– exceeding the typical average of 8.5. However, as in this study Bubble Sort does not deal with 
clearly distinct numbers, but with ambiguous evaluation tasks extra iterations for reconsideration 
in tight decisions are expected. However, for investment attractiveness, the algorithm made only 
5.2 passes on average, which is below the theoretical, average midpoint for perfectly distinct 
evaluation elements with no ambiguity. This essentially means, that even if assumed the model’s 
pairwise comparisons are consistently correct, by the law of large numbers, the number of passes 
performed is insufficient to achieve an optimal solution. Thereby, it can clearly be stated that the 
Bubble Sort did not just perform worse than the global optimum (i.e., a correlation of 1 with the 
human benchmark), but also stayed below its local optimum. The term local optimum in this 
context describes the solution that would occur when the Bubble Sort could look across the duals 
of items and determine a ranking – with all ties being randomly sorted. With an average number of 
passes that is below the theoretical average, it could – under the premise of the law of large 
numbers – not achieve its local optimum. This dynamic implies that, although OS-Ranking is more 
robust against construct ambiguity than OS-Scoring, Bubble Sort introduces an additional 
nuance of systematic nature: In contrast to OS-Scoring it does not necessarily force the model to 
produce a definite order in a single global step, which seems to be its weak spot for constructs 
with higher ambiguity. The fact that the theoretical average number of passes is undershot 
indicates that it prefers not to change items if it is uncertain about changing them. This 
phenomenon was not found in other academic work on AI-based evaluation and is therefore 
referred to as ‘evaluation laziness’ in the following. In a broader sense, traditional organizational 
studies advocates for iterative filtering (e.g., staged gate processes) for improving decision quality 
(Criscuolo et al., 2017). However, as the mechanism of evaluation laziness suggests that in a 
highly ambiguous domain the model stops making swaps too early only producing insufficient 
passes to fully reorder the list, evaluation laziness can turn the theoretical construct-robustness 
==PAGE:75==
68 
 
of ranking into a significant disadvantage. The finding opens a research gap in innovation literature 
by illustrating the limits of a purely mechanical sorting algorithm when it encounters ill-structured 
criteria. Respecting its comparative advantage over OS-Ranking for idea novelty, it opens 
questions about adaptive or smart sorting methods, where the algorithm might adjust the number 
of passes based on measured uncertainty or add targeted prompts to resolve ambiguous 
comparisons.  
Discussing evaluation depth from the AI model perspective, it was initially plausible to assume 
that a larger model (4o) would outperform a smaller one (4o-mini), given arguments that bigger 
architectures capture subtler patterns (Ahuja et al., 2023). However, the empirical results for this 
thesis show scenarios in which 4o underperforms 4o-mini, particularly in less ambiguous tasks 
like scoring novelty. This finding does not outright contradict ML principles but suggests an 
optimal match must exist between model depth and task complexity, indicating a partial blind 
spot in innovation literature. The present data thus refine general assumptions of ‘bigger is better’ 
by highlighting that increased capacity does not guarantee improvements in every context. The 
findings especially connect optimal model choice with its interaction with task ambiguity. Factors 
such as the structuredness of a construct and the number of elements being evaluated in OS-
Ranking variants, are found to also moderate the accuracy of different model depths. Here, 4o’s 
depth seems to add robustness as accuracy declines for more ill-structured tasks has a less 
strong effect on accuracy as it has for the smaller 4o-mini model. 4o’s deeper parameterization 
appears better able to synthesize these interdependencies and thus close the gap. Such 
behaviour parallels the principle of algorithmic boundedness, where advanced architectures 
perform better under scale precisely because they can encode more robust relationships among 
multi-item comparisons (Townsend et al., 2024). Thereby, the findings confirm portions of the 
notion  that larger models handle complexity better (Boussioux et al., 2024) but refine it by 
demonstrating a tipping point. The results do not deny the advantage of smaller models in simpler 
domains but emphasize that under conditions of increased ambiguity, 4o has more robust 
capacity to integrate various signals. While this study does not aim for model benchmarking, it 
introduces important insights on how model size interacts with the dimensionality, encouraging 
future research to investigate whether advanced models hold a clear edge only if the problem 
surpasses a threshold of multi-faceted complexity.

Effect of Model’s Determinism 
Addressing the final sub-question SQ3, the interplay between high determinism and low 
determinism partly emerges as a meaningful predictor of AI performance. Specifically, in narrow, 
well-structured tasks like novelty detection, tight parameter settings yield more consistent 
outputs and improve accuracy in narrow – by contrast, looser settings can capture more angles or 
weak signals in multi-dimensional evaluations. Organizational theory often discusses exploration 
vs. exploitation (March, 1991), and the data here suggest an analogous dynamic in generative AI. 
High determinism fosters exploitative, stable answers – ideal for unambiguous criteria – while a 
more exploratory configuration might better handle tasks where creativity or contextual nuance is 
critical. This result enriches the discourse on AI bias, underscoring that one cannot treat 
generative outputs as a fixed entity – even small adjustments to model parameters can change 
the AI’s vantage point and, thus, alignment with human benchmarks.  
 
Accuracy–Cost Trade-Off 
Finally, the analysis of computational cost relative to accuracy addresses the economic issue of 
tying gains from broader and deeper structures to token usage and execution expenses. The 
present results suggest a clear trade-off: methods with additional depth or breadth, such as 
chain-of-thought or ensemble approaches, do yield improved alignment with human benchmarks 
but incur proportionally higher token (and thus financial) costs – especially under commercial 
pricing structures (OpenAI, 2025). In a broader sense, these findings map onto organizational 
arguments positing that screening tools must balance thoroughness against resource constraints 
(Blohm et al., 2016). From a managerial standpoint, such adaptive strategies could be vital if firms 
want to scale screening without incurring prohibitive AI expenses. An adjacent dimension 
significantly present for OS-Ranking variants is the production of non-valid outputs (incomplete 
or malformed rankings). The data show a notable increase in these invalid responses, particularly 
under high determinism conditions, smaller AI models, and larger set sizes. Assuming the AI is 
supposed to provide a fixed number of valid responses in order to ensure that outliers are 
flattened out, this study integrated a validity factor 𝛼𝑖. This constant is especially of economic 
meaning as it can influence which variant is the most economically efficient one. However, the 
exact prediction of this constant’s value poses an open question on the interplay of model 
robustness based on model choice, model determinism, and ranking set-size.

Further Research Avenues 
Beyond the immediate implications for AI-based evaluations, the exploratory findings of this study 
also reveal avenues for deeper investigation. Four such avenues are highlighted below, which are 
considered to add the most value to the academic discourse. 
First, as this study showed a decline in accuracy for the AI model 4o-mini when facing larger sets 
(as well as a non-significant but still negative decline for the 4o model), real-world idea evaluation 
scenarios, typically involve far more than 17 ideas under consideration, indicating that ranking 
may be less useful in such scenarios. However, as the general determinism of ranking-variants 
can unfold significant advantage over scoring variants, future research could explore modular 
ranking structures that cut larger pool of proposals into manageable subsets, rank them 
separately, and then merge or reconcile the partial results. Such an approach might maintain 
ranking’s advantage in ambiguous tasks also for very large numbers of ideas. However, research 
must show whether this assumption proves true.  
Secondly, and adjacent to the call for decomposition strategies in ranking scenarios, Bubble 
Sort’s ‘evaluation laziness’ under high ambiguity suggests potential gains from mitigating this 
issue systematically – especially since forcing algorithms to provide distinct evaluations proved 
to be valuable under ambiguity (i.e., using OS-Ranking). Experimental work on whether forcing 
Bubble Sort to perform a minimum number of passes can improve its task-based robustness, 
could make it a more universally-applicable alternative – especially promising as it did show 
accuracy dominance over OS-Ranking in evaluating idea novelty. 
Third: While CoT and ensemble structures showed that deeper and broader structures can 
improve AI's alignment with human judgement – especially under ambiguous constructs – 
questions remain about how far this benefit extends. Future research could explore whether there 
is a point of diminishing returns beyond which the addition of further refinement or aggregation 
agents no longer improves accuracy, or even begins to introduce new biases. Relatedly, examining 
tasks that range from highly structured to extremely ill-defined may clarify whether a minimum 
threshold of task complexity needs to be reached before such structures actually pay off. Such 
research would not only be valuable to find local accuracy optima but would also enrich 
information on the cost-accuracy trade-off. 
Fourth, as research by Falchetti et al. (2022) highlights, specific wording or rhetorical choices 
(framing) can significantly affect how evaluators perceive a proposal’s value. Additionally, and 
specifically acknowledged in the venture capital context (Colombo et al., 2023), information 
asymmetry puts objective evaluations at risk. While this thesis used uniform, generative AI-
==PAGE:79==
72 
 
generated summaries containing potentially loaded terms (e.g. ‘disruptive’), the effects of more 
or less exaggerated self-presentation were not systematically explored. In practice, entrepreneurs 
might try to manipulate automated screens by implementing buzzwords such as ‘extremely 
profitable’ or ‘highly disruptive’ in their submissions. Investigating how such strategic framing 
influences AI-driven assessments and testing whether standardised language or framing 
reduction filters could mitigate manipulation for consecutive AI-based evaluations, is an 
important direction for future work. Such research would be important to not just make AI-based 
evaluation a scalable solution under laboratory settings, but a reliable and sustainable solution 
under contexts of reacting environments. 
 
Methodological Limitations 
While this research advances the understanding of AI-based screening, certain limitations on 
generalizability apply. First, this study condensed each startup pitch into a roughly 200-word 
summary. Although this approach enhances comparability across cases, it inevitably neglects 
richer contextual nuances such as founder passion, or detailed product or market information. 
By focusing on simplified, text-only descriptions, the study does not capture the full complexity 
of live pitches or deck-driven presentations. In real-world settings, AI could be exposed to images 
containing important information on aesthetic appeal, as well as visual information integrated in 
charts and graphs. Furthermore, it must be acknowledged, that the empirical analysis relied on 
17 business proposals. Although sufficient for revealing patterns and interactions among the 
tested variables, real-world scenarios typically include a multiple of such in evaluation pipelines 
– the limitation especially bounded insights when assessing the effect of different set sizes in 
ranked evaluations. Therefore, future research must validate whether the identified advantages, 
disadvantages, and moderation effects hold true at scale. Third, and even though the results 
consistently highlight how task ambiguity, model depth, determinism, and various scoring or 
ranking approaches interact, these insights are grounded in the controlled setting of this study. In 
more diverse environments, where business ideas span multiple industries, languages, or funding 
stages, factors such as cultural context, regulatory diversity, or drastically different data formats 
may moderate the effectiveness of AI-based screening. Consequently, organizations seeking to 
implement the presented approaches should consider running smaller-scale tests to verify local 
validity. However, overall, these limitations do not undermine the core contributions regarding the 
interplay between task ambiguity and AI evaluation design. Instead, they explain the contexts in 
which this study’s findings apply most robustly, underscoring the need for further research.

Conclusion 
In conclusion, this thesis has shown that AI-based idea evaluation cannot rely on a simple, one-
size-fits-all solution. Addressing the main research question of how AI-based scoring compares 
to AI-based ranking for assessing idea novelty and investment attractiveness, the results illustrate 
a strong influence of task ambiguity. When constructs are relatively well defined (as in novelty), 
scoring approaches tend to be more closely aligned with human judgement. However, when 
constructs become more multi-dimensional (as in investment attractiveness), ranking methods 
offer a clearer advantage. This pattern contradicts assumptions from human-based studies that 
scoring is always superior and shows that generative AI responds differently once cognitive fatigue 
is taken out of the equation (Cui et al., 2019). Sub-questions about whether the number of items 
to be scored affects AI accuracy, how increasing the breadth and depth of the AI's scoring 
framework improves performance, and how the model's behavioural settings shape outcomes 
point to important moderating variables. First, the size of the ranked set was found to influence 
ranking accuracy, with larger sets causing a decline in performance, though larger AI models did 
show more resilience in handling bigger sets. Secondly, the incorporation of depth (such as chain-
of-thought processes) and breadth (through ensemble approaches) consistently enhanced 
accuracy, though at the cost of increased computational demands. Finally, the tuning of AI’s 
parameters to higher determinism, had a positive moderating effect on overall accuracy for tasks 
characterized by clear, narrow criteria, while this effect diminishes when the task becomes more 
ambiguous.  
Taken together, these findings are important because they highlight the need for organisations to 
tailor their AI strategies to the specific types and volumes of suggestions they face. The 
implications are both practical and theoretical. Managers can use the results to tailor screening 
pipelines - using scoring for narrower constructs, ranking in poorly structured domains, and 
opting for deeper, iterative approaches where a modest increase in cost is acceptable. 
Furthermore, the mixed evidence on 'bigger is better' for model size - with smaller models 
sometimes outperforming larger ones in simpler domains - cautions against blanket investment 
in more powerful architectures. 
Future research could explore how adaptive prompts or hybrid ranking algorithms can overcome 
'evaluation laziness' in ambiguous tasks, or whether different forms of artificial diversity in 
ensemble models can generate truly independent viewpoints. Ultimately, these findings highlight 
that the promise of AI-based evaluation lies not simply in automating human review steps, but in 
purposefully configuring how generative AI thinks about and compares new ideas.
