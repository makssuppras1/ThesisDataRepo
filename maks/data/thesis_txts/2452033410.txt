Abstract
The goal of this thesis is to make a system that can be conﬁgured to mine websites
for travel data. By looking into how a metasearch engine works, and how the inter-
net is structured, this thesis suggests an easier way of making a metasearch engine
that can be conﬁgured by a user, instead of programmed by a developer.
A metasearch engine use data mining to extract information from the internet and
present it to a user. This thesis explains in detail, how a data mining system can be
developed and serve as a metasearch engine. To ensure this thesis contributes with
new knowledge, the principle of Design Science is used.
The outcome of this thesis, is a a metasearch engine application which can be conﬁg-
ured by a user. The application oﬀers a GUI to ease the making of the conﬁgurations
and a service to test for possible errors in the conﬁgurations. Through user testing,
the developed application is tested to establish if the application can save time de-
veloping a meatsearch engine, and put less workload on a developer.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Domestic travels within Denmark has become increasingly complex for travellers
since private travel providers has entered the market of domestic travels. Because
of the size of Denmark and good infrastructure, domestic travels has mainly been
in private cars and public trains. Now, travelling by bus, airplane, boat or sharing
cars has become more popular than ever. The means of alternative transportation is
now competing with the train industry, with cheaper ticket prices and faster travel
duration.
Finding the cheapest, fastest or most convenient travel has become a task of com-
paring multiple websites, of diﬀerent travel providers, with each other to ﬁnd the
travel that ﬁts the customer’s needs. Looking at special tickets like student-, family-
or senior tickets, ﬁnding the right ticket is time consuming for the traveller. This is
the reason the company Travelit was created.
Travelit is a startup company developing a metasearch engine.
The metasearch
engine collects the cheapest and fastest tickets from various travel companies, that
oﬀers transportation services within Denmark. Customers simply enter their desired
destination and date of travel, to get the best oﬀers matching their traveling needs.
Travelit’s product consists of two applications: a front-end application that presents
travel suggestions to customers, and a back-end application, that ﬁnds these travels.
The latter is called a Metasearch Engine and according to Adele E. Howe and Daniel
Dreilinger, which wrote an article about their own take on a metasearch engine, de-
scribed it in their article as follows: “Metasearch engines reduce the user burden by
dispatching queries to multiple search engines in parallel”[1], which means that the
metasearch engine retrieves data from other data sources at the same time. After
the data retrieval, the data is systematized and presented to a user.
In this thesis, the goal is to create an easier and less time- and money consuming
way of making a metasearch engine for a company like Travelit. To achieve this goal,
several steps needs to be taken and these steps are structured in this thesis as follows:

In this chapter the problems of making and maintaining a metasearch engine, in
relation to the travel domain, is explicated. The travel domain has been chosen as
an example of making a metasearch engine for, and travel information is therefore
the data that will be mined. The problems presented, in this thesis, are also present

2.1
Metasearch engines
Metasearch engine is a word that has already been used a couple of times in this
thesis. To elaborate what a metasearch engine is, we will look at the properties of
it, how it conceptually works and what it can deliver.
A metasearch engine use data from other search engines and/or websites and/or
APIs to produce a result within a certain domain. As an example, the metasearch
engine is, in this project, initially be developed for the travel industry, with the
purpose of extracting travel information from various travek provider websites. Af-
ter the data extraction, the extracted data will be interpreted and presented to the
customer using the metasearch engine. The concept of gathering travel data can be
extended to gathering of other kinds of data such as hotel- or food data.
There are diﬀerent kinds of metasearch search engines, ranging from web search
engines such as Google Search to travel search engines such as Momondo. Google
Search is a well known search engine, often used when you want to ﬁnd information
on the internet. Momondo is a search engine used to search for ﬂights and hotels.
The main diﬀerence of the search engines, is the content that is returned to the user
when initiating a search on a metasearch engine. Some are more general, and some
are more specialized.
In this project, the content returned by the metasearch engine to the customer, is

2.2
Background
Developing a metasearch engine, that searches the web for information, is not a
new concept. Retailers use metasearch engines to compare their prices with their
competitors and shoppers use them to ﬁnd products at the lowest price. This has
created a more competitive marked for the retailers. Costumers are also becoming
more informed than ever and loyalty to a provider decrease if they can not get their
desired product for the cheapest price. It is important for businesses ranging from
retail to travel providers, to be up to date with their competitors and their products.
Considering the growth in e-commerce, online shopping makes it easy for a customer
to choose the seller with the lowest price. There is a growth in online shopping, and
it is predicted that this growth will continue. Appendix B shows Statista.com’s
statistics about the number of digital buyers and a prediction in growth. The travel
industry has already seen many metasearch engine that compares diﬀerent travels
by price and duration, but mostly within the domain of air travels.
As described in the Introduction Chapther 1, Travelit is a start-up company making
a metasearch engine for domestic travels within Denmark. For a start-up, resources
such as money, time and number of employees, are often low compared to well es-
tablished companies. This means, that development and other processes might take
longer than expected and is often depended on a few people that have to acquire
all the required knowledge to develop a metasearch engine. Hiring developers is
expensive for all companies and the demand for them is increasing [2].
Developing a metasearch engine arise both business- and technical challenges, con-
cerning money and development. Implementing and maintaining a metasearch en-
gine eﬃciently while keeping the cost low can be problematic. This thesis will cover
how a metasearch engine works and what problems there might be with implement-
ing and maintaining it.
After looking at the problems, it is suggested how the
problems can be solved.

Metasearch engine concepts and problems
A metasearch engine retrieves data from data providers with the use of deep link-
ing, data mining and requests to APIs. Deep linking refers to the act of accessing
a resource on the WWW, while data mining refers the act of retrieving data for a
speciﬁc purpose. Mining data that contains information about various travels within
Denmark is the speciﬁc purpose of this metasearch engine.
Developing a system that collects data from diﬀerent providers often results in a
developer implementing a tailored part of the search engine that collects data from
each provider. Depending on how the provider presents the data, e.g. by a web-
page, with the use of an API etc., a unique implementation has to be made for each
provider. This can be time consuming for a developer and cost a lot of money to
develop.
Data sources
Data can be obtained by a metasearch engine, from a data provider, in diﬀerent
ways. Typically, a provider is responsible of managing their own data in their own
systems and presents them on their own websites. If a provider wish to distribute
their data to other instances, such as other companies or metasearch engines, the
providers oﬀers an API where their data can be retrieved from.
It is common to have an API for data distribution, but this is not always the case
and it depends on the provider. Some providers only show their own data on their
own website, which leaves two kinds of methods to retrieve data from a provider:
• API
• Websites
API
An API1 is made with the purpose of distributing data from one system to another[3].
Retrieving and using data from an API is relatively simple, compared to data mining.
The data from a REST API is often structured in a logically way, to give a better
overview of the data.
Websites
Websites contains information often structured in a user-friendly way. A website’s
content is structured by using HTML code, beautiﬁed with CSS and any function-
ality on the website is typically made with JavaScript. Mining data from a website
consists of extracting values from the HTML code. HTML code is not structured
to be readable by a human but to be translated by a browser, into a website that is
user-friendly and human readable.
1In this thesis, the API referred to is a REST API. The REST API is a widely architecture
used to make web services that to provide inseparability between systems on the WWW.

Data mining
Data mining is the act of extracting and mining useful knowledge or information
from a source. This thesis will look into a category of data mining called Web min-
ing. Web mining consists of subcategories which is; Web usage mining, Web content
mining and Web structure mining. Bing Liu and Kevin Chen-Chuan Chang deﬁne,
in their editorial: Special Issue on Web Content Mining [4], the subcategories of
Web mining as follows:
• Web usage mining refers to the discovery of user access patterns from Web
usage logs.
• Web structure mining tries to discover useful knowledge from the structure
of hyperlinks.
• Web content mining aims to extract/mine useful information or knowledge
from Web page contents.
Retrieving data from a provider with data mining often consists of Web structure
mining but mostly Web content mining. Relative to retrieving data from an API,
this can be a tedious task for a developer, since a metasearch engine needs to extract
speciﬁc parts of the data from a data source containing a lot of irrelevant data also
known as noise.
2.3
Problem summary
Building a metasearch engine comes with diﬀerent issues depending on the domain
the metasearch engine is build for. As aforementioned, this thesis will use the travel
domain as a reference and the issues presented will be from this domain. Other
domains can raise other issues, but many of the issues with building a metasearch
engine easily, at a low cost, are still present in other domains.
Developing and maintaining a metasearch engine can be time consuming and costly.
Businesses want to develop the metasearch engine at a low cost but developers are
expensive. It is more challenging and costly to retrieve data from a provider that
does not provide an API.
Implementing a provider into a metasearch engine takes time and requires an unique
implementation of each provider, which can be time and money consuming.

Template-Based Information Mining from HTML Documents
Jane Yung-jen Hsuz and Wen-tau Yih [10] describe the making of a mining tool that
extracts FAQ information from HTML documents. They have considered the same
problems as in this thesis, where developing a data mining tool can be diﬃcult and
require a lot of eﬀort as stated in Section 2.3 page 16. They suggest using templates
and match them with HTML documents and thereby extract relevant information.
Their templates are predeﬁned structures of a websites that they compare to a real
webpage. The template that matches a given webpage the most is considered the
most accurate for mining relevant data, and from there on, data can be extracted.
In comparison to this thesis, the use of template suggests that the DMS should have
some predeﬁned structure of a webpage that is used to help the system extract data
with. The DMS could have a template describing where on a page data can be found.
HT2X[ML]: An HTML Converter
Hossein Shahsavand Baghdadi and Bali Ranaivo-Malan¸con [11] describes a method
of converting a HTML document into the XML format. The purpose for this, is to
be able to distribute the information from a webpage into other systems in a format
that is well known. The format could, as an example, be an XML schema deﬁning
what data to extract. The tool they describe and develop (HT2XML) is a converter,
where the user of the converter edits a HTML document with the help of the tool,
and converts it to the XML format. In short, the tool removes ”empty” tags (tags
that does not contain a value) and removes tags with attributes the user decides
should not be kept. The suggested tool is great at removing content that most likely
does not contain any usable information, but does not go into depth with the actual
data nested inside the tags unless it is empty.
From this paper, it can be concluded that a system that extracts data from an
HTML document, must be able to remove or ignore data that is not useful in the
context. This can be done looking at tags, and only use tags that has a meaning to
the value that is to be extracted.

on several other search engines. The result from other search engines is then fused
together by a predeﬁned policy which creates a result set. The result set is then
presented to the user making the query. The improved suggested architecture, seen
in Figure 5.1, considers user preferences and modiﬁes the query from the user, to the
other search engines. Then, Inquirus 2 retrieves and analyze the results returned
from the other search engines, before then again fusing and ordering the data into
a result set.
Figure 5.1: The architecture of the Inquirus 2 search engine [12]
In reference to the DMS, the improvement on the Inquirus 2 metasearch engine
architecture can be used as an inspiration for the design of the system that will be
developed in this thesis. The Inquirus 2 implies that there is a common architecture
of a metasearch engine, and that the policies is what makes a metasearch engine
specialized. The specialization can be used to get data from within a speciﬁc domain
or help return a search result that is unique to a user of the metasearch engine. This
thesis will look further into how the policies can be used to make a metasearch engine
for a speciﬁc domain.

front-end application requesting it.

File format of conﬁguration ﬁles
Before deﬁning the content of a conﬁguration ﬁle, this section establish the ﬁle for-
mat and syntax rules.
A ﬁle format of the conﬁguration ﬁles can be developed to ﬁt only the DMS, with
a syntax that is only understood by the DMS. Developing a new syntax can have
the advantage of only supporting what is needed by the system, without anything
else that may need to be interpreted by the system using the conﬁguration ﬁles. It
is also easy to develop upon the syntax to ﬁt the needs the system might have. One
of the disadvantages with developing a new format, is the work that has to be put
in to develop it, which is not needed in this system.
Instead of deﬁning a new format, a known and well established format can be cho-
sen. The advantage of a well established format is the amount of documentation
that can easily be found and there exists many tools for working with them. Exam-
ples of a well established formats is JSON (JavaScript Object Notation) and XML (
Extensible Markup Language). JSON and XML are both ﬁle formats with a clear de-
ﬁned syntax, that is usually used to parse information between two or more systems.
The DMS does not need to have a special syntax, and it does not need a complicated
syntax. The JSON format is chosen for its simplicity and ease to work with. JSON
is somewhat human understandable and there exists many good and well tested
tools that can be used when working with JSON. It is generally easy to used with
object oriented programming languages. The ﬁle extension of the conﬁguration ﬁles
is therefore .json. The JSON structure supports objects, arrays and standard value
types such as strings and integers and always has a name/value pair [17].

The URLs are created, by the DMS., when a search is initiated on an API endpoint.
This section provides an overview of how the DMS constructs URLs and a design
of the URL part of the conﬁgurations. The URL conﬁgurations are used by URL

Constructing URLs
A URL always follow the same base structure with the same relative positioning of
the protocol, domain and path. The DMS can therefore, in most cases, assume that
the ﬁrst part of the URL is the same for each travel provider and the beginning of
every URL always have the following structure:
protocol + :// + domain + / + path + ? = https://domain.com/path?
The above example is the ﬁrst part of an URL, that is saved as a conﬁguration. This
part will be referred to as baseURL and path, when an example of a conﬁguration
==PAGE:59==
54
Design
is shown later in this section.
Some parts of the URL are not static, as in the example above. In the end of Sec-
tion 6.1.1 page 46, it was seen, that every URL from each of the investigated travel
providers had three parameters in common: departure city, arrival city and date of
travel. The three parameters changed accordingly to the travel that was searched
for. These three parameters are the search parameters as mentioned in Section 7.2.
The search parameters, are a part of the URL query string. The query string con-
sists of one or more name-value pairs in the end of a URL after a ”?” (question
mark). The structure of an URL with a query string is shown below:
https://domain.com/path?name=value
The red coloured name, is a static name of a variable. This variable could be one of
the aforementioned three common parameters. The red coloured value, is the value
assigned to the variable. In the conﬁgurations, the DMS needs to know the variable
name and what the variable means. To simplify the three common parameters, they
will be called: ORIGIN, DESTINATION and DATE. ORIGIN, DESTINATION
and DATE, are the name of the name-value pair from the query string.
To summarize what a conﬁguration will contain up until now, Listing 7.2 show an
example of a URL conﬁguration. The red text are variables that the DMS have spe-

8.3.2
Mining data
As mentioned in the Design chapter 7 Section 7.4.2, a search is initiated with search
parameters: origin location, destination and a search date. These parameters are
used, with the URL conﬁgurations, to create an URL. The URL is used to retrieve
a HTML document which is then mined and mapped to the data model, seen in
Figure 7.2 from Section 7.3. This process was shown in Figure 5.3 Section 5.2.
When a HTML document is retrieved, the DMS loads the mining conﬁguration of
the provider to ﬁnd all elements with a path corresponding to the ones in the mining
conﬁguration. After ﬁnding the elements, the attributes of the elements are com-
pared with the attributes from the mining conﬁguration to ensure that the elements
contains the values that can be mapped to the data model. The elements, that are
left after comparing the attributes, are then mapped to the data model. Elements
are mapped using the property name from the mining conﬁguration, which corre-

10.3
Application evaluation
After performing the testing the DMS on test subjects, it is clear that some things
have to be improved on the DMS application. This section explains where the DMS
can be improved and possible issue that might arise.
The DMS is not able to mine data from other pages than the one conﬁgured in the
URL conﬁgurations. Sometimes, data like departure times and price is found on
diﬀerent pages of a website, but they still have a relation that describes a travel.
The DMS should be able to gather all data related to a travel. Therefore, the DMS
should be able to mine data from several sites and combine the data to one travel.
With the current method of mining data, with the use of HTML code in a table,
this is a hard task for the DMS system. It is especially hard to mine data from
diﬀerent pages when the page’s URL, containing the related data, is not known be-
fore a search is made, like the current URL construction do. The suggested solution
to this problem, would be to look for places in the HTML code that refers to the
page containing the related data. This solution would make the creation of mining
conﬁgurations more advanced, since the required knowledge of HTML and internet
structure would increase, but make a system that could be used in more use cases.
The GUI should be easier to use. Creating a GUI can take a lot of time and requires
more testing with test subjects. From the tests already conducted, several points
were already made on how to make the user experience better. This includes provid-
ing the user with a conﬁrmation when a button is clicked, and saving conﬁgurations
automatically.
Looking at the Status And Test page, errors were only found if a data set was in-
complete. An incomplete data set is a travel missing a values such as the price.
Fixing an error would require a reconﬁguration of the provider with an error. With
the current state of the DMS, it was not possible to gather all information from all
the pages the system was tested with. Therefore, errors found by the DMS in the
Status and Test page could not be ﬁxed. The validation functionality still works as
intended, where it points out which provider conﬁguration might have an error. If
a data set is incomplete, the system comes up with a warning, on the status page,
and the user knows which provider and part of the conﬁguration, needs to be recon-
ﬁgured.
The focus of this thesis has been to develop a metasearch engine that could mine
travel data from travel provider’s websites. The DMS is not able to mine data from
websites, that loads content into the website after a page is loaded. These websites,
injects HTML code into the website after a page is loaded using JavaScript. The
Jsoup library is not capable of handling execution of JavaScript function, which
means that the DMS is not capable neither.
Some providers oﬀer an API to access their data, instead of having to mine that
data from the provider’s websites. API data is often well structured, and would be
==PAGE:92==
10.4 Research Questions revisited
87
an easier task for the DMS to mine. For further development, the DMS should be
able to mine data using provider APIs.
The DMS is only able to map speciﬁc values from a travel provider website, such
as the aforementioned departure time, arrival time and price. To make the DMS
viable in other domains, the DMS should be able to mine other values speciﬁed by
the user. The values could be in the travel domain, but also in other domains such
a the car sales or even food. In other words, the DMS could act as a metasearch
engine for many domains. This would require that the user of the DMS is able to
conﬁgure a new data model, instead of having a static data model implemented in
the code base. Implementing this functionality, would require more programming
knowledge from the user, since the user would have to know about diﬀerent data
types, such as strings, integers, dates etc..
10.4
Research Questions revisited
The scientiﬁc contribution of this thesis is provided by answering the research ques-
tion set for this thesis in section 3.3.2. This section answers the research questions
based on the conclusions made throughout this thesis.
RQ1
How can we develop a system that eases the implementation for a developer of a
metasearch engine?
The DMS answers the ﬁrst question by being conﬁgurable. Through testing, it was
established that users, with no prior knowledge of developing a metasearch engine,
was able to conﬁgure the DMS with at least two providers within an hour. By
developing a system that is conﬁgured instead of programmed, it takes less time
and knowledge to make the metasearch engine mine data from a new provider.
RQ2
How can we reduce the domain- and software developing knowledge required to de-
velop a metasearch engine?
Developing a metasearch engine, requires programming knowledge and knowledge
about the structure of the internet, URL and HTML. Making a tool (the DMS),
which can be conﬁgured instead of programmed, requires programming knowledge,
but when the tool is developed, several metasearch engines can be conﬁgured instead
of programmed. The tool needs to be maintained and developed upon, but using
the tool does not require programming knowledge.

RQ3
What can be done to help a developer make a metasearch engine?
By taking out the programming aspect of making a metasearch engine, conﬁguring
the DMS should be faster than programming a metasearch engine. Conﬁguring the
DMS requires the user to learn how URLs are composed by each developer, as it
would programming it, but the structure of a URL is already given. The GUI is
therefore helps the user to understand how to retrieve an internet resource. Giving
a user a GUI, instead of making the user with program a metasearch engine, helps
the process of developing a metasearch engine. The GUI of the DMS also gives a
higher level of abstraction when it come to making the conﬁgurations. When giving
a developer a tool, the developer needs to learn to use that tool. Therefore, it helps
the developer to have documentation of the tool, as the handbook made for the
DMS in Appendix D.
Since metasearch engines have a similar architecture, as established in the Inquirus
2 project 5.1.2, a general system can be created to retrieve and mine data with.
The Inquirus 2 project expanded upon the Inquirus project by making policies that
deﬁned what content that should be returned to the customer. The policies of the
general system can be made into a conﬁgurable system as in the DMS. By giving
a developer the aforementioned general system, a developer can make a special-
ized metasearch within a certain domain, without the need to develop the actual
metasearch engine.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This thesis has looked into the subject of creating an easier and faster way of de-
veloping a metasearch engine.
A system, referred to as the DMS (Data Mining
System), is developed and tested to prove that the data, that a metasearch engine
mines, can be conﬁgured by a user instead of programmed. The DMS has proven,
that data from the travel domain can be mined only by conﬁguring the DMS.
The DMS is a tool, providing a structure of a metasearch engine, that is then
conﬁgured to be a metasearch engine for a speciﬁc domain. By giving a developer
a tool to create a metasearch engine, a lot of the work that would be needed to
create a metasearch engine has already been done. It has not been fully proven,
that the DMS helps developers make a metasearch engine or helps the process of
making a metasearch engine. The user tests, conducted in this thesis, insinuates
that conﬁguring a metasearch engine is easier and faster to do for people with no
prior knowledge of making such a system, though it is not proven.

Chapter 1
General Information
The Data Mining System (DMS), is a system made for mining data from various websites
and make the mined data available through a REST API. The content the system mines is
conﬁgured by the user and this handbook will present how the system is conﬁgured.
This handbook is developed for users which posses basic knowledge on how the mechanism
of the internet works. This includes how an URL is structured, basic HTML knowledge and
and what is means to retrieve and manage internet resources.
1.1
System Overview
The DMS application is a metasearch engine, supplying a front-end system with with data.
The DMS oﬀers a REST API, which can be used to perform a search on the DMS, returning
a list of travel information from a range of travel providers. The DMS allows the you to
add and conﬁgure travel providers, which will appear in the searches that is perform via the
REST API.
The travel information the system collects includes where a travel starts (also referred to
as origin/from), where it the travel ends (also referred to as destination/to), ticket prices,
departure- and arrival times. In this handbook, it is explained how to conﬁgure the system
to mine travel information from one of more providers, into one complete list. A search
requires a starting location, destination and date and will return the list of travels. The list
of travels will only contain information from travel providers that oﬀers a travel within the
start location, destination and the date constraints.
To maintain the system, and minimize downtime on the system, the DMS oﬀers you the
functionality to set up daily tests that will report if any search returned a result missing
data. Its operational status is under development. The DMS provides a GUI for making
conﬁgurations.
1.2
Organization of the Manual
The handbook consists of three sections: General Information, Installation and a tutorial
for how to use and conﬁgure the system.
• General Information section explains in general terms the system and the purpose for
which it is intended.
• Installation section provides an overview of what is required to compile and host the
system.
• Tutorial sections presents an overview of the system GUI and how it should be used
to conﬁgure the system.

URL conﬁguration
Note: This step requires a provider conﬁguration has already been loaded (see section 3.3.1).
The URL conﬁguration consists of breaking up a URL into the diﬀerent pieces that it consists
of, and input the pieces into the input-boxen in the URL conﬁguration. We will go through
an made-up example of how to acquire an URL and what to input as a conﬁguration. If you
9
==PAGE:117==
already know how an URL acquired and deconstructed, the URL deconstruction example
can be skipped and go to the section URL conﬁguration inputs instead.
URL deconstruction example
Firstly, an URL is needed. The URL can be seen as a unique code to access a speciﬁc re-
source on the internet. In this example, the resource that is needed is a website with travel
information. To acquire an URL, go through the following steps:
1. Open a new browser window (in the web browser of your choice) and go to the selected
provider’s website, that you wish to mine data from.
2. Perform a search on the provider’s website.
3. Copy the URL from the page with the results of the search.
The URL acquired from the website should have a structure similar the example URL seen
on Listing 3.1.
https://example.dk/search?from=city1&to=city2&departuredate=2019-06-18&
,→return=false
Listing 3.1: URL example
The red coloured part of the URL is what we will refer to as the base URL The base URL
is everything up till the word including the ”/” slash.
The orange coloured part search part, is the URL path. The URL path may consists of
several parameters all separated by a ”/” (slash).
Everything from the ”?” (question mark) are parameters used to deﬁne which constrains
there is for the travel we are looking for. The green coloured words are the keywords that
deﬁnes where a travel starts from, where the destination is and on which date. It is impor-
tant that only the parameter keywords is used (the word on the left side of the ”=” (equal)
symbol), since the black words shown as city1, city2 and purple 2019-06-18 will be changed
with each search.
Lastly, the parameter with the brown colour is a constant parameter that is needed to per-
form a search, but will never change. There can be several constant parameters required by
the travel provider’s website to perform a search.
Some searches may not require all constant parameters to make a usable URL.
With a URL and the knowledge about the diﬀerent parts needed from it, we are now able
to make the conﬁguration. The colour coding from Listing 3.1 matches that seen on Figure
3.4.

Making a search
To make a search, we need the input boxes marked within the red box. The input simulates
what a user would input into the system to make a search. The inputs are which city the
travel starts in (From), the destination city (Destination) and the departure date (Date).
After entering the input, click the Get HTML Page-button to retrieve the HTML code from
the provider’s website, which will appear in the table in the purple box.
opening the travel provider’s website
To check that the search ﬁnds the correct content, the full link can be used that is shown
within the green box. Open this link in a new windows and conﬁrm that there is content to
mine. This web page is also gonna be the reference for the next step: Mining Content.
Mining content
To mine content, look at the web page we opened in the previous step. Find what content
to mine and write it into the search input box. When travel information has been found,
use the dropdown menu, shown within the black box, to choose what that travel information
correspond to. It can correspond to one of the following parameters:
• Origin which is the start location of a travel.
• Destination which is the destination of a travel.
• Departure Time which is the time of a departure.
• Arrival Time which is the time of a arrival.
• Price which is the cost of the travel.
Use the search functionality to ﬁnd all the possible values and select the meaning of the
values in the dropdown menu. A value is considered ”better” if it only contains the value
you are looking for, but should it contain HTML code encapsulating the values, it will not
have ruin the mining.
As an example, a departure time on a travel provider’s website might be ”08:30”. Search for
this value and assign in as departurTime
Once every relevant value has been found an categorized with the dropdown menu, click the
Accept conﬁgurations-button to save the mining conﬁguration.

the travel provider conﬁgurations. The page is divided into two subsections: Status and

3.4.1
Status
The Status subsection provides an overview over recent errors in the system caught by the
daily tests. The system check the result of each search to see if its complete with all the
information the search should have gathered. In the case of an incomplete result, the system
logs the error.
Start/stop scheduled tests
Screen capture in Figure 3.7 outlines, within the red box, where the daily tests can be start-

Error counter

ﬁgurations
Log management
The green box outlines log management. To retrieve X newest lines from the logs, input a
number of lines into the input box and click the Get recent error logs-button.
To delete the log, click the Delete Error Logs-button.
The logs contains the following information:
• Which conﬁguration had an error.
• A time-stamp of when the error occurred.
• Which type of provider conﬁguration had the error (This is for future releases, when
the DMS can mine APIs etc.).
• Search details; From, To, and departure date.
• Number of errors in the search.
• A speciﬁcation of the error(s) found in the search.
