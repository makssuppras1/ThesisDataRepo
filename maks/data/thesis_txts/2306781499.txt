Abstract 
Environmental footprints are tools used to quantify the pressure caused by human activity on the 
environment. National environmental footprints can be calculated through the use of 
Environmentally Extended Multi-Regional Input Output (EE-MRIO) models, which combine economic 
data with emission inventories. A review of the two EE-MRIO models Eora and EXIOBASE revealed 
that human toxicity and ecotoxicity were poorly addressed. Even though they have a significant 
contribution to these impact categories, heavy metal emissions to water and soil are not included in 
these models. In this project, we developed methods to build national inventories of heavy metal 
emissions to water and soil resulting from industrial activities and manure application on agricultural 
soil. Based on an investigation of the relationship between industrial emissions to water and land 
reported to the European Pollutant Release and Transfer Register (E-PRTR) and external parameters 
such as economic gross output and production volumes, a strategy for gap-filling was established and 
applied to 30 European countries between 2007 and 2013. The analysis revealed the presence of 
outliers in the E-PRTR that greatly affect the quality of the inventory and should be tackled. In 
addition, a general framework was established to estimate emissions to agricultural soil resulting 
from manure application. A literature review of the heavy metal content of manure was performed 
and an inventory of the heavy metal emissions caused by manure application was developed for 215 
countries across the world between 2000 and 2014. The study highlighted the sensitivity of methods 
used to measure the heavy metal content of manure and the need to establish a standardized 
method to allow for better country differentiation. Such inventories constitute a first step in the 
development of environmental footprints focusing on human toxicity and ecotoxicity.

1. Introduction 
Footprints are tools commonly used to measure ‘human appropriation of the planet’s natural 
resources and carrying capacity’, the carbon footprint, water footprint, nutrient footprint and 
ecological footprint being the most popular ones (Ercin and Hoekstra, 2012; Cucek et al., 2015). At 
the product scale, they are tools that companies can use to measure their sustainable performance 
in absolute terms (Bjørn et al., 2016). At the national scale, they can help governments monitor their 
pressure on the environment and define environmental policies (Ercin and Hoekstra, 2012). 
Footprints can be calculated using Environmentally Extended Multi-Regional Input-Output (EE-MRIO) 
models that document monetary flows between different regions and the industries operating in 
them and couple them to the emission factors of various substances (Leontief, 1970). This method 
enables one to calculate footprints according to the concept of ‘consumer responsibility’, which 
implies that the demand for consumption is considered as the drive of the environmental impacts 
caused through the international supply chain network, as opposed to the ‘producer responsibility’ 
(Lenzen et al., 2012; Stadler et al., 2014). Eora (Lenzen et al., 2012; Lenzen et al., 2013) and EXIOBASE 
(Tukker et al., 2013; Wood et al., 2015) are two global EE-MRIO models with a large coverage of 
countries and industries. They account for only a limited number of substances, which implies also a 
limited coverage of the different environmental impact categories (Appendix A).

2. Methods 
2.1. Overview of the sources of emissions 
Among toxic substances, heavy metals are recognized to have a significant contribution to human 
toxicity and freshwater ecotoxicity (Table 1). In particular, arsenic, cadmium, mercury and lead are 
on the list of ‘Ten chemicals of major public health concern’ established by the World Health 
Organization (WHO, 2016). While atmospheric emissions are well monitored, waterborne and 
soilborne emissions are less documented, even though releases to water may double the amount 
emitted to air and soil is acknowledged to be an important sink (Laurent and Hauschild, 2015; Pacyna 
et al, 1995). Therefore, this study focused specifically on the emissions of heavy metals to water and 
soil.

Emissions of heavy metals to freshwater may be due to industrial release, domestic wastewater or 
atmospheric deposition. Emissions of heavy metals to soil may originate from the application of 
manure, sewage sludge and fertilizers on agricultural soil, industrial release and atmospheric 
deposition (Nriagu and Pacyna, 1988). Industrial releases to water and soil and manure application 
on agricultural soils are the main focus areas of this study. Heavy metal emissions related to 
domestic wastewater are included in the industrial emissions as part of the waste water treatment 
activities. Other sources of emissions were arbitrarily left aside and should be investigated in further 
work. Furthermore, it is considered that only domestic emissions are to be included in the 
inventories as the EE-MRIO models introduce the consumption approach through the accounting of 
trade between countries and industries. Finally, accidental releases were excluded from the analysis 
as they are exceptional events.

mandatory for facilities that are exceeding thresholds related to their activities (e.g. production 
volume or input rate) and thresholds related to their emission intensity (expressed as kg/year), as

Completeness and consistency of the E-PRTR 
According to the European Commission, the reporting of waterborne emissions is of acceptable 
quality even though it is less complete than for air borne emissions, with missing reports potentially 
due to the lack of adequate methods to quantify the releases. The commission also notes limited 
reporting of releases to land, as well as confusion around the interpretation of them (EC, 2013). The 
European Topic Centre for Air Pollution and Climate Change Mitigation identifies inconsistent time 
series for the releases to water and soil, indicating reporting errors (ETC/ACM, 2015). The European 
Environmental Agency also concludes that reporting may be incomplete in a study of mercury and 
cadmium emissions to water (EEA, 2011). Inconsistencies in reporting may also rise from the diversity 
of methods used to determine emission quantities (measurements, calculations or estimations 
methods). 
Coverage of the E-PRTR  
The capacity and emissions thresholds have been set to cover 90% of the total mass emissions to 
each environmental compartment from facilities regulated under the E-PRTR for each pollutant (EEA, 
2016b). Finland, Norway and Spain have however adopted lower or no thresholds to cover a higher 
number of facilities and pollutants (Umweltbundesamt, 2012). A threshold analysis based on a 
statistical approach demonstrated that the 90% coverage was reached for most of the pollutants 
released to air or water for which the reporting is complete. No conclusion on the thresholds could 
be drawn for releases to land due to the incomplete reporting (Umweltbundesamt, 2012).

Focus of the present study 
Some data gaps could be noticed in the E-PRTR with no report existing for a year or a country where 
it is expected. The goal was therefore to find proxies to systematically complete the database ad 
obtain a full inventory.  
The E-PRTR distinguishes releases and transfers of waterborne emissions, the first being the direct 
introduction of pollutants in the environment and the second being the movement of pollutants in 
waste water destined for waste water treatment. Releases to land are defined as waste that is 
subject to ‘land treatment’ or ‘deep injection’ (EC, 2006). As air borne emissions are known to be 
usually well monitored, the study focused only on releases to water and land. 
For emissions reported since 2007, the economic activity is indicated using the NACE2 Rev.2 
nomenclature, which is a standardized classification of European productive economic activities and 
presents a hierarchical structure defined in Appendix B.1. Emissions reported in 2001 and 2004 to 
the EPER used the previous version of the nomenclature: NACE Rev.1. Due to a different level of 
details in both versions, no direct matching of the two nomenclatures could be performed in a 
systematic way (EC, 2008). As the E-PRTR database (Version 8; EEA, February 2016a) covers all years 
between 2007 and 2013, it provides more data points, making the statistical analysis more 
trustworthy. The investigation of potential correlations with physical parameters such as production 
volumes was therefore performed on this data set only.  
Concerning the substances of interest in toxicity assessment, the following eight heavy metals and 
their compounds are included in the E-PRTR: arsenic (As), cadmium (Cd), chromium (Cr), copper (Cu), 
mercury (Hg), nickel (Ni), lead (Pb) and zinc (Zn). Table 3 presents an overview of the number of 
countries, economic activities and individual facility reports covered by the emissions to water and 
soil documented in the E-PRTR. As can be seen, reporting of emissions to water has a larger coverage 
of countries and activities than for emissions to soil. Furthermore, it was noticed that 98.8% of the 
heavy metal emissions to soil were reported by facilities in France, which confirms the 
incompleteness of the reporting of releases to land (Umweltbundesamt, 2012).

2.2.2. Identification of proxies 
The choice of an external parameter for a correlation analysis is based on the following criteria: it 
must be representative of the domestic activity level of an industry, publicly available, easily 
retrievable, and with a complete geographical and temporal coverage (i.e. all European countries and 
all years between 2007 and 2013). Economic gross output and production volumes were considered 
as potential proxies and the interrelationship between heavy metals was examined.  
Economic gross output 
Both waterborne and soilborne releases of heavy metals were compared to the economic gross 
output by industry which was retrieved from Eurostat (2016a). While the gross domestic product 
(GDP) only accounts for the sales to final users in the economy, the gross output also includes the 
sales to other industries (intermediate consumption) and is therefore believed to better represent 
the activity level of an industry (Sala et al., 2014). Although considering the intermediate 
consumption may result in a double counting as the outputs of an industry are inputs to another 
industry, it has no influence on the statistical analysis as each sector is examined separately. 
The NACE Rev.2 nomenclature used to classify the industries in Eurostat is more aggregated than the 
one used in the E-PRTR. Both nomenclatures were therefore matched according to the level of detail 
of the Eurostat data set (Appendix B.2), resulting in respectively 38 and 14 economic activities 
reporting heavy metal emissions to water and soil. 
The economic data set covers 30 European countries until 2010. The economic gross output appears 
unavailable for some countries since 2011, and can be obtained only for Norway and Malta in 2014. 
Production volumes 
An overview of the E-PRTR data set revealed that all economic activities do not have the same level 
of reporting, as can be seen in Appendix E (sheet I1). Due to the low quantity of data points 
concerning soilborne emissions, no comparison with physical external parameter was carried out. 
Concerning waterborne emissions, sectors such as ‘mining of metal ores’ (07), ‘manufacture of paper 
and paper products’ (17), ‘manufacture of chemicals and chemical products’ (20), ‘manufacture of 
basic metals’ (24), ‘electricity, gas, steam and air conditioning supply’ (35), and ‘sewerage’ (37) are 
subject to the highest frequency of reporting. The ‘fishing and aquaculture’ division (03), and 
especially the marine aquaculture, presents important levels of emissions despite a very few data 
points. Such industrial sectors require therefore specific investigation. 
Due to the high variety in processes and products, no parameter was identified for the ‘manufacture 
of chemicals and chemical products’ (20). Even though emissions released by the energy sector 
(NACE 35) could be related to the energy mix of the country, no single parameter was found 
representative of it. Discharge volumes from waste water treatment plants can be retrieved from 
Eurostat (2016b) but the coverage of countries and years is too incomplete to allow a reliable 
statistical analysis. Similarly, discharge and generation of wastewater by pollutant are documented in 
Eurostat (2016c), but only very few data points are available for copper and zinc.

2.3. Emissions to soil from manure application 
Manure is commonly applied on agricultural soils due to its fertilizing potential. It is however 
recognized that manure contains also a considerable input of heavy metals (Eneji et al., 2008). 
Present in livestock diets at background concentrations, heavy metals may be added to animal feeds 
for health and welfare reasons, with most of what is consumed being excreted (Nicholson et al., 
2003; Faridullah et al., 2014). In intense pig farming, the amount of Cu and Zn eliminated through the 
animal manure corresponds respectively to 72-80% and 92-96% of the amount ingested (Mantovi et 
al., 2003). While the level of heavy metal content in animal feed is regulated by the European 
Commission (EC, 2002; EC, 2003), only a few countries have set concentration limits for the heavy 
metal content of animal manure (Eckel et al., 2005; Lupascu et al., 2009).

2.4. Statistical analysis 
When investigating the potential correlation between some emissions and another parameter, the 
ratios between the two are calculated and their geometric mean (geomean) and geometric standard 
deviation (GSD) are used as representative statistical indicators. The geometric descriptive 
parameters were preferred over the arithmetic ones as the geometric mean of the ratio of 
corresponding observations in two series is equal to the ratios of their respective geometric means. 
As a result, the geometric mean is more adapted to the analysis of normalized results (Fleming and 
Wallace, 1986). Secondly, the presence of outliers in a data set has a limited influence on the 
geometric mean compared to an arithmetic mean (Alexander, 2012). Linear regression was also used 
as an indicator to evaluate the quality of a correlation.

3. Industrial emissions: Results and discussion 
After an analysis of the emissions reported to the E-PRTR and the potential proxies, we established a 
method for developing national inventories of industrial heavy metal emissions and applied it to 30 
European countries between 2007 and 2013. The results are presented separately for emissions to 
water and land. 
3.1. Industrial waterborne emissions of heavy metals 
3.1.1. Total waterborne emissions 
An analysis of the reported total waterborne emissions over time showed that no clear tendency 
could be observed in general, as can be seen for example in Figure 2a. When normalizing the 
emission values by the economic gross output of the corresponding activity, the same pattern was 
obtained (Figure 2b) as the gross output does not present important variations over time. This 
implies that, according to the E-PRTR reporting, there has not been any improvement in pollution 
control technologies. It also means that the important fluctuations noticed in the E-PRTR data are 
most possibly not due to changes in the level of activity of the industry itself, but rather to reporting 
inconsistencies (influence of the reporting threshold, change in estimation methods, etc.).  Some 
outliers could indeed be detected, like the amount of waterborne emissions of nickel reported for 
the mining industry in Czech Republic in 2009 (Figure 2a).

For each industry and each heavy metal, the GSD of the raw emissions and the GSD of the emissions 
normalized by the economic gross output were compared, by country and across all countries. In 
both cases and for releases to water, raw emissions generally have a lower dispersion than 
normalized emission (Appendix E – sheet I3), which demonstrates that the economic gross output is 
not a reliable parameter for extrapolating industrial emissions to water. The reporting 
inconsistencies spotted in section 3.1.1 may be a first explanation. Another potential reason is that 
the economic gross output is influenced by the changes in the price of goods whereas emissions are 
not sensitive to inflation. In addition, an improvement in pollution control technologies would result 
in a decrease of the emissions normalized by the economic gross output, which also limits the use of 
the economic gross output as a proxy for developing national inventories of heavy metal emissions.  
3.1.3. Total waterborne emissions vs. production volumes 
Marine aquaculture and fish production volume 
Copper and zinc emissions resulting from intensive aquaculture have been reported only by Norway, 
Malta and the United Kingdom. Despite their important aquaculture activities, France and Greece 
have not reported any pollutant release, highlighting the potential incompleteness of the E-PRTR 
reporting (Umweltbundesamt, 2012). Plotting the zinc emissions against the production volume of 
the marine aquaculture in reporting countries shows an apparent correlation with a coefficient of 
determination of 0.989 as the three countries actually form two clusters (Figure 5a). However, zinc 
emissions normalized by the fish production volume are quite similar in Norway and United Kingdom, 
but show a very different profile for Malta (Figure 5b). While the GSD of all normalized emissions is 
1.9, Norway performs remarkably well with a GSD = 1.07, which might be due to the absence of 
capacity and emission threshold in this country. The absence of threshold in Norway has also for 
consequence to report more emissions and thus to logically have higher normalized emissions than 
in the United Kingdom, where the capacity and emission thresholds are applied. Consequently, the 
fish production volume is a potentially interesting proxy for zinc released by the marine aquaculture, 
but needs to be verified once other countries with high marine aquaculture production will have 
performed a completeness check concerning their emissions in this sector. 
Copper emissions have a very different profile in Norway and the United Kingdom, as can be seen in 
Figure 5a. While both countries have a similar number of facilities reporting copper emissions, the 
average level of emission per facility is 23 times higher for the United Kingdom than for Norway. An 
important difference in copper reporting was also observed by the ETC/ACM when comparing copper 
emissions to TOC emissions at the facility level (ETC/ACM, 2011). Emitted quantities were calculated, 
but the method used was not specified when they were reported. The difference in result may come 
from a difference in the calculation method, or from a difference in the technology used (e.g. 
antifouling paints, cages).

3.1.6. Inventory results  
Following the hierarchical method defined by Equations 3, 4 and 5, an inventory of industrial 
emissions of arsenic, cadmium, copper, chromium, mercury, nickel, lead and zinc to water was 
developed for 30 European countries between 2007 and 2013. Whereas Serbia has reported 
waterborne emissions of heavy metals to the E-PRTR since 2010, it was excluded from the inventory 
due to the absence of economic data in Eurostat (2016a). The complete inventory of waterborne 
emissions is available in Appendix E with results by activity, country and year (sheet I7), and 
aggregated totals by country and year (sheet I8). 
As the inventory is based on reporting to the E-PRTR which is limited by capacity and emissions 
thresholds, it actually underestimates real total emissions. This might be emphasized in 2007 and 
2008 due to the incomplete reporting noticed for the first of implementation of the E-PRTR 
regulation (Umweltbundesamt, 2012). There is also a higher uncertainty in the results for the years 
2012 and 2013 compared to the other years as the economic gross output was often extrapolated 
from previous years due to incomplete data in the Eurostat database.  
Figure 6 displays the temporal evolution of aggregated results for a few countries with the year 2007 
as the reference point. While most of the countries, like Germany or Norway, have rather consistent 
results from one year to another, some peaks may be observed for others. An analysis at the activity 
scale showed that these peaks are related to outliers in data reported to the E-PRTR, mainly in the 
activities related to sewerage and waste treatment (E37-E39).

3.1.7. Recommendations for further work 
Considering the results, several recommendations were drawn for further work on industrial 
waterborne emissions of heavy metals. To begin with, identifying outliers in the E-PRTR database 
appears to be crucial to limit their influence on any statistical analysis. This might be done by setting 
a threshold on the maximum contribution of a facility to the total release of a pollutant in a country 
for an activity, or through a Weibull analysis that accounts for the size of the reporting facility and 
the level of emission reported (Umweltbundesamt, 2012). Furthermore, including the number of 
reporting facilities in the statistical analysis would help account for the completeness of the database 
when looking for proxies. Concerning waterborne emissions, a study should be carried out to 
investigate if combining releases and transfers to water improves statistical results when looking for 
potential correlation. At the same time, the activity E37-E39 would require a more detailed analysis, 
with a distinction between urban wastewater treatment plants and independently operated 
industrial wastewater treatment plants. The emissions from the urban waste water treatments 
plants could then be compared to the inventory developed by Sala et al. (2014) concerning non-
industrial releases of pollutants in freshwater via wastewater to identify limits and opportunities for 
improvement of both methods. In addition, the interrelationship between the different heavy metals 
should be further investigated, with for example a cross pollutant analysis at the facility level.  
Secondly, the inventory itself is solely based on data reported to the E-PRTR which is itself limited by 
capacity and emission thresholds in order to reach 90% coverage. Estimating total emissions of all 
facilities through a statistical cumulative distribution analysis would enable to scale up the inventory 
(Umweltbundesamt, 2012).  
Finally, a study should be carried out to investigate how to build inventories of industrial heavy metal 
emissions in non-European countries, and investigate if the European geomean of emissions 
normalized by the gross economic output is still an acceptable proxy.

4. Emissions from manure: Results and discussion 
4.1. Application of the framework 
The framework defined in Equation 1 was applied to make the inventory of heavy metal emissions 
(arsenic, cadmium, chromium, copper, mercury, nickel, lead and zinc) in 215 countries across the 
world from 2000 to 2014, with a strong focus on European countries due to data availability. 
4.1.1. Manure applied Mc,y,l  
Based on the data retrieved from FAOSTAT on the quantity of manure applied in Europe in 2012 
(expressed in kg N-content), 57% of the manure comes from cattle (dairy and non-dairy), 20% from 
swine and 17% from poultry. Manure from horses, camels, lamas, mules, asses and buffaloes 
represent only 0.4% of the total manure applied in Europe, and 7.2% in the world. Among these 
livestock, the heavy metal content of manure from buffaloes and asses were analysed by only one 
study each, and others were not investigated at all (Appendix C, Table C3). The heavy metal content 
of solid manure from cattle (non-dairy) was therefore used as an approximation. 
4.1.2. Manure management system P(t)l,c,y 
The analysis of the proportion of animals kept on a liquid manure management system shows some 
clear differences between the livestock: while most of the swine are kept on liquid manure 
management system, almost all layer chicken are kept on solid manure management systems. For 
cattle, results vary a lot among countries, ranging respectively from 0% to 100% and 3% to 100% for 
non-dairy and dairy cattle (Appendix C, Table C1). The choice of the manure management system 
depends on many factors such as the livestock, the herd size or the availability of water in the area 
(BIOFerm, 2009). Laying hens kept on a liquid management system are a clear minority, and no data 
on the heavy metal content of poultry slurry was found. It was therefore assumed that all poultry is 
kept on a solid manure management system in all countries. For countries where there is no 
information on the manure management system for cattle and swine, the geometric mean of the 
available data was used as an approximation. 
4.1.3. Heavy metal content C(HM)l,t,c,y 
Whereas new European regulations concerning the heavy metal content of animal feeding stuffs 
were implemented in 2002 and 2003 (EC, 2002; EC, 2003), the AROMIS database has not been 
updated since the end of the project in 2005, meaning that its data is outdated. However, in Europe, 
heavy metal contents reported in recent studies remain in the same range of values (e.g. Schultheiss 
et al., 2004, Sager, 2007) and no clear tendency could be observed for any of the metals, suggesting 
that the means reported in the AROMIS project are still applicable (Appendix C, Tables C2 and C3). 
Furthermore, several publications highlight the influence of different parameters on the measured 
heavy metal content of manure (e.g. Faridullah et al., 2012; Eckel et al., 2005). They show that, 
irrespective of the livestock, the heavy metal contents of animal manure vary in a broad range across 
studies as it highly depends on the animal diet, age and living conditions (Bolan et al., 2004). Manure 
treatments such as composting or ashing also influence the heavy metal concentration (Faridullah et 
al., 2012; Faridullah et al., 2014; Gul et al., 2015). Moreover, unrepresentative sampling due to 
manure inhomogeneity may results in up to 50% error in measures of heavy metal concentrations 
(Eckel et al., 2005; Gonçalves Júnior et al., 2007). In addition, heavy metals exist in manure under 
different forms (e.g. water-soluble, linked to organic substances, exchangeable, ions, occluded or co-
precipitated) and depending on the reagent used, the measured extractable heavy metal content 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
differs by up to factors of 5 (Irshad et al., 2013; Faridullah et al., 2012). A lack of details regarding the 
procedure used to measure heavy metals concentrations was observed in most of the publications, 
making any data comparison difficult. Due to the high influence of living conditions (including feeding 
diets) and measuring methods on the results and considering the low amount of studies in each 
country, it did not appear relevant or statistically significant to differentiate heavy metal 
concentrations in animal manure over years or between countries in Europe. For manure content of 
cadmium, chromium, copper, nickel, lead and zinc, it is therefore recommended to use the weighted 
mean calculated in the AROMIS project for European countries. For arsenic and mercury, the 
geometric mean of the values reported by studies focusing on Europe may be used as an 
approximation.  
Concerning non-European countries, it can be seen in Figure 9 that heavy metal contents are 
regularly higher than in Europe (values higher than 1). For copper and zinc however, concentrations 
remain in the same order of magnitude as in Europe or score even lower (e.g. Faridullah et al., 2014; 
Adesoye et al., 2014). Higher heavy metal content in manure in non-European countries may be due 
to less restrictive regulations concerning animal feed, or to higher background concentrations in the 
environment caused for example by pollution (Wang et al., 2013). In the absence of more complete 
data on the heavy metal content of manure in non-European countries, values established for Europe 
can be used as a first approximation. It should then be considered during the interpretation that 
results may be an underestimation of the reality.

4.2. Inventory results 
Using the described framework, an inventory of the releases of arsenic, cadmium, chromium, copper, 
mercury, nickel, lead and zinc resulting from the application of manure was developed for 215 
countries across the world between 2000 and 2014. The complete inventory by country and year is 
available in Appendix E with results by country, year and livestock (sheet M1), and aggregated totals 
by country and year (sheet M2). 
For each heavy metal and each country, it was observed that the contribution of a livestock to the 
emissions of a country is first and foremost related to its contribution to the total quantity of manure 
applied. This correlation can be observed for example with the nickel emissions resulting from 
manure application in Belgium (Figure 10). Another parameter that influences the contribution of 
livestock to heavy metal emissions is the heavy metal content of manure, which varies by up to 
factors of 13 between livestock. As a matter of fact, the copper content of manure is the highest with 
swine livestock, thus the contribution of swine to the copper emissions is higher than its contribution 
to the quantity of manure applied (Figure 10). A difference in the type of manure management 
system also introduces different patterns among countries. The dairy cattle contribution illustrates 
the influence of the manure management system as the average copper emissions by quantity of 
manure applied is 9.36E-04 g/kg N-content in France and 8.54E-04 g/kg N-content in Denmark. This 
difference can be explained by the proportion of animals kept on a liquid management system (19% 
in France and 88% in Denmark) and the fact that the heavy metal content is lower in the liquid slurry 
than in the solid manure for this livestock.

4.3. Validation of the framework 
The results of the inventory could be compared to two studies assessing heavy metal emissions 
resulting from manure application in England and Wales and in the whole world (Table 9). 
Nicholson et al. (2003) assessed the heavy metal emissions resulting from manure application based 
on livestock numbers, excreta production quantities, and mean manure heavy metal concentrations. 
Heavy metal inputs calculated in this inventory are slightly higher as the area considered is the whole 
United Kingdom (England, Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland), but remain in the same range of 
values (from 1.4 to 2.2 times higher). The difference is more important for zinc releases, which is 
probably due to the lower zinc content used by Nicholson et al. (2003).  
As visible in Table 9 for the whole world, copper, mercury, nickel and zinc have consistent results, 
whereas the heavy metal inputs of arsenic, cadmium, chromium and lead are up to two times lower 
than the minimum of the range indicated by Nriagu and Pacyna (1988). Nriagu and Pacyna made 
their inventory based on the annual global discharge of animal waste and manure and ranges of 
emission factors for heavy metals regardless of the livestock, which implies a higher uncertainty in 
the results. Furthermore, their assessment was performed in 1988 and heavy metal concentrations 
might have changed since then due to more stringent regulations. 
Considering the uncertainties in these two assessments, the developed inventory shows in general 
good consistency with them. It can therefore be concluded that the framework defined in this study 
performs well when it comes to estimating heavy metal emissions to soil resulting from manure 
application. At the same time, it has the advantage of allowing for a systematic approach in all 
countries.

4.4. Recommendations for further work 
Updating data on the heavy metal content of manure could significantly improve the quality of the 
inventory. As seen in the literature review, measuring heavy metal content of manure is a very 
sensitive process, and only a few values have been reported during the last decade. Having reliable 
and comparable data across countries calls for establishing an international standardized method 
that specifies sampling and extraction process. A European pilot project on manure application could 
for example focus on heavy metal content and investigate manure management practices. When 
investigating the heavy metal content of manure in non-European countries, priority should be given 
to China, the United States of America, India, Brazil and the Russian Federation as they are the top 5 
contributors worldwide.

5. Conclusion 
As part of this project, an inventory of industrial emissions to water and soil was built for 30 
European countries between 2007 and 2012, based on data reported to the E-PRTR and Eurostat. 
The analysis was however limited by the presence of outliers among the waterborne emissions, and 
by the lack of reports concerning soilborne releases. 
In addition, a method for developing inventories of emissions to land of heavy metals resulting from 
manure application on agricultural soil was developed and applied to 215 countries across the world 
between 2000 and 2014. Except for the quantities of manure applied, all parameters’ values are 
based on European data. Improving the quality of the inventory requires standardized measures of 
the heavy metal content of manure, especially in the top contributing countries (China, the United 
States of America, India, Brazil and the Russian Federation). This would help update the inventory 
and refine the country differentiation. 
Other sources of water and soilborne emissions that were initially left aside (atmospheric deposition 
and sewage sludge application on agricultural soil) should also be investigated to build the 
corresponding inventories.

Appendix A – Literature review on MRIO models 
 
1. Introduction 
Multi-Regional Input-Output (MRIO) models document monetary flows between several regions and 
their industry sectors (Leontief, 1970). When coupled with emission factors of substances for 
different types of industries, so called Environmentally Extended MRIO (EE MRIO) enable to calculate 
the footprint of a large scale region in terms of greenhouse gas emissions or water consumption for 
example. By documenting flows between countries and sectors, global EE MRIO table is a tool 
commonly acknowledged to support accounting of ‘consumer responsibility’, as opposed to the 
concept of ‘producer responsibility’, meaning that the demand for consumption is considered as the 
driver of the environmental impacts caused through the international supply chain network (Lenzen 
et al., 2012; Stadler et al., 2014).  
The present reports aims at reviewing the two databases Eora (Lenzen et al., 2012; Lenzen et al., 
2013) and EXIOBASE (Tukker et al., 2013; Wood et al., 2015), believed to be amongst the best EE 
MRIO models. After a short introduction to the concept of EE MRIO, both models are compared with 
regards to their main features with a focus on their environmental coverage. Such analysis then 
allows for the identification of potentials of gap-filling for the environmental coverage of EE MRIO.  
2. Description of EE MRIO models 
2.1.  Single-region Input-Output Tables 
An Input-Output Table (IOT) consists of inter-industry flows (matrix T) in monetary units as well as 
flows between industries and the final demand categories such as households, government 
spending, capital investment and exports (matrix y) as presented in Figure A1 below. IO tables 
therefore allow for tracing goods from the final demand down to manufacturing and the extraction 
process by providing information on the inputs required by an industry to produce its output. Such 
tables are provided by national statistical offices from all around the world with an interval of 
typically a few years. In single-region IOT, imports are considered as primary inputs and exports are 
included in the final demand.

Input-Output Tables can be derived from a variant called Supply and Use Tables (SUT) which make 
the distinction between industries and products. Such table allows one industry to produce more

demand is therefore represented by two separate matrices called the use matrix (T) and the supply 
matrix (V) as can be seen in Figure A2 below.

one. They can exist at the sub-national level or at the international level as in Eora and EXIOBASE.

2.3.  Environmental extensions 
SUT and IOT can be extended with so-called ‘satellite accounts’ to indicate pressures on the 
environment due to an activity. Examples of environmental extensions are resource consumption, air 
emissions, water uses, land uses, biodiversity impact, or even social pressure.  
Current Environmentally Extended MRIOT (EE MRIOT) allow for analysing greenhouse gas emissions 
acidification, and to a lesser extent ozone depletion, particulate matter, eutrophication, or 
acidification photochemical oxidant formation (Tukker et al., 2013). 
3. Overview of Eora’s and EXIOBASE’s features 
Funded by the Australian Research Council (ARC), the Eora project resulted in a database providing 
continuous coverage since 1990 for 187 single countries and 32 emissions (Lenzen et al., 2012; 
Lenzen et al., 2013). It is publicly available in two versions: the high-resolution one with a 
heterogeneous classification of 26-500 sectors per country, and the 26-sectors harmonized 
classification. A reliability analysis is also included in the model with estimates of standard deviation 
for all results. 
The EXIOBASE model is the product of a European Union-funded project aiming notably at improving 
awareness of external costs caused by environmental pressures (Tukker et al., 2013; Wood et al., 
2015). First released in 2012 under the name of EXIOPOL, a second version (CREEA) came out in 2014 
with a main focus on a more detailed disaggregation of countries in sectors and products and a 
broader emissions’ coverage. A third version (DESIRE) is expected for the end of 2015, which should 
additionally provide time series for the period 1995-2014. The present report focuses on the 
EXIOBASE 2. 
The performance of an EE MRIO can be evaluated through an overview of their main features: time 
coverage, country coverage, sector coverage, and environmental coverage (resources consumption 
and emissions). A summary of these characteristic parameters comparing Eora and EXIOBASE is given 
in Table A1. Each feature is further developed in the following sub-sections, with a focus on the 
environmental coverage and the methodology applied for its construction.

3.1.  Country coverage 
The ability of an EE MRIOT to account for all trades and hence to track potential emissions depends 
on the number of countries covered by the database. This is all the more important as production 
technologies and therefore related emission factors can differ from one country to another.  
The Eora database covers 187 individual countries while the World Data Bank lists 217 geographical 
entities. Those not included in the Eora database are small nations, disputed territories and small 
dependencies, and do not constitute explicitly a Rest of the World (RoW) region. A RoW region is 
however created to ensure that the table is overall 100% balanced (Lenzen et al., 2013). 
More focused on the European Union, the EXIOBASE covers 43 individual countries, the EU27 and 16 
non-EU countries, which cover together around 95% of the global GDP (Tukker et al., 2013). Other 
countries are gathered in 5 RoW regions: RoW Asia and Pacific, RoW Africa, RoW Middle East, RoW 
Europe, RoW America. 
3.2.  Industry coverage 
The industry coverage includes two dimensions: industries and products. As well as an industry can 
have several products (e.g. the mining industry produces more than one type of metal), a product 
can be common to several industries as represented in SUT. Having a detailed industry coverage 
enables MRIOT to give a more explicit picture of international trades and is of great matter when it 
comes to environmentally extended models as various products within a same industry may have 
highly different emission factors. For environmental analyses purpose, Tukker et al. (2013) 
recommends a minimum resolution of 100-150 sectors, with an increased differentiation in sub-
sectors with highly different impact intensities such as agriculture, mining, transport or energy 
production. 
The Eora database is a heterogeneous table, meaning that all countries do not have IO tables with 
the same quantities of industries and products covered. As a matter of fact, this model includes both 
IO and SU tables depending on the countries. This is due to a strong will to maintain and use data as 
provided by their information supplier as it is thought to minimize approximations and uncertainties. 
As a result, among the 187 countries considered in the Eora database, a considerable amount of 118 
countries are covered by simply 26 products with no differentiation in industries, and only 74 
countries actually have national IO tables. A simplified and homogeneous version of Eora is also 
available where the sector coverage is reduced to a common 26-sector classification, which is based 
on the common International Standard Industrial Classification (ISIC) and composed of agriculture, 
fishing, mining and quarrying, food and beverages, textiles and wearing apparel, wood and paper, 
petroleum, chemical and non-metallic mineral products, metal products, electrical and machinery, 
transport equipment, other manufacturing, recycling, electricity gas and water, construction, 
maintenance and repair, wholesale trade, retail trade, hotels and restaurants, transport, post and 
telecommunications, financial intermediation and business activities, public administration, 
education, health and other services, private households, others, re-export and re-import. 
The EXIOBASE model is constructed as a harmonized table with 163 industries and 200 products in its 
current version. Table A2 summarizes the sector coverage of the EXIOBASE by domain of activity. 
Such comprehensive industry coverage was made possible through a process of disaggregation of 
sectors based on detailed IO tables of a country assumed to have a similar industry, detailed

3.3.  Environmental coverage 
The environmental coverage is represented by indicators representing how much pressure is put on 
the environment by a certain activity. Both models focus on emissions to the environment, and 
water uses. While Eora provides separate environmental extensions for energy, ecological footprint 
and Human appropriation of Net Primary Production (HANPP), the EXIOBASE takes into account land 
uses and resource consumption on a regular basis.  
This section aims at providing detail information regarding the emissions coverage of Eora and the 
EXIOBASE as well as the data sources and methods that were used to construct their environmental 
extensions. 
3.3.1. Emissions coverage 
The Eora model covers 32 substances emitted to air as can be seen in Table A3. An interesting 
feature is that Eora has an important level of details concerning hydrofluorocarbons (11 individual 
HFCs) and perfluorocarbons (8 individual PFCs).  
The EXIOBASE model takes into account 33 individual substances, including emissions to air and 
emissions of nitrogen N and phosphorus P to water (Table A3). On the contrary to Eora, the 
EXIOBASE only reports HFCs, PFCs and HCFCs as groups of substances in t-CO2eq and does not 
individuate them. In overall, the EXIOBASE covers nearly all substances existing in Eora, as well as 
particulate matter, heavy metals, and some toxic organic compounds such as PAHs. In addition, 
emissions to water of nitrogen and phosphorus in agriculture are distinguished between 13 different 
kinds of culture.

3.3.2. Methodology applied for building environmental extensions 
One main constraint for the construction of environmental extensions is that they should match the 
level of detail of the industry coverage. The Eora and EXIOBASE models being very different on that 
point, they also apply different methods to calculate country-specific emissions, as summarized 
below. 
3.3.2.1. 
Eora data sources 
The Eora model explicitly includes satellites accounts for Energy, Air emissions, Ecological footprint, 
Water footprint, and Human Appropriation of Net Primary Production (HANPP) which measures to 
what extent land conversion and biomass harvest alter the availability of biomass (NPP) in 
ecosystems. The Eora model follows usually a top-down approach when including environmental 
extensions. 
Energy 
For energy, national energy data are preferred as initial estimate because they are built for input-
output tables. If national data are not available, the International Energy Agency (IEA) extended 
energy balances or U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) data are used as an initial estimate. 
All available energy data are then considered as constraints and an optimization algorithm was run 
(Lenzen et al., 2012).  
Air emissions 
Eora accounts for 32 emissions to air which include 26 greenhouse gas emissions (Table A3). These 
are based on numerous data sources and updated annually (Lenzen et al., 2012). 
 
For GHG emissions, initial estimates were based on the Emission Database for Global Atmospheric 
Research model (EDGAR, 2013) as it provides absolute emission volumes according to the IPCC sector 
classification defined in the UNFCCC reporting guidelines for 223 countries up to 2010. Emissions 
from international aviation and shipping are however accounted for separately, showing that this

Ecological footprint 
The Ecological Footprint is defined as “a measure of how much area of biologically productive land 
and water an individual, population or activity requires to produce all the resources it consumes and 
to absorb the waste it generates, using prevailing technology and resource management practices” 
(Global Footprint Network, 2015). It is included in the Eora model based on data from the National

Water accounts 
The WaterStat database provides statistics on the water footprint of products from 1996 to 2005. 
When allocated to the industries covered by Eora, such data enables to calculate the water footprint 
of countries (Lenzen et al., 2012).  
Material accounts 
Even though they are not directly included in the Eora model, a data set focusing on the Material 
Footprint of countries is available. The main data source is the CSIRO Global Material Flow Database, 
which documents domestic extraction and physical trade of 250 primary resource categories across 
191 countries in yearly time steps for 1970–2008, following international guidelines on material flow 
accounting. To be included as an extension in Eora, the 250 primary resource categories were first 
aggregated to 35 material categories, which were matched with the industry sectors in the MRIO 
using the OECD Harmonized Commodity Description and Coding System (Wiedmann et al., 2015). 
3.3.2.2. 
EXIOBASE data sources 
The EXIOBASE explicitly includes environmental extensions for air emissions, raw material 
consumption, water uses and land uses. It usually follows a bottom-up approach when including 
environmental extensions. 
Air emissions 
The EXIOBASE includes 31 emissions to air (Table A3). The United Nations Framework Convention on 
Climate Change (UNFCCC) gathers several data on greenhouse gas emissions as reported in a 
common format by ratifying countries. For the EXIOBASE model, it has been estimated that air 
emissions reported under the UNFCCC were not appropriate to support environmental accounting 
for the following reasons (CREEA, 2013b): 
- 
According to the UNFCCC guidelines, emissions are reported following the ‘territory 
principle’, meaning that emissions belong to the country where the activity occurs, 
regardless of where the emitter originates from. However, the EXIOBASE intends to follow 
the methodology defined by the System of Environmental-Economic Accounting (SEEA) in 
which air emission accounting refers to the functional border of the economy; such approach 
is therefore called ‘residence principle’: emissions belong to the country of residence of the 
consumer. In certain cases, such as transportation or tourism, accounting differs 
considerably between the two principles (United Nations, 2014). 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
- 
The sectoral (industry) and product disaggregation is insufficient compared to the level of 
detail in the EXIOBASE. 
- 
Countries that are reporting their emissions on an annual basis are mainly EU countries and 
other industrialised countries. For developing countries, including major upcoming 
economies such as China, India or Brazil, such data set is not available.  
Due to the aforementioned limitations of the official emissions reporting, an emission model was 
developed to calculate air emissions for the EXIOBASE project through a bottom-up approach. This 
methodology is similar to the TNO Emission Assessment Model (TEAM) which combines activity rate, 
emission factor, and technology penetration grade (Pulles et al., 2005). More specifically, the 
EXIOBASE project distinguishes three types of air emissions: 
- 
Air emissions related to the combustion of energy products 
 
In this case, the activity rate is equivalent to the amount of energy used and is based on 
energy statistics from the IEA, while emission factors are selected from the emission 
estimation guidance documents available in the EMEP/EEA Emission Inventory Guidebook 
(TFEIP, 2009). 
- 
Air emissions related to the non-combustion of energy products, which result from the 
extraction, processing, transformation and distribution of fossil fuels. 
- 
Air emissions related to the non–energy products, whose sources are mainly industries or 
solvent and other product use.   
Activity rates are based on a combination of several individual statistics, and GDP was used 
as a proxy for countries where the activity rates were not available. 
 
For the category ‘product use’, the GAINS model approach for NMVOC emissions was used as 
it considers a broad range of activities, and averaged emission factors were used for 
countries not included in the model. 
 
Concerning SF6, HFCs and PFCs, determining their emission factors requires data that cannot 
be collected on a global level. Emission data reported in UNFCCC were therefore directly 
used for these pollutants, and data from the EDGAR emission model (EDGAR, 2013) have 
been used to complete the data set for countries which are not reporting under the UNFCCC. 
Total emissions calculated through the EXIOBASE model demonstrated good agreement with 
national and global accounts (CREEA, 2013b).  
Material accounts 
The EXIOBASE takes into account 48 raw materials including metal ores, non-metallic minerals, fossil 
fuels, animals, and primary crops’ products, and then distinguishes used and unused domestic 
extraction for each material. Data for raw material consumption are based on the Sustainable Europe 
Research Institute (SERI) Global Material Flow Database. The latter provides a global coverage of 
resource extraction data for all material categories in annual time series (Wood et al., 2015). 
Land accounts 
The EXIOBASE includes 15 different types of land use. For the accounting, the Food and Agriculture 
Organization FAO provides data on agricultural land (cropland, permanent meadows and pastures) 
and forests. Annual reporting under the UNFCCC includes land use data concerning human 
settlements, and the data set is completed using single-country data sources or international data 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
such as the European Corine Land Cover project for countries which are not reporting to the UNFCCC 
(Wood et al., 2015). 
Water accounts 
The EXIOBASE model takes into account green water (water from precipitation), blue water (fresh 
surface and groundwater) and grey water (a measure of freshwater pollution) with a total of 47 
different types of water consumption or withdrawal. For the livestock sector, the manufacturing 
sector, the thermal electricity production sector and the domestic sector, data were retrieved from 
the WaterGAP model. Water Footprint data were used to calculate blue and green water 
consumption for the agricultural sector (CREEA, 2013a).  
For estimations of emissions of Nitrogen and Phosphorus to water, data on the inputs of mineral 
fertilizers and animal manure, nutrient fixation and deposition, crop harvests and residues, and gas 
emissions are required to perform mass balances as described in CREEA (2013a). 
Finally, to estimate the amount of heat rejected to freshwater, thermal efficiencies are first 
calculated via the Rankine cycle and then combined with the generation of electricity of each unit 
(CREEA, 2013a). 
3.4. Other features 
Solving of data conflicts 
Eora is constructed using a constrained optimization approach. When compiling data on international 
trade or environmental emissions, conflict of data may arise. To solve them, all data points are 
considered as constraints bounding the solution with a defined level of confidence and an 
optimization algorithm is applied. It results in a solution which satisfies the best all constraints and is 
balanced (Lenzen et al., 2012). As a matter of fact, when building the MRIOT, a higher level of 
confidence is assigned to data from national statistics bureau than to aggregate source like the 
OECD. For every year, quality reports are provided which summarize standard deviations in absolute 
and relative terms for all constraints. Such feature does not seem to be present in the EXIOBASE 
model. 
Time series 
The Eora model is available for every year between 1990 and 2012. Its monetary time series are built 
through an iterative process where the year 2000 is the initial estimate as it provided the best overall 
availability of national input-output tables. In practical, all constraints for 2000 are solved and this 
year is used as initial estimate for 2001 and so on until today. Back-casting to 1990 is done similarly 
(Lenzen et al., 2012). In comparison, the EXIOBASE database has only been created for 2000 
(EXIOPOL) and 2007 (CREEA) so far. The DESIRE project (3rd version of the EXIOBASE) should however 
include times series from 1995 to 2012 and even have the potential of now-casting the MRIO to the 
current year (Stadler and Wood, 2014). Environmental extensions do not have time series and are 
only based on existing databases. 
Resource efficiency indicators (DESIRE) 
As previously mentioned, the third version of the EXIOBASE should include times series from 1995 as 
well as now-casting. In addition, it aims at incorporating more indicators on biodiversity, such as 
HANPP, or species-area relationships (SAR), as well as indicators of resource efficiency (Stadler and 
Wood, 2014). DESIRE actually stands for Development of a System of Indicators for a Resource 
efficient Europe, and the projects investigates resource efficiency indicators that measure how much 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
value has been produced using one unit of resources, and make the link between resource use and 
socio-economic drivers.

4. Potential for gap-filling 
In terms of environmental impact categories, Eora does not include land uses while the EXIOBASE 
model demonstrated the availability of data through the FAO and the UNFCCC reporting. Accounting 
of raw material consumption is not available on a regular basis either and could be included as well. 
Concerning more particularly air emissions, toxic substances such as heavy metals or PAHs are not 
represented in the data set. 
In comparison to Eora, the EXIOBASE could potentially improve its level of detail concerning the 
coverage of the greenhouse gas emissions by individuating HFCs and PFCs in different substances 
using data from the EDGAR database. 
For both models, improving the coverage of the environmental impact category Toxicity (human 
toxicity and freshwater ecotoxicity) could include accounting of heavy metals to water and soil as it 
was demonstrated that such emissions are the main contributors to human and freshwater toxicity 
(Sala et al., 2015; Laurent et al., 2011). Including Persistent Organic Pollutants (POP) could also be 
relevant; some data being available through the EMEP database with a detailed sectoral reporting.  
Investigating the environmental impact category Ionizing Radiation could also be a possibility, with 
some radionuclides emissions being available in the UNSCEAR database or retrievable from the 
Ecoinvent database (Sala et al., 2015). 
It should however be mentioned that to examine the incorporation of additional substance 
emissions, it is first recommended to review existing databases and to take into account the 
following criteria: 
- 
Which database provides emissions data? 
- 
How were data measured or estimated? 
- 
For which countries are data available? 
- 
For which years are data available? 
- 
For which level of sector disaggregation are data available? 
- 
Is extrapolation possible in case of uncomplete data set? On which basis?

5. Conclusion 
The present review of the Eora and EXIOBASE environmentally extended MRIO models highlighted 
the differences between them. In comparison to the EXIOBASE, Eora focuses more on the countries 
coverage and prefers maintaining the integrity of data to disaggregating sectors. Eora has the 
advantages to include solving of data conflict as well as time series, but the latest should be also 
developed in the soon-available third version of the EXIOBASE. Concerning their accounting of air 
emissions, the EXIOBASE covers more impact categories than Eora but lacks details concerning 
greenhouse gas such as HFCs and PFCs. Both models are missing coverage of the environmental 
impact categories Toxicity (both human toxicity and eco-toxicity) and Ionizing radiation, for which 
emissions of organics, heavy metals to air, water and soil and radionuclides are relevant.

B.3. Production volumes as proxies 
Fish production 
For the NACE economic class ‘marine aquaculture’ (03.21), emissions of copper and zinc have been 
reported by only 3 countries: Norway, Malta, and the United Kingdom. The total mass of these heavy 
metals released in the environment is extremely high compared to the number of reports (Appendix 
E, sheet I1). This may be due to the use of copper and zinc in animals’ feed (Monteiro et al., 2010). 
Copper may also be released from its use in antifouling paints to protect the cages (Sneddon and 
Tremblay, 2011; Ayer et al., 2016). Facilities with a production capacity of more than 1,000 tonnes of 
fish or shellfish per year are expected to report emissions exceeding the release thresholds (EC, 
2006). 
Quantities in tonnes of fish produced through marine aquaculture by country were retrieved from 
FAO7 (2016). This data set presents a complete coverage of countries and years from 2000 to 2014. 
The European Topic Centre on Air and Climate Mitigation points out that European countries with a 
high aquaculture production but no reported emissions (e.g. France, Greece) should proceed to 
completeness checks concerning their activities (ETC/ACM, 2011). 
Mining production volumes 
Mining of metal ores is known to cause important release of heavy metals to the environment due to 
for example tailings and mine drainage (Dudka and Adriano, 1997; Liao et al., 2008; SDWF, 2004). In 
the section ‘mining and quarrying’, the NACE division 07 ‘mining of metal ores’ is the one subject to 
the most reporting, with 91% of the reports concerning the ‘mining of other non-ferrous metal ore’ 
(NACE 07.29). All underground mining facilities are expected to report emissions exceeding the 
release thresholds. For opencast mining and quarrying, only facilities where the surface of the area 
effectively under extraction is larger than 25 hectares shall report their emissions (EC, 2006). 
Mining production volumes in metric tonnes of metal content were retrieved from the World Mining 
Congress (Reichl et al., 2013, 2015). Every by-products are also collected in content figures for the 
same country (Reichl, 2016). For each heavy metal, the emissions were compared to its respective 
production figures when available. 
It was observed that some countries producing some heavy metals do not report any corresponding 
emissions to the E-PRTR, indicating potential missing reports. Reciprocally, some countries are 
reporting emissions but appear not to have mining activities of the heavy metal of concern. Looking 
for example at chromium, Finland has important mining production volumes but does not report any 
emissions whereas Poland reports Cr emissions despite having no mining.

Pulp, paper and paperboard production 
The pulp and paper industry has a large consumption of water and discharges heavy metal 
contaminated effluents, resulting in important total emissions of metals (Mishra et al., 2013). The 
NACE classification distinguishes the pulp production (17.11) and the paper and paperboard 
production (17.12), which are the two activities with the most reporting in this industry. While all 
facilities producing pulp are expected to report their emissions when exceeding the release 
thresholds, facilities producing paper, paperboard and other primary wood products have as a 
threshold a production capacity of 30 tonnes per day (EC, 2006). 
Production volumes in thousand tonnes of pulp and paper and paperboard were both retrieved from 
Eurostat (2015) and cover all European countries from 2007 to 2013.
