Abstract
The t-cell receptor is a heterodimeric surface receptor composed of an α and a β-subunit.
They are constructed by genomic rearrangements of the germline t-cell receptor locus;
V(D)J recombination.
V(D)J recombination ensures immense diversity in the t-cell
receptor, enabling widespread pathogenic recognition. The pathogenic recognition is
mediated by the complementary determining regions CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3 of the t-
cell receptor as they bind the presented antigen on the major histocompatibility complex.
Despite the immense diversity, recent research indicates that t-cell receptors binding the
same antigen often share conserved sequence features on the complementary determining
regions. This implies that it may be possible to predict antigen specificity, and thereby
predicting which antigens a specific t-cell will bind. Predicting specificity of t-cell recep-
tors is a prerequisite to tailor therapeutic regimens and develop new treatment options
for an individual based on elements most likely to evoke an immunological response.
Even though recent studies have suggested techniques to predict the antigen speci-
ficity, there is still no clear scientific consensus on how measurements should be con-
ducted has not yet been established.
The aim of this thesis is to systematically investigate the predictive power of protein
comparison techniques for complementary determining regions of the β-subunit. The
protein comparison techniques include secondary structure comparison, which has not
yet been described in the literature. The protein comparison techniques were analyzed
using feature selection with the ensemble approaches Random Forest and AdaBoost to
combine and exclude techniques to produce a measure for future prediction based on
the primary sequence and gene usage.
The resulting metric, BetaDist, uses structural comparisons for CDR1 and CDR2
in combination with the Pam10 alignment score with a gap-penalty of 30 for CDR3
sequences. BetaDist outperforms the β-subunit distance metric to which it was compared
when evaluated on classification power. This thesis emphasizes the need to use structural
predictions in determining binding motifs in t-cell receptors to characterize binding
predictions adequately.

Resumé
T-celle receptoren er en heterodimerisk overfladereceptor bestående af en α- og β-kæde.
Kæderne konstrueres under genomisk om-arrangement i t-celle receptor locus i en process,
der kaldes V(D)J om-arrangement. V(D)J om-arrangement sikrer en enorm diversitet i
t-celle receptoren, hvilket understøtter bred patogen genkendelse. Denne patogene genk-
endelse styres af de komplementær determinerende regioner: CDR1, CDR2 og CDR3
på t-celle receptoren, da disse binder det præsenterede antigen på major histocompati-
bility complex. På trods af den store diversitet har ny forskning indikeret, at der kan
forekomme bevarede sekvensmotiver i de bindende regioner. Dette antyder, at det vil
være muligt at forudse antigenbinding på baggrund af aminosyrersekvenser for CDR1,
CDR2 og CDR3. I forhold til præcisionsmedicin er en forudsigelse af denne binding et
interessant forskningsområde, da disse kan blive nødvendige for at skræddersy terapi og
udvikle nye lægemidler baseret på de elementer, der højst sandsynligt vil fremkalde en
immunologisk respons hos et individ.
Indenfor de sidste tre år har forskning desuden produceret metoder til at forudse
t-celle bindingsspecificitet, men eftersom dette er et relativt nyt forskningsfelt, er der
endnu ikke videnskabelig konsensus omkring, hvordan disse målinger skal foretages.
Denne afhandling forsøger systematisk at kvantificere den forudsigelseskraft en række
proteinsammenligningsværktøjer besidder ved at undersøge disses effekt på β-kæden.
Disse sammenligningsværktøjer inkluderer strukturelle sammenligninger, hvilket tidligere
ikke har været anvendt i specificitetsmålinger for t-celle receptorer. Protein- sammen-
ligningsværktøjerne blev analyseret ved at anvende parameterudvælgelse ved hjælp af
ensemblemetoderne Random Forest og AdaBoost. Efter fravalg af de teknikker, der ikke
viste anvendelse indenfor bindingsforudsigelse, blev der produceret et mål for forudsigelse
af bindinger baseret på primærsekvenser for CDR3 og brug af gener.
Den resulterende metode udviklet i denne afhandling, navngivet BetaDist, anvender
strukturelle sammenligninger for CDR1 og CDR2 i kombination med Pam10 alignment
for CDR3 sekvenserne. BetaDist klarer sig bedre end andre β-kæde målinger, når der
evalueres på klassifikationskraft. Denne afhandling understreger behovet for at anvende
strukturelle forudsigelser til identifikation af bindingsmotiver.

CHAPTER 1
Predicting T-cell binding
Medicine has greatly advanced within the past century, targeting diseases in a range of
specific ways. However, creating universal and effective treatment of cancer and autoim-
mune conditions has proven a significant challenge to solve. Simultaneously, infectious
diseases caused by bacteria are at risk of becoming even more challenging to treat due
to the diminishing effect of antibiotics. Additionally, many viral and parasitic infec-
tions are still untreatable today. Hence, the need for novel treatments is as essential as
ever. Today, a one-size-fits-all approach is prevalent, without much consideration of the
differences in the genetic material of patients. Herein lie an unexplored therapeutic po-
tential both regarding choosing the most appropriate treatment regimen and regarding
developing new types of personalized medicines. To bridge the gap between potential
and solution, an understanding of the immunological responses and genetic differences
is required.
1.1
Fundamentals of the Immune System
The human immune system is a multi-component system that protects the host from
pathogenic invasion and expressed cellular mutations. It is composed of two cellular
systems of immunity: the innate and the adaptive immune system. The innate system
is the first line of defense, detecting intruders and abnormal cells, then initiating an
inflammatory response.
The adaptive immune system is the second line of defense,
requiring activation by the innate system, after which it can generate memory about and
fight, pathogens and abnormalities in specialized ways. The adaptive immune system
comprises the lymphocytes termed B and T named by the lymphoid organs they originate
from, respectively bone-marrow and the thymus. The T-lymphocytes, regularly called
T-cells, are distinguished by the presence of the heterodimeric t-cell receptor (TCR),
which regulates induced cell death [139], [34].

There has been a development of research into the mechanisms and behavior of the adap-
tive immune system in response to different therapy regimes, both experimentally and in

1.3
Calculating the presence of Conserved
Sequence Features
There exist several relevant distance measures in the field of T-cell receptor comparison
[156]. Dash et al. [52] applied pairwise sequence comparisons with an alignment scor-
ing metric, while Glanville et al. [76] calculated the frequencies of k-mer stretches of
amino acid (AA) sequences. Both approaches yield an arbitrary score that can only be
compared to the method itself, which makes it difficult to compare. Since 2017, other
ways of quantifying the presence of conserved sequence features have been published.
Most prominently, Sidhoms et al. in 2018 [181] and Thakkar et al. in 2019 [190]. These
are distinguished by being distance measures where a short distance between two t-cell
receptors indicate a higher probability for binding of the same antigen.
1.4
Purpose
Metrics defining the distance between T-cell receptors is a new research area, and there
is still no clear scientific consensus on how measurements should be conducted. Until
now, the approaches to measure the distance between T-cell receptors have relied on
comparisons of primary sequence information alone. Including structural similarity of
the t-cell receptors in these measurements has not yet been investigated. This thesis aims
to propose a way to measure the distance between two t-cell receptors based on their
main-chain structure and residue sequence. The hypothesis investigated in this thesis
is: Including structural elements in a distance metric will be better at differentiating
between antigen binders.

Assumptions and Limitations
The research presented in this thesis has two limitations. Only the β-subunit of the
heterodimeric T-cell receptor is analyzed due to data availability. This limitation is set
in place due to the lack of paired sequences. The choice to exclude the α-chain is justified
based on the fact that the β-subunit is primarily in contact with the epitope, and the

tool is developed on human data, why it cannot be applied to any other mammal.
1.6
Structure of thesis
Following this introduction are three consecutive theoretical chapters aiming to disclose
information regarding the theoretical foundation needed in order to define a distance
between T-cell receptors.
Firstly T-cells and their receptors are described in detail.

2.1
Overview of the T-cells
T-cells are distinct by the presence of the T-cell receptors (TCRs), yet they are classified
based on their co-receptors, CD4, and CD8 [73]. Both groups bind the major histocom-
patibility complex (MHC) on an antigen-presenting cell (APC). If activation should elicit
an immunological response, the TCR recognizes the protein fragment presented by the
MHC as non-self, as the MHC does not discriminate between self and non-self. In the
presence of co-stimulation interaction the TCR triggers the cascade of phosphorylations
that results in the activation of transcription factors: Nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-
enhancer (NFKB), Nuclear factor of activated T-cells (NFAT), and Activator protein 1
(AP-1) [139]. The transcription factors induce specific gene transcription resulting in cell
proliferation and differentiation into effector cells [139], [34]. The TCR is a membrane-
bound receptor that comprises two chains: an α and β chain or a γ and δ chain. The
γ/δ subtype represents a small subset of the T-cells in peripheral blood, with distinctive
characteristics and different mechanisms of interaction and activation, why only α/β
T-cells are analyzed in this thesis. The two subunits collectively interact with a peptide
presented on the MHC molecule, as shown in figure 2.1. The interaction is conducted by
the complementary-determining regions (CDRs) of the TCR facing the MHC molecule.
Each subunit has three CDRs named accordingly; CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3 [73], [139].
The CDRs are part of the paratope, but they have different roles. While CDR1 and
CDR2 mainly bind the MHC itself, CDR3 binds the antigen, accounting for most of the
pathogenic recognition [139], [52], [190]. The terms antigen presented peptide fragment,
and epitope are, in this case, interchangeable.

As one of each V, D, and J segment is used to create the β chain, there are 2700
combinatorial options, accounting for the combinatorial diversity. The random joints
account for junctional diversity, which is harder to calculate but has been estimated to be

the α chain undergoes recombination as well. The α chain locus is located at the 14th
chromosome and contains approximately 70 V and 62 J segments. An estimate of the
total diversity of TCRs is approximately 1018 [139], [26], [166], [202].

2.2.1
Diversity
The mathematical estimation of the combinatorial and junctional diversity and the re-
sults on diversity measurements from the laboratory have hitherto not been compatible
[168], as the diversity measured in the laboratory has proven much smaller than the math-
ematically derived diversity. The discrepancy can partly be ascribed to the fact that it
has been troublesome to sequence significant amounts of t-cells until reason development
of next-generation sequencing techniques. Partly because different research groups have
extracted and prepared samples differently, making the comparison of results challeng-
ing. It might also suggest that the diversity of the TCRs are not as seemingly large and
random as first believed [168]. Holt argued in 2017 [90] that the TCR repertoire could
be read but not fully understood, and highlighted, that one of the reasons for this is the
TCR promiscuity. One TCR can bind a variety of p:MHC and one p:MHC can bind a
variety of TCRs.
Expression of diversity is often a number calculated from two parameters: abundance
and similarity. An assortment of ways to define these parameters has previously been
described in the literature. A generic notion is that abundance describes the proportions
in which a unit is present, and similarity describes how similar two units are [192].
To assess abundance and similarity in t-cell receptors, the following questions need
to be answered: How do we assess abundance? The abundance of different t-cells is
dynamic, as some specific clonal types are present when t-cells proliferate in response
to non-self.
Moreover, how similar do two TCRs need to be, for them to be of the
same species? Similarity, s, or dissimilarity, d, is a numerical measure of how alike or
different two data objects, TCR1 and TCR2, are; s(TCR1, TCR2) = −d(TCR1, TCR2).
Methodologies to assess similarity or dissimilarity rely on a distance measurement, which
in turn needs to be defined.
Thus, the similarity function relies on the method of
measuring distance(TCR1, TCR2).

3.1
Protein conformation and modeling
The function of a protein is highly determined by its conformation. Defectively folded
and unfolded proteins lead to malfunctioning and deregulated proteins, which can po-
tentially promote diseases such as cancer [204]. Protein conformation is determined by
thermodynamic stability, hydrophobic interactions, and disulfide bonds. Thus, proteins
are dynamic molecules that often change conformations in response to binding [58].
3.1.1
Protein structure
Proteins are a product of translation performed in the ribosomes based on mRNA relay-
ing information in the form of nucleotides. Three nucleotides, a codon, are translated
into one amino acid (AA). When describing protein structure, there are four structural
levels: the primary structure, secondary structure, tertiary, and quaternary structure,
as seen in figure 3.1
The word primary protein structure is interchangeable with primary sequence, as both
are the amino acid residue sequence, e.g. MSSVLLGHI. This sequence does not relay
any direct structural information, such as dihedral angles. Secondary protein structures
are the structures resulting from the combination of residues and the dihedral angles
between said residues. Secondary structures are helices, sheets, and loops. Tertiary
protein structure is the arrangement of secondary structure elements within one protein
chain. E.g. how the β chain have a β-sheet sandwich. The quaternary protein structure
is the assembly of subunits into a protein complex.
TCR has a quaternary protein
structure consisting of the α and β chain [208].

Three-dimensional protein structure can currently be determined by X-ray crystallogra-
phy, NMR spectroscopy. Protein NMR is difficult to interpret and, therefore, suitable
only for small proteins up to 15 kDa. On the other hand, X-ray crystallography is suited

son using BLAST to achieve high accuracy, the CDRs cannot be structurally predicted
with the same success. Despite their difference in sequence, the CDR loops are each
associated with a small number of distinct tertiary main-chain conformations; canonical
structures [111]. Canonical structure is a term describing the specific, limited, number
of possible main chain conformational structures a CDR loop can assume. Canonical

3.2.1
Pre-defined Substitution Matrices
A comparison of primary structures of proteins is frequent and forms the basis for align-
ment by the heuristic BLAST algorithm. Comparison and alignment generally rely on a
substitution matrix that quantifies the change when two amino acids exchange position.
Such quantification can be based on the log odds ratio for each substitution possible
[6], as seen in the most common alignment scoring matrix families; BLOSUM (BLOcks
SUbstitution Matrix) and PAM (percent accepted mutations).
The substitution system includes a gap penalty when the sequences are not equal in
length. The objective is often to maximize the score [68]. The BLOSUM substitution
scoring is based on a calculation of frequencies on sequences falling within a threshold
of percentage identity.
The blosum62 is, perhaps, the most commonly used scoring
matrix, where sequences used to create the matrix had approximately 62 % identity.
The other family, the PAMs, is based on substitution rates at closely related proteins
and extrapolated for all other. A PAM10 matrix, for instance, accepts 10 mutations pr
100 amino acids [15]. Both the Blossom and PAM families are subject to inaccuracies.
The matrices measure inaccurately on non-conventional amino acid compositions [96],
where they inaccurately align distantly related sequences [157].
Neither account for
dynamics [203] and most importantly: they do not incorporate structural information
[201], [78]. In an attempt to obtain more precise information from sequence alignment,
researchers have incorporated evolutionary rate data [142] or included known motifs [57].
The Jones-Taylor-Thornton alignment (JTT) matrix that is phylogenetic-specific, but
not TCR specific, is an example hereof [97].
When aligning, more than the substitution matrix is important; both the choice of
local or global alignment and the underlying alignment algorithm are deciding factors.
Using global alignment allows the ends of the sequences to be aligned, thus forcing the
gaps to exist in the middle of the sequence. In contrast, a local alignment identifies
smaller subsections with the best fit. Alignment algorithms include Needleman-Wunsch
[143], Gotoh [8], and Smith-Waterman [184]. Needleman-Wunsch and Smith-Waterman
rely on dynamic programming, while Gotoh is a superstructure based on Needleman-
Wunsch. The Needleman-Wunsch algorithm, published in 1970, globally align sequences
by dividing the task of full alignment into smaller segments in a grid. Sequences are
aligned by continually optimizing the score based on a substitution matrix and a gap
opening penalty [143]. The Smith-Waterman algorithm was published in 1981 and base
the alignment on local sequence scores by setting all negative substitution scores to zero,
which effectively allow more gaps and, thus, local alignments [184]. The Gotoh algorithm
was developed primarily to reduce the computational steps in the Needleman-Wunsch
algorithm and to introduce the affine gap penalty, resulting in larger gaps due to the
relatively lower cost of keeping a gap open [8]. According to Barlowe et al. 2017 [15],
the biggest pitfall of using one substitution matrix instead of another is, that the utility
is data-dependent, and how does one know which one is the best suited for one’s data?

3.2.2
Alternatives to pre-defined Substitution Matrices
There are ways to quantify and measure amino acid sequences without a pre-defined
matrix. One method is to create an ad hoc matrix, such as the position-specific sub-
stitution matrix, PSSM, where, as the name indicates, the substitution scores between
amino acids depend on their position within the protein. Construction of the PSSM is,
therefore, dependent on the data input. Positive scores indicate that the amino acid sub-
stitution is more frequent in the alignment than it would have been by random chance
[7]. A Markov Chain, a statistic modeling tool, can be used to generate such a matrix
[129].
Another method is Atchley factors. The Atchley factors published in 2005, to solve
the protein sequence metric problem [11] by numerically denoting the amino acids. Analy-
sis of an abundance of attributes and clustering of the results lead to five physio-chemical
variables. Roman numerals indicate the factors and all five are attached to each amino
acid. The factors include a polarity index, a secondary structure factor, molecular size,
relative amino acid composition, meaning how many codons are used, and finally, a
number for the electrostatic charge [11]. The Atchley factors are pre-calculated for each
amino acid converting a letter into a vector with the five factors.
Lastly is the k-mer approach, which is the most widely used alignment-free method,
as the applications are vast. The k-mers function by dividing the sequences into k-mers
often 3, 4, or 5, in TCR context, and compute the k-mer frequency within the sequences
analyzed. The rationale is that the repetition of k-mers indicates similar sequences [221],
[124].
Other physio-chemical properties measurements might be useful, such as measure-
ment of hydrophobicity or charge, where it can be derived that proteins with similar
hydrophobic attributes or charge will bind the same epitope. Hence, proteins can also
be compared using the Kyte & Doolittle index of hydrophobicity. Traditionally this is
done using a k-mer. Nevertheless, when discussing only a single protein domain, such as
a CDR3 sequence, it might be plausible to consider the whole region rather than k-mers
[109]. Another physio-chemical property that affects binding and allostery is the flexi-
bility of the region [200]. The flexibility can be measured with the amino acid b-values
(atomic temperature factors), as there exists a correlation between average flexibility
and a protein’s thermal stability. Hence, higher thermal stability correlates to higher
flexibility. All CD regions will inadvertently be flexible, as they are loops [200]. Finally
is the physical volume of the amino acids. There exist multiple ways of measuring the
size of the CDR loops, where the simplest is considering the length. However, the bulk-
iness of the residues is also relevant to describe the physical space where the epitope
needs to bind. Ways of measuring volume include molecular mass and Å3 [217], [155].

Structural Comparison
There exist two main methods for structural comparison. Root-mean-square-deviation
(RMSD) and Template modeling score (TM-score). RMSD is a pairwise approach to
compare molecules by moving and rotating one of the structures to superimpose the other
and gaining knowledge of their difference by measuring the difference in the position of
the aligned atoms [48]. The most common unit is Ångström (Å). If the structures are
identical, the RMSD is zero Ångström. TM score uses a similar approach as RMSD
but is adjusted to measure global fold similarity. It is less sensitive to local structural
variations and is length independent. TM scores have a value between 0 and 1, where 1
indicate a perfect match [219], [212].

A distance can be measured using a variety of methods, but its core is in principal a
function of two observations a,b, and their internal relationship. In this last theoretical
chapter, an explanation of how to develop and evaluate a distance metric as a classifica-
tion tool is presented. Including a characterization of state of the art methods for TCR
distance measurement is presented.
4.1
Mathematical basis of a Distance Metric
Five fundamental criteria need to be satisfied to have a distance metric as defined by R.
Engelking in Outline of general Topology from 1968 [19]:
• Symmetry: The distance should be the same, regardless of it being measured from
a to b or b to a, d(a, b) = d(b, a)
• Non-negativity: A distance cannot be negative, two points cannot be closer than
identical, d(a, b) ≥0
• Identification Mark: When measuring the distance to itself, it need always be 0,
d(a, a) = 0
• Definiteness: A distance between a and b can only be zero if a = b.
• Triangle Inequality: The distance from a to b directly should be shorter than the
distance from a to c plus c to b, d(a, b) ≤d(a, c) + d(c, b)
The most uncomplicated way to measure distance is to quantify the magnitude of differ-
ence, which requires a and b to be numerical values that can undergo subtraction and
get a norm d(a, b) = ||a−b||. Calculating the norm is not possible for protein structures
without three-dimensional coordinates. Distance measurements, quantification of amino
acid components, and comparison of protein-structures are as a result closely related.
All methods to compare proteins can be defined as a distance. The substitution
matrices use a variation of the Hamming distance. A Hamming distance is defined as

4.2
Metric Generation
The goal of metric creation is:
• A metric that upholds Engelking’s principles.
• A metric based on protein comparison tools.
• A metric able to identify conserved sequence features by differentiating between
TCRs that bind the same or different epitopes.
To allow the evaluation of the different protein comparison tools, they can be analyzed
as classifiers in a binary classification system. A classification system is a model that
maps a data object to a discrete class. Classification is, in general, done for two reasons:
to understand data and to predict future outcomes. Classifiers are evaluated based on
a decision three where all data begin at the root and are separated based on conditions
[159]. It is possible to compute the purity gain, either by calculating entropy or the Gini
purity. A good condition provides the highest amount of purity at the next branch.
All the protein comparison tools presented in chapter three can be used as classifiers
for TCRs, yet, neither are a strong classifier as a stand-alone-method.
In this case,
there are many weak classifiers. Therefore, it is possible to use ensemble methods that
combine weak classifiers into one strong classifier [117], [56]. Ensemble methods can be
divided into bagging methods and boosting methods. When using a bagging algorithm,
multiple data-sets are generated based on the input data; then, each data-set is fitted
to one classifier.
Majority voting yield the most significant classifiers.
An example
of bagging is Random Forest [27].
Random forest is an algorithm where each three
is generated by sampling data and employing a random subset of the attributes when
splitting.
Subsequently, a large subset of the threes are combined, and the features
that allowed the highest purity gain based on majority voting are the features that are
most likely the be the best classifiers. Boosting, instead emphasize on wrongly classified
elements, by continually assigning weights to the pool of training data in a way that
up-weigh the wrongly classified elements. An example of a boosting method is adaptive
boosting termed AdaBoost. AdaBoost is an algorithm that iterates over the data by
continually up-weighting the data-points that were not correct classifies. The iterations
end when majority voting agree on classification.
When developing a model over-fitting is a concern.
As the model face the bias-
variance trade-off. The model should be as complicated as possible without over-fitting.
A large bias and a small variance will lead to poor approximation, but a robust model,

• Hold out

• K-fold
The data is divided into K partitions. Each partition is divided into a test- and a
training set. K is the size of the test set, and the rest is the training. [169].
• Leave-one-out
One data point is taken out of each run and used to validate. These methods are
more computationally expensive than the hold out method [169].
The cross-validation methods can be nested within each other. For instance, the training
set in the hold out method can undergo a K-fold validation. The choice of the method
relies on the size of the data-set. In a larger data-set, a hold-out or perhaps a k-fold of
3 will suffice, while a smaller data-set might benefit from leave-one-out [213].

5.4
Distance Metric
The distance measure between two TCR beta-chains is thus a function over the distances
of CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3 with weights attached to the different methods.
The
distance measure is trained on a dataset, and should for further application generate a
distance matrix with all pairwise distances between the input TCRs. Therefore a wrap
around script is created to allow parallel processing of these calculations.

Performance evaluation
The performance of each method is evaluated based on 1) how well the method predicts
if two TCRs bind the same epitope 2) how well it allows clustering of specific epitopes
and 3) how fast it runs. To evaluate how well a method predicts if two TCRs bind the
same epitope, two binary files are generated of the same size as the distance matrices
for each method. The first is based on an exact match between Epitope and HLA genes.
Hence the binary file has 0 as negative when the two TCRs do not bind the same epitope
presented on the same MHC, and 1 when it does. An example is presented in fig 5.4.
The other binary file is based on epitope similarity, and allow very similar epitopes and
epitopes bound on different MHCs to be identified as positives, as presented in fig 5.5.
These two files are achieved based on string identity, and a library imported dictionary.
Hence, to compare, a truth matrix is used.

method alone, they are combined. To determine which methods are good classification
tools, logistic regression was applied to the distance matrices. However, the dataset is
imbalanced as the binary matrices that contain the information of whether two TCRs
bind the same epitope have a vast majority of negatives (0) and a vast minority of
positives (1), why doing logistic regression on the dataset as it is, is improper [220],
[148]. The problem with an imbalanced dataset, where the majority is negative, is that
if every case is classified as negative, the accuracy of the model will be very high, as
most cases by far are negative. Nevertheless, no real information gain has been achieved.
It is, therefore, necessary to even out the imbalance. There exist several ways to solve
the imbalanced data problem; they can be divided into three groups: the algorithmic
approach, data-preprocessing approach, and feature selection approach [149]. In this
thesis, the data-preprocessing approach was used. The data-preprocessing approach is
a way to balance the dataset before classification, by either upsampling the minority
or downsampling the majority group. In the interest of keeping the file sizes down and
running, the downsampling of the majority group is conducted. Hence, each line of the
distance matrices is read and reformed into a data frame where each method is a column,
and the column contains the distance to one TCR. The last column is the binary file
informing if there is a match. Each match is kept, and the same number of non-matches
are kept, using sklearns resample [150], thereby making a balanced set. There is looped
over every line in all files, while it is only the resampled set that is kept. The risk of
this approach is a potential loss of valuable data. Alternatives to this approach include
feature selection, which does not fit the aim of this thesis, as the features have been
carefully chosen. Finally an algorithm such as kernel logistic regression or maximum-
likelihood logistic regression (MLLR) [125], [149] could also have been used. Yet these,
too, rely on subsampling at their core, and there does not seem to be any particular

7.3
Assigning Coefficients
In order to combine these weak classifiers in each fold, a method of making coefficients
is needed, to ensure that the features with the ability to make the best classifiers are
given the largest weight. There are several approaches when assigning coefficients, from
choosing a numeric value based on the immunological impact such as Dash et al. [52],
who assigned the weights 1 for CDR1 and CDR2 and 3 for CDR3 to a statistic approach.
When evaluating a binary classifier statistically, the obvious choice is Logistic regression.
Logistic regression is theoretically able to assign coefficients to each classifier based on
the log-odds of a data point being in either the positive or the negative group. However,
doing logistic regression require that logistic regression can be fitted to the data - which
requires a clear motif that shorter distances indicate binding to the same epitope. This
is not necessarily the case for either fold. Fitting logistic regression to the data in the
folds is presented in figure 7.13. The Logistic regression cannot be fitted to the data;
the R values are 0.2 at best. No non-binders have a distance below 0.1 for fold 1 and
below 0.2 for fold 2 and 3, yet, binders do have distances up to 1. Meaning that binders
stretch the entirety of the distance scale. Looking at these graphs, the best classifier
might be: if distance < 0.2 == binder, elif > 0.2: chose binder/non-binder at random.
This of cause is not feasible, why other measures are considered. Firstly, a return to
ensemble methods with AdaBoost. Here AdaBoost’s ability to quantify which classifiers
are most important can be used to assign coefficients in an iterative fashion. AdaBoost
is run on the folds after removal based on random forest, and the coefficients for each
method is calculated. If a coefficient is below 0.1 (10%), the classifier is removed, and
AdaBoost is re-run, and coefficients are re-assigned until the accuracy score demises. A
table of AdaBoost coefficients and accuracy scores for fold 1 is available in table 7.2.
Even-though there is practically no information gain in reducing classifiers; there is no
information loss and a reduction of the computational time. The model for distance
measurement based on fold 1 is described in equation 7.1 based on equation 5.3. This is
applied to the validation set. The same process is applied to fold 2 and fold 3, which can
been seen in tables 7.3 and 7.4. The metrics is written in equation 7.2 and 7.3. However,
accuracies of approximately 65% on the training data itself is not a good prognosis for
the validation accuracy. There is a long list of options for errors, to mention a few:
We do not know if the TCRs with a low distance, that is classified as non-binders are
binders due to the limited data and TCR promiscuity. Potentially the binders (1) and
non-binders (0) are assigned too conservatively. Potentially, reducing the problem to a
binary classification rather than an epitope classification is simplifying so much, that
this too introduces errors - or perhaps the missing structural element of CDR3 is crucial
in identifying binders. Introducing coefficients will not rectify these sources of error.
Yet, introducing coefficients that hold biological significance rather than mathematical
optimization could potentially be useful.

Thus, the principles are tested.
• Symmetry: d(a, b) = d(b, a)
The small subset is perfectly symmetrical, so is the whole validation set.
• Non-negativity: d(a, b) ≥0
No scores are below 0
• Identification Mark: d(a, a) = 0
The diagonal is 0, no other distance is.
• Definiteness: A distance between a and b can only be zero if a = b.
There are no distances of 0 outside the diagonal. This is good, as we know, none
of the four TCRs were identical.

distance from a to c plus c to b, d(a, b) ≤d(a, c) + d(c, b)

8.1
Overall structure and computational time
The distance metric can be downloaded from the attached zip file and run by running the
TCRdistancestart.py script in terminal (only tested on the Debian like system ubuntu):
./BetaDistanceStart.py > run_distance.sh The user is prompted to enter the file path
to the directory where they have placed the folder and the name of the tsv file to be
examined. There is no GUI for the program, as the method is supposed to be integrated
into diversity calculations. Writing the two parameters into the prompt will write out
the steps needed to run the algorithm for that particular file in a bash script.
To
overcome large file sizes and handling making an n(TCR)*n(TCR) matrix, the tsv file is
partitioned into sections of 50 TCRs, where each TCR is calculated against every TCR
in the .tsv and saved in one file, termed chunks. Chunk 0 is then TCR 1 until TCR
50, and chunk 1 is TCR 51 to TCR 100 and so on. To calculate the betadist distances,
four scripts are used.
The TCRdist script is the wraparound script that allows the
chunks to be calculated and printed. This script employs the distance calculations from
two scripts, the CDR1, and CDR2 library importer and the CDR3 distance calculator.
Hence, in order for the algorithm to run a directory with the libraries must be present in
the file path. The distance measure algorithm takes a tsv file as input containing CDR3
sequences in the first column, V genes in the second and Epitopes in the third column.
The distance can run without this third column, which is only applicable if evaluations of
the distance metric are desired. The V-gene format needs to follow IMGT nomenclature
with the deviation that the Asterix is exchanged for an underscore, TRBV09*01 become
TRBV09_01.
After reading the input of the chunk, the wraparound parallelize the
distance calculations to speed up the process. The overall flow of the core program to
calculate the distance is illustrated in figure 8.1.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
Novel therapeutic solutions are needed in an ever-changing environment. Employing
t-cells to fight these diseases is a paradigm shift from classical drug development. The
scientific community is still in the relatively early phases of developing drugs that specif-
ically target to aid t-cells. Research into binding specificity and differences in these
t-cells is needed to lay the foundations for these potential solutions. This discussion
will shortly summarize the findings in this thesis, interpret and describe the significance
of them as well as explore implications. Lastly, limitations and future research will be
discussed.
9.1
An integrative T-cell receptor distance
The produced results indicate that combining multiple well-known protein comparison
tools into one tool performs better when differentiating t-cell receptors than using one
tool alone. The analysis confirms that structural predictions of CDR1 and CDR2 are
appropriate tools to determine the distance between TCRs. Surprisingly, it was not ap-
parent in the feature selection, that CDR3 mediate the main binding with the epitope, as
CDR1 and CDR2 comparisons were given almost equal weights. The understanding that
CDR3 is not the most important loop does not correlate with the general understanding
of the TCR:p:MHC interaction. This might suggest one of three things:
• That CDR1 and CDR2 have more control than previously anticipated.
• That insufficient methods for CDR3 were chosen in this thesis.
• That structure is all-important.
Nevertheless, it was proven possible to create a relatively robust distance metric that is
able to identify if two t-cell receptors will bind the same epitope. Unfortunately, this
separation is not perfect. The test-data suggest that this metric is better than readily
available methods of distance measurements published within the past couple of years,
respectively Immunodist [181] and CDRdist [190], why there is a potential for future
work on this metric.

9 Discussion
9.2
Utilization and real-world application
The utilization of this metric is in diversity measures. Diversity is defined by abundance
and similarity; having a biological significant distance measure will imply a different
similarity. This will map genetic differences between individuals and can, together with
information about their response pattern, allow practitioners to map out which genetic
fingerprints correspond to different therapeutic regimens. This method has proven ap-
plicable in cancer research, where one of the questions in modern cancer treatment is:
Why do some patients respond well to immunotherapies, while others do not? In autoim-
mune diseases, the developed method is a step on the way to assessing, how personal
does personal medicine need to be? Are there any groups with a specific pattern, and
how does this pattern or fingerprint differ from a healthy individual? Finally, in in-
fectious diseases, allowing us to find differences and understand why some individuals
do not become ill with specific infectious diseases could be an approach to find novel
therapeutics. In conclusion, a distance metric does open up more questions than it an-
swers, but can, when integrated with other measures, be a powerful tool in predicting
therapeutic regimens.
9.3
Future perspectives and improvements
Even though this metric was shown to be just as good or potentially better than the
baseline distance metric, there are some clear limitations to the method. A TCR is not
just one subunit, and with the larger deposit of paired data, the alpha chain should be
included in the metric. Furthermore, the metric is not able to differentiate epitopes or
epitope groups. However, epitope similarity concerning what TCRs bind might not be
mediated based on what protein theory perceives as similar based on Blosum62. There
are still many questions to be answered in regard to t-cells. However, with more time,
the following parameters would be interesting to investigate in order to improve the
metric. Firstly, more methods for testing could have been investigated, as the choice of
methods limits the final model, and integrating the LYRA framework for loops modeling
for CDR3 could be an interesting study.
Another improvement to this study is to optimize the imbalance of the data in a more
sophisticated fashion. A suggestion could be to use an up-sampling algorithm, thus not
losing any data. There is a possibility that logistic regression could have been successfully
used in this thesis, and pushing of the AdaBoost algorithm to force coefficient would
not have been needed. In relation to coefficients, testing physio-chemical distances as
coefficients would also be an interesting approach. This was tested briefly with volume
without any information gain. A more structured approach to these parameters would
be interesting to test. Converting physio-chemical properties into distances was done
in a considerably ad-hoc way to ensure a mathematical distance rather than deeply
analyze the methods. In conclusion, there are many possible improvements to develop
this metric, and it is suspected that many and hopefully better metrics will follow this

9.3 Future perspectives and improvements

one, as both understanding of t-cell receptors and the ability to model CDR3 sequences
expand. To develop a good distance metric for t-cells, the two following parameters
should be established:
• Epitope binding - The characteristics of promiscuous TCRs.
In order to better the evaluation of metrics, a clear understanding of binding motifs
for CDR3s would be beneficial.
• CDR3 structure
Structures of CDR1 and CDR2 proved themself advantageous, why a way to mea-
sure the similarity of structures for CDR3 without solving the structures should
be included in the metric.
Finally, there is room for code optimisation that would increase efficiency and decrease
time and memory usage. This could be done by only calculating the upper triangular of
the n*n matrix. Another approach to making the code more efficient is by collecting the
libraries. The program would not then fetch distances from four libraries, two for CDR1
and two for CDR2, but instead one library. This library should then be constructed as
one V-gene score based on the distances and coefficients of CDR1 and CDR2. Fetching
the scores in the libraries is already a fast process, why it would not have the same
effect as only calculating the upper triangular. Secondly, there might be more efficient
ways of calculating Pam10 than using biopython. Developers of Immunodist [181] were
able to make a fast tool in MatLab where Needleman-Wunsch alignment is native rather
than a package. Potentially there are options in using another framework than python.
Furthermore, there might be better methods to scale the results of the alignment. At
present, all self alignments are calculated and saved in an array to be used for scaling.
This considerably decreased the calculation time; however, other measures might be used
to achieve this result, again drawing inspiration from Immunodist.
All of this is left to be investigated in future work.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to produce a distance metric to calculate the dissimilarity between
TCR β-subunits, where a short distance would indicate binding of the same epitope, and
a long-distance would indicate binding of different epitopes. The distance metric was
build by collecting different protein comparison techniques and applying these to the
CDR sequences. Afterward assessing which techniques were able to achieve the largest
purity gain based on feature selection algorithms. The concluding distance metric is:
BetaDist(TCRβi, TCRβj) = (0.15 ∗Blosum62(CDR1i,j ) + 0.17 ∗TM(CDR1i,j )
+ (0.28 ∗TM(CDR2i,j ) + 0.21 ∗RMSD(CDR2i,j )
+ (0.19 ∗Pam10(CDR3i,j )
(10.1)
Hence, all three complementary determining regions are included with the largest em-
phasis on CDR1 and CDR2, which is in direct opposition to current knowledge of T-cell
binding. Betadist showed itself better than direct β-subunit competitors in differenti-
ating between binders and non-binders based on distance, proving the hypothesis that
structural elements would give a better distance metric true. Although Betadist is not
able to differentiate perfectly and this thesis can, therefore, conclude, that structural
distances have the greatest potential and developing a distance based on structural com-
parisons of CDR3. Future work should integrate this structural element.

Cancer
Cancer is a broad term for the formation and proliferation of malignant cells in a host.
Cancer arises from failures in the mechanisms that usually control cell growth and pro-
liferation. Therefore, it follows that cancer occurs when these mechanisms malfunction
and allow the cells to proliferate uncontrollably. Malfunctions are a result of genetic
damage, and the inappropriate cell growth is either due to; Lack of regulation, increased
gene expression, or expression of an error. Usually, DNA damage is repaired efficiently
and conservatively by cell growth checkpoints and specialized repair systems. These
retain the cell growth and repair the different types of lesions. One mutation does not
result in cancer, but a cell with the ability to grow uncontrollably without detection gives
favorable conditions for additional mutations [123]. If mutations lead to cancer, the im-
mune system usually fights it using its surveillance system. Here the innate and adaptive
immunity recognize and destroy developing tumors before they are clinically apparent.
The adaptive T-cells eliminate cancer cells based on expressed protein fragments, while
the innate NK cells eliminate cancer cells based on surface-expressed abnormalities. If
this system fails, the cancer cells grow and induce an immune-suppressive microenvi-
ronment and become clinically apparent tumors in need of treatment. The traditional
cancer treatment includes surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy. Surgery and radiation
are local treatments, while chemotherapy is a systemic class of drugs. They differ as the
local treatments remove or attack the primary tumor site, while the systemic chemother-
apy induces apoptosis in fast-dividing cells, thereby reducing the primary tumor and
attacking metastasis. The faults of chemotherapy are, however, that dosage can be un-
predictable, drug resistance is prevalent, and the side effects are many and severe. Once
the patient develops resistance to one type of chemo, other resistances follows swiftly
after [45]. Consequently, the problem with traditional treatment is ineffectiveness and
harsh side-effects. Presently immunotherapy has become a relatively routine practice.
Methods vary from checkpoint inhibitors to adoptive T-cell transfer and monoclonal anti-
bodies. All these therapies have the objective of modulating the immunological response
and turn the patients’ immune cells against the malignant cells. Immunotherapies show
promising results, although not all patients respond in a unified way. Survival rates
are still not understood in detail, and there is a largely empirical approach to designing
immunotherapies [73]. Despite its success, one of the main questions still associated
with immunotherapy is: why do some patients respond so well, while others do not? A
hypothesis is that responders have some genetic similarities within their T-cell receptors,

Autoimmune disease
Autoimmune disease is a broad term for diseases caused by a reaction to self-antigens;
they affect approximately 3-5% of the studied populations and have considerable modal-
ity and mortality rates [35].
Autoimmune reactions arise when the mechanisms for
self-detection fails. Thus, letting self-reactive lymphocytes leave the thymus. The self-
detection is a system consisting of multiple layers. Most prominent negative selection,
where self-antigens are tested for binding by the new cell in the thymus. Subsequent
layers of protection incorporate Antigen Segregation, a physical barrier to self-antigen ac-
cess to the lymphoid system. Peripheral anergy, inactivation due to weak co-stimulation.
Regulatory T-cells are suppressing the autoimmunity through the release of suppressive
cytokines. Finally, induced cell death by apoptosis. Hence, developing an autoimmune
disease requires evasion of multiple checkpoints [77].
Autoimmune reactions become
a disease when self-binding T- or B-cells react to self tissue. Self reaction initiate in-
flammation by generating a positive feedback loop, where the tissue presented by an
antigen-presenting cell (APC) to the T-cell lets the T-cell clone proliferate and generate
immune responses to more self tissue. At the same time, the B-cells secrete antibodies
that too bind more of the own tissue. Consequently, using all the immunological tools
developed to fight an intruder to fight self. Some of the autoimmune diseases are local
organ-specific, while others are systemic [112]. In order to develop an autoimmune dis-
ease, the individual is either infected by an immunomodulating agent or is pre-disposed
due to genetic factors, respectively, such as with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV)
and Diabetes type 1 [77]. The infections are some of the more studied causes of au-
toimmune disease, yet even with correlation, causation seems hard to decipher. One
example of this is the Ebstein-Barr virus (EBV) found as a cofactor in several autoim-
mune diseases, including systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis [33], [1],
and primary biliary cirrhosis [76]. However, there has also been established a correlation
between EBV and distinct cancer types [38]. Treating autoimmune disease has tradi-
tionally been a difficult task, as the immunological response is dependent on the host.
For particular autoimmune diseases, the function of the damaged tissue can sometimes
be replaced, such as with insulin. Nevertheless, the typical treatment of autoimmune
disease is immunosuppressive drugs [93] — new drugs to treat autoimmunity attempt to
re-establish tolerance using biological agents to block immunological pathways. Hence,
autoimmunity can be treated today but not cured [112].

Infectious disease is a term describing diseases arising from infection with a foreign agent,
causing an immune reaction in the form of inflammation. The broad groups of foreign

treatment regimens, such as antibiotics, towards bacteria. However, the deceased effect
of antibiotics [63] can lead to modality and even mortality after infection, why novel

Appendix 8: Hidden
Markov Model
The seperation of the training and the test set was done using Blosum62, yet it was not
the most sophisticated way. A hidden markov model was tested for seperation. This
analysis was done by generating 140 fasta files, one for each epitope containing the CDR3
sequences to which it binds. The CDR3 sequences underwent multiple alignment, using
the Clustal Omega approach. Clustal Omega work by pairwise sequence comparison
as words and hmm seeds, the main force is that this approach is quite fast [182]. The
output was aligned fasta files. When only one sequence was assigned to one epitope,
alignment was naturally unnecessary. For epitopes only binding two CDR3 sequences,
the option ”–singlemx” was chosen to force alignment. The aligned fasta files were run
through the HMMER package [66] to generate HMM profiles for each epitope group.
The output was, besides the hmm profile file, also a measure of similarity within the
sequence and relative entropy per position. The output gave an overview of the similarity
of the CDR3s within each group. After running all aligned fasta files though the HMM
builder, a database was generated by concatenating all 175 HMM profiles. This allowed a
search through the database with the aligned fasta files to find the epitope groups which
they resemble, hence creating a profile-profile analysis. This output E-value, score, and
bias for the models within the database, both for the full sequence and the best one
domain. As the CDR3 itself is only a domain, only the results from the full sequence
were considered. The E-value, which annotates the quality of the search, was used to
assess similarity within the epitope groups. A lower E-value indicates better quality but
is not a static measure - it depends on the size of the database. However, as all Epitope
groups were searched within each other, the score is relative within the parameters [191].
This profile-profile analysis allowed the network illustrated in figure ??. This illustration
group together all the epitopes, meaning that the CDR3 regions are so similar to each
other that no further information is gained from considering this approach. Nevertheless,
it should be considered that this alignment approach might not be well fitted for CDR3s,
for which multiple alignments should be done in a biological sense by inserting the gaps
in the middle of the sequences, as the CDR3s are loops and the logic illustrated in figure
5.2 also apply here. Since no new information seems to be relayed and time constraints
apply, the alignment was not redone; however, this should be considered as a source of
error.
