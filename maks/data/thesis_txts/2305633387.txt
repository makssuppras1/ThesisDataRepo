Abstract
Due to the growing global energy demand and environmental impact of the tra-
ditional energy sources. A new and sustainable energy sources is need. To that
end this study is part of the eﬀort to ﬁnd new and cheaper nonprecious metal cat-
alysts for the Oxygen Reduction Reaction (ORR). The purpose of this study was
to synthesise a carbon encapsulated iron catalyst to be used at the cathode in a
hydrogen Fuel Cells (FCs). This was done by high temperature autoclave pyrolysis
using decomposing precursors. The precursors used are ferrocene as the iron source
and durene as additional carbon source. The autoclave is sealed in Ar atmosphere
to ensure that no nitrogen is present during the pyrolysis. The synthesising of a
nitrogen free catalyst was done to obtain information about what part iron takes in
the activity of the catalyst. The catalysts was synthesised with varied iron content
from 4 to 14.4 wt% iron, at 600, 700 and 800 ◦C and at short and long pyrolysis time.
In order to remove any iron not encapsulated by carbon the samples were washed
in 0.5 M H2SO4. To explore the signiﬁcance of nitrogen functionalised carbon for
the ORR activity samples were post synthesis treated in NH3 gas at 850 ◦C. The
resulting catalysts was characterized by Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM),
M¨ossbauer spectroscopy, X-ray Powder Diﬀraction (XRPD) and Rotating Disk Elec-
trode (RDE). The catalyst consisted of interwoven Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs) and
iron particles encapsulated in graphitic layers. Iron particles could be seen having
an inner encapsulating layer of graphite and a further ”growth”layer where it looked
as if new carbon nanotube was formed. M¨ossbauer spectroscopy conﬁrmed the pres-
ence of Fe3C and α-iron. Which was cooperated by the results from XRPD. An
unknown phase only seen in the samples additional treated with NH3 at 850 ◦C was
found by XRPD. Furthermore, it was seen that the α-iron phase was removed by
the acid washing.
A three electrode setup with a glassy carbon RDE was used to determine the cat-
alytic activity. The catalytic activity increased as the iron content was increased
and by the treatment with NH3 at 850 ◦C. The catalyst with the highest activity
was FD-700-NH3; a Fe wt% of 7.7 and treated with NH3.

1. Introduction
The global energy demand is increasing and the environmental impact and availabil-
ity of the traditional energy sources are a growing challenge. For future mobile and
stationary power generation Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC) has
been recognized as a promising solution for providing clean and sustainable energy.
FCs generate electricity by directly converting chemical energy to electric energy
by electrochemically reducing O2 and oxidizing H2 into H2O as the waste product.
The catalyst is a crucial component of a FC. The sluggish kinetics of the cathode
reaction, the ORR, is a major drawback preventing commercialization of hydrogen
based energy technology [1]. The most commonly used catalysts are Pt-based as
they exhibit the highest activity towards the ORR. However, the cost and avail-
ability of Pt is a signiﬁcant bump on the road towards a hydrogen based energy
infrastructure.
To overcome the current challenges associated with speciﬁcally the ORR tremen-
dous eﬀorts are being made to develop alternative catalyst to the Pt-based. As part
of that eﬀort; catalysts based on non-precious metals such as Fe and Co are being
developed. To preserve the metal in the harsh environment of a PEMFC eﬀorts
are being made to encapsulate the metal in a layer of graphite or graphene. The
carbon encapsulated metal catalyst is typically synthesized from iron- and nitrogen-
containing precurosers.
The goal of this thesis was to synthesise a nitrogen free catalyst comprised of iron
and carbon. A part of the goal was to synthesise the catalyst with a layer of carbon
protecting the iron in order to stabilize the catalysts in an acidic environment. This
was to be done in order to gain information about the active site and what moiety
attributes to the catalytic activity.

2. Literature Review
2.1
Catalysis in General
This section is based on the book Catalysis - Concepts and Green Applications by
Gadi Rothenberg [2].
This section introduces the concepts of heterogeneous catalysis and why catalysis
in general is important for sustainable development of renewable energy and many
other areas of science and industry.
The importance of catalysts cannot be exaggerated. Catalysts are essential in sup-
plying energy, medicines, fertilizers and much more. A consequence of increasing
demand for raw materials has been a desire for higher utilization of the raw mate-
rials available. But in order to achieve an increase in utilization, better production
processes are needed and at the core of most chemical production plants are cata-
lysts.
Catalysis deals with the kinetics of a reaction by changing the route to equilibrium,
but with no change to the thermodynamics of the reaction.
First to deﬁne catalysts types, there are three types of catalysis; homogeneous catal-
ysis, heterogeneous catalysis and biocatalysis. In homogeneous catalysis, all the ac-
tion takes place in the same phase, meaning the catalyst, reactants and products are
in the same phase, most often being liquid or gas phase reactions. Biocatalysis is
somewhere between homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis. The biocatalyst are
mostly enzymes and the reactions associated whit biocatalysis takes place in living
cells. As the catalyst, prepared in the context of this thesis, and catalysts used in
electrochemical devices are heterogeneous, biocatalysis and heterogeneous catalysis
will not be mentioned further. Heterogeneous catalysis is deﬁned by the catalyst
and the substrate not being in the same phase. Usually heterogeneous catalysis refer
to the case where the catalyst is solid and the reactants are gasses. The solid/gas
combination is referred to as the “classic” heterogeneous catalysis as the origin of
most heterogeneous catalysts are in the petrochemical and bulk-chemical industry.
Due to reactivity and process volume continuous processes at high temperature were
favoured or mandatory. This meant that a solid catalyst was by far the best, or only
option. Due to the solid nature of the catalyst it remains in the reactor or device if

2.1.1
Electrocatalysis
Electrochemical reactions, by deﬁnition, are heterogeneous redox reactions. Elec-
trochemical reactions take place at the interface between an electronic and an ionic
conductor i.e. the electrode and electrolyte. The reaction rate, in electrochemistry,
is deﬁned by the current (I in A) or current density (i in A/cm2). The current is
linearly related to the reaction rate through Faraday’s law [3]:
ν = nFI
(2.1)
Where ν is the reaction rate, n the number of electrons participating in the reaction
and F is Faraday’s constant. The potential diﬀerence between the anode and cath-
ode is the force making electrons move from one to the other. The potential, E in
V, is the work of the electron transport between the electrode and the electrolyte.
An important characteristic of electrochemical reactions is the electrochemical po-
tential. The electrochemical potential of the electrons in an electrode is equal to
the Fermi level, the work required to add or remove one electron from a body, equal
to the highest occupied energy level. When the electrode is polarized the Fermi
level is changed. As electrons are pumped to the electrode it is charged negatively,
meaning that the Fermi level increases. And if the electrode is charged positively,
electrons are pumped from the electrode, the Fermi level decreases. The change in
polarization of the electrode, thereby, aﬀecting the Fermi level which changes the
activation energy for a electrochemical reaction.
The electrode provides a surface to function as a heterogeneous catalyst for the elec-
trochemical reaction.
For a reaction to take place at the electrode one of more
reactants must adsorb onto it and the products must desorb and diﬀuse away.
Chemisorption of the reactants on the surface of the catalyst is essential for the
reaction, because the proximity to the catalyst decreases the activation energy bar-
rier. As a result of the chemisorption the energy levels of the adsorbent is shifted
and broadened. Because many catalysts are d-metals and Fe, the metal examined
here, is a d-metal. The interaction of the adsorbent with a d-metal creates a set of
bonding and antibonding chemisorptions orbitals, showed in Figure 2.1. The energy
levels in the adsorbent below the Fermi level of the metal are occupied by the d
electrons. As the antibonding and bonding both aﬀect the chemisoption bond, ﬁll-
ing the antibonding orbital will strengthen the chemisoption bond but weaken the
intramolecular bond, leading to dissociation of the adsorbed molecule. The polar-

cathode side in a hydrogen FC, but still, the cathode remains the major source of
energy loss [16].

2.3.1
Precious Metal Catalyst
The most eﬀective catalysts for the ORR is Pt and Pt group metals and alloys of
Pt and 3d transitions metals [5, 21–27]. The limited availability, durability and
cost of Pt and Pt-based catalysts hinder widespread adoption of PEMFCs [16, 27].
The Pt-based catalyst accounts for 46% of the total cost of a PEMFC stack if
500,000 systems are produced per year, according to the DOE Fuel Cell Technologies
Oﬃce [28]. To overcome the cost and limited availability of Pt, tremendous eﬀort
has been spend to improve utilization by decreasing the loading, alloying Pt with
transition metals and dispersing nano-structures on high surface area supports [6,
24]. However, no design approach can change the fact that Pt is a rare metal and the
amount available is limited [6, 16, 24]. Therefore the development of Non-Precious
Metal Catalysts (NPMCs) has been the main focus of many researchers for decades
[6, 16, 24].

2.3.2
Non-precious Metal Catalysts
The NPMCs can be grouped in four general categories: The M-Nx/C catalysts,
Fe3C/C catalysts, the M/C catalysts and the CN catalysts.
The terms will be
explained in the following sections. The role of the metal in these catalysts are
a subject of discussion [6, 29]. In general the views can be divided in two main
groups; either the metal is directly involved in catalysing the ORR as an active site,
going through a redox-type mechanism, interacting directly with O2, or the metal
only indirectly contributes to the ORR by modifying the electronic properties of the
active site by the presence of the metal entity [6] or as a catalyst for formation of
the active site.
Common for all the catalyst, apart from the nitrogen free, reviewed here is the
incorporated nitrogen in the carbon structure. Even though the nitrogen in the
carbon structure may not be regarded as a contribution to the catalytic activity, as
in for example, the M-Nx/C catalysts. The nitrogen is incorporated by replacing
a sp2-hybridized carbon atom with a nitrogen atom. Figure 2.3 shows the types
of N-C structures believed to be relevant for the ORR [6, 9, 30]. The preparation
methods are numerous and some will be described in the following. In general the
preparation involves heat treating of one or more precursors containing the desired
elements and the use of Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD) to the same end.

The metal site in the M-Nx/C catalyst is considered to be essential for the active

Van Veen et al. [36] reports that the highest activity for ORR is achieved when the

temperatures, 500-600 ◦C. The moderate temperature means that the M-N4 moi-

involved. Yeager et al. [18, 37] suggests that the N4-metallomacrocyclic complex de-

and nitrogen-carbon species is achieved above 800 ◦C. Following this track Yeager et
al. [18, 37] proposes that the active sites for ORR were formed, as the prepared cata-
lyst comes in contact with the electrolyte after the pyrolysis. The metallic and oxide
species dissolve and subsequently adsorb or coordinates to Nitrogen-modiﬁed Car-
bon (NC) forming the catalytic active site. An opposing proposal by Wiesener [38]
suggest that the metallic species (Fe or Co) in the N4-metallomacrocyclic complexe
only facilitate the formation of NC from the N-containing ligands, which constitutes
the actual active sites. Here the metal is considered to take no part in the ORR.

2.3.4
The Fe3C/C Catalyst
The Fe3C/C catalysts have a core-shell type structure with a core of Fe species and
a shell of protective graphitic layers. The outer graphitic layers protect the inter
metal, stabilizing the catalyst in acidic environments [14, 42–45]. It is suggested
that the inner Fe3C plays a role in activating the outer graphitic layer [14, 46] and
it is likely that a new type ORR site on the surfaces of the graphitic layer [46, 47].
The Fe3C/C type catalyst prepared by Hu et al. [14, 42, 43] is synthesized by a
one-step high-temperature autoclave pyrolysis. The precursor used were cyanamide
and ferrocene. They were heat treated in a homemade autoclave of stainless steel
parts with a quartz cup inside, containing the precursors. To prevent formation of
oxide species the precursors were mixed and the autoclave prepared in a nitrogen-
ﬁled glove box. The pyrolysis was performed at 500, 600, 630, 660, 700 and 800 ◦C.
The obtained catalysts were examined by Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) and
TEM, showing that the obtained catalysts consisted primarily of uniform spheres.

2.3.5
The CN or Metal Free Catalysts
The metal free catalysts are, as the name suggests, catalysts which does not contain
any metal elements. Or if any metal is present, it may not have any inﬂuence on the
catalytic activity. In addition, it should be proved that there is no metal residues in
the ﬁnal catalyst if the precursors contain metal. In the context of ORR catalysts
the most common is carbon matrix modiﬁed with one or two nonmetal elements as:
nitrogen, boron, ﬂuorine, phosphorus or sulfur [6, 30]. The most common metal free
catalysts for ORR are NC. There is no consensus in the literature on the nature
of the active site in the NC catalysts [6, 30, 48]. The electrocatalytic activity has
been suggested to be due to a change in electron density at the C atoms adjacent
to the N atom, facilitating dissociative chemisorption of O2 [49, 50]. Also it has
12
==PAGE:21==
Jakob Sværke
2016
been suggested that the activity was related to variation in the work function of the
carbon as an eﬀect of the inserted dopant atom [51].
The carbon structures seen in NC catalysts include SWCNT, Multi-walled Carbon
Nanotube (MWCNT), graphene, carbon blocks and mesoporous carbon. In general
the synthesis of NC catalysts can be divided into two categories, direct growth and
postsynthesis. In direct growth the incorporation of nitrogen takes place simultane-
ously with the formation of the carbon structure. The postsynthesis approach is as
the name hints at, where the already prepared carbon structure is treated with a
reactive nitrogen agent such as NH3 or nitrogen plasma [6, 30].

Energy Dispersive X-Ray Analysis
Energy Dispersive X-ray (EDX) analysis is used for elemental analysis of a given
sample. EDX analysis is based on the fundamental principle that every element
has a unique atomic structure allowing a unique X-ray emission spectra. To induce
X-ray emissions from the target sample a high-energy beam of charged particles
are needed and the electron beam already employed in the TEM is well suited. To
take advantage of this, an energy-dispersive spectrometer is mounted on the TEM
apparatus. The incident electrons excites an electron from the inner shell of the
sample element and an electron from the outer shell will jump in to ﬁll the vacancy.
Doing so, releasing the diﬀerence in energy as an X-ray. The energy of the released
X-rays are measured and used to identify the parent element.

3.3
Rotating Disk Voltammetry
Electrochemical techniques are sensitive and easily inﬂuenced by convection in the
electrolyte due to bulk movement. To control the eﬀects of convection on the ob-
tained voltammetric curve the movement of the bulk electrolyte must be controlled.
17
==PAGE:26==
Jakob Sværke
2016
Using a RDE this can be achieved.
The RDE is a hydrodynamic Working Electrode (WE) employed in a three electrode
experimental setup, along with a Counter Electrode (CE) and a Reference Electrode
(RE). The RDE is a conducting disk of glassy carbon embedded in a round poly-
mer holder. The conducting disk can be of any conducting material, as needed in
the speciﬁc experiment, usually a noble metal or glassy carbon. As the disk rotate
the centrifugal force pushes the electrolyte away from the centre of the electrode,
forming the hydrodynamic boundary layer. To replace the electrolyte forced away,
new electrolyte ﬂows up, perpendicular to the electrode, to replace the electrolyte
forced away. Renewing the hydrodynamic boundary layer results in a laminar ﬂow
of fresh electrolyte to the centre and across the electrode. The ﬂow, controlled by
the rotation, creates a condition where the steady-state current is controlled by the
inﬂux of fresh electrolyte and not by diﬀusion. As the desired molecule moves close
to the electrode diﬀusion becomes more important, as the ﬁnal movement to the
electrode is across the thin layer of electrolyte known as the diﬀusion layer.
The electrode rotation rate (ω) is given as the angular velocity of the electrode
around the origin in units of radians/second (rad/s) or revolutions per minute (rpm).
The rotation is controlled by an electric motor connected to a control box displaying
the rpm of the electrode.

6. Conclusion
Carbon encapsulated iron carbide based catalysts samples were successfully synthe-
sised using nitrogen free precursors in order to obtain clues about the role of iron
in the oxygen reduction reaction. Employing transmission electron microscopy the
morphology of the catalysts was investigated. The catalysts consist of interwoven
carbon nanotubes and iron particles encapsulated in graphitic layers several nano
meters in thickness. Iron particles can be seen having an inner encapsulating layer
of graphite and a further ”growth” layer where it looks as if new carbon nanotube is
formed, catalysed by the iron particle. The samples additional treated with NH3 at
850 ◦C at showed what looked like ruptured carbon nanotubes but the iron particles
still appears to be encapsulated in carbon.
The results obtained from M¨ossbauer spectroscopy are sadly limited, but they do
conﬁrm the presence of Fe3C which is thought to contribute to the catalytic activity
either by catalysing the formation of active sites or as a part of it. Keeping the
transmission electron microscope images in mind it is clear that the iron particles
play a role in determining the morphology of the carbon phase.
By X-ray powder diﬀraction the results from M¨ossbauer spectroscopy are conﬁrmed.
The α-iron and Fe3C phase is identiﬁed along with a unknown phase only seen in
the samples additional treated with NH3 at 850 ◦C. The α-iron phase disappears
after acid washing of the samples. This could indicate that the α-iron is not covered
by a protective layer of graphite.
To determine the catalytic activity towards oxygen reduction a three electrode setup
with a rotating disk electrode was used. The catalytic activity increased as the iron
content was increased; indicating that iron is doing more than facilitate the forma-
tion of the carbon phase. Iron participation as an active part of the catalyst was
further cooperated by a loss of catalytic activity after acid washing. If the catalytic
activity was provided by a part not connected to iron the acid washing would not
aﬀect the catalytic activity. The samples that showed the highest catalytic activity
were all additionally treated with NH3 at 850 ◦C. This point to nitrogen as an in-
tegral part of providing activity, but the NH3 treated samples all have an unknown
phase. It is not possible to say if addition of nitrogen to the carbon phase or the
change in the iron phase is responsible for the increase in activity. To determine this,
samples subjected to a secondary heat treatment without NH3 have to be prepared.
The prevailing paradigm is that the activity is provided by the nitrogen incorporated
in the carbon structure. However, the catalysts synthesised and presented in this
47

Jakob Sværke
2016
thesis indicate that the iron, even though isolated from the reactants, is providing
catalytic activity towards oxygen reduction.
