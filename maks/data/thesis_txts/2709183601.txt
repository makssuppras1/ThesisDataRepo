Abstract
With the increasing market demand for industrially relevant proteins, the need to develop pro-
duction platforms that can produce heterologous proteins in sufficiently high titers has grown.
Whilst the major conventional production platforms, such as Aspergillus niger, Aspergillus oryzae
and Trichoderma reesei have garnered more attention as hosts in the development of heterologous
protein production platforms, the model organism Aspergillus nidulans is a promising candidate.
However, current progress in developing heterologous protein production platforms has been ham-
pered by bottlenecks identified in the secretory pathway that significantly limit the secretion of
heterologous proteins. This thesis aims to explore different strategies for improving the production
of the heterologous Aspergillus aculeatus endoglucanase II in the multi-protease deficient A. nidu-
lans strain NID2807 by targeting three genes for deletion with functions in hyphae morphology
and twelve genes with functions in the secretory pathway. The double and triple deletion mutant
combinations of the morphology genes were also included in the assessment. In addition, from
the secretory pathway four gene targets were chosen for deletion and eight gene targets were cho-
sen for over-expression. Of the nineteen planned mutant A. nidulans strains only seven strains
were successfully created. These include knockout mutant strains of spaAδ and chsCδ morphology
gene targets, and insertion mutants of rabD, ssoA, synA, cyp7 and the putative heat-shock protein
AN1047.
Resumé
Med den voksende markeds behov for industrielle relevant proteiner har den eftertraktede ud-
vikling af produktions platforme der kan producere heterolog proteiner med tilstrækkeligt høje
vokset. Mens de mest benyttede konventionelle produktions platforme, så som Aspergillus niger,
Aspergillus oryzae og Trichoderma reesei har fået mere opmærksomhed som værtsorganismer i
udviklingen af heterolog protein produktions platformer, så er model organismen Aspergillus nidu-
lans fortsat en lovende kandidat. Men fremskridt i udviklingen af en heterolog protein produktion
platform er udfordred af flaskehals punkter der er blevet identificeret i det sekretorisk system, som
har ført til signifikant begrænsninger af de titre af heterolog proteiner der kan opnåes. Formålet
med denne speciale er, at undersøge forskellige strategier der kan forbedre A. nidulans stammen
NID2807, en stamme med en række protease gen deletioner, evne til at producere det heterolog
protein endoglucanase II taget fra Aspergillus aculeatus. Dette var gjort ved at slette tre udvalgte
morfologiske gener og fire udvalgte sekretoriske gener. Derudover var otte gener med funktioner i
det sekretoriske system udvalgt for overudtrykelse og alle dobbelte og tre-dobbelte slette morfolo-
giske gener også inkluderet i undersøgelsen. Af de i alt nitten planlagte mutantstammer var kun
syv stammer succesfuld, som inkludere enkelte deletions mutant stammer af de to morfologiske
gener spaAδ og chsCδ, or insertion mutanterne til de følgende gener: rabD, ssoA, synA, cyp7 og
den putative heat-shock protein AN1047.

Author’s remarks
The author of this project was given a six week extension. The six week extension was granted to
the author by DTU’s Board of Exemption on the grounds that: the necessary pandemic guidelines
enforced by the DTU administration to ensure the continued safety of staff and students on campus
caused unexpected delays; a crucial piece of equipment in the laboratories in building 223 on Lyn-
gby campus was non-operational through-out much of the projects duration; and files crucial to the
projects development were inaccessible for an extended period of the project duration.
The appendix of this Master’s thesis contains all the images and tables that are crucial for the pur-
poses of understanding the work presented in the main body of work for this projects. Any files with
raw data are stored on online drives and are linked to in the appendix.
All figures presented in this Master’s thesis, excluding figures of genetic sequences, were created by
the author through the use of the open-source vector graphics editor software Inkscape. All figure of
genetic sequences presented in this Master’s thesis were generated from CLC Main Workbench 21, a
comprehensive analysis package for advanced DNA, RNA, and protein analyses.

1
Introduction
Historically fungi have played an important role in food production and processing in many cultures
spanning the globe. The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae has long been used in producing leavened
bread and brewing fermented beverages; and Aspergillus oryzae and Aspergillus sojae are used in the
production of koji and soy sauce, just to name a few [1]. By the early twentieth century James Curries
discovered that cultures of Aspergillus niger is exceptionally good at inexpensively producing citric
acid, when cultivated on a defined medium [2]. Currie’s discovery sparked the field of scientific discov-
ery that is biotechnology that has seen enormous development in the decades that followed. In 2008
it was estimated that the global sale volume of white biotechnology products totalled €110 billion and
saw a more than 5-fold increase, when the market was valued at €580 billion in 2020 [3].
This market growth is only expected to continue to grow in the next decade, and with it the demand
for cost-effective production platforms that can produce industrially relevant heterologous proteins
[2]. To date, a broad range of production platforms are utilised in the industrial production of het-
erologous proteins. These production platforms belong to broader classifications, such as bacteria,
yeasts, filamentous fungi, insect and mammalian cells, and transgenic plants and animals [2]; each of
which are better suited to producing certain proteins. For instance, the utilisation of bacteria, such
as Escherichia coli, as a production platform is well suited to the production of small, less complex
proteins that do not require past-translational modifications, because it is a simple, well-understood
system with a high growth rate and can achieve high product titers at low production costs [2]. In
contrast, the utilisation of yeasts, such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae, is better suited to the produc-
tion of larger and more complex proteins that require additional post-translational steps. However,
the hypermannosylation of glycoproteins from yeast systems has been shown to impact the product
quality by shortening the half-life of the protein in vivo and potentially cause immunogenic reactions.
In comparison to yeasts, filamentous fungi have been shown hyperglycosylate glycoproteins to a lesser
extent [2]. This is advantageoues for certain products with important post-translational steps.
Currently the most widely used filamentous fungal organisms that are used in the industrial synthesis
of proteins are Trichoderma reesei and Aspergillus spp. [4, 5], but further advancement towards im-
proving heterologous production systems has encountered widely identified bottlenecks in the secretory
system.
1.1
Aim
The aim of this Master’s thesis is to assess different strategies with the intention of improving As-
pergillus nidulans as a possible alternative production platform for heterologous proteins. This the-
sis uses the reference strain A. nidulans NID2807, developed in house at the DTU Department of
Biotechnology and Medicine. This reference strain is designed to be arginine auxotrophic with multi-
ple protease deficiencies, multiple chromosome integration sites, and be deficient for non-homologous
end joining DNA repair.
The scope of this thesis focuses on two overarching strategies to improve protein production and se-

cretion in A. nidulans. The first approach builds upon the work of the 2020 DTU Biobuilders iGEM
team in their project ’RESHAPE’. In their work they identified three genetic targets with homologoues
in A. niger that individually or in combination altered the morphology to stimulate hyperbranching
and remodel hyphal growth to increase protein secretion. It is expected that the deletion of the homo-
logues of these gene targets in A. nidulans could produce similar results. In addition to individual gene

In the second approach, genes with predicted functions in the secretory pathway with homologues

The A. nidulans mutant strains produced from both approaches will be assessed by cultivating the

well as examining composition of protein molecular weights in samples and identifying the heterologous
A. aculeatus endoglucanase II expressed from both insertion sites IS5 and IS37.
2
Background
2.1
Aspergillus
The Aspergillus genus are a group of ubiquitous soil-borne fungi that are taxonomically classified as
Ascomycetes [5]. Aspergillus proliferate through the extension of septate vegetative mycelium in a
radial growth out from single germinating spores. Although, hundreds of Aspergillus spp. have been
documented and are known to reproduce asexually by the development of conidia to release spores,
a smaller portion have been documented as teleomorph and be able to reproduce through a sexual
phase. As an example, Aspergillus niger does not have a sexual phase and as such can only reproduce
asexually, where as A. nidulans is a teleomorph and is also referred to as Emericella nidulans.
Like many filamentous fungi Aspergillus are saprotrophs that feed on a plethora of substrates found
in the natural world by secreting enzymes to catalytically decompose their surroundings [6]. The
prolific array of enzymes that they produce include glucoamylases, lipases, cellulases, xylanases, glucose
oxidase, and catalase, just to name a few [7, 8, 9].
The application of these hydrolytic enzymes
span everything from industrial waste water treatment with laccases and hydrolysis of lignocellulosic
biomass with endoxylanases to the production of probiotic xylooligosaccharides by xylanases [8]. For
these reasons, Aspergillus and other filamentous fungi have a tremendous value in the production of
industrial enzymes, as they can produce high titers of native hydrolytic enzymes [6]. There are also
notable advantages in using Aspergillus as industrial cell factories in that they can grow under a wide

range of different conditions, such as temperatures of 10-50 °C, pH of 2.0-11.0, osmolarities from pure
water to 34% salt, and on solid substrate with a water activity as low as Aw 0.40 [6].
2.1.1
Aspergillus nidulans
A.nidulans is a model organism that has been widely used in bioengineering research, as its genome
has been fully sequenced and has been well characterized in terms of physiology, morphology and
optimal growth conditions [8]. This makes A. nidulans an ideal candidate for a heterologous protein
production platform, as gene editing tools have been developed for a number of strains. In addition, an
important characteristic of A. nidulans asexual reproduction cycle is that the conidia grown in chains
from conidiophores are uninucleate. The uninucleate conidia renders the process of spore isolation
of homokaryotic strains significantly easier, as the isolation of a single spore under sterile conditions
ensures that the extending hyphae from the germinating spore only replicates from a single nucleus
[10].
In this thesis, the two A. nidulans strains NID2531 and NID2807 were used as controls during trans-
formations and tissue PCR reactions, and their genotypes are listed in Table 1. NID2531 encodes
three mutant genotypes: an argB2 mutant allele for ornithine carbamoyltransferase in the arginine
biosynthesis pathway, according to the KEGG database [11], and renders the strain auxotrophic for
arginine; a veA1 mutant allele that encodes a single G - T transversion in the translation initiation
codon of the veA gene, which causes delayed and reduced fruiting bodies [12]; and the mutant nkuAΔ,
which is an A. nidulans homologue to the human KU70 gene required in non-homologous end joining
DNA repair [13]. The reference strain NID2807 is multi-protease deficient strain developed in house
and has the parent strain NID2531. In addition, the NID2807 strain has several insertion sites across
its eight chromosomes of which insertion sites IS5 (chromosome IV), IS36 (chromosome VIII), and IS37
(chromosome VII). For sites IS5 and IS37 insertion mutations of an Aspergillus aculeatus glycoside
hydrolase family 5 endoclucanse II, ACU23336, is expressed from a tef1 promoter and terminator. All
overexpression mutants in this thesis targeted IS36 for insertion mutations.
2.1.2
The secretory pathway of Aspergillus nidulans
As saprotrophs filamentous fungi have a well-developed pathway for the secretion of native proteins.
The secretory pathway begins following the transport of mature mRNA out in to the cytosol, where the
mRNA sequence is translated by a ribosome and the growing nascent polypeptide is transported in to
the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) through co-translational translocation by either the signal recognition
particle (SRP)-dependent pathway or through post-translational translocation by the SRP indepen-
dent pathway. In the SRP-dependent pathway the, SRPs target the signal sequence (comprising of
an N-terminal region of positively charged residues, a central hydrophobic core region and the C-
terminal region with polar residues) in the elongating nascent polypeptide, and directs the nascent
polypeptide to a peptide translocation complex on the cytosolic face of ER organelle. The peptide
translocation complex translocates the nascent polypeptide into the lumen of the ER. In the alternative
Page 5
==PAGE:16==
2.1
Aspergillus
SRP-independent pathway the nascent polypeptide instead forms a complex with cytosolic chaper-
one heat shock protein Hsp70 along with co-chaperone proteins that direct the nascent polypeptide
to a ER transmembrane Sec complex, where the nascent peptide is translocated into the ER lumen
[4, 14, 15, 16].
In the ER lumen the nascent polypeptide is folded into its final conformation by an array of molecular
chaperone proteins and folding enzymes, and undergoes post-translational modifications in the form of
glycosylation. The chaperone proteins and folding enzymes include heat shock proteins of the Hsp70
family, binding protein (BiP), protein disulfide isomerases (PDI), and calnexins [4, 17, 18]. The most
abundant chaperone protein in the ER is the ATP-dependent chaperone protein BiP, which ensures
that unfolded nascent polypeptides do not aggregate in the ER lumen. As part of the folding process
in the ER lumen PDIs regulate the formation of disulphide bonds in the protein structure. The quality
control process in the ER is a crucial verification step as only properly folded secretion proteins are
transported out of the ER [18].
However, when the ER lumen is overwhelmed by protein over-expression or the accumulation of mis-
folded protein then the unfolded protein response (UPR), ER-associated degradation (ERAD), and
the repression under secretion stress response (RESS) mechanisms are induced to relieve the stress
on the ER [14, 16, 18, 19]. The accumulation of unfolded protein in the ER lumen causes BiP to
dissociate from the ER membrane protein Ire1. This allows the Ire1 membrane protein to oligomerise
thus activating its cytosolic RNase domain. The Ire1 is then able to splice a unconventional intron
from the mRNA precursor of Hac1 to form the induced hacAi mRNA. The translated HacA acts a
transcription factor to a large number of chaperone proteins and folding enzymes in the ER, such as
pdiA, prpA, bipA, clxA, and lhsA [4, 14, 20]. Furthermore, the induction of the UPR also activates
other genes that play a role in the secretion pathway, as well as genes identified in ERAD [20]. Mis-
folded N-glycosylated proteins in the ER lumen are targeted for degradation by the ERAD system by
first being recognised by chaperone proteins, such as calnexins, calreticulums and BiP. Glycoproteins
that are terminally misfolded are then retranslocated by the sec61p translocon complex and targeted
for proteolytic degradation by ubiquitin-conjugating enzymes [20, 21].
Finally, the RESS regulatory mechanism is a unique feedback mechanism that inhibits the expression
of proteins on a transcriptional level to reduce the "stress" applied to the ER through either an over-
whelming burden of protein expression or accumulation of misfolded proteins [4].
Post-translationally glycosylated proteins undergo further modifications of their N-linked oligosaccha-
rides by glycosyltransferases that are resident-specific to Golgi cisternae. The further modifications
of the glycoprotein N-linked oligosacchardies are subject to further quality control systems that di-
rect correctly glycosylated glycoproteins for anterograde transport, retrograde transport, or vacuolar
degradation. In the anterograde direction COPII coated vesicles bud from trans-facing membrane,
whilst in the retrograde direction COPI coated vesicles bud from the cis-facing membrane [22]. The
intracellular transport process of the secretory pathway is a complex system that involves cargo re-
ceptors, Rab GTPases, soluble NSF (N-ethylmaleimide sensitive factor) attachment protein receptor
(SNARE) proteins, and vesicle coat proteins. Proteins that pass the quality control system in the ER
are directed toward cargo receptors bound to the ER membrane for transport to the Golgi apparatus

==PAGE:17==

to undergo further glycosylation and sorting. Transport from through the secretory pathway from the
ER through the Golgi apparatus to the plasma membrane is mediated by both an anterograde and
retrograde network of microtubule networks driven by Rab GTPase motor proteins [23, 24].
Post-translationally glycosylated proteins undergo further modifications of their N-linked oligosac-
charides by glycosyltransferases, which are resident-specific to Golgi cisternae, before reaching the
plasma membrane for exocytosis. The further modifications of the glycoprotein N-linked oligosaccha-
riies are subject to further quality control systems that direct correctly glycosylated glycoproteins for
anterograde transport. Glycoproteins that are recognised by the quality control system as incorrectly
glycosylated are targeted for retrograde transport, or vacuolar degradation. In the anterograde trans-
port COPII coated vesicles bud from trans-facing membrane, whilst in the retrograde transport COPI
coated vesicles bud from the cis-facing membrane [22]. The transport of vesicles between organelle
compartments is a three stage process that begins with the previously mentioned budding of cargo
receptor bound protein in COP protein coated vesicles. Vesicles are then actively transport along
actin filaments and microtubules by Rab GTPase motor proteins. Vesicles are then finally fused with
the organelle membrane, or plasma membrane. The fusion of the vesicle membrane with the organelle
membrane is a three stage process: tethering, docking and fusion. In the tethering stage Rab GTPases
bound to the vesicle membrane form a complex with tethering factors in the organelle membrane, thus
holding the vesicle in near proximity to the organelle membrane. The vesicle can subsequently dock
with the organelle by the formation of a trans-SNARE complex between a single v-SNARE protein
bound to the vesicle membrane and three t-SNARE proteins bound to the organelle membrane. Once
the trans-SNARE complex is formed the two membranes can fuse, thereby releasing the cargo into the
organelle [25, 24]. As an additional note, SNARE proteins can be further classified into four groups,
which are Qa-, Qb-, Qc-, and R-SNAREs. These four classification are based on their sequence sim-
ilarity and the amino acid residue in the zero-layer of the binding motif. Typically the t-SNAREs
belong to one of the three Q-classifications, whilst v-SNAREs are classified as R-SNAREs. Due to
the specific interactions between the conserved SNARE motifs of the v- and t-SNARE proteins, the
SNARE proteins are essentially organelle-specific [23, 24].
Only once glycoproteins pass the final quality control system in the trans-Golgi cisternae are the
proteins transported from the Golgi apparatus to the Spitzenkörper for exocytosis at the hyphal tip.
2.2
Bottlenecks in the secretory pathway
The secretory pathway is a complex system that involves many processes in different compartments
that spans the ER and the Golgi apparatus, and whilst filamentous fungi have an incredibly well
adapted system to express and secrete native protein the expression of heterologous proteins have
produced a reduced yield.
The current challenge in heterologous production is widely attributed to proper folding in the ER,
as only proteins that pass the quality control system of chaperone proteins are transported out of
the ER. This has been addressed in a number of studies where genes with a function in the UPR
have been over-expressed by introducing additional gene copies or by expressing targeted genes from
Page 7
==PAGE:18==
2.2
Bottlenecks in the secretory pathway
a constitutive promoter [14, 4, 20]; for example, in a study by Valkonen et al. the UPR transcription
factor hacA was constitutively expressed from the A. niger glaA promoter and terminator in its active
form to investigate the production of the Trametes versicolor laccase in Aspergillus niger var. awamori.
They found that under constituitive expression of hacA to induce the UPR that there was a seven-fold
increase in laccase [26].
Similarly the over-expression of the bip gene, has likewise shown improved results in heterologous
protein production, as the greater abundance of BiP in the ER ensures that the ER can process a
greater volume of nascent polypeptide before a stress response, such as the UPR or ERAD is induced
[4]. In itself activating the UPR is a resource demanding response that further burdens the ER to
facilitate protein folding, however, because the UPR is closely linked to the ERAD pathway misfolded
proteins are also being targeted for degradation.
A more reasonable strategy would be to target
individual components of the UPR in an effort to stave off activating either stress response.
Beyond the bottleneck attributed to protein folding in the ER, the post-translational modification of
glycosylated proteins in the Golgi apparatus have been considered as subsequent bottlenecks in the
secretory pathway [27]. Even though, there is currently a scarcity of studies investigating the precise
role of glycosylation on heterologous protein secretion it has been shown that glycosylation has an
effect on protein stability and activity. It has also been noted that the glycosylation of N-link glycans
differs not only between fungal species, but can differ between fungal strains and can furthermore
differ under different cultivation conditions [27]. Whilst there is enough potential to warrant further
study to fully characterise the effect of glysolation on heterologous protein secretion and activity, with
the current available scientific literature it remains largely speculative and is beyond the scope of this
thesis [14].
As a final note, a common bottleneck in achieving high titers in heterologous protein production in
filamentous fungi is the protein degradation bottleneck. In their capacity as saprotrophs filamentous
fungi exocytose an array of hydrolytic enzymes, of which proteases are a significant detriment in
producing high yield. This is a primary bottleneck that other studies have addressed by developing
protease deficient production strains, as well as this thesis in which the A. nidulans strain NID2807
is used as the parent strain for all mutant strains [19, 18].
2.2.1
Gene targets of the secretory pathway
There are numerous targets that could potentially improve the secretory ability of the model organism
A. nidulans, such as over-expressing Rab GTPase motor proteins in the anterograde vesicular traffick-
ing; Over-expressing or deleting cargo receptors with a function in trafficking proteins in respectively
the anterograde or retrograde; over-expressing SNARE proteins; overexpressing chaperone proteins or
foldases involved in the UPR; or deleting chaperone proteins involved in the quality control system of
ERAD. In this thesis twelve genes with different functions were chosen from the secretory pathway.
The twelve genes were selected with the criteria that the resulting mutant strain would potentially
improve the titer of the Aspergillus aculeatus endoglucanase II titer, and the mutation does not disrupt
the degradation pathway of terminally misfolded proteins.

The genes chosen in this thesis with a function in the secretory pathway are: AN6307 (Aovip36 [28]),
AN7302 (Aoemp47 [28]), AN4281 (rabO [23]), AN6974 (rabD [23]), AN8880 (vpsT [29]), AN12492 [30],
AN3416 (ssoA [31, 32, 33]), AN8769 (synA [31]), AN0170 (trxA [34]), AN4583 (cyp7 [30]), AN3592
(clxA [35, 16]), and AN1047 [30]. The gene accession numbers to the A. nidulans homologues were
found on FungiDB [36].
The genes AN6307 and AN7302 are A. nidulans putatitve homologues of respctively the A. oryzae
putative cargo recptors Aovip36 and Aoemp47. The localisation of these two cargo proteins and the
effect of their deletion on the secretion of an α-amylase-fused bovine prochymosin (amyB-proCHY) was
investigated by Hoang et al., in which they found that the cargo receptor Aovip36 was predominantly
localised to the Golgi, whilst Aoemp47 appeared to cycle between the ER and Golgi [28]. Furthermore,
Hoang et al. determined that the two cargo receptors appeared to retain the carrier-fused heterologous
protein, as evident from the comparably reduced concentration of carrier-fused heterologous protein in
an immunoblotting analysis of enriched ER fractions of mycelia expressing amyB-proCHY from indi-
vidual deletion mutants [28]. The tentative results of this study are an indication that cargo receptors
may have a more significant role in the quality control system. As a note, it is uncertain whether the
homologues, AN6307 and AN7302, in A. nidulans will in anyway interact with the expressed heterol-
ogous endoglucanase II in the strain NID2807, as cargo receptor are typically very specific in terms of
the ligands that they recognise.
The genes AN4281 and AN6974 are a pair of Rab GTPases, respectively rabO and rabD, that have
been chosen for over-expression in A. nidulans strain NID2807. The gene rabO is an essential putatitve
GTPase with a role in regulating the vesicle traffic in the ER and early Golgi and between cis- and
medial-cisternae [23, 36]. Little is known regarding the gene rabD, except that it is localised to the
plasma membrane and has a role in coordinating exocytosis at the fungal tip [23]. This pair of Rab
GTPases were selected from the broad range of Rab GTPases that have been identified and localised
in the fungal Rabome, because they had essential roles in anterograde vesicular trafficking through
the secretory pathway. It could be speculated that over-expressing both of these Rab GTPases will
establish a bias for anterograde traffic of vesicles through the secretory pathway. The gene AN8880
(vpsT) is a putative vacuolar sorting protein that has been chosen for deletion. vpsT is a homologue
to the A. oryzae gene Aovps10 the localisation of which was investigated by Yoon et al. and was
found to localise to vacuoles by fluorescence microscopy. Furthermore, Yoon et al. created amyB-
proCHY expressing A. oryzae strains deficient in Aovps10 and determined based on the increased
yield of amyB-proCHY that Aovps10 likely has a role in vacuolar sorting protein between late-Golgi
and pre-vacuolar compartments [29]. vpsT was selected for its role in sorting proteins in the late-Golgi
for degradation.
The genes AN3416 and AN8769 are a pair of putatitve syntaxin SNARE proteins respectively referred
to as ssoA and synA. The two aforementioned genes are homologous to the A. oryzae SNARE proteins
respectively sso1 and snc1. In a systematic analysis by Kuratsu et al. in the localisation of SNARE
proteins in A. oryzae they determined by fluorescence microscopy that the pair of aforementioned
SNARE proteins were localised to the plasma membrane and septum [25]. The pair of putative syn-
taxin genes were chosen for their localisations and their role in mediating membrane fusion between

The gene AN0170, otherwise referred to as trxA, encodes a thioredoxin that has a function in cell

The gene AN4583, also referred to as cyp7, is a putative peptidyl-prolyl cis-trans isomerase. cyp7 cat-

chosen based on the results of a "comparative RNA-seq based transcriptomic analysis of recombinant

The gene AN3592, also referred to as clxA, is a putative calnexin chaperone protein localised in the
ER membrane. This gene was chosen for its essential function as a chaperone protein in the ER, and
furthermore as a result of a study by Conesa et al. in which they found that the over-expression of clxA
increased the extracellular concentration of the Phanerochaete chrysosporium manganese peroxidase
in A. niger by four to five-fold [16]. However, it is worth noting that clxA has a role in the quality
control process within the ER and targeting of terminally misfolded proteins to the ERAD pathway.
This could be advantageous as over-expression of clxA will facilitate protein folding and potentially,
due to its abundance, more rapidly target terminally misfolded proteins for degradation.
For the final pair of gene targets AN12492 and AN1047 very little is known about them except that
they each showed an increased transcript level in the comparative RNA-seq based transcriptomic anal-
ysis performed by Calzado et al. [30]. In addition, according to the fungiDB entries for the two genes,
AN12492 is predicted to have domains with GTP binding, and GTPase activity, whilst AN1047 is a
putative heat shock protein that is localised to the cytoplasm and the nucleus. Despite the scarcity of
information detailing the function of the encoding genes or the absence of attention in the scientific
literature, these genes were chosen with the intention exploring the potential affects from still poorly
understood gene encoding sequences.
2.3
Morphology of filamentous fungi
Filamentous begin their vegetative state from germinating spores by the formation of a polarisome
and Spitzenkörper in a germ tube, which determines the apex of the growing hypha [10, 37]. The
hyphae are long tubular structures that extend exclusively from the hyphal tip and sustains continuous
polarised growth by orienting the Spitzenkörper to determine the direction of the hyphal tip extension
[38]. The Spitzenkörper is a cluster of apical vesicles and microvesicles and has a critical function in
the exocytotic apparatus in the hyphal tip to secrete a deluge of hydrolytic enzymes to decompose
its surroundings. A crucial part of the growing hyphal tip is maintaining the turgour of the hypha
as it extends into a solid substrate further decomposing its surroundings and feeding on the nutrients
degraded by the digestive enzymes. However, the hyphae must maintain the turgor, which has been

Morphology of filamentous fungi
measured to a few atmospheres in hydrostatic pressure, by constantly regulating the influx of water
osmotically drawn in to the cell by osmoresponses that regulate the concentration of solutes inside the
cell [10, 39]. In the natural environment the water activity of the surrounding substrate can be subject
to extreme fluctuations that poses a constant risk for cells to rupture due to the osmotic pressure,
which is why the osmoresponses and integrity of the cell wall is critical for growth of the hyphal tip
[39].
The structure of filamentous fungi is characterised by a plasma membrane, encapsulating the cytosol
and organelles of the cell, and a porous macromolecular composite assemblage that maintains the
tubular cell morphology and turgor within the cell. The structure of the fungal cell wall is composed
of a layer of stress-bearing hydrogen bonded chitin chains arranged in an antiparallel array to form
microfibrils. In addition, a highly branched network of β-(1-3) glucan chains that are cross-linked to
β-(1-6) glucan chains create an elastic polymer network encompassing mature hyphae [10].
2.3.1
The role of morphology in protein secretion
It has been widely reported that exocytosis of proteins by filamentous fungi is primarily localised to
the hyphal tip [9, 10], though as a note Hayakawa et al. have demonstrated by fluorescence recovery
after pho- tobleaching (FRAP) analysis of an enhanced green flourescent protein (EGFP) fused with
amylase-α in oryzae that vesicular trafficking of proteins by a microtubule-dependant process does
occur [40].
In an effort to improve the secretory ability of filamentous fungi assessing the effects of mutations
in genes identified as having morphological function has attracted a lot of attention. These genetic
targets include genes with a reported function in: chitin synthase, such as chsA, chsB, chsC in A.
nidulans, chsC in A. niger, and chsC and csmA in A. oryzae [41, 42, 43, 44]; and hyphae polarisation
maintenance and regulation, such as spaA and AN1158 the A. nidulans homologue to the Neurospora
crassa RNA binding protein gul-1 [44, 45, 46].
In this thesis, A. nidulans homologues of the three deletion gene targets gul-1, spaA, and chsC inves-
tigated by the DTU Biobuilders 2020 team in the organism A. niger were chosen to investigate the
effect of morphology on secretion. The A. nidulans homologoues to the three aforementioned genes
had the following gene accession codes on FungiDB AN1158 (gul-1), AN3815 (spaA) and AN4566
(chsC) [36].
The AN1158 A. nidulans homologue to the N. crassa gul-1 is an RNA binding protein (RBP) that
plays a role in polar hyphal growth and cell wall remodelling [45]. In N. crassa Herold et al. demon-
strated by means of RNA immunoprecipitation (RIP) and RIP-RNAseq analysis that gul-1 interacted
with more than 2,500 RNA transcripts indicating that the RBP was involved in a diverse array of
signalling pathways associated with fungal development and spore formation in response to nutrient
availability, such as interacting with the mitogen activated protein kinase (MAPK) cascades [45, 37].
The morphological and growth characterisations performed by the DTU Biobuilders 2020 team showed
that gul-1δ deficient A. niger strains had increased branching frequency and increased specific activity
of glucoamylase compared to a non deficient A. niger ATCC1015 [44].

The spaA gene was localised to the Spitzenköper in the hyphal tip by differential interference contrast
imaging of SpaA-GFP fusions in A. nidulans, and deduced by Virag et al to be a component of the
Spitzenkørper and function solely at the hyphal tip. Furthermore, Virag et al. found that strains
deficient in spaA showed an increased branching frequency, which they speculate indicates that spaA
has a role in stabilising the hyphal polarity axes [46].
The purpose of targeting genes that increase the branching frequency is to potentially increase the
concentration of protein secreted from an increased apical surface area [16]. However, as Booking et
al. states the supposition assumes that "the total area of extending cell wall is also increased when
the number of tips per unit biomass is increased" which is not a certainty [9]. The chsC gene is a
class I chitin synthase that plays a significant role in the synthesis of the chitin microfibrills in the
cell wall and conidiophore development [42]. It has been reported that A. nidulans strains deficient in
chsC do not show any significant phenotype abnormalities, and has been speculated by both Fujiwara
et al. and Ichinomiya et al. that redundancy in the family of chitin synthase genes ensures that the
synthesis of chitin is not impaired by a loss of function mutation [42, 41]. This was proven through
double deletion mutants chsB chsC that showed severely morphology abnormailties in terms of lower
cell densities and lower hyphal density. The reason for selecting chsC as a gene target in this thesis
is to investigate, whether weakening of the cell wall integrity by possibly increasing the porosity will
improve the Aspergilus nidulans capacity to exocytose proteins from the cell.
2.4
USER fusion cloning
The USER fusion cloning technique is a precise and seamless technique that allows for the rapid fusion
and cloning of multiple PCR products in a bacterial host organism [47]. The technique developed in
2007 by Geu-Flores et al. improved upon the uracil-specific excision reagent (USER) cloning technique
developed by Bitnaite et al. [48]. The USER cloning technique is a ligase-free directional cloning
technique that uses PCR amplifications to manipulate the DNA and introduce mutations of interest
into a target sequences. The technique requires that a Taq DNA polymerase or a proof-reading DNA
polymerase, such as Pfu X7, is used during PCR amplification.
The principle of the technique involves the design of primers that consist of a down-stream priming
sequence and an up-stream non-priming sequence, and in which the two sequences are separated by a
uracil residue. The non-priming sequence is otherwise referred to as a USER-tail and is the sequence
encoding a desired mutation, which can include: site-specific mutagenesis, sequence insertion, sequence
deletion or sequence assembly [48]. This technique requires a cloning vector with a cassette flanked
by two inversely oriented nicking endonuclease Nt.BbvCI sites. The linearization of the cloning vector
by cleavage of the cassette and nicking of the flanking Nt.BbvCI restriction sites extends the 3’ DNA
overhangs sequences that can complement USER-tails of the amplified fragments. By transforming
chemically competent E. coli strains with the DNA fragments and USER enzyme the host internal
DNA repair mechanism can excise uracil residues to form the necessary overhangs, to assemble and
ligate fragments [48].
The USER fusion cloning technique takes this a step further by allowing for the assembly of an

sequences of USER tails connecting DNA fragments are designed to create overhangs of 8 nucleotides,
but Geu-Flores et al. notes that longer overhangs have been reported to work efficiently [47].

2.5
The CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing system
The CRISPR-Cas9 system is a powerful and versatile genome editing tool that consists of two com-
ponents: a single-guide RNA (sgRNA) with clustered regularly interspersed short palindromic se-
quences (CRISPR), and a Streptococcus pyrogenes Cas9 endonuclease [49]. The function of the native
CRISPR-Cas system in prokaryotes was experimentally proven in 2007 as an acquired immune system
that enables bacterial and archaeal organisms to capture foreign viral and phage DNA, and integrate
the sequences as sgRNA in CRISPR [50]. The trans-activating CRISPR RNA can then guide the Cas
endonuclease to the target sequence to induce a double stranded break, as illustrated in Figure 2.

2.6
Bradford protein assay
The Bradford assay is a colourimetric protein assay that measures the differential colour change in a
protein solution mixed with the dye Coomassie Brilliant Blue. The dye binds non-covalently to the
protein forming a protein-dye complex, and in so doing stabilises the negative charge of the anionic
molecule resulting in a blue colour change. The colour differential of protein solutions is calculated by
Page 16
==PAGE:27==
2.7
SDS-PAGE
the absorbance of 595 nm light passing through the solution [52]. It is standard procedure to create
a standard curve using a protein concentration standard, such as bovine serum albumin (BSA).
2.7
SDS-PAGE
Sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis is a technique for separating denatured and
charged molecules through a gel matrix by passing an electric current through the gel matrix. This
technique is used in the determination of the composition and homogeneity of protein samples and
can furthermore be used as a purification step. Before loading proteins samples, samples are treated
with dithiothreitol (DTT) during an incubation period with sufficiently high temperature to denature
dissolved proteins. The function of DTT is to reduce the disulfide bonds of the protein. Sodium
dodecyl sulfate (SDS) is an anionic detergent that binds to the hydrophobic side-groups of proteins
ensuring that the protein is covered in a negative charge, and unable to reform any secondary or
tertiary structures. Proteins solubilized in SDS have a large negative charge relative to the protein
mass that spans the protein and ensures that proteins migrate towards the anode (positive pole) of
an electric field [53].

incubated at 37 °C for 25-45 minutes, whilst chemically competent E. coli DH5α cells were thawed on

mixing before incubating on ice for 5-15 minutes. The E. coli DH5α cells following incubation were
heat shocked for 45-90 seconds at 42 °C, before repeating the incubation on ice.

Extracellular protein assessment
The mutant strains verified in this study by tissue PCR, as shown in Table 1 were further assessed
for their capacity to secrete extracellular proteins. This assessment of the different strains includes
Bradford assays of broth samples from liquid cultures at various time points, analysis of crude protein
by SDS-PAGE, and analysis of the specific activity of a GH5 endoglucanase II expressed by a tef1
promoter from integration sites IS5 and IS37, as shown in Table 1.
Spore suspensions were prepared by harvesting spores from MM plates with arginine cultivated at 37
°C for 4-6 days. The incubation varied with respect to the growth rate of individual strains. Spores
were harvested by pipetting 400 µL Milli-Q water onto plates and firmly rubbing colonies with a
sterile Drigalski spatula. The spore slurry was transferred to the first eppendorf tube of a 3-step 10
fold dilution series. 5 µL of spore suspension from respectively the x10, x100, and x1,000 dilutions
was sequentially loaded onto Bürker Türk counting chamber and counted under a Motic BA310E
microscope. The Bürker Türk counting chamber was wiped with 70% ethanol between each spore
suspension sample. Cell concentrations were calculated using the following formula:
Cell concentration(cells
mL ) = counted cells × dilution factor × conversion factor
counting area volume
(1)
Where, the counted cells is the average number of cells counted within the area of 4 large squares,
and any cells touching 2 arbitrarily selected edges of the area; the dilution factor corresponds to the
sample spore suspension dilution; the conversion factor is 1,000 from mm-3 to mL-1; and the counting
area volume was 0.004 mm3 per large square.
Spore suspensions with a concentration of 104-105 cells/mL from each strain listed in Table 1 were
prepared for inoculation in shake flasks containing 50 mL MM with supplemented arginine and addi-
tional carbon source supplements as shown in Table 9 under Appendix A. Spore suspensions of the

in the set of cultivations.
All liquid cultures were incubated on shake tables at 150 rpm at 37 °C for 72 hours. Samples of 5

0 hours, 48 hours, and 72 hours of cultivation. Any biomass in the samples were separated from the
broth by centrifuging with an acceleration of 9 and deceleration of 9 at 3,000 g for 10 minutes, and
decanting the supernatant into new collection tubes. All samples were stored at 4 °C.

A 2 mg/mL bovine serum albumin (BSA) protein stock was prepared to generate a standard curve. The

with respectively 0 µg/mL, 25 µg/mL, 50 µg/mL, 100 µg/mL, 250 µg/mL, 500 µg/mL, 750 µg/mL,

concentration series. The reactions were left for 5-40 minutes before using a SpectraMax® iD3 plate
reader to measure the absorbance of the solutions at 595 nm.

The protein sample concentrations determined from the Bradford assay were used in the formulation
of the required volumes to load 10 µg into wells of a polyacrylamide gel. A volume of protein solution
not exceeding 37 µL was mixed with 4x Laemmli sample buffer containing 400 mM DTT for a four
fold dilution of the loading buffer. Likewise, the PAGE-ruler was similarly mixed with loading buffer
containing DTT. All prepared protein solutions, including the PAGE-ruler, were subsequently heated
at 99 °C for 5 minutes.
The samples were loaded into wells of a polyacrylamide gel and run in a Gel electrophoresis BioRad
Mini-PROTEAN® tetra system with TRIS-glycin-SDS buffer for 25 minutes at 80V before increasing
to 100V for the remainder of the run. The gel was extricated from the gel cassette and submerged in
Coomassie Brilliant Blue solution on a shake table at 40 rpm for 1 hour. The gel was subsequently
washed with boiling water thrice by submerging the gel for 10 minutes at 50 rpm. Images of the gel
were taken in the BioRad Molecular Imager® GelDoc™XR+ Imaging system.

CRISPR vector sequencing
All CRISPR vectors, pAC2032-pAC2042, were prepared according to the specifications stipulated by
StarSEQ for sequencing using the C259 primer listed in Table 3. The results of the plasmid sequencing
were provided by StarSEQ and illustrated using the Sanger Sequencing Analysis tool in CLC Main
Workbench 21 to assemble sequences to a reference and are shown in Figures 17 and 18, as well as
Figures 54, 55 and 56 under Appendix F.
The sequencing result from pAC2032 is used as a general example to illustrate the common conflicts
found in all 11 sequencing results, which can be found in the remaining sequencing results in Figures
54, 55 and 56 under Appendix F. The two conflicts of interest in Figure 17 show a frame-shift mutation
caused by the insertion of the nucleotide pair AT immediately down-stream of the Aspergillus fumigatus
U3 terminator sequence. The frame-shift mutation, therefore, should have no affect on the efficiency of
expression of the gene targeting CRISPR substrate nor on the efficiency of the gene targeting CRISPR
substrate itself.
Furthermore, the conflicts that appear with increasing frequency beyond 1 kb of sequence length is
expected. Beyond 1 kb the degradation in signal becomes significant, such any signal beyond should
reasonably be discounted.
The sequencing results received from StarSEQ for pAC2040, as shown in Figure 18, indicate that there
are additional sequencing conflicts to the reference sequence. These conflicts appear at approximately
500 bp and 650 bp from sequencing start in the A. fumigatus U3 Promoter.
These conflicts are
localised in the sgRNA sequence and the down-stream tRNA sequence, respectively. This conflict is a
potential point of concern as the correct folding of the sgRNA is critical for the editing efficiency of the
Cas9 enzyme. The conflict appears at the 19th nucleotide in the sgRNA, where a Guanine nucleotide
is replaced with a thymine nucleotide, which is within the secondary structure of the core hairpin loop
[54]. This conflict may explain any deficiency in gene editing efficiency found in the CRISPR vector
pAC2040 targeting an7302.
The second conflict of interest is less significant, as it is appears in the down-stream tRNA sequence.
Whilst the exact extent to which the secondary structure of the tRNA is affected, it is possible that
the point mutation does not affect the extent to which the 5’ and 3’ splice sites can be accessed by
the A. nidulans RNase splicing system.

5.1
Verifying DNA fragments
The DNA fragments created in the study, as listed in Table 4 and the purified fragments were verified
by gel electrophoresis, as shown in Figures 6, 7, 8 and 9. The empirical approximations of the DNA
bands for each respective fragment confirm that the migration distance of each band is approximately
equivalent to the expected fragment lengths shown in Table 4.
A point worth mentioning is that errors during the elongation phase of PCR remain a possibility.
However, the DNA polymerase used in all of these reactions was the high-fidelity Pyrocuccus furiosus
(Pfu) X7 polymerase with a reported error rate in PCR of 1.3×106 mutations/bp/duplication. This
low error rate is attributed to the 3’-5’ exonuclease that it possesses, which functions as a proof-reading
Page 54
==PAGE:65==
5.2

step for mis-incorporated nucleotides [56]. With this reported error rate it is highly unlikely that DNA
fragments with point mutations arise during PCR.
Furthermore, all of the DNA fragments used in the USER cloning step for the CRISPR vectors
pAC2032-pAC2042 were verified by sequencing with the C259 primer in Table 3. The results of which
are shown in Figures 17 and 18, as well as Figures 54, 55 and 56 under Appendix F. These results
confirm that DNA fragments are of the correct length, with the exception of pAC2040 as shown in 18.
The DNA fragments of the eight genes encoding secretory genes intended for integration by homologous
recombination in insertion site IS36 constitutive expression, and expression by a tef1 promoter and
terminator were not similarly verified.
5.2
Verifying USER fusion clones: CRISPR vectors and GE-oligo plasmids
The CRISPR vectors and plasmid GE-oligos presented in this report, namely pAC2032-pAC2050, that
are presented in Table 5 were cultivated with both positive and negative controls. Cloning attempts
were repeated if either the positive or negative control failed to produce an expected result of DH5α
CFUs, the negative control not exceeding 5 CFUs.
During the formulation of all USER fusion cloning mixtures, volumes of individual DNA fragments
were added according to an empirically approximated concentration determined by the intensity of
DNA fragment bands from analytical gels. It was not possible to qualitatively determine DNA frag-
ment concentrations, as the final volumes of purified DNA fragment collected from the Zymoclean™gel
DNA recovery kit was limited to < 16 µL. Due to the inherent inaccuracies in empirically estimating
concentrations of purified DNA fragments according to DNA band intensities from gel electrophoresis
results it was not possible to formulate USER fusion mixtures with a standardised concentration of
fragments.
The inaccuracies associated with the estimated DNA fragment concentrations were further com-
pounded by the fact that the lengths of individual DNA fragments were not taken into consideration.
The SYBR Safe DNA gel stain binds to the DNA and fluoresces under UV light, however, it is the
quantity of DNA that determines the band intensity and not the concentration of DNA fragment of
any given length. Hence, if two DNA fragments of differing lengths show the same band intensity
then the shorter DNA fragment will have a greater concentration. Both of these aforementioned in-
accuracies are important, but are of greater relevance to the construction of the plasmid GE-oligos
pAC2043-pAC2050, which require DNA fragments of genes with widely varying lengths between 672-
2,436 bp, see Table 4.
All of the plasmids in this report, as seen in Table 5, were verified by restriction site analysis with the
restriction endonuclease BspEI. Whilst at least one purified sample of each CRISPR vectors pAC2032-
pAC2042 were successfully verified by restriction site analysis there were several discrepancies in the
observed DNA fragment bands for the GE-oigo plasmids, pAC2043-pAC2050. The most notable dis-
crepancy across all replicates of pAC2043-pAC2050 treated with BspEI was the deviation in band
length from the relative DNA fragment size. DNA fragments less than 2 kb in length were far more
accurate with respect to their expected fragment size than larger DNA fragments.

5.3
Verifying mutant A. nidulans strains
The multiple protease deficient NID2807, developed in house, was transformed with GE-oligos tar-
geting and the verified CRISPR vectors pAC2032-pAC2042 with protospacer-sgRNAs targeting seven
A. nidulans genes for deletion. Included in these eleven PEG-mediated fungal transformations were
four multiplexing CRISPR vectors, pAC2035-pAC2038, designed to target the genes AN1158, spaA
and chsC in all double and triple combinations. The solid-state cultures on osmotically stabilised TM
plates with selective pressure for transformations with all multiplexing CRISPR vectors, pAC2035-
pAC2038, produced very few colonies, as shown in Table 23 under Appendix G, none of which could be
verified as succesful multiplexed mutant strains. This failure to create multiplexed knockout mutants
of genes encoding sequences with function related to hyphae morphology can in-part be attributed to
a poor gene targeting efficiencies for both the AN1158 and spaA protospacer sequence, which were
calculated to be less than 15%; A more precise gene-targeting efficiency could not be calculated from
plates with a quantity of CFUs greater than 50, as distinct CFUs became indiscernible.
Transformations with the GE-oligos and pAC2039-pAC2042 CRISPR vectors targeting genes encoding
sequences with functions in the secretory pathway were similarly unsuccessful. However, the avail-
able sample set of CFUs from each of the four transformation plates on osmotically stable TM agar
plates was between 1-3 CFUs, with a similarly small samples set from the TAPE controls. Whilst
the low quantity of CFUs found on the TAPE controls is a desirable outcome, indicating that the
gene targeting efficiency of the designed protospacers could be within acceptable limits, the likewise
comparatively low CFUs on the transformation plates with mutant strains renders the gene targeting
efficiency unreliable. Furthermore, an average, standard deviation, and the statistical significance of
the TAPE calculations can not be determined from an unreplicated experimental design [51].
Based on the low number of CFUs found on both the TM plates and the TAPE controls for the four
secretory genes (AN6307, AN7302, AN8880 and AN12492), as seen in 24 under Appendix G, the
functional role of the genes in the secretory pathway should be further assessed in terms of whether
the deletion of these genes in A. nidulans are detrimental to the cell.
The genes AN6307 and AN7302 are putitative lectin-type cargo receptors that were localised to re-
spectively the golgi compartment and the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) by Hoang et al. [28]. The gene
AN6307 is the A. nidulans homologue to the Aovip36 putitative lectin-type cargo receptor found in
A. oryzae, while AN7302 is the A. nidulans homologue to the Aoemp47 putitative lectin-type cargo
receptor found in A. oryzae. Both of these proteins are thought to mediate the trafficking of proteins

through the early stages of the secretory pathway with a quality control function. However, as Hoang
et al. notes, cargo receptors typically recognise a narrow array of oligosaccharide binding profiles on
glycosylated proteins [28]. It is therefore unlikely that the deletion of either of these putitative lectin-
type cargo receptors should be so detrimental to the cell as to render the mutant strain unviable.
The gene AN8880, otherwise referred to as vpsT, is a vacuolar hydrolase sorting protein. Its orthologue,
vps10, in A. oryzae was reported by Yoon et al. to be localised at both the late-golgi compartment
and early endosome vesicles [29]. Yoon et al. found that vps10 deficient strains of A. oryzae did not
show any abnormal growth. Based on the role of vpsT in trafficking proteins from the late-golgi com-
partment to early endosome vesicles it does not seem likely that the deletion of vpsT is an explanation
for the poor transformation efficiency.
Very little is documented on the gene AN12492, except that it is predicted to have a domain with
GTP binding, and GTPase activity, and has been reported in a transcriptome analysis to show an
increased transcriptional response to heterologous protein secretion [30].
For any future transformations using A. nidulans NID2807 as a parent strain to create viable mutant
strains with deletions in the following genes AN1158, AN6307, AN7302, AN8880, AN12492 and the
multiplexed mutant strains of the genes encoding morphology genes, it would be advisable to construct
new CRISPR vectors by USER fusion cloning using alternative protospacer sequences.
The PEG-mediated transformations of A. nidulans strain NID2807 with the multiplexing CRISPR
vector pAC1836, targeting the IS36 insertion site on chromosome VIII, to create insertion mutant
strains that constitutively express genes encoding sequences with functions in the secretory pathway,
were assessed by tissue PCR analysis. It was possible to verify the mutant strains NID2862-NID2863
and NID2865-NID2866 in Figures 21 and 22 with the genotypes presented in Table 1. The tissue
PCR analysis for the strain NID2864 showed a successful integration of GE-oligo through homologous
repair, however the GE-oligo repair sequence was incorrectly integrated. This incorrect integration
was evident by a 62.5% increase in DNA fragment length. In addition, no colonies from transfor-
mations with pAC1836 and either of the GE-oligos containing sequences for AN4281 and AN3592
were successful, and only possessed DNA fragment bands corresponding to the 300 bp gap-checking
sequence.
In contrast to the designed knockout mutants, the failure to produce successful mutant strains by
the integration of tef1 expressed genes into IS36 in NID2807 can not be explained by the gene tar-
geting efficiency of the protospacer sequence used. The eight aforementioned transformants used the
same CRISPR vector, pAC1836, which has a pair of protospacer sequences recognising an overlapping
sequence in IS36 site on the sense and anti-sense DNA strand. Therefore any significant change in
transformation efficiency is not attributed to the cutting efficiency of pAC1836.
The most significant error during the transformation of A. nidulans NID2807 with pAC1836 was us-
ing the non-linearized GE-oligos as homologous repait template. Even though, using circular DNA
template for homologous recombination has been reported to work the strategy is prone to integration
errors by integrating extraneous flanking DNA sequences, as described by Lubertozzi et al. [57]. The
choice to use circular GE-oligo, rather than linearised GE-oligo fragments was made out of necessity
during extenuating circumstances. At the time that the fungal transformations were performed with

5.4
Preliminary protein production assessment
The results collected from the Bradford assay quantifying the crude extracellular protein showed that
the strain NID2531, a strain with a close equivalence to the A. nidulans wildtype, produced 126 µg/mL
extracellular protein, whilst the reference strain NID2807 produced 196 µg/mL extracellular protein
measurable in the medium after 72 hours. With both of these base lines for comparison the three
strains NID2860, NID2865 and NID2866 stood out as the most promising candidates. These results
give a cursory indication that the genes AN4566 (chsC), AN3592 (clxA) and AN1047 may have a
significant effect on heterologous protein secretion.
In the chsC deficient strain NID2860 the 50% increase in the secretion of extracellular protein com-
pared to the parental strain NID2807. This could be attributed to a diminished cell wall integrity in
the hyphae, however, because of functional redundancy between the two genes chsC, a class I chitin
synthase, and chsA, a class II chitin synthase, the deletion of either does not result in observable
growth and morphology defects [42, 41]. In comparison, in a separate project a chsC deficient ATCC
1015 strain of A. niger showed increased branching frequency but diminished growth rate and protein
secretion [44].
The clxA insertion mutant showed a greater than two-fold increase in the secretion of extracellular
protein compared to the parental strain NID2807. This is attributed to the constitutive expression
from tef1 promoter of the protein calnexin, a lectin-like chaperone protein with a major role in protein
quality control in the ER, as a component of the UPR [16, 35, 2]. The over-expression of a compo-
nent of the UPR without inducing the ERAD response is advantageous, and the results are a minor
indication that the over-expression of clxA is a possible strategy for overcoming the widely known
bottleneck in ER for heterologous protein production[58]. The AN1047 insertion mutant, much like
the clxA insertion mutant, showed a greater than two-fold increase in the secretion of extracellular pro-
tein compared to the parental strain NID2807. However, much less is known about the gene AN1047
except that it encodes a putative heat shock protein that is localised to the cytosol, according to its
UniProt entry under the accession code: Q5BEI3 [59]. This gene was selected based on results from a
transcriptome analysis that showed a slight increased transcriptional response to heterologous protein
secretion [30].
The results from the Bradford assay in this report should only be used as a preliminary assessment
as there is no way to verify, whether the results are outliers on the basis of an unreplicated experi-
mental design. It is also important to note, that the broth samples were kept chilled for three days
after the end of cultivation as the SpectraMax® iD3 plate reader was not accessible immediately after
the final samples were taken. This is less significant for all the mutant strains NID2859-NID2866,
as well as the parent strain NID2807. However, this may have had significant implications for the
measured absorbance of all samples taken from the reference strain NID2531, which did not share the
same protease gene deficiencies, and as such the measured absorbance is likely smaller than the actual
absorbance of the sample after 72 hours at 595 nm wave length.
Finally, the results of the SDS-PAGE are of little value to this report, as it was not possible to include
samples from the two reference strains for comparison. The SDS-PAGE instead serves as a preliminary
Page 59
==PAGE:70==
comparison to assess the distribution of molecular weights and band intensities of the crude proteins in
the sample of the most promising candidates from the Bradford assay. The results of the SDS-PAGE
show that there is a degree of homogeneity between the samples, as they share the same three protein
bands of approximately 32 kDa, 43 kDa and 70 kDa.
However, as previously mentioned without the inclusion of samples from both reference strains as
controls with which to compare to it is not possible to draw any conclusions from the SDS-PAGE.
As such, in a future experiment the SDS-PAGE should be repeated and all samples should be up-
concentrated if need be. Furthermore, in the similar event that a Bradford assay and SDS-PAGE can
not be performed shortly after the collection of sample from the final time point of cultivation, then
the samples should be frozen in the interim.
6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to explore different strategies for optimising protein production in filamen-
tous fungi, and more specifically in the model organism A. nidulans. In this report a strategy was
defined in terms of the effect of individual gene mutations, as well as the combination of multiple
mutations, on the production of the A. aculeatus heterologous protein endoglucanase II constitutively
expressed from tef1 promoters and terminators from integration sites IS5 and IS37 on respectively
chromosome IV and VII in A. nidulans NID2807. The genes targeted in A. nidulans in this report
are genes that have been reported in the scientific literature to improve protein production in other
filamentous fungi that are regarded as industrial cell factories, such as A. niger, A. oryzae, A. awamori
and T. reesei. In total 15 genes with orthologues in A. nidulans were chosen as targets to be assessed
in this report; 12 of these genes encoded sequences with functions across the secretory pathway. Of
these 12 secretory genes, the constitutive expression of 8 of these genes were reported to improve
protein production, whilst the deletion of the remaining 4 genes were reported to improve protein
production. The remaining 3 genes encoded sequences with a function in hyphal morphology, the
deletion of which in A. niger by the DTU Biobuilders iGEM team of 2020 in their project RESHAPE
improved protein production.
Whilst this thesis set out to comprehensively assess the effect on the production of a heterologous
endoglucanase II of the mutations in the A. nidulans reference strain NID2807, it was not possible to
successfully complete all stages of the project. This project has, however, successfully constructed and
verified CRISPR vectors for all genes targeted for deletion, pAC2032-pAC2042, including 4 multiplex-
ing CRISPR vectors designed with protospacer-sgRNAs targeting all paired double deletion mutants
and a single triple deletion mutant of the three genes AN1158, spaA, and chsC encoding sequence
with functions in hyphal morphology. Plasmids with GE-oligo sequences for the integration of the 8
genes for over-expression in A. nidulans was also constructed and verified. In addition, seven success-
ful mutant strains of A. nidulans, namely NID2859-NID2863, NID2865 and NID2866 were created.
However, only strains NID2859 and NID2860 were confirmed as homokaryotic strains.
Finally, a preliminary quantification of extracellular protein from a 72-hour liquid cultivations were

performed on the eight mutant A. nidulans strains. The concentration of extracellular protein in
the 72-hour time point samples showed that the strains NID2862, NID2865 and NID2866 merit fur-

statistically significant.

Future perspective
Any future experiments with the intention of completing the creation of knockout and insertion mu-
tants that failed to produce successful mutant A. nidulans strains may include the following recom-
mendations to improve the experimental strategy.
The failure to produce viable knockout mutant colonies in this thesis can be improved by selecting al-
ternative protospacer sequences with a potentially greater gene-targeting efficiency. During the design
stage of the CRISPR vectors used in this report multiple protospacer sequences were identified the 100
bp of DNA sequence flanking each gene region in the A. nidulans region. These protospacer sequences
were designed and assessed for their suitability for USER fusion cloning and two primer pairs were
selected for each each gene. The primer pairs for the secondary protospacer sequences designed for
USER fusion cloning are readily available for use. To improve the transformation stage into the A.
nidulans strain NID2807 a replicated experimental design of the transformation and TAPE control
plates. The purpose of this replicated experimental design is to ensure that a statistical analysis can be
performed to acquire a more accurate gene-targeting efficiency of the protospacer sequences. However,
due to the size of the designed sample set of gene targets a full replicated experimental design may
be impractical, and as such a smaller sample set of gene targets could be selected for the replicated
experimental design. An important recommendation for the A. nidulans transformation sample set of
over-expression gene targets is that the plasmid GE-oligos expressed from a tef1 expression cassette
are linearized with the swaI restriction endonuclease to minimise the probability of an incorrect inte-
gration of the GE-oligo into IS36.
The final stage in verifying that mutant strains could be further improved by adding an additional
to the spore harvesting step prior to spore isolation on a tetrad dissection microscope. By adding an
additional dilution to the creation of the spore suspension the total cell concentration can be further
reduced in the spore suspension samples deposited on the plates designated for spore isolation. The
purpose of lowering the concentration of spores is to reduce the concentration of germinating spores
on the deposited trail of spore suspension, and reducing the overall growth of mycelium during the
subsequent incubation at optimum conditions.
In addition to the previously set out recommendations to the experimental strategy in this report,
future investigations into the characterisation and performance of mutant strains could act as the next
steps. This would include characterising the morphology of mutant A. nidulans strains, as well as
using a confocal microscope and imaging software to map and fully characterise the hyphal tip growth
rate and branching frequency [44, 60].
As a subsequent step, a chromogenic activity assay to quantify the specific activity of the heterologous
Page 61
==PAGE:72==
endoglucanase II in cultivation samples of the mutant and reference strains could be performed. The
proposed experimental strategy would require a dye substrate with β (1,4)-glycosidic links, such as
the Megazyme product Azo-CM-cellulose. The Azo-CM-cellulose substrate is a soluble substrate that
depolymerizes by endo-1,4-β-D-glucanase activity and releases the dye Remazolbrilliant Blue R [61].
Samples of the chromogenic activity assay would be measured in a spectrophotometer at the 595 nm
wavelength.
