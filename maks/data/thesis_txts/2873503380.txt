Abstract
This thesis investigates the evolution of the Fundamental Metallicity Relation (FMR) at
redshift z = 1 −9.5, using a sample of 657 galaxies with uncorrected fluxes, 71 galaxies
corrected for dust via the Hα/Hβ Balmer decrement and 516 galaxies corrected via SED­
fitted dust extinction values (AV ). All galaxies were identified with JWST/NIRSpec and
compiled from seven photometric and spectroscopic programs: CEERS, GOODS (North
and South), PRIMER UDS (North and south) and PRIMER COSMOS (East and West)
covering over 6000 sources. Given significant discrepancies between flux measurements,
a consistent data reduction procedure was established to ensure reliable emission line
fluxes and uncertainties. The primary aim was to assess the evolution of the FMR by
deriving stellar mass, star formation rate (SFR) and gas­phase metallicity both with and
without dust correction. Without dust correction, no evolution in the FMR with redshift was
observed. However, applying the Hα/Hβ Balmer decrement dust correction improved
the Spearman correlation between [OII]­based SFR and stellar mass from ρ = 0.305 to
0.614, and between stellar mass and metallicity from ρ = 0.302 to 0.428. Using AV based
corrections from Eazy also strengthened these correlations by ρ = 0.305 to ρ = 0.480
and ρ = 0.302 to ρ = 0.553 for [OII] stellar mass­SFR and mass­metallicity respectively.
We confirm the existence of the stellar mass­SFR and mass­metallicity relations across
this redshift range. The combined FMR remains stable up to z ∼7, but declines beyond
z > 7. Finally, we find that the offset from FMR correlates with SFR, specific SFR and Hβ
equivalent width, suggesting a change in star formation, inflow and outflow.

Introduction
The study of astronomy and astrophysics is a constantly evolving discipline, driven by
humanity’s endless fascination with the cosmos. For thousands of years, the mystery of
the night sky and the desire to understand the universe and our place in it ­ has inspired
curiosity, discovery and scientific advancement. With the launch of The James Webb
Space Telescope (JWST) in December 2021, the exploration of the high­redshift Universe
has dramatically advanced. This thesis studies the secrets and analysis the fundamental
properties of the early star forming galaxies and their evolution trough cosmic time.
1.1
Cosmology and the Universe
Cosmology is the study of the structure of the Universe, it combines the elements of the­
oretical physics and observations. Most of the Universe is composed of dark energy or
dark matter. Luminous matter otherwise called normal baryonic matter makes up about
four percent of the mass­energy density of the Universe. The most common model de­
scribing this is known as the standard model of cosmology, also known as The Lambda
Cold Dark Matter model (ΛCDM) of cosmology.
1.1.1
ΛCDM Theory
The Lambda Cold Dark Matter model states that the Universe began in a hot, dense state
known as the Big Bang, where the initial expansion of the Universe was driven by a period
of exponential cosmic inflation (e.g. Guth and Weinberg 1981). The ΛCDM is currently the
most widely­accepted and straight­forward description of the Universe. It explains many
of the discovered and observed properties of the Universe, such as the accelerating ex­
pansion of the Universe, the cosmic microwave background radiation (CMBR), the large
scale structure of the Universe and the chemical abundance of elements (e.g. Hawking
1982). This cosmological model consists of three distinct elements: Λ, the cosmological
constant, describing the accelerating expansion of the universe driven by dark energy,
CDM, cold dark matter, and normal baryonic matter. The cosmological constant, Λ, was
first introduced by (Einstein 1917). It was not until the acceleration of the expansion of the
universe was measured by (Riess et al. 1998) by studying type Ia supernovae, that the
cosmological constant had concrete observational evidence. One of the main foci of the
standard model of cosmology is the existence of non­luminous dark matter, which makes
up around 23 percent of the mass­energy density of the universe as opposed to the bary­
onic matter that encompass all visible materials, such as stars, galaxies, even you and I
(Riess et al. 1998).
The first observational proposition of Dark matter was suggested by Fritz Zwicky in the
1930’s, by studying the Coma Cluster he established that there must be significantly more
mass in the cluster than what he observed from the luminous observations alone (Zwicky
1933). This unobservable and missing component was dubbed ’dark matter’, an astro­
physical phenomena too faint to be detected.
This evidence was further reinforced by observation studies by (Rubin and Ford 1970),
measuring an excess of rotation velocity of Hα emitting regions in the Andromeda Galaxy
(M31). The outer parts of the galaxy were rotating much faster than what was expected
based on baryonic mass (visible mass), there was a larger gravitational pull comming from
something that wasn’t visible.
To understand the evolution of galaxies through cosmic time, it is essential to first under­
stand how they formed.

1.2
Galaxy Formation
According to the standard model of cosmology, structures in the universe formed from
small perturbations in an otherwise uniform early universe. The concept of gravitational in­
stability was first proposed by (Jeans 1902), and describes how perturbations grow when
gravity overcomes internal pressure. In (Eggen, Lynden­Bell, and Sandage 1962) they
suggested that the formation of elliptical or spiral galaxies depends on how quickly stars
form during the collapse of gas clouds. They suggested that rapid star formation lead to
pressure­supported elliptical systems, while slower collapse allows gas to dissipate en­
ergy and settle into rotating disk structures. (Larson 1975) later showed that the ratio
of star formation time to cooling time influences galaxy morphology, with feedback from
supernovae playing a major role, particularly in low­mass galaxies, where outflows can
prevent star formation and reduce metallicity. However, these early models lacked one
critical component: dark matter.
By the late 1970s, it became clear that dark matter plays a key role in galaxy formation,
with rotation curves of spiral galaxies indicating the presence of extended dark matter
haloes. Numerical modes suggest that collapsing dark matter forms an isothermal dis­
tribution through violent relaxation in collisionless systems undergoing rapid gravitational
changes, such as mergers (Shu 1978). This process quickly leads to equilibrium, inde­
pendent of initial conditions. As baryonic matter collapses with the halo, it is shock heated
to the virial temperature, far above the ionization threshold of 104K, leaving the gas ion­
ized (White 1996). If the gas can cool efficiently, via bremsstrahlung, line emission and
recombination faster than it collapses, it sinks to the center of the halo (White and Rees
1978). If angular momentum is roughly conserved during this process, a centrifugally
supported disc forms, leading to galaxy formation (Athanassoula 1983).
1.3
Main Sequence
Following the formation of galaxies, their subsequent growth is governed largely by how
efficiently gas is converted into stars. One of the clearest observational signatures of
this growth is the main sequence (MS) of star­forming galaxies (SFGs), which reveals a
tight correlation between stellar mass and star formation rate (SFR). The MS has long
been considered one of the most useful tools in modern astrophysics to uncover galaxy
evolution, as it reveals a tight correlation between stellar mass and SFR. The relation
indicates that SFGs grow in a relatively steady and regulated manner, and deviations from
the MS can signal some key evolutionary processes like starburst activity. By studying the
MS across cosmic time, we can gain insights into how galaxies build up their stellar mass
and how different mechanisms influence the growth. This tight relation between SFR and
stellar mass (M⋆) has been observed from redshift z ∼0 to z ∼6 in (Speagle et al. 2014).
The evolution of the slope and scatter have been largely studied in the past 20 years, and
extended up to higher redshifts with the use of JWST data (e.g. Popesso et al. 2023).
Studies such as (e.g. Speagle et al. 2014) suggest the relation to be a power law shape
in the form of SFR ∝Mα
⋆, in local Universe and at high redshifts. This in log­space will
end up being a linear relation.

1.4
Galaxy Emission Lines
To study the physical processes driving star formation along the Main Sequence, we rely
heavily on the emission lines produced by ionized gas in star forming regions. From the
emission lines emitted from hot ionized gas in HII regions of a galaxy, it is possible
to trace the dynamical motions of the interstellar medium. When atoms recombine, the
electrons cascade down the energy levels, releasing emission at a range of characteristic
wavelengths. From this recombination of atoms, we observe one of the most common and
abundant emission in the spectra of star­forming galaxies, known as the Balmer emission
lines.

1.5
Gas­Phase Metallicity
The gas­phase metallicity is described as the abundance of heavy elements relative to
hydrogen. This is a fundamental property of galaxies that combines the effect of stellar
mass buildup and gas flows that can add or remove mass and metals from the galaxy
medium. With theoretical models of galactic evolution, we can describe how the gas­
phase metallicity of the interstellar medium (ISM), detected by the oxygen abundance
O/H, has a dependency on the star formation rate (SFR) and stellar mass (e.g. Lilly et al.
2013; Torrey et al. 2019). The relative abundance of certain chemical elements can be
measured from their emission line flux, which is a function of the electron temperature,
the density and the abundance of the element. When assuming constant temperature
and density, temperature sensitive auroral lines such as [OIII] λ4363, λλ7320, 7330 , can
be used to measure the metallicity of the gas. This is known as the ’direct method’, this
requires a very high signal to noise emission line ratio, and pre JWST was only available
for very nearby galaxies (Scholte et al. 2025). For galaxies where aurora lines are faint
or have lower signal to noise ratio, the metallicity can be measured using strong line
calibrations, however with a reduced accuracy.

1.6
Fundamental Metallicity Relation
The Stellar mass ­ metallicity relation (MZR) has been observed to correlate with the star
formation rate of the galaxy, giving rise to a fundamental plane known as the Funda­
mental Metallicity Relation (FMR) first observed by (F. Mannucci et al. 2010) later with
a new parametrization in (Curti, Filippo Mannucci, et al. 2020). Observed was that for
lower stellar masses, the metallicity decreased sharply with an increase of SFR, while
at high stellar mass the metallicity did not depend on the SFR. These constraints on the
gas­phase metallicity for star­forming galaxies and their correlation with stellar mass and
evolution, provide a crucial tool to understanding the processes behind galactic evolution.
In recent times this FMR has been studied at high redshifts with spectra from JWST (e.g.
Nakajima, Ouchi, Isobe, et al. 2023; Curti, Mirko et al. 2024; Scholte et al. 2025). The
FMR from (F. Mannucci et al. 2010) is described as a 2D projection of the M­Z­SFR re­
lation on the Z −µα plane. From this Z −µα plane a newer parametrization of the FMR
have been described by (Curti, Filippo Mannucci, et al. 2020) and has the form of:
Z(M, SFR) = Z0 −γ/β log(1 + (M/M0(SFR))−β)

2.3
Photometry fitting
To measure the galaxy properties: Mass, dust extinction value, U,V and J we will fit the
publicly available photometry files on (Dawn JWST Archive (DJA) 2024) using the python
package: Eazy (Gabriel Brammer 2021; G. B. Brammer, van Dokkum, and Coppi 2008).
Eazy is run with a fixed zspec from the table provided to us by (Gottumukkala et al. 2025).
This ensures that eazy will not look for a redshift, but can be used as a tool to measure
physical properties like the stellar mass and to quantify the dust and gas. To run Eazy, we
need to also give the code a translation file as well as a zeropoint correction file, these
are also publicly available using (Dawn JWST Archive (DJA) 2024).
Eazy is a template based SED fitting tool designed primarily for photometric redshift esti­
mation. It can be used in deep optial­NIR broadband surveys. It operates using minimal
set of synthetic templates based on stellar population synthesis (SPS) models. These
templates are constructed from composite stellar populations (CSPs), which themselves
22
Investigating the Evolution of the Fundamental Metallicity Relation

are built from combinations of simple stellar populations (SPSs), incorporating assump­
tions about the star formation history (SFH), metallicity, dust attenuation and age. This
results in a grid of synthetic galaxy SEDs precomputed across a range of physical pa­
rameters. Eazy uses this grid to match observed photometry via χ2 minimization. This
methodology for constructing these templates are explained in detail and visualized by
(Conroy 2013, see Figure 1), which outlines the pipeline from SPS to full SED modeling.
2.4
Star Formation Rate
The Balmer recombination lines originate from star­forming HII regions, where especially
Hα is a robust indicator for star­forming on short (≤20 Myr) timescales (Glazebrook et al.
1999). SFR is an important part of studying galaxies, it has been found to increase with
stellar mass (e.g. Whitaker et al. 2012; Speagle et al. 2014). With the JWST, SFR can
be measured directly from the Balmer recombination lines for galaxies at z > 6, revealing
intense star­forming galaxies (Popesso et al. 2023). Besides the Hα, the SFR can also
be found using Hβ and the doublet [OII]λ3728 emission line (Kennicutt 1998) using the
following equations:
SFRHα(M⊙yr−1) = 7.9 × 10−42LHα(erg s−1)
(2.1)
The emission lines from msaexp are given in flux × 10−20(erg/s/cm2). We therefore have
to derive the emission line luminosity via:
Lline = Fline × 4πD2
L(z)
(2.2)
where D2
L(z) is the luminosity distance at the given redshift. Given that Hα is only de­
tected for a fraction of the sources and may be comtaminated/blended by [NII], we rely
on the [OII]λ3728 and the Hβ emission lines following:
SFR[OII](M⊙yr−1) = 1.0 × 10−41L[OII](erg s−1)
(2.3)
SFRHβ(M⊙yr−1) = 5.5 × 10−42LHβ(erg s−1) × fHα/Hβ
(2.4)
We assume fHα/Hβ = 2.86 from the Case B recombination scenario at Te = 104 K (Os­
terbrock and Ferland 2006).
From the SFR we determine the error by method of bootstrapping. Assuming the fluxes
are gaussian distributed, we draw randomized samples from each flux and their associ­
ated error. For each method of SFR (eq 2.1, 2.3 and 2.4) we utilize the associated Flux
and Flux error of that emission line. From these gaussian distributed values we find the
SFR from the same equations, plotting them in a histogram (Figure 2.8) with the x­axis
covering the log value of the SFR, in this case of the Hβ converted to Hα SFR. Where
the standard deviation is denoted as the SFR error.

One of the fundamental characteristics of galaxies are their metallicity. This is a difficult
task to measure directly, especially for high redshift galaxies, this motivates the use of
strong­line emission diagnostics, using the most prominent nebular emission lines. For
some rare cases, the auroral [OIII]λ4363 emission line can be detected directly, this en­
ables Te sensitive measurements of the gas­phase metallicity (direct method). With the
release of JWST this direct­method metallicity has been detected in star­forming galaxies
at (z > 6) (e.g. Nakajima, Ouchi, Xu, et al. 2022; Sanders, Shapley, Topping, et al. 2024;
Curti, Mirko et al. 2024). To measure the metallicity we use the set of diagnostics from
(Sanders, Shapley, Topping, et al. 2024) for the different line ratios. These diagnostics
are derived for line ratios, and have been derived from galaxies at z = 2 −9 through the
direct Te method. We specifically utilize the strong­line ratios mentioned in equation 1.2
to 1.6, due to limited Hα detections and the risk of contamination from [NII] we therefore
exclude equations 1.7 to 1.9. Following the method described in section 3.6 in (K. E.
Heintz et al. 2024). We start by using the calibration lines to convert the strong­line ratios
to metallicity. For any linear line calibration (O2, O32 and Ne3O2), the convertion between
strong­line to metallicity is straightforward and depending on the quality of the calibration
line (equation that is created), the linear calibration will only convert the strong­line into
metallicity in one point or several points very close to each other. For the case of linear cal­
ibrations we utilize the maximum matched calibration value. For the case of the non­linear
calibrations (O3 and R23) this method is different. These two calibrations are quadratic
24

==PAGE:33==
meaning that the metallicity is double valued. For these two calibrations we utilize the lin­
ear calibrations to determine the value of the metallicity. This is utilized by converting the
determined metallicity back in to an expected log(strong­line) value via the calibrations
from (Sanders, Shapley, Topping, et al. 2024). We then convert the log(strong­line) value
back into linear space and find the difference between this new expected value and the
strong­line value for the double valued calibration (O3 and R23) in linear space. The value
that has the smallest difference in the linear space is to be the metallicity value chosen.
For this method we prioritize the O2 linear calibration line, if in a scenario the O2 or any
of the other linear calibration is not detected, the maximum detected value from O3 and
R23 will be chosen instead. From here, we construct a probability density function based
on the line­flux measurements and their errors for each diagnostic. We then combine the
PDFs from the available calibrations, inversely weighted by their scatter, this is done for
each individual source to determine the gas­phase metallicity through the median and
the 16th and 84th percentiles of the joint PDF, illustrated in Figure 2.9. We derive the
gas­phase metallicity ranging from 12 + log(O/H) = 7.4 to 8.3 as per (Sanders, Shapley,
Topping, et al. 2024).

3.2
Best case dust reddening correction
We have also explored two different methods of dust correction to asses how dust influ­
ences our measurements of SFR and metallicity. In this section we explore the results of
applying dust reddening correcting using the Balmer decrement, specifically the Hα/Hβ
ratio as a tracer of internal reddening.
This method relies on the deviation from the theoretical recombination value (Case B,
Hα/Hβ = 2.86) to derive the extinction values, which are then used to correct the emis­
sion line fluxes.
3.2.1
Balmer Decrement correcting

Discussion
In this thesis, we analyzed a large compilation of JWST/NIRSpec data from (Dawn JWST
Archive (DJA) 2024) to investigate the chemical properties across a broad redshift range
(z ∼1 −9.5). Our sample includes over 500 galaxies drawn from the DJA, supplemented
by additional sources from previously published JWST studies.
4.1
No dust reddening correction
From the result Section 3.1, we display the 657 JWST sources, that have not been dust
reddening corrected. We observe no apparent evolution of the SFR with stellar mass
in Figure 3.1, this is seen in the bins described in Table 3.1 and in the bin plot. We
also see this from the Spearman rank. The Spearman rank is determined to be ρ =
−0.050, meaning that there is a negligible negative correlation between the Stellar mass
and SFR, however looking at the p­value we cannot statistically conclude anything from
the Spearman rank. From literature this relation is known to increase SFR with stellar
mass (e.g. Speagle et al. 2014; Popesso et al. 2023), as this is not seen in the plot, we
utilize the [OII] emission line as the SFR indicator as per equation 2.3, and seen in Figure
3.2. In this plot we observe an evolution of SFR with Stellar mass, this is confirmed to be
a weak correlation following the Spearman rank (ρ = 0.305), with a statistically significant
p­value (<< 0.05). This is visually seen in the plot for the bins and the scatter, as well
as seen in Table 3.1. This result is more consistent with what is seen in the result from
literature, but still off, this is highlighted in the bin plot, where the results from literature
(Nakajima, Ouchi, Isobe, et al. 2023, red) and (Curti, Mirko et al. 2024, blue) appear to
follow the (Speagle et al. 2014) Main sequence calibrated lines for redshift z = 2, 4, 6,
where as our JWST sources do not appear to follow them. These results are not dust
reddening corrected, this has a possibility to bias the measurements of the emission lines.

5
Summary & conclusion
In this thesis has presented an observational analysis that aimed to understand the devi­
ation of the offset FMR at high­redshift galaxies. In the preceding chapters of this thesis,
we have established that the dust reddening correction has been crucial to uncovering
the deviation from offset FMR at high­redshift.
Chapter 3 presents the relation between stellar mass and SFR, the relation between the
stellar mass and gas­phase metallicity, and the relation between ∆log (O/H) and redshift
for uncorrected­, best scenario­ , and SED derived extinction dust reddening correction.
In Section 3.1 we determine there to be no correlation between SFR derived from Hβ and
the stellar mass, but find a weak positive correlation between the [OII] derived SFR and
stellar mass. We find a positive, but weak correlation between stellar mass and metal­
licity. We find several outliers in the low metallicity, high mass galaxies area. Stemming
from only one low metallicity indicator. We find no deviation from the ∆log (O/H) medi­
ans. From Section 3.2 we introduce the Balmer decrement (Hα/Hβ) extinction value
derivation to correct for dust reddening. We saw an increase in positive correlation be­
tween [OII] SFR and stellar mass. The Spearman rank increased from ρ = 0.305 to
ρ = 0.614 indicating a strong positive correlation, as also seen in literature. We also saw
an increase in Spearman rank for the mass­metallicity relation (ρ = 0.302 to ρ = 0.428),
from a weak positive correlation to a moderate positive correlation. We saw a significant
fewer sources due to the little coverage of the Hα emission line (from 657 uncorrected for
dust to 71 Hα/Hβ dust corrected).
In Section 3.3 we introduced the SED derived extinction value AV to dust reddening cor­
rect our 516 JWST sample. We saw an increase in Hβ derived SFR versus stellar mass
in the averaged bins compared to dust reddening correction, the same can be seen in
the [OII] derived SFR versus mass. In the mass­metallicity relation we no longer ob­
serve these low metallicity, high mass galaxies, as they were either filtered out or there
was found no metallicity calibration. We observe an increase of correlation measured by
Spearman correlation. By dust reddening correcting the emission lines, we observe an
evolution in the median bins for our FMR offset results, for the lower redshift galaxies we
observe galaxies moving closer to their expected area of FMR offset by dust reddening
correcting. We observe no evolution up to redshift z ∼7, at higher redshifts than z ∼7 we
start to observe a deviation from the ∆log (O/H), strongly suggesting that dust correction
has a large impact on the MS, MZR and offset FMR. However, noting the small amount of
galaxies in the high redshift area is not sufficient to statistically conclude a clear deviation
off of the ∆log (O/H).
In Chapter 4 we discuss and explore the reasons as to why this deviation occurred. We
find correlations between the ∆log (O/H) and SFR, sSFR, Hβ (EW) and metallicity, as
well as a strong negative correlation between gas­phase metallicity and Hβ (EW). We
find that this indication of low metallicity, high Hβ (EW) and smaller SFR coincides well
with the deviation of the ∆log (O/H).
We also highlight that we did not correct for the JWST slit coverage as some other litera­
ture have done and that this might have a small impact on our final results. We discovered
that some of the coverage for our JWST sources can impact the final result, if the slit does
not cover the entire galaxy or if other nearby galaxies are in the same redshift and impact­
ing the targeted galaxy in question.

Future work
In this thesis we focused on the 516 galaxies that have been dust corrected with the dust
parameter from Eazy, analyzed with different methods. It would be interesting to further
look into this dust correcting parameter, and how it might change the outcome of our re­
sults. As mentioned in the Best case scenario exploration of our results, a better way to
correct for dust reddening of the emission lines are with the Balmer recombination lines
(e.g. Hα and Hβ). It would therefore be interesting to expand our analysis to the newer
version four of the JWST spectra instead of version three as we have in this thesis in
hopes of more detected Balmer recombination lines. This might ensure a consistent dust
reddening correction.
Along side the dust reddening correction, a look into different Stellar mass derivations
would be interesting. We used Eazy for our Stellar mass derivation, this SED fitting tool
is based off of templates compiled in 2008. As we are comparing the results from (Curti,
Mirko et al. 2024), where they use the Bayesian SED fitting tool, BEAGLE as their tool to
derive Stellar mass. We ultimately end up comparing different SED fitting tools for some
relations. Not directly, but it plays a factor into our comparisons. It could also be interest­
ing to explore another Bayesian SED fitting tool, e.g. Bagpipes.
The most interesting future work would be to expand our FMR results for more high red­
shift sources, as we only have six sources around z ∼8 and above. This amount of
sources is not a statistically significant sources. It is therefore interesting to collect more
galaxies above redshift z > 8.
Finally, we propose an interesting direction for future research: exploring a potential de­
pendence of the FMR on galaxy morphology. As a brief preliminary investigation, we
examined whether there is a correlation between galaxy density (derived from radius and
mass) and the offset from the FMR.
