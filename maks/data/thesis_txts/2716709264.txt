Abstract
The building sector generates more than 15% of the total CO2 released into the
atmosphere annually. Environmental concerns and price ﬂuctuations direct fuel di-
versiﬁcation for heating and cooling purposes. Decarbonization can combine the
diversiﬁcation of thermal supply through district heating systems. Further, new
converters are coupling multi-modal supply systems for electrical and thermal in
future district heating systems. Moreover, thermal energy storage is needed to com-
pensate for the time shift between supply and demand. Short-term thermal energy
storage is already widespread in district heating systems. Long-term thermal stor-
ages are still in the project planning stage but are gaining importance in the future.
The thesis presents how to introduce long-term thermal energy storage in a use case
through energy system design inspired by a real district heating system.
Consequently, its potential is assessed by comparing the use case with or without
the thermal energy storage option. The potential evaluation is based on the cost
per thermal unit produced, known as the cost of heat. Quantitative results reveal
thermal storage as an attractive technology, especially in the long-term perspective
in district heating systems with high availability of solar thermal. With the avail-
ability of thermal energy storage as a technology option, the cost of heat of the use
case drops on average by 10 % up to a maximum of 25 %, with a favourable scenario
for the instalment of solar thermal district heating systems.
Keywords: Long-term thermal energy storage, Energy system design, District
heating.

Introduction
Earth’s climate change is a common phenomenon that has occurred in the last
800000 years due to slight changes in the Earth’s orbit. Today, the climate is chang-
ing as it has not been seen for millennia and the reasons are entirely diﬀerent than in
previous cases. The main reason is that the development of complex anthropogenic
societies has led to increased energy usage, mainly provided by fossil fuels.[1]
Fossil fuels and their derivatives have been the most convenient sources exploited
for energy usage. They are characterized by high energy density, are accessible to
transport and easy to store, contain a high carbon content with a correspondingly
high heat value and have been highly available on Earth’s subsurface.
On the other hand, the scarcity of fossil fuels brings political and economic in-
stability due to disparity that, in turn, creates monopolies. Moreover, fossil fuels
cause severe damage to the environment due to greenhouse gas products that keep
the energy of the sun’s rays trapped in the atmosphere, leading to an increase in
the average temperature of the globe and an imbalance in the water cycle. Figure
1 shows that the most emission-intensive sector is the energy sector, which includes
electricity generation, heat generation and transport, which equals 73.2% of total
emissions. Therefore, a complete restructuring of the energy sector is needed.[2]
The fundamental aspects of restructuring the energy sector concern controlling
emissions in thermal systems, as the amount of the heating share of global ﬁnal
energy consumption, is about 50%.[3]. Furthermore, one of the main sections of the
thermal energy sector is energy use in buildings. Figure 1 shows that the heating
usage in buildings represents 17.5% of the total emissions and is the second most
pollutant section behind energy use in industry.

One of the solutions is district heating: the interconnection between diﬀerent
heat sources and consumers through hot water(or steam) where the networks serve
to heat buildings. The beneﬁts of district heating are both technical and economical:
• System eﬃciency increases, and heat production is more ﬂexible on a large
scale.
• Avoid the costs of small private boilers.
• Centralized emissions are more controllable. Furthermore, the exploitation of
surplus heat from industries and power plants is more straightforward.[4]
Unfortunately, the interconnection between supply and demand is only part of the
transition to a more sustainable heating infrastructure in cities: new converters must
exploit commodities that do not produce emissions. Heat pumps, for instance, use
electricity to generate heat from various sources, including industrial wastewater. In
addition, power plants must produce electricity from non-polluting renewable tech-
nologies, such as wind and solar power plants.
However, the volatile availability of sustainable technologies, combined with the
excessive heat supply by combined heat and power plants, leads to a mismatch be-
tween heat demand and supply. [5] Further, the heat production from renewable

sources is remarkable during summer, especially in solar district heating, while the
demand occurs during winter. As a result, storing the heat is a fundamental chal-
lenge for future district heating systems.[6]
Short-term thermal energy storage allows the buﬀering of thermal energy for
hours. For instance, heat pumps exploit electricity by solar in the daytime while
most heat demand emerges during cloudy days or nights. Besides, storing thermal
energy over extended periods, for example, several months, can be beneﬁcial when
excess heat from industrial processes is available year-round and can be shifted from
summer to winter heating. Whereas short-term thermal energy storages are already
omnipresent in decentralized heating systems, such long-term thermal energy stor-
ages are currently rarely installed.
The present work aims to contribute to long-term thermal energy storage research
and its impact on district heating applications. Furthermore, the work focuses on
how to model a simple energy system and introduce long-term thermal energy stor-
age. Introducing new technologies is possible when the approach to energy system
modelling is holistic, meaning it is necessary to model an entire energy system and
deﬁne the technologies that play a role in it. Therefore, the ﬁrst task is to decide
on a use case, a control surface with input and outputs in which the energy system
technologies are introduced.
Secondly, the system has to be optimized, meaning that the new technology is
valuable only if it takes part in the optimal solution. The optimal solution needs
an objective function that, in this case, is the cost of heat minimization. The cost
of heat is the sum of the annuity of investment costs for the installation of new
components, the yearly cost of fuel for their use, and the yearly operation and main-
tenance costs per unit of heat produced. The optimization process is possible only
through software that leads to an optimum choice of energy conversion, storage and
transmission technologies, including their size and operation schedule. This process
is called Energy System Design.
In conclusion, the main question that the thesis tries to answer is:
What is the main contribution of long-term thermal energy storage in district
heating applications?
To answer the main question, the thesis needs to answer to two main sub-questions:
• How can a long-term thermal energy storage be introduced in an energy system
design model?
• What is the best solving strategy for an energy system optimization model
that includes long-term thermal energy storages?

The chapter aims to understand thermal energy storage technologies and conduct
an in-depth study of the working principle and costs of the storage technology.

As can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1, thermal energy storage technologies are split into
three sub-categories:
1. Sensible heat storage that occurs when the stored temperature changes
according to heat exchanged with the system. In general, equation 1.1 gives
the amount of energy stored :
Q = m · cp · ∆T

Where Q is the amount of stored heat in MWhth, m the mass of storage in kilo-

during the charge and discharge phase in Kelvin. [8]
2. Latent heat storage, on the other hand, exploits latent heat of the storing

Q = m · ∆h
(1.2)
Where ∆h is the enthalpy change between solid and liquid phase. [8]
3. Chemical heat storage accumulates heat by exploiting a reversible chemical
reaction.
During the charge phase, an endothermic reaction absorbs heat
and generates products. Further, the products are stored and reused for an
opposite and exothermic reaction during discharge. [8]
In general, three main phases characterize thermal energy storage:
• During the charging phase heat is delivered inside the storage through a heat
exchanger.
• During the storage phase heat is retained inside the system.
• During the discharging phase heat is released and fed back into the system.
Furthermore, thermal energy storages are described based on other properties, such
as:
• Power shown as ˙Q in the project, describes how quick the thermal energy
storage can be charged and discharged in MWth
• Storage period deﬁnes how long can heat be stored without being lost in the
environment in hours unit
• Speciﬁc cost can be either deﬁned per unit maximum energy content in e/MWhth
or per unit of power in e/MWth.
• Capacity shown as Q in the project, described the amount of heat that the
storage can stock in MWhth
Thermal energy storage through sensible heat has signiﬁcantly developed in energy
systems compared to latent and chemical due to lower costs, both from auxiliary ma-
terials and investment. The primary ﬂuid used for sensible thermal energy storage
is water due to its high availability and low prices [9]. On the other hand, the pres-
sure of thermal energy storage is commonly ambient, meaning that the maximum
temperature that water can reach is 95◦C, showing low energy density. Therefore,

they require high volumes to allow a reasonable storage capacity. The most sensible
thermal energy storages are installed below the ground to limit environmental losses,
which are referred to as Underground Thermal Energy Storages (UTES). [10]
Long-term thermal energy storages are sensible thermal energy storages that

1. Tank Thermal Energy Storage (TTES): TTES show limited sizing, high

2. Pit Thermal Energy Energy storage (PTES): PTES, on the other hand,

systems. Further, the high power supply oﬀers the potential to exploit the
technology as weekly storage.[11]
3. Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES): Aquifer systems show high
maximum capacity in MWhth, low installation costs per power in e/MWth,
and good power supply for being installed in large district heating systems.
However, soil properties and geomaterial instalment constraints limit technol-
ogy spread in most district heating systems. Therefore, soil analysis through
drilling before instalment is necessary. [12]
4. Borehole Thermal Energy Storage (BTES): This type of underground
thermal energy storage involves storing high-temperature water in vertical un-
derground cylinders. BTES shows limited thermal capacity, higher costs than
aquifer thermal energy storage, and fewer instalment locations than pit ther-
mal energy storage. Furthermore, the power released is small compared to pit
technology and similar to aquifer thermal energy storage. On the other hand,
it is easier to install and cheaper to maintain than ATES, and fewer drilling
tests must be necessary before installation. If ATES systems’ instalment is
possible, BTES are less eﬃcient than PTES, more expensive than ATES and
can be neglected from this optimization analysis. [13]

Tank Thermal Energy Storages
Tank thermal energy storage is a well-established technology in which most of
the tanks are installed near large biomass plants to increase the supply ﬂexibility of
the plant. Further, it is a solid solution in most district heating systems; e.g. the
total volume of thermal storage present in Denmark amounts to 875000 m3 [14].
1.2.1
Working Principle
Figure 1.2 shows the design of tanks thermal energy storages: huge cylinders
made of about 450 mm of steel and concrete containing high-temperature water.
Like the pit thermal energy storage technology, the tank thermal energy storage
accumulates heat through the stratiﬁcation of temperatures. Thanks to these prop-
erties, the tank can be discharged and charged at diﬀerent temperatures through
the diﬀusers at diﬀerent heights.[15]
Figure 1.2: Tank thermal energy storage design [16]
Since the cylindrical geometric structure is not optimal for containing the exer-
getic losses in the storage ﬂuid, internal diﬀusers based in the centre of the technology
limit the internal losses. [15]

1.3
Pit Thermal Energy Storages
1.3.1
Working Principle
The pit thermal energy storage is an excavation in the ground in the shape of an
overturned and truncated cone, in which an impermeable plastic covers the lower
and lateral surfaces to contain the heat and prevent the ﬂuid from spreading over the
ground. A lid covers the upper part of the storage in contact with the air, limiting
losses. [20] During the charging phase, water is taken from the lower diﬀuser and
released at a high temperature in the higher one. On the other hand, water ﬂows
from the top to the bottom during discharge.
As ﬁgure 1.5 shows, PTES working principle is very similar to the traditional
tank, as the thermal gradient created by the density diﬀerence between ﬂuid tem-
peratures regulates the charge and discharge phase. On average, the temperature
diﬀerence between the top and bottom is around 50◦C, with a maximum storage
temperature of 90◦C, since thermal storage is at ambient pressure and low energy
density. Water is later extracted and introduced by two diﬀerent diﬀusers. The liner
limits the losses between water and soil, while the lid covers the pit and limits the
losses between water and ambient air. Air is pumped between the water stored and
the lid to limit corrosion and decrease insulation losses.[20].
Figure 1.5: Working principle of PTES systems and system design [21]
In conclusion, ﬁgure 1.5 shows that the pit thermal energy storage is a closed
system that exchanges heat through a heat exchanger or a heat pump. Further, it
is often associated with large solar thermal systems and biomass or gas boilers to
increase eﬃciency and regulate the temperature according to the district heating
temperature.

1.4
Aquifer Thermal Energy Storages
1.4.1
Working Principle
Aquifers are deposits of underground water in a porous material and are limited
by waterproof rocks that can spread in the subsurface for hundreds of meters. It is
possible to introduce and extract water from the surface permitting an inﬁnite cycle
of immersion and extraction.
As shown in ﬁgure 1.7, an aquifer thermal energy storage involves two heat ex-
changers on the surface interacting with external heat sources. During the charging
phase, water is extracted from the groundwater, heated and re-immersed in the
ground: it is impossible to re-immerse the heated water in the same cold water
source, as the system will lose most of the heat due to the mixing process between
hot and cold ﬂuid. Therefore, the technology shows two separate sources: the hot
well during charging through the hot heat exchanger that absorbs heat, and a warm
well, ﬁlled during the discharging phase with a warm heat exchanger that releases
heat.

2 | Energy System Design Optimization
The diﬀerence in costs and thermal storage eﬃciency raises doubt about the best
technology to introduce into an energy system. For this reason, it is necessary to
develop a simulated energy system based on the principle of mathematical optimiza-
tion. A purely holistic approach introduces new technology into a predeﬁned energy
system. The adjective "holistic" refers to a process that sees each element or system
as an entirety and not just as composed of individual parts. Therefore, it is essential
to structure an energy system that works without new technologies and understand
how the elements interact. Furthermore, the system is analyzed based on how the
system will change with the introduction of new technology.
2.1
Fundamentals of Energy System Design
The Energy system design (ESD) objective is to determine the optimal solution
from a set of parameters describing the technologies applied to a predeﬁned use case
and deﬁning the constraints. In short, energy system design analyzes the energy
system as a big optimization problem.
In general, the parameters to be deﬁned are almost inﬁnite: they can describe the
place, the prices of the commodities, the loads, the climatic conditions, and politi-
cal, economic and social conditions. Consequently, the ﬁrst step of an energy system
designer is to deﬁne a use case or a place of analysis to apply its constraints. Finally,
the optimization model conducts decisional research on an energy system’s future
investments. Consequently, forecast models need economic and technical predic-
tions, such as the future demand of the energy market, considering past experiences
or the trend in commodity prices.
The energy system design is part of the research analysis of possible technologies
to improve the system. Therefore precise planning in all aspects is not necessary.
The previous chapter shows that long-term thermal energy storages consider large
volumes of stored heat, so the use case must be a comprehensive system that involves
simpliﬁcations.[34]
2.2
Optimization Software
The numerical solution through computation in an optimization software avoids
the analytical solution of complex systems, which is almost impossible in a limited
period. For this thesis, the mathematical optimization model is already pre-set in
Matlab R2015A and resolved by a central computer via an internal client VPN. All
the equations considered in the optimization model are described by Thiem [34].

Furthermore, it is possible to deﬁne the hourly variations of the pre-established con-
straints since the software analyzes the system annually in hourly timesteps.
Mathematical optimization foresees the point that maximizes or minimizes the
objective function within a domain by considering constraints, and it is composed
of the following main elements:
• Parameters: data of the problem and represent ﬁxed quantities that depend
on the system’s diﬀerent elements.
• Sets: Sets group diﬀerent elements. The main sets used in the software are:
1. Commodities are all primary sources that create value in the system, such
as electricity, heat, gas and biomass.
2. Grids transport commodities inside and outside the system.
3. Converters are all the elements that transform a commodity into one or
more commodities, i.e. the waste incineration plant transforms commod-
ity waste into electricity, heat and ﬂue gas.
• Decision-making or control variables: the system variables of which the
value has to be optimized and set by the model as an output. In general, the
software deﬁnes the following variables:
1. Technical variables: based on costs minimization, the system chooses the
optimal capacity of every converter. Every capacity is sized and optimized
on a initial chosen unit (in the thesis model is MW) while the storages
are optimized on the maximum energy content (MWhth).
2. Economic variables: based on the optimal capacity, the annual costs are
given by the operation and maintenance expenses and annualized invest-
ment cost based on converter lifetime and weighted average cost of capital
(WACC).
3. Timeseries: hourly state of charge of storages and hourly operation of
the converters that are part of the optimized solution.
• Objective function: it is the quantity to be maximized or minimized, ex-
pressed as a function of decision variables.
In general, many parameters diﬀerentiate the technologies applied in the system.
For instance, the main parameters of the grid in the optimization model are the
unit price of the transmitted commodity, aids and CO2 emissions of the transmitted
energy.
These elements are fundamental to understanding the working principle of the
optimization model. However, since the objective of the thesis is not to specify the

2.2.2
Solving Processes
Based on the nature or domain of the decision variables, two solving strategies
are possible:
• Linear Programming models (LP) if all the variables can assume real values;
• Mixed Integer Linear Programming models (MILP) if some variables can as-
sume real and other integer values.
In a nutshell, in the linear model, the system can analyze the system only as a
function of continuous values, i.e. speciﬁc investment per unit of constant MWhth
and steady eﬃciency during operation if a manual time series is not introduced.
Simplicity implies a limited time from a computational perspective, and a secure
optimal solution for each model installed.
On the other hand, the mixed linear programming model allows ﬁxing binary
variables that limit the system decisions. In terms of modelling the energy system,
it is possible to introduce additional variables, such as:
• Choice of minimum capacities below which the component is excluded by the
optimizer.
• An online and oﬄine status of components, including start-up cost, runtime
cost and part-load eﬃciency.

5.1
Choice of Solving Strategy
In subsection 2.2.2, the diﬀerences between mixed integer linear programming
(MILP) and linear programming (LP) are brieﬂy described. It is now necessary to
understand which is the best solving strategy to introduce thermal energy storages
into the system. There are various reasons why linear programming was chosen
instead of mixed integer linear programming:
• In any case, a lower computational time is obtained, and it is possible to run a
more signiﬁcant number of simulations and more possibilities for a sensitivity
analysis.
• An energy system of a vast scale is analyzed. Therefore, each energy conversion
technology component, e.g. heat pump, can be seen as a large ﬂeet of many
units. A low part load, e.g. 5% power, corresponds to one unit running at
a still signiﬁcant load, whereas all others are at a standstill. In conclusion,
running such a model without limiting the minimum part load is suﬃcient, as
it would only be possible in a MILP calculation.
• The assurance of an optimized result of each simulation process leads to a dras-
tic reduction of results analysis and an increase in the quality of the sensitivity
analysis.

9.3
Use case limits and future improvements
The implications of the result are limited to the structure of a particular use
case. Furthermore, storage losses are based only on the installed capacity and not
the state of charge. In the actual case, the state of charge deﬁnes the temperature
of the stored water and, consequently, the thermal losses in the environment during
the storage period. The simpliﬁcation of deﬁning losses as a function of capacity ne-
glects losses that change with the variation of the stored temperature. Furthermore,
it involves a constant temperature during discharge, which in turn inﬂuences the
eﬃciency of the booster heat pump, according to the 5.7 equation. Moreover, the
capacity market is an electricity market scheme that provides for a series of mea-
sures aimed at guaranteeing the safety of the system and the supply of electricity,
with resources always available to cover the load peaks in each network area. Both
existing CHP plants and new ones can participate in the capacity market. However,
the capacity market is not part of this analysis. These simpliﬁcations will lead to
deviating optimization results, as CHP plants and CCPP can be part of the optimal
solution even if the operating hours are limited.
Moreover, the equation of heat pumps has been simpliﬁed.
The heat pump
system eﬃciency applied in equation 5.7 is 55%. Such system eﬃciency would be
preferable to use the Lorentz equation instead of the Carnot equation, according to
Elmegaard et al. [77]. On the other hand, introducing the Lorentz equation and the
heat losses per state of charge would slightly increase the COP of heat pumps. Still,
as heat pumps are already highly convenient to future energy systems, the equation
shift will not signiﬁcantly improve the model results. On the other hand, the model
implemented by Varmeplan Hovedstaden scenario for 2050 in reference [42] consid-
ers diﬀerent commodity prices compared to Danish Energy Agency in reference [56]
that would lead to diﬀerent results.

9
Conclusions
A cost of storage analysis compares several thermal energy storage types in their
techno-economic viability. Furthermore, storages require "booster"-technologies to
exploit the stored thermal energy and adjust the storage temperature with the dis-
tribution temperature of the district heating system. Therefore a separate techno-
economic analysis shows the best technology to apply. Finally, such thermal energy
storage technologies with boosters are coupled through energy system design for a
typical large district heating system in Europe.
Through the cost of storage analysis, the tank thermal energy storage shows
considerable potential for peak load suppliers and short-term storage. On the other
hand, the pit thermal energy storage oﬀers a cost optimum for weekly, monthly, or
seasonal storage with a high thermal power supply. Finally, aquifer thermal energy
storages show huge potential for seasonal thermal energy storage applications at
the low thermal power supply. In conclusion, the results can vary as a function
of capacity and power, as they are the main factor that changes expenses in the
aquifer’s thermal energy storage.
Furthermore, a separate cost of heat analysis chooses the optimal solution for the
booster technology. Based on the study, if the operation time is low, the best option
is either a gas or electric boiler, while with a high operation time, the heat pump
is the best solution. Finally, the results can vary from the commodity prices involved.
Moreover, thermal energy storage is introduced in a use case through energy sys-
tem design optimization, representing the thesis’s core. The energy system design
results are independent of the techno-economic analysis before the use case mod-
elling. On the other hand, the prior study is conﬁrmed by the storage behaviour in
the optimization software.
The energy system design optimization shows the considerable potential of ther-
mal energy storage in district heating applications, both from a technical and costs
