Abstract
The urge for developing a sustainable transport network is of utmost
importance and is listed as a major priority for the SDGs. Electric
vehicles constitute a key element for this transition but yet, their
market shares are still low in most European countries, including
Denmark.
Further insights on the factors preventing their adop-
tion should be investigated. The current master thesis addresses
the barriers on electric vehicle demand by taking into account at-
titudes and perceptions of the population for this technology. Be-
yond examining which factors related to vehicle characteristics and
charging infrastructure affect the preferences, it is investigated how
does enthusiasm towards the technology, skepticism over practical-
ity and environmentalism, influence people’s choices. The sample is
collected through a stated choice experiment conducted by DTU in
2020. An integrated choice latent variable panel mixed logit model
is formed and the parameter estimates are analyzed in detail. The
main findings reveal that individuals who are enthusiast about the
technology have higher preferences for EVs. Young individuals ex-
press more interest in purchasing an electric vehicle while they are
highly concerned for CO2. Skeptics are negatively related with al-
ternative fuel vehicles and they are less concerned for emissions.
Driving range is found to be a significant obstacle in the demand
for the specific group. Individual’s attitude towards the environment
positively affects the preferences and environmentalists demonstrate
the highest sensitivity to emissions. It is confirmed that purchase
price plays an important role in the EV uptake for almost all the
targeted groups. The whole sample exhibits a negative marginal

1.1
Motivation and Scope of the thesis
It is a certain fact that emissions from the transport sector, both
worldwide and in Denmark, are a significant contributor to the over-
all amount of greenhouse gases released into the atmosphere. Trans-
port is a noteworthy source of air pollution and contributor to cli-
mate change that during the second quarter of 2022, accounted for
a substantial 14% of the total Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions.1
In Denmark, the public sector made a commitment of reducing the
carbon footprint attributed to transport by implementing policies
and initiatives aimed at increasing the popularity of Electric Vehi-
cles (EVs). Tax incentives for purchasing EVs, subsidies for charging
infrastructure, and investment in public charging networks are some
of those policies (Calearo et al.[7]). The government has set a polit-

1.2
Problem Statement and research ques-
tion
The main objective of the thesis is to investigate and analyze ob-
stacles in the demand for electric vehicles by differentiating between
technology enthusiasts and skeptics. Among the goals, vehicle at-
tributes, charging infrastructure, socio-economic and demographic
characteristics are investigated, in order to examine differences in
preferences for electric vehicles. The analysis will explore attitudes
and perceptions towards technology enthusiasm, skepticism, environ-
mentalism and by identifying the barriers, it can help policymakers
and industry stakeholders to develop more effective strategies for
increasing EV adoption.
Moreover, understanding consumer seg-
mentation by addressing the needs and preferences of the targeted
groups, can be useful for market analysis, as well as for establish-
ing policies that will educate consumers on EV technology and its
benefits. Finally, exploring attitudinal factors, can provide insights
into the factors that are driving or inhibiting the adoption of the
new technology. It is inspected whether technology enthusiasts are
motivated by environmental concerns, while skeptics are more fo-
cused on practical concerns such as cost, convenience or charging
infrastructure.
Analytically, the thesis will focus on exploring the following:
• Explain and discuss how attributes, individual characteristics
and attitudes affect the EV uptake.
• Analyze different ways of including enthusiasts and skeptics in
the modelling of EV demand.
• Model how heterogeneity affects the market shares of Electric
Vehicles by using mixed logit models.
• Examine interactions of latent constructs with attributes and
characteristics through an integrated choice latent variable model.
• Assess existing policies and suggest strategies to support EV
uptake for different target groups.

Introduction
The project is based on data retrieved from a stated choice ex-
periment called ELISA, that was set up from DTU in 2020 and
was answered by 2961 individuals. In the experiment, the respon-
dents had to make 8 sequential decisions (8 scenarios) on their most
likely future vehicle purchase. The vehicles included were a combi-
nation of 3 different fuel type technologies and 6 different car seg-
ments (Jensen et al.[14]). Discrete choice modelling is applied on
respondents’ stated choices through the open-source Python pack-
age Biogeme that has been developed by Prof. Michel Bierlaire and
has been specifically designed for maximum likelihood estimation of
parametric models.4

2.1
Fuel Type Technologies
There are 3 different fuel type categories of vehicles considered in
the thesis, namely the Internal Combustion Engine Vehicles (ICV),
the Battery Electric Vehicles (BEV) and the Plug-in Hybrid Electric
Vehicles (PHEV).
Internal Combustion Engine Vehicles as their name indicates,
make use of their Internal Combustion Engine (ICE). They are the
==PAGE:21==

conventional vehicles that despite their harmful environmental foot-
print, are still dominating the market. Power is generated by com-
busting fuel within the ICE’s combustion chamber, facilitated by an
oxidizer.1 Although the use and popularity of bio fuels (ethanol,
hydrogen) has increased dramatically within the last years with the
Fuel Cell Vehicles (FCVs), conventional cars still rely mostly on
fossil fuels, such as petrol or diesel. Low production costs, proven
efficiency and robustness of fossil fuels, are just some of the reasons
why today EVs are still not considered a competitive alternative
(Vigild[29]).
The ICV vehicles considered in the experiment are
either diesel or gasoline(Jensen et al.[14]).
Battery Electric Vehicles are a sub-category of EVs that rely ex-
clusively on electricity that is stored in a rechargeable battery pack
onboard of the vehicle. The battery pack uses electric motors or
motor controllers for propulsion that do not produce tailpipe emis-
sions. A conventional external power source supplies the vehicle with
AC current that is transformed to DC through the onboard charger
that charges the battery.2 BEVs are characterized by producing a
significantly lower amount of emissions compared to conventional
cars while their fast acceleration, the efficiency of the electric motor
and their low operational costs by charging overnight, are the most
noteworthy advantages of the technology.3 In contrast, their lim-
ited driving range accompanied by long lasting charging times are
factors that still drive people away in purchasing such a vehicle. As
elaborated in section [2.2], numerous studies have delved into these
factors and how they can be reversed.
Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles combine both the technology
of ICVs and BEVs, by having a small battery adapted onboard and
an ICE. Compared to conventional Hybrid Electic Vehicles (HEVs),
they have a larger battery pack and they can be plugged into an

2.2
Previous studies on Electric Vehicle
Demand
The importance of shifting to clean energy, has increased tremen-
dously the research in the field of alternative fuel vehicles. Numerous
studies can be found in the existing literature regarding the barriers
and the factors affecting EV adoption. Thus, this section will serve
as the connection between conducted studies and this project. Dis-
crete Choice Modelling has been traditionally the tool for analyzing
demand in transport among different car type technologies.
Most of the studies related to EV demand, are built on Stated Pref-
erences (SP) data. Stated Preferences is the type of data that people
make their choice in hypothetical scenarios; their properties are dis-
cussed in detail in section [4.1]. Revealed Preferences (RP) have

been used to a lesser extent since the share of EVs in the current

preferences change after experiencing an EV. As described in Mabit

concern and individual choices change dramatically after experienc-

the individuals. Buhman et al.[6] suggest that having children, be-

the EV uptake. Jia & Chen[15], support that males have higher
preferences for BEVs than females and that the availability of fast
charging stations affects significantly the demand. They conclude
that cost attributes and driving range are considerable barriers in
the future demand.
Although in many studies, older people are
associated with a negative preference on EVs, the study of Jia &
Chen[15] reveals that the age effect is insignificant. In the research
of Cirillo et al.[8], it is estimated that young people are more likely
to purchase BEVs compared to middle-age and older individuals,
while being male and highly educated increases the probability of
selecting an EV.

et al.[10] gives significant insights by incorporating both BEVs and
PHEVs, while at the same time accounts for substitution effects
among vehicle segments, when most studies do not consider them.
This kind of set up allows individuals to switch between fuel types

positive and significant correlation between environmentalists and

fluenced before and after experiencing an EV. In a extension of this
study (Ortuzar et al.[11]), the latent constructs appreciation of car
features, technology interest, pro EV attitude, and skepticism are
added to the model and their performance is measured before and
after experiencing an EV. The results of that study reveal that EVs
are selected less times after experiencing them, while perceptions on
EV’s features such as acceleration improve after the experience.

conclude that there is a positive contribution on the choice of EV.

3.1
ELISA project description
The experiment was launched in 2020 under the supervision of pro-
fessors in the Transport Division of the DTU Management depart-
ment. The primary goal of the project was the creation of a model
that will be able to forecast the demand for BEVs and PHEVs in fu-
ture scenarios while at the same time account for substitution effects
across different vehicle fuel type technologies and segments (Jensen
et al.[10]). In particular, ICV, BEV and PHEV are the fuel type
technologies to be considered while Mini, Small, Medium, Large,
Premium and Luxury/Sport are the car segment categories. The
==PAGE:27==
12
Data
participants involved in the project were selected randomly through
their CPR number and in June of 2020, a total of 25209 invitations
were sent via the digital mailbox (E-boks). The survey was com-
pleted with 2961 participants and a response rate of 11.7%. Each
participant was presented with a total of 8 scenarios and thus, had
to make 8 sequential decisions on which vehicle type and segment
would choose among the showed alternatives (panel effect). For each
respondent and in each scenario, 2 car segments were presented from
each fuel type[12]. For the first 4 scenarios, the participants were
presented with 4 vehicle options (2 ICVs and 2 BEVs) while in the
last 4 scenarios, 2 more PHEV options were added.
The PHEV
alternatives were only included in the last 4 choice tasks, in order
to reduce the complexity of the experiment and simplify the choice
tasks for the respondents. The analytical framework under which
the ELISA project was set up, can be seen in Jensen et al.[14]. The
survey was comprised of the four following parts.
1. Introductory questions
2. Stated choise experiment
3. Background characteristics and car usage
4. Attitudinal statements
3.1.1
Intro questions and customization of the
scenarios
In the first part of the questionnaire, the participants were asked
to answer to questions that would later be used for customizing the
stated choice experiment around each individual’s answers. Some of
these questions were:
• How many cars are available in your household?
• Do you have access to charge an electric car on your own prop-
erty/residence?
• For each car class, indicate the probability that you will choose

this class for your future car purchase.
Based on the responses to the intro questions, the 8 projected sce-
narios were adjusted accordingly for each individual. That is to say,
for those who replied that they either possess or have the ability to
install a private charging box in their residence, the "House Design"
was applied. In contrast, the "Apartment Design" was projected to
the rest of the sample. The difference among these two versions of

Chapter 4
Methodology
The current chapter addresses the methodology used for carrying
out this project. At first, a short description on stated preferences
and panel data is presented. Afterwards, discrete choice modelling
and the respective model categories implemented for delivering the
final estimates are further analyzed.
4.1
Stated Preferences
There are two main data collection methods extensively used in
modelling transport, namely stated preferences and revealed prefer-
ences. SP data are called the ones that are based on stated prefer-
ences while RP reflect choices of individuals that have been taken
in real-world cases (Train)[27]. SP are collected from experiments
or surveys where participants are displayed with hypothetical sce-
narios and are asked to choose their preferred choice among the set
==PAGE:42==
4.1 Stated Preferences
27
of choices. As mentioned in [1.2], the dataset used in the project
is taken from a survey with stated preferences. The fact that SP
data are not restricted to current situations, offers high flexibility in
research where analysts are able to capture variation in attributes,
alternatives and attribute levels. This variation can be extremely
helpful in modelling the demand of a new product, or as is the case
in this thesis, for a BEV or a PHEV vehicle. In parallel, the abil-
ity to incorporate many attributes that can also vary among the
hypothetical alternatives or the respondents, enables for thorough
examination and evaluation on those attributes. However, the un-
certainty surrounded on whether individuals would actually choose
their stated option in a real-world situation, subjects the reliabil-
ity of SP data on predictive terms. Moreover, potential errors that
according to Bates[1] can emerge in SP surveys include: Response
Fatigue where individuals do not have complete understanding of
all the underlying projected scenarios and Policy response bias in
which respondents may purposely have predefined choices to influ-
ence policy making.
Panel data analysis deals with situations where information is gath-
ered in consecutive points in time for the same individual or in the
case of SP data where there are multiple observations from the same
decision maker. Compared to cross-sectional data, panels give the
opportunity to study temporal effects, travel behaviour and develop
more realistic models(Washington et al.)[32]. In the context of this
thesis, the formulation of mixed logit models on the panel data,
accounts for heterogeneity by estimating random parameters that
capture the variation in preferences across individuals. In fact, it
is proven through many applications, that the use of error compo-
nents in the ML model can significantly improve the model fit when
dealing with SP data(Ortúzar & Willumsen)[23].

28

4.2
Discrete Choice Modelling
Discrete choice modelling (DCM) is a widespread approach of mod-
elling discrete variables with a finite number of outcomes. DCMs
are used in cases where individuals are faced with a distinct set of
choices called alternatives. They have witnessed extreme popularity
among the transport sector where they have been studied for mod-
elling route choice, mode choice, or as is the case in this thesis, the
choice of a type of vehicle. DCMs are considered as micro-models
since we model at individual or household level and aggregate the es-
timates (Rich)[25]. Their implementation is based on random utility
maximization (RUM) which has been studied extensively by Daniel
McFadden[20] who is considered a pioneer in the field.

4.2.2
Multinomial Logit Model (MNL)
The MNL model has been studied more than any other model in
transport modelling. In its simplest form, the utility of an alterna-

4.2.3
Mixed Logit Model (ML)
Due to its high flexibility, mixed logit is currently one of the most
powerful models in the existing literature. In the context of this
thesis, it allows for substitution effects among vehicle fuel type cate-
gories and segments by accounting for correlation among alternatives
(does not hold the IIA property). Moreover, it is suitable for taking
into consideration the nature of the dataset (panel data) by account-
ing for correlation among observations from the same decision maker
(McFadden & Train)[27]. As stated in Train, the mixed logit over-
comes 3 major limitations of the classical logit model by accounting
for: a) random preference variation b) unrestricted substitution pat-
terns and c) correlations in unobserved factors over time. The two
major structural forms of ML models are the Random Coefficients
(RC) and the Error Components (EC) specifications.
Although
these two specifications are formally equal, the specification changes
according to the researcher’s interest (Ortúzar & Willumsen)[23]. In
the RC specification, the random parameter is associated directly to
an attribute in order to capture variations in tastes related to this
observed attribute among individuals or in other words, to account
for the heterogeneity in the individual preferences for a specific at-
tribute. In the EC model, the error component is linked to the ASCs
of the utilities in order to account for heterogeneity that exists in
people’s preferences for specific alternatives. For the project, the
EC specification is followed so that error components will account
for the correlation among the 18 alternatives.

4.2.4
Integrated Choice Latent Variable Model
(ICLV)
By definition, subjective and latent concepts such as perceptions
and attitudes, cannot be observed.
In order to retrieve informa-
tion for these concepts, indirect measurements called psychometrics
are used. As mentioned in [2.2], studies aiming to investigate how
attitudes affect the EV uptake, incorporate unobserved latent con-
structs in their models. The current project examines how skep-
ticism, enthusiasm for EV technology can affect the demand and
the preferences and thus, the creation of latent variables expressing
these attitudes is the basic core of the analysis. The integration
of latent variables in the models, is performed under the theoretical
framework of Integrated Choice Latent Variable models as presented
by Walker[31] and Ben-Akiva et al.[2] to name a few, while the tech-
nical implementation and set up of the models is taken from Bier-
laire[3].
Consequently, individuals’ perception on EV technology,
EV practicallity and Environmental Concern is expressed through
latent variables that enter the utility function as explanatory vari-
ables.
Under the ICLV framework, both a sequential and a simultaneous
approach has been developed. In the former, latent variables are
built prior to their inclusion in the utility while the later approach
estimates all the parameters simultaneously. In the sequential ap-
proach, the latent variable model is calculated first by estimating the
parameters that link these variables with socio-economic character-
istics and perception indicators (questions), and then the expected
values of those variables for each individual are entering the utilities
of the classical choice model. This approach has been criticized that
it can involve overestimation of the significance of the parameters
(Bollen)[5]. Although more accurate, the simultaneous approach is
used to a lesser extent due to its computational complexity(Ben-
Akiva et al.)[2].

5.3
Model with fitted latent variables
Many techniques have been performed over the years in order to cap-
ture the effect of psychological factors in choice models. Previous
approaches included directly the responses (indicators) of individ-
uals to survey questions, into the utility functions as explanatory
variables. As stated by many in literature, this approach is rejected
since it is subject to measurement errors and the inability to use the
indicators for forecasting. For that reason, the first approach used
in the thesis to extract latent variables, is to first perform a factor
analysis on the indicators, and then use the fitted latent variables
in the utility function of the choice model.
The results from the factor analysis revealed that the first latent con-
cept that is to be modelled namely technology enthusiasm, has high
factor loadings with 3 indicators. Accordingly, the second variable
expressing skepticism is linked with 6 questions while environmental
concern is linked to 3 indicators. Since most of the statements in
the second factor imply similar beliefs, it was decided to keep only 3
indicators for each latent variable. Therefore, the correlation matrix
for these 6 statements is calculated, and one of the two questions in
pairs that are most correlated, is excluded arbitrarily.
In order to retrieve the factor scores, the answers of individuals in
the Likert scale are standardized so that the results are scaled and
then, they are multiplied with the respective factor loadings for each
individual. In this way, the values for the 3 latent variables for each
individual in the sample are obtained. The latent variable for every
individual n that is linked with indicators x,y and z is calculated as:

As stated in Walker[31], the approach of incorporating fitted latent
variables in the utility function can lead to inconsistent estimates
that contain measurement errors. Measurement bias is highly con-
nected to the fact that the scale of the survey questions is arbitrary
and thus, people may perceive it differently. On top of that, some
people have the tendency to overreact while there are many that
state their preferences, in order to purposely affect the results. For
that reason, the sequential and simultaneous approach of integrated
choice latent variable models is performed, in order to produce effi-
cient and consistent estimates.

sample into enthusiasts and skeptics. In particular, factor analysis
is used as input while the silhouette score is calculated for a range of
2 to 5 clusters. K-means algorithm is implemented and the 2 cluster

6.1
Conclusion
The political goal for increasing electric vehicle ownership has been
set by most European countries including Denmark.
The catas-
trophic consequences of fossil fuels demand immediate actions to-
wards shifting to clean energy.
The current thesis constitutes a
supplementary research in the existing literature for identifying and
analyzing obstacles in the electric vehicle demand by taking into con-
sideration attitudes and perceptions of the population. Individual
preferences over BEVs and PHEVs are examined through the latent
constructs technology enthusiasm, skepticism and environmentalism
under an integrated choice latent variable mixed logit model. The
main findings reveal that choosing an electric vehicle is indeed de-
pendant on individual perceptions. Enthusiasm towards their tech-
nology and environmental concern positively affect the preferences,
while skepticism around their usage constitutes a significant driving
==PAGE:86==
6.1 Conclusion
71
factor against their adoption. It is further confirmed that the in-
creased cost of electric vehicles is a significant concern even among
individuals who appreciate EV’s features and environmentalists. For
people who are skeptical, it is found that the limited driving range,
constitutes a remarkable barrier, confirming that "range anxiety" is
evident. It is also estimated that young individuals express higher
interest towards car features and greater environmental concern.
Adding up to the above, it is also investigated how does systematic
heterogeneity and specific attributes affect the preferences.
High
income, young age and access to home charging, are all connected
to higher preferences for EVs. These findings come along with the
existing literature, including the research presented in section [2.2].
It is further acknowledged that high revenue classes have less con-
cern over the purchase cost and higher environmental awareness. In
contrast, low income groups are affected to a much higher extent
from the purchase price, while older individuals appear less sensi-
tive to emissions.
Although males exhibit a high interest in the
technology of EVs, their preferences are lower compared to females;
a finding that contradicts with many relevant studies. In general,
the elements of the charging infrastructure network are found to be
significant factors affecting the choice of a vehicle.
The formulation of a mixed logit model takes into account preference
heterogeneity and the panel nature of the dataset. The inclusion of
latent variables provides a realistic portrayal for the choice procedure
and it results in a model with high explanatory strength. The im-
plementation of an ICLV model leads to consistent and efficient es-
timates. The construction of latent variables through measurement
and structural equations, accounts for measurement errors that can
be derived from respondent’s answers on the survey. The simulta-
neous estimation revealed that characteristics of the individuals are
poor predictors of the latent variables and that great care should be
taken when constructing the structural equations. Aligned with the
existing literature, it is proven that the sequential methodology can
lead to overestimation of the significance for specific parameters.

Conclusion and Discussion
6.2
Discussion
A further examination and validation of the model results would
require the calculation of elasticities. Elasticity describes the per-
centage change in the demand of an alternative given a percentage
change in an attribute of an alternative. Since the modelled shares
have not been calibrated to reflect the actual shares, elasticities have
not been estimated for the project. Furthermore, it should be noted
that the findings of the project, are based on SP data and thus, the
interpretation of the results is highly dependant on the validity of
respondent’s answers. As stated in [4.1], SP data come along with
several drawbacks and their reliability is subjected for prediction. A
combination of RP and SP is considered more appropriate for the
creation of more powerful models that can be used for forecasting.
As stated by Bierlaire[3], models incorporating latent variables are
mostly employed to understand consumer segmentation and capture
heterogeneity and not for forecasting.
The findings of the current project verify that there are indeed obsta-
cles in increasing electric vehicle demand and that perceptions about
EVs are closely related to their low penetration in the market. Al-
though certain strategies have already been established, additional
measures should be taken in order to achieve faster results. Since
driving range and high costs are some of the most important factors
driving people away for purchasing an alternative fuel vehicle, em-
phasis should be given in improving the technology and decreasing
the costs. The technological improvement of charging capabilities
and the development of an efficient charging infrastructure network,
are facilitating elements for that cause. EV practicality and use, as
reflected by respondent’s answers on the survey, is highly questioned
and thus, it is necessary now more than ever, to educate consumers
over the environmental benefits of electric vehicles.
