Abstract
This project will investigate how to improve the existing upper-limb LEGO exoskele-
ton design used by the DTU ReHyb group. This project will especially focus on de-
veloping methods for generating unique exoskeletons to fit each individual user,
along with making it more user friendly, by incorporating 3D scanning and printing
when designing a new exoskeleton.
.
This project, begins with designing a user adaptable upper-limp exoskeleton. The ex-
oskeleton is manufactured using a standard FDM 3D printer. For non printable parts,
like motors and sensors, standard off the shelf components are used. Mechanically,
the design provides one DoF, located at the elbow joint. A key characteristic is the
ability to be adapted to the individual user, using the four parameters: Wrist width,
lower-arm length, biceps width, and upper-arm length. The exoskeleton uses an en-
coder for position sensing and a current sensor for user input sensing. The current
sensing technique utilizes the relationship between motor current and motor torque,
to determine the torque provided by the user. However, this method is not validated,
as the motor can not provide enough torque for moving the exoskeleton, without the
motor current reaching its maximum. The exoskeleton does not provide the required
torque for moving or even nudging an arm in the desired direction. This is a conse-
quence of its direct drive design, where a Nema 14 stepper motor is used for ac-
tuation. This design also results in a lack of precision, since the Nema 14 does not
come with a built-in encoder. An AS5600 encoder is therefore connected to the mo-
tor through a 3D printed gearbox. These 3D printed gears are not printed as a per-
fected fit, due to the limitations of FDM 3D printing.
.
Secondly, the design of the exoskeleton is carried over into the development of an
exoskeleton configurator. With this configurator, the parts required for assembling
the exoskeleton can be generated. The configurator comes with an easy-to-use in-
terface, where the user can enter the four specified parameters. A key aspect of the
configurator, is the ability to run on all operating systems. The program is distributed
over GitHub with an open-source license.
.
Finally, this project looks into the use of 3D scanning for exoskeleton design. A method
for creating exoskeletons by 3D scanning a person’s arm is presented. With this
method, a person’s arm is scanned with a smartphone or tablet. An exoskeleton is
then designed around the 3D model of the arm. This method is tested using an arm
from a mannequin doll. The mannequin’s arm is scanned with an Apple iPad Pro
using the application Scaniverse. This combination is selected because of the iPad
Pro’s built-in LiDAR scanner and Scaniverse’s easy-to-use interface. The 3D model
of the arm is imported into a CAD software, where a sleeve is designed around the
arm. The outcome states that the dimensions of the arm’s 3D model is to inaccurate,
hence the sleeve is to small. Therefore, the 3D scanning technology must mature
before the method can be incorporated into exoskeleton design.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Every year 12.000 people in Denmark are diagnosed with apoplexy, which is com-
monly known as having a stroke. Out of these 12.000 people, 2-3.000 dies as a re-
sult of the stroke. The rest have to live with the consequences of the stroke[1]. But
what is a stroke exactly? A stroke occurs when a clot of material is closing one of the
many arteries in the brain. This leads to a loss of control in one half of the body. This
is often seen as loss of movement in arms or/and legs in the particular side. For peo-
ple surviving a stroke, this loss of control is not just temporary and results in the need
for long term rehabilitation. With rehabilitation, a stroke patient’s loss of mobility can
be significantly reduced. A patient, who participate in the correct physical therapy
program, will perform 65% better than patients who is not undergoing treatment[2].
.
The high number of strokes are not just present in Denmark. In the European Union
back in 2017, 1.12 million people were hit by a stroke while 9.53 million are living in
the repercussions of a stroke[3]. The number of stroke victims has risen dramatically
the last decades, with a 68% increase in first time stroke victims globally between
1990 and 2010[4]. With the life expectancy of Europeans steadily increasing[5] and
with 75% of stroke victims being over the age of 65[6], the number of stroke victims is
projected to increase even more in the upcoming decades.
.
With the increase in stroke patients and the constantly increasing pressure on the
healthcare sector, an alternative for stroke rehabilitation is needed. This alternative
can be the use of technology. Currently several robotic technologies intended for
upper-limp rehabilitation exists, such as endpoint manipulators, cable suspensions
and exoskeletons[7]. Of these three listed options, exoskeletons are the most uni-
versal and portable option. It is also the most user-friendly and non-frightening solu-
tion, as it allows for rehabilitation within people’s own homes instead of a hospital or
physiotherapy clinic. The problem with exoskeletons, compared to end-point manip-
ulators and cable suspensions, is the lack of a one-fits-all solution. Unlike these two
technologies, exoskeletons are dependable on the patient’s arm’s dimensions. One
type of exoskeleton does therefore not fit all patients, as people’s arm length and widt
varies from person to person. This is apparent in even small local areas, as a study
conducted by the Department of Anatomy at the IMS & SUM Hospital in Odisha, In-
dia showed in 2015. In this study, 213 males and 87 females aged between 17 and
25, showed male arm length ranging from 30.5 to 37.9 cm and female arm length
ranging from 29.2 to 34.7 cm[8]. This shows the need for either an adaptable ex-
oskeleton, which easily can be adapted to the individual user or a method for fast and
easy generation of user-specific exoskeletons.
1.2
Project context
This thesis project was carried out as a part of the European ReHyb project. The Re-
Hyb project aims to develop an upper-limp exoskeleton which uses its own sensing
and actuation to generate a digital twin of the user. The idea is, to create an ad-
vanced cognitive system which will interact with the digital twin, to control the ex-
Page 1 of 86
==PAGE:7==
oskeleton in a way that respect the user’s neuromechanical and psychological sta-
tus. This cognitive system will have the capability to predict user behavior and derive
personalized interaction strategies to motivate the user[9].
In shorter terms, the project aims to develop an upper-limb exoskeleton and ecosys-
tem, which allow elderly stroke patients to be rehabilitated in their own homes.
.
As a part of the consortium of the ReHyb project, the Technical University of Den-
mark (DTU) has its own dedicated research group for developing the digital twin en-
gine. However, due to delays from the ReHyb exoskeleton manufacturer IUVO, the
group has no dedicated exoskeleton for collecting data to the digital twin engine. To
overcome this obstacle, a prototype LEGO exoskeleton was developed. This ex-
oskeleton works by obtaining data to the digital engine, but is very basic in its data
collection, as it only measures the angle of the elbow joint and the angle of the wrist.
This exoskeleton also has the flaw of not fitting all people, as it was designed to fit
its developers arms. This means it only fit users who are around the same height
and weight as the original developers and not people who either have longer arms,
thicker arms or shorter arms. Another issue with the LEGO exoskeleton, is the use
of LEGO Mindstorms motors, which the LEGO corporation discontinued in 2022[10].
New motors are consequently, no longer available if needed. Therefore a new solu-
tion is required until the IUVO exoskeleton finishes its development.
.
This thesis project aims to develop a new exoskeleton which easily can be adapted
to fit the individual user. This exoskeleton’s main purpose is to replace the current
LEGO exoskeleton used by the ReHyb research group at DTU. This project will also
focus on developing methods for generating user-specific exoskeletons, to make it
easier and faster to build an exoskeleton for research and digital twin developing pur-
poses. Therefore, the use of 3D scanning and 3D printing for exoskeleton develop-
ment will be investigated, with the long term goal of implementing these methods in
clinics across Europe.
1.3
Added value
The main contribution from this project, is a new and improved exoskeleton for the
DTU ReHyb research group. As this exoskeleton is based on 3D printing and open-
source components, is it easy to develop further for the group’s particular needs. It
will therefore function as a basis for future projects as it easily can be modified. With
the exoskeleton also being adaptable, can it be configured to fit whomever is working
on a project with the group. This will make testing and implementation of exoskeleton
data in the digital twin easier.
.
The project will also look into the development of methods for generating user-specific
exoskeletons. The short term value of this method, is the ease of user-specific ex-
oskeleton creation for the DTU ReHyb group, which allows for more exoskeletons
being in use at the same time. Every time a student starts a project, they can easily
generate a form-fitting exoskeleton, which they can use for their specific project. This
method is made easy to use, so everyone regards of educational background can
generate an exoskeleton for their project.
.

1.4
Research questions and objectives
This thesis addresses the development of a new user-specific exoskeleton platform
and a method for easy generation and production of said exoskeleton. This exoskele-
ton needs to be able to replace the current LEGO based platform and will therefore
need to be able to communicate with already developed software platforms. In partic-
ularly the digital twin engine. The overall scope for the project is as follows:
”How can we use 3D scanning and printing to develop a method for creat-
ing user-adaptable upper-limp exoskeletons, which can communicate with
existing software platforms?”
This research question covers a broad range of tasks, especially with the transition
from the old LEGO exoskeleton platform to the new platform. The research question
can therefore be split into the following objectives of the project.
• Identify ways to use existing 3D scanning technologies to create user-specific
exoskeletons using 3D printing technologies.
• Identify and select the most capable motor hardware to satisfy system specifi-
cations and compatibility.
• Convert existing exoskeleton control software to work with new hardware.
• Implement user input sensing to exoskeleton for data acquisition and improved
control logic.
• Identify ways to keep the exoskeleton sanitary for long term use.
• Evaluate the end product in terms of user experience.
Each of these objectives will be covered throughout the thesis in a natural order of
development and not chronically in the way they are listed.

2.1
Upper-limp exoskeletons
Upper-limp exoskeletons are electronic and mechanical systems which are designed
to assist, augment, or enhance a humans upper-limp capabilities. Upper-limp ex-
oskeletons are used in industrial and military applications, where they are used to
enhance the strength and endurance of the user. They are also used in medical ap-
plications, where they assists patients in rehabilitation and exercise[11]. This section
provides a brief history of exoskeletons, the most important design parameters, and
an overview of the existing exoskeletons, which are relevant to this project.
2.1.1
History
Even though exoskeletons often is presented in media as futuristic devices, they
have existed since the 1960’s. The first powered exoskeleton is considered to be the
Hardiman, which was developed by General Electric and the US Armed Forces[12].
It was a full-body exoskeleton, with the purpose of enhancing soldiers capabilities,
which to this day still is an active research field. The Hardiman was projected to be
capable of lifting 680 kg, which are approximately 25 times stronger than a normal
human being. However, this strength came at a cost. The Hardiman was projected
to weigh around 680 kg[13]. The project was stopped due to being held back by the
technology of the time.
.
As with the Hardiman, most exoskeletons throughout the 70’s, 80’s and 90’s were
held back by technological limitations. Motors and actuators was tethered, which
removed the portability of exoskeletons, and where no longer a better choice, com-
pared to endpoint manipulators for rehabilitation. Rehabilitation exoskeletons did not
leave the laboratories to hit the market until the 21st century. An example of this, is
the Myomo e100 NeuroRobotic System[15]. This exoskeleton was specifically de-
veloped for stroke rehabilitation. The exoskeleton uses EMG1 sensors for sensing
the users desired movement. The EMG signals is then used to move the single joint
of the exoskeleton, which is located at the elbow. The e100 therefore only offers 1
DoF2, with the flexion and extension of the forearm.
.
The next step in rehabilitation exoskeletons is in the control software and the inter-
faces. The ReHyb projects aims to fuel this evolution with the implementation of the
digital twin and the cognitive system[9].

2.1.2
Design parameters
When designing an exoskeleton, many factors needs to be accounted for. Unfortu-
nately, there is no design standard for developing upper-limp exoskeleton systems.
However, the different factors can still be categorized. In this section, the most im-
portant design factors for upper-limp exoskeletons will be discussed, with a focus on
positives and negatives.
• The mechanism of an exoskeleton is related to how the mechanism of a human
arm functions. A human has seven DoFs: Shoulder extension/flexion, shoulder
adduction/abduction, shoulder internal/external, elbow extension/flexion, lower-
arm pronation/supination, wrist extension/flexing wrist extension/flexion, and
wrist radial/ulnar deviation[16]. However, most of these come from the shoul-
der. An exoskeleton can therefore be categorized in number of DoFs, or more
simply in the assistance of shoulder movements. A system which does not in-
clude shoulder assistance has a simpler mechanism, than systems which in-
clude the shoulder movement. The complexity of an exoskeleton’s mechanism
is directly linked to the number of DoFs.
• The actuation is what separates an active exoskeleton from a passive one.
Without actuators, an exoskeleton cannot move the user’s arm and is therefore
not useful for rehabilitation. The choice of actuation methods is dependable
on the number of DoF. For simple exoskeletons with one or two DoFs, elec-
tric motors are the most favorable actuation method. Electric motor choices
Page 6 of 86
==PAGE:12==
are abundant and comes in many shapes and sizes. They provide a quick re-
sponse and high control precision. However, motors generally provide a low
power-to-mass ratio, which compromises the portability of complex exoskele-
tons with more than two DoFs. Complex upper-limp exoskeleton will become to
heavy, with several electric motors. A way to circumvent this, is by either using
a linked transmission or by using other actuation methods like pneumatic- and
hydraulic actuators. These types of actuation works on the same principle: The
use of pressure. Pneumatic actuators uses compressed air and valves to cre-
ate motion while hydraulic actuators use a compressed fluid. These actuators
have a higher power-to-weight ratio than electric motors, but requires heavy
support equipment like pumps and and air compressors[17].
• The transmission of an exoskeleton is dependent on the actuation method. Ex-
oskeletons with pneumatic- and hydraulic actuators uses, by design, always a
linked transmission. The power produced by the pump, or compressor, is linked
to the movement point by tubes. The choice of transmission is more relevant
for electric motors. Electric motors can be attached directly at the movement
point, for example at the elbow joint. This transmission is called direct drive.
The motor can also be geared with a gearbox for increasing the torque trans-
lated into the movement. Lastly, an electric motor can also be linked from a
remote point. This linkage is often in form of a cable running through a PTFE3
tube. This is specifically called a bowden setup. By using a Bowden setup, the
mass of the electric motors can be moved to other parts of the body than the
arm. This allows for a lighter exoskeleton and is useful for complex exoskele-
tons[18].
• The sensing method defines the way an exoskeleton senses the user’s move-
ments. For an exoskeleton to function as intended, one or more sensing meth-
ods are needed. The most utilized sensing method is position sensing. Posi-
tion sensors provide exact information of an exoskeleton’s spatial configuration,
which can be used for analyzing kinematics and dynamics of the system. The
position can be measured either directly or indirectly using a variety of sensors,
including encoders, potentiometers, or transducers.
A sensor type used in assistive exoskeletons, is force sensors. These sensors
sense the direct input from the user, which can be translated into torque. This
is necessary for applications where the user needs assistance, without the ex-
oskeleton doing all the lifting[18]. Another way of sensing the user’s input, is
with EMG sensors. EMG sensors measures the electrical activity produced
by muscles when the muscles are activated. The electrical signal can be di-
rectly correlated with the amount force the user puts into the movement. An
issue when using EMG sensing for exoskeletons, is calibration and noise. EMG
sensor signals are often noisy which is especially apparent with cheap elec-
trodes[19].
• The control approach of an exoskeleton is based on its application. If the ex-
oskeleton is controlled with teleoperation, position/velocity control is often uti-
lized. Here a predefined trajectory is set, which the exoskeleton follows. This

2.2.1
3D scanning
3D scanning is the process of analyzing a real-world object by digitally capturing the
object shape using cameras and sensors[23]. The data is used to construct a digital
3D model of the object. A 3D scanner can be based on a number of different tech-
nologies. These technologies range from structured light 3D scanners, which mea-
sures the shape of an object using projected light patterns and a camera system[24],
to LiDAR4 and Time Of Flight sensors. Both of these sensors determine the range
to an object by shining a laser light at an object and then measure the time for the
reflected light to return to the receiver[25]. This method is also known as 3D laser
scanning and is one of the main methods used in commercial 3D scanners. Another
method for 3D scanning, is the use of photogrammetry. Photogrammetry works by
obtaining reliable information about physical objects using photographic images. For
3D modelling, this reliability comes from taking many pictures from several angles of
an object. This method has been used for hundreds of years. A demonstration of the
concept is described in Albrecht Dürer’s text, A Painter’s Manual from 1525, where it
depicts two men creating a geometrically accurate drawing of a lute[26]. Compared
to the 3D laser scanning techniques, photogrammetry does not require special hard-
ware and is in modern 3D scanners utilized using normal cameras. The heavy lifting
is instead performed by the software. For many applications, photogrammetry based
scanners, like the Creality CR-Scan 015, takes hundreds of pictures and requires ei-
ther the camera to be moved around the object or for the object to rotate. For the
usage of 3D scanning in this project, this can be an issue because of how arms are
attached to the body. As a consequence of this issue, the University of Pisa is cur-
rently developing a handheld 3D scanner, which only needs 6 pictures from different
angles to create a 3D model of an arm[27]. This scanner shows great promise but is
not yet on the market. This thesis project will therefore look into the use of commer-
cial 3D scanning applications for smartphones and tablets. This will be presented in
chapter 5.

The concept of 3D printing covers various additive manufacturing methods for pro-
ducing a 3D object. To keep things simple, only two of these methods are covered:
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) and Stereolithography (SLA). FDM 3D printing is
the most utilized 3D printing technique[28]. A FDM 3D printer manufactures an ob-
ject by depositing molten plastic in layers. These layers are stacked until the desired
model is created, as illustrated on figure 2. To generate a 3D model with a FDM 3D
printer, the model is first parsed into a slicer, which is a program that translates the
3D model into tool paths for the 3D printer[29]. The resolution of a FDM 3D printer is
normally in the 0.1 to 0.4 mm range, due to size of the heated nozzle [29].
.

light. Like the FDM printer, this is done layer by layer, by dipping the build surface
into a tray containing the resin. The UV light is projected unto the build plate using a

Design specifications
As mentioned in 2.3, the aim of this project is to design and develop a new exoskele-

3.1.1
Lego Exoskeleton specifications
The existing LEGO exoskeleton is build using LEGO Technic bricks and is using
LEGO Mindstorms Evolution 3 (EV3) sensors and actuators. Specifically, the ex-
oskeleton is using three large EV3 motors; One for the wrist and two for the elbow.
Each of the large EV3 motors produce a stall torque of 40 Ncm[35]. The new mo-
tor, or motors must produce a stall torque of 80 Ncm or higher. Another desired up-
grade from the research group, is the avoidance of using plastic gears. The LEGO
exoskeleton transmits the motor power to the arm using plastic LEGO gears. Under
heavy load these can slip and the motors will reach their desired motor position while
the arm will not have reached its desired position[36].
Each EV3 motor contains a rotary encoder to determine the motor’s position, even
if the motor is not running. The exoskeleton always knows the angle of the elbow,
which is important for both stability and safety reasons. The new exoskeleton must,
therefore, include an encoder or a similar sensor, for determining the motor position.
To control the LEGO exoskeleton, a Raspberry PI SOC8 with a Dexter Industries
BrickPi39 expansion board is utilized. This allows the Raspberry Pi to control the
LEGO EV3 motors, which normally is controlled by a LEGO Mindstorms control unit.
The new exoskeleton must also run on a Raspberry Pi, to simplify the implementation

Control electronics
As stated on the list of specifications in 3.1.4, the exoskeleton needs to be based

for the project is therefore a Raspberry Pi 4B, as this model is the one the DTU Re-

Motor
The core of each design is a motor. This motor is used as the motor’s elbow joint
and for nudging, or moving the arm, in a desired direction. As mentioned in the list
of specifications, this motor needs to be at least as strong as 2 LEGO EV3 motors or
ideally even stronger. The motor also needs to run on 12V in order for it to work with
the current battery solution. However, as most motors work within a voltage range,
motors with a voltage specified to be over 12V will also be a feasible solution.
.
The first design is based around a GM4632-370 24V DC motor with a built-in en-
coder and a worm-gear gearbox, shown on figure 3. This motor was chosen due
to a long lead time on the ordered motors, while this motor was already in stock at
the DTU ReHyb Gazelt laboratory, where it was taken from a Capra robot. At first,
this motor seems like a perfect choice for the project. It can operate at 12V while still
maintaining a high torque, due to its 80 RPM worm-gear gearbox[39]. It also comes
with a built-in encoder, similar to the LEGO EV3 motor, and will therefore be easy to
implement in existing control software. Yet, this motors biggest strength is also be its
biggest weakness. One of the features of a worm-gear gearbox, is the ability to self-
lock. This means that the axle of the gearbox will not rotate unless the motor axle ro-
tates. It is therefore not possible to rotate the motor axle by turning the gearbox axle.
This locking is possible due to the friction coefficient between the worm13 and gear-
box gear being larger than the tangent of the worm’s lead angle[40] (see figure 4).
This is a problem for the exoskeleton, as the user needs to be able to move their arm
independently of the motor. The motor and gearbox is therefore not suitable for the
new generation of exoskeletons. However, it still has its uses for a special case ex-
oskeleton, where the user does not need to be able to move their arm independently
and the exoskeleton needs to be able to move the user’s arm.

3.2.3
EMG sensor
One of the goals of this project, is the implementation of user input sensing in the ex-
oskeleton. The idea of adding user input sensing is to be able to measure how much
the user is doing themselves, when moving their arm while wearing the exoskeleton.
This measurement can be used in everything from machine learning algorithms and
user progression monitoring to feedback control of the exoskeleton.
At first, the idea is to use EMG sensors for sensing the user input, just like the My-
omo e100 NeuroRobotic System. These EMG sensors needs to be able to connect
to the Raspberry Pi and not require proprietary software to run. The choice of EMG
sensor is the Myoware Muscle sensor from Advanced Technologies. This is an EMG
sensor created for microcontroller applications and is suitable for this project. The
Myoware sensor outputs a voltage signal, which rises towards the supply voltage
when a muscle is contracted[44]. In order to measure this voltage an additional analog-
to-digital converter (ADC) is needed, as the Raspberry Pi does not have a build-in
ADC. An ADS1115 ADC from Sparkfun is used, as it comes with a simple I2C com-
munication interface[45] and an existing Sparkfun supported library for the Raspberry
Pi[46].
.
Before the sensor is implemented in the overall system, a small test-setup is created.
Using the Raspberry Pi, a simple script is created, which prints the output of the sen-
sor to the terminal. This is where the problem with the Myoware sensor occurs: The
sensor do not change its output when a muscle is contracted. The sensor outputs
half the supply voltage with very small noise fluctuations. Even when replacing the
electrodes, nothing happens. The test is therefore considered a failure and the plan
of implementing EMG sensing in the system is discarded. An idea for an alternative
sensing method is then conceived, which is covered in 3.2.5. This method will also
make use of the ADS1115 ADC and the same test code is reused.

on figure 10. However, after implementing the stepper motor in the third design, the
implementation of the ACS172 is not as easy. As seen on figure 11, a stepper mo-
tor requires four wires instead of a DC motor’s two wires. This is due to the stepper
motor having 4 coils, which is turned on and off individually, which makes the motor
Page 19 of 86
==PAGE:25==
move in incremental steps, hence the name ”stepper motor”[50]. Therefore an alter-
native solution is needed. If another motor driver configuration is used, like the stan-
dard size driver modules used in CNC machines and 3D printers, the stepper motors
current draw can be measured directly. However, as this will require either a new
expansion board or a self-made one, another approach is needed. As the Adafruit
Motor HAT do not power the Raspberry Pi, the Motor HAT’s current draw can be as-
sumed to almost exclusively be from the stepper motor. Other current drawing com-
ponents like the ADC and the ACS172 draws significantly less power than the step-
per motor, and can therefore be neglected[45], [49]. The ACS172 is placed in series
with the Motor HAT, to measure the total current used by the system. This can be
seen on figure 11. As shown on the figure, the output of the ACS172 is connected
to the ADS1115’s second input channel A1, while the ADC’s first input channel A0 is
connected to the 3.3V rail on the Motor HAT. The reason for this will be explained in
3.4.3, as the ACS172’s reference voltage VCC affects the conversion from a voltage
signal to a current value.

3.2.6
Encoder
For the first two designs, the choice of encoder is determined by the motor type, as
the two DC motors comes with a built-in encoder. Fortunately both DC motors have
Page 20 of 86
==PAGE:26==
the same encoder, which simplified the firmware development, which will be pre-
sented in 3.4.4. However, as the stepper motor do not have a built-in encoder, an
encoder for the third design has to be chosen. Here the choice is the AS5600 mag-
netic encoder[51]. This choice is based on the use of this particular encoder by the
Youtube Content Creator Curious Scientist[52]. In his video, Curious Scientist uses
the AS5600 encoder for monitoring the position of a Nema 17 stepper motor, which is
a similar type of stepper motor to the utilized Nema 14. It is therefore decided to use
this encoder for the stepper motor exoskeleton design.
The AS5600 is a 12-bit magnetic rotary position sensor. It measures the angle of a
diametric magnetized on-axis magnet, which is included with the encoder. To read
data from the encoder, either an analog signal or a PWM signal can be measured.
However, in this project the AS5600 I2C interface is used. This resulted in a bit more
difficult firmware development since there do not exist any existing Python libraries
for the AS5600. This results development of one specifically for this project. This de-
velopment will be discussed in 3.4.4.
3.3
Mechanical design
As mentioned in 3.2.2, the design of each exoskeleton is based around the selected
motor. This is due to the exoskeletons being of a direct drive design, which means
the motors are placed right next to an anatomical arm joint. In this case the elbow.
This is feasible, as this exoskeleton is a fairly simple exoskeleton with only one DoF.
In more complex exoskeletons, with more DoF’s, like shoulder and wrist rotation, an-
other transmission solution will likely have been chosen to move the weight away
from the arm.
.
During development of the exoskeletons, different parts changed during each itera-
tion. Each of this changes will be explained in the respective design’s section. How-
ever, several parts are reused throughout the different designs. The design of these
parts will be covered during the description of the first design.
.
Before any 3D printing begins, a 3D model of the exoskeleton is designed in a CAD15
program. Specifically the exoskeleton is designed in SolidWorks, which is a paramet-
ric feature-based modeling software developed originally at MIT and later acquired
and published by Dassault Systems. This CAD software is chosen due to its easy to
understand interface and the author’s previous experience with the software.

The first exoskeleton design was the hardest challenge, as both the second and the

parts, a motor, and several nuts and bolts, which formed the mechanical basis for the
exoskeleton. On figure 12 a 3D CAD model of the first exoskeleton design is shown.
On the model only the main mechanical parts, like 3D printed parts and motor, is
shown. Nuts, bolts, straps, wires, and electronics are omitted, as these did not affect
the mechanical structure of the exoskeleton.

Software implementation
The control software for the exoskeleton is written in Python, because of the simplic-

.

script B. Homola and C. Serrie created for the LEGO exoskeleton[32]. The changes

with the new functions, which will be described in the upcoming sections.

3.4.2
Motor control
The main change to the exoskeleton control software, is the motor control script. This
script is used for controlling the elbow motor of the exoskeleton. It is also used for
measuring the current running through the motor, to determine the torque provided
by motor. As stated in 3.4.1, functions from the motor script is called from the UDP
communication script, where inputs from the server makes the exoskeleton nudge
the user in a given direction. The current measuring function is located within the mo-
tor control script, allowing the current to be measured with only one function. This
function will be covered in 3.4.3. This script also includes the functions for controlling
the DC motors and reading the angle from their encoders, as they may be utilized
once again in another exoskeleton.
Script setup
As always, the script starts by importing the necessary libraries. Here the most im-
portant, non-standard, libraries are: The Adafruit Motor Kit library, the Adafruit Mo-
tor library, and the Adafruit ADS1x15 library. The Adafruit Motor Kit library and the
Adafruit Motor library are used for controlling motors using the Raspberry Pi Motor
HAT while the ADS1x15 library is used for the Analog-Digital-Converter. A custom
library for the AS5600 encoder, which is described in detail in 3.4.4, is also imported.
After importing the libraries, the necessary objects are set up. This includes the mo-
tor kit object, the encoder object for the DC motor encoders, the AS5600 encoder ob-
ject, and the ADC object. Using the ADC object, two ADC channels opens for mea-
suring voltage data. The use of these will be covered in 3.4.3. Lastly, the start angle
of the AS5600 encoder is set. More on this in 3.4.4.
DC motor control
The set_DC_motor_position function is the first function created. This function di-
rectly replicates the BrickPi3 set_motor_position function. However, the function is
not a direct replica, as it also includes a fail safe to avoid the exoskeleton trying to
Page 33 of 86
==PAGE:39==
move to an impossible position. The limits are set to 0 and 100 degrees. The 0 de-
grees makes sure the user’s arm is not bend the wrong way, outside a human arm’s
extension ROM18[55]. The 100 degrees is the maximum angle the exoskeleton can
move to from a flat position, without hitting itself.
The function takes an input angle and moves the exoskeleton elbow joint until the
given angle is achieved. It does this by converting the angle to a number of steps,
which is dependent on the gearbox of the motor. The number of steps can be calcu-
lated by
steps = PPR · angle ·
1
360
(3)
where PPR is the Pulses Per Revolution, also known as the number of steps for one
full rotation. This number is found by making a small test script, which continuously
prints the encoder steps to the terminal, while the motor axle is manually turned a full
rotation. This results in a PPR of 1800 for the worm gear motor and a PPR of 101
for the standard geared motor. Using the calculated steps, the function checks if the
input angle is larger or smaller than the current angle. If it is larger, the motor spins in
the positive direction, flexing the arm. If it is smaller, the motor spins it in the negative

3.5
Chapter summary
In this chapter the design of an exoskeleton was covered. This design had to follow
the design specifications listed in 3.1.4, which are based on the previous LEGO ex-
oskeleton’s specifications and some additional specifications wanted by the DTU Re-
hyb group.
The third and final design is based around the Nema 14 stepper motor and an AS5600
magnetic encoder. This design is chosen due to the strength of the stepper motor
and the lack of other options. However, the direct drive motor design came with a
cost: Lack of torque. The stepper motor is stronger than the other motors, but still
only produces a holding torque of 14 Ncm. In comparison, the LEGO exoskeleton’s
motors produces a total holding torque of 80 Ncm, by using 2 motors with internal
gearboxes. Despite this specification not being met, other more important specifica-
tions meet the requirements. These are the specifications which results in the new
exoskeleton being a drop-in replacement for the LEGO exoskeleton. The new de-
sign provides the same elbow angle data as the previous, together with the addition
of input sensing. This input sensing is, however, not optimal with a weak motor, but
functions as a great foundation for a future design with a geared motor.

Configurator development
In the previous chapter it is established, how to design an exoskeleton based on the
four parameters: Wrist width, lower-arm length, biceps width, and upper-arm length.
This allows the exoskeleton to be easy customizable to the individual user. Yet, this
solution requires the user to have experience in SolidWorks or other CAD software
together with access to a CAD license. Another way to generate the files is there-
fore needed, to keep the manufacturing of the exoskeleton as easy as possible. This
leads to the development of a configurator which can run on any Windows or Linux
PC, without the need for any installation of SolidWorks, other CAD software, or even
Python packages. The configurator prompts the user with a simple UI20, where the
user can enter the four parameters. By clicking the ”Generate files” button, all the
STL files are generated in the program folder. The STL files can then be imported
into any slicer software and printed on any regular sized 3D printer.
The configurator is developed in Python using the CadQuery library for 3D mod-
elling[61], the Tkinter library for creating the user interface[62], and the cx_Freeze
library for generation executable files[63].
4.1
3D model generation
When generating the 3D models, the CadQuery API is utilized. CadQuery is an Open-
Source Python library used for building parametric 3D CAD models. According to the
creators, CadQuery uses an intuitive construction principle, where scripts are read as
a human would describe an object verbally[61].
4.1.1
Program and model structure
For each exoskeleton part, the configurator has a generation-function. Each of these
functions takes their respective measurement as input and exports the corresponding
part as STL file.
4.1.2
Biceps and wrist mounts
The biceps and wrist mounts is, like with the SolidWorks CAD model, build almost
identically. The only difference is the mounting structure. To keep the models scal-
able, each mount is based on three parameters: The wrist or biceps width, a wall
thickness, and a length. The wrist and biceps sizes come from the function inputs,
which is the values the user enters into the UI. The wall thickness and length are val-
ues set inside the function. These can easily be changed if needed. This is a useful
design feature, for updating the configurator from the first exoskeleton design to the
third design. The new biceps mounts can be generated by removing the code for the
bottom mount and changing the mounts length from 30mm to 56mm (see figure 14
and 25 for comparison).
Like in SolidWorks, models are created using cuts and extrusions. However, as ev-
erything needs to be coded, model generation is less sophisticated and often re-
quires simplification of models. This is evident from the first extrusion, the arc of the

wrist mount. In the CAD model in SolidWorks, a nice 5mm thick arc is drawn which is
extruded. In the CadQuery model, a half-circle with a diameter of the input wrist size
plus 2 times the wall thickness is extruded. A hole, with the wrist diameter, is then cut
through this model which results in the same arc. It often takes more cuts and extru-

4.1.6
CadQuery limitations
When modelling the exoskeleton using the CadQuery API, some problems and lim-
itations arose. Mainly the lack of features like circular and linear pattern results in
some limitations. These features can be coded into the model using for-loops. How-
ever, especially with the circular pattern, the code for the model will be way more
complex. CadQuery does provide a fillet feature, but the use of it is more compli-
cated than in SolidWorks, where an edge can be filleted with the click of a mouse.
A fully featured and rounded exoskeleton can be modelled with the CadQuery API,
but it requires more time and focus than this project can provide. The CadQuery
model is therefore a simplified version of the SolidWorks model. The model is not as
feature-full and does not have rounded edges on the wrist and biceps mounts. Even
with these limitation, the structure is the same and it fulfills the same role.

4.3
Creating the executable program
An issue when creating programs in Python, is the programs compatibility with oper-
ating systems. Python programs are easier to run on systems running a Linux oper-
ating system, rather than Windows based systems, because of the open-source na-
ture of Linux. Python is especially easy to work with on the Ubuntu operating system
and it derivatives, as these have Python as a part of their base system. However,
Page 45 of 86
==PAGE:51==
as of December 2022 only 2.93% of laptop and desktops uses a version of Linux as
their operating system. Compared to this, 75.34% uses Microsoft Windows as their
operating system[64]. Therefore, the configurator needs to be able to run on Win-
dows, to fulfill the goal of being easy to use.
.
Installing Python on Windows is as easy as installing other programs on Windows.
The problem is the use of the Python Package Index (PyPi) for installing Python li-
braries like CadQuery. PyPi requires changes to environment variables on Windows,
before Python scripts can import various libraries. This can be a haunting task, if you
have no experience in Advanced system settings on Windows. Therefore, an easier
and more user friendly solution is needed. Luckily, this solution comes in the form of
the Python cx_Freeze library[63]. This library can be used to create Python executa-
bles, that can run on any operating system. With the use of cx_Freeze, a program
can be created, which can run on Windows, Linux, and MacOS, which makes it ac-
cessible to all users.
.
Using cx_Freeze is easy. From the documentation, a setup script is provided. In this
setup script, the programs parameters are set. These are the packages which need
to be included, the desired name of the program, a short description, and the name
of the file that serves as the main file of the program. The setup script for the configu-
rator program can be found in appendix D.3.
To create the executable file, the following command is typed into the command line/ter-
minal and run:
1 python setup . py build
cx_Freeze will then create an executable file and package, which can be distributed
and run on all systems.
4.4
3D printing orientation and post processing
An important aspect of 3D printing is the model placement and orientation. To get the
best results with 3D printing, the user must prevent large overhangs and use print
support if needed. Therefore, is it important to place the models correctly, when load-
ing the files into a slicer. As a general rule, the largest surfaces are placed on the
build plate, to avoid overhangs and the use of supports.[65]. This general rule ap-
plies to the exoskeleton parts, which can be printed on a Prusa I3 Mk3S+ without the
use of supports. By not using supports, the models needs very little post processing
and are ready to assemble almost immediately. Unfortunately, all the models cannot
fit on one build plate, which means either two printers are needed or the parts needs
to be printed in two batches.

5
3D scanning for exoskeleton design
In this chapter, the use of 3D scanning for designing exoskeletons is investigated.
This idea involves scanning a patients arm, uploading the 3D scan to a program,
which then automatically generates the files for assembling an exoskeleton, that fits
the patient perfectly. Due to limitation of time, a program which can generate an ex-
oskeleton from a 3D scan is never developed. The large scope of the project is also
a reason for not prioritizing this development any further. However, a test of concept
is conducted, where a mannequin arm is scanned and the model is put into Solid-
Works.
5.1
Idea proposition
The general idea is to develop a method, where a physician or physiotherapist scans
a stroke patient’s arm with a smartphone. Hereafter, the 3D scanned model is up-
loaded to a program and the necessary files for 3D printing a perfect-fit exoskeleton
is generated. The exoskeleton is then printed on a standard 3D printer and ready to
assemble the next day. In this ideal scenario, no CAD experience is needed.
5.2
Test of concept
To test the viability of using 3D scanning for designing exoskeleton, a small test is set
up. In this test, the arm of a mannequin doll from the ReHyb student lab is scanned
using an iPad. The data iss then imported into a CAD program, in this case Solid-
Works, where the model is used to create a fitted sleeve, which in theory will be a
perfect fit for the mannequin arm. This section covers all the steps from the scanning
to the model generation.
5.2.1
Required hardware and software
For 3D scanning the mannequin arm, an Apple iPad Pro 6th generation iss acquired.
This exact model is selected due to its build-in LiDAR sensor[66]. As mentioned in
2.2.1, this sensor determines the range to an object by shining a laser light at an
object and then measure the time for the reflected light to return to the receiver[25].
This method provides more precise models than photogrammetry alone. The great
thing about using a smartphone, or tablet, with a LiDAR sensor, is the combination
of photogrammetry and LiDAR data. This combination of scanning method is used in
the application Scaniverse[67], which from testing proved to be the most reliable app
for scanning and producing STL files. Other applications like Polycam and MagiScan
is tested, but none is as intuitive and easy to use as Scaniverse. Moreover, these
applications requires the scanning device to be moved in a fluent, 180 degree move-
ment around the object, which do not correspond well with scanning an arm. Scan-
ning the underside of the arm is particularly a problem for these applications.
5.2.2
Scanning and data post processing
Using the Scaniverse app on the iPad Pro, the mannequin arm is scanned and the
scan is then processed in the app. The Scaniverse app comes with a simple and

be used for interior designers. For scanning the mannequin arm, the LiDAR distance
is set to 0.8 meters.
Performing the scan is also very intuitive. With the set LiDAR distance, everything

Sanitation test
An issue one quickly can overlook and forget when designing exoskeletons, is the
sanitary problems which might occur during long time uses. As the user wears an ex-
oskeleton over longer periods, bacteria and sweat can accumulate at the wrist and
biceps mounts. This is particular an issue using 3D printed parts, as the layer struc-
ture of 3D printing provides small crevices where microbes can live. A way to over-
come this issue, is by using anti-bacterial PLA for printing the parts touching the skin.
Antibacterial PLA contains cobber, which kills 99.99% of fungi, viruses, and bacte-
ria[68]. Therefore a spool of anti material PLA is used to print a wrist mount and two
biceps mounts. These parts can be seen in figure 50.
To test if any microbes accumulates on a persons arm under an exoskeleton, a small
test is set up. In this test, a test subject works and acts normally for three periods of
two hours. In the first two hours, no exoskeleton is worn. The arm is then swabbed
for microbes, which is added to an agar plate. An agar plate is a plate build out of
agar, which is a polymer made up of the sugar galactose[69]. On this plate, the mi-
crobes, which are to small to see with the naked eye, can grow into colonies, which
can be observed and counted.
After the swab, the subject equips the exoskeleton made out of standard PLA, and
works normally for two hours while wearing the exoskeleton. Again, the arm is swabbed
for microbes which are transferred to agar plates. The procedure is then repeated
again, where the subject wears the exoskeleton with the wrist and biceps mounts
made of anti bacterial PLA. After an incubation time of a day, the number of colonies
is then counted on all the agar plates. The whole procedure can be seen step by
step below and is ready to replicate for future testing.

8
Discussion
This chapter presents a discussion of the project’s results. Here, the final exoskele-
ton design is evaluated and improvements are suggested. The methods for generat-
ing exoskeletons using the configurator and 3D scanning is also discussed and eval-
uated.
8.1
Evaluation of exoskeleton design
The exoskeleton designed in chapter 3 serves as the main contribution from this
project. In this section, the viability of this design will be determined by evaluating it
according to each of the design specifications presented in 3.1. The design will also
be compared to the DTU ReHyb group’s former platform, the LEGO exoskeleton, and
the considered EduExo.
8.1.1
Design specification evaluation
As some of the design specifications are related, like the torque and gearing, the list
of specifications are condensed into fewer points for simplicity.
Motor torque and gearing: The largest issue with the exoskeleton, is its lack of
torque. The goal was to implement a motor, which could provide a stall torque of 80
Ncm or more. With the final exoskeleton’s Nema 14 stepper motor only producing a
stall torque of 14 Ncm, this goal is far from achieved. The lack of torque comes from
the direct drive design of the exoskeleton. With no gears the Nema 14 can simply
not provide the desired torque. A solution to this problem, is the implementation of a
gearbox. However, another desired specification, is the avoidance of plastic gears,
since experience from the LEGO exoskeleton shows these can slip under load[36]. It
is therefore not recommended to simply designing and 3D printing a gearbox. This is
especially apparent with printing PLA on a FDM 3D printer, as the strength and pre-
cision is not suitable for creating small gearboxes. A solution can be to 3D print the
gears using tougher materials like nylon, inspired by the nylon gears in the LEGO
EV3’s internal gearbox. By utilizing a SLA 3D printer and a tough material, like Siraya
Tech Fast Mecha, tough, dimensional correct gears can be produced[71]. However,
as stated in 2.2.2, SLA printing is not as accessible as FDM printing and does not fit
into the open-source profile of this project.
An alternative is to use a motor with a gearbox, like the JGA25-371 used in the sec-
ond design, with a fitting gear-ratio. This solution will be presented in 8.4.
Control unit and software: For controlling the exoskeleton, a Raspberry Pi 4B is
utilized together with an Adafruit Raspberry Pi motor HAT. This allows for the ex-
oskeleton to communicate with existing software, using the UDP protocol. It is this
communication protocol which is the main decision for this choice of control unit, as
other open-source microcontrollers, like the Arduino platform, does not support the
UDP protocol. The functions for controlling the motor and encoder are written as sim-
ilar to the BrickPi3’s functions as possible. This is to allow previous DTU projects to
be easily converted to function with the new exoskeleton.
.
Page 60 of 86
==PAGE:66==
The choice of control unit is, however, a major flaw of the exoskeleton, when looking
from the open-source perspective. For the last two years, a shortage of Raspberry
Pis have affected the market and price of said Raspberry Pi’s[72]. For people and
institutions who have not stocked up on Raspberry Pis, like the DTU ReHyb group
has, acquiring a Raspberry Pi can be either difficult or expensive. Beside this, the
platform is not as plug-and-play as the Arduino platform. However, with the provided
setup script, this is not a huge challenge for anyone with a technical knowledge.
Encoder and precision: To determine the angle of the exoskeleton elbow, an AS5600
encoder is implemented in conjunction with the Nema 14 stepper motor. However,
the tests from chapter 6.2 show a significant lack of precision. The problem is so se-
vere, that the exoskeleton was unable to reach a 90 degree angle, due to the lower-
arm beam hitting the upper-arm beam. In the first test, where the exoskeleton is
commanded to move to a certain angle, the lower-arm is moved 10 to 20 degrees
higher than commanded. There is no direct correlation between the input angle and
the actual angle. Therefore, this offset cannot easily be software correlated. The sec-
ond test shows the difference between the physical angle and the encoder angle ris-
ing, as the physical angle rises. Again, this indicates no linear correlation between
the physical angle and the encoder angle.
.
This imprecision comes from the encoder and the motor-encoder setup. The connec-
tion with the two gears provide a margin of error, as the gears cannot have a tighter
fit, with the possibility of locking the gearbox. This allows the gear mounted to the
motor shaft to turn, before it intersects with the gear containing the small dipole mag-
net. Therefore, all of the stepper motor’s rotation is not measured by the encoder,
which results in the encoder not registering movements of the lower-arm. This is ap-
parent from table 6, where the encoder angles are smaller than the actual angles.
.
The last test with the second exoskeleton design shows the superiority of the JGA25-
371’s built-in encoder. The precision of the second design is not perfect, but it is
within an acceptable margin of error, compared to the third design. This precision
can be improved even further with another gearbox, as the gearbox on the motor is
built for speed. With a gear ratio of 1:4.4, the pulses per rotation is only 101. With a
higher gear ration, the pulse per rotation increases, as seen with the GM4632-370
worm gear motor’s 1600 pulses per rotation. Therefore, a new and improved design
with higher precision is presented in 8.4.
User input sensing: A major design feature, which is desired by the DTU ReHyb
group, is the ability to sense user input. With the EMG sensors not providing any
useful data, another solution is presented by the use of current sensing. The idea
is to use the relation between the current draw of the motor and the force provided
by the user. However, the testing of this feature in chapter 6.3 did not show a direct
correlation, as presented on the graphs on figure 51. This shows the current rising,
when the exoskeleton moves from 0 to 90 degrees. However, the current rises im-
mediately to around 1.1 A in both graphs. The only difference is the amount of time,
where the current is high. For the exoskeleton without load, the current is high for
around a second, while it is high for around 3 second with load. As the current rises
to the same value, the test do not prove the current sensing technique as a valid
method for input sensing. However, the reason for this can be found in the exoskele-
Page 61 of 86
==PAGE:67==
ton’s main issue: The torque and gearing. If the exoskeleton is geared, the motor
will not hit its maximum current when lifting the exoskeleton without a user. This test
must therefore be performed once again, when the exoskeleton is updated to a new
version with a gearbox.
Manufacturing: One of the main specifications is the exoskeletons ability to be 3D
printed on a normal sized 3D printer. The specification is met with all of the exoskele-
ton designs. The only part, where the size can be a problem, is the lower-arm beam.
Yet, with the third design’s adjustable lower-arm beam, which is printed in two parts,
is this problem solved for all arm lengths.
With a design that can be printed on a regular FDM 3D printer and printable in PLA
filament, the mechanical design fits well into the exoskeleton’s role as an open-source
project. This is especially apparent with the exoskeleton in conjunction with the con-
figurator. The open-source possibilities of the exoskeleton an configurator will be dis-
cussed in 8.2.
.
8.1.2
Comparison to existing exoskeletons
The exoskeleton designed in this project only has two competitors regarding its aim
as an educational exoskeleton: The LEGO exoskeleton and the EduExo. The de-
signed exoskeleton solves many of the LEGO exoskeletons issues: It can be adapted
to fit multiple users, it uses open-source electronics which are easily accessible and
not discontinued, and it is expandable due to its part being 3D printed. Yet, the new
exoskeleton falls short of the LEGO exoskeleton when it comes to torque. With two
EV3 motors, each producing 40 Ncm of stall-torque, compared to the new exoskele-
tons Nema 14 producing 14 Ncm, the LEGO exoskeleton exceeds the new exoskele-
ton with 66 Ncm. However, a simple fix to this issue is, as mentioned in 8.1.1, can be
solved by adding a gearbox to the new exoskeleton or use another motor.
Another feature the new exoskeleton lacks, is the wrist turning the LEGO exoskele-
ton has. Potentially, this can be implemented in the future, as the base platform al-
ready exists.
.
When comparing the new exoskeleton with the EduExo, the new exoskeleton is def-
initely the most advanced system. This is both a strength and a weakness. The new
exoskeleton is more advanced due to its main processor being a Raspberry Pi 4B.
This allows the exoskeleton to have wireless connectivity with communication proto-
cols like UDP, together with large compute power. The EduExo’s main processor is
an Arduino Nano, which does not have any wireless connectivity or high compute
power. However, an Arduino is more of a plug-and-play controller compared to a
Raspberry Pi. It is therefore easier to setup and program than the Raspberry Pi on
the new exoskeleton.
A potential issue with the EduExo, is the exoskeleton’s upper-arm mounting points.
The EduExo is side mounted, meaning that the arcs forming the upper-arm mounts
goes over the biceps and triceps. If the user has large upper-arms, the EduExo will
not fit the user. This is why the exoskeleton from this project is mounted to the under-
side of the upper-arm with elastic straps. The mount will not be to tight when the user
flexes their arm, as the elastic strap gives room for the biceps. The mount is, how-
Page 62 of 86
==PAGE:68==
ever, a bit tight when the user flexes their triceps. Though not in a way that destroys
the exoskeleton.
8.1.3
Overall design
With all the specifications and tests discussed regarding the exoskeleton design and
the user experience described in chapter 7.1, the overall design can be summed up
with the following points:
• The exoskeleton is 3D printable on a standard FDM 3D printer.
• It used easily accessible components, with the exception of the Raspberry Pi
SOC.
• The motor only produces 14 Ncm, which does not meet the required 80+ Ncm.
However, there is no plastic gears that can slip.
• Due to the encoder setup, the exoskeleton does not provide a precise enough
elbow angle.
• The design comes with user input sensing, but due to the motor torque issue it
is not completely validated.
• The exoskeleton can be adapted to fit people of all shapes and sizes.
• Users can easily attach the exoskeleton themselves with their free hand.
• Programming the exoskeleton requires a little programming experience but
control software is easily set up using the included setup script.

8.2
Configurator design evaluation
As presented in chapter 4, an exoskeleton configurator is developed. Based on the
exoskeleton designed in chapter 3, the configurator can generate the necessary files
for 3D printing an exoskeleton, based on the user’s four parameters: Wrist width,
lower-arm length, biceps width, and upper-arm length. The configurator ends up
working as expected and is seen as one of the main contributions from this project.
However, the exoskeleton, generated by the configurator, contains the same issues
as the final exoskeleton design: The Nema 14 stepper motor, which the exoskele-
ton is designed around, does not provide the desired torque of the exoskeleton. As
mentioned above in 8.1, the design of the exoskeleton will need to be updated in
the future. The configurator will, therefore, also need to be updated together with
the exoskeleton design. With this update, the addition of padding can also be imple-
mented, as this is a suggestion from the user test, resulting in a better feeling when
wearing the exoskeleton. This feature can be implemented with an addition of two
check marks. The two check marks can be labeled: ”Add padding to wrist” and ”Add
padding to biceps”. When the user checks off a check mark, the configurator can be
set to add a specific width to the wrist or biceps mount, corresponding to the thick-
ness of the padding.
.
With the configurator, the exoskeleton’s place as an open-source project is further
solidified. The configurator allows everyone with access to a 3D printer to create their
own exoskeleton. This project’s exoskeleton can hence be used as an educational
exoskeleton, like the EduExo. Because of the user-adaptability, this exoskeleton can
be used by everyone, from middle school students to university students. This gives
it an advantage over the EduExo, which only comes in one size. With the configura-
tor, no users need any CAD experience to create the exoskeleton.
.
The configurator can in it self also act as an open-source project. With the source
files distributed together with the exoskeleton, people have the access to see and
modify the code. This can lead to the development of modules, add-on, and other
improvements.
.
With further development, the configurator has great potential. In time, the platform
can be developed into a configurator tool for a full body exoskeleton. Parts for lower-
limp exoskeletons differentiates not by large margins from upper-limp exoskeletons.
The biceps mount, for example, can act as a thigh or shin mount, due to the variable
size.
Going forward, the configurator tool can evolve into a tool available to everyone from
student to physiotherapist.
8.3
3D scanning method
3D scanning is a technology which shows great potential for exoskeleton design.
However, it is not without its flaws. The developed method presented in chapter 5
shows both the strengths and the weaknesses of the technology. The method proves
the possibility of creating complex shapes, which follows the shape of a 3D scanned
arm, using a CAD software like SolidWorks. This shows the usage of the subtraction
Page 64 of 86
==PAGE:70==
method, which in the future can be implemented into a configurator based program.
This method is, however, held back by the 3D scanning technology. The 3D scan
performed with the Apple iPad Pro and the Scaniverse application turned out to be
imprecise. As presented in 5.2.4, the 3D model of the arm is smaller than the actual
arm, and the 3D printed sleeve does not fit. This imprecision can come from a combi-
nation of various factors, like the reflectivity of the arm and the data processing algo-
rithm of the phone. The reflectivity of the arm can be combated by covering the arm
with a non-reflective material, or by scanning a human arm. Processing algorithm is
more difficult to work around, as the application does not provide access to its source
code.
.
For future testing of the 3D scanning method, it is recommended to test all 3D scan-
ning applications for this specific use and to look into the use of other 3D scanners.
One particular interesting scanner, is the one currently under development by the
Department of Civil and Industrial Engineering at the University of Pisa[27]. This
scanner is specifically designed for scanning human arms and can construct a 3D
model of an arm with a standard deviation between 0.41 and 1.75 mm, using only
6 pictures. When, and if, this scanner becomes available for commercial use, these
precise models can be used for creating user-specific, form-fitting exoskeletons. It is
therefore recommended to keep investigating the possibility of generating exoskele-
tons from the 3D scan of an arm, even though the current commercial scanning tech-
nology is not ready. It is also recommended to contact the University of Pisa and set
up a collaboration, if a student is interested in this kind of project.
8.4
Improvement suggestions for future exoskeleton designs
With an exoskeleton design which did not meet all specifications, a future update of
the design is needed. This update does not require many changes to the design, as
the foundation is there. The biggest issues are the lack of motor torque and the en-
coder imprecision. Both of these problems can be solved at the same time with a
new motor. Here is it recommended to go back to the second design and find an-
other motor from the JGA25-371 family. This family of motors all come in the same
size, but with different gearboxes. The reason the second design did not work, is it
having a gearbox with low gearing. It is therefore recommended to order a JGA25-
371 with a higher gearing. For maximum torque, a JGA25-371 with a 1:500 gear ratio
could be a solution. This motor and gearbox combination produce a staggering 588
Ncm[73], which may be enough to move an arm.
By going back to a motor with a built-in encoder, the encoder imprecision will auto-
matically be fixed, since the built-in encoder does not have the same issues as the
AS5600. This is also proved by the motor and encoder precision test results pre-
sented in table 7.
.
The recommended next exoskeleton design will be a combination of the second and
third design. The design will use the biceps mounts with elastic straps and the ad-
justable lower-arm beam from the third design, but use the motor mount from the
second design. The only adjustments needed on any of the parts, is the mounting
holes for the biceps mounts on the upper-arm beam. However, with the parts being
designed with adjustments in mind, the holes’ location can be moved by a few dis-
Page 65 of 86
==PAGE:71==
tance values in the SolidWorks model of the second design.
The control software can be adjusted to the new design with only a few changes:
Firstly the pulses per revolution in the set_DC_motor_position function needs to be
adjusted to the new motor. Then the move_stepper function, for moving the arm up
and down in the executeNudge function, needs to be replaced with the set_DC_motor_position
function. With these three simple changes, the new design will be ready to use with
the new motor.
8.5
Reflection on project plan
Throughout the project, minor changes was added to the project objectives. In the
original objectives, decided with the project plan, the addition of user input sensing
was locked on the use of EMG sensors. However, with the failed test of the Myoware
sensors described in chapter 3.2.3, the planned implementation of EMG sensors was
discarded in favor of the current sensing.
Another change was in the selection of motor hardware. Originally, the objective fo-
cused on the motors torque-to-weight ratio. During the design specification phase,
this objective was changed to focus on several specifications, as the addition of an
encoder became essential.
.
The exoskeleton was originally planned to be tested on stroke patients undergoing
rehabilitation at Glostrup Hospital. However, the scope of the project changed from
focusing on building an exoskeleton for stroke patients, to building one for the DTU
ReHyb group. At the beginning of the project, the LEGO exoskeleton was build to
analyze its specifications. However, the author never tested the exoskeleton in sync
with a Unity game, as the LEGO exoskeleton did not fit the authors arm. The same
problem was evident with another student working on a master thesis project with the
group. It was then decided that the lack of suitable exoskeletons was a more press-
ing matter, than developing an exoskeleton for rehabilitation. This decision was also
taken on the basis of overall ReHyb project, where IUVO is developing the final ex-
oskeleton.

9
Conclusion
In this project, a user adaptable upper-limp exoskeleton is developed. The exoskele-
ton can be manufactured using a standard FDM 3D printer and uses standard off the
shelf components for all non printable parts. This include the motor, control electron-
ics and sensors. The design serves as a replacement for the LEGO exoskeleton the
DTU ReHyb group currently uses.
.
The final exoskeleton is the third design iteration. The mechanical design is based
around four variable parameters: Wrist width, lower-arm length, biceps width, and
upper-arm length. From these parameters, the exoskeleton can be configured to fit
various arm lengths and sizes. The exoskeleton has one DoF, located at the elbow
joint. For actuation, the exoskeleton uses a Nema 14 stepper motor configured with
a direct drive transmission. This configuration, however, does not provide the 80+
Ncm torque which it needs to be on par with the LEGO exoskeleton. The exoskele-
ton uses an AS5600 encoder coupled with a small 3D printed gearbox for position
sensing. This does not provide a precise angle of the exoskeleton, due to inaccura-
cies and limitations of the 3D printed gearbox. A new feature added to exoskeleton,
which the LEGO exoskeleton lacks, is user input sensing. this is implemented with
an ACS712 current sensor. This sensor measures the current draw of the system,
and hence the current draw of the motor, which is linked to the torque provided by
the motor. Unfortunately, this method is not validated, as the motor reaches its maxi-
mum current both with and without load, due to the low torque configuration.
A solution to the problems stated above, is using a DC motor with a built-in encoder
and a gearbox with a large gear ratio. A design with this configuration is proposed,
but never created and tested, due to time limitations.
.
This project also resulted in the development of an exoskeleton configurator. In this
configurator, all 3D printable parts for assembling the exoskeleton can be generated
using the four specified parameters. These parameters can be entered through an
easy-to-use interface that also contains a measurement guide. The configurator pro-
gram can run on all operating systems and is distributed with an open-source license.
This allows for students, teachers, and makers alike to built the exoskeleton them-
selves, without any previous knowledge of CAD. The target group for this configura-
tor, is future DTU students working on projects for the DTU ReHyb group.
.
In the project, a method for creating an exoskeleton using 3D scanning is proposed
and tested. However, the test is unsuccessful as the 3D scanning dimensions are not
correct, which results in the 3D printed exoskeleton sleeve not fitting the arm. The
method shows the possibility of creating complex form fitting exoskeleton parts, but is
held back by the 3D scanning capabilities of a tablet.
