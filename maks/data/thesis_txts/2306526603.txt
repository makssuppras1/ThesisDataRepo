Abstract 
 
 
Scientific studies have for many years proven the human health effects of the marine omega-3 fatty acids. 
Of these EPA and DHA are important in brain development of infants and contribute to normal body 
functions, such as vision, brain function and preventing cardio vascular diseases in adults. However, at 
present the world ocean’s supply of sea-food cannot withstand the demand, since both the aquaculture 
industry use fish oil in the feed and production of fish oil supplements, known as nutraceuticals. Alternative 
sources of EPA and DHA is found in microalgae.  Nannochloropsis salina has in this study been cultivated in 
process water from Novozymes in Kalundborg. This is a very promising and environmental beneficial 
production of the much needed EPA and DHA. Using microalgae as an EPA source does not only protect fish 
populations in the ocean but also secure a product that is free from dioxin, PCB and mercury that can be 
present in fish. In addition to their health effects EPA and DHA oxidize very fast, however microalgae 
contains naturally antioxidants in the form of pigments, phenolic compounds, tocopherol and proteins 
which may increase shelf time of EPA and DHA. Our research aimed to study the variations in the EPA and 
DHA as well as natural antioxidants during storage for 8 weeks at different temperatures in lightproof bags 
with/without oxygen. Results showed a decrease in pigments, phenolic compounds and tocopherol 
meanwhile increase of primary and secondary oxidation products were observed. The amount of EPA in the 
biomass did not change significantly during 8 weeks of storage.

1 Introduction 
 
Micro algae are primary produces in the ocean which is the harvest of sunlight into biomass. Every living 
thing in the ocean originates from micro algae. They also produce the majority of the oxygen in the 
atmosphere. Culturing micro algae is a relative new science and many problems are still to be solved. Micro 
algae are promising in biofuel production. Bio fuels are CO2 neutral and can help the word solve the global 
warming problems. 
Micro algae also contain healthy omega-3 fatty acids and essential amino acids. But before humans change 
the diet, micro algae can be fed to fish and other animals.  
The analytical part of the report will show data of the stability of the micro algae during storage. Not much 
is known about the stability freeze dried micro algae and this study aims to enlighten it. Lastly the report 
will investigate the stability of commercial fish meal with 10 % micro algae and compare it with commercial 
fish meal the strong antioxidant ethoxyquin.

2 Background 
2.1 Fish harvest 
The Food and agriculture organization (FAO) has together with World Bank made a report on the global 
fisheries and aquaculture market and the prospects for the future. At present time, the global capture of 
fish is at its highest level and it will most likely not increase. Meanwhile the demand for fish protein and oil 
is increasing. The only way to accommodate the demand is aquaculture. The world bank is estimating a 
total of 180 million tons of fish will be needed to fulfill the demand. Today the total fish consumption is 140 
million tons. Of these 50 million tons is from aquaculture. By 2030 the aquaculture production will be the 
same as the global fish capture equaling 90 million tons. That is nearly a 50% increase.  
China alone accounts for 62% of the world’s aquaculture production. Carp is the main fish that is being 
cultivated. It is a harsh fish that can survive in poor oxygen conditions and can live of a pure vegetarian diet. 
The fish are usually kept in primitive ponds and fed with kitchen leftovers. China is the world’s biggest 
aquaculture producer because many people have small ponds with carp. The nutritional value of carp from 
China will contain very little amounts of omega-3 due to the vegetable diet (Bank, 2013).

2.4 DHA and EPA 
The marine omega-3 fatty acids, eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) are known 
to many positive health effects (Küllenberg, Taylor, Schneider, & Massing, 2012). Manufactures of 
nutraceuticals (such as fish oil supplements) or foods where DHA and EPA are added, are allowed to use the 
following health effect statements on their packaging, according to European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) 
(Botha et al., 2014): 
“Brain, eye and nerve development”  
“Maintenance of normal brain function”  
“Maintenance of normal vision”  
“Maintenance of normal cardiac function”  
“Maternal health; pregnancy and nursing”  
“To fulfil increased omega-3 fatty acids need during pregnancy”  
Omega-3 fatty acids are beneficial for a wide range of health effects. EFSA recommends a daily intake of 
250-500mg DHA and EPA pr day to obtain health effects. The recommended intake variates according to 
which health function and age is target (Opinion, 2011). (Health Canada, 2013) claims that the EPA:DHA 
ratio should be 1:2. The daily recommended total EPA and DHA intake should be minimum 100 mg, and 
maximum 1500 mg for children, 2500 mg for teenagers and 5000 mg for adults. 
People are now, more than ever, aware of the importance of a healthy diet, including omega-3 fatty acids. 
The number of scientific studies that show evidence that intake of DHA and EPA decrease the risk of a 
number of diseases is now that full documented that most people try to fulfill the guidelines for 
recommended intake (Ritter, 2012). However it is difficult for the consumer to get a specific guideline 
because it still varies between organizations and countries.   
 
2.5 Synthesis of EPA and DHA from ALA 
The human body is able to synthesize EPA and DHA from ALA (α-Linolenic acid). ALA is found in terrestrial 
plants such as walnut, flaxseeds and soybean. The rate of synthesis is however very poor. It can be up and 
down regulated according to food intake. (Bradbury, 2011) found that vegetarians who did not eat fish 
were able to up regulate the production of DHA compared to fish eaters. However vegetarians who were 
given 200mg DHA pr day had a 50% increase in serum DHA-phospholipids. This is indicating that the human 
synthesis is not strong enough to fulfill DHA and EPA demands.

2.6 Bioavailability of phospholipids 
 
Omega-3 fatty acids found in phospholipids (PL) has been shown to be more bioavailable than omega-3 
triacylglyceride- (TAG) bound fish oil. PL are found in all cell membranes, and especially micro algae 
contains much PL. Oil derived from fish will mostly be built up by TAG. Studies have shown that even if 
smaller amounts of krill/algae oil is consumed, compared to commercial fish oil, it incorporates better in 
the human body (Kagan, West, Zante, & Calder, 2013b), (Winther, Hoem, Berge, & Reubsaet, 2011). This is 
caused by the phospholipid molecule. It is recognized by the body and it can be used directly and put into 
the cell membrane. Glycerol based fat will have to be split up into glycerol and free fatty acids. Then those 
fatty acids will have to be put onto a phospholipid form before it can be incorporated into a cell membrane. 
This process can lose some of the healthy fatty acids (Schuchardt et al., 2011).

3 Applications of micro algae 
3.1 Micro algae as fish feed 
Micro algae has for many years been used as feed for fish larvae, mollusk and crustaceans. Zooplankton 
eats micro algae and production of zooplankton also requires microalgae. Micro algae has the potential to 
replace some and maybe all of the current fish meal part in fish feed. In aquaculture it is crucial with the 
correct marine protein and lipid profile. Microalgae have both high lipid and protein content. (M. R. Brown, 
Jeffrey, Volkman, & Dunstan, 1997) reported that all essential amino acids for fish were represented in 40 
analyzed microalgae species. (Ferreira, Coutinho, Seixas, F??bregas, & Otero, 2009) enriched rotifers a 
zooplankton species, with Nannochloropsis. In big scale salmon aquaculture in Norway it was a success to 
replace 6 % of fishmeal with microalgae in the feed. The salmon growth rate was the same and even a 
higher lipid in the meat was found (Sørensen et al., 2016).  
 
3.2 Ethoxyquin in fish meal 
Industrial fishmeal comes from fish harvested in the ocean. This is the cheapest source of the healthy 
omega-3 fatty acids. The fish is usually smaller trophic level fish such as sardine and sandeal. The fish is 
grinded and dried into a meal like substance. To secure the stability of the omega-3 fatty acids ethoxyquin 
is added as an antioxidant.  
Marine omega-3 fatty acids from the marine environment oxidize very fast and hence lose their health 
effects. Since the 1980’s ethoxyquin has been used to prevent the oxidation of the omega-3 lipids in fish 
meal. Ethoxyquin has a wide range of uses, from protecting lipids to prevent antiscald formation on the 
surface of pears. The allowed daily intake (ADI) for humans is 0.005 mg/kg body weight. However there are 
concerns regarding the safety of the chemical. In 1987 ethoxyquin (EQ) was found to cause rapid changes in 
liver tissue in rats which was associated as the first stages towards cancer (Manson, Green, & Driver, 1987). 
It is allowed as an antioxidant in animal feed, but not directly in human food. However when added to fish 
meal which in turn is fed to fish, humans get into contact with ethoxyquin and its derivatives. (Błaszczyk, 
Augustyniak, & Skolimowski, 2013) 
Ethoxyquin (EQ) and its main oxidized derivative (EQDM) has been reported to course negative health 
effects on rat, dog and broiler liver and other organs. EQ also showed cytotoxic and genotoxic to human 
lymphocytes. The overall health risks and effects in humans are still unknown however skin exposure to EQ 
showed allergy reactions. 
Due to the concerns the maximum allowed limit in pet food was decreased from 150 ppm to 75 ppm by US. 
Food and Drug Administration (FDA). EQ has been used in the fish feed for salmon in Norway for a long 
time. A study investigating the concentration of EQ and EQDM in the muscle tissue of the salmon, found 
high amounts of EQDM. Toxicity of EQDM has been very poor researched. There was found a linear 
relationship between amounts of EQ in the feed and the amount of EQDM in muscle samples of salmon. 
The salmon farms are forced to have a depurification periode of 14 days. However it seemed to have no 
effect on EQDM concentrations which then make EQ persistent in the meat (Błaszczyk et al., 2013).

3.3 Micro algae - super food for humans 
The cyanobacteria Spirulina (considered a microalgae) has been consumed by humans since the 16th 
century in Mexico (Spirulina, 2016). Spirulina contains, like micro algae, all essential amino acids, however 
the lipid profile only contains the omega-6 PUFA-LA and GLA.  Spirulina has become very popular in 
developing countries due to its fast growth and low technological requirements. Spirulina can be grown in 
simple ponds with high alkaline water. pH is between 8,5 and 10 which outcompetes all other algae spices. 
Spirulina is a vital protein source in developing countries such as Africa and India. Spirulina is selling at 70 
Euros/kg DW in Kenya and 200 Euros/kg DW in Europa and USA. Production and consumption by humans is 
a million dollar industry. The consumption of microalgae by humans has occurred for many years and 
considered a superfood full of nutritional value. It is assumed be the author that it is quite possible for a 
marked in food products that are enriched with microalgae. Nannochloropsis will outcompete Spirulina in 
nutritional value. Both spices contain the same amino acid profile however Nannochloropsis contains high 
amounts of EPA.

The natural occurring pigments can be divided into 3 classes. Carotenes, xanthophylls and chlorophylls.

3.4 Biodiesel 
When oil prices sky rocked in 2005 alternatives for oil, such as biodiesel from microalgae, was put into 
focus. The attempts were focusing on finding the best performing algae for this. In the literature there are 
thousands of articles studying the best performing algae species for bio fuel production. Studies also 
focused on different growth conditions which will increase the oil content in the micro algae. It was 
believed that algae would become the future of biofuel and it was even reported that a species of a 
cyanobacteria had biological metabolisms of producing alkanes, the majority in gasoline and jet fuel.  (Joos 
et al., 2010)

Biodiesel production is an esterification process to produce fatty acid methyl esters (FAME) and glycerol. 
Methanol is a usually the used alcohol and for each triglyceride molecule 3 FAME’s and 1 glycerol molecule 
is produced.  The FAME’s  are the burstable (diesel) product. According to the type of FA attached to the 
glycerol different quality of biodiesel is produced. In general saturated fatty acids are more wanted 
compared to unsaturated fatty acids. This is due the better oxidative stability of the saturated fatty acids 
which give a better quality FAME (Navarro López et al., 2015), (Chen, Liu, Zhang, Chen, & Wang, 2012) 
(Chisti, 2008) a high sited article states that biodiesel production from micro algae is more promising than 
any other crop on land. (Alam, Mobin, & Chowdhury, 2015) found that based on productivity microalgae 
would be able to produce 60000 -240000 litre/hectare/year. In comparison to the highest oil yield plant oil 
palm which only will be able to produce 6000 litre/hectare/year .  1st and 2nd generation biofuel use food 
related biomaterial for biofuel production. The problem with this is, according to (Chisti, 2008),  the 
competition with land crops designed for food production. 3rd generation is production of biofuel from 
algae. The production can take place on the sea, on land at deserted areas where there is no competition 
with food crops. The last decade research on biofuel from algae has focused a lot on different species of 
algae and strains. It is crucial to find an optimum species for a given growth conditions. Seasonal variation 
and growth phase can change the lipid composition in Nannochloropsis enormously. During growth phase 
(exponential phase), the micro often accumulate little TAG. This is due to the growth which require energy 
that will not make the algae store lipid in TAG. After exponential phase the algae will start to store energy 
in TAG. It is also related to the amount of light where high light will cause photo inhibition which stimulates 
TAG production. TAG is a good electron acceptor and during high light conditions a lot of reactive O2 is 
produced due to photosynthesis. The cell then build of TAG which are stored in vacuoles inside the cell to 
avoid oxidative stress on structural lipids and proteins (Navarro López et al., 2015), (Azcan & Yilmaz, 2012).

4 Commercial production of microalgae 
Microalgae are single-celled organisms which are found everywhere from the ice-covered Antarctic to hot 
and dry deserts. The majority is found in the fresh and marine environment. There are more than 50000 
known species, up to 800000 species estimated, which is much greater than terrestrial plants. Size wise 
micro algae can range from a few micro meters to hundreds of micro meters. Microalgae are very diverse 
both in size and morphology. Diatoms for instance are known to grow very fast and use silica for their outer 
cell wall. Other species have a calcium carbonate outer cell wall. The rest of the microalgae have a fluid cell 
wall made typically of cellulose, algaenan and other carbohydrate molecules (Scholz et al., 2014).  
Microalgae are highly specialized according to which environment they are found. Micro algae have 
different life survival strategies.  Fast growth rate, ability to live in high salinity, production of toxins to 
outcompete other species and small cell size to avoid predators. The list could go on but whatever micro 
algae species is out there, it has done something right in order to survive (Matsunaga, 1990) , (Alam et al., 
2015). 
    
The majority of micro algae are phototrophic, which is the process of converting C02 and solar energy into 
organic matter. The process is much faster and more efficient compared to terrestrial plants. This is due to 
the fact that the micro algae grow in aqueous suspension, where there is more access to water and other 
nutrients (Moody, McGinty, & Quinn, 2014).  
 
 
4.1 Phototrophic and heterotrophic growth 
Phototrophic, is also known as photoautotrophic, and is the process where light is the energy source and 
CO2 is the carbon source. Some production designs use artificial light and bubbles CO2 through the liquid 
medium to enhance productivity. Other designs use natural sunlight and open pond raceway systems. This 
is assumed to be the cheapest algae production method. However open pond systems are highly 
vulnerable towards grazers and other algae contaminants (Apt & Behrens, 1999). The geographical location 
for open ponds algae systems will only make sense in areas with stable year round temperature and many 
sunlight hours. Commercial open pond systems can be found in Hawaii, Israel and Australia. The chosen 
algae for this kind of setups are usual algae that have specialized in a niche in nature. This can be high 
salinity, temperature ect. This way the risk of contaminants and grazers will be reduced. 
Another and more scientific approach in photoautotrophic growth systems is in flat screen panels or tubes 
in enclosed controlled environments. These systems can either use natural sunlight or artificial light. Light is 
the limiting factor of cell growth in photoautotrophic microalgae production. Cell densities can only reach 5 
grams per liter at peak density. This is not very impressive compared to heterotrophic cultures that can 
reach 100 grams per liter (Apt & Behrens, 1999).  
In photoautotrophic systems the algae simply shade for one another due to the “self-shading” mechanism. 
To solve this problem high surface to volume ratio growth systems have been designed. The tube system is 
where this is achieved the best. Tube of 65 mm in diameter is the most effective for the majority of algae 
production. Glass is the best material when it transmits light better than any other material. Glass is also 
resistant to chemical cleaning agents (Moody et al., 2014). 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
The heterotrophic growth system is very similar to already existing fermenter designs found in the 
biotechnology. This is done in big steel tanks where factors such as temperature, nutrients and pH can be 
controlled. Heterotrophic growth systems have existed for many years in the industry. Many 
pharmaceutical products are produced this way. In a closed tank system a clean environment can be 
obtained much easier compared to open photoautotrophic systems. Higher cell densities can be obtained 
due to fact that the cells do not need light.  Some diatom species can grow heterotrophic using an organic 
carbon source instead of sunlight and CO2. Glycose or citric acid is typically used. All commercial algae 
produced DHA is using this method.  
 
 
4.2 Harvest and dewatering 
The most energy consuming step in the manufacturing of micro algae in dewatering, which accounts up to 
90 % of production costs (Apt & Behrens, 1999). At the moment different methods can be used to dewater 
the biomass. First step is usually an up concentration of the biomass, which at this stage mostly consist of 
water. Up concentration can be done by a simple filtration through a filter or a flocculation method. 
Flocculation is the principle of the cells clogging together and sink out of the suspension. This can be done 
by adding a reagent to the media where the cells can bind. Other techniques introduces a virus to the cells 
which then kills the cells and make them flocculate together (Samarasinghe & Fernando, 2015) used 
Poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) to make cells migrate to a solvent phase. Hexane was found to be 
the best solvent and 95% of water was removed. The cost of this method was not discussed however it 
sounds promising for big scale dewatering and will be cheaper than centrifugation.

5 Nannochloropsis Salina 
 
The genus Nannochloropsis consists of 6 known species. Nannochloropsis Gaditana was first described in 
1981 by Hibberd from marine water. Later brackish and freshwater species were found. Today N. granulata 
N. limnetica N. oceanica N. oculata N. salina have been added to the list (Krienitz, 2000), (Suda, 2002). 
Within these species many known and possibly unknown strains exists. Nannochloropsis salina belongs to 
the family Eustigmataceae which is in the green algae family. The algae is very small, 2-5 µm in cell size.   
Compared to other micro algae species it is one of the smallest (K. Pan et al., 2011).  Cells densities of 1,4 x 
1010 cells x mL-1 can be obtained under controlled growth conditions (Kagan & Matulka, 2015). 
Nannochloropsis salina can grow under a wide range of temperatures and salinities. Nannochloropsis has 
been studied for many scientific uses mainly biodiesel and as a food source in fish farms (K. Pan et al., 
2011).  
 
Figure 7 - Microscopic picture of Nannochloropsis salina 
 
Nannochloropsis salina is a promising micro algae for biodiesel production (also known as 3rd generation 
biofuel) due to the high lipid content. For fish feed use, Nannochloropsis salina is excellent. The biochemical 
profile of freeze dried Nannochloropsis salina is around 25-30% oil 8% Carbohydrate and 35% protein. The 
oil extracted from Nannochloropsis salina comprises up to 8-10% phospholipids (PL) and 30-40% 
glycoplipids (GL) as polar-lipids, with 30-40% EPA as the major fatty acid. The remaining lipid is TAG (Van 
Wagenen et al., 2012), (Xu, Hu, Cong, Cai, & Ouyang, 2004).  
The biochemical profiles changes much according to species and growth conditions. For biodiesel and fish 
feed use, it is the oil and protein that is wanted. When the micro algae is stressed with limited nitrogen 
availability the algae will begin to produce vast amounts of storage lipids. Storage lipids are typically TAG 
found inside the cytoplasm of the cell. Storage lipids are usually found the be more saturated fatty acids 
such as C:16 (Jia et al., 2015). When an increase in lipid is taking place it is influenced by a decrease in the 
protein content (Fakhry & El Maghraby, 2015).

6 Lipids in microalga Nannochloropsis salina 
 
The fatty acid profile in Nannochloropsis Salina is changing according to seasonal factors such as 
temperature, light regime, salinity and nutrient availability. (Olofsson et al., 2012) found that the major 
fatty acids in the algae are C16:0, C16:1(n7) and C20:5(n3). The difference in the concentration between 
them varies a lot through the season. In November the amount of C16:0, C16:1(n7) and EPA C20:5(n3) was 
29 %, 28 % and 17 % respectively. Same year in June the ratio was 32 %, 6 % and 3 % respectively. The EPA 
changes from 3 % in summer to 17 % in winter. It is believed that decreasing temperature affects the 
fluidity of the cell membrane. The algae cope with this by increasing the amount of EPA in the cell 
membrane which makes the membrane more fluid in the cold water.  
(Meng et al., 2015) Found that most of EPA in Nannochloropsis Oceanica is phospholipid (PL) and glycolipid 
(GL) (fig 9). It depends on growth conditions and the specific growth phase the alga is in. When the alga is 
stressed by nitrogen limitation more total lipid is produced. However nitrogen limitation makes the alga 
produce more saturated lipids, specifically C16:0 and C16:1. For highest EPA yield its best to harvest when 
the cell mass is at its highest.  
(Meng et al., 2015) found the biochemical composition of Nannochloropsis under normal growth conditions 
to be: 10% carbohydrate, 36% protein and 28% lipid. When stressed with limited nitrogen available the 
composition changed to: 15% carbohydrate, 10% protein and 58% lipid.  
(Kagan, West, Zante, & Calder, 2013a) made a study about the bioavailability of algae oil from 
Nannochloropsis compared to krill oil. Both high in PL and glycolipids but the krill oil had a higher 
proportion of polar lipids compared to the alga oil, 33% and 17% respectively. The study only included 10 
male test persons, however results showed a significant difference in bioavailability between the oils. The 
persons who were given krill oil had EPA plasma blood levels of 15 µg/ml compared to 35 µg/ml for the 
persons who were given algae oil after 6 hours. The data was significant in all time steps. To date this is the 
only study that have compared the oils and more studies must confirm this before it is concluded that algae 
oil is more bioavailable.

The fatty acid EPA acid has 4 bis-allylic carbon locations. Any of these locations can be subjected to 
oxidation. The oxidation process is categorized into 3 steps. Initiation is the first step. Marine PL and GL can 
initiate oxidation in the presence of catalysts such as metals, light, heat, enzymes (lipoxygenases), 
metalloproteins, and microorganisms resulting in lipid autoxidation. The loss of a hydrogen atom (initiation) 
and formation of a lipid radical (L•) is the first step. The lipid radical will quickly react with any O2 available 
forming peroxyl radical (LOO•). This is the propagation. The LOO• radical is not stable and will quickly react 
with any Hydrogen source. Typically this would be another lipid (LH) and the reaction continues as long as 
there is oxygen available. It could also be an antioxidant and further oxidation stops. If it is another LH the 
reaction creates another lipid radical which can undergo propagation and termination. The termination is 
the last step in the oxidation process which creates stable non-radical products LOOH. During propagation 
and termination many different stereo-chemical configurations can occur. This could be the positioning of 
the hydrogen (cis and trans), and where on the FA the radical formation take place.

7.2 Lipid oxidation and water activity 
 
A good way to increase stability of food is to achieve low water content. A low water content reduces 
growth of bacteria and yeast, which can be harmful to human health.  In food science the correlation of 
water activity to food stability is good. Water activity is defined as the relative humidity in an enclosed 
chamber surrounding the sample. It can be complicated to measure the relative humidity of a sample this 
way. Measuring the water content is much easier and it can be converted to water activity.  Lipid oxidation 
in wet algae paste with a water content above 80% oxidize very fast compared algae with less water 
content. Especially the reaction of lipases and formation of FFA is fast. (Nelson, 2015) studied the oxidation 
of lipids in wet micro algae paste. TAG decreased from 72% to 2% of total lipid, during 1 day of storage at 
20 °C. Meanwhile the formation of FFA increased to 70%.

7.3 Effect of natural antioxidants in micro algae 
 
The primary antioxidants (AH) found in the micro algae in this study are mainly: α-tocopherol, carotenoids 
and phenolic compounds. They all can act as antioxidants by donating a hydrogen atom to any lipid radical 
(fig 15). Therefore antioxidants can interfere in a lipid oxidation process by forming stable lipid oxidation 
products, which could have induced further lipid oxidation. The antioxidants become radicals (A•) which 
are much less reactive. A• can further react with lipid radicals in the termination process forming stable LA, 
LOA or LOOA molecules. The antioxidative effect of antioxidants is highly correlated to the molecular 
structure and how willing there are to donate hydrogen atoms. For example α-tocopherol is known to be 
able to donate 2 hydrogen atoms, and thereby being twice as effective compared to antioxidants that only 
can donate 1 hydrogen atom (Bakir et. al. 2013). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Lastly it is worth to mention that secondary antioxidants also could be found in micro algae. Secondary 
antioxidants can chelate reactive metals and oxygen. Secondary antioxidants can also be organic acids such 
as citric acids and phosphoric acid.

8 Materials and Methods 
8.1 The microalgae Initial condition of freeze dried Nannochloropsis salina 
The microalga was cultivated in big scale (200 l) photobio-reactors at Novozymes in Kalundborg. The light 
source was natural light from the sun and the light from indoor factory lighting. The time of harvest was in 
December 2015, the darkest and coldest month of the year in Denmark. Therefore the biochemical 
composition of the alga is very different compared to the summer with high light irradiance. In winter the 
lipid content will be at its highest due to cell plasticity and fluity of the cell membrane. The microalga was 
harvested and immediately filtrated to get the majority of the water out. The next step was centrifugation, 
which resulted in a thick paste with approximately 70% water content. The algae paste was then freeze 
dried to obtain 5 % water content. The micro alga was stored in a plastic bag in -80°C.                                          
8.2 Sampling and storage 
The freeze dried algae was put into light- and air proof bags. Approximately 1 gram of sample was put into 
each bag.  The bags were then either vacuum packed or packed with atmospheric air. To obtain the same 
amount of air in each bag the algae was first put into 30 ml tubes with the cap off, which then were placed 
inside the bag. The bag was then sealed. The samples were placed in different storage conditions: 5°C, 20°C 
and 40°C. For each condition 2 bags were analyzed for pigments, total oil, PV, tocopherol, FFA and FAME. 
The other bags were used for volatile analysis and the leftover was used for total phenolic content. When 
samples were taken out for each time, analysis was immediately performed except the bags for volatiles 
which were stored at -80°C. All volatile bags were analyzed on GC-MS at the same time. Samples were 
taken out for analysis after 2,4,6 and 8 weeks of storage.  
A second experiment was carried out (see fig 1). Fish meal (FM) from FF Skagen a/s with ethoxyquin and 
FM without ethoxyquin but with 10% of the freeze dried algae. These samples were vacuum packed and 
placed at 20°C storage condition. All bags had duplicates which mean a total of 2x samples (n=2) for storage 
condition. The experiments were performed with replicates and would give 4 replicates in the end for each 
sample. 
The micro algae was put into bags quickly to prevent light and air which to affect analysis. During the  
sampling of micro algae, it was noticeable that the remaining micro algae was more wet. This could be due 
to the longer time the remaining algae has been exposed to moisture in the air for. 
It was observed that there was a difference between particle sizes between the samples. It was tried the 
best to mix it all very well so each sample would be representative however there was a big difference 
between some of the samples. To avoid extra contacts with oxygen, heat, light and trace metals which can 
influence the results, the micro algae was mixed inside the big bag of which it was stored. A fast transfer of 
the micro algae into each individual bag for storage was performed and finally the bags were sealed. This 
resulted in a few bags having larger particles than others, which could affect oxidation (Fig 1 shows the 
experiment-setup).

absorbance was measured at 500 nm on Shimadzu UV-1800 with auto sampler. 
 
8.7 Tocopherol 
Tocopherol was determined from B&D extracts. The chloroform was evaporated and the remaining oil with 
tocopherol was dissolved in 1 ml heptane. The solution was transferred to GC-vial and to be analyzed twice 
on HPLC. The HPLC colone was 5µm Silica 250 mm *4,6 mm and the UV-detector was shimadzu UV-160. 
The flowrate was 1 ml/min and the injection volume was 40 µl. The HPLC mobile phase was heptane/2-
Propanol in the ratio 100:0,4 Volume %. The tocopherol analysis was performed the same day or the day 
after the oil extraction. 
 
8.8 Volatiles 
The analysis uses the dynamic headspace procedure which involves nitrogen gas. The samples are mixed 
with 10 ml water and 1 ml antifoam and added to a purge flask. The flask is put in 45°C warm water bath to 
get a better release of the volatiles. Be letting an inert gas through the medium the volatiles will be 
released from the matrix and trapped on the tenax GR material. Approximatly 0,5 gram of sample was used 
and 3 drops of the standard (25mg) 4-methyl-1-pentanol. The time for collecting volatiles on the tenax 
tubes was 30 min and 20 min for degassing, to get any water out. The flowrate for this was 150 ml/min and 
50 ml/min respectively. Tenax tube were run on GC-MS. After the run of samples interesting volatiles were 
selected. The pure concentration from each volatile (Sigma Aldrich) was mixed in ethanol in different 
concentrations. The mixed standard volatiles were put directly on the tenax tubes and degassed for 5 min. 
The standards were then run on GC-MS. For each standard a curve was calculated using software: MSD 
Chemstation F.01.03.2357 and Library: Wiley 138K. Each sample was treated manually to ensure the 
program had done the integration correctly.    
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
The principle in this analysis is to run all samples as close to each other as possible. It is a very sensitive 
analysis and require 3 replicates due to big standard deviation between samples. The initial condition was 
calculated from 5 samples run 3 months earlier than the rest of the samples. The GC-MS had between 
those 2 measurements been tuned. The standard added to the samples was compared and it was found 
that the standard in the initial condition was over estimated by a factor of 2,8. The initial concentration of 
volatiles was calculated from this difference. To have ideal comparison all samples should have been run at 
the same time. Therefore the initial condition might be even lower or higher. It is still better to include the 
initial condition to have some idea of the development of the volatiles. 
8.9 FAME  
Fatty acid methyl esters was performed on B&D extract. 1 g of extract was evaporated under N2. 100 µL of 
internal STD C:23, 200 µL heptane with 0,01% BHT and 100 µL toluene was added to each sample and 
mixed. After 1 mL of BF3-MeOH was added and the methyl glass was sealed with a Teflon cap. The samples 
were methylated in a 500 watt microwave for 5 min. After cooling the samples were opened up and 
another 0,7 mL of heptane and 1 mL of saturated saltwater was added. Samples were shaken and the top 
heptane phase was transferred to GC-vial. The samples were analyzed on GC-FID using the FAME method. 1 
µL was injected to the GC-FID. The transesterification can also be done with other alcohols such as ethanol. 
8.10 Free Fatty Acids  
FFA was performed from B&D extract. This was also a very easy and fast method. However due to the high 
amounts of PL in samples the analysis may overestimate values. The extracts (10-15g) were mixed with 20 
mL chloroform in a flask with big bottom surface area. 25 mL ethanol and 5 drops of indicator 
phenolphthalein was added. The mixture was titrated with 0,09501 M NaOH until a pinkish color occurred.  
The amount of FFA was calculated from the molar weight of EPA (302,45 g/mol) and result given in % FFA 
of total oil.

8.11 Amino Acids 
Approximately 50 mg of sample was weighted of into a glass vial. 1 ml of 6 M HCl was added to each 
sample. The glass tubes were sealed and microwaved for 60 min at 110 °C. The samples are filtered using a 
0,22 µm Q-Max RR syring filter.  Reagents were prepared and added according to the EZ:FASST kit from 
Phenomenex. 100 µl of hydrolysate sample is mixed with 1500 µl NaCO3 to a Ph of 1,5 and 5. 50 µl of 
sample hydrolysate is mixed with 100 µl reagent 1. A sorbent tip pipette is used to slowly take up the 
sample. 200 µl of water is added to each sample together with 200 µl reagent. The mixture is mixed with 
100 µl reagent 5. The whole thing is mixed a allowed to stand for 5 min. Then 130 µl of the organic top 
layer is transferred to GC-vial. It is then evaporated under nitrogen to complete dryness. 1:2 ratio of water 
and methanol is added to each sample and it is mixed. The samples are finally analyzed on GC-MS together 
with standards of different concentrations.  
8.12 Total phenolics 
The total phenolic content in micro algae was determined by using the Folin-Ciocalteau method. 
Approximately 50 mg of sample was put in a plastic tube with 5 ml methanol. The tubes were put in 
sonication for 30 min and centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min. This was performed twice. 200 µl of the 
extract (10 ml in total) was moved to a new plastic tube together with 1,5 ml (20% NaCO3) and 0,5 ml Folin-
Ciocalteu reagent. The samples were mixed and let to react in the dark for 30 min. The absorbance was 
measured at 760 nm. Amount of total phenolics was calculated according to the standard curve, which was 
made with different concentrations of Gallic acid. 
 
8.13 Statistics 
Data for the different variables (temperature, packing condition and time) were subjected to an analysis of 
variance (one-way ANOVA). For data showing significant differences (p<0.05) across variables, the least 
squares difference (LSD) was calculated. This allowed the statistical comparison of variables based on their 
means, thus allowing comparison of data from the combined, duplicate replications. 
 
The statistics has been made using the program statAdvisor.  The following hypothesis has been used for all

9 Aim 
The overall aim of the project was to find the best packing method and storage temperature of freeze-dried 
micro algae in order to secure a stable product for further use. Literature on the stability of 
photoautotrophic micro algae oil and freeze dried algae poweder is very scare. Knowledge is needed on the 
stability of micro algae in order to succeed in big scale production and storage. 
An extra part of the study was to test the antioxidative effect of micro algae added to commercial fish meal. 
Micro algae has the potential to be added to fish meal. The micro alga Nannochloropsis salina used in this 
study has a protein and lipid profile that has the potential to improve the nutritional value of fish feed and 
enhance the stability. 
The 3 overall aims of the project: 
 
 
Too see what effect storage at 5°C, 20°C and 40°C degrees will have on the oxidative stability of freeze 
dried microalgae powder  
 
 
Too see what vacuum packed versus non vacuum packed will affect the oxidative stability of freeze 
dried microalgae powder  
 
 
To see what antioxidative effect microalgae has in a mix of 10% microalgae in fishmeal compared to 
industrial fish meal with EQ 
 
9.1 Project hypothesis 
 
 
Storage at 5 °C will increase lipid stability compared to storage at 20°C and 40°C  
 
Storage at 40 °C will show the highest lipid oxidation in primary and secondary oxidation products 
 
Storage at 40 °C will show the biggest antioxidant degradation 
 
The lipids in normal packed samples with more oxygen available will oxidize faster compared to 
vacuum packed 
 
Natural antioxidants in Nannochloropsis salina decreases the rate of lipid oxidation in fish meal, 
compared to industrial fish meal with ethoxyquin

10 Results and Discussion - Part 1  
 
The following part will present results for the micro algae only. In results part 2, the Fish Meal will be 
presented. For all analysis it was tested if there was a significant difference between the 4 replicates for 
each sample. The P-value was for all replicates between all analysis above the 0,05 confidential interval, 
indicating that replicates were the same.  
There are hundreds of studies focusing on fish oil stability such as (Venkateshwarlu, Let, Meyer, & 
Jacobsen, 2004), (Song, Inoue, & Miyazawa, 1997), (Lu et al., 2014), (Crapiste, Brevedan, & Carelli, 1999).  
They all show increase in PV and the formation of volatiles. Storage temperature and storage time are main 
factors influencing the stability. Major lipid oxidation can happen from only a few hours of storage to 
almost no oxidation as seen in krill oil.  This study is getting knowledge about the changes in micro algae 
powder. It has been shown to be complicated to analyze micro algae due to the mixture of protein, 
pigments, phenolic compounds ect. The lipid oxidation data has been difficult to explain and a complete 
answer may never be found. The focus has been put on the trends in the data using statistics. The results 
are presented to the best knowledge of the author. 
10.1 Total oil content 
Extraction of oil with chloroform and methanol on freeze dried micro algae does not only extract the oil but 
also other compounds such as pigments and tocopherol to the extracted phase. Moreover a perfect 
extraction where all the oil is extracted is impossible. This is due to the cell wall and cell membranes 
including dead cells with can block the chloroform. Different solvents such as hexane and isopropanol will 
extract much differently amounts of oil. Freezing in -80 °C will increase the yield of oil due to cell wall 
rupture. But when all micro algae samples has been kept at -80 °C  and for the same time then this will not 
influence the comparability. 
It is likely that primary oxidation has happened very fast within week 0 and week 2. This could explain the 
increase in oil content in the microalgae samples, as seen in fig 21.  Formation of primary oxidation 
products (peroxides) can increase the molecular weight of the fatty acids. Further oxidation into pyrrols and 
polymerization which are lipophilic can also explain the increase in oil content (Lu et al., 2014). Later the oil 
content is decreasing, indicating that the rate of primary oxidation has decreased and the degradation of 
peroxides, pyrrols and other polymerization products into volatiles has begun. Packing and storage 
condition had no significant effect p>0,05. The oil content is calculated pr gram dry weight sample, with 
water content of 12% for all samples. An increase in moisture content was found. It increased from average 
of 8,2% to 12% from initial to week 8. Moisture content may increase due to the formation of water in the 
lipid oxidation process but the increase has only been reported to be maximum 4% (Meste, Simatos, 
Prdaud, & Precaustafg, 1985). The increase in moisture is most likely due to micro leakages in the bags or 
the sealings.

Pigments found in micro algae are known to be fragile to heat and light. (Lin & Chen, 2005) studied the

storage temperatures were 4 °C, 15 °C and 25 °C. According to their findings, light and heat were the main

degraded completely and have found some sort of steady state. 
In the world of microalgae pigments act as a way to categorize families and species. Almost each individual 
alga has a unique composition of pigments (Bartley, Boeing, Corcoran, Holguin, & Schaub, 2013). Most 
algae can change the amount and pigment class according to environmental factors such as light and 
temperature. Chlorophyll-a also gets converted into pheophytin, and other related products such as 
pheophorbide, during breakdown (Louda, Li, Liu, Winfree, & Baker, 1998). The increase of pheophytin can 
be seen in appendix. Temperature is the biggest factor of Chlorophyll-a breakdown. The samples stored at 
5°C have significantly higher values compared to samples stored at 20°C and 40°C during storage. It is not 
known why the 5°C samples are increasing in chlorophyll-a. It is most likely the initial condition that has 
showed lower value than the actual value. samples stored at 20°C and 40°C are decreasing significantly  
during storage and samples stored at 40 °C is decreasing the most as expected.  Samples stored at 5°C show 
good stability and no difference between packing conditions. 
The decrease in total xanthophylls is very significant between time, temperature but not storage condition, 
indication that temperature is the main factor that degrades xanthophylls. Other studies also indicate the 
heat lability of xanthophylls (Podsedek, 2007). It seems that the degradation is fastest within initial 
condition and 2 weeks of storage. Samples stored at 5°C and 40°C seems to have found the lower values 
after 2 weeks, except the 20°C samples that continue to decrease. To preserve the xanthophylls it is very 
clear to store the micro algae at 5°C. Packing condition is not significant.  
Total carotenes show the same decrease in the beginning as the other pigments but not the same 
difference in temperature and packing condition, P= 0,50 and 0,30. β-carotene, and to some degree 
xanthophylls, can act as prooxidants (Bakir et al., 2013), (Carocho & Ferreira, 2013).  
The data from the pigments importantly indicates the complete homogeneousity in samples. The 
questionable results and high standard variation in the other analysis are therefore less likely not due to 
the samples being homogeneous, but more instruments and human handling.

10.7 Volatiles 
 
Volatiles are important in foods. Some of them give a specific food its special smell and taste e.g. fresh 
fruits and vegetable. Some of them are unwanted because they course bad smell and taste. These are 
typically lipid oxidation products. The focus has been on volatiles known to be oxidation products from EPA. 
 
Micro algae have been shown to produce many kinds of metabolic volatile components. These compounds 
can act as signaling molecules and many other functions. (Gressler, Colepicolo, & Pinto, 2009) found the 
dynamic headspace method to be best to collect volatiles in microalgae. They also found that microalgae 
are able to produce hundreds maybe thousands of different volatile compounds. These are hydrocarbons, 
terpenes, phenols, alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, esters and halogen or sulfur-containing compounds. 
When looking at the results in figure 33, volatiles 1-penten-3-one, 1-penten-3-ol, 2-penten-1-ol are not only 
increasing slightly. However 2,4-heptadienal and 3-methyl butanal are known oxidation products from EPA 
as seen in figure 13. 
It is assumed that 2,4-heptadienal and 3-methyl butanal would continue to increase If stored for longer 
time than the 8 weeks tested in this study. The formation of 3-methyl-butanal can be a Stecker Degration 
product from leucine, where 3-methyl-butanal is a decarboxylation product  (Smit, Engels, & Smit, 2009). 
Due to the high amounts of amino acids in the microalgae powder this is much likely. The SD is a reaction 
between an amino acid and a lipid oxidation product such as in the study, epoxy keto fatty esters, 
epoxyalkenals and hydroxyalkenals (Safafar, van Wagenen, Møller, & Jacobsen, 2015b),(Fung & Lu, 2013). 
2,4-heptadienal can also react with amino acids to form 2-methyl-2-pentenal and 2-methyl-2-butenal. 
Moreover the SD can form various pyrazines and pyridines which might explain decrease in volatiles.

11 Discussion and perspectives 
 
More stability studies should be carried out on micro algae powder that has been heat treated to deactivate 
enzyme activity. It is most likely that a heat treatment would increase the stability of lipids, especially decrease 
the formation of FFA. 
Variation in sample may have caused instability in results. The freeze dried algae had different particle sizes, 
and specific areas in the sample might differ in composition compared to other areas in the sample. Some 
particles were fine and small, and some were big clumps packed together.  
Micro algae shows to be most stable in lipids and least stable in pigments and tocopherols as expected. The 
pigments act as antioxidants and protect the lipids. Heat is believed to be the most important factor of 
destruction of pigments. The antioxidative effect in samples stored at 40°C is believed to be very small due to 
rapid decrease in pigments. Samples stored at 5°C and vacuum packed has been shown to be the most stable 
storage condition. Pigments and tocopherol were more intact compare to 40°C samples. Lastly the formation 
of lipid oxidation products was lowest which also indicates 5°C vacuum packed storage is the best method in 
order to obtain the best nutritional value for fish feed. 
(Edwin N. Frankel et al., 2002) tested the stability of fish- and microalgae oil high in PUFA. In 40°C storage for 
96 hours they saw an increase in PV from 1 to 250 mEq O2/kg. The increase in PV was correlated to the increase 
in the volatile propanal. The rate of oxidation was compared to micro algae oil that had half the amount of 
carotenoids. It showed that the more the carotenoids the faster the oxidation of the oil. They indicated that 
the prooxidant activity of carotenoids is superior to the antioxidative effects of tocopherol and other pigments.  
In this study the antioxidative effect of the natural pigments have not been calculated. There are different 
essays that can estimate the antioxidative activity. However the formation of PV and volatiles can justify the 
antioxidative effects of tocopherol, pigments and possibly the phenolic. Compared to other studies of fish oil 
and algae oil the stability of freeze dried is stabile regarding lipid oxidation. Pigments are degrading very fast 
but are almost kept stabile in low oxygen and low storage temperature conditions.

13 discussion and perspectives 
 
Micro algae is stored best at 5°C and vacuum packed. The degradation of the pigments show that they are the 
least stabile components in freeze dried micro algae. After 8 weeks of storage there is only little change in the 
fatty acids and possibly proteins.  
Micro algae added to fish meal does not only have antioxidative effects but also  valuable amino acids and EPA, 
which is a great food source. More over the EPA will be more bioavailable due to its assimilation in PL and GL. 
Further studies should focus on the feeding possibilities of micro algae. Focus could be the bioavailability of 
freeze dried algae compared to commercial fish meal, and as a 10% mixture. (Makkar et al., 2015) concluded 
that small amounts of algae meal can improve the overall health and survival of pigs and poultry. It is fair to 
assume the same in the aquaculture industry. Micro algae can more over add valuable fatty acids such as EPA 
for overall health and essential amino acids optimal growth of the fish. Further studies could add nitrogen in 
bags instead of vacuum packed. It might be possible that it would decrease oxidation more than the vaccum 
packed studied here. 
Adding micro algae to fish meal does not only improve the nutritional value but also enhances the stability. 
Peroxides and volatiles were less formed compared to fish meal with EQ was observed in this study. The big 
formation of FFA could however be a problem, but can be solved by heat treatment to deactivate the enzymes. 
For further studies on the storage stability on micro algae it can be advised to improve the analysis where the 
pigments have been a problem. Conjugated dienes can be correlated to PV. It is also a spectrophotometric 
method and pigments might still give problems in the analysis. The p-Anisidine value can be used for 
determination of secondary oxidation products such as aldehydes and ketones. The method does however also 
use spectrophotometric measurement at 370 nm which is many pigments in the sample absorbs light. For 
microalgae research it is advised to find a good, standardized and validated method for measuring PV.  
It could also have been interesting to see changes in volatiles measuring at daily basis. This could clarify what 
happened between initial condition and 2 weeks of storage. It will be expected that the peak formation has 
happened in that time period. It is also suggested that measuring tertiary oxidation SD products and browning 
reaction products such as pyrroles could verify the further oxidation of the volatiles.  
To get more knowledge on water content in freeze dried micro algae during storage future studies could plan 
an experiment where all bags were measured for water content. Maybe enclosed tubes could be used for a 
complete sealing. There is little research on the formation of water during oxidation and information would be 
valuable to be able to explain the combined processes of enzyme activity, lipid oxidation and antioxidant 
activity.

14 Conclusion 
 
 
This study has enlightened the stability of freeze dried micro algae stored in the dark for 8 weeks at different 
packing- and temperature conditions. The results show that the complex matrix of antioxidants in the 
microalgae is decreasing while the lipids only decrease slightly. Pigments, tocopherol, phenolics and possible 
amino acids are protecting the lipids against oxidation. The primary and secondary oxidation products from the 
lipids increase during storage. The biggest oxidation was seen in samples stored at 40 °C and normal packed. To 
obtain the highest amount of pigments during storage it is advised to store samples at 5 °C. Packing condition is 
not relevant for protecting the pigments. Vacuum packing is the best storage condition for stability of 
tocopherol. Vacuum packing seems also to be the best storage condition of lipid stability. 
The fish meal with 10 % micro algae seems to have same or even better stability of the omega-3 fatty acids 
compared to fish meal with ethoxyquin. The formation of FFA was however much bigger in the fish meal with 
10 % algae. For fish feed industry it might not be a problem.  
It was difficult to give a concrete answer of the effect for each antioxidant, regarding the antioxidative effect. It 
is most likely a combined effect of tocopherol, phenolics, β-carotene, amino acids and the xanthophylls that 
give the micro algae powder it’s strong antioxidative effect.
