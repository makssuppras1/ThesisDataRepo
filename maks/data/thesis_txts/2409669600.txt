Abstract 
Viral infectious clones are instrumental to understand the function of viral genomic sequences and 
proteins in a genetically defined context. They not only give a great insight into the virus at the 
genetic level, but also allow their genetic manipulation which makes it possible to induce and 
analyze the effect of mutations. Infectious clones also enable the possibility of generating 
rationally attenuated viruses that could be exploited as live vaccines. This study was an attempt to 
generate infectious clones for two Flaviviruses: Zika virus (ZIKV) and Usutu virus (USUV). To 
this aim we first established the methods for the amplification of USUV and ZIKV full genomes, 
using in both cases a strategy involving PCR amplification of two overlapping fragments since we 
failed to obtain a single amplicon containing these viruses’ full genomes. We have been successful 
in cloning a large fragment of ~7.7 kb covering most of ZIKV genome (10.8 Kb), that can be 
instrumental in the development of an infectious clone soon. The cloning of the remaining 
fragment for ZIKV (3.5 Kb) has remained elusive so far. Likewise, I have also been unable to 
obtain clones for diverse amplicons of USUV tested. In the course of this study, we also have 
established the basic methods to quantify ZIKV RNA by a real-time RT-PCR method. This 
protocol will be of use to the accurate determination of the number of molecules in a sample. This 
can be employed for different purposes such as determining the number of viral RNA molecules 
generated by in vitro transcription of ZIKV infectious clones, or analyze the amount of ZIKV 
genomic molecules in a given sample.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Family Flaviviridae  
The name of the family Flaviviridae comes from the Yellow fever virus (YFV) as ‘flavus’ means 
yellow in Latin. This family of viruses comprises of four genera (Figure 1), namely: Flavivirus, 
Hepacivirus, Pestivirus and Pegivirus.  
Flavivirus contains around 70 viruses, 30 of which are arthropod-borne human pathogens and 
cause diseases ranging from fevers and rash to encephalitis and necrosis (1). Dengue virus 
(DENV), Zika virus (ZIKV), West Nile virus (WNV) etc. are known to be important human viral 
pathogens. These viruses have a Vertebrate/Invertebrate cycle and affect humans and other animals 
such as horses, birds, chimpanzees etc.  
Pestivirus contains pathogens that affect animals, such as BVDV, BDV, CSFV etc. (1). 
Hepacivirus consists of only human pathogens, of which Hepatitis C virus represents one of the 
most important human pathogen in the genus (1). Genus Pegivirus created in 2011 (2), consists of 
virus Hepatitis G variant GBV-A and other related viruses that affect a wide range of animals like 
horses, monkeys, rodents, pigs, bats etc.

1.2. Flavivirus Genome 
The Flaviviral genome (Figure 2a, 2b) is approximately around 11kb in size and is a positive-
sense, single stranded RNA (3). It contains a single open reading frame (ORF) flanked by 5’ and 
3’ untranslated regions (UTRs) of ~100 and 400-700 nucleotides respectively (4). The 5’ end 
contains type I cap structure and 3’ end is non-polyadenylated. The genome codes for three 
structural proteins and seven non-structural (NS) proteins. The structural proteins are capsid C, 
premembrane/membrane prM/M and envelope E. The NS proteins are: NS1-NS2A-NS2B-NS3-
NS4A-NS4B-NS5 (5’ to 3’) (3, 5) 
Figure 2a: Components of the Flavivirus genome [DENV] (II) 
 
 
Figure 2b. Schematic representation of flavivirus. Illustration of viral genome organization. 
The non-structural proteins that participate in genome replication are in green. The assembly of 
the structural proteins surrounding the viral genome renders the viral particle. 
 
==PAGE:12==
 
4 
Flavivirus virions have an icosahedral nucleocapsid (Figure 3) with multiple copies of C protein 
(3). The nucleocapsid is surrounded by the lipid envelope that comes from the host cell, in which 
the prM/M proteins are embedded. prM protein protects the E protein during viral particle egress 
from the cell through acidified sorting compartments (6–9). The E protein, is required for receptor 
binding, host membrane fusion and viral assembly. Viral replication is catalyzed by several non-
structural proteins: the main genome replication activity resides in NS5 where RNA dependent 
RNA polymerase activity is located. A methyl transferase enzymatic activity is also located in NS5 
which ensures that newly synthesized genomes contain a cap needed for translation. Other 
activities relevant for replication are RNA helicase and protease activities found in NS3 and 
protease cofactor NS2B that are needed for replication and flavivirus polyprotein maturation 
among other functions. Viral RNA replication occurs in the cellular cytoplasm in association with 
the rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER) where these and other non-structural proteins associate to 
form virus replication complexes (6–10).

1.3. Flavivirus phylogeny 
Flaviviruses are divided into four groups (Figure 4) based on mode of transmission: (i.) tick-borne 
viruses (ii.), mosquito-borne viruses (iii.), viruses with no known arthropod vector (iv.), viruses 
with no known animal host (1). The mosquito- and tick-borne flaviviruses are maintained in nature

1.4. Zika virus (ZIKV)  
1.4.1. History, epidemiology and prevalence  
Zika virus (ZIKV) was first isolated in a rhesus macaque monkey at its namesake forest in Uganda 
in 1947. The first human infection was observed in Uganda and Nigeria in 1952. Since then few 
outbreaks and a long-term steady spreading in Africa and Asia occurred in the years 1951-83. (11).  
Recently however, ZIKV caused large outbreaks in the Island of Yap (Pacific) in 2007 and French 
Polynesia in 2012-14, although it was previously not known to be a dangerous virus, with 80% of 
infections being asymptomatic (12). This was followed by major outbreaks in Central and South 
America in 2015-16 leading to it being declared a Public Health Emergency of International 
Concern by the World Health Organization (WHO). The Center for Disease Control (CDC) in 
USA had issued travel recommendations (Figure 5), classifying some countries as areas at risk of 
Zika virus infection; these countries are mainly in South America, Africa and some parts of Asia.  
1.4.2. Mode of transmission 
ZIKV is transmitted by arthropods (arbovirus). The most prevalent mode of transmission for the 
virus is therefore via mosquito-borne vectors (13). The Aedes genus is known to be the main carrier 
(Aedes aegypti, Aedes albopictus, Aedes africanus, etc.). However, other species such as 
Anopheles coustani and Mansonia uniformis, have also been shown to carry the virus (13).  
Transmission is not limited to mosquito vectors. The virus can also be spread via sexual contact, 
blood transfusion and organ transplantation, urine or saliva (14). The most critical transmission 
method would however be the perinatal transmission with the virus being able to cross the placenta 
barrier to infect the fetus (15). This perinatal ZIKV transmission can lead to microcephaly in 
newborns (16). In addition to microcephaly cases associated with ZIKV infection, there is an 
increased incidence of Guillain-Barre syndrome cases, a serious neurologic disorder (17). 
Immunocompromised or immunosuppressed people may be more susceptible to ZIKV infection 
(18).

1.5. Usutu virus (USUV) 
1.5.1. History, epidemiology and prevalence  
USUV is a mosquito-borne flavivirus belonging to the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV) 
serocomplex and is closely related to JEV, Murray Valley encephalitis virus (MVEV) and West 
Nile virus (WNV) (19, 20). USUV was first isolated from Culex neavei mosquitoes in South Africa 
in 1959 and this strain, South Africa-1959 is now considered the reference strain (21).  
USUV has recently emerged in wild and zoo birds in Central Europe (Austria, 2001) (22). Virus 
emergence has been associated with significant mortality in blackbirds, but not in mammals. 
Retrospective analysis of archived tissue samples from bird deaths in Tuscany, Italy in 1996  
proved that USUV had been introduced into Europe much than earlier assumed (23). In the 
following years, USUV was found to have circulated in several other European countries by 
==PAGE:16==
 
8 
mosquito-displacement or by infected birds (24, 25). The first case in human was in Africa with 
symptoms of mild fever and rash. However, the human cases in Europe were more serious with 
typical flavivirus-related neuroinvasiveness and neurovirulence, persistent fever of 39.5 °C and 
headache (26, 27). 
1.5.2. Phylogeny 
The phylogenetic analysis of all currently available complete USUV sequences from Africa, Spain, 
and central Europe revealed a significant genetic diversity of the virus (28). The viruses circulating 
in Europe belong to (at least) three different genetic clusters. USUV detected in mosquitoes in 
Spain are genetically more closely related to African isolates than to central European ones. The 
viruses differ apparently in their virulence for avian hosts (28). 
1.6. Infectious clones 
Infectious clones are powerful tools in molecular biology that are used to study and manipulate 
viral genomes. These systems allow the rational design of infectious virus containing those desired 
mutations in a genetically defined context. Infectious clones can be exploited for many different 
purposes such as: i) characterize the genetic determinants of virus infection, replication and ii) 
rational design of viruses containing attenuating mutations in the design of safe live vaccines (29). 
Infectious clones make it possible to recover virus with a defined genetic sequence and therefore 
make it possible to study the impact of mutations. 
An infectious clone is simply a double-stranded DNA copy of the viral genome carried in a 
bacterial plasmid (30). It is produced by reverse-transcribing the viral RNA into cDNA, which is 
further cloned into a suitable plasmid vector and then transfected into host cells, mostly E. coli. 
Infectious virus is then recovered from the transfected cells (Figure 6).  
Transfection is the process of introducing naked or purified DNA or RNA into eukaryotic cells. It 
may also refer to other methods and cell-types (31, 32). This term was first introduced when 
bacteriophage lambda was produced from cells introduced with viral DNA (33). It is different from 
transformation, which is introduction of any exogenous DNA into bacterial cells.

1.7. History of infectious clones 
Development of methods in recombinant DNA technology (34) and the discovery of reverse 
transcriptase in the 1970s (35) have made genetic manipulation, including introduction of 
mutations at any location in a viral genome, possible. For RNA viruses, the discovery of reverse 
transcriptase permitted the development of reverse genetics systems to obtain infectious cDNA 
clones that could be easily maintained and manipulated. The first infectious clone was produced 
in 1978 with bacteriophage QB cDNA encoded in a plasmid. Infectious virus was recovered after 
transfection of a plasmid containing the full genome cDNA into E. coli cells (33). The next major 
step in this field was in 1980 when the infectious clone was produced using DNA from retrovirus 
that had integrated into the host cell genome (36). In 1981, Poliovirus cDNA encoded in a plasmid 
==PAGE:18==
 
10
was transfected into mammalian cells, leading to the recovery of the virus (37). Conventional 
plasmids have been used for cloning small length genome viruses e.g. polyomaviruses, 
papillomaviruses, and adenoviruses. However, as many viruses that are human pathogens have 
larger genomes, cosmids and BACs are now used to clone these large inserts (30).  BACs also 
permit the cloning of DNA sequences exerting cytotoxicity as the very low copy number of BACs 
(1 BAC per bacterium) facilitates the stability of toxic DNA regions (30).  
1.8. Infectious clones of Family Flaviviridae 
Despite that infectious clones have become a widespread tool to analyze the genetic determinants 
of viral replication and infectivity, the development of reverse genetics methods for flaviviruses is 
typically problematic, possibly due to the instability of certain viral sequences and the toxicity of 
some parts of the flaviviral genome to the cells (38). The reasons for this are not clearly understood 
yet. However, there has been some success achieved in producing an infectious clone of some 
flaviviruses. Recently developed strategies for full-virus genome amplification have facilitated the 
generation of infectious clones as they typically lead to infectious clone in a single cloning step. 
This strategy has been recently adapted in the generation of infectious clones of >10 Kb such as 
dengue virus (39) and other viruses from genus Pestivirus (within Flaviviridae) such as bovine 
viral diarrhea virus (BVDV), classical swine fever virus (CSFV) etc. (40, 41).  
Japanese encephalitis virus (42) and dengue virus (39) cDNAs have been produced for the full-
length genome of these viruses and inserted into Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes (BACs). BACs 
are suitable for stable maintenance of large inserts from viral genomes (43). Full-length genome 
amplification allows direct full-length sequence analysis, virus rescue and insertion of amplicons 
into suitable cloning vectors (41). Further advantages of full-length genome amplification are: 
same protocol can be applied to a wide range of strains, limited terminal nucleotide sequence 
information is enough to amplify the whole genome, multiple cloning steps can be avoided as the 
full-length amplicons can be directly in vitro transcribed or inserted into plasmid vectors, 
genetically modified viruses can be constructed using stable cDNA clones (41). 
1.9. Infectious clones of ZIKV  
The emergence of ZIKV as a global health security threat has propelled several research studies to 
understand the viral pathogenesis and infectivity, to develop vaccines (44). Studies are being 
==PAGE:19==
 
11
conducted to generate infectious cDNA clones for ZIKV to help us understand the virus better at 
the genetic level.  
An infectious clone has been generated for ZIKV using a clinical isolate of the Asian lineage (18). 
They have constructed this infectious cDNA clone using a five-fragment strategy, cloning two 
fragments A and B into low-copy number plasmid pACYC177 (and to assemble full-genome 
cDNA) and cloning the remaining three fragments (D, E, F) into high copy-number plasmid 
pCR2.1-TOPO. This strategy is to overcome the high toxicity of fragments A and B towards E. 
coli cells; fragments D, E, F are non-toxic. The five fragments are then assembled in a low-copy 
plasmid pACYC177. This vector is designed to encode for a self-cleavable hepatitis delta 
ribozyme after transcription rendering an authentic ZIKV genomic 3’ end. ZIKV RNA molecules 
generated after transcription are infectious in cell culture and in vivo (18). The authors also 
demonstrated this ZIKV cDNA clone is stable (18).  
An alternate system to produce ZIKV infectious clone has been described by Wegner-Lucarelli et 
al in 2016 (45). In this method, infectious ZIKV was generated from a two-fragment cDNA clone 
system. The authors have cloned the ZIKV genome into two separate pieces of ~3.5 kb and ~7.7 
kb respectively, into the pACYC177 vector using Gibson assembly. The resultant plasmid from 
the cloning step contained the full-length virus sequence with a T7 promoter and hepatitis D virus 
ribozyme sequence, which was later transcribed into RNA using T7 polymerase. The resulting 
RNA was electroporated into Vero cells, producing cytopathogenic effect (CPE) 8 days later. 
RNA-sequencing (using RNA-Seq) confirmed that the viral RNA isolated from the recovered virus 
contained the expected synonymous mutation at position 8489, thereby indicating that the virus 
was the infectious clone derived. 
An infectious clone for the ZIKV epidemic strain (Brazil, 2015) was produced by Tsetsarkin et al 
in 2016 (46). To generate this infectious clone, the full-length viral cDNA was cloned into the low-
copy number vector pACNR1811 under the control of a cytomegalovirus promoter (Figure 7). An 
antigenomic HDV ribozyme was inserted downstream ZIKV genome to ensure an authentic 3’ 
end. To increase the stability of this infectious clone there were inserted intron sequences in the 
genomic positions 2711 (NS1 gene) and 8882 (NS5 gene). It was also introduced with an adaptive 
mutation to grow in Vero cells.

1.10. Infectious clones of USUV  
Although it has been established that USUV is an emerging virus that poses a threat to many 
European and Asian countries (47), an infectious cDNA clone is yet to be generated for this virus.  
Given the urgent need to study these viruses and the importance of infectious clones in genetic 
study and manipulation of viruses, the main objective of this thesis work is therefore to establish 
the methods to generate rapidly cDNA clones in the laboratory for these two flaviviruses: ZIKV 
and USUV. These methods when standardized could be rapidly applied to the generation of newly 
emerging flaviviruses with pathogenic potential (e.g. Bagaza virus). 
1.11. Objectives 
The main objectives of this study are: 
1. To obtain cDNA amplicons covering the full genome of ZIKV and USUV by using 
combined reverse transcriptase and high-fidelity PCR amplification methods 
2. To clone these cDNA fragments covering full genomic sequences into plasmids with the 
aim of recovering infectious virus directly from plasmid containing the whole genome or 
indirectly making use of two-plasmid strategies 
3. To establish the protocols to quantify ZIKV viral RNA by qPCR methods in the laboratory 
of Dr. Arias.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS 
2.1. Cell infection, viral passaging and RNA extraction  
Vero cells are infected with Usutu virus (USUV) or Zika virus (ZIKV) at a multiplicity of infection 
of 5 TCID50 per cell. The cells were grown in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium with 5% foetal 
calf serum, 100 U/ml penicillin and 100 mg/ml streptomycin and 10mM HEPES pH 7.6. At 48 
hours after infection, the cells were harvested and total RNA extracted using GeneJet RNA 
purification kit (ThermoFisher Scientific).  
The cell infection, viral passaging and RNA extraction for both ZIKV and USUV were performed 
by the supervisor, Dr. Armando Arias, and the extracted RNA samples were provided for further 
steps. 
2.2. Reverse transcription 
Viral RNA is reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) using protocols two types of 
reverse transcriptase enzymes (Appendices 1, 2): Maxima (ThermoFisher Scientific) and 
SuperScript III (Invitrogen), to check which among these two enzymes work better for the two 
(ZIKV, USUV) viral RNA samples. Reverse transcriptase Maxima was used in both its undiluted 
form as well as in a 1:10 dilution. cDNA produced in this step can be stored at -20˚C. To the 
reverse transcription reactions, they were used primers that are complementary to the 3’ end of 
ZIKV and USUV genomes, respectively. 
An optional step to improve subsequent PCR procedures involves treating the samples with 
enzymes RNAseH and DpnI (New England Biolabs) at the end of the incubation period. RNAseH 
degrades any RNA that is bound to DNA (RNA-DNA hybrid) and any other short RNA fragments 
that are unstable. DpnI degrades methylated DNA which is useful to eliminate cellular DNA that 
is typically co-purified during total cellular RNA extraction. Contaminant genomic DNA may 
decrease the efficacy and specificity of subsequent PCR reactions. DpnI does not digest cDNA as 
it is non-methylated. These steps ensure that there is only cDNA, with its purity and integrity 
intact, at the end of reverse transcription.

2.3. PCR amplification of viral cDNA 
Reverse transcription (RT, protocols described above) combined with subsequent polymerase 
chain reaction (PCR) generate large amounts of cDNA amplicons.  
2.3.1. Full-length amplification of USUV and ZIKA viral RNA  
The initial attempts to produce full-length amplification of USUV (Genbank no.AY453411.1) and 
ZIKV (Genbank no.KU501215.1) genomes were unsuccessful (both ~11kb) respectively. Two 
DNA polymerases, AccuPrime Taq DNA polymerase (Invitrogen) and Q5 High-Fidelity DNA 
polymerase (New England Biolabs), were tested to check which polymerase worked better for this 
system. Minor changes were made to reaction conditions in every attempt, based on the results of 
preceding experiments. The different PCR protocols for using AccuPrime Taq DNA polymerase 
and Q5 High-Fidelity DNA polymerase are summarized in appendices 3-4 respectively.   
2.3.2. Two-fragment amplification of USUV/ ZIKA viral RNA using RT-PCR

3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
3.1. Establishing the methods for full cDNA genome amplification of ZIKV and USUV 
3.1.1 Reverse Transcription (RT) 
To determine what enzyme will give greater yields in our attempt to amplify the full genomic 
cDNA, we compared two diverse reverse transcriptases: SuperScript III (Invitrogen) and Maxima 
RT H minus (ThermoFisher Scientific). We compared reactions using 1 U/µl and 10U/µl of 
Maxima (as recommended by the manufacturer) with Superscript III methods (10U/µl). Among 
these, it was observed that SuperScript III worked better for the reverse transcription of both USUV 
and ZIKV samples, when compared to Maxima. The cDNA generated from RT using SuperScript 
III was amplified to the expected size by subsequent PCR reactions (Figures 15a and 15b). The 
same PCR with cDNA obtained with Maxima did not achieve the same results (data not shown). 
Hence, SuperScript III was preferred to generate cDNA for further experiments.

4. CONCLUSIONS  
The main aim of this study was to generate infectious clones for ZIKV and USUV that could be 
used in the recovery and genetic manipulation of these viruses by the host lab. To this aim we have 
develop several methods for the amplification, cloning, and quantification of viral genomes. The 
main conclusions are:  
 1. Reverse transcription of USUV and ZIKV viral RNA seems to be more efficient when 
performed with SuperScriptIII (Invitrogen) than with Maxima H Minus (Thermo Fisher 
Scientific), as evidenced by greater yields and specificity of subsequent PCR reactions. 
 2. We have established the methods for the PCR amplification of ZIKV and USUV full-
length genomes from reversed transcribed cDNA with Q5 polymerase (New England 
Biolabs). We have successfully amplified the whole genomes by generating two 
overlapping amplicons in each case.  
 3. We have successfully cloned a large fragment of ~7.7kb of ZIKV cDNA both in 
pBeloBAC11, using recombination methods (In-fusion kit), and TOPO XL-2. This is 
a step forward towards the development of an infectious clone for ZIKV although the 
cloning of the remaining ~3.5 kb has not yet been accomplished. Recent publications 
suggest that, despite this region is more toxic for the bacteria than the larger fragment 
already cloned, its cloning is feasible. 
 4. We have not been able to clone any of the diverse amplicons of USUV that we have 
obtained in different vectors tested (i.e. pBeloBAC11, TOPO XL-2).  
 5. We have established a basic method to quantify ZIKV RNA by real-time RT-PCR 
procedures. This protocol will be of use to the host lab to the accurate determination of 
the number of molecules in a given sample. We foresee that this method can be 
employed for different purposes such as determining the number of viral RNA 
molecules generated by in vitro transcription of ZIKV infectious clones, or analyze the

5. FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
In order to achieve the overall aim of the project on the generation of infectious clones, there are 
several strategies that can be tested to overcome some of the current negative results. For USUV, 
we plan to attempt the cloning of individual shorter fragments containing overlapping regions, 
such as the strategy designed for ZIKV, that can then enable the recovery of full genomic DNA. 
Instead of using recombination procedures in the cloning of USUV fragments we could consider 
using restriction digestion enzymes may be increasing the number of clones containing the full 
fragment without deletions. As discussed earlier, USUV is an emerging virus and generating an 
infectious clone for it would give us a great insight into its genome and the critical determinants 
of the proteins that it codes. An infectious clone for USUV could be instrumental in the rational 
attenuation of the virus, and this could be exploited in the design of vaccines in case the virus 
becomes more relevant to human health (e.g. increased incidence and virulence) or animal industry 
(i.e. infection in poultry).  
In our attempt to obtain a full ZIKV genome infectious clone, we are only missing the cloning of 
a short fragment of 3.5Kb containing the 5’ genomic region. This plasmid together with those 
already obtained could lead to the recovery of infectious virus by a two-plasmid based approach 
strategy as was reported by Shan et al in 2016 (18). A possible strategy now is to design new 
primers to amplify any of the vectors already obtained for the 5’ region containing deletions and 
amplify this vector with specific primers (Figure 29). Then, amplify from reverse transcribed RNA 
followed by PCR the fragment missing in the deleted clone(s) and clone with In-Fusion. Since in 
strategy only requires a shorter fragment amplification of ZIKV cDNA than before, we 
hypothesize that it will be easier to get sharper PCR products without secondary bands that may 
facilitate the cloning without introducing new deletions. Alternatively, PCR amplicons of these 
deleted regions could be first cloned into TOPO or any other suitable vector and from there transfer 
this DNA to the corresponding pBeloBAC11.  
In a last attempt to minimize deletions of this 3.5 Kb region, we also plan to clone these amplicons 
into TOPO XL-2 and then transform the ligation into CopyCutter EPI400 Chemically Competent 
E. coli cells (Lucigen). These bacteria used allow the plasmid to propagate only to a low copy 
number. This method is used in general to clone unstable DNA. We have carried a preliminary 
attempt with these cells but unfortunately, we were unable to obtain any colonies. We plan to 
==PAGE:73==
 
65
repeat this experiment again as in our unsuccessful attempt we used an old stock of bacteria (>9 
months) that could have lost efficiency along this period and during our recent moving of -80˚C 
freezers to our new location in Lyngby. This strategy could be tested again with freshly ordered 
cells. 
In conclusion, given the emergence of ZIKV as a major global threat and existing strategies of 
producing ZIKV infectious clones being laborious, it could be beneficial for the host lab led by 
Dr. Arias to obtain reverse genetics methods for the recovery of infectious ZIKV. As far as USUV 
is concerned, there is still no infectious clone available as discussed earlier. USUV has been 
declared an emerging virus circulating in Europe causing large mortality among blackbirds and 
sporadic cases of severe disease in humans (27). Therefore, an infectious clone for USUV would 
help in developing strategies to treat infections if there is ever an outbreak.

discarded and the column was placed back into the same collection tube. 
8. 500µl of wash solution (with ethanol) was then added to each sample and centrifuged for 
1 minute at 12,000 rpm. The flow-through was discarded and the column was placed back 
into the same collection tube. 
9. Step 8 was repeated. 
10. The columns were next placed in dry collection tubes and air dried for 1 minute (to remove 
any residual ethanol). 
11. Nuclease-free water (30µl) was pre-heated to 50˚C and used to elute each sample into new 
1.5 mL micro centrifuge tubes.  
12. The columns were discarded and DNA concentration of each sample was measured using 
a Nanodrop Spectrophotometer device (Appendix 6). 
13. The purified plasmid DNA samples were stored at -20˚C.

Appendix 9. Ethanol precipitation  
1. To each sample, 0.1 volumes of 3M sodium acetate at pH 5.2 were added. 
2. Next, 2.5 volumes of 96% ethanol (calculated before adding sodium acetate) were 
added and the samples were shaken. 
3. The samples were incubated on ice for 15 minutes and DNA precipitated at 
>14,000g for 30 minutes at room temperature. 
4. The supernatant was discarded carefully to avoid disturbing the pellet. 
5. The next step was rinsing with 75% ethanol and centrifuging again for 15 minutes. 
6. The supernatant was discarded and the tubes air-dried for >90 minutes.  
7. DNA pellets were then dissolved in H2O 
8. Each tube was vortexed twice during 1 minute each time to allow the pellet being 
dissolved.

11.1. Transformation protocol for In-Fusion recombination cloning 
1. A maximum volume of 5 µl of In-Fusion reaction were added to 50 µl of Stellar competent

2. Cells were thawed on ice bath prior to use 
3. 2.5 µl (1-5ng) of In-Fusion reaction mixture was added to 50 µl of competent cells in a

6. The tubes were then placed on ice for 1-2 minutes.

Appendix 12. PCR product purification using QIAquick PCR purification Kit (Qiagen) 
*Ethanol (96-100%) was added to Washing Buffer (PE) before use 
*A 1:250 volume of a pH indicator I solution (provided by Qiagen) was added to Buffer PB.  
*All centrifugation steps were carried out at 17,900g (13,000 rpm) in a conventional table top 
micro centrifuge at room temperature. 
1. 5 volumes of Buffer PB were added to 1 volume of the PCR reaction and mixed.  
2. The QIAquick column was placed in the provided 2mL collection tube.  
3. The sample was applied to the column, to bind DNA and centrifuged for 30-60seconds. At 
the end of the centrifugation step, the flow-through was discarded and the QIAquick 
column was placed back in the same tube. 
4. To wash the sample, 750 µl of Buffer PE were added to the QIAquick column and 
centrifuged for 30-60 seconds. At the end of the centrifugation, the flow-through was 
discarded and the QIAquick column was placed back in the same tube. 
5. Next, the column was placed in a 2mL collection tube and the centrifugation was repeated 
for a dry spin, to remove any residual ethanol. 
6. Each QIAquick column was then placed in a clean 1.5 mL micro centrifuge tube. 
7. To elute the DNA, 50 µl nuclease-free water preheated to 55˚C were added to the center of 
the QIAquick membrane and the columns were centrifuged for 1 minute. 
8. The DNA concentration of the purified PCR products were measured using the Nanodrop 
spectrophotometer (Appendix 6).

Appendix 14. DNA Extraction from Agarose gels  
*DNA extraction from agarose gels was done using Nucleospin gel and PCR clean-up kit 
(Macherey-Nagel) 
*All centrifugation steps were done at room temperature 
 
1. A clean scalpel was used to excise the DNA fragment from the agarose gel (TBE 0.5x) and 
all the excess agarose was removed.  
2. The excised gel slice was transferred to a clean 1.5 mL micro centrifuge and its weight was 
determined. 
3. The volume of buffer NTI added was calculated based on the following calculation given 
by the manufacturer i.e. for each 100 mg of agarose gel of <2%, 200 µl of Buffer NTI was 
recommended to be added. For gels containing >2%, double the volume of Buffer NTI was 
recommended to be added.  
4. After the addition of buffer NTI, the samples were incubated for 5-10 minutes at 50˚C. The 
sample tubes were vortexed briefly every 2-3 minutes until the gel slice was completely 
dissolved. 
5. Next, the Nucleospin gel and PCR clean-up columns were placed into a 2 mL collection 
tubes and up to 700 µl of sample was loaded respectively. 
6. The columns are then centrifuged at 11,000g for 30 seconds. The flow-through was 
discarded and the column was placed back into the same collection tubes. 
7. If there was any remaining sample, it was loaded into the same column and the above 
centrifugation step was repeated. 
8. 700 µl of Buffer NT3 was then added to the Nucleospin gel and PCR clean-up columns 
and the columns were centrifuged for 30 seconds at 11,000g. The flow-through was 
discarded and the column was placed back into the same collection tube. 
9. The previous washing step was repeated. 
10. The columns were then centrifuged for 1 minute at 11,000g to remove Buffer NT3 
completely. It was made sure that the spin column did not come in contact with the flow-
through. 
11. The Nucleospin gel and PCR clean-up columns were then placed into new 1.5 mL pre-
fabricated tubes. 
==PAGE:99==
 
91
12. Elution was done with 30 µl nuclease-free water (preheated to 50˚C) and the tubes were 
incubated at room temperature for 1 minute and then centrifuged at room temperature for 
1 minute at 11,000g. 
13. The DNA concentration in each elution was measured using the Nanodrop 
Spectrophotometer (Appendix 6) and the samples were stored at -20˚C, if they were not 
used immediately.
