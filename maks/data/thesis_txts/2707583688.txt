Abstract
This thesis deals mainly with the numerical formulation of a thin-walled beam cross section
using generalized beam theory, and a novel approach to the derivation of the fundamental
distortion modes. The thesis is mainly based on the following papers by Jeppe J¨onsson and
Anders Bau Hansen
’Displacement modes of a thin-walled beam model with deformable cross sections’ [1]
’A GBT-framework towards modal modelling of steel structures’ [2]
’A thin-walled beam element based on semi-analytical solution modes’ [3]
From the literature cited above, we expect there to be twelve fundamental beam modes
with a polynomial variation. The idea behind this thesis was to solve directly for these twelve
beam modes using a simple procedure of inserting a guessed solution form into the governing
diﬀerential equation.
The problem was complicated by the fact that the stiﬀness matrices
include singularities which had to be eliminated.
The ﬁrst part of the thesis starts out with deriving a displacement ﬁeld and a strain ﬁeld
using kinematic assumptions. A diﬀerence between this thesis and the cited literature is the
assumption that the Poisson eﬀect is equal to zero. This choice was made to avoid potential
unforeseen complications in the mathematical derivations that were noticed in some projects of
similar nature during the literature study. The justiﬁcation is that if the derivations have been
made successfully without the Poisson eﬀect, retracing the procedure with the Poisson eﬀect
included is much simpler.
Based on the displacement- and strain ﬁeld, the principle of potential elastic energy has
been used to derive a governing diﬀerential equation and the related local and global stiﬀness
matrices.
The second part details the original idea behind the solution method, where a guessed
polynomial solution is inserted into the governing diﬀerential equation to obtain a polynomial
equation of equilibrium, which includes the diﬀerent displacement vectors. Qualiﬁed guesses for
the diﬀerent solution modes are presented, based mainly on [1], and inserted into the polynomial
equation, systematically solving for higher and higher order modes.
The third part deals with reducing the complexity of the system of equations by eliminating
enough known singularities that it is possible to solve for the unknown displacement vectors
that form the fundamental beam modes.

Introduction
Thin walled structures are becoming more and more prominent in modern construction. From
wind turbine blades to airplane wings to slender building constructions, the sleekness of the
building parts opens up new possibilities within construction, as well as having big potential
positive economic eﬀects. However this puts demands on the abilities of engineers to be able
to properly analyse the behavior of thin walled beams, taking into account cross-sectional
distortion that normal analytical approaches, such as Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, does not.
Generalized beam theory uses a semi-dicretization approach, which means that the cross section
is dicretized using prismatic thin-wall elements, while the axial variation along the length of the
beam is determined by a function of variation. This makes the approach more computationally
eﬃcient that traditional ﬁnite element models, where the entire beam has to be dicretized by
plate elements.
This thesis is split into three main parts. In the ﬁrs part kinematic assumptions are intro-
duced for a deformed beam cross section. Based on linear elastic material behaviour a strain
ﬁeld is derived. Through a potential strain energy approach local stiﬀness matrices are derived
by discretizing the cross section into local wall elements. The local elements can be assembled,
which allows us to create arbitrary cross sections and obtain global stiﬀness matrices for them.
Using energy principles the governing diﬀerential equation is derived based on cross sectional
stiﬀness matrices and axial variation functions.
In the second part an attempt is made to solve directly for the fundamental beam modes
that we expect to ﬁnd. We expect there to be twelve fundamental beam modes because that
is the case for the literature mainly used for this thesis [1][3].
In the third part, problems arising from stiﬀness matrix singularities are being dealt with,
and a solution is found for the displacement vectors that make up the twelwe fundamental
beam modes.

Kinematic Assumptions
In order to describe the spatial displacements of an arbitrary cross-section, kinematic assump-
tions must be deﬁned.
Let us imagine a thin-walled beam positioned in a global, three-
dimensional, coordinate system (X, Y , Z). The cross-section of the beam is located in the
(X, Y ) plane. Apart from the global coordinate system, there is a local coordinate system (s,
n, z), where s runs along the center-line of the entire cross-section of the beam, n runs normal
to s and the local z-coordinate is parallel to the global Z. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 1

Figure 1: Global and local coordinate system
In the formulation of the displacement ﬁeld of the cross-section we distinguish between in-
plane displacements (s, n) and out-of-plane displacements (z), which is referred to as warping
displacement. Additionally, we want to describe the variation of the cross-sectional displace-
ment along the the beam, in the z-direction. For this purpose a couple of amplitude functions
are introduced, one for the variation of the in-plane displacements, ψ(z), and one for the vari-
ation of the warping displacements, η(z). The displacement ﬁeld along with the displacement
parameters and functions are shown in ﬁgure 2.
On the bottom of ﬁgure 2 we see a section of the beam wall in the undeformed and deformed
state. On the left the ﬁgure has the same orientation as the above cross-section, while the right
one is a section cut through the bottom wall, perpendicular to the cross-section.
Looking at the bottom left cut-out in the (s, n)-plane, the movement of the wall element is
described in terms of center line displacements. Looking at a points along the center line, the
displacement in the s-direction is decribed by a displacement function ws and displacements in
the n-direction is described by a function wn. The cross-section of the beam wall remains plane
during deformation, compliant with Kirchoﬀ-Love plate theory, which means the angle of the
deformed section is described by the diﬀerential of the n-displacement with respect to s, wn,s.
The displacement along the thickness of the beam wall is then given by the angle multiplied
by the distance from the undeformed center-line, −nwn,s. All the in-plane displacements are
multiplied by the amplitude function, ψ(z).
The displacement in the z-direction is described by on the bottom right of ﬁgure 2. Diﬀerent
the displacement assumptions in the s-direction, in the z-direction we allow for shear deforma-
tions in the beam wall, consistent with Mindlin-Reissner plate theory. The inclination of the
angle is described by the angle α(s), which gives us an expression for the displacement through
the thickness of the element nα(s).

Conclusion and further work
The goal of the thesis was to develop a simpler solution to ﬁnding the twelve fundamental
polynomial solution modes that are known to exist in the solution space in this sort of Gener-
alized Beam Theory formulation, and then, having found those solutions, so ﬁnd the rest of the
exponential solutions either approximately, solving for part of the generalized eigenvalue prob-
lem in eq. (17.1), or in the same manner as was done in section 14, only this time, instead of
elimination four singularities, we would attempt to eliminate all the known singularities found
in section 15.
Although the method was supposed to be simple, the derivation proved diﬃcult. The end
result is that we were able to solve for the polynomial displacement modes in the form of nine
displacement vectors.
No further progress was made in solving for the exponential modes. For further work, it
would be interesting to explore the ideas that we did not get to in this thesis.

Eliminating more singularities
To solve for the polynomial displacement vectors in this thesis, it was necessary to eliminate
the known singularities found in Ks
ww. Using this same logic, it might be possible to make a
new displacement ﬁeld, this time containing all the nine known displacement vectors, removing
more singularities and solving for the remaining exponential displacement vectors in a similar
way to what was done in this thesis for the polynomial displacement vectors.
Including the Poisson eﬀect
Looking at the solution to the bending modes in eq. (15.24), it seems that the Poisson eﬀect
would not pose any problem. The addition of two in-plane displacement vectors relating to
Poisson coupling could be solved directly, since all the other vectors are known.
