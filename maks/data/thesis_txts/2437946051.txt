Abstract
The purpose of this project was to analyze how the direction and distribution of energy
aﬀects the listening eﬀort. This was achieved by using Odeon for the simulation of diﬀerent
room designs in which the directional energy was controlled by varying the absorption,
scattering, furniture, and source and receiver positions, maintaining the reverberation
time, and other objective parameters to make sure the listening eﬀort was only aﬀected
by the energy distribution. The impulse responses B-Format generated from Odeon were
used to calculate diﬀerent energy directional ratios, and then for the auralization with
anechoic recordings to used as stimuli for the a listening test. A paired-comparison test
was done to evaluate which scenarios required more eﬀort and a statistical analysis was
done with Rstudio, which calculated a worth value for each scenario depending on the
eﬀort comparison. A least square regression was done after to evaluate if there was a
relation between the diﬀerent energy ratios and intensities and the eﬀort worth value. In
this tests there was a clear eﬀort decrease for sound coming from the Z direction, and
more eﬀort needed for a strong direct sound from the X direction, but the correlations
found on the statistical analysis are weak and the amount of data is not enough to make
a reliable conclusion. However, we can conclude that even though scenarios have good
listening conditions on the current objective parameters, diﬀerent amounts of eﬀort are
needed to understand. A further analysis should be done to analyze which are the factors
that inﬂuence the preference.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The quality of the acoustics in a classroom has a huge impact on the ability of students
to understand and learn.
The noise, echoes, reverberation, and modes interfere with
the performance of students, showing signiﬁcant diﬀerences [1]. The acoustic design of
classrooms tend to focus on acoustic conditions that aﬀect the speech transmission index
(STI) or other speech recognition parameters, but they do not asses how much eﬀort the
people have to make to understand. Listening eﬀort refers to the exertion of physical
or mental power required to understand speech [2], this is not taken in consideration in
STI tests, since diﬀerent amounts of mental eﬀort can achieve the same results on the
tests. However, it has a big impact on the performance of students and their ability to
learn, specially on the hearing impaired. The perception of sound involves both subjective
and objective factors which make it a complex process, therefore it is very important to
evaluate the diﬀerent properties and determine their relevance for the diﬀerent acoustical
needs.
1.1
Motivation
The listening eﬀort that has to be done by students in regular classrooms has a big impact
on their ability to understand and process information. Students that have to make a
bigger eﬀort to listen, for instance, hearing impaired, have shown to suﬀer from fatigue,
having a negative consequence on their learning and in some cases causing sick leaves
[3]. There is a growing eﬀort in understanding the concept of listening eﬀort. Various
methodologies have been used for its research, however there is a lack of clarity on the
clinical meaning of this concept [4].
Acoustics is a very subjective discipline, and there is a big eﬀort on ﬁnding objective
parameters to help on the design of the best listening conditions. There are no objective
parameters for assessing the listening eﬀort, and being able to design a classroom taking
it into consideration can have a big impact on people’s education. It is very important to
further research this concept and ﬁnd a way to measure it.The main motivation for this
project is to analyze if the energy direction can be used as an an objective parameter for
assessing listening eﬀort and thus be useful on future classroom designs.

1.3
Background
1.3.1
Listening eﬀort
The listening eﬀort is deﬁned as the exertion of physical or mental power required to
understand speech [2]. There has been a growing interest in the concept on the past
years. However, there is lack of clarity on the clinical meaning of the concept. Research
studies use a variety of methodologies including self-report, behavioural and psychological
measures [4].
In noisy environments our brain has the ability to carry out the important operations to
select particular sounds and ﬁlter out irrelevant information [5]. This becomes more dif-
ﬁcult in noisy environments, which require a higher level of concentration to understand.
In a classroom, students that have to exert a higher eﬀort experience more fatigue, and
thus suﬀer from stress and sick leaves, having a negative impact on their understanding
and learning.
Students who are hearing impaired need to make a bigger eﬀort, making them more
prone to suﬀer from fatigue caused by an increase of concentration or eﬀort required to
understand during classes. This eﬀort can cause chronic feelings of fatigue and stress,
therefore having an increase of sick-leaves, and a big impact on the students classroom
performance.[3].
1.3.2
Potential Measures
The self-reported measure is often assessed with a closed-set questionnaire or a rating
scale. It measures the extent of listening diﬃculties experienced in real-world settings. It
includes questions that relate to the eﬀort required in listening situations. The answers
are within a scale, normally from 1 to 7, with 1 being the highest diﬃculty, and 7 the

lowest. This tests can be done in real-time with listening tests [4].
This measures are quick and easy to do and do not require an expert to administer it.
They provide an insight to how the person experiences the eﬀort, but the measures are
limited by their subjective nature. For instance, two persons might have a diﬀerent in-
terpretation of what eﬀort is or on its threshold. Therefore, an increase in mental eﬀort
could go undetected using this method [4].
The dual-task performance is a behavioral methodology used very often to asses eﬀort.
It consists of two tasks, the primary task uses the majority of the mental capacity, and
the second one the spare mental capacity. When the primary task becomes more diﬃcult
there is less capacity for the second one, this is interpreted as evidence of increase in
eﬀort [6]. This is normally assessed by the seven sentences experiment, in which people
are asked to remember the last word of each sentence and then repeat it.
Physiological methods are also used, aiming to capture changes in the autonomous ner-
vous system caused by a certain task. This is normally measured in skin conductance,
heart rate variability, or with pupillometry. The last one measures changes in the pupil
diameter, and has been used to estimate changes in attention and perception. When a
task needs a high mental eﬀort, the pupil dilates, until too much eﬀort is needed. Re-
search in this ﬁeld has grown fast and is very useful for evaluating listening eﬀort when
performed correctly, as other factors can alter the pupil dilation [7].
A paired comparison test allows people to choose between two stimuli, i and j, where each
of the stimuli is paired once with every other stimuli. This method is very useful when
the subjective assesment of people is needed. The methods developed by Thurstonian
and Bradley–Terry, have been used to analyze stimuli like light, sound intensity, wine
preferences, social values, and more [8].

inﬂuence of reverberation [9]. Clarity and many others were suggested as a ratio too.

1.4. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
1.4
Experimental Design
To accomplish the goals a multidisciplinary approach was used. First the rooms where
designed aiming to good listening conditions, this rooms were simulated in the acoustic
software Odeon to evaluate their quality and generate impulse responses. This were used
to analyze the proposed energy direction ratios and then for auralizations. A listening
paired-comparison test was done to evaluate the listening eﬀort of the diﬀerent auralized
scenarios, and then the relation of the eﬀort and the energy ratios was found.
The design approach used for designing the diﬀerent simulated scenarios is explained.
The purpose of this is to vary the surface properties, absorption and scattering, changing
the energy direction and distribution, while keeping similar objective parameters to be
able to do a correlation of the energy direction to listening eﬀort without it being aﬀected
by other parameters. Then an explanation is given of the diﬀerent acoustic objective
parameters used for speech, which are calculated to evaluate the acoustic quality of the
diﬀerent scenarios.
Odeon is used to analyze the room acoustic quality, and how it changes with the diﬀer-
ent variations. This is done by positioning sound sources and receivers, and generating
impulse responses, which contain the information necessary to calculate the objective pa-
rameters, and the proposed energy ratios. The impulse responses are then convolved with
anechoic recordings to generate the auralizations which are used to evaluate the listening
eﬀort.
Energy ratios were calculated from the impulse responses, to evaluate how the energy
direction is aﬀected by the diﬀerent room properties, and to evaluate if they have an eﬀect
on the listening eﬀort. A paired-comparison pilot test was done using the auralizations
to evaluate which scenarios required more eﬀort, and a statisctical analysis was done to
evaluate if there was a relation between the energy direction and the listening eﬀort.

CHAPTER 2
Acoustic Design
2.1
Acoustic Objective Parameters
The objective parameters describe the quality of the sound ﬁeld in a room in terms of
measurable quantities, giving objective measurements to subjective properties. They are
described in ISO 3382 [10]. They are used to asses diﬀerent kind of rooms, which can be
for music or for speech. For each purpose a diﬀerent value is suggested. In this project
we focus on the parameters used to asses speech. One of the most important parameters
is reverberation time. This parameters are calculated by Odeon and used to asses the
listening quality of the diﬀerent simulated rooms.
2.1.1
Reverberation Time
Reverberation time is described as the duration required for the space-averaged sound
energy density in an enclosure to decrease by 60 dB after the source emission has stopped
[10]. It can be approximated by using the Sabine formula 2.1. Where V is the volume
of the room, c is the speed of sound in air, Si are the surface areas, and αi are their
respective absorption coeﬃcients. In practice it is diﬃcult to reach the 60 dB because of
the background noise, so the decay range of 20 or 30 are used, and multiplied by 3 or 2.
This ranges are called T20 and T30. T30 is the value used in this project.

2.2
Acoustic Design Approach
Due to the big amount of sound reﬂections, the boundaries in a classroom create indoor
sound ﬁelds that are very complex, the quality of speech transmission from the teacher
to the students is inﬂuenced by many parameters, and a multidisciplinary approach is
required to achieve good listening conditions in which people can understand and learn
without diﬃculties [15].
This section focuses on the structured approach that was used to achieve the desired
listening conditions according to the acoustic objective parameters. There was focus on
designing for the reverberation time, speech transmission index, deﬁnition, and sound
strength, which are the parameters used to asses speech. This were kept the same for all
the rooms, while the direction of sound was varied for the diﬀerent simulated scenarios,
by changing surface properties like absorption, and scattering, and by adding furniture.
The rooms were modelled with SketchUp, since it is the recommended tool for Odeon,
and were then converted into the Odeon modeller format using the su2odeon plugin.

CHAPTER 3
Acoustic Simulations
Computer modelling has become an established tool in architectural acoustic design
thanks to the improvement in computer techniques for acoustic simulations. They are
ﬂexible because the geometry and surface properties are easily modiﬁed, which decreases
time and cost in comparison to scale models. They can be very reliable and eﬃcient, and
the results can be visualized and analyzed deeply thanks to the information that can be
found in a computer model [20], which can be very useful, in this case, to understand how
to modify the sound ﬁeld direction.
For this project the acoustic simulation software Odeon is used to simulate the diﬀerent
room variations and analyze their acoustic quality. With the simulation, a B-Format
impulse response is generated which is then used to analyze the energy direction of the
diﬀerent sound ﬁelds by calculating the proposed energy direction ratios. Later an au-
ralization is made by convolving the impulse responses with the selected stimuli for the
listening test.
3.1
Acoustic Modelling Method
In acoustics there are diﬀerent types of modelling methods which can be described by
particles or by waves, and the preferences vary depending on the characteristics of the
room that is being modelled and what is desired to achieve. The most common methods
are the ray tracing method and the image source method.
For the ray tracing method a large number of particles are emitted from a source point in
various directions. They are traced around the room and their energy decreases each time
it bounces on a surface, the decrease depends on the absorption coeﬃcient of the surface,
and the new direction is calculated according to Snells law [20]. The image source method
is based on the principle that a specular reﬂection can be constructed geometrically by
mirroring the source in the plane of the reﬂecting surface.
The hybrid method combines the best of both methods, with the idea that there is a higher
chances of image sources being valid if a ray is traced from the source to the surface it
hits.
Odeon implements the hybrid method, based on an image source algorithm for
the prediction of early sound reﬂections, and on ray-tracing for the later part, using a
stochastic scattering process with secondary sources [22]. A binaural impulse response is
calculated from the simulated impulse response, where a lot of information can be found

were selected by following the recommendations of Odeon User’s Manual [23]. To obtain

3.3
Auralization
Auralization is the technique of creating audible sound ﬁles from numerical data, in this
case simulated. It is analogous to visualization, according to Vorlander [24], the word
is used to describe the process of signal generation, processing and reproduction, as well
as its result: the perceivable sound as auralization of an acoustic problem, in this case a
classroom.
To be able to listen to the diﬀerent simulated scenarios, auralizations are generated us-
ing Odeon. It does this by modelling room impulse responses and convolving them with
anechoic recordings of speech or music. The auralizations are ﬁrst used to analyze the
preceived listening eﬀort and choose the relevant cases for testing. They are later used
for the listening tests.
The scenarios used for the test were selected by personally listening to the created au-
ralizations comparing the self assessed listening eﬀort and evaluating if it was possible to
recognize a diﬀerence between them. The energy ratios were also taken in consideration
to be able to evaluate a varied range of energy ratios and compare the diﬀerent directions.
3.3.1
Impulse response
When the source emits a sound, the sound waves are reﬂected from the diﬀerent surfaces
and eventually reach a position in the room which in the simulations is selected as the
receiver. The impulse response is composed ﬁrstly by the direct sound, then the early
reﬂections, followed by the late reﬂections which occur 80ms after the direct sound and
form the reverberation. All of this characteristics aﬀect the perception of the sound in a

a source and a receiver. The impulse response is given as h(t), with it being pressure over

calculate the energy ratios.

3.3.2
Ambisonics
Ambisonics is a surround system for encoding and rendering a 3D sound ﬁeld [26]. The
recorded sound is encoded in a varied number of channels, increasing the channels increases
the level of accuracy. The order of ambisonics depends on the amount of channels, with
the zero-order having one channel and ﬁrst-order having four, which is known as B-format.
The higher orders are more complex.
3.3.3

directions relative to the head of the receiver, where the W-channel represents the omni-
directional signal, which is an average of the others. The X-channel contains the direct
information from the front and back, Y-channel from the left and right, and Z-channel
from the top and bottom. They represent the pressure of the recorded sound ﬁeld, and
are convolved with the selected anechoic recording for the auralizations. In ﬁgures 3.9

CHAPTER 4
Subjective listening tests
A subjective test was done to evaluate if there is any correlation between the proposed
energy directional ratios and the listening eﬀort. A paired comparison test was chosen
because it allows to analyze if there is an object dependent variable aﬀecting the perceived
eﬀort of the subjects. A double-wall booth was used from psychophysics HEA lab in
building 354 in DTU. A total of 6 volunteers were tested, with ages ranging from 20 to
30 years. Each test lasted 30 minutes, having a total testing time of 6 hours.
4.1
Paired Comparison
The law of comparative judgment is a direct method for psycho physical scaling devel-
oped by Thurstone [28]. It allows n things to be ranked based on the preferred choice
for each of the paired combinations. The ranking determines relative discriminability of
the pairs. It postulates a one-dimensional psychological scale onto which the stimuli are
mapped, and each stimuli belongs to a point in that scale, with the location determined
by a discriminal process. It is assumed a normal distribution around a mean.
The information obtained form this test can provide a lot of important information, if
the stimuli are so alike that it is not possible to choose one over the other, the judges
consistency in making decisions, a weight for each stimulus, and if the property under
judgment may be considered a linear variate [29].
This type of data is very useful when the subjective assessment of people is needed. The
methods developed by Thurston and Bradley–Terry, have been used to analyze stimuli
like light, sound intensity, wine preferences, social values, and more [8].
This test allows people to choose between two stimuli, i and j. Each of the stimuli is
paired once with every other stimuli, and presented to all of the participants. If we have t
objects and n participants, the number of comparisons will be 1/2 ∗rn ∗t ∗(t −1), where
r is the times that a participant judges one speciﬁc comparison, in this case it is just one
time so it can be discarded. A rank order is derived with the most chosen stimuli as the
highest rank, and viceversa [29].

4.2. PILOT TEST
4.2
Pilot Test
The pilot test was performed with a small group of 6 students from the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark. They were 4 men and 2 women from ages between 20 and 30. They
were volunteers and therefore it was agreed that there would not be an economical remu-
neration. To evaluate their hearing abilities before the test, an audiometric examination
was done using the free application named uHear. This made it possible to know that
the answers would not be compromised by hearing diﬃculties.
For the paired comparison test, the auralized stimuli, consisting of pairs of sentences from
the diﬀerent tested scenarios were played. There was a pause of 2 seconds between the
samples and a pause of 10 seconds between the pairs. Each set had 28 pairs and lasted
10 minutes. This was repeated for female voice recordings and male voice recordings,
having a total of 56 pairs, and a total test time of 30 minutes, having a total of 3 hours of
test. The visuals stimuli has a big inﬂuence on the perception of the participants, for this
they were given an eye covering mask to make sure that the surroundings did not aﬀect
the evaluation of the participant and that there was a full focus on the listening sensation.
4.2.1
Instructions to participants
The participants were given a set of written instructions and were asked to read them
and made sure they understood correctly. It was made clear that there was no wrong
or right answers because a subjective perception was being evaluated. They were given
diﬀerent pairs of recording and asked to choose which one they felt that more eﬀort was
needed to understand what was said, and given the option to call a tie in case they did
not perceive a diﬀerence in the amount of exerted eﬀort. In this case ties are allowed
because it will help to understand if there is truly a change and not force the participants
to select an option if there isn’t in fact a preference. The answers were said out loud since
their eyes were covered, the answers were written down in the format needed to input in
the R software, as described in 4.3.4. The participant could say if a break was needed to
make sure their eﬀort was not aﬀected by being tired.
4.2.2
Reproduction through Headphones
When using headphones, there can be front-back confusion because of inaccuracies of the
HRTF information. The localization in frontal horizontal plane will not be very aﬀected.
However, in building acoustic this is not as crucial as in audiological research. The output
samples are binaural to avoid in-head localization. Open design headphones are preferred,
as closed ones change low-frequency response depending on the ”goodness-of-ﬁt” to the
listener’s head, and as the booths were the test takes place are acoustically insulated,
there is no need for closed ones [30].

Discussion
Eﬀect of room properties on the energy ratios
Increasing the scattering coeﬃcient of a wall decreased the energy on the directions where
the sound is coming from, and increased it on the others. This happens because when
the sound waves hit a wall and they are reﬂected, the amount of scattering tells what
the direction of the reﬂections is going to be, specular or scattered. A high scattering
coeﬃcient means that most of the reﬂections will be scattered into diﬀerent directions
and away from where it came from.
The surfaces that were added as tables have the same eﬀect as scattering in the way that
they create more reﬂections with diﬀerent angles. However the energy ratios are diﬀerent
because of the angles in which the energy is reﬂected. Adding table surfaces in the room
decreased the distance which the sound waves travel before being reﬂected, causing the
energy intensity to increase in every direction. Adding bookshelves in the back of the
room had the same scattering eﬀect, directing the reﬂections on the directions diﬀerent
to where it is coming from. causing the Γy ratio to decrease, by increasing the energy on
the Y and Z channels and not in the X channel.
The absorption placement has a big eﬀect on the energy distribution as well. As shown
in ﬁgure 5.11, the distribution of room X2 which is uniformly distributed on the walls has
a very high energy intensity on the X channel and very low in the others, this happens
because there are no reﬂective surfaces that scatter the sound and create diﬀusion. Room
R1 has absorption on the whole back wall, absorbing most of the energy in the X axis,
as that is the ﬁrst wall from which the sound is reﬂected, the energy on all the axis are
decreased as well, but having surfaces without absorption on the ceiling provide reﬂections
that increase the energy in the Z axis compared to when it is fully absorbent. Room R2
has the highest intensities because having absorption only on the edges allow more energy
to reﬂect and scatter through the room.
Room R3 also has absorption on the entire
back wall, decreasing the energy on the channels compared to R2, but having most of the
ceiling and side walls without absorption provides a higher energy than R1.

CHAPTER 7
Conclussion
The position of the sound source and receiver has the biggest eﬀect on the energy di-
rection since the energy intensity of the direct sound is higher than the energy intensity
of the reﬂections. However, changing the properties of the room has an eﬀect on where
the reﬂections are directed towards and their intensity. The diﬀusivity of the sound ﬁeld
in a room can be controlled by changing the scattering of the walls, the placement and
amount of absorption, and the furniture. The computer modelling tools are very use-
ful to analyze the way sound travels in a room from the source to a receiver, they make
available a lot of information that allows to know more about the energy in the sound ﬁeld.
From the data analysis of the eﬀort worth and the diﬀerent energy ratios, the trend
showed that the eﬀort made to understand is increased when the frontal energy is in-
creased. However, the correlations are weak and the amount of data is not enough to
have reliable results. Nevertheless, there is a clear diﬀerence on the preferences for the
diﬀerent scenarios, even though their acoustic parameters for speech are all in the same
range of quality. An objective parameter to assess the listening eﬀort can not be proposed
from the results. Further testing and analysis should be done with more participants and
a wider variety of scenarios to be able to clearly analyze if there is a correlation between
the energy direction and the listening eﬀort.
7.1
Future Work
Further research into this topic could be beneﬁcial to understand better how the energy
direction aﬀects how much eﬀort a person has to do to understand what is being said, and
which direction distribution is the best one. After understanding this, a room design that
improves the listening conditions can be suggested for classrooms. Further research could
include test with more people and scenarios to increase the data amount to be able to do
a deeper statistical analysis and ﬁnd a clear correlation between the energy direction and
the listening eﬀort. A Pupillometry test can be done to evaluate if the answers to the
perceived eﬀort matches the eﬀort found with the physiological measure. When a clear
relation is found between the energy direction and the eﬀort, the same analysis can be
done with participants who are hearing impaired, to compare results and provide more
information on how to design classrooms. The data could potentially help on the design
of hearing aids. A Round Robin could also potentially be done to compare results with
diﬀerent tests and scenarios.
