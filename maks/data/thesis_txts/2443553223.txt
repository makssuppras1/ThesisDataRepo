Abstract
Disruptions are inevitable in modern communication networks. Physically
repairing or conﬁguring the failing entities, in order to provide recovery of
the network services can be a time consuming task. Network protection and
restoration strategies should be taken into consideration, when operating a
network, in order to maintain services upon disruptions.
This thesis will present diﬀerent solutions addressing this problem, together
with an Register-Transfer Level (RTL) design and implementation of the time
sensitive networking standard 802.1CB Frame Replication and Elimination for
Reliability (FRER), that provides seamless redundancy in a network. The
design is then veriﬁed through simulations and physically tested on a Field-
Programmable Gate Array (FPGA).

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The requirements to digital communication increases, as technology evolves,
and society becomes increasingly dependent on it. Network failure or malfunc-
tions, can cause severe disruptions for the users. A major concern for network
providers is to maintain service under the occurrence of failures, as maintaining
services can be critical. Some existing technologies can provide resilience to the
network, in terms of protection or restoration, but with ﬂaws in terms of time
critical information, due to recovery time.
One of the latest additions to the Ethernet family is the Time-Sensitive Net-
working (TSN), which refers to a set of standards that enables the ability to
provide deterministic performance for time critical applications with Ethernet.
As Ethernet is a widely used communication technology which can convey data
frames in smaller or larger networks, the support for time sensitive data, could
make the technology more attractive for future applications.
1.1
Background
A network is not prone to failures in an ideal scenario, however this is not
the case. Failures can occur due to malfunctioning equipment, congestion, or
physical parameters that can aﬀect the transmission, causing disruptions in the
system etc. Diﬀerent solutions, have been proposed to recover from failures in
a resilient network, however most solutions introduces downtime, before the
connection is established once again[1][2].
The TSN standard 802.1CM, deﬁnes proﬁles for fronthaul networks, trans-
porting time sensitive streams, where the frame loss tolerance is 10−7[3]. In
the TSN family 802.1CB FRER provided by the Institute of Electrical and
Electronics Engineers (IEEE), speciﬁes procedures for end-systems and bridges
to ensure a reliable communication system, tolerant to frame loss/corruption
due to failures or congestion etc. The idea is to replicate frames on diﬀerent
paths towards the receiving end system[1]. The intermediate nodes, and the
receiving end-system has to be aware of replicates, forwarding only a single
instance of a frame on a given link. Transmitting on several paths towards
the destination, provides seamless redundancy, which eﬀectively increases the
tolerance to frame loss or corruption in the network. However, providing this
seamless redundancy comes at a cost - as replicates are introduced, the capacity
needed for each stream is increased.
IEEE is not the ﬁrst to provide this seamless redundancy for Ethernet. Airbus
invented the Avionics Full-Duplex Switched Ethernet (AFDX), where frames
are transmitted on diﬀerent virtual links, with individual bandwidth allocations,
in order to ensure a deterministic behaviour[4]. Only the ingress and egress
nodes has to be aware of the duplicates, with the AFDX[4][5], where in CB all

Parallel Redundancy Protocol (PRP) and High-availability Seamless Redun-

Ethernet Linear
Protection Switching
A backup path would be an ideal way of providing protection in a network
where redundant paths is present. Providing this sort of protection in a network
has been proposed by the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) in the
G.8031/Y.1342, where linear protection switching architectures are proposed
to enable protection as a recovery mechanism, restoration is an alternative to
protection, which are introduced in Section 3. ITU proposes an 1+1 and an
1:1 scheme for both uni- and bidirectional protection [7]. Common for both
schemes is that bandwidth is reserved for the working entity, on two paths/links
in order to have a working transport entity, and a protection transport entity,
as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Bidirectional protection switching for 1+1 & 1:1
A protection entity is dedicated for each working entity in the 1+1 architec-
ture, and the traﬃc traverses on both entities at the same time. Hence, the
traﬃc on the working entity is copied to the protection entity.
A protection entity is also dedicated for each working entity in the 1:1 linear
architecture. However, the traﬃc is only transported on the working entity.
If the working entity is subject to failure, traﬃc is switched to the protection
entity.
In both architectures, the receiver is the master - hence, determining whether
the traﬃc should be received on the working entity or the protection entity,
is done using the Automatic Protection Switching (APS) coordination proto-
col [7][8] an will be described in Section 2.1.2. This is used for exchanging

the failure. This means the fault detection time is by default 11.66 ms. On a 1

The receiver is able to switch over to the protection entity in the 1+1 architecture,
without notifying the source prior to switching, due to the traﬃc already being
available at the protection link. In the 1:1 scenario, however, the source has to
be notiﬁed in order to forward the traﬃc on the protection entity instead of
the working entity.

Restoration can be provided by protocols, such as the RSTP as an addition/al-
ternative to protection. In protection where the recovery is pre-planned prior
to the detection of an occurring failure. The restoration mechanism RSTP
provides recovery in a network dynamically when an entity fails, or are being
added to the network, to minimize frame loss and avoid loops in the network
[2][9][10].
In order to discover new- or failing entities, each bridge in the network trans-
mits Bridge Protocol Data Units (BPDUs) as presented in Section 3.1, which
contains information to identify the root bridge, and calculates the shortest
path for the bridge to reach the root bridge.

3.3
Summary
The RSTP is a protocol that allows dynamical recoveries from topology changes
in the network. Redundant paths in the network discards information, to avoid
loops. This also implies that the bandwidth of the redundant paths is wasted

4.1
Virtual Local Area Network
Modern institutional Local Area Network (LAN)s are often conﬁgured hierarchi-
cally. An example could be at Technical University of Denmark (DTU), where
each department has their own LAN communicating with other departments
through a switching hierarchy.
A drawback of this traditional LAN is the traﬃc isolation [13]. The traﬃc
between each group member stays within the network group. Broadcasted
traﬃc as well as traﬃc destined for users not yet discovered in the switch,
must traverse the entire network. Limiting the scope of broadcasted traﬃc,
would increase the security as department related information would not risk
traversing outside the scope of recipients. Within a department, groups might
be needed as students might "sniﬀ" traﬃc, and researchers might want conﬁden-
tial information only distributed between a few recipients. Another drawback
of the traditional LAN is the management, since users often moves around -
cabling for a user has to be reordered i order for the user to stay within a group,
and even more problematic if user may be belonging to two or more diﬀerent
network groups [13].
The VLANs, as the name indicates, makes it possible to make virtual groups,
instead of physical groups. This means that several VLANs can be deﬁned over
a single physical LAN [13]. Each VLAN has its own broadcast domain, hence
the information originating from a VLAN will only be broadcasted to ports
of the same VLAN, isolating the traﬃc. A port from each VLAN has to be
connected to an external switch without VLAN enabled ports, if two hosts on
two diﬀerent VLANs on the same switch wants to communicate.
Ports on a switch, can be conﬁgured as either an access- or trunk-port [14].
Access-ports as the name indicates, is for data entering/exiting a VLAN. An

4.2
Time Sensitive Networking
TSN is a set of standards deﬁned by IEEE aimed at addressing the Quality of
Service (QoS) and determinism in the network, making Ethernet a more viable
solution for real-time applications, such as automotive- and industrial control
communication [17][18]. The major aspects of TSN is:
• Time Synchronization. The devices in the network should be synchronized
to a master clock using the Precision Time Protocol (PTP) [19].
• Stream Insurance. Establish a contract between the transmitter and
receiver in order to allocate resources for a given stream. This includes a
reliable delivery of frames.
• Traﬃc Prioritization. Prioritizing classes of traﬃc in order to provide
QoS for time-sensitive applications.
Enabling these features will increase the interest of using Ethernet with TSN
for time-sensitive applications. In traditional Ethernet, all traﬃc is treated as
best-eﬀort [16], where delay is non-deterministic due to congestion in buﬀers
and no prioritization making the frame loss random.
In the following subsections are the most essential TSN standards described.
Note, that it is a partial list of TSN standards.

4.3
802.1CB - Reliability
Traditional Ethernet relies on ELPS or RSTP as mentioned in Section 2 and
3, in order to recover from network failures. Frame-losses may be handled by
the upper transport layer, such as Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) to
re-transmit dropped/corrupted frames [13][17]. Common for these solutions is
that recovering the network or retransmitting lost or corrupted frames is time
consuming.
The TSN standard 802.1CB, speciﬁes procedures in order to provide seamless
redundancy, where replicates (copies) of frames are sent on multiple paths
through the network in order to guarantee latency, hence FRER reduces the
likelihood of a frame not being delivered [1]. Procedures are also speciﬁed for
detecting and eliminating duplicate frames in order to only forward a single
copy on each link.
The paths for FRER are static and conﬁgurable. In case of bridge failure,
a new path is not recovered dynamically, but the stream relies entirely on
the remaining paths conﬁgured for the stream. A new path can be manually
conﬁgured in order to meet the requirements of the particular stream. Receiving
frames on multiple paths, will most likely reduce the need of recovering frames
from upper layers such as TCP, as the chance of a dropped frame is received
on another path is present.

listener (egress) end-system, as shown in Figure 4.5, where a network is shown.

Individual Recovery Function
The Individual Recovery Function (IRF) needs to be implemented on each port,
to avoid traﬃc from a stuck transmitter sending the same frame continuously in
order to meet the robustness goal of FRER [1]. The IRF should be instantiated
on each input port, in order to discover a malfunctioning adjacent bridge
early. The frames received should be discarded on the input in order to avoid
processing erroneous frames.

4.3.6
Time Out
The sequence generation function may not be aligned with the sequence recovery
functions of the network, due to an accepted rogue packet, or reboot etc. This
would result in no frames being forwarded by the network until the sequence
generator catches up with the recovery functions of the addjecent bridges. The
same applies for rogue frames that have been accepted. A timer is therefor
needed on each stream, in each bridge of the network, in order to ensure quick
recovery. The standard prescribes that a stream that has been subject to time
out, should be detected within 10 ms [1].

4.5
Summary
The fan of TSN-standards, proposes FRER which provide seamless redundancy
as an alternative or addition to ELPS and RSTP, where the recovery time is
reduced signiﬁcantly, since the detection of a malfunctioning entity is omitted,
as the receiver is capable of receiving redundant traﬃc from multiple paths. The
recovery time is thus reduced to the propagation delay between the redundant
paths. Having this seamless redundancy makes the solution very attractive to
time critical applications. A downside of having redundant information in the
network, is the capacity utilized on redundant information. This extra capacity
requirement should be taken into consideration when designing a network with
CB.

5.2.4
Frame Replication and Elimination on Input versus
Output
The implementation comes with its pros and cons whether the procedures is
performed on the input or the output of the switch.
An implementation with a centralized instance of an Arbiter and two LUTs
on the input, with a queue to handle the requests from each port is necessary
due to detecting replicates from other ports.
Some pros and cons of this
implementation are listed below:
+ Area: With a centralized information base, a signiﬁcant reduction in logic
and memory utilization can be achieved, by only having a single instance
of the Arbiter and memory for CB enabled streams.
+ Processing: Since duplicates and rogue frames are removed at the input
these frames are not processed by the switch fabric. The throughput
could be heavily aﬀected by the duplicates if a large fraction of the traﬃc
is duplicated.
−Delay: The centralized implementation scales very poorly since ports
has to queue for information. As the frames are only proven valid on
the last byte of the frame received from the ethsw_eth_receiver where
the integrity of the frame has been validated, the lookup can ﬁrst be
performed hereafter. For a non-tagged frame however, the lookup can be
performed prior to the validation, where the tag is acknowledged after
validating the integrity of the frame.
−Overﬂow: Frame(s) will be lost if the rx_buﬀer overﬂows, and then the
duplicates will have no eﬀect since they will be removed due to already
been seen.

5.4
Cuckoo Hashing
Cuckoo Hashing is a dictionary based algorithm where the lookup time is con-
stant [26], making the algorithm a good candidate for time sensitive information,
as it reduces the processing delay.
The dictionary is used to store information in a table, in relation to a stream.
Assume information is stored at a random available position, the worst case
lookup time is O(n) where n is the number of positions in the dictionary. Using
information such as MAC-address’ as the address or key to the information,
knowing where the information is located in the dictionary is more eﬃcient
and makes the lookup time O(1). In Ethernet traﬃc however, the stream
speciﬁc information can be in relation to the destination MAC-address 48 bits
[13], and the VLAN-ID 12 bits [15] information resulting 48 + 12 = 60 bits of
information. A key of 60 bits would then result in a dictionary or LUT with
260 memory positions, this may be unnecessarily high and may lead to bad
resource utilization depending on the number of entries needed. This can be
avoided by reducing the key size of the stream related information by hashing
the information. Hashing the information can, however, result in collisions
where two diﬀerent streams hashes into the same key, hence, the same position
in the dictionary as information is left out.
The cuckoo hashing algorithm uses multiple hash functions h [26][27], to handle
collisions, by calculating multiple keys, a single entry can be stored at multiple
locations. The cuckoo hashing algorithm can be stored in a single LUT or in h
LUTs(diﬀerent address spaces for each hashing function). Using multiple LUTs
has the advantage that even though the diﬀerent hashing algorithms hashes to
the same value, it is not the same position in memory making the algorithm
more robust.

Summary
A system design for the 802.1CB has been presented in order to enable the TSN
feature of 802.1CB in the Comcores ApS Ethernet Lite Switch. The design
is versatile as if port speed is changed, only the eth_frer_extractor has to be
ﬁtted to the new interface.
The stream splitting function using multicast feature of the Comcores Aps
Ethernet Lite Switch should be based on the SIFs of the 802.1CB, in order to
provide a correct stream splitting function.
The Cuckoo hashing algorithm should be used to provide a constant lookup time,
in order to minimize the processing delay the forwarded frames experiences.
Having this constant lookup time, my introduce a long learning time as the
tables might have to be reordered, in order to insert the entry. The whole
lookup environment has to be reordered with new hashing values, if the learning
ends up in a loop, this procedure is to be performed until no loop exists and all
entries has been inserted.

6.6
Individual Recovery Function
The IRF is implemented as a pass-through component, where the component
extracts the header information of each frame. The information is then used
to discard frames from a stuck transmitter see Section 4.3.3. This is done by
detecting frames carrying the same stream ID and sequence number as the
previous frame. If it is the case, it is discarded, to prevent processing corrupted
frames. The reason for using both the stream ID and the sequence number,
is due to not discarding frames from diﬀerent streams containing the same
sequence number.
6.7
Statistics
The statistics is done on a per port level, and is reset every time it has been
read from the API.The counters roll over, if the statistics has not been read
before reaching its maximum value of 232. The statistical values implemented
are:
• frerCpSeqRcvyPacketsPassed: This statistic is the number of frames passed
by the CB component.
• frerCpSeqRcvyDiscardedPackets: This statistic is the amount of discarded
frames of the CB component. This counts if it is a duplicate, or the frame
is out of range.

6 Implementation
• frerCpSeqRcvyOutOfOrderPackets: This statistic, counts the number of
out of order frames received, that is within the range of acceptance, even
though it is a duplicate.
• frerCpSeqRcvyRoguePackets: The statistic is the frames that are outside
the range of acceptance.
• frerCpSeqRcvyTaglessPackets: The statistic, is the amount of un-tagged
frames, that is enabled for CB, and is therefore encoded with the R-tag,

Implementing the per stream counters, would be simple, as it is just additional

6.9
Summary
The implementation is made generic, and has various conﬁgurations in order to
match diﬀerent use-cases.
All components (if timing can be met) except for the ethsw_frer_extractor,
can be implemented on any given port speed. The ethsw_frer_extractor is
a pipelined 8-bit parallel component speciﬁc for the GMII-interface, a new
extractor component will then be needed for other interfaces.
The counters implemented, makes it easier for any management to monitor the
network, and ensure the behaviour of the conﬁguration. The implementation
has the functionality of working as both a talker-, relay- and listener end-system.

7.2.5
Throughput
The GMII interface is running on a 125 MHz clock (8 ns period) [34], as does
the core clock of the switch implementation by default [23]. The duplicates
introduced by the CB could lead to a signiﬁcant reduction in throughput, as
duplicates has to be processed with the implementation.
Figure 7.30: Throughput with 125 MHz core clock - two identical input streams.
Figure 7.30 shows the eﬀective throughput. Two identical R-Tagged streams is
received on port 0 and 3 with 100% utilization (TX%), and only a single copy
is forwarded on each port. As the duplicates are eliminated, the processing
time used is wasted, reducing the throughput signiﬁcantly to 51.397% (RX%)
on port 1 and 2.
A solution could be to increase the core clock, enabling the switch to process
more frames in a given time interval.
Figure 7.31: Throughput with 250.025 MHz core clock - two identical input
streams.

This is however only suﬃcient as long, no more than two redundant paths is
processed by the node, as more redundant paths would lead to processing of
more replicates.
An implementation, where upon detection of a replicate, the processing of the
duplicate can be stopped, moving to next possible queued frame to reduce
the processing of duplicates. However, in the switch implementation a decent
eﬀort has to be put into the scheduler to be able to perform this operation.
Multicasting duplicates can lead to heavy unnecessary head of line blocking, as
the design involves input and output queues from Section 5.2.1.
7.2.6
Summary
The design has been tested through simulations, with the expected behaviour.
An automated simulation testing veriﬁes the behaviour of the implementation
has been performed using VUnit. The design has also been undergoing tests
in hardware, using an Ethernet tester from Xena, where the implementation
behaves as expected.
Increasing the core clock of the switch can reduce the inﬂuence that processing
duplicates introduces, a faster core clock together with the ability to stop the
processing of duplicates upon detection can compensate for the introduced
duplicate processing.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This thesis, has provided information on how to provide resiliency in networks,
and the pros and cons for the diﬀerent solutions. TSN provides features, that
makes the widely distributed Ethernet technology available for real-time appli-
cations, providing deterministic end-to-end delays, traﬃc prioritization, as well
as reliability. The primary features, together with the most essential standards
has been described in Section 4.2.
A design together with an RTL implementation of the TSN 802.1CB stan-
dard providing seamless redundancy in Ethernet has been presented. The design
uses cuckoo hashing for stream entries, providing a constant lookup time, to
decrease the forwarding delay. A versatile design is implemented to provide an
implementation that suits diﬀerent use-cases, as well as functioning as both
talker-, relay- and listener end-system. The system has been implemented
with port statistics, that can be read through the Comcores ApS Ethernet Lite
Switch API, and the ability to pass stream entries that should be enabled for CB.
The design has been tested with various test cases, presented in Appendix
A.1.1, where the system has been veriﬁed through simulation and hardware
testing.
Processing duplicates, lead to a reduction in throughput at a 125 MHz clock
rate, in order to compensate for the processing of duplicates, the clock was
increased to 250.025, and a 1 Gb
s throughput was achieved. A better approach
would have been to stop the input queues from forwarding, upon detection of
duplicates, in order to process any pending frame faster.
Providing this reliability in the network, 802.1CB can be a key feature in
order to meet the frame loss tolerance required by 802.1CM. It does however
introduce a signiﬁcant amount of redundant information in the network, and
may therefore not be the most viable solution in the fronthaul network.
