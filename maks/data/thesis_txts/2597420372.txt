Abstract
Natural ventilation has been used for many centuries and can, if applied properly, create a comfortable
indoor environment while being sustainable and saving energy. There are many ventilation techniques and
innovative solutions that have been applied in modern buildings and proven to maintain a good indoor
climate. Nowadays, with computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) it is possible to simulate the airﬂow and its
eﬀect on the thermal indoor comfort.
In this thesis time-dependent natural cross-ventilation and its inﬂuence on thermal comfort and air quality
were studied with the CFD software OpenFOAM. First, a computational model was set-up and validated
with experimental research data. The validated model was simpliﬁed in order to save computational time.
Second, a parametric study was carried out where two ventilation techniques, pulse and ﬂush ventilation,
were analysed. Three parameters were chosen to be studied: air velocity, outdoor air temperature and
ventilation time. The results were evaluated with thermal comfort design criteria according to the Danish
building regulations. Long-term thermal indoor comfort was found to be highly dependent on outdoor
temperature as well as the ventilation technique. Air velocity and ventilation time had only local eﬀects in the
studied room and created larger ﬂuctuations with increasing velocity and time.
Lastly, a case study was set-up to apply the ﬁndings from the parametric study and research to a more
realistic scenario. Two critical days of the year were selected, in the range where it was found possible to use
natural ventilation. An oﬃce room for six to eight people was designed with interior walls placed strategically
to constrain the path of the airﬂow through the room. Based on outdoor temperature, the occupancy and
the CO2 concentration in the oﬃce, a ventilation strategy combining pulse, ﬂush and constant ventilation,
was designed for both days. The simulations showed that indoor temperature was very sensitive to heat
generation and solar gain in the room, and the outdoor temperature. The room design and inlet placement
helped to redirect the airﬂow as desired and avoid draught at unwanted areas in the room. However, with
high outdoor temperatures draught was able to improve the indoor environment. Furthermore, it was found
that the predicted mean vote changed signiﬁcantly with diﬀerent levels of clothing and the results of PMV
needed to be taken cautiously.
Many factors inﬂuence the performance of natural ventilation, such as building and room geometry, location
of the building, local climate or usage. For implementing natural ventilation it is important to consider these
factors already in the early design stage. Building regulations (BR) have strict requirements when it comes to
ventilation and these can be diﬃcult to satisfy with natural ventilation. The results in this thesis showed
that by combining diﬀerent ventilation techniques tailored to the present conditions, it is possible to create a
comfortable indoor environment that satisﬁes the BR requirements. However, several other factors like noise
or rain, inlet geometry and placement or the usage of the room were not analysed and could require diﬀerent
ventilation strategies.

Introduction 1
1.1 Motivation . . . . . . . . . . . . 2
1.2 Process and Software Programs2
Until the 20th century, natural ventilation was common practice to create
a comfortable thermal indoor environment and get rid of unwanted
pollutants. Throughout history several methods have been used to create
natural ventilation, depending on location and climate. However, all
methods rely on the concept of either wind or buoyancy driven ventilation.
Ventilation driven by wind results from pressure diﬀerences created by
the wind around a building while buoyancy is a force that occurs due
to temperature diﬀerences and as a consequence density diﬀerences
between the indoor and outdoor environment.
During the last century general living standards have increased. Tighter
buildings decreased the natural air exchange and the development of
new technology made it possible to have more control over the indoor
environment. With increasing complexity and need for space, mechanical
ventilation has been brought more and more into use. However, many
mechanical systems do not ensure a healthy indoor climate. Dry air,
for instance, can be harmful to not only occupants’ health but also the
interior building materials [1]. Nevertheless, mechanical ventilation is
still the ﬁrst choice today. The danish building regulations (BR) require
proof of a ventilation system that can supply a good indoor climate
within the set boundaries. Mechanical ventilation is simple to control and
thereby easy to set a constant performance. On the contrary, it consumes
more energy compared to natural ventilation. Natural ventilation, on the
other hand, can be implemented with little to no energy demand, but
mainly depends on outdoor conditions. The constantly changing outdoor
conditions make the ventilation performance very diﬃcult to predict and
therefore diﬃcult to prove that the requirements set by BR are met.
Today, the introduction of computational ﬂuid dynamic allows to predict
airﬂow, pressure, turbulence, pollutant concentration and temperature
without having to do experimental studies. Nevertheless, the challenge
lies in the computational power needed and the time-consuming solving
process, especially for large buildings or complex conditions.

2
1 Introduction
1.1 Motivation
The main goal for this thesis was to gain data and knowledge about the
eﬀect of natural cross ventilation on the indoor climate. The focus lied on
automatic time-controlled window openings with direct air supply to a
single room. Even though natural ventilation has been used for many
centuries and there is a lot of knowledge about diﬀerent techniques,
there is almost no data to prove it. However, to fulﬁl the requirements
and standards in the building industry, proof is needed. The reason for
mechanical ventilation being the ﬁrst choice when designing a building
is most often the simple control of the indoor environment and the
easy fulﬁlment of the regulations. Unlike mechanical ventilation, natural
ventilation is more complex to control and predict. Hence, in order to
use natural ventilation in a building, a proper analysis of its possibilities
and eﬀects on the indoor environment needs to be performed. While
mechanical ventilation is ﬁrst taken into account in a later stage of the
building process, the analysis of natural ventilation should already be
carried out in the conceptual phase since the geometry and indoor
constellation can have a great inﬂuence on the eﬀectiveness of it. This can
therefore only be done with experimental or computational studies. Since
experimental studies are very time-consuming and expensive, analysis
with computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) is a good alternative. However,
even CFD has its limitations and therefor valid data about the eﬀect of
natural ventilation can be another solution.
1.2 Process and Software Programs
Before starting the analysis, a computational model was set up and
validated in order to ensure accurate results. A model can be validated
with experimental or other numerical studies. However, experimental
studies are preferred because they are more reliable. For this, an ex-
perimental study with a suitable context and solid results was selected.
After validating the model, a parametric study was done, investigating
some chosen parameters. The gathered data was analysed from which a
conclusion was drawn. Afterwards, a case study was carried out to apply
the ﬁndings from the parametric study to a speciﬁc real life setting.

For each of these steps the CFD platform OpenFOAM was used. Open-

2.1 Natural Ventilation
The use of natural ventilation can reduce energy consumption and
provide a good thermal indoor comfort. Many people spend most of
their time indoors. Not only indoor activities but also the building itself
releases air pollutants which need to be removed constantly to ensure
a good indoor environment. Ventilation is the most eﬀective way to
reduce these pollutants. While pollutants from activities such as cooking
or showering only occur occasionally, the CO2 release by people is a
constant factor. The degree of utilisation is strongly dependent on the
climate zone, especially for wind-driven ventilation. When it comes to
natural ventilation, outdoor temperature, wind speed and humidity are
the main factors which inﬂuence indoor comfort. However, these factors
are diﬃcult to predict and control. Research has shown that occupants
are more satisﬁed with the thermal comfort and indoor air quality in
naturally ventilated buildings because it lowers the risk of sick building
syndrome [2].
There are two types of natural ventilation: wind-driven and buoyancy-
driven ventilation. Wind-driven ventilation is based on pressure diﬀer-
ences created by wind around a building and ﬂow through openings in
the building envelope. The eﬀect of warm air rising and cold air dropping
is called the buoyancy eﬀect and results in an airﬂow between the exte-
rior and interior of a building. While single-sided ventilation is mainly
buoyancy-driven, cross-ventilation relies on wind-driven ventilation with
inlet and outlet on opposite walls. The eﬃciency can be improved with
several techniques where the choice of geometry has a great impact on
cross-ventilation. [3]
2.2 Indoor Climate and Thermal Comfort
Evaluation
For a person to feel thermally comfortable in a room there are several
factors which need to be satisﬁed. However, these factors are subjective
and vary therefore for each person. The quality of the indoor climate of a
room not only determines the thermal comfort of a person but also their
health. Environmental parameters temperature, air velocity, humidity
and mean radiant temperature are the main parameters inﬂuencing the
thermal comfort of a person while air quality can be evaluated by the
measurement of the CO2 concentration in a room.

2.2.1 PMV and PPD
The predicted mean vote (PMV) is a 7-scale index, describing the thermal
comfort of a group of people in the same environment. The equation
for calculating the PMV index is based on Fanger’s comfort equation.
Fanger’s equation describes the thermal balance of a person. When the
internal heat production and the body heat loss of a person are equal a
thermal balance has been reached. The PMV index depends on several
factors which can be seen in Figure 2.1. From the PMV, the percentage of
people who will likely feel either warmer or colder than the mean can be
predicted, the predicted people dissatisﬁed (PPD). The PPD will always
be at least 5% which can be seen in the graph.

Mean Radiant Temperature
Mean radiant temperature (MRT) is the mean temperature of surrounding
surfaces in a room which inﬂuences a person’s heat gain or loss due to
radiation. During winter, the mean radiant temperature is nearly equal
to the indoor air temperature for well insulated buildings, while during
the rest of the year, the mean radiant temperature varies locally in the
room and can be higher than the indoor air temperature, for instance
next to a window or shading on a sunny day. [4]
Occupant activity level and clothing
The activity of a person deﬁnes the internal heat production of its body
and is called the meatbolic rate M with the unit met where 1 met = 58
W/m2 of body surface area. The clothing of a person keeps the heat
produced by the body and is therefore described as an insulation value
of clothing with unit clo where 1 clo = 0,155 m2K/W. [4]

2.3 Computational Fluid Dynamics
Computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) uses numerical algorithms to
predict ﬂuid ﬂow and heat transfer in a speciﬁed domain and is used
in a wide range of industries. The three main elements of CFD are
preprocessing, solving and postprocessing and will be explained in the
following sections to give a better understanding of the methods used.
The settings for creating a model and running a simulation can diﬀer
depending on the CFD program and correspond to those of the program
OpenFOAM in this thesis. [8]
2.3.1 Preprocessing
Firstly, the object to be investigated needs to be modelled as a compu-
tational domain. There are two diﬀerent approaches to create the com-
putational domain, a coupled and a decoupled technique. The coupled
technique consists of creating a mesh outside and inside the geometry
of interest. With the decoupled technique a mesh is only created either
outside or inside of the geometry, which saves computational time. [8]
Figure 2.2: Concept of a coupled and de-
coupled approach.
The mesh grid is generated with hexandron cells by using the function
blockMesh. To ensure an accurate and stable simulation, the quality of
the mesh needs to be ﬁne enough and of a certain standard. The larger
the number of cells the better the results. However, a larger number also

Furthermore, the mesh needs to be checked for its quality. The main

boundary conditions of the initial state have to be set corresponding to
the investigated case. These mainly include the geometry, surface and
room temperature, inlet and outlet type.
2.3.2 Turbulence Model and Solver
To select the right solver several ﬂow properties need to be deﬁned for
each individual simulation case. Flow can either be laminar or turbulent
and is deﬁned by the Reynolds number. With a higher Reynolds number
the ﬂow becomes unstable and turbulent which is usually the case for
natural ventilation. As a consequence, a turbulence model is needed.
According to several studies [11–14] the Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes
(RANS) equation with the 휅−휖turbulence model shows good accordance
with the experimental studies and is commonly used for simulations of
natural ventilation because of its low computational time compared to
other models. It can be assumed that the air is incompressible and therefor
its density remains constant with changing pressure. OpenFOAM oﬀers
several solvers for heat transfer and buoyancy-driven incompressible
ﬂows. Since time-controlled ventilation was analysed, a transient solver
was necessary and therefore buoyantBoussinesqPimpleFoam was used
throughout the thesis. PimpleFoam is a combination of the SIMPLE
(steady-state solver) and PISO (transient solver) algorithm which allows
to solve bigger time-steps in a more robust way. [8]
2.3.3 Postprocessing
After running the simulation the data needs to be processed and checked
for its quality. It is important to verify that the solution converged. By
using PimpleFoam every solution of a time-step already converged.
However, to compare results a nearly steady-state needs to be achieved.
This can be achieved by solving a certain amount of time-steps. Lastly, a
grid independence study should be performed to conﬁrm that the mesh
is ﬁne enough to give accurate results which are independent of the mesh
resolution.

2.4 Previous Work
Several papers studied cross-ventilation with diﬀerent aspects, experi-
mental as well as numerical. Many studies focused on comparing diﬀerent
numerical approaches with experimental data. It was found that the
common turbulence models, the standard 휅−휖, the RNG 휅−휖and the
"realizable" 휅−휖models all showed good agreement with the exper-
imental data [12, 15]. Further, validation and parametric studies have
proven the potential of computational ﬂuid dynamics [16, 17]. These
parametric studies mostly focused on window location and incidence
angle of the airﬂow and the application of ventilation strategies, while a
few papers also studied the eﬀect of interior obstacles [18, 19]. However,
the time-consuming process of CFD and the unpredictable parameters
of natural ventilation result in a lack of solid data to create general and
proven design rules for architects. The book Designing Spaces for Natural
Ventilation - An Architect’s Guide by Ulrike Passe and Francine Battaglia
tried to create a comprehensive guideline with help of theory, principle,
concepts and case studies [20]. Cross-ventilation can cover larger areas
with a suﬃcient air change compared to single-sided ventilation. Fur-
thermore, due to the higher air change rate, cross-ventilation can be used
for ventilating several rooms which are connected. However, it cannot
suﬃciently ventilated a building with a large depth and is limited by
obstacles in the room. As a rule of thumb, cross-ventilation can be used
up to a depth of 5 times the ceiling height[21].

It is a common practice to validate a computational model with an exper-
imental model in order to ensure accurate results from the simulation. In
this case, a full-scale experiment of a single room in a wind tunnel, carried
out by Tine Steen Larsen [22], was chosen as a benchmark because it was
a generic room without any greater obstacles inside and had suﬃcient
data available, experimental as well as numerical. The experimental study
focused on measuring the inlet and outlet velocity proﬁle, the air-change
rate and the temperature gradient inside the room and their dependency
on the incidence angle of the wind, temperature diﬀerence and wind
velocity.
Because of the lack of information about the measurement results inside
the room, it was decided to model the whole wind tunnel including the
room inside, even though only the inside of the room was of importance
for this thesis. Furthermore, it allowed to recreate a computational model
that was more similar to the experimental one. For the simulation, a
coupled approach was used to validate the model. Even though this
approach took up more computational time it allowed to create a valid
set-up of the inside of the room. This allowed to use a simpliﬁed model,
consisting only of the room, for a more detailed analysis and thereby
save computational time.

For this thesis, two ventilation methods were analysed: pulse ventilation
and ﬂush ventilation. Pulse ventilation is a ventilation technique where
small volumes of airﬂow are allowed in the room for short periods of time.
Flushing, on the other hand, uses big volumes of air for ventilating in a
period of time. This technique is often used for cooling down buildings,
for example during the night or before going to sleep. To investigate
the inﬂuence of time-controlled natural ventilation, ﬁxed and studied
parameters as well as design criteria for the thermal comfort evaluation
were set up.
The studied parameters were changed during the simulations and their
eﬀect on the thermal comfort were analysed. For this thesis three param-
eters were chosen: air velocity, outdoor temperature and the ventilation
time. While the investigated range of air velocity and outdoor temper-
ature was set according to the danish climate, the ventilation time and
particularly the pulse time needed to be deﬁned ﬁrst. The method for

clo. The heat generation in the room was assumed to be constant and

temperature ranges [4, 25]. The initial room temperature was assumed to

The outdoor temperature was set according to the danish climate. To sim-

4.5 Discussion
Considering the results, evaluation tools and literature it is clear that one
of the main factors inﬂuencing thermal comfort is the temperature [4].
According to the parametric study, with an outdoor temperature of 15°C
and above, natural ventilation is a valid option, remaining just above the
recommended limits of the BRs. However, comfort is a subjective feeling
and, looking at the PMV and PPD results, occupants felt slightly cold
with indoor temperatures just above 20°C. Important factors such as the
mean radiant temperature or the clothing insulation were kept constant
and could inﬂuence the outcome of the study. This can be observed in
Figure 4.10 where the results show a lower PMV index for an outdoor
temperature of 20°C. This was due to the clothing level which was set to
0,7 in this case.
To show the potential of natural ventilation, the number of hours where
the outdoor temperature in Copenhagen is above 15°C during working
hours was calculated. The working hours were set from 09:00 to 17:00
excluding holidays. It resulted in 31% of the whole year.

5.4 Discussion
Placing an interior wall in the middle of the room and the inlet for pulse
ventilation just in front of it resulted in the airﬂow being blocked and
redirected around the wall equally. Thereby, the working area just behind
the wall was more protected from draught than the area next to it. This
was particularly apparent at 1,1m. The other interior wall right next to
the inlet created a leeward side towards the working area. Even though it
only showed a small eﬀect it created a higher thermal comfort by lowering
the PMV index by 0,2 and the DR by 2% compared to the opposite side
of the inlet where the meeting table was placed. As anticipated, the
ﬂush ventilation helped to supply fresh air and keeping a comfortable
temperature. Additionally, for the ﬁrst 30 minutes, no ventilation was
necessary and the indoor temperature stayed steady. Nevertheless, the
combination of pulse and non-pulse ventilation for the cold day did not
help with improved indoor conditions and only delayed the reaching
of the steady state temperature. The reason for this was because the
ratio between pulsing and non-pulsing could not be set high enough to
compensate the lost heat during the non-pulsing time. However, it could
still have an eﬀect on energy savings.
In the case of a hot summer day, draught was preferred and the plots
showed a clear diﬀerence between two diﬀerent window area openings
and positions. In the morning, while the outdoor temperature was
around 24°C, the small window areas were suﬃcient enough to keep
the indoor temperature constant but at the same time not create a DR
above 20% at the working areas. During the hour between 13:00 and 14:00
the temperatures reached almost 27°C and the windows were opened
60% to create a high DR. However, the PMV showed a high divergence
and suggested that people felt either too warm or too cold. During
the morning, occupants on the east side felt too warm and during the
afternoon too cold. Assuming people would adjust their clothing during
the day to their thermal comfort as well as the window opening area the
results for PMV could change signiﬁcantly. Furthermore, shading could
be installed and would help to lower the MRT and thereby increase the
comfort of people sitting in direct sunlight.
To show the potential of natural ventilation for this case study, the
hours above an outdoor temperature of 12°C of a year were calculated.
The working hours were set from 09:00 to 17:00 excluding holidays. It
resulted in 52% of the whole year. However, it cannot be guaranteed that

Discussion and Conclusion 6
6.1 Future Work . . . . . . . . . .
44
The goal of this thesis was to evaluate the possibilities of natural venti-
lation. Since there are many factors that inﬂuence thermal comfort and
studying all of them would take up a lot of work, the following param-
eters were selected to be analysed: air velocity, outdoor temperature
and ventilation method and time. The parametric and the case studies
showed that the main factor that ensured suﬃcient indoor thermal com-
fort was the balance between outdoor temperature and heat generation
in the room. At an outdoor temperature below 15°C, the air supplied
lowered the indoor temperature below the recommendations, creating
an uncomfortable indoor environment. However, the case study showed
that setting up the room and heat generation diﬀerently gave a better
performance than expected compared to the parametric study. With an
outdoor temperature of 10°C it was still possible to create comfortable in-
door temperatures. In fact, the simulation of the morning hours of a cold
spring day showed the best results of the case study. Furthermore, the
history of natural ventilation suggests that there are several techniques
to heat up air for natural ventilated buildings such as double-skin facade,
solar chimney or the use of courtyards [20, 21]. Utilising these techniques
could make it possible to use natural ventilation with lower outdoor
temperatures than the ones considered in this thesis. Research and the
results from the case study indicate that it is possible to inﬂuence the
ﬂow through a room and even a building intentionally with the design
and layout of the room [20]. The position of the inlet in relation to the
local wind and climate characteristics needs to be considered in order
to use natural ventilation eﬃciently. Although it was not a parameter
analysed in the parametric study, the diﬀerent use of the inlet area and
position had a signiﬁcant eﬀect on thermal comfort for a cold spring and
a hot summer day, in the case study. Draught, which is avoided at cold
and moderate outdoor temperatures, can be used as a cooling eﬀect for
indoor environments that are too warm. It shows that during summer,
when people would most likely keep the windows open, mechanical
ventilation is not only nonessential but uses energy unnecessarily. Never-
theless, natural ventilation has obstacles which have not been discussed
in this thesis such as noise pollution and rain. Because natural ventilation
creates a direct connection between the outdoor and indoor, not only
fresh air can enter the room but noise and water as well. However, there
are techniques such as acoustic louvre or splitter attenuator [30] which
take these issues into account.
The building regulations have strict requirements when it comes to
ventilation. A recent article disclosed that regulations make housing
associations break the law to provide a healthy indoor climate for the
residents [1]. An indoor climate that is too dry is a common problem,
not only for the residents’ health but also the building materials, such as
wood, which dry out or develop cracks. The BR forces the administration
of buildings to ventilate with the required minimum ventilation rate
without considering the varying occupancy of a room. For instance, the

air supply of apartments does not take into account how many people are
actually in the room but focuses only on the type of room. This problem
could be solved by adapting the ventilation rate to the CO2 concentration
or humidity in the room. However, it would require a change of the
regulations, not only basing the ventilation system on air change rate.
6.1 Future Work
CFD simulations take a long time to set-up run and process, making the
scope of a parametric CFD study always limited by the time available for
the analysis. The study showed part of the potential of natural ventilation,
however, there are more parameters to be considered and analysed that
either were neglected or assumed in this thesis. The case study showed
the inﬂuence of the room layout but only used the concept of an interior
wall and inlet outlet placement for directing the air ﬂow through the
room. Many other design strategies exist and could have been considered.
Another interesting aspect could be to look into the ventilation of several
rooms and how their geometric relation inﬂuences air ﬂow.
The geometry of the inlet was ﬁxed for this thesis. Considering acoustic
and weather conditions, certain geometries might be more practical and
create a better indoor environment. The wind incidence angle and speed
could be inﬂuenced with diﬀerent geometries and eliminate the problems
that occur with air velocities that are too high.
CO2 concentration was used as an indicator to set-up a ventilation strategy.
However, a complete mixing of the air was assumed. For more accurate
results, CO2 concentration would need to be simulated. Besides CO2,
humidity is an important factor for the indoor climate which was not
modelled or taken into account in this study. Instead of designing the
ventilation strategy based on CO2, humidity could be used too.
