Abstract
One of the expected consequences of climate change is an increase in the frequency of
extreme heat events, which, combined with the prevalence of low energy buildings, greatly
increase the risk of overheating in buildings. Overheating in dwellings is particularly risky
when it comes to heat-sensitive groups, such as the elderly. The aim of this project is to
study possible combinations of building design and design of building services that would
be able to ensure high indoor environmental quality in future nursing homes with a focus
on thermal comfort and daylight.
The analyses in this project were made using the dynamic simulation tool IDA ICE to
study the performance of diﬀerent measures for reduction of overheating. The impact
of the measures on parameters such as daylight availability, overheating, and energy
consumption, as well as performance in a future climate, was assessed ﬁrst on a component
basis, and the ﬁndings were subsequently used to create new design proposals for future
nursing homes.
The study found that using projected climate data for 2050 yielded an 80% reduction in
degree hours compared to the current reference year ﬁle. The 2050 climate data has a
56% lower direct normal radiation compared to the current reference year ﬁle, which is
thought to be the cause of the reduction in overheating in the building. Based on this
study, it is suggested that instead of/as a supplement to using projected future weather
data, the future weather conditions could be taken into consideration by assessing the
heat exposure of the occupants in the building during a heatwave.
The applied solar control coating was found to reduce overheating by up to 53% (measured
in hours with temperatures above 26◦C) by reducing the solar heat gain. However, the
solar control coating also causes a reduction in spatial daylight autonomy of up to 13%
and an increase in heating energy consumption of up to 6%. It was found that the indoor
air quality could be kept within the target 900ppm by using an airﬂow 50% smaller than
what is recommended in EN16798 for sensitive groups [1].
Based on the ﬁndings in this project, it is suggested that the conducted component study
can be used as a tool box for designers to use in the design process of a speciﬁc nursing
home.

Chapter 1
Background
Climate change is one of the biggest challenges facing modern society. One of the expected
consequences in Northern Europe is longer lasting summers with more frequent and
intense heatwaves in the second half of the 21st century[2]. Ruosteenoja et al. [3] found
that if the global temperature rise is kept at 2◦C relative to the pre-industrial era, the
summers in Northern Europe will increase by nearly 30 days by the middle of the 21st
century, and the winters will be 30-60 days shorter.
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has developed a set of pathways
called Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs), which describe diﬀerent climate
futures depending on the level of greenhouse gas emissions in the coming years[4]. The
RCPs take both socioeconomic and emission scenarios into account with variables such
as socio-economic change, technological development, energy and land use, and emissions
of greenhouse gases [5]. The RCPs are named based on the radiative forcing target level.
Each pathway will result in 2100. Radiative forcing describes the diﬀerence between the
solar irradiance absorbed by the Earth and the amount of energy radiated back into
space, e.g. RCP 1.9 has a radiative forcing target level of 1.9 W/m2. RCPs span over the
best-case scenario called RCP 1.9, which is based on reaching the target set in the Paris
Climate Accord to limit the increase in global temperature to 1.5 ◦C to the worst-case
scenario, called RCP 8.5 [6]. In the RCP 8.5 scenario the combination of population
increase, limited growth in wealth and a modest rate of technological improvement will
cause a drastic increase in energy demand and, consequently, greenhouse gas emissions.

CHAPTER 1. BACKGROUND
RCP 8.5 is referred to as ”business as usual” and is a representation of the consequences
of limited or no action to combat climate change [7].
The future climate will have a signiﬁcant impact on the design and performance of new
buildings, as they are often designed to last 50+ years.
According to IPCC [4] the
built environment accounted for 19% of global greenhouse gas emissions in 2010, which
is why implementing green solutions in the built environment is crucial to reaching the
Paris Climate Accord. The increasing understanding of the impacts of climate change
has increased the political focus on reducing greenhouse gas emissions in all sectors. To
reduce the environmental footprint of the built environment, the requirements to energy
consumption have been tightened continuously since the 1960s, see Figure1.1.
Figure 1.1: Development of building energy requirements in Denmark since 1961.
In Denmark and other northern European countries, the increased requirements regarding
operational energy have mainly manifested through increased air-tightness and insulation
to reduce heating demand during winter [8]. These solutions lead to energy savings dur-
ing the heating seasons, but a limited inﬁltration can also cause elevated temperatures
during periods with a high heat gain, where solar radiation is responsible for the largest
contribution [9].
The requirements regarding daylight availability in dwellings have also increased, neces-
sitating large glazing areas, which improves daylight availability but it can also elevate
temperatures to uncomfortable levels [10].
Simone et al.
[8] found that overheating
occurs not only during summer but also in the shoulder seasons in a study of Danish
low energy houses. In a UK context, Ibrahim et al. [11] found that designing for the
low-energy standards increases the tendency to implement active cooling systems, which
works against the purpose of low-energy houses.

==PAGE:17==
The combination of low energy housing standards and the consequences of climate change
means that the problem regarding overheating in dwellings will only increase during the
21st century, and as such action is required to ﬁnd solutions.
One example of an approach to solving these challenges can be found in the Energy in
Buildings and Communities Programme (EBC) which was established by the Interna-
tional Energy Agency (IEA) in 1977 to do research on building energy eﬃciency [12].
The EBC divides its research into programmes called Annexes. The EBCs Annex 62 on
ventilative cooling and the VentiCool platform focus on utilising ventilative cooling to
reduce the need for active cooling while still maintaining a high-quality indoor climate,
including thermal, visual and acoustical comfort as well as indoor air quality [13]. The
VentiCool project is one of several working with resilient cooling strategies because of
the expected temperature increase in Europe. Annex 80 on resilient cooling is another
example. The deﬁnition of resilient cooling used in Annex 80 is as follows:
”Resilient cooling is used to denote low energy and low carbon cooling solutions that
strengthen the ability of individuals, and our community as a whole to withstand, and
also prevent, the thermal and other impacts of changes in global and local climates,
particularly with respect to increasing ambient temperatures and the increasing frequency
and severity of heatwaves.”
As deﬁned in Annex 80, resilient cooling strategies include [14]:
• Reduce externally induced heat gains to indoor environments.
• Oﬀer personal comfort apart from space cooling.
• Remove heat from indoor environments.
• Control the humidity of indoor environments.
These strategies describe the functionality of typical solutions for reducing elevated
temperatures in low-energy buildings, e.g. solar shading or solar control glazing to
reduce solar heat gain or increased ventilation to remove the heat.

CHAPTER 1. BACKGROUND
Solar shading and solar control glazings are very eﬀective at reducing the solar heat
gain through windows [9]. Studies show that external solar shading is more eﬃcient
than internal shading, as it reduces the transmittance of solar radiation into the
dwelling more eﬃciently [10]. In addition, internal solar shading such as drapes or
internal blinds has the advantage of being easily controlled by the occupant to prevent
glare and give a sense of privacy. However, when the solar shading is active, it reduces
the daylight availability and view out, so the use of solar shading must keep a balance
between daylight conditions and thermal comfort.
Mechanical ventilation systems in Denmark are commonly designed to provide fresh air
to the building rather than cooling [9]. However, with a shift of focus, ventilation can
become a method for reduction of overheating. The term ”ventilative cooling” refers to
the eﬀective use of outdoor air through either natural or mechanical ventilation and is a
popular method for decreasing elevated temperatures in low energy buildings [15]. This
method reduces the cooling energy consumption while maintaining thermal comfort
[16]. Ibrahim et al. [11] found that a combination of nighttime ventilative cooling and
solar shading was the optimal strategy when considering both energy demand and
thermal comfort in northern England. Simone et al. [8] assessed the same two strategies
but with a focus on ventilative cooling in a Danish context, because the study assumes
that danish building users are not very keen on solar shading.
Another aspect of a buildings thermal performance is its thermal mass. Buildings with
a high thermal mass will be more robust with regards to temperature changes inside as
the thermal mass will act as a buﬀer [17]. Meaning high thermal mass buildings such as
concrete or brick structures will be less sensitive to temperature changes than e.g. a
timber construction which will have a lower thermal mass [18]. Heeren et al. [19] found
that in a comparison between timber and concrete buildings, both oﬃce and residential
buildings, the timber construction performed better with regards to the environmental
footprint (based on life cycle assessments), whereas the heavy concrete building had a
signiﬁcantly lower cooling energy demand. Consequently, designers are faced with two
opposing interests; reducing the cooling load through high thermal mass or reducing the
environmental footprint through materials with a lower embodied energy.

increase thermal comfort for the occupants. With the expected consequences of climate
change, it is becoming increasingly relevant, especially when designing buildings for the
most sensitive groups. Studies show that young children, persons with chronic illnesses
and the elderly, particularly those over the age of 75, are much more susceptible to
health risks from overheating [20]. The ability of the body to respond to high
temperatures decreases with age, and older persons are more likely to have pre-existing
cardiovascular and pulmonary illnesses, which is also represent an increased risk [21].
The age group 80+ is expected to increase by 150.000 persons before 2030, which
constitutes a 58% increase [22]. There are approximately 40.000 nursing home spaces i
Denmark today [23], and according to Local Government Denmark (Kommunernes

spaces will require increasing the nursing home building stock by up to 25% over the
next 10 years. This leads to the need of designing buildings that can provide an optimal
indoor climate for the elderly both today and in the future.
1.1
Buildings for the Elderly
The average age of a person moving into a nursing home is 83,7 years [25], which puts
nursing home occupants in the extra sensitive group with regards to elevated
temperatures [20]. Many studies show that the elderly perceive indoor climate and
thermal comfort diﬀerently than younger adults [26] [27] [28]. Accordingly, the nursing
homes being built in the future should be designed speciﬁcally with the particular needs
and demands of this age group in mind.
Currently, standards are focused on building for elderly with physical impairments
rather than for persons with mental or cognitive impairment [29]. Consequently, most
nursing homes are built with those disabilities in mind. However, as 42% of Danish
nursing home residents are diagnosed with a dementia illness [25], dementia-friendly
design should be considered in the nursing homes being built over the next 10 years.
The DaneAge Association (Ældresagen) recommends that all new-built and renovated
nursing homes are designed for people with dementia [30].

SBi has published two guidelines (Anvisning 259 [31] and 263 [32]) on building nursing
homes for people with dementia. These guidelines cover a wide range of aspects
regarding dementia-friendly design, but the main focus in this thesis will be on the
indoor climate. In addition to maintaining comfort, a high-quality indoor climate can
also contribute to decreasing confusion, unrest and agitation in people with dementia
[33].
In the following sections, the eﬀect of age on indoor climate requirements are explored.
According to Indeklimah˚andbogen [34], the indoor climate can be divided into 4
subcategories, which the following sections will also be divided into:
• Thermal: The eﬀect of air temperature, mean radiant temperature, air velocity
and air humidity.
• Atmospheric: The content of pollutants such as dust, air humidity, gasses, fumes
and odours.
• Visual: Access to daylight, glare, colour rendering and view out.
• Acoustics: Sound and noise generated by occupants and equipment and
transmitted through the construction.
1.1.1
Thermal indoor climate
The most commonly used method for evaluating thermal comfort in the built
environment is the PMV model (Predicted Mean Vote) created by P. O. Fanger in the
1960s [35]. The model was based on climate chamber research using 1300 college-aged
students [26]. The purpose of the model is to predict the mean value of the overall
thermal sensation of a large group of individuals as a function of six parameters:
Metabolic rate, clothing insulation, air temperature, mean radiant temperature, mean
air velocity and humidity [36].
The same tests that the PMV scale is based on were later conducted with 128 older
adults with an average age of 68. No signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the thermal
sensations of diﬀerent age groups was found in these trials, and as such the PMV
method does not diﬀerentiate between age groups[36]. However, it has since been found
Page 6 of 117
==PAGE:21==
1.1. BUILDINGS FOR THE ELDERLY
in multiple studies that the thermal comfort temperature of older people is usually
slightly higher than for the younger age groups [37, 28, 38, 27]. Schellen et al. [39]
found that generally, the thermal sensation vote of a group of older subjects (67-73
years old) was 0.5 lower om the PMV-scale than that of the younger group (22-25 years
old). This indicates that older adults prefer higher temperatures than what is predicted
by the PMV-model. Wong et al. [40] found that the preferred temperature in the age
group 80-99 was 1.5 to 2.0 ◦C higher compared to the age group 60-79. That is
interesting in relation to the study conducted by Fanger[36], which found no diﬀerence
in thermal sensation between older and younger persons - the older group was too
young to be representative of nursing home residents, which could be part of the reason
why no diﬀerence was found.
According to J. van Hoof, [41] the PMV model cannot be used without corrections
when designing for children, the elderly and people with disabilities. Yu et al. [37]
concluded that the PMV model is not representative for older adults based on a study
of the thermal sensation of elderly persons compared to calculated PMV values. Wang
et al. [27] made an adapted version of the PMV model using a combination of
PMV-calculations, measurements and questionnaires for occupants in a nursing home.
This information was used to create a so-called adaptive coeﬃcient describing the
ability of the elderly to adapt to cooling, and using that coeﬃcient to create a PMV
model speciﬁcally adjusted to the needs of the elderly. That study represents a possible
alternative to the current PMV-method though it would likely require a larger study to
assess the calibration needed to use the PMV-model for elderly persons. These studies
showing that the PMV-scale, the most common method of assessing thermal comfort, is
not directly applicable when designing for elderly people, shows that the thermal
comfort of the elderly is not optimally included in the current standards.
The cause of the diﬀerences between younger and older adults with regards to thermal
comfort comes from the way the body changes with age;
1. Reduced ability to detect heat and cold.
2. Lower metabolic rate.
3. Decreased thermoregulatory response.
Page 7 of 117
==PAGE:22==

4. Diﬀerent body composition (reduced muscle mass, increased fat percentage).
These factors all contribute to older persons having a diﬀerent thermal sensation than
younger persons [26]. These factors also result in older adults being more sensitive to
extreme temperatures, as their ability to regulate body temperature is decreased.
Consequently, older persons are more at risk during extreme temperature events such as
heat waves [21].
Already today Europe is facing extreme heat waves that cause signiﬁcant excess
mortality rates, such as the heatwave of 2003 that hit France in particular but also in
the rest of western Europe [42]. Western Europe suﬀered 22 000 excess deaths during
the heatwave [42], 15 000 of which occurred in France alone , primarily among the
elderly [43]. Another intense heat wave hit Europe in 2018 [44] where Denmark
experienced 250 excess deaths [45]. The severity of the problem of overheating in
nursing homes will only increase as both the older population grows and climate change
progresses. According to Hughes and Natarajan [46], this could increase heat-related
mortality by up to 257% in the UK in 2050 compared to a baseline of the 2000 excess
deaths in the UK during the 2003 heatwave. The majority of the excess deaths are
likely to be among the elderly. Accordingly, climate change resiliency must be taken
into consideration when designing nursing homes.
1.1.2
Visual indoor climate
The visual indoor climate covers both daylight and artiﬁcial lighting. Nursing homes
are commonly poorly lit, and poor lighting in nursing homes can have negative eﬀects
such as an increased risk of falling, increased fatigue and disturbed circadian rhythm
[47]. Daylight in particular has a large impact on circadian rhythm and, consequently,
sleep quality. Daylight will be the focus in this project.
Karami et al. [48] found that increased exposure to bright daylight, especially in the
morning, had a positive eﬀect on the sleep patterns and quality of nursing home
occupants, and Flores-Villa et al. [49] found that increased exposure to daylight
resulted in nursing home occupants awakening less often during the night. Flores-Villa
et al. also refer to lack of sleep as a contributor to an increased risk of falling for
Page 8 of 117
==PAGE:23==
1.1. BUILDINGS FOR THE ELDERLY
occupants in nursing homes. Both sleep and safety from falling contribute to the quality
of life for the occupants.
Depression is the most common condition in persons with a dementia illness with
20-30% of the group receiving the diagnosis [50] and Konis et al. [51] found that
increasing exposure to bright sunlight decreased the symptoms of depression in persons
with dementia. Konis et al. further describe that occupants in nursing homes often are
under-exposed to bright light such as sunlight, and this causes disturbances to the
circadian rhythm and reduced sleep quality. As nursing home occupants spend an
above-average amount of time inside their home, adequate daylight levels are especially
important in this type of housing [31].
As a person ages, the eyes become less ﬂexible and less reactive to changes in lighting
levels. As the pupil can expand less and thus let in less light, an older person will need
a higher light intensity so reach the same level of vision as a younger person. According
to SBi 259 [31] an older person can require up to three times higher light intensity than
a younger adult to see properly. Another eﬀect of ageing is that the pupil reacts more
slowly to changes in light intensity, making older adults more sensitive to glare and
bright lights[52]. Consequently, SBi 259 recommends solar shading, both to avoid glare
from direct sunlight and to decrease the risk of overheating [31].
There are also speciﬁc challenges to solve when designing for residents with dementia as
they can experience the world diﬀerently compared to healthy adults. Direct sunlight
can create shadows and contrasts between light and dark, where dark surfaces can be
experienced as something lying on the ﬂoor or a height diﬀerence which can cause
anxiety or risk of falling [31, 32]. A way to avoid sharp contrasts caused by direct
sunlight is to focus on diﬀuse light in the design, using e.g. light shelves or skylights
with diﬀuse glazings [52].
1.1.3
Acoustic indoor climate
Even though a person’s hearing deteriorates with age, noise should still be kept to a
minimum when designing for persons with dementia. A person with dementia will not
get used to recurring noises such as alarms or automatic locks, as their short term
Page 9 of 117
==PAGE:24==

memory is impaired [53]. For example, Sweep [53] warns against noise coming from
automatic systems, such as dynamic solar shading or automated window opening, as it
can potentially be perceived as threatening or confusing by a person with dementia.
Consequently, Sweep recommends avoiding dynamic solar shading if possible. Acoustic
indoor climate will not be included in this study except for the above mentioned focus
on reducing the need for automated solutions.
1.1.4
Atmospheric indoor climate
To ensure high indoor air quality in nursing homes, SBi 259 [31] recommends a
combination of mechanical ventilation systems and operable windows. However, there
must be security in place to ensure that people with dementia are not at risk of falling
out or leaving through the windows. That means that windows in nursing homes should
not be able to open up completely [31]. Also, as described above, nursing home
residents are more likely to have a lower activity level and are thus more sensitive to
cold draught [31]. This sensitivity should be considered when designing the ventilation
system and the window opening strategy.
The relative humidity (RH) is also a point of concern in nursing homes; a relative
humidity of over 60% can increase the risk of mould [34] and a low relative humidity of
30% can cause dry skin and membranes for the occupants [54].
Another thing diﬀers between assessing a nursing home apartment and a standard
apartment; surveys conducted in Danish municipalities and among Danish nursing
home employees show that nursing home occupants and the elderly receiving care in
private homes often do not shower more than once a week due to budget cuts [55, 30].
The reduced number of showers compared to standard dwellings also impacts the
humidity levels in the nursing home apartment.

Heating:
All interviewees mentioned ﬂoor heating as the prevalent choice for heating in nurs-
ing home apartments. The main advantage of ﬂoor heating is that it is space-eﬃcient
compared to for example radiators while also being easier to clean. One disadvantage
concerning ﬂoor heating is the long reaction times when adjusting the temperature, which
can cause thermal discomfort in meantime and cause occupants to turn the heat all the
way up or down to get a reaction and ending up with thermal discomfort when the ﬂoor
heating has had time to react.
Cooling:
All interviewees stated that building owners typically do not want mechanical cooling
in nursing homes for budgetary reasons.
Instead, interviewees mentioned alternative
strategies that have been implemented, such as creating an option to increase airﬂow
in common areas, use window opening or solar shading to mitigate overheating during
summer.
Ventilation:
The engineer states that as the budget is such a large constraint on the construction pro-
cess, that the cheapest solution is often chosen, and as such the ventilation is dimensioned
after the minimum allowed ventilation rate. This is often not enough to comply with the
temperature and particularly air quality requirements, and consequently, additional ven-
tilation is often necessary through e.g. natural ventilation from window opening. He does,
however, say that manual window opening is an unreliable method of cooling, as occupant
behaviour is diﬃcult to predict, especially in nursing homes where the occupants might
have various disabilities or be bedridden.
Most interviewees state that constant air volume (CAV) systems are common in nursing
home apartments, and it is cheaper to install for the building owner.
The interviewees express diﬀerent opinions on central or decentralised ventilation. One
project manager is working on a project where they are using decentralised ventilation
with a small unit in each apartment. This solution oﬀers ﬂexibility and does not require
ducts going to and from a central unit. However, it does take up a relatively large amount
of space in each apartment, which can be important in small dwellings. The alternative

is using a central air handling unit, which allows for easier and cheaper maintenance of
air handling units and it removes the need for a technical closet in each apartment.
Windows and solar shading:
As mentioned above the daylight requirements often necessitate large glazing areas,
which can cause overheating.
Rather than using mechanical cooling, solar shading
or natural ventilation can be used. However, several project managers mention rules
about the allowed window opening width, which state that for safety reasons (occupants
falling/jumping out) windows are not allowed to open more than 10cm. This poses some
limitations to the use of natural ventilation in addition to the concern about the reli-
ability of manual window opening for cooling. Alternatively, window opening could be
automated, which would create reliable cooling but also might cause agitation to occu-
pants with dementia, who might not understand why the windows are moving.
One project manager mentions that they are increasingly implementing coated glass to
comply with requirements rather than use external shading or mechanical cooling. When
presented with concerns about colour rendering and natural daylight, he said that they
had found coated glazings with a g-value of down to 0.3 which still had excellent colour
rendering and have been implemented in some of their projects. However, sometimes
they still had to supplement the coated glass solutions with dynamic external shading
such as sun activated screens. All interviewed project managers state that they would
prefer not to have external shading as it can have a high investment and maintenance
cost. However, they would all prefer external shading to mechanical cooling.
Climate change:
When asked about how/if they consider climate change and the predicted consequences
in their design, only one project manager said that they were actively working with it
and that they had adapted their methods because of it. She stated that in many of their
previous nursing home projects that were built without external solar shading they had
been forced to add it later, because the rooms grew too warm during summer. They now
require calculations of overheating risk from early design stage to mitigate the problem.
One engineer states that in they use the Design Reference Year (DRY) weather ﬁle and
dimension after the peak loads based on that data, as there are some extreme days in it.

Moisture production from occupants is included in the occupant component i IDA ICE

such as showering release a signiﬁcant amount of moisture into the air which needs to
be accounted for separately. Smith and Svendsen [64] made a table of diﬀerent scenarios
for moisture production from showering. They cite three studies with diﬀerent rates of
moisture production per shower: A minimum moisture release of 0.25 kg per shower, a

Window opening
Occupant behaviour is a large contributor to discrepancies between simulated and actual
energy consumption in a building, and one of the main parameters is window opening
[66]. Many studies have described methods of simulating occupant window opening more
accurately in oﬃces. Markovic et al. [67] have proposed a window opening model using
deep learning based on window opening data from 3 oﬃce buildings in Aachen, Frankfurt
and Philadelphia, and Fabi et al. [68] used measurements from eight oﬃces in Prague
to create stochastic window opening models. These studies are based on measurements
of the window opening in oﬃce buildings, which is not representative of dwellings, but
even a study such as one conducted by Andersen et al. [69] using data from 15 Danish
dwellings to create a stochastic window opening model would not be representative, as the
behaviour of healthy adults is not necessarily the same as for a nursing home resident. For
example, Section 1.1.1 describes how older persons experience diﬀerent thermal sensations
than younger adults. For this reason and the signiﬁcantly longer calculation time when
using this method, stochastic models were not used to simulate window opening behaviour
in this study.
To account for older persons having a reduced ability to detect heat and cold sensations,
it is assumed that they will not open and close windows based on thermal sensation.
There is also a risk that the occupants are bed-ridden and, consequently, be unable to
stand up and open or close the windows based on temperature. Accordingly, this study
assumes that window opening will only be possible when there is staﬀor guests present

Thermal mass
The buﬀering capacity of the thermal mass described in Section 1 can be utilised to reduce
the peak temperature in a building if utilised correctly. It is particularly the thermal mass
of the internal surfaces that impact a buildings heat capacity [81]. The impact of thermal
mass is assessed in this study by increasing and decreasing the densities of the building
materials in the building components without changing the U-value.
This parameter was included to assess the impact of the thermal mass on thermal comfort
in a building, which is particularly interesting because of the current trend of using lighter
materials such as timber to reduce a buildings environmental footprint and the increasing
temperatures in Denmark. This development can be seen in the context of the Danish
built environment where heavy building materials such as brick and concrete with high
thermal masses have been used traditionally.

Daylight evaluation
The daylight availability in the apartment is assessed using spatial daylight autonomy
(sDA). Spatial daylight autonomy describes how large a percentage of the space receives
suﬃcient daylight, assessed through the percentage of ﬂoor area that receives at least
300lx for at least 50% of the annual daylight hours. Daylight hours are deﬁned as the
50% annual hours with the highest percentage of diﬀuse horizontal radiation [80]. IDA
ICE uses the Radiance calculation engine from the US Department of Energy, which is
used in many other daylight simulation tools.
In Radiance a list of parameters (radiance parameters) must be deﬁned to specify the
desired level of precision. Three levels of precision are used in IDA ICE; low, medium
and high. An example of a Radiance parameter could be the number of ambient bounce
(ab) which deﬁnes the number of diﬀuse inter-reﬂections that will be calculated before a
ray path is discarded, i.e. how many times the light can bounce oﬀa surface. The higher
the number of ambient bounces, the higher the level of precision. However, increasing the
number of ambient bounces signiﬁcantly increases the calculation time so it is important
to assess the required level of detail. For a standard room using 5 ambient bounces is
Page 41 of 117
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 3. METHOD
suﬃcient but if more complicated geometries such as blinds are used, a higher number is
required. That is because there may be several interreﬂections between lamellas before
the rays ﬁnd their way out [88]. According to [89] 7 ambient bounces should be used
when analysing blinds in Radiance.

Ventilation
The starting point for all designs in this models has been a CAV system for two primary
reasons: With a CAV system, it is easy to have a central air handling unit that supplies
the same air to all rooms, whereas with a VAV system a small unit would be required
in each apartment which makes maintenance more complicated and it takes up space in
each of the small apartments. A CAV system also has a lower investment cost compared
to a VAV system and cost is an important factor in municipal building projects. In the
interview with the consulting engineer, he stated that because the budget is often such
a large constraint on the process the cheapest solution is often used, which is usually
a CAV system with the minimum required airﬂow. He also stated that the minimum
airﬂow is often not enough to provide good air quality in the apartment, which means it
is necessary to open windows to achieve that.

5.1.2
Daylight
Towards South, all design proposals perform well with regards to daylight, so other
factors such as view out and thermal comfort are the determining factors. The comfort
of the occupant comes is particularly relevant with regards to the dynamic solar shading
solutions. It was found in the literature study that the automated movement of e.g.
dynamic solar shading can cause confusion and agitation in occupants with dementia.
Consequently, even though dynamic solar shading performs well with regards to solar
shading and daylight, it is not the most preferable solution for nursing homes where up
to 42% of occupants have a dementia diagnosis. Especially not when there are passive
solutions that could do the job: The highest sDA for South is found in the two solutions
using a 1.2m overhang and a solar control coating. These are static solutions that perform
well with regards to both daylight and overheating prevention. The horizontal overhang
also reduced the amount of direct radiation hitting the ﬂoor of each apartment, which is
also an advantage for occupants with dementia. This group can have diﬃculty assessing
large contrasts between dark and light on the ﬂoor and can perceive it as something lying
on the ﬂoor or a height diﬀerence, as described in Section 1.1.2. The microlamellas can
have a tendency to distort the view out slightly at all times instead of obstructing it
completely for part of the time like the dynamic solar shading would. Even so, as there
towards South are other options with a better view out, microlamellas are not considered
the best solution either.
Towards East and West, the relationship between overheating and daylight conditions
is more diﬃcult because the sun is lower in the sky at these orientations. That causes
a higher solar heat gain and it is more diﬃcult to remove with static solutions without
compromising the daylight conditions. In scenario EW1 with the loggia, it was necessary

5.1.4
Overheating
When assessing overheating in buildings, heat exposure is an interesting parameter. Cur-
rently, the Danish building regulations assess thermal comfort based on hours with tem-
peratures above certain thresholds, but that parameter does not address heat exposure
directly. 100 hours above 26◦C could be 100 hours with 26.5◦C or it could be 100 hours
with 31◦C; the ﬁrst is not a big problem, but the second could potentially be fatal to an
old person, particularly if it occurs over a short time period, e.g. during a heat wave. So,
perhaps it would be beneﬁcial to use a diﬀerent parameter to assess heat exposure for
shorter periods of time since that is crucial for heat-sensitive persons?
In the ﬁeld of chemistry, they work with the concept ”chemical exposure limits” which
relates to exposure to airborne chemicals. They provide time-weighted average exposure
limits, which is the maximum average concentration a worker can be exposed to during
an 8-hour workday and a 40-hour workweek, as well as a short-term exposure limit, which
dictates the maximum average concentration a worker can be exposed to for a shorter
period of time, e.g. 15 minutes and a ceiling level which is the concentration that should
never be exceeded [93]. Perhaps a similar method could be used to assess overheating in
dwellings, particularly when designing for sensitive groups? This would allow building
designers to better assess the heat exposure of the occupants in a building during a heat-
wave. Some type of heat exposure assessment could potentially be a good addition to the
existing standards, especially with the expected increase in heatwave frequency towards
2050.
In this project, both the current ”hours above” method and the degree hours method was
used to assess the thermal comfort and ensure that the occupants were not exposed to
too high temperatures. As overheating was a primary focus, all selected design proposals

fulﬁl the requirements regarding ”hours above”, but it would be interesting to have oﬃcial

In this project the approach was to conduct a component study to ﬁnd building blocks
that could be used for the further design process and result in design proposals speciﬁc
for nursing homes. However, the case building shows that even with two geometries as
relatively similar as the test building and the case building, the results are very diﬀer-
ent. This shows that the design proposals based on the combination studies are very
building speciﬁc, and that perhaps the design proposals are not very useful for future
nursing home design. However, instead of using the designs proposed in this project,
the component study might actually be the most helpful tool for future designers: The
detailed component studies could act as a toolbox when designing a new nursing home.
That would allow the designer to use the component study as it was done in this project
by using the components as building blocks to create solutions that work for the speciﬁc
site, geometry and needs of the new nursing home.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In the literature study, it was found that:
• It is recommended that all new nursing homes should be designed for persons with
dementia, causing the following design constraints:
– Avoid the use of automated dynamic solutions such as sun-activated solar
shading.
– Windows should not be able to open more than 10cm.
• The literature study has shown that older adults have diﬀerent needs for their
indoor environment, including:
– A narrower thermal comfort range: 23◦C to 25◦C.
– The elderly are particularly sensitive to heat exposure during, e.g. heatwaves.
– The elderly are considered a sensitive group, and the overheating thresholds
are consequently set to 100 hours over 26◦C and 25 hours over 27◦C.
– The elderly are sensitive to glare and bright light.
– The elderly have a lower metabolic rate and activity level and are more sensi-
tive to draught.
• The budget is a large constraint in nursing home projects.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
The study found that using the current projected climate data for 2050 yielded an 80%
reduction in degree hours compared to the current reference year ﬁle. The 2050 climate
data has a 56% lower direct normal radiation compared to the current reference year ﬁle,
which is thought to be the cause of the reduction in overheating in the building. The
ﬁndings in this study indicate that direct normal radiation is as important as outdoor
temperature when simulating overheating in buildings. Based on this study, it is sug-
gested that instead of/as a supplement to using projected future weather data, the future
weather conditions could be taken into consideration by assessing the heat exposure of
the occupants in the building during a heatwave.
The main ﬁndings from the study of technical solutions include:
The applied solar control coating was found to reduce overheating by up to 53% (measured
in hours with temperatures above 26◦C) by reducing the solar heat gain. However, the
solar control coating also causes a reduction in spatial daylight autonomy of up to 13%
and an increase in heating energy consumption of up to 6%.
The indoor air quality could be kept within the target 900ppm by using an airﬂow 50%
smaller than what is recommended for this geometry in EN16798 for sensitive groups [1].
Using natural ventilation through ventilation hatches could increase the airﬂow from
natural ventilation by up to 10 L/s averaged over a month during summer, meaning that
the increased airﬂow during summer could help mitigate overheating without increasing
ventilation energy consumption.
It was found that for South-facing windows a combination of static horizontal shading,
solar control coating and natural ventilation was suﬃcient, but for East and West-facing
windows, dynamic shading was necessary to reach the thermal comfort target.
Based on the results from the found design proposals and the case building, it is suggested
that the component study can be used as a tool box for designers to use in the design
process of a speciﬁc nursing home.
