Abstract
The current thesis describes the design and implementation of the PlaceMe
application, a mobile personal analytics system aimed to oﬀer visualisations
of the user’s mobility data. The PlaceMe application proposes a set of novel
mobility data visualisations techniques, including views dealing with both the
display of the user’s daily movement and with the emergence of locomotive
behavioural patterns. The system was deployed on a number of 20 regular users
for a period of 3-4 weeks. The application was also analysed based on ﬁrst-sight
impressions in comparison with the popular Moves application. The resulting
evaluations have proven that the PlaceMe system oﬀers an intuitive design and
a well-structured information organisation that promotes self-tracking and aids
the users in order to gain insights about their lifestyle, thus achieving a higher
level of self-awareness.

Chapter 1
Introduction
With the constant advancements of science, the development of more technolog-
ically capable smartphones has enabled every individual to easily gain access to
a powerful and personal self-research tool [OOA12, ROE09, TP15]. Nowadays,
modern smartphones have facilitated the collection of diﬀerent types of sensor
data [PGC+13] describing a vast array of characteristics such as the user’s mo-
bility, physical activity and even social interaction patterns. In this context,
users are becoming increasingly aware of the fact that they can gain valuable
information about themselves through the process of self-tracking [Lup14]. Fur-
thermore, either through pure curiosity or driven by a necessity/desire to change
their daily routines, people can explore and reﬂect upon the collected data sets
in order to reveal certain behavioural patterns and even correct particular ill-
advised habits (e.g. sedentary lifestyle).
Although the concept of self-tracking is not contemporary [CLK15], due to the
current emergence and development of a wide variety of applications and ded-
icated physical devices (e.g. wearables [WLL+15]), the area of lifelogging has
never seemed more appealing to the end-user. Consequently, the quantiﬁed-self
[Lee13] global movement has arisen promoting the idea of integrating technol-
ogy as a means of ensuring both data acquisition and the expansion of one’s
self-knowledge.
The quantiﬁed-self movement can be seen as a sociocultural
phenomenon, as stated by Lupton Deborah [Lup16], involving both end-users
and software developers, where the emphasis has been put on the development

of a network of scientiﬁc practises ultimately aiming for personal development
and increased self-awareness [WK]. Therefore, in a digitalised world becoming
increasingly overwhelmed by the sheer number and types of sensors available
(e.g. GPS, accelerometer, gyroscope), the quantiﬁed-self ideology also proposed
self-reﬂection as an essential component towards the extraction of relevant per-
sonal information.
Self-reﬂection can be expressed as the human capability to perform introspection
and auto-analysis as a means of gaining insight about oneself. Presently, count-
less mobile applications have already been developed in order to promote the
user’s self-reﬂection mechanisms through relevant feedback (mainly in the form
of visualisations, which may imply the incorporation of certain abstractions).
An example of such a mobile application is represented by Moves [EF16]. How-
ever, when it comes to applications dealing with the display of mobility and
activity patterns, what they typically lack is the fact that they do not explore
this ﬁeld to its fullest, thus preferring to incorporate ﬁxed, non-interactive vi-
sualisations of the collected raw data, simple daily summaries or even mobility
trajectories plots. In other words, not only do these mobile applications oﬀer
limited information to their users by means of their integrated visualisations,
but they typically also hinder the user’s ability to further explore, query and
analyse the underlying data.
The current thesis will describe our solution, PlaceMe, an Android mobile appli-
cation that serves the role of a personal analytics system constructed in such a
manner that it will provide both the collection of spatiotemporal data and their
proper investigation. The implemented analysis has also lead to the development
of several novel visualisations designed to aid the user into the identiﬁcation of
his personal mobility patterns and behavioural routines from a wide range of
perspectives. More precisely, the included representations have been specially
devised so that they could answer to questions framed around the user’s tran-
sitions, places visited, step count, usual locations and active time frames. The
thesis will further underline the design and abstractions chosen for the devel-
oped visualisations, highlighting at each point the array of information that the
user can access through thorough analysis.
Furthermore, the current design of the application has been built around a
previous research [PGC+13], which focused around the exploration of the con-
textual sensory data collectable by using a regular smartphone. Our preliminary
study has led to the creation of an extended spatiotemporal context, which was
framed around the integration of several data streams: time, location (in terms
of geographical coordinates) and activity details. Based on our initial research,
a prototype application has also been developed handling only the collection
and the immediate visualisation of the contextual data samples. The analysis of
the data points was limited and consisted mainly of the creation of an internal

in Appendix B.

2.1
Motivation and Goal
In the context of a continuous proliferation of technological advancements, peo-
ple have become more and more dependant on using their smartphones and
even wearables. However, the vast array of sensory data is not used to its full
extent, mainly due to the fact that most of the developed software applica-
tions have been focusing too much on collection with little or no eﬀort being
directed towards the actual interpretation or display of the characteristics ex-
tracted [RCK+15]. As a result, people are rendered unable to observe or discover
their behavioural patterns and more signiﬁcantly, to take action against their
harmful habits.
Moreover, even if the proposed mobile solutions have put a lot of eﬀort into
designing systems focused on the accessibility and encouragement of the self-
reﬂection process, there are also a great deal of contextual factors that may
==PAGE:18==
6
Analysis
ultimately inhibit the user. For instance, the data sets obtained through self-
tracking are fundamentally person-speciﬁc, meaning that the integrated visu-
alisations may not be ﬂexible enough in order to accommodate to the user’s
contextual information. On the other hand, when designing a self-tracking mo-
bile application, the initial development phases often include assumptions with
regard to which data streams should be selected and which should be ignored,
in order to create the proper contextual samples for the emergence of patterns.
Other factors to take into consideration are the user’s actual social environment
and his educational background [Che05]. More precisely, the manner in which
he understands the inner workings of the world around him may lead to an easier
extraction of the patterns at hand. For instance, people with strong technical
background or logical intelligence may interact more naturally with the included
UI elements, leading to the discovery and further understanding of important
features.
All of the speciﬁed factors heavily apply to the ﬁeld of mobility data analysis
due to the wide range of individuals that might be interested in using such an
application. From people interested in extracting meaningful information about
themselves, to persons wanting to adjust their lifestyle, all of these individuals
can be considered potential end-users. Therefore, having such a diverse audience
with diﬀerent backgrounds and qualiﬁcations, the PlaceMe application’s main
goal is to create a system dedicated to oﬀering an intuitive and eﬃcient set
of novel visualisations committed to promoting a straightforward emergence
of mobility patterns. To be more precise, the entire development and design
process has been devised, so that the proposed visualisations will be able to
answer the following set of questions:
1. Single-day information
• What are the locations where I have spent time throughout my day?
• What activities did I perform in the respective places?
• How much time did I spend in each location?
• How did I transition from one location to another?
2. Multiple-day information
• How active have I been throughout the last week?
• When am I usually active or still?
• What are my preferred locations in terms of time spent there?
Another goal of the PlaceMe application was to oﬀer an integrated data collec-
tion system, requiring minimum input from the user.

2.2
System Requirements
In its current state, the developed system has been constructed on top of a set
of prerequisites, which need to be speciﬁed in order to properly describe the
application’s environment.
First of all, PlaceMe is a mobile application built on top of the Android plat-
form, oﬀering compatibility with devices having an integrated operating system
starting from version 18 (Jelly Bean 4.3) and above. However, by applying only
these constraints regarding the application’s compatibility, the PlaceMe systems
is available to a wide range of users. More precisely, our studies have shown that
the mobile application is accessible to about 90% of the total Android monthly
active users (Figure 2.1) [And17], leading to a huge potential market corre-
sponding to about 1.8 billion diﬀerent devices (as of May 2017 [Pro17, Ng17]).
The Android platform is also much more ﬂexible, oﬀering dedicated mechanisms
to the creation of background components, which can perform computationally
heavy tasks.
Figure 2.1: Android platform distribution as of May, 2017 [And17]
Furthermore, the current architecture requires an almost constant connection
to the Internet and GPS in order to oﬀer the best possible spatiotemporal data
samples.
Consequently, this factor may lead to a higher than usual battery
consumption, but there is room for future improvements especially when it
comes to the data collection system.

In addition, based on the user’s needs, the PlaceMe system may require the in-
stallation of Google Fit [Gooa]. More precisely, without Google Fit’s inclusion,
the application will not be able to oﬀer details about the user’s ﬁtness history
in terms of step count. However, the PlaceMe system is also completely inde-

once the users will become more accustomed to the application (in terms of
representations and abstractions used), they will display a higher dexterity and
agility when it comes to accessing all the provided information.

using a regular smartphone and created a mobility user context as a founda-

3.1
Personal Informatics and Contextual Data
In the context of the quantiﬁed-self movement, the constant development of the
personal informatics systems [RRMC14] can be seen as an extremely interesting
direction towards achieving the ultimate goal represented by self-knowledge.
More precisely, in contrast to other devices and platforms dedicated to oﬀering
information concerning the world, these systems propose a diﬀerent perspective
by focusing on increasing each individual’s self-awareness from a wide range of
viewpoints. In other words, once the user becomes conscious about particular
features extracted as a result of the self-monitoring process, he may decide
==PAGE:22==
10
Related Work
to act by inhibiting certain behavioural patterns (e.g.
reduce smoking, the
consumption of alcohol [Hab16]), or by promoting positive habits (e.g. improve
the user’s sleep patterns [Sle16, SCB15], increase productivity [EAB16]).
In short, personal informatics systems provide the foundation for the user to
achieve self-knowledge through self-monitoring. However, studies [Eps15] have
shown that the process of using these tools as a means of acquiring a higher
level of awareness is not as straightforward as it would otherwise seem. In fact,
a fundamental model of this process states that it actually consist of a set of
steps [LDF10], thus oﬀering an extensive overview of the user’s behaviour:
1. preparation - the user’s motivation to start the self-monitoring process
along with his decisions regarding the tools to be used and the information
to be tracked
2. collection - the actual data gathering process
3. integration - the collected data suﬀers some types of transformations and
aggregations so that they can be later observed by the user
4. reﬂection - the observation and exploration of the information presented
in order to extract conclusions about short-term progress and long-term
patterns
5. action - the decision regarding how the discovered information will inﬂu-
ence the user’s behaviour
Figure 3.1: Li et al.’s ﬁve-staged model of Personal Informatics [LDF10]
Furthermore, the abovementioned stages are iterative, meaning that the users
usually tend to integrate more data streams, tools and even visualisation mech-
anisms in order to further extend their self-knowledge. The stages can also be
==PAGE:23==
3.1 Personal Informatics and Contextual Data
11
split into two diﬀerent categories (user-driven vs. system-driven) based on the
entity responsible for the activity at that particular step. However, probably the
most interesting property of the presented model is the fact that the user’s diﬃ-
culties in one particular step inﬂuence the quality of the results of the following
stages.
Other studies [Eps15] have even suggested that the collection, integration and
action are so closely connected that they can be combined into a single step.
Moreover, they propose several new stages, which try to explain the user’s be-
haviour of temporarily stopping the self-tracking process and resuming it at
a later point in time. Nevertheless, all the models suggest the inclusion of a
complex set of mechanisms operating together in order to promote the user’s
self-reﬂection process.
In addition, eﬃcient personal informatics systems usually rely on the construc-
tion of a user context that simpliﬁes the extraction of relevant features. This
approach is frequently integrated in the health and ﬁtness ﬁeld, but it can
be applied to a wide range of areas as well. The personalinformatics.org and
quantiﬁedself.com websites oﬀer a thoroughly constructed database [Per, Qua],
containing relevant examples of personal informatics systems from a vast array
of domains. It is these websites that will provide the case studies dealing with
the analysis of how contextual data is constructed in various circumstances.
For instance, ﬁtness-based systems (e.g. Fitbit [Fit], Nike+ [Nik], Daily Burn
[Dai], Google Fit [Gooa], Polar Beat [Pol]) use an ecosystem of specialised datas-
treams (e.g. heart rate, calories burned, steps, distance, activities) as a means
of promoting an active lifestyle. Additionally, health systems propose diﬀer-
ent sets of contextual data based on the speciﬁc sub-domain they are trying to
tackle. More precisely, tools dealing with stress reduction (e.g. HeartMath [Hea],
StressEraser Pro [Str]) construct their context based on several measurements
such as: heart rate and rhythms, breathing characteristics (rate, exhalation
time). On the other hand, sleep-based systems (e.g. Sleep Cycle [Slea], Sleep
Genius [Sleb], WakeMate [Wak], Fitbit [Fit]) mainly keep track of accelerome-
ter data, heart rate, breathing patterns in order to create the proper detection
environment. Finally, we were particularly interested in analysing the personal
informatics tools that dealt with the collection of mobility-based data. Systems
such as Moves [Mov], Foursquare [Fou], Google Location History [Loc] use a
particular set of data streams consisting of timestamps, GPS data, activity de-
tection mechanisms and steps counter. The same approach has been integrated
within our initial prototype, which is available in Appendix B.

3.2
Data Mining
When dealing with extensive data sets, the process of data mining [HK00] is
designed to lead to the extraction of certain relevant features. Moreover, the
actual data manipulation mechanisms may also include a pre-processing step,
where the collected samples are prepared by ﬁltering out some of the erroneous
results.
For the purpose of the application’s context, we will consider only one of the
data mining classes: cluster analysis. Basically, the clustering process can be
described as a means of grouping data samples together based on a set of sim-
ilarity principles.
In other words, when considering a single data point, the
clustering algorithm will use the appropriate analysis in order to determine the
most suitable cluster to group the sample with (if one such exists). Furthermore,
the interpretation given to a resulting cluster depends not only on the data type
used, but also on the algorithm class.
When considering GPS data in particular, the clustering decisions are usually
made based on the haversine distance [Hav] between the data points. In this
context, clusters are interpreted as accumulations of location samples, where the
user has spent suﬃcient time so that they can become relevant points of interest.
There are two major techniques that stand out as solutions of ﬁltering out the
most important locations through clustering.
While some studies prove the
importance of using grid-based clustering algorithms [MBGP13], others [SSJ14]
have obtained relevant results by using density-based mechanisms (DBSCAN
[EpKSX96] in particular or some modiﬁed versions).
3.3
Spatiotemporal Data Visualisation
The spatiotemporal context contains two diﬀerent data streams: time and space.
On the one hand, the time component can be represented either as a speciﬁc
moment or as an interval [AAMAMB+07]. Based on these two types of rep-
resentations, diﬀerent information can be extracted. More speciﬁcally, when
studying time as a particular moment, one can draw conclusions about the
point in time when a speciﬁc event occurred, thus enabling the chronological
arrangement of multiple results. Considering the time component as an inter-
val may reveal patterns in the succession or intersection of certain occurrences.
Usually, this component is used as an auxiliary dimension for the observation
of certain information as in the case of the spatiotemporal context. Space, on
the other hand, is generally represented by using maps, as they provide the

4.1.1
Gestalt Principles
The Gestalt principles can be seen as a series of fundamental conventions regard-
ing the manner in which the information should be presented to an individual
so that certain data correlations or connections could naturally emerge through
perception [Tod08].
Therefore, in order to ensure another layer on intuitiveness within the applica-
tion’s context, certain principles have been applied and integrated during the
design process. For instance, the proximity principle states that elements close
to each other display a higher tendency of being naturally perceived as forming
a group together. As a consequence, when we have designed the presentation
of the transitional information (Figure 4.2), we have displayed all the respec-
tive details in the form of icons and text positioned near each other, thus being
naturally perceived as logically connected.
==PAGE:30==
18
Design
Figure 4.2: The similarity principle applied to the activity icons
Figure 4.2 highlights the use of another law, the similarity principle. This
convention aﬃrms that elements also tend to be naturally grouped together if
they look similar (based on shape, lightness, colour, size or a combination of
these factors). In our case, some of the activity icons are either grey or vividly
coloured, symbolising whether the respective actions have been a part of the
user’s movement. In doing so, the colour pattern will instinctively direct the
user towards identifying the logical activity classiﬁcation without the inclusion
of any additional explanations in the form of text. Moreover, the size consistency
of the icons will also oﬀer an indication towards the correct conclusion that all
the elements are logically connected.
Figure 4.3: The continuity principle in the case of the list of activities
Furthermore, Figure 4.3 is used to exemplify the use of another Gestalt law,
the continuity principle, which states that the alignment of the elements also
plays an important role in their natural perception as logically linked units.
Consequently, we have chosen to align all the analysis elements (icons, short
description, duration) so that they would spontaneously be recognised as entities
oﬀering information belonging to the same domain or scope.
Additionally, the application also incorporates certain aspects with regard to the
ﬁgure-ground articulation principle [And08] as a means of attracting attention
to the information displayed. This principle explains that the user’s perception
is naturally adapted to distinguish the object displayed from its background,
meaning that a clear balance between the two factors will ease recognition.

4.3.2
Map View
The current layout of the Map View visualisation oﬀers a new approach on how
the spatiotemporal context data could be displayed to a user. The main purpose
of embedding this design into the visualisation was to allow the user, at a glance,
to observe several types of information such as: his most important places, how
he has transitioned between them, the set of activities he has performed during
his transitions. In this manner, the Map View fulﬁls its purpose of oﬀering a
daily overview of the user’s movement.
Digraph Structure (Directed Graph)
Based on our previous prototypes, we have concluded that the most suitable de-
sign choice should be based around the idea that the user’s daily movement can
be represented by means of a network, a digraph [SW] structure incorporating
two logical structures: clusters (the nodes) and the transitions between them
(the vertices). The necessity of integrating eﬀective abstractions within the vi-
sualisation has been pointed out by numerous research studies [Hib99, Joh04]
as a means of promoting the application’s usability.
Figure 4.16: Current design with clusters and transitions
==PAGE:39==
4.3 Single-Day Visualisations

In Figure 4.16, we can observe the current design of the Map View visu-
alisation.
In our current prototype, clusters are an abstraction of a certain
geographical area, which may consist of either one or multiple places based on
the zoom level used. Moreover, clusters may account for a vaster region, as
the user zooms out or for a narrower surface, when zooming in. In terms of
graphical representation, clusters are portrayed as circles with a number drawn
on top of them, describing the number of places (points of interest visited by
the user) within. Furthermore, as the clusters begin to contain more and more
places within, a visual indicator will also be given in the form of the colour tone
of the circle becoming darker and darker.
On the other hand, transitions are an abstraction for the user’s movement from
one location to another. In other words, if two clusters are connected by a tran-
sition, then it means that at some point during the day, the user has moved from
one area to another. Furthermore, transitions are depicted as lines (connecting
the clusters), with the arrow heads illustrating the user’s movement direction.
Additionally, the lines are also colour-coded based on the procentual time-based
distribution of the activities that the user has performed in order to transition
between the respective locations.

Data collection
In order to collect the required data from the user’s smart phone, we have

1. the application should be able to collect data even if the user is not con-

2. the collection mechanisms should be able to recover from error in the case

3. the data collected should be as consistent as possible with the user’s real

4. the data should be collected at a static time interval that oﬀers suﬃcient

Implementation
Thus, our solution implied the integration of an Android service [Goo17a]. This
choice was based on the idea that a service is designed to perform operations
in the background, in our case to collect the necessary data. The service is
created when the user opens the application and it will remain active until the
underlying operating system would try to close it in order to free up resources.
Another aspect regarding the design of the service refers to the static request
interval used in order to collect location and activity data. Basically, this request

the necessary data. After several pre-development tests (Appendix B), we have
observed that the best results in terms of battery usage and data collection

5.2.1
Location Collection
The user’s location can be determined on smartphones by using two diﬀerent
techniques, one of them relying on the network provider, whilst the other is
achieved through GPS. The network provider makes use of cell towers and even
WiFi access points in order to determine the user’s location.
On the other
hand, the GPS provider uses satellites in order to triangulate the user’s current
position.
The PlaceMe application makes use of both of these techniques, as the network
provider has a greater accuracy within buildings, whereas GPS oﬀers better
results outdoors, where it has direct communication paths with the satellites.
In order to achieve this functionality, our data collection system has integrated
Google Play’s Location Services API [Goo17c]. By making use of this API, the
application’s built-in service is able to periodically request the user’s location
at a regular rate of 30 seconds. While the Location Services API oﬀers a wider
range of location-based information (e.g. altitude, bearing), we have chosen to
ﬁlter out only three of them: latitude, longitude and velocity.
5.2.2
Activity Collection
This data type is extremely important, as it oﬀers insight into the user’s be-
haviour not only when he is transitioning from one to location to another, but
also when he spends time in a particular site. In order to accommodate such
a data stream, the PlaceMe application makes use of another external API,
Google’s Awareness API (more speciﬁcally, the Snapshot API) [Goo17e]. This
==PAGE:57==
5.2 Data collection
45
API has been chosen, because it has been speciﬁcally developed to oﬀer contex-
tual information about the user, while incorporating features such as intelligent
caching and cross-app optimisations [Goo17b].
However, preliminary tests have shown that relying solely on the API’s activ-
ity detection algorithm (without any further processing) would lead to high
inaccuracies. These errors in detection would mainly take the form of frequent
variations from one activity to another (observable even for consecutive detec-
tions). The inaccuracies would occur especially during transitions, possibly due
to issues with the device’s built-in GPS that did not record the user’s speed
accurately at all times.
Therefore, a more complex system has been built around the results retrieved
through the Awareness API, one that takes into consideration the conﬁdence
of the API’s activity detection, for instance. As a consequence, a minimum
threshold conﬁdence has been integrated and set to 50%. Based on the preset
conﬁdence threshold, the following logic has been implemented:
1. when an activity is detected with a conﬁdence higher than 50%, the re-
spective activity is saved
2. when an activity with a conﬁdence lower than 50% is detected, then the
respective activity is replaced with the last high-conﬁdence activity de-
tected
3. if no high-conﬁdence activity has been detected (or if there is no response
from the Awareness API due to internal errors or lack of Internet connec-
tion), the application will use its own activity detection system
More precisely, the PlaceMe application will always prefer the high-conﬁdence
results of the Awareness API and will make use of its own detection system only
in special cases. This detection system is built around the velocity characteristic
retrieved from the location requests. The user’s activity will be determined by
comparing the retrieved velocity with certain activity thresholds (expressed in
meters per second):
• velocity = 0 m/s →activity will be detected as Still
• 0 m/s < velocity ≤2 m/s →activity will be detected as Walking
• 2 m/s < velocity < 8 m/s →activity will be detected as On bike
• velocity ≥8 m/s →activity will be detected as In vehicle
==PAGE:58==
46
Implementation
The activity detection thresholds have been set through research, which has
shown that the preferred walking speed of an individual is around 1.4 m/s,
although in some rare cases (especially for short distances) it may rise up to
a maximum of 2.5 m/s [MTCR+07]. We have chosen 2 m/s as a maximum
speed for walking in order to accommodate both cases. Moreover, in order to
adapt to both occasional and regular bike users, we have set the maximum
speed threshold to a maximum of 8 m/s (approximately 29 km/h) [Bik]. Any
speed higher than the biking threshold will result in the activity being set to In
vehicle. However, just by setting these detection intervals and by comparing the
user’s current velocity to certain thresholds, this simple system is error-prone.
For instance, if the user is in a vehicle, but due to traﬃc he is travelling at a low
speed, for a percentage of the road towards his destination he might be detected
as walking or on a bike. This simple activity detection system has only been
developed and integrated, so that it could compensate for the special cases in
which the Awareness API would not react as intended.

The extended spatiotemporal context was constructed in order to help the user
gain insight both into his daily movement and more importantly, into his be-
havioural patterns [JJ13]. These types of information would become apparent
as more contextual data is collected by the PlaceMe application and displayed
to the user through its visualisations.
Figure 5.2: An extended spatiotemporal contextual data sample
As shown in Figure 5.2, the extended spatiotemporal context was created by
integrating the location and activity data streams along with the timestamp.
In other words, a contextual data sample collected by the application would
contain information with regard to the user’s activity (both type and detection

5.3
Location data mining
In order to be able to extract meaningful details about the user’s extended
spatiotemporal context, a clustering API has been integrated. Otherwise, the
location data would just take the form of a large number of scattered geograph-
ical positions where the user has been detected. After thorough research, the
chosen API was provided in the form of an auxiliary library (Google Maps An-
droid API utility library [Goo17d]).
The clustering feature of the library was selected due to the fact that it sup-
ported the inclusion of a large number of points through their integrated parallel
processing logic. Moreover, the clustering API was implemented in a manner
that oﬀered suﬃcient ﬂexibility in order to adapt it to our application’s own
needs both in terms of general logic and for design purposes.
5.3.1
Deﬁning places and transitions
Through the integration of the clustering logic, the PlaceMe application was
able to perform its own analysis in order to extract the user’s movement. In the
context of location data mining, clusters can be viewed as a set of data points
collected by the application, which are positioned within a geographical area.
After the resulting clusters are obtained, they will be thoroughly assessed in
order to determine whether they contained transitional information or a relevant
location visited by the user throughout his day. In other words, the analysis
of the clusters has revealed the necessity of deﬁning two logical units used to
describe the user’s movement:
1. a place is a cluster containing a large amount of collected data points and
represents an important location where the user has been detected
2. a transition describes the movement of the user from one location to an-
other and is constructed from a series of clusters, both visible (source and
destination) and invisible (intermediary locations)
5.3.2
Detecting Places
The place detection logic relies on the idea of ﬁltering out the clusters containing
only transitional information and the ones representing locations where the user
has not spent enough time for the application to consider them relevant.
==PAGE:60==
48
Implementation
To be more precise, each cluster will have its data points investigated in order
to detect the intervals in which the user has been detected. Moreover, whenever
there is a gap greater than 5 minutes between two consecutive samples, it is
considered that the user might have left the current cluster and a new context
should be created. The contexts enabled us to determine the time spent by the
user in a particular place.
Furthermore, since the clustering API did not pay much attention to the con-
struction of a cluster centre, the inner logic of the application has taken care
of this issue. The existence of a cluster centre is an extremely important as-
pect, since the proper detection of a place’s location relies on ﬁltering out the
erroneous samples. In our case, cluster centres are computed only based on the
samples containing a Still activity with a conﬁdence higher than 60%.
Place Detection Requirements
By tracking both detection intervals and the relevant Still data points collected
by the application, the following conditions have been formulated in order for a
cluster to be detected as containing a relevant place within:
1. the cluster must contain at least 40 extended spatiotemporal samples
within, which corresponds to a minimum of 20 minutes of total detection
2. the user must have been detected in the cluster for at least 5 minutes
continuously (with no gaps)
3. the Still percentage within the cluster must be larger than 50%
All the other clusters that do not fulﬁl the above-mentioned requirements will be
marked as invisible within the visualisation. The conditions have been formu-
lated in order to ﬁlter out as much as possible transitional clusters and clusters
containing irrelevant places. However, in its current form, there are cases where
the application does not detect certain places. For instance, when going to the
supermarket the user is mainly moving through it, meaning that regardless of
how much time he spends in a particular location the Still percentage will not
be reached, leaving that place undetected.

Solving GPS Errors
Another step towards solving the place detection mechanisms refers to the pro-
cess of eliminating GPS errors.
Due to hardware problems in detection, we
could observe the formation of small clusters in close proximity to the places
extracted by our algorithm. The small clusters contained supplementary infor-
mation about the place discovered, meaning that their omission would lead to
the exclusion of certain details about that particular location both in terms of
detection intervals and activities performed by the user.
Figure 5.3: Cluster GPS Errors
Instead of simply deleting the small clusters, an alternative uniﬁcation strategy
has been implemented. The integrated solution compares each cluster to the
others by taking into consideration the two particularities (small size and close
proximity). The design of the solution consists of the following steps:
1. the distance between the paired clusters is computed and compared to the
minimum admitted distance(160 meters)
2. if the distance is higher, then the two clusters can function independently
3. if the distance is lower or equal to the minimum distance, then the clusters
will be combined into one: the larger cluster of the two will receive the
other’s data points and will adapt its intervals
==PAGE:62==
50
Implementation
Moreover, additional explanations have to be given with regard to the minimum
admitted distance chosen. Through testing, we have decided that when process-
ing the data points, we will consider that a place occupies a circular area with a
radius of 80 meters. Therefore, all the samples within the radius are considered
to belong to a particular place. Thus, if the distance between two clusters is
lower than 160 meters (double the radius), then it would mean that the two
clusters are overlapping.
Reverse Geocoding
Up to this point, a place is only characterised by a certain set of geographical
coordinates and activities without having any functional meaning for the user.
In order to solve this issue, two web service APIs have been integrated, both of
them dealing with the reverse geocoding process i.e. the translation of geograph-
ical coordinates into a human-readable form. More precisely, the two separate
entities oﬀer access to complementary pieces of information about a particular
place, as follows:
1. Google Places API Web Service [Goo17g] is a service aiming to oﬀer details
about a particular place in terms of important points of interest located
nearby
2. Google Geocoding API Web Service [Goo17f] can be described as a service
aiming to discover addresses in close vicinity to the inputted geographic
location
In order to retrieve the most suitable place information for a particular geo-
graphical location, the PlaceMe application makes use of a combination of both
of these APIs. The reverse geocoding mechanisms will begin by analysing the
results obtained from the Google Places API. If a relevant point of interest is
retrieved, then its information will be extracted (name, type). However, if there
is no point of interest located nearby, the mechanism will return the closest
associated address. Moreover, the results oﬀered by the Google Places API are
always preferred (if they are accurate enough), as we have considered that the
user will ﬁnd it more intuitive in most cases to relate his position to a relevant
point of interest, rather than being given an address that he may not recognise.

5.5
Snapshot and Reconﬁguration
The snapshot and reconﬁguration processes have been integrated as a means of
adding consistency and adaptability to the Map View’s design.
Firstly, the snapshot process implies that the place and transition information
are collected at a very low zoom (17 in our case). This zoom level was carefully
chosen so that it would capture as accurately as possible the important places
visited throughout the day by the user. If the zoom was set higher or lower,
then we would observe either the omission of certain locations or the presence
of invalid places (not visited by the user, but appearing due to GPS errors,
for instance). This mechanism has been introduced due to the fact that the
underlying clustering API oﬀered varying place and transition information based
on diﬀerent zoom levels.
Secondly, the reconﬁguration process ensures the Map View’s adaptability to all
possible zoom levels by deciding which pieces of information (in terms of places
and transitions) should be displayed or not. At each zoom level, the mechanism
analyses the current cluster conﬁguration and decides, based on the snapshot
data, which transitions should be displayed. There are two types of transitions:
• Internal - connect clusters (from the snapshot process) that have been
consumed by the same cluster from the current conﬁguration.
• External - connect clusters (from the snapshot process) that either belong
to diﬀerent clusters from the new conﬁguration or are independent clusters
at this zoom level.

participants were also encouraged to oﬀer additional feedback.

8.1
Limitations and Future Work
Based on the current state of the PlaceMe application and the obtained re-
sults, we can draw several conclusions regarding the system’s limitations and its
further improvements.
First of all, the design of the application requires more extensive research, which
should be based around several population segments in order to thoroughly iden-
tify the possible adjustments. Our set of experiments have proven the potential
of the current design in terms of information organisation and variety, but the
participants have also reported the existence of an associated learning curve.
Therefore, further testing is necessary to reveal what aspects of the application
need to be further reﬁned in order to create a much better user-experience and
an eﬀective environment promoting mobility self-awareness.
Furthermore, the data collection system is another key factor that needs im-
provements. In its current form, this system implies a high battery consumption
due to its static design. However, the data collection component can be further
improved with the addition of a dynamic activity-based adaptation mechanism.
To be more precise, the system could adjust its collection rate proportionally
with the user’s current speed in order to properly detect the movement path.

8.2
Contributions
In this thesis, we have described the design and implementation of the PlaceMe
application, a personal analytics system that extracts and oﬀers information
with regard to the user’s mobility. The aim of the application is to provide
an eﬀective and intuitive environment that promotes self-awareness and self-
reﬂection.
We described the data collection mechanisms and the architecture built in order
to ensure this process. We also proposed a clustering-centred data mining tech-
nique based on raw location data, which dealt with the extraction of relevant
mobility information: place and transition detection.
We presented two novel visualisations oﬀering a set of eﬀective abstractions and
representations meant to simplify the overall design: the Map View, oﬀering an
in-depth analysis of the user’s daily movement and the Overview, presenting a
quick sketch of the user’s day-to-day motion. We have proposed several other
visualisations dealing with behavioural pattern analysis in terms of identifying
active/still time intervals and the temporally dominant set of locations visited
by the user.
A qualitative evaluation of the application has proven the eﬀectiveness of the
application both in terms of promoting the emergence of mobility habits and in
terms of providing a ﬂexible and intuitive design. Regular users of the applica-
tion reported an increased self-awareness level about their lifestyle after using
the application. Moreover, the Moves-PlaceMe ﬁrst-sight comparison experi-
ment has proven the potential of the application both in terms of information
organisation and quality and in terms of oﬀering a pleasant and intuitive user
experience. Since the majority of the received feedback was positive (even from
individuals uninterested in pursuing self-tracking), we can conclude that the
PlaceMe system is a viable personal informatics tool that can help the users
record and understand their mobility data.

1.
INTRODUCTION
The current project seeks to track and gather data about
the user’s context, while also oﬀering straight-forward and
intuitive visualization solution. What we deﬁne as a user
context within the project represents a collection of user-
related data, which can be gathered periodically or repeat-
edly throughout the day, thus oﬀering insight both into the
user’s daily behavioural patterns and into the detection of
changes, irregularities.
However, for an user it may prove extremely diﬃcult and
even cumbersome to keep track of his daily routines. An
immediate eﬀect concerning this aspect is that he will be
deprived of access to in-depth information about his daily
activities, about his patterns in behaviour. Moreover, just
gathering the required data means that we are dealing with
only one aspect of the problem. More speciﬁcally, the user
must also be given access to a tool, which will interpret his
data in a manner that will create intuitive visualisations.
This property will lead to the emergence of certain charac-
teristics about the user’s context, leading to a higher level of
awareness. Therefore, we can state that the main goal of the
project is to develop a system in the form of an Android mo-
bile application capable of handling not only periodical data
gathering (using only the phone’s sensors or external APIs),
but also aspects regarding their interpretation and visuali-
sation. As a secondary goal, the system must be designed
in a manner that will make it versatile enough to be further
extended not only with data about the user’s context, but
also with more data processing engines, feature extracting
algorithms and even visualisations. With these extensions,
the application will be able to help the user become increas-
ingly aware of his beneﬁcial habits and, more signiﬁcantly,
unhealthy routines, thus improving his lifestyle.

1.2
Related work
Since the ﬁeld of self-tracking has always been of high pri-
ority within the mobile community, extensive related work
has been done in this domain. However, few applications
and frameworks have actually focused on creating an ecosys-
tem consisting of multiple data streams, entirely centered
around the user and his behaviour. Moreover, what many
of them lack is versatility, as they have specialised in dealing
with only certain types of data (e.g. ﬁtness tracking, health
status), thus leaving other related data streams neglected.
Some of the mobile applications even integrate multiple data
streams, but they fail in actually interpreting and displaying
the emerging features to the users.
Nevertheless, there are noteworthy solutions dealing with
similar problems as our project. First of all, the Moves[5]
mobile application is an example of such a solution.
As
they state themselves, the application serves the role of an
”activity diary of your life”, meaning that it tracks the user’s
==PAGE:91==
activities throughout the day, his steps and even calories.
The collected data is interpreted and displayed by using a
storyline. However, what the Moves platform lacks is an in-
depth speciﬁcation of each user activity or number of steps
within a node displayed in the story line, meaning that it can
only oﬀer brief insight into the user’s behavioural patterns.
On the other hand, the User Context application displays
all the collected data within the map visualisation, including
the user’s detected activities within a node, the time spent
in that location in that day and throughout the last week.
Secondly, the Google Fit[3] application is another ex-
ample of a framework dealing with multiple data streams.
Google Fit is actually very well suited and designed to han-
dle ﬁtness data, however when it comes to recordings it does
not automatically detect the user’s activity, meaning that it
needs manual input to better collect and approximate the
user’s data. In other words, each recording only refers to a
single user activity, needing manual input in order to switch
between them. Moreover, as in the case of the Moves ap-
plication, Google Fit does not provide in-depth data about
the user when he reaches a certain location, detecting, for
instance, how much time he has been standing still or walk-
ing. On the other hand, by constantly keeping track of the
user’s contexts within a certain location, the application can
detect changes in activities (with no manual input needed)
and can record with accuracy the durations of the respective
activities. However, the ﬁtness data (e.g. step count) pro-
vided by the Google Fit is used throughout the application
in order to detect patterns in when the user is most likely
active or inactive throughout the week.
Last but not least, Google’s Awareness API [4] is the clos-
est framework in terms of trying to reach the same main goal
as our project. This framework collects location and other
user context data (e.g.
weather conditions, user activity,
headphones status) within a single uniﬁed API. However,
just collecting the apropriate data without actually inter-
preting and visualising them, means that the API cannot
be used a standalone application, but it can provide the
foundations for the development of an application centered
around the user’s context detection.
1.3
User context deﬁnition
In order to focus on the structure of the application and
the results obtained, one must ﬁrst discuss in more detail
the current project’s deﬁnition of a user context. As a ﬁrst
remark, it is important to mention that the ﬁrst steps in the
development of the project have been explorative, meaning
that each data gathering tool has been researched properly
and tested in terms of applicability. From this researched
collection of data streams, only the most suitable have been
selected in order to construct what we have deﬁned within
the prototype as a user context.
Figure 1: User context structure
As it can be seen in Figure 1, in the current version of the
prototype, the application periodically collects several types
of data about the user’s context, which are then stored and
used within the visualisations. The types of data vary from
the user’s current location (detected through the user’s
current longitude and latitude), to his present activity
along with information regarding the conﬁdence of the de-
tection (ranging from 0 to 100 %). In other words, These
streams of data are uniﬁed within a single entry both in
the database and in the user’s current context by using the
timestamp information. In other words, the timestamp
is used as a means of diﬀerentiating between several entries
within a single user context and to extract certain charac-
teristics of the present context in terms of durations.
Additionally, it is also important to note that a location
may contain multiple user contexts, as the user may leave
and re-enter a certain area multiple times throughout the
day. Therefore, the current application keeps track of this
kind of information by measuring the time gaps between the
consecutive entries within a certain area. If the time gap is
higher than 1 minute, the application will consider that the
user has left and re-entered the current location, meaning
that a diﬀerentiation in terms of contexts is necessary.
1.4
Application structure
Once the user context concept has been deﬁned, one can
begin to discuss the structure of the mobile application. By
integrating several APIs and services, the design of the ap-
plication allows its easy extension with the integration of
even more data streams.
Figure 2: User context structure
As previously mentioned, in order to properly detect and
interpret all the information regarding the user’s context,
the application has been designed to periodically collect the
required data in the background, on a separate thread, thus
requiring no manual input.
Therefore, every 15 seconds,
the application will send requests to the Location Services
API and to the Awareness API in order to retrieve data
regarding the user’s current location and activity. However,
if the confidence of the data retrieved by the Awareness
API (activity detection) is lower than 50% (the accuracy
of the detection can be considered too low for a trustwor-
thy result), then the application will also collect GPS data
in order to use it as basis for the manual detection of the
user’s activity.
In this case, the user’s current speed will
be extracted out of the GPS data and the obtained velocity
will be compared to certain predeﬁned activity thresholds
in order to obtain his activity. Therefore, we can state that
the GPS requests are optional and depend on the quality of
==PAGE:92==
the activity detection provided by the Awareness API.
Once the detection phase has ended, the data is stored
within a local database (SQLite[1]), as the respective data
may be queried at a later point in order to build the visu-
alisations. Additionally, the application may also make use
of the Google Fit Cloud ﬁtness data, for which it may send
requests, when they are necessary. For instance, the num-
ber of steps will be queried from the cloud in order to build
the visualisation dealing with the user’s periods of activ-
ity/inactivity. This design choice has its advantages. First
of all, since the number of steps are stored in the cloud and
are available by sending a simple request, this means that
there is no real need to store them locally on the device.
Thus, even tough the application is uninstalled, the data can
still be recovered from the cloud. Moreover, the integration
of the Google Fit framework means that the application can
be further developed so that it may make use of even more
ﬁtness data with minimal eﬀort and without aﬀecting the
performance of the mobile device.
1.5
Visualisations
This section is entirely dedicated to presenting the visuali-
sations created by means of the data collected by the mobile
application. These visualisations have been integrated into
the design of the application in order to provide the user
with a means of tracking not only live data during a day,
but also his behavioural patterns (in terms of activities and
locations) throughout a week.
1.5.1
Map Visualisation
The main visualisation within the mobile application is
represented by the map visualisation.
It is available to
the user from the moment the application is started and it
serves as a point of entry into the other auxiliary visualisa-
tions. In terms of design, the visualisation mainly consists
of the standard Google map element (Maps API[2]), used as
means to display the live detection of the user context data.
By using this design, the user will be informed about his
patterns in movement and activities both within a location
or when moving from a location to another.
Figure 3: Map visualisation example
In ﬁgure 3, we have depicted a cropped view of the map,
which focuses on the use of two types of representations:
markers and clusters. On the one hand, a marker (the walk-
ing icon) is a visual representation of a single data point
recording collected throughout the day. In other words, a
marker hints that the user was detected in that location
at a particular moment, whereas the icon used depicts the
user’s activity (in this case, walking). On the other hand, a
cluster(the circle) can be viewed as a collection of markers
grouped together by the fact that they belong to the same
geographic area. The number of markers bundled together
within a cluster is displayed as a label on top of the circle.
The colour of the circle diﬀers as well based on the number
of markers within.
Additionally, clusters are created dy-
namically based on the zoom of the map, with less clusters
being visible the more the user zooms out on the map. To
be more speciﬁc, the more the user zooms in, the more he
will have access to individual marker information, whereas
zooming out means that the clusters engulf more and more
individual markers, thus oﬀering statistics over larger data
sets.
Figure 4: Marker/Cluster information window
Additionally, in terms of interactions with the map, the
user can also click on individual markers or clusters. In each
case, the user will be prompted with an information window,
which will indicate not only the item that was clicked, but
also several statistics. Figure 4 displays the diﬀerent pieces
of information oﬀered by a simple click on either of the two
items. By looking on the data oﬀered by a marker infor-
mation window, one can observe that they correspond to a
simple database entry, containing details about the times-
tamp when the data point was registered, about the user’s
location (longitude, latitude) and detected activity at that
moment. In contrast to the details given by a marker, a clus-
ter information window is more oriented towards oﬀering an
overview of the times when the user has been detected in
that geographical area within the current day. For this rea-
son, the information window of a cluster will oﬀer certain
details about each of the user contexts detected throughout
the day such as: the time spent in that location and within
that context (duration) and the distribution of the user ac-
tivities in percentages.
It is also important to state that
the application ﬁlters out the activities of a context, which
correspond to a duration which is less than 5% than the to-
tal. Thus, the user will be informed only about the most
signiﬁcant activities performed in terms of duration within
a particular location, whereas the other detected activities
have either spanned over an insigniﬁcant period of time, or
they are the result of detection errors.
Figure 5: Marker activity icons
Last but not least, markers can be displayed by using
appropriate icons in relation with the detected activity of
==PAGE:93==
the user. Figure 5 displays the set of possible icons, which
can be used for a marker. However, these icons (with the
exception of the red marker) will be displayed onto the map
visualisation only if the conﬁdence oﬀered by the Awareness
API is higher than 50%, meaning that the detection phase
has ended with no diﬃculties.
On the other hand, if the
accuracy of the detection was low, thus needing the use of
the application’s own detection system (based on live GPS
data), the respective marker will be displayed with a red
colour icon in order to make it stand out from the rest.
1.5.2
Activity pattern visualisation
Although the user may discover certain daily behavioural
patterns in terms of which are his preferred routes, or his
main activities in certain locations, he still lacks access to
a method that can inform him about his periods of activ-
ity/stagnation over a larger period of time. For this exact
reason, a visualisation was integrated into the design of the
mobile application, oﬀering insight into the user’s periods of
activity or inactivity over the previous week.
Figure 6: Activity pattern visualisation
Figure 6 displays an example of such a visualisation, where
the data points are represented by the user’s number of steps
measured hourly over the period of a week. As previously
discussed in section 1.4, the number of steps needs not be
stored internally on the user’s device, but they can be re-
trieved automatically from the Google Fit Cloud, whenever
the user presses the Refresh button, position in the upper
right part of the screen.
The values on the X-axis correspond to the 24 intervals
throughout the day, each of them being one hour long. So,
for instance, an X value of 10 grouped with a Y value of
700 would be interpreted as the fact that the user has been
detected taking 700 steps in the time interval 10:00 - 10:59
on a particular day. In other words, the user’s periods of
activity would correspond to a high number of steps value,
whereas stagnation intervals would appear as constant lines
very close to the 0 value. Therefore, since the visualisation
oﬀers insight over the data points collected throughout an
entire week, this can lead, at a closer inspection, to the detec-
tion of certain patterns: the user’s sleep patterns, his usual
activity periods throughout the day, diﬀerences in behaviour
by comparing week days to weekends (with week days usu-
ally producing more ragged graphs with much higher data
points than weekends).
1.5.3
Location pattern visualisation
Additionally, the user does not have any information about
his behavioural patterns in terms of the locations where he
is detected. Therefore, another visualisation has been in-
tegrated dealing with this exact issue, in the sense that it
indicates an overview of the user’s periods of time when he
has been detected at a particular location.
Figure 7: Activity pattern visualisation
Figure 7 displays the visualisation integrated into the mo-
bile application. In terms of design, the visualisation has
been inspired by Moves’s storyline (timeline), but in this
case, the data points do not correspond to certain locations
where the user has been detected.
Instead, all the data
points correspond to a single location, as this visualisation
is diﬀerent based on the location the user wishes to view.
To be more precise, the location pattern visualisation is
reachable from the map visualisation by clicking on the in-
formation window of a particular cluster. If this interaction
occurrs, then the user will be informed about the times when
the user has been detected in that cluster (so, in a certain
geographic area) over the course of the previous week. More-
over, it is implicit that the information displayed by a cluster
diﬀers from the one illustrated by another one, as the user
cannot be detected in several locations at the same time.
As in the case of the activity pattern visualisation, the X-
axis values correspond once again to the 24 hour intervals
encountered over the length of a day.
The Y-axis values
can be interpreted as a boolean type, in the sense that they
indicate whether the user has been detected in a particular
time interval within the currently displayed location. For
instance, if a data point if displayed with an X-axis value
of 10 in a particular day, then this means that the user has
been detected in the 10:00-10:59 time interval at that certain
location.
In addition to this, the visualisation oﬀers two representa-
tions in order to codify certain information about the user’s
patterns in terms of location. More exactly, the meaning of
a single data point has been previously discussed, but not
if the data points are connected by lines. Therefore, if two
consecutive data points are connected, then this means that
the user has not switched location in that particular inter-
val, meaning that he has no been detected in another area
than the one currently displayed. By using this approach,
locations where the user spends a lot of time will be more
easily distinguishable, as they would present more lines and
less individual data points. Moreover, by integrating these
representations over the course of a week means that the
user will be able to distinguish certain patterns at a closer
look: locations he usually visits during weekdays and during
==PAGE:94==
the weekend, locations where he spends a lot of time (e.g.
home, work) and even become more aware of his actual daily
routines or schedule.
2.
METHOD
The functionality of the mobile application has been sub-
jected to a single experiment containing tests from two dif-
ferent perspectives: one involving the correctness of the data
collection process and the other concerning the battery con-
sumption surplus of the mobile device. It is these tests that
are most critical to the success of the application, as, if the
application is not well calibrated to collect correct data, then
the user’s behavioural patterns will not be able to emerge
as easily.
2.1
Participants
The experiment was carried out by a number of 5 partici-
pants, each of them being Master students and ranging from
23-25 years of age. Out of these participants, 3 of them are
male and have a background in Computer Science, whereas
the other 2 female participants are continuing their studies
in the ﬁelds of Mathematics and, respectively Psychology.
Moreover, two of the participants are also the developers
of the application, whereas the other 3 are close friends of
ours, meaning that the data collected from them is correct
and untempered with. In terms of measurements, each of
the participants are running the application in their current
countries (Denmark, United Kingdom, Romania), meaning
that the application is subjected to diﬀerent beacons and
mobile network providers.
2.2
Apparatus
Each of the participants are running the application on
their own Android mobile devices, as the APK executable
was provided to them by the developers for installation.
However, the speciﬁcations of the devices used in the data
collection process have been provided in Appendix A.1
2.3
Procedure - Battery performance experi-
ment
In order to test the battery consumption aspect, two of the
participants (the developers of the application) have devel-
oped 3 versions of the same application, the only diﬀerence
being the periodic request interval (for activitiy data and
location data). The values for the request intervals were set
to 5 seconds, 15 seconds and respectively, 25 seconds in or-
der to test how much the battery performance was aﬀected.
The participants were also required to keep each of the ver-
sions of the application running for an entire day. Moreover,
the additional conditions were that the mobile device should
not close due to lack of battery (in order to keep the same
battery cycle) and that the phone should be used in normal
conditions, as if the application was not collecting data and
running into the background. In this case, errors in terms
of data collection were not taken into consideration, but the
total amount of data points should correspond to the total
number of measurements required by the respective request

2.4
Procedure - Detection accuracy experiment
The experiment required the participants to keep an elec-
tronic journal of their activities throughout a single day,
while also running the application on their mobile devices.
The format of the journal was provided to them in the form
of a table oﬀering information about the timestamp when
the participants performed an action and the types of the
activities, which may be detected in that period of time.
For instance, if the participant left home and went to the
university, then he would specify when he left home as the
timestamp and [walking, running, biking, in vehicle] as the
list of possible activities to be detected. The activities were
detected throughout the experiment by using both the au-
tomatic detection (provided by the Awareness API) and by
using the application’s own detection system as well. There-
fore, the purpose of the experiment was to show whether the
integration of the additional detection system would lead to
a better accuracy in terms of activity detection.
The experiment required the participants to be as thor-
ough as possible and to have at least three types of activi-
ties throughout the day from the possible ones discussed in
section 1.5.1. Moreover, a measurement error would be con-
sidered if certain data points did not correspond in terms of
activities to the user-speciﬁed set of activities in that par-
ticular time frame. Therefore, the experiment is designed to
tackle only the activity detection aspect, leaving out other
possible error sources (e.g. GPS errors: the user is detected
in another location than he actually was). Moreover, the ex-
periment does not even tackle the issue of checking whether
the sequence of activities detected are actually coherent, but
instead just that they match the set of possible activities in-
dicated by the user.

Still in terms of design, the experiment was built with
several constants:
1. periodic request interval - 15 seconds
2. period of gathering data - 24 hours
3. conﬁdence level threshold - 50%
4. velocity thresholds - the same as in table 2
3.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
The following section is entirely dedicated to presenting
the results obtained throughout the experiments presented
in the latter section. Moreover, the results will be presented
in separate subsections, as the battery test results have been
a precursor of the activity detection accuracy experiment.
3.1
Battery performance experiment
This experiment can be considered as a precursor to the
accuracy experiment, as the results obtained in this case
were used as a means of setting the periodic request inter-
val at a value that could both provide suﬃciently accurate
data points without aﬀecting the performance of the mobile
device.
Figure 8: GPS uptime in seconds over the period of
a day
As expected, a request interval increase will lead to a lower
amount of data points being registered (and a lower amount
of requests), meaning that overall, the application will need
less resources in order to function. For instance, the GPS
uptime (Figure 8 will indeed decrease once the request in-
terval increases, with the diﬀerences becoming less and less
signiﬁcant as the interval becomes bigger. Similar results
have been obtained for both the CPU uptime and for the
amount of data used for the requests and results.
These
results have been provided in Appendix B.1.
However, the 25-second interval, although requiring less
hardware resources, it provides much less coherent results,
especially when moving at high speeds (e.g. on bike, in a
vehicle). Therefore, we have decided to use the 15-second pe-
riod interval as a reference for our future tests, as it did not
oﬀer that much disadvantages in terms of resources, while
also oﬀering the beneﬁt of more readable results.
3.2
Detection accuracy experiment
Once the experiment has been carried out for all 5 partici-
pants, the respective entries from their local databases were
provided as results. Since the number of entries for each test
subject was large, as described in table 1, we have developed
a Java script dealing with comparing the markers with the
information provided by the users within the tables. The to-
tal number of correct activity detection was computed in two
separate cases: when using only the Awareness API’s results
and by using both the Awareness API and our integrated de-
tection (only when the conﬁdence of the ﬁrst detection was
lower than 50%).
Figure 9: Accuracy of activity detection
As it can be seen in Figure 9, in most of our test cases
the integration of the auxiliary detection system has indeed
helped the user’s activity detection. However, we have also
come across exceptions to this rule, as in one case the aux-
iliary detection system has actually worsened the detection,
while in another one, the test results were actually neutral.
Nevertheless, the neutral result can be explained by the fact
that the conﬁdence of the activity detection provided by the
Awareness API was not so frequently under 50%, meaning
that the auxiliary detection was rarely called to aid the de-
tection phase. Moreover, the exception result can also be
very easily explained by the fact that the auxiliary detec-
tion provided by the application does not also detect the
’Running’ activity, as it would be extremely diﬃcult to ﬁnd
a threshold dealing with the separation between when the
user is running, or when he is actually on his bike. Since the
Awareness API is much more well suited to deal with such
situations, it handled the test case better.
Therefore, due to the fact that we still rely on the sub-
ject’s thoroughness in actually handling his personal diary
(especially when it comes to specifying the set of possible
activities), the results may be considered inconclusive, as
another set of test subjects may provide extremely diﬀerent
results. However, it is important to state that, overall, on its
own, the Awareness API has proven capable of dealing with
more complex situations, although there is potential for a
more well-developed auxiliary detection system to be able to
slightly improve the general detection (when the Awareness
API fails from some reason: e.g. mobile network issues).
4.
CONCLUSIONS
All in all, the tests have provided insight into not only the
manner in which the battery is aﬀected through continuous
and periodic requests, but also in terms of the current accu-
racy oﬀered by our proposed solution. In terms of resource
==PAGE:96==
consumption, we have observed that the application does
not require a signiﬁcant amount of Internet data in order
to run and nor does it aﬀect the performance of the mobile
device it is running in the background of. However, instead
of having static periodic intervals, a user-context application
should be built around a dynamic request system, where the
interval would be adapted to the user’s activity and current
velocity.
Nevertheless, the current interval of 15 seconds
can be seen as a very good foundation for the development
of such a dynamic system.
Moreover, in terms of the accuracy of the activity de-
tection, the results (although inconclusive) have provided
insight into the necessity of having a more developed test
case system, oﬀering more reliability when errors are en-
countered. The resulting test case should remove as much
as possible the human error, while also trying to take into
consideration as many niche situations as possible and as
many characteristics as possible (e.g. the coherence of con-
secutive data points).
