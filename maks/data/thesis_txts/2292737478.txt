ABSTRACT 
 
The aim of this project was to design a position controller for a 
tethered hexacopter. The position of the drone was calculated by using 
the measures of the tether tension as well as the angle that the cable 
turns with respect to the ground vehicle. 
In order to do that, a model describing the behaviour of the wire 
has been calculated, under the assumption that the cable moves slow 
enough to consider that it is in equilibrium. Then, a point mass model 
has been used to describe the translational movements of the helicopter. 
Finally, the position controller was designed, attending the height 
achieved by the helicopter and the ground angle turned by the cable.

1-INTRODUCTION 
 
A multirotor or multicopter is a helicopter provided of more than two 
rotors. Although the number of rotors vary from three to twelve 
approximately, the most commonly used have four, six or eight rotors, 
and are called quadcopter, hexacopter and octocopter respectively. The 
main difference between the multirotors and the conventional helicopter 
lies in the fact that the first have the rotors placed in the same plane, 
whereas the conventional helicopterвҖҷs rotors are placed in different 
planes. This difference is translated in a variance in the ways the vehicles 
are stabilized: while the conventional helicopters use their trail rotor for 
compensating the torque induced by the main rotor, the multicopters 
balance this torque by changing the angular velocity of their rotors 
properly. 
Nowadays, these vehicles are being used worldwide in different 
applications, due to their versatility and their ease to be moved and 
stabilized. The unmanned vehicles offer a wide range of applications 
which goes from using them simply as a hobby or for making video or 
photo 
reportages, 
to 
more 
sophisticated 
applications 
such 
as 
surveillance, or the study of an area, relative to fields like cartography, 
agriculture, geology or environmental studies. There are even companies 
which are trying to use them for parcel delivery. 
However, despite the fact that they are small, agile and easy to use, 
they have some aspects that still have to be improved, such as the flight 
time, which is limited by the battery of each vehicle, the taking off and 
landing maneuvers, the position control, or the autonomous flight. In this 
project, the position control of a hexacopter is attended, considering that 
the problem with the flight time has been solved by tethering the drone 
to a power supply, situated in a ground vehicle. The wire provides the 
UAV of energy and also serves for data transferring.  
The addition of the tether to the system, apart from including the 
solution for powering the helicopter and more benefits such as helping in 
the stabilization of the helicopter during translational movements and 
versus disturbances, has the disadvantage of making the system much 
more complicated to study. That is partly due to the fact that the cable is 
not a rigid body, so the equations describing its movements are very 
difficult to solve.

1.1- Problem Formulation 
 
The position of the UAV with respect to the ground vehicle can be 
perfectly defined by two parameters: the height and the ground angle, 
defined as the angle that the wire has turned with respect to the platform. 
The ground angle is directly measured, whereas the height is computed 
from other measured variables. These are the two main variables that 
have to be controlled.  
Concerning the fact that the UAV is tethered, the effects that the 
wire adds to the system need to be taken into account. These effects 
depend on the length of the cable and its linear density, mostly, but also 
on other factors such as the wind, that becomes more important for long 
wires, especially if the cable is not sufficiently taut. 
 Apart from controlling the position, the fact of including the tether 
leads the necessity of controlling also its tension, so that it gets stabilized 
and does not demand much thrust to be compensated.  
 
1.2- Contribution and structure 
 
As said before, there are many areas regarding these helicopters 
that are being studied. This project is focused in seeing the consequences 
of tethering the UAV, and then controlling its position by means of the 
cable parameters that can be measured, given that there is not any other 
device measuring the position accurately. Hence, in this project the 
following steps are performed: 
пӮ· Modelling the cable effects 
пӮ· Modelling the UAV translational movements 
пӮ· Controller design 
пӮ· Introduction to Implementation 
For carrying out these tasks, this thesis has been divided into the 
following parts: in Chapter 2 the whole system is analysed, stating the 
main characteristics of the UAV at hand, as well as the tether and the 
ground vehicle characteristics. Then, in Chapter 3, a non-linear model of 
the overall system is calculated and after linearized for the design of 
linear controllers in Chapter 4. Chapter 5 includes simulations that have 
been considered interesting to show. Chapter 6 attends how the

2- ANALYSIS    
 
This chapter attends to describe each part of the system we are 
working with, never forgetting that the objective is to design a simple 
model for control design purposes. Hence, very elaborate equations such 
as those derived from the aerodynamic studies of the multicopter are not 
required. The main parts of the system described below are: the 
hexacopter, the tether and the ground vehicle. 
 
2.1- Helicopter 
 
In this section a brief introduction to the hexacopter is done with 
respect to three aspects. First, its basic working principles, describing 
why and how the movements are performed. Secondly, its hardware is 
described, attending its main components. Finally, there will be a brief 
description of the software used to communicate with the helicopter. 
 
2.1.1- Basic Working principles 
 
The multirotor at hand is a 6-rotor helicopter, whose movements 
can be divided into two groups: the rotational movements, and the 
longitudinal movements. The rotational movements are called roll, pitch 
and yaw (
h
пҒЎ,
h
пҒў,
h
пҒ§) depending on the axis around which the hexacopter 
is spinning; in this project, xh, yh and zh, respectively. 
Any required movement can be achieved by changing the velocity 
of the propellers in the properly way. For helping in a better 
understanding about how it works, a brief introduction to the working 
principles should be given.  
When a body, in this case a propeller, is spinning, apart from the 
thrust force, it is generated a reaction force against this movement, 
creating then a torque in the opposite of the spinning direction. In order 
to avert the helicopter to remain turning round, the propellers have to be 
placed in such a way that this effect is balanced and the total torque is 
zero. Thus, the propellers have to be divided into two groups depending 
==PAGE:13==
9 
 
 
on the rotation direction: there are three propellers spinning clockwise 
and the other three counterclockwise. Then, when all the propellers have 
the same velocity the hexacopter will remain still, but when a rotation in 
the zh axis is desired, the velocity of one of those groups of rotors has to 
increase and at the same time the other has to decrease, creating a 
difference between the torques in each direction and, consequently, a 
rotation, but guaranteeing that the total thrust is constant. 
Figure 1 shows a simplified scheme of the helicopter indicating the 
rotation direction of each rotor in order to make the explanation more 
understanding. Additionally, there have been depicted also the body-fixed 
axis of the helicopter and its rotation angles. 
 
 
Figure 1 Hexacopter scheme 
 
With respect to the rotation in the other two axis, they are achieved 
by properly changing the spinning velocity and, consequently, the thrust 
produced by each propeller as follows: when a positive roll angle is 
desired, the thrust created by M3 and M5 has to be greater than the 
thrust exerted by M4 and M6. Meanwhile, when a positive pitch angle is 
desired the difference of thrust should be between M4, M2, M5 and M6, 
M1, M3.  
==PAGE:14==
10 
 
 
Concerning the longitudinal movements, the vertical movements 
are reached by changing the total thrust generated. Then, a general 
increase of the speed of all the rotors together is translated in a lifting of 
the helicopter. 
 Moreover, the horizontal displacements are achieved as a 
consequence of changes in the thrust direction that take place by 
modifying the roll, pitch and yaw angles. In addition, it is important to 
tell that the variations in the mentioned angles produce also vertical 
movements due to the changes in the vertical component of the thrust. 
Thus, if it is required to make a horizontal movement without changing 
the height of the UAV, apart from varying the tilt of the helicopter, the 
total thrust should be increased, keeping constant the vertical 
component of the thrust.

2.1.3- Software 
 
The communication between the user and the helicopter can be 
established by a remote control transmitter, from which the commands 
for changing the references can be sent. However, this is a one-way 
communication channel, since it does not give any feedback information 
to the user. Therefore, an open-source software called Qgound Control is 
used in order to obtain the complete information of the state of the UAV, 
collected and sent by the Pixhawk.  
Qground Control is a program developed for helping in the ground 
control station, by creating an interface that allows the user to interact 
with the hexacopter receiving feedback. It is widely used because of its 
compatibility with most of the operating systems and has a large number 
of useful features, such as the possibility of logging the information 
available by the sensors, so that it can be analysed afterwards, or the 
real-time plotting of the telemetry data and the sensor reads, among 
others.  
Other useful possibility given by this software is that the user can 
upload the firmware to the Pixhawk in case of making any modification. 
This is the way the controllers designed in this project have to be 
implemented. For that, a brief explanation of the Pixhawk structure will 
be done in the implementation, in Chapter 6.

2.2- Tether 
 
 
 
In this project, as has been said before, the limitations regarding 
the flight time have been solved by tethering the helicopter to a power 
supply. In this section the main characteristics of the tether are described 
as well as the effects that it produces on the helicopter.  
 
Concerning the dimensions and parameters of the cable, its length 
will be variable, and its linear density is approximately 0.05kg/m. 
However, from now until the simulations the length is considered 
constant, fixed in 10m. 
When it is studied how the cable affects to the whole system, 
different problems are found. On the one hand, due to the fact that the 
tether is not a rigid body, the kinematic study results very complicated, 
because the equations describing the motion of a cable are Partial 
Differential Equations (PDE), which are not always easy to solve. On the 
other hand, regarding the dynamic study, the tension exerted in each 
point of the cable depends on the position and the length of the cable. 
These last parameters are given by applying the catenary equations to 
the cable in each moment.  
 
Since the final objective of this work is to design a controller making 
the behaviour of the whole system smooth and stable, so that 
applications such as filming can be carried out, the cable is not expected 
to move fast. Hence, the assumption that the movements of the cable do 
not affect the dynamics of the system can be taken for simplifying the 
model, and the kinematic study can be avoided. Then, what really affects 
the behaviour of the helicopter is the force applied to the joint between 
the tether and the helicopter, which is the tension in the upper end of the 
cable. This force and its direction will be measured in the real assembled 
system, but in the simulations, they will be computed by applying the 
catenary equations, assuming that the cable is always in equilibrium, 
that is, with no acceleration, and the position of the helicopter and the 
length of the cable are known.

2.3- Ground Vehicle 
 
 
 
 
The design of the ground vehicle and its components is not under 
the scope of this project. However, since the information that it provides 
is used for controlling the helicopter, the main characteristics are going 
to be described.  
 
Although the main idea of using this vehicle is to power the 
helicopter in order to increase the flight time, it introduces some 
advantages such as the possibility of controlling the ground angle and 
the length of the cable.  
 
In this case, the assembled system is supposed to have a device 
that reels the tether in and out, at the same time that calculates the total 
length of the cable. This way, the length of the cable could be controlled 
by controlling the velocity at which the tether is winded or unwinded. 
However, in this project, the length of the wire will be treated as a 
constant value and any change will be considered a disturbance.  
 
Other important device in the ground vehicle is the one measuring 
the ground angle. This will lead the possibility of knowing the location of 
the helicopter assuming that the cable is placed in vertical plane, which 
can be a good approximation while the helicopter remains still or moving 
slowly.

dividing it into two different parts: the tether and the helicopter. The 
objective in this section is to present a model for control design purposes,

3.2 Tethered Helicopter model 
 
The purpose of this section is to elaborate a model describing the 
motion of the Hexacopter. The overall model consists of two different 
parts. The first one is composed of the rotational movements, roll, pitch 
and yaw. In this case, these models have not been taken into 
consideration, so the angle reference is instantly achieved. This 
assumption has been taken because the aim of the project is to control 
the position of the helicopter, not its attitude. The second one consists of 
the translational movements that take place as a consequence of changes 
in the inputs: roll, pitch yaw and thrust which, in our case, will be the 
control actions. 
In order to make a kinematic analysis, it is required to study the 
forces exerted in the helicopter.  
On the one hand, the cable exerts a force to the helicopter that 
changes its value and direction depending on the position of the UAV in 
each moment. This force is not applied to the center of mass of the UAV 
but, in this study, we can consider it due to the fact that the moment 
produced by this force will be balanced by the attitude controller.  
On the other hand, there are six rotors, each one producing a force 
in the Z body-frame axis. In this part, this six forces can be added 
together, resulting in the total thrust applied in the center of the 
helicopter. As said before, if the center of mass does not coincide with the 
point where this forces are applied the attitude controller will counteract 
the moment created. 
Finally, the gravity force, applied to the center of mass is acting 
through the Z axis in inertial frame.

4- CONTROL 
 
This chapter attends to design the control loops to control the 
position of the helicopter, given by the height and the ground angle. Since 
the attitude controller is supposed to be already implemented, the 
position controllerвҖҷs outputs are the references of thrust and roll, pitch 
and yaw angles for the attitude controller. In this section, the control 
actions will be limited so that the thrust reference does not get such a 
high value that the helicopter cannot afford and the roll and pitch 
references do not produce undesired spills in the helicopter. Thus, the 
control is based on changing the orientation and total thrust of the 
hexacopter, depending on the movements required.  
Additionally, the tether tension will be also controlled, in order to 
be stabilized in a low value, so that the thrust does not get very high 
values. 
The control strategy has been decided to be decentralized, forming 
then simple SISO control loops. This way each controller can work 
independently, and they can be designed and added to the system one 
after one. Also, if there is any error in any measurement or any controller, 
the other loops can continue working.  
 
 
First of all, the orientation of the helicopter is desired to be 
opposite to the cable. That is, the xh axis of the helicopter should have 
the same direction as the projection of the cable in the XY plane, xc. It is 
useful because, for filming applications for examlpe, the camera can be 
positioned in the same direction so that the cable and the ground vehicle 
do not appear in the image. For this reason, the measured ground angle 
will be sent to the attitude controller as yaw angle reference, and a yaw 
controller will be calculated here to complete the attitude controller.

4.2- Ground angle control 
 
 
 
The ground angle, defined as the rotation of the projection of the 
cable around the Z axis, will be controlled by means of the roll angle of 
the UAV.   
 
This angle can be controlled with the roll because, as was explained 
before, the yaw angle will coincide with the ground angle. Thus, any

5.2- Variable tether length

6.1- PX4 Structure 
 
For a better understanding of what is already implemented in the 
helicopter and what will be changed in this project, it is necessary to 
study how the Pixhawk operates.  
 
Figure 30 Pixhawk working scheme 
==PAGE:55==

In Figure 30 the working scheme of the Pixhawk is represented. 
There we can see that the signals received from the RC are sent to the 
controllers. In our case, the RC will send the desired altitude and the 
ground angle reference. The position controller receives also the 
estimated position calculated from the sensors reads and the tension and 
computes the error of each variable. Then applies the control equations, 
PID or PD depending on the variable, getting the attitude references, 
which are sent to the attitude controller.  
For controlling the angles, the attitude controller, represented in 
Figure 31, is composed of two loops, making a cascade control whose 
outer loop controls the position of the angle while the inner loop controls 
the velocity. That is, the outer loop gives the spinning rate setpoint from 
the error while the inner loop is responsible for reaching that rate.  
 
Figure 31 Attitude control PX4 
Once the control actions of the attitude controller are calculated, it 
is required the mixer module, which gets the control actions scaled, the 
angles in the range -1 to 1 and the thrust from 0 to 1, and generates the 
output set, composed of the required velocity for each rotor. Then, the 
spinning setpoint is sent to each rotor, which are provided by a controller 
each one to ensure that the desired velocity is achieved.

has been considered a good approximation, given that if there is not any 
controller damping those movements, the helicopter would remain 
oscillating, being pulled downwards by the tether when the tension is 
higher than the thrust, until the thrust is greater than the tension. 
 
During the control design process, it has been observed that the 
tension is necessary to be controlled, because otherwise, the cable would 
be pulling the helicopter, making the system be slower in achieving the 
references.  
 
Concerning the fact that the position of the helicopter is computed 
from the measured tension, in some situations there can be important 
errors. These errors can appear, for instance, when the cable is not taut 
enough, so there is a portion of wire lying on the platform. In this case, 
the length of the portion of cable which is hanging, would be different 
from the measured length of the cable. Then the function that computes 
the position would provide mistaken results. Something similar occurs 
when there are gusts of wind and the cable is not sufficiently stretched: 
the cable in the upper end could move constantly producing wrong 
measures in the tension direction and, consequently, wrong position 
estimations. This could be solved by ensuring a proper tension value but 
always taking into consideration that if the tension is too high, the thrust 
will also increase much, wasting more energy. 
 
Additionally, it would be helpful, for more precision in these 
vehicles to incorporate a better system for providing the position, such as 
GPS or vision based systems. 
The Pixhawk board combined with the Qground control offers a 
wide range of possibilities to the user to control and analyse data, which 
makes the system more interesting to study.  
For future studies in this field, it is necessary to improve and adapt 
the controllers for the taking off and landing maneuvers, by controlling 
the tether length and changing the reference of the tether tension 
depending on this parameter.

function [ac]=computeaccelerations(u) 
  
%read the parameters needed 
Tb=u(2); 
phib=u(4); 
  
roll=u(32); 
pitch=u(31); 
yaw=u(33); 
  
ft1=u(16); 
ft2=u(17); 
fttotal=ft1+ft2; 
  
m2=u(21); 
g=u(24); 
x=u(8); 
y=u(10); 
v1= atan2(y,x); 
  
  
%rotation matrices 0== inertial frame;1==tether fix frame;2==UAV fix 
frame 
% 
R10=[cos(v1) -sin(v1) 0;sin(v1) cos(v1) 0;0 0 1]; 
R01=[cos(v1) sin(v1) 0;-sin(v1) cos(v1) 0;0 0 1]; 
  
Rx=[1 0 0;0 cos(roll) sin(roll);0 -sin(roll) cos(roll)]; 
Ry=[cos(pitch) 0 -sin(pitch);0 1 0;sin(pitch) 0 cos(pitch)]; 
Rz=[cos(yaw) sin(yaw) 0;-sin(yaw) cos(yaw) 0; 0 0 1]; 
R02=Rx*Ry*Rz; 
  
R20=inv(R02); 
  
% Total force exerted in the UAV, in inertial frame 
F=R20*[0;0;fttotal]+m2*[0;0;-g]+R10*Tb*[-cos(phib);0;-sin(phib)]; 
  
%accelerations in inertial frame 
  
ax=F(1)/m2; 
ay=F(2)/m2; 
az=F(3)/m2; 
  
out=[ax ay az v1]; 
ac=double(out); 
  
end

function [out]=velRalpha(u) 
x=u(1);% X coordinate of the helicopter in inertial frame 
xd=u(2);% derivate of X 
y=u(3);% y coordinate of the helicopter in inertial frame 
yd=u(4);% derivate of y 
  
% v= velocity of the helicopter in the Xc axis, needed for computing 
the 
% tension in the cable 
a= sqrt(x^2+y^2); 
b=x*xd+y*yd; 
v=b/a; 
  
% W=derivative of ground angle, angular speed. ground_angle=atg(x/y) -
->  
% d(ground_angle)/dt=1/(1+(x/y)^2)*d(x/y)/dt  
  
c=(-y*xd+x*yd); 
d=y^2+x^2; 
w=c/d; 
  
out=[v, w];  
out=double(out);
