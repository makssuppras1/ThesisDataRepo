Abstract 
This thesis undertakes an examination of the financial and environmental performance of a novel 
water-based transportation system within the EU, as recommended by the Advanced, Efficient and 
Green Intermodal Systems (AEGIS) H2020 project. The primary objective is to address the 
decarbonization of freight transportation by leveraging inland waterways (IWW) for cargo 
distribution between the Netherlands and Belgium. The AEGIS proposal revolves around the 
utilization of an autonomous Roll-on/Roll-off (Ro-Ro) vessel propelled by a battery system, 
thereby ensuring zero-emissions operations during transit. This initiative aligns with the EU's 
aspiration to transition cargo transportation from road-dependent modes to more sustainable 
alternatives. To ascertain the viability of the AEGIS proposal, this study conducts a comparative 
analysis against the conventional scenario of road haulage, employing a specifically selected array 
of economic and environmental Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to facilitate a quantitative 
Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA). The investigation encompasses an examination of the impact of 
varying speeds of the AEGIS vessel and an assessment of the corresponding model response. 
Furthermore, this research endeavors to contemplate the prospective implementation of Market-
Based Measures (MBMs), including the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), 
and its potential influence on the two modes of transport. The findings derived from this study 
reveal the advantageous aspects of the AEGIS vessel in terms of lower operational costs and 
enhanced environmental performance. Additionally, the AEGIS solution exhibits a superior 
payload capacity compared to the delivery of cargo via trucks. However, it necessitates higher 
capital investment and extended transit times in relation to road haulage. Overall, the AEGIS 
solution demonstrates its viability through a combination of emission reductions, higher payload 
capacity, and reduced operating expenses.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Problem description 
In today's interconnected world, the issue of increasing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions poses a 
significant challenge to humanity. These emissions are responsible for climate disruption, which 
is a major problem that societies worldwide will have to confront in the years to come. Human 
activities are the primary cause of anthropogenic climate change and bear full responsibility for 
the current state of the environment. Among these activities, the transportation sector stands as the 
second-largest source of energy-related CO2 emissions globally, accounting for 23% of the total 
(IEA, 2022). The environmental impact of international shipping has become a growing concern 
in recent years, with maritime shipping alone being responsible for 2.89% in 2018 (IMO, 2021). 
The main culprit behind global warming is carbon dioxide (CO2), which is the primary greenhouse 
gas, along with other gases like methane (CH4), ozone (O3), nitrous oxide (N2O), among others 
(European Parliament, 2023).  
To address this pressing issue, it is necessary to implement various transformations in the 
transportation system. These include transitioning to low-emission modes of transport, 
accelerating the adoption of zero-carbon vehicles, and preparing for a shift to zero-carbon aviation 
and shipping. The goal is to mitigate the negative effects of aggregated GHGs emissions and make 
the global shipping industry completely free of emissions in the coming decades (European 
Commission, 2021). 
Maritime transportation has a significant and indispensable role in facilitating international trade 
and commerce, rendering the sector as a significant contributor to GHG emissions, primarily using 
fossil fuels to power vessels (UNCTAD, 2022). To tackle the environmental impact of 
international shipping, several initiatives have been implemented by organizations such as the 
International Maritime Organization (IMO) and the European Union (EU). The European Grean 
Deal includes these policies that are aptly designed to facilitate the reduction of net GHG emissions 
by a minimum of 55% by 2030, in comparison to the levels observed in 1990 (European 
Commission, 2021). The attainment of these emission reductions within the upcoming decade 
holds paramount importance for Europe's vision of becoming the foremost climate-neutral 
continent globally.

Thus, the transportation system within the EU must continuously improve its performance and 
concurrently adopt zero-carbon technologies to meet the Union's latest environmental 
requirements. Furthermore, the EU seeks to promote a modal shift from road haulage to maritime 
transportation to reduce emissions in the transportation sector (European Commission, 2021).  
As of 2020, road transport accounted for 77% of inland freight transport in the EU, followed by 
rail and inland waterways transport, which accounted for 17% and 6% respectively (Eurostat, 
2022). In the pursuit of more sustainable transportation, the EU is exploring alternative concepts 
for freight movement, with a particular focus on short-sea shipping and inland waterways 
(European Commission, 2021). These waterways have the potential to support a novel, cost-
effective, and environmentally friendly transportation system that EU member states require for 
the future. Maritime transportation remains highly advantageous for cargo movement and 
currently serves as the dominant mode of freight transportation worldwide (European 
Commission, 2021). Recognizing the need for carbon-free and efficient maritime transportation, 
the EU has decided to optimize and upgrade the existing water-based transportation network. The 
EU funds several projects aimed at finding innovative applications to enhance freight distribution 
performance, including the Advanced, Efficient, and Green Intermodal Systems (AEGIS) project, 
which is linked to this thesis (AEGIS project, 2020). 
1.2 AEGIS Case Study 
The AEGIS H2020 project (AEGIS project, 2020), for Advanced, Efficient, and Green Intermodal 
Systems, endeavors to optimize cargo transportation efficiency, sustainability, and integration. 
This objective is being pursued through the promotion of an environmentally friendly water-based 
freight transportation system within the EU. Notably, the project concentrates on the advancement 
of novel vessel designs, zero-emissions propulsion systems, automated cargo handling, and port 
automation. By leveraging these innovative measures, the project aspires to surpass the efficacy 
of prevailing road transportation practices, both in terms of cost-effectiveness and the mitigation 
or elimination of detrimental environmental impacts associated with road haulage. The primary 
focus of the AEGIS project is to achieve a substantial reduction in road transport and the associated 
emissions. To attain this objective, the project actively advocates for and supports alternative 
modes of transportation, with particular emphasis placed on the potential of short-sea shipping and 
inland waterways. Acknowledging the adverse environmental consequences and congestion 
==PAGE:14==
3 
 
challenges entailed in road transportation, the project endeavors to cultivate a modal shift towards 
more sustainable and efficient transportation modalities (AEGIS project, 2020). 
The AEGIS project constitutes a collaborative effort involving diverse industry stakeholders, 
encompassing ship operators, port authorities, research institutes, and academic institutions. Its 
principal objective revolves around the development of a novel waterborne transportation system 
for Europe, which leverages the inherent advantages of vessels while concurrently addressing 
conventional impediments such as dependency on extensive terminals, exorbitant transshipment 
costs, limited speed, and frequency, as well as insufficient automation in information processing 
(AEGIS Consortium, 2019). 
The core aim of AEGIS is further subdivided into specific sub-objectives. These encompass the 
reduction of GHGs emissions, pollution, and noise arising from ship transport and terminals, the 
enhancement of ship transport flexibility via the integration of smaller automated lightweight 
shuttle vessels, the facilitation of ship transport accessibility to urban centers through the 
utilization of smaller automated shuttle vessels, and the promotion of automated short-distance 
transportation from terminals to end users, wherever feasible. In essence, the purpose of the project 
centers on augmenting the quantity of freight conveyed through inland waterways or short sea 
shipping as a means of effectively competing with road transportation (AEGIS Consortium, 2019). 
The AEGIS project's Advisory Group (AG) and Consortium have chosen to undertake an 
investigation into the viability of short-sea shipping and inland waterways for freight 
transportation by conducting a comprehensive analysis of three distinct case studies situated in 
Northern Europe. Specifically, Use Case A (UCA) endeavors to assess the potential for optimizing 
Norwegian short sea routes towards the continent. Use Case B (UCB) focuses on addressing the 
transshipment of goods from larger Ro-Ro vessels in the Netherlands and Belgium region to inland 
waterways as a viable alternative to traditional truck transportation. Use Case C (UCC) centers on 
the exploration of collaborative port initiatives in the southern region of Copenhagen, 
incorporating the establishment of new feeder lines from prominent ports such as Hamburg, 
alongside the implementation of automated small ships for localized last mile distribution (AEGIS 
Consortium, 2019). 
The present thesis will primarily concentrate on conducting an evaluation of the potential of UCB, 
which merge Ro-Ro short-sea shipping with inland waterway transportation system in the region

between the Netherlands and Belgium. The research inquiry aims to ascertain whether the 
innovative transportation system proposed for UCB by the AEGIS committee will display a 
superior performance in terms of sustainability when compared with the current road 
transportation system. The overarching objective of this research is to identify solutions that yield 
benefits for multiple stakeholders, which are commonly referred to as "win-win" solutions. 
1.3 Thesis Objective 
The primary objective of this study is to comprehensively investigate the proposed AEGIS solution 
for UCB with a focus on the integration of a sustainable inland waterway transport system in the 
region spanning the Netherlands and Belgium. This solution emphasizes the adoption of an 
innovative autonomous vessel design equipped with a battery propulsion system. At present, 
DFDS, an esteemed AEGIS partner, employs Ro-Ro vessels to transport trailers to the hub ports 
of Rotterdam and Ghent. Subsequently, these trailers are conveyed to their final consignees using 
trucks.  
The UCB initiative seeks to enhance the efficiency of the last mile delivery process by transitioning 
from road transportation to inland waterway systems within the Netherlands and Belgium. This 
transition will be facilitated by the utilization of smaller autonomous Ro-Ro barge vessels 
equipped with zero-emissions propulsion systems. The specific inland waterway route under 
examination in this study lies between the ports of Rotterdam (Vlaardingen) and Ghent.  
To evaluate the efficacy of the AEGIS proposal, a rigorous comparison will be conducted between 
the proposed solution and the prevailing road transportation system. This evaluation will be based 
on a set of meticulously selected KPIs and will incorporate a comprehensive quantitative CBA 
framework. This study will undertake a comparative analysis between the distance covered by 
inland waterway transportation along the above-mentioned route and the equivalent distance 
covered by road transportation. Additionally, the investigation will encompass an examination of 
the ramifications associated with setting different speeds for the AEGIS vessels. Moreover, the 
economic implications of the future establishment of the European Union’s Emissions Trading 
System (EU ETS) will be assessed. 
Overall, the principal aim of this thesis is to ascertain an optimal solution, commonly referred to 
as a "win-win" solution, that not only upholds environmental sustainability but also demonstrates 
cost-effectiveness.

1.4 Study limitations 
It is critical to recognize the study's limits to assure the accuracy and trustworthiness of the 
findings. These constraints may result from data availability, assumptions made during the 
research, or other factors that may influence the results. Given that the AEGIS project, revolving 
around an innovative autonomous Ro-Ro barge, has yet to be operationalized, its evaluation and 
subsequent analysis remains on a theoretical level. For instance, in the CBA, certain factors such 
as the maintenance cost, Terminal Handling Charges (THC) and wages remained as constants over 
time, but these values are subject to variations. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of fuel prices and 
infrastructural changes has not been fully captured within this study. Moreover, the analysis does 
not account for potential job losses within the road haulage industry, the cost implications of 
establishing new industries for vessel construction, and the financial and temporal ramifications 
of transitioning between systems. While environmental considerations factored into the study 
through certain KPIs, the potential environmental impacts from the increase in barge traffic, such 
as harm to wildlife, the environmental footprint of vessel and battery production, and end-of-life 
disposal concerns have been excluded. Additionally, the environmental impacts associated with 
the truck’s production were not considered. Finally, issues relating to weather-induced disruptions 
and the management of barge traffic have not been considered. 
1.5 Methodology and Structure 
The primary objective of this thesis is to discern and propose mutually beneficial solutions, 
referred to as "win-win" solutions, which contribute to environmental preservation while 
remaining economically viable for the UCB, with a focus on the last mile freight transportation 
between the ports of Rotterdam (Vlaardingen) and Ghent. To accomplish this, an extensive 
analysis conducted of the AEGIS proposal, considering the current status quo and relevant 
literature pertaining to both AEGIS and UCB. The data utilized in this study originated from 
preliminary reports provided by various AEGIS stakeholders, including DFDS, the leading 
company in UCB, and ISE, the vessel designer, as well as reports from the AEGIS Consortium. 
Additionally, a comprehensive literature review was conducted to explore the current state of 
international shipping and the imperative to adopt a more sustainable approach. Various initiatives 
introduced by the IMO and EU were examined to comprehend their potential impact on the 
proposed solution and the existing situation.

In parallel, a specific set of KPIs was carefully chosen to facilitate a thorough comparison of the 
two modes of transportation. Subsequently, a CBA was conducted to evaluate the economic and

the AEGIS solution on the project's economic and environmental performance, relative to the 
baseline scenario. Moreover, the potential future establishment of the EU Emissions Trading 
System (ETS) in road transportation was considered, examining its potential economic impact on 
the case study examined. Finally, a comparative analysis of the above-mentioned scenarios was 
undertaken to identify the optimal "win-win" solution for the given context.

2 Maritime transportation and logistics 
International shipping plays a pivotal role in facilitating global trade and economic advancement; 
however, its ecological implications have emerged as a pressing concern (UNCTAD, 2022). The 
environmental consequences associated with international shipping encompass various aspects 
that demand immediate attention. Of particular significance are the substantial emissions of GHGs, 
notably CO2, which exert a considerable influence on climate change. Furthermore, the emissions 
of sulfur oxides (SOx), nitrogen oxides (NOx), and particulate matter (PM) not only degrade air 
quality but also pose risks to human health and ecosystems (IPCC, 2022). To mitigate the impact 
of GHG emissions, concerted efforts are being made to explore sustainable alternatives. This 
includes research into greener fuels, advancements in energy efficiency, and the adoption of state-
of-the-art propulsion technologies. These endeavors aim to curb emissions and enhance the 
environmental performance of the shipping industry (European Commission, 2023). 
The maritime sector serves as the predominant mode of transportation, handling approximately 
80% of the total volume of global goods (UNCTAD, 2022). Seaborne freight transportation plays 
a pivotal role in facilitating the movement of cargo to and from the EU, connecting markets across 
continents through efficient and economically viable logistics systems. Nevertheless, it is crucial 
to recognize the contribution of shipping to global warming, necessitating a careful examination 
of existing regulations and potential enhancements aimed at achieving net-zero emissions 
(UNCTAD, 2022). 
As illustrated in Figure 1, which presents the GHG emissions by sector in the EU for the year 
2021, international shipping emerges as one of the sectors with relatively lower GHG emissions, 
amounting to 122 million tonnes of CO2eq (European Union, 2022). The emissions attributed to 
maritime transportation are significantly lower compared to other human activities. However, 
despite this comparatively favorable position, the significance of the shipping sector cannot be 
overlooked, as it plays a vital role in facilitating global freight transportation (UNCTAD, 2022). 
Therefore, it becomes imperative to address and improve the environmental, social, and financial 
impacts associated with the maritime sector.

2.1 Sustainable maritime logistics 
Achieving a sustainable global maritime transport system necessitates the thorough analysis and 
evaluation of trade-offs involved in the objective of making the maritime logistical supply chain 
more environmentally friendly. A profound understanding of the interconnections between 
economic and environmental objectives, as well as the factors that may impact this relationship, is 
a prerequisite for attaining a desirable solution in the pursuit of greening the maritime logistical 
supply chain. As per (Psaraftis H. N., 2016), green transportation logistics is “an attempt to attain 
an acceptable environmental performance in the transportation supply chain, while at the same 
time respecting traditional economic performance criteria.” The concept of sustainable maritime 
logistics refers to a maritime transport system that effectively balances economic, environmental, 
and social performance factors. The term “acceptable environmental performance” in this context 
primarily concerns the regulation and management of emissions, particularly CO2 and other GHGs, 
as well as SOx, NOx emissions that have been identified as major contributors to climate change 
(Psaraftis H. N., 2016). However, there are other environmental aspects of maritime transport, 
such as noise, hazardous substances, oil pollution, ballast water, dust, residues, and waste, that can

2.2.2 Short Sea Shipping (SSS) 
The intrinsic advantages of maritime transportation, coupled with the potential for future 
improvements, establish shipping as an optimal mode of transportation within the EU. Transport, 
particularly road transport, is one of the largest sources of GHGs emissions in the EU (European 
Union, 2022). Therefore, it is seen as an area where substantial emissions reductions can be made. 
Correspondingly, the European Commission (EC) has formulated a strategic goal to enhance cargo 
movement through SSS and IWW (ESN, 2022), (European Commission, 2020). This strategic 
approach aligns with the EU's overarching transport objective of reducing GHGs emissions and 
transitioning 30% of road freight over distances exceeding 300 km to alternative modes by 2030 
(TRAN Committee, 2018), (European Commission, 2022).

SSS serves as a competitive alternative to the existing congested road network, offering notable 
advantages in terms of environmental sustainability and improved safety. Moreover, SSS has the

2.2.3 Inland Waterway Transport (IWW) 
Inland waterway transport refers to the use of inland waterways such as rivers and canals to move 
goods and passengers. This method of transport is used widely across the EU, primarily due to its 
efficiency, safety, and eco-friendliness. The EU recognizes the value of IWW transport and has 
instituted policies to promote and regulate this sector (European Commission, 2020). Within the 
EU exists a comprehensive expanse of inland waterways, stretching across a distance exceeding 
37,000 kilometers (European Commission, 2023). This intricate network encompasses 13 Member 
States, wherein a cohesive interconnected system of waterways is established, where the 
Netherlands and Belgium have the highest density of inland waterways, followed by Germany, 
France, and Poland. (European Commission, 2023). The significance of inland waterway transport 
in attaining the climate objectives set by the European Commission has been duly acknowledged. 
To harness the potential of inland waterway transport, the EU introduced the NAIADES II 
initiative in 2013. This initiative centers around the progression of the sector through the adoption 
of various strategies, including fleet modernization, extensive workforce training, and 
infrastructure improvements, aimed at facilitating the shift towards zero-emission inland waterway 
transport. (European Commission, 2021).  
IWW presents a multitude of advantages from a scientific perspective. Firstly, it exhibits 
remarkable energy efficiency, leading to a reduction in GHG emissions when compared to road 
transport (European Commission, 2020). IWW characterized by minimal energy consumption and 
noise emissions (European Commission, 2023). Notably, its energy consumption per tonne-
kilometer of transported goods is estimated to be approximately 17% of that observed in road

Additionally, it serves as an effective means to alleviate congestion in road and rail traffic, thereby 
enhancing overall transport safety. Furthermore, this mode of transportation proves to be cost-
effective, particularly for the conveyance of dangerous goods and bulk cargo over extended 
distances (European Commission, 2020). 
2.2.4 Intermodal freight transport 
Intermodal freight transport encompasses the utilization of multiple transportation modes during a 
journey, spanning from the point of origin to destination. This entails the seamless transfer of 
goods through a combination of modes, including road, rail, waterways, or air transport. The 
effectiveness of intermodal transportation relies on the optimal integration of these diverse modes, 
constituting a pivotal factor for achievement. Hence, modal shift emerges as the catalyst propelling 
the realization of a sustainable freight transportation system throughout the EU (European 
Commission, 2020). 
Intermodal freight transport, particularly when encompassing the transition from road to rail or 
water transport, possesses the capacity to substantially diminish carbon emissions and fuel 
consumption (European Court of Auditors, 2023), thereby aligning with the EU's objective of 
establishing a more ecologically sustainable transport system, with the goal of shift 30% of road 
freight over 300 km to other modes (TRAN Committee, 2018), (European Commission, 2020). 
Moreover, intermodal transport frequently proves to be more economically viable than relying 
solely on a single mode of transport, particularly for long-distance shipments. It enables enhanced 
flexibility and accessibility, enabling goods to traverse diverse geographical areas using the most 
efficient mode of transport for each segment of the journey. The adoption of intermodal transport, 
leading to a transfer of freight from road to rail or water, contributes to mitigating road congestion, 
a significant concern in various parts of the EU (European Commission, 2020) 
Although the EU boasts well-developed infrastructure the implementation of intermodal transport 
necessitates substantial infrastructure development encompassing ports, railways, roads, and 
specialized terminals dedicated to facilitating the transfer of goods between different modes of 
transport. Moreover, the involvement of multiple modes of transport in intermodal operations can 
result in slower transit times compared to single-mode transport due to the time required for goods 
transfer between different modes (European Court of Auditors, 2023).

2.3 Market Based Measures 
Market-Based Measures (MBMs) cover an array of environmental strategies, such as emissions 
trading systems and carbon taxation, based on the fundamental concept of making polluters 
accountable and offering financial rewards to persuade stakeholders to reduce emissions. The key 
goal of these methods is to incorporate the external costs associated with emissions, while 
concurrently raising funds that can efficiently tackle the competitive inequality between 
conventional fuels and low or zero-carbon alternatives. The European Union's Emissions Trading 
System (EU ETS), set up in 2005 as a clear instance of a market-oriented approach, is designed 
specifically to tackle climate change (European Commission, 2021). 
2.3.1 EU ETS for the International Maritime Sector 
2.3.1.1 
Extension of EU ETS to Include Maritime Sector 
The EU's commitment to net-zero GHG emissions by 2050 is strengthened by the plan to include 
maritime GHG emissions in the EU ETS. The 'Fit for 55' package's EU ETS modification regulates 
maritime CO2 emissions. Emissions from sailing between European Economic Area (EEA) ports, 
berthing in EEA ports, and international journeys between EEA and non-EEA ports are included. 
Several projects promote shipping's EU ETS participation (European Commission, 2021). The 
Fuel EU Marine Initiative promotes cleaner fuels and alternative technologies in the marine 
industry (European Council, 2023). The Energy Taxation Directive and Alternative Fuels 
Infrastructure Regulation support the EU ETS extension by defining GHG intensity targets and 
increasing port access to sustainable energy (European Commission, 2021). These activities 
attempt to cut maritime emissions and promote sustainable, low-carbon shipping. 
According to Hermeling et al. (2015), expanding the EU ETS to the maritime sector needs careful 
assessment of routes with a substantial proportion of regulated emissions and potential restrictions 
for cost-efficient emission abatement, particularly for short routes within EU territorial waters. 
Franc and Sutto (2014) warn that a cap-and-trade policy in the shipping industry could cause modal 
shifts from maritime to road haulage transport. These studies underscore the need to assess the 
potential impacts and unintended consequences of incorporating the maritime industry in the EU 
ETS. Including the maritime sector in the EU ETS is essential to meeting emissions targets. GHG 
emissions from shipping activities during sailing and at ports, along with programs encouraging 
cleaner fuels and alternative technology, aim to help the marine industry become more sustainable. 
==PAGE:28==
17 
 
However, short routes, future mode shifts, and the need for comprehensive evaluation must be 
carefully considered to ensure the EU ETS's effectiveness and efficiency in the maritime sector 
(European Commission, 2021). 
2.3.1.2 
Legal Feasibility and Impacts on Shipping 
Several studies have thoroughly investigated the legal viability and potential consequences of 
incorporating the shipping industry into the EU ETS. Within the EU ETS system, Hermeling et al. 
(2015) examines the question of the burden placed on routes with a substantial share of regulated 
emissions. They emphasize the difficulties encountered by regulated ships operating on short trips 
within EU territorial waters, as well as potential constraints for cost-effective pollution abatement 
in these situations. The study underlines the significance of considering the individual 
characteristics of distinct routes to ensure that emission reduction strategies are effective and 
equitable. 
Furthermore, Franc and Sutto (2014) highlight the possibility of modal shifts from maritime to 
land transport because of the adoption of a cap-and-trade program in the shipping industry. They 
express concern that such a transition could result in an unanticipated rise in road transport, which 
could harm sustainability goals. The study underlines the importance of thorough consideration 
and balanced strategies to avoid negative results and achieve the targeted environmental 
advantages. Additionally, Psaraftis & Lagouvardou (2019) examine the influence of the MBMs on 
shipping fuel efficiency and conclude that, while the scheme can promote the transition to low-
carbon fuel alternatives and support GHG reduction efforts, the scheme's impact may be limited 
due to industry heterogeneity and potential rebound effects. These studies underline the 
significance of carefully assessing the EU ETS implementation in the maritime sector. They 
emphasize concerns about the burden on certain routes with high emissions, the potential of modal 
shifts, and the necessity for comprehensive policies to effectively control emissions. 
2.3.1.3 
Administrative and Operational Challenges 
The inclusion of the maritime sector in the EU ETS raises administrative, technological, and 
operational issues that must be addressed properly. Enderle (2013) investigates one of these 
concerns, the increased risk of carbon price volatility in the shipping industry. According to the 
report, market demand changes might cause carbon price volatility, posing serious threats to the 
==PAGE:29==
18 
 
shipping industry. This volatility may hurt investment decisions, operational planning, and market 
stability overall. Enderle's findings emphasize the significance of thinking about measures to 
reduce price volatility and create a more stable carbon pricing regime for the shipping industry. 
Furthermore, Lagouvardou and Psaraftis (2022) critically highlight the potential consequences of 
integrating the maritime sector into EU ETS. They assert that such integration may inadvertently 
offer financial motivation for shipping operators to restructure their networks, bypassing routes 
within the EEA and potentially redirecting towards non-EEA transshipment hubs. According to 
their findings, carbon prices below 25 € per tonne of CO2 can instigate a shift towards non-EEA 
transshipment hubs, implicating a potential rise in overall carbon emissions from the shipping 
sector, referred to as carbon leakage. The researchers also caution that strategic policy evasion 
may emerge for shipping companies to reduce their EU ETS costs, leading to further systematic 
complexities. Additionally, such alterations in policy could instigate distortions in competition, 
and induce modal shifts from maritime to land transport. In conclusion, the authors underscore the 
necessity for meticulous scrutiny of the potential side effects of integrating the maritime sector 
into the EU ETS, despite its initial advantages, to ensure harmonization with overarching global 
emission reduction objectives. 
2.3.2 EU ETS for Road Transport 
2.3.2.1 
Introduction of ETS 2 for Road Transport 
The European Parliament and Council of the EU have agreed to extend Emissions Trading System 
specifically intended for road transport (EU ETS 2) and other sectors not covered by the present 
EU ETS as part of the comprehensive 'Fit for 55' package (European Commission, 2021). This 
innovative approach, which is expected to go into effect until 2028 pending formal approval, aims 
to supplement Member States' emission-reduction efforts by regulating gasoline suppliers rather 
than end-users. The EU ETS 2 will help the overarching goal of lowering GHG emissions and 
minimizing the effects of climate change by imposing an absolute restriction on emissions. This 
system's allowances will be distributed through auctioning, supporting a market-based approach 
to reward emission reductions and promote the adoption of cleaner technology and practices in the 
road transportation sector (European Commission, 2021). 
Furthermore, the Impact Assessment accompanying the 'Fit for 55' package from the European 
Commission provides a full summary of the motives and predicted results of the ETS 2 for road 
==PAGE:30==
19 
 
transport. It emphasizes the necessity to regulate industries that are not currently covered by the 
EU ETS and emphasizes the importance of guaranteeing environmental integrity while fostering 
a fair and socially balanced transition. The Impact Assessment provides a rigorous examination of 
the planned ETS 2's possible economic, social, and environmental implications, establishing the 
groundwork for informed decision-making and policy formulation (European Commission, 2021). 
2.3.2.2 
Emission Reduction Targets and Market Stability 
The EU ETS 2 for road transport includes aggressive emission reduction targets to drive long-term 
reform in the industry. The system intends to achieve a linear reduction factor, resulting in a 43% 
reduction in overall emissions by 2030 compared to 2005 levels (European Commission, 2021). 
This commitment to considerable emissions reductions corresponds with the EU's broader climate 
targets and highlights the importance of the road transportation sector's contribution to climate 
change mitigation. Setting defined and quantifiable targets provides a framework for stakeholders 
to implement actions and technology that aid in the transition to a low-carbon transportation 
system (European Commission, 2021). 
The EU ETS 2 includes a market stability reserve to ensure market stability and solve any pricing 
volatility concerns. This reserve mechanism changes the supply of allowances based on market 
conditions, to maintain a supply-demand balance. The market stability reserve helps to maintain a 
steady carbon price by properly regulating the availability of allowances, which is critical for 
giving price signals that promote emission reductions and investment in cleaner technologies. 
Furthermore, the EU ETS 2 contains provisions to encourage innovation by allocating allowances 
to the Innovation Fund. The generated revenues will be allocated to the Innovation Fund, thereby 
fortifying innovation in domains such as low-carbon technologies, carbon capture, renewable 
energy, and energy storage. This strategic framework will effectively contribute to the 
amelioration of climate change. (European Commission, 2021) 
2.3.2.3 
Technical Feasibility and Delay Mechanism 
The EU ETS 2 for road transport respects the sector's particular characteristics, which include a 
considerable number of minor emitters. In this case, the point of regulation is located further 
upstream in the supply chain to effectively limit emissions. The compliance obligation is triggered 
in this manner by the release of the consumption of fuels used for combustion in buildings and 
==PAGE:31==
20 
 
road transport. The EU ETS 2 assures complete coverage of emissions while expediting the 
monitoring and reporting procedure by shifting responsibility to fuel suppliers rather than end users 
(European Commission, 2021). 
Recognizing the possible difficulties associated with unusually high gas or oil wholesale prices, 
the EU ETS 2 includes a mechanism for deferring the cap and ceding commitments for one year. 
This system, which is activated by energy price thresholds, tries to alleviate market uncertainty, 
and ensure market stability. The method allows market players to adjust to fluctuating energy costs 
while meeting their obligations to cut emissions by allowing flexibility in the compliance 
timetable. This approach is critical for ensuring market stability and avoiding unnecessary 
financial obligations on players (European Commission, 2021). 
The review of EU ETS provides valuable insights into the implications of the scheme on the

challenges associated with the inclusion of these sectors in the EU ETS framework. Concerns 
regarding potential modal shifts, carbon price volatility, and the need for stable carbon signals are 
evident. The review also refers to the introduction of the EU ETS 2 for road transport, emphasizing 
emission reduction targets, market stability mechanisms, and additional safeguards. The findings 
from this literature review will serve as a basis for further analysis in evaluating the potential 
effects of the EU ETS on the AEGIS project and identifying optimal solutions for autonomous 
shipping, ports, and intermodal innovations through a comprehensive cost-benefit analysis.

3.3.2 Port of Rotterdam  
The Port of Rotterdam, Europe's largest port, spans an area of 40 kilometers in length and 10 
kilometers in width. It is strategically situated at the meeting point of the Rhine and Meuse rivers, 
connecting to the North Sea. This prime location makes it a crucial center for various types of 
cargo originating from all over Europe. The port benefits from the connectivity to the surrounding 
areas through multiple transportation modes such as river, rail, and road. It has the capacity to 
handle a wide range of goods including crude oil, petroleum products, containers, dry bulk, break 
bulk, and Ro-Ro traffic (Port of Rotterdam, 2021). 
In 2021, the Port of Rotterdam achieved significant success by processing nearly 469 million 
tonnes of cargo. This consisted of approximately 326 million tonnes of imports and over 143 
million tonnes of exports. The total cargo volume included 284 million tonnes of bulk cargo, 30.9 
million tonnes of breakbulk cargo, and 154.5 million tonnes of containerized cargo (Port of 
Rotterdam, 2021). 
The port's impact on the Dutch economy is substantial, providing employment for approximately 
1,270 people under the Port of Rotterdam and generates a revenue of around €770 million. 
Furthermore, it contributes directly and indirectly to the creation of 565,000 jobs in the

Netherlands. The Port of Rotterdam's direct and indirect added value amounts to 63 billion euros, 
which represents a significant 8.2% of the Dutch gross domestic product (GDP) (Port of 
Rotterdam, 2021). These figures highlight the port's immense significance as a vital component of 
the Dutch economy and its crucial role as a major global logistics hub. 
3.3.3 Port of Ghent 
The Port of Ghent in Belgium is a prominent seaport with strategic proximity to the Ghent-
Terneuzen Canal, enabling access to the North Sea and facilitating trade with major European 
markets. Covering an extensive area of approximately 32,000 hectares, it ranks among Europe's 
largest ports and boasts a comprehensive infrastructure of dock complexes, terminals, and 
industrial zones. The port area extends up to 32 kilometers inland from the North Sea, facilitating 
the efficient handling of a diverse range of goods, including dry bulk, liquid bulk, general cargo, 
containers, and project cargo (Port of Ghent, 2022). 
In 2022, the Port of Ghent played a pivotal role in the transshipment of goods, overseeing the 
movement of 73.6 million tonnes through sea routes and an additional 64.5 million tonnes via 
inland waterways. This throughput contributed an added value of 12.6 billion euros to the regional 
economy. The port's versatility allows it to effectively manage various cargo categories, and its 
reliance on inland waterways shipping for over 50% of transportation between the port and its 
hinterland emphasizes its commitment to sustainable and efficient modes of transport (Port of 
Ghent, 2022). To further alleviate congestion and reduce CO2 emissions, the Port of Ghent actively 
advocates for a modal shift strategy, prioritizing rail, inland waterway, and pipeline transportation. 
Currently, truck transport accounts for one-third of the hinterland traffic, whereas rail 
transportation handles approximately seven million tonnes of goods annually, constituting nearly 
10% of the overall hinterland transport volume at North Sea Port (Port of Ghent, 2022). These 
efforts align with the port's commitment to sustainable practices and its goal of establishing an 
environmentally conscious transport system.

opportunities would give vital insights into the overall success of the project.

4 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) 
The transition towards more sustainable modes of transport, such as the shift from road-haulage to 
the utilization of inland waterways with Ro-Ro vessel concept, requires meticulous examination 
and evaluation. In the context of this study, a comprehensive evaluation of potential solutions is 
of the utmost importance, to discern the ramifications of all plausible interconnections and 
dependencies. The solutions are contemplated in UCB, it is imperative to assess them holistically 
to capture the effects of all conceivable cross-linkages and interdependencies to obtain what we 
call “win-win” solutions. 
Considering this objective, the main goal of this thesis is the identification of KPIs, which serve 
to scrutinize the economic, environmental, and social implications of both the baseline scenario 
and the AEGIS solution. The selected set of KPIs will be utilized in conducting a quantitative 
CBA. The primary intention of the CBA is to distinguish advantages and disadvantages and “win-
win" solutions that yield maximal overall advantages at the least conceivable cost. The chosen 
KPIs for this thesis have been carefully selected from the initial set of KPIs identified by the 
AEGIS community. 
Equally important is to mention that despite the utmost diligence in gathering comprehensive data 
for this research, certain information remained inaccessible at the time of finalizing this report. 
Furthermore, accurate data pertaining to certain KPIs would only materialize post the real-world 
execution of the AEGIS project. In cases where data was ambiguous, approximations were made, 
to fill in for the missing values and data. 
4.1 Comprehensive selection and scrutiny of KPIs 
The initial set of KPIs suggested by AEGIS AG and Consortium was comprehensive, 
encompassing three main categories: economic, environmental, and social. The selected KPIs 
evaluate all conceivable impacts and outcomes of the project, encapsulating potential economic 
benefits and costs, environmental implications, and social effects of the implementation of the 
AEGIS project. 
To fulfil the objectives of this thesis, a subset of KPIs was meticulously chosen from the broader 
set originally selected by the AEGIS Consortium and the AG (Table A2.1 within Appendix A2). 
This selection was executed based on the ability of these KPIs to provide an encompassing, yet

the IWW Ro-Ro vessel. This decision was guided by several factors, including relevance to the 
study's main objectives, measurability, and balance between the diverse types of KPIs, for the 
inclusion in a CBA. However, this refined selection is not an indication of the insignificance of 
the other KPIs, but rather a methodological decision to provide a more targeted evaluation within 
the scope of this study.

5 Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) 
A preliminary examination conducted to appraise the financial and environmental feasibility of 
transitioning cargo transportation from road haulage to inland waterways for the last mile of cargo 
delivery. The analysis will be anchored on the selected KPIs for evaluation based on a quantitative 
CBA that will allow to identify the so-called “win-win” solution. The study will scrutinize two 
alternatives, namely, the prevailing situation, which involves road haulage utilizing trucks, and the 
novel solution introduced by the AEGIS project that proposes the utilization of fully electric 
autonomous Ro-Ro vessel that will operate between the ports of Rotterdam and Ghent. 
The analysis aims to determine the viability of the proposed mode of transportation by assessing 
its cost-effectiveness and environmental sustainability. The chosen KPIs will incorporate, among 
others, the capital expenditure, operational expenditure, fuel costs, emission levels, and time 
efficiency. Through comparing the two alternatives, the analysis will provide insights to ascertain 
whether the new proposal presents a feasible option for cargo transportation, especially for the last 
mile of delivery. Furthermore, this research will primarily focus on analyzing the AEGIS solution 
across various speed conditions, while also examining the economic consequences that a 
prospective EU ETS will impose on the operational expenses of the baseline scenario. The 
outcomes of the analysis will be valuable to decision-makers interested in enhancing the efficiency 
and sustainability of the proposed AEGIS solution. 
Over the course of time, CBA has assumed a pivotal role as the principal instrument for conducting 
comparisons, thereby facilitating the evaluation of investment projects anticipated to yield 
substantial socio-economic returns. As per Bruhn and Leleur (2022) projects subjected to 
comprehensive assessment through CBA are more likely to secure political acceptance in 
comparison to those lacking such scrutiny, thereby serving as a robust decision support tool during 
the planning process due to its straightforward nature. The implementation of CBA necessitates 
meticulous collection of detailed information, forming the bedrock for decision-making and 
imparting valuable insights. Nonetheless, ensuring congruity between the theoretical assumptions 
of the CBA method and its practical application poses challenges, particularly in estimating non-
market impacts encompassing transit time savings, emissions, safety, and other pertinent factors 
(Bruhn & Leleur, 2022).

Ro-Ro vessels in operation, resulting in a total of 21 round trips conducted within a week. 
Assuming each Ro-Ro vessel can carry 80% of the total 69 trailers capacity, it can hold 
approximately 55 trailers during a one-way trip from the port of Rotterdam to Ghent. As a result, 
the total volume of trailers transported by the three Ro-Ro vessels during a week is determined by 
multiplying the number of one-way trips by the trailer capacity per trip, which equals to 42 x 55 
trailers = 2,310 trailers. 
Moreover, to establish the number of trucks required to transport 2,310 trailers during a week, it 
is essential to calculate the number of trailers that each truck can accommodate and transport per 
week. The distance between Rotterdam and Ghent is approximately 160 kilometers when traversed 
by road. Taking into consideration the assumption that each truck can consistently maintain an 
average speed of approximately 65 kilometers per hour, the one-way travel is determined at 2.5 
hours. Furthermore, considering the truck's loading and unloading time of 0.03 hours (1.8 minutes)

each, that rely on DFDS data, the total time required for a complete one-way trip would be 2.56 
hours. Thus, each truck can carry two trailers and complete a round trip within 5.12 hours, hence 
can transport around nine trailers per day. On the assumption that each truck operates continuously 
24/7, that means that it can transport approximately 64 trailers per week. Consequently, the total 
number of trucks needed to transport 2,310 trailers in a week is computed by dividing the total

5.2 Environmental KPIs 
5.2.1 Baseline scenario – road haulage 
The significance of recognizing not only the GHG emissions resulting from diesel consumption, 
but also the environmental impact of diesel production from fossil fuels cannot be overstated. 
Thus, two distinct life cycles can be identified in this context, namely: 
• Well-to-Tank (WTT) cycle, which encompasses the emissions that arise from the essential 
steps involved in the production of diesel fuel, including the associated transportation 
process leading to its storage in the vehicle’s tank (Chocholac, Hyrslova, Kucera, 
Machalik, & Hruska, 2019). 
• Tank-to-Wheel (TTW) cycle, which involves the emissions stemming from diesel fuel 
consumption during the operational phase of the vehicle (Chocholac, Hyrslova, Kucera, 
Machalik, & Hruska, 2019). 
==PAGE:59==
48 
 
Considering both the WTT and TTW phases, is imperative in assessing the environmental impact 
of diesel fuel. While the TTW phase accounts for the bulk of the emissions linked to diesel fuel, 
the WTT phase also makes a considerable contribution, particularly with regards to the carbon 
footprint of the fuel (Chocholac, Hyrslova, Kucera, Machalik, & Hruska, 2019).

5.3 EU ETS and EU ETS 2 
The purpose of this study is to investigate whether the upcoming integration of the maritime 
industry into the EU ETS and the proposed design of a new EU ETS 2 dedicated to road transport 
could have an impact on the economic and environmental KPIs of the AEGIS project, which could 
potentially uncover a future “win-win” solution for the project. 
As already mentioned in Chapter 2, MBMs are types of environmental policies that include 
mechanisms like carbon taxes and emissions trading systems, upholding the concept that polluters 
should bear the financial burden of their actions. These measures serve as financial motivators for 
entities to reduce GHGs emissions. The goal is to account for the indirect costs of emissions and 
potentially generate revenue, narrowing the economic gap between conventional energy sources 
and low or zero carbon alternatives. MBMs can take different forms such as environmental taxes 
or cap and trade systems (European Commission, 2021). 
In these systems, one emission allowance permit corresponds to a company's permission to emit 
one tonne of carbon. Currently, the cost of carbon in the EU market fluctuates between 90-
95€/tonne of CO2 (European Commission, 2021). The EU ETS escalates the company's total fossil 
fuel costs, thus expanding the operational expenditure with the intention of inducing changes in 
stakeholder behavior. The anticipated outcome is a shift towards energy efficiency initiatives and 
the adoption of less energy-intensive technologies, which can minimize a company's carbon 
footprint (European Commission, 2021). 
In July 2021, the European Commission put forward a proposition to extend the coverage of the 
EU ETS to include the measurement and regulation of GHG emissions originating from the 
maritime sector. At present, the system applies only to vessels exceeding 5000 gross tonnages 
(GT), but there are plans to expand this to all vessels over 400 GT in the future (European 
Commission, 2021). Moreover, in December 2022, the European Parliament and Council of the 
EU agreed to set up a new Emissions Trading System (EU ETS 2) for emissions originating from 
fuels used in buildings, road transport, and specific industrial sectors not currently covered by the 
existing EU ETS. The carbon price of the new emissions trading system is not expected to exceed 
45€/tonne of CO2 emissions, and in theory, this measure should apply once during a 12-month 
period (European Commission, 2021). 
==PAGE:68==

For this study, the vessels that is going to be deployed for UCB of the AEGIS scenario do not meet 
the 5000 GT threshold required for the application of the EU ETS. This indicates that the EU ETS, 
as it currently stands, does not affect this case. However, a new dimension is set to emerge with 
the forthcoming establishment of EU ETS 2, which is specifically targeted at road transport. This 
expansion of the EU ETS will encapsulate the baseline scenario of road haulage. The consequence 
of this will be the addition of extra cost to the operating expenses in the baseline scenario. As a 
result, businesses operating in this sector will need to anticipate this change and integrate it into 
their future business strategies.

In CBA analysis and decision-making science, the concept of BEP carries profound importance, 
especially when the task at hand involves a comparative study of two or more alternatives. In the 
context of this study, the baseline scenario and proposed AEGIS solutions are the subjects of 
comparison. The BEP represents the temporal juncture at which the accumulated costs of 
implementing AEGIS solution are exactly offset by the costs that would have been incurred if the 
baseline scenario had been maintained. Beyond this temporal juncture, the cost-effectiveness of 
one alternative surpasses the other. Through rigorous calculation, the BEP in years examined 
fluctuates between three to three and a half years, which implies that if the duration of the system 
operation surpasses that number of years, the AEGIS emerges as a more cost-effective alternative 
to the baseline scenario. Barge vessels, exemplified by the AEGIS solution, are renowned for their 
extensive operational lifespan. Although the precise duration of a vessel's service can exhibit 
considerable variability contingent upon factors such as vessel type, utilization, maintenance 
practices, and numerous other variables, the industry often cites a typical lifespan of approximately 
25-30 years (Zis, Psaraftis, Tillig, & Ringsberg, 2020). Consequently, this longevity exceeds the 
calculated break-even points. Thus, if the AEGIS vessel is anticipated to operate for its entire 
designated lifespan, the AEGIS solution is expected to demonstrate superior cost-effectiveness 
over the baseline scenario in the long term.

6.1 AEGIS stakeholders survey

distributed among AEGIS Consortium and the Advisory Group. The primary objective of this

evaluate the chosen set of KPIs, wherein a rating of ten indicated the highest level of significance 
for the respective KPIs. It is worth mentioning that while the survey did not yield a substantial 
level of participation, the collected responses (Figure 12) provide a directional indication despite

7 Recommendations for future research 
Reflecting upon the insights and limitations of this investigation, several potential research 
directions are proposed. These revolve around opportunities for the refinement of the autonomous 
barge concept and the provision of a more exhaustive evaluation.  
Initially, a more inclusive analysis of the lifecycle emissions, extending the current focus on the 
WTT and TTW emissions to encompass a lifecycle emissions analysis. Such an analysis would 
consider emissions derived from both the production and disposal stages of vehicles and Ro-Ro 
barges. By conducting a lifecycle assessment (LCA) of autonomous Ro-Ro barges and road 
haulage vehicles, a more detailed understanding of the environmental footprint across all life 
stages could be established. In addition, a separate LCA dedicated to the batteries employed in the 
AEGIS propulsion system could provide a holistic assessment of the environmental impact of the 
transport modes under consideration.  
Furthermore, considering the static nature of the data utilized in the CBA, primarily resulting from 
the project's pre-commercialized status, it is recommended to revisit this aspect in future research. 
Specifically, as the AEGIS project moves towards implementation and accrues relevant data, 
particularly relating to environmental and social KPIs, a more comprehensive CBA could be 
performed. It is also noteworthy that the current Break-Even Point (BEP) fails to accommodate 
dynamic factors such as increasing maintenance costs over time or potential cost reductions due 
to process improvements, as well as the commercial benefits of the commercialize phase of AEGIS 
project. Hence, subsequent financial analyses should incorporate these dynamic elements, utilizing 
more advanced financial techniques such as Net Present Value (NPV) or Internal Rate of Return 
(IRR). This would provide a broader analysis of the financial evolution of the AEGIS project.  
Additionally, regarding the stakeholder engagement, future endeavors should prioritize the 
development and application of effective strategies for stakeholder involvement. This involves 
devising methods for eliciting stakeholder perspectives on the weighting of the chosen set of KPIs, 
thereby facilitating a more thorough Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA). The intent is to 
facilitate a deeper understanding of the stakeholders' priorities and the relative importance they 
assign to different KPIs. The weights assigned to each KPI reflect their relative importance in the 
decision-making process, and different weightings can significantly affect the outcome of the 
analysis. By thoroughly understanding stakeholder perspectives on KPI weights, the MCDA can 
==PAGE:83==
72 
 
more accurately reflect the values and priorities of the decision makers. It allows the model to 
align better with the actual preferences of those making or influencing the decisions, thereby 
producing more relevant and actionable results. 
The suggestions provided here outline potential pathways for future research. The aim is twofold; 
to optimize the AEGIS vessel and to deliver a deeper assessment of its cost-effectiveness and 
broader impact. By concentrating on these areas, researchers can contribute to the continuous 
refinement of the system and promote a more robust understanding of autonomous barge transport 
potential.
