Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the optimal integration of utility scale PV system with grid
connected BESS. This work was conducted in collaboration with the Technical University of Den-
mark (DTU), the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) and the Institute for
Energy Technology (IFE). The project scope includes determining the potential of utility scale
BESS to facilitate large scale PV systems and to provide effective frequency regulation services to
the power grid. This thesis also has a speciﬁc focus on developing a valid BESS control strategy
and system modelling approach.
The model presented is developed in MATLAB Simulink, specialised power systems. The devel-
oped system is comprised of a 50 MW PV plant, 50 MW/120 MWh battery and a 200 MW gen-
erator, along with the necessary power conversion technologies and grid connected system loads.
In the interest of observing system frequency regulation capabilities, several simulation cases were
devised to create system frequency disturbances. Each case involves a simulation with and without
the grid connected BESS. This enables comparative analysis of results to assess the potential of
BESS to provide frequency regulation services. It also allows a more critical observations of the
developed BESS control strategy.
The simulation cases investigated were: (1) system steady state, (2) sudden load additions of vari-
ous sizes, (3) a brief period of reduced irradiance inputs to the PV plant, and (4) a case of constantly
ﬂuctuating irradiance inputs. Some further investigative cases are also presented in response to
some model uncertainties which arise.
The simulated results are examined for two purposes. The ﬁrst is to observe the comparative fre-
quency control in the cases with and without the grid connected BESS. The second is to examine
the measured variables of the BESS, to analyse battery function and the effectiveness of the con-
trol system. The results conﬁrm the important potential utility scale BESS has to facilitate high
penetrations of PV power to existing energy grids. They also demonstrate how batteries can work
effectively in such systems to provide frequency regulation services. The model is critically eval-
uated to give validation and suggestions into areas for further model development.
The ﬁnal work presented in this thesis highlights the signiﬁcant potential of grid connected bat-
tery systems for frequency control and offers a valuable, reliable modelling tool for investigation
into future utility scale PV and BESS projects. As the present of large scale, grid connected PV
systems and utility scale batteries grows, this thesis can contribute to research facilitating optimal
integration of such systems to the power grid.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In 2021, the current energy landscape is in a period of rapid transition. The damaging effects
of global warning are already widely known, and the effects of climate change are being noticed
around the globe. Along with the evidence of global warming is an onslaught of scientiﬁc research
highlighting the signiﬁcant role carbon emissions play in advancing human-caused climate change.
These emissions are largely produced by conventional generators which rely on fossil fuels, such
as coal and gas plants. In 2019, 25% of global greenhouse gas emissions were attributed directly to
electricity generation, with fossil fuel based technologies amounting to 62% of global energy pro-
duction [1]. In the interests of suppressing the detrimental climate effects of these greenhouse gas
emissions, there is an increasing push to introduce emission-free renewable energies at all scales.
This has changed the energy market globally, with international agreements such as the Paris agree-
ment [2] and the Kyoto protocol [3] compelling governments to push for higher renewable energy
uptakes.
In turn, there has been a large growth in solar photovoltaic (PV) power. From 2019 to 2020,
there was almost a 150TWh increase in solar generation globally [4], making PV technology more
prominent than ever. Norway alone saw a solar PV capacity increase of 40 MW [5], the large ma-
jority of which was grid connected. In the IEA Renewables 2020 report, a large share of new solar
PV installation was found to be focused on utility scale PV generation, and this share is expected
to increase [6]. As such, this thesis will focus on utility scale solar PV and the optimal integration
strategies for it to be prevalent at higher penetrations in the Nordic grid.
Utility scale solar PV plants can hold large generation capacities but naturally production is de-
pendent on incoming solar irradiance. Consequently, solar PV plants produce vastly different and
uncontrollable generation proﬁles than the conventional generators for which the energy grid was
designed. At high PV penetrations, this introduces ﬂuctuations and uncertainty in the power sys-
tem. These sudden load imbalances can cause detrimental frequency deviations from normal grid
frequency [7]. This introduces a subsequent need for peaking powers plants and ancillary service
provision. Traditionally, this role was ﬁlled with gas peaking power plants, however this thesis
will investigate the newer, emissions free alternative of hybridising PV generation with a battery
energy storage system (BESS) to combat these complications.
1
==PAGE:7==
Recent advancements in battery technology have seen improved response time, energy density,
lifetime and ever declining capital costs. In 2021, the Australian Clean Energy Council (CEC)
announced that batteries can now outcompete gas generators as peaking plants across the board
[8]. The storage ability, ﬂexibility and controllability of BESS can equip power systems to manage
the undesired frequency disturbances and give the immediate, initial frequency regulation of fast
frequency response (FFR). FFR can ensure system stability long enough for alternative ancillary
services to come online [9], and thus can enable signiﬁcantly higher penetrations of variable re-
newable energy sources such as solar PV.
Already today, utility scale BESS uptake can be seen globally. Currently, the largest grid connected
BESS is the Moss Landing battery in the United States, providing up to 300 MW rated capacity
[10]. Future projects in the US and Australia include planned battery capacities of more than 1000
MW [11].
This thesis will explore the integration of a utility scale BESS, alongside utility scale PV genera-
tion, to impact grid stability and provide FFR services in the Norwegian power system. It focuses
on forming an accurate model of a section of the Norwegian regional grid in MATLAB Simulink
speacialised power systems [12]. This model is used to assess the frequency impact of large scale
PV and BESS installation.
The regional grid, relevant generators are modelled in Simulink. A PV plant, BESS and control
system are designed be added to this grid. The control approach is a PI based control of battery
charge and discharge switches, in accordance with grid frequency requirements. The model is
simulated under a variety of conditions simulating different load imbalances occurrences. The grid
frequency and relevant battery variables are measured, plotted, and presented in the thesis results
section.
Analysis of these simulated results facilitates insights into the potential of grid connected BESS to
enable high penetrations of PV power. Furthermore, results analysis coupled with a critical eval-
uation of the modelling methodology gives valuable feedback on optimal integration strategy and
modelling procedure for future projects.
The project considers multiple factors of a PV and BESS system to assist in the transition towards
renewables. It focuses on utility scale projects to accommodate for the expected increase in in-
stalled utility scale PV capacity and it addresses the contemporary provision of FFR in the wake
of declining conventional generator penetration and the upcoming FFR market in Norway.
1.1
Project scope
This thesis will speciﬁcally focus on determining if BESS can provide sufﬁcient frequency re-
serves to the regional grid when composition is affected by the introduction of high penetrations
of PV power. It will focus on the optimal integration method for this case description, including
BESS control strategy and battery capacity sizing. The scope of this thesis also includes a critical
evaluation of the best modelling strategy for such a system.
2
==PAGE:8==
The research is conducted through system simulation in a MATLAB Simulink model. The model
was created entirely from synthetic data and was speciﬁcally designed to assess frequency response
and to provide optimal frequency regulation. It includes:
• 50 MW PV plant
• 50 MW/120 MWh Li-ion BESS
• Connection to the Norwegian regional grid
• PI based BESS control approach
• Additional grid connected generators and loads
The ﬁndings of this thesis can be used to assess suitability of utility scale PV and BESS systems
for future projects. It can be utilised as a reference for optimal control and battery capacity require-
ments, or to demonstrate an effective modelling strategy which can be adopted in the development
of future renewable energy systems.

Literature review
2.1
Grid integration challenges
Every year, the global capacity of variable renewable energy generation capacity increases. Figure
2.1.1 shows the steep growth in PV systems present in the current energy mix in Norway. In the
last 5 years, the large majority of new PV installation has been connected to the grid. However,
increasing the penetration of PV generation in the power grid whilst adhering to grid code require-
ments comes with a speciﬁc set of challenges associated with PV power characteristics.
Figure 2.1.1: Installed in solar PV capacity in Norway each year since 2004 [5].
2.1.1
Power ﬂuctuation
The underlying downside of variable renewable energies, such as wind and solar power, is the un-
controllable variability of power generation due to its dependence on external weather conditions
4
==PAGE:10==
[13]. This ﬂuctuation can negatively affect frequency stability, voltage stability and the power bal-
ance in the grid. Kundur [14] wrote that “when a power system maintains a state of equilibrium
during normal operating condition or returns to acceptable state of equilibrium after being sub-
jected to a disturbance, then the system is said to be stable.” This section will address the speciﬁc
ways in which large scale PV integration can threaten this power stability.
With increasing PV penetration, several issues with frequency stability occur in the power system
[7], whilst frequency support within the system is reduced [15]. PV generation systems have very
low system inertia, so at high penetrations, they decrease the total inertia of the power system. This
creates a lesser ability to manage frequency ﬂuctuations caused by load demand changes [16]. Ad-
ditionally, the variation of generation leads to unpredictability in power output, which can create
power imbalances in the grid. As such, it follows that increased PV presence in the power system
contributes to frequency instability and issues of frequency regulation [17],[18].
The variability also leads to problems with over and under supply of voltages. Power surges can
result in overvoltage and overload problems [16]. These are called voltage spikes and over time
they are physically damaging to electronic equipment [19]. Voltage spikes can also cause compli-
cations for other generators on the grid [19]. Conversely, the system also suffers if voltage supply
is too low. A voltage <90% of nominal voltage is classiﬁed as a voltage dip [20] which can lead
to brown outs, which can be identiﬁed by dimming or ﬂickering lights and the inability of the
incoming power supply from the grid to power some equipment [19].
These complications further afﬂict grid management for the operators [13]. High penetrations of
variable energy sources increase uncertainty in power generation prediction and prove a hindrance
to accurately predicting future weather conditions at the generation site, complicating calcula-
tion and operation of the hour-ahead and day-ahead power markets for grid operators. Inaccurate
generation predictions and non-optimal grid management are also contributing factors to power
imbalances on the grid [20], and further decrease power quality.
Finally, it is important for renewable energy sources to be able to provide the same ancillary ser-
vices currently provided by conventional power plants, such as load following, voltage and fre-
quency regulation and contributing to current faults [20]. This will avoid the situation of having
large enough renewable capacity to meet load demand, but still requiring gas plants to provide an-
cillary services, resulting in excess renewable production, as occured in the German grid in 2015
[21].
The IT Power report addressing grid integration of renewable energies [19] concludes by sug-
gesting further studies conducted in this area should investigate high penetrations of large scale
renewable energy sources into transmission networks, which is explored within the scope of this
thesis.
2.1.2
Location
By nature, wind and solar energy are condition reliant. One consequence of this is location de-
pendence of the technology effectiveness, meaning renewable generation often cannot be placed
5
==PAGE:11==
in locations optimal for grid connection. This limits the options for connectivity points to the grid
[20]. It also introduces the need for transmission infrastructure which brings associated challenges
with increased losses, infrastructure upgrades, additional costs and regional politics [13], which all
represent greater obstacles for large scale PV integration.
The IT power report [19] also states the inﬂuence location has on power generation, citing that just
a 2km distance between plants can result in different generation proﬁles. The problematic ﬂuctua-
tion in PV generation could then be slightly alleviated by dispersing generation points to create a
more levelled generation proﬁle, less prone to minor shade interference such as cloud cover. How-
ever, this is economically and practically inconvenient, thus location limitations can exacerbate the
negative inﬂuences of ﬂuctuation on power stability and increase reliance on dependable ancillary
services.
2.2
Battery energy storage
One solution to mitigate the challenges in grid integration of variable renewable energy resources
is energy storage systems. The utilisation of energy storage can effectively transform an uncontrol-
lable, variable renewable energy source into a dispatchable energy resource. This enables active
power control in the system to mitigate the load imbalance challenges outlined in section 2.1.
Currently, there are several forms of energy storage technologies already present in the energy mar-
ket. The sector is predominantly overshadowed by pumped hydro, however there is also industrial
use of chemical storage, compressed air, ﬂywheels, thermal energy storage and electrochemical
storage such as battery energy storage systems (BESS) [22].
Recent years have seen large growth speciﬁcally in BESS technology and its market uptake. The
Australian Renewable Energy Agency (ARENA) predict large scale energy storage is the most
effective solution for managing the power system in the transition to renewable energy [23]. Glob-
ally, there are already several cases of BESS projects which have been integrated to counteract the
challenges listed in 2.1, and there are plans to implement even bigger projects in the future.
Currently, the United States is home to the Moss Landing battery which can provide up to 300MW
of power. In Australia, the TransGrid Wallgrove battery in Sydney, intended to have capacity of
50MW power supply, will consist of a 75MWh storage capacity [24], while the Tesla BESS in
South Australia’s Hornsdale PV power reserve houses a 129MWh storage capacity for a 100MW
capacity power supply [25]. In the UK, InterGen are currently planning a 320MW PV generation
site with 640MWh Li-ion battery storage capacity [26]. Looking forward, there a plans in Aus-
tralia and the United States for future projects which can deliver up to 1500MW [11], [10].
The scope of this thesis focuses speciﬁcally on the potential of grid connected, utility scale BESS to
enable high penetrations of renewable energy generation and provide beneﬁts to the power system.
A review of the literature outlining these beneﬁts is presented in the following sections.

2.2.1
BESS beneﬁts & advancements
Dispatchable energy resource
Batteries are controllable energy sources which are capable of extremely fast ramping rates. Com-
paratively to traditional gas peaking plants, which have start-up times of up to 15 minutes, the
Hornsdale Tesla battery in South Australia has achieved ramping from 0 to 100MW in just 140ms
[27]. These factors make BESS an ideal resource for load following, since it can be controlled to
inject or absorb active power from the grid almost instantaneously. As such, utility scale BESS is
an asset to load balancing efforts and can effectively cover unexpected peaks in demand.
Storage capability
Utility scale BESS technology has evolved to include functional and cost competitive batteries with
a rated capacity of up to four hours of storage duration [8]. This large amount of storage capacity,
coupled with battery controllability, allows energy to be stored from high production times to be
dispatched during high load hours [28]. This process is known as load shifting, and it contributes
to maximising system efﬁciency and minimising excess energy generation. This further aids load
balancing while providing low cost power during peak demand times, reducing costs for end users.
As the grid becomes equipped to absorb more renewable energy, the curtailment of wind and solar
plants can be reduced, meaning battery incorporation results a more optimised power system. The
study [29] states that up to 30% more variable energy production can be absorbed into the grid
without requiring any additional reinforcement to grid infrastructure.
Ancillary services
The fast ramping rate of batteries allows more ﬂexibility in load balancing by assisting in peak
shaving and demand-side management [30]. Namely, there is greater ability to provide ancillary
services in the form of active power control. This can be to provide voltage support or to increase
or decrease system frequency as required [8]. The quick start-up times enable the use of BESS
for FFR almost instantaneously, before primary frequency reserves can come online. Batteries are
also capable of providing primary frequency reserves when required. In lieu of reduced system
inertia due to declining involvement of traditional generators, state-of-the-art batteries are capable
of providing synthetic inertia to strengthen system response to frequency disruptions [24]. This
technology will be implemented through Tesla’s ’Virtual Machine Mode’ in the Sydney Wallgrove
battery project to cover the system shortfalls from the closure of two nearby conventional genera-
tors [24], [31].
Cost
Historically, the key argument against the use of batteries for grid scale energy storage was the
steep capital costs. However, for several years now the cost of utility scale batteries has been
steadily declining with product innovations and large scale manufacturing. As capital costs become
more attractive, the technology is still developing to deliver a levelised cost of energy (LCOE) up
to 30% lower than traditional peaking plants [8]. Batteries also require very little maintenance,
7
==PAGE:13==
providing cost beneﬁts in low O&M costs. With integration to the energy market, BESS can pro-
duce negative price events and facilitate energy market reforms to create a more optimal market.
Several studies are centred around optimal battery participation in frequency regulation markets
for system cost effectiveness [32], [33], concluding that with BESS implementation, higher power
can be bid on the primary frequency regulation market [34]. The load shifting capabilities also
allow system operators to provide lower cost energy during peak periods [35]. Finally, the closer
load balancing provision can result in less stress to electrical equipment, which over time provides
savings through less frequent replacement of machinery. As such, BESS are now able to economi-
cally outcompete conventional generators to provide ancillary services as peaking power resources
[8].
Deployment ﬂexibility
Another advantage of batteries for energy storage is the convenience factors when considering
installation. BESS is geographically ﬂexible, meaning it can be connected to the grid at points
optimal for the power system rather than being limited by geographical factors. This reduces
the transmission infrastructure required and strengthens power quality in the grid. Furthermore,
the BESS can be sized according to project site requirements and this adaptability increases the
chances of BESS suitability for future energy storage projects.
Reduced emissions
Battery technology is fuel independent, which has a variety of beneﬁts. Economically, this means
BESS are not subject to volatile gas prices the same way gas peaking plants are, making BESS even
more cost effective [8]. As an emissions free technology capable of fulﬁlling grid requirements
and provision of ancillary services, batteries represent a clean way to enable higher penetrations of
renewable energy technologies and facilitate decarbonisation of the energy grid [35].
In addition to all the above-mentioned BESS beneﬁts, battery technology is still rapidly evolving.
This means BESS costs can be expected to decline even further and facilitate even greater uptake
of variable renewable energy sources. Batteries are capable of adapting to suit a variety of energy
markets and have the potential to become much more prominent in future energy markets.
2.2.2
Battery speciﬁcations
This thesis will involve a lithium ion (Li-ion) battery based BESS and will conduct further study
in to the optimal battery capacity for the chosen system size.
Lithium ion battery
In current renewable energy projects globally, lithium ion batteries are emerging as the common
choice of BESS in large scale applications. In Australia, the Wallgrove utility scale battery in NSW
[24] consists of a 75MWh lithium ion battery while the Horsndale PV and BESS in South Australia
[25] consists of a 129MWh lithium ion BESS. Meanwhile, the UK is housing a 75MWh lithium
8
==PAGE:14==
ion battery at the Thurcroft PV site and is currently planning a 640MWh lithium ion battery project
in Essex [26].
Lithium ion batteries are becoming widely used and commercialised due to their multitude of
technological advantages. Li-ion battery characteristics which make them suitable for utility scale
BESS applications include that the batteries are reversible, fast, highly efﬁcient, ﬂexible and ever-
increasingly affordable with advancements in technology [28].
Storage capacity
Another important factor in BESS implementation is sizing battery capacity to the system size.
Determining optimal battery capacity to suit system size will be a topic of investigation in this
thesis. However, outside of appropriate load balancing sizing, capacity can also also be considered
as a factor of BESS system health. Battery capacity studies [36], [37] investigate the effects of
varying capacities on operating line currents and battery ageing or lifetime effects. Neither study
comes to a conclusive point of advisement for battery capacity for future projects. These secondary
capacity considerations lie outside the scope of this thesis. which will focus on sizing capacity to
relevant load sizes in the grid.
2.3
Assessment of previous approaches
From section 2.1, the introduction of large scale PV to the grid raises many technical challenges.
Speciﬁcally, the lack of inertia combined with the intermittent nature of PV generation subjects a
power system to unwanted frequency disturbances. Using utility scale BESS for frequency control
is an interesting and multifaceted topic of research. This section will present a review of previous
research approaches investigating the ways BESS can be used to regulate grid frequency and pro-
vide FFR services.
The main attribute making batteries suitable to provide FFR services is that they can be controlled
to add or remove active power from the grid, which has been studied extensively under a variety
of conditions [38], [39], [40], [41], [42], [43].
The paper by Howlander et al. [39] conducted an experimental analysis on smart PV inverters
considering three factors of active power control; active power curtailment, volt-watt control and
frequency-watt control. The conclusions drawn were that this approach utilises active power con-
trol to effectively regulate grid frequency.
Perhaps the most extensively studied area is the frequency effect of small scale PV and BESS on
micro grids. In [40], Johnson et al. present a study focused on providing cost-optimised frequency
reserves to a micro grid in Hawaii. A frequency-watt (FW) curve inverter control strategy was pro-
posed, in which BESS active power output is adjusted as a function of measured grid frequency.
The simulation was run for PV penetrations of 20%, 70% and 120%, ﬁnding that a FW curve strat-
egy resulted in a ‘sizeable improvement’ on frequency control comparatively to simple inverter
curtailment. Johnson et al. conclude that usage of FW function in control is capable of providing
9
==PAGE:15==
frequency regulation to micro grids, and state that ‘the ability of the FW function to reduce fre-
quency ﬂuctuations increases with greater PV penetration’.
In a similar scope, paper [43] describes a frequency control scheme for the Danish Island of Born-
holm, which holds a high penetration of variable renewable energy generation. The study con-
cludes that inverter controlled BESS integration can provide fast acting frequency response to such
a system, but future studies should focus on developing a more advanced state of charge (SOC)
control scheme.
Looking at large scale studies, which are more relevant to this thesis, the authors in [38] determine
that grid frequency stability is achievable with a ﬂowchart control system. The ﬂowchart considers
battery SOC, real-time grid frequency deviation and nominal frequency to determine if the BESS
is in a state or charge or discharge.
Another endorsement of utility scale PV and BESS for grid frequency regulation comes from paper
[34]. Stroe et al. examine a LiFePO4/C BESS, focusing on providing primary frequency regula-
tion (PFR) to a Danish system. The system is deemed capable of providing PFR services, however
the authors emphasise that to predict battery performance, an accurate lifetime model is required
for a lithium ion BESS due to uncertainty of battery performance and wear under complex cycling
conditions. This thesis aims to build on previous research of active power battery control and apply
it speciﬁcally to a larger scale, focused on the provision of FFR services, which react faster than
the above mentioned PFR provision.
It should be mentioned that to effectively integrate large scale BESS to the power grid, there must
also be energy market reforms to accommodate this. With several references supporting the techno-
logical capability of BESS to provide FFR services, [41] highlights the importance of competent
frequency regulation markets, policies and compensation mechanisms as the ‘preconditions for
energy storage to participate in the frequency regulation market’. Similarly, [37] concludes that
future proﬁtability will be dependent on market evolution. The Norwegian TSO, Statnett, have
introduced a seasonal FFR market to accommodate the move towards FFR to enable higher uptake
of renewables in the varied power system scenarios across the seasons. However, the energy mar-
ket management of FFR falls outside the scope of this thesis and should be further investigated in
future studies. This thesis will focus on the modelling and control of PV and BESS to determine
optimal operation and assess project potential.
There are many different approaches to grid frequency control strategies which have been re-
searched. Some of the most common approaches explore PI/PID control [44],[45],[46],[47] fuzzy
logic control [48],[49] and model predictive control [50],[51], all with successful active power
control, but not always within the short timeframe required to qualify as a FFR provider.
Some newer and less established control strategies include a strategy based on incremental cost
consensus (ICC) and utilising a stochastic optimisation algorithm called the genetic algorithm
(GA) [33], which returned promising results. Further, in November 2020, authors Yang et al. [52]
published a study which, to their knowledge, is the ﬁrst research on load frequency control using
double equivalent-input-disturbance (EID) controllers. The study was performed on a PV inte-
10
==PAGE:16==
grated, multi-area, interconnected power system and showed frequency deviation reduced to just
7% of a conventional PI controlled system.
From these previous research approaches, it can be seen that there is a multitude of factors which
can be considered in projects regarding PV and BESS grid integration, and many different control
strategies which could be adopted. This thesis aims to assess speciﬁcally the potential for utility
scale PV and BESS to provide FFR services to the Norwegian regional grid, and the most opti-
mal control strategy to take in this approach. An explanation and critical evaluation of the chosen
modelling approach and methodology will be presented alongside simulated results to gauge the
feasibility of such a system.

3.1
Nordic grid
In order to optimally integrate renewable energy technologies to the power grid, ﬁrst the power
system must be well understood. There are three fundamental parts of the power system; gen-
eration, transmission and consumption. Power generation consists of a range of energy sources,
both renewable and fossil fuel based. For example in 2018, a simplistic overview of Norway’s
power generation showed a composition of 96% hydropower, 2% wind energy and 2% thermal
[53]. However, as seen in section 2.1, solar PV production is a fast growing participant in the
generation side of the power system. Consumption is the energy consumed by the load demands
connected to the power grid. This is typically any regular energy requiring system from factories
and industrial use to residential households.
Transmission is the transportation of energy in the power grid, from its generation site to the
consumer. Typically, a power grid is composed of two grid levels, a transmission grid and a dis-
tribution grid. The transmission grid operates at high voltages and is designed to transport high
volumes of energy over long distances with minimal losses. The distribution grid branches off from
the transmission grid. It operates at lower voltages to transport energy shorter distances, from the
transmission grid to smaller scale, grid connected loads.

3.2
System frequency
The scope of this thesis focuses heavily on electrical frequency in the power grid. Frequency (Hz)
can be deﬁned as how many oscillations alternating current (AC) power makes per second. Glob-
ally, grid frequency is typically either 50 Hz or 60 Hz. This frequency range is designed to avoid
the complications that can come with much higher or much lower frequencies. Higher frequencies
can result in increased series impedance and system losses, while lower values can increase costs
and decrease power supply reliability [57]. At frequencies both above and below nominal fre-
quency, there is risk of system equipment damage and malfunction. As mentioned in section 3.1.1,
the Nordic grid must adhere to grid codes, maintaining frequency within its limits 49.9 < f < 50.1
Hz. The largest threat to this frequency stability lies within the risk of imbalances in the power
market.
Balancing the power market is a multifaceted problem. In the present energy environment, load
balancing within the power grid is reliant on predictable and dispatchable energy production. How-
ever, as addressed in the literature review, solar PV generation is not controllable by grid operators.
In the event that power production is falling short of the load demand, for example due to a large
load increase or a large power generator tripping, the system frequency will begin to fall below the
nominal frequency of 50 Hz. Conversely, in the event of an over production of power compared
to load demand, the system frequency will begin to increase above 50 Hz. Since generators are
designed to operate within a speciﬁc frequency range, exceeding these frequency limits can result
in power plants disconnecting from the grid and the power supply will be interrupted. Further, at
too low frequencies when the load demand is too great, load shedding will be commenced. In the
event that load shedding is not instigated, the system can experience black out.
3.2.1
System inertia
Traditionally, most power grids have a large capacity share of rotating generators which are syn-
chronised to electrical grid frequency [58]. In the event of sudden imbalance between power
generation and consumption, these large rotating masses provide resistance to frequency changes.
This is what provides system inertia, which has previously been the ﬁrst system defence against
frequency ﬂuctuations. System inertia reduces the rate of change of frequency (RoCoF), giving
time for the Nordic frequency containment reserves for disturbances (FCR-D) to start up. This
concept is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.2.1.

3.2.2
Fast Frequency Response
However, with increased penetration of solar PV, which does not provide inertia, faster frequency
regulation ancillary services are required. In 2019, the European Network of Transmission System
Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-E) named FFR the most promising mitigation measure for low

the Norwegian TSO, opened a FFR market to contributors who could effectively increase active
power or reduce load demand within 1 second [9], until the FCR-D system can come online. This
thesis will focus on the potential of a Li-ion BESS to provide effective FFR services to mitigate
future frequency disturbances on the Nordic grid.

3.4.1
PID control
PID control is a proportional-integral-derivative control strategy. It operates by calculating the
error between a system variable actual value and its desired value. An error, e(t), is found by
taking the difference between the measured process variable, y(t), and the desired variable setpoint
or reference value, r(t). This error is taken as an input to an algorithm consisting of a proportional,
integral, and derivative term, each governed by control parameter Kp, Ki and Kd respectively. As
in equation 3.4.1, the sum of the outputs of each term becomes the output correction value of the
control system , u(t). This is fed back into the larger system process. When calculated, the updated
y(t) value is returned through a feedback loop to generate a new error for the next iteration of PID
control. This process is demonstrated in ﬁgure 3.4.1.

Each term in a PID controller serves a different mathematical purpose. The proportional term
speeds up response time and minimises steady state error or offset. It should be noted that by
nature, the proportional term is capable of error minimisation but not elimination. However, the
integral term is designed to eliminate offset but can introduce sustained oscillations. The derivative
term is primarily used to reduced oscillations [67].
The response of each term is dependent on precise tuning of the Kp, Ki and Kd parameters, which
can be a complex process. There a many different tuning mechanisms. Classical techniques in-
clude the well known Zeigler Nichols method and Cohen Coon method. Further computational
methods include the wide spread genetic algorithm (GA), particle swarm optimisation and fuzzy
logic. The paper by Bansal et al. [67] offers further explanation of these tuning techniques and
their comparative capabilities.
Depending on the scenario in which a PID controller is implemented, some systems may only
require P (proportional) or PI (proportional-integral) control. PI control being one of the most
commonly implemented uses of PID strategy. The selection of P, I and D terms may vary as a
factor of control requirements, accuracy needs or process run time.

A phase locked loop (PLL) is a control process which is designed to synchronise PLL output fre-

of the input wave signals. In MATLAB Simulink, the outputs given are measured frequency (Hz)
and ωt (radians), the angle varying between 0 and 2π, synchronized on the zero-crossing of the
fundamental of the input signal [68].

3.4.4
Pulse width modulation
Pulse width modulation (PWM) waves are designed to produces square waves of ones and zeros to
send on/off signals to inverter switches. PWM width modulation (PWM) waves are formed from
the superimposition of a sinusoidal modulation wave and a triangular carrier wave. A comparison
of these two waves produces the square PWM wave of values 0 and 1, which is used to control
inverter switches. When the sine wave has a higher value than the triangular wave, the output is 1
and the respective switch will be on, else the output is 0 and the switch is off. The ratio of switch
time on and off is called the duty cycle and is always within [0 1], constant 0 being the switch is
always off and constant 1 being switch always on.
In a 6 pulse bridge inverter, partnering switches will receive signals from the same PWM genera-
tion, with one receiving a pulse through a NOT logic gate. Six switches allowing power through
the inverter in this controlled manner forms the desired three phase AC wave. The abc modulation
waves used in this process determine the duty cycle and therefore the resulting AC waveform. As
such, the modulation waves are typically the output of a control process.

BESS control
The BESS control design is a vital component to effective function of a grid connected battery.
In this model, the battery is connected to the DC bus by a bidirectional converter, allowing power
ﬂow both from the battery to the bus and vice versa. The bidirectional converter consists of two
IGBT/diodes, one will be connected for charge and one for discharge. As such, the controls system
needs to generate switching signals for both the positive and the negative IGBT/diode connections
in order to effectively control battery SOC. The control system is presented in ﬁgure 4.1.7. It
involves a PI controller, with input being the error between measured and reference frequency, and
output being a duty cycle value. The duty cycle value is taken as input to a PWM generator, which
produces switching signals to be fed to the bidirectional converter. This control over battery charge
and discharge facilitates active power control in the power system.

4.2
Modelling strategy
4.2.1
Frequency control
The BESS is an inverter controlled technology, making it a highly controllable, dispatchable source
of energy. This high level of control enables two possible control approaches to FFR provision.
1. Constant feedback loop and control of frequency, to maintain frequency within the normal
operating frequency range of the Nordic grid, [49.9 50.1] Hz.
2. The activation of BESS frequency response at a speciﬁc frequency value, when frequency
measurements exceed Nordic grid code limits of [49.9 50.1] Hz.
These two approaches represent two important considerations in grid frequency control. The ﬁrst
is having power stability in the grid such that the number of occurrences of activating emergency
frequency control measures are limited. The second is how a system responds when emergency
frequency control measures are required. This thesis has chosen to address the ﬁrst concern by
implementing a constant feedback loop control to maintain frequency within its normal operating
range. This decision was based on the consideration that BESS frequency regulation should be
constantly active to give power stability under constantly ﬂuctuating PV generation.
Furthermore, a reduced number of occurrences of frequency exceeding normal operation range
results in fewer activations of emergency responses such as load shedding. This increases power
reliability and decreases overall system stresses and costs.
4.2.2
Steady state calibration
First, the system steady state was deﬁned independently of the BESS. The PV was run at a standard
constant irradiance of 1000 W/m2 and a constant load of 30 MW was connected. It was found that
the generator required some control over the mechanical power input, Pm, to achieve a system
steady state which could be maintained within normal operating frequency range of the Nordic
grid, [49.9 50.1] Hz. A low level PI control was implemented, taking the error between measured
and reference grid frequency, with the control output feeding straight into the mechanical power
input, Pm, for the generator torque. Since generators are not capable of as swift or precise load
following as inverter controlled technologies, the Pm PI controller was tuned such that it has limited
capabilities and only effectively maintains frequency within the Nordic grid normal operation range
under the conditions which were determined to be the system base case. This was done to increase
validity of a primarily synthetic model.

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Observations
6.1.1
Frequency regulation
These results support the theory that a grid connected BESS to a power system with large PV
penetration can provide effective frequency regulation services. As outlined in section 4.2, the
frequency control system was designed to maintain frequency within its normal operating range of
[49.9 50.1] Hz.
All simulated cases clearly depict BESS capability to provide more precise frequency regulation
than the modelled generator. Under the implemented BESS and active power frequency control, it
was found that the system at this sizing is capable of reliable FFR provision for load imbalances of
up to 21 MW, and effective but limited frequency regulation for load imbalances between 21 MW
and 23 MW.
In the case of irradiance dip to 500 W/m2 for 3 < t < 6.5 s, the frequency exceeds the limits for
normal operation frequency range in two instances. The ﬁrst instance sees frequency exceed upper
limit for 0.36 seconds, with a maximum of 50.187 Hz. The second occurrence exceeds the lower
limits for just 0.11 seconds with a minimum of 49.885 Hz. Despite frequency exceeding the nor-
mal operation range, in these instances frequency is still considered to be controlled since it able
to be regulated back to reference frequency again. As such, this case strengthens the argument for
BESS potential to provide ancillary frequency services to large scale PV integration.
It would be interesting to investigate system resize, and particularly battery resize, to accommo-
date larger load imbalances. It is noted that modelled battery size could be constantly increased to
accommodate larger load imbalances. However, it is assumed that at some point this growth would
no longer be optimal nor cost efﬁcient in a practical case.
In summary, the results conﬁrm the potential of BESS and its capabilities were clearly demon-
strated in the selected simulation cases. The following section discusses the signiﬁcance of these
results with respect to validating the control approach and highlighting the value of the model for
future projects. Following this, the issues undermining model validity are addressed.

==PAGE:58==
6.1.2
Method validation
The results in chapter 5 clearly show the BESS and control functioning as desired. Figures 5.1.6
- 5.1.8, 5.2.2 - 5.2.4, 5.3.4 - 5.3.6 and 5.3.10 - 5.3.12 show clear, appropriately scaled, charge and
discharge of the battery, following respective frequency disturbances. Furthermore, 5.2.3 directly
relates frequency drops with SOC switching from charge to discharge, while 5.3.7 highlights the
direct link between reduced PV generation and battery activity.
This gives validation to the modelling approach adopted in this thesis. Ultimately, the synthetic
nature of this model means the results can only be used to illustrate BESS potential for frequency
regulation and as a critical evaluation of this model methodology. However, the results fulﬁl both
these aims. The effective frequency regulation, as mentioned above, is a strong case for the po-
tential of grid connected BESS to provide cost effective FFR services to power grids with utility
scale PV systems. Furthermore, the effective battery function validates the approach of PI control
of frequency error for PWM generation to control battery charge and discharge switches in a bidi-
rectional converter, connected DC side.
The validation of BESS control approach makes this model a useful tool for future modelling
projects. It should be noted that if the model was to be implemented for future projects, it would
have real data and thus the system sizing would be determined and there would be much fewer
variables in the system sizing. The precise sizing of the battery would become a much more sig-
niﬁcant factor in system and model design.
In summary, this model holds great value as a useful, simplistic, and reliable way to model fu-
ture projects of grid connected BESS to systems with high PV penetration. However, this use is
dependent on appropriate future work to address the model uncertainties which arose.
6.2
Model uncertainties
6.2.1
Steady state calibration
An oversight in the development of the model was to calibrate the system steady state at constant
maximum irradiance of 1000 W/m2, which is largely considered to be the base case for research
into photovoltaic systems. It was designed that battery SOC should remain approximately constant
during steady state due to predetermined balance of the system. However, since steady state was
designed to be maximum PV generation, this leaves the only opportunity for BESS large scale
charging to be in cases of large load disconnections. For future implementations of the model, it
would be recommended to size the BESS such that the SOC is constant at an irradiance of 500 W/2
or at the average incoming irradiance for the speciﬁc plant site. This gives the BESS more mobility
in the system, to charge during maximum PV generation time and to be capable of discharge for
frequency regulation at even lower irradiance levels. This is an important and relatively simple
case for further study, and something to be considered if the model is used in future projects.
53
==PAGE:59==
6.2.2
Spontaneous oscillations
A signiﬁcant shortcoming of this model is the unexplained spontaneous oscillations exhibited at
times t = 2,4,8,16,32,64 s, and assumed to continue indeﬁnitely. This anomaly was investigated
and the cause was unable to be determined. It is thought that the most likely explanation is associ-
ated with the several PI controllers implemented in the model, for VSC control, generator control
and BESS control. It is possible that some improper tuning has created a miscommunication in the
feedback loops which occurs every 2x seconds. Another possible explanation is an inconsistency
in using the generator as a frequency measurement tool. If every 2x seconds, frequency is mis-
measured, that would affect the BESS PI controller to result in ﬂuctuating duty cycle signals, and
consequently, system ﬂuctuations.
It should be noted that this anomaly did not have a signiﬁcant effect on the conclusions drawn
in this thesis. Due to the short simulation times and the rapid nature of frequency control, few
occurrences of this instability impacted the results and those that did were identiﬁed during results
analysis. Further, it is considered unlikely that these oscillations would occur in a real system,
rather they are a shortcoming of the model. As such, they are not considered to undermine the
conclusion that BESS hold great potential for integration of large scale PV plants. However, this
shortcoming is still a signiﬁcant factor to have been identiﬁed in this thesis and should be further
investigated for future uses of the model.
6.2.3
Battery discharge current
The additional investigative case into battery discharge current limits revealed an impairment to
model validity. The resulting battery current appears to be limited to a discharge current of 6, 819
A, just 53% of nominal battery discharge current. From the results in section 5.4.2 showing a volt-
age of 4, 312 V in these conditions, this would impose a maximum discharge power of 29.4 MW,
rather than the designed rated output power of 50 MW.
Additionally, the system has proved capable of covering up to 23 MW loads, less than this apparent
discharge limit of 29.4 MW. Both these discrepancies indicate that there is a limiting factor present
in the model. The results in 5.4.2 suggest that the battery control system is still functioning as it
should. Consequently, it is suspected that there is a bottleneck occurring somewhere in the power
system, which is introducing a limiting factor to current ﬂow, causing much lower maximum dis-
charge currents than expected for the BESS. This is currently considered the largest shortcoming
of the model and further investigation is required in this area.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This master thesis aimed to review the potential for large scale, grid connected BESS to support
high penetrations of PV systems in the Norwegian regional grid. It has ﬁrst presented a compre-
hensive review of the literature and background information relevant to utility scale PV and BESS
projects and their role in the frequency regulation market. A model consisting of a 50 MW PV
plant and 50 MW/120 MWh Li-ion battery was developed to determine the potential of BESS to
provide frequency regulation services in this case. This model was based on synthetic data inputs
and simulated for a variety of cases to identify capabilities and limitations and the modelled BESS.
Several signiﬁcant conclusions can be drawn from the research presented.
Firstly, the hypothesis is supported that large scale BESS holds a high potential for enabling op-
timal integration of utility scale PV systems. The results clearly illustrate the capability of BESS
to control frequency to be regulated within normal operation range under a variety of simulation
conditions.
The system was demonstrated to maintain stable frequency under conditions of large PV generation
ﬂuctuations and up to load imbalances of 23 MW. This serves to limit the number of occurrences
the Nordic grid code emergency frequency response measures are required to be activated. As
such, it was concluded that the inclusion of large scale BESS provides heightened power reliabil-
ity and decreases system costs and stresses.
The implemented BESS control approach involved a PI based control of a bidirectional converter
charge and discharge switches, with the battery connected to the DC voltage bus. The BESS was
shown to effectively control battery charge and discharge as designed. This served to validate the
control approach, concluded to be a valuable, uncomplicated method which can be implemented
in future investigations in this scope.
However, there are certain factors undermining model validity which open a scope for further work
following the research in this thesis. The BESS was expected to provide up to 50 MW of output
power but has been found to be limited to 29.4 MW. It is expected that further investigation into
this discrepancy could increase maximum battery discharge power, enabling the system to regulate
frequency under much load imbalances.
55
==PAGE:61==
Another underlying issue with the model has been detected through the observation of spontaneous
oscillations in the systems. It is concluded that these oscillations do not undermine or discredit the
results or conclusions extracted from the model, however their cause should be determined before
the model is implemented for future cases.
Conclusively, this thesis has determined that large scale, grid connected BESS is a promising tool
to facilitate integration of utility scale PV systems and provide FFR services to the Norwegian re-
gional grid. It outlines a proposed modelling method to investigate system and BESS sizing which
is effective in predicting the maximum load imbalances the system can tolerate.
The validation of the model and results made in this thesis would allow it to contribute to dis-
cussions surrounding optimisation of utility scale PV integration and grid frequency regulation
services. As such, this model is considered a valuable research tool for future PV and BESS
projects. Coupled with real data inputs, it could effectively serve as a cheap, efﬁcient way to inves-
tigate system sizing and predict system frequency stability and power reliability. This convenient
and adaptable method of investigation is hoped to help facilitate higher levels of renewable energy
in the grid. The ﬁndings are thought to be particularly relevant in the current climate of the energy
transition, with rapid growth in both utility scale batteries and PV systems.

Chapter 8
Further work
This thesis is focused on an area of renewable energy integration which is currently in a period
of rapid growth. As such, a wide scope of further work could be considered to further this work
towards future projects. Crucially, the modelling uncertainties must be further researched. Con-
sidering the spontaneous oscillations, it is recommended to begin investigation with the multiple
PI controllers present in the model. This could be examined by removing some PI controllers or
closely investigating the effects of concurrently altering PI tuning parameters and observing the
effects.
A primary concern with use of this model moving forwards is the limiting factor on battery dis-
charge current. To validate the assumption of an electronic bottleneck, it is suggested to resize
the battery with the same nominal voltage 4 kV, but calculated rated capacity (Ah) such that nom-
inal discharge current is approximately equal to the current limiting current of 6, 819 A. If the
BESS produces the same results seen in this thesis up to a discharge current of 6, 819 A, this will
conﬁrm the hypothesis that the limiting factor is introduced in the external power system. This
is the expected outcome and would provide clear direction for further investigation to correct this
miscalculation. In the event that reduced battery rated capacity imposes a further, lower limit on
battery capacity, more in depth research will be required to resolve the issue.
As discussed, future study involving this model should recalibrate the steady state to a reduced,
average irradiance input for the PV arrays. This will increase battery mobility in the system and
allow for investigations into BESS ability to control sudden frequency increases in addition to the
decreases primarily examined in this thesis.
Some further interesting cases of research could also include connecting the battery to the AC side
of the grid. This would involve an additional inverter, transformer and altered, more complex con-
trol system. It would also be valuable to consider the other frequency control approach mentioned
in section 4.2. As explained, this operates by activating BESS frequency response only in the in-
stances when grid frequency exceeds certain frequency limits or activation values.
Finally, the synthetic nature of this model invites the more exciting case of modelling real systems
from data sets. This would enable results observation in more realistic cases, a more in-depth
discussion into BESS sizing, and would expand this project to its full potential.
