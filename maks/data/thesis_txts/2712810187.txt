Abstract
Pluvial floodings of cities are of great concern due to their enormous economic and social con-
sequences. The state-of-the-art hydrodynamic models to assess flooding and associated costs are
computationally demanding, which has given rise to simplified flood screening methods.
Previous studies suggest that a convolutional autoencoder (U-Net), which uses 2D topographic
inputs combined with rainfall statistics to estimate the 2D overland flood depths, can predict the
floodings related to direct runoff. Accordingly, this study will investigate the U-Net’s ability to
predict floodings from sewer surcharges. It will be investigated by stacking an additional spatial
input, to describe the location and magnitude of surcharge volumes. Several surcharge layers are
derived through simple raster calculations in GIS of resulting spilling volumes from 1D simulations
in Mike Urban+ (MU). The model is trained and evaluated against 1D/2D simulations from MU
for different Chicago design storms (CDS).
U-Net demonstrated the ability to predict surcharge-related flooding but with limited performance.
The predictions were most accurate for large rain events, and reached a highest Critical Success
Index (CSI) of 0.29 for flooded cells above 0.05m and 0.11 for flooded buildings when considering a
small (T9) and large rain event (T100).
A surcharge layer which routes the water volumes along the surface (SAmS) was found as the most
efficient way for the U-Net model to describe the surcharge flooding. The model was generally limited
by greatly underestimating the extent and depth of the flooding.
Forcing the model to predict larger water volumes did not increase the overall performance but
revealed that the model was limited by its sink representation inside the SAmS input, which,
especially for large sinks, could result in an unrealistic representation of the flow inside the sinks.
U-Net did not show any ability to generalise its learned features from one catchment into a new
catchment not considered in training, indicating different flow dynamics are present in the catch-
ments. It was suggested that differences in flow dynamics originate from general differences in the
slope of the catchments.
The model estimates can likely be improved through a different representation of the sinks inside
the input layers, including a different location of sinks inside the SAmS input. The differences in
flow dynamics of the catchments can potentially be explained by allowing SAmS to flow in multiple
directions.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
In Denmark, are increasing temperatures, as a consequence of global warming, expected to increase
the frequency and intensity of precipitation in the future (Olesen et al., 2014; IPCC, 2022). In-
creasing urbanisation is furthermore expected to expand the area of non-permeable surfaces and
effectively increase the runoff generation, applying larger pressure on existing water infrastructures
(McDonald et al., 2013; Kaspersen et al., 2017).
Various adaptation strategies are available to mitigate future flood risk in urban areas (Zhou et
al., 2013). Flood models are essential to evaluate the adaption methods to quantify their effects
on flooding depth, flood extent and related damages (Thrysøe et al., 2021). To estimate floodings
related to drainage overloads, have hydrodynamic 1D and 1D/2D models become state-of-the art.
A 1D hydrodynamic model estimates the unsteady flow inside the pipe network by solving the
finite difference solution of 1D Saint Venant equations to provide an accurate description of the
flow (DHI, 2017). In the 1D/2D models, the 1D drainage model is coupled to a 2D surface model,
allowing water to surcharge and spread onto the surface to estimate flooding depths.
It is a common practice to use flood models for strategic planning of different climate change
scenarios or city development scenarios to achieve robust adaptation solutions. This requires mul-
tiple flood estimations to evaluate different adaptation methods for each scenario and demands
fast and accurate flood models. While the hydrodynamic 1D/2D models are commonly known for
being accurate, they are limited by large computation times. Especially for larger areas and for
simulations of higher resolution, these models will not be suitable for multiple scenario planning.
For flood predictions in urban areas, will high-resolution data be needed to represent the flow along
complex structures and, e.g. distinguish between roads and buildings (Berkhahn et al., 2019).
This has given rise to several screening methods aiming to simplify the movement of water to achieve
faster flood estimations. The screening methods vary in complexity and computation time, making
them useful for different applications (DANVA, 2022). For initial screenings of an area, will terrain
models help give an overview while simple runoff models can help identify the most vulnerable
areas (Balstrøm et al., 2018; Scalgo, 2022; Jamali et al., 2019). For more detailed investigations,
can sewer surcharge volumes of 1D simulations be combined with simple flow estimations to predict
the floodings related to sewer surcharges (Jamali et al., 2018; Thrysøe et al., 2021). These screening
methods have shown to decrease the simulation time a lot compared to 1D/2D models and generally
achieve a solid performance for steep catchments or areas dominated by depressions while being
limited in areas of shallow water flow (Thrysøe et al., 2021; Jamali et al., 2018). Many approaches
do not consider time dynamically, which limits them from distinguishing between rainfall events of
the same depth being differently distributed (L¨owe et al., 2021), while considering time dynamically
has shown to reduce the computational speed (Guidolin et al., 2016).
In recent years, data-driven screening methods have arisen, which instead of estimating the water
movement along the surface, learn to translate a set of explanatory inputs into flooding depths
through a training phase. Even though the training phase can be computationally demanding,
are the prediction speed afterwards usually fast. Especially artificial neural networks (ANNs) have

received much attention due to their flexible and non-linear network structure, making them capable
of understanding complex behaviour. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) - a network structure
initially made for image recognition (Aggarwal, 2018) - have recently gained popularity for pluvial
flood modelling because of their abilities to capture complex spatial features of 2D grids.
A recent study showed that a convolutional autoencoder (U-Net) could deliver promising flood
estimates of direct 2D overland floodings by only considering topographical inputs and rainfall
characteristics as input (L¨owe et al., 2021). The study did not consider surcharge-related floodings
as a specific drainage capacity was assumed. This has given rise to the consideration: Is the U-Net
model also capable of predicting urban floodings related to sewer surcharges?
This study, therefore, proposes a U-Net model similar to L¨owe et al. (2021), but where an additional
input will be added to describe the surcharge flooding. Several surcharge inputs will be derived
through simple transformations of 1D simulation outputs from Mike Urban+ (MU). The study
explores which of the proposed surcharge inputs improves the flood predictions the most. The U-
Net model will be trained and evaluated against 1D/2D simulations from MU, where only flooding
related to sewer surcharges is considered, neglecting all floodings related to direct runoff. The
study furthermore aims to describe whether modifications of the objective function can improve
U-Net’s predictions and whether the model is applicable in situations where multiple catchments
are considered to evaluate whether a trained model can be generalised to new catchments.
The study objectives are:
• Which spatial transformation should be applied to point surcharge values to enable U-Net
predictions of flood extent?
• Can predictions of 2D flood depths be improved by modifying the objective function when
training the neural network?
• How does the consideration of different catchments in the training and testing phase affect
the neural network’s performance?

THEORY
2
Theory
This chapter will describe the theory used to develop the U-Net model. First, the fundamental
building blocks and the learning process of artificial neural networks (ANNs) will be described
before covering the network architectures of convolutional neural networks (CNN) and U-Net. The
chapter is not an exhaustive review of all techniques within the field of ANNs but will cover the
relevant theory to understand the U-Net model’s process and training. Popular ANN architectures
such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs), transformer models, and reinforcement learning are
omitted.
In the final part of the chapter, a description of existing flood screening tools will be made, covering
both simplified hydrological screening tools and data-driven models.
2.1
Artificial Neural Networks
2.1.1
Fundamentals
The ANN has in recent years proven to be an efficient tool in non-linear classification and regression
tasks. The networks have taken inspiration from the human brain and can be thought of as simplified
models of the process within the brain. In the brain, billions of neurons process and send information
between each other through electrical signals to give the complex behaviour of humans. Their
response is highly complex due to their constant adaptation to external stimuli (Aggarwal, 2018).

2.1.1.1
Neurons
Similarly to the brain, are ANNs developed as a network of neurons, but instead of processing
electrical signals between each other, can the neurons of ANNs be seen as mathematical functions,
transforming inputs to outputs. Each neuron is associated with its activation function to generate
its outputs. The activation of neurons is an important feature of the ANNs, as non-linear activation
functions enable the models to solve non-linear problems. The nodes can receive multiple inputs
from other neurons, which are then weighted and sent through an activation function before sending
it deeper into the network (see figure 2.1).
One popular activation function for multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) and CNNs is the rectified linear
unit (ReLU). The ReLU is expressed as shown in equation 1, and linearly returns the input value
for values larger than zero while returning zero for values smaller or equal to zero. The simple linear
behaviour of the function for values larger than zero has shown to increase the training speed of
networks and effectively avoid the problem of vanishing gradients because gradients will remain
proportional to their activation (Glorot et al., 2011). The ReLU has subsequently been further
developed into the leaky ReLU (equation 2), which allows the ReLU to generate small values for
negative inputs (see figure 2.2). This has shown to solve the so-called ”dying ReLU” problem when
too many activations become lower than 0 (e.g. due to unfortunate initialisation) and effectively
limiting the learning process of the network by staying inactive for any input (Lu et al., 2020).
The output of a neuron is estimated as the activation of the weighted sum of the inputs plus a bias
as shown in Figure 2.1. Adding biases is a simple way for the network to determine how easily a
neuron should be activated (Nielsen, 2015).

2.1.1.2
Layers
To structure the flow of information between neurons, it is common to place the neurons in parallel
layers and let the flow of data run from the input layer and through a number of hidden layers to
the output layer, which generates the final results of the model (Goodfellow et al., 2016).
The most common type of layer in ANNs is the dense layer (also called the fully-connected layer)
which is widely used in, e.g. feed forward neural networks (FFNNs) and at the end of a CNN to
convert extracted features into a classification. In the dense layer, all of the neurons within are fully
connected to all neurons of the preceding layer (see Figure 2.3). The first shallow layers of an ANN
will base its calculations on only a weighted sum of the input variables, resulting in simple feature
extraction, while more complex and abstract features based on previous features will be extracted
in the deeper layers (Nielsen, 2015).
Figure 2.3: Simple FFNN with 2 hidden layers. The hidden layers are dense as they are fully connected
to the their preceding layers.

The network will typically send data forward through the network to estimate the outputs, meaning
that weight-adjusting must be done backwards to ensure all weights are tuned in the direction of
the lowest error (Rojas, 1996). This is formally known as backpropagation.
The weights are initialised before training and updated through gradient descent by computing the
partial derivatives of the loss function for each weight of the network averaged over the training
data. The derivatives can be estimated analytically by repeatedly applying the chain rule through
the layers of the network, and the step of the gradient descent becomes:
Θnew = Θ −γ ∇L(Θ)
(5)
Where Θ and Θnew represent the set of parameters before and after the update, γ the learning rate
and ∇L(Θ) is the derivative of the loss function. A larger learning rate increases the step size of
the learning process, increasing the training speed, but too large values could potentially lead to
missing minimums.
A commonly used optimising algorithm is the ’Adam’ optimiser, which combines gradient descent
optimisation with a momentum algorithm to accelerate the optimisation.
The backpropagation is a computationally demanding calculation which quickly becomes insufficient
for larger network architectures and data sets. A common procedure has evolved to encounter the
problem, where training data is divided into smaller batches and fed into the network. The batches
will typically be normalised to ensure an even faster and more stable training. At the same time, are
the weights updated for each batch rather than for all training data at once to reduce the memory
requirement during training.
2.1.2.1
Overfitting
A common problem of training large and complex ANN is the issue of overfitting due to the great
flexibility of the networks. To overcome this, different regularisation techniques has evolved to reduce
overfitting. One approach is to split the training data into a training set and validation set to mimic
the test error on unseen data. As the training evolves, the training error typically decreases steadily
with time, while the validation error will reach a minimum before increasing again. Features are
extracted from the training data, which cannot be generalised when the validation error increases;
thus, the training will be stopped to avoid overfitting. This training method is a widely used
regularisation method within the field of deep learning called ’early stopping’ (Goodfellow et al.,
2016).
A different technique to avoid overfitting is by modifying the network through dropout. Dropout
is performed by reducing the network size by randomly deleting a given proportion of the neurons

random selection is made. The technique exploits that dropout heuristically creates a set of many
different networks, averaging the effect of each; the main idea being that networks will overfit in

2.1.3

Convolutional neural networks are a type of ANN especially designed for data having a grid-like
structure. Their ability to extract spatial features across multidimensional data to effectively de-

(Aggarwal, 2018).
The network differs from the FFNN by introducing convolutional operations in the network’s hidden
layers, which are spatially organised with sparse connections between the layers. The convolutional
operation is performed using various filters (also referred to as kernels), with each filter capturing
specific features of the grid.

2.1.3.1
Stride and padding
The padding and stride are two important properties to control the size of an extracted feature
layer. Stride defines the step size of the kernel as it moves across the grid and thereby controls the
resolution of the feature layer. A stride of one is most widely used, but larger values can be used
to reduce the memory of a network or reduce the overfitting of networks having unnecessarily high
resolutions (Goodfellow et al., 2016).
A convolution operation reduces the size of the extracted feature layer, which is not always wanted
due to information loss along the edges. To solve this issue, padding can be performed, adding
zero-cells along the edge of a map and allowing the kernel to step onto the edges. In Figure 2.4,
padding is performed to keep the size of the feature map equal to the input tensor.
2.1.3.2
Pooling
A way to compress feature maps without reducing the number of feature layers is by using a pooling
operation. Unlike a convolutional operation, does pooling not change the depth of the data (and
thereby the amount of features), but a filter is applied to all feature layers to compress the data
and reduce the grid size. The most common poolings are maximum pooling and average pooling,
which compute the applied filter’s maximum and average value. The filters can vary in size and
stride. It is common to not allow overlapping cells in the computation (e.g. by having a 2x2 size
filter with stride=2). However, overlapping has been shown in some cases to reduce the overfitting
(Goodfellow et al., 2016). The pooling operation is illustrated in Figure 2.5.

2.1.3.3
Memory advantages
The CNN architecture has some important advantages of sparse interactions and parameter sharing
(Goodfellow et al., 2016). Using kernels smaller than the grid size ensures a sparse interaction be-
tween inputs and outputs as fewer parameters are needed compared to having parameters connected
to each grid cell. This property reduces the memory requirement and thereby the training efficiency
of CNNs.
The parameter sharing refers to the properties of kernels, which are given one collective weight
instead of being weighted at each position. This makes CNNs more efficient than other ANNs
dominated by fully connected layers.
2.1.3.4

Even though CNNs have become the standard ANN for image classification, they still come short in
localising the predicted classes, where class labels are assigned to the grid cells. This is an essential
property of, e.g. biomedical image recognising tasks and made Ronneberger et al. (2015) propose the
U-Net architecture, which showed promising localisation abilities while also increasing segmentation
abilities for smaller data sets.

expansive path aims at learning how to reconstruct the image from the encoded presentation of the
data.

2.2.1
Terrain model
Using a terrain model as a screening method is the most simple and fast screening method available.
A terrain model is a digitised topographic map showing the elevation of an area. The model can
give a brief overview of an area’s primary flow paths and sinks, appointing areas where flooding
is expected. Estimations using the terrain model are very uncertain and should mainly be used in
areas dominated by sinks and only for preliminary investigations of an area. The terrain models
can be improved by modifying them to consider flow systems leading water from or to sinks or by
considering the duration of the water flow at the surface (DANVA, 2022).
2.2.2
Terrain model combined with simple runoff model
2.2.2.1
Models based on topographic depressions
Simple runoff models are a common tool used by local and regional planners (Balstrøm et al.,
2018). They aim to estimate flood-related consequences, but usually at a more coarse level and
using simpler hydrological properties than advanced hydrodynamic models. The simplified approach
typically has to make some essential assumptions, such as: Only considering direct surface runoff,
assuming all surface area is impermeable, or assuming runoff cannot leave the surface through any
drain or drainage. The governing principle behind the calculations is a breakdown of the topographic
elevation model into sinks, subcatchments and flow paths. The water is then moved downhill along
the flow paths to sinks and into sinks of other subcatchment when filled (Balstrøm et al., 2018). The
estimations are not dynamically modelled, meaning the duration of rainfall and flow time are not
considered. This model allows for different investigations of, e.g. how the impact is of rainfall events
of different return periods and gives a reasonable estimation of the relationship between catchment
runoff and sink volume.

of drainage capacities or accounting for infiltration. Not considering the drainage system will

2.2.2.2
Models based on cellular automata
Another type of simple runoff model which has been rising in recent years is the use of cellular
automata (CA)(Jamali et al., 2018)). This approach differs from the previously mentioned modelling
approaches by routing the water volumes between grid cells based on a set of transition rules,
where the state of the cell and its neighbouring cells are considered. This technique avoids the time-
consuming steps of estimating sinks and flow paths and moving water volumes between depressions.
Jamali et al. (2019) developed a CA-based model for fast flood predictions, where grid attributes
of DEM, cell height, boundary type and cell excess water volume were used to compute the state
of each cell inside the grid. Four simple transition rules were then used to spread the water one
iteration at a time, as illustrated in Figure 2.9, until convergence.

2.2.3
1D hydrodynamic sewer model
A 1D hydrodynamic sewer model is a physical model which estimates catchment runoff from
impervious areas as loading to the sewer network. The flow inside the sewer system is then hydrody-
namically modelled using Saint-Venant equations, and excess water is surcharged onto the surface.
The computation time depends on several factors such as the size of the area, the simulated time
step and the magnitude of the rainfall event. The model will typically be slower than simple runoff
models while being faster than 1D-2D models, where overland flow is also dynamically modelled.
This model will help answer where the water will stand on the surface and in which quantities to
localise the vulnerable areas. The model can be combined with simple terrain models to estimate
where the water will run and which sinks will be filled as a consequence. This was approached by
Jamali et al. (2018), who developed a rapid flood inundation model ’RUFIDAM’, which computes
inundation zones (IZs) and uses the elevation of the communication points to spread sewer surcharge
volumes between them (Figure 2.10).
Another example is the screening tool ’Floodstroem’ made by Thrysøe et al. (2021), which dynam-

2.3
Flood screening tools using deep learning techniques
Another method to overcome the slow hydrological models is using machine learning techniques such
as deep learning, which aims to map a set of explanatory variables to water depth predictions. The
models are characterised by a long training period to learn all relevant features but can afterwards
achieve fast flood estimations.
The models can be divided into two types of models depending on their generalisation objectives: 1)
Generalising boundary conditions, e.g. different rainfall events and 2) Generalising to new unseen
areas (Bentivoglio et al., 2021).
Berkhahn et al. (2019) developed a real-time water level prediction model using an ensemble ANN
which only considered rainfall intensities as input. This approach is an example of the first type of
model, as the model only learns to distinguish between different rainfall events for the considered
area.
The second model type can be achieved by including spatial characteristics as inputs. This was done
in L¨owe et al. (2021), where a U-Net architecture was used to predict pluvial flooding depths, using
spatial characteristics and rainfall attributes as input. The inclusion of spatial features enabled the
model to learn universal features of water flowing on the surface and could, therefore, successfully
generalise onto areas not included in the training phase. However, this approach has only been
tested in a limited amount of studies (Bentivoglio et al., 2021).
2.3.1
U-NET for pluvial flood modelling
Even though CNNs were developed specifically for image classification tasks, they have also shown
strong abilities to solve complex physics-related problems in the field of earth sciences (Reichstein
et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2018). Also in field of hydrology, have CNNs successfully been used for
a variety of purposes (Bentivoglio et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2020); Panahi et al., 2020). Despite
this, is the use of CNNs for pluvial flood modelling not an exploited field with only limited studies
(Guo et al., 2021).
L¨owe et al. (2021) proposed a U-Net architecture built on top of the work by Guo et al. (2021) and
exploits U-Nets benefits of recovering spatial information to predict pluvial flooding depths on a
2D grid. The study only considered flooding related to direct surface runoff by assuming specific
drainage capacity and a constant infiltration rate on pervious areas. Also, only historical rain events
were considered.
The floodings were modelled similarly to an image-to-image translation, where input images of
catchment characteristics and nine rainfall statistics were translated into flood maps. The considered
catchment characteristics were; flow direction, flow accumulation divided by slope, curvature and
sink depth. The framework proposed working on patches of 256x256 pixels instead of considering
large catchment areas, which effectively reduces the need for very deep network architectures (Guo
et al., 2021).

4.1.1
Topographic inputs
The topographic catchment inputs are hydrological properties related to the topography of the
catchment and become a more detailed way of describing the surface processes than using the
elevation data alone (L¨owe et al., 2021). An important property of the inputs is that they can
quickly be estimated using the DEM and simple raster calculations. This property ensures the
model easily can be transferred to new catchments. The chosen inputs are based on the analysis in
L¨owe et al. (2021), where eleven spatial inputs considered potentially relevant for the predictions
of pluvial flood modelling were investigated. Through a forward selection were, six spatial inputs
found most efficient.
This study will follow the procedure of L¨owe et al. (2021) and use the same input variables, except
for the imperviousness, as no overland runoff simulation is performed, and the pervious areas thereby
are ignored in the MU-simulations. The resulting five topographic inputs will always be a part of
the model as shown in Figure 4.1. The inputs are all estimated by simple raster calculations in
ArcGIS.
The 5 spatial input variables are:
1. Sine component of the aspect
2. Cosine component of the aspect
3. Curvature

4.1.3
Technical implementation
This section will describe the technical properties of the U-Net model illustrated in Figure 4.1 and
how the data is configured before being inserted into the model.
Network setup
The contracting path of the network consists of five downsampling steps, each consisting of two
repeated convolutions followed by a 2x2 average pooling with a stride of 2. For each downsampling
step, the amount of features is doubled. The expansive path behaves almost symmetrical to the
contracting path with two repeated transposed convolutions to halve the number of features,
followed by a 2x2 upsampling for each upsampling step. All upsampling steps have data from
the contracting path concatenated through skip connections, and to produce the outputs, was a
final 1x1 convolution performed. Leaky-ReLU with a slope of 0.2 for values below 0 is used as
activation function for all convolutions, and padding is added to keep the size of the feature layers.
Batches of 20 observations were used for each update of weights, and each batch was normalised to
have a mean around 0 and a standard deviation of 1.

All model inputs were transformed to reduce the skewness, which is generally right-skewed for the

information regarding the location nor volumes of the surcharges, but only the rain depth to

assigned, with all surrounding cells being zero.

4.3
Modifying objective function.
This section will describe the second investigation, where the objective function is first changed
from MSE to SSE where after a squared volume error is added and its weight increased.
Flood maps have a large proportion of zero-cells compared to flooded cells. Using objective functions
of MSE and SSE, which computes the cell-wise error between predicted and observed values, would
thus potentially lead to models being unlikely to estimate flooding. A model will, in most cases,
not be penalised for predicting values of zero, while non-zero estimations have to be very confident

to benefit. A specific parameter combination has to be found, which nearly always gives flooding;
otherwise, increasing the amount of flood estimates will also lead to an increased amount of false
predicted. This issue can prevent a model from predicting flooding estimates.
One solution to overcome this issue can be to adjust the objective function by accounting for the
volume error. Accounting for the volume error will force the model to predict more flooding to
compensate for the error related to predicting less than the observed. It is proposed to combine
SSE with a squared volume error (SVE) as shown:

The effect of including the volume error in the objective function will be tested by comparing the
predictions of different α-values using U-Net(SAmS). The chosen α-values are: 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3.
For previously converged models, the SVE has shown to be approximately a factor 3 larger than the

comparing the predictions to some of the input parameters of the U-Net.

4.5
Evaluation
It will now be described, how the U-Net models will be evaluated. All models will produce 2D flood
maps of the test data and evaluated against the 1D/2D estimates from MU.
To describe the flood hazards, will the flood extent be used as an indicator, while the critical
success index (CSI) of flooded cells and buildings respectively will evaluate the models’ accuracy in
describing flood exposure. Finally, will damage estimates related to flooded buildings be used as an
indicator for risk assessment, as these are used for calculating the expected annual damage (EAD)
of an area (Rasmussen, 2021). Neither of the metrics considers flooding depths below 0.05m. The
evaluation metrics will now be further described.
4.5.1
Flooded area
To describe the extent of the flooding, will the flooded area be used. The flooded area describes the
number of flooded cells with a maximum value above 0.05m at any point in time. The flooded area

is estimated by summing all flooded pixels and multiplying them by the cell area.
4.5.2
CSI of flooded cells
CSI describes the fraction of correctly identified cells compared to all observed cases (Bennett et al.,
2013), and is estimated in Equation 8 below:
CSI =
hits
hits + misses + alarms
(8)
Hits are here cells flooded in both the MU simulation and by the U-Net model, while misses describe
cells identified by MU but not by U-Net. Conversely describes the false alarms, the cells identified
by U-Net, but not by MU. A perfect classification achieves a value of 1 and goes towards zero when
the misses and false alarms increases.
The CSI can be estimated for different thresholds, where all hits, misses, and false alarms will only
be estimated above a specific flood depth threshold to describe the performance for different flood
depths. This study will use thresholds of 0.05 and 0.1 to evaluate the performance.
4.5.3
CSI of identified buildings
The CSI of identified buildings is calculated similarly as in Equation 8. The hits, misses, and false
alarms will here be represented by the identified buildings of U-Net compared to the identified
buildings of MU. Buildings are only counted as flooded if they have a flooded depth of 0.1m.
4.5.4
Damage
To estimate the vulnerability of an area, can damage estimation be used. Damage is estimated
from the amount of identified buildings multiplied by a flood-related damage. Damage estimates
are used to assess the expected annual damage (EAD) by integrating the damage over all of their
probabilities. EAD is of great interest to urban water planners and stakeholders because it is a
robust measure of how prone an area is to flooding (Olsen et al., 2015).

explains why the shallow water flows along, e.g. roads are not identified as sink values usually are
not present here.
Comparing the SAmS-input to the MU estimates reveals that the shallow water flows along roads
(light blue floodings in Figure B) are covered by the surcharge input (Figure F), but because flooding
is not present along all surcharge flows, it is difficult for the model to understand precisely at which
locations the shallow floodings will be placed.
The surcharge input has to be very large in general to estimate floodings, which explains the few
predictions of the 9-year event (Figure C), where surcharge accumulation generally is smaller. The
consequence of both events is that large amounts of water volumes are not estimated, resulting in
many missed flood cells, while a small number of cells are falsely predicted.
The placement of the sinks does not seem to be ideally explained by the surcharge input in the
Figures of E and F. The sink volumes should ideally be subtracted at the location of the floodings
in MU (Figure A and B), but especially for the large sinks, does the flow paths run through the
sinks to stop at the pour point at the very end of the sink, which can be placed far away from the
actual flooding. This behaviour could explain why the model has a hard time understanding where
in the sink the flooding should be located, as the input only explains where the pour point is, but
not where the flooding most likely is placed or any other information regarding the process within
the sink. The surcharge input, therefore, becomes less trustworthy for the model, which could be a
reason for the large underestimations.
Another issue could be the sink depth input (Figures E and F), which is not correlated with the
estimated flood volumes of MU (Figures A and B). This is shown by the flooding being placed at
smaller sink depths in some locations and larger sink depths in other locations. This relationship
could limit the understanding of how much water to leave at a sink.
Overall, the model inputs do not consistently explain the location nor extent of the flooding from
MU which seems to have reduced the performance abilities of the U-Net. A better combination of
inputs, which more consistently explain the MU floodings, would be needed to increase the accuracy
and predicted water volumes.

DISCUSSION
6
Discussion
This chapter will discuss the findings of section 5. Furthermore, will technical improvements be
suggested, and the study’s limitations are addressed. In the last section, an outlook will be made,
which will describe the future steps that can be taken.
6.1
Summary of results
Impact of different surcharge transformations on flood predictions
The U-Net model with rain depth input showed not to be efficient enough to get an understanding
of how the flooding was placed on the test data. This behaviour was expected, as the water will
only be placed in areas where water is surcharging, a piece of information the model does not have.
The surcharge inputs all improved the predictions of surcharge flooding compared to the rain
input. U-Net(N) showed decent abilities in describing the flooded sinks with surcharge inside but
limited from describing the movement of surcharge outside sinks. The U-Net(SA) and U-Net(SAmS)
estimates were relatively similar, but subtracting the sinks showed to improve the estimations of
the downstream flooding. This observation was confirmed by the scoring metrics, which suggested
that routing the surcharge volumes along the surface by the SAmS input is the most efficient way
for the U-Net to understand the dynamics of maximum flooding depths for larger rain events.
The U-Net(SAmS) predictions generally relied on large SAmS-values combined with sink and
FLOSLO values. The inputs did not ideally explain the sinks as the model had trouble explaining
sinks of large extents where the SAmS-input travelled long distances inside the sink. Also, the
shallow water flow along flow paths could not be predicted, though the SAmS input did represent
them.
Modifying objective function
Increasing the volume error inside the objective function increased the predicted flood volumes of
U-Net(SAmS) by putting more weight on the larger sink depths, but without increasing the overall
performance.
The new predictions revealed that the U-Net(SAmS) mainly has difficulties explaining the larger
sinks, where the surcharge flow inside the sink does not reflect the flow realistically and avoids the
deepest part of the sink.
Generalisation abilities
Training on multiple areas greatly reduced the test performance compared to only considering a
single area. A training patch of ˚Abyhøj revealed that U-Net was able to learn the dynamics of the
˚Abyhøj area but that the learned features could not be generalised, suggesting that different flow
dynamics are shown in the areas.
An investigation of the areas ˚Abyhøj and Bellinge found that the flooding of ˚Abyhøj has a higher
tendency to split into multiple flow paths, which the SAmS input cannot explain. The reason is
most likely the steeper slope of the ˚Abyhøj area.

6.2
Limitations
Rain data
The study only investigates the effects of CDS rain events evenly distributed in space to reflect a
planning process of an urban water system. The results, therefore, do not reflect the performance
of natural rain events or non-uniformly distributed rain data, which would be relevant for other
purposes (e.g., when used for warning systems). In this setting, however, is non-uniformly distributed
rain data not expected to be an issue, as the 1D simulation will be able to explain these spatial
differences.
Differences in surcharge estimates of 1D and 1D/2D simulations
The SAmS input is simplified by only being considered statically, meaning the surcharge intensities
and flow time along the surface are neglected. The temporal variations of the surcharges will affect
the flow patterns as water surcharging with high intensity in a short period would have different
dynamics compared to lower intensities of larger duration. A dynamic modeling approach could
be achieved by considering surcharge hydrographs as a dynamic input as done in (Thrysøe et al.,
2021).
Another limitation related to the estimation of surcharge volumes is the differences in cover type
between the 1D simulation producing the inputs and the 1D/2D simulation of the MU estimates.
The spilling cover type of the 1D simulation assumes that all water volumes surcharged onto the
surface will end up as flooding. In contrast, can the water be transferred back and forth between
sewer and surface in the 1D/2D simulation. This difference in cover type could potentially lead to
differences between the inputs’ volume estimates and the outputs’ volume estimates, as water from
1D/2D simulations can disappear at nodes and potentially appear in multiple surcharge floodings.
However, the spilling cover type of 1D simulations was proved in Jamali et al. (2018) to only have
minor variations of pipe water levels and flow through upstream pipes compared to similar 1D/2D
estimates of MIKE Flood, indicating that only small differences should be expected.
Comparison with existing screening tools
The results of this study have only been compared against the targeted results of 1D/2D MU
simulations. This could limit the perspective of the results as they ideally should be compared
against the results of other existing screening tools. The study of Rasmussen (2021) compared the
performance of Scalgo against 1D/2D simulations of MIKE Flood and thereby shared similarities
with this study. However, direct overland flooding was also considered in the 1D/2D simulations.
The study found a maximum CSI value of 0.3 for the identification of buildings with a general
overestimation of the number of buildings and the number of flooded sinks. However, a direct
comparison to the SCALGO model is challenging, as the setting of Rasmussen (2021) had large
water volumes originating from direct runoff. In contrast, this study only considers flooding related
to surcharges, which is expected to have a more complex behaviour than flooding from direct runoff.
Network properties
This study does not optimise for any hyper parameters of the U-Net model but has adapted the
architecture of L¨owe et al. (2021). More optimal parameters (such as network depth, base filter size
and kernel size) could potentially have been achieved for this study by optimising for these hyper

Furthermore, has the variability of the model convergence not been investigated as the models only
have been trained once. Different results could potentially have been observed if the models had

6.3
Technical improvements
SAmS input
An explanation for the poor identification and representation of larger sinks can be found in the
representation of sinks inside the SAmS layer. The sink volumes are subtracted at the pour points,
which for larger sinks results in surcharge flow travelling long distances inside the sink before
leaving the water volumes at the pour point at the end of the sink. This property is not a realistic
representation of the sinks, as the flow typically would move towards the deepest point after entering
the sink. A different approach would be to subtract the sink volume at the deepest point of the
sink or the centre of each subsink inside the larger sinks to give a more realistic representation of
the sinks.
The shallow water flows outside sinks were also in L¨owe et al. (2021) found difficult to explain for
the U-Net model. The SAmS layer was expected to improve these predictions because of SAmS’
more realistic representation of surcharge volumes along these flow paths but was generally found
inadequate. Only the U-Net(SAmS) using SSE (α=0) indicated a potential for explaining them.
A possible solution to explain the shallow water floodings could be to consider a spatial input
explaining the height above the nearest surcharge flow path to highlight the areas of high risk for
shallow flooding (Johnson et al., 2019).
Sink input
The sink input, which contains the sink depth, might not be the most efficient way of explaining
the water volumes inside each sink. Coupling the sink depths into flooding volumes could be too
challenging, as no correlation is shown between sink depth and the occurrence of flooding. To better
represent the relationship between sink and flood occurrence, could a sink layer containing the sink
volume for all pixels inside the sinks be included. This input would enhance the understanding of
how much water to place inside each sink.
Objective function
A possible way to avoid the predictions far away from surcharge flow for an increasing α-value,
could be by filtering out these areas and thereby forcing the model to only consider the relevant
areas near surcharges. This way, the model would potentially keep the understanding that flooding
only should be placed near the surcharge volumes. This filtering could be done in several ways, e.g.
by having a spatial kernel which would ensure that the volume error only would be estimated at a
specific distance around the surcharges.
Explaining flow dynamics of different catchments
The inputs should address the differences in flow dynamics due to differences in slope in order to
enable the generalisation of the model.
One way could be to add a topographic layer containing the general slope of the area, which could

enable the model to learn how this changes the behaviour of the surcharge flow.
A more direct way of informing U-Net about the differences in flow dynamics would be to consider
multiple flow directions inside the SAmS input. This change would let the surcharge accumulate in
multiple directions instead of the unidirectional approach, which has been considered so far. One
way could be to use the multiple flow direction(MFD) method by Qin et al. (2007), where flow
partition exponents are created for each downstream cell based on the local topographic conditions.
The method is also a recognised flow direction input of the flow accumulation tool of ArcGIS.
Data sampling
The data distribution is vastly imbalanced as most cells have a flood depth of 0. Such data could
contribute to the general underestimation of the predictions, as the model will have to be very
certain about the placement of flooding to be rewarded. It has been explored whether including
more flooded area increases the performance by including the values below 0.05m in the training
phase, but no notable improvements were shown (see Appendix E).
The sampling of patches during training could be limiting the learning, as only 20% of the patched
sample should be inside the training patches. The 20% criterion ensures a more diverse area
representation during the training. However, it also leads to many empty patches where flooding
is not present due to the small extent of the flooded area relative to the total area. To present the
model for more flooded pixels during training, could the criterion of keeping a sample be increased
from the 20% or changed to only keeping it if a specific fraction of the data inside the sample is
flooded.
Only considering flooded patches would still enable the model to learn when it should not predict
flooding, as the surcharge input will always explain the areas where a specific surcharge input does
not result in flooding. The data sampling would, on the other hand, not work for models that relate
rainfall statistics to flooding depth, as they need to be represented for areas where flooding is not
present to learn the relationship.
6.4
Outlook
Improving model
Further work should first focus on improving the model following the recommendations of sec-
tion 6.3, to reach a higher accuracy and more reliable damage estimates.
A natural course of action would be first to investigate how the model can be improved on a
single area before focusing on how the dynamics between areas can be described to improve the
generalisation abilities of the model.
Larger data set
Once the previously mentioned improvements have been made and the dynamics between areas
successfully can be described, should the training data set be extended to cover various areas.
Training on a more extensive and diverse data set would avoid overfitting to specific types of areas
and thereby make the model more robust and applicable for use in various areas.
Single hydrodynamic simulation output as input
If the proposed improvements of the model are not beneficial, could a new direction be investigated,

where a large single hydrodynamic 1D/2D simulation is made and added as an additional input to
the U-Net model to predict the flooding depths of rain events smaller than the simulated input. The
input could either be binary (1=flood, 0=no flood) or numerically by inputting the actual water
depths of the simulation. This approach would likely yield better predictions, as the model will now
have information about where the flood-prone areas are located.
The time benefit of the model would be reduced compared to the current modelling setup due
to the computation time of the single 1D/2D simulation. Therefore, such a model would mainly
be applicable for investigations where multiple simulations of different rain events are needed in
order to achieve a time benefit. The setup will here ensure that only a single hydrodynamic 1D/2D

Another approach would be to run the hydrodynamic 1D/2D simulations with a coarse resolution
to reduce their simulation times and then use the U-Net to convert the results into estimates of
higher resolution.
Coupling with UFLOOD
If a model of high performance is obtained, would a natural next step be to combine the model with
the one proposed by L¨owe et al. (2021) to consider flooding related to both direct runoff and sewer
surcharge. This should be done by including the imperviousness as a spatial input to take pervious
areas into account and by adding rainfall statistics at the bottleneck of the network to enable the
model to evolve the direct runoff for changing dynamics and intensities of the rainfall events. The
model should be trained against a full 1D-2D simulation where infiltration and 2D precipitation
onto the surface is included.
Emergency applications
Another prospect of the U-Net model is to be used for emergency applications such as cloud burst
warning systems. The U-Net model would be needed to run multiple scenarios to describe the
uncertainty related to rain forecasts. This model could be used for various purposes, such as warning
citizens to protect their properties in time or informing the emergency management agency to
prepare and coordinate their workforces. The model should only be trained on the area of concern
and calibrated with a detailed topography of the area and actual measurements of rain intensities
and water depths through, e.g. radar observations and rain gauges. This way, a specialised and
more realistic model for an area would be obtained.

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
The U-Net model of this study showed the ability to predict maximum flooding depths from sewer
surcharges in an urban setting, but with great limitations. The conclusions of the three main
investigations are outlined below:
A spatial transformation that distributes the water along the surface and accounts for sink volumes
(SAmS) was found as the surcharge input, which overall gave the most accurate flood estimates.
The main findings of the investigation were:
• For smaller rain events dominated by local surcharge flooding of sinks, gave a single point
input (N) the most accurate representation.
• For larger rain events, where larger water volumes are traveling along flow paths, showed the
SAmS input to represent the flooding most accurately, reaching a better cell-wise accuracy
and identification of flooded buildings.
• The U-Net(SAmS) was limited by the location of sinks inside the SAmS input and the non-
linear relationship between sink depth and flood occurrence, resulting in a general underesti-
mation of the flood extent.
Modifying the objective function to account for volume error did not improve the overall accuracy
of the predictions. The main findings were:
• Increasing the volume error of the objective function showed to increase the extent of the
U-Net predictions by mainly putting more weight onto the large sink depth values.
• The increased extent of the predictions increased the amount of correctly and falsely identified
cells, the latter including sinks far away from any surcharge volumes, resulting in no overall
benefit.
• The additional flood volumes of the predictions revealed that specifically, the large sinks, where
the SAmS input does not run past the deeper parts, limit the U-Net from understanding the
placement of the flooding inside the sink.
The U-Net(SAmS) did not show generalization abilities as it could not explain the differences in
flow dynamics between the catchments. The main findings are expressed below:
• The U-Net model showed a great decline in performance on the Bellinge catchment when the
areas of ˚Abyhøj and ˚Arslev were added alongside Bellinge in the training phase.
• The worst performance was found when only ˚Abyhøj and ˚Arslev were considered in the
training phase to predict the flooding of Bellinge and showed that a trained U-Net model
could not be transferred to a new catchment.
• The differences in flow dynamics between the areas were investigated and suggested that
a larger slope of the ˚Abyhøj catchment caused the flow of water to split into multiple

directions, giving a different relationship between the SAmS input and flooding compared
to the catchment of Bellinge.
Several improvements have been suggested, which include:
• Changing the representation of the sink inside the SAmS input, e.g., by subtracting the sink
volumes at the center of the subsinks, instead of at the pour point, to more realistically explain
the flow inside larger sinks.
• Applying a spatial kernel to filter out values not near any surcharge volumes when increasing
the volume error to avoid unreasonable sink estimations far away from any surcharge volumes.
• Allowing SAmS to move into multiple directions, to explain the different dynamics between
catchments due to differences in slope.

CREATION OF SAMS INPUT
A
Creation of SAmS input
This section will describe how the SAmS input is derived using the ArcPy package of Python.
First, the sink and surcharge volumes will be estimated as grid values located at the pour point and
manhole, respectively, after which an algorithm is made to estimate the flow between each sink.
A.1
Estimation of sink and surcharge volumes
A raster of surcharge volumes is obtained by rasterising the shapefile containing the surcharge
spilling volumes from the preprocessing of the data.
Sink volumes are estimated and attached at the cell of the pour point, following the steps:
1. Find the sink volume of each cell by subtracting the DEM from a filled DEM and multiplying
it with the cell area.
2. Make a sink layer by using the ’cutfill’ operation and convert it into a polygon layer (remember
to delete background polygon to only get the sinks)
3. Use ’Zonal statistics’ to sum the volumes inside each polygon and join the sink volumes to
the attribute table of the sink layer
4. Estimate the maximum flow accumulation using the ’Zonal statistics.’
5. Create the pour points ’point’-layer by finding the place where the flow accumulation is equal
to the maximum flow accumulation inside each sink
6. Convert them into raster and use a spatial join to attach the sink volumes
7. Find duplicate pour points using the ’DeleteIdentical’ tool in the data management and then
delete them using the ’DeleteIdentical’ tool
8. Snap the pour points onto the highest flow accumulation nearby to ensure it is placed on the
largest flow accumulation
A.2
Algorithm to estimate SAmS
Initial step
First, the surcharge volumes are accumulated (similar to the SA layer). The sink volumes are then
accumulated and subtracted from the surcharge flow accumulation. The resulting layer is a layer
where all sink volumes have been subtracted. In areas where the sink volume is larger than the
incoming surcharge volume has, a negative sink volume been accumulated downstream. To avoid
this, the ’negative flow’ will be added again.
Algorithm
Adding all the negative flow back at once will give all upstream sinks the correct representation,
while downstream sinks will have upstream flow coming into the sink, which needs to be accounted
for if more space is available in the sink.
A1

The algorithm will, therefore, continue subtracting sink volumes (which are not full but have water
flowing out) from the flow, to then add the negative flow again. After each iteration (where the flow
has been subtracted and added) will solve the representation for all upstream sinks, as they are not
affected by additional flow arriving from upstream, which cannot be accounted for downstream.
The algorithm will continue to ”walk” downstream to solve the sinks’ representation until all sinks
have the correct volume subtracted. The resulting flow will here be the SAmS input.
