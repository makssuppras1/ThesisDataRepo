grating the latest technology advances in order to achieve smart manufacturing.
In this report, through literature review and research, the concept of Digital twin, along with

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
The origin of Industry 4.0 goes back to 2011, when the German government introduced
this concept for the development of the industrial sector [1] by integrating cyber-physical
systems (CPS) [2], the Internet of things (IoT) [3], cloud and cognitive computing [4] [5], big
data [6] and Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) [7] [8]. A new concept that supports these initiatives
and, therefore, emerges from the fourth industrial revolution, is Digital Twin (DT). This
consists of a physical asset, its digital representation, and the interconnection between them [9].
It is said that the Digital Twin is the new trend in simulation, modeling and optimization
technology [10]. Based on that, questions like "Why simulation is not enough?" or "Why
should we move forward to Digital Twin?" are arising.
1.2
Project Description
The aim of this project is to provide a clear view of the Digital Twin concept and, present its
simulation aspect and the beneﬁts of Digital Twin over the simulation.
An extended literature review and research are carried out in order to explain what is
Digital Twin, where it is applied and what are the beneﬁts, as well as its limitations. Further-
more, a review over simulation, its types and applications, beneﬁts, and limitations is provided.
Thereafter, an analysis is done to show the simulation aspect of Digital Twin and the integration
of simulation throughout Digital Twin.

1. Introduction
1.3
Research question
For the purpose of this project, the research question and sub-questions should be answered
until the completion of the report:
"How can Digital Twin be used in production?"
• "What literature exists for Digital Twin?"
• "What value does Digital Twin add to the production?"
• "Which is the link between Digital Twin and simulation?"
1.4
Delimitation of the project
For this project, the employed database is the DTU e-library - DTU Findit [11] -. Therefore,
literature research was limited to this speciﬁc library. The use of more databases would
broaden the chances of more ﬁndings, but this would compromise the liability and quality of
the literature, so only this one was employed for the thesis.
Additionally, Digital Twin is a new concept still in research, so the ﬁndings were restricted in
comparison with other topics and the literature that was used is relatively new and still to be
developed.
Finally, during the research, there were no ﬁndings for Digital Twin with agent-based simula-
tion, and for Digital Twin with system dynamics. Thus, when the simulation is referred to
Digital Twin, it is meant for discrete event simulation (DES) [12] [13].
1.5
Methodology
The methodology that was chosen to conduct this project was the collection of qualitative
data and literature research. The database that was used for the retrieval of the literature
(articles, papers and book chapters) is DTU Findit. The publications related to Digital Twin

2
LITERATURE REVIEW
The aim of this chapter is to provide a thorough literature review of Digital Twin and an
overview of simulation.
Section 2.1 presents the origin and deﬁnitions given to Digital Twin by the years, the ﬁelds
where it has been applied until now, the value that it adds to production and manufacturing,
and ﬁnally, its limitations. Then, section 2.2 provides the history and deﬁnition of simulation,
its types, its applications, the beneﬁts it has oﬀered to the industrial ﬁeld and its limitations.
2.1
Digital Twin
2.1.1
Deﬁnition
The origin of Digital Twin dates back to 2002 when M.Grieves introduced the concept in the
context of the formation of a product lifecycle management (PLM) center [14]. Then, Shafto
and his colleagues described Digital Twin as a system that "mirrors the life of its ﬂying twin"
[15] [16]. In 2012, the term was presented in the road-map of NASA for the aerospace ﬁeld by
Glaessgen [17]. Since then, the interest around Digital Twin started to grow radically and
many deﬁnitions were given by the years.
Reifsnider and Majumdar describe it as a kind of "ultra-high ﬁdelity simulation [18] that
operates in the cloud platform and simulates the health condition with an integrated knowledge
from both data-driven analytical algorithms, as well as other available physical knowledge [19]".
6
==PAGE:17==
2. Literature Review
Similarly, Boschert and Rosen argue that Digital Twin is the next level of simulation [20] and
that it links only the relevant data and the models [21] [22]. Later on, Grieves, after studying
methods of fault prediction for web system, applying and testing them [20], re-deﬁnes the
term as "a set of virtual information constructs that fully describes a potential or actual
physical manufactured product from the micro atomic level to the macro geometrical level" [23].
In the manufacturing domain, Negri et al. deﬁne Digital Twin as a simulation itself "The
Digital Twin is meant as the virtual and computerized counterpart of a physical system that
can be used to simulate it for various purposes, exploiting a real-time synchronization of the
sensed data coming from the ﬁeld." [8], while Kunath and Winkler say that "Digital Twin of a
physical object is the sum of all logically related data, i.e. engineering data and operational
data, represented by a semantic data model [24]". Moreover, Tao et al. propose the concept
of Digital Twin shop-ﬂoor (DTS), as an extension to the Digital Twin concept, where every
physical element of a production shop-ﬂoor has its digital mirror [18].
Therefore, many diﬀerent deﬁnitions were given during the years for the concept of Digital
Twin, from many diﬀerent authors, apart from the ones stated above. However, none of them
was established due to the diﬀerent ﬁelds that Digital Twin is applied.
Furthermore, related terms with Digital Twin, i.e. Digital Model and Digital Shadow, have
been employed and sometimes deﬁned as equivalent to it, although the terms are diﬀerent,
depending on the level of integration. Digital Model is the digital representation of a physical
product, where the ﬂow of data is manually generated, without any automated ﬂow. Digital
Shadow is the digital representation of a physical product, where there is a one-way automated
ﬂow of data. In contrast, Digital Twin has a both-way automated data ﬂow [25].
A table with the most important deﬁnitions of Digital Twin that were given chronologically is
depicted below.

2. Literature Review
2.1.2
Composition of Digital Twin
For Rosen and the colleagues, Digital Twin is not only a collection of digital artifacts. They
argue that Digital Twin is structured and all its elements are connected with each other [10].
Therefore, Digital Twin consists of a physical model, a virtual model and the interconnections
between them [9].

attempts are made to apply the Digital Twin in order to support decision-making in asset
life-cycle management [67] [45]. Finally, Digital Twin has also been applied in the automotive
[46] [68] and wind turbine power plant industry [46].

Beneﬁts
Many use cases and applications of Digital Twin were made since the concept was ﬁrst
presented. Results of these applications show that Digital Twin actually adds value to the
manufacturing ﬁeld, providing signiﬁcant opportunities and beneﬁts.
The use of Digital Twin in production proved to improve the performances of the overall
system [44] [24] and optimize the whole production line [51] [49]. It speeds up the operations
since there are no delays due to the lack of knowledge [44]; hence, reducing the set-up and
cycle-times [71] along with the production waste rate [44]. Additionally, it oﬀers a full view
and transparent monitoring of the whole production line [51]. Since Digital Twin can ensure
reliable and more accurate simulation results, the product behavior can not only be described,
but also forecasted. This means that in the design phase, the designers can predict the product
behavior, ﬁnd the possible inconsistencies and conﬂicts through the virtual veriﬁcation (even
before the actual manufacturing process) and act upon without having to wait until the
product is produced [9] [18].
Furthermore, due to the connection that Digital Twin provides, designers can connect with
suppliers and the rest of shareholders, improving the knowledge sharing and leading to more
eﬃcient and better performance [72]. From the human point of view, a big advantage that
Digital Twin has, is that it oﬀers remote use of the human interaction due to the employed

Challenges and Limitations
Based on the literature and implemented cases for Digital Twin, some challenges and limita-

that implement in practice a Digital Twin are limited [73] [74] [70] [75]. This makes it hard

Another challenge for the implementation of Digital Twin is that it requires a big investment
in order to build a smart production( highly automated) [51] [71]. This aﬀects more the small
and medium-sized enterprises, since they have a low degree of automation and their funds are
limited [70] [55].
Furthermore, issues like how to combine product life-cycle management, manufacturing,
execution system and operations management systems [51] are still unsolved since there is not
a standard process for utilizing it across the company [24]. The construction of high-ﬁdelity
models, the incorporation of big data analytics into the models and real-time synchronization
are more challenges to be taken into consideration [51]. This means that the hardware and
software for the full implementation of Digital Twin are currently non-existent [71].
For example, in the injection molding industry, obtaining data manually, implementing sensors
in the injection molding machines, developing real-time inspection systems, and optimizing
and predicting analytics models are unsolved problems [46].
Lastly, there are concerns regarding the data security against mischievous attacks [18] [24].

Simulation
Since Digital Twin is referred to be "the next wave in simulation" [10], a concept that is
built based on simulation methods among other things, an introduction to the simulation will
follow, in order to give a better understanding and, later on, a comprehensive analysis of the
simulation aspect of Digital Twin is provided in Chapter 3.
2.2.1
Origins of simulation
The origin of simulation dates back in 1977 when the Monte Carlo technique [77] was intro-
duced by Buﬀon with the "needle experiment" [78]. However, no earlier than the mid-1940’s,
simulation started to be used more extensively, due to the construction of the ﬁrst general-
purpose electronic computer and the use of Monte Carlo technique on electronic computers
by Ulam and Neuman. Following, in the 1950’s the ﬁeld of simulation grew even more, when
the availability of the electronic computers increased signiﬁcantly. Since then, advances in
simulation and modeling made simulation an important, powerful and well-established tool in
science and engineering [79].
2.2.2
Deﬁnition
Simulation can be deﬁned as the process of designing a model of a system and conducting
experiments with this model [80]. The purpose of simulation is to analyze, understand and
describe behaviors of the system and evaluate various alternative actions through experiments
[80] [81] [82] [83]. Simulation is performed on a digital computer [84] [83] and can be applied
to any system that can be modeled [83]. Hence, it is a powerful tool for decision-making and
problem-solving, based on the results given from the simulation [85] [81] [82] [80].
In the content of simulation, model is deﬁned as "a representation of a group of objects or
ideas in another form than the entity itself", while system is deﬁned as "a group of related
elements that cooperate to accomplish a stated objective" [80].

2.2.3
Simulation process
In this section, the simulation process of a certain system is brieﬂy described in steps [80] [81]:
1. Problem deﬁnition: The problem is stated, and the goals and objectives of the study
are deﬁned.
2. Project planning: The whole process is planned. The right hardware and software, the
staﬀand management and, in general, all the resources needed to conduct the simulation
are chosen.
3. System deﬁnition: The limits of the system are set and the function of the system is
investigated.
4. Conceptual model formulation: A conceptual model, either graphically or in a pseudo-
code form, is developed in order to deﬁne the components and the overall structure of
the system.
5. Preliminary experimental design: The measures of eﬀectiveness as well as the factors
and the levels of these factors are selected.
6. Input data preparation: The data required for the model is collected.
7. Model translation: The conceptual model is coded and a computer-based operational
model is developed in the right simulation language.
8. Veriﬁcation and validation: The model is veriﬁed that works properly, and that the
generated results represent the output of the real system.
9. Final experimental design: The experiment that produces the desired information is
built, and the decision of how every test will be executed regarding the length and the
number of the runs is made.
10. Experimentation: The simulation runs in order to produce the desired information, and
then, a sensitivity analysis is performed.
11. Analysis and interpretation: The conclusions are made based on the data that are
produced by the simulation.
12. Implementation and documentation: The ﬁnal results are reported and used. The model
and its usage are documented.

2.2.4
Simulation methods
In this section, the three main simulation methods are described: discrete event simulation,
system dynamics and agent based simulation. As mentioned in section 1.4, for the analysis
of the simulation aspect of Digital Twin, simulation is meant only for discrete event simulation.
2.2.4.1 Discrete event simulation (DES)
The discrete event simulation method was introduced in 1961 by Gordon, along with the ﬁrst
general purpose simulation system (GPSS) [86], which was the ﬁrst version of discrete event
modeling. It has been mostly used in operations research (OR) for at least the last forty years
[87].
Time and state are the two concepts that are of great importance for discrete event simulation
[85]. This method shows the chain of every event at a speciﬁc point of change level that
happens in the system in dinstict time [88], using top-down approach and stochastic method
(diﬀerent results on diﬀerent runs). The entities show a series of activities, as well as how
much time the transition from one state to another takes [87]. In the content of discrete event
simulation, entities are objects that constitute people, parts, tasks, machines, etc., and move
through the ﬂowchart blocks [89]. The entities and events that occur to the real system are
represented by animations and graphics [87].
The purpose of this method is to help the modeler ﬁnd the best possible solution for the
stated problem, in order to increase eﬃciency. Hence, DES is mainly used for prediction,
comparison, decision-making and optimization of the real system, based on the outputs of the
simulation. However, in order to obtain the most accurate possible results, a large amount of nu-
merical data is required. Moreover, the method is used at an operational and tactical level [87].
Discrete event simulation is a suitable method for the modeler to use, as it is easy to under-
stand due to the animation and graphics, and straight forward to build once the problem is
clearly deﬁned. Nevertheless, it is not suitable for analyzing model related to human behavior,
as it focuses mainly in the process of the system [87].
Applications of discrete event simulation are mainly in the ﬁelds of manufacturing, service,
logistics, business process, etc. [89]

2.2.4.2 System dynamics (SD)
The system dynamics method was introduced in the 1950’s by J. Forrester, who deﬁned the
method as "the study of information feedback characteristics of industrial activity, to show
how organizational structure, ampliﬁcation in policies and time delays in decisions and actions
interact to inﬂuence the success of the enterprise" [90] [91].
In this method, the real system processes are constituted by stocks, which are the people,
material, knowledge, etc., the ﬂows between them, and the information that deﬁnes the ﬂows’
values [89]. System dynamics consist of two methods, qualitative and quantitative, which are
employed in order to understand the system and identify the problem or potential problem.
Furthermore, system dynamics is a deterministic approach, meaning that at every simulation
run, the results are the same, which focuses on the feedback loop in order to identify why
some events occur, and it is used at the strategic level [87].
The purpose of this method is to give an overall view of the current system, concentrating
on the policy-making. It is employed to give a better understanding of complex systems, in
order to improve inconsistencies. However, since the modeler needs to understand and identify
the problem, if the problem is not clearly stated, it is possible that the implementation of
system dynamics will fail. Moreover, it can be complicated for the modeler to use when it
comes to big systems, since it requires much time and consists of a lot of complex scenarios [87].
2.2.4.3 Agent based simulation (ABS)
Agent based simulation method was introduced in the early 1990’s, but it was not broadly
used until 2000 [87]. It is a relatively new method in operational research [12] and, until now,
there is not a speciﬁc accepted deﬁnition in this area [89].
In this method, agents are used to represent the scenarios happening in the system [87]. These
individual agents follow a sequence of rules that are predeﬁned by the modeler, in order
to reach their goals, while there are interactions between themselves and the environment
[12]. Agent based simulation models are decentralized [89] and the concept of queues is non
existent [12], in contrast with the other methods. Also, there is no global system behavior.
The global behavior arises by the sum of the behaviors that are deﬁned to the agents in an
22
==PAGE:33==
2. Literature Review
individual level by the modeler [89]. Furthermore, it is a stochastic and bottom-up modeling
approach, since the main requirement is the identiﬁcation of the agent’s characteristics and
the interactions between them [12] [87].
In the content of agent based simulation, agents are groups of diﬀerent things, such as people
in the crowd, etc. [12]
The purpose of this method is to describe real-world situations and show the uprising event
that occurs by the interaction of the individual agents [92]. Although agent based simulation is
ﬂexible to use, it requires a lot of time to build and run the simulation model and, additionally,
in case of a big system, it requires high-skill computation from the modeler to build it [12]
[87].

2.2.6
Beneﬁts
Undoubtedly, simulation is high beneﬁcial for the ﬁelds it is employed. It is easy to understand
and an easy way to explain actions based on simulation to managers or customers, especially
due to the fact that it makes possible to visualize the system. It is a reliable method to use
because it uses less assumptions to simulate the system. It gives the opportunity to test the
system without spending resources to implement it. It also gives the chance to explore the
alternatives without stopping the operations of the system, and to understand how the system
works and why some situation occurs. Due to that, the identiﬁcation of constraints,bottlenecks
and potential problems are feasible. Furthermore, the modeler is able to control the time
during the simulation by speeding up or slowing down the processes, in order to investigate
and have a better understanding of the system [93] [80].
2.2.7
Challenges and Limitations
Simulation, despite all the beneﬁts, it comes with some limitations.
Firstly, simulation
modeling highly depends on the skills of the modeler and requires training. Even though, some
diﬀerent simulation models are built for the same system and are similar, it is improbable that
they are the same [93] [80]. Additionally, some models may be time consuming and expensive
to build. However, simulation modeling due to the technological advances, is becoming faster
as the time passes. Another limitation is that sometimes simulation results are not that easy
to translate, depending on the high complexity of the model. Finally, simulation is a tool to
test, understand and investigate the system, it provides insights for decision-making, but it is
not responsible for taking the right decision. It can diagnose problems, but it doesn’t solve
them. This is up to the human side, the modeler or the ones that want to use the model [93]
[80].

ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION
In this chapter, the simulation aspect of Digital Twin is discussed based on the literature
retrieved and it becomes clear what is the diﬀerence between Digital Twin and the conventional
simulation. Moreover, the value added from Digital Twin to the conventional simulation and,
consequently to production, is provided.
3.1
Simulation Aspect of Digital Twin
Simulation, as mentioned in Chapter 2, dates back a long time ago and in the last years it has
been widely used in the industrial society. However, with the arrival of the fourth industrial
revolution, known as Industry 4.0, a new concept emerged, which is described as the new wave
in simulation and modeling by Rosen et al. [10]. Digital Twin is deﬁned by some authors as a
simulation itself, while for others, it is a model that diﬀerent simulation models can emerge
from [24].
According to Zhang et al. [51], the conventional simulation has been employed, so far, in
order to provide premium quality in the ﬁnal product. Similarly, Rosen et al. argue that the
simulation has been used to forecast the state of the product in order to support operations
and planning [10].
Additionally, simulation is based on the historical data that are given as input to the model,
without including the current state of the product [46].
On the other hand, Digital Twin has its own structure [22]. It connects engineering data,
operational data, and behaviors that describe the system through real-time simulation, which is
enabled by the key technologies that were described in section 2.1.2.4 [22]. Hence, Digital Twin
is a combination of real-time data and simulation models [10] [22] [95]. These simulation models
are ﬁrstly developed manually, while they are integrated later on by a model management
system that chooses each time which is the right simulation model to be employed, depending
on the problematic situation [22].
So, as many authors support, Digital Twin cannot be limited to be just simulation, but it

originates from simulation and, with all the key enabling technologies, it builds a new concept
[96].
3.2
Value added over simulation
This new concept of Digital Twin creates value in production. The beneﬁts of Digital Twin
are discussed in section 2.1.3. Moreover, Digital Twin adds value over simulation.
Firstly, Digital Twin, using real-time data combined with the enabling technologies, reduces
signiﬁcantly the calculation time that a conventional simulation would run, providing the
possibility of researching an optimum solution to the current problems of the system [51].
Additionally, it gives the opportunity to reuse the simulation models with real-data input
and not only historical data or estimations [97] [98]. Consequently, it does not only describes
the behavior of the product or production line but also acquires solutions accurately [22].
Furthermore, simulation is not only employed at the design and planning phase, as it used to
do before the emergence of Digital Twin but also during other phases of the product’s life
cycle, in order to diagnose and optimize it [10].
To put it in other simpler words, while the conventional simulation can forecast what can
happen, Digital Twin can show what is really happening in the current system.

This thesis has investigated the concept of Digital Twin and analyzed its simulation aspect.
The deﬁnitions, its composition and the key enabling technologies of Digital Twin are dis-
cussed. Then, the application ﬁelds of Digital Twin are given alongside its application in
small, medium and large companies. Finally, the beneﬁts and the challenges of Digital Twin

– Fault diagnosis
– Operations and predictive maintenance
– Control and optimization
– Product design
– Production planning
– Corrective maintenance
– Decision-making support
