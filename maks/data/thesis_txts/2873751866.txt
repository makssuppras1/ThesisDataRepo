Abstract
Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) systems improve diabetes care but generate sub-
stantial waste, particularly from disposable applicators. This thesis explores how a CGM
device can be designed to significantly reduce environmental impact while maintaining
usability and creating strategic value for manufacturers.
The study combined market review, stakeholder interviews, and reverse engineering of
existing devices to identify functional, regulatory, and environmental requirements. En-
vironmental performance was assessed through a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) in ac-
cordance with ISO 14040/44, revealing that plastic production dominates the footprint.
Design exploration focused on integrating reusable components without compromising
user experience.
Early-stage user testing compared disposable and reusable applicator prototypes, high-
lighting that reusable solutions are feasible if interaction is intuitive and hygiene concerns
are addressed. The final design direction — a modular reusable applicator with a dispos-
able needle module — offers at least 50% CO2e reduction per use compared to leading
disposable designs.
The work demonstrates that combining rigorous environmental assessment with human-
centred design can deliver CGM applicators that are both sustainable and commercially
viable, supporting the medical sector’s transition toward circularity.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement and Background Motivation
Diabetes is a rapidly growing global health concern. According to the International Dia-
betes Federation, in 2024, it is estimated that 589 million adults aged 20-79 were living
with diabetes, among whom over 9.5 million people had Type 1 diabetes. The total number
of people living with diabetes is projected to reach 853 million by 2050. [1]. In Denmark
alone, over 366,000 individuals, or roughly 6% of the population, are diagnosed with the
disease, including approximately 35,000 with Type 1 diabetes [2].
Effective glucose monitoring plays a vital role in managing diabetes and reducing the risk
of long-term complications. Historically, blood glucose has been measured using man-
ual glucometers, which entered the healthcare scene in the 1970s, requiring finger-prick
blood samples and offering only momentary data points. In recent years, however, con-
tinuous glucose monitoring (CGM) systems have gained popularity due to their ability to
automatically collect real-time glucose data, detect trends, and provide short-term pre-
dictions. Clinical studies have demonstrated that CGMs significantly improve glycemic
control, reduce hypoglycemia incidence, and lower rates of hospitalizations and emer-
gency interventions [3].
Despite these benefits, CGM systems pose increasing environmental concerns. Most
commercial models consist of two primary disposable components: a single-use appli-
cator and a short-term sensor with a lifespan of 7-14 days. Over a year, a typical user
may dispose of 26-52 such units. These devices combine plastics, metals, electronic
components, and batteries, often impossible to separate, rendering them non-recyclable.
Moreover, for safety reasons, manufacturers typically advise users against attempting to
disassemble and recycle them [4]. The environmental burden is significant: over 196 mil-
lion disposable CGM units are discarded annually [5]. Globally, this aligns with broader
concerns about single-use products in healthcare, which contribute to nearly 90% of all
medical waste. [6]

In response to growing environmental concerns, some manufacturers have introduced
incremental sustainability measures. Abbott’s Freestyle Libre 3, for example, reduced
plastic volume by 41% compared to its predecessor through miniaturization [5]. Other
market players, like Medtronic, have explored modularity and reusability by offering a
rechargeable transmitter and a reusable applicator — demonstrating the technical feasi-
bility of longer-lifespan components [9].
However, a central trade-off remains between usability and environmental performance.
Research into biodegradable materials, such as PLA or chitosan blends, points toward
future possibilities. However, current options struggle to meet the durability and moisture-
resistance standards required for multi-day CGM wear, keeping most high-usability prod-
ucts dependent on conventional single-use plastics.
Dexcom’s G7 system, often cited as a benchmark for ease of use, achieved a usability
score of 92.8/100 in a formal evaluation [10]. Its intuitive, single-step design contrasts with
more complex reusable systems such as the Medtronic Guardian, which requires multiple
user actions during sensor application. These differences emphasize that usability is not
merely a preference but a critical enabler or blocker for sustainable innovation.
Moreover, existing reusable CGM systems rarely communicate their potential environ-
mental benefits to users. The Medtronic Guardian, for example, adds application com-
plexity without making clear that this effort reduces waste or carbon footprint. The lesson
is clear: sustainable performance must be paired with intuitive operation and visible value
communication, or it will fail to gain acceptance.
Importantly, this does not mean that reusability or added complexity is inherently flawed.
With careful integration of intuitive design and meaningful communication, such approaches
may still offer a viable path toward more sustainable CGM systems.

but often face barriers related to user acceptance or regulatory scalability. This project
explores design strategies that aim to shift toward the bottom-right quadrant — combining

alignment, and respond proactively to evolving market and regulatory pressures.

ical waste through sustainable innovation, especially in products with high impact and
high volume like CGM systems. The overlapping pressures — environmental, regulatory,

1.2
Objectives and Research Questions
The primary objective of this thesis is to explore how CGM devices can be redesigned to
reduce their environmental impact while preserving usability, safety, and regulatory com-
pliance. Additionally, the thesis considers how such a sustainable redesign can provide
strategic value to medical device companies, for instance, by improving competitiveness
in public procurement, enabling cost efficiencies, and strengthening brand credibility.
Main Research Question
How can a CGM device be designed to significantly reduce environmental
impact while maintaining usability — and simultaneously generate strategic
value for medical device companies?
To address this question, the project is structured around three main thematic areas:
stakeholder needs and usability, sustainability and mechanical design, and compliance
and technical feasibility. Each theme is supported by sub-questions that guide the anal-
ysis and design process.
1. Stakeholder Needs and Usability
To ensure that sustainable design choices are embraced in practice, it is essential to
understand the expectations, behaviours, and needs of all relevant stakeholders.
• SQ1.1: What are the needs and expectations of users and other stakeholders (e.g.,
healthcare professionals, procurement officers, regulators)?

• SQ1.2: How can the applicator be designed to be as intuitive and user-friendly as
current single-use options?
• SQ1.3: How can a reusable applicator be made cost-effective and accessible, while

2. Sustainability and Mechanical Design
This theme explores how design choices - such as modularity, material selection, and

1.3
Scope and Limitations
This thesis focuses on designing a medical device specifically for the Danish market,
operating within the framework of European Union (EU) legislation. Although the regu-
latory environment is harmonized across the EU, allowing for potential future expansion
into other European countries, this broader application is beyond the current scope of the
project.
The sensor component is treated as a black box in this thesis. Given the interdisciplinary
nature of the sensor, combining bioengineering and electronics, fields outside our primary
expertise, its detailed design and development are excluded. For comparison purposes,
it is assumed that all devices under consideration, including our own, utilize an identical
sensor. This assumption ensures that the sensor’s influence does not affect comparative
analysis outcomes.
Regulatory considerations will be addressed only at a high level, sufficient to demonstrate
that certification is attainable. A thorough regulatory review or detailed compliance strat-
egy falls outside the project’s remit.
The main objective is to produce a conceptual prototype design rather than a fully realized
device or a detailed engineering blueprint. As such, design for manufacturing processes
and considerations will not be incorporated into this work.
The project scope is limited to the mechanical design aspects of the product. Software
development and usability analysis of the accompanying application — both for our pro-
totype and for competitor products — are not examined here, despite their significant
influence on overall user experience.
Due to time constraints, the project prioritizes the creation of a broad conceptual design
that clearly outlines guidelines for subsequent development phases. The physical proto-
type will be functional in terms of simulating key actions, but will not represent a finalized
product.
Finally, resource limitations prevent clinical trials or testing with real patients. Usability
testing will be informal, conducted with friends and family in a home setting, serving as
an initial validation step.
1.3.1
Broader Definition of Sustainability
Sustainability is a multidimensional concept, rooted in the landmark definition provided by
the Brundtland Report (1987):
“Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.”
[12]
This definition incorporates two key principles: the prioritization of essential human needs
— especially for the world’s poor — and the recognition of environmental limits imposed
by technological and social conditions.
To operationalize this concept, sustainability is commonly broken down into three inter-
connected pillars:
• Environmental sustainability, which ensures that natural systems remain viable
over the long term by minimizing resource depletion, emissions, and pollution.
• Economic sustainability, which focuses on maintaining economic viability and
generating long-term value without exhausting the resources or systems on which

that value depends.
• Social sustainability, which emphasizes the protection and enhancement of social
equity, human rights, working conditions, and community well-being.
This tripartite framework is also at the core of the United Nations Sustainable Development
Goals (SDGs), which advocate for corporate engagement across all three dimensions to
create long-term shared value and reduce systemic risk [13].
How Sustainability Applies to This Thesis
This thesis evaluates deeply the environmental dimensions of CGM systems, with a par-
ticular focus on sensor applicators. The economic and social sustainability are taken into
account in a qualitative way, while the environmental sustainability is considered in depth
and with a scientific approach. The sustainability assessment is structured as follows:
Environmental Dimension
From an environmental perspective, a full LCA was performed on different CGM applica-
tors. The results show that single-use applicators generate significant emissions over the
course of a year of typical use. The Medtronic Guardian applicator, which is reusable,
emerged as the most environmentally sustainable under economic allocation, especially
when compared against a baseline defined by the planetary boundaries framework (ab-
solute sustainability approach). Additionally, the study proposes a redesign that aims to
further reduce environmental impact.
Economic Dimension
While this thesis does not provide a quantitative assessment of economic sustainability,
it is worth reflecting on some potential benefits. It might be economically advantageous
to adopt reusable applicators and modular transmitters, since these components do not
need to be manufactured for every sensor cycle. This could lead to cost savings for both
users and producers.
Social Dimension
Social sustainability is not explicitly measured in this thesis, but some considerations can
be made. One of the key social challenges in the field of diabetes management is en-
suring that all patients, regardless of income or geography, have access to the benefits
of CGM systems. If future cost savings resulting from reusable applicators and modular
transmitters are passed on to users, it might make these systems more affordable and
accessible to a broader population.
Improving affordability could lead to a wider adoption of CGM technologies, especially in
underserved or economically constrained communities. This would allow more patients
to monitor their glucose levels in real-time, helping them manage their condition more ef-
fectively. For many diabetic individuals, this continuous feedback can significantly reduce
the risk of acute complications, also it can, in some cases, be life-saving.
From this perspective, facilitating the broader use of CGM systems through cost-effective
and user-friendly designs should be seen as a socially sustainable objective. Reducing
the economic barriers to adoption would ensure that patients are not forced to sacrifice
the clinical advantages of these devices due to financial constraints. Although these re-
flections are hypothetical and not validated through data in this report, they highlight the
important role that thoughtful product design and affordability can play in enhancing public
health and equity in medical technology.
While the thesis focuses primarily on environmental sustainability through LCA, both eco-
nomic and social aspects are also touched upon in a qualitative and exploratory manner.

Overview of Diabetes and CGM Technology
3.1.1
Understanding Diabetes
Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by elevated levels of blood
glucose, commonly referred to as blood sugar. Glucose serves as the primary energy
source for the body’s cells and is derived from dietary intake as well as endogenous pro-
duction. Under normal circumstances, the pancreas produces insulin, a hormone that
facilitates the transport of glucose from the bloodstream into the body’s cells for energy
utilization. In individuals with diabetes, this regulatory mechanism is impaired: either
the pancreas produces insufficient insulin, the body’s cells become resistant to insulin’s
effects, or both. Consequently, glucose accumulates in the bloodstream, leading to hy-
perglycemia. Persistent hyperglycemia is associated with long-term damage to various
organs and systems, including the eyes, kidneys, nerves, and cardiovascular system.
There are several types of diabetes, with the most prevalent being Type 1, Type 2, and
gestational diabetes. Type 1 diabetes involves an autoimmune response that destroys
insulin-producing cells in the pancreas, leading to little or no insulin production. Type 2
diabetes, the most common form, occurs when the body’s cells do not use insulin prop-
erly, often accompanied by insufficient insulin production. The difference between those
two is presented in Figure 3.1. Gestational diabetes develops during pregnancy and typ-
ically resolves after childbirth, though it increases the risk of developing Type 2 diabetes
later in life. [14]. These three different forms differ in onset and treatment, but all require
careful glucose regulation to prevent complications such as neuropathy, retinopathy, and
cardiovascular disease. Monitoring glucose levels is essential for adjusting insulin doses
and managing dietary intake, physical activity, and stress.
Figure 3.1: Difference between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. Source: own material
Type 1 diabetes is particularly relevant in the context of CGM because individuals with
this condition are entirely dependent on externally administered insulin to regulate their
blood sugar levels. Since their bodies no longer produce insulin, they must carefully
monitor glucose levels throughout the day to prevent both dangerously high and low blood
sugar episodes. Consequently, CGMs are especially targeted toward Type 1 diabetes
management, where tight glycemic control is both more challenging and more crucial.
While CGMs can also benefit people with Type 2 diabetes, particularly those on intensive
insulin therapy, the clinical need and regulatory focus have primarily centred on supporting
Type 1 diabetes.

3.1.2
History of CGM technology
Given the central role of glycemic control in both the diagnosis and day-to-day manage-
ment of diabetes, the ability to measure glucose levels accurately has long been a clinical
priority. Over time, this necessity has driven significant technological innovation. The fol-
lowing section, and its graphic summary in Figure 3.2, outlines the historical development
of glucose monitoring technologies, based primarily on a compendium published by the
American Diabetes Association [3].
The evolution of technologies for glucose measurement reflects a broader narrative of
progress in diabetes management, transitioning from indirect, cumbersome methods to
precise, real-time monitoring systems. Initial efforts in the mid-19th century focused on
detecting glucose in urine, a method that, while pioneering, provided only delayed and
imprecise insights into glycemic status. A significant development came in 1908 with
the introduction of Benedict’s copper reagent, which facilitated semi-quantitative glucose
detection through a visible colour change. This approach, later simplified by the Clinitest
tablet in 1945, remained a clinical staple for decades, though its utility was inherently
limited by its indirect nature.
A transformative shift occurred in 1965 with the creation of the first enzyme-based blood
glucose test strip, the Dextrostix. Utilizing glucose oxidase, this strip allowed for a direct,
albeit semi-quantitative, measurement of blood glucose and laid the foundation for further
innovation. During the 1970s and 1980s, blood glucose meters emerged and gradually
became more reliable and accessible. Advances in sensor chemistry, electrochemical
detection, and user interface design led to smaller blood volume requirements, enhanced
accuracy, and decreased cost. These improvements established self-monitoring of blood
glucose (SMBG) as the standard of care, particularly for individuals with Type 1 diabetes.
The widespread adoption of SMBG was instrumental in enabling clinical studies such as
the Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT), which demonstrated the critical
importance of intensive glucose control in reducing long-term diabetic complications.
The introduction of CGM in the late 1990s represented another pivotal moment. The first
CGM systems approved by regulatory authorities were professional devices used in clin-
ical settings, with data retrospectively reviewed by healthcare providers. These systems,
while technologically significant, were limited in their clinical utility due to the absence
of real-time feedback for patients. One of the earliest attempts at non-invasive CGM, the
Glucowatch Biographer, employed reverse iontophoresis to extract interstitial fluid for glu-
cose measurement. Despite its technical novelty, the device failed commercially due to
skin irritation and inconsistent performance.
More robust CGM systems emerged in the early 2000s, spearheaded by companies such
as Medtronic and Dexcom. These early-generation sensors required frequent calibration
with fingerstick tests and initially served as supplementary tools rather than replacements
for SMBG. However, they paved the way for a new paradigm of diabetes management
by providing dynamic, continuous data. Iterative advancements soon followed, with im-
provements in sensor accuracy, data transmission, wearability, and user interface. The
integration of CGM with insulin pumps marked a significant leap forward, enabling the de-
velopment of sensor-augmented pump therapy and, eventually, hybrid closed-loop sys-
tems.
The most recent phase of CGM innovation has continued to enhance usability and re-
duce patient burden. Systems like Dexcom G6 introduced factory calibration and real-
time data streaming to smartphones, eliminating the need for fingerstick confirmation.
Abbott’s FreeStyle Libre popularized flash glucose monitoring, offering a simplified user

also redefined how individuals interact with their health data, paving the way for increas-

Despite their medical benefits, CGM devices introduce a significant environmental cost.
Most systems include single-use applicators and sensors that are discarded after use.
These components, which often contain both plastic and electromechanical parts, are
difficult or impossible to recycle due to hygiene regulations and material composition.
Only one part — a needle — is considered a hazardous waste after use. Due to safety
reasons, in most devices, it stays enclosed within the applicator housing after the sensor
insertion, and then the whole device is considered hazardous. This design choice reflects
a broader trend in the medical industry, where convenience and sterility often outweigh
environmental considerations.
According to the lifetime of commercially available CGM sensors, CGM users may dispose
of 25-52 applicators and sensors per year. Some manufacturers explicitly confirm that
these parts are non-recyclable and should be treated as general waste [4]. This raises
important questions about the lifecycle of CGM products and their long-term sustainability.
The limited wear time of CGM sensors — typically 7 to 15 days raises sustainability
concerns, as frequent replacement generates significant material waste. This restric-
tion stems from several interconnected factors. Sensor accuracy declines over time due
to enzyme degradation and biofouling, as proteins and immune cells accumulate around
the sensor filament, interfering with signal quality. Adhesives securing the sensor lose
effectiveness with exposure to moisture and skin oils, and prolonged wear increases the
risk of irritation or infection. Power constraints, although not the main factor, also play a
role, as longer operation would require larger batteries, reducing comfort. Additionally,
strict regulatory standards prioritize user safety and performance, reinforcing the current
wear-time limits.
Although researchers are exploring ways to extend sensor lifespan through more durable
adhesives, enzyme stabilization, biocompatible coatings, and alternative power sources,
these remain experimental. At present, significantly prolonging CGM wear time is not
yet feasible, and while future improvements are possible, they remain speculative and
uncertain.
These trade-offs highlight the complexity of designing CGM systems that are both sus-
tainable and widely usable. Achieving this balance is a key focus of the design exploration
presented in this thesis.

3.4
Regulatory Landscape
The scope of this section is to investigate the feasibility of certifying and distributing a
reusable CGM applicator in the EU market and under EU standards and regulations. This
exercise is crucial; the regulatory framework can influence the development of the product
heavily, and it is crucial to understand which characteristics the product must have to be
legally distributed in the target market.
3.4.1
Regulatory Classification and Legal Framework
The reusable CGM sensor applicator is considered a medical device accessory under the
EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR) 2017/745. According to Article 2(2) of the MDR,
an accessory intended to enable or assist the use of a medical device for its intended
purpose must itself be treated as a medical device and be subject to regulatory oversight.
Although it does not perform diagnostic or therapeutic functions directly, the applicator is
necessary for the correct deployment of the CGM sensor and therefore falls under the
MDR definition of a medical device.
3.4.2
Intended Use and Risk Classification
The device is characterized as follows:
• Spring-loaded and mechanical, enabling accurate and user-friendly placement of
a subcutaneous CGM sensor.
• Reusable, for personal use by a single patient.

3.5
Environmental Review of Existing Products
3.5.1
Goal and Scope of LCA
The goal of this Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is to evaluate and compare the environ-
mental performance of different CGM sensor applicator systems—primarily disposable
devices currently on the market and a reusable alternative under development. The study
aims to determine whether and under what conditions the reusable applicator can offer a
reduced environmental footprint over a one-year period of continuous CGM use.
The intended application of the study is threefold: to guide sustainable design decisions
during early product development phases; to inform the internal innovation strategy of
Phillips-Medisize; and to contribute to academic knowledge on sustainable medical device
design. Target audiences include medical device manufacturers, sustainability and R&D
teams, and academic researchers.
The LCA adopts a cradle-to-grave perspective, encompassing all stages from material ex-
traction to end-of-life treatment. It was conducted using the SimaPro 9.4 software and the
Ecoinvent v3.4 database. The assessment follows the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 stan-
dards and uses attributional modelling. The functional unit is defined as the subcutaneous
application of a CGM sensor for one year of continuous use. This allows normalization
across systems with different sensor wear durations and device lifespans.
3.5.2
Boundaries and Assumptions
The system boundaries include the entire product lifecycle of the applicator: material ex-
traction, manufacturing, packaging, transportation, use phase, and end-of-life treatment.
Importantly, the analysis isolates the applicator itself from the rest of the CGM system,
excluding the sensor, electronics, or digital platforms.
The geographical context for usage, storage, and disposal is Denmark, while manufac-
turing locations reflect actual supply chains wherever known. Disposal is modelled based
on Danish municipal waste incineration, as the applicators do not qualify as hazardous
waste due to built-in safety mechanisms complying with ISO 23908.

3.6.1
The Strategic Importance of LCA and Sustainability in MedTech
Life Cycle Assessment is a critical tool for companies operating in the pharmaceutical and
medical device sectors, enabling them to understand the environmental footprint of their
products in a precise and structured way. Rather than relying on intuition or common as-
sumptions, which are often misleading, LCA provides a science-based method to identify
exactly where the emissions and environmental impacts originate within the product life
cycle.
For example, our own analysis revealed that overlooked components like printed man-
uals or packaging can have environmental impacts comparable to the core medical de-
vice itself. Moreover, we showed that by switching from fossil-based ABS to biobased
polypropylene in an applicator, it is possible to reduce global warming impacts by approx-
imately 35%. This is a change that would be very difficult to justify or even notice without
a proper LCA.
But LCA is not only a tool for internal optimization. It has become increasingly important
as a way to position products in the market. Sustainability is no longer just a regulatory
compliance issue — it is becoming a key differentiator. In many European healthcare
systems, tenders are beginning to include environmental sustainability as a criterion for
product evaluation. This trend is particularly visible in Scandinavian countries like Den-
mark, where hospitals and regional healthcare authorities request information about re-
cyclability, packaging volume, and carbon emissions. Being able to document superior
sustainability performance with quantitative data can be the deciding factor for winning
such public tenders.
One practical example of how sustainability can drive competitive advantage is Ambu, a
Danish company that produces single-use endoscopes and other medical devices. Ambu
has made sustainability a core pillar of its product development and market positioning.
Through extensive use of LCA and carbon footprint analysis, they were able to demon-
strate that their single-use endoscopes, such as the aScope 4 Cysto, can have lower
environmental impacts than reusable alternatives. In one study, emissions were reduced
by up to 36% when switching to their solution.
Ambu didn’t stop at using these results internally—they transformed them into a market
advantage. By communicating this data transparently and building partnerships around
sustainable packaging and take-back schemes (such as their “Recircle” program), they
have strengthened their value proposition in a crowded and conservative medical market.
Their investments in bioplastic components and circular strategies are not just ethical
gestures — they open access to new customer segments and improve their chance of
winning environmentally conscious tenders.
This shows clearly that designing for sustainability is not just a burden or a cost. Although
many companies initially view the pressure to become more sustainable as an annoy-
ance or as a cost-increasing requirement, there is growing evidence that these initiatives

Stakeholder and User Analysis
This chapter examines the human and institutional landscape surrounding CGM devices.
Understanding who influences design adoption, how users experience current systems,
and where the pain points occur provides a foundation for developing solutions that are
both environmentally responsible and acceptable to stakeholders.
For CGM devices
specifically, usability remains a top priority. Recent studies have demonstrated that newer
CGM models have significantly improved ease of use, thereby reducing the cognitive bur-
den for users, including older adults and diabetes care educators. These improvements
are critical to maintaining adherence and effective diabetes management, which must be
preserved even as environmental impact is addressed. [29]
Designing for sustainability in medical devices involves a holistic lifecycle approach that
optimizes material selection, energy efficiency, product longevity, and end-of-life man-
agement. User-centred design principles complement sustainability goals by focusing on
essential user needs, reducing unnecessary components, and enabling modularity for re-
pair and reuse. This synergy ensures that devices remain user-friendly while minimizing
environmental harm. Tools such as LCA support informed design decisions that align with
both usability and sustainability objectives. [30]
In this chapter, a thorough analysis of stakeholders and users will be conducted to iden-
tify key participants, their roles, and their specific needs related to the usability of CGM
devices and their environmental impact. This analysis will inform the design process by
integrating diverse perspectives, ensuring that the resulting CGM device not only reduces
its ecological footprint but also maintains or improves the usability standards of current
disposable models. This dual focus is essential for fostering adoption, compliance, and
ultimately, better health outcomes alongside environmental stewardship.

Dexcom, Medtronic) and design consultancies (e.g., Phillips-Medisize). They drive
innovation but must balance cost, compliance, and usability. For sustainability to
become a design driver, it must also align with business incentives and customer

4.4
User Journey and Pain Points
To pinpoint where usability and sustainability challenges converge, a User Journey Map-
ping exercise was undertaken. The mapping was conducted collaboratively using digital
“post-it” notes in Miro, based on the official manuals of selected CGM devices, and our
own experience with the devices that we bought. These provided a detailed, step-by-step
breakdown of how each device is used. The analysis was further enriched by reviewing
YouTube videos showing real users interacting with the devices, which helped capture
nuances of handling, common mistakes, and unspoken user habits.
Journey maps were prepared for Dexcom G7, Abbott FreeStyle Libre 2, Abbott FreeStyle
Libre 3, and Medtronic Guardian — the same devices that were physically purchased
and disassembled for both functionality evaluation and LCA. The complete maps are pre-
sented in Appendix H.
From these mappings, a consolidated user journey for CGM devices was developed, di-
vided into several stages: introduction, access, onboarding, daily use, maintenance, and
disposal.
• Introduction and prescription: Users typically learn about CGM through their en-
docrinologist or diabetes nurse. Healthcare professionals act as gatekeepers, and

product choice is usually limited by predefined procurement agreements, meaning
patients are rarely involved in selecting their specific CGM system.
• Access and procurement: Once approved, the device is ordered via regional or
municipal channels, with logistics managed by distributors such as OneMed. End-

• Onboarding and training: Training is provided either by clinicians or via manu-
facturer materials. Usability at this stage is crucial to reduce cognitive load and
anxiety, particularly for those unfamiliar with medical technology. Issues such as
needle phobia, adhesive concerns, and fear of device failure may arise.
• Daily wear and data use: The sensor is worn continuously for 7–14 days. Known
challenges include skin irritation, discomfort, alert fatigue, and visual intrusion. Nev-

continued use.
• Replacement and disposal: Disposal practices are unclear for many users. Elec-

simply discard the device in household waste. A lack of clear guidance or visible
recycling options often leads to confusion and environmental guilt.
These stages make it clear that the greatest opportunities for combined usability–sustainability
gains lie at the onboarding stage — where training and first impressions are formed — and
at disposal, where a lack of clear, actionable pathways currently undermines sustainable
behaviour.

5.1
Initial Concept Exploration
5.1.1
Framing the Challenge
At the end of Chapter 4, in Table 4.1, a set of design requirements and criteria was es-
tablished, synthesized from user research, stakeholder interviews, regulatory review, and
sustainability analysis. These requirements provided a foundation for the design process
and defined both the boundaries and opportunities for innovation. Some criteria were de-
fined as essential (”must”), such as sharps safety or mono-material disposal. Others were
considered directional or desirable, like enhanced user feedback or compact packaging.
Rather than reiterating the full list of criteria, this section explores how they influenced
the early design exploration. The requirements were not treated as a static checklist
but as a dynamic framework that guided critical design decisions, surfaced trade-offs,
and highlighted tensions, especially between environmental impact and ease of use. For
example, maximizing reusability by reducing disposable components often requires more
complex user interactions. Conversely, the simplest, most intuitive solutions frequently
involved single-use components, contributing to material waste.
This duality became the central design challenge and the lens through which all subse-
quent work was evaluated. Insights from this exploratory phase also fed back into how
some requirements were interpreted and prioritized as the design evolved. Instead of
attempting to resolve this trade-off upfront, it was embedded into the design process by
proposing and comparing a deliberately limited set of three initial design directions, cho-
sen to span the extremes and mid-point of the usability–sustainability spectrum. This
ensured coverage of the design space while keeping the evaluation process tractable.
This exploratory approach enabled the team to explore the implications of each direction
without committing prematurely to a solution.
Additionally, interpreting the requirements revealed several areas where different types of
knowledge were in tension. End-user needs (e.g., confidence, clarity, safety) sometimes
pointed in a different direction than insights from LCA (e.g., minimizing materials, enabling
reusability). The goal of the design phase was not to find a compromise in every case
but to make informed, transparent decisions about which values to prioritize and why.
Regulatory feasibility and manufacturability considerations, though not always dominant
in this phase, acted as constraints that pruned some otherwise promising ideas.
5.1.2

tial exploration phase was conducted. This phase was characterized by a wide range
of divergent ideas drawn from user insights, stakeholder input, sustainability goals, and
material considerations. It included speculative sketches, early prototypes, and strategy-
level discussions that were intentionally open-ended and unconstrained.
Among the ideas explored were:
• Splitting the device into reusable and disposable modules
• Modular needle and transmitter assemblies
• Use of biobased, recycled, or mono-material plastics

• Take-back or recycling partnerships with waste management companies

Before the three distinct design variants were defined, a set of minimum design priorities

not affect the core functionality or architecture of the devices, they influenced key material,
packaging, and communication choices.
The shared design foundations included:
• Use of mono-material or bio-based plastics where feasible
• Reduction of overall component count and material volume
• Simplified packaging with minimal printing
• Visual cues and structural clarity to guide disassembly and disposal
• Provision for quantitative environmental data to support sustainable procurement
These elements were applied consistently across all design variants to ensure that even
early concepts aligned with the environmental and usability goals identified in previous
chapters. The differentiation between the variants was therefore focused on functionality,
modularity, and the trade-offs between user convenience and environmental performance.
5.1.3
Three Design Variants: Overview and Rationale
To explore different trade-offs between usability and sustainability, three initial design vari-
ants were proposed. These variants represented different positions along the usability–
sustainability spectrum. They were selected to represent: (A) minimal user disruption with
modest sustainability gains, (B) a hybrid solution balancing both aims, and (C) a trans-
formative, high-sustainability concept with greater usability challenges. Variant A was
the least environmentally friendly but the most user-friendly. However, it was still more
sustainable than the benchmark device, Dexcom G7, while offering the same level of us-
ability, an important assumption for the starting point. Variant C delivered the greatest
environmental benefits, though it was also the most complex to use; still, the complex-
ity was not expected to exceed the threshold of user acceptance. Variant B occupied a
middle ground, balancing usability and sustainability between the other two. Each variant
interpreted the design criteria through a different lens, emphasizing certain priorities while
compromising others. This approach allowed for an early comparison of strategies before
committing to detailed development work. The visualisation of that usability-sustainability
trade-off conceptual space, for the proposed variants, is presented in Figure 5.1.

5.2
System Architecture Development
The development of the system architecture for EcoSense was built directly on its early
definition as a modular, partially reusable CGM. This concept combined the environmental
advantages of reusability with a modular layout that allowed for safe and hygienic han-
dling. The high-level configuration was established early in the process and consisted of
four main sub-assemblies:
• Reusable applicator – responsible for applying the sensor to the skin, incorporating
the mechanical actuation mechanism.
• Modular, reusable transmitter – housing the battery and electronics for wireless
data transmission, providing both power and data transfer to the sensor.
• Disposable sensor module – containing the sensing probe that remains under the
skin, and an adhesive patch for skin fixation.

==PAGE:62==
• Disposable, modular needle module – enabling skin penetration during applica-
tion, then retracting for safe disposal.
This initial configuration was influenced by decisions outlined in the design requirements
chapter, particularly the emphasis on mono-material separation, minimal disposable vol-
ume, and simplified handling. These guiding principles meant that architecture develop-
ment was not purely a mechanical exercise, but an integrated design activity responding
to earlier research on environmental and usability trade-offs.
A core philosophy behind the architecture was functional minimalism: reducing the num-
ber of elements to the smallest viable set, thereby limiting the number of assembly steps
and minimising complexity for the user. The minimal step count was determined by the
overall EcoSense concept and the chosen boundaries of modularity. Where possible,
individual elements were designed to serve multiple roles. For example, the sensor pack-
aging doubles as a stand for loading the applicator, during which the applicator’s spring
is automatically re-loaded — removing the need for any separate reloading step. This
approach streamlined both manufacturing and handling while reducing material use.
User dexterity considerations also shaped the architecture. Certain groups, such as older
users or individuals with reduced fine motor control, benefit from fewer manipulations
and more direct assembly flows. To accommodate this, the transmitter is attached to the
sensor prior to skin application, when the device is still positioned in front of the user.
This avoids the need to connect small components on the body, improving ergonomics
and confidence. Overall, embedding secondary functions into primary components and
sequencing actions for accessibility ensured that the architecture supported intuitive op-
eration, consistent performance, and a lower risk of user error — without compromising
reusability or environmental goals.

The definition of the architecture developed in parallel with mapping the expected or-

components and determining how they should connect and disconnect. The application
sequence for the sensor was defined as follows (Figure 5.3):
1. Prepare the application site and hands by cleaning.
2. Remove the sterility seal from the sensor enclosure.
3. Attach the transmitter to the sensor.
4. Assemble the applicator with the sensor–transmitter set, using the sensor enclosure
as a support fixture.
5. Lift the assembled set from the enclosure.
6. Position the applicator against the skin.
7. Trigger the insertion mechanism, releasing the sensor to the skin.
8. Remove the applicator.
9. Detach and dispose of the needle module.
The removal process after the intended 14-day wear period followed a similar logic:
1. Peel the sensor–transmitter set from the skin, together with the adhesive patch.
2. Detach the transmitter for reuse.

5.3
Prototyping Process
Following the completion of the system architecture definition, the focus shifted to creating
a physical prototype that could effectively demonstrate the concept to users and stake-
holders. The primary objective of this prototype was to communicate the main functions,
sequence of use, and component interactions rather than to replicate the exact perfor-
mance, materials, or tolerances of a market-ready device.
CAD Modelling
Computer-aided design was carried out using Creo Parametric, a professional 3D mod-
elling environment. The CAD strategy followed a “skeleton” approach, in which a master
reference geometry contained the main shapes, clearances, and positional relationships
between components. This allowed all subassemblies to be parametrically linked to a sin-
gle source, enabling quick and adaptive modifications to propagate consistently across
the entire model. Such a setup was particularly advantageous in early-stage prototyping,
where iterative changes were frequent and often affected multiple parts simultaneously.
52

The model was developed with Design for Manufacturing (DfM) principles in mind, specif-
ically tailored for additive manufacturing. This meant accounting for layer orientation,
avoiding unsupported overhangs where possible, and designing features with tolerances
compatible with fused filament fabrication (FFF) 3D printing. Certain features, which in
a final product would be more complex — such as locking mechanisms, fine snap-fits,
or miniature electronic connectors — were simplified into press-fit placeholders or visual
proxies to accelerate fabrication and focus on usability evaluation.
Beyond guiding the fabrication of the prototype, the CAD model also served as the basis
for generating a bill of materials (BoM). By extracting the volumes of individual parts and
converting them into estimated weights, it was possible to quantify the material composi-
tion of the design. This BoM is included in the Appendix L.
The CAD modelling process was iterative: after each update, selected components were
exported for printing, assembled, and assessed for handling, ergonomics, and interface
fit. Feedback from these checks directly informed subsequent CAD adjustments, ensuring
a continuous refinement loop until the prototype could reliably demonstrate all intended
interactions without functional ambiguity. Figure 5.5 presents selected CAD view of the
applicator’s outer case.
3D Printing and Assembly

User Testing of the Prototype
5.4.1
Objectives and Methodology
The goal of the user testing phase was to evaluate the usability and perceived function-
ality of the developed CGM applicator prototype in comparison to a benchmark device
representing current market standards (e.g., Dexcom G7). The testing focused not only
on operational performance, but also on user perception, moments of confusion, error
rates, and attitudes toward usability–sustainability trade-offs.
To capture these aspects, a qualitative research approach was employed, combining the
“Thinking Aloud” protocol with a short post-test interview. Participants were encouraged
to verbalize their thoughts as they interacted with the devices, providing immediate insight
into their interpretation of visual and mechanical cues.
Following the hands-on task, a semi-structured interview was conducted to gather sub-
jective assessments, such as confidence, ease of use, and perceived safety. Finally,
participants were asked about their willingness to accept a more complex device if it led
to measurable environmental benefits.
Due to limited access to actual diabetic users, the tests were conducted with a sample
of adult participants unfamiliar with CGM technology. This limitation was acknowledged,
but deemed acceptable for evaluating general usability, cognitive friction, and first-time
interaction logic - key concerns for onboarding any user segment.
All participants provided verbal consent to audio recording, and all data were anonymized
for ethical handling.
It is important to acknowledge a limitation in the participant pool: all testers were friends
or family of the author. While this ensured access and familiarity with the test procedure,
it also introduced a certain homogeneity in values and attitudes—particularly regarding
sustainability. When asked whether sustainability influences their daily choices, nearly all
participants expressed a strong preference for environmentally friendly solutions, even at
the cost of convenience or price. This outcome may reflect the shared values within a
close social circle rather than the broader CGM user population. Ideally, a future study

interfaces.

the design; the creation of onboarding materials would be a separate, subsequent design

errors or uncertainties occurred at transition points between tasks, especially during trans-

work in Section 7.3.
In summary, the prototype’s usability appears promising but dependent on refinement of

The findings also validate the sustainable design priorities in Table 4.1 and suggest that
potential users are open to modest usability trade-offs in exchange for credible, visible
environmental — and, in some markets, economic — benefits.

Discussion
6.1
Reflections on the Design Process
The design process for this project combined elements of user-centred design, environ-
mental assessment, and regulatory analysis within a structured yet iterative workflow. Fol-
lowing the Double Diamond framework allowed the team to begin with broad exploration
and progressively narrow towards a viable solution, while ensuring that sustainability ob-
jectives remained integral from the earliest stages.
One of the key reflections is the value of integrating life cycle thinking at the concept
stage. By embedding LCA considerations into early ideation, it became possible to iden-
tify material hotspots and architectural decisions with the greatest potential for long-term
environmental gains. This proactive approach avoided the common pitfall of treating sus-
tainability as a late-stage optimisation, where options are often limited by prior design
commitments.
The multi-disciplinary nature of the project required continuous balancing between di-
vergent priorities. Usability and sustainability often presented conflicting demands: sim-
plifying user interaction risked increasing single-use mass, while maximising reusability
introduced additional complexity and learning curves.
The iterative prototyping cycle was another critical enabler. Rapid 3D printing and same-
day design–test loops made it possible to validate mechanical principles, assess handling,
and refine interfaces without significant delays.
User testing sessions played a decisive role in collecting valuble feedbacks for future
improvements. Even at low-fidelity stages, involving target users provided insights into
handling preferences, intuitive understanding of mechanisms, and perceived hygiene con-
cerns. Observations from these sessions revealed potential usability barriers that were
not apparent during internal evaluations, such as difficulties in aligning components or
uncertainty during actuation. Incorporating these findings into successive prototypes en-
sured that design changes were grounded in actual user behaviour rather than assump-
tions, ultimately improving both efficiency of use and user confidence.
Overall, the process demonstrated that sustainable medical device design benefits most
from early integration of environmental metrics, iterative user testing, and continuous ne-
gotiation between competing priorities.
6.2
Balancing Usability and Sustainability
While sustainability remains the primary goal of this thesis, usability cannot be ignored.
A product that significantly reduces environmental impact but fails to meet user expec-
tations in terms of convenience and intuitiveness risks rejection in the market. From a
practical perspective, a device that is not adopted by users will not deliver any real-world
sustainability benefit, regardless of its environmental potential. In this context, usability is
not in opposition to sustainability — it is a prerequisite for it.
Throughout the development of EcoSense, we aimed to identify and implement design
choices that optimize environmental performance without compromising usability. Sev-
eral such strategies were identified: the adoption of bio-based polypropylene over con-
ventional ABS reduces carbon emissions without affecting product handling or mechanical
Design of a CGM Device with a Reduced Environmental Impact

==PAGE:74==
performance; the design of a reusable applicator eliminates recurring waste and remains
ergonomically consistent with market expectations; and the simplification of packaging
and printed manuals further reduces environmental burden.
Moreover, incorporating a modular, reusable transmitter aligns both with sustainability and
usability objectives. It enables the separation of electronics from disposables, minimizes
electronic waste, and requires no change in day-to-day user interaction once integrated
into the system.
However, some sustainable design options introduced trade-offs. One such consideration
was the integration of a modular battery unit to be recharged by the user. While this so-
lution offers potential environmental gains by reducing the need for integrated single-use
batteries, it imposes a significant usability cost — requiring users to recharge components
regularly. Given the relatively small environmental improvement and the high risk of user
frustration or abandonment, this path was not pursued. Instead, the chosen design fo-
cuses on intuitive, low-maintenance reusability, where the added sustainability does not
interfere with the core user experience.
This highlights a broader lesson: designing for sustainability does not mean maximizing
environmental performance at all costs. Rather, it involves navigating trade-offs and en-
suring that ecological improvements are embedded in a way that supports, or at least
does not obstruct, the usability of the product. Only through this balance can CGM sys-
tems achieve real-world impact, fostering both adoption and environmental responsibility.
6.3
Regulatory Feasibility and Market Adoption
One of the essential conditions for the successful deployment of a medical device is its
regulatory compliance. Regardless of its sustainability or usability benefits, a product that
cannot be legally certified and marketed remains a conceptual exercise rather than a prac-
tical solution. Therefore, an early and thorough investigation of the applicable regulatory
framework was conducted to ensure that the proposed CGM applicator design is both
certifiable and commercially viable.
A key concern at the beginning of the project was the potential risk classification of the
applicator. Given its role in handling a subcutaneous sensor, there was an initial fear that
the device might inherit the higher risk classification of the sensor itself. This would have
imposed more stringent conformity assessments and possibly the involvement of a Noti-
fied Body. However, according to the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR 2017/745), the
applicator qualifies as a Class I medical device. This clarification considerably simplifies
the certification process.
Under this classification, the manufacturer is allowed to self-declare conformity with the
MDR by fulfilling the General Safety and Performance Requirements (GSPRs) and prepar-
ing the required technical documentation. The CE marking can be affixed without Notified
Body involvement, enabling the product to be legally marketed throughout the European
Economic Area (EEA). This regulatory pathway confirms that the EcoSense applicator
can reach the market without facing significant regulatory barriers.
Additionally, a number of harmonized standards provide clear guidance for designing the
applicator in compliance with essential requirements. Standards such as ISO 14971 (risk
management), IEC 62366 (usability engineering), ISO 20417 (information supplied by the
manufacturer), ISO 10993 (biocompatibility), ISO 17664 (cleaning and reprocessing), and
ISO 23908 (sharps injury protection) offer concrete frameworks for addressing the safety,

standards from the early stages, we have paved a straightforward path toward conformity.
Given this context, no major regulatory challenges have been identified. The classifica-

able. From a regulatory standpoint, the EcoSense applicator can be considered ready for
further industrial development and commercialization within the European market.
6.4
Limitations and Assumptions
This project was developed under specific assumptions and constraints that define both
the scope and the interpretation of its results. While necessary for feasibility, these limi-
tations must be acknowledged to properly contextualize the findings.
One major assumption concerns the focus on the Danish market. Denmark represents a
relatively small share of the global CGM market, especially when compared to major mar-
kets such as the United States. However, this deliberate narrowing of scope enabled a
deep and detailed analysis of the local healthcare system, public procurement practices,
and reimbursement structures. Understanding how tenders work and how bureaucracy
operates in the Danish context proved essential to developing a viable market-ready con-
cept. While the design may need adaptation for other countries, the insights gained from
the Danish system offer a strong foundation for future scaling.
Another key assumption was to treat the CGM sensor as a “black box.” While this sim-
plifies the analysis, it also assumes that our reusable applicator design would not signifi-
cantly alter the sensor’s design or function. This assumption is supported by our compar-
ative LCA, which demonstrated that the applicator, rather than the sensor, is the dominant
contributor to the overall environmental impact of CGM systems. Moreover, our applicator
concept does not introduce radically different features or geometries that would interfere
with sensor performance. Nonetheless, this remains a simplification and could be revis-
ited in future work.
Regarding the prototype maturity, it is important to note that the design presented in this
thesis is a conceptual prototype. While it was physically tested and iterated, further refine-
ment would be required for mass production. Some changes in the final design — such
as the need for more robust springs or thicker walls — may affect the material composi-
tion and environmental impact. However, due to the significant margin of sustainability
gains demonstrated by EcoSense compared to existing products, it is unlikely that these
modifications would alter the overall conclusion: that the proposed system remains a sub-
stantially more sustainable solution.
Software and digital user experience were not included in the design scope. This exclu-
sion is justified by the fact that the CGM applicator has minimal interaction with these
aspects. The functions of the transmitter used in our design mirror those of commercial
systems already on the market. Consequently, the user interface, data handling, and
app integration are expected to remain similar, and no major usability or environmental
impacts are due to the digital components.
Usability testing was conducted under time and resource constraints, using a small group
of non-diabetic participants (friends and family). While the feedback was valuable for
evaluating the clarity and intuitiveness of the design, the sample lacked clinical repre-
sentativeness. Broader testing with actual diabetic users would be needed to refine the
design details, assess application ergonomics under real conditions, and confirm long-
term user satisfaction. Nonetheless, the insights gathered were sufficient to inform key

considered. While they introduce limitations, they do not undermine the overall validity of
the findings. The project demonstrates that sustainable and user-friendly CGM design is
not only feasible, but ready for further development and validation in broader regulatory
and market contexts.

Conclusion
7.1
Summary of Key Insights
This thesis set out to design a Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) applicator concept
that significantly reduces environmental impact while maintaining usability and regulatory
feasibility. The process combined stakeholder interviews, market and regulatory analysis,
life cycle assessment (LCA), and iterative design development.
The findings of the LCA clearly demonstrate that the applicator is a dominant contributor to
the overall environmental footprint of CGM systems. Reusability and modularity emerged
as the most impactful strategies, with a reusable applicator and transmitter combination
achieving at least 50% reduction in annual CO2 emissions compared to leading disposable
systems. Bio-based plastics, such as bio-polypropylene, further contributed to lowering
impacts, although their benefits were secondary to architectural changes.
Stakeholder interviews revealed that while clinical priorities often favour reliability and
ease of use over environmental considerations, there is a growing awareness among
both procurement bodies and end-users of sustainability as a differentiator. Public ten-
ders, particularly in Denmark, are integrating environmental criteria, and companies with
credible, data-driven sustainability claims will likely gain a competitive advantage.
The design process underscored the importance of integrating LCA early, using it not only
to identify environmental hotspots but also to guide design trade-offs. Strategic decisions
— such as focusing redesign efforts on high-impact components rather than marginal
optimizations — proved critical in achieving meaningful improvements.
From a user perspective, feedback highlighted that usability must remain uncompromised
for sustainable solutions to be adopted. Simplified workflows, intuitive operation, and min-
imal behavioural change were identified as prerequisites for market acceptance. While
initial testing was conducted with non-diabetic participants, results confirmed the clarity
and intuitiveness of the proposed design, suggesting strong potential for broader accep-
tance pending clinical user validation.
Overall, the project demonstrates that sustainable CGM device design is both technically
feasible and commercially relevant. By aligning environmental strategies with user needs,
procurement trends, and regulatory requirements, companies can develop solutions that
are not only greener but also competitive in increasingly sustainability-conscious markets.

testing confirmed that these changes can be implemented without sacrificing intuitive-
ness or perceived safety, provided that tactile/visual cues and assembly feedback are
integrated. From a business perspective, such a design offers strategic advantages in

Stakeholder Needs and Usability
SQ1.1: What are the needs and expectations of users and other stakeholders?
Interviews revealed that end-users prioritise ease of use, minimal cognitive load, and
trust in device safety, while procurement officers and regulators emphasise sustainability
documentation and cost-effectiveness. These insights shaped the design criteria.
SQ1.2: How can the applicator be designed to be as intuitive and user-friendly as
current single-use options?
Through iterative prototyping and user testing, the reusable applicator achieved compa-
rable usability scores to the Dexcom G7 benchmark after one trial. Key enablers were
ergonomic form, assembly features, and minimal step count.
SQ1.3: How can a reusable applicator be made cost-effective and accessible while
preserving a positive user experience?
By reusing high-cost mechanical components and electronics across multiple sensor cy-
cles, manufacturing volume and cost per use are reduced. This cost efficiency can be
partially passed on to users without eroding manufacturer margins.
Sustainability and Mechanical Design
SQ2.1: Which components, materials, or processes contribute most to the envi-
ronmental impact?
LCA hotspot analysis showed that the applicator body and transmitter dominate the foot-
print, mainly due to frequent replacement in current designs.
SQ2.2: How can sustainable design strategies be integrated early in the process?
Embedding LCA in the concept phase allowed identification of high-impact components
before finalising architecture, steering the design toward modular reusability and lightweight-
ing.
SQ2.3: What material and modularity choices can support both environmental ben-
efits and efficiency?
Switching from ABS to bio-based PP achieved a further 30–35% climate impact reduction.
Modular architecture reduced manufacturing, packaging, and logistics demands.
SQ2.4: How can redesign create added value for companies?
Beyond environmental benefits, the design improves tender competitiveness in markets
like Denmark where sustainability is weighted in procurement. Lower lifecycle costs can
enhance competitiveness and profitability.
Compliance and Technical Feasibility
SQ3.1: What regulatory and market requirements apply to reusable or modular
CGM components in the EU?
The proposed design aligns with EU MDR classification for Class I medical devices and
can be certified under applicable harmonised standards (e.g., ISO 13485, ISO 14971, IEC
62366).
SQ3.2: How can the design meet these requirements while remaining scalable and
affordable?
The modular system maintains sterilisation compatibility, mechanical robustness, and
sharps safety. Its scalability is supported by using established manufacturing methods
and materials already validated in medical contexts.

Recommendations for Future Work
While this project successfully demonstrated the technical and environmental feasibil-
ity of a reusable, modular CGM applicator, several areas merit further investigation to
strengthen and validate the concept before potential commercialisation.
Extended User Testing with Target Population
Initial usability testing was conducted primarily with non-diabetic participants, which pro-
vided valuable insights into intuitiveness and handling. Future work should include clinical
users with Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes to assess performance in realistic, daily-use sce-
narios. This should include testing across diverse age groups, dexterity levels, and visual
acuity conditions to ensure universal accessibility.
Durability and Cleaning Validation
The current design assumptions on component lifespan and cleaning frequency are based
on literature review and conservative estimates. Laboratory and field trials are needed to
validate mechanical durability over repeated use cycles, assess wear on critical parts,
and confirm that cleaning protocols effectively maintain hygiene without compromising
material integrity.
Integration of Sensors and Electronics
Although this study focused on the applicator, integration with sensor and transmitter com-
ponents will require mechanical, electrical, and software compatibility verification. Proto-
typing with actual CGM electronics would help identify unforeseen design constraints,
tolerances, and assembly considerations.
Further Material Exploration
While bio-based PP demonstrated significant environmental benefits, exploring other sus-
tainable materials — such as high-performance biopolymers, recycled medical-grade plas-
tics, or hybrid composites — could further reduce environmental impact or improve me-
chanical performance. Comparative LCAs should be conducted to evaluate trade-offs in
cost, robustness, and recyclability.
Business Model and Procurement Strategy
Future research should explore business models tailored to reusable medical devices,
including subscription-based supply of consumables and return/reuse schemes. Analysis
of public tender frameworks could identify the optimal positioning of sustainability metrics
to maximise procurement advantages.
Broader Regulatory and Market Assessment
While this work focused on EU MDR requirements, expanding the regulatory analysis to
the U.S., Asian, and emerging markets would clarify global feasibility. Identifying certi-
fication pathways in multiple jurisdictions could support a scalable international launch
strategy.
Overall, these research directions would not only validate the technical and environmental
performance of the proposed design but also accelerate its readiness for adoption in clin-
ical practice, ensuring both patient benefit and competitive advantage in a sustainability-
driven market.

Motivation and Problem that is being solved 
Most continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) systems currently available on the market 
utilize single-use sensor applicators. These plastic and electromechanical devices are 
discarded after a single use. While this design offers a user-friendly experience, it imposes 
a significant environmental burden, with each user disposing of approximately 36 
applicators and sensors annually. Moreover, the applicator and the sensor are often non-
recyclable, as explicitly stated by some manufacturers [1].  
Alternative solutions exist in the market. One of the types eliminates applicators entirely but 
requires sensor insertion by trained healthcare professionals, limiting accessibility for many 
users [2]. Notably, only one CGM system allows user insertion with a reusable applicator [3]. 
However, this system is considerably more complex compared to its single-use, user-
friendly counterparts. Document with examples of the available applicator and sensor was 
prepared [4]. 
==PAGE:93==
According to the International Diabetes Federation [5], in 2021, approximately 537 million 
people were diagnosed with diabetes, and it is estimated to reach about 783 million by 2045. 
Therefore, the CGM industry faces a compelling opportunity for sustainable innovation. The 
primary challenge is to develop solutions that balance user-friendliness, accessibility, and 
environmental sustainability. 
Goals and expected outcomes 
The master’s thesis project aims to design a reusable applicator for wearable medical 
sensors, emphasizing sustainability, ease of use and comfort. Beyond reusability, the 
project will explore other strategies to minimize environmental impact. 
 
The project will aim first to create a wide conceptual design, with clear guidelines for further 
steps and implementation. Due to time constraints the physical device design will be limited 
to creating a functional prototype that resembles the final product but only simulates its 
action. 
 
To develop this project and potentially bring it to market, several key questions must be 
addressed: 
1. What are the needs and requirements of users and other stakeholders (e.g., 
healthcare professionals, regulators)? 
2. In the existing products, which are the most impacting components/material in the 
LCA. How can these early considerations be included in the design? 
3. How can the applicator be designed to be as intuitive and user-friendly as single-use 
options? 
4. How can the design minimize waste and environmental impact during the use and 
disposal phases? 
5. What other strategies can enhance the product’s sustainability, such as material 
choices or modular design for disassembly and recycling or repair? 
6. Is there potential for mechanical design innovation? Can the applicator serve as a 
platform for multiple sensor types, such as CGMs and other microneedle-based 
wearables? 
7. What medical device regulations and disposal standards apply to a reusable 
applicator? How can the product meet these requirements while maintaining 
affordability? 
8. How can a reusable applicator be made cost-effective and therefore accessible for 
users and healthcare providers?

1. LCA and Sustainability Communication 
• LCA is a powerful internal tool, especially for communicating with distributors, public 
tenders, and regulators. 
• You should translate LCA results into simple, user-understandable messages (e.g., 
“saves 1 kg of plastic/year”). 
• Customers often care emotionally about waste, not LCA numbers—especially the 
visual of throwing plastic in the trash every two weeks. 
• Benchmarking is key: compare your product’s environmental performance against 
others, with tangible metrics. 
• Avoid greenwashing: sustainability claims must be specific, evidence-based, and 
presented with nuance. 
2. Marketing & Branding 
• To market sustainability effectively, you need a clear, agreed-upon environmental 
benchmark – like energy labels in appliances. 
• Investigate if there are any existing certifications or eco-labels applicable to medical 
devices or health tech. 
• Companies like Medtronic failed to leverage sustainability as a selling point – even 
though they had reusable components. 
• Your product should aim to be “silently sustainable” – not demanding behavioral 
changes from users but still eco-friendly. 
3. User Experience 
• Users clearly prefer intuitive, single-step applicators like Abbott’s – ease of use is a 
top priority. 
• Medtronic’s reusable solution had poor UX, which outweighed its sustainability 
benefits in the eyes of users. 
• There’s a strong resistance to complicated workflows or extra steps – even if they 
make the product greener. 
• You should prototype multiple concepts (e.g., modular vs. all-in-one) and test how 
much complexity users will tolerate. 
4. Pain Perception 
• Pain is not just about needle size - it’s psychological, based on what users see, hear, 
and expect. 
==PAGE:108==
 
11 
• Needle visibility matters – products where users don’t see the needle are perceived 
as less painful. 
• Insertion speed and sound also influence perceived pain. 
• Don’t over-focus on pain unless it becomes a clear differentiator – just make sure it’s 
not worse than competitors. 
 
5. Regulatory and Market Trends 
• The EU is moving toward battery removability and recyclability – a sign that circular 
design will become mandatory. 
• Sustainability is becoming more important in public procurement decisions, though 
it’s not yet the top criterion. 
• Being able to show environmental superiority might become essential to enter certain 
markets or contracts.

• Move toward frequent feedback cycles, especially when specific design decisions are 
on the table. 
• Focus early concept testing on both user experience and sustainability metrics.

significantly.

8.1 Definition of Intended Use 
 
The applicator is a single-patient multi-use, manual medical device intended to insert a disposable 
continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) sensor subcutaneously into the user's skin. It is designed to 
enable safe, reliable, and sterile placement of the sensor with minimal steps and without the need for 
professional medical personnel. The device is operated directly by the user or a caregiver and is 
intended for use in home or non-clinical environments. It is compatible exclusively with the

8.2 Medical Device Clarification 
 
8.2.1 EU – CGM Sensor 
According to Article 2 (1) in the Medical Device Regulation (MDR) [1], EU defines medical device as: 
 
“…any instrument, apparatus, appliance, software, implant, reagent, material or other article 
intended by the manufacturer to be used, alone or in combination, for human beings for one or more 
of the following specific medical purposes: 
- 
diagnosis, prevention, monitoring, prediction, prognosis, treatment or alleviation of disease, 
- 
diagnosis, monitoring, treatment, alleviation of, or compensation for, an injury or disability, 
- 
investigation, replacement or modification of the anatomy or of a physiological or pathological 
process or state, 
- 
providing information by means of in vitro examination of specimens derived from the human 
body, including organ, blood and tissue donations, 
 
and which does not achieve its principal intended action by pharmacological, immunological or 
metabolic means, in or on the human body, but which may be assisted in its function by such means.” 
 
A Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) sensor is designed to continuously measure and monitor 
glucose levels in the interstitial fluid of individuals with diabetes. 
Its primary function is to provide real-time data that supports the monitoring and management of 
chronic disease (diabetes). This information enables early detection of hyperglycemia or 
hypoglycemia, helping users and healthcare professionals take timely action to prevent 
complications. The CGM sensor operates without achieving its intended action through 
pharmacological, immunological, or metabolic. Thus, it falls under the category medical devices in 
EU. 
 
8.2.2 EU – Applicator 
An accessory for a medical device is defined in MDR Article 2 (2) [1] as “…an article which, whilst 
not being itself a medical device, is intended by its manufacturer to be used together with one or 
several particular medical device(s) to specifically enable the medical device(s) to be used in 
accordance with its/their intended purpose(s) or to specifically and directly assist the medical 
functionality of the medical device(s) in terms of its/their intended purpose(s);” 
 
As the Applicator is intended to be used specifically with the CGM Sensor, the Carrying Applicator 
is also regulated as a medical device in EU. 
 
As seen from the MDR definition in 8.2.2, accessories to medical devices are also regulated as 
medical devices, and shall, according to MDR Annex VIII section 3.2 [1], “…be classified in their own 
right separately from the device with which they are used.”

8.4 Definition of the Criticality of Intended Use 
The target patient population includes individuals with diabetes mellitus (Type 1 or Type 2) who require 
continuous glucose monitoring for effective management of their condition. The age range of users can 
vary significantly, typically including both pediatric and adult patients, from approximately 6 years old. 
The CGM sensor applicator is intended to be used directly by the patient or a caregiver in a non-clinical 
(home) environment. It is designed for manual, single-use application of a subcutaneous glucose 
sensor and is operated without the need for healthcare professionals. The device may be used while 
the user is in a range of physical positions (e.g., sitting, standing, lying down) and under various 
environmental conditions typical of the home setting. Its ease of use and ergonomic design aim to 
minimize errors and ensure safe, consistent application. 
Despite its intended simplicity, the sensor applicator could be associated with certain risks that arise 
from the interaction between the user, the device, and the environment. These include incorrect 
insertion depth, improper sensor placement, or failure to fully deploy the sensor, which may lead to 
compromised glucose readings or necessitate replacement of the sensor prematurely. 
Although the applicator is not a life-sustaining or therapeutic device, it plays a critical supporting role in 
the overall CGM system by enabling reliable sensor insertion. Errors in application could indirectly result 
in the delay or inaccuracy of glucose monitoring, which, in turn, could contribute to inappropriate 
therapeutic decisions. 
 
8.5 Determination of Device Class 
 
8.5.1 EU – CGM Sensor Applicator 
 
According to MDR [1] Annex VIII Rule 1 all non-invasive devices are classified as class I, if they are 
not: 
a) intended for channeling or storing blood, body liquids, cells or tissues, liquids or gases 
b) intended for modifying the biological or chemical composition of human tissues or cells, 
blood, other body liquids or other liquids intended for implantation or administration into the 
body 
c) consisting of a substance or a mixture of substances intended to be used in vitro in direct 
contact with human cells, tissues or organs taken from the human body 
d) intended to come into contact with injured skin or mucous membrane 
The CGM Sensor Applicator is therefore judged to be a Class I device.

Preface
This Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) study is conducted as part of a Master’s thesis project in collaboration
between the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and Phillips-Medisize. The study evaluates the environ-
mental impact of a reusable applicator for Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) sensors, aiming to assess its
performance compared to current disposable alternatives.
The work was carried out during the final semester of the author’s MSc in Mechanical Engineering pro-
gram, with support and guidance from professionals at Phillips-Medisize and academic supervisors at DTU.
The study was initiated to investigate how design decisions made in the early development phases of medical
devices—specifically CGM applicators—can influence their environmental footprint across the entire product
life cycle.
The study takes a cradle-to-grave perspective, including all relevant lifecycle stages of the reusable CGM
sensor applicator. Given that the device is under development, several stages of the LCA are based on assump-
tions, estimates, and secondary data sources. The findings are intended to inform sustainability decisions during
the product development process, and to provide a foundational reference for further academic and industry
research into the environmental performance of reusable medical devices.

Glossary
Glossaries where no reference is stated originates from the ISO 14044 standard.
• Allocation: separating the input or output flows of a process or system between the product system under
study and one or more other systems
• Attributional LCA: an LCA that accounts for flows and impacts among processes within a chosen temporal
boundary
• Characterisation: calculation of the extent of the environmental impact per category
• Classification: assignment of LCI result parameters to their respective impact categories
• Co-product: any of two or more products coming from the same unit process or product system
• Comparative LCA: study in which two or more comparable products/systems are assessed. LCA’s are in
general comparative studies as environmental impacts are relative to those of other products
• Consistency check: process of verifying that assumptions, methods, and data are consistently applied
throughout the study and are in accordance with the goal and scope definition

• Consequential LCA: an LCA that attempts to account flows and impacts that are caused beyond the
immediate system in response to a change in the system (e.g., a change in demand)
• Critical review: process intended to ensure consistency between an LCA and the principles and require-
ments of the ISO standards
• Data quality: characteristics of data that relate to their ability to satisfy stated requirements (e.g., refer-
ence, collection method, newness, uncertainty etc.)
• Elementary flow: material or energy entering the system being studied that has been drawn from the
environment without previous human transformation, or material or energy leaving the system that is
released into the environment without following human transformation
• Functional unit: unit of comparison between products that provide an equivalent level of function baseline
• Goal and Scope definition: section during which the purpose of an LCA is defined. The goal and scope
are usually determined by the commissioner of the study and the practitioner in collaboration
• Life Cycle Impact Assessment: phase of LCA in which the environmental impact of assessed products is
evaluated. The assessment can contain several steps or methods such as classification, characterisation,
and weighting
• Inventory analysis: phase during which the LCA model is built according to the goal and scope. Data is
collected, processed, and calculated into the quantitative model
• Impact category: class representing environmental issues of concern to which Life Cycle Inventory Analysis
results may be assigned
• Impact category indicator: quantifiable unit of an impact category
• Process energy: energy required for a process to operate or for equipment within a unit process, excluding

• Raw material: primary or secondary material used to produce a product
• Sensitivity analysis: a systematic evaluation process for describing the effect and correlation of inputs and

Introduction
The growing demand for home-based healthcare solutions and the increasing prevalence of chronic conditions
such as diabetes have led to widespread adoption of wearable medical technologies, including Continuous Glucose
Monitoring (CGM) systems. These systems have significantly improved patient outcomes by enabling real-time
glucose tracking and reducing the need for finger-prick tests. However, the widespread use of CGM devices has
also introduced new challenges related to medical waste and resource consumption, particularly due to the short
lifespan and disposability of components such as sensor applicators.
Single-use medical devices account for a substantial share of healthcare-related waste. In many cases, en-
vironmental concerns are subordinated to requirements for sterility, safety, and ease of use.
Nevertheless,
as healthcare systems, manufacturers, and patients become more aware of sustainability issues, there is in-
creasing interest in exploring alternatives to disposable designs. In this context, reusable medical devices are
gaining attention for their potential to reduce material use, energy consumption, and overall environmental
impact—provided they are designed and used appropriately.
This study investigates the environmental implications of replacing a conventional disposable CGM sensor
applicator with a reusable alternative. The work is part of a Master’s thesis carried out in collaboration with
Phillips-Medisize and DTU, and it aims to quantify the life cycle environmental impact of a reusable applicator
under various usage scenarios.
By applying the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) methodology, this study seeks to identify the key contributors
to environmental impact across the device’s full lifecycle—from raw material extraction to end-of-life. The results
will serve as a basis for informed design decisions and contribute to the ongoing effort within the medical device
industry to align innovation with sustainability goals.

Background
Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) systems are wearable medical devices that allow individuals with di-
abetes to monitor their glucose levels in real-time.
Unlike traditional blood glucose meters that require a
finger-prick, CGM systems continuously measure glucose in the interstitial fluid through a small sensor inserted
under the skin. These systems typically consist of three components:
• sensor, which detects glucose levels in the interstitial fluid.

• reader or smartphone app, which displays the glucose trends and alerts the user to critical levels.

CGM systems have transformed diabetes care by offering continuous feedback and enabling proactive glucose
management. They reduce the frequency of hypoglycemic events, support tighter glycemic control, and improve
quality of life for patients. However, the increased use of CGMs—often requiring sensor replacement every 7
to 14 days—has introduced a new challenge: growing volumes of single-use medical waste, particularly from
the applicators used to insert the sensors. CGM Sensor Applicators The sensor applicator is a handheld device
designed to insert the CGM sensor beneath the skin. It is typically spring-loaded or mechanically actuated
to ensure consistent depth and insertion angle, minimizing pain and user error. Figure 1 illustrates a typical
single-use CGM sensor applicator. Currently, CGM sensor applicators are almost exclusively disposable, with
each applicator used once and then discarded along with its housing and mechanical components. While this
simplifies regulatory compliance and eliminates contamination risk, it significantly increases the environmental
footprint of the CGM system. Many CGM users go through 26–52 applicators per year, depending on the sensor’s
wear time. Given that each applicator includes injection-molded plastic parts, springs, and packaging, this adds
considerable pressure to waste management systems and contradicts broader efforts to reduce healthcare-related
emissions and waste.
Connectivity and Digital Health CGM systems are a leading example of connected health—a model in which
medical devices are integrated with digital platforms to enable remote monitoring, data sharing, and enhanced
patient engagement. As shown in Figure 2, the CGM ecosystem allows data to be transmitted wirelessly to
smartphones and healthcare providers, helping patients manage their condition more effectively.
Case study
This Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) study compares five different Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) sensor
systems, with a particular focus on the environmental performance of the sensor applicator component. The
main objective is to evaluate whether a reusable applicator, currently under development by Phillips-Medisize,
can offer meaningful environmental benefits over the fully disposable applicators used in leading commercial
CGM systems.
The case study includes four market-leading CGM solutions:

Goal and Scope
1.1
Goal
The goal of this Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is to evaluate the environmental impact of Continuous Glucose
Monitoring (CGM) sensor applicators used across several market-leading systems. The primary objective is to
assess and compare the environmental performance of currently available disposable applicators with two alter-
native design strategies aimed at reducing environmental impact: (1) the use of recycled materials in single-use
applicators and (2) the development of a fully reusable applicator. This study adopts a cradle-to-grave perspec-
tive, including all relevant lifecycle stages such as material extraction, manufacturing, packaging, transportation,
use, and end-of-life treatment. A key focus is the reusable applicator concept which is evaluated under realistic
use-case assumptions to determine when and how it may offer a lower environmental footprint compared to
disposable alternatives. The results are intended to support sustainable design decisions during early product
development, inform internal strategy at Phillips-Medisize, and contribute to the academic understanding of
sustainable medical device design. This study is conducted as part of a Master’s thesis project and is intended
for academic dissemination and internal use.
Target audience and intended application
Medical device manufacturers and pharma companies
This study is particularly relevant for companies
involved in the design and production of CGM systems or other drug delivery devices. As regulatory and market
pressures around sustainability continue to increase, understanding the environmental impact of disposable
components like sensor applicators is becoming increasingly important.
Design and sustainability teams at Phillips-Medisize
The results provide actionable insights for internal
development teams by identifying key impact contributors and evaluating alternative design approaches. The
findings support ongoing efforts to improve environmental performance through material choices and product
architecture.
Academia
This study contributes to the academic field of Life Cycle Assessment, especially in the context of
reusable versus disposable medical devices. It may serve as a case reference for future research on sustainable
innovation in healthcare technologies.

1.1.1
Limitations of the study
The ILCD handbook recommends the foreground data should aim at using primary data from the producer/
operator. Primary data in this case would be a detail BOM (bill of material) of all the components, the detailed
average transportation route and the manufacturing location. Because of time and resources limitation, it was
not possible to obtain the primary data from the device manufacturer . The study therefore builds on LCI
database data and secondary data.
The assembly process of all CGM applicators is not included in any product systems due to data unavail-
ability.
Only the material extraction and disposal stage is included in the assessment of these add-on product systems.
Regarding End-of-Life (EoL) modeling, disposal scenarios were based on the official disposal instructions
provided by the device manufacturers. The assessment mainly considers the context of Denmark, where general
waste is typically incinerated.
2
Scope
This study evaluates the environmental impact of CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitoring) sensor applicators,
with a focus on comparing existing disposable applicators to lower-impact alternatives including designs made
with recycled materials and a reusable applicator currently in development at Phillips-Medisize. The scope
is limited to the applicator component of CGM systems and excludes the pharmaceutical function, sensor
electronics, and user data platforms. Those system components are assumed to have the same functions and
performances. Although the sensors might have a relevant role in the impacts, there are considered as a black
box, this thesis is not focused on designing an alternative sensor due to limited time and competences. A
sensitivity analysis is run to show the share of impacts caused by the sensor, and to understand whether it
makes sense to focus the design part on applicator, packing and manuals to reduce the impacts.
The intent is to isolate the environmental footprint of the applicator, packaging and manuals and investigate
the potential for impact reduction through improved material selection and product architecture. The LCA
follows the methodology outlined in the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 standards. The four key phases of the LCA
are: (1) goal and scope definition, (2) life cycle inventory (LCI), (3) life cycle impact assessment (LCIA), and
(4) interpretation of results.
The analysis was conducted using SimaPro 9 software in combination with the ecoinvent v3.4 database
for background data. The geographical scope of the study is the Danish market, with distribution, storage,
and disposal practices modeled accordingly. The manufacturing locations of the different CGM systems are
considered to reflect the real-world supply chains of the devices.
2.1
System Boundaries and Completeness Requirements
The system boundaries are defined as cradle-to-grave, including all lifecycle stages from raw material extraction
to end-of-life treatment. Each CGM applicator product system includes:
• Material extraction and processing: Covers raw material sourcing and processing, including materials
in subcomponents such as springs or casings.
• Manufacturing: Includes foreground processes such as injection molding. The manufacturer process is
assumed to reflect the most common manuf. practice for the specific material and component.
• Packaging: Accounts only for the packaging of the Medical device itself (primary packaging).
• Transportation: Considers transport of materials to manufacturing facilities, intercontinental shipping
to Europe, distribution to Danish end-users.
• End-of-life treatment: Covers disposal, recycling, or incineration of applicators and packaging in Den-
mark, using typical Danish waste treatment pathways.
Figure 3 illustrates the system boundary applied in this study.
To ensure robustness, a minimum completeness threshold of 98% has been set. That is, 90% of the mass and
environmental impact contributions must be covered to consider results representative of real-world scenarios.
Certain elements, such as manual assembly or specific cleaning processes, may be excluded due to lack of data,
but only if they are expected to have negligible influence on final results.

2.2
Cut-off Criteria
Although a full life cycle perspective is targeted, it is not feasible to model all processes with complete precision.
To manage this, a cut-off threshold of 98% by material mass is applied. Components or materials contributing
less than 2% of the total mass may be excluded if they are determined to have an insignificant impact on the
overall results.
When it is not possible to relate excluded processes (e.g., transport stages or auxiliary equipment) to this
mass-based cut-off, a qualitative assessment is used to determine whether their influence on key impact categories
is likely to be significant.

The functional unit defines a measurable and consistent basis for comparing the environmental performance of
different CGM sensor applicator systems. Since the study compares both single-use (disposable) and multi-use
(reusable) applicators, it is essential to normalize impacts over a common functional output. In this study, the
functional unit is defined as:

The function is defined as the safe and accurate placement of a CGM sensor under the skin, in accordance

results are expressed in terms of environmental impact per 1 year of use of CGM applicators to apply the sensors.
This functional unit allows fair comparison between disposable and reusable applicators while accounting for
differences in sensor lifespan and use patterns.
2.3.3
Reference flow
The reference flow defines the quantity of applicators required to fulfill the functional unit: one year of continuous
CGM use, where the sensor is applied at the correct depth and orientation according to the manufacturer’s
intended use. The number of applicators needed per year depends on the lifespan of the sensor associated with
each system. In this study, the reference flow is expressed as the total number of applicators required for one
year of CGM sensor insertions. For disposable systems, the number of applicators is equal to the number of

2.3.5
End-of-life treatment and waste scenarios
CGM sensor applicators are designed according to medical device regulations that require post-use protection
from sharp components. As such, all devices evaluated in this study incorporate automatic needle retraction or
shielding mechanisms that ensure the needle is not exposed after use (ISO 23908). Because of this integrated
safety design, the applicators do not qualify as sharps or hazardous medical waste. The CGM manufactures
give instruction to dispose the product as general waste, therefore in this LCA all CGM applicators — whether
disposable or reusable — are discarded with general household waste and treated through municipal residual
waste.
Since Denmark is considered in this LCA, the Danish data for the treatment of general waste are
considered.
2.4
LCI modelling framework and basis for impact assessment
2.4.1
Attributional modelling
This study applies attributional modelling, as the goal is to assess the environmental impact of existing CGM
sensor applicator systems, including a reusable solution under development, based on their current or expected
material and energy flows. A fundamental criterion for attributional modelling is that the system under study
represents a relatively small share of the total market, such that it does not induce structural changes in the
background system (e.g., infrastructure, material production, or energy systems). In this context, CGM sensor
applicators—although widely used—are not assumed to significantly influence the upstream supply chain or
alter average production capacities in sectors such as plastics, electronics, or waste management. Consequently,
the environmental impacts of the products are calculated using average data from established life cycle inventory
databases (ecoinvent v3.4), without modeling system-level shifts or marginal suppliers. The study uses the cut-
off system model, which is commonly applied in attributional LCAs. While this approach provides a consistent
and comparative basis for evaluating different applicator designs, it does not account for indirect or large-scale
market effects. These effects may be explored qualitatively in the discussion or through future consequential
modelling if a system-level shift is considered likely.
2.4.2

2.4.3
Requirements for system comparisons
This study follows the ISO 14044 requirements for comparative life cycle assessment studies. These include the
need for consistent functional units, equivalent system boundaries, data quality, and interpretation of results.
In this assessment, only the CGM sensor applicators are included within the system boundaries. The CGM
sensor itself, along with its associated environmental impacts, is excluded from the analysis. This is because the
sensors are considered identical across the compared systems or are assumed to be unaffected by the choice of
applicator. Therefore, the applicators are modeled independently of the sensors, and all background processes
related to sensor production, use, or disposal are omitted. This assumption ensures that the environmental
differences observed in the study can be attributed solely to the applicators.
All compared systems share:
• the same functional unit (correct subcutaneous sensor insertion over one year),
• identical boundaries regarding common excluded processes (e.g., sensors),
• consistent data quality and modelling assumptions.
As a result, the comparison between the applicator systems is considered methodologically sound, and the
requirements for a comparative LCA are met.

3.2
Data collection
This section outlines the methodology and quality of data collection used for the life cycle inventory. Primary
data for this study were collected through a combination of reverse engineering of current CGM sensor applica-
tors on the market and the use of design documentation for the new reusable solution. The goal was to ensure
a high level of consistency, specificity, and relevance across all product systems. For commercial CGM applica-
tors (Dexcom G7, Abbott Libre 2, Libre 3, and Medtronic Guardian), physical units were purchased from the
market and manually disassembled. Each component was individually weighed using precision scales. Material
types were identified through expert assessment by a specialist at Phillips-Medisize, leveraging extensive experi-
ence with medical-grade plastics and component design. Although not instrumentally verified (e.g., by FTIR),
this approach provided high confidence in material classification. Where possible, the actual manufacturing
locations of each commercial applicator were considered to model realistic transportation distances. For the
reusable solution developed with this thesis, a bill of materials (BOM) was used as the basis for modeling. This
BOM includes component-level specifications for materials, weights, and subassemblies, reflecting the current
engineering design. All parts are assumed to be produced at designated suppliers or facilities identified in the
development plan. Generic background data for upstream and downstream processes (e.g., raw material pro-
duction, energy generation, and waste treatment) were sourced from the ecoinvent 3.4 database and adapted to
reflect Danish average conditions as appropriate.

3.3.2
Manufacturing
The manufacturing stage models the energy and resource consumption associated with producing the physical
components of each CGM sensor applicator.
This includes the injection molding of plastic subcomponents
and the machining of metallic parts like the springs. All plastic components are assumed to be produced via
standard injection molding processes. The corresponding datasets from the ecoinvent v3.4 LCI database were
used. Since no primary data from manufacturers or suppliers was available for this LCA, global average processes
were selected. A critical modeling detail is the inclusion of process waste. Injection molding of thermoplastics
typically results in small but consistent amounts of waste material, which can occur due to trimming, flow
channels, or defective parts. To account for this, the net material demand per component was divided by a

5.3.4
Impact of Cleaning Reusable Applicators with Wet Tissues
In this section, we introduce a further assumption to account for the environmental impact associated with
the cleaning of reusable CGM applicators. This applies exclusively to the Guardian Medtronic system, which
features a reusable applicator. In contrast, the other systems (Abbott Libre 2, Abbott Libre 3, and Dexcom G7)
rely on single-use applicators that are discarded after each use, and therefore do not require cleaning between
applications. The cleaning procedure considered involves the use of a wet tissue after each use of the Medtronic
applicator. The wet tissue was modeled as consisting of:
• 2 grams of tissue paper, disposed with the Danish mix (98% municipal inceneration)
• 3 grams of alcohol, modeled as evaporated and not contributing to recycling streams
This process is repeated for every sensor change over the course of one year, meaning that 52 wet tissues
are consumed. It is important to note that this assumption is intentionally conservative. The official Medtronic
user instructions do not explicitly require disinfection of the applicator after each use; they merely recommend
keeping the applicator clean.
Furthermore, given the nature of the usage and the lack of direct exposure
to biological fluids, it is unlikely that the applicator would require full cleaning with wet tissues after every
use. Nevertheless, to stress-test the environmental benefits of reusability, we adopted a worst-case scenario by

5.3.5
Why not focusing on the CGM sensor?
This chapter presents a critical update to the assumptions made at the outset of the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
study on Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) systems. Initially, it was assumed that the environmental
burden of CGM products stemmed predominantly from the applicator, while the sensor contributed equally
across different products and could be neglected in a comparative LCA. This assumption justified focusing the
eco-design efforts on optimizing the applicator. To challenge this assumption an assessment of the CO2 emission
of the whole different CGM system (including applicators, sensors, charger..) was run with the time-span of 1
year. The modeling in Simapro of the sensors is reported in the Appendix.
Figure 21 illustrates the total carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2 eq.) emissions generated over one year of usage
for four Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) systems: Medtronic Guardian, Dexcom G7, Abbott Libre 2,
and Abbott Libre 3. The emissions are broken down by component: sensors, applicators, transmitter, and
charger.
Figure 21: kg of eq. CO2 emissions for 1 year of CGM usage.
The plot clearly reveals the significant environmental burden posed by the applicators. In particular, the
Dexcom G7 and Abbott Libre 2 systems exhibit high total emissions, largely driven by their single-use appli-
cators. This contrasts with the Medtronic Guardian, where emissions from the applicator are comparatively
minor, since the applicator is reusable.
Another critical insight emerges from the role of the transmitter.
The Medtronic Guardian employs a
modular transmitter that is reused throughout the year, rather than being disposed of with each sensor. This
design drastically reduces the associated emissions. Despite requiring 52 sensors per year, double the amount
used by Abbott Libre 2 and 3 (26 sensors/year), Medtronic still achieves a lower overall environmental impact for
its sensors because the emissions from the transmitter are excluded from the sensor’s emission. This underlines
the value of a modular and reusable transmitter architecture.
This analysis justifies the design priority placed on minimizing the impact of the applicator and the trans-
mitter. These components are the most impactful across all CGM systems analyzed and thus represent the most
effective targets for eco-design interventions. Conversely, the sensor itself, although produced more frequently,
does not dominate the total environmental impact.
Moreover, we do not propose a redesign of the sensor for two main reasons:

1. The sensor is not the primary contributor to emissions in any of the systems examined.
2. Limited technical knowledge is available regarding the internal structure and material composition of the
sensors, making it challenging to formulate a reliable redesign strategy.

5.4
Absolute Sustainability Assessment
This section presents the Absolute Sustainability Assessment for carbon emissions associated with the use of
CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitoring) applicators over a one-year period. The analysis is conducted according
to two allocation principles: economic allocation and grand fathering. The country considered for economic
data and consumption impact is Denmark.
The goal is to evaluate the climate change impact of different CGM applicators and determine whether they
are within the boundaries of absolute sustainability, using the Planetary Boundaries framework. The functional
unit (FU) is defined as one year of CGM sensor application per user.
5.5
Assumptions and Data
• The Planetary Boundary for climate change is set at 2.83E+12 kgCO2e/year globally.
• The world population is assumed to be 8 billion (as of 15.11.2022).
• The economic allocation is based on the GDP PPP per capita for Denmark (361868 DKK/year) and the
average purchase cost of CGM systems over one year (10800 DKK).
• Grand fathering uses the current average per capita footprint in Denmark (10287.36 kgCO2e/year) to
proportionally allocate the carbon budget.
• The average life cycle cost of all CGM systems is assumed to be the same across all options (10800
DKK/year).
• According to the economic allocation, only the Medtronic applicator (1.890 kgCO2e/FU) stays within
the sustainable boundary (10.56839 kgCO2e/FU).
• The Abbott Libre 2, Abbott Libre 3, and Dexcom applicators exceed this threshold, with footprints of
15.300, 11.800, and 27.700 kgCO2e/FU respectively.
• According to the grand fathering allocation, the sustainable boundary is only 0.4062 kgCO2e/FU. None
of the applicators are within this range, including Medtronic.
These results highlight the urgent need for improvement in the carbon efficiency of CGM systems. Even
though Medtronic is the only option aligned with the sustainability target in economic allocation, none of the
33
==PAGE:169==
systems meet the stricter boundary under the grand fathering approach. Therefore, further eco-design strategies
are necessary and will be proposed in the next sections.
Figure 24: Absolute sustainability analysis of CGM applicators under economic allocation and grand fathering
approaches.
5.6
Recommendations
In conclusion, these updated results validate the initial design direction: the environmental impact of the CGM
product is still primarily driven by the applicator rather than the sensor. As such, targeting the applicator
for redesign remains a sound strategy for improving the sustainability of CGM systems.Nevertheless, from the
sensor hotspot analysis, it is evident that the PCBA is by far the most impactful component of the sensor.
Therefore, a secondary design recommendation is to develop a modular transmitter architecture that can be
reused across multiple sensors. This strategy could significantly reduce the environmental burden of the sensor
component by avoiding the need to discard the transmitter after each use.
5.6.1
Effect of Replacing Fossil-Based Plastic with Bio-Based PP
In this section, a scenario is evaluated in which a reference applicator, the Abbott Libre 3 is manufactured
using bio-based polypropylene (PP) instead of traditional fossil-based polymers such as acrylonitrile butadiene
styrene (ABS). This change was motivated by the growing interest in more sustainable polymer alternatives
and is supported by the insights provided in the review article by Hottle et al. [1], which emphasizes the life
cycle environmental benefits of certain bio-based polymers.
The comparison, presented in Figure 25, shows the relative impact across multiple environmental categories
between the original Libre 3 applicator and the same product manufactured entirely from bio-based PP. The
impact results are normalized to 100% for the current Libre 3 version, and the performance of the bio-based
PP version is shown as a percentage of that baseline.
34
==PAGE:170==
Figure 25:
Comparative environmental impacts of the Abbott Libre 3 CGM applicator using bio-based
polypropylene (PP) versus conventional fossil-based plastic (ABS).
The results demonstrate a substantial improvement in the environmental profile of the device when switching
to bio-based PP. In particular, a reduction of approximately 35% in global warming potential (GWP) is observed,
highlighting the significant potential for carbon footprint reduction. This result is consistent with the findings
in the literature, where bio-based plastics such as PLA and TPS have shown favorable GWP performance when
compared to traditional petrochemical plastics [1].
It is therefore recommended to further explore the feasibility of integrating bio-based PP into the produc-
tion of CGM applicators. Given the considerable GWP savings, the transition to bio-based materials could
significantly enhance the environmental performance of CGM systems with minimal trade-offs.
6
Conclusions
This Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) confirms that the design of CGM applicators has a significant impact on the
overall environmental footprint of continuous glucose monitoring systems. Among the assessed solutions, the
Medtronic Guardian reusable applicator shows the lowest environmental impacts over a one-year usage period
across almost all midpoint and endpoint categories. Despite its higher impact per unit—due to the robust
design and heavier packaging—the reduced frequency of replacement allows its total annual impact to remain
substantially lower than the single-use devices.
The single-use applicators, particularly Dexcom G7, exhibit high cumulative environmental impacts due to
the need for frequent replacements, highlighting the disadvantages of disposable systems in terms of sustain-
ability. The Abbott Libre 2 and Libre 3 perform better than Dexcom G7 but remain inferior to the reusable
alternative.
The analysis also explored several sensitivity scenarios, including waste treatment strategies, the addition
of cleaning procedures, and the material substitution with bio-based polymers.
These tests confirmed the
robustness of the findings: Medtronic Guardian consistently outperforms other systems even under conservative
assumptions such as cleaning after every use. Additionally, the use of bio-based polypropylene (PP) instead of
conventional ABS for disposable applicators like Libre 3 can reduce global warming impacts by approximately
35%, supporting the consideration of material substitution as a viable eco-design strategy.
An absolute sustainability assessment further reinforced the importance of minimizing emissions: only the
Medtronic system aligns with the sustainability targets when economic allocation is applied. None of the devices
meet stricter boundaries under the grandfathering approach, indicating a need for more radical improvements
across the industry.
Finally, while sensors contribute to the environmental burden, especially due to electronics like PCBA and
batteries, the applicator remains the primary driver of impacts. Therefore, focusing design and sustainability
35
==PAGE:171==
efforts on the applicator—and when possible, on the modularization of the transmitter—offers the most effective
path toward reducing the environmental footprint of CGM systems.

Participant Profile
Dr. Anna Kamińska is a specialist in internal medicine, an endocrinologist, and a diabetol-
ogist with over 25 years of clinical experience. She serves as an Assistant Professor in the
Department of Endocrinology and Diabetology at the Collegium Medicum in Bydgoszcz,
Poland.
Purpose of This Interview
The interview aimed to explore a clinician’s perspective on the availability, functionality,
barriers, and sustainability of Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) systems in everyday
medical practice.
Question Framework
• Professional Background & Role
• Experiences with CGM Systems
• Eligibility & Access
• Quality & Functionality
• Alternative Glucose-Monitoring Methods
• Sustainability & Medical Ecology
Summary of Key Insights
1. Healthcare-System Variations
• EU device regulations are uniform, but reimbursement rules and clinic work-
flows differ between Poland and Denmark, significantly impacting CGM uptake.
• In Poland, strict reimbursement criteria dictate which devices and which pa-
tients qualify for CGM, creating a systemic barrier to broader adoption.
2. Benefits of CGM
• CGM is considered “the most important technological advancement since the
introduction of the manual glucometer in the 1970s.”
• The technology provides real-time trend data, reduces the burden of finger-
prick testing, and offers hypoglycemia alerts—particularly life-saving for Type
1 diabetes patients.
3. Barriers & Practical Challenges
• Cost & Access: Even with partial reimbursement, ongoing costs can deter
patient uptake.
• Technological Literacy: Older patients often lack compatible smartphones or
are hesitant to adopt new devices.
Design of a CGM Device with a Reduced Environmental Impact

==PAGE:184==
• Technical Limitations: Challenges include sensor adhesion problems, sen-
sitivity to temperature fluctuations, and occasional inaccuracies.
• Administrative Hurdles: Complex reimbursement procedures can delay or
prevent access to CGM devices.
4. Market Dynamics & Device Choice
• Freestyle Libre 2 is commonly used in Poland due to its ease of use, cost-
effectiveness, and simplicity.
• Device choice is heavily influenced by what is covered under national reim-
bursement schemes. Newer models, such as Libre 3, are not yet available in
Poland.
5. Sustainability vs. Practicality
• Single-use sensors dominate the market due to their convenience and the lim-
ited time available for patient education during clinic visits.
• While reusable designs are acknowledged as more environmentally friendly,
clinical priorities currently favour practicality and reliability over ecological con-
cerns.
6. Technological Progress
• CGM represents a paradigm shift in chronic disease management, elevating
patient safety and setting new standards of care despite persistent challenges
in access and implementation.
Notable Quotations
”Continuous Glucose Monitoring has truly revolutionized diabetes management—
especially for Type 1 patients; it might be literally a life-saving device.”
“In practice, immediate convenience and reliability tend to win out over eco-
logical concerns.”
Implications & Reflections
This interview highlighted that policy and reimbursement frameworks are often more de-
cisive than technological capabilities in determining CGM adoption. Despite the clear
clinical benefits of CGM—especially for Type 1 diabetes - cost barriers, administrative
complexity, and practical constraints significantly limit broader use in Poland.
While environmental concerns are acknowledged, they remain secondary to immediate
factors like convenience, patient usability, and system-level efficiency. This suggests that
future innovations in CGM should prioritize designs that balance ecological considerations
with ease of implementation in existing healthcare workflows.
For this thesis, a comparative analysis of reimbursement models between Poland and
Denmark emerges as a critical next step to understand how systemic differences influence
both market dynamics and sustainable design adoption.

UX Test Script – CGM Functionality Test (Prototype)
0. General Information
Test objective: Evaluate the functionality and usability of two CGM prototypes: our de-
sign and a benchmark device (e.g., Dexcom G7). The aim is to assess how users perform
key tasks and what compromises they would be willing to accept in favour of a lower en-
vironmental footprint. The tester will not be explicitly informed which device is our design
to avoid bias. The benchmark model (Dexcom G7) will be presented first to simulate a
situation in which a user familiar with existing technology and its simplicity is transitioning
to a new device.
Methodology: Think Aloud Protocol + in-depth interview. The tester verbalises all thoughts
while interacting with the device. The session is audio recorded and later analysed based
on the transcript. The interview forms the basis for a questionnaire used in further analy-
sis.
1. Initial Description and Contextual Introduction (to be read to the tester)
Hello! Thank you for taking part in this short test. We are testing two de-
vices used for Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM). They are designed for
people with Type 1 diabetes – a chronic condition requiring daily blood sugar
monitoring.
These devices are more than gadgets – they are life-saving tools. They op-
erate 24/7, sending glucose level data to, for example, a smartphone app,
helping to better adjust insulin doses and warning about hypoglycaemia or hy-
perglycaemia. For people with Type 1 diabetes, such a device is a constant
companion – it cannot be set aside, it must be used for life.
Most CGM devices are designed for patients to use independently at home,
in the same way they self-administer insulin – without a doctor’s assistance.
The sensor has limitations due to the body’s natural reaction to a foreign object
(the measurement probe), requiring replacement every 14 days.
In this test, I will show you two models representing two different types of
CGM devices. One will represent disposable products requiring fewer steps
during self-application. The other will have some reusable elements – but with
certain compromises in convenience. I would like to find out whether these
compromises are acceptable from the user’s perspective.
The models you see are simple prototypes meant to represent the main func-
tions of the device. The final product will differ in appearance, use higher-
quality materials, and not be 3D printed. The prototypes also have limitations
due to their simplified construction – for example, this element cannot be ro-
tated.
In this test, we will use the “Thinking out loud” method. Remember to verbalise
your thoughts – explain what you are doing, what you are thinking, what is clear
and what is confusing. Please do not try to be nice to our design, and do not
evaluate yourself – I am interested in your thought process.

4. Comparison and Preferences
