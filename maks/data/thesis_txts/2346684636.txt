Abstract
Two-dimensional semiconductors are subject to a lot of research, as it is possible to
engineer their electronic structure properties, e.g. by doping or stacking in van der
Waals heterostructures.
Additionally, machine learning is a ﬁeld which is starting to gain traction within
computational materials science, as it provides a synergy with quantum theory to
accelerate the screening speed of large material databases.
In this thesis, electronic structure theory will be combined with machine learning.
The ﬁrst part of this thesis regards predicting formation of deep gap states in mono-
layer 2D semiconductors when a vacancy point defect is introduced to the system.
This part is a continuation of the work by Pandey et al. in ref. [8], in which a de-
scriptor was developed to predict the formation of deep gap states using the projected
density of states for transition metal dichalcogenides by considering the projected
density of states.
Based on this descriptor, three alternatives are formulated and
tested on halides as well as chalcogenides. However, it is found that only considering
the density of states is probably insuﬃcient to accurately describe all the included
systems. Machine learning is therefore employed, which ﬁnds that the amount of ma-
terials that are used is not enough compared to the complexity of the problem, but
shows promise if the database could be expanded.
The second part of this thesis regards predicting the dipole correction of 2D semi-
conductor bilayers with machine learning using electronic structure properties of the
individual monolayers. It is found that the machine learning model is able to ﬁnd a
good correlation between the position of the individual monolayer band edges and the
corresponding bilayer dipole correction, which is further improved when the range of
the monolayer wave functions in the out-of-plane direction is included.

Resumé
Todimensionelle halvledere er eftertragtede, da det er muligt at konstruere deres elek-
troniske egenskaber ved brug af for eksempel dotering eller stabling af van der Waals
heterostrukturer.
Endvidere, er maskinlæring et felt, som er begyndt at blive anvendt i højere grad
inden for teoretisk materialefysik, da det kan bruges i synergi med kvanteteori til
hurtigere at søge igennem store mængder af strukturer.
I denne afhandling vil eketronstrukturmetoder blive kombineret med maskinlæring.
Den første del af afhandlingen omhandler bestemmelsen af, hvorvidt der opstår til-
stande dybt i båndgabet på en 2D halvleder, når der introduceres en vacance defect.
Denne del af afhandlingen er en fortsættelse af det udførte arbejde af Pandey et
al., i henvisning [8]. Her udvikles en deskriptor, som korrekt kan forudsige, hvornår
der opstår defekter i båndgabet ved at der indføres en chalcogen vacance i over-
gangsmetaldichalcogener ved at anvende den projekterede tæthed af tilstande. Denne
deskriptor danner basis for tre udvidede modeller i denne afhandling, som afprøves
på en større klasse af systemer. Det viser sig dog, at den projekterede tæthed af
tilstande sandsynligvis er utilstrækkelig til korrekt at forudsige disse defekttilstande
i båndgabet for alle de afprøvede systemer. Derfor blev maskinlæring anvendt som
et alternativ, hvilket dog løb ind i problemer, grundet en relativt lille database af
materialer, men viste dog potentiale under forudsætningen af at databasen vil kunne
udvides.
Den anden del af afhandlingen omhandler, at forudsige dipolkorrektionen for 2D
halvledende dobbeltlag strukturer med maskinlæring ved brug af de elektroniske egen-
skaber fra de individuelle enkeltlag. Denne model fandt en god korrelation mellem
båndkanterne for de isolerede enkeltlag og den beregnede dipolkorrektion for dobbelt-
laget, som yderligere kunne forbedres ved at inkludere rækkeviden af bølgefunktion-
erne ud-af-planen.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Engineering properties of 2D semiconductors is a ﬁeld which has lately been gaining
a lot of popularity, such as for transistors and photodetection. [9, 10, 11] Transistors
play a large roll in our modern technological society, as it is an essential component in
any computer, from smartphones to supercomputers. These properties can be tuned
by e.g. applying strain, [12], or the stacking of van der Waals heterostructures, [13],
which is a technique often compared to the stacking of Lego blocks.
Recently, with the increase in available computer power, machine learning in mate-
rials science has also been gaining attention in order to search through large material
spaces in short periods of time to engineer speciﬁc properties. An illustration of ma-
chine learning working together with quantum theory calculations is illustrated in
ﬁg. 1.1. The quantum calculations are based on the atomic (and electron) positions,
while the machine learning is trained on a set of attribute vectors, which e.g. could
be designed with quantum calculations. [14, 15]
In the work presented by Pandey1 et al. in ref. [8], they develop a descriptor to
predict the formation of deep gap states in the band gap in 2D semiconductors when
introduced to defects. It will be shown in this thesis, however, that this approach does
not necessarily extend well to other systems. Therefore, the descriptor is expanded
in order to describe this larger class of systems. Additionally, machine learning is
adopted in order to predict deep gap states using electronic structure properties. In
this regard, the machine learning algorithm known as the support vector machine
for classiﬁcation will be derived, as this will be the primary learning algorithm used
in this thesis, and as large part of the carried out work has been to learn the ﬁeld
of machine learning. The machine learning algorithms will initially be applied to a
simple model, and then expand the model to include density functional theory (DFT)
calculations.

Density Functional Theory
Even the electronic Hamiltonian (2.3) is simply too complex to solve in real systems
due to the electron-electron interactions, which where DFT enters the picture.

object, which, for any real system, is a lot smaller than the dimensionality of the
electronic wave function. In 1964 Walter Kohn and Pierre Hohenberg formulated and
proved two central theorems [1, 21]

2.2.2
Exchange-Correlation Functionals
A series of ϵxc functionals already exist, however, only a few of them will be presented
here. In practice a "one-size-ﬁts all" functional does not exist, but they are instead
created in such a way that they ﬁt a particular problem, and thus they are each subject

Discussion of the PBE Functional
The PBE functional is known to have several issues, such as tending to underestimate
the size of the bandgap, and it has a signiﬁcant self-interaction error, which is prob-
lematic as that will aﬀect the description of localized states, e.g. defect states in a
band gap. [8]
It is nevertheless the preferred functional, due to the speed of execution. The PBE
is known to give a qualitatively good picture of the band structure, however it does
not provide a good qualitative picture, meaning that for instance if the PBE found a
defect state in a band gap, the exact energy would be unreliable.

Defects in Semiconductors
A semiconductor is characterized by having a band gap, which, however, assumes a
perfect structure. In a laboratory, it is nearly impossible to create such a perfect
structure, i.e. defects will almost always be present in some shape or form. These de-
fects can introduce states deep in the gap, which can act as recombination centers, i.e.
a state which electrons can use as an intermediary between going from the conduction
band to the valence band and thereby increase the transition rate. Additionally, the
defect can also decrease the mobility of charge carriers by acting as local scattering
centers. [8, 27] Defects exist in many shapes, such as point defects (e.g. vacancies,
interstitial defects), line defects (e.g. edge dislocations, screw dislocations), or more
"exotic" defects such as the Stone-Wales defects. [4, 28, 29]
In this work, vacancy defects will be systematically explored, as a continuation of
the work presented by Pandey et al. in ref. [8], which explored the creation of deep gap
states with chalcogen vacancies in transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) monolayers.
This study will be continued onto the related category of TMDs, exchanging the
group 16 chalcogen atoms with group 17 halide atoms, as well as expanding the group
M atoms from transition metals to also include a larger group of metals, such as
metalloids.
Pandey et al. created a descriptor called the normalized orbital overlap (NOO),
which was developed to quantify the anti-bonding and bonding character between
the valence and conduction bands to predict deep gap states. This descriptor showed
good performance on the TMDs. However, it will be shown in this work that it does
not work as well when oxygen and the expanded set of metals are added, and neither
does it accurately predict defect tolerance for halides. Therefore, the NOO will lay
the basis for three diﬀerent descriptors in order to generalize the model.
As the number of systems is fairly large, the LCAO method was used due to its
speed, coupled with the PBE exchange-correlation functional.

3.1.4
Formation Energies of Vacancies
The energy required to create a vacancy defect corresponds to taking an atom and
isolating it from the layer. Thus, the defect formation energy, ϵform of an atom a in a
pristine system into the system with a defect is
ϵform = ϵpristine −ϵdefect −ϵa,
(3.1)
where ϵpristine is the total energy of the pristine system, ϵdefect is the total energy of
the system with the defect and ϵa is the energy of the isolated atom.
It turns out that this formation energy is correlated to the size of the band gap for
the MX2 halides, see ﬁg. 3.6, which can be understood from the following simpliﬁed
chemical picture: as the halide atoms are in the 17th group in the periodic table, they
each accept 1 electron from the metal atoms. When the halide atom is removed, the
donated electron will no longer have any orbitals in the valence band to go to, and
instead it is forced to go to the conduction band, meaning that in order to remove a
halide atom, an electron must ﬁrst be excited into the conduction band, giving rise
to this correlation.

that a more stable conﬁguration would be the p-doped vacancy defect, i.e. where the
excess electron is removed from the system along with the halide. This could e.g. be
realised by adding a bulk reservoir, allowing the excess electron to go to the Fermi
level of the connected bulk system, assuming this Fermi level is within the band gap
of the semiconductor.

considered.
None of these descriptors manage an overall accuracy which is satisfactory for a
screening model, and therefore machine learning is adopted as an alternate method.
3.5
Discussion and Outlook
There is a heavy bias towards the amount of defect sensitive materials, which makes it
diﬃcult to determine the exact accuracy of the developed models, but it is clear that
they cannot perfectly distinguish the defect sensitivity. The problem is very complex,
and it is therefore likely that a perfect descriptor cannot be created from such a
simpliﬁed chemical bonding picture, as solids are generally much more complicated in
nature than molecules, from which the bonding and antibonding picture originates.
The KS band structures are not entirely accurate, which can give rise to incor-
rect defect state positioning in the band gap, potentially causing a material to be
misclassiﬁed as tolerant when it is in fact defect sensitive or vice versa. The PBE
functional also does not predict very good band gap edge positions, which in princi-
ple then could be inferred from experiments or from more accurate methods, such as
GW or the Slater-Janack theory. Additionally, due to self-interaction errors, the PBE
functional is known to push the energy levels of localized states, such as defect states,
up in energy. [8, 31]
The overlap matrix, Sµν, was disregarded for simplicity, however, could potentially
change the character of a band. A short preliminary study of applying Sµν showed
that the overall picture remains similar and therefore will therefore not improve the
descriptors by a signiﬁcant margin, although in principle a full implementation would
be required as a veriﬁcation.

as a derivation of the support vector machine which is a commonly used algorithm.
This derivation is comprised of parts from refs [14, 5, 6], along with inspiration from
online machine learning lectures given at MIT1 and Caltech2.
Due to the simplicity and ease of implementation, the scikit-learn python package
is used as the machine learning backend which allows simple setups of a wide range of
algorithms, combined with a series of techniques to transform and analyze the data.
[32, 33]
4.1
An Introduction to Machine Learning
Machine learning is used in many areas, e.g. when companies such as Amazon or
Google attempts to target products and advertisements when customers are browsing
their website or when Youtube suggests new videos based on your watch history. In
its core essence, machine learning is simply an advanced form of statistical regression.
With the amount of data being produced on a daily basis skyrocketing, as we are
entering an era of big data, machine learning has been developed as a way to construct
meaningful analyses from these enormous amounts of data and uncover the unseen
patterns. [5, 6]
Machine learning can be split into two categories: supervised learning and unsuper-
vised learning, and within each group exists a large number of models and techniques.

Model Selection
Both categories take an input, known as the training data, which is the input in the
model, but the shape of the training data diﬀers for the two categories. In supervised
learning, the input data, or training data, consists of input values (e.g. the atomic
structure) and a corresponding set of output values (e.g. a certain property of the ma-
terial). The goal of the supervised learning model is then to ﬁt a function through the
input values in order to predict the output values, just like the classic least-squares-ﬁt,
known from statistical regression. The input to an unsupervised learning algorithm
has no output values, and the goal of the algorithm is instead to ﬁnd the patterns in
the input values. [14] In this work, only the supervised algorithms will be considered.
Within the framework of supervised algorithms, the models can further be divided
into two groups: regression and classiﬁcation. Regression models seek to ﬁt a function
with a continuous output, e.g. like in linear regression, while classiﬁcation models
seek to divide the output into discrete sets, e.g. whether a structure is defect tolerant
("true") or defect sensitive ("false").
4.2
Model Selection
Even when constricted to the supervised learning category, the amount of models is
fairly large. Each model is a mathematical construction, and not a model which is
created to ﬁt a certain problem, meaning that there does not exist a universally best
model, which is also known as the no free lunch theorem, coined by Wolpert in 1996.
The assumptions which comprise a given model may ﬁt one problem well, but work
poorly on a diﬀerent problem. [6, 34]
A common way of determining a good model is to estimate the prediction error,
i.e. quantifying a model’s future performance, which for instance can be done by
employing a technique known as cross validation.
4.2.1
Cross Validation
In the cross validation scheme, the model is trained on a part of the data, and then
it attempts to predict the output of the remaining data points. In practice, a method
called k-fold cross validation is used where the samples are split into k groups, and
then the model is trained onto all except one of the groups, and the last group is used
for validation, i.e. the model is used to predict onto the samples in the last group, see
ﬁg. 4.1(a). This process is repeated where each group is left out once, which ensures
that every sample is used for validation exactly once. [6]
A special case of this scheme, is when k = N, i.e. when the number of folds equals
the number of data points, which is known as a Leave-One-Out (LOO) algorithm,
where the model is trained on all the data points except i, and then tested on i.

Support Vector Machines
One of the most popular machine learning techniques, and the technique which will
be employed the most in this work, is the support vector machine (SVM).

4.4
A Simple Model
Designing the input to a machine, is a highly non-trivial process. The inputs must
be suﬃcient in order to describe the systems, but not too complicated, otherwise the
model will never be able to learn anything useful. Therefore, an initial simple model
is used, to predict the deep gap states in semiconductors. Some of the most simple
parameters which could be used to describe an atomic structure, is inputs from the
periodic table, as these are simply look-up values. Additionally, two diﬀerent binary
classiﬁers are used to identify the structures: whether the structure is MX2 or MX3
and the phase, i.e. if it is 1T or 2H.
A machine learning algorithm learns by ﬁguring out similarities between diﬀerent
data point entries. Similarly, one would expect to be able to learn more about elements
which are closer in the periodic table, e.g. for Ti and Zr to be more similar than Ti
and Cl, thus the group and period are used as the periodic table "coordinates".

4.5
Designing an Electronic Structure Fingerprint
Vector
The defect states in the gap are a result of electronic structure properties, and there-
fore it makes sense to construct an input vector, also called a ﬁngerprint, based on
data from DFT calculations. In this section, it will be discussed how such a ﬁngerprint
vector can be designed and targeted for predicting deep gap states in semiconductors
using the PDOS with the ideas presented in sec. 3.2.
The PDOS includes the projection of states onto each atom in the cell, meaning
that this must be an index in the ﬁngerprint vector. Therefore, an algorithm is applied
to sort out sets of equivalent atoms in the system, as these will have identical PDOS
spectra. The reasoning for identifying the unique atoms is to ensure that whether a
system is in a supercell or primitive cell conﬁguration, it should result in identical
ﬁngerprint vectors. Identifying the unique atoms is done by calculating the PDOS for
each atom, and testing whether the densities at a given energy, ϵi, is within a given
tolerance, τ,
|ρa,l(ϵi) −ρb,l(ϵi)| < τ,
(4.20)
where a and b denote diﬀerent atomic indices, and l denotes the angular momentum,
i.e. s (l = 0), p (l = 1) or d (l = 2) orbitals. By comparing a supercell to a primitive

4.5.1
A Reduced Fingerprint
The ﬁngerprint from eq. (4.22) easily becomes extremely long, especially for larger
systems. Therefore, a slightly simpler ﬁngerprint is also suggested which is based on
the NOO. Rather than binning the energy window, the average of a smaller energy
window is used in both the valence and conduction band, which eﬀectively corresponds
to creating two bins which results in a dimension of 6n, where n is the number of
unique atoms.

in Bilayers
Stacking 2D materials in van der Waals heterostructures (vdWhs) is a technique in
materials design which allows for tailoring devices with speciﬁc properties. In a study
by S. Latini1 et al. in ref. [16], it was found that the band structure of such a vdWhs
bilayer could be calculated from the isolated constituent monolayers, but with an
asymmetric shift in energies, which was attributed to the formation of an electronic
density dipole at the interface, see ﬁg. 5.1. The fact that this asymmetric shift is
caused by the electronic density was shown by doing a non-self consistent calculation,
i.e.
combining the two monolayers while ﬁxing the density, such that there is no
charge transfer between the layers, which can be seen in ﬁg. 5.2.
The dipole is induced by the misalignment of the band gap centers of the two
layers which gives rise to an electronic charge transfer, causing a shift in the elec-
trostatic potential, creating a dipole. The shift in the potential is called the dipole
correction (DPC) which can be obtained from a full self-consistent DFT calculation on
the bilayer, which, however, is computationally expensive when using more advanced
techniques, such as GW or meta-GGA’s instead of the LDA or PBE functional for
large amounts of systems. Since the dipole is caused by the misalignment of the band
gaps, one can instead employ machine learning to predict the DPC based from calcu-
lations of the individual monolayers, as these calculations are considerably cheaper.
Additionally, this machine could possibly be trained on a dataset of cheaper calcu-
lations, such as LDA as is done in this work, and then be applied onto monolayer
calculations using the more expensive techniques.
As the DPC is a continuous real number, classiﬁcation techniques are not appli-
cable, but instead regression can be used. The SVM classiﬁcation algorithm shown in

Chapter 6
Conclusions and Outlook
6.1
Conclusion
In the ﬁrst part of the project, the NOO was shown to have trouble with both the
halides and expanded chalcogen category, where oxygen and larger group of metals
beyond the transition metals were added. The binning, HSC and BC descriptors were
developed as possible generalizations of the NOO, however did not manage to overall
describe all of the used 2D semiconductors well at the same time, whereas the NOO
for the TMDs showed a 100% accuracy, as was reported by Pandey et al. An accuracy
of 92 % for the MX2 halides was observed with the binning descriptor which showed
the most promise for this class, although surprisingly did not describe the TMDs
particularly well, even though the binning descriptor was derived as the most similar
to the NOO.
Machine learning was then adopted as an alternative approach, where initially the
SVM classiﬁer algorithm was derived. A simple model was initially attempted, based
solely on the type of the 2D semiconductor as well as the position of the chemical
elements in the periodic table which comprised the structure. This model, however,
could not predict better than the baseline, which states that all of the materials
were defect sensitive. A ﬁngerprint was then developed based on the PDOS spectra,
inspired by the binning approach from the descriptors. This ﬁngerprint could not
accurately predict on all of the systems combined, but managed to do slightly better
than the baseline when only considering the three classes separately, the MX2 and MX3
halides as well as the MX2 chalcogenides. One issue with this approach, however, was
the large number of dimensions in the ﬁngerprint vector compared to the relatively
small sample size and complex problem, which led to a simpliﬁed ﬁngerprint that
used a similar approach as the NOO, where only a single energy window around the
valence and conduction band respectively was considered. This approach showed good
61
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 6.
Conclusions and Outlook
promise for the individual structural categories, but would also beneﬁt from a larger
structural database, particularly with the Gaussian kernel.
It was concluded that the task of predicting deep gap states in 2D semiconductors
is a very challenging task, and the simple picture of a pair of bonding and anti-bonding
conduction and valence bands is likely not enough to fully explain the formation of
these deep gap states.
Machine learning was then applied to the problem of predicting the electrostatic
dipole correction induced by a density dipole using the ϵ-SVR algorithm. The model
was initially applied using only the isolated monolayer band edges, which proved to
accurately predict type 3 bilayers, but which, however, proved more diﬃcult for type
1 and 2. Including the range of the monolayer wave functions in the z-direction (out-
of-plane) was shown to increase the accuracy by 30%, which is a signiﬁcant increase.
It was also observed that using a split estimator, i.e. training a model for each of the
types, did not increase the accuracy by a signiﬁcant margin.
A parameter optimization for the SVR was performed with the Gaussian kernel,
which saw very little overall increase in the accuracy of the model. Additionally, the
linear kernel did not come close to the Gaussian kernel in performance, while other
machine learning models, such as the decision tree, could not outperform the SVR
with a Gaussian kernel either.
The dipole correction prediction model showed overall good promise, although a
main point of focus for improving the model should be made at the small DPC values,
in particular for the regions where the bilayer is of type 1 or 2, as this is where the
model had the most diﬃculty in making accurate predictions. Meanwhile, for type
3 the model was successful with R2 values of up to 0.98 for the isolated type 3, and
0.96 when including all structures, which overall has to be considered a good result.
6.2
Outlook
As mentioned previously, the overlap matrix should be implemented in the developed
descriptors, in order to verify that the picture is still consistent.
A choice was made to set the energy window for the three descriptors to the
bandgap, however this could be changed, e.g. to include a larger energy window if
the band gap is small. Additionally, it was shown that a larger training set would
increase the accuracy of the model, which was presented in ﬁg. 4.8(b) (the reduced
ﬁngerprint for the SVM classiﬁer with the Gaussian kernel).
In order to obtain the DPC, it is required that the Kohn-Sham equations are
solved self-consistently, meaning that techniques such as G0W0 (non self-consistent
GW) cannot be used. As GPAW does not currently have self-consistent GW, nor self-
consistent HSE (a meta-GGA), the model developed in chapter 5 cannot be applied

using G0W0, the DPC is required to validate the model when calculating band edges
using other techniques. Therefore, it would be desirable to test such a machine in
future work, either when GPAW has implemented the more advanced self-consistent
methods, or using a diﬀerent code entirely.
The small artiﬁcial density dipole, which is created by asymmetric 1T bilayers,
should also be corrected, which would decrease some of the random noise in the DFT
calculations when obtaining the DPC, which was not done due to time constraints.
