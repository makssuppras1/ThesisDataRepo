Abstract
This thesis proposes a new method for identifying childhood leukemias, specifically
Acute Myeloid Leukemia, using unsupervised deep learning techniques. The study
aims to develop an RNA-sequencing model that can accurately classify different sub-
types of leukemia by extracting biologically significant features.
To enhance the
interpretability of the model, the study employs Saliency Maps and Gene Set Enrich-
ment Analysis. The proposed method uses autoencoders, which can create explain-
able methods for leukemia classification. The study identifies biological pathways in
healthy datasets and transfers these findings as a disease classifier to distinguish AML
subtypes accurately. The results of this study have the potential to improve diagnos-
tic precision for childhood leukemia and could significantly impact clinical practices
and patient outcomes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The ever-increasing volumes of sequencing data present both an opportunity and a
challenge. While this data is being produced constantly, there is a significant gap in
the availability of eﬀicient algorithms and heuristics for its interpretation. This gap
obstructs the practical application of sequencing data in clinical decision-making and
research, particularly in understanding disease mechanisms. Furthermore, effectively
utilizing the existing data can be crucial for interpreting the genetic information of
new patients.
Therefore, there is a pressing need for new methods to analyze and interpret these
substantial volumes of data. Specifically, generalized and explainable methods are
essential to enhance clinical diagnostics and aid in treatment decisions. Explainable
and interpretable unsupervised deep learning models, like autoencoders, have the
potential to reveal new insights into the fundamental mechanisms of diseases. This
can be valuable for both research and practical clinical applications.
The project is focused on Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML), aiming to build a
robust interpretable RNA-sequencing model using unsupervised deep learning tech-
niques on large datasets.
This model will provide a meaningful representation of
fundamental molecular biological mechanisms and serve as a basis for a disease clas-
sifier, with the final intent of creating a basis for the delineation of some subtypes of
AML.
The main goal is to extract biologically meaningful features from the model us-
ing an autoencoder with orthogonality constraints on the loss function. The model
is trained with healthy hematopoietic stem cell RNA sequencing data and is then
interpreted using Saliency Maps and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis to annotate the
hidden units.
The secondary objective is to develop a model for classifying leukemia patients
into different subtypes. This model will be supervised and built upon the encoder
portion of the already-trained autoencoder.
In more specific terms, this thesis aims to address the following key aspects:
• The feasibility of developing explainable methods using autoencoders with or-
thogonality constraints and whether orthogonality directly contributes to the
model’s explainability.
• Identifying biological pathways associated with three AML cases to enhance
understanding of the molecular functioning of these leukemias.

1.1
Biological Background
Leukemia is a varied group of hematologic malignancies originating in the dysfunc-
tional and excessive proliferation of hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow. It
is classified based on the proliferation speed into acute or chronic and, depending on
the type of dysfunctional cell, into myelocytic or lymphocytic leukemia [1].
This project focuses on AML, characterized by clonal expansion of immature blast
cells in the peripheral blood and bone marrow, resulting in ineffective production of
erythrocytes and bone marrow failure [2].
More specifically, the focus will only be on delineating the following three kinds
of AML subtypes: Acute Erythroid Leukemia (AEL), Acute Myeloid Leukemia with
myelodysplasia-related changes (AML-MRC), and Acute Myeloid Leukemia not oth-
erwise specified (AML-NOS). Myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS) samples will also be
used in this project because these neoplasms are associated with an increased risk of
AML [3].
AEL is a rare and aggressive form of acute leukemia; it represents around 1%
of AML cases.
Given that there is no standard therapy for this subtype, it has
a poor prognosis with a median survival of 3 months.
It is characterized as an
uncontrolled proliferation of maturation-arrested early erythroid precursors.
The
molecular mechanisms that mediate the characteristics of this type of leukemia are
largely unknown [4].
AML-MRC represents around 20% of AML cases, and it is defined as acute
leukemia with ≥20% blast in the peripheral blood or bone marrow with morpho-
logical features of myelodysplasia [5]. It is generally associated with poor prognosis,
especially in cases where patients have an association with secondary diseases and
unfavorable cytogenetic features.
In almost half of the cases of AML-MRC, it is
common in patients with a prior history of MDS [6].
AML-NOS refers to all the cases of AML that do not fulfill the criteria for classi-
fication in one of the more specified AML subtypes [7].
Myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS) are clonal hematopoietic malignancies that
cause morphologic bone marrow dysplasia. An increased risk of AML transformation
characterizes it and has a survival rate of 5 years [3].
Over the years, there has been a lack of consensus on correctly classifying AML
subtypes, and the guidelines have been changed and updated multiple times. The
most problematic subtype is AEL because the parameters for its classification have
changed considerably in each newly revised classification, and there are disagreements
on whether it should even be considered a subtype of AML [8]
The 2016 revised World Health Organization classification of myeloid neoplasms
and acute leukemia [7] divides AML into more than 20 subtypes. Although AML

Computational Background
1.2.1
Feedforward Neural Networks
Feedforward Neural networks (FNN) are one of the simplest forms of neural network
models, where the information moves through the network unidirectionally, and there
are no feedback connections forming cycles or loops in the network. FNNs are orga-
nized in a multi-layer structure, each containing a set of neurons. Neurons within a
layer are connected to those in the adjacent layers, forming a network. The first layer
corresponds to the input, while the last one is to the output; all the layers in between
are called hidden layers. The connections between neurons have associated weights,
representing the strength of the link between neurons [13,14].
The learning process of FNN consists of a series of iterations following three steps:
forward propagation, loss calculation, and backpropagation. The process of forward
propagation involves a series of nonlinear transformations. In a fully connected net-
work, each neuron calculates a weighted sum of the outputs of the neurons of the
preceding layer. This sum then passes through a nonlinear activation function to
determine the neuron’s output [13,15].
When the forward propagation step is finished, the loss is calculated. Depend-
ing on the purpose of the model, its design, and the type of data used, the FNN
needs a cost function to be optimized. This corresponds to the model’s loss function,
quantifying the difference between the model prediction and the actual value.
The subsequent phase is backpropagation, the core learning mechanism of the au-
toencoder, during which the network adjusts its weights in response to the loss [16].
This involves calculating the gradient of the loss function with respect to each weight
and then updating the weights to minimize the loss. The direction of weight adjust-
ment aligns with the calculated gradient. This optimization algorithm to minimize
the error in the network’s output by adjusting its weight is called Gradient Descent.
1.2.1.1
Stochastic Gradient Descent
Gradient descent is a machine learning method used to find the best solution to a
problem. It starts with a random guess and then gradually improves it by moving in
the direction that reduces errors. Think of it as trying to find the lowest point in a
valley: Start somewhere on the slope, figure out which way is downhill, and take a
step in that direction. This process is repeated, going downhill until the minimum
value is reached, representing the best solution to your problem. This process helps
to adjust model parameters and minimize prediction errors.
The optimization algorithm may not be guaranteed to arrive at even a local min-
imum in a reasonable time. Still, it often finds a significantly low value for the cost
function quickly enough to be useful. The standard gradient descent algorithm is
computationally expensive, and in some cases, it has problems converging. Stochas-
tic gradient descent (SGD) is a variation of the standard gradient descent and is more

small batch of data points in each iteration instead of the entire dataset. As a result,

1.2.2
Autoencoders
An autoencoder is an unsupervised FNN designed to encode the input into a com-
pressed and meaningful representation, automatically learning features from unla-
beled data. Its purpose is to then decode this latent representation to output data
that closely matches the original input.. [17,18]
They were first introduced in 1986 [19, 20] as a tool for nonlinear dimensional-
ity reduction, mainly a nonlinear extension of principal component analysis (PCA).
Presently, they are designed to uncover the underlying structure of the data by disen-
tangling sources of variation in the input. This way learning the data’s most valuable
and essential properties. The autoencoder has to prioritize which input aspects should
be kept and learn the most valuable data properties. The resulting latent representa-
tion can retrieve relevant information when building classifiers or other predictors [21].
At first, generating a copy of the input might seem pointless. But in autoencoders,
the important part is not the decoder’s output but its latent representation. The
goal is that by teaching the autoencoder to make this copy, these hidden features will
capture the most critical aspects of the data.
The typical architecture of an autoencoder consists of two main components, an
encoder, and a decoder, each with its own set of learnable parameters. Given an input
x, the encoding part of the network, f(x), reduces the dimensionality of the input data
into a latent space representation. This representation layer holds the input data’s
most important aspects or features. The decoding part of the autoencoder, g(f(x)),
does the opposite of the encoder, takes encoded data, and reconstructs it as closely
as possible to the original input. Figure 1.1 shows an illustrative representation of
the autoencoder model.
Depending on the input data type and the autoencoder model’s purpose, these
components can be implemented using a custom design. However, it is essential to
know that increasing the architecture complexity does not always result in an autoen-
coder that can lead to a more complex representation of the data [22]. Regardless of
the specifics of the autoencoder architecture, its principal objective stays the same:
to reconstruct its input as accurately as possible.
The following sections go into more detail on the specific components of the archi-
tecture, their purpose, and mathematical principles.

1.2.2.3
Regularization
In machine learning, regularization refers to techniques used to prevent overfitting.
Overfitting is when a model learns only the particulars of the training dataset with-
out generalizing well enough to perform well in test or unseen data. This is why
regularization strategies are used to avoid or minimize the risk of overfitting by pe-
nalizing complex models and encouraging the model to learn more general patterns
from the training data. Some regularization methods, like L1 regularization, add an
extra term to the loss function; others, like weight decay, add a parameter to the
gradient descent calculation.
One of the most critical constraints of an autoencoder is to prevent the encoding
and decoding functions from just learning the identity function, which would output
the exact copy of the input. A bottleneck layer is the most common way to add
the regularization needed to avoid this. This means a lower dimensionality that the
input data is imposed in the latent space. Autoencoders with this architecture are
called undercomplete. This regularization forces the encoder to compress the data.
As a result, it represents the data in a reduced dimensionality, facilitating feature
extraction.
L2 regularization, commonly known as weight decay, keeps the weights low. This
additional parameter is added to the gradient descent to aid in the regularization of
the weights of the autoencoder by penalizing large weights in the network. As a result,
the model becomes more independent from the training data and can better generalize
to unseen data. The weight decay regularization is added to the loss function.
L1, or Lasso regularization, adds a penalty equal to the absolute value of the
magnitude of the weights to the loss function, penalizing the model for large weights.
The main characteristic of L1 regularization is that it can lead to sparse models,
where some of the weights become precisely zero. This property is particularly useful
in feature selection or dimensionality reduction, as it can help identify the most
significant features in the data.
In autoencoders, applying L1 regularization can
encourage the model to represent each input with a small number of active neurons,
potentially leading to more eﬀicient and interpretable representations.

1.2.2.5
Training
Since autoencoders are a particular case of feedforward neural networks, they can
use the same techniques for training. Training consists of iteratively adjusting the
weights between the neurons of the whole network.
The learning process can be
described as an optimization problem, finding the network’s weight that minimizes
the loss function.
The training phase of an autoencoder begins with the initialization of weights.
These weights are typically set randomly or using a specific method like Xavier ini-
tialization. This sets the stage for effective learning during the training process.
Next is the forward pass step. Here, the input data is fed into the autoencoder. A
weighted sum of inputs is calculated and then passed through the neuron’s activation
function for each neuron. The output of one layer becomes the input for the next,
allowing the data to propagate through the autoencoder.
Once the forward pass is completed, the loss is calculated. This measures the
reconstruction error, serving as a critical indicator of the autoencoder’s performance.
Finally, this entire process, comprising forward pass, loss calculation, and back-
propagation, is repeated multiple times. With each iteration, the autoencoder learns
more about the data’s structure and characteristics, improving its ability to recon-
struct the input data accurately.
1.2.2.6
Hyperparameter Optimization
Hyperparameter optimization is finding the best (most effective) combinations of
hyperparameters for a model. Hyperparameters are parameters independent of the
training and a set of external configurations. There are a large number of hyper-
parameters to consider when designing the architecture of an autoencoder. These
parameters include the number of layers, the number of neurons in each layer, the
==PAGE:22==

learning rate, the weight decay value, and other regularization hyperparameters. This
results in an enormous list of possible combinations.
It is a complex optimization process; some automated hyperparameter optimiza-
tion methods are grid search, random search, and Bayesian optimization.
Bayesian optimization builds a probabilistic model of the function, mapping the
hyperparameters to a performance metric. The iterative process starts with initial
random sampling and then uses the probabilistic model to predict which hyperpa-
rameters might perform the best, evaluates the performance, and updates the model.
This method is particularly eﬀicient in finding the best hyperparameters with fewer
evaluations compared to grid or random search.
1.2.2.7
Pruning
Pruning is a machine-learning technique that diminishes the model’s size and complex-
ity by eliminating some parameters. Weight pruning is the most common technique
and consists of removing (setting to zero) some of the network’s weights. Magnitude-
based pruning removes the weights with the smallest magnitudes.
There is structured and unstructured pruning. The former eliminates whole struc-
tures or groups of parameters from the models; this is primarily used in convolutional
neural networks. The latter only eliminates individual parameters from the model,
which is suitable for fully connected networks. Unstructured pruning tends to be
more effective and permits more fine-grained pruning.
1.2.3
UMAP plots
A Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) plot is a type of visual-
ization used for dimensionality reduction [23]. It is particularly effective in displaying
high-dimensional data, like single-cell RNA sequencing, in a lower-dimensional space.
UMAP plots can reveal clusters of cells with similar gene expression profiles, aiding
in identifying different cell types or states based on their genetic characteristics.
1.2.4
Saliency Maps
For the use of neural networks in clinical settings, there is a need for transparency on
how models work. The interpretability of the models is key for their use in clinical
classification. Given the need to use methods to explain the neural network model,
in this project, Saliency Maps are used as a bridge between the underlying patterns
described by the model and the biological functions active in the gene expression
dataset.
Saliency maps are a computer graphics technique for image recognition. They
highlight the most relevant features of an input image, corresponding to the areas
more relevant for classifying the image into a category. They provide insight into
which features are the most important for the model when classifying [24].

1.2 Computational Background
11
Saliency maps can be applied to extract meaningful information from the model’s
representation layer and then interpret the model in terms of biological pathways in
the context of gene expression models.
Saliency maps are computed using guided backpropagation. This method calcu-
lates the gradient of the representation units of the autoencoder with respect to the
gene expression input. Each neuron gets a saliency calculation in isolation, consider-
ing only that node has a positive gradient equal to 1, and the rest of the nodes have a
gradient of 0. The gradient is backpropagated through the network, and as a result,
it is possible to quantify which genes are activated in each node of the bottleneck
layer.
This technique involves computing adapted gradients for each node in the bot-
tleneck layer of an autoencoder, where negative gradients are set to zero, and all
gradients in the tested node are assumed to be one, while others are zero.
1.2.5
Gene Set Enrichment Analysis
Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) is a computational method used to determine
how statistically significant a specific set of genes statistically differs in expression
between two biological states.
It was first introduced by Subramanian et al.
in
2005 [25].
Its primary purpose is to interpret gene expression data by extracting
biologically relevant insights from large datasets, focusing on gene sets instead of
individual genes [26]. GSEA is typically used for gene expression analysis, and in this
project, it will be used with Saliency Maps to describe the model.
The standard GSEA has three main steps. GSEA starts by ranking the genes in a
dataset based on their differential expression, the most common way of ranking using
the fold change between two biological states. Other methods to rank the genes can
also be used [27].
Subsequently, the Enrichment Scores (ES) of the sets of genes are calculated based
on prior biological knowledge, pathways, or functional groups. The ES measures the
degree to which the genes in the set are overrepresented at the top or bottom extremes
of the entire ranked list of genes. This ES is normalized for the size of the gene sets.
Finally, the statistical significance of each ES is estimated by assigning a p-value.
1.2.5.1
Gene Sets and Biological Pathways
Gene sets are predefined groups of genes representing a common biological function,
chromosomal location, or regulation. [25] These gene sets are curated based on online
databases.
The most widely used gene set database for GSEA is the molecular signatures
database (MSigDB). It contains collections of gene sets manually annotated based on
the literature review by MSigDB curators. MSigDB has a wide range of gene sets
representing various biological functions, processes, and diseases [28]

1 Introduction
1.3
Previous Work
Autoencoders have various applications, including denoising, data simulation, impu-
tation of missing values, visualization, and decomposition. They have demonstrated
their effectiveness in capturing the critical information in datasets, including mRNA
sequencing data.
The use of autoencoders to extract biological information from
mRNA sequencing has been described in multiple studies.
In biological applications, the interpretability of the model is essential. In this
context, the interpretability of the autoencoder refers to the ability to explain the
biological signal captured in the representation layer. Many techniques can be used
for this purpose.
This thesis builds on the previous work of Kinalis et al. [29]. and Walbech et
al [22]. They showed that autoencoder models can be explainable and interpretable
regarding biology. In essence, the model extracts the biological signal encoded in
the bottleneck layer of the trained autoencoder, and its features can be explained in
terms of biological pathways.
In particular, the work of Kinalis et al. [29] has shown that with specialized
training, it is possible to make autoencoder models interpretable in terms of biology.
The interpretation methodology introduced by Kinalis, where the saliency map of each
node in the representation layer is calculated, introduces a straightforward approach
to directly mapping the relationship between each node and its corresponding active
genes.
In addition, Walbech et al. [22] showed that these models can generalize the fun-
damental biological processes to the extent that the same model can be directly
transferable to unseen datasets. In other words, transferability to a foreign model.
This suggests a model trained with healthy samples can be extrapolated to a disease
dataset. A pre-trained model with a healthy dataset can detect and explain disease
from normal.
Finally, both studies hypothesize that using soft orthogonality constraints will
increase the interpretability of the model. This claim has not been directly tested in
these studies. Therefore, this hypothesis will be tested in this project.

2.2
Autoencoder Architecture
The fully connected under-complete autoencoder was implemented with Python ver-
sion 3.11.5 and Pytorch version 2.1. It is composed of the input, output, and three
hidden layers.
The dimensions of the input and output layers correspond to the
number of genes in the dataset. The dimensions of the hidden layers were selected
depending on the hyperparameter optimization. However, for all the models imple-
mented, the bottleneck layer has the lowest dimension of the hidden layers, and the
two other neighbor layers have a higher but equal dimension.
The autoencoder was trained using the MSE loss function and was implemented
with the native Pytorch MSE loss.
The orthogonality constraint is added to the
loss function, which is imposed in the representation layer to ensure some degree of
independence between these nodes. The degree of orthogonality added to the loss
function depends on the hyperparameter λ.

2.4
Autoencoder Training
The first step is to load the rescaled normalized data. The data is divided into 95% of
samples for training and 5% for testing. The autoencoder was trained using batch size
1 to focus on learning individual samples’ underlying features rather than a collective
batch. The autoencoder is trained using the healthy dataset.
The autoencoder is trained for 300 epochs. Each iteration starts with a forward
pass of the data through the autoencoder, and the loss between the predicted and
original value is calculated. If applicable, the orthogonality constraint and L1 regular-
ization are computed and added to the loss value. Then comes the backpropagation
step, where the gradients of the loss function with respect to each weight of the net-
work are computed and used to update the weights in the direction that minimizes
the loss. This is done 300 times or until the early stopping.
After training the model, the pruning is performed, the UMAP clustering plots
are generated, and the saliency map is calculated for the interpretation steps.

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
By exploring the use of autoencoders for interpreting RNA sequencing data, this
project demonstrates their eﬀicacy, particularly with the addition of orthogonal-
ity constraints and other regularization techniques. These constraints improve the
model’s ability to extract biologically meaningful features and their interpretabil-
ity, which is crucial for the application of autoencoders in clinical settings. These
models effectively aid in comprehending the complexity of gene expression datasets,
especially in the context of AML classification, which is diﬀicult when solely based
on morphological characteristics [12]. However, the classification of AML subtypes
poses unique challenges due to the subtle nuances in gene expressions and the general
heterogeneous nature of cancers [34].
Adding to the previous work done by Walbech et al. [22] and Kinalis et al. [29],
this study further demonstrates the potential of autoencoders in interpreting RNA se-
quencing data and its applications to disease diagnostics. The integration of Saliency
Maps and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis has been instrumental in enabling a deeper
understanding of the biological pathways ingrained in the data.
Furthermore, the potential transferability of autoencoder models, trained on healthy
samples, to disease datasets indicates a significant advancement, opening avenues for
research in other complex diseases. This finding is particularly promising for future
clinical applications and provides a framework as a generalized and eﬀicient method
for gene expression interpretation.
While these findings are promising, some limitations were found, specifically in
adding orthogonality constraints to the autoencoder. It had been hypothesized that
the degree of orthogonality added would directly increase the interpretability of the
model.
The experiments conducted in this project, however, did not support the
correlation between the orthogonality and improvements in the interpretability of the
model.
However, the experiments on orthogonality constraints revealed that using hyper-
parameter optimization can identify the optimal interpretable model. This means
that for each model, there’s an ideal orthogonality value that maximizes its explain-
ability. The results suggest that the optimal explainability depends on the specific
design of the model architecture and the characteristics of the data. Employing a
suitable optimization method, such as Bayesian optimization, can help in identifying
this optimal value. This highlights the importance of tailored model architecture and
configuration for enhancing the interpretability of computational models.
==PAGE:48==
36
4 Discussion
The lack of correlation between the model explainability and orthogonality con-
straint might be due to this project’s definition and method of measuring interpretabil-
ity, which involves the use of saliency maps in conjunction with GSEA analysis and
the calculation of the interpretability coeﬀicient based on the quantification of the
significant and unique pathways found by GSEA.
Future work could involve benchmarking other deconvolution methods to compare
with the methods used in this project. This would provide the necessary context to
know which is the best method for interpreting an autoencoder model and if these
methods are correlated to the orthogonality or not.
Through performing orthogonality experiments, it was observed that even though
this constraint may not directly affect the interpretability of the model, it is still nec-
essary for the autoencoder to accurately capture the crucial features of the data in
its representation layer. This was demonstrated visually in the UMAP plots, where
models lacking orthogonality were unable to maintain the data structure during en-
coding.
As it was previously shown by Walbech et al [22], autoencoder models have the
capability to transfer to unseen datasets. In the context of this project, it was ex-
tended to the transferability of models trained on healthy samples to disease datasets,
broadening the applications of these models for complex diseases. The use of healthy
samples to build a base for classifying patient samples is the gateway for using these
models as a framework for future clinical diagnostics.
This characteristic of autoencoder models was not applied to maximum capability
considering the limitations of the data available, especially when considering the lack
of childhood samples for the study of AML. Additionally, the use of single-cell data to
train and bulk data for the classification also proved to be a limitation. Exploring this
approach with datasets of comparable sequencing types would present an intriguing
opportunity for future work.
Multiple issues arose from the patient datasets used in this project; one of the main
drawbacks was the lack of transparency in the specifics of the normalization present
on the patient dataset. To permit model transferability in a trustworthy manner,
both datasets must have the same normalization scheme; the safest way for this is to
perform the same normalization methods when pre-processing the data. With the use
of Minmax, the distribution of the patient dataset changed dramatically, resulting in
possible removals of significant underlying patterns in the data.
This also had an impact on the model’s transferability when training with different
types of sequencing data. Using single-cell RNA data to train the model and later
use it to classify a bulk RNA dataset is not ideal. It is possible to argue that the
autoencoder model by training is, in fact, generating an average generalization of the
expression data, in theory resulting in a close approximation of what bulk RNA data
is. This point could later be validated with further research.
Furthermore, the lack of availability of childhood leukemia samples hindered the
ability to study childhood leukemias in-depth, depriving us of delineating them, which
was the primary intention of this project.
==PAGE:49==
CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this study, the eﬀicacy of autoencoders with orthogonality constraints in interpret-
ing RNA sequencing data was explored, highlighting their potential in computational
biology.
These models demonstrated a notable capability in extracting biological
meaningful features from complex gene expression datasets, which is crucial for un-
derstanding diseases like AML.
While the addition of orthogonality constraints did not enhance the explainability
of the autoencoder model, it did aid in the autoencoder’s ability to encapsulate the
data in a meaningful way. Making the autoencoder model more transparent is crucial
for using it in healthcare, as doctors and researchers need to trust and understand
its results. Additionally, tools like Saliency Maps and Gene Set Enrichment Anal-
ysis are vital for the interpretability of the model, acting as a bridge between the
computational findings and the actual biology.
The study also highlights the potential of using autoencoders trained on healthy
individuals for studying diseases. This method could accelerate and reduce the cost
of researching complex diseases. These results encourage further use and study of
these computational methods in medical and biological fields.
