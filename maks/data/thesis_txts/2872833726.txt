Abstract
The global transition to renewable energy has significantly increased in recent years. So­
lar energy is one of the most effective solutions against global warming, together with
wind energy. As the number of solar power plants increases, the reliability of their op­
eration and maintenance becomes even more critical. Photovoltaic (PV) modules are a
vital component of solar power plants, and early detection of faults and degradations is
essential to maintain high efficiency and profitability.
The main focus of this thesis is to evaluate the feasibility of infrared thermography (IRT)
for detecting typical faults and degradation in PV modules under varying drone altitude
and solar irradiance levels, using electroluminescence (EL) imaging and current­voltage
(IV) measurements as supportive reference techniques.
In this study, specific faults such as bypass diode open circuit, bypass diode short circuit,
glass breakage, cell cracks, ribbon failure, and potential induced degradation (PID) were
created in different types of PV modules through controlled laboratory stress tests. These
defects were characterized using EL imaging and IV curve measurements. IRT imaging
was then performed under different altitudes (8­10­14 and 20 meters) and irradiance levels
(200­600­800 and above 800 W/m2), using both handheld and drone thermal cameras.
The results show that IRT is highly effective to detect certain types of faults, such as by­
pass diode short circuits and severe cell cracks, especially under high irradiance levels
and at lower altitudes. However, its reliability decreases for low­severity PID and glass
cracks, where clear thermal patterns are not observed in thermal images. The perfor­
mance of drone based IRT imaging also depends on flight altitude, with higher altitudes
leading to reduced spatial resolution and lower defect detectability.
In conclusion, the study demonstrates that IRT can effectively detect certain PV module
failures under optimal conditions, but its reliability depends strongly on fault type, imaging
altitude, and irradiance. The use of EL imaging and IV curve measurements as reference
techniques enables a comprehensive evaluation of IRT’s strengths and limitations.

1.1
Background and Project Motivation
The global transition to renewable energy made significant progress in 2024 [1]. Solar
photovoltaic (PV) energy is becoming an efficient solution to meet this need due to the
increasing demand for energy around the world. PV and onshore wind lead more than
95% of the 473 GW added renewable energy capacity in the world in 2023 [2], and this
increasing trend also consistent with the 666 GW capacity in 2024 [1]. Additionally, global
capacity of renewable energy is expected to increase by 2.7 times by 2030, as climate
and energy policies in almost 140 countries have played a major role to make renewable
energy alternatives cost­efficient and competitive against fossil fuel power plants [3]. In
2024, electricity generation from solar PV and wind surpassed that of hydropower, mark­
ing a significant development in the global shift towards renewable energy sources as
shown in Fig. 1.1 [4]. By 2030, solar PV is projected to become the dominant source
of renewable electricity, followed by wind, with both anticipated to contribute more than
hydropower to the global energy mix [3].
Figure 1.1: Global electricity generation by source, 2014­2025 (reproduced from [4])
PV systems play a major role in the transition to renewable energy, thus the efficiency
of the plant has a major role for meeting the increasing global energy demand [5]. With
the increasing PV capacity, the efficiency and reliability of PV plants is becoming more
crucial. According to the market data, PV plants with high installed power have low profit
margins which means that they may easily be affected by performance reduction and,
thus it impacts the final profit [6]. In the context of PV systems, predictive maintenance
applications can help to improve overall system efficiency, reliability, longevity of the plant
and also may reduce overall maintenance time and costs up to 25% and increase energy
production up to 3%, and also reduce the risk of equipment failure [5]. Moreover, it is
important for the overall profit that PV modules operate efficiently during the 25­year war­
ranty period to amortize the high cost of the modules. This means that the PV power
degradation rate should not exceed 0.8% per year [7].

In order to maintain high efficiency, it is essential to monitor and protect the physical con­
dition of PV modules under varying environmental conditions. PV modules are the most
critical component of a PV power plant, therefore, it is highly important to monitor their
condition and performance during operation. Different environmental conditions such as
high and low temperatures, rainfall, wind, snow, hail, seasonal transitions, etc. can oc­
cur on site and affect the operation of the modules and increase the risk of degradation,
failure and defects on them.
As can be seen in Fig. 1.2, failure types in PV systems can be classified into continuous
and sudden events, both shows different patterns over the years. Cell cracks, potential
induced degradation (PID), backsheet defects and burn marks are most frequent contin­
uous failures within the first six years of operation. Although, their frequency decreases
over time. Some failures such as discolouring of encapsulant material and disconnected
cells or string usually happen in later years. On the other hand, sudden events mostly oc­
cur in first nine years. These failures are caused by external factors such as dust soiling,
glass breakage, snow load, and storms [8].

There are various approaches and techniques used to detect defects and failures in
PV cells and modules. The main inspection methods are based on visual inspection,
current­voltage (IV) curve measurements, various imaging techniques such as infrared
thermography (IRT), electroluminescence (EL), photoluminescence, UV fluorescence [9].
All these techniques and approaches have their own strengths and weaknesses. For in­
stance, visual inspection is the most economical approach to identify defects and faults in
the module, but it can only identify the defects that are visible to the naked eye. The physi­
cal defects such as delamination, browning, yellowing, module glass cracks, misalignment
of module frames, etc. can be easily detected with visual inspection [9]. However, due to
high error ratio and long process time, the visual inspection is not feasible for large­scale
PV systems under real time conditions [10].

On the other hand, IRT imaging is one of the most time­efficient methods for fault detection
and classification in solar power plants. IRT imaging can detect failures such as cracks,
shading (e.g. bird dropping, soil, etc.), which appear as hotspots or thermal difference
between the cells on the same module [11]. It can identify faults not only in solar panels
but also in other components such as power cables, diodes, DC combiner boxes, junction
boxes, and connectors [12]. The main advantage of IRT imaging is it can be performed
while solar power plant is under operation without affecting the system [12].
Electroluminescence imaging is an effective method for detecting failures such as micro
cracks, interrupted contacts, and various process related failures (e.g. shunts or defects
in the anti reflection layer). However, it does not provide insight about how these failures
impact the PV module’s power output [10].
EL and IRT imaging are effective and reliable methods for qualitative characterization of
PV modules. These tools also allow for the detection of faults in a PV plant and the precise
determination of their location with high accuracy [10].
However, in most studies and experiments, each method was conducted under ideal con­
ditions. According to the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) technical spec­
ification IEC TS 62446­3:2017 [13], optimal conditions for thermal imaging are minimum
600 W/m2 solar irradiation, 28 km/h maximum wind speed, and clean PV modules. Simi­
larly, for EL imaging, IEC TS 60904­13:2018 [14] indicate that imaging should be analyzed
in a dark room to ensure high quality EL images [15].
Nevertheless, such ideal conditions may not always possible in real life. In countries like
Denmark, getting the required solar irradiance can be challenging due to frequent cloudy
weather and reduced daylight hours, especially during autumn and winter months. For

1.2
Project Aims, Objectives and Limitations
Based on the background and motivation mentioned in the previous section, this thesis
aims to investigate and assess PV module failures using infrared thermography with both
handheld and drone cameras and electroluminescence imaging techniques. The study
is conducted under varying environmental conditions while focusing on controlled and
known specific failures on the modules. The key objectives of this research are outlined
below:
• To create specific failures in PV modules through controlled laboratory stress tests
and to characterize the resulting defects using IV curve measurements and EL imag­
ing
• To compare the performance of handheld and drone based imaging in terms of clar­
ity, resolution and overall image quality among different irradiance levels
• To evaluate the reliability of IRT in detecting different PV module faults, using EL
imaging and IV curve measurements as supportive reference techniques
• To investigate the limits of altitude and irradiance under which different types of PV
module defects can be reliably detected using IRT
There are various PV module failures described in the literature and observed in industrial
applications. However, due to time limitations, this project focused and analyzed only
six specific failure types: bypass diode open circuit, bypass diode short circuit, glass
breakage, cell crack, ribbon damage, and PID failure. Additionally, three types of PV
modules were used for the analyses in this study: PERC monocrystalline silicon modules,
as well as full­cell and half­cut bifacial monocrystalline silicon modules.

Inspection Methods
2.1
Infrared Thermography (IRT) Imaging
Infrared thermography is a temperature measurement method that captures images based
on the thermal radiation emitted by objects according to their temperature [17]. Through
this technique, analyzing thermal images can provide useful information about the surface
temperature, while further image processing can show possible anomalies in thermal pro­
file of the device under inspection [17]. Fig. 2.1 shows an example of thermal image of
the PV modules.
Figure 2.1: Drone thermal image of the PV modules
IRT is an effective method for detecting problems such as hot spots, mismatch losses
and installation defects on PV modules [18]. If a solar cell has a high electrical resistance
for any reason, the temperature of the cell is significantly higher and can be seen clearly
visible in the IR image [19]. It also offers the advantage of fast, real­time assessment and
non­contact application [19]. However, one disadvantage of IRT imaging is its relatively
low spatial resolution which can make it difficult to identify the sign of failures in the images.
Additionally, IRT measurements can be easily affected by thermal reflections from the
surroundings during the measurement process [20].
One of the things to consider when conducting experiments is environmental conditions.
Stable weather conditions are very important for getting high quality IRT images in outdoor
imaging [18]. For IRT measurements, the ideal conditions can be seen in Table 2.1.

the camera should offer a high resolution rate (320x240 pixels), high thermal sensitivity
(<0.05 K), and adjustable emissivity (0.01­1). Typical emissivity values are 0.85 for glass
and 0.95 for polymer [18].
In PV modules, the incident light generates direct and indirect heat on the PV module,
which can be seen by an infrared camera [19]. Typically, inactive areas of a PV module
or string usually have higher temperatures than active areas. The reason for this is that,
when the sunlight reaches the surface of the PV module, active areas convert some of
the radiation into DC power, which then flows to the inverter. In contrast, inactive areas
cannot convert the sunlight effectively, causing them to heat up more [18]. Defects such
as disconnected PV modules or strings, PID effects, shunted cells, and short­circuited
bypass diodes can all lead to inactive areas in a module or string. Consequently, detecting
such defects can become more difficult under low irradiance conditions [18]. Since most
of the faults significantly affect the thermal behavior of PV modules, they can be detected
as inhomogeneous temperature patterns on the module surface [17].
The IRT imaging does not require the installation of any additional sensors on the PV
modules, as it uses and external IR camera to detect faults, that makes it cost lower than
other methods [12]. Also, this method allows to detect faults while PV modules are under
operation [18]. Additionally, this method is time saving, as it can inspect many PV panels
in a short time [20]. Fig. 2.2a and 2.2b show the required time for IRT measurement with
drone and handheld thermal camera, respectively. For instance, it takes almost 40 hours
to complete an IRT imaging with handheld camera in 10 MWp power plant while with the
drone it takes 10 hours [18].

2.2
Electroluminescence (EL) Imaging
Electroluminescence (EL) imaging is a method that can identify radiative recombination
of charge carriers that are activated under forward bias [17, 22]. This means that brighter
areas in an EL image indicate higher voltages, while electrically inactive cells or substrings
appear as dark regions [17].
For EL imaging, a DC power supply is required that can provide sufficient voltage to reach
the short­circuit current (Isc) of the module under test. If all the modules on the string are
to be tested, the power supply must have higher capacity. Biasing the module at Isc
helps identify defects related to series resistance while biasing with 0.1 Isc is effective for
evaluating shunt resistance characteristics [13]. The method is usually applied using Isc
and 0.1 Isc, since both measurements are needed to analyze the severity of cell cracks
[19].
EL imaging gives better results when the experiment is conducted in dark environment that
is because it minimizes the noise present in the image [18, 19]. This condition facilitates
image correction by allowing the subtraction of a background image ­captured when the
module is not biased­ from the EL image acquired during testing [13].
EL inspection allows for accurate detection and localization of defects in PV modules,
such as cell cracks, shunts, potential­induced degradation (PID), and inactive submod­
ules/strings due to bypass diode or open circuits [17]. In qualitative analysis, EL images
are analyzed for distinctive visual patterns to detect these issues [18, 23]. However, to
identify certain types of degradations such as delamination or front glass breakage, this
method is limited or insufficient and also not possible to determine the impact of these
defects on the module power output [17, 18].
Additionally, in EL images, damaged areas of a solar module typically appear dark or
emit less radiation compared to non­defect areas. Figure 2.3 shows an example of an
EL image of a PV module. The dark regions and visible cracks correspond to electrically
inactive or damaged cells, which can be accurately detected using this method [24].
Figure 2.3: EL image of a PV module showing cell cracks and dark cells with shunting,
suggesting potential induced degradation (reproduced from [24])
There are two alternative methods for applying EL imaging in a dark environment. The first
method is taking the module(s) to be tested from the field and apply the test in a laboratory
environment. However, the disadvantage of this option is that uninstalling the modules
from the field and transporting them to the laboratory is risky as it creates additional stress
on the modules and is also time consuming and costly [25]. The second option is a
night time EL imaging on the field but this method involves several operational and safety
issues, such as working under rough environmental conditions (low temperatures, etc.),

Imaging

the use of light towers, etc. As a result, night time EL imaging is difficult to implement,
especially for the high number of modules [25]. Therefore, outdoor/daylight EL imaging
emerges as a attractive option to analyze PV modules on the field.
To perform outdoor/daylight EL imaging, specific cameras are needed. The camera must
be sensitive to the emission spectrum of the material and has specific types of sensors.
The most common sensor technologies are Complementary Metal Oxide Semiconductor
(CMOS) and Charge Coupled Device (CCD) [18]. Fig. 2.4 shows the emission spectra

IV Curve Measurements
Current­voltage (IV) curve measurement is a fundamental technique for characterizing the
electrical parameters of PV modules and is considered one of the most comprehensive
methods for evaluating their performance [9].
IV curve measurements can be performed either in the laboratory under standard test
conditions (STC, i.e. AM1.5G spectrum with 1000 W/m2 at 25◦C module temperature)
or outside in the field. The testing procedures and standards are defined by the IEC in the
IEC 60904­1 standard [14]. A typical IV measurement setup consists of a sun simulator,
a platform for mounting the module, a light power sensor, and a data acquisition setup to
log the IV values [20].
Voc and Isc are key parameters in assessing PV module performance. A decrease in Voc
may indicate issues such as PID, LID, while Isc can be affected by optical faults such
as discoloration or delamination [20, 28]. Additionally, most faults reduce the module’s
maximum power and fill factor (FF), which are critical indicators derived from the IV curve
and influenced by defects such as cell cracks [15, 28]. These parameters, along with the
maximum power point and fill factor can provide valuable information about the electrical
or optical degradation of the module. The fill factor can be calculated with the equation
shown in Eq. 2.1.
FF = Vmpp · Impp
Voc · Isc
(2.1)

detect and analyze the degradation mechanisms in PV modules. These curve deviations
can serve as indicators of various failure types [9]. The shape of the IV curve is also
affected by changes in series resistance (Rs) and shunt resistance (Rsh). An increase in
Rs causes bending downward near the open circuit, while a decrease in Rsh results in
curvature near the short circuit, as illustrated in Fig. 2.7.

PV Module Failure Types
Photovoltaic module failures can reduce system output power, service life, and the effi­
ciency, or pose a safety risk. In catastrophic cases, they may even lead to fire hazards.
Therefore, monitoring of PV systems has become an essential part of the PV plant main­
tenance in order to prevent failures [32].
A PV module failure is defined by the International Energy Agency (IEA) as either (1) a
degradation in module power output that cannot be reversed by normal operation, or (2)
creates a safety issue. These failures are classified into three categories based on their
occurrence over time, as illustrated in Fig. 3.1 [15]. In addition, Fig. 3.2 shows the degra­
dation modes observed in PV modules over the past 10 years. The most common failure
is hot spots with 33%, followed by ribbon discoloration (20%), glass breakage (12%),
encapsulant discolaration (10%), cell breakage (9%), and PID at 8% [33].

3.1
Cell Cracks
Photovoltaic cells are typically made of brittle silicon wafers, which makes them vulnera­
ble to external stress and/or mechanical loads. These cracks are not visible to the naked
eye and may occur during wafer slicing, cell production, stringing, and lamination. Addi­
tionally, they may happen during module transport, installation, and field operation due to
mechanical or thermal stress [15, 23, 34, 35, 36].
Cracks are usually observed in crystalline silicon (c­Si) cells due to their structural char­
acteristics [23]. Cell cracks can occur in different size and orientation. Fig. 3.3 shows
the different classifications of cell cracks due to their orientation as dendritic, several,
+45, ­45, parallel, and perpendicular to the busbar [34]. While small cracks may initially
have minimal impact on module performance however, environmental and/or mechani­
cal stresses can cause expansion on them which leads to electrical disconnection and
reduced power output over time [28, 32, 37].
Additionally, another classification approach is done based on EL intensity under different
Isc conditions. Fig. 3.4 shows, Mode A cracks have no impact on EL intensity and do
not cause inactive areas and no reduction in EL intensity at Isc. Mode B cracks result in
partially inactive regions, visible as reduced EL intensity under both Isc and 0.1 Isc level.
Mode C cracks lead to fully inactive cell areas, which appear as dark regions showing
only background noise at 0.1 Isc [37].
Figure 3.3: Classification of cracks orientation (reproduced from [34])
Figure 3.4: EL images showing crack modes A, B and C. Left: at Isc, Right: at 0.1 Isc
(reproduced from [37])

effects can potentially create hot spots if more than 8% of the cell becomes non­functional
[28, 32, 35]. In some cases, these cracks can raise the module temperature by more than
7.6◦C [32]. Fig. 3.5 shows the EL and IRT image of the solar cell samples with different
modes and not all the modes generate hotspots [39].

3.2
Glass Breakage
Glass breakage is a common failure in PV modules, caused by internal stresses such as
expansion on the module frame, as well as external mechanical stresses such as hail,
storm, or inappropriate handling during transport and installation [23, 32]. Fig. 3.7 shows
the glass breakage due to external stresses [28].
Figure 3.7: (a) Example of PV module with shattered protective glass ­ (b) Broken glass
due to installation fault by over­tightening a bolt (reproduced from [28])
Glass breakage in PV modules can occur as cracks, shattering, or chipping. Usually,
the broken glass pieces can be held in one place due to the using of laminated glass.
However, glass­glass modules are more fragile, especially in frameless designs, due to
their unprotected glass edges [23]. Reduced light transmittance is the primary failure that
caused by breakage, UV exposure, or dust accumulation [28].
In rainy conditions, glass damage can allow water intrusion which causes a module degra­
dation and also increases the risk of short circuits, damaged electrical components, and
safety hazard [23, 28, 32]. In addition to that, environmental factors such as high temper­
atures increase the risk of glass failure, with study shows increased breakage in modules
operation in hot climates such as India within five years [28]. There have also been reports
of unexpected glass breakage in newer PV modules, even without an obvious external
cause or before operation and maintenance period. The main reason for this appears to
be a decline in average glass quality. Many modules only just pass the basic static load
test or with high safety factors. This issue is related to the trend of using thinner glass,
which is more difficult to temper and increases the risk of breakage [41].
3.3
Bypass Diode Defects
Bypass diodes are integrated into PV modules in parallel with groups of solar cells to
reduce power loss caused by partial shading [15]. They are protective components in­
tended to minimize losses by allowing the current from healthy cells to flow around shaded
or damaged cells, especially during reverse bias conditions [36]. In this way, they can help
to prevent individual cells from reaching their breakdown voltage when exposed to shad­
ing or soiling. Nonetheless, excessive static discharge or mechanical stress can damage
the bypass diodes [23].
In conventional full­cell PV modules, the cells are usually grouped into three substrings,
each protected by a bypass diode. However, in half­cut cell modules, each cell is split in
half and the module is typically divided into six substrings, with a bypass diode for every
two substrings. This means that, when there is a bypass diode failure (open or short

circuit), affects only 1/6 of the module in half­cut modules, compared to 1/3 in full­cell
modules [42]. These connections are illustrated in Fig. 3.8.
The advantage of half­cut PV modules is important especially under partial shading con­
ditions. In vertical shading conditions, the shading can affect 25% or 50% of the module,
and under these conditions, half­cut modules have lower power losses around 40% com­
pared to full­cell modules, which lose up to 80% of module power. Similarly, in diagonal
shading with 25% covering, half­cut modules show lower power loss with a difference of
41% compared to full­cell modules [43]. As a result, half­cut modules show better perfor­
mance under different shading conditions. Therefore, the difference in substring layout is
important for interpreting both EL and IRT images, as the thermal and electrical effects of
open circuit and short circuit faults will appear in smaller segments in half­cut modules.

or faulty cells in the module string which can lead to the hotspot and in severe cases fire
[23]. The main factors of open circuit failures are cyclic thermal stress, hail or wind, and
damages during manufacturing [32].

3.5
Potential Induced Degradation
PV modules are connected in series to form a PV string and also to maximize power
generation. This configuration can create a potential difference for several hundred volts
between solar cells and module frame [32]. In PV systems, metallic components are
usually grounded for safety, which can create a potential difference between the PV cells
and the grounded frame. In certain situations, depending on the bill of materials of the
Extended Evaluation and Fault Diagnosis of Solar Photovoltaics Modules in the Field via Multispectral
Imaging
21
==PAGE:36==
module, the potential to ground of the cells and the ambient conditions the modules are
operating under, this may result in leakage currents that cause a polarization, shunting or
corrosion of the solar cells, which gradually degrades the module performance and this
is called potential induced degradation (PID) [32, 46]. In recent years PID has become a
major concern in PV modules [36].
PID is mainly caused by a potential difference between the solar cells and the grounded
module frame during system operation [23]. It most commonly occurs in PV plants where
PV modules are grounded as a fire prevention method [28]. Moreover, several PV faults
such as cell cracks, bypass diode failures, coating issues, and shading can increase the
risk of PID [35]. Although, all module types can be affected by PID, the risk depends on
the frame glass, and encapsulation material, as well as environmental conditions [23].
PID can cause high power loss by up to 70% [47]. In addition to power loss, PID can lead
increased recombination and, may cause transparent conductive oxide (TCO) corrosion
and p­n junction damage in thin film modules [36]. In high­efficiency n­type cells, power
loss from PID can occur under positive bias which is called polarization [15]. On the
other hand, in c­Si modules, this effect is reversible and appears under negative bias for
p­type and positive for n­type cells [15]. In p­type cells, alkali ions can cause shunting
by reaching the p­n junction, while in n­type cells, surface recombination increases and
reduces both current and voltage. As shown in Fig. 3.13 and 3.14, darker cells in EL
images can appear near the frame and the heat they generate can be detected by IRT
imaging method [23].

7
Performance Evaluation of IRT
IRT is a widely used technique in the inspection of PV modules due to its ability to quickly
detect temperature differences without physical contact [69]. Although IRT is a valuable
diagnostic method, its performance can vary depending on several factors, such as en­
vironmental conditions and the failure type. In this chapter, the IRT method is evaluated
regarding its diagnostic capabilities and limitations. The analysis focus on how solar irra­
diance and drone altitude affect the accuracy and clarity of thermal images. In addition,
the ability of IRT in detecting different types of PV module failures is examined based on
the results obtained during the project.
7.1
Influence of Altitude and Irradiance on IRT Performance
7.1.1
Effect of Drone Altitude
Drone IRT imaging is a popular technique for inspecting PV modules, especially in large
scale solar power plants. However, the altitude of the drone has a significant effect on
image quality and diagnostic accuracy. When the drone flies higher, the spatial resolution
decreased, which means each pixel covers a larger area of the module surface. A lower
spatial resolution, therefore, results in a lower resolution image [70]. This leads to a
decrease in image quality and the number of pixels per cell, which affects the visibility of
small or localized defects.
In this project, thermal images were captured at different drone altitude levels, 8, 10, 14,
and 20 meters for all analyses. Fig. 7.1 shows the same module with ribbon failure taken
at different altitude levels, as an example of the effect on drone altitude.

7.1.2
Effect of Irradiance Level
Solar irradiance is one of the most critical parameters affecting the reliability of IRT imaging
[71]. Higher irradiance increases the current generated by the PV module, which increase
the power output and the surface temperature of the cells. As a result, the thermal contrast
between healthy and defective cells becomes more visible, making it easier to detect
failures in IRT imaging.

at 600 W/m2, the ribbon faults become much more visible due to increased module tem­
perature. This visuals show that insufficient irradiance level does not generate the heat
signature expected when the module is closer to MPP and thus hide localized heating
effects, which makes it difficult or impossible to detect failures with IRT.

IRT Detection Capability for Different PV Failure Types
In this section, the detection capability of IRT imaging is evaluated for each PV module

7.2.1
Bypass Diode Failures ­ Short Circuit
Effect of Altitude
Bypass diode short circuit failures are easily detectable in IRT images across all tested
drone altitudes. At low altitudes (e.g. 8 meters), as shown in Fig. 7.2b, the spatial res­
olution is highest, which allows very clear visualization of the short circuited substrings.
When the altitude increases, there is a decrease in image quality and cell level detail.
However, for this failure type, the thermal pattern remains clearly visible even at higher
altitudes. This makes bypass diode short circuit failure detectable even in large scale
aerial evaluation.
Effect of Solar Irradiance
Higher irradiance significantly increase the detectability of bypass diode short circuit fail­
ures, as expected. At irradiance level above 600 W/m2, both the module temperature
and the thermal pattern between failed and healthy substrings increase, as shown in Fig.
6.6. Although, at low irradiance, the temperature difference is reduced, short circuit fail­
ures still create visible hotspots and patterns, as illustrated in Fig. 7.4a. According to the
IEC 62446­3 and the analyses results, reliable detection is easily achieved at 600 W/m2
and above [21].
IRT Thermal Patterns
The typical IRT pattern of a bypass diode short circuit appear across the affected sub­
strings of the module. In full­cell modules, this generally appears as a more uniform tem­
perature increases across larger area of the substring. In contrast, for half­cut modules,
the pattern mostly consist of localized hotspots, especially near the junction box location,
rather than a uniform heated substring. This difference can be seen in Fig. 6.9.
Overall Detectability with IRT
Overall, bypass diode short circuit failures are the most reliably detectable PV module
faults with IRT. The thermal pattern is robust against changes in altitude and irradiance
levels, which makes IRT a highly effective diagnostic method for this failure. In addition,
both EL imaging and IV measurements also provide clear confirmation of this failure mode,
with EL showing completely dark substrings and IV measurements indicating a significant
drop in power output and open circuit voltage. Therefore, all three methods, IRT, EL and
IV, can be used to detect bypass diode short circuit failures, depending on the accessibility
of the module and available diagnostic equipment.

other types of faults such as junction box failure or shading can also cause hotspots in
IRT images [74], which means that a hotspots is not always indicate the cell crack. For
this reason, EL imaging is necessary to confirm the presence and location of cell cracks.
EL images can show all types of cell cracks, regardless of their electrical activity, and pro­
vide a more complete assessment of cell crack. Therefore, combining IRT with EL gives
a much more reliable diagnosis of cell cracks in PV modules.

Ribbon Damage
Effect of Altitude
The accuracy of detecting ribbon failures in PV modules with IRT imaging decreases sig­
nificantly as altitude increases. As shown in Fig. 7.1, at low altitudes, images are detailed
enough to clearly show localized hotspots along the ribbons. However, at higher alti­
tudes, spatial resolution is decreased, making the defect less visible or even undetectable.
These findings show the importance of low altitude inspections for reliable choice of ribbon
failures.

Similar to the altitude, irradiance is also important for image quality in IRT imaging. Fig.
7.3 shows the comparison of different irradiance level of the IRT images of the ribbon
damaged modules. It is clear that low irradiance level does not provide any sign about the
failure, while at medium irradiance level hotspots become visible on the module surface.
While the effect of low irradiance on defect visibility is clear, it is also important to show
how increasing irradiance beyond the minimum recommended level (i.e. from 600 to
>800 W/m2) affects the image quality and detectability. This can be seen in Fig. 7.10
and 7.12, which compare the temperature histograms of healthy and ribbon damaged
modules under different irradiance conditions.
In Fig. 7.10, increasing irradiance leads to higher mean and maximum temperature in both

At high irradiance level, the temperature differences between healthy and ribbon damaged

can be helpful for identifying the severity of ribbon damage and locating the damaged cells
more precisely. EL images give a direct view of ribbon faults and support more reliable
diagnosis combination.

inspection, especially to detect low severity PID that may not be visible in low resolution

Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to evaluate the effectiveness of IRT for detecting
various faults in PV modules such as bypass diode open circuit, bypass diode short cir­
cuit, glass breakage, cell cracks, ribbon failure, and PID under different conditions. To
achieve this, specific failures were induced in different module types, characterized using
EL imaging and IV curve measurements, and evaluated with IRT at varying irradiance
levels and imaging altitudes, using both handheld and drone­based cameras.
For the IV curve measurements, the main focus was to assess whether how different types
of failures affected the IV characteristics and overall module performance. In the EL analy­
sis, visual inspection and comparison were performed to identify and classify each failure
type. This integrated method (IV and EL) provided a practical approach for evaluating
both methods prior to a more detailed and quantitative analysis using IRT imaging. Ad­
ditionally, for thermal images, image processing was applied to determine, quantify and
also analyze the behavior of the failure under varying conditions such as different altitudes
and irradiance levels.
The experimental workflow began with the initial characterization of full­cell and half­cut
bifacial PV modules through IV curve measurements and EL imaging to confirm the ab­
sence of faults or degradation. Specific stress tests were applied in the laboratory environ­
ment to create the failures. After stress tests, IV curve and EL imaging tests conducted
again to verify the presence of the faults. In total, stress tests were performed on 13
newly prepared modules, with 10 of these installed in the field (three modules with glass
crack could not installed due to safety reasons) alongside 24 previously tested modules
with known faults from earlier studies [40]. Further data collection was carried out using
both outdoor EL and IRT imaging. The IV curve measurements were performed in the
laboratory environment under standard test conditions (25◦C, using a flash simulator in
accordance with the relevant standards).
IRT imaging experiments were performed using both a drone and a handheld camera. IRT
tests were conducted under varying irradiance conditions (200, 600, 800, and above 800
W/m2). For drone based imaging, measurements were also taken at different altitudes
(8, 10, 14, and 20 meters) to assess the effects of flight height. Image processing for the
IRT was done using Python.
Further analysis of IRT imaging was performed separately for each failure. For each
failure, average surface temperature of PV modules was compared under different alti­
tudes and irradiance levels to show how different conditions may affect the temperature
measurements. For all failure types, surface temperature increased with irradiance as
expected, but decreased at higher altitudes due to reduced PV module resolution which
has fewer pixels on the module surface and lower spatial detail.
Moreover, different quantitative analysis was performed for each failure. For bypass diode
failure, substring level showed that substrings with short circuit failure have higher temper­
ature than healthy substrings, regardless of irradiance and altitude level. Additionally, the
thermal patterns of bypass diode short circuit failures were examined. The literature often
describes this failure as showing uniform thermal patterns along the affected substring.
This uniform pattern was also observed in the full­cell modules used in the project. How­
ever, in half­cut modules, the expected uniform temperature pattern was not observed.

Instead, there are clear and localized hotspots, especially near the junction box. To val­
idate these patterns, temperature profile of the substrings were analyzed. Temperature
profiles also showed that there are some sharp peaks for half­cut modules instead of
uniform profile along substring.
Cell level analysis was performed for glass breakage failure. In this project, only two
PV modules were subjected to glass crack stress test and EL imaging showed that only
low severity cell cracks occurred after stress tests. As a result, there is not significant
temperature differences in the module compared to healthy cells. The thermal distribution
is uniform across the surface, indicating that low severity glass cracks may not always
cause high severity cell crack and may not generate enough temperature differences to
be detected with IRT imaging.
For cell crack failure cell level analysis was also performed. The severity of cell cracks
are grouped as no crack (0%), less severe (0–15%), medium severe (15–30%), and high
severe (>30%), based on the EL images. Afterwards, healthy and cracked cell were
analyzed under high irradiance conditions at different altitude levels. The results showed
that less severe and medium severe cracks tend to show higher temperature than healthy
cells, especially at 8 m and 10 m altitudes. Interestingly, high severe cracks did not show
the highest temperatures. This is due to inactive or fully disconnected cell regions, which
receive less current flow and therefore do not heat up significantly. These findings are
also consistent with previous study, where cells with larger crack areas show reduced
temperature due to loss of electrical connections [65].
Ribbon damage was also analyzed on cell level. Ribbon severity was categorized into four
types based on the number of disconnected ribbons observed in IRT and EL images. The
evaluation focused on whether there is a meaningful correlation between the EL signal
level (indicating defect severity) and the temperature measured with IRT. For this anal­
ysis, the standard deviation of EL signal values and the maximum cell temperatures of
the ribbon affected cells were evaluated. The analysis showed that, higher defect types
generally corresponded to both higher maximum temperatures and greater EL signal de­
viations. This correlation was consistent at all altitudes. Cells with more severe ribbon
damage showed higher maximum temperatures compared to other types and healthy
cells.
Finally, for PID failure, only module based analysis was conducted because there is no
high severity PID failure on the modules used in this project. The comparative analysis
was performed between the results from this study and from previous project [45]. The
comparison showed that the PID affected cells do not produce a temperature increase that
can be observed and there is not significant differences between the results of the two
projects. Furthermore, the PID pattern on the module could not be observed either. This
indicates that modules with low severity PID failure do not create hotspots or temperature
differences detectable by IRT imaging.
To better understand the limitations of IRT imaging and how it is affected from different
conditions, a performance evaluation of IRT was examined. This analysis focused on the
impact of drone altitude and solar irradiance on the accuracy of IRT. The results showed
that lower drone altitudes and higher irradiance levels significantly improve the spatial
resolution and thermal contrast of drone IRT images, thereby increasing the detection
capability for localized defects such as ribbon damage and cell cracks. Conversely, some
failure types such as bypass diode short circuit, produce clear thermal patterns that can
be detected even at higher altitudes and under varying irradiance levels.

For each failure mode, the typical thermal patterns, overall detectability, and the limitations
of IRT were examined. The findings indicate that IRT is highly effective for detecting
failures such as bypass diode short circuit, and can also be effective for cell cracks and
ribbon damage depending on the severity of the failure, altitude and irradiance levels.
However, its reliability decreases for defects such as glass breakage and low severity
PID, which may not show clear thermal patterns.
Overall, the results indicate that the effectiveness of IRT is highly affected by both the
type and severity of the failure, as well as inspection conditions such as altitude and
irradiance levels. Therefore, complementary diagnostic techniques such as EL imaging
and IV measurements are recommended to achieve a comprehensive and accurate fault
diagnosis in PV modules.
