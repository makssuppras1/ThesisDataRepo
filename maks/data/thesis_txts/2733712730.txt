Abstract
Non-destructive test methods in the form of ultrasonic pulse velocity and rebound hammer
were utilized together with destructive strength tests on extracted cores to develop conversion
models with the intent of estimating the compressive strength of pre-fabricated concrete columns
with a design strength of fck = 30 MPa.
The developed conversion models and subsequent
strength estimates were highly affected by errors occurred in the compressive strength tests on
extracted cores. Nonetheless, careful outlier analysis and comparison with previous studies led
to a somewhat reliable conversion model able to estimate the characteristic strength of columns
to fck,est = 18,9 MPa. Furthermore, reuse of columns was investigated and suggestions for how
it could be carefully removed from its erected spot were made. This involved the disassembly
of column-column connections with diamond sawing being the least damaging and thus most
promising in terms of reusing entire columns. Unfortunately, present economic, environmental
and technical barriers means that involved stakeholders hesitate in absorbing the principle of 1:1
reuse of structural elements. However, incentives from decision makers could burst the bubble
and turn reuse in to the greatest trend within the construction industry.

1
Introduction
Urban areas in the 21st century are characterized by never-ending structures and tangled infras-
tructure networks all being evidence of tremendous human innovation and technological prosper-
ity. Within this built environment, concrete emerges as a key construction material, providing
significantly to the durability of our structural accomplishments.
Concrete - composed of cement, aggregates and water - is a fundamental pillar of state of the
art construction.
Its widespread usage is attributed to its exceptional structural properties,
long-lasting nature, and adaptability. However, the production of concrete is known to have a
substantial environmental impact, mainly due to its high energy consumption and the release of
carbon dioxide during the cement manufacturing process. This environmental challenge compels
us to seek creative solutions to mitigate the ecological footprint of construction. One promising
avenue is the reuse of structural elements as they are responsible for 70 to 80 % of the embodied
CO2 [22; 23]. Unfortunately, multiple national and international barriers [24] makes it a rather
unpleasant and inconvenient direction to follow.
The concept of reusing structural concrete offers a compelling approach to address this challenge.
It aligns with the principles of a circular economy, wherein materials are repurposed, extending
their useful life and reducing the need for new resources. However, for the effective implementa-
tion of concrete reuse, a profound understanding of its mechanical properties is essential. This
is where non-destructive testing (NDT) comes into play.
Non-destructive testing techniques have provided opportunity for evaluating materials and struc-
tures without causing damage. These methods encompass a wide array of technologies, from
ultrasonic testing to ground-penetrating radar, offering non-invasive and efficient ways to collect
essential data. In the context of structural concrete, NDT enables us to investigate mechanical
properties, assess structural integrity, and determine suitability for reuse.
Multiple recent studies such as [1; 18; 19] showed that NDTs could be used to estimate the
compressive strength of concrete. Furthermore, [18; 19] showed that a combination of different
non-destructive test methods provides a greater correlation, which increases the accuracy of the
compressive strength estimation of concrete.
An overview provided in [1] has shown that the use of NDTs on their own are insufficient in terms
of strength estimation as a lot of factors affect the test methods. Data obtained from destructive
tests is a necessity in combination with conversion models to estimate strengths. However, the
number of destructive tests should be kept at a minimum to reduce time and money spent on
the tests. Whereas former studies might have circulated on exact determination of strengths [16]
suggests that the focus should be on ”estimating its value with a controlled tolerance interval
and a limited risk of being wrong” leading to less time spent on exact strength determination
and more time spent on estimations and risk management. Different conversion models have
been developed to estimate strengths ranging from simple and quick models shown in [17] with
limited accuracy to advanced, time-consuming and highly accurate models developed in [15].

Findings from the above-mentioned scientific research showed promising results, therefore, it is
expected, that the compressive strength of concrete can be estimated with a reasonable precision.
How reasonable depends on a great variety of factors ranging from concrete properties and the
element’s condition to the chosen test methods and conversion model.
With the current knowledge available this master’s thesis will explore the crossroads of concrete
reuse and non-destructive testing, investigating the mechanical properties of concrete elements in
the built environment utilizing the ultrasonic pulse velocity and rebound hammer tests in com-
bination with compressive tests on cores. The objectives encompass the key aspect of developing
dependable methods for non-destructive strength assessment of concrete elements hopefully con-
tributing to enforce a paradigm shift where existing load-bearing elements are viewed just as
convenient and logical to choose as brand new ones.

Theoretical background
2.1
Concrete
This theoretical section is based on [3] unless otherwise stated.
Concrete is a fundamental
construction material known for its versatility, durability, and wide-ranging applications in the
modern construction industry. It is a composite material composed of three primary ingredients:
cement, aggregates (such as sand and gravel), and water.
When mixed together in precise
proportions, these components form a cohesive and robust building material able to withstand
nearly any kind of weather phenomenon that the Earth has to offer.
The key to concrete’s strength lies in the process of hydration starting as water is added to the
cement, leading to a chemical reaction binding the cement particles and forming a matrix. Over
time, this matrix hardens and sets, resulting in the solid substance recognized as concrete. The
setting process is influenced by factors such as temperature, humidity, and the type of cement
used.
Concrete possesses a variety of mechanical properties with compressive strength and durability
being the primary. Tensile strength is not ideal (about 10-15 % of the compressive strength)
but can be achieved to a significant degree adding reinforcement to concrete’s cross-section. An
approximate stress-strain curve of concrete is shown in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Typical stress-strain curve of concrete.
Multiple factors influence the strength, including: water-cement ratio, aggregate properties, the
use of admixtures and curing conditions. The strength itself is typically determined after 28 days
of curing as the strength increase tends to stagnate after this point.

2.2
Non-destructive test methods
This theoretical section is based on [6] unless otherwise stated. Non-destructive testing methods
are a group of techniques widely employed in the field of civil engineering and construction to as-
sess the structural integrity, quality and properties of materials/components without causing any
damage. NDT plays a critical role in ensuring the safety, durability, and performance of various
structures, from buildings and bridges to dams and pipelines. These methods provide valuable
data that aid in making informed decisions regarding maintenance, repair, and retrofitting of
structures.
One of the primary advantages of non-destructive testing is its ability to identify flaws, defects,
or anomalies in materials and structures without the need for destructive sampling or disman-
tling. This allows engineers and inspectors to assess the condition of concrete elements, steel
reinforcement, and other structural components while preserving the integrity of the structure.
This project will utilize two different testing methods namely the ultrasonic pulse velocity and
the rebound hammer. Combining these is usually labeled as SonReb (abb.: SonicRebound).
This exact combination is a common NDT technique for estimation of concrete strength with
multiple former studies [1; 18; 19; 44] utilizing it.
The SonReb approach leverages the complementary strengths of ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV)
and rebound hammer (RH) testing to offer a comprehensive evaluation of concrete elements. It
allows inspectors and engineers to gather data on concrete density, elastic modulus, uniformity,
and potential areas of concern. By combining these non-destructive methods, the SonReb ap-
proach provides a more robust assessment of structural concrete, aiding in decision-making for
maintenance, repair, and structural integrity evaluations - and for this project - also estimation of
concrete strength. Former studies have even showed that the accuracy of the strength estimation
increased with the SonReb approach instead of just using one of the two methods [52; 53].
2.2.1
Ultrasonic pulse velocity
This theoretical section is based on [5] unless otherwise stated. UPV testing involves the transmis-
sion of high-frequency ultrasonic pulses through concrete elements. These pulses travel through
the material, and their velocity is measured. The speed at which the waves propagate depends
on the concrete’s density, elasticity, and integrity. By analyzing the time it takes for the ultra-
sonic waves to travel through the concrete in combination with the length it has traveled, UPV
testing can identify variations in material properties, voids, cracks, and other anomalies. It is
particularly useful for assessing the uniformity and quality of concrete. UPV tests utilizes the
theory of wave propagation. Thus, wave propagation theory is briefly explained in the following.
When dynamic loads affect a solid elastic medium’s surface, three mechanical waves emerge:
• Compressional waves (longitudinal or P-waves)

The less favorable transducer setups (semi-direct and indirect) can be useful in situations where

Unfortunately, UPV readings do not directly correlate with concrete strength due to variations in
concrete properties. In practice, strength estimation can be achieved through conversion models
based on UPV data, often calibrated with core strength measurements as also mentioned in [1].
Factors affecting ultrasonic pulse velocity test are summarized in the following.
• Concrete properties
– Age: The pulse velocity in concrete exhibits a pattern of rapid initial increase followed
by a plateau, akin to the strength development trend with concrete age. The velocity
curve flattens more quickly than the strength curve, and beyond this point, estimating
strength accurately becomes challenging due to experimental errors as per [73].
– Aggregates: Multiple studies [66; 67; 68; 69] have shown that the type and quantity
of aggregate influence the test. Generally, cement paste has lower pulse velocity than
aggregate. Different aggregates yield varying pulse velocities, with rounded gravel
having the lowest, crushed limestone the highest, and crushed granite in between as
per [70]. The type of aggregate does not significantly impact the pulse velocity versus
modulus of rupture relationship. Higher aggregate content tends to result in higher
pulse velocity at the same strength level according to [71]. Additionally, the proportion
of coarse aggregate affects the relationship between pulse velocity and compressive
strength, with higher aggregate-cement ratios leading to lower compressive strength
for a given pulse velocity.
– Admixtures: According to [70] air entrainment does not seem to impact the connec-
tion between pulse velocity and concrete’s compressive strength. Other admixtures
affect pulse velocity similarly to their influence on hydration rate. For instance, adding
calcium chloride decreases concrete setting time and boosts the pulse velocity increase.
– Cement type: The type of cement used does not notably affect the pulse velocity in
concrete [70]. However, the rate of hydration, which varies among different cements,

==PAGE:14==

can influence pulse velocity. Increasing hydration leads to higher modulus of elasticity
and, subsequently, an increase in pulse velocity. According to [72] the use of rapid-
hardening cements can result in higher strength at a given pulse velocity level.
– Water-cement ratio: [71] found that both compressive and flexural strengths, along
with the pulse velocity, decrease as the water-cement ratio increases, provided no other
concrete composition changes occur.
• Other effects
– Moisture: Saturated concrete has higher pulse velocity compared to air-dry con-
crete, with moisture having a greater impact on low-strength concrete due to poros-
ity differences according to [72]. the pulse velocity can increase by 4 to 5 % when
dry, high water-cement ratio concrete is saturated. Additionally, [74] showed that
laboratory-cured specimens exhibit higher pulse velocity than site-cured specimens,
and columns from the same concrete show lower pulse velocity compared to site-cured
and laboratory-cured specimens.
– Path length: Theoretically, path length and wave frequency should not affect pulse
velocity. However, [73] found that in practice, smaller path lengths may lead to slightly
higher and more variable pulse velocity due to concrete’s inhomogeneous nature. [75]
recommends minimum path lengths of 100 mm for concrete with a maximum aggregate
size of 30 mm and 150 mm for concrete with a maximum aggregate size of 45 mm.
– Reinforcement: The presence of steel reinforcement significantly affects concrete’s
pulse velocity, generally making it faster than in plain concrete as seen in for instance
[45; 47]. Readings near reinforcement are typically higher. Correction factors are used
when steel is present in the wave path, and these factors depend on pulse velocity and
path lengths. The correction factors may also consider bar diameters, especially in
heavily reinforced sections.
– Size and shape of specimen: Tests are usually independent of specimen size and
shape, but equation (2.1) requires that the smallest lateral dimension of a specimen
should be greater than the wavelength of the pulse. For concrete with a velocity of
about 3700 m/s and a transducer frequency of 54 kHz, the wavelength is approxi-
mately 68 mm. So, a specimen tested with this setup should have a minimum lateral
dimension of 70 mm. If it’s smaller, equation (2.1) may not be accurate, especially
for longer path lengths.
– Stress level: Pulse velocity is typically unaffected by stress levels in concrete. Still
according to [76; 77], when concrete undergoes very high static or repeated stress, like
65 % of its ultimate strength or more, microcracks form, significantly reducing pulse
velocity.
– Temperature: Variations in the range of 5 to 30 °C have no significant impact on
pulse velocity as per [72]. Corrections need to be made outside this range.

2.2.2
Rebound hammer
This theoretical section is based on [4] unless otherwise stated. Rebound hammer testing, also
known as the Silver Schmidt hammer test, assesses the surface hardness. It involves striking the
concrete surface with a spring-loaded hammer and measuring the rebound velocity of the hammer.
The rebound value is then correlated with the concrete’s compressive strength. RH testing is a
quick and portable method that provides an indication of concrete quality and potential defects,
making it a valuable tool for field inspections.
The rebound hammer consists of a body containing a latch, indicator, hammer mass, spring
and plunger. The testing procedure starts by holding the plunger perpendicular to the surface
of the concrete. The body is then slowly pressed towards the concrete extending the spring
that connects the hammer mass with the body. As the body reaches its limit the latch releases
automatically and the potential energy stored in the spring launches the hammer mass towards
the tip of the plunge ending in a collision between mass and plunge shoulder followed by a
rebound of the mass. During rebound, the indicator moves with the mass measuring the covered
distance. The rebound number is then calculated according to equation (2.3)

Factors affecting the rebound hammer test are summarized in the following.
• Concrete properties
– Age: Surface hardness of concrete increases rapidly up to 7 days, with minimal gain
afterward. Interestingly, [61] found that 7-day-old concrete can have higher rebound
values than 28-day-old concrete of equal strength. For testing old concrete, direct cor-
relations between rebound values and core compressive strength are essential. How-
ever, the rebound hammer is not recommended for testing low-strength concrete at
early ages or when strength is below 7 MPa, as rebound numbers are too low for
accuracy and the test hammer can severely damage the concrete surface [57].
– Aggregate type: [62] found that aggregate type affects rebound numbers signif-
icantly, with concretes using crushed limestone showing lower numbers compared
to those with gravel, indicating a difference in compressive strength.
Even if the
coarse aggregate type is the same, variations in sources require different correlation
curves according to [60]. Lightweight concrete made from different types of aggregates
also yielded different results, but rebound numbers were proportional to compressive
strength within a specific aggregate type as per [59].
– Cement type: Rebound numbers are notably influenced by concrete type. High-
alumina cement concrete can exhibit strengths twice as high as ordinary portland
Page 15 of 80
==PAGE:17==
Thesis
DTU Sustain
cement, while supersulfated cement concrete may have 50 % lower strength in com-
parison to ordinary portland cement concrete as per [63].
– Test surface: Surface texture significantly influences test accuracy.
Rough sur-
faces lead to reduced rebound numbers due to excessive crushing by the plunger tip.
Smoother, uniformly troweled or metal form surfaces yield higher rebound numbers
as per [59; 64]. Special correction charts or correlation curves are needed for these
surfaces. Troweled surfaces result in greater result variation and lower confidence in
estimated strength.
• Other effects
– Carbonation: Carbonation of concrete, especially in older concrete, impacts results
significantly. Carbonation can lead to a thick carbonated layer, which according to
[63] can result in rebound numbers up to 50 % higher than those on uncarbonated
surfaces. To avoid strength overestimation, appropriate correction factors must be
established.
– Moisture: The level of concrete saturation and surface moisture significantly influ-
ence test results [58; 61; 65]. When well-cured, air-dried specimens were soaked and
tested in a saturated surface-dried condition, they showed rebound readings 5 points
lower. Differences of up to 10 to 12 points in rebound numbers represented about
14 MPa difference in compressive strength for 3-year-old concrete as per [62]. Pre-
saturating the surface before testing and using the correlation for saturated surface-
dried specimens is advisable when the concrete’s actual condition is unknown.
– Size, shape and rigidity of specimen: Small concrete specimens may experience
movement during impact, causing lower rebound readings. To ensure consistent re-
sults, it is essential to rigidly hold or back up the member with a heavy mass. The
effective restraining load for stability appears to be around 15 % of the ultimate
strength according to [57], although some studies [58; 59; 60] have shown varying
values, ranging from 1 to 2 MPa.
– Internal stress: Rebound hammer results are affected by internal stresses in the
concrete. [54; 55] found that higher internal stresses lead to higher rebound numbers.
Thoughtful placement of test locations should accommodate differences in internal
stresses.
– Extreme temperature: Concrete elements exposed to extreme temperatures (for
instance in a fire) are not suitable for strength estimations with a rebound hammer.

2.4
Structural reuse
The concept of structural reuse has gained prominence as a transformative approach towards
minimizing environmental impact and maximizing resource efficiency. At its core, structural
reuse aligns with the principles of a circular economy, seeking to extend the lifecycle of materials
and reduce the demand for new resources. The need for a paradigm shift in construction practices
is crucial in the fight of reaching climate goals. The paradigm shift is advocating for a departure
from conventional demolition and reconstruction toward a more adaptive and sustainable model.
A sketch from [37] of reuse versus demolition is shown in figure 2.6.
Page 19 of 80
==PAGE:21==
Thesis
DTU Sustain
Figure 2.6: Structural reuse vs. demolition from [37].
In buildings, load-bearing structures have the greatest amount of embodied CO2 being in the re-
gion of 70-80 % according to [22; 23]. As for buildings facing demolition, their structural elements
are many times in fine condition due to being sheltered from the harsh climate. Furthermore, de-
molitions are rarely caused by the structural elements being weary as per [81] making it possible
to reuse them in new structures. As for concrete, [25; 26] found that reuse of extracted elements
in new constructions lead to cuts in CO2-emissions and less need for new raw materials.
Advancements in non-destructive testing (NDT) technologies, including but not limited to ultra-
sonic pulse velocity and rebound hammer assessments, have emerged as critical tools in evaluating
the structural integrity of existing elements. These technologies provide a nuanced understanding
of materials’ mechanical properties, enabling engineers to make informed decisions regarding the
feasibility of reuse. Former studies [25; 28; 48] have investigated the theoretical act of element-
wise reuse, while others [30; 49; 50] either describe previous examples or have done it in practice
themselves - although mostly as pilot projects.

Imperfect contact zone
An imperfect contact zone between the machine’s top head and the specimen was spotted for the
last tested core (L4.2.3.C). The imperfect contact zone led to the force being applied to an area
being smaller than anticipated thus leading to a premature failure as stresses in the subjected
area exceeded the strength of the concrete.
Furthermore, surfaces of test specimens were not completely flat as it was quite difficult to obtain
entirely flat surfaces with the applied concrete saw. However, instructions obtained for the test
machine prior to testing suggested that the top head was able to rotate and thus make up for the
not entirely flat specimen surfaces. It was later discovered that the top and bottom head had
been switched meaning that the rotatable head was placed in the bottom and not at the top as
expected. Thus, there was no way for the machine to correct for crooked surfaces as expected
prior to testing. An example of the flaw is shown in figure 4.6.

Discussion
The following section discusses results presented in section 4 with relevant studies. Results from
the exact same column types in building B/building 2 from [1] are included when relevant.
5.1
Non-destructive tests
5.1.1
Rebound hammer
Generally, rebound hammer tests were far easier and faster to conduct than ultrasonic pulse
velocity tests. The results on the other hand saw great deviation and vague correlation. This is
clearly shown in figure 5.1 showing linear regression between rebound number from columns and
measured strengths of extracted cores.
Figure 5.1: Linear regression of rebound number and measured compressive strength.
The coefficient of variation does not exceed 0,3 even after outlier analysis confirming the inferior
correlation. The trend of increasing strength with increasing rebound number is slightly visible,
however few points have similar rebound number but vary 4-5 MPa in strength. This although
could be a result of the flawed compressive strength as much as a flaw in the rebound hammer
Page 54 of 80
==PAGE:56==
Thesis
DTU Sustain
test. Notice the prediction error going from 8,9 MPa to 2,2 MPa which is below the EQL3 limit
of 4,5 MPa. The poor correlation was also the case in [1; 19] with [19] even stating that ”the
statistically validity of the model is questionable”. Local variations in concrete composition and
homogeneity, operator dependency (more experienced operator will be more consistent in general
technique) as well as the depth of measurements (rebound hammer tests only provide information
about concrete’s surface layer - not able to provide information about internal conditions) are
mentioned as some reasons for the less reliable results.
5.1.2
Ultrasonic pulse velocity
The ultrasonic pulse velocity test was rather circumstantial to conduct. Physical dimensions
of the column made it complicated to reach both sides of it when conducting direct tests by
yourself. The pre-test measurements of where to place the transducers were time consuming
and also demanding to do. Furthermore, as tests were done by a single person it was difficult
to check whether the transducers were placed and remained at the pre-measured points during
each test. Results on the other hand, were nonetheless more alike and showed moderately better
correlation than the rebound hammer results. Figure 5.2 shows linear regression of velocity and
compressive strength from extracted cores.
Figure 5.2: Linear regression of velocity and measured compressive strength.

lation between velocity and measured strength. The prediction error goes from 6,7 MPa looking
at all eight cores to 1,9 MPa after outlier analysis which is below the EQL3 limit of 4,5 MPa. The
trend of increasing strength with increasing velocity is more apparent than for rebound hammer,
however decreases in strength are seen at increased velocity. As with the rebound hammer this
error could be a result of the inaccurate compressive strength test as well as errors occurred in
the UPV test. [1; 19] saw good correlation between velocity and measured strength with [19]

this study and still had the trend of increasing strength with increasing velocity.
5.1.3
Combination of rebound hammer and ultrasonic pulse velocity
A comparison between SonReb results between this study and the study mentioned in [1] is made
as the tests being made in [1] are made on the exact same column types (ES301) in Nauticon’s
building B/building 2, but with a year apart. Furthermore, some of the conclusions drawn from
test results in [1] are also drawn in this study. The comparison can be seen in figure 5.3.
Figure 5.3: Comparison of RH and UPV data between [1] and this study.
The two data sets have some things in common (data points centered in a cluster with a few
outliers and same TRP & EQL), but [1] generally showed lower velocities and rebound numbers.
Looking at the specific data points it seems as if the obtained data in this study is just an
unknown factor greater than data from [1] which could suggest that the difference in sample

size is of little influence. Furthermore, the coefficient of determination is a factor 50 greater
than the one achieved in this study with [1] showing mild correlation and this study showing no
correlation at all between rebound number and velocity. [1] saw a tendency that greater rebound
numbers led to greater velocities. However, this is not similarly distinct for the data obtained in
this study.
The general difference in rebound numbers and velocities could be explained by two reasons
1. The columns in building A (this study) are cast in a different strength class than in building
B [1].
2. Columns are in the same strength class and quality, but unknown factors have a significant
impact on the test results.
Although differences are seen in the destructive tests with huge variation in this study compared
to [1], the average compressive strength of extracted cores varies by just 1,7 MPa from 25,69
MPa obtained in this study to 27,39 MPa in [1]. This suggests that columns in building A is
cast in the same strength class as columns in building B (the ones tested in [1]). Furthermore,
the existing drawings state that the examined columns (ES301) in both building A and building
B are supposed to have the exact same strength: fck = 30 MPa, thus eliminating the first reason
mentioned above.
This leads to unknown factors having a significant impact on non-destructive test results. These
factors include but are not limited to potential differences in the surrounding environment and
human factors in relation to test execution. As mentioned in section 2.2.1, temperature is only
a factor for UPV tests if the variations are outside the range of 5 - 30 °C, while it has no greater
influence on RH tests. Changes in humidity leading to absorption or release of concrete moisture
are mentioned as factors influencing both test types. Tests in this study and [1] were done in
more or less the same environment; a heated office building, meaning that differences in outdoor
relative humidity and temperature are highly unlikely to have caused the visible displacement
of data. Human factors in terms of test execution could be an explanation, but both this study
and [1] obtained the exact same test result precision and estimated quality level.

5.4
Element reuse
Reliable strength estimates from non-destructive tests makes it realistic to reuse elements in
a new construction if the service life allows it. Load bearing structures in concrete have an
average lifetime of 120 years according to [42]. Since the columns investigated in this study is
roughly 35 years it means that they potentially could serve another 85 years in a (or multiple)
’new’ structure(s). For the columns to be reused they would have to be carefully removed from
their erected spot. This could practically be done by first removing installations and structures
supported by the columns before breaking down the existing column connection. The disassembly
is sketched in figure 5.5 as a four-step process.
Figure 5.5: Sketch of disassembly process. Slabs, beams and columns.
The four steps are described below and resemble the erection process but in reverse order.
• Step 1: Removal of interior, installations, etc. until only load bearing structures are left.
• Step 2: Crane-removal of slabs.
• Step 3: Crane-removal of beams.

• Step 4: Crane-removal of columns.

crane-removal. The column-column connection can according to [25] be broken down in different
ways. The least damaging method involves sawing the connection with a diamond saw while it
is attached to a crane preventing crushing/breaking of the saw. If column dimensions prevent
using a regular diamond saw, a diamond wire saw can be used instead. The wire saw requires
space on all sides of the column as the wire has to be placed around the column to cut it. The

After removal the column can be reused in another structure. While the concept of structural
reuse holds immense promise, multiple factors influence its feasibility. Some of these factors are
distributed into the following categories: economic, environmental and technical. The following
section describes some pros and cons in the different categories.
• Economic factors:
– Pros: Profitable models are present for element reuse under certain conditions, with
economic savings identified due to cuts in salvaged material costs and fewer operations
on reclaimed pieces according to [26]. A study [27] found that extracted panels cost a
third of the price of new ones. Positive labor impacts are anticipated by prioritizing
local labor over importing new materials, fostering employment within the community
as per [34]. Reusing materials presents opportunities for new jobs and business models
[35].
– Cons: Contrary to profitable models, non-profitable models in certain circumstances
also exist, meaning that the use of extracted concrete elements can be more expensive
than conventional construction methods. Extra costs are often attributed to inconve-
nient processes as per [33]. Deconstruction and reconditioning operations are currently
Page 62 of 80
==PAGE:64==
Thesis
DTU Sustain
costly, emphasizing the need to minimize sawing and drilling for economic competi-
tiveness according to [26]. The limited availability of financial records in the reference
set hinders planners from making fully comparable cost estimations.
• Environmental factors:
– Pros: The primary driver for reuse of extracted elements is the reduction of global
warming potential and environmental impact as per [25; 26]. It also minimizes waste
generation and raw material consumption.
– Cons: Inferior handling of available elements might lead to extreme transportation
stretches. However, multiple studies [25; 28; 36] found the governing distance to be
well into the hundreds of kilometers.
• Technical factors:
– Pros: Material properties such as strength can be determined with great reliability
through non-destructive testing as already shown in multiple studies [1; 18; 19; 44].
Possible decrease in construction time as opposed to in-situ cast concrete, which have
to undergo curing time as per [38]. Buildings to be demolished typically have load-
bearing structures in a great shape meaning that the elements are suitable for reuse
as mentioned in [81].
– Cons: Lacking standards makes it time-consuming for each company to develop meth-
ods for determination of material properties. Incorporation of extracted elements are
subject to limitations in terms of physical dimensions being predefined. Furthermore,
[39] mentions that fire and sound requirements may not be up to date on reused ele-
ments with today’s harsher demands. Unlike new materials, both material quality and
geometrical tolerances in extracted elements depend on assessments by engineering,
deconstruction, and construction companies, necessitating a new liability approach.
Careful disassembly with the intent of structural reuse is time-consuming and the
circulation of extracted concrete elements is scarce as per [40].
If most of the cons are overcome, then structural element reuse could be turned into a prof-
itable and thriving business model, hopefully making it the ’new normal’ and thus significantly
contributing to the reduce of CO2-emissions.

6
Conclusions
Based on experiments, results and a thorough discussion, the following can be concluded:
• A combination of the non-destructive test types of ultrasonic pulse velocity and rebound
hammer can in collaboration with compressive tests on some extracted cores be used to
conservatively estimate compressive strengths of pre-fabricated concrete columns to 18,9
MPa with a prediction error of ± 3,3 MPa.
• The reliability of the developed conversion model largely depends on the trustworthiness
of the obtained data from destructive tests. Test errors in the destructive test will lead to
useless conversion models with prediction errors way above the suggested limit of 4,5 MPa
for EQL3.
• A conversion model based on destructive and non-destructive data gathered from a specific
column type is not usable for newly collected data on the exact same column type without
calibrating it, unfortunately maintaining the need for core extractions.
• Disassembling a column by diamond sawing makes it applicable for 1:1 reuse. However, it
requires incentives to choose over using brand new elements as economic, environmental
and technical obstacles currently exist.
