ii. 
Abstract 
In recent years, the space-travel industry has developed at a ‘skyrocketing’ pace. Today, space-travel is for 
the first time offering commercial services to the public people. As space-travel is becoming everyday news 
and no longer a government or military exclusive experience, it is likely that many people will seek to 
experience a journey to space. 
A growing number of private players are entering the game of providing sub-orbit, orbit and space-travels, 
such as Blue Origin, SpaceX, Virgin Galactic, and others. The increasing competition is leading to more 
advancement in technological capabilities, and the reduction of cost over time allows entrepreneurs to 
further entry for a larger customer base. 
The expansion of this industry will presumably become commercialized to a degree of being accessible by 
even middle-class citizens, as the case of the ordinary aviation travel industry. This will result in a growing 
number of people seeking a space-travel experience and therefore, accommodating preparations for such 
industries is a necessity. 
Spaceports will be requisite in the future for securing commercialized operation. Just as airports facilitate 
travel in airplanes in the aviation industry, spaceports will be needed when rockets and other spacecrafts 
will operate to take humankind to space. A spaceport is a collective term, a typology, for any building and 
site complex facilitating space operations, whether it is for military and scientific research, satellite, or 
commercial use. A spaceport consists of a site complex with buildings serving as an operation unit for 
space-travel activities.  
This thesis occupy itself with producing a framework for designing spaceports. Knowledge about spaceports 
is not easily accessible, and relevant information and literature are limited due to the current very early 
stages of development. So far only few spaceports exist, whereof only one was designed specifically for 
commercial use.  
The main objective of this thesis was to provide a guiding tool for spaceport design taking the form of a 
framework that can be employed by future designers. This framework is designed in an encapsulated order, 
providing a chain of steps to support a design project of any spaceport requested by a potential client.  
As an overall result of this project, it may be concluded that the framework is ready for testing and is the 
first of its kind. It provides a foundation for further research and development regarding the focus on 
spaceports and the importance of their designs.

1 Introduction 
In the beginning of the Space Age dating back to 1957, a new technological movement emerged as the first 
satellite was launched to space. The Space Race of space exploration and space technologies began their 
emergence and momentum increased significantly in the early 21st century as private competitors entered 
the spaceflight industry.  
Regardless of the space activities conducted by government or commercial bodies, the industry has 
expanded immensely and continues to do so. All over the world more competitors join, and in 
contemporary times a few companies already offer space-touristic activities to space at a commercial level 
for regular civilians.  
It is soon expected that the technological developments will accelerate as aviation did in the past, and 
space-travel will be highly accessible by normal citizens without major incomes or fortunes. Travelling with 
airplanes today is affordable for most and hence the development and construction of airports can be 
found all over the world.  
The same effect is expected for commercial spaceports in the not-so-distant future, as the first commercial 
space trips are being conducted already in the year 2021. It is presumed that the space tourism industry 
will grow rapidly and boarding a rocket will be an everyday event. This expansion of industry will most likely 
be a global expansion and countries like Brazil, India, US, Russia, and China are already grooming their 
respective industries to further develop technologies within space exploration and tourism.1 
Companies are, however, less focused and concerned with the spaceport, except for Virgin Galactic, and 
more focused one optimizing the spacecraft and spaceships.2 Commercial spaceports are yet to be 
developed and built. 
Spaceports will spread all over the world and rockets will become the state-of-the-art travel vehicle for 
humanity in the future, as a result of the growing market of space tourism.3 This new type of travel will 
revolutionize human travelling and take us into new eras. However, this must be prepared. 
Currently, spaceports are reserved for mostly research, and the existing ones do not provide the necessary 
experience for commercialization. Hence, the spaceport must be rethought into a commercialized entity. 
Modern day airports do not only serve as a hub for planes to travel but has evolved to a space where 
people can meet, shop and travel. The airport is designed not only for plane operation, but for the human 
experience to be understood, pleased, and cherished. Spaceport with such features do not exist yet, 
however, this thesis aims to change that. 
The aim of this thesis is to provide a framework for future designers as a guiding tool for designing 
spaceports. The framework should assist the designer in mapping and understanding of such project and 
the requirements necessary to achieve a well-functioning design solution. The spaceport typology is

1.1 State the problem 
If one assumes that the space industry will advance and develop to the level of importance and impact as 
global aviation, and that space-travel and space-tourism will grow equally exponentially in usage and 
popularity, how can future engineers and architects provide solutions for commercialized buildings serving 
travelers and other citizens. 
The spaceport typology is relatively new and not much is known about this typology and its specifics. As 
designers have limited access to existing spaceport solutions, alternative ways of finding the correct 
solution must be explored. 
The current problem is there is not a template or manual for designing spaceports. Spaceports must be 
customized to their specific project and their use.  
There are many elements to consider when designing a spaceport, and one can easily overlook crucial 
details in the process. If there was a template or a guide of what to do while designing a spaceport, then 
many problems and potential mistakes can be avoided.  
The objective becomes that a form of a guiding tool must be created to assist the designers of spaceport. 
The thesis aims to produce a framework for which designers and others can work under.  
1.2 Structure  
The structure of the thesis provides a logical setup for the reader to gradually be introduced to concepts 
and ways of understanding the topics of the project. The objective of structuring the thesis in this manner is 
to ensure the reader with brief and necessary background knowledge.  
In chapter 2 the background introduces a brief historical development of technology. This sets the 
timeframe for how the technology evolves over time and what the current development status is.  
This chapter then explains system information architecture, which is a key component of this thesis, 
followed by an introduction of Zachman framework, both its general setup and the employment of it. This 
provides the basis for the methodic approach of the thesis. 
In chapter 3, the methodology is introduced. This chapter provides the understanding of the techniques 
and methods employed in this thesis. The established framework is the result of thesis and is obtained 
through methodic data collection.  
The next chapter, Chapter 4, offers a first draft of the framework in a generic manner. This chapter explains 
the different elements of an architectural design framework, initially presented in a simple table schematic. 
Each row of the table is elaborated upon, to facilitate a better understanding of the conceptual and 
functional essence of each section for the designers, which subsequently prepares the reader for the next 
chapter. 
Chapter 5 introduces the main product of this thesis’ project, the spaceport framework. This framework is 
obtained using the generic architectural framework of chapter 4, but customized and tailored specifically 
for the design of spaceports. This chapter allows designers to quickly recognize crucial considerations and 
decisions that must be included in a design scheme for spaceports. The contend of this chapter is what 
should be practically applied or used as support for the designing phase.

1.3 Scope  
The scope of this paper can be roughly divided into two categories.  
1.3.1 The scope of the thesis:  
Approaching this subject of spaceports with the accessible literature and data to 
generate a functioning framework as tool. The thesis excludes quantitative data 
and other numerical data, as this was not considered fit for architectural analysis. 
The data relevant for background knowledge were broad and extensive, and used 
primarily for understanding the field of space-exploration and rocket industry. 
However, once the framework became the objective, the scope had to be limited 
to focus on the data relevant for generating the framework. 
 
1.3.2 The scope of the framework:  
The scope of this thesis was not to produce an actual design of a spaceport or 
produce detailed illustrations or architectural renderings. 
Instead, the scope of the framework widened to be adjustable and useful for other 
types of architectural projects, however, the specific framework established in this 
project confines itself to work particularly well for the architectural designs of 
spaceports. The intention of the framework is not to facilitate a full solution nor to 
provide a complete tutorial on the design of spaceports. Rather it aims at serving as 
an assisting and guidance tool in the design phase of a spaceport. Conclusively, the 
scope of this project is to establish a foundation and a useful tool with simple 
guidelines and ideas to accelerate future architectural projects of commercial 
spaceports. The objective of this thesis is to provide designers a tool for designing 
spaceport.  
To specify the goal, as a tool can be widely defined, the main objective is to generate the framework as 
mentioned accommodating and initiating the first steps for enterprises or projects concerning designs of 
spaceports.

2 Background 
Before getting into this thesis, it must be clarified for the reader, that in the field of architecture and 
engineering there rarely is a final or best solution; rather the goal is first to reach a possible solution, 
second to optimize the scheme with respect to the parties involved. It is through experience and data 
collection, that solutions can improve over time. 
As technological development advances, new types of buildings and systems are demanded to facilitate the 
ever-evolving technological societies. New types of buildings most be developed to suit and accommodate 
present tasks and requirements demanded.  
In ancient times, some of the few architectural needs were housing and storage, primarily. Since knowledge 
about designing and building houses has existed for millenniums, it has had time to be improved and 
optimized through-out time. Modern single-family houses offer much more luxury and comfort than the 
average medieval royal castles.  
The architectural development through-out time is a result of advancements constructed on prior 
knowledge. Designers have had the task of adjusting design to their contemporary times and needs. 
Though existing knowledge may not always be freely available, designers must adapt to find new solutions 
when new types of buildings are needed. New types of buildings emerge from outside factors, which are 
not directly related to architecture, but demand architectural solutions. 
To put this into context, historical perspectives can be drawn to the emergence of trains and airplanes. 
These technological developments have changed societies globally. When these advances in technology 
emerged, certain logistical difficulties occurred due to the increasing capacities demands from users and 
operators. A calling for solutions to these problems, could be solved by architectural approach to design 
buildings.  
When locomotives emerged, the need for central stations was not immediate. However, as passengers 
increased and capacity limits were reached, Concourse buildings for train stations began to take shape. The 
first attempts of designing central station provided architects, engineers, and other designers, the 
necessary groundwork that could be utilized as data for further developments and improve modern central 
stations. In this way, modern central station has become better overtime and facilitate better travel for 
both the passengers, and the vehicles. 
The same is true for the history of airports, and the gradual advancements of them. A new transportation 
vehicle was emerging. Airplanes were being designed, developed, and improved for their times travel 
industry. As this new type of transportation technology was becoming more reliable, its use got expanded 
into commercial use. 
Once it got into a commercial level, frameworks for airplanes, processes for human flow and capacities had 
to be thought into the design. Designers have had almost 100 years to perfect the design of airports.

• 
The owner  
• 
The designer 
• 
The builder 
In the section, the 3 roles will be presented, examined and studied to understand their tasks and their 
respective relationship to each other. However, note that “The Designer” will be the focus of this paper. 
In recent centuries, the complexity of many fields including architectural projects has increased significantly 
due to technological advances and a gradual increase in universal quality of life. Consequently, systematic

and well considered methods and outlines are needed to carry out intricate and interconnected problems

In doing so various methods and strategies have been developed through out time and are being very 
handy as tasks difficulties get harder. Current technologies are rapidly removing both conceptual and

One method is called information system architecture which is an approach for obtaining, categorizing and 
applying information and data to help and simplify tasks for a business or organizational information 
system. Noteworthy, is the so-called Zachman framework developed by A. J. Zachman in the 1980’ies. 
In pursuit of an unbiased, independent foundation to develop a framework for information systems 
architecture upon, it seemed reasonable to investigate the field of classical architecture itself, learning from 
the thousands of years of experience that has been accumulated in that field. The classical architect has 
since the origins of the field been tasked to deliver from unique designs and concepts to specifics and 
details. The architects have through experience acquired a method that helps them reach a final goal. 
Designing is an infinite trial-and-error process   to reach a specific goal, which in most cases is most 
certainly elusive, been nearly reach with a general agreement, appreciation and satisfaction.  
An example could be a classical scenario, that could have taken place during the iron age; a person, in this 
scenario denoted the perspective owner, approaches another individual, who appears to have 
architectural flair, called the architect: 
Owner : “ I’d like to build a building” 
Architect: ”For what purpose do you have in mind for this building. Is it to live in? 
Pray in? Or work in?” 
Owner: “ Well, I’d like to live in it” 
Architect: “Then it’s a house you need?” 
Owner: “Yes! I’d like a house!” 
architect: “How large a house do you have in mind?” 
Owner: ”My lot size is 50 meters by 100 meters. I have a family. We are two adults 
and two kids” 
Architect: ”So you want a house of with at least 3 bedrooms?” 
Owner: ”Sounds about right” 
Architect: ”Alright, let’s sketch some ideas, and tell me what you like in the 
meantime” 
This conversation initiates the beginning of a long thread of communication between the owner and the 
architect.  Such conversation is a key element of communicating desires and needs of the owner and to be 
translated into specific designs by the architect. Note that each question serves to pose a constraint (lot 
size) or identify a requirement (the number of bedrooms) in order to establish the “ballpark” or 
approximate conditions, within which any design will take place. The drawings of the architect are a 
transcription of the owner’s perceptual requirements.

This leads to the first architectural tasks: to generate conceptual representations, a “bubble” chart, which 
crudely depicts the size, shape, spatial relationships, and basic intent of the final structure. This bubble 
chart is a result of   the initial conversations between the owner and the architects.  
The architect will be depicting what the owner has in mind in a series of “bubbles”, each bubble 
representing a room, its gross size, function, shape, spatial relationship etc. the architect prepares bubble 
charts like these for two reasons, firstly, the owner must express what he/she has in mind that will serve as 
a foundation or basis for the architect’s actual design work. Secondly, the architect must convince the 
owner that the owner’s desires are understood well enough so that the owner will pay for the creative 
work to follow, and in effect initiate the project.  
Once the architect has established a basic understanding with the prospective owner, the architect 
produces the next set of architectural deliverables, which is called the architect’s drawings. 
The architect’s drawings are the transcription of the owner’s perceptual requirements, a depiction of final 
product from the owner’s perspective. 
The drawings include horizontal sections (floor plans), vertical sections (cross sections cut) and pictorial 
representations depicting the artistic motif of the final structure. The purpose of these drawings is to 
enable the owner to relate to them and to agree or disagree; “that is exactly what I had in mind!” or “make 
the following modifications.” 
The drawings can be very detailed; however, they are normally developed only to a level of detail required 
for the prospective owner to understand and approve the design. Once the owner is satisfied that the 
architect has captured what he or she has in mind, and further agrees to pay the price of continuing the 
project, the architect produces the next set of architectural deliverables, which are called the architect’s 
plans. 
Architect’s plan. The architect’s plans are the translation of the owner’s perceptions/requirements into a 
product. The plans are a designer’s representation of the final product (as opposed to an owner’s 
representations, which is embodied in the the drawings). The designer’s representation(plans) puts an 
explicit specification around the material composition of the final product. 
The plans are composed of the categories of detailed representations, including site-work, electrical 
system, masonry wood structure, etc. they describe the relationship between materials in forms of 
diagrams and drawing as well as bills-of-materials. These plans are the final deliverables prepared by the 
architect and ultimately become the official “record” of the finished structure. 
The architect’s plans are prepared to serve as basis for negotiation with a general contractor. The owner 
takes the plans to a contractor and says, “build me one of these.” If the contractor builds “one of these,” 
which is represented in the architect’s plan, then the owner knows that there is a high probability of getting 
the desired product, as depicted in the architect’s drawings. 
As a result of the negotiations between the owner and general contractor, the plans may be modified 
because of cost or price and other considerations, but finally serve to represent what is committed to 
constructions. 
Contractor’s plans. The contractor redraws the architect’s plans to produce the contractor’s plans 
represententing the builder’s perspective. Such plans are prepared because complex engineering products 
are not normally built in a day. Some phased approach is required which in the case of a building, may

comprise first some sitework; next the foundation; then first floor, and so on, until the building is 
completed. Furthermore, the contractor may have technology constraints. Either the tool technology or the 
process technology may constraint his ability to produce precisely what the architect has designed. Either 
way, the contractor will have to redesign the architect’s drawings, to produce and build into the real world

Shop plans. Subcontractors are hired. They prepare other representations for the structure. These

First observation suggests that the stakeholders could roughly be divided into three ‘roles’ if you may. For 
each role a fundamental architectural representation. The roles are: the owner, the designer, the builder: 
1. The owner, who is primarily the one to fund, to consume, utilize and sets the demands 
and needs for product. Has the ability to dictate and control what purposes the product 
should serve and roughly envisions an output based on the inputs granted by the 
owner/user/consumer. The owner has therefore perceptions of a product- this is the 
owner’s perspective. 
2. The designer, who is primarily acts as a medium for the owner, to translate the owner’s 
vision of what ever product he/she has in mind to serve som specific purpose. The 
designer is not a single person or discipline, but rather a collection of different disciplines 
to serve as information extractors from the owner to convert and translate from abstract

3. The builder, who is the in the final stage as the executer of the real world – taking

not depict the final product in total, as they are focused around the implementation activities, fabrication 
and local montage. These representations must be included as they contribute to the holistic general 
activity of construction and production and is a necessary phase even though they might be overlooked in 
the very beginning. These presentations are the necessary entities to complete the project/product though 
short of being the final physical product. E.g. designing of a toilet, a door, etc.7

not simply a set of representations, they each display a level of detail greater than the previous one. It is 
important to understand that with each phase of representations is crucial and necessary.  
The level of detail is an independent variable, varying within any one architectural representation. The 
designer’s representation in architect’s plans is not simply a succeeding and increasing level of detail or the 
owner’s representation in the architect’s drawing. It is different in its content, visual language, setup and 
approach as it represent a different perspective, which needs to be manifested at a level corresponding to 
the phase. The level of detail og the designer’s representation, like the plans, is variable and independent 
of the level of detail of the owner’s representation which is the architect’s drawings.  
Therefor there is for each of the architectural representations a difference from one another in essence, 
not only in level of detail.

3 Methodology 
One challenge associated with this paper was the under-researched nature of a non-standard design, and 
subsequently, a very limited access to relevant research material and literature. To produce an 
architectural framework for spaceports, vast knowledge, and data within many fields associated and 
relevant to the space industry are required.  
Research of knowledge and data for such problem accumulated much research time due to the limited 
access and scarcity of information. This resulted in exploratory research, as spaceports are still considerably 
new even-though the first ‘prototypes’ were constructed in the 1960’s and forward. The race-to-space by 
competing governments lead to increasing secrecy and confidentiality and enormous amounts of research 
classified from the public.  
As the aim of this paper was to generate a framework for spaceport design, the method for collecting data 
and producing the spaceport framework has been divided into multiple levels. 
3.1 Methodological approach 
As this topic has been under-researched until private companies entered the race-to-space competition in 
contemporary times, not much quantitative data was expected to be released or accessible. However, 
qualitive data, such as articles, interviews, and public announcements, could be extracted through different 
sources. 
The goal was to gather as much information and knowledge as possible within the field of space technology 
and spaceports. This allowed the researcher to understand the extension of the field, and what literature 
and other information sources are relevant. 
3.2 Methods of data collection 
In order to better understand the precise definition and content of a spaceports, all relevant information 
was gathered from different sources. This provided a vast amount background knowledge which led to 
better understanding. The obtained background knowledge allowed the researcher to make competent 
assumptions for the architectural framework for spaceports.

4.1.2 The site 
 
Where is the project taking place? Geographical information is a crucial element, as it will dictate the 
circumstances of how construction and operation will turn out. The landscape will determine the shape of 
the structure and overall design as it must be fit and adjusted to work in unity and coherence with it. Is it an 
open field? Is it a dessert? Is it near water? Is it in the middle of the ocean? etc.  
The choice of site is undeniably one of the most, if not the most, crucial decision that has to be made as it is 
the parameter that would affect every part of decision-making for designers and stakeholders along the 
way. This decision will be the core foundation of realization of the project, therefor a deep and exhaustive 
analysis must be performed in order to advance any decision.  
Surely, one must understand the Underlying needs, essentials and requirements of the project in order to 
determine the designated site, however a paradox might occur, as it is nearly impossible to possess all 
future data.  
The choice of the site will dictate the list of needs and requirements; however, the list of requirements and 
needs will depend on the chosen site. Therefore, the decisionmaker will be in the paradox dilemma of 
where to start as the interdependency of this situation is clear. It is the classical question of whether it was 
the egg or the chicken that came first.  
However, in order to come past this dilemma, one must return to the project and get an overview of 
external factors that will assist and dictate the location of site. Such factors are irrelevant for the 
architectural elements of the project, such as economical concerns and logistical issues.  
These external factors that will influence and ultimately decide the site's location come from a different 
perspective that engineers, architects and other designing stakeholders, who do not primarily share with 
the owners, investors or other parties in the project. An example of how an external factor can decide the 
site could be, as mentioned, the economic perspective. The project is often a business, if the not the 
business model itself. The economic perspective wants the project to be optimized regarding its profit and 
monetize its facilities. How it is shaped or how it precisely works is irrelevant for economics. The economic 
perspective will ask the designers, engineers, and final decision-makers, to increase and maximize the 
number of users(consumers), make it as accessible for users, and locate it as convenient as possible for the 
users. Knowing these external factors, the decision-maker will take this perspective into account and adjust 
according to it. However, the decision-maker must balance all perspectives there are regarding the choice 
of site in order to get the best desired results. 
Another external factor might the builder’s perspective as the job of constructing will be heavily dependent 
on the accessibility and logistical approach of building materials to arrive to the site. This is a very 
important element since the level of difficulty of accessibility will influence the price and overall economics 
of the project. Those external factors become requirements on their own, and therefore will set the course 
of the designers and engineers’ ability to decide on a side. Since the external factors therefore become the 
requirements of the project, it is natural that all these considerations and thoughts are already mapped out 
in the ‘project step’, which is the previous step of deciding on a site.  
4.1.3 The processes 
In all aspects of any given enterprise, project, construction or business etc. it is a universal observation, that 
some sort of process will take place. A process is a series of actions taking place one after another in order

to reach a final step which delivers the desired output. Whether it being a chemical, algorithmic, or physical 
process, it all starts with an input triggering, starting or igniting the process to begin its movement. 
Understanding how processes works and why they work as they do, is a necessary step in order to achieve 
the knowledge in which one can be able to replicate, manipulate or control any given process. Such 
understanding is achieved through many ways, one being collection of data, experiences or mapping the 
logic that surrounding it.  
Processes are observed everywhere in all industries. All industries undergo some sort of series of actions in 
their respective fields to achieve their goals, productions, or delivery of services. Even though all industries 
have their different processes, they all essentially do the same job: planning the series of action in order to 
achieve, optimize or reach their targeted goal.  
What is learned from all these processes is there exist a universal guideline that dictates all processes in all 
aspects. This is extracted from the descriptive model of the Zachman framework mentioned earlier in this 
paper as the ‘Input-process-output’- model.  
 
Figure 2 Input-process-output model 
 
 
This model is applicable for in almost all occurring issues that might show up. Its simple layout allows any 
stakeholder to set up the logic for their desire: 
1. The input:  
This is the element in beginning, before any process. This element could be anything; a human, an 
item, a molecule, a number or amount or area or size etc.  
The input is existing data or knowledge in it pre-processed stage. It is the raw material of this 
model.  
2. The process: This is the part where the transformation of the input occurs. This is a universal 
occurrence that happens in all aspects of life, any field or discipline etc. the process can be 
instantaneous or occur over a duration of time. This depends on the existing factors of the specific 
subject. The process is the translation of input resulting into a new entity becoming the output. 
Now, to understand any result, product, output etc. one must understand the process it has 
undergone. Processes exists in many forms and variants, in which an input is treated.  
The process could be pooled, meaning an input could be processed differently or by different 
people leading to somehow the same result or outputs. For example: a client asks different 
architects for a house design. The client provides the exact same demands and requirements for 
the architects to design by, meaning all inputs the architects are working with are identical. The 
architects therefore become the processing machine. Now, as architects are different from one 
another, they will each interpret the inputs differently, process them differently leading to different 
results/outputs. However, they essentially reach the same typological output: a house!  
This is one example of how processes, who might seem the same, with the same inputs, can lead to

part of the project further and handover his work to the next in line.

4.1.4 The user 
For every product, every service, every idea, or every building ever proposed, produced or built, the final 
goal has always been and will always be the satisfaction of the user. The user is an abstract idea, rather 
than a person, who just uses and utilizes whatever product or service he/she consumes. This abstract idea 
of the user is not just being a single entity, but rather a collection of affected entities that results into the 
term user. This is not a redefinition, rather a statement that helps expand the understanding of the 
potential contend of what is understood, when the word user is presented at a conceptual level. An 
example could be: imagine an airport, the traveler arrives and must hand in the luggage at check-in. The 
luggage will then be transported through-out the entire airport complex arriving to its designated plane. 
Who’s the user in this case? Is it the traveler? The airport staff? Or is it the piece of luggage that actually 
uses the transportation infrastructure built inside the airport? Understanding that the user in this case is 
actually all of them at once, installs a new sense of observation for the reader. This helps designers and 
others map out, who and what is actually being designed, produced and built for.  
Before starting any project, it is important to understand, what and who the project is for. Once this is 
cleared and mapped, many of the following needed information will present itself. Asking the right 
questions from an early stage provides the necessary information to adjust the project to facilitate and 
serve the purpose(s). The user becomes the benchmark of valuating and validating a given solution or 
design. 
The user is the core of all considerations. When designing or making decisions, it is important to relate it 
and understand the effects on the user. As the user is the main target for satisfaction, reaching optimal 
solutions is going to be a requirement by itself. In this step it therefor important to list and map out all the 
involved parties participating as the users. This does not limit itself to only humans, but also any item, 
subject or system involved. For example; designing a parking lot. The design of a park lot building requires 
the understanding of the multiple layers of users. Stairs and lifts are needed for the people to move

4.1.5 The spaces 
Spaces in the architecture are one of the primary focuses of an architect. Before any space is placed, 
designed or shaped, the architect will collect the necessary information to address how spaces will 
accommodate and satisfy. 
Architects must systemize and categorize all needs and functionalities. All needs and functionalities are 
products of what are retrieved through the thorough investigations and research done in the previous 
steps/headlines. The need of spaces is derived by understanding the following: 
1. What are the motives of the project.  
2. Where is the project’s site located, built and taking place. 
3. How is everything going to run, what processes are involved  
4. Who is/are the user(s) of project. 
As information is being collected asking interrogative questions, the answers and results will be the 
foundations of how spaces become what they are.  
Taking the 1st question which leads back to the first step of the framework; The project. Before any project 
takes place, there must be something or someone setting it in motion. There has been some incentive to 
initiate a such project to accommodate a specific target, goal or desire. The incentive could be anything 
depending on what the current need or desire is. In construction or real estate business the incentive 
usually is the economic benefits of pursuing a such project. Another incentive could be practical needs such 
as lack of housing, space or general accommodations, and therefor profits might not be the main objective 
e.g. government housing projects. A third example of an incentive could be totally unrelated to the project 
but results to/in a project; this could be if society experiences high unemployment rates. To reduce 
unemployment infrastructure projects are often pursuit; not because the infrastructure is bad or needs 
immediate expansion or improvements, but simply to let the project generate the jobs to minimize the 
unemployment. It is therefore important for all stakeholders of any project to understand what the 
incentives of the enterprise, project or prospect is. Once this is cleared, the motivation of the target can be 
unified and pursuit in unison. 
Once the incentives and motivation are set, it is time for specifying how to achieve such goal and sketching 
ideas of what the project is; a building, a complex, a system, a website, a port, a ship, airport etc.

4.2 Typology 
Before diving any deeper, a definition must be in place for what type of project the stakeholders are 
involved in. The typology is the classification of characteristics, usually physical, commonly found in 
buildings, urban spaces, and other built environment projects, according to their association with different 
categories.15 Often used to identify a given project’s presuppositions and clarifying the needs and frames. 
For example, a single-family home is a widely known typology of a house, whos’ characteristics and 
functions are known and predefined.  
In many projects the typology is given beforehand, as the client’s request will often include the typology of 
building, they seek to build. Sometimes, that can be the only information given and no other restraining 
factors be presented such as economy or site. This is especially true in conceptual architecture 
competitions, where the typology can be the only information presented along the problem to be solved.  
However, as technologies evolve and innovation strikes beyond existing typologies, it is a task for 
architects, engineers, designers and researchers to define new categories for emerging typologies. An 
example could be the ports when ships began increasing in sizes, or the central stations for locomotives or 
the new architectural concept of airports facilitating the new technology at their time, airplanes.  
Typology is a way of systemizing and categorizing a collection of building types in architecture if they share 
the same overall concepts. Residential-buildings, office buildings, hospitals may differ in shapes, size, sites 
etc. but they all share the same users, functions, and frames. 
If a project doesn’t yet fall under a specific typology, then it becomes a challenge on its own to define and 
conceptualize a non-existing typology into existence. This will allow pioneers to set standards based upon 
thorough research to develop a such typology, which will be the foundation of future similar projects. 
This paper is a guideline of how to reach such definitions of typologies, specifically for what is mentioned in 
the title. For now, the core points are to understand the steps and conceptual understandings of each step 
to develop such a typology.

project is defined, a building or buildings needed to serve the project is now a clear task. Since the site is 
defined, the location and whereabouts of the buildings are mapped. Since the processes are defined, the

4.2.4 Concretization

5.1.1 Project: SPACEPORT  
The project is to design a spaceport facilitating future space tourism and travel. As previously mentioned, 
technologies are rapidly developing and evolving within the space industry, enabling a variety of new 
opportunities with corresponding challenges. One such challenge is how a facility can accommodate 
commercial space activities such as space travel and tourism. Roundtrips around the moon, trips to the 
boundary of the atmosphere, and long-distance space travels all launch from the same location: from 
Earth. This starting point of journey we denote the name point A. A known journey today could e.g., be 
travelling from London to Sydney. If travelling by ship, then the starting point would most likely be on a 
port near the sea or ocean. If travelling by airplanes, then the starting point would most likely be in an 
airport. In both cases some sort of building located in a suitable geographical position is required as well of 
some sort of vehicle performing the travelling activity. The template table above is somewhat universal and 
will be applied in this project as well. Starting point A will therefore be equipped with a new type of 
building to facilitate the new type of travel vehicle. Since the vehicle already is assumed; A rocket! The task 
is then to develop buildings with a new typology and new functions accommodating the transportation 
vehicle of a space rocket. 
A spaceport is one of the newest typologies within architecture and engineering, and therefore has limited 
references to relate to. A spaceport is a building or site complex designed to serve as travel hub for 
travelers seeking space adventures or a faster means of transport. Its main function is to serve the 
stakeholders, users, and facilitate all necessary operations for its vehicles and other technical, 
organizational, and operational needs.  
 Put another way, a spaceport is a site complex with buildings with their main objective being to facilitate 
launching and landings of spacecrafts; similar to a seaport facilitating ships, and airports for planes. A NASA 
vision Spaceport Partnership Report on the spaceports defines them as the “facilities, equipment, 
personnel, and vicinity required to prepare space-bound craft for flight, initiate and manage the flight and 
receive the craft at the end of the flight. For Earth-based spaceports, ‘vicinity’ refers to the land occupied by 
the facilities and equipment.” 17  
Inspirations can be drawn from the existing spaceport facilities such as the Kennedy Space Center (KSC) and 
the Cape Canaveral Airforce station, who traditionally served governmental and military operations, 
however recently expanded its accessibility for commercial and research usage by private companies such 
as SpaceX.18  
The project of designing a spaceport is a joint consortium of many stakeholders, who each have their own 
needs and motives. The main objective of the project is to have a complex facilitating space-bound travel. 
Assuming some of the stakeholders are SpaceX, Blue Origin, and other similar companies, then they will 
serve as both as clients of the construction project, but also end-users and operational managers after 
project completion. A spaceport should be constructed with the type of space vehicle preferred by the 
client in mind. For SpaceX or Blue Origin, vertical launch and landings systems should be included, and for 
companies such as Virgin Galactic systems for more traditional aviation facilities should be integrated, due 
to the horizontal takeoff and landing systems. For vertical takeoffs following criteria must be met as 
authorities seek safety measures for stakeholders, environment and other involved parties.

5.1.2 Site  
 
Selecting a site for the spaceport requires extensive research, satisfying the requirements and construction 
criteria. Streamlining Space Launch Range safety (2000) is a guideline produced by the Committee on Space 
Launch Safety and Aeronautics and Space Engineering Board. This book contains the relevant criteria and 
considerations a designer must address when dealing with in the design of spaceports.  
 
 
Figure 10. Streamlining Space Launch Range Safety(2000) 
The guidelines set standards and recommendations and provides the understanding of how to access and 
address Space Launch situations. The safety standards evolved as a result of the potentially dangerous 
space launches. In the early days in space program, risks were largely unknown and as a precaution, 
isolated areas were selected as launch sites. The range safety measure was developed to accommodate the 
following 4 factors: 
• 
The increasing range and explosive power of launch vehicle 
• 
The increasing encroachment of civilian populations and municipalities on the launch sites  
• 
The increasing sensitivity to public risk  
• 
The growing concern that a serious accident involving the general public would inhibit important 
space programs23

The goal was to ensure that the public would not be subjected to more risk from space launches than that 
imposed by the overflight of conventional vehicles. Though this is constantly adjusted with the evolvement 
of analysis techniques, technology and regulations, the report states two specific standards: 
• 
The standard of collective risk to the general public, expressed in terms of casualty 
expectation (Ec), must be less than 30 × 10–6 for each launch. This implies that one 
serious injury or fatality can be expected to occur for every 33,000 launches, or once 
every 1,000 years for a launch rate of 33 per year. 
• 
 The overall goal for reliability of the flight termination system (aka FTS) is 0.9981, with 
air and ground subsystems each meeting a reliability of 0.999.24 
Range safety is based on two primary, complementary elements: 
• 
The launch hardware is designed to be safe and is then managed, maintained, and 
operated to ensure that the design levels of safety are achieved at all times. 
• 
The range operators can reliably monitor launches inprogress, and, if something happens 
in flight that could compromise public safety, they can shut down the vehicle propulsion 
system, so the vehicle follows a ballistic flight path to a known, safe impact point.25 
Range safety personnel and procedures are established to ensure the protection people and property. In

solved perfectly, if the user doesn’t feel it intuitively welcoming or too overwhelming, then the solutions

At this stage there’s a distinction that needs to be made. There are two types of users at a conceptual level. 
1. The human user: this involves travelers, staff, crew, visitors and whoever who has a perception of 
understanding the surroundings of that given environment. This type of user is interested in having 
a smooth experience through the procedures and processes while on spaceport complex. The only 
interest of this type of group is that the spaceport facilitates all necessities of a human without 
compromising the comfort or experience of the stay. Put in other words, designers should not only 
consider solving potential users from a technical or practical point of view, but rather think from 
the view of the users. an example could be studying soviet housing projects; brutal architecture 
with only one benchmark: optimizing housing capacity in volume. This leads to the construction of 
thousands of apartment complexes, which served the purpose of housing as many as possible, 
providing the necessities of plumbing, electricity, and roof over people’s head. However, the 
architecture suffered as users generally experienced that the housing didn’t offer beauty, 
individualism or aesthetics. The user was simply not really considered in this enterprise. They 
designed the housing as if it was machines that would occupy these spaces. Therefor this type of 
user demands, requires and deserves a design that gives a welcoming, intuitive and pleasing 
experience, in order to have a successful operating spaceport from this type of users -view.  
2. The non-living operating users. now, this must be understood at a conceptual level, as this involves 
all vehicles, items and tools in motion and circulation at the spaceport.  
They too require a well-thought design to operate optimally. The infrastructure should not only be 
designed for human, as rockets will not fit inside buildings. The infrastructure should facilitate both 
humans and the vehicles and others occupying the spaces. Depending on who or what is going to 
be the main users determines the output of the design. Taking the “Rocket maintenance and 
preparation hall” mentioned in the previous section, this type of building is focused on the rockets 
and spacecrafts. Therefor this building should expectingly be exceptionally large facilitating space 
enough for multiple rockets roofed. This will result in an abnormous large building. This building 
will be very suitable for this type of user: the rocket. But will be perceived as monstrous and 
grotesque by the human user. The size is unwelcoming and belittles the human user. The type of 
architecture is often found in plants and factories, where the main user is not the human, but the 
production equipment and operation are in focus. Interestingly enough, the knowledge of how 
architecture affects the human user is almost ancient old. Courthouses of premodern architecture 
are often designed with large entrances with huge columns and an ascending elevation from 
entrance side. This is type of designed was deliberately made to intimate any person standing in 
front of the courthouse, to remind and manifest that the law and the system is larger and superior 
to the individual. Architecture therefore has the ability to induce specific feelings, experience and 
understandings, and this affects the users. hence the architect and designers must be aware of this 
while designing. For the non-living operating type of users, which boils down to machinery, 
vehicles, items and tools, the designers must secure practical and functional design that ensures 
smooth operation, while integrating the spaces to serve optimally for both users. 
The reason for the distinction is to clarify for the designers, who and for what is being designed. The 1st 
type of user requires designed at human level, while the 2nd requires more practical and strict functional 
design to secure operation.

5.1.4.1 
The astronaut traveler cycle 
Travelling to space on a commercial basis requires to be systematized into a clear process, of how this 
procedures work. One of the crucial elements of this venture is the limited data possessed by experts, at 
the moment. Human space travel has existed for the past 60 years, and some data has been gathered 
regarding the human body response to out-of-earth experiences.  
Venturing into the environment of space can have negative effects on the human body. Significant adverse 
effects of long-term weightlessness include muscle atrophy and deterioration of the skeleton (spaceflight 
osteopenia). Other significant effects include a slowing of cardiovascular system functions, decreased 
production of red blood cells, balance disorders, eyesight disorders and changes in the immune system. 
Additional symptoms include fluid redistribution, causing swollen faces of astronauts experiencing 
weightlessness, loss of body mass, nasal congestion, sleep disturbance, and excess flatulence.40 
Returning astronauts describe the return to earth as a physical challenge for the human body, as brushing 
your teeth with a toothbrush feels like lifting a hammer.41 Garrett Reisman, who is a trained astronaut with 
a stay in space of 95 days in a row, explains how returning to earth impacts the human body. He describes 
it as trying to recover from a major sports accident, taking him one year to fully recover from the 
spaceflight. The human body, which is used to be constantly pulled by gravity, starts deteriorating in space 
as the body sees no reason to regenerate muscle and skeleton mass.  
As travelers return to earth, it is therefore necessary to ensure optimal treatment immediately after 
landing, providing access to medical facilities and staff to take care of returning spaceflight travelers. 
Including a sort of integrated on-site hospital facility within the spaceport can secure travelers the 
necessary treatment by experts of respective medical, pharmaceutical, physio therapeutical fields etc. 
This will also serve as a research and development center, as the spaceport secures access to fresh data at 
a massive scale as space tourism expands. The more data gathered on site, the better preparation and 
development can be achieved to optimize the spaceport and further improve general space travel.

5.1.5 Spaces  
The spaces are derived from the understanding of the processes and the users. Each space serves a 
function. This function is dictated by the process in which it has to convert the input into the desired 
output. To specify, an example could be the space of the main concourse building, which has multiple 
purposes and involves multiples users. Each user is an input to this building. The function of the building is 
to process each user into the desired output. A person, who seeks to travel, will arrive at the “main 
concourse building”, purchase a ticket to a given space destination, and the result of this process will 
transform the person into a traveler. Hence the function turned the input (a random person) into the 
output (a targeted traveler). The process can be an activity or a series of activities, and it is important that 
the main focus of design is to facilitate the activities of the processes. Facilitating for process involves 
understanding the space, the needs and the relationship between spaces and the allocation of them.  
In this section it is important for the reader to realize that the definition of spaces in this paper is not 
necessarily physically or visually defined, rather it’s an abstraction, a conceptual entity used to define and 
group different entities into boxes or clusters, which serves as a tool to understand the functions, needs 
and utilization of such spaces.  
From the previous sections, two flow charts presented the processes that will occur in the spaceport. 
However, the presented is neither exhaustive or exclusive, and only serves as likely examples of expected 
processes and how user flowcharts might be. The spaces are extracted from the flow charts and derived 
into functional entity which must be carefully thought about when designing. The spaces also include the 
links between steps, which in this sense becomes a space.  
Just like in public transports, when switching from one vehicle to another, e.g. from bus to train. The 
process is to understand the space BETWEEN the vehicles to increase accessibility and comfort in the 
activity of switching. This translates into the taking the links between steps into account and treat those as 
spaces as well. D

5.2.2 Shape 
The shape of buildings is determined overall by the geometric properties. The geometry of designing such 
buildings have their limitations due to different factors.  
One factor is the design of buildings that will influence the wind dynamics of the site. Buildings have the 
capabilities to strengthen, weaken or redirect winds. This information is crucial for designers, as the 
spaceport site operate rockets, who are highly vulnerable to unstable and strong winds.  
Another factor affecting the shape is the design of the interior. The interior design will affect the façade 
and another exterior element. The shape must be well-though as geometrical properties have their 
influences on different aspects of the building.  
The shape of the building is also influenced by the orientation. Depending on needs, architectural solutions 
can manipulate the sun to provide heat, light and electricity. If the site is located near equator, daily 
temperatures can reach high degrees. The buildings used by humans, must be designed in a fashion that 
prevents them from overheating.  
Operating a spaceport demands a lot of energy due to its colossal size. Solar panels can be a solution to 
accommodate the energy demands. If such solution is decided to be implemented, then the orientation 
and shape of building is important to maximize and optimize the harvested energy. 
Due to the spaceport’s function of providing space travel with rockets, it is important to keep the buildings 
distant to the launch sites. In the spaceport complex, the control tower, monitoring operation and 
departures, has the need, as mentioned before, of being highly elevated and safely distant to the launch 
and landings areas. Therefor shaping most buildings in the site as flat as possible helps the people in the 
control tower, having the total 360 overview, undisturbed.

5.2.3 Allocation of spaces

evolves over time and adjustments will be made through the project progression. However, as Zachman 
stated in his paper of information systems architecture, it is important to make the bubble charts of any 
given design enterprise, to map the potentials. Afterwards, which brings us to this step, is the model of an 
information system from the designer’s perspective. As learned, it is the part where the model of “entity-
relationship-entity’ is used.  
The relationship between the spaces and buildings determines their allocation to one another. An example 
could be the main entrance and the main concourse building. In this case the buildings are entities, and 
their relationship is they have the common goal of providing for the passenger and visitors flow, through 
the buildings and site. These two buildings, each have a function, but depend on each in the holistic view of 
the spaceport. The relationship is between other sets or collectives of buildings and spaces. It does not limit 
to building-to-building or space-to-space, but also include space-to-building and vice versa. For example, 
the relationship between the maintenance hall and the landing sites. 
The allocation of spaces is not limited to site and building-level either but serves as a step to decide the 
exact allocation of each element according to function and relationship.

6 Discussion  
In the previous chapter, a framework was presented as a guiding tool for future engineers, architects and 
other designers. However, this is a product of a brief research done by limited resources. It is not obvious, 
whether the framework can be considered an exhaustive and complete tool or it simply working as a 
supplement to existing ways of working and designing. 
6.1 Testing 
The framework has not been tested, so its difficult to draw conclusions on its usability and reliability. The 
framework can become subject to several tests, to examine its proof of concept and reliability. Also, in the 
process of testing a lot of data can be accumulated to use for feedback. Any feedback data from users of 
this framework can help redesign, expand or even further reduce the size of it to optimize it for future use. 
A backwards procedure can also be done. Spaceport America, which is an existing spaceport located in New 
Mexico, US, can be examined for fitness to the framework. Despite the Spaceport America being a modern 
airport for sub-orbit planes, it still categorize itself as a spaceport. Investigating the process of design of 
spaceport America, will lead to some of the elements of the framework turning out to be useful and 
relevant. An example could be the choice of site, the understanding of processes, and the facilities needed 
to secure smooth operation at the spaceport. 
However no tests were conducted in this paper, as it served to solely provide the framework and the 
origins of its becoming  
6.2 Performance 
The performance of this framework can be measured through conducting several tests and attempts of 
designing spaceports with this as a tool. The way of measuring such performance can be tested through an 
experiment designed to assess the effect of help gained by using this framework. 
An experiment could be conducting by creating an architectural/engineering challenge for multiple design 
teams.  
Several teams of architects, engineers etc. provided with challenge of designing a spaceport. Each team is 
given different factors to consider while designing the spaceport. The different factors could be: 
• 
the type of rocket/vehicle the spaceport must facilitate 
• 
The number of yearly passengers 
• 
The number of staff members 
• 
Etc. 
Now, all teams are given different factors to consider, based on what clients desire for their spaceport. As 
any architectural competition certain limitation and goals are predefined. The timeframe for finishing the 
design is set to 2 months. However, the design teams will be split into two groups; a Control group and the 
Experimental group. 
6.2.1 The Control group 
Must work in traditional ways, figuring out how to solve the different challenges occurring 
when designing. They will have access to internet and can rely on the expertise provided by 
stakeholders and other leading figures in the industry. Consultants and other advisors are 
available.

6.3 Future 
The future is always unknown; however, some evidence suggest that the spaceports will be needed all over 
the world.45 In the future as the space industry develops into a trillion dollar industry, spaceports will be 
built in large numbers, and this will provide the necessary data to improve and optimize frameworks and 
perhaps actually develop full templates with minor adjustment.  
The spaceports can be stored in large databases for studies. The studies will find the similarities and 
generate the data, that this thesis framework was lacking. The template or framework generated from such 
data can, be developed further. An actual manual with exhaustive examples and details on how to do every 
single bit of designing a spaceport could actually become reality in the future when enough spaceports are 
made.  
However, until that time is reached, designers have the option of using this tool provided here and help 
develop this piece of framework to the next level. The idea of this thesis was to help people understand 
spaceports and what needs it has to become fully operational. In the future, several advancements are 
expected, and no solution is static as experience and knowledge will teach how to improve and adjust. 
Space-hotels, moon and Mars as possible destination might require different types of spaceports on earth 
which might evolve to different typologies which isn’t covered exhaustively in this framework.  
The stakeholders of today might not be the same stakeholder in the future, it is therefore unknown limits 
designers of today to work with presumptions.

7 Conclusion 
Through an investigation of the framework created and reviewed in this thesis it was seen that it in fact was 
able to support a design team in the process of designing a spaceport. It performed the desired and 
requested tasks and acts current state-of-the-art framework and stepwise execution of designing a 
spaceport. The framework was developed through understanding of processes in design and inspired by 
the knowledge acquired studying the Zachman framework.  
The framework is to the author’s knowledge the first of its kind and provides a brief 101 on how to get 
started with the design of spaceports and additionally providing information about useful background 
knowledge b. The build-up of the framework is designed to provide a logical order of necessary steps to 
design a spaceport. The most extensive component occurs in the beginning and as the user descends in the 
framework table more knowledge is gathered and used for the next row.  
The visual tables illustrate, how each of elements is contained with each other and hence the importance of 
doing it in the right order. In this manner, the framework serves as a manual, which was originally intended  
As a conclusive statement, one can wonder how this state-of-the-art framework can evolve when more 
data can be retrieved over time. Until then, this piece serves proof-of-concept of the importance of having 
a map guiding you through the phases of designing the spaceports of the future.
