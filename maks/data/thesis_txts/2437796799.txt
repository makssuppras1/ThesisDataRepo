Abstract 
The diffusion phenomena of methanol across organic coatings was studied by the 
diffusion cell method which is a simple and relatively fast way to estimate the diffusion 
coefficients of fast evaporation chemical molecules. 
A custom-made one-chamber diffusion cell was used as the main experimental set-up 
for the diffusion experiment. The diffusion phenomena of methanol across Yellow 
Novolac coating was first investigated using the diffusion cell. An unexpected diffusion 
phenomenon of methanol was obtained. The diffusion rate of methanol across the 
coating kept decreasing with time which is different from Fickian diffusion theory 
which claimed that the diffusion rate gradually increased with time and eventually 
remained stable.  
Different hypotheses were made and investigated to determine the main reason causing 
the continuous decreasing diffusion rate of methanol. Diffusion experiments with 
different coating systems were done and a similar continuous decreasing diffusion rate 
was found when methanol penetrate across all coating systems. Coating properties were 
believed to change during the methanol diffusion process, which might account for the 
continuous decreasing diffusion rate. 
The Differential scanning calorimetry was also employed to analysis the changing 
properties of the coating systems during diffusion process. The glass transition 
temperature of the coating films was found to increase with the immersion time.

Part I. Literature research 
This thesis project focused on the diffusion of methanol through organic coating 
systems. The purpose of the literature research is to give a basic understanding of the 
protective coating and the diffusion process including the mathematical models and 
experimental methods.  
Two types of protective coating, PU-based coating and epoxy resin coating were 
included in the research. Moreover, Fickian diffusion theory and non-Fickian diffusion 
theory were discussed for a better understood of the diffusion process and mathematical 
model. At last, a comparison between different experimental methods were included.

1 Corrosion 
Corrosion is a natural process which deteriorates the metal because of chemical 
reactions between substrate surface and surrounding environment. This reaction 
converts metal into either oxide, hydroxide or sulfide.  Serious consequence may 
happen to buildings, bridges and pipelines, leading to collapse or leakage due to the 
corrosion. 
Galvanic corrosion is one of the most common corrosion types, happened when two 
different metals have physical or electrical contact with each other or are immersed in 
a common electrolyte [1]. A well-known galvanic corrosion is rusting which forms iron 
oxides.  
A copper bolt and steel nut are used to test the corrosion between two contact metals in 
damp environment. Figure 1-1 and Figure 1-2 shows the experimental result, and 
corrosion is clearly observed on the steel nut and bolt [2]. Corrosion also occurs in 
materials other than metals, such as ceramics and polymers, which will degrade the 
useful properties of materials.

Various methods, including surface treatment and cathodic protection, were developed 
and tested to extend the lifetime of the substrate. Both methods will be introduced in 
the following chapters.  
 Cathodic protection 
Cathodic protection is used to control the metal corrosion by connecting the substrate 
to an easier corroded “sacrificial metal” [3]. After introducing the “sacrificial metal”, 
the current will flow from the newly introduced anode and the protected metal becomes 
cathodic which create a galvanic cell. The oxidation reactions will then transfer from 
the substrate surface to the anode to protect the metal structure.  
Cathodic protection method was first described by Sir Humphry Davy in 1824 [4]. After 
the invention, cathodic protection systems have applied to a wide range of metallic 
structures, such as fuel pipelines, steel storage tank and offshore concrete structures. 
Figure 1-3 displays the structure of the Sacrificial Anode Cathodic Protection: [5] 
 
Figure 1-3. Structure of the Sacrificial Anode Cathodic Protection [5] 
However, disadvantages have been reported for the cathodic protection. A side effect 
which produces atomic hydrogen will happen if the cathodic material is improperly 
applied on the substrate [6]. These hydrogen atoms are small enough to pass through 
the metal crystalline structure and leads to a hydrogen embrittlement [7].

 Surface treatment 
Surface treatment, including galvanization and applied coating, is also considered as an 
efficacious way to prevent metal corrosion. Metal substrates are commonly vulnerable 
to aggressive species such as acids. A chemical resistance coating for example, 
Polyurethane or Vinyl Ester based coatings is used to cover and protect the substrate. 
The basic mechanism for the coating applied on the surface is the barrier effect which 
prevents the direct contact between the aggressive environment and the substrate and 
also impedes the diffusion of aggressive species to the substrate surface [8].  
Metal compounds used in the protective coatings, such as the Zinc and Tin, provide 
excellent protective properties. The most common application of these metal barrier is 
the can. Besides, the use of the metal contained coating provides a galvanic protection 
which the protective metal can be regarded as an anode [52]. Figure 1-4 displays the 
protection mechanism for the Zn coating. 
 
Figure 1-4. Protection mechanism for Zn coating [53] 
Barrier property of protective coatings depends on different parameters, such as the 
polymer molecular weight, filler type and film thickness. Therefore, a thicker barrier 
will perform better in the corrosion prevention under the same environmental condition. 
Further investigation was needed for a protective coating with better barrier properties 
and thinner thickness.

2 Corrosion caused by organic solvents 
Organic solvents are widely employed in numerous industrial process, such as paints, 
varnishes, printing inks, and pesticides. Approximately 300 000 metric tons of 
chlorinated solvents were sold in Europe every year [54]. The largest end user of the 
organic solvent is the coatings industry where solvents play an important role in 
performance of the coating [54]. 
However, the use of the organic solvents has created new corrosion problems, involving 
the failures of metallic materials under the influence of aggressive organic solvents. 
This type of failures leads to not only a deterioration of the mechanical properties of 
material, but also unwanted change in the solvent used in industry [59]. Numerous 
literatures studied the corrosion process of metallic materials in organic solutions. Table 
2-1 summarized four different metal corrosions happened with different organic 
solvents [59].  
Table 2-1. Different corrosion type happened to different metals and solvents [59] 
 
Leakage of organic solutions caused by the metal corrosion can result in severe damage 
to both the environment and the human body.  
Organic components, including benzene, toluene, and xylenes are heavy polluters of 
environment [54]. Exposure to the organic solvents will result in a serious consequence 
to human health. The acute health effects of organic solvents will influence the central 
==PAGE:16==

nervous system, leading to headache, dizziness and seizures [55]. Throat, eyes, and 
nose irritation will happen when exposed to organic solvent [56]. Most of gasoline 
organic compounds were extreme toxic to mammals. Numbers of long term adverse 
effects of solvents were reported. For example, leukemia was found in benzene exposed 
workers [57], scleroderma, and cancer were also found when exposed to organic 
solvents.  
Due to the serious consequence on both environment pollution and human health, it is 
important to prevent the corrosion happened in industry. Among all protective methods 
mentioned in previous chapter, protective coatings method is one of the most widely 
used method in industry. It is important to understand the protective mechanisms of the 
applied coatings and also extend the protection time of the coating systems. 
This project will mainly focus on studying the diffusion process of organic solvents 
through different organic protective coating systems.

3 Protective coating 
Two major types of protective coatings including the polyurethane-based coatings and 
epoxy resin coatings will be introduced in this section. 
 Polyurethane 
Among different protective coatings, Polyurethane based coating is one of the best 
option for the long-term protection of a wide range of substrates due to the tough and 
durable resistance to extreme and corrosive environment [11].  
At the beginning of World War II, polyurethane was first developed as a replacement 
of rubber. By the end of the war, specific polyurethane-based coating, for example, 
airplane finish, was manufactured in industrial scale. In 1990s, two pack PU spray 
coating were developed which provides a better moisture resistance [11].  
Polyurethanes are made from the reaction between an isocyanate and a polyol. A linear 
polyurethane was produced by reaction with a diisocyanate and the simplest diol,

 Epoxy resin 
Epoxy resin is characterized by the presence of one or more three-membered ring, for 
example, the epoxy, epoxide, or ethoxyline group. In most cases, cured epoxy resins 
provide an excellent physical strength, corrosion resistance, and thermal ability [16]. 
With its outstanding formulating versatility and reasonable cost, epoxy resins were 
widely applied as metal can coating, automotive primer, and adhesives. 
The synthesis of epoxy compounds was first discovered in the late 1890 [15,16]. In the 
1940s, DeTrey Freres Co. in Switzerland [17] and the DeVoe and Raynolds Co. [18] in 
the United States recognized the commercial used of the epoxy resins simultaneously 
and independently. The development of waterborne coating based on the epoxy resins 
helped established the domain position of epoxy resins in the market. 
Commercial epoxy resins contain various functional group and are available in different 
molecular weight. The most widely used epoxies are the glycidyl ether derivatives of 
bisphenol A. 
3.2.1 Bisphenol A resins 
Liquid epoxy resin (LER) or the Bisphenol A resins is one of the most important 
intermediate in the epoxy industry, which is the reaction product of the bisphenol A and 
epichlorohydrin. Figure 3-3 gives an illustration of synthesis of LER. 
 
Figure 3-3. Illustration of synthesis of LER [16] 
Liquid epoxy resins are widely used in the coating industry due to the relative low 
viscosity. Typical unmodified LERs have a viscosity of 11000-16000 mPa under 25℃. 
Cured LERs provide tight cross-linked networks with excellent strength and hardness. 
Besides, LERs are used as the starting materials to produce other higher molecular 
weight solid epoxy resins or convert into other epoxy derivatives [16].

3.2.2 Bisphenol F resins 
Bisphenol F is the lowest molecular weight member of phenol novolacs.  
The Bisphenol F resin is prepared by the reaction between bisphenol F and

crystallization compare with Bisphenol A resin. However, coating based on Bisphenol

[8,10]. Thus, it is important to study the diffusion of these chemicals across organic 
coatings to prolong the coating lifetime. Different models were set up for the diffusion 
process and will be introduced in the following chapters.

5 Parameters influencing diffusion 
 
 Nature of the polymer 
For given penetrant solvents or gases, different polymer matrix performs various 
transport behaviors. It was found that the diffusion properties depend on the free 
volume of the polymer and the segmental mobility [32]. To be more specific, the 
segmental mobility of the polymer depends on the cross-link density, extend of 
unsaturation and degree of crystallinity. These properties enormously influence the 
glass transition temperature (𝑇𝑔) of the polymer matrix. Polymer with higher segmental 
mobility or low cross-link density will result in a low glass transition temperature and 
higher diffusivity.

 Penetrant  
Besides of the polymer matrix, the size and the shape of the penetrant molecule will 
also influence the diffusion coefficient and the transport behavior. Variety studies had 
reported that a larger size of the penetrant results in a lower diffusivity within the same 
polymer matrix [38]. 
Hopfenberg et al. [35] was first reported to have the systematic analysis of a set of linear 
alcohols (methanol to n-heptanol and four butanol isomers) diffuse through poly(ester 
urethane) coating. The study reported that linear and branched alcohols behave very 
different from each other. It was also found that the swelling of the polymer increased 
with the increasing of alcohols carbon atoms length and related to the penetrant 
solubility and permeability [35].  
The shape of the penetrant was also reported to have an enormous effect on the transport 
coefficient. For same carbon chain length, elongated molecule has higher diffusion 
coefficients than spherical molecule [32]. For butanol isomers, the swelling of polymer 
materials depends to a large extent on the number of branches, and it was found that 
the degree of swelling increased in the order tert-butanol, sec-butanol, iso-butanol and 
n-butanol [35].

6 Experimental methods 
 Gravimetric method (GM) 
Gravimetric method is the simplest and most widely used method to measure the 
transport behavior and diffusion coefficient. The basic mechanism used for this method 
is by measuring the weight gain during absorption or measuring the weight loss during 
diffusion and evaporation. Depend on the penetrant and polymer, different method and 
equipment are used [20].  
An intermittently measurement is used within different experimental time to determine 
how much liquid is absorbed or lost during the experiment. The immersion test will 
have a relative large error when testing a high diffusion coefficient liquid due to the fast 
evaporation or the residue liquid remain on the polymer surface [20].  
Koresmeyer R. W et al. [39] tested the diffusivity of water through N-vinyl-2-
pyrrolidon and 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate coating. The diffusion coefficient of the 
water was around 0.30 × 10−6 ± 0.05 𝑐𝑚2/𝑠 and it was greatly depending on the free 
volume of the polymer. 
Diffusion cup method, which is a kind of the gravimetric method. The diffusion 
behavior of different penetrants across the polymer coating can be determined in the 
cup test by intermittently measuring the weight loss of the diffusion cell. Another 
advantage for the cup experiment is that it can be used to determine the diffusion rate 
of volatile solvent.  
Ming Zhu et al. [61] used a one-chamber diffusion cell to investigate the diffusion 
mechanism of methanol. Figure 6-1 displays the structure of the diffusion cell used in 
the literature. The desorption test was performed by placing the diffusion cells in the 
objective environment and intermittently measuring the weight of the diffusion cell. 
Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) films with different thickness were measured. The 
diffusion rate of methanol across PMMA films was found to be critically dependent on 
the state of the polymer matrix including the density and orientation of molecular chains.

 Light microscopy (LM) 
Light microscopy was generally used for measuring the penetration distance of the 
penetrant. Hartley [20] first developed the LM method in 1949. He placed a polymer 
disc between two glass slides and then immersed into a dish of penetrant. After that the 
diffusion front is measured by the light microscopy.  
Different improvement has been made for the light microscopy method. For example, 
by using the microtome sections to cut the polymer after immersion in solvent for 
different time. However, the polymer matrix cutting section will be distorted after 
cutting, which leads to an error in finding the diffusion boundary. Besides, this method 
can be only used for penetrants with low volatility. 
Thomas N. L. and Windle A. H [42, 43] tested the diffusion coefficient of methanol 
through the Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) coating by using the light microscopy. 
The shape of PMMA changed during swelling which leads to a decrease of diffusion 
coefficient.

 Electron microscopy (EM) 
Compare with visible light microscopy, the electron microscopy provides a higher 
resolution. This is due to the help of X-ray analysis, a spatial resolution around 1µm 
can be detected [20].  
However, the major disadvantage of the electron microscopy is the difficulty of locating 
the diffusion boundary under the x-ray image. The chemical change and the vacuum 
environment will cause an evaporation of all volatile compounds which leads to an error 
in the diffusion front and transport behavior [20]. On the other hand, the beam damage 
of the x-ray analysis also needs to take into consider since it happened simultaneously 
with the solvent evaporation.  
Gilmore P. T et al. [44] measured the Poly(vinyl chloride) (PVE) melted liquid across 
the poly(ε-caprolactone) by the EM method and found that the PVE melted molecular 
weight strongly influence the diffusion coefficient.  
 
 Nuclear magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) 
Various approach has been made toward using nuclear magnetic resonance imaging 
(MRI) technology to study the diffusion process [20]. Compared with other 
technologies, MRI provides a better sensitivity to the certain chemical composition and 
spatial distribution. It is also non-destructive for the polymer which means this 
technology can be used for in situ experiment.  
Weisenberg L. A. and Koenig J. L [45] measured the diffusion coefficient of methanol 
and acetone across the PMMA coating with the help of MRI method. 
However, this method is limited by the space inner the magnet which is only around 5 
mm diameter. Another disadvantage is that the polymer needs to be cut before applying 
in the magnet which leaves a rough surface under the microscopy.

7 Analytical method 
Various analytical methods are used to obtain information regarding the size and 
changing properties of the coating sample. A coating thickness gauge is used to measure 
the thickness of the coating sample. Differential scanning calorimetry is used to 
determine the glass transition temperature of the coating sample before and after 
diffusion. The structure changing during diffusion is analyzed by an optical microscope. 
These analytical methods are introduced in this chapter. 
 Coating thickness gauge 
Elcometer 355 coating thickness gauge, which is shown in Figure 7-1, is used to 
measure the coating film thickness in this master thesis project. It is a non-destructive 
magnetic coating thickness gauge which is used for measuring non-magnetic coatings 
on ferrous substrates, such as steel. The basic mechanism of measuring the film 
thickness is that the magnetic field will be influenced when there is a spacing between 
the probe and the substrate surface. This changing magnetic field will result in a voltage 
difference and the film thickness can be then calculated.  
Before measuring the film thickness, calibration should be performed to ensure the 
accuracy of the measurement. At first, the gauge was adjusted to zero on a smooth 
substrate surface and then adjusted with a known thickness standard film at or near the 
measured coating samples. After calibration, the thickness of the coating film was 
measured by pressing the probe on the film and collected the data. To accurately 
measure the thickness of the paint, at least 15 different points at different locations need 
to be measured and collected.

 Differential scanning calorimetry measurement 
Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) is a thermo analytical technique in which the 
difference in the amount of heat required to increase the temperature of a sample and 
reference is measured as a function of temperature. In this master thesis project, the 
glass transition temperature (𝑇𝑔) of the coating samples with different immersion time 
is measured. 
The glass transition temperature is known as when below this temperature, the polymer 
matrix segments do not have enough energy to rearrange or to rotate themselves [58]. 
In DSC test, the coating samples are continuous heated and there will be a small 
increase in both volume and energy until reaching the glass transition temperature. 
Chains will become more mobile and the polymer will be more plastic or rubbery when 
the temperature is higher than 𝑇𝑔. Besides, the heat capacity of the sample also changes 
after reaching the glass transition temperature [58]. Therefore, a changing heat flow 
rate will be found when heating up the sample as shown in Figure 7-2 [58]. The glass 
transition temperature occurs over a temperature range, 𝑇𝑔 is typically defined as the 
midpoint of the changing heat flow rate.

8 Part I conclusions 
Organic solvents are widely employed in numerous industrial process. However, 
improper protection to the equipment may lead to a leakage of organic compounds and 
cause serious health consequence.  
Protective coating, as a surface treatment procedure, was one of the major protective 
methods to prevent corrosion in industry.  Polyurethane based coating and epoxy resin-
based coating are widely applied to protect the metal substrate. Based on the previously 
studies, the diffusion can be divided into two different theories, Fickian diffusion and 
non-Fickian diffusion. Different mathematical models were built under different 
polymer or penetrant situation. To estimate the diffusion coefficient, numerous methods 
have been developed and tested. Different methods are introduced in the literature 
research.   
In this project, the diffusion cell method is selected as the major experimental method 
due to its feasibility to estimate the volatile solvents and simpler operation compared 
with other experimental methods. The diffusion phenomena of organic solvents across 
different coating systems will be studied.

This increasing diffusion rate of methanol at the beginning is consistent with the 
phenomenon described by the typical Fickian theory which was introduced in chapter 
4.1 where the diffusion rate of methanol gradually increased at the beginning and finally 
stabilized and diffused with a fixed diffusion rate. 
However, as shown in Figure 12-3, the diffusion rate of methanol starts to decrease 
with time after reaching the peak point which was contrasted with Fickian theory. To 
figure out the main reason of the continuous decreasing diffusion rate, three different 
assumptions were made as followings: 
• 
The increasing pressure difference between the atmosphere and inside the 
diffusion cell might be a possible reason. Increased pressure difference inside 
and outside the coating causes air to diffuse from the outside to the inside and 
might affect the diffusion of methanol. 
• 
The component used in the coating system might influence the diffusion 
behavior and leads to a decreasing diffusion coefficient. For example, pigments 
used in coating systems will affect the diffusion of methanol which was 
discussed in Chapter 5.1. However, most literature claimed that when pigments 
were added to the coating systems, an increasing diffusion rate was found. 
Whether the pigment used in Yellow Novolac coating film has a negative effect 
on the diffusion rate needs to be examined. Other properties, such as the cross-
link density of the coating film and the filler used in the coating systems are also 
considered as a possible reason causing the continuous decreasing diffusion rate 
of methanol. 
• 
The properties or the structure of the coating systems might change during the 
diffusion process. The swelling of the coating film which may lead to a lower 
glass transition temperature and changing cross-link density. A possible 
changing in the pathway and rearrangement in the structure might occur. 
Methanol might form physical bonds to the binder during diffusion leading to a 
permeability difference. 
All these three assumptions will be examined in the following chapters.

13 Further investigation on swelling factor 
Based on the obtained data, a continuous decreasing diffusion rate of methanol was 
found in both diffusion experiment with intermittently open the top screw and diffusion 
experiment with different coating system, which indicates that the pressure factor and 
the binder or filler used in the coating system was not the main reason causing the 
continuous decreasing diffusion rate. Besides, the structure or the physical property of 
the coating system was changed during the diffusion process. 
An additional hypothesis was made to investigate the decreasing diffusion rate based 
on the obtained experimental data and literature: 
• Hydrogen bonding might be formed between methanol and polymer chain 
during the diffusion process. 
Zhou et al. [62] claimed that a hydrogen bonding was formed between polymer chains 
during the diffusion process of water across epoxy systems. These hydrogen bonding 
result in a secondary crosslink as Figure 13-1 displays. 
 
Figure 13-1. Possible bound water complexes in epoxy network [62] 
Because of the secondary crosslink, a continuous decreasing weight reduction might 
happen. These secondary crosslinks will also lead to blockage in the diffusion channel 
which result in a lower diffusion rate. 
A weight desorption test and Nuclear Magnetic Resonance spectroscopy (NMR) was 
used to determine the hydrogen bonding formed between the water and epoxy system 
and the experimental result is shown in Figure 13-2 and Figure 13-3.  
Two types of desorption are shown in the Figure 13-2. Retained water molecules which 
are easily removed under lower temperatures are consistent with a so-called Type I 
bound water. Moreover, retained water molecules which are difficult to remove by 
thermal desorption are referred to as a Type II bound water. This type II bound water 
can only be removed under high temperature [62]. 
==PAGE:74==
64 
 
An NMR spectroscopy was used for 1H NMR test. Figure 13-3(a) exhibits the free water 
and a small amount of bound water. After just 10 min, free water molecules abate 
revealing that absorbed water molecules have become impeded as shown in Figure 
13-3(b). This result also reveals that a hydrogen bonding was formed between water 
and epoxy resins [62]. 
It was known that aromatic rings, carbon-carbon triple bonds, double bonds, and 
hydroxyl groups can act as electron donors in some cases, forming hydrogen bonds 
with strongly polar chemical branches. Both water and methanol were strongly polar 
chemical molecules, which means methanol has great possibility to form hydrogen 
bonding with polymer matrix during the diffusion process. 
As mentioned in Chapter 5, the nature of the polymer will influence the diffusivity. The 
formation of the hydrogen bonding between methanol and polymer system leads to an 
increasing amount of side chain and also a higher crosslink density which will decrease 
the diffusion coefficient of methanol across the coating systems.

14 Diffusion simulation 
A moving boundary theory was assumed and used to determine the diffusion process 
of methanol across the coating film. It was assumed that the sharp front concentration 
must reach the saturated concentration of methanol to allow the diffusion process.  
Figure 14-1 displays the structure of the moving boundary. In which, the dash line 
represents the sharp diffusion front of methanol.  
 
Figure 14-1. Sketch figure of the moving boundary 
To simplify the diffusion process, variety of assumptions were made. 
• The diffusion coefficient was assumed to be constant during the diffusion 
process. 
• The evaporation rate of methanol was assumed to be much higher than the 
diffusion rate of methanol transport to the coating out surface. 
• Methanol concentration inside the diffusion cell was assumed to be constant 
during all time. 
• Methanol concentration at the original polymer was assumed to be zero. 
• Methanol concentration at the moving boundary (x=δ) was the saturated 
concentration. 
• The thickness of coating film during the diffusion process was assumed to be 
constant.  
• The diffusion of methanol across coating film was only considered as one 
direction diffusion from inside to outside.  
• The swelling of the coating film only increased the coating thickness. The radius 
of the coating film was assumed to be constant.  
• The contact area between the coating film and organic solvent was assumed to 
be constant. 
==PAGE:80==

• There is no back flow of air or other liquids. 
A constant diffusion coefficient was assumed before methanol penetrate the coating 
film. The diffusion process was described by Fick’s second law:

To determine the diffusion coefficient, boundary conditions were needed. Four

15 Conclusion 
The diffusion phenomenon of methanol across different coating systems including YN, 
BN, PU, VE, and PMMA film was investigated by using the diffusion cell method. 
The diffusion rate of methanol across different coating systems was found to increase 
at the beginning. After reaching a peak point, an unexpected decreasing diffusion rate 
was found which is different from the typical Fickian diffusion process where the 
diffusion rate gradually increases and eventually remains stable. 
Different hypotheses were made and investigated to determine the main reason causing 
the continuous decreasing diffusion rate of methanol. The results of the gravimetric 
method indicated that the different component used in the coating systems or the 
pressure difference between inside and outside the diffusion cell was not the main 
reason causing the decreasing diffusion rate. 
The last hypothesis made was that there was a changing in the structure or properties 
of the coating systems during the diffusion process. Diffusion experiments with swollen 
coating samples which dried for more than two weeks under room temperature were 
measured. It was found swollen coating samples had a longer penetration time and 
lower diffusion rate compared with original coating systems which means a better 
resistance to methanol. The glass transition temperature of the coating samples with 
different immersion time was also investigated and the glass transition temperature of 
the coating systems was found to increase with the immersion time. Based on the 
obtained data, a changing in the structure and properties of the coating system took 
place during the diffusion process and this changing was irreversible under room 
temperature. 
However, the gravimetric method was not able to figure out the actual reason causing 
the decreasing diffusion rate of methanol. Later literature study claimed that a possible 
physical bonding might be formed between methanol and polymer matrix which leads 
to a decreasing diffusion rate. A desorption test was then investigated. A certain amount 
of methanol was found to remain in the coating systems under the room temperature. 
The remaining methanol quickly evaporated when drying under 120 ℃ which also 
agrees with the literature that a possible physical bonding was formed between 
methanol and polymer matrix. 
==PAGE:84==
74 
 
A moving boundary theory was used to calculate the diffusion coefficient of methanol 
across different coating systems. Among all coating systems, Blue Novolac coating 
film preformed best in resistance to methanol which had the lowest diffusion coefficient. 
YN coating film and PMMA film had very similar methanol diffusion coefficient. 
Besides, swollen coating samples had a lower diffusion coefficient compared with 
original coating samples.

16 Future Experiment 
In this project, a continuous decreasing diffusion rate of methanol was found in all the 
organic coating systems by the diffusion cell method. However, some verification 
experiments for different hypothesis were still missing.  
An NMR spectroscopy method which determine the type of 1H in the immersed coating 
systems still needs to be done after the solid phase NMR spectroscopy is available to 
examine the hydrogen bonding hypothesis. 
Besides, PMMA films that do not contain methanol need to be dried at 120 ℃ to verify 
whether the components in the PMMA film will decompose and evaporate at high 
temperatures. If there is no obvious decreased in the weight of PMMA films after 
measurement, it can be confirmed that methanol was trapped in the coating systems 
which might formed a physical bonding with the polymer matrix. 
Other different possible reasons causing the continuous decreasing diffusion rate needs 
to be examine. For example, there might be a changing in the surface tension of the 
coating system during the diffusion process.  
Since the simple mathematical model built in this project assumed a constant diffusion 
coefficient during the diffusion process. An appropriate mathematical model with less 
assumption should be developed to estimate the changing diffusion coefficients of 
methanol. 
Besides of the theoretical reason causing the continuous decreasing diffusion rate of 
methanol. The physical properties of the coating system, including hardness, resistance 
to scratches and gloss should also be compared between the swollen and original 
coating systems. 
Future discussion on how the better corrosion resistance of the swollen coating films 
can be used into practical application can also be studied. It was found that the swollen 
coating systems have better resistance to methanol diffusion. A pre-penetration of 
methanol in the coating film to form a swollen coating system might be a post-cure 
method to improve coating preformation if there is no obvious decline in the physical 
properties of the coating.
