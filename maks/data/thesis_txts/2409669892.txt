Abstract
Selenium is a suitable wide-bandgap top cell material (≈1.8 eV) for tandem device applica-
tions. Achieving a large voltage with a meaningful current is a requirement for the tandem
photoelectrolysis device where a large potential is needed to run both the oxygen and hy-
drogen evolution reactions (OER and HER). The performance of selenium is more easily
investigated when included as the main absorbing layer of a selenium-based single junction
solar cell. During this project, the focus has therefore been to optimise the performance and
fabrication process of the selenium-based solar cell with particular interest in improving the
open circuit voltage, Voc, along with reproducibility. Optimising the annealing conditions
and adding nickel oxide (NiO) as a hole conducting layer was found to greatly improve the
cell performance.
The solar cell thin ﬁlm layers were fabricated with physical vapour and electrochemical
depositions and were visualised using scanning electron microscopy (SEM).
Current-voltage (I-V) measurements were performed to investigate the photovoltaic and
conductive capabilities. X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) was used to verify the hexagonal crystal
structure of annealed selenium.
The optical transparency was measured by ultraviolet-
visible spectroscopy (UV-Vis) that indicates a large absorbance of crystalline selenium below
the bandgap of Eg = 1.93 eV, which was extrapolated from the corresponding Tauc plot.
Lastly, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used with depth proﬁling to investigate
the back contact interface layers and the eﬀect of adding NiO.
Finally, the thesis provides a broad outlook for some of the future research required to im-
prove the selenium-based solar cell in accordance with the obtained results and observations
from the work presented. Furthermore, the outlook presents some necessary investigations
needed in order to achieve photocatalytic OER and HER.

Introduction
As an introduction to this thesis, a motivation consisting of the current global energy land-
scape will be presented to understand the depending need of sustainable energy solutions
such as photovoltaics. The current photovoltaic technologies will be explained including
how selenium can play an important role in solar cell applications. In this context the work
performed during this project is to be introduced as well as the future perspective.
1.1
The global energy crisis
The world is currently facing an energy crisis due to an escalation in global energy demand,
caused by a continuously growing population that currently consists of 7.6 billion people.
There is a continued dependence on fossil fuels which is not only depleting limited natural
resources, but also increasing carbon dioxide emissions, which experts believe is responsi-
ble for an increasing average global temperature. There is widespread agreement among
scientiﬁc communities and governments that the climate change is accelerating as a result
of human emissions and that considerable countermeasures on a global scale is required to
reduce harmful impacts.[1] Human activities such as burning fossil fuels and deforestation,
emit the greenhouse gas carbon dioxide (CO2), among others, which traps more heat within
the atmosphere of the Earth, that in turn result in larger global temperatures. The con-
centration of CO2 in the atmosphere for the past four hundred thousand years is shown in
ﬁgure 1.1.

1.2
Current photovoltaic technologies
Solar cells are a renewable energy technology that can generate electricity from sunlight
through the photovoltaic eﬀect. The photovoltaic eﬀect was ﬁrst experimentally observed
by the French physicist Alexandre Edmond Becquerel in 1839.[19] This discovery laid the
foundation for several related discoveries throughout the century and in 1883 the American
inventor Charles Fritts made the ﬁrst solar cell from selenium wafers and thin layers of
gold.[20] Since the creation of this ﬁrst solar cell, diﬀerent photovoltaic technologies have
entered the energy market (see ﬁgure 1.8). Silicon based solar cells were the ﬁrst generation
of solar cells and today the technology still dominates the market. However, silicon solar
cells have 3 major limitations.[21] Silicon is ﬁrst of all a material which in itself is the second
most abundant material on earth. It is however rarely found in nature in its pure elemental
form. Transforming silicon dioxide, which is essentially beach sand, into silicon takes large
amounts of energy to melt the oxygen away.
Electrode arc furnaces are used reaching
temperatures of 1500-2000 C◦.[22] This not only limits the lower cost limit of silicon but
also causes a certain amount of greenhouse gas emission during manufacturing. Secondly,
silicon solar cells have the limitation that they are heavy and rigid. Large-scale installations
are a requirement which again limits the cost but also the ﬂexibility for small installations.
Silicon production costs have declined signiﬁcantly since 1976 and have for years followed
a tendency which has now been named Swanson’s law, named after Richard Swanson, the
founder of SunPower. The law states that for every doubling of production of silicon solar
panels, there is a 20 percent reduction in cost.[23] However, considering the average selling
price on the spot market for the solar cell modules, installation and maintenance remains
almost unchanged.[24][25] Figure 1.7 shows the change in silicon solar panel related costs
from 2010 to 2015.
Figure 1.7: Average selling price (ASP) on the spot market for hyperpure silicon, multicrystalline
silicon wafers, cells and modules from 2010 to 2015. Figure taken from [24].

silicon technologies.

perovskite cells seem to have future potential. Perovskite cells have only recently entered
as a photovoltaics technology, but have already shown an impressive increase in power
conversion eﬃciency with a maximum eﬃciency in 2017 of 22.7%.
This has caused an
explosion in research popularity with more than 2000 publications on the subject in just
a few years and the eﬃciency is already high enough for commercialisation, which is most
likely to occur within a few years.
The perovskite cell consisting of methyl ammonium
lead iodide ((CH3NH3)PbI3 or MAPbI3) is currently the most studied. However, the key
challenge for this technology is its stability and long term performance. Water and higher
temperatures can cause the perovskite absorption layer to dissolve, so solving how to protect
the cells and modules is a necessity. Furthermore, the possibility of lead contamination from
the cells has to be dealt with.[31][32]
Looking at the multijunction cells (also called tandem cells) in ﬁgure 1.8, the eﬃciencies
are far greater than for single junction cells. The reason for this will be explained in section
2.3. Simply put, a tandem cell can split the solar spectrum into parts. It contains multiple
absorber materials, which individually are more eﬃcient at absorbing energy equal to their
bandgaps.
By having absorber materials with diﬀerent bandgaps, a higher eﬃciency is
obtainable. Single junction solar cells are theoretically limited to 33.7% eﬃciency whereas
double junction cells have a potential limit of 46.1%.[33] This limit is determined by the
Schockley-Queisser limit which will be explained in section 2.2.5. Until recently, tandem cells
have been limited to low-eﬃciency amorphous and expensive III-V semiconductors acting as
wide-band absorbers.[34] Perovskites have arisen as a suitable wide-bandgap material, but
has as explained still a few key problems to solve. This is where selenium has reappeared
as a potential suitable wide-bandgap material.

1.3
The selenium-based solar cell
The recent increased interest towards wide-bandgap absorbers for tandem applications
has drawn attention to the element selenium.
It is only recently that the 32 year old
eﬃciency record of 5.0% [35] was beaten by [36] reaching 6.5%.
Hexagonal selenium
has a bandgap which is reported to be between 1.77 and 2 eV depending on fabrication
conditions.[37][38][39] Such a bandgap is well suited for a wide-bandgap top cell along with
silicon which has a bandgap of 1.1 eV. Apart from having a suitable bandgap, selenium has
numerous other advantages. Selenium has a high optical transparency in wavelengths below
its bandgap and a low toxicity. Furthermore, selenium is air-stable and has a low melting
temperature of 220 ◦C, and it is a single element, like silicon, which makes it simple to
fabricate.[36][40]
The work of this project has been focused on the fabrication of a single junction selenium-
based solar cell to optimise the fabrication of the selenium by itself with respect to morphol-
ogy, coverage and bandgap. Various characterisation methods were used to optimise this
fabrication process. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was used to investigate the cell
coverage, morphology and layer thickness. In conjunction with using SEM, energy dispersive
X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) was performed to verify visualised particles as being selenium.
X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) was used to verify the crystallinity of the selenium. Ultraviolet-
visible spectroscopy (UV-Vis) was used to investigate the optical transparency of selenium
for the wavelengths of interest along with its bandgap. Current-voltage (I-V) measurements
were performed in order to measure the current voltage dependency of the fabricated solar
cells, which is a highly characteristic measurement quantifying a range of properties which
are useful to determine the quality of a solar cell. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS)
was used to investigate the possibility for silver and gold diﬀusion into selenium along with
possible contamination elements.
1.4
Future perspective: The two photon water splitting de-
vice
It should be mentioned that among the possible tandem applications, the two photon water
splitting device is a concept which could be highly beneﬁcial for future renewable energy.
Simply explained, it is a tandem solar cell which instead of generating an electrical current,
uses the absorbed energy from sunlight to split water into hydrogen and oxygen.
The
principle behind this is brieﬂy explained in section 2.3. In essence, it creates the possibility
for a sustainable production of hydrogen which is a potential renewable fuel. Currently
the key problem with creating such a device is to ﬁnd a suitable wide-bandgap material.
This material should be able to provide a large voltage great enough for oxygen evolution
to take place. Nevertheless a range of other issues have to be addressed once a suitable
material has been found. Suitable catalysts for oxygen and hydrogen evolution are needed
along with suitable protective layers in order to prevent corrosion. Finally, the device has
to be economically competitive with regular electrolysis. It should be noted that this work
is far beyond the scope of this project. However, selenium has the potential to be the wide-
bandgap top cell material suitable for the two photon water splitting device and therefore
the selenium-based solar cell was optimised with the requirement of a high voltage in mind.

Theory required for understanding the working principles of the selenium-based solar cell is

2.1
Semiconductor physics
To understand the working principles of a solar cell, a basic understanding of semiconductor
physics is required.
All solid materials can be categorised as insulators, conductors or
semiconductors. Each have unique electric and thermal properties and these depend on the
energy gap between the valence band and the conduction band of the material. This energy
gap is appropriately called the bandgap, Eg. The bound electrons in the valence band can
be excited, with suﬃcient energy, to the conduction band where they become free electrons,
as shown in ﬁgure 2.1. When excited, the electrons leave behind a hole in the valence band.
Neighbouring electrons in the valence band can ﬁll this hole, which essentially moves the
hole as if it had a positive charge.[41]
Figure 2.1: Schematic of electron-hole pair generation. Figure taken from [41].
11
==PAGE:28==
Chapter 2. Theory
Semiconductors can be conductive via these electron-hole pairs, but for insulators the
bandgap is generally too large for the electrons to be excited. For conducting materials
the bandgap is zero.
The electronic properties of a semiconductor can be altered. In intrinsic semiconductors the
concentration of electrons in the conduction band, ni, equals the concentration of holes in
the valence band, pi, and the intrinsic Fermi energy level, EFi, will thereby be situated near
the midgap energy (depending on the potential diﬀerence between the eﬀective masses of the
electron and hole). The concentration of holes and electrons can be changed by doping the
semiconductor with controlled quantities of impurity atoms. This makes the semiconductor
an extrinsic semiconductor and by doing so the Fermi energy will shift, thereby changing the
electronic properties of the material. Most commonly, elements from group 3 and group 5
from the periodic table are used as dopants. These elements have one valence electron more
or less than the materials from group 4 respectively, which are typical semiconductor mate-
rials. Alternatively, semiconductors can be doped due to native defects in the material.[42]
Semiconductors that lack valence electrons are called p-type. These materials have acceptor
sites that have energy levels near the valence band inside the bandgap. The acceptor sites
can trap free electrons from the valence band that thereby create holes which can act as free
carriers. Similarly, an n-type semiconductor has donor sites which create an excess amount
of valence electrons with an energy level near the conduction band inside the bandgap. The
names n-type and p-type semiconductors originate from the majority of either positively
charged holes or negatively charged electrons, respectively. The Fermi-energy, EF , becomes
larger EF > EFi for n-type materials and smaller EF < EFi for p-type than the intrinsic
Fermi-energy.[43]
2.2
Solar cell working principles
The basic principle behind a solar cell device is to utilise the photovoltaic eﬀect to create
electricity. The photovoltaic eﬀect is the generation of electron-hole pairs in an absorbing
material upon exposure to light. With an electric ﬁeld the light generated charge carriers
can create a current in an external circuit. This electric ﬁeld is obtained by creating a p-n
junction.
2.2.1
The p-n junction
A p-n junction is formed when a p-type and an n-type semiconductor are brought in contact
with each other. A band diagram of a p-n junction is shown in ﬁgure 2.2.
The resulting outcome of making a p-n junction is that the majority carrier electrons from
the n-region near the junction diﬀuse into the p-region leaving behind positively charged
donor atoms in the n-region. Similarly, majority carrier holes from the p-region diﬀuse into
the n-region leaving behind negatively charged acceptor atoms in the p-region.[42] When
equilibrium occurs a region with no mobile charges remains. This area is called the depletion
region (or space charge region). Within this region an inbuilt electric ﬁeld is present which
can separate electron-hole pairs by drift transport. The electrons will drift in the opposite
net direction of the electric ﬁeld and vice versa for the holes. The carrier movement is
described by the mobility, µ

a result, an electrical current can be generated by exposure to light and this phenomenon
is called the photovoltaic eﬀect.

2.2.5
The Shockley-Queisser limit
The theoretical maximum eﬃciency of a solar cell with a single p-n junction is deﬁned as
the Shockley-Queisser limit. This limit is inﬂuenced by three primary contributions.[46]
1. Blackbody radiation. A solar cell working at room temperature, T ≈300 K, will emit
blackbody radiation. This energy is obtainable by the solar cell and represents about
7% of the available incoming energy. Under realistic performing conditions the solar
cell will increase in temperature under sunlight and can reach as high as T = 360 K,
which increases the blackbody radiation further.
19
==PAGE:36==
Chapter 2. Theory
2. Radiative recombination. Some generated electron-hole pairs can and will recombine
through either radiative recombination or non-radiative recombination. Some radia-
tive recombination can lead to new generated electron-hole pairs if reabsorbed, but in
any other case the energy of the incoming photons is not captured by the solar cell.
3. Spectrum losses. High energy photons will generate electron-hole pairs at the front
side of the solar cell. Similarly low energy photons will generate electron-hole pairs
at the back side. Electron-hole pairs generated away from the p-n junction are not
separated and will recombine leading to more recombination losses. Furthermore, low
energy photons below the energy of the bandgap are unabsorbed.
Reﬂection also
decreases the potential eﬃciency of the solar cell. One can measure the eﬃciency of
the solar cell for every photon wavelength to characterise the spectrum losses. This is
also known as the quantum eﬃciency (QE) of the solar cell.
The largest Shockley-Queisser limit for a single junction solar cell is 33.7% for a bandgap of
≈1.2 eV. The bandgap of silicon is close to an optimum with 1.1 eV and should theoretically
be able to reach a maximum eﬃciency of ≈30%.[46] The highest recorded eﬃciency for a
single junction silicon solar cell is 26.6% as seen in ﬁgure 1.8.
The Shockley-Queisser limit for a single junction selenium-based solar cell with a band gap
of 1.8 eV is ≈25%.[47]

2.6.2
Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS)
Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS or EDX) is a technique which can be used to
analyse the elemental composition of the bulk of a material. The technique is based on
the detection of characteristic emitted X-rays of a surface. X-rays can easily be generated
by bombarding a surface with electrons. The technique is thus most commonly used in
conjunction with electron microscopy. When the electron beam bombards the material,
many secondary electrons are generated, which will cause the remaining vacant holes in the
inner electron shells of the atoms to become unstable. Electrons in the outer shells will ﬁll
the vacant spaces and emit their excess kinetic energy as X-rays. The emitted X-rays are
characteristic for the material and allows for the investigation of elemental composition of
the sample.
EDS is an eﬃcient qualitative technique where a total spectrum of detected X-ray energies
from 0.1 keV to e.g. 15 keV can be obtained in a short amount of time (≈10-100 s). The
energy of the electron beam should as a rule of thumb be twice that of the highest required
excitation energy peak. Therefore a beam energy of E0 ≥10 keV is usually a requirement.
For a quantitative EDS analysis a beam energy of 15-30 keV is recommended.[62]
For the EDS analysis performed during this project an electron beam energy of 15 keV was
used for qualitative measurements.

2.6. Characterisation methods
The optical bandgap of the material can be determined from a Tauc plot. A Tauc plot shows
the quantity (αhν)1/r as a function of the energy of the light, hν, where α is the absorption
coeﬃcient of the material and the exponent, r, is related to the nature of the band transition:
r = 1/2 for direct allowed transitions; r = 3/2 for direct forbidden transitions; r = 2 for
indirect allowed transitions and r = 3 for indirect forbidden transitions. The Tauc plot will
have a distinct linear regime which denotes the onset of absorption. Extrapolating a linear
equation from this region yields the energy of the bandgap of the material.[65][66] Selenium
has a bandgap with a direct allowed transition [38], so for the determination of the bandgap
of Se, r = 1/2 was used.
In this project the UV-2600 UV-Vis Spectrophotometer by Shimadzu was used.

Chapter 3
The thin ﬁlm selenium-based solar
cell
In this chapter the structure and fabrication process of the selenium-based solar cell will
be presented. In between the sections describing the structure and fabrication of the cell, a
section has been included to brieﬂy explain the change in physical properties of selenium and
tellurium with temperature. This is valuable information when fabricating the selenium-
based solar cell and it is particularly useful for physical vapour deposition of selenium as
well as for the investigation of ideal annealing conditions.
3.1
The structure and layer functions
The single p-n junction solar cells fabricated and characterised during this project consist of
(from the bottom and up), bulk glass with a transparent thin ﬁlm front contact of ﬂuorine
doped tin oxide (F doped SnO2 or FTO), a titanium dioxide (TiO2) electron collector
thin ﬁlm, a tellurium (Te) layer, a selenium (Se) absorber layer, a nickel oxide (NiO) hole
collector thin ﬁlm and a silver or gold (Ag or Au) back contact grid. A sketch of the device,
a cross-sectional SEM image of a fabricated cell and its corresponding energy diagram can
be seen in ﬁgures 3.1, 3.2 and 3.3. The function of each layer along with the used fabrication
methods will be explained.
To brieﬂy explain the working principle of the sketched energy diagram, the Se solar cell
should ideally work as a p-i-n cell. If the Se is close to intrinsically doped (making it the
i-layer), the TiO2 and the NiO layers will act as the n- and p- doped regions respectively.
As the layers are in contact, the Fermi levels must align. There are ideally no charges in
the i-layer, which should cause a linear band bending through the Se layer, meaning that
all generated electron-hole pairs should be separable.

3.1.1
Fluorine doped tin oxide (FTO) - front contact
The FTO layer acts as a transparent front contact. The absorbed and captured electrons
move from the TiO2 into the conducting FTO. The FTO layer therefore has to conduct
well while maintaining a good transmission. The glass substrates are bought with already
applied ≈500 nm FTO.
3.1.2
Titanium dioxide (TiO2) - electron transport layer
The TiO2 has a conduction band which is slightly below the conduction band of Se, that
allows for electron transfer after absorption. Furthermore, because the valence band is so far
below the valence band of Se, the holes should due to electrostatic repulsion be prohibited
from ﬂowing into the TiO2. Therefore, TiO2 acts as an eﬃcient electron transport material
in the Se based solar cell. TiO2 also has the added beneﬁt that it according to studies
can operate as a robust and adequate cathodic protective layer against acidic and alkaline
electrolytes, which can be beneﬁcial if used for photocatalysis.
It is also believed TiO2
should be able to reduce surface recombination due to the its low valence band causing hole
repulsion.[69][70][71]
A TiO2 layer thickness of 50 nm was chosen on the basis of the success of the previous record
holding cell made by [35] with 5.0% eﬃciency. 50 nm should be suﬃcient to avoid electron
tunnelling while maintaining a homogeneous layer covering the FTO. It is to be expected
that making the layer thicker should only increase the series resistance which would limit
the potential Isc and Voc of the solar cell.

3.1.3
Tellurium (Te) - adhesion/wetting layer
According to [40] and [36], applying a thin ﬁlm of Te (≈0.5 −2 nm) in between the TiO2
and the Se, can greatly improve the adhesion of Se. Furthermore it is hypothesised that Te
acts as a wetting layer improving the homogenousness of the Se layer. This should prevent
the formation of pinholes which should lead to an improved shunt resistance. Therefore,
the Te layer is implemented only for structural fabrication purposes. However, as Te has
a bandgap of around 0.36 eV[72] it must not be too thick. If so, it would cause the cell
to become a low bandgap solar cell and signiﬁcantly lower the open circuit voltage. The
Te is to diﬀuse into the Se after annealing. It should ideally not inﬂuence the band energy
diagram, which is why it is not included in ﬁgure 3.3.
Ideally the addition of Te was to be fabricated by thermal evaporation in vacuum, but was
not possible, mainly due to contamination issues. The previous and current Se cell world
record was made with Te by thermal evaporation.[35][36] Electrochemical deposition was
instead used as a simple and also proven fabrication method, which in addition allowed for
the fabrication of many Se based solar cells.[40]
It is still a mystery why Te acts as a wetting agent as no reported research has been able
to explain this.
3.1.4
Selenium (Se) - absorber layer
Trigonal Se has a bandgap of ≈1.8 eV.[37][38][39] According to the theory seen in section
2.3, a tandem device consisting of a wide-bandgap material, such as Se, in conjunction with
Si is expected to be highly eﬃcient. In order to optimise the fabrication parameters of Se on
its own, single absorber Se thin ﬁlm solar cells were made. Coincidentally these solar cells
are by themselves promising cost-eﬀective and low toxic devices compared to the commercial
thin ﬁlm solar cells, CIGS and CdTe.
Se has a low melting temperature of 220 ◦C and is therefore easily fabricated. An optimal
thickness is diﬃcult to determine as [36] reached an eﬃciency of 6.5% for a 100 nm thick Se
layer, which beat the 32 year old record of 5.0% obtained by [35] with a 2 µm thick Se layer.
Nevertheless, a range of thicknesses between the two was produced during the project.
3.1.5
Nickel oxide (NiO) - hole transport layer
NiO has a similar function as TiO2, but acts instead as a hole collector. As the conduction
band of NiO is far above the conduction band of Se, it blocks the ﬂow of electrons. Fur-
thermore, its valence band is slightly lower than the conduction band of Au, allowing the
further transport of holes into this back contact.
NiO has the added beneﬁt that it works well as an anodic protection layer and a mediocre
catalyst for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER). However, by adding trace amounts of Fe
incorporated into the NiO structure, the catalytic performance can be drastically improved.[73]
It is hoped that optimising the Se based solar cell with NiO can lead to an improved future
fabrication process of the tandem water splitting device.

3.1.6
Silver/gold (Ag/Au) - back contact
Ag was initially used as the back contact for convenience, as other thermal evaporation
users were using Ag in their fabrication process. However, from XPS measurements while
sputtering it was realised that Ag diﬀuses into the Se creating a very poor contact (see
section 4.5). This is in agreement with the alloy phase diagram of Ag-Se where they share
a eutectic line (see appendix B, ﬁgure B.1). Furthermore, it can be veriﬁed from the work
of [74] that Ag can easily diﬀuse into Se forming the alloy Ag2Se down to a depth of several
micrometers.
The measured XPS results will be shown in section 4.5.
NiO was added
as a hole conductive layer later in the project, but this ﬁts poorly with Ag, which has
a work function (4.14-4.46 eV depending on crystal structure)[75] lower than the valence
band energy level of NiO. This would cause an upwards band bending which will act as an
energy barrier for the hole transport. Au acts as a more suitable contact and can collect
the generated holes from the NiO or Se since its conduction band lies just above the valence
band of both, as seen in ﬁgure 3.3.
It was not possible to verify whether Au and Se mix or not, due to large roughness of the
sample measured with XPS (see section 4.5).

3.3.4
Se thermal evaporation
Se was deposited by thermal evaporation. Unconventionally, this was done in a load lock
chamber which can be seen in ﬁgure 3.11.
The samples were loaded into a holding stage through the loading window where a lift is
able to move them up and down. The evaporation source consists of a solid pellet of Se on
a thin tungsten boat ﬁxed by two electrodes. By applying a voltage across the electrodes
the boat can be heated causing the Se to evaporate onto the sample target. The quartz
crystal microbalance (QCM) can be moved just in front of the sample position to measure
the deposited rate of Se. When a suitable constant rate is measured, the QCM is moved
away from the direct evaporation path towards the sample. Evaporation was made under
UHV at ≈10−8 mBar. This not only decreases the evaporation temperature of Se but also
ensures a mean free path of λ ≈10 km. Moreover the monolayer formation time is in the
order of 10 minutes to an hour.
Heating tape and aluminium foil was applied to the walls of the load lock to make heating
of the chamber during deposition possible. When depositing Se during external heating,
the sample was placed in the center of the heating zone, at a ﬁxed height of ≈7.0-7.5
cm, away from the evaporation source. Without external heating, the sample position was
just above the QCM position at ≈2.5-3.0 cm away from the evaporation source. It was
hypothesised that if the sample was maintained at a temperature close to the melting point,
Se should be able to create a more homogeneous ﬁlm.
Furthermore, Se should change
phase into trigonal without the need for a subsequent annealing step. Se has a high vapour
pressure and evaporates readily at a low temperature as seen from the vapour pressure plot
in ﬁgure 3.7. For Se deposition during external heating (170-175 ◦C), rapid Se deposition
was necessary to reduce reevaporation from the surface of the sample. Heating the chamber
also causes the Se to reevaporate from the sides of the chamber and onto the cooler sample
surface. Nevertheless, creating a reproducible Se thickness with external heating was found

than what is actually deposited on the sample since the distance to the QCM is slightly
smaller and reevaporation from the surface of the sample is possible, due to heating from
the source. Nevertheless, this short experiment illustrates how diﬃcult it can be to stabilise
the deposition rate of Se. Ideally the QCM is to be positioned next to the sample so the
rate can be measured continuously during deposition.
At the end of the project the reproducibility of Se thickness became more consistent (≈±50
nm), partly due to the use of a newer and more stable power supply, increased experience,
patience and several alterations to the QCM, causing it to measure a less ﬂuctuating rate.
Nevertheless, it could be beneﬁcial for future work to properly quantify a correlation between
the actual deposited Se thickness and the expected thickness from the QCM rate. Currently
the SEM cross-sectional images indicate that the deposited Se thicknesses are approximately
4-6 times lower than expected (see section 4.4.5).

Subsequent annealing of the sample with deposited Se was proven to greatly increase the

deposition was 35 cm3/min.
3.3.7
Final annealing
Annealing the solar cells as a ﬁnal step in the fabrication process proved to enhance their
performance. For most of the solar cells this was done at 190 ◦C for 4 minutes. According
to the results of [73], the transmission of NiO is improved when deposited at higher tem-
peratures, with a measured optimum near a deposition temperature of 300 ◦C. NiO was
deposited at 110 ◦C and it is hypothesised that subsequent annealing should yield similar
transmission improvements. Annealing close to, but below the melting temperature of Se
is therefore necessary. Annealing at 190 ◦C was chosen due to the improved I-V results
around this temperature, which will be explained in section 4.1.2. The annealing conditions
are still to be optimised in future work.

Amorphous to trigonal Se by annealing
From theory and from previous successful Se based solar cells (e.g. [35] and [36]), it is known
that obtaining trigonal Se is a requirement for good cell performance. Amorphous Se can
be transformed to trigonal Se by annealing below the melting temperature of
220.8 ◦C. XRD was performed on a Se based sample before and after the Se annealing step,
just after Se evaporation (see ﬁgure 3.9).
A SEM image of the sample after annealing can be seen in ﬁgure 4.7.
Figure 4.7: Cross-sectional SEM image of a Se sample after annealing at 190 ◦C for 4 minutes. The
thickness of Se is ≈250 nm. The Te thin ﬁlm is to diﬀuse into the Se during annealing and cannot
be seen in the image.
The thickness of the Se is approximately 250 nm and was found from SEM images to cover
the FTO well. Having a good Se coverage after annealing was not found to be possible
without having a thin ﬁlm of Te. This is investigated and explained in section 4.4.2.
The XRD results from the Se sample, before and after annealing along with an FTO sub-
strate, can be seen in ﬁgure 4.8. The measurements were made with the technique grazing
incidence X-ray diﬀraction (GIXRD).
The XRD results show that trigonal Se is formed after annealing. Before annealing no ma-
jor Se diﬀraction peaks can be seen and the measured peaks only correspond to the Bragg
peaks of the FTO substrate. It is noticed that the XRD signal from TiO2 is unobserved,
even though it should have the anatase phase after annealing at 400 ◦C during sputtering.
It is believed that this is due to the small thickness of the TiO2 of 50 nm.
The reason for the very broad intensity peaks for the pre-annealed sample, as well as the
increasing background intensity for lower angles, was found to be due to a common misprac-
tice when doing thin ﬁlm XRD analysis. For thin ﬁlm analysis with GIXRD it is essential
to use a parallel plate collimator in front of the detector in order to avoid the detection
of diverging X-rays. The measurement was repeated with the parallel plate collimator for
the annealed sample and the FTO substrate, which both clearly show much narrower and
distinctive Bragg peaks. A new measurement before annealing could for obvious reasons
not be repeated.

2.36 eV).
The eﬀect of annealing was similarly noticed visibly. When the Se is amorphous it is reddish
and semi-transparent and when annealed it becomes metallic grey and non-transparent.
This correlates well with the obtained UV-Vis results.
The location of the calculated bandgap of Se, Eg = 1.93 eV, found from the Tauc plots
(see ﬁgure 4.11) is included to indicate how much of the light is transmitted below this
energy. In short, the transmission above the bandgap energy is T = 0.5 −15%. For tandem
applications there should ideally be a 100% transmission through the Se, below its bandgap,
since the transmitted light is to be absorbed by the low bandgap material (for instance Si
with Eg = 1.12 eV (1107 nm)).
The measured transmission below the bandgap energy is very low and for future optimisa-
tion, the reﬂection of the Se cell could be reduced by roughening the surface and applying
anti-reﬂective coating.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and outlook
5.1
Conclusion
Throughout the work of this thesis, Se based solar cells have been fabricated with physical
vapour and electrochemical depositions.
It has been shown that Se cells can provide a
large open circuit voltage, Voc, which is suitable for promising tandem applications, when
combined with a low-bandgap material such as Si. Furthermore, the Se based solar cell can
in itself act as a cheap alternative thin ﬁlm solar cell.
The largest obtained Voc from a fabricated cell was Voc = 0.72 V, which is still lower
than Voc = 0.93 V, which has previously been achieved by D. Bae. However, it has been
shown that optimising the annealing temperature and time, as well as including a NiO
hole conducting layer can greatly improve the Se cell performance. An optimal annealing
temperature is currently estimated from measurements to be within 190-200 ◦C.
Se has been shown from XRD to crystallise from amorphous to trigonal through either
annealing at 190 ◦C or while being evaporated with external heating of 170-175 ◦C.
The transmission of trigonal Se has been measured by UV-Vis to be within T = 0.5-15%
below its measured bandgap of Eg = 1.93 eV, indicating a large absorbance/reﬂectance of
the cell. Further optical investigations are required in order to distinguish the absolute
absorbance.
The reproducibility of cell fabrication thickness and morphology along with the adhesion
of the Se layer has also been greatly improved for cells with Se being thermally evaporated
without external heating. Current cells are estimated to be fabricated with a Se thickness
of 1 µm with an accuracy of ±100 nm. The Se thickness is from the current fabrication
process and measurements/observations expected to be optimal around 1 µm.
Furthermore, the inclusion of Te has been shown to be highly important for the adhesion
and wetting of Se during annealing. Annealing a cell with no Te layer results in large visible
diﬀusion of Se from the surface, causing the Se to ball up. Smaller Se island growth was
also observed for cells with poor Te coverage. How the Te is able to improve the adhesion
of Se in this manner is yet a mystery.
Se pillar growth was observed for cells with Se deposited under external heating. Subsequent
annealing can greatly increase this pillar growth. Extensive pillar growth was especially
found for samples made with Au contacts. From the XPS survey of an Au contact, the
presence of Cu was observed, which could potentially act as a seed for the crystal pillar

Chapter 5. Conclusion and outlook
growth of Se. The Cu contamination was found to originate from the Au sputtering, which
could potentially be causing thinner Au contacts than expected, which in turn could be the
reason for the observed parasitic resistance eﬀects from I-V measurements. On average the
FF of cells with planar coverage was greater, followed by cells with pillar and island surface
morphology. Surface pillar growth is nevertheless a potential beneﬁt for future research
where Se is to be used for photocatalysis, due to the increased surface area and edge sites.
XPS depth proﬁle measurements showed that Ag diﬀuses into Se, forming the alloy Ag2Se,
and is therefore a poor contact for direct contact with Se. The same experiment was to be
performed on a Se cell with direct Au contacts, but the results were inconclusive, due to
low Se thickness and large roughness. Nevertheless it was from a third measurement shown
that NiO can act as a separating layer between Se and Au, preventing mixing or diﬀusion.
Ultimately, the results of this project can give insight into how an improved future fabrica-
tion process for the Se based solar cell can be obtained, along with some essential further
research required to achieve a full tandem device for photoelectrolysis.
5.2
Outlook
5.2.1
Optimising the Se based solar cell
The key issue to be addressed, for the optimisation of cell performance, is to improve the Te
coverage and thickness control from deposition. Experiments are to be made to investigate
the exact Te thickness and coverage during electrochemical deposition, as a function of
time, current and voltage. A homogeneous Te thickness of 0.5-2 µm is to be obtained for
optimal cell performance. The thickness could be investigated with XPS depth proﬁling
before depositing Se.
Ideally the cells are to be fabricated with Te and Se deposited during subsequent thermal
evaporation within the same vacuum system. Therefore, the current Te contamination issue
in the main vacuum chamber is to be solved as soon as possible. The analyser for the XPS
within this system is also to be repaired, which will allow for XPS analysis before and after
each deposition step.
Alternatively, other solutions for Se adhesion are to be investigated, as this could give insight
into the current mystery of how Te theoretically is the reason for good adhesion and wetting
of Se.
Cells with reproducible Se thicknesses of 1-2 µm are to be fabricated with homogeneous
surface coverages after annealing. As a ﬁrst step, the QCM is to be calibrated to take the
current 4-6× deposition factor into account. Furthermore, the annealing conditions are to be
optimised. By achieving greater reproducibility, more experiments investigating an optimal
annealing temperature and time could be performed. Annealing the cells in vacuum under
a partial pressure using an inert gas, such as argon, is also to be investigated to prevent
cavity and pore formations as well as unwanted Se evaporation.
Other alternative cell layers than FTO, TiO2 and NiO could potentially also be investigated.
The current world record cell with η = 6.51% has for instance been made with ZnMgO and
MoOx as replacements for the TiO2 and NiO layers, respectively.[36]
During the project, the photoluminescence of several Se samples was investigated with a
newly bought FS5 Spectroﬂuorometer from Edinburgh Instruments, but without success.
82

5.2. Outlook
One early fabricated sample showed poor photoluminescence at ≈1.4 eV with a lifetime of
≈6 ns from a poor exponential ﬁt (see results in appendix E). It is hoped that with better
performing cells, improved photoluminescence results can be obtained.
For future cell characterisation, the quantum eﬃciency (QE) of the Se based solar cell is to
be measured, in order to investigate its spectral losses. Furthermore, capacitance-voltage
(C-V) measurements are to be performed to estimate the acceptor density, Na, of the Se
absorber layer. If the acceptor and donor densities of the cell layers can be determined,
the depletion region width, W, can be estimated, which could lead to the determination of
optimal cell layer thicknesses.
Finally, the theoretical band diagram and maximised performance parameters of the Se
based solar cell can be calculated and plotted by using the simulation program SCAPS,
if all characteristic material parameters (such as the acceptor/donor densities of the cell
layers) can be determined from measurements or found from literature.[96]
5.2.2
Investigation of OER and HER
At the end of the project, cells with deposited NiO were investigated as photoelectrochemical
cells (PECs) for OER. Epoxy was used to cover the cells, so only NiO was exposed. The OER
performance of these areas were studied in 0.1 M and 1 M KOH solution after treatment
in an Fe-containing electrolyte (which improves the catalytic performance of the NiO).[73]
The latest and best results have been measured by D. Bae and are shown in appendix C.
While measuring on one of the cells, bubbles generated from the surface were observed.
The measured I-V curve shows that a generated current, due to OER, is measured at a
low potential V = 0.26 eV during illumination. This means that the voltage generated by
the PEC is very large, and when comparing to the measured dark I-V curve, it is around
≈1.4 V−0.26 V = 1.14 V. The generated current is however minimal and it is hypothesised
to be caused by only small active areas of the cell. After the measurements, the Se easily
peels oﬀ, which indicates that some or all of the OER has happened due to photocorrosion.
Furthermore, cracks on the surface of the cell are observed. Nevertheless, when looking at
the I-V curve, the cell looks to be stable under dark current. NiO is found to still be present
at the surface of the cell, from EDS measurements seen in appendix D. However, nothing
conclusive can be said about the remaining coverage or thickness of the NiO.
For further work, the corrosion of the cell is to be investigated from stability experiments
and subsequent SEM imaging, where corroded areas of the cell surface could potentially
be observed. The cracks in the surface of the cells are to be prevented, either from an
improvement of the Se cell fabrication process or by wetting some insulating material into
the cracks. It is also to be investigated whether atomic layer deposition of NiO could be a
possibility for greater coverage and protection of the Se.
Furthermore, gas chromatography is to be used to verify that the observed bubbles are in
fact oxygen.
In the future, Si could be added to an altered cell fabrication process so that HER could
similarly be investigated with TiO2 at the surface. Finally, it is hoped that a fully functional
two photon water splitting device with Si and Se absorber layers can eventually be fabricated.
