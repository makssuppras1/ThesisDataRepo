ABSTRACT
In mitigating climate change and achieving the targets for renewable energy generation, Building Inte-
grated Photovoltaics (BIPV) are expected to play a significant role. To advance BIPV technology, it
is essential to increase the number of available applications by limiting iridescent behavior leading to
angular dependent color appearance.
This thesis introduces a measurement setup for the Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function
(BRDF) of BIPV modules in a single plane, which enables the calculation of colorimetric parameters
determining the angularly dependent color appearance. Furthermore, BIPV samples of two different
colorization technologies are presented: effect pigments on the front cover glass and pigments added to
the encapsulant layer. Additionally, the BRDF was obtained for samples with textured front glass and
for sample components such as colored encapsulant, glass, and silicon Photovoltaics (PV) cell.
Based on BRDF measurements, a ray tracing model was built using ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio (AZO)
to replicate measured results and separate the contributions from each BIPV layer. The model integrates
scattering and material data by fitting to the ABg model and utilizing meticulously crafted data files.
Results shows applying a textured glass surface decreases variation in the sample chroma and hue,
thereby increasing color uniformity. Furthermore, placing a colored encapsulant in between a glass and
PV layer causes the color to intensify around the specular reflection angles, resulting in larger color
changes depending on the viewing angle.
The AZO model was tested for the specular reflection angle and lambertian reflectance achieving an
accuracy within 1◦and 0.001sr−1 respectively. Additionally, multilayer simulation was not obtained,
however a single layer addition approach yielded a Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of approximately
0.58sr−1 from the measured BRDF.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
To mitigate climate change, the 2050 net-zero emission goal of the Paris agreement was founded, with
a shift towards renewable energy generation being seen as a result of the agreement.
According to
the International Energy Agency (IEA) Photovoltaics (PV) has the fastest growing energy capacity
of any renewable energy source with a predicted growth of 76% over the next five years [1]. This is
largely because PV cells are an easily scalable modular technology, which means that both utility scale
installations and small scale roof top systems contribute to the rapid growth of solar PV[1]. As more
than a third of the world energy consumption can be attributed to buildings, making the localized
small scale systems a sensible point of focus[2]. To this end, the preference of the Building Integrated
Photovoltaics (BIPV) increases as it offers better visual attractiveness compared to its counterpart,
namely the Building Applied Photovoltacis (BAPV).
The concept of BIPV is to match the color of the building-facade of installment, and it is something that
in recent years have caught the attention of private companies and institutions. The current technologies
suffer in varying degree from performance loss and color characterization[2]. While performance assess-
ments have standardized procedures and evaluations of efficiency from the International Electrotechnical
Commission (IEC) , there is no standardized way of assessing color shifts and appearance of the cells[3].
The applications available to the BIPV technology are highly reliant on limiting iridescent behavior [2].
The aim of this thesis is to accurately and reliably characterize the effect on the overall module color
appearance of individual components. This is achieved by utilizing an experimental setup to measure the
Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) and calculate various colorimetric parameters,
which are used to assess the angular-dependent color appearance of BIPV modules.
Furthermore,
samples utilizing various colorization techniques are measured and compared to investigate the difference
in iridescent behavior. A single colorization technology is broken down into its constituents and measured
to determine their contribution to the color appearance of the assembled sample.
Based on experimental measurements of the BRDF a ray-tracing simulation model in ANSYS Zemax
Opticstudio (AZO) is built to make BIPV color appearance assessment and design easier. The aim of
the ray-tracing simulation model is to enable the alteration of layered materials and precisely determine
the resulting colorimetric behavior, while omitting the need to construct a physical module. This thesis
highlights the issues with constructing such a model and determining the areas of improvements to
reliably characterize and trace angular color changes for BIPV products.

2.2
Colorimetry
Color is generally described as a link between the electromagnetic waves produced from light sources
or reflected from surfaces and the neural response of the photoreceptors in our eyes.
This forms a
mental image, thus making the perceived color an interpretation of a physical phenomenon called a
percept. For Building Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV), the color perception is of utmost importance as
the technology stands out in its ability to blend into building facades and its visual superiority. In this
thesis, the focus is colors in their surface mode rather than their illuminant mode, which refers to colors
from a reflecting surface and an emitting light source respectively. To accurately describe the changes
in color perception of BIPV technologies, the following descriptors of color are used [7]:
Chroma: Chroma is defined as a relative perceived difference between a color and the immediate
surroundings with similar lightness. This means that chroma is always defined in regards to a reference,
which is typically grey. A perceived difference in color can be a result of the surrounding colors in view,
this phenomenon is called chromatic induction. As chroma is a relative percept, then chromatic induction
can either be assimilating or contrast, depending if the color shift is towards or away from the color of
interest. This chromatic effect is what gives rise to dark colors as grey and black are imperceptible for
any isolated color.
Hue: Hue describes what in the percept differentiates a color from white. Hues can’t be contributed to a
singular wavelength as the hue of any given wavelength changes with illumination level. All hue percepts
can be described through a subset of unique hues, namely red, green, yellow and blue. The unique hues
can’t be described as each other or any combination of the other hues in the subset. Opponent color
theory describes any combination of unique hues using two bipolar coordinates, as two combinations are
never perceived, namely red(+)/green(-) and yellow(+)/blue(-). The yellow and red has been assigned
positive by convention and any color containing a null is defined as a unique hue. According to Internation
Comission of Illumination (CIE), the hue can be represented using polar coordinates, thus correlating
a change in angle to a change in the hue. The polar coordinates of the hue are calculated differently
depending on the color space of choice.[8].
Saturation: Saturation describes in which degree does the color differentiate itself from white regardless
of the brightness/lightness level. Perception of saturation at equal luminance is not spectral uniform as
high/low wavelengths (blue/red) correspond to high saturation and at wavelengths between 510-540nm
(green) a low perceived saturation is found. Additionally, saturation dependent on light level, thus colors
appears more saturated as intermediate light levels.[7]
Brightness: Brightness describes the perceived overall level, ranging what is described as either dim
or dazzling on each end of the spectrum. Brightness is not linearly related to radiance but closer to
a power function at fixed wavelengths. Perceived brightness is enhanced at low and high wavelengths
similarly to saturation.
Lightness: Lightness is the perceived level of emitted light relative to light from a region appearing
white. Lightness describes an achromatic percept ranging from white to grey to black.
An important color concept when working with BIPV is color constancy for reflecting surfaces. Color
constancy refers to the change in color deception depending on the spectral reflectance of a surface and
the spectral power distribution of the illuminating light source. As this thesis wishes to explore the
nature of the reflecting surface of colored PV cells comparatively to the solar spectrum. Therefore, the

2.3
Colored BIPV overview
The International Energy Agency (IEA) recommends five different coloration technologies as the state
of the art for coloring Building Integrated Photovoltaics modules. These technologies are identified from
currently marketed BIPV modules and refers to the give best in terms of reliability, safety, electrical
efficiency and aesthetics . The five categories are [2]:
1. Anti-reflection coating on solar cells.
2. Colored and semi-transparent PV-active layers.
3. Special solar filters as layers, coatings or interlayers with colors and patterns.
4. Colored polymeric encapsulant films.
5. Modified front glass by printing, coating or alternative modifications.
For each of these advanced BIPV technologies, various colorization mechanisms are employed, demon-
strating that each technology can utilize multiple colorization methods. The colorization technologies
are presented and their applicable colorization techniques are explained.
Anti-reflection coating on solar cells Interference coatings In the case of applying coating on
the cell itself anti-reflective (AR) coating is used. The AR coating is typically optimized to achieve
maximum energy conversion, which results in their blue color.
Therefore, the reflective minima of the cell is changed by modifying the AR thickness, thus changing
the color and the performance of the cell [2]. This type of technology it not widespread today due to
the incapability of producing small batches resulting in higher prices.
Extending AR technology to encompass all interference coatings establishes a foundation for a widely
adopted and popular colorization method, namely the application of interference coatings on various
surfaces. A promising technology is applying the interference coating on glass or polymer foil, thus
forming a multiplayer structure to control the amount of visible light.
This colorization method is
applicable to group one,three and five as they all involve interference coatings of various types on
interlayers, solar cells or the front cover glass respectively.
One such concept is MorphoColor, which uses Bragg diffraction of a multilayer stack over a textured
surface. The method achieves highly saturated colors as it reduces angular dependencies, however it has
a significant cost increase associated with it. [9][10]
Colored and semi transparent PV-active layers
This technology uses the semi-transparency qualities of amorphous silicon (a-Si) to partially remove
the active layer, thus improving the transparency of the cell. This technology is implemented in loca-
tions where high power solutions are unnecessary, such as fully glazed buildings .One such company,
Solibro Research, has utilized sandblasting and water jet polishing techniques to partially remove the
semiconductor layer to achieve additional transparency [2].
Colored encapsulant films
This technology uses the addition of organic and inorganic pigments added to the base PV encapsulates.
The translucency of these encapsulates can be changed by either changing the thickness or the concentra-
tion of the added colorized material. Lenzing Plastics (LP) is one company that uses added interference
pigments in the encapsulate to modify light reflection. They produce thus being able to attain multiple
different colors. Additionally, Onyx solar uses colored Polyvinyl butyral (PVB) to laminate a-Si thin
film solar cells to rear glasses of BIPV cells. This method offers high color homogeneity, however offers
low power conversion due to the substitution of the usual use of crystalline silicon. [9]

This technology includes various colorization methods including digital ceramic printing, using effect
pigments, using mass-colored glass and various coatings both inside and outside the PV-glass. The
module series Colorquant from Ceramic Colors Wolbring (CCW) utilizes screen printing to imprint
effect pigments into the glass. Screen printing covers an array of PV colorization techniques that print

3.1
Setup description
The following sections describe the measured samples and the measurement setup used to to obtain
their BRDF. The sample holders are 3D printed and depend on sample size and measurement type
(reflectance/transmittance). However properly fastened samples is a requirement for accurate measure-
ments.
The setup consists of a Laser Driven Light Source (LDLS), which is refocused using two identical OAP
mirrors, and collimated using a third, with a different RFL, as shown in Figure 3.1.
The collimated light then hits the PV-sample at an angle θe and the sensor detects the photon counts at
an angle α. The sensor and sample angles are controlled independently using two independent stepper
motors, which both have an uncertainty of ∼1◦.
The sensor employed is a reflective collimator, which is an encased OAP with an optic fiber connector
attached, sensor parameters can be found in Table 3.2. The sensor signal is transferred to a spectrometer
using an optical fiber with a core diameter of 600µm. A reflective collimator measures photon counts,
which is limited to the fringe spacing of the spectrometer, thus resulting in a 1nm resolution [11].
Alignment of the collimated beam happens by connecting an optic fiber to an alternative light source
and rotating the sensor until a visual overlap between the light beam and sensor occurs. The stepper
motor adjusting the sensor uses this point as the 0◦reference point.
Having only two stepper motors means that a full hemispherical analysis of the BRDF is unavailable.
Therefore, only reflectance measurements in one plane are obtained. This means that when the sensor
angle equals the sample angle α = θe, the illumination of the sample is blocked and makes reflectance
measurement at these angles impossible.

3.1.1
Measurement process
In this thesis BRDF measurements are typically made for sample angles in the range θe = [0◦; 60◦]
at 15◦spacing. Measuring only positive sample angles is sufficient as the BRDF upholds Helmholtz
reciprocity, see eq. (2.2). For Angle Of Incidence < 10◦obstruction of the detector occurs, and not
enough distributed light applied to the sensor. This results in only being able to properly measure the
diffuse reflections. Therefore, Angle Of Incidence > 10◦is recommended for measurements requiring
specular reflections [12].
For higher sample angles θe a larger uncertainty is expected, this is because of the beam area becomes
elliptical as a function of cos(θe). The absolute sensor angles, often referred to as the viewing angle,
spans the entire measurement plane of 360◦and thus varies between -180◦and 180◦. For convenience
relative sensor angles α are used, relative sensor angles ranges between -90◦and 90◦relative to the
sample angle θe at 5◦increments. Relative sensor angles shows high uncertainty, when approaching

For each measurement series a background measurement is taken. The approach is to cover the LDLS
and measure the ambient light reflections present for all angles in the interval α = [−180◦, 180◦]. These
"dark" measurements are then subtracted for any sample measurements in said measurement series.
Reference ("white") measurements are taken for any series using a perfectly diffuse lambertian scattering
material in the form of spectralon. The reference measurements makes it possible to set the magnitude
of the measured reflectance into context of the incident light, the sample measurements are calibrated
using the reference sample. The spectrometer integration time is adjusted to avoid detector saturation
around the specular reflection angles. The usual integration times used is between 0.05s and 1s and
averaged over five measurements to reduce noise.
Laser alignment of the sample stepper motor is done before each measurement to ensure alignment of
the sample surface plane with 90◦of the stepper motor. This is important, as sample thickness may
vary and misalign the sample plane and stepper motor, creating an angular shift in the measurements,
thus introducing larger systematic errors. The drifting and error associated with the stepper motors
becomes particular apparent, when increasing the sensor angle resolution to ∆α = 1◦instead of the
usual ∆α = 5◦otherwise used in this thesis. The error is most apparent around the specular reflection
angle and further exploration is found in section 5.2. All experiments are made at room temperature
and the Laser Driven Light Source has been turned on minimum one hour before use to ensure a stable
intensity distribution.

3.3
BRDF analysis
In this section the specifics of samples used in this thesis are introduced and the colorimetry and
scattering behavior is analyzed.
The samples are examples on two cutting edge BIPV colorization
technologies and the difference between them is further explored using the concepts described in the
previous chapters.
The sample analysis consists of using the conversion of the spectral reflectance to XYZ tristimulus values
using equation (2.9). As the area of interest in this thesis is the color appearance of the cells, the spectral
part BRDF is of interest. The obtained XYZ tristimulus values are then converted into the CIELAB
color space, where the CIELAB color coordinates L,a⋆and b⋆are used to further describe the color
appearance.
Visualization of the color appearance is conducted by using the calculated RGB values for all measure-
ment points in the experiments. Glare effects results high lightness and saturation of the spectrometer,
causing the RGB values to exceed allowed levels, in this case their color is set to be white [1,1,1].
Quantification of the angular difference in color appearance is described through the use of the CIELAB
lightness, chroma, hue values along with their L, a⋆, b⋆counterparts. The analysis is based on a heatmap
showing the angular color change and their respective chroma and graphs showing the evolution of the
of the CIELAB color coordinates. Additionally, a comparison of chroma and hue for each sample of the
same technology is presented.

BRDF & colorimetry analysis of Lenzing Plastics samples
This section will compare the scattering and colorimetric properties of a subset of the LP samples found
in Table 3.4. In Figure 3.7, the spectral BRDF has been calculated at a tilted sample angle θe = 15◦for
textured and non-textured front cover glass.
Considering the spectral reflectivity of the samples, all the colored samples experience multiple distinct
peaks. The pattern suggest that the sample structure is similar in pigment size and material. The green
samples differ only in the concentration of the colored pigment applied, with a higher concentration
resulting in a darker green color. They both show comparable low reflectivity values and same spectral
behavior. The high concentration of pigments (Green) are observed to absorb more light, resulting in a
lower reflectivity comparatively to the light green sample. Therefore, the size and material drives the
spectral shape of the reflection, while the concentration solely changes the magnitude of the reflection.
As expected the reference have a singular peaks at ∼350nm, which is a result of the reflected light from
the silicon cell. Furthermore, the peak can be observed in other samples as well, indicating that the
composition of these samples is sufficiently transparent to allow the contribution from the silicon cell
to pass through without being absorbed. A small increase in the reflectance of the textured samples
can be observed, this can attributed to the diffuse properties of the front glass by distributing the light
from the specular reflection to multiple view angles. The textured glass have the property of causing
additional light trapping, thus increasing the light in the measured plane.

3.4
Intercomparison of samples
This section compares LP, CCW, and LP encapsulant samples, focusing on the colorimetric behavior
and the BRDF of the samples analyzed from previous figures.
First, comparing the LP and the bare encapsulant measurements, the spectral BRDF shows various
differences. Firstly, none of the samples exhibited the peak around ∼350nm, as they did not include
the silicon cell contribution. Comparing Figure 3.7 and Figure 3.23, the red and green reflectivity are
higher for the encapsulant samples. This is dictated by the additional layers as they interfere and absorb
the incoming light. The gold sample is near identical for the two, indicating that the additional layers
absorbs less light at yellow wavelength than red or green. Comparing the total BRDF of the two samples,
it is clear that the magnitude of the specular reflection is dominated by the front glass, as the peak is
substantially smaller for the bare encapsulant. The diffuse scattering is similar in magnitude of the LP
samples, however is not completely symmetric around the specular reflection. This is likely caused by
improper alignment of the front surface of the encapsulant as the material is flexible.
A notable colorimetric difference between LP and the bare encapsulant are the appearance of the desired
color for a broader range of both sample and viewing angles for the bare encapsulant. This effect is
similar to that of the textured glass surface as the encapsulant material is more diffusive, creating a
larger spread of colors. In conclusion the application of additional laminated layers concentrates the
colors around the specular reflection, thereby increasing the angular dependency of the color appearance
compared to the bare encapsulant.
The magnitude and behavior of the total BRDF for CCW are similar in contrast to the LP samples. This
is the consequence of the front cover glass dominating the specular reflections for both sample types.
The comparison of the spectral BRDF for similar colors showed spectral distributions with peaks around
their respective center wavelengths. A difference in the sample composition is that the LP samples have
a silicon PV cell layer, in contrast to the CCW samples. The magnitudes of the reflectivity differs,
however this could be a result of the different manufacturers wishing to create a different shade of the
desired color.
The biggest colorimetric difference between the CCW and LP samples are the variation of the hue.
The CCW samples exhibit a smaller spread in the hue, which results in a more uniform color across
viewing and sample angles. As the bare encapsulant samples exhibit similar behavior to that of the
CCW samples, it indicates that embedding the color into the backside of the front cover glass reduces
the impact of the glass and lamination process.
The BRDF and colorimetric analysis is both time consuming and prone to experimental uncertainties
and errors. The measurements are in particular difficult on the raw encapsulant samples as the polymer
material is flexible and harder to properly secure in the sample holder, leading to higher uncertainties in
the measurements. To more easily make colorimetry analysis of colored Building Integrated Photovoltaics
samples and isolate the different material contributions to the scattering and colorimetric properties, a
ray-tracing simulation model is proposed. The following sections will describe the structure, testing and
performance of such a simulation model with the goal of replicating the measurements and isolating the
constituents contributing to the samples scattering properties.

4.1
Simulation model setup
The goal of said simulation model is to accurately replicate the BRDF and colorimetric results found
in chapter 3. Additionally, the simulation model gives the opportunity to further investigate the con-
tribution of each layer of the PV stack.
The encapsulant layer of the Lenzing Plastics samples are
particularly interesting because it is the primary component influencing the reflected color. A simu-
lation model makes it possible to predict the influence of the lamination of different layers and use of
different PV structures. The entire model consists of two main components, firstly the basic framework
of the model is constructed using ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio (AZO), which is a ray-tracing software.
The basic framework will be referred to as Zemax and refers to the geometry, attributes and placement
of objects and components. Secondly, automation, filtering, ray-tracing and data analysis is done with
MATLAB through the use of the built-in ZOS-API functions. The ZOS-API functions is a set of com-
mands and functions available to AZO, connecting the MATLAB software with the Zemax components.
The ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio allows for three different model types, sequential, non-sequential and
mixed. The sequential mode trace rays between each object in the order they are defined, meaning its
limited to tracing single transmission or reflection paths. This makes the sequential mode suitable for
designing and testing the performance of afocal and imaging systems. The non-sequential mode is not
limited to any object order and the rays may interact with each object multiple times. This mode allows
for modeling of stray light, scattering and illumination. The mixed mode combines the two previous
systems by allowing non-sequential components into a otherwise sequential system. This allows specific
object to have scattering behavior and allowing for stray light calculations for grouped non-sequential
objects. The approach for this model is based purely on the non-sequential system, as the application
of scattering properties and tracking of stray light throughout the whole system are necessary for the
calculation of the BRDF. A mixed approach is also feasible and would be the preferred method, if the
collimation method of the beam would be included in the modeling, as the modeling of three Off-Axis
Parabolic (OAP) mirrors requires sequential modeling. This purely non-sequential model assumes a
collimated beam through the use of the source object.
Figure 4.1: Left: Simulation setup. Source size is not in scale. Right: Definition of polar angle θp and
azimuth angle φ.
The non-sequential method operates purely in a global coordinate system, as there is no specific object
order in this type of modeling. The system relies on referencing other objects to achieve proper spacing
between objects, however all coordinates are still treated in the global coordinate system of the model.
The incidence angle θi is obtained by tilting the source around the center point of the reflecting surface
in the y-direction. The hemisphere is treated in spherical coordinates, namely the polar angle θp and
the azimuthal angle φ, see Figure 4.1. The polar angle describes the angle between the z-axis and a
point on the hemisphere. Comparatively, the polar angle θp is the simulation equivalent to the relative
sensor angle for the experimental setup. The azimuthal or in short azimuth angle describes the angle of
rotation in the xy-plane. The calculation of these angles will be done in later sections.

4.1.1
Zemax components
The model has seven main components referred to as objects. These objects consist of four different
types: null, volume, source and detector, see Table 4.1. The detector object is used to extract data
of any ray hitting the object and can be found in multiple geometrical shapes to suit the needs of the
model. A polar detector is used for the measurement of reflecting rays in the shape of a hemisphere
placed to align with the edges of the first reflecting volume object. The alignment is important to ensure
the detected ray angles correspond correctly in regards to the global coordinate system. A rectangular
detector is applied to measure transmitting rays and measures rays inside the PV stack.
The model have a single source object modeling a collimated white light source with a defined spectrum
according to the D65 spectrum from Figure 2.2. The source is defined as an elliptical source of finite size
instead of a point source, to more closely emulate the LDLS in the experiment. The volume object is the
most versatile object, as it functions as an object, source and detector. Three volume objects are used
to portray the layered structure of the Photovoltaics cell. The first, second and third volume object
is made to represent the cover glass, encapsulant/EVA and silicon PV cell respectively. The volume
shape used are shallow cylinders closely resembling the shape of a petri glass, however, any geometrical
shape of sufficient size can be utilized. The null object does not interact with the ray-tracing and is
undetectable for rays. The object is used as an alignment reference point in the global coordinate system
for specifically the source and glass volume object.
As stated earlier, the transmission detector is located inside the silicon layer due to silicon being a
highly absorptive material. The thickness of the layer would otherwise have to be unrealistically thin
for a detector outside the silicon layer to detect the incoming rays. The transmission capabilities of
the model is not further discussed and tested in this thesis due to time constraints. The base model is
implemented for future use of the model.

reference object, object connections and material properties. Unique inputs are available depending on
the object type and geometric shape including dimensions of the object, mesh size and emitted power.

4.1.2
Definition of ray properties
The ZOS-API is built up around the ray-tracing capabilities of ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio (AZO).
TTherefore, this section will delve into the inputs, outputs, and the manner in which AZO handles
ray-tracing, specifically in relation to the aspects that are crucial for this model. The ZOS-API of AZO
includes a ray-tracing toolbox to define the ray behavior of a given simulation.
The functions of significance is scattering, ray-splitting and ray polarization. As the naming suggest
the scattering function allows rays to scatter off an intersection. An intersection is the point at which
the ray hits the face of an object, meaning that a volumetric object possesses two intersections [14].
Ray-splitting allows rays to divide at an intersection, creating segments. A segment is the portion of the
ray between two intersections and ray-splitting allows for any given segment to split into a transmitted
and reflected component. A segment loses energy at each intersection based on intersection properties
and ray polarization, making the polarization function crucial in modeling.
Numbering and ordering of the segments is important, when wanting to separate the reflected energy
contribution of each object. The ordering follows the transmitting segment until it reaches a relative
energy threshold set to ϵϕ = 10−6 or is absorbed by a material surface property. The relative energy
threshold is defined as the fraction between the radiant flux of the ray and the segment Φray/Φseg.
The quantity Φray is determined from Ps and the total number of simulated rays used denoted Nray.
Upon reaching the relative energy threshold, the tracing starts at the latest occurrence of ray splitting,

When inputting a scattering model into AZO, it is necessary to understand how it processes the inputted
data. ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio separates the coating step, scattering step, and specular step, whereas
in reality, these steps are applied simultaneously, as is the case with the measured data [19]. Figure 4.5
displays the order in which ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio applies the inputted data. When a segment
hits a surface, it initiates the coating step, which dictates the amount of the incoming flux that is
absorbed and reflected by the surface. This is followed by the Total Integrated Scattering (TIS) step,
that determines the fraction of the reflected flux that is scattered on the surface, while the rest is assigned
to Zemax’ internal specular reflection calculation. Separating the specular and scattering steps leads
to an overestimation of specular reflection, as the ABg-model includes data with specular reflections in
addition to the separate specular step calculated by AZO. The separation of the coating step means

5.4
Encapsulant BRDF broadening model
As observed earlier the pigments in the colored encapsulant causes a broadening of the total BRDF.
This section attempts to predict the magnitude of the broadening and the impact of the incident angle
and pigment material parameters. The model is based on electromagnetic theory or more exact Mie
scattering theory. Mie theory describes the scattering caused by an embedded particle within a medium
with a different refractive index [20].
This situation closely resembles that of the pigment particles
within the encapsulant material. The Mie theory is exact for all sphere sizes, indices of refraction and
wavelengths [20]. This makes the theory extremely comprehensive, which leads to various simplifications
of the model.
The model and code used is based on the functions from the article by Christian Mätzler [21] and parts

colors in the vicinity of the specular reflection than what is visually observed. The simulated model
closely replicates the variation observed among the different colored samples. In both instances the gold

are close together in terms of broadness and magnitude in contrast to the measured values, where

DISCUSSION & FURTHER WORK
In this section, potential improvements for the AZO model are discussed, focusing on using the BRDF
and measured optical properties to predict the final color of a PV stack in a broader context of the
future of colored BIPV.
In this thesis it was possible to reasonably replicate the measured BRDF with a large overestimation
of the specular reflection. Interlayer interaction could not be obtained because multilayer simulation
was unachievable due to an error in the input file. Correcting the radial orientation error in the input
file would allow for precise importation of measured BRDF data and facilitate multilayer simulation as
intended. Multi-layering simulation enables the use of the simulation model for design purposes. The
number of layers can easily and cheaply be increased and changed depending on the desired PV stack of
interest. An accurate multi stack model enables testing of different material combinations without having
to laminate, construct and measure a sample. Additionally, combining single and multilayer simulations
enables the attainment of interlayer interaction, which can predict color changes associated with adding
additional layers. A well-known issue is the change in color appearance related with laminating and
adding layers to a PV stack.
One of the issues to reaching the set goals of the AZO model are the processing speed and storage and the
accuracy of the inputted data. Extending the measurement series from a single plane to a full hemisphere
enables the direct input approach of the BRDF data with fewer assumptions attached. Furthermore,
increasing the resolution of the measured BRDF from 5◦to 1◦increments aligns the resolution of the
model and data, which are currently operating at two different resolutions. This mismatch does not
pose an issue for data imported through fitting, but direct inputted data is interpolated by Zemax.
The issue of data storage and accuracy can be resolved by using a Dynamic Link Library (DLL), which is
applicable for both the direct and fitting approaches, as well as the ZOS-API calculations. Implementing
the heavy computational tasks of executing and storing a ray-trace in a DLL allows for an increase of
the number of rays and by extension the accuracy of the model.
The simulation model is limited by requiring measured BRDF data for the material layer or stack of
interest . In extension, the AZO model cannot simulate small changes in the same material, such as
variations in colored pigment concentration (e.g. green or light green LP samples), without a completely
separate measurement. Expanding the Mie theory to a multiple scatterer model and incorporating it
into the AZO model would enable it to take inputs like particle concentration and predict changes in
BRDF and color appearance. The Mie scattering extension and similar predictive models are significant
for the industrial functionality of the AZO model, as they reduce the amount of measured scattering
data required.

Therefore, to achieve industrial value the AZO model is required to predict the structural composition
of the BIPV stack based on specified CIELAB color coordinates.
Because the inputs to the model
are based of experiments and not CIELAB coordinates, the predictive models, such as the Mie model,
becomes paramount to adjust material properties to achieve the desired color. Iterating over the model
composition would be required, in conjunction to applying the color appearance knowledge obtained
through the measuring of the samples.
For instance, a customer might aim to develop a light green colored BIPV module, that maintains a
consistent color appearance even when viewed from different angles. To achieve a light green colored
BIPV module with low angular color dependency, a green encapsulant is selected and a textured front
glass applied for color uniformity. Finally, the pigment concentration is increased to mitigate any loss
in chroma from the textured front glass. The AZO model would then be iterated over the pigment
concentration or different textured surfaces to gain the desired color appearance.
In conclusion, adding measurement points, extending the measurements from a single plane to a hemi-
sphere, and moving the computational tasks to a DLL would enable the AZO model to be used as a
BIPV color appearance designing tool.

CONCLUSION
The aim of this thesis was to develop and test a ray-tracing simulation model to accurately determine
and separate the contributions to the angular dependent color appearance of BIPV modules.
This
has been achieved through a multi step process of measuring the Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution
Function (BRDF) for constituents of colored BIPV samples using a single plane setup and implementing
it into a stacked layerPV model designed in ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio (AZO). A secondary goal,
was the characterization and description of colorimetric variations between two different colorization
technologies.
Firstly, using the single plane measurement setup provided by DTU Electro, the spectral and total
BRDF was successfully obtained for an ensemble of colored samples from two different BIPV colorization
technologies. The measurements are obtained for various Angle Of Incidence (AOI) and view angles,
which is converted into color coordinates for the CIELAB color space.
The first group of samples was provided by Lenzing Plastics (LP), and is based on colored TPO encap-
sulant laminated between both textured and smooth low iron PV glass, a PV cell and black backsheet.
The smooth surface exhibited high chroma and hue variation resulting in a high color variation and
intense colors near the specular reflection angle. Applying a textured front glass reduced the variation
of the chroma and hue allowing for a larger spread of colors relative to the specular reflection angle
resulting in a more uniform color appearance. Additionally, the bare colored encapsulant provided by
LP was measured and showed that the introduction of additional layers, especially a front cover glass,
concentrates the colored appearance around the specular angle creating a higher angular dependence.
A significant increase in scattering was observed for colored encapsulants compared to the transparent
reference sample, due to the addition of pigments to the encapsulant.
The second group of samples was provided by Ceramic Colors Wolbring (CCW) and is based on Mica flake
pigments embedded in the front cover PV-glass with a black backsheet. The CCW samples demonstrated
high chroma and low hue variation leading to higher color uniformity across different AOI and viewing
angles. The biggest disparity between the two technologies were the application of the colored pigments
to the front cover glass rather in the encapsulant. The application to the front cover resulted in lower
hue variation and higher color uniformity for all the measured samples.
Secondly, a single scatterer Mie theory based model was made to predict the broadening associated
with increased scattering for colored encapsulant. The model did severely underestimate the broadening
observed indicating a multi scatterer model is required to accurately model the additional scattering
provided by the added pigments.
Finally, a ray-tracing model was constructed with ANSYS Zemax Opticstudio (AZO) as framework and

utilizing MATLAB ZOS-API for controls to calculate the BRDF for a multilayer PV stack. The model
accuracy obtained a 1◦uncertainty for pure specular reflections and a 0.001sr−1 deviation in the BRDF
tested on a lambertian scattered surface. The simulated BRDF is emulated using scattered data fitted
to the ABg-model, achieving a minimum accuracy of R2 ∼0.93 for colored samples. Alternatively, the
BRDF was imported directly using a .bsdf file, which was applied in the case of the bare silicon cell.
Model input includes material dispersion curves for PV glass, encapsulant POE and bare silicon. The
simulation of all three layers was unsuccessful due to an error in implementing the silicon scattering func-
tion. A three-layer PV stack was simulated by summing the simulated individual single layers, resulting
in an Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) between [0.5, 0.65] sr−1 compared to the measured BRDF. The
greatest deviation occurred around the specular reflection angle attributed to an overestimation of the
specular angle, despite Total Integrated Scattering (TIS) normalization being applied to the inputted
data.
Due to model speed and storage a Dynamic Link Library (DLL) based method is required to increase the
number of simulated rays, and with that accurately replicate and determine reflectivity of constituents
of measured BRDF. Furthermore, extending the measurements to the full hemisphere enables a precise
estimation of the hemispherical TIS, making directly inputted data a more accurate and viable option.
