Abstract  
The integration of machine learning and automation tools can help to speed up the design, build, test, and learn 
(DBTL) cycle and facilitate the development of optimal cell factories. Nevertheless, the accomplishment of an 
efficient high-throughput cell engineering pipeline requires the optimization of several dry lab and wet lab 
procedures. Hence the aim of this project has been to investigate the use of SignalP 6.0, DeepLoc 2.0, H2O 
automated machine learning platform (AutoML), and in vivo assembly for the engineering of signal peptides 
in Aspergillus oryzae. The results obtained from this project showed that there are major challenges to be 
tackled both in dry and wet lab procedures. Investigation of dry lab approaches showed that the prediction 
tools such as SignalP 6.0, DeepLoc 2.0, and H2O AutoML can aid the prediction of potential signal peptides, 
however, if not utilized correctly they can result in confusing prediction and undermine the learning process. 
Analysis of wet lab procedures, on the other hand, demonstrated the need for implementing an automated 
experimental design that can help to solve the challenges of conventional in vivo assembly and transformant 
screening in filamentous fungi. Overall, the results obtained elaborated on some of the challenges which can 
be optimized to get one step closer to achieving machine learning-guided engineering of many fungal cell 
factories with an automated and high-speed approach.

1. Introduction 
The rising living standards and the world population necessitates significant development of the production 
processes to sustain the impendent demand for valuable resources such as food, medicine, medicine, and 
construction materials. This increases the demand for achieving a scalable production platform that can provide 
a sustainable synthesis of the resources in large quantities. Fortunately, the development of fermentation 
systems coupled with digitalization approaches (e.g., high-throughput systems and machine learning) are 
paving the way to utilize the potential of cell factories like filamentous fungi which can be engineered to 
leverage an efficient and automated synthesis of high-value products. Although this approach shows potential, 
several challenges (e.g., data readability and wet/dry lab optimization) have to be tackled to sustain the 
implementation of a mature machine learning-guided engineering of cell factories in a high-throughput setup.  
 
In this respect, the aim of this thesis is to investigate the performance of computation tools for machine 
learning-guided engineering of signal peptides (SPs) in Aspergillus oryzae for high-throughput applications. 
Hence, the objective of the thesis is to investigate whether the utilization of prediction tools (SignalP 6.0 and 
DeepLoc 2.0), automated machine learning platform (H2O AutoML) and in vivo DNA assembly strategy can 
be integrated to achieve a high-throughput cell engineering pipeline. As an additional objective, the 
implementation of automation tools such as Flowbot and QPix might also be investigated depending on the 
progress of the thesis.  
 
In the direction of the thesis objectives, it is important to acquire an elaborate understanding of filamentous 
fungi secretion mechanism, signal peptide characteristics, supervised machine learning algorithms, and 
automation systems. Therefore, this section is dedicated to providing background on filamentous fungi, SPs, 
and digitalization approaches to provide insight that can help to emphasize the potential of integrating machine 
learning and automation for the engineering of the renowned industrial fungal cell factory A. oryzae, which 
has the potential to be utilized in a mature pipeline within the foreseeable feature. 
1.1. 
Filamentous fungi as cell factory 
Filamentous fungi encompass a diverse group of organisms that are mainly characterized by three outstanding 
features, namely; multicellular hyphal network (mycelium) formation, well-tuned secretory mechanism, and 
highly versatile metabolism for the synthesis of bioactive metabolites 2. The presence of these features is 
mainly attained due to the evolution of filamentous fungi to maintain a saprotrophic lifestyle, which 
necessitates an efficient secretory mechanism and a diverse hydrolytic enzyme profile (e.g., amylases, 
cellulases, proteases, etc.) to sustain adequate nutrients from complex natural substrates at different habitats. 
This makes filamentous fungi unique cell factories for the efficient production of natural and recombinant 
products for numerous industries such as food, beverage, agriculture, and pharmaceuticals 3. 
Possessing such a well-developed toolbox for protein synthesis and secretion facilitated the utilization of 
filamentous fungi. This favored the formation of industrial model cell factories such as species from the 
Aspergillus genus (predominantly A. nidulans and A. oryzae), Overtime, engineering of the fungal cell factories 
facilitated their industrial application for the production of commercial products by well-established companies 
including Chr. Hansen, Novozymes and others 3. Besides Aspergillus, numerous other fungal species have 
been adapted for industrial production over time, thus making over fifty percent of commercial protein 
production sustained by filamentous fungi through processes such as fermentation 4.  
==PAGE:7==
 
 
 
7 
Filamentous species that are generally regarded as safe (GRAS) can be cultivated in different setups 
(submerged/solid-state fermentation) and can be used to utilize complex carbon sources such as lignocelluloses 
5. In addition, filamentous fungi also show superior secretion capabilities, which under certain conditions 
surpass the capacity of industrial hosts such as bacteria (e.g., Escherichia coli) and yeast (e.g., Pichia pastoris) 
6,7. Furthermore, filamentous fungi possess a plethora of secondary metabolite gene clusters and well-
developed post-translational processing mechanisms which can be utilized for the synthesis of novel bioactive 
compounds such as triterpenoids that can be used for pharmaceutical applications 8. Despite having an 
improved understanding of their metabolic mechanism, a complete understanding of the intrinsic secretory 
mechanism of filamentous fungi is yet to the completed due to their complex nature.  
Over the years, the development of target-specific engineering technologies such as the CRISPR/Cas system 
(i.e., clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats/CRISPR-associated protein) has been 
introduced as a highly efficient engineering tool that can be used for editing single or multiple (multiplexing) 
gene targets, thus resulting in a huge milestone for the engineering of fungal cell lines as proven by many 
experiments. Although this expedited the engineering process, the determination of beneficial modification 
still remained a laborsome and unfavorable process mainly due to a lack of complete understanding of the 
fungal secretory mechanism 9 Hence, there has been a rising interest to develop an automated pipeline which 
can utilize machine learning algorithms to assist the engineering of fungal metabolism in order to capture the 
true potential of the fungal secretory pathway. Consequently, implementation of easily readable (literate) 
machine learning approaches and high-throughput technologies have recently been on the front burner as the 
next step towards achieving fungal cell factories with optimal secretory capabilities 10.

1.1.1. 
Molecular mechanism of protein production and secretion 
In filamentous fungi, protein synthesis and secretion is a multi-step process that is documented to occur under 
the regulation of two main secretion pathways, the conventional protein secretion (CPS) and unconventional 
protein secretion (UPS) as illustrated in Figure 1 9,11. 
 
 
Figure 1: The figure demonstrates different protein trafficking systems that can lead to the secretion of intracellular proteins. The 
conventional protein secretion (CPS) pathway takes place via the ER-to-Golgi transport system which requires the presence of a leader 
(SP) sequence. The unconventional protein secretion (UPS) pathway, on the other hand, can occur both in the absence (Pore formation, 
ABC transporter, and Leaderless vesicular secretion) and presence (Golgi-bypassing secretion) of SP sequence, which is also known 
as Type I, II, II and IV unconventional protein secretion mechanism, respectively. The figure is extracted from Kim et al. 2018 12 
In conventional pathways (Figure 2), secretion of the proteins needs to be assisted by short leading sequences 
located at the N-terminus which are known as SPs. Hence, after transcription of the corresponding mRNA, the 
initial step of protein secretion begins by co- or post-translational transport of the polypeptide chain from the 
ribosome to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). In the co-translational pathway, SPs trigger signal peptide 
recognition particles (SPRs) which direct the growing polypeptide chain into the ER, where the SPR and SP 
units get detached, thus releasing the polypeptide chain directly to the ER lumen 8. In the post-translational 
pathway, on the other hand, translation of the growing polypeptides is completed in the cytosol and later 
transported to the ER via the chaperone complexes. In the next step, a series of chaperons and folding enzymes 
(foldases) in the ER lumen facilitate correct folding and modifications such as glycosylation which has a 
substantial influence on the stability, localization, and secretion of the proteins. Once correct folding is 
achieved, the final step begins, where properly folded proteins get transported to the Golgi apparatus and later 
directed to the apical plasma membrane via Spitzenkörper apical vesicle clusters before being secreted to the 
cell exterior.  
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
9 
Under conditions where incorrect protein folding or hypersecretion occurs, the cell can trigger ER stress 
response (unfolded protein response - UPR and ER-associated protein degradation - ERAD) to ease the burden 
through enhanced foldase synthesis and misfolded protein degradation, which is important to keep in mind 
when optimizing protein secretion 8. 
 
 
Figure 2:The figure illustrates the conventional protein secretion (CPS) pathway mechanism in filamentous fungi. The transcription 
of mRNA is followed by SP-guided co- or post-translational localization of the nascent polypeptide in the Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER), 
where the critical modification processes (e.g., glycosylation) occur to facilitate the correct folding of the polypeptide structure. In the 
next step, ER-to-Golgi transfer occurs, where final modification such as hyperglycosylation and O-mannosylatoin takes place before 
the polypeptide sequence gets encapsulated in a Golgi-derived cargo vesicle and directed to the apical plasma membrane to be 
secreted. The figure is extracted from Wang et al, 2020 8 
Albeit the generality, SP-guided protein secretion is also documented to occur by Golgi-bypassing (Type IV) 
UPS pathway. In contrast to conventional secretion, proteins that undergo Type IV secretion utilize vesicular 
carriers to bypass the entire or part of the Golgi complex 9,11,12. Several experiments have shown a direct 
relation between ER stress and the translocation of proteins through unconventional secretion 9,12–14. Despite 
the noticed mechanism, an understanding of unconventional secretion mechanisms and vesicular carriers still 
need to be elucidated.

1.1.2. 
Optimization strategies for protein secretion  
Regardless of the already existing efficient secretion mechanism, optimization of the secretion mechanism of 
filamentous fungi is a requisite step to achieve commercial-scale production of valuable bioproducts such as 
proteins. In the course of time, numerous process and strain engineering strategies have been developed to 
improve the synthesis of valuable products from fungal cell factories. 
 
In the area of process engineering, most of the endeavors include the determination of the optimal fermentation 
setup (e.g., solid state / submerged) and the optimization of fermentation conditions such as media 
composition, carbon source, aeration, agitation, and growth temperature. At the current state, submerged 
fermentation is a preferred setup for industrial processes which use filamentous fungi as a production host 4. 
This is because a submerged setup provides better control over the fermentation conditions and has a manifold 
of digitalization toolbox, thus increasing its likelihood to be integrated with a data-driven high-throughput 
setup. Despite its supremacy, the fermentation setup and its respective conditions still largely depend on the 
choice of production host, as it can influence production performance. For instance, the setup of fermentation 
conditions (e.g., oxygen levels and pH level) can influence cell metabolism and mycelial morphology (e.g., 
hyper-branching and tip density), thus altering the nutrient uptake and protein secretion performance of the 
fungal cells 4. 
 
In the area of strain engineering, prior optimization of the fungal strains was mainly achieved through processes 
such as protoplast fusion and UV mutagenesis, which suffered from low efficiency and randomness 4. 
Nevertheless, as our understanding of fungal biology improved and novel genetic engineering tools were 
investigated, various strategies were developed for the optimization of protein synthesis and secretion. As an 
example, promoter swapping and codon optimization can be used to enhance transcription and expedite 
translation to achieve an optimized gene expression mechanism for homologous and heterologous proteins, 
respectively 15. As an alternative approach, engineering the working mechanism of hyphal compartments 
through strategies such as engineering secretory signals, nascent polypeptide localization and maturation, ER 
stress mechanisms (e.g., UPR and ERAD), protein modification in the Golgi, and transportation via cargo 
transporters can facilitate improving protein secretion 4. For example, the engineering of UPR and ERAD 
mechanisms through upregulation of accessory proteins (e.g., chaperons and foldases) and downregulation of 
degrading enzymes (e.g., proteases) can enhance the protein folding efficacy while restraining the degradation 
activity on the protein of interest, thus improving the secretion profile potentially with reduced misfolded or 
aggregated protein formation. However, engineering of UPR and ERAD pathway genes invariably showed to 
have adverse effects and be host-specific as illustrated by experiments such as bip1 upregulation in A. awamori 
and A. nidulans 8. 
 
In contrast to ER stress engineering, manipulation of secretory signal mechanisms (e.g., SPs) can provide more 
generic and lucid outcomes by explicitly targeting the long-known CPS pathway. In this strategy, introducing 
an efficient SP at the beginning of a gene that codes for the protein of interest can increase the recognition of 
the nascent polypeptide chain by the SPRs, thus increasing protein secretion 9. In fact, the engineering of α-
galactosidase with the well-researched SP, glucoamylase (glaA) showed nearly a nine-fold increase in the 
secretion, thus proving the efficacy of SP engineering as demonstrated in Table 1 16. Recognizing the potential 
of SPs motivated the investigation of machine learning-guided engineering approaches (e.g., machine and deep 
learning models) in search of finding novel SPs, which affirmed their potential to be used for optimizing protein 
secretion17–22.

1.2. Signal peptides  
Leader sequences, also known as signal peptides (SPs) are short amino acid extensions at the amino (N)-
terminal of the polypeptide chain that regulates the recognition and localization of the nascent proteins at the 
correct translocation pathway within cells 9. For cells, SPs play a vital role not just in localization but also in 
the regulation of protein folding, thus preventing the formation of misfolded protein aggregates and securing 
the occurrence of correct metabolic activities. For industry, on the other hand, SPs are mainly associated with 
protein secretion as they guide proteins to the CPS pathway. Consequently, unveiling the link between the 
structural pattern of SPs and secretion performance gained importance over time for the optimization of 
industrial processes. 
1.2.1. 
Molecular structure  
The molecular structure of the SPs can vary depending on the host, secretion mechanism, and peptide 
functionality. In addition to these variations, SPs can also have diverse lengths (15-60 amino acids) and amino 
acid compositions depending on the protein to be secreted and the specific needs of the cell 9. Despite their 
diversity, SPs still share common structural patterns that can be associated with their functionality, and thus, 
potentially with increased protein secretion. In general, classical SPs have a tripartite structure that is composed 
of a positively charged N-terminal of 1-5 residues (n-region), a short hydrophobic core (h-region) of 7-15 
residues, and a polar carboxyl (C)-terminal of 3-10 residues (c-region) as illustrated in Figure 3 23,24. The n-
region is mainly associated to engage with electrostatic interactions with the membrane phospholipids, whereas 
the h- and c- regions are generally correlated with SRP recognition and protease interaction for the cleavage 
of the SP from the premature polypeptide sequence, respectively 9,23.  
 
Figure 3: The figure shows the generic tripartite molecular structure of the SP molecules, which is composed of three main regions 
before the mature protein sequence, namely; the positively charged unstructured/folded amino (N)-terminal region of 1-5 amino acids 
(n-region), the central hydrophobic core region of 7-15 amino acids (h-region) which is mostly alpha-helix, and the carboxyl (C)-
terminal region of 3-10 amino acids (c-region) which has a polar charge. The molecular model is inspired from Liaci et. al 2021 24 
and created by using Biorender.com. 
Nevertheless, the generic molecular motif of SPs can show variation depending on the translocon and signal 
peptidase that participate in the process. Translocons (e.g., SecYEG and TatABC) are protein secretion 
channels that mediate as a pathway for the translocation of unfolded/folded proteins across membranes 25. In 
general, there are a variety of translocons, however, the secretion (Sec) and twin-arginine (RR)-translocation 
(Tat) translocon pathways are the most highly conserved protein secretion mechanisms that have been observed 
in all life domains (i.e., bacteria, archaea, and eukarya) 26,27. However, in order to pass through the translocons, 
the SP motif needs to be processed. This process is handled by signal peptidases (SPs), which are membrane-
bound serine proteases that catalyze the cleavage of the SPs from the premature protein sequence based on the 
cleavage motif at the c-region of the SP sequence. 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
13 
In eukaryotic cells, the cleavage site of the secretory proteins that contain a c-region generally contains Gly, 
Ser, Thr, and Cys at the −1 position, and Ile, Leu, Val, Ser, and Thr at the −3 position which facilitate cleavage 
by type I signal peptidase (SPI/Les). At the same time, in cases where the c-region is substituted with different 
motifs, such as the lipoprotein box and basic region, the SP cleavage is catalyzed by type II and type III/IV 
signal peptidases (SPII/Lsp and SPIII/SPIV/PilD), respectively. Consequently, the molecular SP motifs can be 
generalized to different classes, as illustrated in Figure 4. 
 
 
Figure 4: The figure illustrates the molecular structure of five different signal peptide classes observed in different domains of life. The 
structures are composed of the amino (N) terminal region (n-region), the hydrophobic core region (h-region), the carboxylic (C) 
terminal region (c-region, lipobox, and basic region), and finally the mature protein region. The molecular motifs shown are based on 
the secretion (Sec) and twin-arginine (RR)-translocation (Tat) translocon pathways, which are pulled together with different signal 
peptidases (SPI/Les, SPII/Lsp, and SPIII/SPIV/PilD) which are membrane-bound serine proteases that catalyze the cleavage of 
different cleavage motifs (c-region, lipobox, and basic region, respectively). In eukaryotic organisms such as filamentous fungi the 
predominant signal peptide class is the Sec/SPI structure. The figure is inspired from Teufel et al. 2022 22 and created by using 
Biorender.com.

1.2.2. 
Industrial importance 
The extracellular localization of most proteins occurs through the CPS pathway. This makes SPs a potential 
target to optimize industrial processes. Hence, this approach promises great potential to alleviate several major 
hurdles of fermentation, as summarized in Figure 5 9,28,29. 
 
Figure 5: The figure summarizes some of the improvements that can be achieved by using signal peptide engineering such as regulation 
of protein localization, ease of cellular burden, unconventional host utilization, host adaptation, and up-/ down-stream processing. In 
addition, it can also assist the improvement of cellular fitness, production yield, and protein secretion efficiency. The figure is created 
by using Biorender.com and Flaticon. 
In one particular approach, SPs can aid the recognition and translocation of recombinant proteins in the host 
cell, thus returning a chain of benefits including alleviated cellular burden, and improved secretion of the 
desired product 4,30,31. At the current industrial level, where the vast majority of recombinant proteins are 
synthesized by a particular set of renowned industrial cell factories, this approach can provide a solution for 
intracellular aggregation and degradation of heterologous proteins, which lowers the overall yield in many 
large-scale production processes 3. 
Hence, the implementation of SPs can help to improve upstream and downstream processing by increasing the 
production titer and easing the purification processes required. In addition, improving the SP-guided secretion 
of proteins can also help to enhance cellular fitness and ease the adaptation process of conventional and 
unconventional cell factories at an industrial scale. In this regard, SPs can potentially facilitate the investigation 
and utilization of novel cell factories for industrial production, thus increasing the diversity of available 
industrial cell factories 28,29.  
 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15 
Besides protein secretion, utilization of SPs can provide control over the translocation process, which can 
potentially be used for the directed delivery of target molecules into specific organelles such as ER. As a result, 
this can help to increase the localization efficiency of the proteins and enhance the overall protein secretion 
4,30,31. In spite of their potential, the diverse molecular nature of SPs necessitates the perscrutation of numerous 
features (e.g., amino acid composition and peptide length), which makes their engineering a complicated and 
laborious process. For this reason, the implementation of digitalization approaches such as simulation models 
and automation systems is greatly favored for the engineering of SPs. 
1.3. Digitalization 
Utilization of cell factories for the manufacturing of valuable bioproducts generally requires iterative engineering 
of the host cells which is typically done under four phases, namely; design, build, test, and learn (DBTL). In the 
DBTL cycle, the first (D) and second (B) phases involve gathering information and developing tangible products, 
whereas the third (T) and fourth (L) phases involve product assessment, and feedback gathering to repeat the cycle. 
Hence, to align with the rationale of the DBTLengineering cycle, each cycle iteration should be based on the data 
generated in the previous cycle run. However, the complex and susceptible nature of biological systems makes the 
engineering process vulnerable to small variations in biological factors (e.g., epigenetic regulation mechanism) and 
environmental conditions (e.g., nutrient availability), which can lead to fluctuating experimental outcomes. 
Consequently, this undermines information transfer between each DBTL cycle, thus reducing the reproducibility 
and reliability of the data generated. Considering that the biotechnology sector requires strict regulation, high 
expenditure, specialized expertise, and labor, achieving quantitative is a pertinent challenge for biomanufacturing 
and cell factory engineering 32.  
 
This incentivized the adoption of digitalization strategies (e.g., big data, machine learning, and automation) within 
the life science sector to carry out reliable and replicable DBTL cycles. Over the years, the integration of 
digitalization technologies has facilitated the development of a wide range of strategies within different fields of the 
biotechnology sector, including drug development 33, cell factory construction 34, protein engineering 35, and SPs 
18,19,22. Hence, within the scope of this thesis, machine learning algorithms can facilitate the data-driven 
identification of patterns for the construction of optimal SP structures for enhanced secretion, and high-throughput 
technologies can speed up the DBTL engineering cycle, thus enabling rapid screening of a large variant space. 
1.3.1. 
Protein language models 
Proteins are composed of amino acid sequences that are arranged in a particular order to retain a specific 
molecular conformation. Although the protein structure, and thus functionality, is dynamic and context-
dependent, the core information of the protein is mainly defined by the strings of amino acids which can be 
akin to strings of letters in human language 35. In this analogy, algorithms from the field of natural language 
processing have gained importance for the prediction of protein features, thus leading to a computational 
revolution in protein characterization and engineering.  
 
Over the past decades, the machine learning algorithms and neural network architectures used in the field of 
natural language processing have been held up as an example for the development of protein language models 
(LMs), which can be trained on large protein datasets in a supervised (with labeled data) or un- / self-supervised 
(with unlabeled data) manner to capture complex patterns 36. For instance, machine learning LMs such as 
support vector machines and random forests showed successful results in predicting the antimicrobial activities 
of various peptides based on the physiochemical predictors 19. 
 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
16 
In recent years, innovation in neural network architectures and model training strategies, together with 
increased computation power and data accessibility, has emphasized the potential of transformer neural 
network architectures, in protein engineering 37. The transformer architecture is a self-supervised encoder-
decoder model that was introduced in 2017, where the encoder translates the input data (e.g., amino acid letters) 
into an internal vector map representation, which the decoder interprets and creates an output sequence based 
on the internal representation 36. This allows transformers to have a self-attention mechanism by learning 
intrinsic relations among all possible amino acid pairs within the provided peptide sequence, thus achieving 
an improved representation of the data. Two successful implementations of this strategy in the field of SPs 
include SignalP 6.0 and DeepLoc 2.0 18,22. The SignalP 6.0 architecture is a bidirectional encoder representation 
from transformers (BERT) that is trained on the amino acid sequences from UniRef100 as predictors to 
determine all five classes of SP structures illustrated in Figure 4. The DeepLoc 2.0 architecture, on the other 
hand, is a transformer-based LMs that is combined with a multi-layer perceptron to predict the subcellular 
localization (e.g., nucleus, extracellular, mitochondria, etc.) of the protein sequences provided. 
 
However, since all LMs are based on the data provided, the simulation performance of the models is dependent 
on the type, quality, abundance, and representation of the protein information provided. Therefore, the protein 
features to be predicted (e.g., physicochemical properties, peptide sequence, and protein structure) generally 
need to be encoded to make information readable by a specific language model. In addition, different problems 
generally demand a specific combination of modeling and encoding approaches 19. This makes each developed 
LM approach uniquely designed for one problem within a specific niche, thus restricting their implementation 
for different problems, even within the same area. The realization of this limitation promoted the adoption of 
automated machine learning (AutoML) platforms, which can test various LMs and determine the optimal 
model architecture for a given dataset without needing an extensive understanding of coding and machine 
learning. 
1.3.2. 
High-throughput technologies 
The advent of automation technologies has revolutionized the life sciences field, enabling engineering cycles 
to be done in a high-throughput manner with reduced information loss between the DBTL engineering cycles. 
Integration of robotics, in particular, has emerged as a powerful tool to achieve high-throughput automation in 
laboratory environments, thus reducing the dependence on humans for several tasks within the life science 
field. In general, the research and development process is a labor-intensive and time-consuming process, thus 
limiting the number of experiments that can be performed within a given time 38. In contrast, robotic automation 
enables parallelization of the experimental trials, thus improving the efficiency and throughput of the 
engineering process. In addition, the implementation of high-throughput technologies also reduces the 
occurrence of human errors that can cause inconsistencies in results, consequently, undermining the 
reproducibility and reliability of the data generated from each DBTL cycle.  
 
Realizing the underlying potential of high-throughput technologies facilitated the ongoing integration of 
automation technologies within the upstream processes, consequently, supporting the development of 
renowned automation systems such as the flowbot ONE unit from flow robotics, the microbial colony picker 
(QPix) module from Molecular Devices, the digital genome engineering platform (Onyx) from Inscripta, and 
the advanced micro-scale bioreactor (AMBR) unit from Sartorius. The Flowbot ONE unit is a programmable 
liquid handling system that can be used for automated pipetting applications to achieve efficient and accurate 
liquid sampling in several engineering tasks (e.g., media preparation, sample transfer, and inoculation) that are 
important for the construction of cell factories such as filamentous fungi.

code.  
 
Implementation of machine learning-guided engineering and high-throughput technologies requires the 
generation of executable code for several tasks, including data analysis, machine learning, and robotic 
programming. This makes the documentation process an important step to achieve understandable, and 
therefore executable, datasets. Consequently, the notebooks generated in this thesis are based on three 
commonly used literate programming platforms, namely; Jupyter Notebook, Visual Studio Code, and GitHub.

text and executable code in multiple programming languages (e.g., Python, R, and Julia) within a single 
document. In a similar manner, Visual Studio Code is another open-source and multi-language notebook 
platform that is used as an integrated development environment that is generally utilized to facilitate the editing 
and debugging processes of the source course. However, it also contains integrated extension packages that 
can be built to achieve Jupyter Notebook and GitHub integration, thus making it a perfect platform to mediate 
code editing and interactive data analysis. Unlike the former platforms, GitHub is a web-based data 
management platform that is composed of centralized destinations (i.e., repositories) for storing and managing 
notebooks through features (e.g., workflow tracking, push/pull requests) in order to facilitate transparent data 
trafficking between repositories to support collaboration and data accessibility.  
 
Hence, the utilization of literate programming approaches in this thesis is important to achieve an articulate 
and reproducible documentation environment that enables understandable storage of the code and datasets on 
a publicly accessible storage platform. As a result, this facilitates collaboration and information transfer among 
different research lines, which can accelerate the digitalization of bioprocesses in an effective, reliable, and 
repeatable manner. Therefore, the Jupyter Notebook and Visual Studio Code platforms are planned to be used

2.1.2. 
Data analysis  
The structure of SPs shows significant variation between species, thus making it difficult to predict generic 
structure from open-source datasets. This makes data analysis a vital step for understanding the native SP 
structures of A. oryzae ORY7. Considering the scope of this project is to investigate SP-guided secretion, 
carrying out proteomics analysis on the secreted protein structures can provide information regarding the 
abundance of various secreted proteins. However, considering the epigenetic modification mechanisms of 
fungi, proteomics analysis might not provide access to the entirety of the SP profile. This is because 
biotic/abiotic factors (e.g., translational regulation and nutrient availability) can regulate the expression or 
repression of the genes through heterochromatin/euchromatic formation, which will lead to the synthesis of 
different proteins. For this reason, the data analysis process is assisted by online simulation tools (SignalP 6.0 
and DeepLoc 2.0) and databases (FungiDB) to predict potential SP structures and perform gene ontology (GO) 
analysis on the proteomics data, respectively.  
 
As described in Chapter 1.3.1, SignalP 6.0 and DeepLoc 2.0 are transformer-based LMs that can be used to 
predict five different SP classes and subcellular localization, respectively. The SignalP 6.0 machine learning 
algorithm is based on several LMs that are trained on large amounts of unannotated protein sequences from all 
domains of life to generate a semantic representation of proteins. With this approach, the SignalP 6.0 algorithm 
is trained on a comprehensive dataset compiled of protein sequences harbored with five SP classes and 
intracellular/transmembrane sequences, which is then evaluated based on threefold nested cross-validation. In 
addition, the algorithm is also defined with the region-specific features (e.g., hydrophobicity and charge 
distribution) as illustrated in Figure 4 to capture potential SP structures from the computational analysis 22. 
The DeepLoc 2.0 model transformer algorithm, on the other hand, is trained on a large homology-partitioned 
protein sequence annotations of eukaryotic and human proteins that are obtained from the SwissProt and the 
human protein atlas projects, respectively. Additionally, the transformer architecture is aided with an attention 
pooling mechanism to capture potentially important regions on the sequence, and a multi-layer perceptron 
classifier to predict 10 subcellular locations (e.g., nucleus) and 9 sorting signals (e.g., SP sequence) 18. Hence, 
these tools can be utilized to carry out computational analysis on the genome sequence of A. oryzae ORY7, 
which can be retrieved from online databases such as FungiDB. With this approach, the SignalP 6.0 server can 
be used to investigate the SP profile of A. oryzae ORY7 to capture the native SP structures, and the DeepLoc 
2.0 server can be utilized as a conformation tool to check the localization of the SP structures from proteomics 
analysis and SignalP 6.0 predictions.  
 
Besides the prediction tools, carrying out GO analysis on the gene products (in this case, protein sequences) 
can provide additional information regarding the cellular component, molecular function, and biological 
process that the protein sequences are likely to involve. Hence, this approach can be used to gain insight into 
the functional role of proteins and their relationship with specific epigenetic modifications. In this context, 
performing GO analysis on the SP structures obtained from proteomics and SignalP 6.0 can provide valuable 
information regarding the functionality of the SPs in A. oryzae ORY7, which can be potentially associated 
with epigenetic factors that triggered their synthesis.

2.1.3. 
AutoML models 
Generating machine learning models for solving complex biological problems generally requires multi-
disciplinary knowledge covering life sciences (e.g., biology and chemistry) and data sciences (e.g., statistics 
and programming). However, with the ever-growing development and adoption of machine learning, the 
cultivation of multiple fields in a single mind falls short to meet the increasing demand in academia and 
industry. This led to the development of automated machine learning (AutoML) models, which can provide a 
unified interface with a predefined machine learning architecture that is easier to understand without requiring 
expert knowledge in coding or neural network topology. Implementation of this approach can enhance the 
adoption of machine learning models, thus facilitating digitalization and innovation in the biotechnology 
sector. 
 
In general, the working mechanism of a typical AutoML platform can be summarized in five steps. In the first 
step, the data to be investigated (e.g., amino acid sequence) is input to the AutoML platform to train the 
machine learning algorithm. This data is generally composed of large datasets that are curated after 
preprocessing and cleaning procedures such as encoding. In the second step, the AutoML platform generally 
implements data processing measures such as normalization in order to make the data more suitable for 
machine learning models and to increase the statistical significance of the model results. In the third step, the 
processed data is used to train multiple machine-learning models with diverse combinations of algorithms and 
hyperparameters (i.e., settings that control the model behavior) to determine the model architecture that 
provides the optimal performance based on the data. Following this step, the AutoML platform evaluates the 
performance of the resulting models based on various performance metrics (e.g., accuracy, , mean squared 
error, and mean absolute error) and selects the optimal model. In the final step, the selected model is provided 
as an output to make predictions. It is important to state that this generic description may not apply to all 
AutoML platforms. In addition, despite being a machine learning model, the performance of the model and the 
significance of the predictions are largely depending on the quality, abundance, and representation of the input 
data provided.  
 
In the course of time, several AutoML platforms have emerged, including Google Cloud AutoML, Auto-
Sklearn, AutoKeras, Amazon Lex, and H2O AutoML. In this project, the H2O AutoML platform is utilized to 
generate a machine-learning model for the prediction of novel SP structures. H2O AutoML is a fully-automated 
open-source platform that can be scaled to large datasets with application programming interfaces in 
programming languages R, Python, Java, and Scala. In addition, the platform also provides a large number of 
algorithm choices (e.g., deep learning and random forest) and stacked ensemble methods to find the optimal 
combination of prediction algorithms. After training, the results of the AutoML run are provided in a 
leaderboard, where the tested machine learning models are ranked in descending order based on the 
performance metric of choice. Besides the performance metric, the models can also be ranked according to 
other model attributes such as training time and prediction speed. In addition, the platform allows defining 
individual training conditions (e.g., cross-validation and training time) prior to model training, thus providing 
a user-friendly interface for the training and evaluation of the models 40.

2.2.1. 
CRISPR-Cas system 
The CRISPR/Cas system is a gene editing technique that has emerged as a powerful tool for target-specific 
genome engineering in various organisms, including filamentous fungi. This system was discovered as an 
immune system that is utilized by prokaryotic cells such as bacteria and archaea to defend against viral 
infections 41. The acronym CRISPR stands for clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats, in 
other words; a family of viral DNA sequences that are acquired from previous infections and incorporated into 
the genome of the organism. These CRISPR sequences are utilized as a guide by the Cas (CRISPR-associated) 
nuclease to recognize and cleave complementary DNA strands at a specific location, thus preventing the 
prognosis of the viral infection by hindering the transcription of viral compartments 41. By this analogy, the 
specific CRISPR sequences can be used to guide the Cas nuclease to the desired genomic location to generate 
a DNA double-strand break, thus resulting in site-specific genetic engineering 42. 
 
In recent years, significant advancements in the CRISPR/Cas concept facilitated the development of diverse 
systems that utilize distinct Cas proteins and CRISPR sequences. One of the predominant systems is the 
CRISPR/Cas9 system, which is developed by engineering the Cas9 protein of the system host, Streptococcus 
pyogenes 43. This engineered system is based on a single-guide RNA (sgRNA) that is composed of CRISPR 
guide RNA (crRNA) attached with a trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA). The tracRNA acts as a 
scaffold protein facilitating the formation of the mature sgRNA:Cas9 complex, whereas the crRNA, which is 
complementary to the targeted DNA sequence, serves as a guide for the localization of the Cas9 at the target 
site to introduce the double-strand break 44. However, for this mechanism to function, the crRNA sequence 
target must be located downstream of a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM), which shows variation between 
Cas proteins (e.g., NGG for Cas9) 45. Hence, an attentive design of the sgRNA sequence and the PAM location 
is important to achieve efficient site-directed genome editing. Once the double-strand break is introduced, it 
can be repaired through the homologous recombination (HR) or non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) 
pathways, which can be utilized to achieve genetic engineering at the target site, as illustrated in Figure 7.  
 
 
 
Figure 7: The figure illustrates the CRISPR-Cas9-associated genome engineering system, which is composed of single-guide RNA (i.e., 
CRISPR RNA:trans-active CRISPR RNA fusion - crRNA:tracrRNA) and Cas9 nuclease. The sgRNA:Cas9 complex utilizes the sgRNA 
to locate the complementary DNA sequence in the targeted genome, where the Cas9 nuclease catalyzes the formation of a double-
strand break at the specific site (i.e., three bases upstream of the protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) sequence). The resulting break 
triggers the DNA repair mechanism, which can occur through non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) or homologous recombination 
(HR) mechanisms. The NHEJ mechanism (1) is based on non-guided repair, thus resulting in random DNA insertions or deletions, 
whereas the HR mechanism (2) is based on homology-assisted template repair, which can be utilized to introduce desired point 
mutations, insertions, or deletions. The figure is adapted from Löbs et al., 2017 46 and created by using Biorender.

2.2.2. 
Fungal optimization 
The development of the CRISPR-Cas9 system facilitated the engineering of eukaryotic organisms such as 
filamentous fungi, which are generally more difficult to engineer due to possessing larger and more complex 
genetic structure compared to prokaryotic cells. Hence, it is important to optimize the wild strains with specific 
modifications such as the addition of auxotrophic marker genes and deletion of the NHEJ pathway to achieve 
an efficient CRISPR-Cas9 associated in vivo-HR assembly.  
 
One of the optimizations includes the deletion of the ku70 gene, which is crucial to hindering the NHEJ 
mechanism, and will compel the cell to use the homology-assisted HR mechanism for survival, thus providing 
control over the gene modifications. By this approach, any gene of interest that is fused with homology 
sequences upstream and downstream of the break will trigger the HR mechanism, leading to the insertion or 
deletion of the interested gene sequence 47. Besides engineering, disruption of the pyrG gene (which encodes 
the orotidine 5-phosphate decarboxylase protein) is also a beneficial optimization that will inhibit de novo 
synthesis of uridine monophosphate, consequently, making the cell auxotrophic (i.e., inability to synthesize 
compounds required for growth) for uracil and uridine. Hence, this modification can be utilized as a selectable 
marker, as the cells that received the pyrG gene containing plasmid can easily be selected by growing in a 
selective medium lacking the corresponding supplement, thus resulting in an efficient transformation 
procedure 48. 
 
As an additional optimization, engineering the Cas9 endonuclease is also important to facilitate its localization 
inside the nucleus. To achieve this approach, in A. nidulans, the C-terminal of the Cas9 protein was attached 
to the SV40-NLS (Simian virus 40 Nuclear Localization Signal) sequence, and the cas9 gene was codon 
optimized 49. After the modification, the altered cas9gene sequence was cloned into an AMA1 vector (A. 
nidulans sequence that is capable of extrachromosomal plasmid replication), which is combined with one or 
more sgRNAs to introduce target-specific double-strand breaks 50. In this structure, Cas9 expression was put 
under the regulation of the transcriptional elongation factor (TEF) promoter and terminators, whereas sgRNAs 
were placed under the regulation of the Aspergillus fumigatus U3 promoter and terminator. In addition, the 
cloning of the sgRNAs was spaced between glycine tRNAs to increase their accessibility by Cas9 and to enable 
multiplex CRISPR gene engineering. Finally, the modified sequence was incorporated into the pAC330 
plasmid backbone to be used as a vector for the genetic engineering of fungal species, such as A. oryzae ORY7.  
 
As a final optimization, the construction of strain libraries with standardized genomic integration sites can 
provide significant improvement to the engineering process by reducing the workload required for the 
construction of gene-expression cassettes (GECs). In recent years, an optimized multi-species fungal 
engineering platform has been developed under the name DIVERSIFY 1. The platform utilizes common 
synthetic gene integration (COSI) sites to facilitate heterologous gene expression in different filamentous fungi 
species. At its current state (DIVERSIFY 1.0), the platform has successfully constructed the COSI1 site in four 
Aspergilli species, including A. oryzae which is used in this project. The COSI1 site is composed of two 
homology regions (namely; A and B) and the Escherichia coli reporter gene (uidA gene) under the regulation 
of the gpdA promoter and trpC terminator from A. nidulans. The uidA gene encodes for ꞵ-glucuronidase 
(GUS), which catalyzes the degradation of the chemical compound 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-beta-D-
glucuronic acid (i.e., X-Gluc) which leads to blue color formation. Although this approach is efficient for 
transformant screening, a more suitable method, such as fluorescent screening, is required to assess the 
influence of SPs under an automated high-throughput system.

2.2.3. 
Transformant screening 
The screening of transformants is a critical step for the identification of mutants with the desired traits. In a 
conventional setup, the cultivation of transformants on a selective media, such as DIVERSIFY 1.0 strains on 
X-Gluc media, can be utilized for the screening of the positive transformants based on the absence of blue 
color formation. In addition, coupling this strategy together with a fluorescent screening (i.e., the addition of 
fluorescent genes to the strain) such as red fluorescent protein can provide an additional screening step to 
further prove correct insertion. 
 
However, conducting conventional screening for large sample sizes undermines the practicality of the 
engineering process due to time, labor, and resource constraints. For this reason, various high throughput 
screening strategies have been developed to facilitate automated rapid screening of transformants in 
parallel.One of the most commonly utilized high-throughput screening methods is based on the 
compartmentalization of the colonies in microtiter plate wells. With this approach, transformant colonies 
cultivated in multiple wells (in the range of 4 to 9600) 51 can be screened by using photon-sensitive detectors 
such as a spectrophotometer and spectrofluorometer, to generate a linear relation between photon intensity 
measurements and sample concentrations based on the Beer-Lamber Law and photoelectric potential, 
respectively. Hence, implementing the microtiter plate method can provide a multi-purpose screening approach 
that can be utilized to carry out transformant analysis in an automated, high-throughput manner 52. 
Nevertheless, even when optimized with automated handling robots, the throughput of such systems is limited 
by the plate capacity. 
 
For this reason, several other methods have emerged to compensate for the limitations of microtiter plates. For 
instance, the utilization of quicker methods such as fluorescence-activated cell sorting can provide a screening 
rate of 70,000 cells per second 53. However, this method can only be utilized at the earlier stages of germination 
due to size incompatibility between filamentous fungi and the sorting nozzle 54. In addition, implementation of 
this method becomes impractical for screening the activity of secreted proteins or extracellular metabolites, as 
fluorescence detection is done in a continuous stream55 in the absence of compartmentalization for cultivation. 
As an alternative method, droplet-based microfluidics technology can be used to encapsulate fungal colonies 
in nanoliter droplets to cultivate and screen fungal transformants 56. Implementation of this approach to 
quantify α-amylase activity in A. nidulans, showed that the system can sustain 24-hour cultivation of colonies 
inside the droplets and screening of 7,000 fungal transformants per hour. In addition, the system also achieved 
196-fold enrichment for α-amylase activity after following a single round of screening on the A. niger library 
that was mutated with ultraviolet light. Nevertheless, analysis of filamentous fungi in droplets is challenging 
due to apical growth and branching mycelial networks that can exit the droplet and cause coalescence. Hence, 
the choice of screening method should be determined based on the specific requirements of the experimental 
setup and available technologies.

2.2.4. 
In vivo assembly 
Over the years, the development of tools for fungal genetic engineering has been a fast-growing area, which 
resulted in an enriched toolbox including circular mini chromosomes, synthetic building blocks (e.g., selection 
marker, promoter, and terminator), and efficient target-specific genome engineering techniques (e.g., 
CRISPR/Cas system). Despite achieving a diverse toolbox, implementation of most tools in high-throughput 
systems still remained limited due to having reliance on DNA assembly strategies such as Escherichia coli 
cloning or in vitro assembly (e.g., fusion-PCR), which are considered time-consuming, expensive, and 
inefficient at multiplexing 57. For this reason, there is a demand to achieve high-throughput compatible efficient 
DNA assembly strategies, such as in vivo assembly mechanism observed in the yeast Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae. In this strategy, the amplification of PCR fragments with short homology regions (nonpriming tails) 
facilitates the HR repair mechanism of the host, thus leading to DNA assembly through the annealing of 
homologous sequences, as illustrated in Figure 8. 
 
 
Figure 8: The figure illustrates the in vivo assembly mechanism, where the transformation of a given host with homology-assisted 
fragments can facilitate a homologous recombination mechanism to achieve in vivo assembly of the fragments. The figure is adapted 
from Chen et al., 2021 58, and created by using Biorender. 
However, for this in vivo-HR mechanism to function properly, the nonpriming tails should have a specific 
length (typically 20-60 nucleotides) to reduce expense and enhance priming efficacy, as longer tails can 
influence the primer function through intrapirmer secondary structures and primer dimer formations 57. In 
addition, implementation of in vivo-HR assembly in filamentous fungi species such as Aspergilli is generally 
considered more challenging due to requiring long non priming tails (e.g., 500-2000 base pair homology) to 
achieve efficient gene engineering 59,60. In contrast, several experiments have concluded that genetic 
engineering simulated in the presence of several critical conditions (ΔNHEJ mechanism together with a 
double-strand break in the target sequence) can aid in achieving in vivo-HR assembly with 60 and 90 bases in 
Penicillium and Aspergilli 49. In addition, a recent study that investigated the influence of 25-1600 non priming 
tail lengths has also emphasized the importance of the aforementioned critical conditions, documenting a ~40% 
increase (~50% to ~90%) in the transformation efficiency under ΔNHEJ conditions. Furthermore, the research 
paper by Jarczynska et al., 2022 57, also highlighted the importance of the tail length by stating that having ≥ 
400 tail length would ideally be required to have an efficient in vivo-HR assembly in the presence of an NHEJ 
mechanism, whereas having a short tail length such as 25 bases, could lead to the formation of undesired 
background constructs. However, in the absence of NHEJ mechanism 25 bases homology arms could 
potentially yield positive results 57.

3. Problem statement 
In the context of the background provided, the main aim of this thesis is to investigate the optimization 
strategies required to improve the integration of dry lab and wet lab procedures for machine learning-guided 
engineering of fungal cell factories. More concretely, the objective is to explore the performance of the 
computational tools for developing an intelligible machine learning-based pipeline that can be used to 
accelerate the DBTL engineering cycle and facilitate the construction of fungal cell factories with enhanced 
secretion capacity.  
 
In order to achieve this goal, the thesis is mainly dedicated to investigating the construction of ten native SP 
sequences in the host organism A. oryzae ORY7 by using the CRISPR/Cas9 associated conventional in vivo 
homologous recombination mechanism and analyzing the results by measuring the red fluorescence emission. 
Parallely, Jupyter Notebook, Visual Studio Code, and the H2O AutoML platform are utilized to facilitate the 
optimization of the wet lab procedures by generating literate programming documentation, SP analysis, and 
machine learning predictions, respectively. 
 
In this respect, the main intention of this thesis is to seek answers to the following research questions: 
● Can we use SignalP and DeepLoc to capture the underlying features of signal peptides?  
● Can we use the H2O AutoML platform to predict novel signal peptide structures? 
● Can we use in vivo assembly to construct signal peptides in a high-throughput manner?

4. Materials and methods 
4.1. 
Data analysis 
As an initial step in the thesis, the SP profile of A. oryzae ORY7 had to be analyzed to obtain information about 
the abundance and structure of native SPs in the species. In order to achieve that, data sets on extracellular 
proteomics analysis, computational SignalP 6.0 predictions, and intracellular transcriptomics analysis were 
decided to be analyzed. With this approach, the proteomics and signalP datasets can be used to make 
comparisons between the observed and predicted SPs, whereas the transcriptomics dataset can be used to 
normalize the SP abundance observed in the proteomics analysis, thus reducing the noise for AutoML training. 
 
Hence, the former two datasets were provided by the Bioengineering department of DTU, whereas the latter 
dataset was obtained from Wang B. et al., 2010, respectively. The proteomics dataset was generated by 
growing A. oryzae in triplicates in two different media compositions (minimal media - MM and minimal media 
with added nitrogen - MM+N) and analyzing the supernatant proteins by mass spectrometry, which resulted 
in a total of 865 unique proteins. The signalP dataset, on the other hand, was generated by submitting the 
annotated proteins file obtained from FungiDB to the SignalP 6.0 server, which generated 1061 SP predictions. 
Hence, the proteomics dataset provided information on peptide abundances, whereas the signalP dataset 
provided information regarding peptide length and sequence. Considering that the datasets had different 
origins, the structure of the datasets had to be standardized. Hence, all three datasets were processed prior to 
data analysis to remove unnecessary information and achieve a common data structure, which was documented 
by using Jupyter Notebook and Visual Studio Code to align with the literate programming approach.  
 
Peptide abundance 
To investigate the most abundant SPs found in A. oryzae ORY7, the proteomics and signalP datasets were 
used. In the first step, the proteomics dataset was utilized to calculate the mean of the peptide abundances 
observed per sample in MM and MM+N compositions. In the next step, a two-sample t-test was used to assess 
the statistical significance of the abundance variation observed between the two media compositions. 
Afterward, the mean values were aligned with the SignalP 6.0 predictions with respect to the stated accession 
numbers (aligned dataset), and sorted in descending order to isolate the most abundant 20 peptides observed 
to carry out gene ontology (GO) analysis.  
 
Gene ontology analysis  
Different media compositions can trigger epigenetic mechanisms to alter the composition and abundance of 
the secreted peptides. Hence, to anlayze the influence of MM and MM+N compositions, the accession numbers 
of the most abundant 20 peptides observed in each media were uploaded to the UniProt server to retrieve ID 
mapping of the accession numbers from UniProt/Gene Name to UniProt Knowledgebase (UniProtKB). The 
resulting findings were then downloaded as an Excel file, which was customized to contain the entry name, 
protein name, and GO molecular function. Once downloaded, the Excel file was processed to remove the 
unidentified accession numbers, and the remaining accession numbers were aligned with their respective 
peptide abundances to generate a single data frame for each media. As a result, the MM and MM+N data 
frames were compared to assess molecular functions and the respective abundances observed under different 
media compositions.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:28==

DeepLoc analysis 
The DeepLoc 2.0 server was utilized to assess the accuracy of the proteomics analysis and SignalP 6.0 
predictions by testing the subcellular localization prediction of the accession numbers. In order to achieve that, 
the sequences of the SPs from proteomics and signalP datasets were covered in several SeqRecords each 
containing a maximum of 400 peptides, as the server has a limit of 500 sequences at a time. As a result, the 
SeqRecords were submitted to the DeepLoc 2.0 server which was set for Hig-quality (Slow) and Long output. 
After this step, the prediction results were downloaded as comma-separated values (CSV) file and the 
sequences which scored > 95% to be extracellular were isolated. The number of extracellular predictions was 
then compared with the proteomics and signalP datasets by using Venn diagram illustrations.  
 
AutoML dataset 
The dataset for AutoML was generated based on using peptide abundances as a weight metric to depict SP 
importance. In order to achieve this, the mean peptide abundances observed in MM+N media were divided by 
transcriptomics abundances to normalize the peptide abundance by removing the native transcription 
background. In the next step, the proteomics dataset containing the normalized abundances was merged with 
the singalP predictions to create the AutoML dataset containing peptide sequences and normalized abundances. 
As a result, sequences that were common in both datasets were assigned respective peptide abundances, 
whereas the sequences that were present only in the signalP dataset were assigned zero peptide abundance. 
This allowed to separate the peptide sequences with important features from the rest of the predictions. After 
this step, the AutoML dataset was processed to separate amino acid letters into individual columns. However, 
since all sequences were not the same length, the empty columns of short peptide sequences were represented 
with dashes (-). In the end, the resulting dataset was saved as a CSV file to be loaded into the H2O AutoML 
server for training.  
4.2. 
AutoML 
In order to start training the machine learning models, the H2O AutoML server was initiated by using the 
“h2o.init()” function which was set to ip="localhost" and min_mem_size_GB=8. In the next step, the AutoML

AutoML dataset where all the amino acid letters per column were represented as categorical data. Afterward, 
the AutoML dataset was filtered with respect to SP lengths to generate three training datasets containing 
peptides that are ≤ 22, ≤ 30, and ≤ 68 in length, respectively. Following this step, the setup parameters of the 
H2O AutoML class were stated and the training of the AutoML model was initiated after defining the feature 
and prediction columns as illustrated in Figure 9.

4.3. 
Cell factory construction 
In the context of this project, the CRISPR-Cas9 system was utilized to cleave the uidA gene sequence, which 
was then replaced with the SP gene expression cassette. Consequently, this hinders the encoding of the uidA 
enzyme and makes the colonies remain white instead of blue on the X-Gluc media. Hence, this enables the 
uidA gene to be utilized as a selection marker for the selection of successful transformants. As a result, a 
previously constructed pDIV073 plasmid, which is based on pAC330, was used as CRISPR/Cas9 vector. To 
facilitate the integration of the SP sequences, the strain was also transformed with two building blocks 
harboring homology arms to act as a repair template for in vivo assembly and COSI1 integration by the HR 
mechanism. Hence, the SPs composed of ~60 nucleotides were split in half and attached to the primer tails. 
The primers were used for the amplification of the two building blocks together with the tails, which were 
constructed by the repair mechanism of the host. To enable the annealing of SP tails, 18 base pair homology 
was used between the two halves of the SP fragments, thus leading to in vivo generation of the GEC. With this 
approach, the CRISPR/Cas9 system was used to catalyze the break of the targeted uidA gene sequence, which 
the HR mechanism used to repair by integrating the GOI. Consequently, this hinders the X-Gluc degradation 
capability of the mutated strain, resulting in white colony formation, which can then be selected as positive 
transformants. After this step, the mutants can be screened for red fluorescence o further confirm positive 
transformants as illustrated in Figure 10. 
 
 
Figure 10: The figure illustrates the theoretical working mechanism of the CRISPR-Cas9-mediated genetic engineering approach. 
Figure A illustrates CRISPR/Cas9-assisted double-strand break formation on the uidA gene sequence located at the common synthetic 
gene integration (COSI) site. This triggers the homologous repair (HR) mechanism, thus compelling the repair of the break based on 
the homology-assisted building blocks, which contain SP tails (SP1A and SP1B) with 18 base homology. The integration of the building 
blocks followed by the annealing of the SP tails restores the gene expression cassette (SP) at COSI site 1. As a result, this hinders the 
uidA gene function of the strains, resulting in white colony formation that can then be selected as positive mutants. Figure B 
demonstrates the screening method of positive transformants by first growing the transformants on a selection medium (transformation 
medium) and then making random isolation of transformants on a screening medium (Minimum medium with Triton+Xgluc+ura+uri). 
Once isolated on the screening medium, the positive transformants can be screened by identifying white colonies and checking for red 
fluorescence. Finally, the positive transformants can be compared with positive control which will have blue coloration and dim 
background red coloration under the fluorescent light. The illustration is adapted from Jarczynska et al., 2022 57 and created by using 
Biorender.

Primer design 
To design primers, the annotated coding sequence file from A. oryzae was obtained from FungiDB and 
converted into SeqRecords. In the next step, the aligned dataset was used to isolate the most abundant 25 
accession numbers of SPs with a length smaller than or equal to 22 amino acids. The accession numbers were 
then used to identify and isolate the respective coding sequences on the positive strand, thus taking away the 
need for codon optimization. Once the coding sequences were obtained, the annealing sequences, the end of 
Ptef, and the beginning of red fluorescent protein (CCACAAAACATAACACAACCTTCACC, and 
GCCTCCTCCGAGGACG), were incorporated into the 5’ and 3´ end of the sequences, respectively. 
Following this, the sequences were split into equal halves while conserving the codon structures, and 9 bases 
were interchanged between the ends of two SP halves to create 18 base pair homology for in vivo assembly. 
Afterward, the first half sequences were converted into reverse complement sequences to be used as reverse 
primers, whereas the second half sequences were kept based on positive strands to be used as forward primers. 
As a result of this process, 25 primers were designed, and the top 10 primers that had a length smaller than or 
equal to 60 bases were purchased. In order to make the primer design process high-throughput, the described 
steps were converted into an algorithm that can be utilized to automate the primer design process by providing 
important parameters such as the coding sequence list, up homology sequence, down homology sequence, and 
desired in vivo homology length. Finally, the developed algorithm was subjected to a stress test to investigate 
the performance and robustness of the algorithm. The summary f the design process is illustrated in Figure 11.

DNA purification and precipitation 
Purification of the DNA amplifications can be achieved in several ways, such as agarose gel purification and 
ethanol precipitation. For the purification of linear templates, gel purification was used to isolate only the 
specific band lengths, whereas, for two-step PCR, ethanol precipitation was performed as this process is more 
suitable for a high-throughput setup. In gel purification, the 1.4kb and 2kb bands were identified by using a 
transilluminator and isolated by cutting around the desired band using a scalpel that was cleaned with 96% 
(v/v) ethanol. After cutting, the band was dissolved and purified by using the Illustra™ GFX PCR DNA and 
Gel Band Purification Kit from MERCK.  
 
In ethanol precipitation, the building blocks were precipitated by adding 0.1 volumes of 5M sodium chloride 
solution, followed by 2.5 volumes of ice-cold 96% ethanol relative to the volume reached after sodium chloride 
addition. After the additions, the mixtures were homogenized by pipetting, and suspended with 
microcentrifuge (full speed at 4°C for 20 minutes). Once completed, the supernatants were decanted, and the 
pellets were washed (half suspended) with 2 volumes of ice-cold 70% ethanol. Afterward, the centrifugation 
and ethanol washing steps were repeated, and the decanted microcentrifuge tubes were placed into a heating 
block kept at 37°C to evaporate the remaining ethanol. Finally, the purified pellets were resuspended with 
MilliQ water and used to measure concentration. Both in purification and precipitation, the DNA quantification 
was performed via Nanodrop measurement by blanking the instrument with the same solution in which the 
DNA samples were dissolved (MilliQ) and measuring the sample concentration (ng/μl) as well as purity ratios. 
 
In isopropanol precipitation, on the other hand, the building blocks were precipitated by adding 0.2 volumes 
of 3M sodium acetate solution, followed by 0.7 volumes of isopropanol relative to the volume reached after 
sodium acetate addition. After the additions, the mixtures were homogenized by pipetting and incubated at 
room temperature for 5 minutes. Once completed, the mixtures were suspended with microcentrifuge (16000g 
at room temperature for 10 minutes). Following this step, the supernatants were decanted, and the pellets were 
washed with 2 volumes of ice-cold 70% ethanol. Afterward, the centrifugation and ethanol washing steps were 
repeated, and the decanted microcentrifuge tubes were placed into a heating block kept at 37°C to evaporate 
the remaining ethanol. Finally, the purified pellets were resuspended with MilliQ water and used to measure 
concentration. Both in purification and precipitation, the DNA quantification was performed via Nanodrop 
measurement by blanking the instrument with the same solution in which the DNA samples were dissolved 
(MilliQ) and measuring the sample concentration (ng/μl) as well as purity ratios. 
 
Fungal transformation 
The transformation of the building blocks and CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid was performed by using the protoplast 
transformation method. The A. oryzae ORY7 protoplasts were prepared as described in Appendix A and 
provided by DTU Bioengineering. For the transformation protocol to work, the volume of DNA to be added 
should not exceed 25% of the protoplast-DNA mixture volume to prevent disruption of the protoplasts due to 
osmotic stress. As an initial transformation step, the volume for each linear and circular DNA used was 
calculated to be equivalent to 750 ng, based on the Nanodrop concentrations. Once the volumes were 
determined, 50 μl of protoplasts and the linear/circular DNA to be introduced were added into sterile 2 mL 
microcentrifuge tubes. In the next step, 150 μl of PCT was added to each tube and mixed by slowly pipetting 
until the solution was homogenized. Afterward, the mixtures were left to incubate at room temperature for 10 
minutes. After incubation, 250 μl of aspergillus transformation buffer (ATB) was added to each tube and mixed 
by pipetting up and down, followed by plating on a transformation medium (TM) without supplements. Finally, 
the resulting plates were incubated at 30°C for 4-7 days.

For introducing SP building blocks, the pDIV073 plasmid was transformed with the 1.4kb and 2.0kb building 
blocks. For positive control, a self-replicating plasmid containing an AMA1 sequence and pyrG gene was

As an initial step in cell factory construction, the implementation of touch-down PCR to amplify the 1.4kb and

Once all the building block fragments were amplified, the ethanol precipitation technique was used to 
concentrate the amplified building blocks, and amplicon concentrations were validated to be > 200 ng/μL by

amplicons showed poor results. Most of the transformation trials (7 out of 8) failed to yield any colony

6. Discussion 
6.1. 
Dry lab 
The results obtained from the dry lab approaches showed that the quality and quantity of data play an important 
role in data analysis and machine learning training. The analysis of proteomics data showed that different 
media compositions might influence the abundance and diversity of SPs. Although the scope of the data 
provided was not adequate to derive a significant conclusion regarding peptide abundance, the GO analysis 
results showed that the diversity of peptides changes depending on the media composition. Besides proteomics, 
the implementation of SignalP 6.0 and DeepLoc 2.0 showed that the scope of the experimental data can be 
extended by analyzing the data stored in databases such as FungiDB. The prediction results of SignalP 6.0 
provided approximately 200 additional SP predictions compared to proteomics analysis, whereas DeepLoc 2.0 
predictions aided the assessment of extracellular localization of the SPs. The SignalP 6.0 server was able to 
predict 1061 peptide structures, which was more than experimental observations (865). However, regardless 
of increased peptide detection, the SignalP 6.0 predictions only captured 256 out of 865 (256/865) experimental 
observations (Figure 14). In a similar way, utilization of the DeepLoc 2.0 server was able to capture only 
53/1061 of SignalP 6.0 predictions and 83/865 proteomics observations as extracellular peptides (Figure 15). 
Hence, both the SignalP 6.0 and DeepLoc 2.0 tools performed poorly at capturing the experimental and 
predicted SP observations used in this thesis. Nevertheless, this can be due to the presence of dead cell proteins 
in the analysis. Hence, more experimentation is required to assess the true performance of both tools. 
 
Besides data analysis, utilization of the AutoML approach was also observed to be difficult due to several 
aspects such as data size and data encryption. Machine learning models rely on large datasets to capture 
important patterns and make accurate predictions. In this thesis, the training of algorithms was based on 1061 
peptide sequences, of which only 256 contained abundance values to be used as a weight metric. Despite being 
already small, this number was further reduced when filtered with respect to peptide lengths. Utilization of 
such a small data size reduces the generalization potential of the trained algorithm which could describe the 
prediction performance observed in Table 5. In addition to data size, data representation could be another cause 
of prediction performance observed. Implementation of encryption, also known as tokenization, methods such 
as one-hot encoding can improve the readability of the data for the machine learning algorithm. As a result, 
this can enhance the ability of the algorithm to capture the underlying patterns in the SP sequences and improve 
the prediction results. As an additional aspect, the implementation of deep learning architecture was observed 
to perform better compared to other supervisor learning models investigated (Table 4). This can be explained 
by the ability of the neural networks to capture non-linear relationships, which can provide an effective high-
dimensional analysis of the SP sequences, thus leading to improved prediction performance.

6.2. 
Wet lab 
The results obtained from the wet lab trials emphasized several experimental aspects that can be improved to 
make the genetic engineering of cell factories suitable for high-throughput systems. One of the key aspects 
includes the primer design. The efficiency of the PCR amplification depends on many parameters including 
the design of the primers. The PCR amplification trials done on the template DNA and building blocks showed 
that primers can have a significant impact on the amplification of the desired bands and the formation of 
unspecific bands or primer dimers. The primers designed in this thesis were based on the genome sequences 
of A. oryzae, hence, no codon optimization was required, however, the primer overhangs for SP sequences 
were not checked for any dimer formation, which might explain some of the short-sequenced unspecific bands 
observed in the gel results (Figures 18). Besides primer design, the determination of the optimal PCR settings 
was observed to be a challenging task that could undermine the implementation of high-throughput systems. 
Hence, despite knowing the facts of the designed primers, it would be optimal to apply an automated 
experimental design prior to the amplification of the bands to speed up the identification of optimal PCR 
settings. 
 
Another aspect to take into account would be DNA precipitation. Implementation of the NaCl precipitation 
strategy showed good results in the Nanodrop measurements. However, the observed concentration levels do 
not correspond with the strength of the gel band results (Figure 20). This can be due to the absence of 
precipitating agents such as isopropanol, which play an important role in facilitating the clumping of DNA 
during the precipitation procedure. In fact, the validity of this assumption was also supported by the 
isopropanol precipitation results, which showed a more associable relation between the Nanodrop 
concentrations and gel bands (Figure 21). This emphasized that implementing an experimental design 
mediated by automation systems such as Flowbot ONE could assist in the determination of the optimal 
precipitation strategy and make the experimental setup more suitable for digitalization approaches.  
 
Besides PCR and precipitation, the in vivo assembly approach is also a critical aspect to consider. 
Implementation of this approach in organisms like Saccharomyces cerevisiae, which are naturally talented in 
homologous recombination, was shown to be an efficient strategy for heterologous expression. Nevertheless, 
utilization of this approach in filamentous fungi species was documented to be a more difficult process even 
when the strain was optimized for the homologous recombination mechanism (Δku70). According to 
experimental results listed in Chapter 2.2.4, long homology arms (> 100 bases) can increase the efficiency of 
homologous recombination in filamentous fungi, whereas short homology arms (< 25) can lead to undesired 
background constructs. Considering that the homology arm length used between SPs in this thesis was 18 
bases, the results obtained from fungal transformation could be considered to occur due to the unspecific 
construction of the cleavage site. As a possible solution, a fusion-PCR setup can be tested to construct the gene 
expression cassette by annealing the two building blocks before using it for transformation. As an alternative 
approach, the Flowbot ONE setup could be used to test protoplast transformation with different homology arm 
lengths to determine the optimal base length for A. oryzae.

7. Conclusion 
The results achieved in this thesis emphasized major challenges in dry lab and wet lab procedures that can 
undermine the implementation of machine learning-guided engineering of SPs with an in vivo engineering 
strategy. Due to the amount of troubleshooting encountered within the given time period, it was not feasible to 
investigate the implementation of automation systems. Nevertheless, it was still possible to gather information 
for answering the research questions:  
 
● Can we use SignalP and DeepLoc to capture the underlying features of signal peptides?  
● Can we use the H2O AutoML platform to predict novel signal peptide structures? 
● Can we use in vivo assembly to construct signal peptides in a high-throughput manner? 
 
The results obtained in dry lab analysis showed that the SignalP 6.0 and DeepLoc 2.0 tools are capable to 
predict SP structures and extracellular subcellular localization. Despite their ability, the tools showed poor 
prediction performance at capturing the SP features observed in experimental and predicted results (Figures 
14 and 15). Hence, the results obtained failed to achieve the desired results of the initial research question. 
However, the difference observed between the proteomics, SignalP 6.0, and DeepLoc 2.0 results showed 
potentially undiscovered features to be investigated. 
 
In a similar way, the application of the H2O AutoML approach did not yield the expected results as the training 
of supervised machine learning algorithms based on the amino acid sequence and peptide abundances resulted 
in poor prediction results (Table 5). Hence, the best algorithms determined from AutoML training were not 
able to capture the underlying SP features generated and thus, failed to generate novel SP structures, as 
illustrated in Figure 17. Despite the outcome, the AutoML approach demonstrated which supervised machine 
learning algorithms would be best suitable for SP predictions and emphasized the importance of data 
tokenization in machine learning approaches. 
 
Overall, although it was not possible to investigate automation systems, the thesis project was able to bring 
forth multiple major challenges that can potentially undermine the implementation of machine learning-guided 
engineering for fugal cell factories in a high throughput manner.

8. Future perspectives 
Considering the challenging circumstances encountered through the thesis, numerous lessons and aspects have 
been determined for further analysis, which can be summarized under the statements; data encryption, media 
analysis, homology arm for in vivo assembly, fusion-PCR setup, integration of automation systems, and high-
performance computing approach. 
 
Tokenization of the data is an important encryption step that can play a critical role in machine learning models 
to capture existing patterns. The results obtained without applying any tokenization failed to yield promising 
results, as demonstrated by the position weight matrix generated based on the H2O AutoML model predictions 
(Figure 17). Hence, it would be interesting to investigate the influence of different tokenization approaches, 
such as one-hot-encoding, on the prediction performance of the models determined through H2O AutoML 
training. Besides tokenization, considering the results observed from data analysis, an investigation of the SP 
profile observed under several media compositions can provide more insight regarding the influence of media 
compositions. In addition, generating data for this approach can also aid the analysis of peptide abundances, 
SignalP 6.0, and DeepLoc 2.0 predictions by increasing the accessible data size and diversity. 
 
Furthermore, exploration of the homologous recombination approach tested in this thesis with different 
homology arm lengths between SPs can help to understand the influence of homology length on the in vivo 
assembly approach in A. oryzae ORY7. In return, this can bring forth novel genetic optimization targets for 
achieving an optimal fungal cell factory. Additionally, testing the application of the fusion-PCR approach 
would also be interesting to observe the change in positive transformation efficiency with respect to the in vivo 
assembly approach. Investigation of the two aforementioned wet lab approaches can help determine the 
optimal transformation strategy that can be integrated with automation systems to achieve an efficient cell 
factory engineering platform. 
 
Moreover, once a successful wet lab trial is achieved, investigating the application of automation systems such 
as Flowbot ONE and qPix can demonstrate the effectiveness of the optimization approaches determined in wet 
lab trials. In addition, this approach will allow the investigation of biological challenges such as sporulation 
that may arise once integrated with automation systems. Finally, it would also be optimal to investigate the 
application of high-performance computing approaches to achieve a server-based machine learning strategy. 
Integration of a high-performance computing approach can provide access to high computation power, parallel 
processing, and memory resources for the investigation of large datasets. Hence, considering the amount of 
time spent to run the H2O AutoML models and artificial SP generation algorithm, the utilization of a high-
performance computation approach can help to improve the efficiency of data analysis and machine learning 
training.
