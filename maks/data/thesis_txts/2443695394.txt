Abstract
This thesis concerns a kinetic characterization of tryptophan hydroxylase isoforms. Tryptophan
hydroxylase (TPH) is the enzyme catalysing the hydroxylation of L-tryptophan (L-Trp) into 5-L-
hydroxytryptophan (5-HTP), which is the ﬁrst and rate limiting step in the biosynthesis of serotonin.
Serotonin is associated with diseases as depression and schizophrenia, which causes TPH to be a
potential therapeutic target. It is therefore important to characterize TPH, which due to insta-
bility of the enzyme is not fully characterized yet. The hydroxylation of L-Trp requires Fe(II) as
co-factor, and O2 and tetrahydrobioterin (BH4) as co-substrates. TPH exists in two homologous
isoforms, TPH1 and TPH2, of which TPH1 is found to be substrate inhibited while TPH2 is not.
Both isoforms exist as homotetramers, with each monomer consisting of a regulatory domain (r), a
catalytic domain (c), and a tetramerization domain (t). In this project, the kinetics of humanTPH1
(hTPH1) and humanTPH2 (hTPH2) were investigated using both in-silico- and in-vitro methods.
3 truncation variants of hTPH2 were characterized in-vitro: chTPH2, cthTPH2, and N∆47-rchTPH2.
All 3 variants were puriﬁed to homogeneity, and the stability of the variants were assessed by com-
paring the yield from puriﬁcations and the loss of activity upon incubation at 30 ◦C. Additionally,
analytical size exclusion chromatography was used to determine the oligomeric state of cthTPH2.
Apparent kinetic parameters were determined for each variant with L-Trp or BH4 as the only
varying substrate, to elucidate inﬂuences of the regulatory domain and tetramerization domain on
the kinetic parameters. This was done using a continuous ﬂuorescent assay to determine initial
rates of 5-HTP formation.
Km,BH4 values were found to be similar for all truncation variants,
whereas Km,L−Trp was found to be slightly larger for cthTPH2 compared to the two other variants.
kcat,L−Trp values were found to be slightly larger for cthTPH2 than N∆47-rchTPH2, while smaller
kcat,L−Trp values were found for chTPH2 compared to both previously mentioned variants. kcat,BH4
values were found to be similar for cthTPH2 and N∆47-rchTPH2, while smaller kcat,BH4 values
were determined for chTPH2.
Recently, an isoform-speciﬁc inhibition mechanism for hTPH1 has been published in which a tyro-
sine residue in the binding pocket of BH4 ”ﬂips” to block the entrance of the BH4 binding pocket
of hTPH1 upon binding of L-Trp as the ﬁrst substrate. This inhibition mechanism, that is not ob-
served in TPH2, was further investigated, by analysis of molecular dynamics simulations of chTPH1
and chTPH2, both with L-Trp and Fe(II) bound in the active site. The aim was to identify struc-
tural diﬀerences between the two isoforms, resulting in inhibition only for chTPH1 and to identify
a hydrogen bonding network behind the inhibition mechanism. Residue Thr368 in chTPH1 and
the corresponding Thr414 in chTPH2 were found to reside in very diﬀerent conformations in the
two isoforms, which based on the analysis is believed to be important for the diﬀerence in inhibi-
tion. However, the input structure for the simulation of chTPH2 was of low quality in the area of
interest, and further validation is therefore needed to substantiate the observations. Results from
the analysis of the chTPH1 simulation were in agreement with a mechanism in which Thr367 has
a central role in the substrate inhibition of chTPH1 by formation of a hydrogen bond to L-Trp.
Further studies are however needed, to fully elucidate the hydrogen bonding network behind the
inhibition mechanism.

Dansk resum´e
Dette projekt omhandler kinetisk karakterisering af tryptophanhydroxylase isoformer. Tryptophan
hydroxylase (TPH) er et ensym der katalyserer hydroxyleringen af L-tryptophan (L-Trp) til 5-
hydroxy-tryptophan (5-HTP), som er det første og hastighedsbestemmende trin i biosyntesen af
serotonin. Serotonin er forbundet med sygdomme som depression of skizofreni, hvilket medfører
at TPH er et potentielt terapeutisk ”target”.
Det er derfor vigtigt at karakterisere TPH, som
p˚a grund af ustabilitet af enzymet endnu ikke er fuldt karakteriseret. For at hydroxylere L-Trp
bruger TPH Fe(II) som co-faktor og tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) og O2 som co-substrater.
Der
ﬁndes to isoformer af TPH, TPH1 og TPH2, hvoraf kun TPH1 er substrat inhiberet. Begge iso-
former er homo-tetramerer med hver monomer best˚aende af et regulatorisk- (r), et katalytisk- (c)
og et tetrameriserings-domæne (t). I dette projekt er de kinetiske egenskaber af TPH1 og TPH2
undersøgt ved brug af b˚ade in-silico og in-vitro metoder.
3 hTPH2 trunkerings varianter blev karakteriseret in-vitro: chTPH2, cthTPH2 og N∆47-rchTPH2.
Alle 3 varianter blev oprenset til homogenitet og stabiliteten af varianterne blev vurderet ved at sam-
menligne udbyttet af oprensingerne samt tabet af stabilitet n˚ar varianterne blev inkuberet ved 30
◦C. Det oligomeriske stadie af cthTPH2 blev desuden undersøgt ved brug af analytisk gelﬁltrering.
Kinetiske parametre blev bestemt for alle varianter med enten L-Trp eller BH4 som det varierende
substrat. Dette blev gjort ved brug af et ﬂuoressens assay til at m˚ale start hastigheder for dannelsen
af 5-HTP. Km,BH4 værdier blev bestemt til at være ens for alle 3 varianter, mens Km,L−Trp værdien
for cthTPH2 blev bestemt til at værre større end for de andre varianter. kcat,L−Trp var en smugle
større for cthTPH2 end N∆47-rchTPH2, mens kcat,L−Trp var mindre for chTPH2 sammenlignet
med de to først nævnte varianter. Mindre kcat,BH4 værdier blev ligeledes bestemt for chTPH2 sam-
menlignet med chTPH2 og N∆47-rchTPH2.
For nyligt blev en isoform-speciﬁk inhiberings mekanisme for hTPH1 publiceret, hvor en aminosyre
nær det aktivte site drejer sig og derved blokerer indgangen til bindingslommen for BH4 n˚ar L-Trp
binder som første substrat. Denne mekanisme blev undersøgt nærmere ved at analysere molecular
dynamics simuleringer af chTPH1 og chTPH2 med L-Trp og Fe(II) bundet i det aktive site. M˚alet
var at identiﬁcere strukturelle forskelle der leder til at kun TPH1 er inhiberet af L-Trp og at identiﬁ-
cere et hydrogenbindingsnetværk der leder til mekanismen. Analysen viste, at der er stor forskel p˚a
konformationerne af aminosyre Thr368 i chTPH1 sammenlignet med tilsvarende aminosyre Thr414
i chTPH2. Baseret p˚a analysen af MD simuleringerne er det vurderet at disse forskelle har betyd-
ning for at kun TPH1 er substrat inhiberet. Ved at undersøge krystalstrukturerne der blev brugt
som input til MD simuleringerne, blev det imidlertid fundet at strukturen af chTPH2 var af d˚arlig
opløsning i det relevante omr˚ade og validering af observationerne er derfor nødvendigt. Analysen
af chTPH1 simuleringen underbygger en mekanisme hvor Thr367 har en central rolle i substrat
inhiberingen af TPH1 ved at danne en hydrogenbinding til L-Trp.

Introduction
This project concerns tryptophan hydroxylase which is the enzyme catalysing the ﬁrst and rate
limiting step in the biosynthesis of serotonin. In the reaction L-Tryptophan (L-Trp) is hydroxylated
by TPH to yield 5-L-Hydroxytryptophan (5-HTP). 5-HTP is further decarboxylated by aromatic
amino acid decarboxylase (AADC) into 5-hydroxytryptamine (serotonin) as illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1
[1].
Figure 1.1: Hydroxylation of L-tryptophan in the biosynthesis of serotonin.
Dysfunctional levels of serotonin is associated with diseases as e.g. depression, which it is estimated
that more than 300 million people world wide suﬀers from [2, 3]. This makes tryptophan hydroxy-
lase an important enzyme in the search for better therapeutics. However, due to the low stability
of the enzyme, characterization and crystallization remains challenging.
To hydroxylate L-Trp, TPH uses Fe(II) as co-factor and tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) and molec-
ular oxygen as co-substrates [1]. There are two genes encoding two diﬀerent isoforms: TPH1 and
TPH2, of which TPH1 regulates the serotonin level in the peripheral system while TPH2 regulates
the serotonin level in the central nervous system [1].
TPH1 and TPH2 both consists of 3 domains: A regulatory domain (r), a catalytic domain (c),
and a tetramerization domain (t) [4]. The catalytic domain of the two isoforms is highly conserved,
while the regulatory domains vary both in sequence and in length [1]. Despite the high identity of
the catalytic domains, variations in kinetics between the isoforms are still observed. An important
diﬀerence is that TPH1 is substrate inhibited by L-Trp while TPH2 is not [5, 6]. What mediates
this diﬀerence is not fully understood yet, however, an isoform speciﬁc substrate inhibition mech-
anism has recently been proposed in which a tyrosine residue in the binding pocket of BH4 ”ﬂips”
to hinder access when L-Trp binds as the ﬁrst substrate [6]. The scope of this project is to further
characterize the kinetics of both isoforms.

1.1
Project goal and project strategy
This project has two main objectives: To determine the inﬂuence of the regulatory domain and
tetramerization domain on kinetic parameters of humanTPH2 (hTPH2), and to investigate the
isoform-speciﬁc substrate inhibition of humanTPH1 (hTPH1). The inﬂuence of the regulatory do-
main and the tetramerization on the kinetic parameters will be investigated using in-vitro methods.
3 variants of hTPH2 will be under investigation: chTPH2, cthTPH2, and N∆47-rchTPH2. Thus,
the 3 variants allow for comparison of kinetic parameters of the isolated catalytic domain with
kinetic parameters when either the tetramerization domain or part of the regulatory domain is

Tryptophan hydroxylase and aromatic amino acid hydroxylases
Tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH) is a member of the family of aromatic amino acid hydroxylases
(AAAH) which also includes phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH) and tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) [4].
PAH hydroxylates phenylalanine (L-Phe) to yield tyrosine (L-Tyr), and TH hydroxylates L-Tyr to
yield dihydroxyphenylalanine [4]. To hydroxylate their respective amino acid the AAAH all require
iron (II) as co-factor and tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) and molecular oxygen as co-substrates. The
AAAH all exists as homotetramers with each monomer consisting of 3 domains [4]: A regulatory
domain, a catalytic domain, and a teramerization domain. The catalytic domain is highly homolo-
gous among the AAAH, while the regulatory domain is less homologous and vary in length [7]. The
tertiary structure of a monomer is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1a, and the diﬀerence in the length of the
primary structures is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1b. Due to the similarity of the AAAH, studies of PAH
and TH can help shed light on aspects of TPH that are not fully mapped yet. In the following, a
theoretical background for TPH is provided, supported by information on PAH and TH.

2.1
Structural aspects of TPH
2.1.1
Tetramerization domain
As the name suggests, the tetramerization domain is essential for the tetramer formation of the
AAAH. The tetrameric assembly of monomers is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.2. The tetramers are formed
due to an alpha helix in the tetramerization domain, which contains a 4,3-hydrophobic repeat [10].
The hydrophobic repeat causes one ﬂank of the helix to be highly hydrophobic, mediating the
formation of coiled-coil hydrophobic interactions between 4 tetramerization domains [10].

2.1.2
Regulatory domain
Due to instability of the regulatory domain of TPH, much of the knowledge of this domain is in-
ferred from studies of PAH and TH. Structures of PAH and TH show that their regulatory domains
contain an ACT motif (aspartate kinase - chorismate mutase - tyrA - motif) preceded by a ﬂexible
N-terminal tail [11, 12]. The ACT motif is deﬁned as a βαββαβ fold and enzymes containing the
domain are thought to share a common ligand binding mode in which ligands bind between two
ACT domains [13]. This has been observed in a structure hPAH, where two L-Phe ligands are
bound between two regulatory domains, illustrated in ﬁgure 2.3a, conﬁrming the existence of an
allosteric site in PAH. The binding of L-Phe in the regulatory domain activate PAH by movement
of the N-terminal tail, which otherwise partly occludes the active site [14], illustrated in ﬁgure 2.3b.
This is supported by kinetic studies showing a cooperative eﬀect of L-Phe [15]. Opposite to PAH,
isolated regulatory domains of TH forms dimers independent on its amino acid substrate [12, 16].
The presence of an ACT domain in TPH has not been conﬁrmed, since no structure is available
yet. However, it is recognized by Uniprot that both hTPH1 and hTPH2 contain an ACT motif
[17, 18]. The regulatory domain of hTPH2 contains an additional 46 residue in the N-terminal
domain compared to hTPH1. These additional residues are associated with instability, and various
N-terminal deletion mutants show increased expression levels of TPH2 [19]. Studies show, that
N∆47-rchTPH2 is stabilized by L-Phe [20]. Additionally, N∆47-rchTPH2 exists in a monomer-
dimer equilibrium which is shifted towards the dimeric state in presence of L-Phe [20], indicating
the presence of an allosteric site similar to PAH[20]. However, kinetic studies with full length TPH2
have not shown a cooperative eﬀect of L-Trp [21], which indicates that L-Trp might not be the
allosteric ligand. Studies with the isolated regulatory domain of TPH1 shows that it exists as a
stable dimer independent on presence of L-Phe, suggesting that this isoform does not contain an
allosteric site in the regulatory domain[22].

2.2
Catalytic mechanism
Studies related to the catalytic mechanism of the AAAH indicate that they share a common catalytic
mechanism [30, 31]. The mechanism can be divided into two parts: Formation of a reactive FeIVO
intermediate and hydroxylation of the amino acid by FeIVO [31]. Little is known about the formation
of the FeIVO intermediate, but the formation of a FeII-peroxypterin intermediate is suggested [31].
The mechanism of FeIVO formation is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.8. FeII reacts with oxygen and BH4
to form the FeII-peroxypterin intermediate, resulting in the formation of FeIVO and 4a-hydroxy-
BH4. 4a-hydroxy-BH4 is subsequently condensed into quinonoid dihydrobipterin (qBH2), which is
enzymatically regenerated by dihydropterine reductase (DHPR) to BH4 [32]. If not regenerated,
qBH2 will rearrange to form dihydrobipterin (BH2). In absence of FeII, BH4 will autooxidate to
form qBH2 [33]. In the laboratory, DL-Dithiothreitol (DTT), can be used to reduce qBH2 to BH4
and thus mimic the function of DHPR.

2.3
Kinetics of TPH
Despite of the homologous catalytic domains of TPH1 and TPH2, they do not share all kinetic
properties. Km values for L-Trp (Km,L−Trp) have been reported in similar ranges for both isoforms
while larger Km values of BH4 (Km,BH4) have been reported for TPH1 relative to TPH2. Thus,
Km,L−Trp have been reported in the range of 11-48 µM for TPH1 variants [5, 6, 21, 26, 34–37] and
in the range of 13-77 µM for TPH2 variants [5, 6, 21, 25, 38–42]. Km,BH4 for TPH1 variants have
been reported in the range of 27-315 µM [5, 6, 21, 26, 36, 37] while Km,BH4 for TPH2 variants have
been reported in the range of 6-68 µM [5, 6, 21, 25, 38, 41–43] The large span of Km values is likely
a reﬂection of the diﬀerent assay types and conditions used to determine kinetic parameters in the
various studies of TPH.
2.3.1
Substrate inhibition
A notable diﬀerence in the kinetic properties is that hTPH1 is substrate inhibited by L-Trp, while
hTPH2 is not [5, 6]. Though, inhibition of hTPH2 have been reported with Ki values of 418±107
µM and 970±328 µM [21, 38]. To compare, inhibtion constants of hTPH1 have been reported in
the range of 72-385 µM [5, 6, 21]. The inhibition of hTPH1 by L-Trp have been proposed to be
mediated by ”ﬂipping” of Tyr235 when L-Trp binds. This causes that the entrance to the BH4
binding pocket to be partly closed [6]. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.9.
The active site loop comprising residues 124-139 in hTPH1 and 170-185 in hTPH2 resides in diﬀer-
ent conformations in the two isoforms [6]. Notable is that the side chain of Tyr125 (hTPH1) faces
in towards the BH4 binding pocket, while the corresponding Tyr171 (hTPH2) faces outwards. This
is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.10. This causes Tyr125 to be able to form interactions with Tyr235, and
therefore might be involved in the ”ﬂipping” of Tyr235 in hTPH1, while the corresponding interac-
tion between Tyr171 and Tyr281 in hTPH2 is not possible. By mutagenesis studies with residues in
this loop it has been shown that the loop is indeed implicated in the diﬀerence in inhibition between
the two isoforms [6].

Experimental theory
In this chapter, theory relevant for experimental methods used in the project is outlined. It concerns
the following methods: Chromatography, SDS-PAGE analysis, and a continuous ﬂuorescent assay.
4.1
Chromatography
Characterizing proteins ﬁrst requires obtaining protein in suﬃcient purity and quantity. The most
common methods today, all involve chromatography [61]. Chromatographic methods separates on
the basis of diﬀerences in properties between the protein of interest (target protein) and other
proteins and molecules present in the sample [61]. Thus, developing a good puriﬁcation strategy
requires some knowledge of the protein of interest. Common chromatographic methods include,
ion exchange chromatography, aﬃnity chromatography, gel ﬁltration, and hydrophobic interaction
chromatography [61]. In this project, hTPH2 variants are puriﬁed using a two step puriﬁcation
procedure with aﬃnity chromatography followed by gel ﬁltration.
4.1.1
Aﬃnity chromatography
Aﬃnity chromatography relies on a reversible interaction between a protein and ligand [62]. The
method is very selective and has high capacity, and is therefore often used as a capture step [62].
The method can be used by tagging the target protein with an aﬃnity tag as e.g. maltose binding
protein (MBP), histidine, or gluthathione S-Transferase [62]. Running a sample on the column with
favourable conditions for protein-ligand interactions, will cause the tagged target protein to bind to
the column, while other materials are washed away. Switching to unfavourable binding conditions
then causes the tagged target protein to elute from the column [62].
4.1.2
Aﬃnity chromatography with MBP-tags
In this project, hTPH2 variants are expressed as MBP-tagged proteins. The MBP-tag does not only
allow for high purity in a single step, but often also increases the solubility and expression of the
protein [62]. MBP has high aﬃnity for dextrins, and MBP or MBP-tagged proteins therefore bind
to Dextrin Sepharose columns. Since the aﬃnity of MBP for maltose is larger than for dextrins,
running a maltose containing buﬀer through the column causes MBP to elute [62].
Proteases can be used to obtain the non-tagged protein by cleavage of a special recognition site
sequence. In this project, human Rhinovirus 3C (3CP) is used. It recognizes the amino acid se-
quence: LEVLFQ↓GP, thus cleaving between the Q and P amino acid [63]. Therefore, hTPH2
variants in the project are expressed as MBP-linker-LEVLFQGP-TPH2 and all the variants con-
sequently contain additional GP amino acids at the N-terminal after cleavage.
To simplify the
puriﬁcation procedure, the protease is tagged with an MBP-tag as well, allowing for on-column
cleavage.
4.1.3
Gel ﬁltration
Gel ﬁltration, also called size exclusion chromatography, is used to separate molecules on the basis
of size [64]. The basis of the method is, that a porous column media causes a larger column volume
to be available for small molecules than large molecules, and larger molecules therefore elute ﬁrst
17
==PAGE:36==
from the column [65]. It is the pore size distribution that determines which range of molecular
weights that can be separated on a given column[64].
A huge advantage of this method, is that no interactions occur between the column media and
protein [64]. Therefore, the liquid phase can be chosen to provide the most suitable conditions for
the protein of interest without interfering with the column resolution [64]. Unlike aﬃnity chro-
matography, gel ﬁltration columns has low capacity and gel ﬁltration is consequently often used as
a polishing step [61].
Analytical gel ﬁltration relies on the exact same principles. However, using a column with small par-
ticle diameters and a uniform particle size distribution results in a resolution suitable for analytical
purposes [64]. Creating a calibration curve by correlating molecules of known molecular weight to
the retention volume can be used to determine the unknown molecular weight of an eluting protein.
However, molecular weight of the protein is not directly linked to the retention time on the column,
which is more accurately described by hydrodynamic radii [65].
4.2
Sodium dodecyl sulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE)
SDS-PAGE is an analytical method often used to determine the molecular weight of proteins and
the purity of a protein puriﬁcation [66]. The method relies on the principle that a protein with a net
charge will move in an electric ﬁeld [67]. The proteins run through a polyacrylamide gel, which is a
porous material causing smaller molecules to run faster through the gel than larger molecules.[67].
To separate only on the basis of size, all proteins in the sample must be of equal charge to size
ratio. This is achieved, by treating the protein sample with sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS) and
mercaptoethanol. This causes the proteins to unfold, and SDS-anions bind to approximately every
other amino acid, resulting in the negative charge of the Protein-SDS complex to be more or less
proportional to the weight of the protein [67].
4.3
Continuous ﬂuorescent assay for tryptophan hydroxylase
To determine the kinetic parameters for TPH2, a continuous ﬂuorescent assay as described by Moran
et al. [33] is used. In the assay, initial rates are measured by the increase in ﬂuorescence at 330 nm
upon product formation. It relies on the diﬀerent absorbance and emmission spectra of tryptophan
and 5-HTP, allowing the production of 5-HTP to be monitored selectively at 330 nm by excitation
at 300 nm. This diﬀerence in absorbance properties is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1a.
To enhance the sensitivity and stability of the assay, several components are added [33]: (NH4)2Fe(II)-

All chemicals used were of analytical grade and solutions were prepared using 18.2 MΩ·cm water

making a linear ﬁt for an interval of 0.04 min of the curve starting as close to the addition of BH4
as possible but avoiding noise. The slope of the linear ﬁt is the initial rate. This is illustrated in

ensure that the trend would not be aﬀected by e.g. day to day errors.

6.6.6
Standard curves
To convert the unit of initial rates from AU/min to µM/min and to correct for diﬀerent amount
of quenching at diﬀerent concentrations of BH4, a standard curve at each BH4 concentration was
made. This was done by measuring the ﬂuorescence of 5 diﬀerent concentrations of 5-HTP at each

mM Hepes, 200 mM (NH4)2SO4, pH 7 and signals were measured at 15 ◦C. Standard curves for
[BH4]<100 µM were measured using a PMT V of 600 V and for [BH4]≥100 µM a PMT V of 700
V. The standard curves are provided in appendix C.

Trouble shooting deviations and adjusting settings
From the initial observations, 3 problems were detected: Low initial rate, low base line, and too ﬂat

was slightly degraded, as a small amount of NH3 was present in this sample. The second sample did
not contain NH3, but leaving the sample exposed to air over night caused NH3 to be detectable in
the spectrum. Thus, the second batch of BH4 was likely to contain fewer impurities. However, no
signiﬁcant diﬀerence was observed when measuring standard curves with one batch relative to the
other. It is therefore assumed that it is not likely the BH4 batch is oxidized signiﬁcantly. Using a
diﬀerent TPH2 variant (cthTPH2) resulted in larger initial rates, even though cthTPH2 have been
reported to result in smaller initial rates than chTPH2 [48], indicating something might be wrong
with the chTPH2 variant. However, the rates of cthTPH2 were found to be 3-fold smaller than
previously [48].
7.4
Conclusion on trouble shooting
As moving the sample holder only ﬁxed the baseline problem, initial rates for chTPH2 were still
very small. To ensure the signal would be measurable even with low concentrations of L-Trp, it
was therefore decided to run experiments with a PMT V of 700 instead of 650 as previously. Other
options had been to increase the concentration of protein or increase the slid width on the machine,
however, this would require too much protein or result in too much noise, respectively. Due to time
restrictions, no conclusion was made as to what continued to cause trouble with initial rates and
standard curves.

8.4.1
Oxygen concentration during kinetic experiments

created. Thus, each box plot is constructed from around 30 data points. The box plots are shown

possible to compare the data from the variants directly. It should be noted, that the spreads of the
box plots can not be fully ascribed to diﬀerences in oxygen concentration, as experimental errors,
e.g. the precision of the oxygen electrode, contribute as well. Previously, kinetic parameters have
been determined using an O2 concentration of 500 µM. In this project the overall average oxygen
concentration was 309 µM. Assessing the inﬂuence of oxygen concentration determined by [48], the
diﬀerence in oxygen results in only around 15% lower activity, see ﬁgure 8.13. Thus, kcat values of
L-Trp and BH4 will be expected to be a bit lower than those determined with an oxygen level of

Molecular dynamics simulation study of chTPH1 and chTPH2
Tidemand et al.
published in 2017 an hTPH1 speciﬁc inhibition mechanism, in which Tyr235
”ﬂips” after binding of L-Trp and causes a blocking of the entrance to the BH4 binding pocket [6].
It was further identiﬁed, that Tyr125, which π-stacks onto Tyr235, forms an H-bond to Thr367
upon binding of L-Trp [79]. The following mechanism was proposed [79]: Binding of L-Trp pulls the
Thr367 loop towards the active site, causing formation of a hydrogen bond to Tyr125. Movement
of Tyr125 then causes Tyr235 to move as well [79]. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 9.1. Thus, the active
site blockage is possibly caused by two things: Closing of the Thr367 loop and ”ﬂipping” of Tyr235.
Figure 9.1: Figure taken from [79].
Illustration of closing of the BH4 binding pocket upon
binding of L-Trp in chTPH1. L-Trp is coloured in pink. Loops coloured in green are from a
simulation without L-Trp and loops coloured in blue are from a simulation with L-Trp.
The purpose of this chapter, is to further elucidate the isoform-speciﬁc inhibition of hTPH1. The
chapter concerns an analysis of MD simulation data behind the paper from Tidemand et al., as well
as an additional MD simulation 4. The focus on the analysis will be the loop containing Thr367, thus
throughout the analysis ”loop closing” refers to the motion of this loop towards L-Trp. The goal is
to identify what initiates the closing of the loop and to ﬁnd structural diﬀerences between hTPH1
and hTPH2 leading to closing of the loop only in hTPH1. The MD simulations used for analysis
are of chTPH1 with Fe(II) and L-Trp and chTPH2 with Fe(II) and L-Trp, from now on referred to
as the simulation of chTPH1 and chTPH2. The ﬁrst part of the chapter concerns an analysis of the
diﬀerences between the simulation of chTPH1 and chTPH2, with emphasis on the interactions of
L-Trp, leading to an assessment of conformational diﬀerences between the active site loop of the two
isoforms. The second part contains a more in-depth analysis of the loop closing in chTPH1 with
emphasis on the interactions of Thr367 with nearby residues throughout the simulation. To facilitate
reading of the chapter, conserved residues between TPH1 and TPH2 will be written RES111/222,
with the residue name and number for chTPH1 ﬁrst, and the corresponding residue number in
chTPH2 after the slash. A sequential alignment of hTPH1 and hTPH2 is provided in appendix F.

Glu340/Glu386
Diﬀerent from the simulation of chTPH1, L-Trp does not bind to Glu386 in the simulation of
chTPH2. Therefore, interactions of Glu340/386 with other amino acids throughout the MD sim-
ulation were analyzed. It was found that for chTPH1, Glu340 interacts with Tyr373, Ser337, and

all conserved and therefore, these distances were measured throughout the simulation to map the
diﬀerences of the 2 isoforms. The results are provided in ﬁgure 9.7.
The most striking diﬀerence is the distance between the carboxyllic oxygen of Glu340/386 and the
side chain OH of Thr368/414 in ﬁgure 9.7C. After around 12 ns in the TPH2 simulation, a strong
interaction appears to be formed between Glu386 and Thr414, seen by the narrow distribution of
distances around 2 ˚A. In the structural alignment, ﬁgure 9.7A, it is seen how the corresponding
Thr368 in chTPH1 faces in the opposite direction relative to Thr414 in chTPH2, thus, preventing
the formation of an interaction between Thr368 and Glu340. Thr368/414 is positioned in the active
site loop which closes in the simulation of chTPH1, by binding of Thr367 to L-Trp. The bond
between Thr414 and Glu386 could therefore prevent the loop from closing in chTPH2, and thus
also prevent inhibition. Interestingly, the distance between Thr368/414 Cα and Glu340/386 Cδ
does not change signiﬁcantly for either chTPH1 or chTPH2, seen in ﬁgure 9.7F, but the distance
distribution is more narrow for chTPH2 indicating less ﬂexibility for this loop.
The distance between the OH group of Tyr373/419 and the carboxyllic oxygen of Glu340/386
in ﬁgure 9.7B is very consistent throughout the simulation, with a narrow distribution for both
chTPH1 and chTPH2. For chTPH1, there is a bump in the distance between 30 and 40 ns. This
is merely caused by ﬂipping of the glutamic acid oxygens. This strong interaction indicates that it
acts as an anchor between the ﬂexible loop and the alpha helix containing Glu, to retain the tertiary
conformation.

From the loop alignment in ﬁgure 9.8, it is seen how the backbone before Thr367/413 and after
Thr368/414 are very similar with a good alignment of the side chains as well, whereas the diﬀerence
in conformation of Thr368 and Thr414 is obvious. Furthermore, it is noticed that a cis-peptide
bond is present between Thr414 and Phe415 in chTPH2 which is in trans-conformation in chTPH1
(Thr368-Phe369). Due to higher-energy for cis-peptide bonds than trans peptide bonds, cis-peptides
occur far less frequently. In fact non-proline cis-peptides have been found in less than 0.05% of all
peptide bonds in PDB [85]. Studies showed, that non-proline cis-peptides are occurring and often
involved in ligand binding properties of proteins [86]. It is therefore a possibility that a cis-bond
has been introduced to elude the inhibition mechanism observed in hTPH1.
To determine if it is possible to make conclusions based on conformational diﬀerences in this loop,
and thus if the cis-bond is plausible, the quality of the 2 structures were assessed. First, the res-
olution of the chTPH1 structure (PDB ID: 1MLW) is 1.71 ˚A, while the resolution of the chTPH2
structure (PDB ID: 4V06) is only 2.63 ˚A, meaning that the quality of the chTPH1 structure is supe-
rior to the chTPH2 structure. Assessing the electron density maps of both structures, see ﬁgure 9.9,
it is seen how the cis-bond in chTPH2 is placed at the edge of the electron density map in this area.
It appears, that the electron density map around Thr414 is big enough for the peptide bond to be in

trans-conformation as well, thus, lowering the conﬁdence of the cis-peptide bond. Additionally, it is
seen that the oxygen atom of the cis-bond and the side chain of Thr414 are protruding the density
map, further lowering the conﬁdence of the structure. For chTPH1, ﬁgure 9.9a, the main part of
the loop is well ﬁt within the density map. However, Thr367 and Ile366 protrudes the density map,

Since the loops are subjected to simulation, and thus energy minimization, backbone phi-psi angles
will be changed slightly (especially in a ﬂexible loop region), and small deviations from the ”true”
backbone angles of the protein are not critical for the input structure in an MD simulation. The cis-
or trans-conformation of the bonds are less likely to be changed during simulation and are therefore
of greater importance. Thus the conclusion is, that MD simulation data of the loop comprising
Lys361-Val372 in chTPH1 can be analysed with conﬁdence, while MD simulation data of the loop
comprising Leu407-Ala418 in chTPH2 should be analysed with caution. The PDB structure 4V06
is to this date the only available crystal structure of TPH2. Therefore, the data presented in this
study is not conclusive and a structure of chTPH2 with a better resolved active site loop is needed,
before the MD analysis of chTPH2 and the presence of a cis-bond can be validated.

Overall discussion and future perspectives
Apparent steady-state kinetic parameters were determined for chTPH2, cthTPH2, and N∆47-
rchTPH2 to identify the inﬂuence of the regulatory domain and the tetramerization domain on
kinetic parameters. It was found that including the tetramerization domain results in an increase
in Km,L−Trp, while including the regulatory domain did not have any signiﬁcant eﬀect on Km val-
ues. kcat values were found to increase when either the regulatory or tetramerization domain was
present, but as discussed in section 8.4.5, this was very unexpected and is contributed to problems
with the catalytic variant. This study has only examined kinetic parameters of L-Trp and BH4,
and experiments with O2 as the varying substrate should therefore be carried out as well. cthTPH2
was in this project not found to be a stable tetramer, section 8.2, and N∆47-rchTPH2 exists in a
monomer-dimer equilibrium [20]. To fully characterize the inﬂuence of the regulatory and tetramer-
ization domain, experiments should therefore be conducted with N∆47-hTPH2, which is expected
to be in the native tetrameric form. This would be particularly interesting for investigating the
inﬂuence of the regulatory domain, since the regulatory mechanism of PAH is reported to require
tetrameric assembly [14]. Ideally, experiments should be conducted with the full length enzyme,
but stable conditions need to be found for the regulatory domain before it is possible to conduct
reliable kinetic studies with this variant. Nevertheless, an eﬀort should be made to obtain stable
conditions of the full length enzyme, as it would not only allow for conducting kinetic studies, but
additionally facilitate characterization of the enzyme in general.
An MD simulation study was conducted to investigate the isoform-speciﬁc inhibition mechanism of
hTPH1. Comparing a simulation of chTPH1 with Fe(II) and L-Trp and chTPH2 with Fe(II) and
L-Trp, a signiﬁcant conformational diﬀerence was identiﬁed between residues Thr368 in hTPH1
and Thr414 in hTPH2, mediated by a cis-peptide bond between Thr414 and Phe415 in hTPH2.
This conformational diﬀerence likely causes interaction to be possible between Thr367 and L-Trp
in chTPH1 but not between the corresponding Thr413 and L-Trp in chTPH2.
This should be
investigated further by an MD-simulation of chTPH2 where the cis-bond is inverted to a trans-
bond, to see if interactions between Thr413 and L-Trp will occur and lead to loop closing. In any
case, statistical uncertainties related to both the simulation of chTPH1 and chTPH2 should be
assessed by conducting multiple MD simulation runs. The inhibition of chTPH1 was in found to
be initiated by interaction between Thr367 and L-Trp followed by formation of an H-bond between
Thr367 and Tyr125, in agreement with the mechanism proposed by Tidemand [79]. The estab-
lishment of the Thr367 L-Trp interaction was found to be facilitated by water bridges to Tyr125,
Asp130, and Pro266. This is however solely based on the MD-SID analysis in section 9.2.1, and
further studies are therefore needed. This could include simulation of chickenTPH1, to see if the
interactions are maintained between the two variants. Additionally, interactions of other residues
in the loop, i.e. Ile366 and Thr368, should be investigated to clarify if these residues contribute to
formation of the Thr367 - L-Trp interaction as well. The importance of Thr367 in the inhibition
mechanism of chTPH1 could be investigated further in-vitro by a kinetic mutagenesis study with
Thr367. Mutation to e.g. alanine would remove the possibility of side chain interaction to L-Trp,
and kinetic studies would reveal if the variant retained substrate inhibition or not. Finally, the
interplay between Thr367-Tyr125-Tyr235 should be analysed to further clarify the mechanism, es-
pecially since mutagenesis studies with Tyr235 have been reported to yield an hTPH1 variant that
does not display inhibition [37].

Conclusion
The scope of this project was to kinetically characterize hTPH isoforms. This was achieved using
both in-silico and in-vitro methods.
3 truncation variants of hTPH2 were characterized in-vitro: chTPH2, cthTPH2, and N∆47-rchTPH2.
All 3 variants were successfully puriﬁed to homogeneity using a two-step puriﬁcation procedure com-
prised of aﬃnity chromatography followed by gel ﬁltration. The oligomeric state of cthTPH2 was
additionally analysed by analytical gel ﬁltration, and a distribution between tetramers, trimers, and
dimers was found, with the largest fraction being trimeric. Within the concentration ranges 2.5-15
µM cthTPH2, the oligomeric state was not found to depend on concentration. The relative stability
of the variants was assessed by comparing the yield from puriﬁcations, and by comparing the loss of
activity upon incubation at 30 ◦C. This revealed, that cthTPH2 was signiﬁcantly more stable than
the two other variants. Furthermore, the stability of chTPH2 was found to be smaller than what
has previously been described by the group.
chTPH2, cthTPH2, and N∆47-rchTPH2 were subjected to steady state kinetic studies, with the
aim of identifying the inﬂuence of the regulatory domain and the tetramerization domain on the
kinetic parameters. Initial rates were determined using a continuous ﬂuorescent assay, and exper-
iments were conducted at varying substrate concentrations for L-Trp and BH4, while keeping the
concentration of BH4 and O2 or L-Trp and O2 ﬁxed, respectively. The data were ﬁtted to the simple
Michaelis-Menten equation to obtain apparent kinetic parameters for L-Trp and BH4. Km,L−Trp
was found to be slightly larger for cthTPH2 than for chTPH2 and N∆47-rchTPH2 in agreement
with previous studies, while similar Km,BH4 values were found for all variants. kcat,L−Trp values
for cthTPH2 was found to be slightly larger than for N∆47-rchTPH2, while kcat,BH4 values were
found to be similar for cthTPH2 and N∆47-rchTPH2. Both kcat,L−Trp and kcat,BH4 for chTPH2
was found to be signiﬁcanlty smaller than for the two other variants. Compared to the literature,
kcat values were expected to be largest for chTPH2. Along with a lower stability for chTPH2 than
expected, it is suspected that an unwanted point mutation may have occurred. This should be
checked by sequencing of the chTPH2 genes used for expression. Furthermore, additional kinetic
studies should be conducted with N∆47-hTPH2 to further characterize the inﬂuence of the regula-
tory domain and the tetramerization domain.
chTPH1 and chTPH2 were characterized in-silico by analysis of molecular dynamics simulations of
chTPH1 with Fe(II) and L-Trp and chTPH1 with Fe(II) and L-Trp. The aim was to ﬁnd structural
diﬀerences between the isoforms leading to substrate inhibition of only TPH1. Additionally, the
goal was to identify a hydrogen bonding network behind a proposed inhibition mechanism. An
active site loop residue, Thr368 in chTPH1 and the corresponding Thr414 in chTPH2, were found
to reside in diﬀerent conformations in the two isoforms, which based on the simulation analysis is
believed to be of importance to why only hTPH1 is substrate inhibited. However, the only available
crystal structure of chTPH2 (PDB ID: 4V06) was found to be ambiguous about the conformation of
Thr414, and a crystal structure with better resolution of the loop is therefore needed to validate the
observation. Thr367 was found to be central to the inhibition mechanism of chTPH1, and initiate
the mechanism by forming an H-bond to the aromatic part of L-Trp. The analysis indicated, that
formation of this bond was facilitated by initial water bridge interactions to Tyr125, Asp130, and
65
==PAGE:88==
Pro266, placing the side chain of Thr367 in a favourable position for interaction with L-Trp. Future
experiments could include mutagenesis experiments with Thr367 to investigate the importance of
the residue on substrate inhibition.

G
Molecular dynamics simulation details
This appendix contain an outline of the procedure used by Tidemand to conduct the MD simulations
of chTPH1 with L-Trp and Fe(II) and chTPH2 with L-Trp and Fe(II) which have been part of the
MD simulation study in this project. The detailed procedure is originally discribed in his Ph.D.
thesis [79].
G.1
MD input structure of chTPH1
A model of N∆90/C∆31-hTPH1 was build as a prime energy based model using 1MLW (chTPH1
structure) as the main template and 4V06, chain A (chTPH2 structure) to model residues in the C-
terminal which were not included in 1MLW. Residues GPDGME in the N-terminus were modeled.
Fe was included in the model and reduced from +III to +II in the model, and resolved water
molecules were retained. BH2 was removed from the structure. The model was prepared by Protein
preparation wizard in the Maestro suite, which was used to add hydrogen atoms, assign bond orders,
and delete overlapping water molecules. The model was reﬁned by iterative restrained minimization
(OPLS, 0.3˚A) and PropKa optimization at pH 8.0. L-Trp was introduced in the binding pocket by
superimposing with the structure of chickenTPH1 (3E2T). The ligand was prepared by charging the
amino group to +1 and carboxyl group to -1, and H-atoms were added using the protein preparation
wizard. The model was placed in an orthorombic box with 10˚A dimensions, using Desmond software.
0.3 M NaCl was added to the system and 10593 TIP3P water molecules.
G.2
MD input structure of chTPH2
A model of N∆145/C∆31-hTPH2 was build as a prime energy based model using 4V06 chain A
as template and modelling missing N-terminal residues by prime. The iron atom from 4V06 was
included and reduced from +III to +II. Resolved water molecules from 4V06 were retained. Water
molecules coordinated to the iron atom in 1MLW were superimposed into the structure. Protein
preparation wizard was used to add hydrogen atoms and assign bond orders. The model was reﬁned
by iterative restrained minimization (OPLS 3˚A) and PropKa optimization at pH 7.0. L-Trp was
added to the system in the same way as described for the chTPH1 input structure. The model was
placed in an orthorombic box with 10˚A dimensions, using Desmond software. 0.3 M NaCl and 6
additional sodium ions were added to the system along with 10176 TIP3P water molecules.
G.3
Simulation conditions
100 ns simulations were conducted with the Desmond Package using OPLS3 force-ﬁeld and periodic
boundary conditions in all 3 cartesian coordinates. Particle-mesh Ewald method was used to calcu-
late long-range electrostatic interactions. Short-range electrostatic interactions and van der Waals
interactions were smoothly truncated at 9 ˚A. An NPT ensemble was used with the temperature
set to 300 K controlled by a Nos´e-Hoover thermostat. The pressure was maintained at 1.01325
bar with a Martyna-Tobias-Klein barostat method. Desmond software used RESPA integrator to
advance the system in time, with a 2.0 fs time step. Conﬁgurations and energies were saved at 5 ps
intervals.
