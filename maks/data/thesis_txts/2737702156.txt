Abstract
Ensuring good indoor air quality (IAQ) is of utmost concern in hospitals. Exhaled air from a patient
with airborne infectious diseases can impose health risks on other patients, healthcare workers,
and visitors. Thus, this study’s main objective was to study how to ventilate general hospital pa­
tients’ rooms efficiently to provide good indoor air quality (IAQ) and low infection probability risk
from airborne respiratory diseases. The research was based on numerical modeling, experimen­
tal work, and energy simulations. The exposure risk of an exposed patient’s inhaled air quality
and the air quality of the entire room was investigated in a general two­bed patient room using dif­
ferent ventilation configurations, including displacement ventilation (DV), mixing ventilation (MV),
and localized exhaust ventilation (LEV). The most common and conservative ventilation method in
patients’ rooms is mixing ventilation. Displacement ventilation can, however, provide higher ven­
tilation effectiveness (VE) than mixing ventilation. LEV was used in combination with either DV
or MV. The effect of diffuser arrangement, the behavior of the infected patient in bed (i.e. his/her
position in bed, covered/not covered with a duvet), and the presence of a standing person was
examined. Energy simulations were used to examine the potential energy saving by using lower
than 4 ACH (recommended in present hospital standards) to save energy. Displacement ventila­
tion was found to improve air quality effectively, particularly in the breathing zones of the exposed
patient and healthcare personnel. The highest VE was achieved when a semicircular supply dif­
fuser was positioned close to the beds and patients’ heads. The results show that a localized
exhaust diffuser placed behind the infected patient combined with a general ventilation system
effectively minimized the spread of contaminants in the room. The exposure risk when using LEV
combined with DV at 1.5 ACH was lower than MV standalone at 3 ACH. The findings in this study
indicated that it is possible to use lower than the recommended 4 ACH to save energy without
compromising the IAQ. None of the ventilation configurations posed a risk of thermal discomfort.
Keywords: Indoor air quality, cross­infection, air distribution method, patients’ rooms.

1
Introduction
The transmission of respiratory diseases has been (and still is) a matter of utmost concern due
to their potential to cause massive outbreaks, as observed at least through the last decade. In
2003, the outbreak of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) caused a worldwide health
crisis (Kek et al., 2023) [1]. The influenza A (H1N1) pandemic in 2009 led to patterns of death and
illness not typically seen in influenza infections (World Health Organization) [2]. In 2012, the Middle
East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS­CoV) caused concerns due to its potential to cause severe
respiratory illness with high fatality rates [1]. The COVID­19 pandemic caused a profound and
unprecedented global impact due to the most recent outbreak of the SARS­CoV­2 virus in 2019
[1]. The World Health Organization (WHO) has reported more than 775 million COVID­19 cases
and more than 7 million deaths worldwide (World Health Organization) [3]. Lockdowns, social
distancing, quarantine, and testing were some of the means used to control the virus’s spread
[1]. The outbreaks have exemplified the fact that airborne transmission of infectious diseases is a
serious and lethal threat that may cause devastating social and economic disruptions in society.
1.1
Healthcare Facilities
Healthcare facilities are places where medical and surgical treatment and care are provided to in­
dividuals. They vary widely in the nature and complexity of services they provide and the patients
they treat ­ from general practitioners’ offices to more specialized places, such as hospitals and
medical centers (Koenigshofer, 2013) [4]. What they all have in common is that they are obligated
to provide a safe and healing environment for their patients. A crucial aspect of ensuring patient
safety is maintaining good indoor air quality (IAQ). Poor IAQ may affect the recovery of immuno­
compromised patients and contribute to hospital­acquired infections (HAIs) [1]. The concentration
of pollutants in indoor spaces is a substantial issue that affects patients’ health. Patients’ comfort
may also be negatively affected by uncomfortable IAQ, including certain odors and air dryness,
which can impact their satisfaction with the care they receive (Uścinowicz, 2023) [5]. It is also im­
portant to consider the safety and health of healthcare workers (HCWs). When medical treatment
or care is provided, HCWs often work near the patients, resulting in a higher risk of short­range
exposure to respiratory diseases.
Requirements related to the indoor environment quality (IEQ) in healthcare facilities is affected
by the complexity and nature of the services provided to patients. In that regard, HVAC systems
become more important with increasing complexity of the medical services provided and the acu­
ity of illness of the patient population [4]. The catastrophic number of casualties caused by the
worldwide outbreak of the SARS virus may have been the result of poor ventilation (Li et al., 2005)
[6], which signifies the importance of ventilation.
1.2
Cross­infection in Hospitals
Cross­infection refers to the process where infectious disease is transmitted between people, of­
ten facilitated by environmental factors within a shared space, such as hospital patient rooms
(Nielsen et al., 2012) [7]. Many studies have found that the main pollution source in multi­bed
patient rooms is one the patients themselves (Sheng et al., 2024; Su et al., 2024) [8][9]). In such
cases, other patients, healthcare personnel, and visitors are at risk of cross­infection.
Cross­infection can occur through:
• Contact Transmission: Direct physical interactions (e.g. medical examinations), or indirect
contact with contaminated objects, such as bed lines (Capital Region of Denmark) [10].

• Droplet Transmission: Infectious droplets are produced when coughing, sneezing, or talk­
ing. Typically transmitted at short distances [ibid].
• Airborne Transmission: Caused by pathogens that can be transmitted through the air, typi­
cally via small droplets or particles that can be inhaled by individuals [ibid].
1.2.1
Airborne Infections
Airborne transmission depends on the droplet size, and is usually divided into ’close contact com­
bined droplet and aerosol transmission’ and ’long­range aerosol transmission’. When an individ­
ual coughs, sneezes, talks, or breathes, they expel droplets that carry infectious agents. Larger
droplets (> 60 µm in diameter) typically fall and settle on nearby surfaces relatively quickly, leading
to what is known as short­range transmission. These droplets can range from less than 0.1 μm
to more than 100 μm in diameter and can contaminate surfaces or be directed to another person
through close contact (up to 1.5 ­ 2 meters). In contrast, smaller droplets (< 60 µm in diameter)
can undergo a process of desiccation. These smaller droplet nuclei have the potential to remain
suspended in the air for prolonged periods and can be carried over greater distances by airflow
(beyond 1.5 meters), enhancing the risk of what is known as long­range airborne transmission
(REHVA, 2021) [11].
Airborne transmission is considered the primary transmission route for COVID­19, especially in
enclosed, and poorly ventilated rooms or spaces. Direct contact and droplet transmissions can
also occur [11]. Since the first patient with COVID­19 was registered, the routes of transmission
have been subject of discussion; it was not until more than a year after the outbreak started that
the Danish authorities and WHO recognized this transmission route (Ugeskrift for Læger, 2023)
[12].
The SARS­CoV­2 virus can affect infected persons in many ways; the most common symptoms
include fever, chills, and sore throat. In more severe cases, it may cause severe respiratory illness
that can lead to death. A person can still get COVID­19 after vaccination ­ they are, however, more
likely to have mild or no symptoms (World Health Organization, 2023) [13].
The size of the coronavirus is around 80­160 nanometers, and the virus has demonstrated sub­
stantial aerosol and surface stability, indicating that it can spread not only through direct contact
and large respiratory droplets but also via smaller aerosols that can linger in the air and spread
over distances greater than typical face­to­face interactions [11]. Coronaviruses, including SARS­
CoV­2, are notoriously resilient to environmental changes. They are only susceptible to extremely
high relative humidity levels above 80 %RH and air temperatures above 30 °C [ibid] ­ conditions
that are outside the range of thermal comfort in any building environment.
1.2.1.1
Mitigation of airborne transmission
Several means can be used and implemented to mitigate the risk of airborne transmission, thus
ensuring a safe environment for patients, visitors, and healthcare workers. The REHVA guide
emphasizes a variety of strategies to reduce the transmission risks of COVID­19 within buildings.
As shown in Figure 1.1, a hierarchy of the different means is suggested. In this hierarchy, meth­
ods involving ventilation and other HVAC adjustments (engineering controls) are prioritized over
administrative controls and the use of personal protective equipment, such as face masks and
gloves, although it has been proven that certain face masks are highly effective. A study has
shown that Coronavirus was detected in respiratory droplets in 4 out of 10 (40 %) of the samples
collected without face masks. By wearing a surgical mask, Coronavirus was detected in 0 of 11
(1 %) of the samples collected (Leung et al., 2020) [14]. Despite the efficiency, some people may
experience severe physical or psychological discomfort when using a mask for a long time. Also,
it should be used properly, which include washing hands or using hand sanitizer before and after
use (Danish Health Authority, 2023) [15], which may be time­consuming when in a rush .

One approach is to increase the ACH to dilute infectious particles circulating in the rooms. How­
ever, higher ventilation rates can sometimes raise particle concentrations in occupied areas due
to undesirable airflow patterns, and it can potentially increase draught risks [11].
The air distribution method may have a substantial effect on the concentration of contaminants in
room air. It can remarkably reduce or increase concentrations locally. A strong directed airflow
toward an infected individual may carry contaminants toward a susceptible person in a very high
concentration, which may propagate the contaminant within a specific part of the room [ibid].
If re­circulation of the air is used, High­Efficiency Particulate Air (HEPA) filters can be used to
remove pathogens from the air. HEPA filters are particularly effective at capturing particles that
include viruses and bacteria, thus providing an additional layer of protection against airborne trans­
mission [ibid].
1.3
Standards and Guidelines for Healthcare Facilities
Various international and regional standards and guidelines exist for the design of HVAC systems
in healthcare facilities, such as ASHRAE Standard 170­2017 Ventilation of Healthcare Facilities
[16], ASHRAE Design Manual for Hospitals and Clinics [4], REHVA COVID­19 guidance [11], and
FprCEN/TR 16244 Ventilation for hospitals (pre­standard) [17].
The following subsection provides an overview of the requirements for hospital patient rooms,
related to the ACH, room air temperature, and pressurization (if any).
1.3.1
Air Change Rate
Most standards provide a minimum value of mechanically supplied air to ensure good IAQ.
The ASHRAE Standard provides a value of minimum outdoor ACH and in addition, a minimum
total ACH to supplement ventilation “air cleaning” or to establish adequate distribution and circula­
tion of air within a space (Koenigshofer et al., 2013) [4]. The Danish pre­standard provides a value
of L/s per person. This may result in conflicting recommendations since one is based on room
size and the other on occupancy density.

1.4
Air Distribution Methods in Hospitals
Using mechanical ventilation to separate occupants and indoor aerosol pathogens is the primary
intervention in many hospital ward designs. The design of ventilation systems in healthcare set­
tings is crucial for protecting patients, visitors, and staff from airborne infections and maintaining
a comfortable indoor environment.
The primary methods of air distribution in hospitals include mixing ventilation (MV), displacement
ventilation (DV), and unidirectional airflow ventilation (UDAF). All methods mentioned are based
on total volume ventilation, which affects the entire space.
1.4.1
Mixing Ventilation
The principle of mixing ventilation (MV) is to create a uniform thermal and air quality environment
throughout the entire space. Either through ceiling­mounted diffusers or grilles, the air is supplied
at high velocity, inducing room air to provide mixing and temperature equilibrium. The air is typically
extracted from the ceiling, but can also be extracted at the floor level.

==PAGE:20==
Figure 1.2: Schematic of mxing ventilation [20]
MV systems can be used for heating, cooling, and ventilation and can cope with quite high room
loads. Its simplicity makes it the common choice for many commercial buildings, such as office
buildings (Int­Hout, 2015) [21].
In healthcare settings, especially when an infected individual is present, mixing ventilation may not
address the risks of localized pollution sources. A study conducted by Bolashikov et al. (2012) [22]
emphasizes the difficulties associated with using MV in hospital patient rooms. It was reported that
even at 12 ACH, the exposure to coughed air and the risk of cross­infection of a standing doctor
might be increased due to the complex airflow interaction around the doctor. Also, the risk of
draught was observed at such a high ACH.
1.4.2
Displacement Ventilation
In displacement ventilation (DV), low­temperature air is supplied at floor level, from where it spreads
across the floor. This design takes advantage of the natural buoyancy uplift where the air is heated
up by internal heat sources (such as occupants and equipment) and pushed upwards, aiming to
improve air quality within the occupied zone. The contaminated air is typically extracted near the
ceiling, where a hot, polluted air layer is formed. This vertical stratification makes DV particularly
suitable for spaces with localized sources of contamination (Yang et al., 2024) [23]. Also, it pro­
vides higher ventilation effectiveness than mixing ventilation (Nourozi et al., 2024) [20].
Figure 1.3: Schematic of displacement ventilation [20]
Displacement ventilation can operate with lower supply flow rates and at higher supply air tem­

perature, thus being more energy­efficient compared to traditional mixing ventilation. However,
it has certain limitations. DV may be less effective in spaces with disrupted airflow that could
be caused by the movement of healthcare workers or in rooms without any positive buoyancy
force, potentially leading to insufficient air mixing and contaminant removal (ASHRAE, 2011) [24].
Qian et al. (2006) [25] experimentally studied the dispersion of exhaled droplets in a two­bed hos­
pital ward with different air distribution methods, including displacement ventilation. They found
that displacement ventilation effectively could remove contaminants, but they also found that the
exhaled air penetrated a long distance when the patients were facing each other and the exposure
risk was high.
DV may also have difficulty effectively removing larger particles, which tend to settle instead of
rising [24]. Habchi et al. (2015) [26] found that particle deposition acts as a removal factor for
particle diameters above 50 μm. Yin et al. (2009) [27] found that displacement at 4 ACH could
provide an equal level of air quality as mixing ventilation at 6 ACH in a single­bed patient room.
However, the results showed a lockup phenomenon in the breathing zone of the HCW.
1.4.3
Unidirectional airflow ventilation
Unidirectional airflow ventilation (UDAF) is an air distribution method mostly used in restricted
areas where high airflow rates are required to maintain certain cleanliness levels defined by the
number of particles per cm3. UDAF is used in operating theatres, clean rooms, and some isolation
wards. The air is supplied by laminar airflow diffuser(s) placed in the ceiling and extracted from
grilles at a low­level wall.
Figure 1.4: Schematic of UDAF ventilation [20]
The UFAD ventilation ensures that the air is delivered in a directional and laminar flow (Re <
2000), minimizing the risk of contamination in clean areas. When designing such systems, it
is important to consider the air velocities, movement of occupants, internal heat sources, and
airborne contamination removal efficiency (Whyte el al., 2021) [28].
A study conducted by Nielsen et al. (2010) [29] simulated a two­bed hospital ward with a ceiling­
mounted low­impulse semicircular supply diffuser. They found that the ACH did not affect the ex­
posure index. The deposition of particles larger than 10 µm was within 1 m from a horizontal source
manikin, which underscores higher protection for immunocompromised patients. Yang et al. (2015)
[30] conducted a CFD study of a protective isolation room, and they found that the required supply
air velocity to control particle dispersion from the patient’s body and breathing was at least 0.2 m/s
when the manikin was lying.

1.4.4
Advanced Ventilation
In addition to total volume ventilation methods, advanced ventilation methods aim to enhance the
micro­environment near the user’s breathing zone to ensure personal thermal comfort and inhaled
air quality of susceptible occupants. The following sections present advanced ventilation methods
that have been studied in hospital patient rooms.
1.4.4.1
Stratum Ventilation
Stratum ventilation (SV) is a non­uniform air distribution strategy that relies on thermal stratification
to enhance indoor air quality and thermal comfort efficiently. Air is supplied directly to the occupied
zone at the breathing level.
Figure 1.5: Schematic of stratum ventilation [20]
Lu and Lin (2022) [31] investigated the dispersion of coughed droplets in a two­bed with SV at 12
ACH. They reported that the horizontal airflow led to a strong deposition at the initial dispersion
stage that decreased droplet concentration. Compared with DV and MV, SV reduced the exposure
risk of the other patient due to the deposition of droplets at the initial dispersion stage and dilution
at the breathing zone by the horizontal air jet.
1.4.4.2
Ventilated Mattress with Local Air Cleaning
Bivolarova et al. (2016) [32] developed a bed­integrated local exhaust ventilation systems that
combine air cleaning technologies to enhance indoor air quality (IAQ). The system compromised
a ’ventilation mattress’ (VM) with perforated sections on its surface area. Through these sections,
the contaminated air can be expelled directly from the micro­environment of the occupant, with an
emphasis on bio­effluents like ammonia gas released from the body. Incorporating acid­treated
activated carbon fiber fabric within the mattress enhances the air cleaning even further, as it ab­
sorbs and neutralizes pollutants before they are released or recirculated back into the room.

1.5
Justification of present study
Numerous studies have been performed on the ventilation of general patient rooms. The most
typical air distribution method used in hospital wards is mixing ventilation, but displacement ven­
tilation has become more popular (Yin et al., 2009) [27]. The mixing principle has been proven to
provide insufficient control of pollutants generated by an infected individual. By the time the sup­
plied air reaches the breathing zones of exposed individuals, it has already become contaminated.
The ventilation rate specified by the standards ranges from 4 ACH in general patient rooms to 12
ACH in infectious wards (ASHRAE, 2017) [16]. Using a high supply airflow rate could further dilute
the contaminants, but this approach is not energy­efficient and poses an increased risk of thermal
discomfort Bolashikov et al. (2012) [22]. Also, the ventilation rate is restricted by the capacity of
the ventilation system and space for duct work.
Displacement has been proven to provide much better IAQ than mixing ventilation in certain con­
ditions (ASHRAE, 2011) [24]. Many studies using displacement ventilation in hospital wards did
not investigate the effect of patient behavior in bed and human movement, which are considered
some of the limitations of displacement ventilation. Therefore, experiments considering these
factors were conducted with displacement ventilation.
Localized and personalized ventilation methods may perform better in preventing infectious dis­
eases with less energy consumption than controlling the total volume concentration in hospital
wards. Shu et al. (2021) [37] tested three novel air terminal devices for localized supply (ATD with
perforated inner plater, ATD with honeycomb diffuser, and ATD with lobbed nozzle). It was re­
ported that, on average, this kind of system provided 20 % cleaner air to the breathing zone of the
lying patient, compared to fully mixed conditions. However, it also caused local thermal discom­
fort due to the high draught rates. It was recommended to use the lobbed jet, which provided a
draught rate of 20 % close to the face of the lying patient.
In another study, it was found that combining a general ventilation system and a personalized ex­
haust system (PES) in a hospital room significantly reduced the exposure of HCWs to airborne ex­
haled contaminants, compared to only the use of a general ventilation system (Sánchez­Jiménez et
al., 2024 [38]. Personalized ventilation devices might be difficult to implement in hospital settings,
so it was decided to explore localized exhaust ventilation (LEV), which was assumed to be rather
easy to implement. LEV is considered the most effective method of contaminant extraction, and
it is widely used in industrial ventilation, particularly where localized pollution sources are present
(Awbi, 2003) [39]. However, it is rarely used in commercial or public buildings. To explore this prin­
ciple further in hospital premises, experiments with mixing ventilation and displacement ventilation
combined with a localized exhaust diffuser were conducted.
Hospitals differ from most ordinary residential, office, and institutional buildings; e.g., they op­
erate around the clock, and requirements for operating rooms and similar facilities require high
ventilation rates and a lot of lighting (the Danish Building Regulations, 2018)) [40]. It has been re­
ported that hospitals have more than 2.5 times the energy intensity of commercial office buildings
(Koenigshofer, 2013) [4]. Energy consumption in hospital buildings is mostly used by the HVAC
systems to maintain a good indoor environment (Grosskopf and Mousavi, 2014) [41]. Most studies
suggest that most ventilation enhancements will lead to energy savings, as they involve decreased
ACH and more efficient ventilation design (Sánchez­Jiménez et al., 2024; Sheng et al., 2024; Su et
al., 2024) [38][8][9]. Therefore, the energy saving potential of the studied in this thesis ventilation
strategies at low ACH was examined.

1.6
Objectives
The project’s main objective was to study how to ventilate general hospital patients’ rooms ef­
ficiently to provide good indoor air quality (IAQ) and low infection probability risk from airborne
respiratory diseases. This was evaluated based on the performance indicators ­ Ventilation Ef­
fectiveness (VE) and Infection Probability Risk (IR), with IR calculated based on the modified
Wells­Riley model by Shao et Li (2020) [42]
The specific objectives were:
• To identify the effect of different air distribution methods, including mixing ventilation (MV),
displacement ventilation (DV), and localized exhaust ventilation (LEV) on the VE and IR in
the room.
• To identify the effect of different supply­ and exhaust diffusers, their number, and locations
on the VE and IR in the room.
• To identify the impact of the infected patient’s behavior in bed on the VE and IR in the room.
• To identify the impact of a healthcare worker’s presence, both static near the infected patient
and dynamic moving between beds on the VE and IR in the room.
• To identify the potential of providing sufficient ventilation effectiveness at lower than the
recommended air changes per hour (ACH) to save energy.
Based on the specific objectives studied, the project presents suggestions for a novel ventilation
approach in general patient rooms, aiming to reduce energy consumption and enhance IAQ.

2
Methods
The research was based on numerical modeling, experimental work, and energy simulations.
The assessed two­patient room had an identical geometrical setup and internal heat loads in all
three parts. One of the patients was assumed to be an infected person with a respiratory disease
such as COVID­19. The other patient was considered an exposed patient recovering from surgery
or similar.
Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) simulations were used to assess the exposed patient’s in­
haled air quality and the air quality of the entire room. The experimental work was conducted in a
controlled environment to validate the CFD models. The ventilation system was considered a con­
stant air volume (CAV) system with no recirculation. Tracer gas was used to mimic small aerosol
particles exhaled by one of the patients (as an infected person). The performance of each ven­
tilation strategy was evaluated by calculating the Ventilation Effectiveness (VE) using the tracer
gas concentration measured at various points in the room, including the exposed patient’s mouth.
A Building Performance Simulation (BPS) tool was used to evaluate the energy consumption and
to explore the potential of providing sufficient VE at lower than the recommended ACH to save
energy. The following subsections describe the methodology in more detail.
2.1
CFD simulations
Simcenter FloventTM was used to build the CFD model of the occupied two­patient room.
The CFD software can predict 3D airflow, heat transfer, and contamination distribution in and
around buildings (SimcenterTM FloventTM User Guide) [43]. This study used it to evaluate the ef­
fect of different ventilation strategies on the VE under steady­state conditions. To validate the CFD
models with full­scale measurements, the air temperature, ­pattern, and ­velocities were also ex­
amined in strategically placed locations (hereafter: monitoring points).
Setting up the CFD model included several steps, including modeling the patient room, defining
ambient conditions, heat­ and ventilation loads, grid size, and the solver controls. The following
subsections describe the entire setup. Variables that are not mentioned in the subsections were
kept as default.

The radiant part was attached to the enclosure to simulate the total heat load. To do so, the wall

was obtained by applying either interpolation or extrapolation.

The domain geometry was subdivided into smaller, non­overlapping subdomains (cells). The gov­

flow properties, such as velocity, pressure, and temperature, could be approximated.

The Flovent software used a structured Cartesian grid that could be localized (as a region with

and consistently aligned with x, y, and z in three dimensions. Each cell had six faces that corre­
sponded to six orthogonal directions. The Cartesian grid structure is valued for its robustness,
ease of use, and compatibility with complex geometry (Bornoff, 2012) [48].

The Flovent software offered three types of solutions: Flow and Heat Transfer, Flow Only, and
Conduction Only. In compliance with conducting these CFD simulations, the Flow and Heat Trans­
fer solution was selected.
2.1.4.2
Air Movement Modeling
The air movement in most building models, whether ventilated using forced­ or natural convection,
exhibits turbulent motion (SimcenterTM FloventTM User Guide) [43]. Based on this, it was assumed
that the air movement was turbulent.
The Low Velocity K­Epsilon (LVEL K­Epsilon) turbulence model was selected. This model is com­
monly used in Flovent simulations with substantial open space and turbulent variation.
2.1.4.3
Convergence Criteria
The Flovent software re­solved the algebraic equations until a convergent solution was obtained
(within the outer iteration limit). A converged solution ensured CFD simulations’ accuracy, relia­
bility, and validity. A solution was considered converged when the following criteria were fulfilled:
1. All conservation equations no longer changed with subsequent iterations at the location of
the monitoring points (evaluated on the air temperature and ­velocities).
2. Mass­ and energy balance were obtained. In case of any mass­ or energy imbalance, the
net imbalance should be less than 0.1 % and 1.0 %, respectively.
3. A residual number of “1” was achieved. If the residuals did not reach this tolerance, they
should at least be stabilized.

==PAGE:34==
2.1.4.4
No. of Iterations
The no. of outer iterations should be higher than the expected no. of iterations to achieve conver­
gence. In all the simulated cases, the limit was set to 15,000 iterations.

Results
The results of the CFD simulations, full­scale experiments, and energy simulations are presented
separately in the following subsections. For the CFD simulations and the full­scale measurements,
the performance of the tested ventilation configurations is presented and evaluated against the VE
and IP. Airspeed, air temperature, and turbulence intensity (Tu) have also been considered to some
extent, as these parameters could be used to explain the results, and they may affect the risk of
local thermal (dis)comfort, such as the risk of draught. For the energy simulations conducted, the
primary consumption and thermal comfort (overheating hours) are presented to evaluate potential
energy savings.

classified based on the specific aim of carrying out a test, e.g., investigating the no. of diffusers

diffuser, etc.
The presented results were based on values obtained through the monitoring points placed in the
models, such as near each patient and between the beds.

4
Discussion
Creating a healthful and comfortable environment in hospital patient rooms supports fast patient
recovery and protects patients, visitors, and HCWs from airborne cross­infection. The present
hospital standards and guidelines suggest high ACHs to increase dilution and decrease the risk
of cross­infection. However, high ACH results in high energy consumption.
4.1
Displacement Ventilation vs. Mixing Ventilation
Displacement ventilation has been proven in this study to provide good IAQ with high ventilation
effectiveness due to its stratification of the air. This process helps in efficiently removing con­
taminants and airborne particles by ’displacing’ them upwards and away from the occupied zone.
Compared with mixing ventilation, the most common and conservative air distribution method in
patient rooms, it provided at least a 27 % lower infection risk in the breathing zone of a person
who potentially could be standing between the beds (EX 9 vs. EX 6).
While displacement ventilation effectively removes smaller airborne particles, heavier particles
may settle before being lifted to the exhaust(s). Studies have suggested that displacement venti­
lation might not sufficiently remove larger particles. Also, it may not reduce exposure risk due to
coughing substantially compared to mixing ventilation (Melikov et al., 2012) [58].
To enhance the performance of mixing ventilation, it was combined with a LE diffuser behind the
head of the infected patient. In all measuring locations, the capture efficiency was enhanced by up
to 44 %, minimizing the exposure risk by up to 44 %. Although the exposure risk was decreased,
this setup provided up to 221 % higher exposure risk than displacement ventilation (reference
case) at the same ACH.
Implementing DV can be challenging. It is common for DV supply diffusers to be placed on low­
level walls, but this can cause issues in rooms with limited wall space, particularly when considering
the near zone. If the system is designed properly, DV has proven to provide superior IAQ and lower
infectious risk than MV. It is recommended to advise visitors and HCWs not to place any obstacles
in front of diffusers, as the effectiveness of displacement ventilation could be compromised by such
things. On the other hand, MV ceiling diffusers do not conflict with the room layout to the same
extent. However, to provide good IAQ and low infectious risk, a high ACH is required. Hence,
larger ducts are needed.
4.2
Behavior of the infected patient in bed
The VE of displacement ventilation was affected by the behavior of the infected patient in bed;
when the infected patient was facing the exposed patient, the VE decreased by up to 41 %.
This finding was also reported by Qian et al. (2006) [25] who performed a study on the exposure
of occupants to exhaled air in a similar double­bed hospital room. It was found that the exhaled
air stream was dispersed towards the ceiling exhaust when the infected patient was facing up.
When both patients were facing each other, the infected patient’s exhaled smoke could reach a
considerable distance, posing an increased exposure risk to the other patient.
Interestingly, when a duvet was used to cover the infected patient, the VE increased substantially,
which could reduce potential exposure risk. A reasonable explanation could be that the duvet
modified the free convention boundary layer around the dummy, causing the convective currents
to move along the two sides of the dummy toward the head of the infected patient. As a result,
the flow rate of the thermal plume of the head increased providing a stronger upward flow, which
Enhancing Indoor Air Quality in Patient Rooms:
67
==PAGE:82==
directed the contaminants upward and away from the other occupants in the rooms, lowering the
potential exposure risk. Figure 4.1 illustrates this by arrows indicating the flow direction.
Figure 4.1: Illustration of assumed flow direction in EX 3
Changing the position of the infected patient in bed using mixing ventilation did not result in any
substantial change in the VE and IR. In a similar room setup, Bolashikov et al. (2012) [22] found
that when the infected patient was facing the other patient, the exposure to coughed airborne
pathogens was the highest when the patients were facing each other compared with when the
infected patient was facing up. The increase of the ventilation rate (up to 12 ACH) increased the
dilution of the coughed air, which resulted in twice as low exposure risk compared with the two
lower ventilation rates (3 ACH and 6 ACH). Interestingly, it was also reported that at a distance of
1.1 m from the infected patient, the exposure risk of a standing doctor was much higher at 12 ACH
than at 3 ACH. This shows the importance of air distribution and the location of supply/exhaust
diffusers.
4.3
Diffuser Arrangement
When using displacement ventilation, the utilization of two displacement diffusers was not a suf­
ficient ventilation configuration to minimize the exposure risk when using the same diffuser size.
Compared to the best­performing ventilation configuration with the DV supply diffuser located near
the patients in bed, the exposure risk was up to 232 % higher (CFD simulations). In this case, the
VE and IR showed to improve since clean air was supplied near the pollution source. However,
placing the diffuser close to the patients showed that the supplied cool air first started to stratify
on the opposite side of the beds, away from the zone close to the patients’ heads, due to the lack
of heat sources at floor level. As a result, slightly higher contaminant concentration accumulated
in this zone.
For the mixing ventilation configuration, only the diffuser type was a parameter investigated in this
study. When using mixing ventilation in the Flovent software, the VE was up to 3.4 (at the exposed
patient’s mouth) when using the 3­way diffuser. This indicates that the ventilation configuration
68
Enhancing Indoor Air Quality in Patient Rooms:
==PAGE:83==
performed better than a fully mixed room, although the intention was to achieve the same results
as in the experimental work, in which the VE did not exceed 1.3. According to Figure A.22, the
airspeeds were not too high. A swirl diffuser (with an inlet angle of 45°) was tested in the Flovent
software to compare with the 3­way diffuser, and it was found that it performed worse but closer
to what was expected when using mixing ventilation (maximum VE of 2). Taheri et al. (2021) [81]
investigated the effect of swirl angle on the distribution of contaminant particles when using swirl
diffusers. In their study, it was demonstrated that the optimal inlet angles were between 45° and
60°, which substantially enhanced the removal of particles from the breathing zone, compared to
the other tested angles (30° and 90°). While this study did not test other inlet angles, the reduction
in VE compared to the 3­way diffuser could be due to the modeling approach used in the Flovent
software, including the number of fixed flows and how these were angled.
4.4
Presence of Healthcare Personnel
In the case where the DV supply diffuser was located near the bed, the thermal plume generated
by a standing person promoted an upward airflow, which disrupted the spread of air, enhancing the
VE at the lower part of the occupied zone in the room. Figure 4.2 illustrates the effect of enhanced
air stratification when a person is standing near the infected patient. The VEs between the beds
increased substantially, pronounced at the height of 1.1 m (abdomen level of the standing person)
where the VE increased by 66.7 % (experimental work). The VE at the exposed person’s mouth
(standing dummy) was still high. However, it should be noted that at this short distance, in this
case 40 cm from the infected patient, the risk of inhaling or ingesting large droplets also exists
(Hodgson et al., 2009) [82].

It is a concern to use DV in rooms with excessive movement. This may cause the airflow to be
quasi­mixing (ASHRAE, 2011) [24]. In the case conducted with a moving dummy, the average VE
during the last moving session was 3.1 (a decrease of 63.5 %). However, the spikes in contaminant
concentration rapidly decreased after each moving session ­ after seven minutes, the VE was
reduced by only 35.1 %. Still, displacement ventilation provided better IAQ and lower infectious
risk than mixing ventilation. In reality, the HCWs may only be in the room for a few minutes, and
most of the time, it is only going to be the patients lying in bed.
The presence of healthcare personnel when using mixing ventilation was not examined in this
study. However, it has been reported that the presence of a healthcare worker or doctor standing
near an infected patient increased their exposure to airborne pathogens; the closer he/she was
standing, the higher exposure risk. At 12 ACH, the peak concentration level (PCL) was reduced
by only 27 % compared to that at 3 ACH when the doctor was standing 0.55 m from the coughing
patient. This result indicates that a high ACH is needed to dilute the pollution when using mixing

4.6
Potential Energy Savings
Based on a consultation with a consultant working with hospital ventilation [85], it was revealed
that ventilation systems used for patient rooms are typically sized based on thermal heat loads in
the room. To explore the potential energy savings and evaluate the thermal indoor environment by
using lower than the recommended ACH in the standards, three ACHs (4, 3, and 1.5 ACH) were
simulated in IDA ICE. The energy simulations demonstrated that ACH had an impact on both
energy consumption and the thermal indoor environment. The reduction of the ACH decreased
energy consumption, but it also caused an increase in the number of overheating hours above
25.5 °C.
Using the recommended 4 ACH, the thermal indoor environment was almost within the specified
range during all the time (with the exception of 0.1 % of the occupied hours). When using 3 ACH,
the amount of overheating hours increased to 2.9 % of the occupied hours. At 1.5 ACH, the amount
of overheating hours would be 36.9 %, which is above the maximum value of 6 % suggested by
the standards [86]. Ultimately, it is up to the building owner (in Denmark: one of the regions) to
determine to which extent the criteria can be exceeded. Nevertheless, this result indicates that
when using lower than the recommended ACH to save energy, the ventilation system may not
be able to maintain the thermal indoor environment. In such cases, a cooling system is required.
Implementing a radiant cooling system could be a viable and sustainable option. A system like
this has multiple benefits, such as the ability to provide high­temperature cooling, which increases
plant efficiency. Also, it has a low risk of draught and low transportation energy. The risk of
condensation should be taken into account when designing a radiant cooling system. This can be
avoided by setting the temperature set­point 1­2 °C above the dew­point temperature (Rhee et al.,
2017) [87]. Using a cooling system at 3 ACH resulted in a 26.4 % decrease in primary energy
consumption compared to 4 ACH without a cooling system. At 1.5 ACH (62.5 % decrease), the
primary energy consumption was decreased by 59.7 % when compared to 4 ACH with no cooling
system.
The energy simulations were conducted assuming that the fans (inside the AHU) had identical
SFP values, regardless of the ACH and diffuser number, ­type, and ­size. This may not actually
be the situation. To obtain a specific SFP value for each tested system, it is recommended to
calculate the pressure drop in the system. It is common for DV diffusers to have a lower pressure

drop than those used for MV, as shown in Appendix A.3. Additionally, reducing the ACH would
most likely enable the selection of smaller HVAC components with lower initial and operational
expenses.
In a typical hospital setting, general patient rooms can have several orientations. According to
the consultant, an air handling unit (AHU) is commonly used to ventilate rooms in two orienta­
tions, such as an east­facing and west­facing facade. In such cases, the ventilation system is
typically based on a Variable Air Volume (VAV) system, where the supply air temperature is held
constant, while the airflow rate is controlled according to either the air temperature, the air qual­
ity, or the air humidity. By utilizing air temperature control, east­oriented rooms would receive a
higher airflow rate during morning hours, while west­oriented rooms would receive a lower air­
flow rate, and vice versa during the afternoon hours. During the remaining time, the ACH can
be lowered to a minimum value. However, the ventilation system in this study was a CAV sys­
tem. It is likely that this would affect the potential energy savings. Also, HEPA filters were not
included in the experiments conducted, which would add an additional pressure loss of 80­250
Pa for each filter added in the system (DTU Byg, 2003) [88]. Operating theaters that need up to
20 ACH (ASHRAE, 2017) [16] require HEPA filters, as re­circulation can be utilized to supply such
high ACH (Danish Building Regulations, 2018) [89].
Although the energy simulations did not reflect these considerations, they are still considered
applicable since the main objective was not to investigate the exact energy consumption but the
potential energy savings. If these considerations were incorporated, it is possible that the energy
savings could be even greater (e.g. due to a lower SFP value) when the ACH was reduced.
4.7
Practical implications
For the LEV system, several circumstances should be considered. In the lab setup, the LE diffuser
was connected to another ventilation system using flex ducts. Flexible ducts are known to have
a corrugated interior surface (Lindab) [90], which leads to increased friction and pressure loss.
If such a device can be connected to the general ventilation system using ’normal’ ducts, that
would be ideal in terms of energy efficiency. Also, the ducts should match the exhaust airflow rate.

The LE diffuser’s location is not considered flexible because it is affected by the position of the

4.8
Limitations
The limitations of this study include:
• The CFD simulation was simplified by the Flovent software’s ability to operate only in square
geometries. The airflow could have been disrupted by harsh lines and edges.
• Testing of the suspended ceiling exhaust and LE diffuser was restricted to the infected pa­
tient. In reality, both patients should have had such a diffuser since it could be unknown
which patient would be infected with a virus­laden infection.
• Most measurements were conducted under steady­state conditions, which implies that the
breathing process was continuous, which is not in reality. Thus, the measured variables
represent the worst­case scenario. However, data obtained using steady­state conditions
is highly accurate.
• It was assumed that the size of the LE diffuser was large enough not to cause noise issues
since it was so close to the patient’s head. However, the noise level was not measured in
this study.
• The air distribution method was not reflected in the energy simulations, and the location of
the supply­ and exhaust diffusers were disregarded due to the limitations of the software.
4.9
Further research
This study has demonstrated that DV is more effective at removing contaminants than MV. When
DV was combined with the LE diffuser behind the head at 1.5 ACH, it also provided lower infectious
risk than MV at 3 ACH in all measuring locations. However, the use of an LE diffuser increased
the infectious risk at the exposed patient’s mouth when using DV and ceiling exhausts at 1.5 ACH.
Further research is needed to examine the effects of combining DV with ceiling exhausts and LE
diffusers to capture more of the contaminant before it spreads in the room. More studies could
also be done examining the performance of the LE combined with MV including different supply
diffusers and their location.
Testing the effect of a curtain between the beds on the VE and IR could be an additional param­
eter to study. A curtain may reduce the horizontal spread of contaminants and improve VE by
maintaining vertical air stratification. However, the concentration of contaminants might be higher
near the infected patient. Hence, it could increase the infectious risk of a person standing near
the infected patient.

5
Conclusions
This research studied how to ventilate general hospital patients’ rooms efficiently to provide good
indoor air quality (IAQ) and low infection probability risk from airborne respiratory diseases.
Based on numerical modeling, experimental work, and energy simulations, the study’s conclusions
can be summarized as follows:
For each conclusion in () is specified whether the conclusion was based on experiments, CFD
simulations, both or IDA ICE simulations.
• In a general two­patient room with 3 ACH, displacement ventilation was more effective at
providing a low IR than the conservative and more commonly used mixing ventilation. Even
when mixing ventilation was combined with localized exhaust ventilation near the infected
patient’s head, displacement ventilation consistently demonstrated superior performance in
minimizing airborne disease transmission (based on experiments and CFD simulations).
• The utilization of a semicircular DV diffuser positioned close to the beds and patients’ heads
maximized the VE and minimized the IR. In general, the number of diffusers and their loca­
tion had a substantial impact (based on experiments and CFD simulations). The use of a
suspended ceiling exhaust did not show any improvements (based on experiments). When
using mixing ventilation, a 3­way diffuser was more effective at reducing IR than a swirl
diffuser (based on CFD simulations).
• The behavior of the infected person in bed (i.e. his/her position in bed, covered/not covered
with a duvet) substantially affected the VE and IR when using displacement ventilation. In
contrast, when using mixing ventilation, the behavior of the infected person did not show
any substantial change in the VE and IR (based on experiments and CFD simulations).
• When displacement ventilation was used near the beds and patients’ heads, the presence
of a static individual standing near the infected patient increased the VE and decreased the
IR. When a person moved between the beds, fluctuations in tracer gas concentrations were
observed, peaking at certain levels. However, these concentrations stabilized after a short
period of time (based on experiments).
• The VE was affected by the ACH in the room ­ a lower ACH was associated with lower VE
and higher IR. Compared to mixing ventilation at 3 ACH, displacement ventilation at 1.5 ACH
still provided higher VE. The use of displacement ventilation with localized exhaust ventila­
tion near the infected patient’s head improved the VE even further in most room locations
(based on experiments).
• Using lower than the recommended 4 ACH due to the high VE of displacement ventilation
demonstrated energy savings up to 62.4 % when a CAV system was used. However, the
ventilation system could not maintain the thermal indoor environment. In such cases, a
radiant cooling system could be a viable and sustainable option to implement. When such a
cooling system was implemented at 3 ACH, the primary energy consumption was reduced
by 26.4 % when compared to 4 ACH with no cooling system. At 1.5 ACH, the primary energy
consumption was decreased by 59.7 % when compared to 4 ACH with no cooling system
(based on IDA ICE simulations).

A.5
Ventilation System in Climate Chamber
The initial ventilation system setup included finding the correct duct dimensions and ­fittings and
assembling the system on top of the climate chamber. According to section 2.2.3, all examined

cases used one supply diffuser and two exhaust diffusers (with an evenly distributed airflow rate).
The supply system was not balanced at an ACH of 3 h­1, but it was balanced at an ACH of 1.5 h­1
because the ventilation system could not adjust the supply air temperature accordingly at such low
airflow rate. The exhaust system, on the other hand, had to be properly balanced in all conducted
cases. In this study, the balancing was done using iris orifices (Halton and Lindab), the main
advantage of which is their pressure tolerance and ease of installation and replacement. The
pressure difference across the orifices was measured by connecting the orifices’ pressure taps to
a Fluke 922 Airflow Meter with the accompanying plastic tubes as illustrated in Figure A.7. The
