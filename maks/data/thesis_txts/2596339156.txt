Abstract
Objective: One of the most signiﬁcant diﬃculties in extracting geothermal energy is the cost of drilling
which can be up to 40% of the total cost of the geothermal plant [4]. Diﬀerent problems during drilling can
interrupt or delay the drilling procedures, increasing the overall cost of the well. As a result, information
on drilling and geology could be signiﬁcant for the overall drilling project and cost reduction.
During the last decades many deep wells were drilled onshore Denmark. These wells can provide valuable
information, on lithology, drilling eﬃciency and cost. The purpose of this report is to extract and analyze
this information and identify the most common factors aﬀecting the drilling eﬃciency, geological formations
and their complications in drilling and map the most common causes that led to the reduction of the
drilling eﬃciency and to the increase of the well cost. The current research focuses on the classiﬁcation
of Non-Productive-Time (NPT), regarding their source of occurrence (geological formation, equipment
failure, planning and execution) and the geothermal potential of the wells regarding their properties
(bottom hole temperature (BHT), depth, inclination, target formation, time eﬃciency, drilling purpose
and cost).
Method: The methods can be separated in two parts, data gathering and data statistical analysis, which
has the scope of producing necessary results, taking into consideration missing data. Calculation of BHT
when missing, geothermal temperature gradient, time eﬃciency values (Average time performance,Average
operational rate, Rate of penetration) and costs. Gathering data on BHT when given, target formation,
total depth, inclination, problems while drilling and NPT while drilling. Correlating the data, in order to
extract speciﬁc results and evaluating the safety of the results in relation with the amount of missing data.
Results: The vast majority of deep onshore wells in the examined part of Denmark (Zealand, Fyn and
Southern Jylland) were drilled for hydrocarbon searching purposes and most of them were drilled to the
Permian Zechstein Formation. The most active drilling period was the decades of the 50s and the 60s for
Denmark onshore exploration wells. The average BHT and geothermal temperature gradient appeared to
be potent for future geothermal potential projects with values more than 60◦C and 25◦C/km respectively.
Correlation analysis between inclination, time eﬃciency (total elapsed days, operational days, rate of
penetration), years of drilling and NPTs was performed. It was found that inclined wells started being
drilled from the 80s in this part of Denmark, whereas newer wells, especially after the 80s, appear to have
a high rate of penetration (ROP) and be more time eﬃcient. The NPT values are, in general, very low,
less than 10 days, for the vast majority of the wells (34 out of 38 wells), which is a good indicator for the
well drilling performance. Regarding the cost, the average well cost was calculated at 6.6 million USD ,
but this result is having an uncertainty because only 42% of the well completion reports revealed the cost
of the wells.
Conclusion: The current thesis managed to keep up with all the objectives set in the beginning of the
project. Light was thrown on the diﬀerent aspects of onshore wells and their future perspective. All
in all, the results are very encouraging for potential geothermal projects, scheduling and studying of
well completion reports of already drilled wells, resulting to time and cost reduction. Furthermore, the
ﬁnancial part poses as a real risk due to the high drilling cost and the uncertainty produced by the large
amount of missing data. A future investment should thoroughly examine the aforementioned factors and

Introduction
2.1
Background
Geothermal word etymology comes from the greek compound word ”Γϵωθϵρµια”, which can be divided in
the words ”γαια” means earth and ”θϵρµoς” means hot.
From a historical point of view, 10000 years ago geothermal energy has played a vital role in many
civilizations from the Native Americans, Romans, Greeks, French and the Vikings who discovered Iceland.
Hot water springs were used for cooking and bathing. In the 14th century, the ﬁrst district heating system
was installed in Chaudes-Aigues, France and in 1892 geothermal heat was delivered in the ﬁrst residence
in the USA. In Iceland, by 1930s, 99% of the city of Reykjavik received geothermal water. In Larderello
of Italy the ﬁrst geothermal electricity power plant was made in 1904 and other applications followed in
diﬀerent places in the world. Even nowadays many places are famous for thermal baths such as Budapest
in Hungary [5].
Recently, geothermal energy seems to be under the microscope. This can be attributed to the environ-
mentally friendly policies, regarding energy production and reduction of the carbon footprint, Denmark
and Europe as whole, have agreed to adopt. To that end, the spotlight is on geothermal sources, as they
are capable of providing continuous energy supply with low environmental impact [6], [7].
2.2
Motivation
Geothermal energy is a clean and eco-friendly energy source, whose availability is not aﬀected by the
switching of day and night nor by the weather as other ”green” solutions do. However, one big disadvantage
is the increased costs that the drilling of the geothermal well comes along with, because of unexpected
incidents that end up adding up to the overall cost of the project. As a result, the scope of the current
thesis is multilevel. On the one hand the thesis is focusing on the collection and analysis of valuable
data from the already drilled and abandoned deep (more than 500m) onshore wells of Denmark such
as, the geographical location, the geology, the well trajectory, the objective formation, the density of
the drilling ﬂuid used (mud-weight) and the Bottom Hole Temperature (BHT). On the other hand, the
thesis is an attempt to throw light into drilling events, which are unwanted and non-planned interruptions
on drilling operations due to problems, such as tight hole, losses of drilling ﬂuids from the well into the
formation, inﬂows of formation ﬂuids into the well, stacking or jamming of drilling pipe and equipment, rig
capacity or compatibility problems, non productive times and cost. The investigation and analysis of the
aforementioned properties of the wells can provide the reader with all useful information to understand
the characteristics of the wells and eventually evaluate better the potential of a future geothermal project.
The center of attention for the current project were abandoned onshore deep wells because of a possible
cost reduction that could be achieved in a potential geothermal installation. Pre-existing wells can be
safer during an attempted re-entering and thus more cost eﬃcient. The reason for that is the existing well
report, which reveals valuable information on the drilling and operation procedures. Consequently, some

3.3.1
Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR) Calculation
Those two methods are used by companies in order to ﬁnd out if an investment will be worth pursuing, by
generating economic proﬁts, or not worth pursuing if generating economical losses for the company in a
speciﬁc period of time. As a result it was thought that these two methods will help in the understanding
of potential future proﬁtability of the wells.

Missing Data
The data collected showed uniqueness because of the variety of time points and companies involved in
the drilling of the wells. In such a plethora of diﬀerent sources it is very unusual to ﬁnd all the data
needed. In this case diﬀerent types of data were reported from diﬀerent companies and in general newer
well completion reports appear to be completer and more structured. This later type of ”standardization”
in a way data are reported can be possibly ascribed to policy changes enforced by laws and regulations,
but also to changes in the policies of companies.
The missing data also can show the quality of data that are gathered. In some cases, missing data can be
calculated with simple formulas, mentioned above in the method section. In other cases missing data
cannot be estimated or calculated and that is left as missing data. The lesser the missing data, the better
the quality of the well report and in general the better the quality of this research.

It seems that 60% of the wells provide information about drilling events. By observation of the drilling
events due to formation, a classiﬁcation in three categories is possible. The ﬁrst category includes tight
spots, tight hole, bit balling and gumbo. The second category includes kicks and leaks and the third one
includes the diﬃculties in drilling hard formation. The ﬁrst category events are happening mainly due
to formations with swelling soft lithologies, like clay, shale, limestone and chalk. These lithologies swell
because of the drilling ﬂuid and expand. As a result, the drilling pipe stucks and cannot rotate neither
move up nor down. So, there are overpulls and small rotation trials as well as use of ﬂuids which can
work as lubricants to lower the friction between the drilling pipe and the formation. Bit balling occurs
also when formations contain water sensitive clays, cuttings from these formations contain clay pieces,
which react with the water based drilling ﬂuid and expand, sticking to the cones of the bit, blocking the
nozzles and eliminating the cutting ability of the bit, decreasing the drilling progress [22]. Gumbo is also
related to formations containing swelling clay and shales, the cuttings of these formations expand by
reacting to the water based drilling ﬂuid and can block the annulus of the well, the ﬂowline, the shale
shakers screens as well as creating it balling [23]. In order to counteract bit balling and gumbo, the well’s
drilling ﬂuid (drilling mud) is transported from water based to oil based or to salt saturated and other
chemicals are added. If there is a kick or a leak in a well, the main reason is the formation properties
Page 49 of 132
==PAGE:51==
DTU
July 2020
(pore pressure and fracture pressure), so the properties of the drilling ﬂuid (mud) such as the mud-weight
(density of drilling ﬂuid), salinity and viscosity have to be adjusted[24], [25]. When there is a kick, inﬂow
in the well the mud-weight is raised and viscosity and salinity change to anticipate this unwanted event,
on the other hand if there is a leak, the drilling mud is getting into the formation, and if the formation is
swelling that can lead to tight spots, so the mud weight has to be decreased and salinity and viscosity
has to be adjusted. For those procedures special materials are used to achieve the wanted result. In
addition, sometimes LCM (Lost circulation material) [26] pills are used which can viscosify the mud and
increase the mud-weight, but sometimes can also lead to pipe stuck because of build up around the bit. If
a very hard formation is encountered, mostly lower Permian Zechstein due to hard layers of anhydrite and
dolomite or Precambrian Metamorphic basement due to hard layers of gneiss, the bit is damaged and
has to be changed with another one with the appropriate type and hardness properties. This happens
because, either the formation is really hard to drill with any kind of bit or because the predicted lithology
which is followed before drilling the well to Total depth, has predicted the top of the hard formation in a
deeper depth, so the bit was not changed on time.

5.5
Time Analysis
Time analysis is a very good indication of the overall operations time performance during the drilling of a
well. The amount of time spend on a well can reveal how well the drilling and operation practises worked,
as well as it can anticipate for possibility of problems during operations.

amount of time is given for all the wells in this data set and can be calculated using equation 6. Dividing
the total measured depth (TMD) of a well with the result value form equation 6, using equation 11 another
useful value is extracted. This value is named Well’s Average time performance (m/day) and can indicate
how time eﬃcient were the operations in total. This value is very signiﬁcant because it can be calculated

6.1.1
Tønder-3, objective gas potential
Tønder-3 is one of the wells drilled in South West Jylland, located close to the city of Tønder, with a
population of 7581 people. It belongs to a group of 5 name-fellow wells Tønder-1,2,3,4 and 5. Tønder-3
was drilled as a vertical exploration well, in order to evaluate the gas potential of Upper and Lower Bunter
Sands.
After evaluating the Upper and Lower Bunter Sands, the gas potential was found to be zero, as no
hydrocarbons were encountered and the only gas which was detected was nitrogen. The porosity in the
Bunter depth interval was fount to be 20% with 40% of water saturation in the Upper Bunter and 100%
of water saturation in the Lower Bunter.
The BHT of the well was given from the well completion report at 67 ◦C at the depth of 1840m following
a geothermal temperature gradient of 32.07 ◦C/km. This geothermal temperature gradient seems to be
higher than the average geothermal gradient of Denmark (25-30 ◦C) followed by BHT higher than the
average BHT of the wells examined table (30). The overall pure drilling time was 159 hours or 6.6 days,
so the rate of penetration was calculated to be 277.95 m/day, which is one of the highest values among
other wells. The present cost of the well is calculated to be 4.8 million USD, which is lower than the
average cost of 6.6 million USD.
During the Operations in Tønder-3 no signiﬁcant problems were encountered. The density of the drilling
ﬂuid (mud-weight) used was from 1.09sg-1.29sg. There were no problems related to formation reasons
reported. The only problems reported were those related to equipment failure, such as a packer failure at
1605min Røt Salt formation, during the production test, which was caused by a problematic valve causing
a leak in the production string to annulus.

Tønder-4, objective gas storage
Tønder-4 is one of the wells drilled in the South West Jylland and belongs to the same group of wells as
the above-mentioned well Tønder-3. This well is a vertical exploration well, which was drilled to evaluate
the gas storage within the nitrogen gas-bearing part of Tønder structure in Bunter Sandstone Formation.
Tønder-4 reservoir properties estimated to be good, with highly porous Bunter Sandstone layers. The BHT
was given from well completion report as 63 ◦C at the Depth of 1870m, following a geothermal gradient of
29.41 ◦C/km. This gradient is very close to the highest value of the geothermal temperature gradient of
Denmark (25-30 ◦C/km). The overall pure drilling time was 4.97 days, so the rate of penetration was
calculated to be 376.26 m/day, which is the highest among all the wells examined.
There problems which were encountered due to formation were a fresh water ﬂow at 344 m in Quaternary
and Tertiary Formations, which was cured by raising the drilling ﬂuid’s density (mud-weight) at 10.4
ppg. Also tight hole conditions were encountered int he interval 1033 m - 1433 m in Triassic Keuper and
Muschelkalk formations, which was cured by increasing the salinity of the drilling ﬂuid (salt addition).
There were some equipment problems, too. The 7 inches liner did not have the appropriate hangers to be
hanged on the 9-5/8 inches casing, as it was set at the bottom of the well an was poorly cemented.

Tønder-5, objective gas storage,geothermal
Tønder-5 is one of the wells drilled in the South West Jylland and belongs to the same group of wells as
the above-mentioned wells Tønder-3 and 4. Tønder-5 is a vertical exploration well, which was drilled to
evaluate the gas storage potential in the Upper Bunter Sand, with secondary objective the geothermal
potential in Low Cretaceous, Vedsted formation.
Tønder-5 reservoir properties in Lower Bunter Sand found in depth interval 1855m-1889m were estimated
to be good both for gas storage and geothermal purposes. The sandstone is highly porous an unconsoli-
dated. The Upper Bunter Sand found in the depth interval 1702 m - 1744 m with net sand thickness of 17
m is characterized as very poor in continuity, nitrogen bearing and cemented with halite, anhydrite and
carbonates. This formation has a porosity of 15%.
BHT was reported 63 ◦C at 1915 m by the well completion report, leading to a geothermal potential of
28.72 ◦C/km. This gradient is very close to the highest value of the geothermal temperature gradient of
Denmark (25-30 ◦C/km). The overall pure drilling time was 8.15 days, so the rate of penetration was
calculated to be 234.97 m/day, which is one of the highest among all the wells examined.
The drilling problems which were encountered due to formation was the inﬂow of fresh water at 691 m
in Vedsted formation, which was cured by raising the density of the drilling ﬂuid (mud-weight) to 1.4
lb/gal. Also swelling claystone from 964 m - 1672 m caused bit balling in Triassic Keuper, Muschekalk
and Røt Salt formations, which was cured by increasing the salinity in the drilling ﬂuid and the use of
center jet bits. Problems due to equipment were encountered will coring, with core barrel in core no.1,
when it jammed and only 82% of the core was recovered.

6.2
Three challenging wells
6.2.1
Søndeborg-1/1A, geothermal
Søndeborg-1/1A was drilled in South East Jylland and it is considered on of the most challenging wells,
because many problems were encountered while drilling, related to formation as well as planning and
execution and led to the decision of abandoning the well. Søndeborg-1/1A is an inclined explorational
well, with 2 wellbores.
Søndeborg-1 was drilled to prove the presence and quality of Bunter Sandstone and Gassum formations, re-
garding geothermal production. The porosity found was 29.4 % and the permeability 1932 mD(milliDarcy).
Fjerritslev formation was found from 864 m - 980 m. At 850 m in Fjerritslev formation the drilling string
became stuck and needed 54 tons of over-pull to get free. Gassum formation was 90 m thinner (260 m),
than expected (350 m). In Gassum formation the drilling string became stuck and freed by 40 tons of
over-pull. In Tønder formation at 1660 m and 1480 m the drilling string stuck and 2 days were spent to
get it free. At 2401m measured depth more than 400 m of evaporites were drilled so it was decided to
plug and abandon the well.
The sidetrack of Søndeborg-1, Søndeborg-1A kicked oﬀat a vertical depth of 929 m into Fjerritslev
formation, it was previously decided to kick oﬀthe sidetrack at 913 m but this was not done because of
13-3/8 inches casing wash-out, which led to absence of cement til 1017 m. So, a cementing job was done
and the sidetrack kicked oﬀat 929 m.
Søndeborg-1A was drilled to hit Gassum and Bunter formations in a better angle. During the drilling,
drilling pipe stuck in Gassum formation at a measured depth of 1133 m, but freed. Also the string
diﬀerential stuck across Gassum formation.
In addition, problems related to equipment were encountered. The 18-5/8 inches casing connections were
proved to be troublesome and demanded the need of torque changing from the rotary table of the rig when
connected. Also, there was a failure in the pumps, and the drilling string stopped at 104 m. Furthermore,
there were issues with the mud pumps, which needed repairs and the thread compressor also proved
problematic. Finally the sand screens, which were used, needed to be re-ran 3 times because of stand up
on ﬁll.
Most of those equipment related problems were a result of decisions made during planning prior to drilling.
As the rig was unable to make 7-5/8 inches connections due to high torque required from the rotary table
which was not of API standards leading to mismatching with API equipment. Also the characteristics
of rig were not the appropriate for dismantling the bits, so re-thinking was required.Finally the derrick
capacity was not enough to hold the weight of the drilling pipe and equipment so an extra lifting crane
was used. This increased the total elapsed days and the cost of the well.
The BHT of Søndeborg-1 and 1A were given in the well completion report as 76 ◦C and 47 ◦C, at 2409.5
m and 1211.48 m vertical depth, following a geothermal temperature gradient of 28.22 ◦C/km and 32.11
◦C/km respectively.
The well’s NPT is 21.92 days, with a total cost of 9.04 million USD, 2.4 million USD more than the
average cost.

Felsted-1, exploration well for hydrocarbons
Felsted-1 is an inclined well drilled in South East Jylland. Felsted-1 appears to be inclined for a very
unique reason. The reason is that the surface location, which was located vertically above the target was
an environmentally protected area.As a result, the surface location of the well had to be moved 300m
south of the target and deviation was inevitable.
Felsted encountered many problems before and after inclination starts, at 1171m vertical depth. Before
the start of inclination, in intervals 280 m - 340 m and 720 m - 860 m gumbo was experienced due to
tertiary plastic clay formation which led to overpulls of 5-15 tones.
After deviation kicked of in Triassic Keuper fm at 1171 m, tight spots due to swelling formations were
experienced in 1475 m - 1587 m in middle and lower Bunter and in 1835 m - 1854 m in Zechstein formation
and overpulls required. Aslo, MDT tool (Modular formation Dynamics Tester) stuck in 2477 m and
interval 2310 m - 2280 m (Zechstein formation), while pulling out of hole so there was overpull of Bottom
Hole Assembly (BHA) up to 25 tons.
Felsted-1 gas shows proved to be rich in nitrogen (90%) so the well was suspended. After plugging and
abandoning, gas leaks were observed, because of a cement plug’s failure.As a result, the well had to be
re-entered and plugged again. This re-entering process lasted 20 extra days. So ,53 days was elapsed on
the well plus 20 on the re-entering process due to gas leak.
The BHT for Felsted-1 was given in the well completion report as 65 ◦C at 2215.2 m vertical depth,
following a geothermal gradient of 25.93 ◦C/km, which seems to be in the lower part of the interval of
the average geothermal temperature gradient of Denmark (20-30 ◦C/km). The well’s NPT IS 25 days,
including the re-entering or 5 days excluding the re-entering and the present total cost is 10.6 million
USD, 4 million USD higher than the cost average of the wells examined.

7
Discussion
Potential Geothermal projects seems to be a challenge for the future, both because of potential drilling
problems and high cost.
Out of the 38 wells examined in this thesis, the average Bottom Hole Temperature found to be 63.8 ◦C in
an average total vertical depth of 2244.19m following a geothermal gradient of 26.28 ◦C/km. These values
are very positive for the potential use of the wells in a potential geothermal project.
Inclined wells started to be drilled in this part of Denmark, after the 80s. Time eﬃciency is higher in
wells drilled after the 80s and in general NPT times are lower than 10 days in the vast majority of the
wells, leading to the thought of a very good well drilling performance.
The major problems related to formation were mostly caused because of tight hole due to swelling
formations and equipment damage due to hard formations. These problems can be avoided in a future
geothermal project by studying the existing well completion reports and identifying the properties of each
lithology layer and its depth.
Problems related to equipment failure and scheduling, can be avoided, if there is good planning prior to
drilling and other processes like logging, testing, cementing, casing etc. used when needed.
The average cost of the wells in this region studied is 6.6 million USD. This number at ﬁrst sight seems
rather high, so potential geothermal projects might be ﬁnancially risky. However, it would be wise to
start with some small geothermal projects and if they prove to be worthy, new bigger projects can be
pursuit in the future,learning from the smaller once and getting more eﬃcient.
In addition, there is a very important aspect that has to be taken care of. This is, the missing data.
Missing data constituted a signiﬁcant amount of the data needed for the improvement of the current
research and the decrease of uncertainty. So, maybe the missing data if found, could change some results.
The wells proposed are those of Tønder-3,Tønder-4 and Tønder-5. This happened because of their
advantages such as, high BHT, high drilling time performance, relatively low cost and location, which
could help those wells to work as a network of injector-producer geothermal wells.
This thesis shows high information density and concentration.As a result, it could be used as a guideline
for readers, who want to get a general picture of the geothermal potential of the geographical region
studied, or a more speciﬁc view of onshore wells, for which a lot of information are provided.

Conclusions and recommendation
The current thesis managed to keep up with all the objectives set in the beginning of the project. Light
was thrown on the diﬀerent aspects of onshore wells and their future perspective. In conclusion, it seems
that geologically and thermally, this geographical region of Denmark has potential on geothermal projects,
due to the good values on bottom hole temperature and geothermal temperature gradient. A good
scheduling prior to drilling and a deep research on well completion reports is necessary for problems to be
eliminated or avoided, resulting in higher drilling eﬃciency and lower costs.
In addition, it would be wise for potential geothermal projects to be ﬁnancially supported from the state
of Denmark or/and from the EU at least in their ﬁrst steps, because of the economical diﬃculties that
such projects can encounter, when primarily developed.In this way new geothermal projects can be a path
to develop more cost eﬃcient geothermal plants for the future.
To sum up, potential geothermal projects could be challenging because of a variety of problems during
implementation, but also due to ﬁnancial sustainability. So this report, could be used as an indicator or a
source of information on deep onshore wells for further studies on geothermal projects.
Lastly, it is recommended to search for the missing data of those wells in drilling operator databases if
possible, in order to eliminate the uncertainty of the results, and also conduct a better ﬁnancial analysis
including more factors (loans,tax rate changes,tax reduction,EU/state ﬁnancial support,etc.), which might
improve the ﬁnancial sustainability of potential geothermal projects.
