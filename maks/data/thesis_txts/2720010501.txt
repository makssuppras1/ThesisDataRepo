Abstract
Degenerative retinal diseases like Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP) and age-related macular
degeneration are disorders of the eyes that cause vision loss due to the degeneration of
photoreceptors in the retina. A potential solution for patients suffering from these eye
diseases is to implant a chip behind the retina in the form of a small array of micro-sized
photovoltaic diodes. These arrays could be illumminated with a near infrared laser signal,
converted from real video footage, to electrically stimulate the remaining neurons and
improve visual function. In this work a design and cleanroom fabrication process for the
micro-sized photovoltaic pixels that make up the arrays are proposed and carried out.
Consideration for the design of a final implantable device are made. Different sizes of single
diode pixels are fabricated on crystalline-Si substrates by means of several lithographic and
semiconductor doping steps. The fabricated diodes are characterized using conventional
photovoltaic methods.
It is shown that it is possible to fabricate the single pixel diodes using the selected
equipment and methods from a structural point of view and an initial fabrication process
has been established. However, the single pixel diodes showed bad ability to be probed
resulting in low yield, and only few photovoltaic characteristics could be extracted. Low
currents and characteristic S-curves are generated by the diodes that showed rectifying
and light-active behavior. It is thought there is a problem related to an electrical contact
barrier between the doped Si and metal contacts. This is most likely the consequence
of a fabrication defect and some possible reasons are argumented. Recommendations
regarding the design and fabrication process, as well as characterisation methods to aid
process optimisation are given to help solve similar problems in future iterations.
A design for pixels that contain two diodes in series is presented and an initial investigation
towards inter-pixel electrical isolation by passivated physical trenches is conducted. Some
recommendations for the continuation of this research are given, for which trench filling
methods seem the most promising.

1
Introduction
1.1 Motivation
Degenerative retinal diseases like Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP) and age-related macular
degeneration (AMD) are disorders of the eyes that cause vision loss due to the degeneration
of photoreceptors (PRs) in the retina [1]. These photoreceptors capture (visible) light and
trigger a change in the cell’s membrane potential which in turn stimulates the axons of the
retinal ganglion cells (neurons) in the inner retinal layers. From there electrical signals are
carried along the optic nerve to the visual cortex [2]. While the light sensitive cells in the
outer layer of the retina are severely affected by these diseases, the neurons in the inner
retinal layers are relatively well preserved [3][4]. Electrical activation of these neurons
can produce visual percepts (phosphenes [5]), thereby providing an alternative route to
visual information, raising hope for the restoration of sight to the blind [6]. Therefore, a
potential solution for patients suffering from these eye diseases is to implant a chip that
replaces the function of the light sensitive photoreceptors in the form of a small array of
micro-sized photovoltaic diodes. These solar cells could potentially provide the electrical
stimulation to the inner retina to improve vision.
1.2 Background
A retinal implant is an eye prosthesis that is placed beneath or on top of the retina and is
designed to stimulate sensation of vision, figure 1.1. Epiretinal implants are situated on
the retina surface adjoining the retinal ganglion cell (RGC) layer. Subretinal implants are
placed outside (behind) the retina in place of or adjoining the photoreceptor layers. Most
clinically certified prosthesis, both epiretinal and subretinal, are powered through bulky
systems that deliver energy to stimulating electrodes via intra-ocular cables, requiring
complex surgical methods to implant [8][9]. This type of design is difficult to scale to
a large number of densely packed electrodes required for higher resolution, i.e. higher
visual acuity [6].
It has been shown that subretinal photovoltaic arrays can stimulate the retina in vivo
and provide signals to the central visual system when illuminated by an intense full-field
near-infrared (NIR) flash [10]. The Photovoltaic Retinal Implant bionic vision system
(PRIMA) [6] takes this idea and uses photovoltaic diodes in a hexagonal array of pixels
that specifically stimulate the nearby neurons. Each pixel receives visual information in
the form of pulsed NIR light and uses multiple diodes in series to generate an electrical
current that provides the charge injection that polarizes the adjacent neural tissue. This
approach is thought to both improve spatial resolution and be more readily scalable to a
larger visual field, while having more simple surgical procedures [11] [12] [8].

Feasability studies have shown that subretinal microphotodiode prosthesis could produce
electrical stimulation and visual sensations (phosphenes) in animal models. From subse-
quent clinical trials it is shown that for patients with late-stage RP subretinal implantation
of these chips (size 2mm, 25 μm thickness) would not cause further substantial injury to
the retina. Substantial and persistent visual function improvements have been noted in
patients who underwent implantation with this type of chip [13]. A major advantage is
that this type of implant preserves the natural link between image perception and eye
movement.
1.3 Subretinal photodiode implant solution
The proposed solution is similar to PRIMA, mentioned in the previous section, namely
a sub-retinal photovoltaic array of micro sized diodes that does not require an external
power supply. The array will be made up of solar cell ’pixels’, where each pixel should be
able to individually electrically stimulate neurons close to its surface vicinity if it receives
an adequate light input. When scaling photovoltaic pixels to smaller dimensions, the

The subretinal placement of the implant is advantageous because by positioning the device

successfully stimulate the retina using sputtered iridium oxide film electrodes. It is safe to
apply larger voltages in a physiological environment [6] and this voltage increase can in
principle be realized by putting multiple diodes in series within the same pixel.
1.4 Scope of the work
The goal of the project is to design and fabricate single diode pixels and pixels with 2
diodes in series and characterize these tiny solar cells using conventional PV methods. This
research is a starting point to establishing a fabrication process and an operational process
line for the photovoltaic pixel arrays at DTU Nanolab. It is this works’ goal to bring the
challenges in design, fabrication and characterization of these diode pixels to light and try
to find solutions and recommendations for future work.
More specifically, the effect of increased voltage injection from established series con-
nections between diodes in these pixel structures is attempted to be demonstrated. It is
hypothesized that pixels with 2 diodes in series (2D) should give about twice the voltage
of single diode pixels (1D) under the condition that the total photosensitive area is the
same. On the other hand a lower current (about half) is expected to be generated (under
the same illumination).
Two separate designs are made namely the 1D and 2D pixels. The 1D pixels will have a
circular design to be in line with previous work done on the project, namely the fabrication
of 3D carbon electrodes. Of both designs different sizes of pixels are made to investigate
differences in photovoltaic characteristics and have an outlook on the possibilities and lim-
itations regarding the resolution, both from a future device performance and a fabrication
standpoint.
Conventional PV characterization methods cater towards applications that use the full
solar spectrum. Even though these methods are applied in this work, the considerations
made for the design keep in mind the NIR pulsed light that may be used in the application.

2
Photovoltaic Theory
This chapter will cover the basic theory of photovoltaics and that which is relevant to
the application. This includes information that is crucial to consider during the design
and fabrication phase, but also covers key metrics and characterization methods that are
investigated and applied in this work.
Photovoltaics is the process of converting (sun)light directly into electricity using solar
cells [19].
2.1 Semiconductors & Doping
Photovoltaic systems and many other electronic devices exploit the ability to easily change
the conductivity of semiconductor materials. Semiconductors, such as silicon (Si) are
made up of individual atoms that form a lattice such that each atom is surrounded by eight
electrons, forming covalent bonds with its four neighbors. Si is a group IV material in the
periodic table of elements, defined by the amount of electrons in its outer shell. Semi-
conductors act as insulators at low temperatures and as conductors at high temperatures.
This duality is due to the materials’ energy-band structure, or more specifically the band
gap of the material. In semiconductors and insulators electrons are confined to a number
of bands of energy, and there are no allowed states outside or in between these bands.
Within the bands the energy levels are quantized into closely spaced orbitals. The band gap
energy is the minimum required energy for an electron to reach the conduction band from
the valence band, i.e. break free from its bound state. When an electron gains an energy
greater than the band gap it is free to move throughout the semiconductor and participate
in conduction. At the same time a so called hole is left behind in the valence band. A hole
is simply the absence of an electron in a lattice leaving a net positive charge at the hole’s
location. Conductors may have overlapping valence and conduction bands, and there is
no band gap with forbidden energy states. (Crystalline) Si has a band gap of about 1.1
eV. A simple representation of the band structures for conductors, semiconductors and
insulators is shown in figure 2.1.
A distinction is made between direct and indirect band gap materials. The minimal-energy
state in the conduction band and the maximal-energy state in the valence band are each
characterized by a certain crystal momentum (k-vector) in the Brillouin zone, dependent
on the periodicity of the crystal lattice. If the crystal momentum of the electrons and holes
is the same in the minimal-energy state of the conduction band and the maximal-energy
state of the valence band, the material has a direct band gap. If the k-vectors of these

states are different, the material has an indirect band gap. Examples of materials with a
direct band gap are amorphous silicon and some III-V materials. Crystalline silicon is an
indirect band gap material.
The concentration of thermally excited carriers in an intrinsic material, a semiconductor
material which has not had impurities added to it in order to change the carrier concen-
trations, is called the intrinsic carrier concentration. It depends on the band gap of the
material and logically is a function of temperature. The intrinsic carrier concentration of
Si at 300 K is ni = 9.65 × 109 cm−3 [21].
It is possible to shift the balance of electrons and holes in a silicon crystal lattice by
introducing relatively small amounts of other atoms. This is called doping. Typical doping
concentrations for silicon semiconductors may range anywhere from 1013 cm−3 to 1020
cm−3. Doping grants allowed energy states within the band gap, but very close to the
energy level corresponding to the dopant type. Atoms with one more valence electron
than Si (group V) like phosphorus (P) or arsenic (As) can be introduced to the lattice to
produce an n-type material (’n’ for negative). N-type dopants are also known as ’donors’ as
they introduce an extra electron at a lattice site. The extra valence electron present when
the dopant takes a place in the lattice is free to participate in conduction. On the other
hand, atoms with one less electron in their outer shell than Si (group III) can be added to
produce a p-type semiconductor material (’p’ for positive). P type dopants such as boron
(B) and aluminium (Al) are sometimes denoted as ’acceptors’ as they have an unfilled site
leaving an unsatisfied bond with a neighboring atom. An electron from a neighbouring
bond can jump to repair the unsatisfied bond thus leaving an electron hole. In this fashion
the hole can move around the crystal as a charge carrier.
In doped materials there is always more of one type of carrier, i.e. either electrons or holes.

2.2 Generation
Three things can happen to a photon that is incident on a semiconductor surface. It will
either be reflected by the top surface, it will be absorbed in the material, or it can be
transmitted through the material. Assuming it is not reflected, if a photon has an energy
that exceeds the band gap energy (Eg) of the material it will be absorbed and excites an
electron that would be in a covalent bond into the conduction band. In other words, the
absorption of the photon generates an electron-hole pair, and both parties of the pair can
wander off independently. The energy of the incoming photon (Eph) is a key metric for
determining whether it gets transmitted or absorbed, and additionally whether the light
absorption is efficient.
• Eph < Eg Transmission: photons interact only weakly with the semiconductor and
pass through.
• Eph = Eg Absorption: incoming photons have just enough energy to create an
electron hole pair and are efficiently absorbed.
• Eph > Eg Absorption: Most photons are strongly absorbed, however the excited elec-
trons thermally relax back down to the conduction band edges. From a photovoltaic
efficiency standpoint this excess energy is lost.
The absorption depth is a useful parameter which gives the distance into the material at
which the light drops to about 36% of its original intensity, or alternately has dropped by a
factor of 1/e. Higher energy photons are absorbed closer to the surface of the semicon-
ductor than lower energy photons because they have a higher absorption coefficient. This
means that light with shorter wavelengths (blue) gets absorbed in the top of the material,
while longer wavelength light (red) undergoes absorption deeper into the material. Figure

2.3 Recombination
Generated electron-hole pairs are free to wander around for only a short period of time
until they meet another electron or hole and recombine. When an electron recombines
with a hole it stabilizes to the empty space in the valence band and gives up the energy
to produce either heat or light. Recombination is promoted by any defects or impurities
within the material or at the surface of the device. The three types of recombination are:
1. Radiative recombination
2. Auger recombination
3. Recombination through defects
Figure 2.3 describes the three recombination types.
Fig. 2.3.: Schematic describing the three types of recombination.
In radiative recombination, an electron from the conduction band directly combines with a
hole in the valence band and releases a photon. The emitted photon has an energy similar
to the band gap and is therefore only weakly absorbed. Radiative recombination is less
important in indirect band gap materials as assistance from a phonon is required.
Auger recombination is a three carrier process. An electron and a hole recombine, but
rather than emitting the energy as heat or as a photon, the energy is given to another

Recombination

electron in the conduction band. This electron then thermalizes back down to the con-
duction band edge. Auger recombination is most important at high carrier concentrations
caused by heavy doping. The more heavily doped the material is, the shorter the Auger
recombination lifetime [24].
Recombination through defects, previously known as Shockley-Read-Hall (SRH or RHS)
recombination, occurs via a trap level or defect energy level in the band gap. Recombina-
tion through defects happens in two steps: 1) An electron (or hole) is trapped by a defect
induced trap energy state, releasing its energy by either a photon or multiple phonons;
2) If a hole (or an electron) moves up to the same energy state before the electron is
thermally re-emitted into the conduction band, then they recombine. The electron can
also fall back to the valence band, releasing its energy as a photon or a phonon [25][26].
These defects can be unintentional or intentional, like dopant atoms. The probability of
recombination through this method depends on the difference between the conduction
and valence band energies and the trap level energy. Mid gap energy levels are more
effective for recombination.
Since the surface of the solar cell represents a severe disruption of the crystal lattice, the
surfaces of the solar cell are a site of particularly high recombination [22]. This is referred
to as surface recombination. The interruption to the periodicity of the crystal lattice causes
dangling bonds at the semiconductor surface. Thus surface recombination can be reduced
by growing a thin layer on the surface that removes these dangling bonds. This is known
as surface passivation.
The recombination rate depends on the number of excess minority carriers. Two parameters
that are fundamental to the recombination rate are the minority carrier lifetime (τ) and
the minority carrier diffusion length (L). The carrier lifetime is defined as the average
time for recombination to occur after electron–hole generation. For crystalline silicon, this
is typically around 10 μs, but can be as high as 1 ms [27]. The carrier diffusion length is
the average distance a carrier travels from the point of generation before it recombines.
This is about 100-300 μm for silicon. The lifetime and diffusion length are related through
the diffusivity (D) of carriers in silicon:

2.4 p-n Junctions
A p-n junction separates the electron and hole carriers in a solar cell to create a voltage and
useful work. P-n junction diodes form the basis not only of solar cells, but of many other
electronic devices such as light emitting diodes (LEDs) and some types of transistors. A p–n

junction is formed by joining n-type and p-type semiconductor materials. In practice this is
usually done by (heavily) doping a pre-doped substrate to a certain depth, e.g. through
boron ion implantation into n-type Si. Once the materials are joined excess electrons
from the n-type material diffuse over to the p-type side and excess holes from the p-type
material diffuse over to the n-type side as a result of the carrier concentration gradients
across the junction. The electrons and holes leave behind exposed charges on dopant
atom sites, fixed in the crystal lattice. Between the negative ion cores on the p-type side
and positive ion cores on the n-type side an electric field ˆE is formed. In this region, free
carriers are quickly swept away through drift from the electric field, hence why this is
called the ’depletion region’. Most majority carriers that enter the depletion region due to
diffusion move back to the region they originated from. Still under thermal equilibrium
some will cross the junction generating a diffusion current. Minority carriers which reach
the edge of the depletion region are swept across it by the electric field. This current is
called the drift current. In equilibrium the two currents cancel out.
A built-in potential Vbi will be formed due to ˆE. It forms a barrier for electrons to cross
from the n to the p region and vice versa for the holes it is a barrier to cross from the
p to the n region. This is represented by the difference in band energies in figure 2.4.
When a positive voltage is applied on the p-side and a negative voltage on the n-side, the
diode is under forward bias. The electric field at the junction is reduced and carriers can
more readily diffuse across due to the lowered potential barrier. The band energies of
the two regions are brought closer together (right in fig. 2.4). The current flow increases
exponentially with the applied voltage. In reverse bias a voltage is applied across the
device such that the electric field at the junction increases. The higher electric field in
the depletion region decreases the probability that carriers can diffuse from one side of
the junction to the other, hence the diffusion current decreases. The difference in band
energies between the two regions is increased (left in fig. 2.4).

2.5 Light Generated Current
The absorption of incident photons on a solar cell will generate electron-hole pairs granted
the photon energy exceeds the band gap energy as explained in section 2.2. Generation
can occur anywhere in the cell, however excited electrons in the p-side and holes in
the n-side, the minority carriers, will only exist, on average, for a length of time equal
to the minority carrier lifetime before they recombine. If the carrier recombines, then
the light-generated electron-hole pair is lost and no current or power can be generated.
Separation, also known as collection of the pair by the p-n junction is required to prevent
this recombination. If the light-generated minority carrier reaches the p-n junction, it is
swept across by the electric field, after which it becomes a majority carrier on the opposing
side. If both sides are connected to a load by an external circuit through metal contacts,
power can be extracted if carriers are transferred from the semiconductor to these contacts.
In general, the closer the point of electron–hole generation to the p–n junction, the better
the chance of collection. Collected carriers are those that generate a finite current when
V = 0.

2.8 Optical Losses
As mentioned above in the explanation of the external quantum efficiency, in reality there
are optical losses that affect the photon flux into the material. Optical losses affect the
power from the solar cell by lowering the short circuit current. Optical losses consist of
light which could have generated an electron-hole pair, but does not, because the light is
reflected from the front surface, or because it is not absorbed in the solar cell [22]. This

can be due to shading of the surface by electrodes and leads; light can reflect directly from
the top surface; or light can be transmitted or reflected by the rear surface of the cell and
exit the material before it had the chance to generate an electron-hole pair.
There are a number of ways to reduce optical losses.
• Anti-reflection coatings (ARCs) are used on the top of the cell to minimize surface
reflection. These consist of a thin layer of dielectric material with a thickness specially
chosen to enable interference effects from the coating. The thickness of the anti-
reflection coating is chosen so that the wavelength in the dielectric material is one
quarter the wavelength of the incoming wave. ARCs are often made of silicon oxide
or silicon nitride or a combination of the two.
• Some solar cells employ surface texturing to minimize reflection [31].
• A thicker solar cell can increase absorption and limit transmission (up to a limit as
the collection probability is low if the generation happens more than a diffusion
length away from the junction).
• Top contact coverage can be reduced to minimize shading, this could however
increase series resistance if the contacts and leads have small dimensions. Some high
efficiency solar cells have back contacts only to completely remove shading by the
leads. For example, interdigitated back contact (IBC) solar cells use this principle
[32][33].

3.1 RCA clean
The RCA clean is a standard set of wafer cleaning steps which need to be performed before
high-temperature processing steps. RCA stands for Radio Corporation of America, where
the cleaning technique was developed in 1965 by Werner Kern [34][35].
RCA is a two-step process using two mixtures that are mixed manually into two separate
heated quartz vessels, namely RCA1 and RCA2. RCA1 contains: H2O, NH4OH and H2O2
(5:1:1). It is used for removal of light organics, particles and metals. RCA2 contains: H2O,
HCl and H2O2 (5:1:1). It is used for removal of heavy metals, metal hydroxides, and
residual metals including alkali metals. A crucial part of the RCA cleaning procedure is
the oxidation by H2O2 at elevated temperatures. The optimal processing temperature is
70 °C. Since RCA1 can oxidize silicon to silicon dioxide, a 5% HF dip is sometimes used
between RCA1 and RCA2. The HF will remove the formed oxide and this will also remove
contaminants that can be trapped in the formed oxide.

3.4.1 Ion Implantation
In ion implantation, ionized impurity atoms accelerated through an electrostatic field
strike the surface of the wafer. The dose can be tightly controlled by measuring the ion
current. Doses for the process range from 1011 cm2 for very light implants to 1016 cm2 for
low resistance regions such as source/drain contacts, emitters, and buried collectors, even
above 1018 cm2. Apart from the dose penetration depth of the impurity ions can also be
controlled by controlling the electrostatic field. Thus the doping profile in the substrate
can be tailored to some extent [37]. Ion implantation has several drawbacks that have
implications for the performance of the device. Most importantly, the incident ions damage
the semiconductor lattice. Secondly, very shallow and very deep profiles are difficult or
impossible. Finally, ion implantation equipment is expensive, which is probably one of the
main reasons why there is not one available at DTU Nanolab.

3.4.2 Phosphorus doping using high temperature furnaces

simultaneously passivating the surface and forming an electrical barrier. The required
depth of the trenches depends on the device thickness or for a bulk wafer device on the
diffusion length.

5.1 Methods
Important contamination considerations have to be made when fabricating solar cells. Any
defects and contaminants introduced to the semiconductor material can be detrimental for
the performance of the solar cells. Therefore fabrication steps and equipment are carefully
selected to keep the contamination from in particular metals as low as possible. In every
process step the cleanest method/equipment/alternative was chosen unless otherwise
stated.
The substrate is a 4" Si wafer with p-type (boron) background doping. It is double
sided polished (DSP) with a thickness of 350± 15 μm. The resistivity is 1-20 Ω·cm, which
roughly corresponds to a maximum bulk concentration of C ≈1016 boron atoms/cm3 [52].

qualitative inspection of these structures was in agreement with these results, as some of

5.1.6 Metal deposition & Liftoff
For the metal deposition (fig. 5.1 (j)) the liftoff technique will be applied. For good
results and a clean liftoff it is best to have negatively tapered sidewalls at the photoresist
layer edges defining the pattern. A negative tone photoresist like AZ nLOF 2020 would
be a good candidate for this. However, nLOF is not sensitive to the 405 nm wavelength

an alternative image reversal is attempted with the AZ 5214E resist. To achieve image
reversal a couple of additional steps are needed. First, a 2.2 μm film of resist is spin coated
on the wafers using automatic spin coating. The lithographic design pattern should be
inverted and this is done in the MLA interface. The inverted pattern is exposed using
the required dose. After the exposure a reversal bake will serve to crosslink the exposed
area. The entire wafer is then flood exposure, usually with double the dose of the normal
positive process for that resist. The area that was not is now made soluble in the developer.
When developed in the automatic TMAH developer the resist pattern should have negative
sidewalls.

For the metal film deposition that will form the electrical contact to the silicon (e.g. the
electrodes), the leads to the contact pads away from the diodes and the contact pads
themselves, e-beam evaporation is used. The equipment is a Ferrotec Temescal E-beam
evaporator. As described in chapter 4 the contacts will be made of gold (Au). The adhesion
of gold on Si or (native) SiO2 is not very good. An adhesion layer is often deposited before
the gold deposition. A few nm of chromium (Cr) or titanium (Ti) works well and they
react with the oxygen in the oxide and present a metallic bond with gold. In this work
a 20 nm layer of Cr is used to improve Au adhesion. The depositions have to be done in
succession without opening the chamber. If the vacuum chamber is opened in between, the
surface of Cr or Ti will get oxidized and that will give a poor adhesion [54]. The thickness
of the Au film that is deposited was chosen to be 400 nm. 1
The contact etch and metal deposition were performed on the same day to minimize the
amount of native oxide growth at the contact sites that can be detrimental to contact
quality and in turn device performance.
After the Cr-Au layer was deposited liftoff was performed, fig. 5.1 (k). AZ 5214E is solluble
in NMP (N-Methyl-Pyrrolidone), supplied in the cleanroom as "Remover 1165". The wafers
to be processed are placed in a special metal cassette which is then submerged in a bath of
remover 1165. The remover dissolves the photoresist layer aided by ultrasonic agitation.
The bath can be temperature controlled, but this was left at ambient temperatures. The
ultrasonic agitation was set to continuous at medium power/intensity (5-7 out of 9). Once
the areas that should not have gold were visibly free of metal the wafers were transferred
to an ultrasonic IPA bath for 5 minutes, whereafter they were rinsed with water for an
additional 5 minutes. Lastly, the wafers were spin-dried.

6.1 Methods
Lifetime
The lifetime of the device wafers is characterized by means of a microwave detected
photoconductivity (MDP) lifetime mapper. The MDP lifetime mapper is a contactless
characterization tool, that measures parameters like carrier lifetime, photoconductivity, re-
sistivity and defect information in semiconductor substrates. The carrier lifetime analysis is
done with the microwave detected photoconductivity (MDP) technique. Optical generation
of excess carriers is performed using laser diodes, until a steady state photoconductivity is
reached. After the optical excitation is switched off, excess carriers will decay, and cause
a decrease in the measured photoconductivity. The lifetime is then calculated from the
observed exponential decay curve. This rise and decay of photoconductivity is measured by
microwave absorption where the wafer is part of a resonant microwave cavity [57][58]. A
schematic setup of the MDP lifetime mapper by Freiburg Instruments is shown in figure 6.1.
One input parameter the user can change is the resolution at which the mapper measures
across the wafer and takes the average lifetime. Carriers are generated by shallow light
absorption in an area limited by the spot size.
Fig. 6.1.: Setup for the MDP lifetime mapper. Adapted from [59].
Device wafers are measured after the AR coating has been deposited and before contacts
are etched and metal is introduced. The wafers that were measured (D05 and D06) are
different wafers than those characterized by I-V measurements (D01 and D04). Meaning
the assumption is made that the lifetime will be comparable. This is a pretty crude
51
==PAGE:66==
assumption, but it is a necessary one as it is not possible to measure the lifetime using this
technique on substrates that contain metal on the surface. 1
Results will be used to estimate the light-sensitive area around the pixel in order to give
an estimate of the current density from I-V measurements.
I-V characterization
I-V characteristics, both dark and under illumination, are measured using a Keithley 2561A
source meter with four-terminal sensing setup. The four probe system, removes the effect
of probe-to-cell contact resistance. An AM1.5 G illumination is provided from a Newport
solar simulator equipped with a 1000 W Xe arc lamp with appropriate optical filters. A
simple schematic of the setup is shown in figure 6.2. The diodes’ characterization structures
are represented as a solar cell for generality. In both the dark and light I-V measurements
the voltage is swept and the current through the cell is measured.2 The lamp is ignited
continuously to ensure a stable spectrum and the cell is illuminated by opening the shutter.
The temperature at the cell position is monitored, but is not shown here. The intensity
in the plane of the device under test is calibrated using a reference solar cell from Orion.
Thus approximately 1 sun of illumination (1 kW/m2) is ensured at the sample height by
maintaining the lamp-to-sample distance.

6.2 Results
Lifetime
A lifetime map of wafer D05 was obtained with the MDP lifetime mapper and can be
seen in figure 6.3. The mean lifetime of the whole wafer was found to be 2.52 ± 0.69
μs. The minimum and maximum measured lifetimes were 1.28 μs and 9.27 μs. The
regions with highest lifetime are actually the device regions where the diodes are located.
Squares can be made out which indicate the locations of arrays of diodes. The increased
minority lifetimes in these regions are thought to indicate the presence of p-n junctions,
as the junction collects these carriers they enter a region where they have a much lower
recombination rate, i.e. an increased lifetime. This would indicate that at least the n+
doping was partly successful.
The average lifetime is relatively low. The main cause of these low acquired lifetimes is
the fact that the laser wavelength used to excite carriers is 977 nm, meaning carriers are
mainly generated in the bottom of the Si substrate. Combined with the fact that there was
no backside passivation, low lifetimes are a logical consequence, as these deep generated
carriers easily recombine with the backside surface.

histogram with bins denoting lifetimes is included. The mean lifetime of the whole wafer
was measured as 2.52 ± 0.69 μs. Increased lifetime at the doped areas is a strong hint
towards the presence of a junction.

The photo-sensitive area of each pixel is estimated from the measured lifetimes in the MDP
measurements. The photo-sensitive area is defined as the area in which generated carriers
can be collected by the junction of the diode. This can be used to give an estimate of the
current density from current measurement results from the I-V characterization while also
making it possible to calculate efficiency from the estimated incident power.
The diffusion length is calculated from the measured lifetimes and the electron diffusivity
in Si of De = 35 cm2s-1 [49][48]. Even though increased lifetimes were found in the
doped regions from the MDP map we remain conservative as it is not visible for all diodes,
and estimate the photo-diode area using the mean lifetime of τav = 2.52 μs. The average
estimated diffusion length becomes L ∼(Dτ)0.5 ∼90 μm and is added to the diode
perimeter, thus in principle increasing the pixel radius. The argument here is that minority
carriers in the p-region (electrons) have a chance to be collected if they are generated a
maximum distance L from the p-type contact region.

Discussion
7.1 AR coating & contact profile
The fabricated AR coating turned out to be approximately 242 nm, 60% thicker than the
designed 152 nm. This is a consequence of the fact that the oxide mask for the boron (p)
pre-deposition was not completely removed in BHF. Either an inaccurate etch rate from
the BHF bath or hindrance of the etch due to the boron phase layer which appears by
the pre-deposition are the likely causes. Figure 7.1 shows the simulated reflectance of
the fabricated anti-reflection film under perpendicular light incidence compared with the
simulated reflectance for the optimal thickness of 152 nm [43]. The simulated optimal
minimum reflective loss for NIR light with 880 nm wavelength was 6.7% with an oxide
layer of 152 nm. The simulated reflectance of the actually fabricated AR coating is 25.1%
at the same wavelength.
Fig. 7.1.: The simulated reflectance as a function of wavelength of the obtained/measured 242
nm SiO2 anti-reflective layer compared with the simulated optimal thickness for NIR-
wavelengths (880 - 905 nm). Simulations are done with the OPAL 2 software [43].
The contact profile as seen in figure 5.9 is complex as there is quite a large difference
between the total profiles of the n (central) and p (outer) region. The search for an
61
==PAGE:76==
explanation is complicated by the differences in total profile from figure 5.8 as it is thought
the previous oxide mask was not totally removed. Care was taken to have an adequate
oxide etch time to etch all the way down to the underlying Si, given the measured
passivation layer thickness. Highly doped Si has a higher oxide growth rate due to the
crystal defects, and the resulting SiO2 also has a higher etch rate in BHF [61]. Differences
in doping concentration between the n and p-region are thus thought to be the cause
of some initial profile differences between these same regions. From a process flow
perspective it makes sense that the impurity concentration in the n-region would be lower
as more of the highly doped top layer has been consumed as it has gone through an extra
oxidation step during the mask growth for boron (p-type) pre-deposition. Additionally,
because the oxide mask for the boron pre-deposition was not completely removed before
growing the passivation layer, the p-region already started out at a deeper depth when the
surface passivating oxide was grown. The growth rate in the p-region was higher relative
to the rest of the wafer because the oxygen still has to diffuse through the already present
oxide layer, whereas the p-type region has no oxide to begin with. This also means the
p-region has consumed more Si when growing the passivation layer. As such the etch depth
is increased in these regions. Here it is important to note that the actual oxide thickness
at the p region is unknown and may not have been etched adequately. As such it may
contribute to the contact problems we are seeing. Without the adequate doping profile
characterization the concentration and depth of the doped regions can not be validated.
The more shallow profile in the n-region would support the hypothesis that the doping
concentration was lower here. The total difference is probably a combination of the above
mentioned reasons.
7.2 Device characteristics
The I-V results were only obtained from measurements on device wafer D01 as device D04
showed no established contact (D02 and D03 were discarded). Lowered performance is
expected from D01 because these diodes had not been corrected for the etch bias and
alignment problems and had some problems with Au adhesion (see tables 5.2 and5.3).
The results in figure 6.4 and 6.5 obtained from chip C5 where two ’giant’ diodes were
connected in series shows promise for the chosen fabrication method. The measured open
circuit voltage of 556 mV is on the high end for silicon solar cells, especially for a planar
homojunction and at low light generated currents. Therefore there is reason to believe
the VOC has been increased through the series connection. The chips with single ’giant’
diodes gave no results (made no contact) and as such this cannot be said for sure. In
the light I-V curve in figure 6.5 an S-shaped current-voltage characteristic is observed.
Initially a detrimental shunt and series resistance were suspected. However, the simple
modification of the diode model with a constant series and a shunt resistance cannot
explain the occurrence of S-shaped I–V curves [56]. S-shaped I–V curves are reported
to result from charge transport barriers at one of the (selective) contact layers [60] and
can significantly reduce the fill factor and in some cases Isc and VOC. It appears to be a

common problem in organic and thin film solar cells [62][63] . Injection-level-dependent
VOC measurements (Suns-VOC ) have shown to be a successful method to determine the
height of these barriers [60][64]. From these observations it was decided to try shortly
annealling chip C5 to see if it would have an effect on the contact. No change in the

The I-V curve of the A1 chip (single tiny diode) in figure 6.6 shows apparent change under

For chips B3 and C6 (figures 6.8 and 6.9) it is hypothesized that because of a fabrication
defect the diodes are not contacted during the measurement, but current can still reach
the opposite probe through the device. It is hard to say what causes the shape of the
curve to not be fully ohmic, i.e. a straight line through the origin as this is what one
expects from accidental short circuits. One could think about charging effects due to an
accidentally built in capacitor, but this is hard to justify. In order to find out what process
causes the problems in the device and to prevent this from occurring, more devices should
be fabricated aiming to eliminate any highly resistive barriers preventing contact between
the diodes and the metal leads. Extra care should be taken to prevent gold causing short
circuits through accidental interconnections and accidental probing of the silicon substrate.
With the bad gold adhesion, puncturing of the contact pads is a realistic risk, which would
result in noisy data and introduces unnecessary uncertainties.
There was no backside protection during the contact etch, meaning the oxide was removed
from the back of the wafer and the Si surface was exposed. This has led to additional
surface recombination as was also seen in the lifetime measurements, although the carriers
that recombine here probably had a very low chance to be collected, as they are generated
pretty far into the material. I-V Measurements done on the wafers could be influenced due
to the exposed silicon on the backside. To mitigate conduction to and from the stage block
the chips were placed on insulating tape.
Some of the estimated series and shunt resistances are unreasonably high. The estimation
method is not flawless, an impedance measurement may be a better alternative. Some of
these inflated numbers can also be appointed to the irregular shape of the I-V graphs.
From the lack of diodes that could be probed, the supposed carrier transport barriers at the
contacts and big series resistances, one of the main problems with the fabricated diodes is
assumed to be bad contact between the doped Si and the metal. Bad contacts between the
doped Si and the metal could have multiple explanations, but is most likely a fabrication
defect and is thought to be due to either or a combination of the following:
• From the total profile measurements before and after the contact etch it is not clear
what the profile of the oxide layer in the p-region is. It is highly possible that not all

• An insulating oxide layer residing between the Si and metal contact, either due to not

• Inadequate doping levels under the contacts leads to a Schottky instead of an ohmic

concentrations introduced by the pre-depositions were too low.
• A boron dead layer, also known as a boron rich layer (BRL), resulting from the
boron pre-deposition process may be the culprit. A BRL is a thin layer containing a
high concentration of boron at the Si surface, resulting from inadequate annealing
after pre-deposition. This causes among others low efficiency in the blue part of the
spectrum and low lifetimes close to the contact, preventing the efficient collection of
minority carriers [66]. Especially because such a big area of the diode contains boron
dopants this could prevent current collection and may be what forms the charge
transport bottleneck.
• As was mentioned in section 5.2 for the D01 wafer the pressure in the Temescal
increased beyond specifications during the deposition of the Cr that was meant to
improve Au adhesion to the Si and SiO2 surface. There is strong reason to believe
that due to the increased oxygen the Cr layer got oxidized to an extent that the
Au layer showed bad adhesion. This leads to increased resistance at the metal-Si
interface. Additionally, the quality of the deposited gold film surface was not great
as seen from SEM images in 5.13.

about the quality of these junctions and the doped regions. The doping was verified by
resistivity measurements on test wafers, but this does not say much about the final profile
in the device. Moreover, different sized devices may experience different junction depths
due to differences in material quality arising from dopant diffusion.

7.3 Considerations & Improvements
Some application specific considerations and design features like the device thickness and
electrical isolation did not come to fruition in the fabricated device. This can partly be
attributed to limitations of available wafer types, cleanroom processes, and time. In the
end the goal was to show the possibility of fabricating these photovoltaic pixels in the
Nanolab cleanroom facility and build up an initial process flow. The ability to demonstrate
working diode behavior through conventional I-V characterization on very few pixels is
promising. However, improvements can be made to the fabrication process and design in
order to achieve more reliable and better performing diodes. Optimization of the process
and additional characterization should clear up and give insight into the main problems
and their causes encountered during this project. Some considerations for improvement of
the fabrication process, characterization and device design are made.
Wet etch processes
The etch profiles obtained during the process revealed that the doping combined with
multiple thermal oxidations is not only a matter of doping profile control and redistribution
of dopants. The doped regions show different oxide growth rates and as such also different
etch rates relative to eachother and the undoped substrate. A better control of the etch
times will give more insight into what happens to the grown layers. At the mitigable risk of
extra etch bias it is advised to increase the wet etch times to remove any ambiguity around
the etch profiles. Variations in etch rate and oxide growth rate for doped and non-doped
Si can be more readily explained and anticipated if one etches long enough.
Characterization
Both the VOC and the Isc have been reported under solar spectrum conditions. The
value of the Isc and light generated current as measured can partly be ignored from an
application perspective. A NIR laser source gives the ability to increase the intensity and
thus increase current output while the VOC stays mostly constant. Slight changes in VOC
are expected due to current dependent resistances in the device. Changes in VOC due
to temperature differences should be negligible if the physiological limits mentioned in
section 1.3 are respected. A metric which would be more representative of the device
would be the spectral response, especially because it is wavelength specific. The spectral
response is the ratio of the current generated by the solar cell to the power incident on the
solar cell.
Try to characterize the diodes with the intended light source, i.e. a NIR (pulsed) laser. This
could also open the door to a dedicated setup which may make it possible to perform EQE
measurements. Additionally it would be interesting to look at the spectral response at the
specific laser wavelengths. The spectral response is the ratio of the current generated by
the solar cell to the power incident on the solar cell.

Doping profile
The fabrication process involves doping steps followed by a number of high temperature
processes. Where the initial doping profile can be rougly estimated from prior investiga-
tions conducted by DTU Nanolab, the change in concentration and depth due to diffusion
caused by the various thermal processes can only be speculated. Furthermore, the con-
sumption of the doped Si surface during thermal oxidations removes some of the high
doping concentration regions near the surface. To gain insight into the doping profiles and
concentrations and to have more control over these parameters, an optimization of the
process could be realized by the assistance of SIMS (Secondary Ion Mass Spectroscopy)
analysis. In SIMS analysis either oxygen or cesium ions are accelerated by a high tension
to sputter of the surface of the sample as ions. These ions are analysed in a mass spectrom-
eter with which one can determine the elemental composition as a function of depth. If
compared to signals from reference materials one can quantify the atomic composition - in
certain cases down to extremely low concentrations (ppm). Doping levels and impurities
may be determined with this technique. Characterizing the doping profile and finding
reliable doping parameters with the assistance of this tool was considered at the start
of the project. However the Atomika SIMS system that was previously available at DTU
Nanolab had been decommissioned.
A second way of having an idea of the doping profiles and which may help in optimizing
the process including various thermal steps is by conducting numerical simulations. If the
simulation results are accurate, one can optimize performance and test process sensitivity
with far fewer runs through the fabrication, resulting in tremendous savings in cost and
time. One could for example use a set of software programs collectively called ATHENA©
(www.silvaco.com). This was not further explored in this thesis, but could be of great
value if the work is continued.
Another note that can be made about the doping process is the order in which the two
dopant types are introduced to the device. The order of the doping steps, i.e. first n and
then p, has not been chosen for any particular reason other than that it is conventional
at DTU Nanolab. It could be argued that it is beneficial to switch the order as the boron
doping generally happens at a higher process temperature and as such introduces a
larger diffusion of the previously deposited phosphorus. Additionally, boron has a lower
diffusivity than phosphorus and as such might require a longer drive-in or more subsequent
thermal processes to obtain the desired profile depth.
Design
A low collection efficiency can be the consequence of a relatively small junction and the
device having top side contacts only. A good chunk of p-type majority and minority carriers

will diffuse into the bulk as the actual junction between the n+ doped region and the
p-type material is small compared to the bulk wafer volume. Carriers may diffuse both
down and radially into the bulk material where they will eventually recombine. Moreover,
the big p+ region presents a large volume with a high defect density and should be made
smaller. At the same time the p-n junction between the n+ and bulk p-type region can be

to decrease the amount of the pixel covered by metal. An approximate optimal contact
ratio could be found by testing pixels with different contact ratios and comparing their
spectral response.
A top surface homojunction is inefficient for the absorption of NIR light as the absorption
depth is relatively high for these wavelengths. It could be considered to have back contacts
to reduce the path length the carriers have to travel to be collected. However, the implant
being sub-retinal means the electrodes need to be situated top-side. One solution as
suggested by Huang et al are vertical p-n junctions which collect carriers at all of the
diodes’ photoactive area bounds while simultaneously decreasing the loss of photosensitive
area from etching isolation trenches and reducing surface recombination [15].
To improve light absorption one could consider the addition of a silicon nitride layer to the
AR coating.
It did not prove to be a problem in this work due to low yield, but it needs to be ensured
that the diodes in series to be measured are separated far enough to eliminate crosstalk
from the equation. The estimated light active area could be a good indication/starting
point for this.

Design of 2D Pixels & Pixel Isolation
The following chapter covers the considerations made for designing the 2D pixels, i.e. the
pixels that have two diodes connected in series on one pixel area. Where the 1D pixels
were circular in order to accommodate 3D carbon electrode research on this project, it was
decided to design the 2D pixels with a hexagonal shape. Hexagons can be very densely
packed in an array, maximizing the pixels per area. This results in 1) a higher resolution,
i.e. higher visual acuity and 2) maximal use of the light sensitive area, resulting in higher
currents per pixel.
The 2D pixels contain one central active electrode and one return electrode around the
perimeter. The mask design can be seen in figure 8.1. These diodes will be fabricated on
the same p-type Si substrate (1-20 Ω·cm). Three sizes are designed, namely 70 μm, 140
μm and 280μm which roughly corresponds to the sizes of the small, medium and large
1D pixels. The central active electrodes have a diameter of 20, 40 and 80 μm respectively.
The outer return electrodes have a width of 5, 10 and 20 μm respectively.
Each pixel is divided into two diodes where each diode takes up about half of the pixel
area. Similarly to the 1D pixels the diodes are planar homojunctions with heavily doped
n+ and p+ regions to form the ohmic contacts to the metal. The n+ region will form a
junction with the p-type bulk material. The doped areas are contacted with gold through
contact vias in the top side passivating layer. The central active electrode is contacting the
n+ region of one diode. In order to connect the diodes in series the p+ region of the first
diode is connected to the n+ region of the other diode through a top-side lead. The p+ of
this second diode then makes contact with the return electrode.
The 2D pixel design already implements improvements from the 1D design. Firstly, the
sizes have approximately the same layout and are scaled up, meaning the performance
of each size can be compared in a more meaningful way. The p+-type region is made
smaller relative to the n+ region to reduce the amount of recombination sites close to
the metal contacts. Additionally, the contact ratio is reduced to 6.5% and 9.6% for the n
and p regions respectively. These ratios were calculated after the design was drawn and
are chosen for no particular reason other than similarities to designs found in literature,
for instance by Palanker et al [6]. This reduction of contact is mainly in order to reduce
shading. At this stage it is not known what electrode sizes are suitable for good charge
injection into the tissue, hence why they have been simply scaled up linearly with the pixel
size to reduce the parameter space.

adapted as was the case for the 1D pixels. An example of this is shown in figure 8.2. The

8.1 Trench etch for pixel and diode isolation
High aspect ratio trenches are etched to define the pixel isolation barriers and the diode
bounds using anisotropic reactive ion etching methods. The vertical etch creates significant
crystal damage in the adjacent areas, resulting in traps and mid-gap recombination centers.
Thermal oxidation of the sidewalls helps mitigate the effect of some of the etch induced
defects, as it results in consumption of some of the defective silicon caused by etching.
There is still a finite surface recombination velocity at the Si/SiO2 interface meaning the
smaller the pixels become the greater the fraction of generated carriers recombine at the
sidewalls, resulting in a decreased responsivity with reduced pixel size [15].
Having physical trenches reveals a problem with the design in figure 8.1. The gold lead
that connects the p+ region of the right diode to the n+ region of the left diode has to
cross this isolation trench, which is not trivial. An additional problem with these physical
trenches is the highly structured topology of the wafer. Spin coating layers of photoresist

the other. To solve both this and the topological issues a third solution is proposed which

Trench filling
It could be considered to fill the trenches with a thermally grown oxide, however this
is not suitable for the wet etching steps in the process flow. The wet etching steps are
hard to control and there is a risk of overetching down into the trenches, removing the
support for the metal connections. Additionally, some dips will remain at the trench
centers, characteristic for thermal oxide trench filling that cause the same problem.
It has been shown that it is possible to planarize the wafer by Wang et. al. [69] after the
trenches have been etched and the top surface and sidewalls have been pacified. This
process would involve an LPCVD (low pressure chemical vapour deposition) process to
fill the trenches with polysilicon. The top surface is thereafter planarized by means of
chemical-mechanical polishing down to the top oxide layer. Chemical-mechanical polishing
provides great control over the stop-height and little to no defects in the bulk due to the
protective oxide layer. The result is a planarized surface with isolating vertical oxide layers
sandwiching a polysilicon layer where the previously etched trenches resided. At the end
of the fabrication it may even be desirable to remove the polysilicon from the trenches
between pixels to allow diffusion of nutrients [69] or to introduce some flexibility to
the device. At nanolab this could for instance be done by dry silicon etching in the ASE
(advanced silicon etch) as it is possible to process wafers with some metal on it.

Conclusion & Recommendations
Conclusion
Single diode pixels (1D) of sizes down to 40 μm were successfully fabricated from a
structural point of view and an initial process towards fabricating photovoltaic arrays for
implantable devices has been established at DTU Nanolab. Despite a low device yield, some
cells demonstrated rectifying behavior and current generation under solar illumination.
It is believed that bad contact between the doped Si ohmic regions and the Au electrode
contacts due to an electrical contact barrier prevented good performance of the diodes and
resulted in a low overall yield. A decent VOC of 556 mV is obtained for two 400 μm diodes
in series, which likely shows the ability to increase the voltage output of these diodes by
connecting them in series. In the carried out process the inadequate removal of an oxide
mask layer may have been the primary contributing fault, but problems with the doping
profiles are also suspected.
From this it can be concluded that the fabrication process needs improvement in order to
solve the problems related to contact issues and current barriers. Especially the doping
process needs optimization in order to obtain the desired concentration levels and profile
for adequate junction formation and carrier collection.
Pixels with two diodes connected in series (2D pixels) have been designed taking principles
and improvements from the 1D design. These diodes were however not fabricated due to
lack of time due to (but not only) a more complicated design and the necessity of inter-
pixel electrical isolation. An initial investigation towards inter-pixel electrical isolation by
passivated physical trenches is conducted. Some recommendations for the continuation of
this research are given, of which trench filling methods seem the most promising.
Recommendations
Based on the findings of this study, several modifications can be made to the fabrication
process and designs to improve the device performance. The main recommendations for
future research are given below.
• Use SIMS to investigate the doping profile of the fabricated devices and in turn
to optimize the doping process and redistribution of dopants by subsequent high
temperature processes. The order in which the different doping steps are conducted
could be an important facet of this optimization process.
• Characterize the diodes with the intended light source, i.e. a NIR (pulsed) laser. This
could also open the door to a specialized dedicated setup which may make it possible
79
==PAGE:94==
to perform reliable EQE measurements for the relevant wavelengths. Additionally it
would be interesting to look at the spectral response at the specific laser wavelengths.
The spectral response is a great metric to show the efficiency of these wavelength
specific tiny PV devices.
• Consider trench filling as a method to planarize trenches to enable simple subsequent
fabrication steps (e.g. spin coating) for the process development involving diode
isolation for 2D pixels and 1D and 2D pixel arrays.
• Consider a design with vertical p-n junctions to increase carrier collection probability
and reduce surface recombination at the isolating barriers.
• Attempt Suns-VOC measurements to determine the contact barrier resulting in S-
shaped I-V characteristics.
• Make 1D diodes in hexagonal design for maximum area utilisation and packing
density. This also ensures a better comparison in performance with the 2D pixels.
Some considerations for an outlook towards a final implantable device are:
• Combine the systems of PV and carbon electrodes and measure charge injection on
the electrodes. Compare with data from electrophysiologcal testing of the electrodes
and see if improvement in current density is needed.
• Use SOI (Silicon On Insulator) wafers with the insulating layer at a depth of
approximately the desired device thickness. This will make it possible to completely
isolate diodes from its surroundings and more importantly from neighboring diodes
and pixels in order to reduce cross-talk. Additionally, it gives a physical indication of
the device thickness, which should ease wafer thinning, while reducing the risk of
wafer thinning induced defects at the back of the device.
