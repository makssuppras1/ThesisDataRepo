Abstract 
This research examines the innovation and entrepreneurial ecosystem at DTU through 
a literature review on student competencies and the normative ecosystem with focus 
on how the concrete system adapts to the further development of the students 
entrepreneurial skills. Specifically the system will be examined by the use of semi-
structured interviews with stakeholders within and closely related to the system.

1 Introduction 
The way companies compete is changing, moving away from standard competitive 
advantage and towards innovation competencies (Find Podcast interview – 
RitaMcGrath.com, University of Columbia). This is also seen when looking at the 
Fortune 500 companies from 1955 and again in 2014. 88% of the companies which 
were on the Fortune 500 List in 1955 had disappeared in 2014. The reasons can be 
anything from bankruptcy or simple decreasing revenues. (Aei.org, 2016) The 
constant turnover of the fortune 500 list is an indication that the increasingly 
competitive markets require more and more innovation in order to adapt to customer 
demands. In 1964 the life expectancy of a fortune 500 company was 75 years, in 2014 
this expectancy declined to less than 15 years (Aei.org, 2016). Many young 
companies, such as Facebook, Uber, and AirBnB, quickly rise to fame with 
impressive revenues. This tendency to have relatively young companies quickly rise 
to levels that was previously reserved for old well know companies, have increased 
the consumers acceptance of entrepreneurs. This is a golden opportunity that many 
entrepreneurs seize, which further increases the competition and requirements for 
innovation.  
A growing number of small companies are doing disrupting innovation since it is 
getting increasingly easy for entrepreneurs to start a business. They find novel 
solutions to problems, already known technologies to new market needs etc. Such 
companies  are posing as strong competition for the settled companies, that are 
focussing on improving their current competitive advantage in the markets, which 
they have always been in without considering, whether these markets might be 
existing in the future. New technology creates new markets and new business 
opportunities and affects the restructuring of whole production systems. (Spilling, 
1996) 
This realisation, which is becoming increasingly known to all, creates a need for big 
businesses to do their own disrupting innovation, so they don’t lose the customers on 
the day that the market or the consumer need change. A well-known example of a 
company that failed to do so is Kodak. Kodak chose to use all it´s efforts to improve 
the current technology instead of embracing new products and markets springing from 
the evolution of relevant factors as technology and people skills. Kodak lost it´s

1.1 Aim of the report 
The aim of the thesis is to create a basis for developing more DTU related start-ups by 
investigating the surrounding support system for educating entrepreneurs at DTU. 
This will be done through a literature review on the subjects of student entrepreneurial 
competencies and student entrepreneurial intention followed by an investigation of 
the current university ecosystem. These sections will be held up against each other to 
understand where and how the ecosystem affects the student competency 
development. The study will present a number of possible recommendations for DTU 
to take into consideration.

1.5.1 The relation between entrepreneurial readiness and a system producing 
more new ventures 
Prior to the analyses an important segregation is made between creating an 
entrepreneurial ready student and optimizing a system to produce more new ventures. 
This project defines entrepreneurial readiness of a student as the level of 
entrepreneurial competencies the student has developed. Hence, an entrepreneurial 
ready student is a student that possesses competencies to such an extent that he/she 
will benefit from these competencies in entrepreneurial endeavours. There is a thin 
line between fostering entrepreneurial readiness and developing a system that fosters 
more ventures, as an entrepreneurial ready student most likely will pursue 
entrepreneurship before a student without the right competencies. So in some ways, 
the focus on skill development is a way of optimizing an entrepreneurial ecosystem’s 
venture creation amount.  
As Burg et al. (2008) puts it there are multiple channels through which you can 
develop a university’s venture creation capacity. Amongst these is the action to 
support entrepreneurial interested students in providing them with the relevant mix of 
skills and knowledge through advice, coaching, education and training.

1.5.2 University students 
This report divides university students into five groups, based on the degree of 
interest and experience in the field of entrepreneurship. The different types of students 
in regards to an entrepreneurial mind-set are divided as follows:  
Entrepreneurial unaware  
This group consists of the university students who have never been presented with 
entrepreneurship neither as a possible nor realistic way to go. 
With the scope of improving entrepreneurial readiness this group is of course 
interesting, however the group is not included in this project.  
Entrepreneurial interested - Baby eagles 
The entrepreneurial interested is the group of students who find the environment 
surrounding entrepreneurship intriguing and who possibly would like to form his/her 
own business, but haven’t found the right project nor haven’t received the suiting 
competencies yet.  
These “Baby eagles” have the appetite to grapple with the challenges of 
entrepreneurship, but are still waiting to be fed with the right inputs so they can fly off 
on their own one day. 
Entrepreneurial minded - Eagles 
The next group is the entrepreneurial minded. This group covers the students who 
already have engaged in entrepreneurship and thus have taken the interest to the next 
level, not necessarily started a business yet, but is in the environment.  
Entrepreneurial experienced – Wolves 
This group consists of those students who have a profound experience with 
entrepreneurship, by having been involved with more than one start-up.   
Entrepreneurial uninterested - Professional Career Seekers 
This group consist of those students who have a minimal to no interest in improving 
entrepreneurial skills as well as starting their own business at one point. This group 
mainly consist of students who seek to settle with the traditional work life after the 
end of their studies. This group is named the professional career seekers.

2 Method 
This section will present the research approach and strategy that has been used 
throughout the project process to attain the final result. 
2.1 Research approach 
This section describes the different steps that was taken in regards to data collection.  
Desktop research 
The initial research step was performed to gain an initial overview of the different 
initiatives and actors present in the entrepreneurial ecosystem at DTU by investigating 
webpages, brochures and visiting certain information hubs on DTU campus. 
1st interview round - Exploratory  
The first round of interviews served the purpose of delimitating the scope and 
following create the holistic picture of the ecosystem. 
Desktop Research 
A second round of desktop research was performed to discover best practises for 
entrepreneurial ecosystems in similar communities and to gain insight in 
entrepreneurial competencies. This was done through a literature review. 
2nd interview round - Detailed  
The second interview round was performed to investigate the suspected pitfalls and 
possible value creation areas within the ecosystem, from actual stakeholders in the 
ecosystem, both from the students and the student start-ups whom are the users, and 
from the managers and professors, whom are the influencers. 
Survey to support 2nd interview round 
By approaching DTU students with a survey will provide tangible evidence to the 
previously suspected pitfalls in the ecosystem. Getting the data from the persons 
actually affected by the system, will give the best possible idea of how the situation 
actually is.

• Commercialisation  
o Ideas and inventions 
o Creating new start-ups 
o Profit through selling and licensing of intellectual properties 
• Industry collaboration 
o Research collaboration 
o Technology through patents 
o Student projects 
o Access to facilities 
• Student innovation 
o Providing students with knowledge, facilities etc. 
o Student Hubs - DTU Skylab

of patents and the creation of spinouts DTU commercialises and generates profit from 
the ideas and invention that are created every year in DTU settings. 
The second area of interaction is Industry Collaborations. DTU collaborates with 
industry partners in various ways to share knowledge. Collaborative research, contract 
research and consulting. 
Thirdly there is Student Innovation. DTU seeks to support the students’ innovative 
efforts through education, facilities, events, competitions and student hubs.

Resource acquisition 
Resource acquisition is being defined as the ability to develop and integrate the 
internal and external resources needed to develop the venture. Such resources include 
financial capital, physical assets, technological resources, human capital, and 
organizational resources. (Rasmussen & Wright, 2015) 
A successful creation of a new spin-off venture therefore depends on the ability to 
assemble and organize the internal and external resources that are needed. In the early 
stages of a venture development intangible (soft) resources are considered more 
valuable than tangible resources.

Self-­‐efficacy	  
A person’s self-efficacy translates into a person’s belief in his or her own ability in 
regards to doing a certain task. This ability ensures that the person will persist in 
situations of adversity, be better at handling both positive and negative feedback, 
setting higher goals for him or her self and provide higher efforts over a longer period 
than a person with a relatively smaller self-efficacy. (Ventureprise.org, 2016) 
Regarding the relationship between entrepreneurship and self-efficacy people with 
higher self-efficacy are more willing to take on tasks that by first sight might seem to 
exceed their capabilities, as for example starting a business when they have not been 
involved in a start-up earlier. People possessing this trait in a lower degree will assess 
their capabilities to be only sufficient as an employee where the risk of failing will be 
easier to control. Finally self-efficacy is positively related to entrepreneurial activities, 
as the belief in one self will increase the incentive to act. (Sanchez, 2011) Research 
furthermore indicates that start-ups with entrepreneurs that possess this trait are 
closely related to company growth. (Ventureprise.org, 2016)

5 THE ENTREPRENEURIAL ECOSYSTEM 
Based on a state-of-the-art-literature review this section presents the optimal 
conditions for an entrepreneurial ecosystem held up against the current situation at 
DTU. 
5.1 What is an entrepreneurial ecosystem 
The generic definition of the term ecosystem is: 
 
“a system, or a group of interconnected elements, formed by the interaction of a 
community of organisms with their environment.” 
(Dictionary.com, 2016) 
The term ecosystem is applied across many different scientific fields as it relates to 
the interaction of elements within the ecosystem. When you look at an entrepreneurial 
ecosystem, you look at entrepreneurial processes within an already existing system of 
economic and sociocultural structures (Spilling, 1996). Spilling further elaborates on 
the term:  
“The entrepreneurial system consists of a complexity and diversity of actors, roles, 
and environmental factors that interact to determine the entrepreneurial performance 
of a region or locality.” (Spilling, 1996) 
The advantage of a system like this is its ability to provide new ventures with help 
and support in perceiving and exploiting new opportunities. However this is not the 
full picture, and a well-functioning ecosystem can also help companies modernizing, 
restructuring and pivoting companies in new directions. It provides the environment 
with the opportunity of continuous development and on-going value creation for the 
stakeholders. (Spilling, 1996) 
As has been emphasized earlier in this article, the existing business structure and 
sociocultural structure are important environmental factors that may be summarized 
to constitute the entrepreneurial climate of an area, i.e., the "temperature" of the 
entrepreneurial system. In the short-term, the entrepreneurial system will tend to be 
fairly stable, but over time industrial restructuring may also work back on the basic 
==PAGE:38==
 
38
structures. In the long-term, industrial restructuring also implies the changing of the 
entrepreneurial system. (Spilling, 1996) 
As every ecosystem is unique (Isenberg, 2011), it is not possible to determine the 
optimal constellation of an ecosystem as local parameters will play a significant role. 
However, research has provided a list of factors that would be beneficial in obtaining 
an optimal ecosystem.  These will be presented below.

The Law of Jante 
The Scandinavian expression The Law of Jante refers to a mentality that de-
emphasises individual effort and places all emphasis on the collective, while 
discouraging those who stand out as achievers. (Wikipedia, 2016)  
The Law of Jante can be a strong opponent of entrepreneurship. It directly restrains 
students’ self-efficacy and diminishes the intention of being part of an entrepreneurial 
activity where risks are involved. Swedes as an example have, however, defied this 
with the highest level of venture creation capital per capita moved against the 
destructive state of mind. (NY Times, 2016)  
So the Law of Jante is slowly changing, partly due to the increase of the 
entrepreneurial trends and awareness. (Entrepreneur.com, 2016)

Findings from investigating DTU 
Looking at DTU this project has identified some areas, where efforts could be 
introduced to optimize the entrepreneurial situation. 
At this moment DTU is a rather closed community (the contact with students from 
other institutions is rather scarce) 
Learning-by-doing was identified as a strong determinant of entrepreneurial 
competency development. Learning-by-doing is obtained through presentation in 
classes, case competitions, and more. These initiatives disserve more attention if DTU 
is to develop entrepreneurial competencies. 
Closer collaboration is a key factor, if the goal is to have the most effective 
entrepreneurial ecosystem. As it is at this point DTU isn’t a complete system. To 
make the system more effective and resemble highly effective ecosystems 
collaboration is necessary. This means closer collaboration to industrial partners, but 
also universities in the proximity. 
To meet these gaps three models to boost entrepreneurship efforts are being proposed: 
 
==PAGE:61==
 
61
1. Challenges from the industry, where they are in the need of new products, 
services or business models, should try to incorporate students in a higher 
degree, and let these students see the projects to an end. 
2. Matching students across the different institutions, working together on 
commercialising products and services cross-functionally. 
3. Projects put aside by companies, that haven’t yet been exploited due to lack of 
resources or importance, could be addressed by students to build a base for 
intraprenerial efforts or spinoff creations. 
  
Challenges/Threats 
Each model is an opportunity to create more entrepreneurial readiness, however a 
challenge and limitation that repeatedly emerges is the limitation in the students’ time. 
Close collaborations require an open mentality from all involved institutional and 
industry partners as well as students themselves. This is highly affected by the Danish 
culture. 
Professors must move focus and interest a bit towards education, but what will most 
likely affect the amount of research they can produce. 
Policy makers must slack on rules to give room for entrepreneurship and educational 
efforts to grow in innovative ways. 
Opportunities 
Each model has viable chances to add value to universities, students and industry, but 
asks for easy accessible and clear interfaces to facilitate the matchmaking. This ranges 
from events to courses to webpages.

10 Conclusion 
This project has raised the question if educational institutions like DTU might 
consider doing an active effort in training and affecting student personal traits.  
As described in the student competency section, the right personal traits are crucial 
for students when adapting the entrepreneurial mind-set. Through this investigation, 
there haven’t been found any direct connections between the improvement of personal 
traits, and the entrepreneurial ecosystem on DTU.  
These personal traits are basically the foundation for being a successful entrepreneur. 
They must be appropriately supported and developed. 
 
There is a gap between DTU wanting to be an institution that wants to foster 
entrepreneurial activities and what they actually provide students with.
