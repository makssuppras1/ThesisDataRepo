Statement of problem 
For years, solventborne coatings have, due to their superior protective properties, been 
used as the preferred industrial coatings. However, in recent years, the industrial protec-
tive coating industry has moved towards waterborne protective coating systems. Initially, 
this sprung from concerns centered around the health hazards of solventborne coatings. 
Still, as environmental awareness has increased, the benefits of solventborne coatings 
are no longer limited to human health but include ecological benefits.  
Although the potential benefits of waterborne coatings are immense, the industrial pro-
tective coatings market still predominantly consists of solventborne coatings. This is 
mainly due to the challenges associated with waterborne coatings, one of which is the 
poor coating-substrate adhesion observed for waterborne epoxy primers compared to 
the corresponding solventborne primer. Furthermore, it has been observed that water-
borne coatings are susceptible to the substrate surface and have a lower tolerance to 
surface impurities.  
 
To gain an understanding of the fundamental mechanisms related to poor coating-sub-
strate adhesion, this project aims at conducting experiments wherein a waterborne coat-
ing is compared to the corresponding solventborne coating. Furthermore, the project 
seeks to understand the effect of the chemical interface structure on the adhesion be-
tween coating and substrate. The project also targets an understanding of the driving 
force behind the coating sensitivity to the substrate surface by conducting experiments 
wherein various pretreatment scenarios will be tested to gain knowledge of how different 
parameters affect the coating sensitivity to the substrate surface.  
 
To better aid the understanding of the challenges related to waterborne coatings, a litera-
ture study will initially be performed. The literature study will briefly explain the basics of 
coatings and what they are comprised of before it will go more in-depth with protective 
anti-corrosive coatings and the various anti-corrosive mechanisms utilized by these. 
Moreover, the study will explain what epoxy coatings are and what the differences are 
between the formulation of waterborne epoxy coatings and solventborne epoxy coatings. 
Finally, the study will explain adhesion and adhesion theories, what parameters impact 
adhesion, and what methods could potentially be used to analyze the adhesion.

Abstract 
The industrial anti-corrosive coating market was for many years, and still is, dominated 
by solventborne coatings based on organic solvents. The interest in anti-corrosive water-
borne epoxy coatings has, however, grown over the past 60 years as awareness of hu-
man health and the environment has increased.  
One of the main problems associated with waterborne coatings is that they exhibit poorer 
adhesion compared to solventborne coatings. In this report four simple coating formula-
tions, a waterborne with the pigment titanium dioxide and one without as well as a sol-
ventborne with titanium dioxide and without, were analyzed through IR spectroscopy, 
pull-off tests, and SEM and EDX. The four coatings were investigated on four types of 
steel substrates; standard panels with a roughness of 25 μm, standard panels with flash 
rust, standard panels with oil contamination, and panels with a higher roughness of 80 to 
130 μm.  
Through IR spectroscopy no significant differences were found between the WB and SB 
formulations. Pull-off tests showed that the SB coatings were significantly less sensitive 
to oil contamination, and that all coatings were very sensitive to flash rust.  
SEM and EDX revealed a layer close to the substrate within the coatings with pigment 
which was completely free of the pigment titanium dioxide. This layer was found most 
prominent for the WB coatings on the oil contaminated panels and less prominent for the 
SB coatings. This indicated, again, a higher sensitivity of the WB coatings. Furthermore, 
it was found that this layer was observed in more cases on oil contaminated and flash 
rusted panels than on high roughness panels.

1 Introduction 
Coatings are everywhere; on walls, as decorations, on cars as protection against corro-
sion, on food packaging [1], and many more places. A coating is defined as a thin layer 
of a substance spread over a surface and it can have both decorative and protective pur-
poses. Decorative coatings have been seen as early as 25 000 years ago as cave paints 
made from earth pigments ground to a fine powder [2]. Protective coatings have been 
dated back to 3 000 BC when beeswax, gelatin, and clay were used for roofs in the old 
Egypt [3],[4]. 
 
For many years the coating industry primarily used solventborne (SB) coatings, and in 
the 1970s, more than 90% of the coatings market consisted of low-solid SB coatings [5].  
However, in the 1960s the interest in waterborne coatings grew significantly primarily 
lead by the US [6]–[8]. This growing interest was driven by legislation, cost, and human 
health as well as environmental safety [6].  
The WB coatings industry has, in recent years, grown substantially, and the global WB 
coatings market is projected to grow with a CAGR of between 3.5 and 6.7% and reach a 
value between 83 and $117 billion within the next few years1 [9]–[11]. 
WB coatings are widely applied for architectural and decorative purposes, where they 
have proved to work very well and in 2018 it was estimated that WB coatings accounted 
for 70 to 80 % of the architectural coatings market share [12].  
Still, for industrial protective uses, WB coatings pose many challenges some of which are 
the toxicity of the additives required for the formulation of WB coatings [13]. Moreover, 
drying of the coating has also been observed to be a problem, resulting in an increase in 
viscosity, and ultimately a coating that is difficult to apply and work at specific tempera-
ture ranges [13]. Finally, it has been found, through experience and experiments per-
formed by Scientists at Hempel, that the adhesion between coating and substrate, one of 
the most crucial properties of an anti-corrosive coating, is weaker and more sensitive to 
the substrate and substrate impurities for WB coatings as compared to SB coatings. 
 
The purpose of the literature study, presented in the following sections, is to understand 
SB and WB epoxy coatings and their differences in formulation. In addition, the aim is to 
understand what adhesion is, what factors may influence the adhesion, and how adhe-
sion can be measured.

2 Coating technology 
A coating is defined as a film of substance spread over a surface, referred to as the sub-
strate. It can be applied as a liquid, a solid, or a gas.  
This report will focus on coatings applied as a liquid. Thus, coatings used as a solid or 
gas will not be explored further.  
 
2.1 Contents of a coating 
The chemical composition of the coating influences its properties, such as drying time, 
hardness, and protective abilities. Generally, the composition of a coating can be 
grouped into four categories: Binders, solvents, pigments, and additives [1]. 
 
2.1.1 Binders 
Binders, also known as resins, are polymers and arguably the most important coating 
component. They are what the coating gets its name from, e.g., epoxy coatings, polyure-
thane coatings, and acrylic coatings. The resins also form the material that adheres to 
the substrate, and, to a large extent, govern the properties of the coating [1],[14]. Binders 
can be both natural and synthetic. However, for industrial uses, the binders are exclu-
sively synthetic [14]. 
A suitable binder must ensure good adhesion to the substrate and good cohesion within 
the coating layer. Furthermore, it must ensure high mechanical strength and low permea-
bility which is one of the most crucial properties of a protective coating [15].  
 
Binders can be either thermoplastic or thermosetting, depending on their behavior when 
exposed to heat, and their mechanism of film formation. The mechanism of film formation 
of a thermoplastic binder is exclusively by solvent evaporation, and is a reversible pro-
cess. After film formation, the binder will remain plastic, and when heated the molecules 
are supplied with energy, becoming more mobile, resulting in softening of the binder. Fig-
ure 2.1a illustrates a thermoplastic binder network after film formation. 
Thermosetting binders undergo film formation by solvent evaporation and cross-linking 
reactions. The cross-links are permanent chemical bonds that will inhibit redissolving and 
remelting [16]. They are generally hard and stiff materials that usually exhibit good sol-
vent and thermal resistance [17],[18]. A simplified drawing of the network of a cured ther-
moset binder is displayed in Figure 2.1b.

2.1.2 Solvents 
Resins are usually high molecular weight molecules with high boiling points, that form 
viscous liquids. Solvents are added to provide the coating formulation the ideal con-
sistency for application. They are usually low molecular weight organic compounds with 
low boiling points. The solvent should dissolve the resin completely, resulting in a liquid 
that can disperse the pigments and additives [19]. Additionally, the role of the solvent is 
to evaporate after application to cure the coating [1]. 
 
For many years the solvents were primarily organic compounds, often hydrocarbons de-
rived from petroleum [20]. However, in 1963 the Clean Air Act was established to protect 
public health and welfare from air pollution in the US [21],[22], thus forcing the coating 
industry to be more conscious of human health. In recent years, the well-being of the en-
vironment has also been a prominent concern, thus the coating industry must not only 
strive to create coatings that are more friendly towards human health but also to create 
coatings that are more friendly towards the environment.  
Two main approaches have been developed to achieve these two goals: (1) To use sol-
vents that are less hazardous to the environment and human health as well as to use 
equipment that can collect the hazardous fumes released during application and (2) to 
develop solventless, waterborne, and high solid coatings [12]. 
 
2.1.3 Pigments and fillers 
Pigments and fillers are small insoluble particles that can be dispersed in a medium, e.g., 
a coating formulation [23]. 
The primary role of fillers is to enhance mechanical properties as well as to act as vol-
ume fillers to decrease the price of the coating. The primary role of pigments is to provide 
color to the coating, which they do by scattering, reflecting, and absorbing light. The 
color, which the pigment provides, is usually chosen for aesthetic reasons, and it must be 
==PAGE:16==
Page 5 of 118 
durable and able to hold its color (i.e., not fade or darken) [24],[25]. However, pigments 
can also provide a decrease in water permeability and protect the cured coating against 
the UV radiation [24]. In addition, pigments can also act as volume fillers to decrease the 
cost of the coating [24]. 
 
The properties of a coating can be significantly altered with the chemical composition of 
the pigment, the pigment concentration, the pigment particle size distribution, and pig-
ment dispersion [24]. 
 
2.1.4 Additives 
Additives are added to a coating in small amounts to modify some of the coating proper-
ties [1]. Generally, additives are added to a coating formulation to overcome certain de-
fects or provide specific abilities to a coating, and they are considered an integral part of 
a coating formulation [26]. 
There is a wide variety of additives, which can be divided into subcategories depending 
on what property of a coating they provide or enhance. An example is wetting and dis-
persing additives, which aid in the wetting and dispersion of pigments and fillers within a 
coating. Flow aid additives, aid in eliminating surface defect by promoting leveling and 
flow of the coating upon application. Foam control additives, prevent the formation of 
foam which can cause many problems, among other bubbles or craters on the film, as 
well as within the film. This can cause the film to look satisfactory, but the mechanical 
properties to be impaired [26].  
 
3 Protective coatings 
Protective coatings are a large group of coatings specialized in protecting a substrate 
from a particular environment. A protective coating can, for example, be protective 
against chemicals, heat, high impact, corrosion, UV light, and fouling [23]. In this report, 
the focus will be on anti-corrosive protective coatings, hence other protective coatings 
will not be covered. Moreover, the anti-corrosive system in focus in this section will be a 
three-layer system. Such a system consists of (1) a primer, a two-component anti-corro-
sive coating that also must ensure good adhesion to the substrate, (2) a topcoat, usually 
a two-component, UV stable, hydrophobic coating, and (3) an intermediate coat which 
provides adhesion between the primer the topcoat [27].  
 
3.1 Corrosion 
To better understand how a protective coating works, it is beneficial to understand how 
corrosion works. This phenomenon will be explored further in this section.  
Corrosion is a naturally occurring, electrochemical reaction in which a material, and its 
properties, degrade or deteriorate over time due to exposure to a specific environment 
[28],[29]. Corrosion may be divided into either dry corrosion or wet corrosion, depending 
==PAGE:17==
Page 6 of 118 
on the environment in which the corrosion occurs. Dry corrosion is also called chemical 
corrosion, and it occurs when no water is present. It is generally not as damaging as wet 
corrosion but is susceptible to temperature [30]. The most common environment in which 
corrosion occurs for engineering materials in nature is an aqueous environment [31]. Dry 
corrosion will thus not be explored further in this report. 
 
Wet corrosion occurs in an environment where water is present and requires the estab-
lishment of a corrosion cell. This requires the presence of [31]:

An anode 
• 
A cathode 
• 
A metallic path 
• 
An electrolyte

lic path is often provided by the structure (i.e., the substrate) itself, and the electrolyte is 
the aqueous environment in which the anode and the cathode are immersed [31]. In Fig-
ure 3.1, the corrosion of a steel surface in the presence of water and oxygen is depicted.

Every year corrosion has a significant economic impact. In 2016, NACE (the National As-
sociation of Corrosion Engineers) released the article "National Association of Corrosion 
Engineers" wherein the estimated annual cost of corrosion was USD 2.5 trillion [34]. In 
addition, corrosion could threaten human safety and the environment [35]. Consequently, 
it is essential to either slow or prevent the corrosion process completely.  
One method to protect against corrosion is by utilizing anti-corrosive coatings, of which 
there are three main types: (1) barrier coatings, (2) inhibiting coatings, and (3) sacrificial 
coatings. 
 
3.2 Barrier coatings 
A barrier coating can be applied as both the primer, intermediate, or topcoat. The pur-
pose of the barrier coating is to create a barrier between the substrate and the environ-
ment, to prevent air, oxygen, chlorides, ions, and other corrosive agents from getting to 
the substrate. With this, the formation of a viable electrochemical cell is avoided, thus 
preventing corrosion [15],[36]. 
One parameter affecting the efficacy of a barrier coating is the coating thickness. Gener-
ally, a thicker coating results in improved barrier properties. Furthermore, a barrier coat-
ing made up of multiple thin layers of coating will exhibit better barrier properties than if 
that same coating was made up of one single layer [15]. 
Lower pigment volumes can also improve barrier properties, resulting in a more dense 
and cohesive coating. However, too little pigment will result in, firstly, a costly coating 
and, secondly, a coating that is very viscous and thus does not have desirable properties 
for the application [15]. 
 
==PAGE:19==
Page 8 of 118 
3.3 Inhibiting coatings 
Inhibiting coatings are primarily used as primers, as it is ineffective if it is not in direct 
contact with the substrate. This coating type designed to prevent corrosion completely by 
interfering with the electrolytes required to start the corrosion process. 
Inhibiting coatings rely on the passivation of the substrate surface using inhibition pig-
ments, typically inorganic salts. When moisture penetrates the coating, the pigments will 
partly dissolve. The partly dissolved pigments will then be transported to the surface of 
the substrate, where they react to form a product that passivates the substrate surface. 
When an inhibiting coating passivates a surface, a protective layer (also known as a pas-
sivation layer) is generated on the surface of the substrate. This protective layer is typi-
cally an oxide or nitride layer, which can act as a barrier layer [15],[37]. 
The efficacy of the inhibiting coating is highly dependent on the barrier properties of the 
protective layer, and an ideal coating should form a barrier against corrosive agents. Still, 
it should release enough inhibitor to form the protective layer.  
 
3.4 Sacrificial coatings 
Sacrificial coatings are metallic coatings, almost entirely limited to zinc and aluminum 
[38], which rely on the principle of galvanic protection by use of a metallic sacrificial an-
ode [15],[39]. The metallic sacrificial anode is more electronegative than the substrate, 
acting as the cathode, which it protects. The sacrificial anode will thus oxidize more read-
ily than the substrate [39],[40]. 
These coatings must be, as with inhibiting coatings, applied as primers, as they will only 
be effective if in direct contact with the substrate [15]. Furthermore, there must be a me-
tallic contact between the individual protective particles. Thus, the pigment volume con-
centration of sacrificial coatings is usually very high, just under the critical pigment vol-
ume concentration [15]. 
In the case of a sacrificial zinc coating, the zinc will produce an anodically active primer. 
The resistance towards corrosion depends on the transfer of galvanic current by the zinc 
primer. The protection provided will last if the conductivity is preserved and sufficient zinc 
acts as an anode. When the system is damaged, the zinc corrodes, and a zinc corrosion 
product is formed. The zinc corrosion product seals the pores between the zinc particles 
within the coating, and the system is no longer electrically conductive. However, the zinc 
corrosion product provides a barrier effect towards the environment [15]. 
 
4 Epoxy coatings 
The history of epoxies dates back as far as 1863, but its commercial use has roots in the 
late 1930s and early 1940s, when Pierre Castan in Switzerland and Sylvan Greenlee in 
the US, independently of each other, synthesized the first epoxy resins from bisphenol A 
and epichlorohydrin [41],[42]. 
==PAGE:20==
Page 9 of 118 
Since its development, this type of epoxy resin is still the most widely used commercial 
epoxy resin, and the most significant single-use end for epoxy resins is in coatings [42]. 
Epoxy coatings are known for their mechanical properties, and they generally consist of 
two distinct elements: (1) an epoxy resin and (2) a polyamine hardener. They exhibit vari-
ous excellent characteristics: exceptional adhesion, corrosion and chemical resistance, 
high strength and toughness, good heat resistance, and electrical properties [42]. Epoxy 
coatings are thus widely used as corrosion-resistant primers, and their resistance to 
chemicals makes them suitable in highly corrosive environments.  
 
In this section, epoxy coatings, as well as the chemistry of epoxy resins, will be explored. 
Epoxy coatings are especially interesting, as this type of coating will be used later in the 
experimental work.

4.2 Solventborne epoxy coatings 
A SB epoxy coating is generally comprised of four key ingredients: (1) epoxy resin, (2) 
curing agent, (3) film formation aid, and (4) additives. However, epoxy coatings may also 
commonly include more ingredients like solvents, adhesion promoters, or UV stabilizers. 
On the other hand, an epoxy coating containing only two ingredients (resin and curing 
agent) is also commonly found. It is also common to find more than one specific epoxy 
resin and curing agent within one coating formulation [44]. 
 
An epoxy coating can either be classified as a one component system or a two compo-
nent system. In a one component system, the curing agent and the epoxy resin can be 
combined well in advance of application because the curing, at ambient temperatures, 
will occur at a very slow rate and sometimes not even occur. The curing process will 
speed up only after heat is applied after application of the coating to the substrate [45]. In 
a two component system, resin and curing agent must be kept apart until just before ap-
plication. This is because the chemical curing reactions initiates immediately upon mix-
ing. Consequently, two component epoxy coatings can only be applied within a limited 
time after mixing the curing agent and the resin. This time interval is usually referred to 
as the pot life of a coating [44].

binders, or (2) solvent evaporation followed by chemical cross-linking reactions, applies 
for thermoset binders [46],[47].

4.3 Waterborne epoxy coatings 
Like SB epoxy coatings, WB epoxy coatings are comprised of epoxy resin, curing agent, 
and other additives which give the coating its desired properties. As the name indicates, 
the difference is that water is used as a solvent instead of the traditional organic com-

hydrophobic and thus not very compatible with water as a solvent. The first two strate-

and (2) using a solid epoxy resin that comes in an epoxy-water dispersion. These strate-

formulation of WB epoxy coatings, as they both create and stabilize the emulsion by

4.3.4 Main challenges 
One of the main challenges associated with WB coatings is the presence of surfactants. 
One challenge is the weaker links formed within the dried coating, leading to increased 
water sensitivity. This is especially disadvantageous for the anti-corrosive properties of 
the coating. Furthermore, if smaller surfactants are used, they may migrate to the surface 
of the coating, further increasing the water sensitivity of the coating. Moreover, this phe-
nomenon may also affect inter-coat adhesion [45].  
Due to these challenges, a minimal concentration of surfactants is preferred. High shear-
mixing may also be utilized to relieve these problems and to achieve appropriate micelle 
sizes and a stable emulsion. Furthermore, surfactants containing reactive epoxy groups 
may be utilized to counteract the increased water sensitivity coming along with the use of 
surfactants. This will “lock” the surfactants in place and thus prevent the surfactants from 
migrating to the top of the coating surface [45]. 
 
5 Adhesion 
Adhesion is one of the most important properties of an anti-corrosive coating. It ensures 
that the coating will physically stay on the substrate and that the coating maintains good 
contact with the substrate throughout its service life.  
 
5.1 Adhesion theories 
The mechanisms of adhesion are not yet fully understood [51], but four adhesion theo-
ries have been proposed: (1) Mechanical theory, (2) electrostatic (electronic) theory, (3) 
diffusion theory, and (4) adsorption/surface reaction theory [52]. These theories relate 
the physical and chemical properties of materials to the actual strength of an adhesive 
bond. 
 
The four traditional adhesion theories will be explored in the following sections. 
 
==PAGE:26==
Page 15 of 118 
5.1.1 Mechanical theory 
The penetration of the coating into pores, cavities, and other irregularities on the sub-
strate surface facilitates the adhesion mechanism supported by this theory. When the 
coating cures, it provides mechanical means by which the forces across the interface can 
be transmitted [53]. A simple illustrations of a mechanically interlocking adhesive can be 
seen in Figure 5.1 [54]. 
 
Figure 5.1: Illustration of mechanical interlocking [54]. 
Generally, it applies that rougher surfaces will provide a stronger adhesion. Additionally, 
it is crucial, for good adhesion to be formed, that the adhesive has an appropriately low 
viscosity. This will allow the adhesive to flow freely into the pores, cavities, and other ir-
regularities on the substrate surface. If the viscosity of the adhesive is too high, air may 
be trapped within these irregularities of the substrate surface. This may lessen the adhe-
sion strength [53].  
 
The adhesion bonds that this theory focuses on are on a microscopic scale, and the spe-
cific surface roughness of the substrate can be detected using optical and electron mi-
croscopy [55]. 
 
5.1.2 Electrostatic theory 
The electrostatic theory postulates that adhesion is by electrostatic effects between the 
coating and the substrate [52]. It is believed that the electrostatic adhesive forces contrib-
ute very little, in comparison to the Van der Waals (VdW) forces, to the total adhesive 
forces [56]. 
According to this theory, adhesion is caused by forces, in the form of an electrical double 
layer at the coating-substrate interface. These forces will primarily be caused by disper-
sion forces that arise from the interaction of permanent dipoles, and they will only be op-
erational at very small distances (less than 0.5 nm) [56]. 
The theory of electrostatic adhesion is supported by the findings of electrical discharges 
observed when an adhesive and a substrate are pulled apart. Electrostatic adhesive 
forces have, however, also been observed without breaking the bonds [56]. 
 
5.1.3 Diffusion theory  
Diffusion theory primarily applies to systems where the adhesive and the substrate are 
polymeric materials made from larger, more mobile polymers.  
==PAGE:27==
Page 16 of 118 
The theory relies on the solubility of the two materials being bonded together. It claims 
that when two materials are soluble in one another, they will be bonded together by inti-
mate contact. This will happen because they, at the point of contact, will form a solution, 
and thus an adhesive interphase consisting of both coating and substrate will be formed. 
The adhesion between the two materials will be improved when the materials have the 
same solubility parameter [57]. In Figure 5.2 a depiction of diffusive adhesion on a mo-
lecular level is displayed. 
 
Figure 5.2: Depiction of diffusive adhesion. 
Solvent welding is an example of adhesion supported by the diffusion theory. It includes 
two materials which are bonded by the presence of a solvent, allowing molecules to dif-
fuse into one another, thus forming an interphase upon evaporation of the solvent. Ultra-
sonic welding is another example of adhesion supported by this theory. In this form of 
adhesion heat is utilized to enable polymer chains of the adhesive and the adherend to 
diffuse into one another [57].  
 
5.1.4 Adsorption/surface reaction theory 
The adsorption theory proposes that adhesion between coating and substrate is obtained 
when intimate contact between the two is made. This will allow for pairwise interaction of 
the atoms/molecules on the coating and substrate. It is thought to be the VdW forces that 
constitute the most to the adhesion by adsorption. The VdW forces can further be divided 
into London, Debeye, and Keesom types. The type of VdW force is dependent on 
whether none (London type), one (Debeye type), or both (Keesom type) of the involved 
atoms/molecules of an interaction pose electric dipoles [58].  
The adhesive forces within this theory are only capable of strong bonding when the ad-
hesive and the adherend are in very close contact, and separation by only a few Ang-
strom will cause the adhesion to fail [56]. Thus, a crucial part of good adhesion, accord-
ing to this theory, is good wetting. Poor wetting will result in poor adhesion, and it may 
also result in air being trapped between the coating and substrate. This weakens the 
VdW forces or completely prevents the formation of these. A good wetting, however, will 
ensure a continuous layer of adhesive, which will promote adhesion by adsorption be-
cause it allows for intimate contact to be established throughout the entire interface 
==PAGE:28==
Page 17 of 118 
between coating and substrate. This ultimately leads to establishing VdW forces through-
out the interface [56]. 
 
Adhesion through adsorption can also occur by forming covalent or ionic chemical 
bonds. These chemical bonds are much stronger than the VdW interactions and will, as 
with the adhesion by VdW interactions, be promoted by a continuous film of coating on 
the substrate.

5.2 Parameters which can affect adhesion 
The parameters which affect the adhesion between coating and substrate may be either 
chemical, physical, or mechanical. Some parameters may be inherent to the materials 
used, like the surface tension and energy of the coating. Still, three basic requirements of 
good adhesion are [59]: 
• 
Good contact between the coating and the substrate 
• 
The absence of a weak boundary layer 
• 
Absence of stress after drying, curing, and during service 
 
One of the most critical parameters affecting adhesion is the wetting of the substrate sur-
face by the coating. This phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 5.3. 
==PAGE:29==
Page 18 of 118 
 
Figure 5.3: Illustration of poor and good wetting. 
Figure 5.3 shows that good wetting is obtained when the contact angle is of a lower 
value,  and complete, spontaneous wetting occurs when the contact angle is 0°.  
When the coating is adequately wetted, it allows for a continuous coating film to form. 
This will allow for more VdW interactions, more chemical bonds, and an increased inter-
face area as it allows coating to flow freely into all irregularities of the substrate surface. 
All of this ultimately enhances the adhesion. 
 
Another factor that can affect the adhesion is the viscosity of the coating. Generally, a 
lower viscosity will provide better wetting and, thus, better adhesion. A lower viscosity will 
allow for better mechanical bonding and may give rise to dispersion forces [59]. 
The rate at which the solvent evaporates may also influence the adhesion. If the solvent 
evaporates very fast, it may start evaporating during application to the extent that the 
coating will quickly become too viscous, negatively affecting the adhesion [59]. 
The cleanliness of the substrate surface may also play a significant role in whether a 
coating will adhere strongly or not. It has been documented in many studies that the 
coating will adhere more strongly to a clean metal surface. This is because of the tensile 
strength of the polymer chains within the coating. This will be larger for polymers retained 
at the interface by adsorption of polar groups onto the metal oxide surface or by London 
forces between polymer chains and the substrate than for polymers within the bulk of the 
coating [59].  
If the substrate contains contaminants, which may include oily materials, salt crystals, 
and plain dust and dirt, the polymer chains will not be able to be retained at these points, 
which ultimately will result in a weaker adhesion [59]. 
 
When comparing WB and SB epoxy coatings, the WB coatings are much more at risk of 
exhibiting poor adhesion. SB coatings are due to the lower surface tensions observed for 
==PAGE:30==
Page 19 of 118 
organic solvents compared to water. Furthermore, the cleanliness of the substrate may 
have a more significant effect on WB coatings because residue, like oil, on the substrate 
can be dissolved in the organic solvent and not the water. Thus, a continuous film can be 
formed by SB coatings regardless of potential contaminants [60].  
 
A mechanical effect may be stress relief. It is a phenomenon that can occur in two cases. 
The first may arise when a plastic coating requires a paint bake and a subsequent cool-
ing cycle. This will cause the coating to be exposed to temperature changes, thus under-
going dimensional changes. If the coating is not flexible enough, this can cause stress to 
the system, which is relieved through the loss of adhesion [59]. 
The second case, which can cause adhesion loss due to stress relief, is caused by the 
curing of the coating. When the coating cures, it goes from being a liquid to a solid by a 
cross-linking reaction. This will accumulate little unrelieved stress. As the coating be-
comes stiffer, the stress will accumulate due to the growing polymeric network. If this ac-
cumulated stress is not relieved, the loss of adhesion may occur when the coating is ex-
posed to the service environment [59]. 
 
5.3 Analysis methods 
Adhesion is arguably one of the most essential properties of a protective coating, and 
several different methods have been developed to analyze adhesion between coating 
and substrate. One of the most used methods is the tape test (standardized as ASTM3 
D3359 through ASTM D3387 [61]). In this test, cuts are made through the coating to the 
substrate, and pressure-sensitive tape is subsequently adhered onto the cuts. The tape 
is quickly ripped off the coating, and results are assessed based on how much of the 
coating is removed from the substrate. Figure 5.4 shows the basic setup for a tape test.

The advantage of this test is that it is a straightforward and not very costly test to per-

very dependent on the skill and experience of the operator. Furthermore, it depends on 
the type of tape, and the stickiness of the tape used may produce different results de-
pending on the tape used [59],[61].

Another commonly used test is the knife test. This test uses a utility knife, firstly, to cut an 
X-cut into the coating, and subsequently pick at the edges of this cut. It is evaluated 
based on two parameters; (1) how difficult it is to remove the coating from the substrate, 
and (2) how much of the coating can be removed. Like the tape test, this is a highly sub-
jective test dependent on the operator. Moreover, this test may produce erroneous re-
sults due to the nature of the coating. Brittle coatings may fracture very easily upon cut-
ting and thus exhibit weaker adhesion than coatings with a high degree of cohesive 
strength [62]. 
 
A more quantitative method is the pull-off adhesion test. This test is standardized in ISO 
4624:2016, "Paints and varnishes - Pull-off test for adhesion". The standard describes 
how to assess the adhesion of a single coating (or a multi-coating) system by measuring 
the tensile stress necessary to break the adhesive bond between coating and substrate 
or to cause cohesive failure within the coating [63].  
To perform this test, a fixture, usually referred to as a dolly or a stub, is fastened to the 
coating using a strong adhesive. A pull-off tester is then loaded increasingly until the fix-
ture is pulled off the substrate. The setup used for this test is illustrated in Figure 5.7.  
 
Figure 5.7: Pull off test setup [64]. 
When the dolly is pulled off the coating, the measured pull-off strength is noted, and it is 
assessed whether the failure mode is adhesive failure between coating and substrate or 
cohesive failure within the coating. The failure mode may also be adhesive failure be-
tween the adhesive used for fastening the dolly to the coating, or cohesive failure within 
this adhesive. In many cases more than one failure mode may be observed [63],[65].  
 
To test the shear stress of the coating, a test like a scrape test can be performed. This 
test involved application of a layer of uniform thickness onto a flat panel, usually a piece 
of sheet metal. When the coating has fully cured, the panel is placed beneath a rounded 
stylus or loop and pushed around. The stylus or loop is loaded increasingly until the coat-
ing is removed from the substrate. This test is usually performed in laboratories. Many 
==PAGE:33==
Page 22 of 118 
parameters may affect this test, such as the coating thickness, the extent of cure, and 
the uniformity of the coating and the substrate surface. Moreover, this test is limited to 
testing coatings on smooth, flat panels.  
 
Applicable for all the tests mentioned above are that they all are very subjective to the 
operator which makes them very difficult to replicate. Moreover, two methods rely on ad-
hering items onto the coating, and it is thus important that the adhesive used is strong 
enough. If not, these tests will not only measure the adhesion between coating and sub-
strate but will also partially test the adhesion between fixture and coating. It is also note-
worthy that all the above-mentioned tests only test the strength of the adhesive bond be-
tween coating and substrate by relying on breaking the adhesive bonds.  
 
However, if the chemical composition of the interface is of interest, other test methods 
must be utilized. One method could be to use scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The 
SEM method can achieve resolutions of 10 nm, and some advanced methods can even 
achieve resolutions as small as 2.5 nm [66]. SEM works by radiating a low energy elec-
tron beam to the material from a so-called electron gun. When the electrons reach the 
surface of the specimen to be analyzed, they will interact with its surface and thus emit 
protons from the surface or near the surface. This is depicted in Figure 5.8.  
An image of the surface is then generated by detecting the electron-surface interactions. 
Different detectors can be selected depending on the selected operating mode [67].  
One of the properties of the SEM is its ability to perform energy dispersive X-ray spec-
troscopy (EDX). The principal of EDX is that upon interaction between the electrons emit-
ted from the beam, X-ray photons are emitted. By detecting these, the elemental compo-
sition of the specimen can be calculated [68].  
This method was utilized in the study “Enhancement of the adhesion and anticorrosive 
properties of an epoxy coating by the application of a cerium based film” by LoperenaI, 
Lehr, and Saidman, wherein a cerium-based film was synthesized on an alloy surface to 
improve the adhesion of an epoxy resin to that same alloy surface. SEM and EDX was 
used to study the morphology and chemical compositions of the samples used in the 
study both from a surface and a cross-sectional view [69].  
 
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) may be used to get an even higher resolution. 
This type of microscopy usually achieves resolutions within the range of 0.1 - 0.2 nm 
[70]. TEM works like the SEM, but the electron gun is located within a vacuum chamber. 
The electrons will in less dense areas of the specimen completely pass through, and in 
more dense regions, the electrons scatter (i.e. are absorbed) [67]. The basic principles of 
TEM are illustrated in  Figure 5.8.  
Both SEM and TEM have previously been utilized in studies of the adhesion interface, 
where they were used to investigate a cross-sectional area of coating [71],[72].

Another analysis method is to use X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, a quantitative spec-
troscopic technique that allows for determining the elemental composition of a specimen. 
It works by irradiating a beam of X-rays at the test specimen while simultaneously meas-
uring the number of electrons that escape from the top 1-10 nm of the specimen as well 
as the kinetic energy. However, it is important to note that for XPS analyses to be suc-
cessful, ultrahigh-vacuum conditions are required. This is because the electrons escap-
ing from the test specimen surface must not collide with other background gasses on 
their way from the specimen surface to the detector. 
The result is a XPS spectra that can be evaluated and interpreted [74]. The ability of this 
method to only analyze the top 1-10 nm is what makes it very suitable for studying the 
adhesion interface. In Figure 5.9 a simple depiction of how XPS analysis works is dis-

In the category of spectroscopic techniques, time-of-flight secondary ion mass spectrom-
etry (ToF-SIMS) also belong. The concept of this spectroscopic analysis is that the sur-
face of a test specimen is bombarded with high energy ions. This will cause secondary 
ions to be emitted from the surface, which is then detected. The ions are analyzed based 
on the time it takes for the ions to fly from the specimen surface to the detector. As with 
XPS, ToF-SIMS requires a high vacuum environment to prevent the emitted ions from 
colliding with background gasses [77]. 
An example of the use of this analysis method is the study "ToF-SIMS depth profiling of a 
complex polymeric coating employing a C60 sputter source" by Hinder, Lowe, and Watts. 
In this study, ToF-SIMS was used to obtain depth profiles in the outer nanometers of a 
pVdF topcoat [78]. 
 
The methods available for analyzing the adhesion interface itself are, however, not per-
fect. Technologies like SEM and TEM requires a cross-section to be cut, which may not 
always without complications, as the cutting itself may case interruptions to the interface. 
XPS and ToF-SIMS require the adhesive layer to, ideally, be peeled off the substrate to 
analyze it, or alternatively to be cut at an angle very close to parallel with the coating 
layer to expose the largest possible area of the interface. 
 
6 Summary 
Throughout this literature study, the basics of coating technology has been laid. The con-
tents of the coating has been explained. Binders are polymers which give name to the 
coating, and also gives the coating its adhesive properties as well as many other im-
portant properties. It can be either thermoset or thermoplastic depending on its mecha-
nism of film formation. Solvents give the coating its appropriate viscosity for application. 
For a long time various hazardous organic solvents were used, but in recent years the 
solventless, WB, and high solid coatings have been of interest to help protect human 
health and the environment. Fillers are added to the coating formula for many reasons, 
among other for rheological purposes and to reduce the cost of the coating. Pigments 
are also categorized as fillers, and the main purpose of the pigments is to help achieve 
the desired aesthetics for the coating. Finally there are additives which are added in 
small amounts to overcome defects or to provide the coating with a desired property.  
 
Anti-corrosive protective coatings were then explored by, firstly exploring the phenome-
non of corrosion. Corrosion is an electrochemical naturally occurring reaction that re-
quires the establishment of a corrosion cell. This requires an anode, a cathode, a 
==PAGE:36==
Page 25 of 118 
metallic path, and an electrolyte. Furthermore, it was found that the presence of both wa-
ter and oxygen is crucial for the corrosion reaction to occur.  
Anti-corrosive coatings and the mechanisms they utilize were then explored. Barrier 
coatings were able to act as both primer, intermediate, and topcoats. They prevented 
corrosion by creating a physical barrier between the substrate and water, oxygen and 
other corrosive agents, and their anti-corrosive abilities could be improved by increasing 
the thickness of the coating. The inhibiting coatings and the sacrificial coatings were both 
primarily used as primers, as they could only work if in direct contact with the metal sub-
strate. Inhibiting coatings passivate the substrate surface by forming a passivation layer 
which then acts as a barrier layer, and sacrificial coatings rely on the principle of galvanic 
protection by use of a sacrificial metallic anode.  
A large group of protective coatings are epoxy coatings, known for their great properties 
like exceptional adhesion, and high strength and toughness. They are all, as their name 
indicates, based on epoxy resin, the most common being synthesized from bisphenol A 
and ephichlorohydrin.  
Epoxy coatings can be both SB and WB. The SB epoxy coatings are the traditional ones. 
They cure by evaporation of the solvent which results in the coalescence of curing agent 
and epoxy resin. The epoxy resin cross-links, and a film is formed. In SB coatings, the 
epoxy and curing agent can be directly dissolved, but for WB epoxy coatings it is not 
nearly as simple. Two main types of WB epoxy coatings have been developed: (1) Using 
liquid epoxy dispersed in water and (2) using solid epoxy dispersed in water. It is evident 
that for both these types, an emulsion must be made. For the first type, the curing agent 
used also acts as an emulsifier, but for the second type, surfactants must be added to 
the formulation to produce a stable emulsion. The addition of surfactants can pose many 
challenges for WB coatings, among other the formation of weaker links within the dried 
coating. Furthermore, if smaller surfactants are used, they can migrate to the top of the 
coating. Both phenomena will increase the water sensitivity of the dried coating, and thus 
it will exhibit poorer anti-corrosive properties. The presence of surfactants may also af-
fect the intercoat adhesion.  
 
One of the most important properties of an anti-corrosive coating is its adhesive proper-
ties. Adhesion is a complex phenomenon, and it is not yet fully understood, but four theo-
ries are well known and accepted:  Mechanical interlocking, diffusion theory, and adsorp-
tion/surface reaction theory.  
% Mechanical interlocking occurs when the liquid coating penetrates irregularities in the 
substrate surface, giving the coating mechanical means by which forces across the inter-
face can be transmitted. The electrostatic theory proposes that adhesion occurs through 
electrostatic effects. Diffusion theory is mostly applicable for systems where both coating 
and substrate consists of long, mobile polymer chains which can diffuse into one another 
by means of solvent or heat. Finally, adsorption/surface reaction theory postulates that 
==PAGE:37==
Page 26 of 118 
adhesion occurs due to dispersion forces or actual chemical bonds between the coating 
and substrate. 
It is important to note that adhesion is usually a consequence of a combination of these 
theories.  
The parameters which may affect adhesion where then discussed. These may be proper 
wetting of the substrate, the viscosity of the coating upon application, and the cleanliness 
of the substrate. Furthermore, it was discussed that these parameters may affect the WB 
coatings significantly more than the SB coatings due to the nature of the organic solvents 
used.. They may be more forgiving as they promote better wetting and are able to dis-
solve some contaminants on the substrate surface. 
 
Finally, methods to evaluate adhesion were explored like the tape test, knife test, scrape 
test, and the pull of test. However, all of these were found to rely on breaking the adhe-
sive bond, to be difficult to recreate, and rely heavily on the subjectivity and skill of the 
operator. Furthermore, these tests were only able to evaluate the strength of the adhe-
sive bond.  
If the adhesive interface itself were to be analyzed, tests like SEM, TEM, XPS, and ToF-
SIMS were found to be more suitable. SEM and TEM were both able to analyze a cross 
section of the coating, and XPS and ToF-SIMS were able to analyze the interface itself. 
These methods do, however, also have some weaknesses. In cutting the cross section 
of the coating used for SEM and TEM the interface itself may be damages, and ideally, 
for XPS and ToF-SIMS, the coating should be completely ripped off the substrate which 
may be difficult. Alternatively, it should be cut at an angle very close to parallel with the 
coating surface which may be hard to achieve.

7 Hypotheses 
Based on the conclusions drawn from the literature study, three hypotheses are pro-
posed: 
1. The adhesion of SB epoxy coatings is less sensitive to small particulate contami-
nants than that of WB epoxy. 
2. It will be possible to observe a difference in SB and WB epoxy coatings by utiliz-
ing adhesion analyses methods like pull off test and SEM analysis.  
3. Via SEM/EDS analysis, a difference in chemical composition, will be observed for 
the SB epoxy coatings and the WB coatings near the interface.  
 
In the following experiments, these hypotheses will be tested.

8.1 Components of the coating 
In this section the components of the coatings will be explained further.  
 
8.1.1 Binder 
The binder used for the coating formulations is a bisphenol A diglycidylether epoxy. This 
is an organic compound which is synthesized from a reaction of a bisphenol A compound 
with an ephichlorohydrin compound as shown in Figure 8.1. 
 
==PAGE:41==
Page 30 of 118 
 
 
Figure 8.1: Reaction of bisphenol A with ephichlorohydrin to form bisphenol A diglycidyl ether 
epoxy [79]. 
This type of epoxy resin is a thermosetting resin, usually cured with polyamines, amino-
amides, or phenolic compounds. It is also possible to synthesize a bisphenol A diglcidyl 
ether of higher molecular weight with a structure as shown in Figure 8.2. 
 
Figure 8.2: Structure of a higher molecular weight bisphenol A diglycidyl epoxy resin [42]. 
The bisphenol A diglycidyl ether epoxy used in this formulation, is, however, of a lower 
molecular weight with n values of 0, 1, 2, or maybe 3. The lower molecular weight results 
in a more liquid epoxy resin.  
 
8.1.2 Curing agent 
In this case, the curing agents are the two amine adducts. These are made by reacting 
excess primary amines with epoxy [80]. The purpose of using an amine adduct instead of 
pure amines is twofold.  
Firstly, amine adducts are used to avoid amine blushing. Amine blushing is a chemical 
reaction occurring between water, carbon dioxide, and the amine curing agents in an 
epoxy coating. It can result in white patches or a milky, hazy effect in clear coatings [81], 
and in some cases may also result in a sticky, oily, or waxy appearance on the coating 
surface [82],[83]. Amine blushing can ultimately affect the coating performance. Espe-
cially the overcoatability can be affected negatively, resulting in a poor adhesion of a po-
tential subsequent coating layer, and thus influencing the mechanical properties and the 
desired aesthetics of the final coating [81]. Amine adducts are less hygroscopic and have 
lower vapor pressures than pure amines, and are thus less sensitive to moisture, and are 
thus less prone to amine blushing.

The second reason for using amine adducts is that epoxy resins are hydrophobic and 
that amines are hydrophilic. By combining the pure amines with a small amount of epoxy 
resin, the resulting amine adduct will have a higher compatibility with the epoxy binder.

9.1 Free film analysis 
When the coatings applied to the polycerate film were fully cured, a free film was ob-
tained by peeling the coating from the plastic sheets. The free films were then analyzed 
by IR spectroscopy and SEM and EDX. 
 
9.1.1 IR spectroscopy 
An IR spectrum was obtained from both the coating-substrate interface side, referred to 
as the backside, and the coating-air interface side, referred to as the frontside. The pur-
pose of this analysis was to investigate whether a difference in chemical composition 
could be observed for the SB and the WB coating. 
The IR spectrum obtained from the WB and SB coating, is displayed in Figures 9.1a and 
9.1b, respectively.  
It is generally observed that these spectra exhibit the same tendencies. Firstly, it is ob-
served that the two spectra obtained from the coatings with pigment exhibit a single 
broad peak towards the lowest wave numbers, app. in the interval 785 to 500 cm-1. As 
this peak is not observed for the coatings without pigment, it is concluded that this is the 
peak for TiO2.  
It is observed that the TiO2 signal is much stronger for the WB coatings than for the SB 
coatings. This is as expected as the PVC is 14.86% for the WB coating and only 7.58% 
for the SB coating (see Table 8.3).  
 
For all spectra a broad peak in the interval 3691 to 2112 cm−1 is observed. This peak 
may stem from C-H vibrations from alkenes, alkynes, or aromates. However, it most 
likely stems from the aromatic rings within the backbone of the epoxy resin. N-H vibra-
tions also contribute to this peak. These are present in the coating if there is some unre-
acted curing agent left. Finally, O-H vibrations could also contribute to this peak. These 
functional groups may come from reacted epoxide groups, which, when reacted, form a 
hydroxyl group. 
In the range 3082 to 3015 cm−1, a small peak can be observed. This peak comes from 
sp3 C-H vibrations (i.e. alkanes), and must therefore stem from the epoxy resin. The 
same applies for the more prominent broad peak to the right of this smaller peak. 
Finally, it is observed, that when the wavenumber decreases, there are more peaks. 
These mostly lie within the fingerprint region, i.e., below a wavenumber of 1500 cm−1. 
In Figure 9.2, the wavenumber axis has been adjusted from 1700 to 500 cm−1, to better 
visualize the peaks within this region.

This indicates that the level to which the WB coating can protect the panel is very de-
pendent on the thickness of the coating. Furthermore, the indication is that if the coating 
is too thick, it will only provide poor protection, but if it is too thin, it will also provide poor 
protection. It has thus been found that there must be an ideal DFT for the WB coating 
somewhere between 55.33 and 63.52 μm. This phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 9.9. 
It should be noted that this illustration is based on qualitative observations. 
 
Figure 9.9: Curve illustrating the level of protection provided by the WB coating as a function of 
the DFT. It is important to note, that this curve is made based on qualitative observations from the 
two standard panels and the two flash rusted panels with the WB coating without pigment.  
This phenomenon is most likely caused by the binder network within these coatings. For 
thinner coatings, it is thought that the binder network will have fully formed, but for the 
thicker coatings the binder network will not have fully formed. This will allow for easier 
penetration of water, and thus poor protection. However, if the coating becomes too thin, 
the water will have a shorter distance to penetrate to reach the panel, thus also providing 
a poor protection.  
 
The DFTs are, however, measured with standard deviations which overlap. It is, for ex-
ample, read from Table 9.8, that the standard deviation of the DFT of the coating on 
panel 14 is 20.95 μm, meaning that the coating on panel 14 could potentially be thicker in 
some places than in the coating on panel 13. It can thus be discussed whether these 
measured DFTs differ from each other. However, the trends observed point to the validity 
of the phenomenon just discussed.

When investigating Figure 9.15a, it is found that the WB coating without pigment exhibits 
higher pull-off strengths than the coating with pigment. This applies for all the panels ex-
cept for the oil contaminated panels. This is most prominent for the clean and the high 
roughness panels. However, for the oil contaminated panels the measured pull-off 
strengths are very similar for all coatings, and when taking the standard deviations into 
account, the difference is almost nonexistent.  
The phenomenon of the coating without pigment exhibiting a decreased pull-off strength 
compared to that with pigment is not as expected as pigments often mechanically rein-
force coatings. However, for the pigments to properly provide increased mechanical 
properties to the coating, the pigments and the binder need to have properly interact with 
each other. 
In literature, it has been found that a WB coating with glass spheres, used to emulate 
pigments, exhibits a higher degree of water permeability than that of a WB coating with-
out glass spheres. This was shown to be caused by a layer around the pigment facilitat-
ing water transport and thus increasing the effective diffusion constant because of lower 
interactions between binder and pigments [91]. This layer could potentially also cause 
the decreased pull-off tensile strengths measured for the WB coating with pigment.  
 
It is important to note that the error bars in bar charts (Figures 9.15a and 9.15b) somewhat 
overlap. It can thus be discussed whether there is an actual difference in pull-off 
strengths is observed. It must, however, be concluded that the observed tendencies in 
the data which point to the above discussed results.

as for the coatings without pigment observed that when the atomic% of iron decreases, 
that of carbon and oxygen increases. However, it is observed that the atomic% of tita-
nium (i.e., the pigment) does not increase until a little after the carbon and oxygen has 
started to increase. This indicates that there, as observed is a layer wherein no pigment 
seems to be present. This area is indicated with a dashed line in Figure 9.23c, and it is 
observed that the titanium level, as well as the carbon and oxygen level, will first become

It is also observed that the titanium level in the commercial WB coating will not start to 
increase before the atomic% of iron has decreased almost to 0. This indicates that there 
is, just as for the WB coating with pigment, a region near the substrate where the tita-
nium particles are not present. This region is, however not as well defined visually as it is 
for the WB coating with pigment. This indicates that there, in this region may be other 
filler particles present, and that the effect causing this phenomenon may only affect the 
titanium particles.  
 
These observations could possibly indicate an exclusion zone (EZ), i.e., a zone in which 
colloidal and molecular solutes are excluded [92]. The mechanisms of formation of these 
zones are not yet fully understood, but it is thought to be caused by the formation of a 
fourth phase of water [93]. This phase forms near highly hydrophilic surfaces like the pol-
ymer nafion and hydrogels such polyethylene alcohol, gelatin, and starch [92],[94]. Some 
studies have even observed the formation of an EZ near metal surfaces such as zinc, 
aluminum, lead, tungsten, tin, gold, and platinum [95],[96]. Moreover, it has been ob-
served that the EZ can grow to measure several hundred micrometers [92],[94],[96]. 
However, this may be unlikely as the indication, observed from the WB commercial coat-
ing, is that the effect observed may only affect the titanium particles, and leave other par-
ticles unaffected.

10    Conclusions 
Throughout this project, a literature study was conducted leading to three proposed hy-
potheses: 
1. The adhesion of SB epoxy coatings is less sensitive to small particulate contami-
nants than that of WB epoxy. 
2. It will be possible to observe a difference in SB and WB epoxy coatings by utiliz-
ing adhesion analyses methods like pull off test and SEM analysis.  
3. Via SEM/EDS analysis, a difference in chemical composition, will be observed for 
the SB epoxy coatings and the WB coatings near the interface.  
 
To test these hypotheses, IR spectra were obtained for free films of the WB and SB coat-
ings with and without pigment. From these, it was found that the titanium peak was much 
stronger for the WB coating than the SB coating. This was expected as the PVCs of the 
WB and SB coatings were 14.86 and 7.58%, respectively. However, no other significant 
difference could be detected between the WB and SB coating.  
The free films were also analyzed via SEM and EDX. From these analyses, it was found 
that no significant difference could be visually observed through the SEM images.  
However, from the EDX line scans, a higher atomic% of oxygen, and thus a lower 
atomic% of carbon, was observed for the WB coatings compared to the SB coatings. 
This may be caused by trapped solvent (i.e., water) in the coating.  
 
Investigations of the blister box panels throughout their exposure time revealed blisters 
and further corrosion in the form of black rust, on many of the panels.  
On the standard panels, only the WB commercial coatings exhibited blisters after 16 
days of exposure, whereas none of the remaining panels exhibited blisters, even after 20 
days of exposure.  
The flash rusted panels generally all exhibited blisters after just 7 days of exposure, indi-
cating that all coatings were equally sensitive to this form of surface contamination.  
On the oil contaminated panels, all WB panels exhibited defects in the form of blisters 
and black rust after 20 days of exposure. In contrast, none of the SB coatings exhibited 
defects on these panels. This indicated that the SB coatings were far less sensitive to the 
oil contaminated surfaces than the WB coatings.  
For the high roughness panels, the two WB commercial coating panels as well as one of 
the SB commercial panels were found to be more sensitive to the exposure than all other 
panels, and it exhibited blisters after 13 days of exposure.  
In conclusion, it was found that the SB coatings were less sensitive to oil contaminations, 
that all coatings were equally sensitive to flash rust, and that the WB commercial coat-
ings provided the least amount of protection on the standard and high roughness panels.  
 
From pull-off tests performed on the unexposed panels, it was found that the WB coating 
without pigment as well as the commercial WB coating on oil contaminated and flash 
==PAGE:120==
Page 109 of 118 
rusted panels exhibited 100% adhesive failure of the coating or very close to that. How-
ever, all remaining pull-off tests were mainly dominated by cohesive failure of the coating 
or adhesive failure between epoxy glue and coating or epoxy glue and dolly. This indi-
cated that the measured pull off strengths could not solely be regarded as adhesive 
strengths.  
For the SB commercial coatings on standard, oil contaminated, and high roughness pan-
els, it was found that the failure mode was 100% adhesive failure between the epoxy 
glue and the coating. This indicated that both the adhesive and the cohesive strength of 
the coating must be higher than the measured pull off strengths measured for these. Fur-
thermore, this indicated that the SB commercial coating was more sensitive to the flash 
rust than all other surfaces.  
 
Upon investigation of the measured pull-off strengths it was found that the WB coatings 
without pigment generally exhibited a higher pull off strength than those with pigment. 
This may be caused by a poor interaction between the pigment and the binder.  
In contrast, it was found for the SB coatings that the pigments provided the coating with 
mechanical strength resulting in higher pull-off strengths measured for the SB coatings 
with pigment compared to those without.  
Furthermore, it was found that the pull-off strengths measured for the WB coating with 
pigment were significantly lower than the pull-off strengths measured for SB with pig-
ment. When the WB coating without pigment was compared to the SB coating without 
pigment, it was observed that the WB pigment exhibited the highest pull off strengths on 
the clean and high roughness panels, but that the SB coating exhibited higher values on 
the oil contaminated and the flash rusted panels. This indicated that the SB coating was 
less sensitive to small particulate contaminants than the WB coating when they were not 
exposed to the blister box environment.  
 
Finally, it was indicated by observations made for the WB coatings without pigment on 
the standard and flash rusted panels, that the level of protection provided by these coat-
ings was very sensitive to variations in the DFTs, and it was observed that if the DFT 
was too thin, the coating would provide poor protection. In contrast, it was also found that 
a coating with a higher DFT would also provide poor protection. The ideal DFT was 
found to be somewhere in between 55 and 63 μm. 
 
From the pull-off tests performed on the exposed panels the failure modes observed 
were mainly 100% adhesive failure between the coating and the substrate except for the 
SB commercial coating on the clean and oil contaminated panels. This indicates that the 
adhesive strengths for the coating on these panels may be higher than the pull off 
strengths measured and that the SB coating performs very well on these two surfaces.  
 
==PAGE:121==
Page 110 of 118 
Moreover, it was generally observed that the SB commercial coating exhibited far higher 
pull-off strengths than the other coatings. This applies for all the panels except for the 
flash rusted panels where all coatings performed similarly, and for the high roughness 
panels where the WB coating without pigment performed incredibly well.  
Furthermore, it was generally observed that the WB coatings exhibited lower pull-off 
strengths than the SB coatings. Except for the WB coating without pigment on the high 
roughness panel.  
 
Finally, it was found from SEM/EDX analysis of the exposed blister box panels that a 
layer excluding titanium could be found in WB and the SB coatings with pigment as well 
as for both the commercial coatings. This layer was thicker for the WB coatings than for 
the SB coatings. The thickest area was observed for the WB coating with pigment on the 
oil contaminated panels, and the thinnest for the commercial SB coatings with pigment 
on the oil contaminated panels and the commercial SB coating on the flash rusted pan-
els. Furthermore, it was observed that this phenomenon was found on four of the oil con-
taminated and four flash rusted panels, but only on two standard panels and one high 
roughness panel. This indicated that the effect causing the formation of this layer is 
strongest on the contaminated and flash rusted panels.  
It was first thought, that this layer was an EZ caused by the formation of the fourth phase 
of water which, in literature, has been observed near hydrophilic surfaces. This theory 
was, however, quickly disproved, as the formation of the titanium excluding layer was 
also observed for the SB coatings.  
 
In conclusion, through the experiments performed and the analysis of the results, all 
three of the proposed hypotheses were confirmed.  
 
11    Future work 
For future studies within this topic, it would be of interest to perform the same experi-
ments and analyses as investigated within this project. Especially more panels to be ex-
posed in the blister box should be tested. This would generate more reliable results, and 
thus a deeper understanding of the coatings.  
Other types of exposure tests could also be performed such as salt spray and full immer-
sion tests. These would give further insight into how the coatings respond to exposure.  
 
Further investigations of the layer excluding titanium could also be conducted to get a 
better understanding of this layer, and to try to understand what parameters could affect 
this layer. Ultimately, leading to a method of controlling this layer.  
It could also be investigated whether this effect can be observed for other small particle 
fillers and pigments.  
 
==PAGE:122==
Page 111 of 118 
Finally, it could also be of interest to investigate the free films through XPS, as this gives 
a unique insight into the chemical composition of the very top layer of a surface. This 
could lead to an even greater understanding of the chemical differences between SB and 
WB coatings at the adhesive interface. It could, thus, ultimately lead to an understanding 
of what chemical compounds cause the poorer adhesion observed for WB coatings com-
pared to SB coatings.
