ABSTRACT
Magnesium alloys are characterized by an favorable combination of ductility and strength while
being light, biodegradable and biocompatible if alloyed with equally biocompatible element. Those
properties make them suitable for medical applications. The ZXK100 alloy, consisting of magnesium
with additions of zinc, calcium and zirconium, is investigated as a promising material for bone
fracture implants.
An important part in the synthesising of the alloy is hot deformation with
the main microstructural change the dynamic recrystallization mechanism, which aﬀects the alloys
macroscopic properties strongly.
The precise understanding of its evolution is therefore of high
interest for the material design. In this study, the 2D Cellular Automaton (CA) model describing
the dynamic recrystallization mechanism of ZXK100 was implemented for the ﬁrst time. The initial
microstructure was developed following a multistep nucleation and grain growth method, considering
the initial grain distribution and topography. The dynamic recrystallization evolution was deﬁned
by transformational rules that follow a probabilistic approach. Hot compression tests were used as
the input for the model as well as for its validation, whereas extrusion tests were conducted to assess
whether the simulated results can be transferred to diﬀerent manufacturing processes. Simulated
and experimental micrographs and ﬂow stress curves are strikingly similar at early stages of the
deformation, while the simulated grain growth at higher strains deviated from the experiments as a
consequence of irregular grain evolution. These results indicate that the method developed in this
project is highly promising for predicting the microstructure of ZXK100, providing vital information
for improving the design of the alloy and its properties.

Thesis overview
Magnesium alloys oﬀer advantageous properties for their use in lightweight applications such
as the automobile and aerospace industries. Their high strength-to-weight ratio, low density, non-
toxicity and biodegradability are also of high potential for medical applications. The ternary system
Mg-Zn-Ca proved successful for bone implants due to its optimum degradability rate and resistance.
The addition of Zr traces can further optimise its performance by reﬁning the microstructure, making
ZXK100 a very promising material.
When a material is plastically deformed at high temperatures the dynamic recrystallization
mechanism takes place, which is relevant for the microstructural evolution analysis. Understanding
this phenomenon may open up the gate to tailoring the mechanical properties of materials in an
optimised manner, as they are highly related to the material’s microstructure.
By conducting
metallographic analysis, one can obtain information on diﬀerent phases, morphologies, imperfections
and the manufacturing history of the material, which can be related to the macroscopic in-service
behaviour of the material.
Although metallographic analysis is without doubts a very powerful experimental tool, techno-
logical advances in computational materials provide a possibility of going one step further in the
characterization by complement the laboratory work with microstructural simulations deﬁned by
mathematical models. The possibility of predicting microstructures in advance can therefore lead
to a more eﬃcient material development. Combination of experimental and computational meth-
ods oﬀers a synergistic approach, where the simulations rely on experimental results and provide
guidance for further experimental design.
A Cellular Automaton (CA) model was implemented with the objective of predicting the mi-
crostructural evolution of ZXK100, which is used for biodegradable implants during hot deformation.
The simulations describe the dynamic recrystallization mechanism, analysing the grain nucleation
and growth taking place throughout the process. While diﬀerent models have been developed to
investigate other materials, this project will assess the alloy ZXK100 for the ﬁrst time. The program-
ming was carried out for deﬁned strain rate and temperature values, allowing for ﬂexibility from these
values within a deﬁned interval, according to a constitutive model. For the purpose of our simula-
tions, the initial microstructure was generated through a multi-step nucleation and growth method.

Microstructural heterogeneities, such as the initial grain growth following the lowest grain-boundary
energies, grain size distribution and grain topology, were also taken into account.
Hot compression tests were performed with two aims: to obtain the main input information for
the model and serve as output validation. Moreover, extrusion tests are conducted to determine
whether the model can predict microstructure results of a diﬀerent manufacturing process.

Master thesis aim
The main aim of the thesis is the implementation and validation of a CA model designed to predict
the microstructural evolution of ZXK100 when compressed at elevated temperatures. This model
will give detailed information on the dynamic recrystallization of ZXK100, providing insight into
its manufacturing process and material properties.

−III. Conduction of hot compression tests used as input information and output validation
−IV. Recrystallized fraction estimation at diﬀerent strains
−V. Model processing and validation
−VI. Evaluation of the model scalability when applied to a diﬀerent deformation process

Why magnesium? Origin and
role as biodegradable material
Magnesium was ﬁrst discovered in 1808 by the British chemist Sir Humphry Davy of London,
and electrolytically produced for the ﬁrst time by Michael Faraday in 1833, giving raise to promising
prospective possibilities and applications [1].
Especially after the London World Exhibition in 1863, where a variety of innovative magnesium
items and products were exposed, the material became highly popular. Magnesium was no longer
used only for wires, pyrotechnic and photography industry and started to revolutionize the market.
Regarding magnesium’s medical use, it was already in 1878 when Edward C. Huse practised in
vivo experiments with magnesium wires in human bodies, used as ligature for bleeding vessels. He
was already aware about the biodegradability of the material, enhancing the healing process while
being gradually substituted by the healthy human tissue, giving the implant a temporal function.
Furthermore, in 1900 Erwin Payr was already developing optimised shapes which allowed to use the
material in diﬀerent applications with higher success, as he could partially overcome the magnesium’s
brittleness. Diﬀerent experiments were performed during the next decades where tubes, arrows,
sheets, plates and rods where introduced, each of them designed for a diﬀerent purpose [2].
During the beginning of the 20th century, a signiﬁcant boost for the medical sector took place,
when a more methodological approach was developed. The Belgian Albin Lambotte introduced the
no-touch technique, which made surgeries more hygienic and aseptic [3]. He would also be the scien-
tist introducing the term ”osteosynthesis” [4], lasting until today. In 1906, one remarkable clinical
case taught him to not implant magnesium together with iron, after the generation of signiﬁcantly
big gas cavities in a patient’s body due to galvanic corrosion occurring between the magnesium
implant and the steel screws coupled with it [2].
Several researchers got inspired by these scientists and continued developing diﬀerent ideas such
as E.W. Andrews with the concept of absorbable metal clips in 1917 or G. Gossrau improving the
mechanical properties by meshing thin wires around a stronger guiding one in 1935 [2].

==PAGE:16==

Skeletal, vascular and muscular surgery were since then the main focus. However, it is also worth
to mention the research of G. Fontenier, who introduced the use of dissolvable anodes in bio-electric
batteries to feed pacemakers, implanted in dogs in 1975 with successful results [5].
Going back to the middle of the 20th century, the ﬁrst magnesium alloy was used, leaving behind
the highly corrosive non-alloyed magnesium. Troitskii and Tsitrin, employed Mg-Cd alloys to treat
pseudoarthrosis whereas Stone and Lord chose Mg-Al wires for aneurysm treatment [2]. Both shared
the objective of optimising magnesium through alloying, playing with diﬀerent compositions.
Nowadays, an extensive variety of magnesium alloys can be found in the market. However, the
commercialization of a suitable composition for implant use or any biomedical application in general
is at least challenging, due to the requirement of fulﬁlling several regulations and requirements
regarding mechanical properties, biodegradability, biocompatibility and controlled products release.
The system Mg-Zn-Ca has already proven outstanding results and beneﬁts, which can even be
improved with the addition of small amounts of zirconium.
The next chapter oﬀers a detailed
description of the speciﬁc advantages of using this quaternary system, oﬀering an understanding of
its huge interest for the biomedical ﬁeld.
This concept for implants that might sound new and innovative even though its origin goes
back to more than a century ago, still has to be improved and optimized to obtain the best of it.
Nevertheless, its accomplishment would mean a huge improvement in the quality of life for a great
number of people: post-surgery would become unnecessary, thrombosis risks lower, infections and
inﬂammations preventable, costs reduced and bleeding complications lessened, among others. For
these reasons and many related more, it is highly motivating and relevant to model and optimize the
microstructural evolution during its manufacturing process, developing a tool able to help predicting
the future performance.

3.1
Magnesium in detail
Magnesium is a very advantageous material in a very wide range of ﬁelds such as automobile,
aerospace or structural applications due to its beneﬁcial combination of properties. In the biomedical
sector, its biodegradability and similar mechanical behaviour compared to the human bone, makes
it an excellent choice for implants [6]. Regarding its remarkable properties, it is worth mentioning
the comparable value between natural bone and magnesium’s density and elastic modulus: 1.8-2.1
g/cm3, 3-20 GPa and 1.74-2.0 g/cm3, 41-45 GPa, respectively, where the similarities are considerable
if compared to other materials used for the same purpose such as titanium (4.4-4.5 g/cm3, 110-117
GPa) [7]. Furthermore, magnesium is an essential element for humans, which implies that the body
will not identify it as an unknown body [8].
Because of being a material used for medical purposes, diﬀerent criteria must be fulﬁlled:
• Mechanical properties: it is paramount for the implant to withstand the required load and strain
while the bone is curing without a premature failure
• Corrosion properties: implant degradation rate must be aligned with the bone healing capac-
ity, meaning that both mechanisms act in parallel and magnesium products are continuously
removed, preventing the formation of concentrated stagnant regions
• Bio-compatibility: the implant must not imply any risk for the right functioning of organs, tissues
or any part of the body, not only during the degradation process but also in long term

Many studies have been conducted focusing on each of this topics separately. The purpose of
the present project is to provide a useful tool that allows to optimize the material’s mechanical
properties during its manufacturing.
3.1.1
How can it be improved?
Magnesium alloys’ limitations are based to a high extend on its restricted strength and ductility
when compared to Ti-alloys for example. In metals science, four well known strengthening techniques
have been devised to adjust ductility, toughness and strength: work hardening, grain reﬁnement,
precipitation hardening and solid solution strengthening.
The mechanical behaviour of lean magnesium alloys can be enhanced through thermo-mechanical
deformation after being alloyed, which leads to a ﬁne and smooth grain distribution and therefore
material strengthening by the addition of numerous grain boundaries. Although the presence of
precipitates and/or solute elements can improve the material’s properties by pinning the boundaries
or distorting the lattice, its inﬂuence cannot be compared to the high strength-to-weight ratio which
is achieved through deformation.
Magnesium alloying
To consider a magnesium alloy an optimal and competitive material for biodegradable implant man-
ufacturing, it has to be compatible with the human body and outstandingly fulﬁl speciﬁc mechanical
and corrosive properties in accordance with its application.
In addition to the mechanical similarities to that of the bone, magnesium is naturally found in
the human body and its daily intake consumption recommended to be 240-420 mg/day for adults
according to the National Academies of Science, Engineering and Medicine, which is for far greater
than the one for iron (8-18 mg/day) or zinc (8-11 mg/day).
However, due to the high degradation rate of its commercial pure form in simulated body ﬂuid,
which has been estimated at 2.89 mm/year in 0.19% NaCl solution, and the low strength in the
as-cast condition (under 30MPa) [9], the addition of secondary elements in the system is required.
The need of controlling toxic compounds release throughout the body, makes it imperative to ﬁrstly
analyse which elements are biocompatible with the human body and therefore suitable. Degradation
products, once developed, will be cleared from the operative area either through absorption by
surrounding tissues or excretion via the kidneys [9].

3 – Theory
While aluminium and rare earth have been widely applied for the magnesium properties im-
provement in diﬀerent applications with very positive outputs [10], they have been proven to not be
practicable for implants production: aluminium being classiﬁed as neurotoxican which can develop
dementia and Alzheimer disease [11], and some rare earth elements are highly toxic and responsible
for the promotion of various injuries [12].
Zinc, being a vital element for the human biologic functioning and playing an essential role in
more than 300 enzymes [13], is one of the preferred alloying elements for magnesium due to its
strengthening capabilities [8], being the Mg-Zn system commonly referred as the second strongest
alloying system after rare earth based alloys [9]. Even though its solubility limit in magnesium is
6.2% wt., its presence should not exceed 4% wt. in order to achieve maximum ultimate tensile
strength and elongation [9].
Still having room for further improvement, diﬀerent elements have been investigated with an
emphasis on reﬁning the microstructure: calcium, zirconium, strontium, manganese and silicon are
usually suggested for the formation of ternary systems [9].
Firstly, calcium is an abundant mineral in the body which presence is primarily found in the
skeleton, being beneﬁcial for the bone growth and able to accelerate healing. It is known to be
an eﬀective grain reﬁner for magnesium systems, then enhancing the strength by hindering the
dislocation motion, according to the Hall Petch Law [14,15]. Moreover, it weakens the texture when
added to the binary Mg-Zn system, decreasing the stacking fault energy and therefore improving
the ductility [16].
Mg-Zn-Ca microstructure shows diﬀerent phases depending on the chemical composition of each
element, but it fundamentally consists of an α −Mg matrix together with diﬀerent intermetallic
compounds.
Although maximum solubility of calcium in magnesium is 1.34% wt., it is not recommended
to alloy beyond 1% due to enhanced cracking risk and ductility decrease [9] together with a pro-
nounced increase in the corrosion rate explained by the presence of Mg2Ca precipitation along grain
boundaries [14]. Moreover, hydrogen evolution rate escalates with calcium content, which is not
desirable [15].
If alloyed in small amounts, small binary and ternary precipitates formed together with magne-
sium and zinc can restrict the grain growth during dynamic recrystallization providing the so-called
Zener pinning eﬀect.

==PAGE:20==
3 – Theory
Continuing with the elements’ analysis, the addition of manganese to the Mg-Zn system, has
previously shown rapid corrosion during in vivo experiments and therefore is not taken into account
as a promising option [17].
Strontium has been used as a calcium substitute in biomedical applications, as they share chem-
ical, biological and metallurgical properties [8, 13, 18]. While it is known to better the corrosion
rate and powerfully reﬁne the coarse magnesium microstructure, due to its low solubility promoting
the formation of precipitates which pin the boundaries, there is not a clear agreement regarding
optimum concentrations.
Firstly, the addition of 0.5% wt. is suggested as the best option in order to obtain optimised
results [13, 19], which does not show toxicity nor detrimental eﬀects for cells’ viability. However,
there is a lack of understanding of the mechanism by which Mg-Sr compounds degrade.
It has also been suggested to improve the implant performance, manifesting in a better osseoin-
tegration, with the addition of higher concentrations, i.e. 2% wt. [18]. Nevertheless, experiments
proved grade I of cytoxicity, which must be avoided, and therefore not considered as an optimum op-
tion even though increased presence of strontium provides ﬁner grains and therefore higher strength,
when added to both pure magnesium or the Mg-Zn-Ca system.
Zirconium competes with strontium for the position of better reﬁnement agent to the Mg-Zn-Ca
system. It has been shown that the combined addition of calcium and zirconium to magnesium,
synergistically enhances its properties compared to the addition of calcium alone [20]. Like in the
case of strontium, its low solubility in magnesium causes the precipitation throughout the melt in
the form of diminutive particles. Furthermore, its high melting point is responsible for the more than
likely presence of undissolved zirconium acting as heterogeneous nucleants during the casting [21].
Zirconium’s crystal structure with the one of magnesium, both being hexagonal close packed
(HCP) and with very similar lattice parameters, suggest that its reﬁning potential is higher than
the one of strontium (which is face centered cubic, FCC) [22]. Zirconium addition does not lead to
the generation of additional phases with compositions below 0.8%, showing good bio-compatibility
and absence of toxicity issues [23]. Therefore, a quaternary system consisting of magnesium, zinc,
calcium and zirconium will show those intermetallics compounds that are formed by calcium and/or
zinc with the magnesium matrix: binary Laves phase (Mg,Zn)2Ca and Mg-Zn, and ternary IM1.
IM1 precipitates are known for promoting the apparition of new grains by particle stimulating
nucleation (PSN), where precipitates behave as nucleation sites and therefore beneﬁcial for the
mechanical properties [16].

Concluding with the alloy analysis, the Mg-Zn-Ca ternary system has been widely proved to be
the optimal one, which can be enhanced by small additions of zirconium. Accordingly, the interest
of Mg-Zn-Ca-Zr alloys for bioimplant applications is justiﬁed.
3.1.2
Deformation Mechanism
Magnesium alloys are very promising materials for lightweight and biomedical applications due to
its biodegradability and combination of low density and high strength. However, ductility at room
temperature needs to be enhanced to oﬀer an optimum performance.
The reason behind the restricted ductility can be explained by the lack of active slip systems con-
sequence of its hexagonal closed packed lattice (HCP), yielding to a complex mechanical behaviour.
Polycrystalline metals are required to have ﬁve independent slip-systems to show uniform ductil-
ity, according to the Von Mises or Taylor criterion [24]. Magnesium only oﬀers two when deformed
at ambient temperature, failing to fulﬁl the criteria, and therefore oﬀering a limited capacity of
plastic deformation when loaded.
Critical resolved shear stress (CRSS) is deﬁned as the necessary shear stress in the slip direction
for the grain to slip, highly dependant on the applied temperature. Magnesium independent slip
systems are the ones involving basal planes (0001), usually named athermal due to the signiﬁcantly
low critical temperature [24], having a hundred times lower CRSS at room temperature than the
non-basal ones – namely prismatic and pyramidal planes [25].
Non-basal planes can only become active and therefore increase the materials’ formability when
the CRSS is lowered. Bringing together basal and non-basal planes, there is a total amount of four
active systems. Deformation twinning has been suggested to account for an extra half contribution
by Kocks and Westlake when material is subjected to tensile stress or compressed in the solidiﬁcation
direction, raising the number of slip systems to 4.5, almost reaching the ﬁve required independent
systems to fulﬁl homogeneous ductility. However, twinning inﬂuence greatly depends on diﬀerent
aspects: texture, alloying elements, precipitates, strain and strain rate, temperature, dislocation
density and grain size [26].
When magnesium is plastically deformed at elevated temperatures, signiﬁcant changes in the
microstructure can be observed, including the introduction of high dislocation densities, nucleation
of small grains and its growth, defects such as stacking faults and twins. Presence of alloying elements
further helps to reﬁne the microstructure through precipitates, slowing down the grain growth and
9

even promoting the apparition of new recrystallized grains through particle stimulation nucleation
mechanism (PSN) [27], which is known to be the case for intermetallic IM1 [16].
Grain reﬁnement is the ultimate purpose of the process and known to be the most eﬃcient
strengthening mechanism for our case, oﬀering a balanced mechanical behaviour where in addition
to high strength, there is an improved ductility.
By analysing the Hall-Petch relation (which shows correlation between the strength and the grain
size) for diﬀerent materials, it can be seen that grain size inﬂuence for HCP compared to FCC or
BCC materials is signiﬁcantly higher due to a several times larger strengthening coeﬃcient [9].
Typical thermomechanical processes for magnesium are extrusion, forging or rolling, together
with severe deformation methods. During the deformation of the material at elevated temperatures,
the Dynamic Recrystallization mechanism (DRX) is responsible of the overall grain size reduction,
as new grains are nucleated followed by its growth as long as the the required driving force is present.
3.1.3
Dynamic Recrystallization Mechanism
Recrystallization is deﬁned as the microstructural change in a deformed material by the formation of
new grains. By performing plastic deformation, the material becomes thermodynamically unstable
and its free energy increases due to the generation of defects, mainly dislocation accumulation. A
raise in the high stored energy serves as driving force for the mechanism to happen, restoring (fully
or partially) the material to the undeformed state and properties.
Recrystallization can be static (SRX) or dynamic (DRX), depending on whereas the grains’
transformation takes place after the deformation of the material or while it is being subjected to it.
Following conditions are required before the recrystallization can start:
• Minimum deformation: a critical strain has to be reached, related to the critical dislocation
density. The material has to be able to oﬀer nucleation sites for the new nuclei together with
enough driving force for its growing. The lower the Zener-Hollomon parameter, and therefore
higher temperature and lower strain rate, the lower critical strain is required
• Critical temperature: recrystallization requires grain boundary migration which takes place by
atoms exchange, i.e. by a thermally activated diﬀusional process. Commonly, recrystallization
temperature for pure metals is referred to be 0.3-0.6Tm, with melting temperature expressed
in Kelvin [28]. However, in case of magnesium, the mechanism has been assessed in a wider
range switched towards increased temperatures: 293-773K [29]
10
==PAGE:23==
3 – Theory
Focusing on dynamic recrystallization, a classiﬁcation of diﬀerent types can be listed, which can
happen in the material individually or as a mixture:
• Continuous dynamic recrystallization (CDRX): nucleation and growth of new nuclei cannot be
considered independent stages with clear diﬀerentiation, usually consequence of a signiﬁcant
thermal activation in materials with high stacking fault energy
• Discontinuous dynamic recrystallization (DDRX): nucleation and growth of new nuclei take place
at diﬀerent times, and can therefore be distinguished
• Geometric dynamic recrystallization (GDRX): discovered in materials with high stacking fault
energy, the grains go through a geometry alteration while being deformed before the nucleation
of new grains happen
• Twinning dynamic recrystallization (TDRX): new nuclei are generated in the high strained special
grain boundaries surrounding twins
Two additional types named meta-dynamic recrystallization (MDRX) or post-dynamic recrys-
tallization (PDRX), can take place when deformation is stopped once the critical strain value is
reached with the temperature dropping very slowly. In this case, driving force for nucleation is still
suﬃcient and new grains can continue nucleating and/or growing as long as temperature exceeds
the critical value [30].
Discontinuous Dynamic Recrystallization
Discontinous Dynamic Recrystallization (DDRX) takes place when a material reaches critical strain
and temperature, and grain nucleation and growth can be identiﬁed as independent steps.
Previously to DDRX initiation, when the material is already being deformed and defects created,
Dynamic Recovery (DRV) mechanism occurs which consists of the homogeneous annihilation and re-
arrangement of dislocations by cross-slip and climb (depending on having screw or edge dislocations,
respectively). Contrary to DRX, high angle grain boundaries (HAGB) do not develop or move [31].
DRV, being a restoring mechanism, competes with work hardening as both take place simulta-
neously at early strains. Some dislocations are generated and accumulated strengthening the matrix
while others’ annihilate producing the softening.
Materials with high stacking fault energy (SFE) and low SFE feature diﬀerent behaviour in this
matter. SFE is related to the capacity of the material to have the dislocations dissociated into
Shockley partials, depending on its atomic bonding. Materials with high SFE, have the dislocation’s
11
==PAGE:24==
3 – Theory
dissociation hindered and therefore are easily capable of glide or cross slip, experiencing major dy-
namic recoveries. On the other hand, materials with low SFE will have the dislocations favourable
dissociated and its mobility obstructed. In the later case, which is the case of magnesium, work
hardening has a stronger inﬂuence in the microstructure than dynamic recovery and critical disloca-
tion density can be reached more easily. Moreover, while the material is being subjected to plastic
deformation, incompatibilities between grains may cause boundary shape ﬂuctuations, hindering the
sliding and/or shearing of grain boundaries and enhancing dislocation accumulation, favouring work
hardening [32].
It has been shown that HAGBs can quickly achieve critical dislocation densities by the mechanism
of migration and bulging [31]. Named strain-induced grain boundary migration (SIBM), the bulging
of part of the grain boundary leads to a gradient in dislocation density, having as result two diﬀerent
regions: one with lower strain and other with induced-strain where critical dislocation density can
be achieved [33].
Regardless of the process by which the required local strain is fulﬁlled, nuclei are generated in
small volumes, usually in pre-existing sub-grains of the deformed structure, where the largest stored
energy due to energetical diﬀerences across the boundary is found [30]. These sites are usually found
in grain boundary vicinities, triple points or sometimes internal defects.
Nucleation kinetic is the slowest of the DRX mechanisms, and therefore the rate-controlling one
[31]. Once a nuclei is generated, it starts growing, consuming the deformed matrix and softening the
material reducing dislocation content. Grain growth takes place through grain boundary migration,
where atoms jump from the shrinking grain to the growing one, reducing the grain boundary stored
energy. The bigger the diﬀerence in dislocation density across grain boundaries, the more pronounced
grains’ growth rate and boundary speed [34].
Inﬂuencing factors for Dynamic Recrystallization
Diﬀerent features can modify the ﬁnal microstructure of a material undergoing Dynamic Recrystal-
lization (DRX), which are described and assessed in this subsection.
Firstly, the intrinsic properties of a material have a strong impact. Depending on the nature of the
crystal lattice, diﬀerent slips systems can be activated, involving diﬀerent deformation mechanisms
and inﬂuencing dislocation motion.
Stacking fault energy (SFE), as mentioned earlier, classiﬁes materials between those with high
energy where dislocations easily glide and slip, and with low energy characterized by dissociated
dislocations and impeded mobility, creating wider stacking faults and retarding dynamic recovery.
12

Secondly, the initial grain size determines the amount of initial grain boundaries which will
hinder dislocation motion, being easier to reach the critical dislocation density in case of having
small grains compared to a coarse microstructure. Further, these defects will oﬀer more nucleation
sites enhancing recrystallization kinetics.
Thermo-mechanical conditions such as temperature and strain rate, also modify the material’s
behaviour while recrystallizing. Both parameters are usually kept constant and can be conveniently
integrated for its assessment in the Zener-Hollomon parameter. The higher strain rate and lower
applied temperature, the bigger value it has. The variation in this parameters causes a modiﬁca-
tion in the stress-strain curves. A rise in recrystallization kinetics is found when Zener-Hollomon
parameter is small, together with an increase in the recrystallized grain size [30].
Finally, the presence of second phase particles is convenient to be considered as it can accelerate
the nucleation step and/or slow down the grain growth kinetics. Dominant eﬀect will mainly depend
on the phase’s size.
On the one hand, when having small homogeneously distributed particles, a retarding eﬀect
on low-angled and/or high-angled grain boundaries’ motion can be introduced, mainly inﬂuencing
recovery and grain growth steps. The phenomenon is called pinning Zener drag eﬀect, and its impact
can be estimated by introducing the additional pressure [33], deﬁned as: ZP = γGB∆Vf

Stress-strain curve
Typical stress-strain curve of a material undergoing DRX, can be divided into multiple or single
peak behaviour, being inﬂuenced by diﬀerent factors. Strain rate, temperature, initial grain size and
the ﬁnal-initial grain size ratio play an inﬂuential role in the curve characteristics, together with the
recrystallization kinetics and ﬁnal microstructure. An illustrative explanation of these factors can
be found in ﬁgures 3.1,3.2, and summarized in the following points:
• When the material is being reﬁned (DS/D0 < 2, being D0 the initial and DS ﬁnal grain size [30]),
there is only one peak in a smooth curve as the process evolves in a continuous manner.
On the other hand, when having grain coarsening (DS/D0 > 2), multiple and consecutive
peaks can be seen, each of them associated to a individual DRX cycle, consequence of grain
impingement [35]. Nevertheless, under equal deformation, ﬁnal grain size in the steady-state
is independent from the initial grains, being therefore the same for both initially ﬁne and
coarse sizes. In summary, when starting from small grains, microstructure will experience a
coarsening eﬀect, whereas in the opposite case a reﬁnement will be the result
• Strain rate and temperature, which can be integrated in the Zener Hollomon parameter, deﬁnes to
a great extent the ﬂow curve shape. Low parameter values, move the curve towards multiple-
peak behaviour with low critical strain and peak stress. The reason behind lies in the kinetics’
acceleration boosted by the powerful thermal activation. In the opposite case, the curve is
moved towards single peak behaviour
• Similarly, small initial grain size switches the curve to lower stress values due to the high amount
of defects, and therefore dislocation accumulation, prompting recrystallization nucleation
• Low Zener Hollomon parameter values and small initial grain size boost recrystallization fraction
at early stages, reaching a fully recrystallized matrix at low strains
• The dimension of the ﬁnal grains, considering an identical starting size, depends on the applied
temperature and strain rate, as they inﬂuence the DRX kinetics [34]. The higher the Zener
Hollomon parameter, the lower steady state grain size

Cellular Automaton Model
Cellular Automaton (CA) is a very well consolidated algorithm in computational materials, which
has been successfully applied for DRX simulation in 2D. Generally applied in the mesoscopic scale,
it was initially designed as a deterministic model, which means that randomness is not involved and
therefore the ﬁnal result is always identical when having same input. However, it evolved throughout
the years towards the implementation of stochastic and probabilistic rules, signiﬁcantly improving
the output and therefore its acceptance [36]. Nevertheless, it is still being reﬁned by the addition
of diﬀerent features with the purpose of obtaining more realistic results. It stands out among other
models due to the possibility of considering both spatial and temporal evolution, while taking into
account microstructural heterogeneities.
The initial microstructure, which can be either generated or imported from, for example, an
EBSD image, will consist of a set of grains with diﬀerent sizes, morphologies and orientations.
During the pre-processing, diﬀerent parameters are deﬁned:
• Grid discretization into ﬁnite cells characterized by the same size and boundary conditions, which
can be periodic, symmetric or mirror-like [37]. Cell dimensions are important as they will
deﬁne the minimum nuclei size that can be generated
• Cells’ neighbouring design is speciﬁed, where the most typical ones are the so called Von Neumann
and Moore, taking into account four and eight neighbours respectively
• Starting dislocation density is assigned homogeneously to all cells in agreement with the initial
stress, emulating the soft state of the material before being plastically deformed
• Every initial grain has an orientation value, assigned either randomly or in a justiﬁed manner
The CA model is based on discrete time steps, also called cellular automaton steps (CAS) [36].
For each of them, the new value for every cell is calculated according to its previous state and local
neighbourhood, following a transformation rule. As a consequence, the speciﬁed changing conditions
are the most important part of the mathematical model determining the grains’ evolution.
16
==PAGE:29==

New values are not automatically updated after the corresponding calculation, but instead all
cells in the grid have its status simultaneously updated at the end of every simulation step. In other
words, state of a cell at time t + dt, is fully dependant on its status at time t. Once the time step is
ﬁnished and all cells updated, the local neighbourhoods for changed cells are redeﬁned.
The fact of having the cell’s update only dependant on the local neighbouring system and its own
state, means that a reduced amount of information is required to be passed between cells in each
time step [37]. This simpliﬁcation allows eﬃcient parallelization and optimal scalability. Moreover,
it oﬀers a huge ﬂexibility when representing diﬀerent physical features in the microstructure, and
the possibility to escalate to 3D. However, for big or complex systems, length and time scales can
be expected to involve high computational costs [36].
It should be taken into account the huge dependence on the discretized grid, which makes it
diﬃcult to simulate coarse microstructures due to the high ratio between original and nucleated
grain sizes, and the possible underestimation of grain growth due to the individual cell’s assessment
in each CAS. Other disadvantages are the poorly represented grain boundary curvature compared
to other models, namely phase ﬁeld or level-set model, and the incapability of tracing texture
evolution [37].
Monte Carlo Model
Monte Carlo (MC) is the most widely used model together with CA for DRX simulation. Sharing the
same pre-processing, a grid and neighbourhood system are deﬁned, and the corresponding variable
values assigned to each cell.
Instead of analysing the full grid during each simulation step having every cell individually
assessed, N cells are randomly chosen, being N an integer and deﬁned number. Speciﬁc conditions
for the cells’ transformation are applied to the selected cells, and therefore its value changed if the
requirements are fulﬁlled. In the same way as CA, cells update is done simultaneously at the end of
each Monte Carlo step (MCS).
While CA model evolution relies on theoretical rules applied to the full grid, Monte Carlo model
is based on the discrete minimization of the total energy of the system during each MCS [38].
Among its advantages, it should be mentioned its ﬂexibility to incorporate diﬀerent physical
features, oﬀering high versatility.
However, the simulation length and time scale might not be
optimal and there is a big dependence on the chosen grid discretization [37], together with the
simpliﬁcation of assessing random cells for each MCS.

Continuum Mechanical Model
Developed with the intention of solving the discrepancies between experimental and simulated data
in the case of MC or CA, continuum mechanical models operate with the modeling of two diﬀerent
stresses combined.
The ﬁrst one corresponds to the hardening stress, unaﬀected by the DRX, being the second one
a softening stress usually based on an Avrami equation [39].
Macroscopic ﬂow stress is related with the average grain size, which can be calculated through
the Hall-Petch equation. During the deformation and strengthening of the material, dislocation
density is obtained from the increasing hardening stress. Once DRX has started, and therefore the
softening stress plays an important role, the combination of both stresses is taken into account.
The biggest advantage of using continuum mechanics lies in the possibility of modelling the mi-
crostructure in a macroscopic level. Also, it is convenient when choosing a model for its implantation
in an already existing FEM model, due to its good incorporation.
Nevertheless, the positive features carry limitations such as the inexistent ﬂexibility for modelling
microstructure heterogeneities, and the diﬃculty for implementing diﬀerent variables such as grain
orientation and texture [37].
Phase Field Model
The method is based on the reduction of the free energy in the system. Each grain is assigned phase
ﬁeld variables, which are continuous in space and can be divided in two types: conserved, referred
to the whole grain and unchanged throughout the simulation such as the material composition,
and non-conserved, when referring to those variables which undergo transformations and updates
throughout time, such as the grain orientation.
Grain boundaries are deﬁned as “diﬀuse transition regions”, and phase variables evolve according
to the numerical calculation of pre-deﬁned partial diﬀerential equations [37]. The fact of not having
precise grain boundaries that diﬀerentiate grains, allows it to assume arbitrary morphologies without
specifying interfaces.
This model is mainly used for the modelling of grain growth, without taking into account the
nucleation step. In case of being the grain texture evolution or speciﬁc geometries and morphologies
of interest, modelling is not straightforward due to the lack of clear interfaces.
Moreover, the
computational cost is signiﬁcantly high compared to other models used for the same purpose.

Vertex Model
Contrary to the Phase Field Model, precise geometry is speciﬁed in case of using the Vertex Model,
being only applied for 2D modelling. Grain boundaries are represented by line segments, where its
interceptions are deﬁned as nodes, placed in triple points, and intermediate points along boundaries.
As a consequence, grain morphology is represented by the nodes position, and the velocity of each
grain boundary described by a vector.
Driving force for the microstructural evolution is based on the reduction in grain boundary energy
calculated through diﬀerential equations, which include the grain boundary speed and mobility.
Throughout time, smaller grains than a speciﬁed value disappear, and the combination of diﬀerent
junctions take place.
Zener pinning eﬀects can be incorporated inﬂuencing the boundary mobility together with a
very realistic grain boundary curvature, consequence of the ﬂexibility and versatility of geometry
representation. However, its computational cost is high and there are not many references of its use
for DRX modelling and simulation.
Level Set Model
In a similar way to the Vertex Model, the Level Set Model is dependent on space. Its main advantage
is the possibility of tracing interfaces’ position in time, following a continuous function.
Level Set Models have been recently developed and there is for the moment a lack of evidence
of its application for DRX computational modelling [37].

Models describing the Dynamic Recrystallization mechanism assume constant conditions of temper-

• Grain orientation of every grain, assigned to each of its cells
• Dislocation density, which initial value is calculated from the experimental initial stress and equal
for every cell in the grid
• Distance variable deﬁning grain boundary motion

4.3
Development of Cellular Automaton Model
4.3.1
Microstructure Generation
Initial microstructure in the simulated model is generated according to the experimental grain size
distribution and observed topology in the material prior its deformation. The method is based on a
multi-step grain nucleation and growth, which is sequentially explained in ﬁgure 4.10.
First, input information describing the scale is deﬁned, which values mainly depend on the initial
and steady state recrystallized grain values, with the objective of simulating a representative and
detailed area of the microstructure. As a rule of thumb, a minimum of ten grains is required.
Grids are commonly designed with squared shape, where number of cells for each dimension
is below thousand to prevent excessive computational cost with cell’s size going from hundreds of
nanometres to few micrometres. The bigger the initial size and smaller recrystallized grain, the
bigger total area should be considered. Even though most common grids for CA modelling found
in literature go from 100·100 to 800·800 cells [42, 45–49], the coarse initial microstructure of the
Mg-Zn-Ca-Zr alloy makes it necessary to use a bigger grid, at the cost of a longer execution time.
Grid dimension of 1500·1500 with 0.75µm cell size is chosen for this model.
The deﬁned neighbouring design will play an inﬂuence in the grain growth step due to the
consideration of diﬀerent cells for the transformation. Moore neighbourhood is implemented in case
of cells surrounded by more than one grain with the objective of taking into account the biggest
number of close cells, namely eight. On the other hand, if the cell is only surrounded by one grain,
Von Neumann neighbourhood is considered in order to promote equiaxial growing. Each cell in the
grid will have an indexed local neighbourhood, which numbering has been assigned according to
ﬁgure 4.11 and 4.12 enabling the use of the desired neighbourhood in each case.

Discussion
The dynamic recrystallization mechanism of a magnesium alloy, consisting primarily of high
purity magnesium, zinc, calcium and zirconium has been simulated using a Cellular Automaton
model in 2D. Following a probabilistic approach, diﬀerent transformation rules are implemented to
analyse the material’s behaviour.
The initial microstructure for the modeling was successfully generated with a multi-step nucle-
ation and growth method. Grain size distribution is in accordance with the experimental ﬁnding
and twenty-eight grains are generated in a 1500*1500 grid of 0.75 µm cell size, constituting a repre-
sentative region of the material.
The consideration of the initial grain size distribution and grain morphology represents an in-
novative approach for DRX simulation, in contrast to the usual practice the implementation of
a homogeneous microstructure or imported image.
Moreover, it oﬀers a wide ﬂexibility regard-
ing nucleation rounds and number of generated nuclei together with growth steps, showing a high
adaptability for the microstructure creation with diﬀerent grain size distributions. In case of hav-
ing a microstructure that exhibits ﬁne and numerous grains, the energetically favoured orientations
referred to the basal plane have their growth promoted.
Input parameters for the model simulating DRX mechanism were determined from the obtained
experimental results of hot compression tests. Flow curves were used to derive necessary parameters
whereas the microstructure at diﬀerent strains was analysed serving as validation for the model.
Sellars and Tagart equation was implemented to create a model adjustable to diﬀerent temperatures
and strain rates within a range (300◦C - 450◦C, 0.01 s−1 - 1 s−1).
The model was executed in MatLab software installed in a standard computer for one pair of
parameters (375◦C, 0.1 s−1) with a relatively large CAS time (1 s) and maximum strain of 0.9.
The huge computational cost that implies the execution of models of this nature and size, limited
to a very high extend the results’ generation and the subsequent optimisation and validation. A
representative grid for ZXK100 requires a very numerous amount of cells, each of them going through
several calculations for each step.
The comparison between experimental and simulated ﬂow stress determines a high similarity in
the curves’ behaviour in the early deformation stage. The simulated stress increases as a consequence
75
==PAGE:88==
7 – Discussion
of the material work hardening. Once the peak is reached, the experimental curve drops signiﬁcantly
explained by a material softening, whereas simulated curve reaches a nucleation and growth steady
state where stress is kept constant at a value of 90.3 MPa.
The experimental decrease in stress after reaching the peak value (softening), can be explained
by the heat generation that takes place during the plastic deformation. This inﬂuence is magniﬁed
at high strain rates where temperature raise is more pronounced.
The simulation result for the nucleation step is in accordance with the expected behaviour im-
plemented in the model. Taking into account that nuclei generation rate calculation, based on the
experimental results, was overestimated, nucleation takes place as expected homogeneously along
the grain boundaries. However, nuclei generation observed in the experimental microstructure oc-
curs in clusters locally positioned in speciﬁc regions along the grain boundary, very often in triple
points. Therefore, the model represents the real distribution of DRX grains found experimentally
once all the boundaries have gone through the recrystallization phenomenon, being less accurate for
an early stage.
Regarding grain growth, ﬁrst steps are found to be coherent with the material’s behaviour.
However, simulation increasingly deviates from the experimental ﬁndings with the strain increment.
Instead of having the preferential growth of those grains characterised by a beneﬁcial driving force,
the migration of every boundary takes place. Driving force is deﬁned by the dislocation density
diﬀerence with the neighbouring grains, the boundary energy (dependant on the misorientation
angle) and the grain curvature.
First explanation for the transformation of every cell located in a grain boundary is the excessive
CAS time used for the modeling. The distance variable, which controls the migration, is calculated
with the velocity times the CAS time, meaning that an outsized time between steps cancel the pur-
pose of the condition established for the transformation. When a grain boundary is not transferred,
the reason is explained by the probabilistic approach of the model instead of a failure to fulﬁll the
requirement.
Regarding the suppressed growth of DRX grains, it was found that the grain radius curvature cal-
culation method, as reported in Ref. [42], prompts the driving force and therefore boundary velocity
of grains with concave shape, when favoured condition should be a convex shape. Moreover, DRX
grains that theoretically experience the largest driving force for their growth, have their boundary
velocity reduced when taking into account the radius curvature.
Despite the distance variable being dependant also on the CAS time, mobility and dislocation
diﬀerence between grains, this could have had a signiﬁcant inﬂuence in the transformation evolution.

7 – Discussion
Nevertheless, nucleation and early stages of grain growth show promising results and can be
considered as a positive starting point for the further optimization.
Extrusion tests were performed to assess the predictability of the microstructure evolution result
of a diﬀerent manufacturing process. Due to the signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the simulated maxi-
mum strain and the advanced deformed state of the extruded rod, validation cannot be performed.
7.1
Model Strengths and Limitations
Cellular Automaton models are based on an extremely ﬂexible approach that allows to generate dif-
ferent heterogeneities and features in the microstructure, providing relevant and meaningful results.
The possibility of developing a probabilistic model discretised in time and space, where diﬀerent
transformation rules deﬁne the material’s behavior, oﬀers a close representation of the reality when
compared to deterministic models.
The downside of the advantageous adaptability is the excessive computational cost that requires
the simulation with proper time and length scales. Specially in case of having a big ratio of original
to DRX grain size, a representative grid of the material’s microstructure implies a huge processing
time. In consideration of the Cellular Automaton principle, every cell in the system is taken into
account for the calculations for every step.
While the model can be very extensively optimised implementing diﬀerent features, very detailed
simulations also imply a deviation from a universal model. Equilibrium between a simpliﬁed and
rigorous model needs to be reached to achieve the optimum.
For the DRX simulation, diﬀerent assumptions were made. While some of them are necessary
and do not involve a signiﬁcant inﬂuence, others might oversimplify the simulation results, therefore
not representing the reality in a good manner.
Grain nucleation is programmed to happen randomly in those grain boundaries that reach the
critical dislocation density, causing an homogeneous nuclei distribution. However, experiments show
a nuclei generation in clusters localised at speciﬁc boundary regions. The assumption of starting with
an equal dislocation density for every grain together with the random nuclei distribution approach
implies a physical limitation for the realistic representation of the nucleation mechanism.
For each nucleation round, the same amount of nuclei are generated following a constant nu-
cleation rate. Nevertheless, reality shows a progressive increase. While an adjustable rate can be
programmed as a function of the strain, the ﬂexibility for simulating diﬀerent temperature-strain
rate parameters would be limited.
77
==PAGE:90==
7 – Discussion
From 0.4 strain deformation on, nucleation in twin boundaries was observed, a consequence of
being sites with high stored energy and therefore favourable for the nucleation mechanism. The
model does not incorporate the presence of twins in the microstructure, meaning that twinning
nucleation is not considered.
Finally, the inability to trace the texture evolution should be taken into account when using this
model in those cases where orientation transformation during the deformation plays a big role. For
this project, new grains are given a random orientation and that is kept unchanged, whereas for
example during an extrusion process grains get aligned due to a preferential ﬂow plane.

8
Conclusion
A Cellular Automaton model in 2D was used for the implementation of the dynamic recrystal-
lization (DRX) mechanism of the ZXK100 alloy, consisting primarily of high purity magnesium, zinc,
calcium, and traces of zirconium acting as grain reﬁner. The evolution of the simulated microstruc-
ture under hot deformation was deﬁned by transformational rules following a probabilistic approach.
While the model was based on speciﬁc conditions of temperature and strain rate, the Sallars and
Tagart constitutive equation was implemented to allow the execution at diﬀerent parameter values.
Initial microstructure was generated through the multistep nucleation and grain growth method,
where grain size distribution and topography were taken into account. This approach is characterised
by its high adaptability because it is able to consider diﬀerent microstructural features, oﬀering a
realistic starting point for the DRX modelling. Grain growth was designed in such a way to promote
energetically most-favorable orientations.
Early stages of DRX nucleation and growth were successfully simulated, as conﬁrmed by their
great similarity to the experimental results obtained through hot compression tests. Grain generation
took place in simulation cells that represented grain boundaries at which the dislocation density
was at the critical level. Grain growth occurred in a coherent manner during the ﬁrst steps, but
continued irregularly when the strain increased. The abnormal behaviour can be explained by the
imprecise deﬁnition of the curvature radius and long step time, which was selected to minimize the
computational costs due to limited time. Similarly, simulated and experimental stress ﬂow curves
for the initial steps exhibited a high degree of similarity, demonstrating that stress initially increased
with strain because of work hardening. While the experimental results showed a subsequent stress
drop due to material softening, the simulated curve maintained high stress values due to the above-
mentioned grain growth irregularities.
The approach to initialize the microstructure and simulate dynamic recrystallization mechanism
described in this thesis showed promising results. Its further development would be able to predict
the microstructural evolution with high precision, which would provide enormous beneﬁts to the
alloy design and selection of optimal manufacturing conditions.

9
Outlook and Further Work
This section covers diﬀerent suggestions for the further development of the model, together with
the expected experimental results from the simulation at diﬀerent conditions of temperature and
strain rate.
Firstly, it is suggested to develop the model for a smaller grid which even though it might not
be representative of the material microstructure, serves to validate the grain growth processing in a
shorter period of time, not directly simulating a proper grid. Once it is developed, the grid can be
escalated, and the model can be further assessed.
Moreover, with the objective of optimising the computational time, cells occupied by a grain
boundary can be assigned a ”1” in a matrix from the very beginning, when generating the initial
microstructure, and therefore during each step only those cells are assessed. Every time there is a
creation of a new boundary or a migration, the matrix should be updated.
Intermetallic particles (IMPs) make an impact on the DRX process by hindering boundary
motion. This mechanism can be implemented in two diﬀerent manners. On one hand, it has been
found in literature the downgrading of the boundary velocity for a randomly chosen speciﬁc number
of cells located in grain boundaries, reproducing the obstacle that implies ﬁnding an IMP. The
reduction percentage is usually found by simulating diﬀerent trials while optimising the result. On
the other hand, intermetallic compounds can also be generated in the microstructure. When a grain
encounters a IMP and tries to migrate its boundary towards it, there is an extra force to take into
account which would reduce the calculated driving force.
The particle stimulated nucleation (PSN) can also be introduced if IMPs are generated in the
microstructure. It is possible to force the nuclei appearance in their surroundings assigning a higher
probability for nucleation compared to the original grain boundaries, increasing the chances to
have PSN. Also, it was found in very advanced models the implementation of a further deformed
region around particles where the critical dislocation density is reached faster compared to the other
boundaries, enhancing as well the grain nucleation in those sites.
In this work an overestimation of the nucleation rate and its variation as a function of the strain
was observed.
A practicable method to calculate this parameter should be developed to obtain
more realistic results. A compromise needs to be reached between a ﬂexible and a strict model,
80
==PAGE:93==
9 – Outlook and Further Work
because it is very complex to develop a method for calculating an adjustable rate that depends on
the deformation degree and can be extrapolated to diﬀerent conditions of temperature and strain
rate.
During the DRX evolution, geometrical diﬀerences between the simulated and experimental
results were found. What happens in reality is an elongation of the original grains with increasing
strain, that can inﬂuence the nucleation and growth step. Also, the local generation of nuclei in
clusters at early deformation stages, before all new grains cover the whole boundary length, and the
appearance of grains in twin boundaries at increased strains was observed experimentally. However,
these aspects are not considered for this project.
Despite of Cellular Automaton not being the most eﬃcient model to trace texture evolution, the
implementation of diﬀerent possible sets of orientations with the subsequent comparison with the
experimental microstructure with EBSD have been described in literature before. After diﬀerent
veriﬁcation steps where false assumptions are discarded, a reasonable representation of orientations
can be achieved. However, the approach is completely based on a trial and error process and not
based on any physical model.
