Abstract 
The development of denitrification technology assures the expansion of seawater 
recirculating aquaculture system (RAS). To avoid the problems of clogging, channeling 
and sulfide production that commonly existed in media-based denitrification system, 
sequencing batch reactor (SBR) as a new method was under the evaluation. The 
effects of operational cycle time (OCT) and carbon sources on denitrification 
performance in SBR were assessed in this study. 
 
Acetate, fish organic waste (FOW) and fermented fish organic waste (FFOW) were 
chosen in 6h, 4h and 2h OCT tests. Denitrification rates with acetate increased 
significantly with the reduction of OCT, and reached 85.77 mg NO3
--N/h/g xCOD (xCOD 
represented activated sludge biomass) and 80.56 mg NOx
--N/h/g xCOD in 2h cycle. 
Similar positive impact on nitrate removal was observed in FOW test, but the impact 
on NOx
- removal was not evident. However, denitrification rate with FFOW was more 
or less consistent regardless of OCT. Activated sludge obtained endogenous carbon in 
two pathways: available VFAs in the solution and self-fermentation. FOW group relied 
more on self-fermentation, while FFOW group had opposite preference.  
 
Carbon source tests were conducted under 4h cycle condition with two extra 
exogenous carbons – ethanol and propionate. The denitrification rates ranked 
descendingly as acetate, propionate, ethanol, FFOW and FOW. C/N ratios of acetate, 
ethanol and propionate in this study followed the assumption that around 20% of COD 
contributed to cell synthesis. Based on it, new Stoichiometric equations were 
developed.  
 
Total ammonia nitrogen (TAN) level maintained below 0.2 mg N/L in exogenous 
carbon groups regardless of OCT, no evident pattern showed in FOW group while it 
reduced in FFOW group. As for total sulfide production, most effluents remained 
below 0.4 mg S/L. The foam formation resulted in the big variance of activated sludge 
biomass, especially in endogenous carbon groups where the thick brownish floating 
flocs occurred.   
 
Overall, the results revealed the potential of applying SBR in marine aquaculture 
wastewater treatment.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Worldwide aquaculture development  
With the improvement of living standard, aquaculture industry serving as a big 
nutrient producer is in a booming stage. According to Food and Aquaculture 
Organization of the United Nation (FAO) (Fisheries, F.A.O., 2016), the share of world 
aquaculture in fish production (including non-food uses) has been continuously 
increasing, for an example, from 31.1% in 2004 to 44.1% in 2014.  
 
However, European aquaculture grows arduously. In last 30 years, the share climbs 
less than 10%, and the production only grows 2 million tons while the number in Asia 
tends to hit 60 million. Despite the high investment cost, one of the main reasons 
restraining European aquaculture is that the regulation of environmental impacts is 
getting stricter in EU. In addition, some countries like Denmark put more rules on the 
effluent from inland aquaculture. Fish farm licenses are sized according to the 
nutrients (N, P, and organic matters) discharged. Therefore, the future of aquaculture 
production in Europe relies on the availability of environmentally sustainable and 
cost-efficient cultivation system (Suhr et al., 2013). 
 
1.2 Denitrification in seawater aquaculture  
Last three decades have witnessed the fish farming technology developing from 
simple flow-through system to more advanced recirculating aquaculture system (RAS) 
where water renewal rate is kept in a low level.  
 
Daily feeding introduces the nitrogen waste to RAS in the form of total ammonia 
nitrogen (TAN) (both NH4
+ and NH3). To prevent NH3 accumulation which is a huge 
threat to fish health (Van Rijn et al., 2005), nitrification facilitated by the biofilters is 
introduced in RAS to remove TAN into less toxic form – nitrate (NO3
-). Apparently, 
without desirable water treatments, nitrate will accumulate to the critical level which 
impairs aquaculture production. Usually, 5-10% of the system volume needs to be 
replaced with new water every day (Van Rijn et al., 2005). However, European 
environmental regulations size the permissible nitrate level under 11.3 mg NO3
--N/l in 
the effluent (European Council Directive, 1998). Despite the daily water renewal, the 
alternative is to introduce denitrification which transforms nitrate into nitrogen gas 
(Eq. 1).

Denitrification has two major functions in RAS. On the one hand, it can lower the 
nitrate level which allows less water exchange. On the other hand, it serves as the 
end-of-pipe treatment to reduce the environmental impacts of the effluent. As RAS

tends to develop into more intensive scale, a bigger denitrification unit with the higher 
efficiency is in great demand.  
 
In seawater RAS, pioneer work on denitrification was conducted in 1976 (Balderston 
& Sieburth, 1976) by means of packed columns. Later on, different denitrification 
systems have been evaluated, such as packed bed reactor for Japanese flounder 
cultivation (Honda et al., 1993), moving bed reactor for gilthead seabream (Morrison 
et al., 2004) and fluid bed reactor for the aquarium (Grguric & Coston, 1998). However, 
traditional media-based reactors for marine aquaculture denitrification require 
relatively high maintenance as clogging, filter channeling and harmful sulfide 
production occur commonly. 
 
Therefore, a more cost-efficient and easy-to-use solution for the marine aquaculture 
wastewater treatment is in the demand. Following the suggestion of Dalsgaard et al. 
(2013) to learn from urban wastewater treatment, this study chose a candidate from 
activated sludge systems called sequencing batch reactor (SBR) which has widely 
applied in the sewage plant since the 1920s.  
 
1.3 The properties of sequencing batch reactor 
SBR consists of 5 simple stages (Fig. 1). (1) Fill: wastewater and the carbon substrates 
are added into the reactor. (2) React: after the addition, water is stirred and mixed 
thoroughly to ensure the full contacts between activated sludge and different 
substrates. (3) Settle: Liquid-solid separation is achieved. (4) Decant: the supernatant 
is removed by different apparatus, commonly floating or adjustable weirs. (5) Idle: this 
phase is set as a time buffer to achieve multi-basin application (Wang & Shammas, 
2010).

reactor-sbr/)

By applying the activated sludge, all aforementioned problems in media-based 
reactors can be potentially avoided. SBR has even more advantages: (1) Less footprint 
and simple setup: every step is operated in one single reactor, no need for clarifiers. 
(2) Reliable operation: Strous et al. (1998) estimated a reliable and continuous 
operation duration to be more than one year. (3) Multiple functions: by adjusting 
aerobic, anaerobic and anoxic conditions, nitrification, denitrification and phosphorus 
removal can be achieved (Al-Rekabi et al., 2007). (4) Better settling characteristics: 
due to the settling phase of SBR, activated sludge was cultivated to have good settling 
properties (Al-Rekabi et al., 2007). Comparing to the up-flow reactor where bacteria 
needed to grow into the granule form to avoid being washed out, SBR required less 
time to stabilize and then officially start wastewater treatment. (5) Stable condition 
and homogeneous distribution: unlike the other activated sludge systems with 
constant flow rate, SBR has a stable reaction phase with no water exchange that 
allows substrates interact evenly and thoroughly. Therefore, microbial activities such 
as denitrification rate and carbon consumption can be monitored time-wisely. 
Furthermore, it has a strong tolerance for the variance of flow rate and substrate 
contents (Wang & Shammas, 2010). All the merits motioned above facilitate the 
feasibility study that (6) upscaling to the pilot reactor even the commercial size is 
relatively easy (Strous et al., 1998).  
 
SBR has already been well studied in municipal water treatment. However, the trials 
of using SBR in marine or even general aquaculture denitrification are scares. From 
what has been found, most SBRs were designed to operate both nitrification and 
denitrification to achieve better management of aquaculture wastewater. Boopathy 
et al. (2007) conducted an experiment on treating low salinity shrimp wastewater. 
After 3d anaerobic operation, 400 mg/L total nitrogen (TN) was reduced by 
approximately 85%. Lyles et al. (2008) further tried a pilot scale SBR on saline shrimp 
wastewater. Within 4d anaerobic operation, about 50 mg/L TN was removed. Both 
experiments were conducted without additional carbon input. Luo et al. (2013) found 
that feeding glucose to the SBR, 99% of NO3
--N reduction in jade perch (Scortum 
barcoo) wastewater was achieved within 5 hours.  
 
Apparently, the operational cycle time (OCT) of each study differed a lot from days to 
hours. Wang & Shammas (2010) emphasized that the sophisticated part of SBR was 
usually the setup of different time units. Normally, a longer OCT provides a more 
thorough contact between bacteria and substrates (Guo et al. 2016). However, it 
would require a larger working volume to achieve the same daily handling capacity. 
To set up SBR cycle, denitrification efficiency is the prerequisite, because rate and time 
are inversely proportional to each other if the nitrate is more or less constant in the 
influent. However, the investigation of SBR denitrifying aquaculture wastewater is still 
insufficient because most experiments focused on its qualitative performance. Thus, 
a quantitative analysis of denitrification rate is important to help determine basic cycle 
setup.

1.4 Carbon sources in seawater denitrification  
By means of denitrification, oxidized inorganic nitrogen compounds such as NO3
- and 
NO2
- are transformed into nitrogen gas (N2) (Eq. 1), conducted by the anaerobic 
bacteria with the presence of electron donors derived from both organic and inorganic 
sources (Van Rijn et al., 2005). Normally in marine aquaculture system, denitrification 
is based on the heterotrophic bacteria which are facultative anaerobes. They are able 
to utilize electron donors in the preferential order: dissolved oxygen, nitrate, then 
sulfate (Sauthier et al., 1998). When it comes to serving as efficient electron donors 
for denitrification, the carbon source needs to be small and readily biodegradable 
(Suhr et al., 2015), such as acetate and propionate. The amount of carbon source 
required depends on its chemical nature and bacterial species. As a rule of the thumb, 
after achieving anoxic water condition, the carbon source is used to denitrify NO3
- as 
well as support the bacterial growth. For most readily available organic carbon sources, 
a C/N ratio (COD/ NO3
--N) of 3-6 enables complete denitrification (Van Rijn et al., 
2005). An insufficient C/N ratio would result in uncompleted denitrification process, 
where intermediate products, such as NO2
- and N2O would accumulate and they all 
are a toxicological threat to the cultured species and environment. Conversely, the 
excessive supply of carbon leads to the production of hydrogen sulfide (H2S) derived 
from sulfate (SO4
2-), which is also lethal to aquaculture products (Hamlin et al., 2008). 
It is a big concern for marine aquaculture, as seawater contains a big amount of SO4
2- 
(approximately 850 mg SO4
2--S/L in this study). In addition, excess carbon is also 
money-consuming and environmentally hazardous. 
 
Because the feed composition and digestibility are already well described by the 
producer, fish organic waste (FOW) produced in RAS is believed to be relatively 
constant and predictable in terms of both quality and quantity (Letelier-Gordo et al., 
2017). Approximately 50 kg COD/ton feeds is produced in seawater RAS (calculated 
by the model from Dalsgaard & Pedersen, 2016), which mainly originates from fish 
excretions and a small portion of uneaten feeds. Due to the high organic concentration 
and high salinity, disposal of marine aquaculture solid waste into sewage plants is 
inhibited. Discharging into natural water body will also pose a huge threat to the local 
environment (Mirzoyan et al., 2010). Therefore, the treatments of FOW are often 
limited to biogas production or simply leave it to some certain company to handle, 
which are either problematic or expensive.  
 
A cost-efficient and environment-sustainable solution is to use this endogenous 
organic matters in the denitrification process. This method is called single-sludge 
system which has been approved technically feasible (Klas et al., 2006). In RAS, 
because of the stable properties of FOW, it is expected to be a reliable carbon supply 
for aquaculture denitrification. Due to the membrane structure of denitrification 
bacterial, only readily available organics are possible to cross then utilized. Large 
organic matters derived from solid wastes need to be pretreated first (Suhr et al., 
2015).

Anaerobic digestion consists of four steps hydrolysis, fermentation, acetogenesis and 
methanogenesis (Mirzoyan et al., 2010) (Fig. 2). Hydrolysis breaks organic matters into 
small fragments; Fermentation generates VFAs and ethanol; Acetogenesis further 
transforms VFAs into acetate (Suhr et al., 2015) which can directly join TCA to generate 
energy for denitrification. The first two steps are the main purpose of anaerobic 
digestion. While acetogenesis can initiate in the cells once denitrification bacteria 
absorb VFAs. 
 
 
Fig. 2. The process of anaerobic digestion (from http://water.me.vccs.edu/courses/ENV149/ changes/Feat11_picII-
1.jpg). 
 
Apparently, acetate seems to be the best candidate for serving electron in aquaculture 
denitrification according to the bacterial physiology. With the stable anaerobic 
reaction phase, fermentation of organic waste is also feasible within SBR (Sun et al., 
2016), which further contributes to the release of VFAs. Although the fermentation-
denitrification ability activated sludge might hold is not clear, the potential of SBR 
working as single-sludge system should not be ignored. Therefore, acetate, fish 
organic waste (FOW) and fermented fish organic waste (FFOW) were chosen to 
represent the main categories of carbon sources in this aquaculture denitrification 
study. 
 
The products of RAS fermented waste have been described by Letelier-Gordo et al., 
(2017): acetate, ethanol, butyrate, valerate, propionate and formate. Studies showed 
that these readily biodegradable carbons were capable of impacting on denitrification 
rates, sludge production and C/N ratio (Yatong, 1996; Aboutboul et al., 1995; 
Elefsiniotis & Wareham, 2007). Acetate was believed to have the great denitrification 
rate as it directly associated in energy production (Yatong, 1996). Studied showed 
denitrification rates with propionate (Elefsiniotis & Wareham, 2007) and ethanol 
(Yatong, 1996) were half of acetate. The aquantitive analysis of denitrification process 
with fermentation products of FOW has not been well described in aquaculture 
wastewater treatment, especially when SBR is applied. Understanding their effects on

denitrification performance will not only offer management instruction to RAS 
practicer, but also provide supporting to the future single-sludge study. Therefore, 
two extra exogenous carbons (propionate and ethanol) were introduced in this study. 
 
1.5 Research objectives 
Despite SBR’s great potential in denitrifying marine aquaculture wastewater, basic 
information is still lacking. Therefore, this study conducted tests on operational cycle 
time and carbon sources with stimulated saline wastewater, to evaluate their effects 
on denitrification performance by analyzing the denitrification rate as well as total 
ammonia nitrogen and total sulfide production. To further illustrate the potential of 
SBR in the end-of-pipe treatment, assessments on OCT modification, carbon choices 
and horizontal comparison were conducted.

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1 Simulated aquaculture wastewater 
In order to simulate marine aquaculture wastewater, 30.36 g NaNO3 (Sodium nitrate, 
VWR International, USA) were added into 100 L seawater (36 ppt, 15±2 ) to make 
50 mg NO3
--N/L.  
 
2.2 Setup and operation of sequencing batch reactor 
Six sequencing batch reactors (a 10 L glass bottle) (ISO 4796, PYREX, USA) were set up 
to conduct experiments, with 8 L of working volume and 2 L of deposition volume (Fig. 
3). Each rector had a rubber plug with 4 tubes functioning as substrate inlet, 
supernatant outlet, sampling outlet and air outlet. The denitrification process in each 
reactor was complemented by 2 pumps (WT600-2J, LongerPump, China), one for 
feeding SBR and one for drawing the supernatant. The SBR operational cycle was 
further controlled by 3 programmed timers (TS-MNN4, E-line, China), two for the 
aforementioned pumps and one for the stirrer. Mixing was provided by the stirrer (VS-
C10, VWR, USA; C-MAG MS7, IKA, Germany) with a rotation speed of 100 rpm. 
 
 
 
Fig. 3. Sketch of sequencing batch reactor (SBR) 
 
Each operational cycle consisted of 4 steps: fill, react, settle and decant. To make it 
feasible for programming timers, fill lasted 15 min, settle 30 min and decant 15 min.

as SBR was already able to complete the cycles on the daily base.

2.4 Tests of carbon sources  
After OCT tests, a suitable cycle was chosen for the further trials on different carbon 
sources. This time, two more exogenous carbon sources (propionate and ethanol) 
were introduced. Prior to the sampling, activated sludge was cultivated under the 
specific condition for 2 weeks to acclimate. 
 
2.5 Sample analysis 
All the tests were conducted in 4 replicates. Regarding the variation of OCT, different 
time intervals were set for chemical parameter sampling (Table 2). In addition, 
activated sludge biomass was sampled at the halfway of the reaction. 
 
Each sample for water parameter measurements was immediately centrifuged at 
4500 rpm and 5  for 15 min right after they were obtained, then filtered through 
first 0.45 µm then 0.20 µm by syringe filters (Filtropour S, SARSTEDT, Germany). 
Filtered samples for VFA and ethanol measurements plus the biomass samples were 
subsequently preserved with an addition of 1% v/v sulfuric acid (4 mol/L H2SO4, Merck 
Millipore, Germany). Filtered samples for total sulfide analysis was added with 10% 
v/v Zinc-acetate solution. All the samples were maintained at 4 until further analysis.  
 
Biomass samples without filtration were for total COD (TCOD) analysis. Soluble COD 
(sCOD) was acquired by filtering activated sludge through 0.20 µm syringe filters. Both 
were determined by digestion vials (LCK 314, 514, 1414, Hach Lange, Germany). The 
particulate COD (xCOD, calculated as TCOD - sCOD) stood for activated sludge biomass.

30min with a flow rate of 0.9 ml/min at 45. The system was able to detect 0.226-

denitrification performance. The concentration variations of NO3
- and NO2

Ekama & Wentzel (2008) divided the possible carbon sources in general denitrification 
system into 3 categories: readily biodegradable organics (RBCOD), slowly 
biodegradable organics (SBCOD) and the biomass of denitrification bacteria 
themselves through cellular death and lysis. RBCOD can pass directly through cell 
membrane resulting in high denitrification efficiency (7-10 times of SBCOD). While 
SBCOD needs to be broken down into smaller compounds first and then can be utilized 
by denitrification bacteria. However, the extracellular break-down is slow and 
becomes the limiting factor. Based on the aforementioned sorting method, the 
common carbon sources in aquaculture denitrification can therefore be classified. 
Take this study as instance, acetate obviously belonged to RBCOD, FOW mostly to 
SBCOD and FFOW partially to the both. Their theory was able to explain that in this 
SBR tests, acetate, FOW and FFOW groups could obtain stepwise denitrification rates 
regardless of OCT.

According to the theory of Ekama & Wentzel (2008), ethanol and propionate also 
belonged to the RBCOD group. Thus, their denitrification rates stood higher than the 
FOW and FFOW groups. 
 
Furthermore, in order to generate energy, RBCOD needs to find a way to form Acetyl-
CoA and enter tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA). The formation of Acetyl-CoA requires 
acetate as the substrate along with coenzyme A (CoA) and ATP. Due to the direct 
engagement in TCA, acetate group showed the highest denitrification rate among all 
5 carbon groups. As for the propionate, the biodegradation pathway is complicated. 
First, it formed Propionyl-CoA. Then a series of enzymatic carboxylation and enzymatic 
epimerization reactions (Yatong, 1996) converts it into Succinyl-CoA which is an 
intermediate component in TCA. As such, propionate group had lower denitrification 
rates than acetate group. There are several pathways for ethanol biodegradation. The 
most significant one is initiated by alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) where ethanol is 
oxidized into acetaldehyde. It further enters mitochondria and gets oxidized into 
acetate by mitochondrial aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH). Compared to acetate and 
propionate, ethanol requires an extra step of acidification which slows down its 
utilization efficiency in the denitrification process. However, the alternative pathways 
might be able to compensate to some extents or maybe acidification of ethanol only 
occupies a small portion of energy, so that ethanol group had slightly lower removal 
rate of NOx
- then propionate. In Yatong’s (1996) freshwater batch study, acetate, 
propionate, and ethanol had the same ranks of denitrification rate as in this study, 
meanwhile propionate performed only 3.7% better than ethanol (Table 7). However, 
Aboutboul et al. (1995) observed higher denitrification rate with propionate than 
acetate (Table 7). The dominant bacteria which belonged to genera of Pseudomonas 
might favor propionate.

of activated sludge was achieved, it might affect the cellular structure of sludge flocs 
and extracellular polymeric substance production (She et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2015). 
Therefore, they recommend a relatively high C/N ratio in saline wastewater 
denitrification. Overall, considering the dissolved oxygen in the influent, the addition 
of external carbon in denitrification process should be conservative.

clear decline along the time in each OCT test. However, it showed no significant 
correlation between TAN variation and OCT length, as TAN removal rate varied in a 
random pattern. In 2h cycle test, FFOW group had the highest TAN removal rate 
(1.97±1.18 mg N/h/g xCOD) among all the tests.

3.5 Total sulfide in SBR operation 
As for the total sulfide, the variations were not clear either (-0.28 to 0.38 mg S/h/g 
xCOD). Only 2 cases in FFOW group reached around 2 mg S/L after the 6h operation 
while the effluent of the rest groups remained below 0.4 mg S/L at the end of the 
operation. There was no clear correlation between the content variation and 
operational time or carbon source.  
 
With the presence of adequate carbon source, NO3
- and NO2
- are the better terminal 
electron acceptors than SO4
2- in seawater denitrification. However, once NO3
- and 
NO2
- are depleted, SO4
2- becomes the next candidate to receive the electron from the 
carbon and initiate sulfate reduction which generates sulfide ions (S2-) (Lee et al., 
2000). Sulfide (S2-) or as hydrogen sulfide (H2S) is extremely toxic. Because the 
seawater generally contains a high concentration of SO4
2-, denitrification becomes a 
common problem in this perspective. H2S monitoring and control systems are 
essential in the end-of-pipe treatment.  
 
In this study, 2 mg S/L in the effluent might be the byproduct of fermentation in FFOW 
group. Under this assumption, SBR seemed to have a control over sulfate reduction in 
this seawater study especially when external carbon sources were introduced. Van 
der Hoek et al. (1988) found that a hydraulic residence time (HRT) of 5 hours would 
initiate sulfide production in an upflow sludge blanket denitrification reactor fed with 
methanol. However, they denoted that it took 10 hours to start sulfide accumulation 
in the batch experiments and the equilibrium concentration stayed at around 3.6 mg 
S/L. Based on their findings, sulfide production required certain time to be fully 
activated after NO3
- or NO2
- was totally depleted. Apparently, in this study the reaction 
times (5, 3 and 1h) of SBRs were not long enough to fully arouse sulfate reducing 
bacteria.  
 
Still, H2S is extremely toxic to most fish even below 1 mg S/L (Balderston & Sieburth, 
1976). And SBR is not a guarantee of zero sulfide production especially when FFOW as 
an endogenous carbon is introduced into the reactor. Therefore, to avoid the potential 
environmental hazard by the effluent, certain processes are essential in the end-of-
pipe treatment. Balderston & Sieburth (1976) suggested a quick aeration to oxidize S2- 
to SO4
2- rather than pumping the water back to RAS as hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and 
sodium sulfide (Na2S) were able to impair the efficiency of nitrification biofilters. 
 
3.6 Foam formation in SBR 
The biggest challenge in this experiment was to control the bacteria biomass, as the 
big variations existed within and between groups (Fig.15). Foam formation was the

primary cause. The activated sludge started to rise once the stirrer stopped, later the 
floating flocs (Fig. 16) were decanted from SBR.  
 
 
 
 
Fig. 15. The biomass of activated sludge in operational cycle time tests. n=4 
 
Foam formation is usually caused by 2 main factors: the presence of M. parvicella, and 
insufficient release of N2 gas from activated sludge (Jenkins et al., 2003). M. parvicella 
tends to attach N2 air bubble and rises to the surface forming a thick, chocolate-brown 
colored float (Jenkins et al., 2003). As a microaerophile, it also favors low DO condition 
(even < 0.4 mg/L) in activated sludge. Studies showed that VFAs were barely used by 
M. parvicella, instead it preferred more complex compounds (Rossetti et al., 2005). 
FFOW from RAS could also be a big nutrient supplier for M. parvicella, because even 
after fermentation, it still contained desirable amount of organic matters. Shortening 
the HRT in SBR could minimize its growth which unfortunately would disturb the 
denitrification process (Rossetti et al., 2005). Another possible reason for foam 
forming locates on denitrification bacteria themselves. Denitrification is a process that 
generates N2 gas. Once the water is saturated with N2, it released as small air bubbles 
which firmly attached to activated sludge (Jenkins et al., 2003).  
 
Roughly based on the ratio between floating activated sludge and settled activated 
sludge (Fig. 16), the conclusion could be drawn that it might be the combination of 
two causes. After all, the biomass of M. parvicella could not outcompete the 
denitrification bacteria in SBR.  
 
The foam formation in SBR is a big concern due to the existence of decant stage where 
the supernatant is discharged. In this experiment, it resulted in the big biomass 
variation of denitrification bacteria. Compensative re-enrichment of activated sludge 
was conducted between tests, however, still could not eliminate the problem. 
Therefore, the improvement of systematic setup is essential in this case. Increasing 
the reaction time in SBR may solve the over-saturated problem, but it lowers the daily

3.7 Optimizing operational cycle time in SBR denitrification 
Statistic tests strongly indicated that the reduction of OCT had positive impacts on 
denitrification rates with acetate and FOW. However, the impact in FFOW group was 
not clear. As for the FOW and FFOW groups, it was clear that the denitrification 
process went through 3 stages: VFA-present stage, VFA-limiting stage and a 
transitional period. In FOW group, 6h cycle condition (consisted of all 3 stages) did not 
favor the performance of denitrification bacteria. When OCT reduced to 4h or 2h, 
somehow bacteria were not restrained by VFAs, thus denitrification rate had evident 
enhancement but at the cost of insufficient nitrogen removal. Conversely, the 
denitrification efficiency with adequate VFAs derived from FFOW did not differ along 
the cycle tests. The impact of OCT was slightly evident in VFA-limiting stage. 
 
Reduction of OCT is essential for the enhancement of handling capacity. Due to the 
relatively low denitrification efficiency with FOW especially in the VFA-limiting stage, 
the reduction of reaction time is not recommended in order to achieve 99% removal 
rates. As for the FFOW, the reduction of OCT did not change the denitrification rate in 
the VFA-present stage. However, it seemed to have some positive impacts when 
activated sludge obtained carbon mainly by self-fermentation. Thus, under the 
premise of completed denitrification, shortening the SBR cycle is recommended 
regarding FFOW. Exogenous carbon sources are commonly used in commercial 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
31
denitrification. In this pilot test, denitrification rates with acetate improved when OCT 
reduced from 6h to 2h. However, the data points were not enough to describe the 
exact regression relationship between OCT and denitrification rates. In this 2h cycle 
test, a completed denitrification with acetate was achieved in less than 45min. Based 
on it, reducing OCT is highly recommended. 
 
On the other hand, the rest stages of SBR can also be optimized. Fill and decant stages 
can be shortened in time if the flow rates increase. In a settling efficiency test (Fig. 14), 
within 5min a significant decrease (P<0.05) of dry matter content (presenting the total 
suspended solids) in the solution was observed. However, the following 25min 
witnessed no evident variation. Based on this small experiment, prolonging the time 
did not affect the settling efficiency. Therefore, the reduction of settle time is also 
possible.

sulfide and total ammonia nitrogen in the effluent. Denitrification performance with

3.9 Comparing SBR with media-based reactors: a case study 
The denitrification rates determined in this study only represented the kinetic 
performance of activated sludge which could not be directly applied in system 
dimension as SBR ran cyclically with non-reaction periods too. Therefore, a 
modification was conducted to offer a comprehensive assessment of SBR.  
 
Kinetic denitrification rate reached around 80 mg NOx
--N/h/g xCOD in 2h cycle test 
with approximately 0.8 g xCOD/L bacterial biomass (Table 11). It successfully achieved 
nitrate depletion of 400 mg per reactor. As SBR run in a daily basis, handling capacity 
could reach about 4.8 g NO3
--N per day per reactor. In other words, the dimension 
unit of SBR was 600 g N/d/ m3 working volume. Besides, the reactor was able to handle 
a spike nitrate load of 80 mg N/L.  
 
In order to evaluate the potential of applying SBR in seawater aquaculture 
denitrification, a comparison with traditional media-based systems was conducted in 
a unified situation. If a RAS farm has an annual production of 1000 tons of marine fish, 
usually daily NO3
--N production can reach 119 kg (calculated by the model from 
Dalsgaard & Pedersen, 2016). 
 
The denitrification rate of approximately 80 mg N/h/g xCOD was chosen to represent 
the performance of commercial SBR, as it was also feasible to operate it in 2h cycle. 
To avoid accumulation, the daily nitrate production (199 kg NO3
--N) needs to be 
removed sufficiently. Ideally, SBR should be upgraded to have a total working volume 
of 198 m3 (Table 12).

The traditional media-based denitrification system has been studied in aquaculture 
wastewater treatment. Hamlin et al. (2008) conducted an experiment on commercial 
scale upflow biological filters in freshwater condition, and maximum handling capacity 
was 670–680 g N/d/m3 media regardless of carbon source types. In order to avoid 
nitrate accumualtion, a total volume of approximately 178 m3 is required (Table 12). 
Dupla et al. (2006) and Labelle et al. (2005) all studied denitrification with methanol 
in seawater moving-bed bioreactors (MBBR), and respectively got volumetric 
denitrification rates of 2 and 1.77 kg N/d/m3 biomedia. In the same scenario, 
considering the media filling rate in order to ensure the free-moving of biomedia, the 
systems should be expanded to around 147 and 269 m3 (Table 12) accordingly. Fixed 
bed system had similar denitrification rate as moving bed (Dupla et al., 2006; Labelle 
et al., 2005; Sauthier et al., 1998), but required no filling rate. Apparently the total 
working volume was much downsized, but more systematic problems disturbed the 
maintenance and operation. Sauthier et al. (1998) conducted a pilot denitrification 
experiment on fixed bed. With only 8 L packing, a backwashing was already required 
once every three days to avoid clogging. And total sulfide production reached 2 mg 
S/L in the effluent.  
 
In terms of denitrification efficiency, SBR is still competitive comparing to traditional 
media-based system (Table 12). In the same scenario of processing daily RAS 
wastewater, SBR required similar level of total working volume to media-based 
reactors, except the fixed bed system. Furthermore, as mentioned above, the OCT can 
be further shortened in practical operation. If fill, decant and settle stages reduce to 
30min meanwhile the kinetic denitrification rate sustains at the same level for 1h 
reaction, then the handling capacity of SBR can upgrade to 800 g/d/m3 working 
volume, which further reduces the required total working volume to 149 m3. 
Traditional media-based systems require regular backwash which can impair 
denitrification efficiency periodically, thus the accumulation of nitrate and even nitrite 
has a chance to occur in the effluent. Conversely, the activated sludge in SBR is reliable 
for a long-term operation with less maintenance (Strous et al., 1998).

4. Conclusion  
To evaluate the potential of applying SBR as an alternative denitrification system in 
seawater aquaculture, different tests on OCT and carbon sources were conducted. 
 
Denitrification rates with acetate increased with the reduction of OCT. The similar 
positive impact on nitrate removal was also observed in FOW group, while the impact 
on NOx
- removal was not evident. A short OCT meaning less starvation stress, might 
increase the cell viability and select better bacterial strains. However, denitrification 
rate with FFOW was not much affected by OCT. Overall, the reduction of OCT is 
recommended when acetate and FFOW are applied. Due to the low denitrification 
rate of FOW, a long OCT ensures nitrate depletion. Other than the reaction stage, the 
rest stages of SBR can also be optimized.  
 
Activated sludge fed on the endogenous carbons, experienced VFA-present stage and 
VFA-limiting stage in denitrification. In the VFA-present stage, activated sludge fed on 
FOW relied more on self-fermentation to obtain VFAs while it tended to acquire most 
carbon from available VFAs in FFOW group. 
 
Ethanol and propionate were introduced in the 4h cycle test. With the adequate 
presence of readily biodegradable organics, the denitrification rates ranked 
descendingly as acetate, propionate, ethanol, FFOW and FOW. C/N ratios of 
exogenous carbons in this study followed the assumption that approximately 20% of 
COD contributed to cell synthesis. New Stoichiometric equations were developed 
based on the findings.  
 
TAN level maintained below 0.2 mg N/L in exogenous carbon groups regardless of OCT, 
no evident variation pattern showed in FOW group while it reduced in FFOW group. 
However, the application of endogenous carbon sources led to the TN residue in the 
effluent. As for total sulfide production, only 2 cases in FFOW group reached around 
2 mg S/L after 6h reaction while the effluent of the rest groups remained below 0.4 
mg S/L. The total sulfide in SBR was mostly under control, but no guarantee for zero 
production. Besides, foam formation was a common problem in this study which 
caused the variance of activated sludge biomass.  
 
Comparing to media-based reactors, SBR is still competitive in daily handling capacity, 
system dimension and system maintenance through a case study. Therefore, further 
tests on a larger or commercial scale SBR treating marine aquaculture wastewater are 
recommended.
