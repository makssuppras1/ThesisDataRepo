Abstract 
 
Hemophilia is common bleeding disorder that comes in 3 different types, hemophilia A, B, and 
C. Hemophilia A is the most common form, and affects 1 in approximately every 10.000 males 
all around the world, and 70 % of these have inherited it from their mothers. 
 
When a person gets hurt, a cascade of coagulations factors gets initiated, which ends in a 
cross-linked fibrin clot that seals the wound, so a lowered amount of any of these factors, will 
prolong the time it takes to clot, or here called the clotting time.  
Hemophilia A is defined as having a lowered amount of coagulation factor VIII, where 
Hemophilia B is and decrease in FIX, and hemophilia C is a lowered level of FXI.  
In hemophilia A and B, the disease comes in 3 different intensities, based on the amount a 
patient has, compared to a set baseline. Roughly 50 % of all the people suffering from 
hemophilia have the most severe case, which are levels below 1%. 
 
The normal way of treating patients with hemophilia, is replacement therapy, where they are 
injected with the missing factor, but there is a chance that the body will produce antibodies 
against the treatment, as it sees it as foreign. The antibodies renders the treatment 
ineffective, so new treatments is needed in order to treat the patients. When treating people 
with hemophilia, the weekly injections have a big impact on the customer compliance so if a 
treatment that can be given e.g. subcutaneous could be found, the customer compliance would 
increase a lot. A big aspect of increasing the customer compliance, is that since the majority of 
people suffering from hemophilia inherited it from their mothers, so a lot of babies have to get 
weekly IV injections, which have its accompanying toll on the babies and parents alike. 
 
This report works with the optimization of an Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time assay, 
which here measures light absorbance as the fibrin clot is made. The assay have been modified 
in order to run in a 96 well microtiter plate, to increase the throughput of the assay, so it can 
function in a High Throughput setting. There have been experimented with further optimization 
to a 384 plate format, but this adaption needs more work before it can be used reliable.  
The modified APTT assay (my assay) has been compared to a Thrombin Generation time assay 
as well as a Coatest assay, in order to see if there was a correlation between the 3. All 3 
assays have compared the effect of 2 different anti FVIII antibodies, a monoclonal anti-FVIII 
antibody called 4F30, and a polyclonal version. All 3 assays showed that the antibodies 
inhibited the clotting in human plasma, but the TGT could not differentiate the 2 from each 
other, and the APTT and Coatest did not agree on which antibody was most potent. 
All the assays have also been used to compare porcine plasma to the human plasma, in order 
to see if there is a correlation. The TGT showed that the polyclonal antibody had no effect on 
the porcine plasma, and the Coatest and my APTT showed that the porcine plasma clotted 
faster with the antibody present, which is opposite of what was expected, but both assays 
agreed on the results. The assay has also been tested on Cynomolgus monkey, where my 
assay was also showed to work properly across species.  
 
Finally, my assay has been compared to a state of the art machine, ACL TOP 550®, currently 
used in hospitals, to see how viable my assay is in terms of throughput, price, and precision. 
My assay was around 40 times less precise, when calculated the level of detection, which stops 
it from being used in a hospital setting, where precision is key.  
The comparison also showed that my assay is at least 5 times faster than the ACL TOP, and 
since the main expense of the ACL TOP is the man hours needed, my assay is at least 5 times 
cheaper, which makes it viable in an early screening of new potential drugs, my assay have 
also been tested in lower volumes to save reagents, which will further decreasing the cost per 
compound tested. The decreased price makes it viable in a screening setup, but the decreased 
precision makes it unavailable for hospital screening of hemophilia patients.

Introduction 
Bleeding and Hemophilia 
Hemophilia is a bleeding disorder, and is a serious threat, even in a modern lifestyle, if it goes 
untreated. In short, hemophilia affects the blood’s ability to properly mount a defense against 
bruising and wounds. 
When a person gets injured, a cascade of blood coagulation factors gets activated in order to 
form a blood clot, and seal the wound. In healthy individuals, the coagulation factors 
(shortened F e.g. FX) get activated (e.g. FXa), and the cascade starts, ending up creating the 
Cross-linked Fibrin clot, as seen in Figure 1, which seals the wound.  
 
Hemophilia is normally found in 3 different variants: A, B, and C, where A is the most common 
form, B is more uncommon, and C is rarely found. 
People suffering from hemophilia A have a lowered amount of FVIII, which is part of an 
amplification step[1], so if this step does not get initiated, inadequate amounts of fibrin is 
produced, and the wound will either have a much higher clot time, or in worst cases, no 
clotting will occur at all.

The intensity of the bleeding disorder is generally linked to the amount of the corresponding 
factor, where the lower the amount, the longer the increase in clotting time, and in people with 
e.g. severe Hemophilia A (<1% of normal levels [1]), the response is so hindered that it can 
even lead to spontaneous bleeding into joints and muscles, which causes pain and 
inflammation.  
Before effective treatment/therapy was developed, people with the Severe cases of hemophilia 
have also died of incidents that does not seem possible to die from, such as a bitten tongue, or 
a cut to the finger[1]. 
 
Hemophilia A currently affects 1 in approximately 10.000 males, and of all the people suffering 
from hemophilia A, around 50% have the severe version[5]. To make things worse for the 
hemophiliac, there is a chance of patients developing inhibitors towards the normally given 
treatment, which is replacement therapy[6], since the body sees it as foreign. As a result from 
the inhibitors, treatments need to be modified in order to work, or new treatments have to be 
found.  
Another big aspect of optimizing the treatments for hemophilia patients, is that most patients 
inherit the disease from their mothers [7], meaning that treatment also have to be applied to 
newborn babies, as IV injections, which causes a lot of complications for both the patient, but 
also for the parents. 
If a treatment could be found that is capable of e.g. subcutaneous administration or in best 
case, oral liquid administration, it would be a major improvement to the customer compliance.  
 
This report works with the optimization of an Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT) 
assay, in order to have it to function in a microplate setting, which will ease a possible 
screening process, and save valuable reagents, such as hemophiliac blood plasma.  
If the assay gets optimized properly to microplates, it can be used as a drug discovery 
screening test, so a better hemophilia treatment can be found, easing the treatment of 
newborn babies and adults alike. 
Furthermore, other coagulation assays will be used to compare with, in regards to strengths 
and weaknesses of the assay. 
 
Clotting 
Clotting is nature’s way of stopping blood loss, this is achieved through 2 separate but linked 
pathways, called primary and secondary hemostasis. 
Primary hemostasis refers to platelet aggregation and platelet plug formation, where the 
secondary hemostasis is a cascade event of coagulation factors (Figure 1), which normally 
starts with a trauma or a damaged surface, and ultimately ends in a fibrin mesh that stabilizes 
the plug[8], which seals the wound.  
This report primarily works with optimizing the APTT assay, so only the secondary hemostasis 
pathway will be used and described. 
 
The cascade event goes through multiple steps involving a lot of factors, and a deficiency in 
one of these will cause a prolonged clotting time, resulting in a larger and more frequently 
blood loss.  
Hemophilia A is defined as having a decreased amount of FVIII, which in the cascade, in its 
activated state(FVIIIa), places FIXa and FX close together, and enhances the catalytic effect of 
FIXa [9], so the FIXa can cleave the FX into FXa, which then aids in the last part of the 
cascade. 
Since a single FIXa molecule can cleave multiple FX molecules, if placed close enough 
together, the FVIII step is often referred to as an amplification step[1].

Hemophilia B, which is rarer than hemophilia A, is referring to a decrease in the FIX, which is 
also a big part of the hemophilia A, so the 2 types are clinically indistinguishable from each 
other[1]. 
Hemophilia C, in short, is an FXI deficiency but unlike hemophilia A and B, people with 
hemophilia C rarely experience spontaneous bleeding, and significant hemorrhage, only under 
surgery or extreme trauma[10]. 
There are more bleeding disorders, but this report will focus on hemophilia and mainly 
hemophilia A. 
 
Whether you suffer from hemophilia A or B, the severity of the diseases are categorized the 
same, which is by the amount of the respective factor that you have, compared to normal 
levels “as defined by the current World Health Organization International Standard for Plasma 
Factor VIII:C (as distributed by The National Institute for Biological Standards and Control, 
Potters Bar, Hertsfordshire, UK).” Cite White, Rosendaal [11], a normal level is then set to an 
amount called 1 ‘International Unit’ (IU) /ml ([12] ). 
Generally, if you have < 0.01 IU/ml (<1%), then you suffer from severe hemophilia, and this 
comes with a lot of problems as described above, and below in this report. If the FVIII levels 
are between 0.01 and 0.05 IU/ml then the disease is defined as moderate, and above 0.05 and 
below 0.40 IU/ml is categorized as mild[1, 11]. As listed in Table 1, the symptoms greatly 
improves from the severe to even the moderate group, and this can be done by “only” 
increasing the FVIII levels to 1 %, which is why many studies try to obtain this level[13, 14]. 
 
Both hemophilia A and B are X-linked disease disorders, so it primarily affects males that have 
received it from their carrier mother, but up to 30 % gets it from spontaneous mutations [7], 
and from all the people suffering from hemophilia A, an inversion of the first 22 exons (and all 
introns in between) is the most prevalent, which affects 45 % of people suffering from severe 
hemophilia [1].

Hemophiliac plasma 
As mentioned before, people suffering from hemophilia, have problems stopping bleeding 
without aid, and in the most severe state, it could kill them if no aid is given. Because of the 
associated risk from getting wounds and bruises, hemophiliac plasma is a sparse resource that 
should not be used on non-essential experiments, like optimizing an assay or early screening 
of libraries, if it can be done without. 
In order be able to optimize the assay and compare different equipment, normal plasma has 
been used with an added antibody, which makes the plasma mimic hemophilia A plasma, by 
eliminating FVIII with an anti FVIII antibody (αFVIIIa).  
In order to have the plasma mimic the hemophiliac plasma for the assay, the normal frozen 
plasma (Frozen Unassayed NORMAL Control Plasma) has been thawing for 15 min at 37 °C, 
then adding the αFVIIIa to a final concentration of 250 nM, unless other is specified, and then 
letting it incubate 15 min again at 37 °C. 
Since this report focuses on optimizing an APTT assay, hemophilic plasma has only been used 
to gain control references to compare with the used normal plasma. 
 
The clot time and behavior of the normal plasma with the added αFVIIIa, have been compared 
to real hemophiliac plasma, in order to assess the quality of the tested αFVIIIa, as well as the 
different assays. This is described in the respective assay sections.

Purpose of the assays 
Different assays have been used throughout the project in order to fully determine:  
a) The viability of the microplate version of the APTT, and

c) And also to compare human plasma to that of different animals, to see if the assay can

Experimental work 
APTT 
Theory 
An APTT assay is used to analyze the intrinsic and 
common pathway of coagulation from Figure 1[17], 
in this setup the assay is used by measuring a 
difference in light absorbance, which occurs when 
fibrinogen is cleaved to fibrin, which is used to make 
the cross linked fibrin clot mentioned earlier. 
The assay will then produce a graph with increasing 
absorbance, as more and more fibrin is produced, as 
illustrated in Figure 2. Each graph can then be fitted 
to a curve (here a nonlinear regression and 
log(inhibitor) vs. response -- Variable slope (four 
parameters have been used) [18], and a parameter 
for when the well is clotted, is selected. 
In this report, the clot criteria are set to the point, 
where 50% of the difference in absorbance is 
reached, as marked on Figure 2 by the dotted line. Other assays might use the 2nd derivative, 
but what is important is that the same criteria are used, so the results can be compared. 
 
The clot time can then be compared to a control, and different compounds can then be 
evaluated on their impact on the specific clotting aspect, in various concentration. 
The assay works by thawing the plasma and then let it incubate with or without an antibody, 
then mix it with a sample of interest (which can also be the antibody), add phospholipids, and 
last, Calcium Chloride (CaCl2) in order to start the coagulation. 
 
The name ‘partial’ comes as a modification of the original assay, where the phospholipid 
concentration was the only thing that was controlled. Now the phospholipid and a surface 
activator concentration are controlled, “which accelerated clotting but did not correct the 
prolonged clotting times of hemophilic plasma” [19] line 7 at May 2017. 
The phospholipid used in this report is Siemens pathromtin® sl without modifications, it 
contains a vegetable phospholipid as well as a silicon dioxide activator[20].

Assay quality 
In order to get meaningful results, the data needs to be significant, and to test this, results 
have been subjugated to a Z-factor value comparison, and/or the more known, two-tailed, 
two-sample equal variance, t-test, which is a well-used tool to clarify whether or not a result is 
significantly different to its corresponding control.  
The Z-factor is a statistical tool used in HTS, to predict hits from a library of compounds. 
The Z-factor works with the sample and control mean as well as the standard deviation, in 
order to assess the quality of the data. The assumption is made that data is normal 
distributed, and that the sample is different from the control. The data distribution in a Z factor 
should behave like Figure 4.

Automation 
In order to fully control the addition times of all the reagents, and to minimize human errors in 
pipetting, as well as the previously mentioned incubation times, and error handling, the assay 
has been modified to be run on a HAMILTON® Microlab Standard Enclosed NIMBUS4. 
A Venus program has been made for the NIMBUS4 in order to run the majority of the assay, 
since the used NIMBUS does not have a reader attached, the plate still needs to be read 
elsewhere. 
In order not to waste too much plasma, this was added by hand to the plate, the reason being 
that the robot leaves a fair amount in the reservoirs in order to function. If the assay is made 
in large scale, the percentage left behind will be more acceptable, but since this is an 
optimizing test, small amounts are used. 
 
  
The NIMBUS4 is also “only” equipped with 4 pipetting channels so there is still a difference 
between when the individual wells get CaCl2 added and when they get measured, but since it is 
the same each time the difference can be subtracted.  
Figure 15 shows the data obtained from the NIMBUS, before (A) and after (B) correction of the 
incubation time. When comparing the result and the associated Z’ from Figure 15 with the 
“handmade” data in e.g. Figure 9A, the improvement in quality is clear, even when comparing 
to the unadjusted version. 
If the results in Figure 15B are closely examined, then a small separation of col 1 and 3 can be 
seen. This can be due to imprecise addition time in the machine, under more controlled 
observation, the Z’ might be even higher. Since the used machine only has 4 channels, even 
better results might also be possible if it was run on a bigger workstation with more channels 
to get even more precise data.

Thrombin Generation 
Another assay that has been used to compare the antibodies effect on clotting, is a ‘Thrombin 
Generation Time’ (TGT) assay, whereas the name indicates, measures the thrombin 
generation, which first gets generated in usable amounts after minutes to hours, rather than 
seconds, when compared with the APTT. 
As seen from Figure 1, Thrombin is needed in order to cleave fibrinogen into fibrin, so the 
thrombin generation shows both an earlier and later aspect of coagulation as described below. 
Setup 
A TGT assay, uses fluorescence in order to detect the amount of thrombin, (in this case) a 
plasma sample can generate over time.  
The assay works by mixing the plasma (with optional antibodies), a trigger that starts 
coagulation, and a fluorescent substrate [23].  
The fluorescent substrate used in this report comes from a FluCa-kit from ‘Thrombinoscope®’ 
that contains the fluorescent substrate Z-Gly-Gly-Arg-AMC [24], which is cleaved by thrombin 
to its fluorescent form. 
An advantage with the Fluorescent substrate is that both Platelet-Rich-Plasma and Platelet-
Poor-Plasma (PPP) can be used without the need for defibrination [25], giving a greater range 
of reagents to choose from. 
 
In my setup 35 µl of plasma is mixed with 5 µl antibody in a suitable 96 well plate, and 
incubated for 10 min, so the antibody can inhibit, then 10 µl of the above mentioned FluCa, 
and 10 µl of a trigger is mixed, and the mix is then measured over 2 hours in a suitable 
reader, and the result is then analyzed as described later. 
The trigger can be of different kinds, where either Tissue Factor (TF), or kaolin is used to start 
either the extrinsic or intrinsic pathway respectively, in most of the experiments done in this 
report. The chosen trigger has been PPP-Reagent LOW from Thrombinoscope [26], which is a 
TF trigger. 
 
The intensity, or amount of fluorescence present at the end of the TGT assay, represent the 
amount of thrombin generated in the given time span. 
When analyzing the result of a TGT, it is more important to look at the speed, at which the 
fluorescent substrate is made, than the end 
point, since e.g. a tenth amount of 
thrombin generated will not necessarily 
produce a tenth of the fluorescent due to

of

non-cleaved 
substrate.

The experiment was run with a comparison of the previously used monoclonal 4F30 antibody, 
and the pAb, in order to see if the TGT can determine a difference, like in the APTT, and if the 
antibodies even influence the thrombin generation. 
The result can be seen in Figure 20, and from the figure it can be seen that both of the 
antibodies have an impact on the overall thrombin generation, but what is also interesting, is

Determination of Antibody Potency 
As a further validation of the found antibody activity, another assay was conducted with the 
previously found antibodies. 
Coatest 
The Coatest is a very simple 2 step hemophilia test, that has been known since the 50’s, which 
determines the potency of the FVIII antibody[29], by measuring the generation of FXa from 
FX[30].  
As mentioned above FX does not need FVIII in order to be cleaved, but since the FX and FIXa 
molecules almost never meet and react on their own, if a test is conducted with an excess of 
FIXa and FX the speed of the generated FXa is directly linked to the amount/potency of the 
FVIII[29]. 
 
The assay is here used in combination with the other 
assays to see if there is consensus between the 
results. 
I have used a “COATEST SP FVIII” kit from 
CHROMOGENIX, which uses a chromogenic substrate 
that is cleaved by FXa, freeing the chromophoric 
group called pNA[29], which gives a clear yellow 
color that can be read at 405 nm.  
By using an excess of FIXa and FX, the kit 
determines the amount of substrate cleaved in a 
given time period (here 5 min exactly).  
The assay is here used to test the antibodies used 
and found during the APTT assay optimization, on 
human plasma as well as the later described pig 
(porcine) plasma. 
My results are found in Figure 24, indicating that 
both tested antibodies work on human plasma 
(inhibiting FVIII) at a certain concentration, and that 
the 4F30 is more potent on human plasma than the polyclonal version, which does not match 
the results from my APTT assay. 
I have compared my data to the departments previously obtained results, and the trend is the 
same.

Precision 
When the ACL TOP test a sample, it runs a parallelism test, which works by comparing the 
measurement from multiple concentrations, of the unknown sample and comparing them to a 
standard curve[36]. 
The ACL TOP obtains a standard curve by making 8 different concentrations with calibration 
plasma, which have an exact FVIII amount present (different from each batch, but stated on 
the container). The calibration is tested in triplicates, from 150 % of the calibration amount to 
0 %, the data is then transformed by √𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑐𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 𝑎𝑛𝑑
1
√𝑐𝑙𝑜𝑡 𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑒 [37], and the 6 highest 
concentrations is then fitted to a 2nd degree polynomial curve, and the lowest 6 is fitted in a 3rd 
degree polynomial. The 2 polynomial curves are then merged, and the data can be compared 
to the fit[35, 37] (how the 2 curves are merged is not specified for the machine). 
After the standard curve is made, the ACL TOP tests each sample 3 times in parallelism, by 
testing different concentration[38] in order to see if there is correlation in the dilutions. The 
measurement is then compared to the previously obtained standard curve, in order to find an 
FVIII level of the sample. 
In this setup, 6 different samples were prepared with different amounts of N8 or N8-GP, which 
is Novo’s own FVIII products (a recombinant FVIII, and a glycoPEGylated version, which have 
a longer half-life[13]), at 20%, 60%, and 90% of normal plasma levels, so that the end result 
of the assay is roughly known, so that the precision can be directly compared to what the 
levels should be. All 6 samples were then tested in triplicates, and parallelism test was run on 
all 18 samples (data not shown). 
The same setup was conducted in my assay, with my own run-through of a calibration curve, 
and tests with the same dilutions. 
The results from the ACL TOP, gave readings in close proximity of what was expected at the 
given FVIII level. When the setup was made on my assay, the clot times did in most cases, get 
higher when a sample was diluted to 50 % and 25 % in the parallelism test, but when 
comparing the triplicates, there was a rather large gap between readings. Furthermore, in my 
assay, a lot of the clot times were higher than what was calibrated to 0 % FVIII, resulting in 
some points being calculated to contain down to -600 % FVIII. 
The overall results of precision in my assay, when comparing to the parallelism test, were 
therefore far from impressive, which we did not think was a fair test towards my assay, as I 
did not have time optimize the setup.

Discussion 
Throughout this project, I have optimized an APTT assay to function in a High Throughput 
setting, by having it yield stable results when run in a microtiter plate. The assay have been 
able to work with normal plasma with an added αFVIIIa, in order to not screen in hemophiliac 
plasma, which is a valuable resource, due the reasons mentioned previously in this report. 
 
The optimal setting for the assay has been found to be shaken for 1 second before each 
reading cycle, to remove double plateaus. The normal plasma mimicked the hemophiliac 
plasma better when a polyclonal αFVIIIa was used in 750 nM. Lastly the assay should be 
performed on a pipetting robot to remove human errors, as well as fully control the addition 
times of the different reagents, for better and more reproducible results. 
 
When comparing my assay to the ACL TOP, the assays have different strengths and 
weaknesses. The ACL TOP is far more precise in terms of measuring and classifying hemophilia 
states in humans, compared to my assay. When comparing the price and speed of the 2

easy to adjust compared to the ACL TOP, which also makes it suitable to test other species, or

patients are born with the disease, and treating newborn babies with IV injection on a 
daily/weekly basis, is not a good solution. In order to get better treatments, a large library of 
compounds needs to be screened, and the adjustment my assay have, makes it more viable 
solution, towards faster and cheaper treatments that can ease the medication of thousands of 
people as well as newborn kids.
