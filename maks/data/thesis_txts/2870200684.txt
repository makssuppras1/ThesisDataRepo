Structure
Structure of the Thesis
This master’s thesis is divided into two main parts: the core content and the supplementary material (Ap-
pendix). The core content follows a structure similar to a traditional research paper, including an abstract,
introduction, method, results, discussion and conclusion. The appendix contains additional data and details
that support the main findings. Below is a short overview of each section.
Introduction:
This section presents the aim of the study, which is to assess the potential shift in environmental burden from
climate biodiversity impacts when replacing conventional products with biogenic alternatives in low-carbon
building design. Additionally, the section introduces the study’s research objectives, the Doughnut Biotool
(Biotool v1) and the case study ”Wood in Construction” used to assess if a burden shift occurs.
Method:
This section outlines the study’s methodology, divided into four parts. The first and second part, it details
the development of a new version of the Doughnut Biotool (Biotool v2), including the development of a new
product library and biodiversity impact factors for the Danish energy grid. The third part explains how
climate and biodiversity impacts of the cases in the ”Wood in Construction” project are assessed using the
tools LCAbyg, Biotool v1 and Biotool v2. Furthermore, contribution analyses are conducted on the cases and
to identify key products and evaluate potential burden shifts. The fourth part explains how the robustness
of Biotool v2 and its results are evaluated.
Results
This section presents the results of the development of Biotool v2 and compares the upgrades with Biotool
v1. Additionally, the results of the impact assessment using the three tools on the buildings are presented,
and the robustness of the tool and its results is evaluated.
Discussion
In this section, the study is placed in the context of existing research in the field, where its findings are
compared to a relevant study. The limitations of the study are discussed and how alternative methods or
data may affect the results and conclusions. Additionally, recommendations for the construction industry
arising from the findings are highlighted and discussed.
Conclusion
This section summarises the main findings of the thesis. It also presents how the study fulfills the research
objectives presented in the introduction.
Appendix
The this section consists of two parts. The first part is included in this document, while the second is stored
in an attached folder due to it containing extensive data. The second part provides additional information,
including extensive data sheets that require an Ecoinvent license to access.

Abstract
Abstract
Climate change and biodiversity loss pose a critical threat to humanity by risking abrupt and destabilising
environmental shifts. This study aims to assess potential shifts in the environmental burden from climate to
off-site biodiversity impacts, when replacing conventional products with biogenic products to reduce climate
impacts in buildings in Denmark. The study assessed environmental impacts through a life cycle assessment
(LCA) of the case study ”Wood in Construction”, which contains six building typologies (BT), each with
four variants (cases) featuring an increasing replacement of conventional building products with biogenic
alternatives. The assessment was carried out using three tools: LCAbyg for climate impact calculation, the
Doughnut Biotool (Biotool v1) and an updated version (Biotool v2) for off-site biodiversity impact evaluation.
The study developed Biotool v2 with new biodiversity impact factors for products used in the case study and
energy reflecting the Danish energy grid from 2025 to 2075. The study found that with Biotool v1, energy is the
main contributor to biodiversity impact across all cases. However, using Biotool v2 the impact from building
products increased but reduced operational energy impact by 74-80%, resulting in a lower total biodiversity
impact.
As product impacts dominate, the study emphasises the importance of considering them when
selecting building products. This study found that replacing conventional building products with biogenic
alternatives reduces climate impact but increases biodiversity impact, highlighting a shift in environmental
burden. Furthermore, the study makes a valuable contribution to the limited research on assessing off-site
biodiversity impacts in the construction sector.
The study highlights the need for further research, including applying an Absolute Environmental Sustain-
ability Assessment (AESA) to evaluate whether the observed burden shifts are problematic when compared
against planetary boundaries. This approach could help the industry prioritise design choices.

Introduction
1 Introduction
The stability of the Earth system relies on nine critical Earth system processes, as defined by the ”Planetary
Boundaries” framework. This framework establishes a Safe Operating Space (SOS) for humanity, representing
the environmental limits within which societies can develop and thrive without causing damage to the planet.
[Rockstr¨om et al., 2009]. In 2023, the framework was updated and addressed that six of the nine bound-
aries have transgressed the SOS [Richardson et al., 2023]. Two of the six transgressed boundaries ”Climate
change” and ”Biosphere integrity” are identified as ”core boundaries” [Birgisdottir et al., 2023]. This is due to
a breach of these independently can push the Earth system out of the stable Holocene state, increasing the risk
of crossing critical tipping points [Dr¨uke et al., 2024]. Such tipping points can trigger abrupt environmental
changes that risk detrimental changes for humanity such as destabilisation of climate e.g. change of average
temperature and biosphere change [Dr¨uke et al., 2024; Kemarau et al., 2024].
Over the past 50 years, humanity’s use of natural resources has tripled. Today, the extraction and processing
of material resources used to support our global economy account for over 55% of the world’s greenhouse gas
emissions and over 90% of total land use-related biodiversity loss [United Nations Environment Programme
International Resource Panel, 2024]. The construction sector contributes heavily to both climate change and
biodiversity loss. Globally, the industry contributes 37% of all CO2 equivalent emissions (CO2-eq). emissions
and consumes nearly 50% of all natural resources such as sand, gravel, clay and wood [R˚adet for Grøn Om-
stilling, 2024]. A similar pattern is seen in Denmark where the construction sector is responsible for 33% of its
greenhouse gas emissions and 31% of the country’s natural resource consumption [R˚adet for Grøn Omstilling,
2024]. Worldwide, it is estimated that the construction completed over the next 40 years will equal all human
construction to date, emphasising the urgent need to address the sector’s environmental impacts [FRI, 2035].
In 2023, the Danish Building Regulations (BR18) set a requirement to carry out life cycle assessments (LCAs)
for all new construction projects to assess the environmental impacts associated with all stages of a build-
ing’s life cycle, such as production and End-of-Life (EoL) [Social- og Boligstyrelsen, n.d.]. In 2025, threshold
values on climate change impact measured in CO2-eq. for each building typology will be provided [Social- og
Boligstyrelsen, n.d.]. To meet this requirement, the Danish construction sector widely uses the digital tool
LCAbyg to quantify and compare the climate impacts of building projects [CONCITO, 2022].
The environmental impacts from buildings are primarily calculated for building products and energy con-
sumption.
Historically, operational energy consumption for buildings has been the largest contributor to
the climate impact of buildings in Denmark [CONCITO, n.d.]. Thus, there has been an increased focus on
reducing carbon emissions from the operational energy consumption of buildings supported by the transition
of the Danish energy grid to renewable sources [Energinet, 2023]. As a result, the focus has shifted and today
the majority of climate emissions are attributed to the embedded carbon in building products [CONCITO,
n.d.].
Hence, reducing embedded carbon is crucial for lowering CO2-equivalent emissions in construction
projects, often achieved by using lower-impact products [CONCITO, 2021]. Biogenic alternatives such as
wood have gained traction due to their low carbon impact [CONCITO, 2021]. However, focusing on biogenic
products to reduce CO2-eq. emissions may risk shifting the environmental burden from climate impact to
other environmental concerns, such as biodiversity impacts. The increasing demand for natural resources both
biogenic and non-biogenic puts pressure on the ecosystems, leading to e.g. change of lands to accommodate
the material demand and thus increase the risk of biodiversity loss [Marques et al., 2019]. Unlike the defined
climate thresholds in BR18, Denmark has not yet established any specific threshold values for biodiversity
impact in the construction industry.

Introduction
Biodiversity impacts from construction can be divided into ”on-site” and ”off-site” impacts. On-site biodiver-
sity impacts occur directly at the construction site, where land use change can disrupt local ecosystems on or
near the building site. In contrast, off-site biodiversity impacts arise throughout the value chain during raw
material extraction, processing and transportation, affecting biodiversity on a larger, often global scale due
to e.g. land use change. Both biodiversity measures have received increasing interest from the construction
sector [ConTech Lab, 2024]. In LCA, land use changes are classified into two main types: land transforma-
tion, which involves altering land for a specific purpose and land occupation, which refers to how the land
is utilised after transformation. Both aspects play a crucial role in assessing the environmental footprint of
construction projects [Koellner et al., 2013]. Currently, on-site biodiversity impacts are easier to assess and
multiple tools exist in Denmark [Dansk Industri, n.d.; Habitats and Molio, 2024]. Off-site biodiversity impacts
are more challenging to assess due to the wide variety of materials sourced from multiple places, often far
from the construction site. This complexity makes it difficult to quantify the consequences and link them to
a specific activity Given the complexity of off-site impacts, it is important to focus efforts on understanding
and mitigating these effects. However, tools for assessing off-site biodiversity are still limited.
The Doughnut Biotool is an off-site biodiversity impact assessment tool in its early stages, developed as
part of the ”The Doughnut for Urban Development” project [Birgisdottir et al., 2023]. This open-source
Excel tool uses inputs from LCAbyg’s result files to calculate impacts of building products and operational
energy consumption throughout a construction project’s life cycle. The impact is expressed in ”species.years”,
detailed in Appendix B.1. The unit is used to describe the local species loss integrated over time [Huijbregts
et al., 2017]. The tool has a product library containing biodiversity impact factors for 65 products. However,
the tool faces several limitations and uncertainties. Its existing product library has a relatively low level of
detail and many commonly used building products are not included. An example of this issue is products
made from multiple materials, such as windows made of glass and wooden frames, which are modelled as
glass only. Similarly, products like paint typically involve multiple paint types in a building, but there is only
one paint product in the library. By comparison, the widely used tool LCAbyg includes a product library
consisting of +400 generic products and their climate impacts. The tool assesses the biodiversity impact of
the operational energy consumption using a static European market mix, mainly consisting of coal and oil and
thus not representative of Denmark [Ecoinvent, n.d.-a;Ecoinvent, n.d.-b]. Furthermore, it does not account
for future changes in the energy grid, including the expected shift towards renewable energy sources [R˚adet
for Grøn Omstilling, n.d.]. An article from BUILD highlights the need for improving the representativeness of
the Danish energy system using the Doughnut Biotool [Francart et al., 2024]. Moreover, the Biotool requires
substantial manual data input, making it time-consuming to use.
This study is developing a new version of the tool referred to as Biotool v2. The study aims to assess if
a burden shift from climate change to biodiversity impacts occurs, when replacing conventional products with
biogenic products in construction to reduce climate impact. This study uses the ”Wood in Construction”
project, developed by Rambøll, referred to as the ”case study”. This case study is further elaborated in
Section 1.1. This study only works with off-site biodiversity impacts (not on-site impacts), which will be
referred to as ”biodiversity impacts” throughout the rest of the report. The following research objectives will
be explored in this thesis:

Method
2 Method
This section outlines the methodology used to develop Biotool v2 and how the three tools were used to assess
the climate change and biodiversity impacts on the ”Wood in Construction” cases. The section is structured
into four key parts, as shown in the figure below:
Figure 2: Overview of the methodology used in the study divided into four steps.
1. Developing the Product Library in Biotool v2 – A new product library with biodiversity impact factors
for building products was developed for Biotool v2, using building products from the six building typologies
(BTs) in the ”Wood in Construction” project.
2. Developing New Biodiversity Impact Factors for Energy in Biotool v2 – Biodiversity impact factors for
the projected electricity and district heating for the Danish energy system (2025-2075) were established to
quantify the impacts of operational energy use of a building.
3. Comparing the Impact Assessments from LCAbyg, Biotool v1 and Biotool v2 – The environmental perfor-
mance of the 24 cases was analysed using the three tools. The results were used to evaluate case performance
across typologies and to conduct a contribution analysis to identify if an environmental burden shift occurs.
Furthermore, the midpoint indicators contributing to the biodiversity impact were examined to identify which
have the most significant effects on biodiversity.
4. Evaluating the Robustness of Biotool v2 – A robustness analysis of Biotool v2 was performed. It tested the
sensitivity of the modelling of products, energy projection and energy source for building typologies BT1-3.

2.1
Developing the Product Library in Biotool v2
This section outlines the methods used for product modelling in Biotool v2 in alignment with the first
research objective. All building products used across the six building typologies (BT) were represented by
generic LCAbyg products. The study used Ecoinvent processes to model the biodiversity impact factors of
the products. Hence, when modelling products in the library, choosing Ecoinvent processes aligned with the
characteristics of the LCAbyg products was essential.
2.1.1
Modelling Biodiversity Impact Factors for Products
The six building typologies comprised a total of 109 unique products. The climate change impact is calculated
in LCAbyg for the technical installations using standardised factors for drainage, water and heating, venti-
lation and cooling installations [Teknologisk Institut and Sweco, 2022; MOE, 2022]. However, no equivalent
values existed for the biodiversity impact. Thus, the technical installations were omitted from this study,
reducing the number of products modelled in the Biotool v2 library to 94.
The biodiversity impact factors in Biotool v2 were modelled using the same approach as Biotool v1, by
summarising the impact factors from the production and End-of-Life (EoL) phases. The production phase
was modelled to resemble the corresponding generic LCAbyg products, while the EoL phase was modelled to
reflect Danish conditions. If a product consisted of multiple materials, the impact was calculated by calculat-
ing the material’s percentage share of the total mass and applying to the relevant processes. In cases where
material separation during EoL was possible, different disposal methods were considered.
The biodiversity impact factors of the products were modelled using the same method as Biotool v1, utilising
Ecoinvent processes from the LCA database ”Cut-off by classification v.3.9.1” Ecoinvent database. The envi-
ronmental impact factors were calculated using the Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method ”ReCiPe
2016 v1.03, endpoint (H)” for the endpoint indicator ”Total: ecosystem quality”, expressed as species.year.
The biodiversity impact factors of the Ecoinvent processes were originally provided per different units. As
all LCAbyg products contain a mass in kg, the impact factors were converted to species.year/kg using the
material densities. The density values from Ecoinvent were used, or if unavailable, from LCAbyg or a web
search. As a result, Biotool v2’s product library only contains the biodiversity impact per kg of product. The
impact factor for a given product is calculated as shown in Equation 1 below.

2.2.1
Modelling the Danish Energy Mix for 2025-2075
To determine the biodiversity impact per kWh for each year of electricity or district heating production, the
Danish energy grid from 2025 to 2075 had to be mapped. The energy grid is comprised of a mix of various

changes throughout the 50-year lifespan of a building.
The energy mix was modelled using the Danish Energy Agency’s ”Energy projection 2024”, referred to as
AF24 [Energistyrelsen, 2024]. The AF24 dataset contains values for the energy production by technology in
Denmark from 2024 to 2050. It includes values for electricity and district heating for each year from 2024-2030

assumed for the years between the data points from AF24, with energy production calculated using linear

during transport from the production site to the user, as well as the import and export of energy between
Denmark and other countries, were excluded from the analysis due to complexity and time constraints.

2.4
Analysing the Robustness of Biotool v2
This section describes the methods used to analyse the robustness of Biotool v2. To address uncertainties
in the tool’s development, its robustness was tested on both the product library and the utilised energy
projection, AF24. Additionally, for building typologies (BT) 1-3, the assumed energy consumption of 100%
was tested.
2.4.1
Analysing the Product Library
Products within three main contributing product types, ”Concrete”, ”Wood”, and ”Insulation”, were selected,
as they are either replaced or used for replacement across all BTs. The selected products were remodelled
using alternative Ecoinvent processes and applied to the case study, in which their environmental impact and
case ranking were compared to those of the original Biotool v2.
2.4.2
Analysing the Energy Projection (AF24)
The robustness of AF24 was tested by comparing it to four alternative energy scenarios. Since no alternative
energy projections, other than those from the Danish Energy Agency, were found, AF24 was compared
with previous projections from the agency: ”Energy Projection 2022” (AF22) and ”Energy Projection 2023”
(AF23). To address the uncertainty of relying on data from only one source, two extreme scenarios of AF24
were developed:
• AF22: Biodiversity impact factors were modelled based on data from the 2022 energy projection.
• AF23: Biodiversity impact factors were modelled based on data from the 2023 energy projection.
• Extreme 1 (Accelerated renewable energy transition): Impact factors representing an energy projection
with an accelerated transition to renewable energy sources. AF24 was adjusted by advancing the 2050
energy mix to 2040 and the 2040 mix to 2035. A new linear interpolation was performed between the
data points for 2030-2035 and 2035-2040. From 2040-2075, no further changes in the mix were assumed.
• Extreme 2 (No renewable energy transition): Impact factors for a modified energy projection of AF24,
assuming no development between 2025-2075.
All four energy projections were modelled using the same approach as AF24, as outlined in Section 2.2.
However, in AF22, natural gas in the pipeline network is phased out in 2030, whereas in AF23, it is phased
Page 11 of 64
==PAGE:20==
Method
out in 2029. The robustness of AF24 was assessed by comparing it to the biodiversity performance of all BTs
using the alternative energy biodiversity factors in Biotool v2.
2.4.3
Analysing the Energy Source of BT1-3
As mentioned in Section 2.3.1, the energy consumption for BT1-3 was not stated in the LCAbyg data and
was modelled as 100% electricity in this study. To address the uncertainty of this assumption, its robustness
was tested comparing it to an alternative energy scenario. According to Statistics Denmark, similar building
typologies are typically also heated with district heating [Danmarks Statistik, 2024]. The alternative scenario
therefore incorporated a mix of electricity and district heating (14% electricity, 86% district heating). The
Energy consumption was calculated using the same method described in Section 2.3.1. A distribution between
electricity and district heating was assumed based on the Row house complex (BT5), which also uses electricity
and district heating. The distribution in BT1-3 follows the percentage distribution between district heating
and electricity as BT5. A detailed calculation of this alternative scenario is provided in Appendix C.4.
The case ranking and percentage distribution between products and energy, using both 100,% electricity and
the mix of electricity and district heating, were compared to assess the robustness of the 100% electricity
scenario.

3.4.1
Evaluating the Product Library
In the robustness analysis, six alternative products from the product types ”Wood”, ”Concrete” and ”Insu-

the alternative library was used in Biotool v2 on the case study and compared to the original results using
Biotool v2.
A specific product from the ”Concrete” product type was selected for remodelling, based on the concrete
product with the highest impact across all BTs in C1, as C1 contains the largest quantity of concrete. Sim-
ilarly, a product from ”Wood” product type was chosen based on the highest impact across all BTs in C4,
as it contains the highest amount of wood. Four specific products containing mineral wool were selected as
insulation products for remodelling due to their identical composition and structure, despite differing in name
and representing different products. Furthermore, these four products are the most commonly used insulation
products in C1-C3. The alternative Ecoinvent processes are found in Appendix D.4.
The results of the analysis showed that all six alternative products have a higher impact per kg product
than their Biotool v2 counterparts. The impact factor for the concrete product increased by 18%, the wood
product by 7%, and insulation products by 45%.
When comparing the case ranking using the original and alternative libraries, the ranking remains the same
for almost all cases, except C2 and C4 in BT5. Using the original product library in Biotool v2 for BT5, C2
ranks 2nd and C4 ranks 3rd. However, using the alternative product library, C2 changes to being the 3rd,

4 Discussion
4.1
Contributing to the Research Field
The research field on off-site biodiversity impacts of constructions is limited.
However, in 2024 BUILD
published an article, which examined the impact of biodiversity in two case studies using the Doughnut Biotool
(Biotool v1) [Francart et al., 2024]. The findings indicate that energy consumption in buildings constitutes
a significant share of the total off-site biodiversity impact, accounting for 64% in an Office building and 39%
in a Single-family house. The article highlights the need for impact factors that better reflect Danish energy
conditions, as the energy impact factor is expected to be lower than the European mix in the tool. The article
tested a static Danish energy mix for electricity production, showing a 20% impact reduction. Comparing the
results of the BUILD article to similar building typologies analysed in this study (BT1 Single-family house
and BT4 Office building) using Biotool v1 shows similar results. In Biotool v1, energy on average contributes
46% and 41% of the total off-site biodiversity impact in BT1 and BT4, respectively. In contrast, with Biotool
v2, energy accounts for only 17% and 15% of the total impact in BT1 and BT4. The decrease stems from
the updated impact factors for energy reflecting Danish conditions, highlighting the importance of modelling
based on local factors, such as the Danish energy mix and its development, rather than a static European
mix.
Additionally, the BUILD article reveals that wood-based products are the main contributor to the total
biodiversity impact from the building products in both case studies (36% of the Office building and 51% of
the Single-family house). However, they have a lower contribution to the total impact on climate change. In
contrast, products such as concrete, insulation and metal have a higher contribution to climate change while
having a lower impact on biodiversity loss. This study showed a similar pattern when using both Biotool v1
and Biotool v2.
Thus, the findings of this study support the observed trends from the BUILD article, indicating that similar
patterns may exist across different studies. While further research is required to confirm this, this study
contributes with additional data to the field.
4.2
Addressing Limitations and Future Research Potentials
The selected Ecoinvent processes represent state-of-the-art technologies and do not account for technological
development in e.g. energy production, manufacturing, or EoL processes. As a result, potential advancements
are not taken into account. For instance, improvements in off-shore wind turbine efficiency could increase
energy output, resulting in a reduction of the biodiversity impact factor.
No exact 1:1 match was found in the Ecoinvent database when modelling the LCAbyg products. Consequently,
the selected processes serve as proxies for the building products. Identifying the most suitable processes was
challenging, as some cases offered multiple options without a precise match, while others lacked representative
processes altogether. For example, all four types of biomass for modelling the energy production are modelled
using the same process, as no other suitable alternatives were found. As biomass significantly contributes to
the impact factor of district heating and electricity, accurately modelling these biomass processes is important
to model more accurate impact factors.
The biodiversity impact factors used to model the impact from operational energy use in this study are
based on production data from the Danish Energy Agency. This data reflects production capacities rather
than actual energy generation and does therefore not account for fluctuations in the renewable energy pro-
duction, such as periods of low wind or sunlight. Additionally, imports and exports were excluded. This

omission is important, as Denmark often imports electricity from neighbouring countries such as Germany
during low-production periods. Since Germany relies more on fossil fuels associated with higher biodiversity
and climate impacts, the modelled biodiversity impact factors are likely underestimated [International Energy
Agency, n.d.]. Consequently, this would not only increase the total impact of the case buildings in Biotool
v2 but also alter the relative distribution between energy and product-related impacts of the cases. However,
the biodiversity impact factors of the electricity, which Denmark imports, should be modelled as the import
mix considering the biodiversity impacts of each supplying country.
This study uses the LCIA method ReCiPe 2016 LCIA methodology, which measures the endpoint indica-
tor ”Ecosystem Quality” (EQ) with the unit ”species.year”. As different LCIA methods include different
impact categories and units, using a different LCIA method could lead to different results and conclusions. A
master’s thesis from 2024 compared impact assessments of five buildings using four different LCIA methods
(ReCiPe 2016, Impact World+ (IW+), LC-IMPACT and PB-LCIA) [Baun et al., 2024]. The study compared
how the four methods assessed the impact on EQ from the buildings. The different LCIA methods lead to
varying rankings of the buildings’ total impacts. While ReCiPe and IW+ show the same ranking, LC-Impact
switches the ranking of the buildings with the highest and second-highest impact and PB-LCIA produces a
different ranking altogether. However, all methods agree on which building has the lowest impact. Despite
this consensus, significant differences exist in the calculated total impacts across the methods. The discrep-
ancies arise from the methods’ differing combinations of impact categories and characterisation factors. For
example, ReCiPe and PB-LCIA use different global characterisation factors, while LC-Impact and IW+ assess
impacts across various ecoregions, offering a higher spatial resolution. The four methods assess biodiversity
loss in different groups of species, with LC-Impact and IW+ covering the broadest range. Although three of
the methods treat the loss of species groups to be equally important, LC-Impact incorporates a vulnerability
factor based on IUCN’s red list of threatened species, weighting highly threatened species higher than non-
threatened species [“IUCN’s redlist”, n.d.].
Given these variations, using a different LCIA method in this study would likely change the percentage con-
tributions of the impact categories in the midpoint analysis. For assessing EQ, using LC-Impact and IW+ in
this study would likely result in a higher impact compared to ReCiPe, due to their higher spatial resolution,
with IW+ potentially obtaining the same building ranking as ReCiPe. The study also compared how many of
the five direct drivers of biodiversity loss (land use, climate change, pollution, overexploitation and invasive
species) are included and which of the three biodiversity aspects (genetic, species and ecosystem diversity)
are considered. However, none of the methods address all five drivers of biodiversity loss or all three aspects
of biodiversity. Thus, the study emphasises that this may lead to an underestimation of the impact on the EQ.
Looking at the results using Biotool v2 and LCAbyg, the cases where both the load-bearing structure and
the facades have been replaced by wood (C3) appear to have the best ranking when considering both climate
change and biodiversity impact. In four out of six BTs, C3 ranks with the second lowest climate change impact
and either the second-lowest or the lowest biodiversity impact. This study found that a burden shift occurs
when conventional products are replaced with biogenic alternatives. However, it is not investigated whether
the C3 and the burden shift are problematic in relation to the planetary boundaries. Whether the shift is
problematic can be assessed using an Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment (AESA). Within the
safe operating space for climate change and biosphere integrity, an ”assigned share” can be allocated to, for
example, a construction project in Denmark. This approach allows an investigation into whether the impact
exceeds the assigned share, thus breaching the planetary boundaries.

4.3
Evaluating Biotools for Construction Industry Use
There are already requirements in the construction industry for documenting climate impact through LCAs, as
well as established threshold values for emissions in the construction of new buildings.[Social- og Boligstyrelsen,
n.d.].
This raises the question of whether similar requirements should apply for quantifying the off-site
biodiversity impact on buildings. While most construction companies in Denmark aim to address biodiversity,
a key challenge is the lack of knowledge, common frameworks and tools [ConTech Lab, 2024]. If requirements
for quantifying off-site biodiversity impacts of buildings are established, the industry can use the Doughnut
Biotool (Biotool v1) and Biotool v2. When using Biotool v1, energy consumption is the primary contributor to
the total biodiversity impact, which encourages users to focus on mitigating energy-related impacts. However,
using Biotool v2, shows that the impact from energy decreases by 74-80% while the impact from building
products increases. Thus, users are encouraged to focus on mitigating impacts from building products. Since
Biotool v2 contains improved modelling of both building products and energy, it is considered to provide a
more accurate representation of the biodiversity impact of Danish buildings.
Therefore, if a user relies on Biotool v1 rather than Biotool v2, efforts to reduce energy-related impacts might
be ineffective, as focusing on reducing the impact from building products would lead to a more significant
reduction in the total impact of a building. Despite discrepancies between the tools regarding the primary
contributing driver, both consistently indicate that cases with the lowest content of biogenic products are the
most favourable to construct with regards to biodiversity loss.
To use Biotool v1 for calculating biodiversity impacts in the construction sector, it would require an update
and expansion of the product library, as well as adjustments of the biodiversity impact from operational
energy use to reflect Danish conditions. This study recommends using Biotool v2, as the tool already includes
updated impact factors for 94 new products and the future Danish energy mix. To improve Biotool v2, the
remaining 300+ LCAbyg products would also need to be modelled to be fully aligned with LCAbyg.
Biotool v1 and Biotool v2 are both open source tools, but require an Ecoinvent database license to access
environmental impact data. Such licenses are often costly, creating a barrier for resource-limited businesses.
This restricts their access to use the tool and prevents them from considering biodiversity impacts when
making decisions on building projects.

Conclusion
5 Conclusion
This study examined potential burden shifts from climate change to biodiversity, when biogenic products are
replacing conventional ones in construction projects. Climate and biodiversity impacts were assessed for 24
case buildings across six typologies, using LCAbyg for climate impact and Biotool v1 and Biotool v2 for off-site
biodiversity impacts. The study developed Biotool v2, which integrates a new product library, energy impact
factors reflecting the Danish grid from 2025 to 2075, and an update of the user flow to enhance efficiency and
reduce manual errors.
A comparison of Biotool v1 and Biotool v2 shows an increased impact from building products in Biotool v2,
while the total impact decreases due to the updated energy factors. Operational energy impact is reduced by
74–80% across all cases, making product optimisation a key opportunity for further reductions. The analysis
of results from all cases indicates that a burden shift occurs, when conventional products are replaced by
wood-based products. Cases with the highest conventional content have the highest climate change impact,
but the lowest or second-lowest biodiversity impact in 5 out of 6 building typologies, when using both biotools.
In contrast, cases with the highest biogenic content show the lowest climate change impact, while biodiversity
impact ranks either highest or second-highest using Biotool v2. While using Biotool v1 does not show a clear
trend in C2-C4, using Biotool v2 shows a more apparent trend of a burden shift occurring. The midpoint
analysis shows a burden shift from climate to biodiversity occurs when the most used concrete product is
replaced by wood.
A contribution analysis was carried out on the cases using LCAbyg and Biotool v2. The analysis highlights a
clear distinction in the primary contributors to the total climate and biodiversity impacts, respectively. Wood
and energy are the main contributors to the biodiversity impact, while insulation and concrete are the primary
contributors to the climate change impact. The study reveals a clear trend in the LCAbyg results, showing a
gradual decrease in climate impact as biogenic products replace conventional ones, while the introduction of
biogenic products often leads to an increase in biodiversity impact. However, the trend is not as clear with
Biotool v2 as with LCAbyg. The highest absolute change in both climate and biodiversity impacts occur
when the load-bearing structure is replaced by wood.
This study supports the findings of an existing article [Francart et al., 2024]. The article and this study both
identify wood as the primary contributor to biodiversity impacts from products. Furthermore, the studies
also showed that when using the original Doughnut Biotool (Biotool v1), energy is the primary contributor
to the total impacts of the buildings. However, the study showed that when updating the impact factors
to represent the Danish conditions, the impact from energy consumption significantly decreased. Thus, this
study provides additional data and insights to further expand the research field.
Furthermore, Biotool v2 serves as a valuable tool for quantifying the off-site biodiversity impacts as the tool
uses LCAbyg files as input, which are already used in the industry. To improve alignment with LCAbyg,
the tool’s library should be expanded to cover the remaining 300+ LCAbyg products. However, the tool’s
reliance on an Ecoinvent database license limits access for resource-constrained businesses to calculate building
impacts.
This study found that an environmental burden shift occurs when replacing conventional building products
with biogenic building products. However, the study does not evaluate whether this shift is problematic,
which can be done using an AESA to compare the impacts of the cases against the planetary boundaries.

Appendix - Introduction
B Appendix - Introduction
Biodiversity can be divided into three different levels: species, ecosystem and genetic diversity. Practically,
it means the number of species, the diversity of the world’s ecosystems/habitats and the genetic composition
of the species [Huijbregts et al., 2017]. The LCIA method ReCiPe 2016 is used in this study to quantify the
species diversity [Huijbregts et al., 2017].
B.1
ReCiPe 2016 (H) and Biodiversity
The ReCiPe method is used to translate elementary flows in life cycle assessments (LCA) to environmental
impacts at both midpoint and endpoint level. This method identifies three endpoint indicators, so-called ”Ar-
eas of Protection” (AoP), which include Human Health, Resource Scarcity and Ecosystem Quality (EQ). This
study specifically focuses on damage to EQ, quantified in the unit species.year [Huijbregts et al., 2017]. The
endpoint is expressed in the unit species.year, which describes the local species loss integrated over time due
to i.e. death, reduced reproduction, or immigration Huijbregts et al., 2017; Hauschild et al., 2018]. According
to the Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES), there are
five direct drivers responsible for biodiversity loss: land use, climate change, pollution, overexploitation, and
invasive species Brond´ızio et al., 2019. The LCIA Recipe 2016 method, takes into account land use, climate
change, and pollution Baun et al., 2024.
Midpoint indicators such as climate change and land use contribute to damage to the ecosystems and biodi-
versity loss. The midpoint indicators can be converted into endpoint indicators using characterisation factors
and thus translate environmental flows to environmental significance and ecosystem damage [Huijbregts et al.,
2017]. For example, the different types of land use’s impact on the ecosystem can be quantified into the num-
ber of species lost. The ReCiPe method offers three social perspectives, each weighing environmental impacts
differently. In this study, the hierarchical (H) perspective is applied, meaning that e.g. the calculated Global
Warming Potentials (GWP) for climate change are weighted according to their effect on global warming over
a 100-year time horizon.
Land use is a significant contributor to biodiversity loss due to loss, fragmentation and modification of
habitats [Hauschild et al., 2018]. In LCA, two main types of land use changes are considered: land transfor-
mation (when land is changed to suit a specific purpose) and land occupation (the way the land is used after
transformation) [Koellner et al., 2013]. Both types of land use changes have a significant impact on ecosystem
quality. These impacts affect the ability of ecosystems to support biodiversity and provide essential ecosystem
services, such as clean air, water filtration and climate regulation [Koellner et al., 2013]. Characterisation
factors for land transformation and occupation depending on the type of land are used to quantify these effects
and calculate the total impact on biodiversity. If land is no longer occupied, nature will gradually begin to
recover. However, due to human activities, it is often unlikely that the land will return to its original state
[Koellner et al., 2013].

C.2
Modelling the Operational Energy Consumption
For the Single-familily house (BT1), the Multi-storey residential building (BT2) and the Production facility
(BT3), the specific heating source and energy consumption were not provided and therefore had to be mod-
elled. The heating source was assumed 100% electricity as it is accessible across the country, unlike district
heating, which is more geographically restricted.
A report from BUILD examined the climate impact (CO2eq/m2/year) of 163 buildings, distinguishing between
building products and operational energy use. The study found that using the new emission factors per 2025,
on average, operational energy accounted for 9% of the total climate impact [Tozan et al., 2023; Artelia,
2023]. Thus, it was assumed that the climate impact from building products in LCAbyg accounted for 91%
of the total impact. This assumption allowed for the calculation of the operational energy contribution and,
consequently, the energy consumption for Case 1 (C1) in BT1-3. To do this, the average emission factor for
electricity and heat was calculated, which was provided in advance as kg CO2eq./kWh.

Figure 13: Electricity production in kWh distributed by energy technologies between 2025-2050 for AF24.
The total electricity production is expected to increase by 453% from 2025 to 2050, with a gradual shift

The figure shows that the total biodiversity impact from the electricity production is expected to increase

A comparison between the electricity production and the corresponding biodiversity impact shows no direct

not increase at the same rate. The technology that produces the most electricity is not necessarily the largest
contributor to the total biodiversity impact. Therefore, biodiversity impact from 2025 to 2050 evolves from
being primarily driven by biomass in 2025 to by photovoltaics in 2050. Although offshore wind produces
more electricity than photovoltaics, its lower biodiversity impact factor means it contributes less to total bio-
diversity impact. Biomass, with its high biodiversity impact factor, contributes relatively more to the total
biodiversity impact, even though it only makes up 0.9% of total production in 2050.

It is expected that between 2025-2050, the energy production for district heating will increase by 12%. In

Between 2025 and 2050, the biodiversity impact decreases by 55%. In 2025, the biodiversity impact comes

Although the total district heating production increases, the biodiversity impact decreases. This might be due

Comparing Insulation Products
Figure 19 below illustrates the impact per kg for insulation products in Biotool v1 and Biotool v2 used to
model three insulation types in the the study. In Biotool v1, mineral wool (modelled as glass wool) has the
highest impact factor, followed by wood wool and cellulose fibre. However, in Biotool v2, wood fibre insulation
has the highest impact factor, followed by mineral wool (modelled as stone wool) and cellulose fibre.
Although switching from mineral wool to biogenic insulation (wood fibre/wood wool or cellulose fibre) between
Case 3 (C3) and Case 4 (C4) in Biotool v1 may appear to reduce impact, this trend is not observed in the
Production facility (BT3), the Row-house complex (BT5) and the Daycare institution (BT6). This is likely
due to additional product changes required to maintain insulation performance, which can cause an increase
of the total impact of C4. For example, in BT3, replacing mineral wool with wood wool and cellulose fibre
also introduces more bitumen sheets (asphalt) on the roof and additional wood in both the roof and outer
walls, leading to a higher total impact. Similarly as described earlier, these changes contribute to a decrease
in climate change impact, making C3 the lowest-impact case and C4 the second lowest in this BT3 and the
highest increase between cases for Biotool v2. Data used to generate Figure 19 is found in the attached Excel
document ”Product library” in sheet ”Appendix D.3.7”.

D.4
Evaluating the Robustness of the Product Library
The original products in Biotool v2 and their alternatively modelled products are seen in the attached Excel
document ”BT results” in sheet ”Appendix D.4”. Furthermore, the calculations using the case study for both
the original and alternative products are included in the sheet. Note that only the production processes of
the products were remodelled using other plausible Ecoinvent processes, while the EoL processes are assumed
to be identical with the original product.
The products included in this analysis are ”ready mixed concrete (C20/25 SCC, exposure classes X0, XC1)”,
”construction wood (pine and spruce, wet and sawn, incineration EoL)” Mineral wool (facade insulation),
Mineral wool (partition wall insulation), Mineral wool (pitched roof insulation), and Mineral wool (floor in-
sulation).
Concrete: The Ecoinvent process ”Market for concrete, 25MPa” is chosen as an alternative production pro-
cess to the original concrete product. The alternative process meets the same strength requirements as the
LCAbyg product (C20/25). Both Ecoinvent processes represent a product intended for indoor use in dry or
soil-covered structures, where frost resistance is not required, corresponding to exposure classes X0 and XC1.
Wood: The Ecoinvent process ”Market for sawnwood, beam, softwood, raw, dried (u=10%)” is chosen as
an alternative to the original process in the wood product. The original process describes natural air-drying
of sawn wood beams to approximately 20% moisture content, while the new alternative process refers to
kiln-drying of sawn wood beams to approximately 10% moisture content. The desired product has a moisture
content of
16%, but the drying process is not stated in the used EPD, resulting in whether is it natural
air-drying or kiln-drying is not specified.
Insulation: In Biotool v2, mineral wool is modelled as stone wool, as this is considered the most realistic
choice for Denmark, given that the company Rockwool is a major player in the Danish construction market.
However, in the LCAbyg descriptions of four mineral wool, they are as a mix of glass wool and stone wool
(50/50). Therefore, the alternative materials will also be modelled as 50/50.
