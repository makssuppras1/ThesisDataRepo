Abstract
The VanishProp marine propeller designed by Morten Wedel, from Esmar Engineer-
ing, received the patent in December 2018. A scaled model was subject to an open
water test in a towing tank at FORCE Technology in August 2018. CFD simulations
prior to this thesis obtained a minimum deviation of 20% in torque and 8% in thrust.
The main goal of this project is to understand why such high deviations occur. An-
other goal is to develop a computational model that can be validated with the open
water test to predict the performance of the full scale propeller.
STAR-CCM+ was used to solve the Fluid Dynamics of the flow around the pro-
peller. Based on literature review, MRF and sliding mesh motion definitions were
compared, the contribution of the γ-Reθ transition model was studied and roughness
was implemented.
Taking advantage of the solid stress solver from STAR, Fluid-
Structure Interaction was activated as “two-way” interaction to compute the blade
displacement due to the pressure difference between the faces. Regarding the full
scale propeller, the Schnerr-Sauer model and IAPWS-IF97 water model were selected
to simulate cavitation.
Analysis of the results demonstrate the need of transition as well as the impor-
tance of the FSI. The deviation in torque was reduced from 20 to a maximum of
4% overestimate while thrust is underestimated by a maximum of 6%, yet there is
still room for improvement. On this basis, stainless steel, austenitic 316 or marten-
sitic 410, are recommended for the full scale propeller. These materials ensure no
mechanical failure and the displacement only drops the eﬀiciency by 0.6% to rigid
propeller. On top of that, a rotation rate lower than 650 rpm around the optimal
advance ratio would avoid cavitation for the propeller. Further research is needed to
improve structural mechanics calculations making use of a 3D shell type cell and the
cavitation model needs data for validation.

This master thesis was prepared at the department of Mechanical Engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements for acquiring a
Master of Science in Mechanical Engineering (Engineering Design and Applied Me-
chanics).
The master project has been carried out during the spring semester of 2019, from

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The performance of a marine propeller is determined by carrying out a test (OWT)
in a water towing tank. The results of such test are vital for a propeller designer.
However, an Open Water Test is rather expensive and complicate to run due to the
geometry of the propeller. Such facts open a possibility for the Computational Fluid
Dynamics, CFD, to come into play and provide a service to complement or substitute
the experimental test.
In December 2018, Morten Wedel obtained the patent for his new propeller design
namely “VanishProp”. In August of the same year an OWT was performed by FORCE
Technology located in Lyngby, Denmark. The tested propeller was a scaled prototype
and it was equipped with the FORCE Technology Model Thruster Dynamometer
Unit designed for propulsion. Such unit measures the propeller thrust and torque
simultaneously. On Figure 1.1 a picture of the test running is shown.
Figure 1.1: Scaled VanishProp during OWT, August 2018.
From this moment, Morten Wedel sought a CFD simulation that predicts the ex-
periment. His colleague Willy Tobias Maschen from OSK-ShipTech aided Morten by
replicating the OWT into CFD. Nevertheless, the obtained CFD results had about
20% discrepancy from the experiment and it becomes the reason and motivation for
this Master Thesis. Can the CFD provide similar results to the experiments? Can
it help understand how the water flows around and off the blades? Could this infor-
mation be used to optimize the propeller design anyhow? Many different questions

3.3
Transition
Fluid mechanics had from the beginning a experimental vocation. Experiments were
the base to formulate correlations that could be used in theoretical, and afterwards
computational, calculus.
One can describe two highly differentiated flow regimes:
laminar and turbulent. For a given kind of flow, there is a critical Reynolds Number
that one can use as a guide to predict whether the flow is turbulent or not. In many
cases a rather high or low Reynolds Number can easily define the flow regime, however
there exists a certain region in which the flow does not act as fully laminar or fully
turbulent. Indeed, the flow is transitioning from laminar to turbulent and therefore,
this phenomena is widely known as “transition”. On Figure 3.6 the diagram of the
boundary layer on a flat plate shows the different regions.
Figure 3.6: Laminar, transition and turbulent regions on a flat plate [6].
Transition occurs for three various reasons [7]:
• Natural transition, weak disturbances in a laminar boundary layer become un-
stable and waves start to grow.
• Bypass transition, large disturbances outside the boundary layer originates tran-
sition.
• Separation-induced transition, due to separation of the laminar boundary layer.
This phenomena can be found due to roughness or cross-flow and it also appears
on the blades of the scaled propeller. For this reason, transition models are necessary
to avoid theoretically derived models for fully turbulent flows. Even though low-Re
or K-ε models include a sort of damping functions that can describe transition in
some cases, these are mathematical half-way between stable solutions and desired
feature. Star-CCM+ offers a turbulence suppression model and transition models
coupled with the SST K-ω turbulence model.

3.4
Fluid Structure Interaction
Fluid-structure interaction, FSI, is the thermo-mechanical interaction of a fluid and a
solid structure [3]. FSI occurs in many engineering fields such mechanical or aerospace.
A marine propeller is subject to forces that can bend the blades and it is worth study-
ing. The recent advances of software such STAR CCM+, allow engineers and scientist
to simulate complex problems hardly solvable 15 years ago.
The FSI Coupling can be defined as “one-way”, meaning that the fluid actuates on
the structure but the response of the structure to the fluid loading does not affect the
==PAGE:40==
3.4 Fluid Structure Interaction
19
fluid motion. On the other hand, the “two-way” coupling accounts for both effects.
The solid structure responds to the pressure and viscous shear traction while the
fluid motion is affected by the shape of the structure. In this project the “two-way”
coupling was selected to study the performance change of the bent blades. Neverthe-
less, the “two-way” coupling can lead into divergence problems, and hence, the FSI
Under-Relaxation Displacement factor has been reduced for convergence reasons.
Figure 3.8: Displacement of the blade under load. Deformed blade in gray.
FSI implies transient simulations, yet the static solution is important for the case.
The static deformation of the blades is due to a steady-state water flow, and thus
the simulation does not need to be time accurate. The time-step can be considered a
pseudo time-step and 0.005 s has been chosen from literature [12]. On the structural
side, the material or Rayleigh damping can be increased since the grid flux terms are
zero in a static solution. Setting the critical damping of the structure is the fastest
way to reach the steady state solution.
Owing to the fact that the blades bend, the fluid mesh needs to move and deform
accordingly.
For that purpose, the Morphing has been activated for the rotating
fluid region as to conform to the solid structure and maintain a reasonable quality
fluid grid. The cells keep the same neighbors whilst the shapes change over time. Fig-
ure 3.8 shows a blade of the VanishProp where the displacement magnitude is plotted
on the non-deformed bladed. The gray translucent blade is the deformed form where
a significant change is visible.
Previous work has been conducted around FSI in marine propellers leading into

3.4.1
Solid Stress Solver
It has been previously mentioned that STAR-CCM+ has been used to solve the solid
mechanics involved in the FSI problem. However, it should be reminded that such
software is not a broadly accepted tool to solve the physics of a solid using the FEM.
ABAQUS or ANSYS seem to be a preferential option when it comes to FEM. In this
section a review of the physics that STAR-CCM+ solves in regards to solid mechanics
will be done.
Solid Mechanics describes the behavior of a solid continuum in response to applied
loads. Applied loads induce a stress field in the structure and can cause displacement
of the structure from an initial undeformed configuration to a deformed configuration.
STAR-CCM+ allows modeling the response of a solid continuum to applied loads, in-
cluding body forces, surface loads and point forces. The Solid Stress model computes
the displacement of the solid structure using the finite element method and then cal-
culates the corresponding stresses and strains. In order to compute this model the
solid or structural mesh must fulfill some geometrical requirements. A tetrahedral
quadratic cell type has been selected in this project. The model uses a sparse direct
solver, MUMPS, to calculate the induce forces. Memory capacity is and has been a
big issue to solve the matrixes for solid stresses using STAR-CCM+.
The Finite Element Method, FEM, is a tool for finding approximate solutions to
the continuous problems by approximating continuous partial differential equations
with discrete algebraic equations. The displacement of a solid is calculated on the
principle of virtual work using FEM by STAR-CCM+. This approach follows the
lagrangian displacement formulation by Zienkiewicz and Taylor [15].
The main purpose of studying the displacement and stresses on the propeller is
to discard mechanical failure. Therefore, an exhaustive study of strain and stresses
will not be done. Instead, two main magnitudes will be mentioned: displacement and
the Von Mises stress. The fluid mesh morphs following the deformed body. Such
displacement of the fluid mesh will a matter of study for its implications on the
performance due to pitch changes. The Von Mises stress represents the deviatoric
stress, is independent of the mean stress and is an important measure of the ductile
failure. It is calculated as in equation 3.3 being σi a principle stress.

3.5
Cavitation
Cavitation is a physical phenomenon that occurs due to the rapid formation and
collapse of vapor bubbles in a liquid flow in a low pressure region. Vapor bubbles
implode and can generate a shock wave and it is a matter of concern in a turbo-
machinery such a propeller.
Such implosions near the metal surface cause cyclic
stress resulting in fatigue or erosion that can be observed on Figure 3.11.
Hydrodynamic cavitation will only occur if the local pressure declines to some
extent below the saturated vapor pressure. It typically has its origin as the result of
an increase in the kinetic energy, which translated to marine propellers, it means that
the higher the angular velocity of the propeller is, the higher chances to encounter
cavitation are. Aside the previously mentioned non-desirable effects, there are many
others such noise, vibration and performance drop. For all of this reasons, cavitation
is not desired on a propeller. Hence, the goal of the project is to study cavitation
on the VanishProp to be able to provide an angular velocity recommendation such
cavitation is not present.

The saturation pressure of water, pv, is the pressure at which it changes phase and

the Caribbean Sea, T = 27 ± 3◦C, implies higher pv and more chances to encounter
cavitation. In any case, the temperature of the experiment will be taken for the full

In regards to CFD, cavitation requires the activation of a series of models: eu-
lerian multi-phase, to define the liquid and vapor phases; multiphase interaction, to
specify the type of interaction between liquid water and its vapor; and volume of
fluid, numerical method to simulate immiscible flow that assumes that the properties
of the interface are derived from the volume fractions.
The multiphase interaction defines cavitation with the Schauner-Sauer model,
which is a simplification of the Full Rayleigh-Plesset model. The model considers
spherical bubbles of a certain diameter called Seed Diameter, which needs to be cho-
sen. Due to lack of literature for the specific case, the default value of 10−6m has
remained unchanged.
After some work with the cavitation model, some sort of error was introduce by

This water model from the International Association for the Properties of Water and
Steam includes calculations of density and other thermodynamics properties. Based
on the Gibbs free energy, it is able to model the change of phase from liquid to steam
which cavitation requires.

4.1
Mesh Convergence Study
The nature of the propeller open water test flow requires a certain mesh with two
highly differentiated regions: the static region and the rotating region. Indeed, the
rotating region is the most important part of the mesh since that is where the pro-
peller is actuating and turning that movement into thrust and torque. More than
90% of both thrust and torque have its origin at the blades. Hence, the mesh needs
to be properly resolved at the surface of the blades and around them.
One could say that there are two factors to converge into a trustworthy mesh to re-
solve the flow correctly. The first one is the surface mesh of the blades and the second
is the base size of the mesh. Thus, a mesh convergence study has been performed by
decreasing the minimum surface size of the blade and another one by decreasing the
base size of the whole mesh.
Regarding the meshing settings, three meshers have been chosen: Trimmed Cell
Mesher, that allows running in parallel; Prism Layer Mesher, vital to properly resolve
the different boundary layers; and Surface Remesher, to control the surface mesh
specially on the blades. The peculiar geometry of the blades requires a considerable
amount of cells to characterize the domain truthfully. Indeed, the blades are very
thin and the leading edge needs to be perfectly resolved. A small variation on the
pressure profile at the leading edge can lead up into a significant difference in the re-
sultant forces. Hence, the first step of the mesh building needs to focus on the surface
meshing of the blades. On Figure 4.1 one can see how does the mesh on the blade
look like. Larger cells are placed at the middle of the blade where large gradients are

4.3
Open Water Test: Sliding mesh
The sliding mesh aims to get rid of any non physical results from the MRF, since
now the fluid mesh is actually rotating, whilst the reference frame stays still. A first
consequence of the sliding mesh is that the physical model is not steady anymore and
a time step needs to be chosen. A time step corresponding to 2 degrees has been
chosen as it is the recommendation from ITTC for CFD simulations. On top of that,
the thrust and torque will fluctuate and the mean value over a full rotation needs to
be considered.
The goal of this section is to elucidate whether the sliding mesh results show more
fidelity to the experiments than MRF. The sliding mesh provides a more physical
model than MRF, but it is also computationally expensive. While a steady simulation
needs around 500 iterations to reach a stable solution, the unsteady sliding mesh
requires more than 3,000 iterations.
Once again, computational results have been achieved for J = 0.7 for both with
and without transition.
Figure 4.19 shows the thrust monitor of the simulation without transition model.
After 2,000 iterations seems to stabilize, yet the mean value is not stable. A close
up shows the mean value only changed 0.2 N for the last 2,000 iterations which
corresponds to 0.14%, and hence the value can be considered stable.
Another red point when it comes to unsteady simulations is the Convective Courant
Number. For stability criteria, such number must be lower than 1. On Figure 4.20
the Convective Courant Number is plotted and it is below zero in most of the fluid
domain. Its value increases around the blade where the cell size is very small, spe-
cially on the LE and TE where it reaches around 50. However, the system is stable
and the results can be trusted.

4.5
Post OWT Propeller Scanning
After discarding roughness the project seem to sink in a dead end. Is the CFD unable
to predict torque properly? What about transition? Another line of investigation
needed to be opened since the efforts done so far did not help understanding why
the CFD was not able to replicate the experiment. At some point it was considered
the fact that the experiment might had introduced a large uncertainty, but then, the
experiment will not be useful for CFD comparison. Any of these suppositions were
unlikely, the experiment was very well documented and the source of error seems to
be in the CFD.
Then the following question came up: what if the propeller geometry subject to
simulation is not exactly the same as the one from the experiment? Slight changes in
the pitch of a propeller infer drastic change in performance. The blades underwent
more that 70 N each the most critical point of the OWT (J = 0). Hence, each blade
supported more the equivalent of 7 kg which might have bent the blades even in a
permanent form.
In order to dissipate such doubt, the propeller was scanned following the procedure
presented on the method chapter. The propeller and on side of the body part have
been scanned as one can observe on Figure 4.25. The pictures were taken in the
metrology room in building 427 at the DTU Campus in Lyngby. The scanner was
operated by Klaus Liltorp, laboratory technician at the MEK department of DTU
in metrology. The propeller was posed on a prism to give it some height and the
scan took several pictures pictures every time the rotating table was turned. Picture
after picture, a geometry was formed on the computer connected to the scanner (e).
Once the scanning was considered completed, the geometry file was post-processed
to isolate the propeller or the body.
The CAD file was then saved into an STL format that turned the geometry into
triangles. The obtained geometries are not perfectly closed and the nature of the
generated CAD makes its handling hard. Meaning that there are not clear plane
surfaces to use as references to placed them with the original geometries that are
used for the CFD. On top of that, the geometries obtained from the scanning have
not been used for CFD simulations for the previously mentioned diﬀiculties.
On Figure 4.26 the obtained CAD is over-posed to the geometry used for the CFD
and it seems to very similar. No change in rake is observed and hence, it is likely
that there was no plastic deformation during the test. The accuracy of the scan and
the diﬀiculties to place the propeller exactly parallel make the results of this section
inconclusive. On one hand, it is sure that there are not large deformations, yet one
cannot confirm whether small permanent deformation occurred and that is the reason
why the CFD results are off.
After the scan the hypothesis of plastic deformation has been discarded. Never-
theless, there are high chances deformations might have happened during the OWT.
Therefore, the next step of the project will go in the direction of studying the propeller
deformation during service.

4.6.1
Solid mesh convergence
A mesh is a discretization of the space and CFD uses it to simulate fluid dynamics.
In a similar way one can mesh a solid to calculate strain and stresses. In fact, last
versions of STAR-CCM+ contain new features that allow FEM calculations. When it
comes to a marine propeller, the blades are under loads that produce stresses that can
end up in displacements. In order to be able to use those features for the VanishProp
propeller, a solid mesh have been performed and consequently, a mesh convergence
was needed to ensure results quality.
In this case, a tetrahedral meshing has been chosen to discretize the rotating solid
since it was recommended for FEM. A base size of 5 cm has been chosen to ensure
large cells in the hub while the blades will be refined. Indeed, a rectangular toroid
that encloses the root and blades has been created for a volume refinement. Such
toroid can be seen on Figure 4.27.
The target size of this refinement range from 5 to 0.9% the base size. Further
refinement was not allowed as the finite element method solver of the software could
not handle such cell amount due to lack of memory.
The minimum surface size
chosen is 0.1mm and was kept constant for every mesh. From a visual inspection,
such minimum surface size value gave a nice geometrical shape. Although smaller
surfaces sizes were tried, they increased the amount of cells substantially while not
adding value to the simulation.

is selected along with time through the unsteady model. The motion of the solid
propeller could be defined while the fluid region could not be defined with a motion
combining the mesh morphing and rotation. Instead, MRF needed to be chosen to
solve this first problem to manage to activate the morpher for the fluid region. The

converged simulations.
In second place, it needs to be mentioned that the mesh is not the optimal due
to the solid mesh cell options available in STAR-CCM+. The solid solver of STAR-
CCM+ rather falls short compared to other structural mechanics software such AN-
SYS or ABAQUS. Therefore, the mesh used might introduce some uncertainty to the
results. On the contrary, the uncertainty of the CFD is very likely to be as large
as the uncertainty of the OWT itself. The fact that the tendency of KT , KQ and
ηO curves matches such of the experiment means that the computational results are
reliable. Yet the relative difference to the experiment, always lower than 6%, might
have its origin in both the uncertainties from the experiments and solid solver STAR.
Last but not least, once the importance of adding FSI to the simulation has
been more than demonstrated, a new propeller design method can be considered.
Propellers are designed for the optimal point, yet what if a deformed geometry is the
optimal? This question might open an interesting sort of possibilities to try different
materials and propeller geometries.
Indeed, this can be done with very low time
consumption and can save large economic and time expenses in open water tests.

4.7.1
Cavitation
In this subsection, five different advance ratios are going to be considered, J= 0.1,
0.3, 0.5, 0.7 and 0.9, for another five rotation rates, namely 200, 400, 600, 800 and
1000 rpm.
The first point to discuss about is the flow around the full scale propeller. Is
it fully turbulent flow? The slowest flow is the one at J=0.1 and 200 rpm. For this
point, the propeller has been simulated with and without transition turning the exact
same results. From the computational point of view transition does not affect the
flow. Indeed, the Reynolds Number in this point is around 1 million (Re = 9.9 · 105).
Therefore, none of the simulation results shown in this chapter include transition. On
the other hand, the highest Reynolds Number corresponds to 1000 rpm and J = 0.9
which is Re = 2.75 · 106, so the range of Reynolds number is Re ∈[9.9 · 105, 2.75 · 106].
Secondly, gravity has been added to the model by defining a reference altitude
given by the propeller designer of 1.2m. Hence, the model takes into account the water
column offering a more realistic picture of the flow. Before activating the cavitation
model, a steady simulation has been run for the previously mentioned 25 cases. The
eﬀiciency of this simulation has been plotted on Figure 4.45. The eﬀiciency for every
rotation rate except 1000 rpm, is almost identical. The Reynolds Number dependency
is very little until the rotation rate reaches 1000 rpm with gives the highest Re. In
fact, this drastic change in eﬀiciency happens due to a change in the flow on the
pressure side of the blade. There is a large separation region next to the leading edge
consequence of the high velocities to which the propeller is subject at such rotation
rate as can be seen on Figure A.2. The flow does not attach to the pressure side
from the beginning and as a result, the thrust decreases and so does the eﬀiciency.
Nonetheless, one can not trust this results since on Figures A.1 and A.2 can be seen
that the pressure in some areas are not correctly solved. In fact, in these rotation
rate and advance ratios cavitation was expected. Then, it seems that the eﬀiciency
curves for 1000 and 800 rpm are not trustworthy.
Pressure did not go below the vapor pressure in any of the steady simulations
which means that cavitation could not be predicted with this model. However, it is
known from the experiment that cavitation was present in the range of the simulations
run.
Therefore, it is time to activate the cavitation model.

cavitation model has been chosen since it is simpler that the Full Rayleigh-Plesset

defined for the Scaling Fac+ node, which scales the positive bubble growth rate. The
time-step chosen corresponds to 0.5◦, selected to avoid any stability problem. The
obtained eﬀiciencies have been plotted on Figure 4.46 where it can be seen that
the rotation rates 200, 400 and 600 rpm gave the same eﬀiciencies as the steady

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this chapter a series of conclusions drawn after the obtainment of results are pre-
sented.
The conclusions can be divided into two categories: regarding the scaled
propeller and regarding the full scale propeller.
The first conclusion that one can draw on behalf of the OWT on the scaled
propeller is that transition was very important, and it is so for scaled models of this
magnitude.
Regarding the definition of the propeller motion on CFD, MRF gives the same
results as the sliding mesh setting while requiring less computational time.
Thirdly, roughness does not make an impact in the flow. It does not add enough
turbulence to break the transition zone into turbulent flow.
The scanning is inconclusive and hence, one cannot assure whether permanent
deformations happened during the test. The accuracy of the scanned CAD file is not
enough to draw any conclusion in this behalf.
The CFD simulations are validated when using FSI. The relative difference to the
experimental results is lower than 6% and it can be concluded that the blades bent
during the test. In fact, the displacement of the blades decreases both the thrust and
torque that the propeller turns affecting the eﬀiciency.
The stress map of the blades achieved with the “two-way” fluid-structure interac-
tion shows that the blade will not fail mechanically. The stresses are concentrated
on the blade surfaces and fatigue concerns might be a problem.
The final conclusion that one can highlight out of the CFD work performed around
the scaled propeller is that the CFD is able to replicate the experiment. Therefore,
the main goal of the project is fulfilled.
Owing to the fact that CFD has been validated, the full scale propeller can be simu-
lated for performance prediction. From the results it can be extracted that cavitation
is a problem in the VanishProp propeller. The cavitation model and IAPWS-IF97
real water model allows studying this physical phenomenon. The results suggest not
running the propeller above 650 rpm. Indeed, such rotation rate should only be used
for its optimal point around J = 0.7.
When talking about material candidates for the blades, the highest young modulus
is sought because it causes less displacement and therefore, less decrease in eﬀiciency.
From the FSI simulation at the critical point of operation, where the highest thrust

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
In this project various fields have been studied to the extent the limited time allowed.
Therefore, one can propose some guidelines to continue digging and deepening in
some issues. Some of this ideas or wishes could become a bachelor or master thesis
eventually.
In this project the CFD simulations have been carried out taking into account
the whole propeller. The hub angle was varied to find out whether any effect change
was obtained, none was observed though. Hence, one way to go could be to build
an axisymmetric fluid domain capturing one blade out of the five. The total amount
of cells will be reduced to a fifth and it could be refined to seek more accuracy in
results. One could analyze the differences to using the complete propeller to decide
if axysimmetric is the a correct option. If that is the case, enormous computational
time can be saved for unsteady simulations including FSI or cavitation.
Regarding FSI simulations, it has been mentioned that STAR-CCM+ does not
offer the most desirable structural mesh tools. Indeed, a 3D shell type cell is the most
recommended for FEM meshes. Thus, another future work could go into translating
the mapped interface from the STAR FSI simulations to a FEM commercial soft-
ware such ABAQUS or ANSYS. This process could be done iteratively until reaching
convergence.
Moreover, the FSI module in STAR-CCM+ allows a further study in flexible
propellers. A complete study of different material could be performed to chose the
most convenient one. Then, the final pitch distribution can be found for the displaced
blade. Using this information the initial blade design could be changed to an off-design
that would account for future deformations. This procedure would allow predicting
whether an initial non-optimal design can lead into better eﬀiciencies due to eventual
deformation due to the fluid-structure interaction.
Finally, cavitation is a very interesting topic to continue studying. Some validation
work needs to be done to achieve more experience and better grasp of the real water
IAPWS-IF97 model and cavitation models available in STAR. This sort of study
can be done in more that one propeller to do some research in the influence of the
propeller and blade geometry in cavitation.
