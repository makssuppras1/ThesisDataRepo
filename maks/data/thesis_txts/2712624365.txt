Abstract
Temperatures in Southern Europe are rising due to the effects of climate change,
with a consequent increase in the heat waves frequency. Therefore, new ap-
proaches to deal with overheating are required. Solar shading is a sustainable
solution to reduce overheating but presents a trade off between thermal and
visual comfort. Although lack of daylight is not a problem in Southern Europe,
overheating is an issue for most of the year. As a result, occupants may be forced
to choose between thermal and visual comfort, with negative repercussions in
both cases. The master thesis presents a study focused on the potential of solar
shading solutions to reduce overheating and cooling consumption without com-
promising daylight conditions in a typical Southern European dwelling. The
study used IDA ICE 5 beta version coupled thermal and daylight simulations
to assess the performance of various solar shading solutions in different modes
of use, as well as the VELUX ACTIVE shading control, in terms of thermal
comfort, visual comfort, and cooling consumption. According to the findings,
the use of all solar shading devices reduce cooling consumption and improve
thermal comfort while maintaining the required minimum of daylight. External
roller shutters require active use by the occupant to provide thermal comfort
without the need for mechanical cooling. However, they should be used with
caution because if completely closed, they can block the required amount of
daylight. Internal Venetian blinds have the lowest performance in terms of re-
ducing overheating and cooling consumption, but they do let in enough light for
most of the day. The solar control glazing allows for comfortable temperatures
to be maintained without the need for any shading or mechanical cooling on
the part of the user. The electrochromic glass even maintained mostly in the
clear state, it enables thermal comfort without the need for mechanical cooling.
When compared to manual use, the external roller shutter controlled by VELUX
ACTIVE offers up to an 8% reduction in discomfort time, which translates to
413 hours. Furthermore, it implies a reduction of the total cooling consumption
from 20% to 41% compared with the case without shading.

Chapter 1
Introduction and
Background
Recently, to fulfill increasingly stringent energy efficiency regulations, new and
refurbished houses frequently have heavy, highly insulated and airtight facades.
Large triple-glazed windows are frequently chosen and are highly popular among
homeowners in order to provide good visual comfort and gain free heating in
the winter. However, inconveniences of this design during warm seasons are not
considered, especially in Southern European countries which normally experi-
ence very hot summers. As a result, overheating has become one of the leading
causes of occupant discomfort and it is an emerging issue.[1]
Furthermore, the amount of time occupants spend in their residences has in-
creased as a result of the post-covid 19 crisis, emphasizing the importance of
maintaining a good indoor climate in dwellings. Because many homes have be-
come workplaces, it is necessary to ensure the occupants’ well-being in terms of
thermal comfort, visual comfort, and air quality. [2]
As global temperatures rise and heatwaves become longer and more common,
solutions to reduce overheating in private houses in the Mediterranean zone are
required. Air conditioning provides temporary relief from the summer heat, but
it is not a long-term solution due to its high energy consumption and impact on
local temperatures.
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction and Background
The use of solar shading is a sustainable solution to deal with this problem and
they are already present in most of the homes in Southern Europe. The issue is
that it usually reduces daylight, which is undesirable since a minimum of natural
light is essential for human well-being.
However, appropriate solar shading
device and its correct use can reduce indoor temperatures while maintaining
good daylight conditions.
Moreover, solar shading can offer further advantages.
The common features
of all solar shading types are the solar protection blocking solar radiation, the
improving of energy savings when used as insulation, as well as form a barrier for
the occupant privacy. Other benefits depending on the material and location of
the shading would include acoustic protection against environmental noise from
outside, permeability to air and water, resistance to wind load and finally it is
an extra layer of security against burglars.
1.1
Traditional solar shading devices
In recent years, different types of shading devices have been proposed, dependent
of building orientation, location, window characteristics, etc. Generally, solar
shading devices can be divided according to their position in external or internal,
and according to their mobility in movable or no movable.
The external solar shadings are placed in the outside of the windows, and they
provide control of indoor temperature, light and ventilation, security and ex-
ternal noise attenuation. In the case of the movable ones, the occupant can
adjust them manually. Some of the most remarkable movable external shading
are roller shutters and awnings. In the group of external no movable a louvre is
a window blind or shutter with horizontal slats that are angled to admit light
and air, but to keep out rain and direct sunshine. However, they usually block
most of the view. Figure 1.1 presents examples of external solar shading.

1.2
Manual control of solar shading
It is important in the simulation to represent the manual use of the occupants
with their solar shading devices if they are not using automatic control. There-
fore, research has been done on this topic, in addition to the habits of the
inhabitants of Southern Europe when using solar shading that will be explained
in section 1.3.
The manual operation of blinds is preferred by occupants because their status
influences both thermal and visual comfort and other comfort-related factors
[3]. However, motorized blinds have limitations in their effectiveness because
occupants tend to move the blinds only when the direct solar radiation makes
conditions uncomfortable [4].
In office buildings O’Brien [5] and Foster [6] found by monitoring that occu-
pants are totally or partially inactive in the interaction with window shading
systems, and when active, interactions occurred on a weekly or monthly basis.
As for dwellings, Verbruggen [7] demonstrates in Ghent by monitoring dwellings
that the solar shading are not often adjusted and are thus in the same position
for a long time, independent of the temperature or solar irradiance . Finally,
a study of a residential apartment in Naples (Italy) assumes the almost null
interaction of the occupants with their solar shades [8]. In the simulation they
consider this lack of use, increasing the closing threshold of shading system from
the typical 150W/m2 to 800 W/m2.
In most dynamic simulations on the use of shading, solar radiation control with
temperature control is applied [8][9][10]. It is assumed that the occupant closes
the blind when the radiation on the window is greater than a threshold between
150 W/m2 and 300 W/m22 because of glare or/and the outside temperature
is above a certain threshold depending on the location. These values of solar
radiation have been obtained from studies carried out in offices where the risk
of glare is higher due to the increased presence of computer screens[11].
1.3
Current situation of solar shading in South-
ern Europe
A study was carried out by means of literature review to find out the routines
and reasons of the residents of southern Europe for using solar shading. The

dwelling studied was located in Valencia (Spain), so the information gathered
in this study has been more focused on Spanish habits and housing.
Solar shading is used in 95% of Spanish households, in all of their windows or
at least in the bedrooms [12]. The sociologist Juan Carlos Barajas published in
the Spanish newspaper "El Pais" that if the use of shadings in most of Europe
is something residual or anecdotal, in Spain it is part of the popular culture
and they are usually always down [13]. In addition to the determining factor
that the country has more hours of daylight on average per year -between 2,500
and 3,000- the other reason that justifies the shading being so widespread is the
great need for privacy that the occupants have. Figure 1.3 presents a building
apartment in Spain.

Most people have external roller shutters, and use them mainly for security
reasons and to reduce solar radiation entering the house. They completely block
the entry of light and insulate the window from the outside. Occupants tend
to close the shutters at night for security. For the same reason, it is common
to keep these shutters closed when the house is empty, though some people
leave some shutter open to hide the fact that the house is unoccupied. In the
hot season it is usual that the shutters are lowered at night while leaving the
window slightly open to let in fresh air.
The most popular type of internal solar shading is Venetian blinds. Their main
function is privacy and glare control. Their use is sporadic, only in situations
where privacy is desired and in those of direct glare.

1.4
Literature Review
Several studies have been conducted to investigate how to reduce building over-
heating through solar shading while maintaining a proper visual comfort. The
keywords that were used in the search were "Southern Europe" or "Mediter-
ranean", "overheating", "daylight" and "solar shading". This section presents
studies that have evaluated the use of traditional solar shading, fixed solar shad-
ing, smart glazing (thermochromic and electrochromic) and automatic shading
control.
Among the studies of traditional solar shading , Evola [10] carried out in South-
ern Italy the assessment of the effectiveness of various solar shading devices in
an existing office without overhangs or fixed shading devices. The elements ana-
lyzed were external rolling shutters, white internal Venetian blinds and external
solar control film. In the simulations the shading devices were closed when the
solar radiation on the window surface exceeded 200 W/m2 and the zone air tem-
perature was equal to or higher than 26 °C. The results concluded that the use
of solar shading considerably reduced the cooling load and overheating, as well
as maintaining an appropriate level of daylight to perform tasks and limiting
glare. However, internal blinds turned out to have lower efficiency than external
blinds and solar film had good performance in all directions.
Max Tillberg, Chief Executive Officer at EQUA Solutions AB, wrote an ar-
ticle stating that highly reflective internal solar shading in combination with
solar control glazing with low reflection have better results in terms of thermal
comfort and visual comfort than the use of external shading [14]. This is sup-
ported by simulations with two IDA ICE models, one with external shades and
the other with internal shades and solar control glazing.
In very hot climates, fixed solar shading is quite common. This type of de-
vice is usually external and the occupant cannot change the position. Freewan
[15] studied in an office building in Jordan the use of fixed solar shading. Specif-
ically, it was analyzed the performance of three fixed shading devices (vertical
fins, diagonal fins and egg crate). All shading devices reduced the air tempera-
ture but each performing better at different times of the day.
The effects of solar shading strategies on thermal comfort in low-income tropi-
cal housing in Uganda are examined by Hashemi [16]. Using dynamic thermal
simulations, the effects of various shading strategies, such as curtains, roof and
window overhangs, and trees, on solar heat gain and thermal comfort are in-
vestigated. According to the findings, while shading reduces solar heat gain, it
is less effective in meeting thermal comfort requirements in low-income tropi-
cal houses. Solar shading, on the other hand, is extremely effective during the
==PAGE:17==

hottest months of the year, reducing the risk of extreme overheating by up to
52%..
Other research has investigated the use of automatically controlled solar shad-
ing. A case study was conducted at an office in Malaga (Spain), in which the
originally manually-controlled interior vertical blinds were replaced with auto-
matically controlled interior roller blinds [17].
In the simulation the control
strategy is applied to the motorized roller blinds in order to maximize view con-
tact with the outside and daylighting while controlling glare and overheating.
The results showed that the occupants have uninterrupted view contact with
the outside for 60% of the occupied time. Furthermore, when compared to the
scenario in which the original blinds are always closed, the number of hours in
which the room’s daylight is insufficient decreases by 84% of the occupied hours
without increasing cooling energy demand.
Blinds are widely used in many buildings to save energy and provide comfort
to occupants. Manual or motorized blinds, on the other hand, have a limited
ability to reduce energy consumption and provide comfort because occupants
must operate the blinds themselves to block direct solar radiation. As a result,
using automated blinds would allow to get the most out of the blinds [18].
Kim[18] explores the theory that building performance and thermal comfort
can be improved by the application of an automated Venetian blind instead of a
manual or motorized Venetian blind. Through this study, the potential energy
savings and the comfort enhancement when using the automated blind was con-
firmed.
Carletti [4] analyzes the thermal and lighting performance of a full-scale test
room according to a typical Italian home, with variable control of an external
Venetian blind system, fixed positions, and automatic control based on outside
air temperature and illuminance.
The findings show that different Venetian
blind configurations have a significant impact on indoor thermal and lighting
performance. The automated venetian blind’s dynamic behavior, in particular,
is capable of producing the highest benefits.
Skarning [9] mapped and compared energy, daylighting and thermal comfort
for various combinations of window size and glazing properties, with and with-
out dynamic shading. In the dynamic shading the shading was activated when
the outdoor temperature was above 18 ºC and the set-point of 300 W/m2 for
direct solar radiation for the windows was exceeded. The more flexible solution
space with dynamic shading made it possible to reduce the time with operative
temperatures exceeding the comfort limit by 40%–50%.
Other papers focus on the performance of smart glazing ( electrochromics and

Constanzo [19] investigates, by means of dynamic simula-

Chapter 2
Aim of the project
Research on the effect of solar shading on building performance in dwellings in
the Mediterranean climate is quite limited, which has a great potential to im-
prove the indoor climate quality and reduce energy consumption. In Southern
Europe, the assessment of shadings are focus on office buildings. Also, research
on the use of shading and their performance in dwellings is mostly conducted
in countries with cold climates.
Therefore, the results for dwellings in cold climates cannot be applied to dwellings
in warm climates in Southern Europe. Apart from the influence of the large dif-
ference in outdoor temperatures between both cases, other factors such as the
construction materials of the dwellings, the glazing to floor ratio, and the shad-
ing routines use by occupants have a considerable influence on the results.
Also, the findings of studies in Southern European offices regarding thermal com-
fort and daylight are not applicable to residential buildings. People in dwellings
have other preferences in the use of shading, not only to have a good visual
quality at the workplace as in offices. Also the occupancy time in dwellings is
longer than in offices.
So, the aim of this work is to assess the potential of solar shading solutions
to reduce overheating and cooling consumption without compromising daylight
conditions in a typical Southern European dwelling through the performance

various solar shading devices in different modes of use, as well as the VELUX

thermal comfort, cooling consumption and daylighting.

Chapter 3
Methodology
This chapter includes the explanation of the decisions made regarding the solar
solutions explored and their mode of use. It will be provided the description of
the selected building and climate file, as well as a detailed explanation of the
IDA ICE models and the assessment to be made in terms of cooling consump-
tion, thermal comfort and daylight.
The simulation models are created in the simulation tool IDA Indoor Climate
and Energy (IDA ICE 5 beta version), which is a whole-year detailed and dy-
namic multi-zone simulation application for study of thermal indoor climate,
energy consumption and daylight of the entire building.
3.1
Shading solutions
Following all the collected information on the background of solar shading in
Southern Europe in section 1.3, the solar solutions selected for the purpose of
this project will be presented.
The most representative solar shadings in the Mediterranean climate are exter-
nal roller shutters and Venetian blinds. External roller shutters produce almost
total darkness inside the house, gives an extra layer of security and reduces

considerably the entrance of solar radiation.
Venetian blinds allow light to enter but reducing glare. They can be easily ad-
justed to control daylight, Also, they are easy to raise and lower when necessity
of privacy is required at some point.
Max Tillberg’s previously explained theory that internal blinds in combination
with solar control glazing worked better than external shading has also been

A solar control glazing has a special coating that reduces the amount of heat

cooling. Figure 3.1 illustrates the operation of a solar control glazing.

The performance of the state-of-the-art electrochromic glazing has also been
tested. These are currently not widely used in Southern Europe due to their
high price.
However, their great potential to reduce the overheating of the
dwelling while maintaining natural light as well as the benefit of maintaining
outside views were explored.
Electrochromic glass (smart glass or dynamic glass) is an electronically tintable
glass used for windows.
It has different tintable states that can be directly
controlled by building occupants, from untinted to fully tint. It is popular for
its ability to improve occupant comfort, maximize access to daylight and outdoor
views, reduce energy costs and provide architects with more design freedom [22].

Mode of use
In the simulations, a distinction shall be made between manual use by the
occupant and automatic control of the shadings.
The mode of use of solar
shading have a significant impact on visual and thermal comfort, building energy
consumption, and peak load.
Despite this, research in this field is limited,
fraught with uncertainty, and primarily focuses on commercial office buildings.

Manual control
The manual operation of blinds cannot be perfectly represented because it differs
from each occupant. Solar radiation, occupants’ visual and thermal preferences,

generally do not regularly adjust their solar shading actively.
However, this
project wants to test that proper use of solar shading can improve the comfort
of housing. Therefore, the effect of 3 occupant behaviour has been tested. The
factors that vary in occupants’ behaviours are the periods when the shadings
are closed, the possibility of glare and the outside temperature. Glare has been
estimated by incident solar radiation, as has been done in previous studies.
• Occupant 1 will close all the shadings at night and during periods when
no one is at home for security reasons. During occupancy daylight hours,
they will set the solar shading to half according to temperature and glare.
• Occupant 2 will follow the same behaviour as Occupant 1, but will keep
the shading open during the night.
• Occupant 3 will only close the blinds due to glare.
The representation of these profiles in the simulation will be explained in detail
in section 3.6.7
3.2.2
Automatic control
The manual control of solar shading is limited to the presence in the home,
and the occupants are unable to perform a more complex control than an au-
tomatic control system does. The use of automated shading would more fully
exploit the full benefits of shading, as it has been confirmed the comfort im-
provement and the potential energy savings [18]. Therefore, in this project it
has also been analysed the VELUX ACTIVE automatic control of shading. The
detailed explanation of this control will be given in section 3.6.8. In contrast to
an automatic control of window openings which is influenced by the occupancy
time (because if the window is opened when the house is empty, people can
enter), the automatic control of solar shading does not have this problem, so it
has more freedom of use.
The heat protection algorithm is proactive and works by closing the shutter or
blind before the room gets too hot. Based on input from an online weather
service (including temperature, forecast conditions and length of the day), the
==PAGE:27==
3.3 Models
17
algorithm calculates the risk of a room becoming overheated and then deter-
mines during which hours shutters and blinds should be rolled down to prevent
overheating.

Room units
Since one of the objectives of the project is to find out how much cooling is

It was found that the values of sDA300/50% of the remaining solar shadings
were consistent, so they have been considered for the study.

thermal comfort. According to the ATC model, the comfortable operative tem-

The upper limit for thermal comfort in this model is a variable temperature that

5.1
Analysis of the results
In relation to the performance of solar shading, roller shutter is dependent on
the mode of use. So, when used more actively by Occupants 1 and 2 closing the
shading during non-occupancy hours, the incident solar radiation is reduced and
it ensures Category 2 in all rooms without the necessity of mechanical cooling.
However, when used occasionally in the case of glare, the use of mechanical
cooling is necessary to maintain adequate thermal comfort.
Electrochromic glazing and solar control glazing present the best thermal com-
fort results without sacrifying daylight. Independently of the room and control,
both solutions do not exceed the minimum of hours above Category II while
meeting the required sDA300 according to EN17037. The implementation of
solar control glazing ensures the comfort without the need to use the blind
constantly as the results with the different controls are similar. The fact that
Occupant 3, who changes the state of the electrochromic glass from clear state
to intermediate 1 state sporadically due to glare, benefits from belonging to
Category II means that only by maintaining the clear state for a large part of
the time, adequate temperatures are maintained.
Internal Venetian blind has the lowest performance in terms of reducing over-

5.2
Manual behaviour
The motivations of occupant’s interaction with shades are not only attributable
to environmental parameters and optimization of energy use. In this project,
both in manual and automatic use, only the reasons of non-conformity (glare),
energy optimisation (temperature) and security (night-time enclosure and non-

occupancy) have been taken into account.
However, there are reasons that
cannot be taken into account in the simulation such as, occupants’ visual and
thermal preferences, personal values, economics and even emotional condition.
A deterministic representation of the use of shading leads to an inaccurate sim-
ulation result (overestimation or underestimation) of the impact of manually
controlled shading devices on building performance due to the stochastic char-
acter of user behavior [25].
The closing area of the roller shutters plays an important role.
During the
daytime hours of occupation, for a fair comparison it has been assumed that all
solar shades are closed by 50%. This value is not very important in the case
of internal Venetian blinds, because even if they are 100% closed, they can still
allow more than 300 lx to pass through. However, for the roller shutter it is
more influential. When kept closed at 50% it does not let in more than 300 lx
all hours of the day, as shown in the results, and if it is kept completely closed
it reduces significantly daylight. So, if this value had been higher or lower it
would have had a considerable impact on the results.

There are other climates in Southern Europe, and also there are differences
between the variants of the Mediterranean climate such as temperatures, solar
radiation and wind speed. The difference between outside temperatures and
solar radiation may affect the shading controls since both manual and automatic
control in this project depend on them. It would lead to a discrepancy in results.
The selected orientation of the dwelling also plays an important role in the re-
sults. For example, the largest window areas in the bedrooms are in a Southern
orientation, which influences the increase in temperatures due to solar radi-
ation. If these windows had been located in the north, indoor temperatures
would probably have been considerably lower in these bedrooms. However, this
orientation was chosen to cause the most unfavourable situation in relation to
overheating in Bedrooms 2 and 3, which also have the longest occupancy period.
5.4
Daylight Analysis
When studying the performance of the external roller shutter in terms of day-
light, the parameter DA300 had to be used instead of sDA300 like the other
solar shading. Therefore, only the amount of daylight from a central point of
the rooms has been calculated. This can lead to an over- or underestimation
depending on the position of the window. If the window just gives direct radi-
ation to the point in the room, the DA value will be high but it is not certain
that the rest of the room is properly illuminated. The same with the opposite
case, that the room actually has a value of 300 lux 50% of the time in 50% of the
space but if just the central point at which the DA is measured is not included
in this area, it would produce a lower DA value.
5.5
Implementation of project results
The application of solar shading together with natural ventilation has been
found to reduce temperatures and electricity consumption on all casess, even
without requiring the use of mechanical cooling to maintain comfort on some
occasions.
This project has taken into account the routines and reasons why people in
Southern Europe use their solar shading. Thus, two traditional solar shading
have been analysed there such as the external roller shutter and the internal
Venetian blind and 3 solar solutions have been explored (solar control glazing,

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to evaluate the potential of solar shading solutions
to reduce overheating without compromising daylight in Southern European
countries. This chapter will answer the objectives of the thesis.
1. The effectiveness of each solar solution has been proven. The external
roller shutter has been shown to be effective in reducing temperatures,
but only if the occupant actively adjusts it during the hours of maximum
solar radiation and temperatures. This solar solution should be used with
care as it can eliminate all daylight if fully closed, but if kept in inter-
mediate positions it contributes to improve thermal comfort and energy
savings while maintaining an appropriate amount of daylight. Internal
Venetian blind has the lowest performance in terms of reducing overheat-
ing and cooling consumption, but allow an adequate amount of light to
pass through most of the time of the day. Their function should be limited
to glare reduction and in case of seeking privacy from the outside. The so-
lar protection glazing allows comfortable temperatures to be maintained
without the need for the user to interact with any devices.
Combined
with an internal Venetian blind, it allows for a slight reduction in indoor
temperatures and provides visible protection from the outside world. The
electrochromic glass allows a view of the outside while reducing the ther-
mal component of solar radiation entering the house. The use of the 4

2. It has been shown that closing the blind at night does not significantly

Chapter 7
Future work
The investigation performed in this master’s thesis is based on only one dwelling
with three bedrooms, two bathrooms, an office, a kitchen and a living room.
This is not representative for the entire Southern Europe housing, even if it is
what characterises Spanish housing the most. Further studies of other dwelling
types such as smaller or larger apartments and houses with different orientations
could show better or worse performance of solar shading solutions.
As learned from the literature review, there is a lack of research on the use of
solar shading in Southern European dwellings. It is necessary to expand studies
on this topic, in order to be able to perform more accurate simulations. This
study could have been carried out in an existing dwelling with sensors on the
shadings to better understand the use of them. Then, the same house would be
simulated and the results obtained when simulating the manual control would
be added.
In the manual control studied, the solar shading only had 3 positions: fully
open, fully closed and half closed. In the VELUX automatic control 2 positions:
fully open and fully closed. Improvements of the implemented macros could
be investigated in terms of controlling the closing area of the blind according
to room illuminance, indoor and outdoor temperatures and solar radiation, in
order to optimise visual comfort, thermal comfort and energy savings.
