Abstract
The operation, maintenance, and investment in future smart distribution grids require a
deeper understanding of the conditions of their technical components. As we move for­
ward, data­driven technologies and artificial intelligence have emerged as powerful tools,
particularly for distribution system operators assessing the health status of the grid, among
other applications. However, the data used to train these models often faces quality is­
sues, such as a lack of historical records, the use of unique and non­standard data and
traits, and often insufficiently informative or relevant features.
The primary objective of this thesis is to design an effective workflow, implementing both
conventional data pre­processing techniques and innovative strategies, for enriching data
for machine learning models in reliability assessment tasks. The novel aspect of our
workflow includes the use of model­based deep generative algorithms to tackle prevalent
challenges in the field, such as data scarcity and availability issues. As a validation of
our methodology, we apply this workflow to a specific medium­voltage cable failure and
outage dataset in Denmark. The enriched data is then evaluated using descriptive metrics
to ascertain the effectiveness of our approach. Through these investigations, we found
that enriching medium­voltage cable failure data with Variational Autoencoders leads to
more accurate classifications in the face of data uncertainty.
The implementation of this workflow is not only confined to the present analysis but serves
to set a foundation for future research in this field. By demonstrating the potential benefits
of such a workflow, this work sets the ground for its wider adoption in enhancing the
reliability assessment of smart distribution grids, paving the way for future advancements
in this field.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The transition towards smart energy systems has been accelerating with the progress
of Information and Communication Technologies (ICT) and digitalization. In this context,
data plays a crucial role, particularly in the distribution grid (DG) planning and decision­
making processes [1]. The ability to handle large volumes of data, manage its variety and
velocity, and provide much faster computations compared to classical analysis tools has
driven the rise of Artificial Intelligence (AI) techniques in the energy industry [2].
The application of AI in the energy industry opens up exciting possibilities. For instance,
AI may enhance the reliability of the grid through better decision­making [3], optimize the
operation and maintenance of energy facilities, and improve the accuracy of demand­side
predictions. AI is particularly useful in the maintenance of underground assets, which is
notoriously difficult due to the challenging accessibility and high costs associated with
sensor installation such as those for partial discharge, or the costs of cable tests which
are often unfeasible due to the high number of cables in the system. Thus, the need
for better preventive actions arises. Additionally, AI holds great potential for forecasting
algorithms for renewable energy integration and has demonstrated proficiency in tasks
such as fault finding and diagnosis, load management, and real­time control in energy
systems. Furthermore, predictive algorithms can estimate the remaining useful life of
assets and predict impending failures, thereby facilitating preventative maintenance. This
becomes a huge advantage compared to conventional methods [4] which often involve
time­consuming and costly processes where Distribution System Operators (DSOs) must
physically access the grid, frequently requiring excavations [5]. The economic benefits are
substantial, as adopting these AI techniques can lead to avoiding expensive, unexpected
outages and overall enhanced efficiency for DSOs.
However, the transition to AI is not without its difficulties. AI techniques often need mas­
sive amounts of data for training, and the interpretability and transparency of AI models
are necessary for the energy sector, where making the wrong decision could have se­
rious repercussions, thereby demanding a focus on the dependability and credibility of
such models [6].
In this landscape, DSOs face challenges in integrating data­driven solutions and machine
learning (ML) algorithms into DG assessment processes. Often, DSOs do not apply data
enrichment algorithms to improve their data quality and enhance the accuracy of their
algorithms. For instance, they typically do not implement a proper data cleaning pipeline
nor enrich the data with non­operational data [6]. This is detrimental for the performance of
the AI models, as poor quality, incomplete, or inaccurately processed data can significantly
hinder the learning process, leading to suboptimal or incorrect predictions [6]. Therefore,
it is essential to establish robust data management strategies, including data cleaning,
preprocessing, and enrichment, to maximize the efficiency and accuracy of the AI models
employed in DSO assessments. To better illustrate this process, a visual flowchart is
shown in Figure 1.1, where the AI integration process in the context of energy distribution
systems is presented.
Furthermore, DSOs typically do not adopt advanced machine learning models, often due
to a lack of expertise, computational resources, or concerns about model interpretabil­

ity. This is a significant missed opportunity, as these models have the potential to unlock
novel insights from large datasets and improve the efficiency of distribution systems. For

Despite these challenges and the rapid development of AI techniques and their application

challenges.
1.2
Thesis objectives and scope
Data enrichment strategies using AI tools within the distributed generation paradigm can
have a broad spectrum of applications, including topology inference, predictive mainte­
nance, forecasting, energy management systems, and more [7]. In our endeavor, we
put the focus on medium­voltage cable failure data due to its relevance in improving grid
reliability, reducing costs, and asset life extension [5]. Therefore, for our study case we
primarily utilize a Danish dataset of component failures and outages, known as ”El selsk­
abernes fejl og afbrudsstatistik” (ELFAS). Additionally, our quest for suitable techniques
for enriching this dataset is not restricted only to ELFAS. We also consider other publicly
available datasets that may contribute to, or hold the potential to enhance our enrichment
tasks.
Another aspect to be clarified scope­wise is that this thesis embodies an exploratory pro­

The thesis objectives, in alignment with the scope mentioned above, can be listed as

• Determine the data prerequisites for training ML models aimed at grid health as­

It is important to highlight the limited availability of publicly available data on DGs, as
several required data, such as root data, electrical parameters, date of components in­

aim to make use as much as possible of non­operational publicly available data.
Furthermore, we intend to delve into the implications and potential utility of our data en­
richment toolbox for other facets of grid management beyond reliability assessment. The
effective use of enriched data could significantly contribute to areas such as demand re­
sponse management, fault detection and localization, or renewable energy integration.
Analyzing the impact and potential applications of our toolbox in these areas can provide
additional insight into the versatility and scalability of our proposed approach.
1.3
Research Questions
The research objectives of this thesis are focused on identifying data quality issues that
may exist in the data provided by DSOs and can potentially be utilized for reliability assess­
ment through AI algorithms. While the goal of this thesis is to present a broadly applicable
data processing pipeline for the data that DSOs may have, we have narrowed down our
research objective to a specific study case, namely the DG medium­voltage (MV) cable
failures and outages dataset in Denmark (ELFAS), which is provided by Green Power
DenmarkTM.
Therefore, the research questions we sought to answer in this thesis are as follows:
• What are the main data requirements and which solutions can be applied to ad­
dress them? We identified several preliminary solutions for preprocessing, clean­
ing, and enriching the datasets to overcome the main issues encountered in the raw
data.
• What are the most effective solutions for addressing persisting issues in the
distribution grid reliability assessment data? One of the main data requirements
we encountered in this thesis was the unexpectedly large number of missing values

• Are the suggested solutions potentially applicable to other datasets and tasks?
While this thesis performs an overview of possible solutions and later focuses on the

1.4
Thesis outline
Based on the research questions presented in Section 1.3, our thesis covers the following
items:
• Data Definitions: We initially present a series of important definitions related to var­
ious types of data relevant to DGs. These definitions establish a solid foundation for
accurately comprehending grid health assessment. From this base, we identify the
general challenges that this data introduces and provide a framework for workflow
development.
• Data Enrichment Workflow: We outline the existing techniques to overcome the
previously identified challenges. The data enrichment consists of two key compo­
nents: cleaning and pre­processing, and synthetic data generation.
– Cleaning and pre­processing: We apply a range of strategies discussed during
the data overview phase to ensure the data is accurate, consistent, and suitable
for subsequent analysis.
– Synthetic data generation: This second part, serving as the focal point of this
thesis, is devoted to addressing challenges related to data availability, scarcity,
and other identified difficulties. We explore, conceptualize, and implement gen­
erative models to generate synthetic data points.
• Case study identification, data collection, and overview: We provide some more
arguments for the choice of medium­voltage cable failure data as our study case and
we present the process of relevant data collection. Besides, we conduct a broad
overview of the dataset to identify key aspects of the workflow’s application.
• Workflow application to case study: We explain how the presented enrichment
workflow is tailored to and implemented in the case study. We provide the justifi­
cation for inclusion or exclusion of several presented enrichment strategies and we
present a comprehensive explanation of how we put it into practice.
• Evaluation and demonstration of workflow efficacy: To evaluate the perfor­
mance and quality of the generative models, we extract relevant key performance
indicators. These metrics serve as objective indicators to evaluate the performance
of our generative models, helping us determine the suitability of the synthetic (and
enriched) data for reliability assessment. To further validate the utility of the work­
flow, we perform a proof of concept by comparing the performance of simple clas­
sification models using both the enriched and non­enriched datasets.

2
Types and challenges of distribution
grid data used for reliability studies
In Chapter 1, we discussed the interest of DSOs in making use of ML models. These
models help assess grid reliability, monitor its health status, and enable predictive main­
tenance tasks. The objective of this chapter is to provide a comprehensive introduction
and solid foundation on the types of data that DSOs typically use for DG evaluation. We
have organized this chapter into two main sections. The first segment involves classifying
and defining all the data types and sources that are pertinent to DSOs. The second part
conducts a thorough review of all the challenges and issues related to the utilization of
this data for data­driven assessments of the distribution grid’s condition.
These discussions aim to provide a comprehensive understanding of the data require­
ments and challenges associated with both online and offline tasks, thus laying a solid
foundation for the subsequent chapters.
2.1
Data types
In this section, we explore the various data sources that can provide valuable insights into
the reliability and health status of the DG. These sources encompass both operational,
non­operational and static data, and their integration offers a comprehensive understand­
ing of the grid’s condition (see Figure 2.1). This section also serves as a literature review
on the types of data employed in previous works.
2.1.1
Operational data
• PMU ( phasor measurement unit) data provides highly accurate information about
the state of the power system [12]. PMUs measure voltage, current, and phase
angle at high speeds, typically 30 to 60 samples per second. This level of detail
allows for highly accurate monitoring of power system dynamics and, for this thesis,
is relevant due to its potential in identifying patterns of high loading and loading
cycles that can affect the reliability of the system. Given its wide usage in areas such
as wide­area monitoring and control, transient stability analysis, and power system
state estimation, PMU data undoubtedly holds relevance for reliability studies. Its
mention and brief discussion here provides a deeper understanding of data types
involved in such studies, even if not directly used in this work, and set the ground

for potential incorporation in future research.
• SCADA (supervisory control and data acquisition) data is used to monitor and con­
trol power system equipment such as generators, transformers, and switchgear.
SCADA systems typically include remote terminal units (RTUs) that collect data from
sensors and other devices, which are then transmitted to a central control system.
It is important to note that in some DG contexts, such as in our study case with
medium voltage cables, not all data regarding current, voltage, and loading is re­
layed to the DG SCADA. This occasionally leads to an incomplete picture of the
loading information for all cables. Despite this, SCADA data, collected at slower
rates than PMU data, still delivers valuable insights into the status and performance
of power system equipment. Furthermore, it proves useful in applications including
fault detection and isolation, load forecasting, and equipment maintenance.
• AMI (advanced metering infrastructure) data is collected from smart meters installed
at customer premises. Smart meters measure energy consumption and other pa­
rameters and can be used to provide customers with detailed information about their
energy usage. AMI data can also be used by DSOs for applications such as load
forecasting, demand response, and outage management.
• Failure Data, often recorded in logs or incident reports, are critical for understanding
the reliability and health status of the distribution grid. This data includes informa­
tion about the date, time, location, type and cause of equipment failures. It may
also contain information about any associated downtime or repair actions. By an­
alyzing failure data, engineers can investigate root causes and prioritize predictive
maintenance tasks to improve the overall reliability of the DG.
All these operational data sources are typically owned by DSOs and might not be read­
ily available from public sources. Consequently, sometimes it is challenging to develop
novel digital or data­driven tools, as the lack of accessible, high­quality data can inhibit
the feasibility of prototyping, testing, and validating these technologies in real­world con­
ditions.
2.1.2
Non­operational data
In addition to operational data, non­operational data plays a crucial role in understanding
the health status and conditions of the DG. By integrating this information with operational
data, a more comprehensive view can be achieved. Key non­operational data sources
include:
• Weather data: Weather data has been widely used to predict electricity demand
and renewable energy production. By using weather forecasting data, DSOs can
anticipate changes in energy demand and adjust their operations accordingly. Tem­
perature and humidity data can lead to accelerated aging of cables and have a high
correlation to other impact drivers such as water ingress, partial discharge, loading,
or soil conditions. Aggregating exposure to climate effects becomes relevant when
considering the cumulative number of very cold days, which can indicate additional
aging of underground cables due to freezing and thawing of the surrounding soil,
resulting in additional mechanical stress [13]. Weather data can also be used to
predict the likelihood of power outages caused by extreme weather events such as
storms, high winds, or snow [14, 15].
• GIS data: Geographic information system (GIS) data can be used to map the phys­

2.2.1
Data Integrity
The initial task of our data analysis essentially involves a thorough cleansing of the dataset
to ensure its validity and integrity. The cleaning process entails the detection and rectifi­
cation of ’dirty values,’ or errors in the data. These could arise from various sources such
as human errors during data entry, system glitches, or discrepancies in data format.
Furthermore, special attention is given to ’outliers,’ data points that significantly diverge
from the rest of the data set. While outliers could sometimes reflect genuine anomalies,
they often result from errors and can distort analytical models if not properly handled. For
instance, in the context of DGs, a sudden spike in current readings from a transformer,
significantly higher than its usual operational range, could be considered an outlier. This
could correspond to either a real but unusual event or a measurement error. In any case,
such outliers need to be carefully evaluated and, if necessary, corrected or excluded to
prevent them from skewing models and leading to incorrect conclusions.
2.2.2
Data Scarcity
A significant challenge encountered in DG reliability assessment stems from the innate
scarcity of the data. This scarcity refers to the limited amount or even a complete lack
of labeled training data that can be used to improve and validate the predictive models.
By design, DSOs strive to minimize grid failures and outages. Consequently, instances
of these events, and consequently, data documenting them, are infrequent.
Adding to this challenge is another layer of complexity. Sometimes these events are
inadequately or inaccurately recorded. To provide an example, a power outage might be
indeed reported, but the specific cause of the outage such as a transformer failure or a line
fault might not be identified or properly recorded. This lack of detailed information leads
to an even sparse dataset at the DSO’s disposal, limiting the potential for comprehensive
analysis.
We propose data augmentation as a viable solution for utilizing this sparse data in ML
tasks such as predictive maintenance and grid status monitoring. Data augmentation
involves artificially generating observations based on the limited real­world events doc­
umented by the DSOs. This technique has proven successful in other fields like image
and audio processing, where extensive research has been done and numerous solutions
proposed.
For instance, in image processing, data augmentation can involve flipping or inverting

2.2.3
Missing values
Handling missing values is of paramount importance in preparing datasets for machine
learning (ML) models. Missing values can have a significant impact on the performance
and reliability of ML models. They can bias the model’s estimates and predictions, reduce
the effectiveness of the learning algorithm, and ultimately lead to incorrect conclusions.
Most ML algorithms require numerical input and cannot handle missing values, leading
to errors during model training. Moreover, missing values may mask the true underly­
ing relationships between variables, which can make a model’s predictions less accurate.
Therefore, it is of utmost importance to handle these values correctly with the appropri­

2.2.4
Class imbalance
One of the prevalent challenges in machine learning, and by extension, in the task of
assessing DG reliability based on failure data, is dealing with class imbalance. In simple
terms, this refers to a situation where the number of observations in one class (or category)
far outweighs the number in another class.
In the context of DG reliability assessment, the class imbalance is typically quite severe.
For example, if the goal is to identify characteristic features of failed components, the
available dataset comprises only instances of such components. In a typical ML clas­
sification task, this could result in a model being biased toward predicting the majority
class.
Such an imbalance poses a problem as most standard classification algorithms are de­
signed to maximize overall accuracy, and therefore, may perform poorly when one class is
significantly underrepresented. These algorithms could overlook the minority class (in our
case, the absence of non­failed component data) and subsequently provide an inaccurate
understanding of the grid’s reliability.
A common method for addressing class imbalance in other ML applications is through
oversampling the minority class or undersampling the majority class. However, following
with the example, we have no data about non­failed components. Therefore, the tradi­
tional solutions to the class imbalance problem may not be viable.
Nevertheless, it is worth noting that even though we lack data about non­failed compo­
nents, our task’s specific nature – identifying characteristics of failed components – allows
us to handle this seemingly imbalanced dataset differently. Here, the ’class imbalance’
might not be a drawback but a characteristic feature of our task. By focusing on analyz­
ing the patterns and regularities in the failure data, we can derive useful insights that can
inform reliability assessment and contribute towards predictive maintenance strategies.
In future work, we could explore the development of specialized machine learning algo­
rithms designed to handle such single­class scenarios, or potentially consider methods
to synthetically generate plausible ’non­failed’ component data while adhering to the con­
straints of our problem context. However, such strategies need to be approached with
caution, given the high stakes associated with DG reliability.

3.1.1
Dealing with Missing Values
Missing values can be attributed to various reasons, such as random occurrences and
failures in measurement or data recording processes. A crucial aspect of handling missing
values is visualizing their distribution using methods like heatmaps or similar tools, which
can help detect patterns in the missingness of values [19]. Being mindful of and informed
about missing values is essential as many ML models are not equipped to handle them.
There are several ways to deal with missing values: deleting rows and/or columns or
performing imputation algorithms to fill the missing data. Being able to perform imputation
would be the ideal scenario since it would involve not losing a whole observation when
only a few of the entries are missing or invalid. Classical imputation algorithms include
mean imputation, median imputation, mode imputation, and linear regression, and they
try to impute the missing values by benefiting from statistical properties. Nevertheless,
they present several limitations [20]:
• Loss of variance: When missing values are replaced by mean or median, the
variance of the data is reduced. This can potentially lead to underestimating the
standard errors in the data and bias in subsequent analysis.

• Bias in correlation: Mean imputation can weaken the correlation between vari­
ables, as imputed values are independent of other variables. This can impact the
performance of many ML algorithms that rely on the correlation between features.
• Inappropriate for non­random missingness: These methods are generally not
suitable for data that is not missing at random (NMAR). If missingness depends on
unobserved data or the missing data itself, mean/median/mode imputation could
lead to biased results.
• Cannot handle large amounts of missing data: If the dataset has a high propor­

results.
• No uncertainty estimation: Simpler imputation methods don’t account for the un­
certainty inherent in imputing missing data. They replace each missing value with a
single estimate and do not reflect any variability that would naturally result from the
imputation process.
Consequently, devising an effective strategy to handle missing values, specific to the
dataset in question, becomes critical to minimize the impact of data deletion. Although
imputation techniques are preferable, as mentioned earlier, if they are not feasible due to
dataset limitations, the impact of deleting missing values must be carefully assessed.
When addressing missing value deletion, it is essential to weigh the advantages and dis­
advantages of deleting specific observations or entire features, a decision that hinges
on the degree of missingness and the feature’s relevance to our model; this represents
a trade­off between preserving the number of observations and maintaining the integrity
and predictive power of the feature set.

3.1.3
Outlier detection
After generating a fully populated dataset augmented with informative external data, the
subsequent stage of our pre­processing workflow involves outlier removal. An observa­
tion is typically classified as an outlier if it significantly deviates from the bulk of the data
[21]. The presence of outliers can be attributed to several factors such as errors in mea­
surements, data corruption, and rare occurrences.
Outliers can potentially compromise the performance of ML models by distorting statistical
properties and negatively impacting the model’s robustness, stability, and training perfor­
mance. The criteria to determine whether a data point is an outlier may vary, depending
on whether the feature is numerical or categorical. For numerical features, outliers often
display extreme values considerably distant from the main body of data points, leading
to a skewed or long­tailed distribution. As a result, they can be detected and filtered us­
ing statistical algorithms. In contrast, outliers in categorical features are data points with
significantly lower or higher frequencies in the dataset, disrupting the balance among the
categories. However, there is no universally accepted definition of outliers, and in many
instances, expert knowledge is required to identify actual outliers due to the dependencies
on the nature of the task and dataset.
In our devised pipeline, we limited our outlier removal efforts to numerical features. The
issue of class imbalance potentially caused by categorical outliers is addressed more
thoroughly in the second stage of our enrichment pipeline. To detect outliers, we utilized
two distinct methods: the Z­score and Interquartile Range (IQR).
3.1.3.1
Z­Scores
If we assume our numerical data follows a Gaussian­like distribution, the Z­score method
is an effective strategy for identifying outliers. This technique quantifies a data point’s
distance from the mean in terms of the standard deviation. The Z­score reads as follows:

Not all datasets follow a Gaussian distribution. For non­Gaussian datasets, the IQR is

IQR = Q3 −Q1
(3.2)
Percentiles can be calculated by sorting observations and selecting values at specific
indices. The 50th percentile, the median, corresponds to the middle value in an ordered
dataset.
Outlier detection techniques leveraging IQR consider normal data points to be located
within high­probability regions of a stochastic model, while outliers fall within low­probability
regions. Outliers can be identified by setting boundaries based on the IQR. Specifically,
values below (Q1−k·IQR) or above (Q3+k·IQR) are flagged as outliers, with k typically
set to 1.5. A k value of 3 or more might indicate extreme outliers.

Feature engineering
The effectiveness and interpretability of ML models are greatly influenced by feature en­
gineering, which includes feature selection, creation, and transformation. By removing
irrelevant or redundant variables, feature selection helps to decrease overfitting, increase
accuracy, and hasten training [19]. On the other hand, feature creation entails construct­

transformation—which includes normalization, standardization, and encoding—assures
that the features are in an appropriate format and scale for the selected methods, thereby
lowering the computational load and enhancing model performance. These procedures
improve model effectiveness, efficiency, and interpretability as a whole, which is crucial
for effective ML applications.

3.2.1
Deep generative models
DGMs are neural networks encompassing numerous hidden layers, and they are trained
to emulate complex, high­dimensional probability distributions. In essence, the objective
in training DGMs involves learning a typically elusive or unmanageable probability distri­
bution from a generally small set of independent and identically distributed samples [23].
When effectively trained, these models can be utilized to estimate the likelihood of a given
sample and to generate new samples that bear resemblance to samples from the uniden­
tified distribution, thus providing a suitable solution for the data augmentation issue.
DGMs also offer benefits over traditional tabular data augmentation methods like SMOTE,
since they have the potential to accurately represent the true data distribution. Recent
studies across various domains demonstrate that synthetic data created through DGMs
can enhance subsequent models [24], particularly for making predictions on smaller sub­
groups [18]. This is crucial, given that these small subgroups might represent underrep­
resented minorities.
Moreover, DGMs play a crucial role in addressing the issue of missing value imputation.
They operate by learning the underlying data distribution, thereby enabling the generation
of new, plausible data points. By modeling joint distributions of observed features, they
provide a sophisticated way to impute missing values that are informed by the correlations
among features [25, 26].
3.2.2
Introduction to model­based approaches
The core premise of the model­based technique in ML revolves around devising a unique
and customized model specially designed for each novel application. The main objectives
of a model­based methodology comprise the following [27]:
• The capability to formulate an extensive assortment of models, accompanied by
appropriate inference or learning algorithms.
• Every distinct model can be optimized according to the unique needs of the spe­
cific application: for instance, if the application requires a fusion of clustering and
classification in the framework of time­series data, it is not required to combine tra­
ditional algorithms for each of these components, but rather a unified model that
encapsulates the desired functionality can be developed.
• Separation between the model and the inference algorithm: if alterations are en­
acted on the model, the related altered inference software is automatically gener­
ated. Similarly, progress in methods for effective inference can be used across a
wide array of models.
• Clarity of functionality: the model is represented by condensed code within a stan­
dard modeling language, making the structure of the model easily discernible. Such
modeling code can be effortlessly shared and expanded within a community.
Following on from the exploration of model­based methods in ML, we now proceed to in­

3.2.3.3
Synthetic data generation using VAEs
The goal of training VAE models is to outcome data scarcity and missing values issues.
In this section, we present the steps that we followed once the models were trained in
order to tackle these tasks. For data augmentation and missing value management, we
used standard VAEs. To do so, the steps to follow are:
1. Sample from the Latent Space: The first step to generate new data is to sample
a point from the latent space. This is done by drawing from a standard normal dis­
tribution, z ∼N(0, I), where I is the identity matrix. This is possible because, dur­
ing training, the VAE’s encoder network is regularized via de KL­divergence, which
ensures that the distributions in the latent space do not diverge too far from this
standard normal distribution.
2. Decoding the Sampled Point: The sampled latent point z is then passed through
the VAE’s decoder network. The decoder, which has been trained to reverse the
encoding process, maps the latent point back to the original data space, generating
new data that resembles the training data.
This process can be repeated any number of times to generate as many new data points
as required. These data points will have, ideally, the same underlying structure as the
training data, because the VAE has learned the distribution of the training data in the
latent space.

Another issue that can be overcome by VAEs are missing values. VAEs learn the joint
probability distribution of all the observed features in the data, and then use the learned
distributions to predict missing values. The imputation process can be described by the
following steps:
1. Encode the partially observed data: Given a data point xobs with missing values,
we can still pass it through the encoder of the VAE, for example, by changing the
NaN for another value, such as 0. The encoder will output parameters (µ, σ) of the
approximate posterior qϕ(z|xobs). The encoder will try to locate the observed point
in the latent space, so if there is a small number of missing features, the observation
has a high chance to be located close to the actual point.
2. Sampling Latent Variables: The next step is to draw samples from the approximate
posterior. This is typically done using the reparameterization trick, which allows us
to backpropagate gradients through the random sampling operation. We sample z
from qϕ(z|xobs) by generating an epsilon ϵ ∼N(0, I), and computing z = µ + σ ⊙ϵ.
3. Decode to Impute Missing Values: Finally, the sampled z is fed to the decoder,
which gives us the parameters of the likelihood pθ(x|z). The output of the decoder
is a reconstruction of the complete data point, filling in the missing values. If the
output variables are real­valued, we can assume a Gaussian likelihood and use
the mean of the output distribution as the imputed values. If the output variables
are categorical, we consider a multinomial likelihood and take the most probable
category as the imputed value.
One must consider, however, that this method for handling missing values becomes more
unreliable the more missing features there are in a given observation. This is because if
xobs has a lot of missing values, the encoder will struggle to generate the correct posterior
parameters µ and σ since the point will not be representative in the joint probability distri­
bution learned by the VAE. As an example, in the MNIST data shown in Figure 3.3, this
would be the equivalent of removing some pixels from our input image, which represents
the number 3. If few pixels were removed, the VAE would still recognize that the input
image is a 3 and it would reconstruct something resembling a 3. But if there were too
many missing pixels, the VAE would struggle to identify the input image as a 3 in the la­
tent space, so the reconstruction would be expected to be something more different than
an image of the number 3.
3.2.4
Conditional Variational Autoencoders
Up to this point, we presented VAEs as a solution to handle two of the common issues
in dealing with data quality we presented in Section 2.2. The first one is being able to
handle data scarcity by synthetically generating more samples with the same statistical
properties, and the second one, imputing missing values when the number of missing
features in an observation falls below an acceptable number. Conditional VAEs (CVAEs)
are an extension of VAEs that aim to tackle more efficiently the problem of data generation
in the case we want to generate synthetic data of a specific class.

Validation of synthetic data generation using VAEs and

As previously discussed, the primary objective of the VAE (and CVAE) models is to gener­
ate samples that bear the same statistical attributes as the original data. In simpler terms,
we strive to ensure that the probability distribution, associated with the generation of an
observation having specific features, closely mirrors the probability distribution that the
actual data follows.
Ideally, a comparison of the joint probability distributions would be the most comprehen­
sive approach. However, performing statistical tests and extracting metrics from high­
dimensional datasets can be complex and computationally taxing.
Initially, we can evaluate the similarity between the marginal probability distributions for
each feature. For two joint probability distributions to be considered similar, it is necessary
(but not sufficient) that their marginal probability distributions also exhibit similarity [31].
Therefore, one can start by assessing the similarity between the real and synthetic data
distribution for each feature with the following indicators and metrics:
• Distribution Visualization: This is the simplest way to compare two distributions. It
involves plotting the histogram or density plot of two distributions to visually compare
their shape, spread, and central tendencies. There is no mathematical formulation
for it as it’s primarily a visual tool for comparison.
• KS Statistic (Kolmogorov­Smirnov Statistic): This non­parametric test quantifies
a distance between the empirical distribution functions of two samples. The KS
Statistic is defined as the maximum distance between the cumulative distribution
function (CDF) of the two samples. Mathematically, it is defined as:
Dn,n′ = sup

of the largest discrepancy between the two CDFs, indicating how much the distri­
butions differ across the entire range. The KS statistic is sensitive to differences in
shape, location and scale between the distributions.

4.4.1
Missing value deletion
The ultimate objective was to develop a dataset devoid of missing values. In our case,
and given the characteristics of our dataset, it is hard to implement classic imputation
algorithms, since they fulfill some of the aforementioned limitations such as non­random
missingness (some features were systematically not recorded during a certain time pe­
riod), and the large amount of missing values. More advanced imputation methods, such
as multiple imputation or model­based imputation, can help to address some of these
limitations. However, these methods come with their own assumptions and complexities
and should be chosen carefully based on the context of the data.
This approach was necessitated by the numerous limitations in our dataset which made
the use of classical imputation algorithms unsuitable, as explained in Section 2.2.3.
Nevertheless, we were confronted with a trade­off between the significance of the fea­
tures and the number of missing values that we deemed acceptable. In essence, our
tolerance for missing values in a feature was higher if we deemed the feature to be partic­
ularly informative. For instance, despite a large number of missing values in the feature
Item_Origin, we preserved this feature due to its perceived importance. Conversely, we
discarded columns like Description if they exhibited numerous missing values, especially
given that these were not always filled out and, when populated, each observation was
unique.
At this stage, we reassessed the relevance of GIS data, such as Coordinates, City, and
zip. Although crucial for appending non­operational data from other sources, these fea­
tures presented too few data points for training. Their availability ranged from 32 entries
for Coordinate2Lat and Coordinate2Long, to around 400 for zip.
Following the removal of the aforementioned columns, we proceeded to eliminate all ob­
servations containing at least a missing value. The resulting dataset, although much
smaller in size than the original one, was thus entirely populated, with no missing values
or invalid entries present.
4.4.2
Data appending
As detailed in Section 4.2, we collected several types of non­operational data from publicly
available sources to append to our failure dataset. The inclusion of these extra features
aims to enhance our understanding of the underlying behavior of the observations. How­
ever, we had to reconsider appending data related to geographical location.
Despite this, we observed that most potential features to be appended were actually de­
pendent on location. For the sake of this study, we chose to append weather conditions
under the assumption that the weather at all failure points is relatively similar to that in
Copenhagen. We speculate that most failures occurred in the Copenhagen area due to
its higher population density and larger distribution grid infrastructure.
The procedure for weather data appending was the following (Figure 4.2):
• Load the weather data time series: Initially, we load the time series data that con­
tain weather information. This dataset includes various features like temperature,
humidity, wind speed, precipitation, etc., typically recorded at regular intervals, such
as hourly or daily. In our case, we gathered the hourly data.
• Identify matching observations: After loading the weather data, we identify the
timestamps in the weather data that match with those in the ELFAS dataset. This

• Append weather data to the ELFAS dataset: Once we identified the matching

4.5.1
VAE architecture adaptation
The VAE architecture that we applied is the same as the standard architecture defined in
Section 3.2.3.2, but adapted to the requirements of the ELFAS dataset.
For instance, the ELFAS dataset has the particularity of having a quite high dimensionality
with a large number of categorical variables. The cardinality (number of categories) of
these categorical variables ranges from 2 (binary variables) to 35 for some variables.
The usual technique for handling categorical variables in neural networks is to apply one­
hot encoding. In this case, however, this would lead to excessive sparsity and too high
dimensionalities, which is inefficient and may lead to poor performance when used with
machine learning models. A more effective approach to handle such categorical variables
is to use embedding techniques.

Embedding is a form of feature learning where categorical variables are mapped to vectors
in a lower­dimensional space. Mathematically, an embedding of a categorical variable is a
function E : C →RD, where C is the set of all possible categories, and D is the embedding
dimension.
Suppose we have a categorical variable xcat with a cardinality of K and we decide to
represent it in a D­dimensional space. Each category k ∈{1, . . . , K} is then represented
as a unique D­dimensional vector ek.
In the context of the VAE, the embedding function E is typically implemented as an em­
bedding layer, which can be represented as a K × D matrix WE. The embedding vector
for each category is learned during the training process. For this, we now suppose that our
dataset x consists of data points each made up of a combination of K categorical variables

k=1 and L continuous variables xcont = {xcont,l}L
l=1, that is, x = [xcat, xcont].

Model training and hyper­parameter tuning
As a deep­learning model, the performance of the generative algorithm depends on a
large extent on the hyper­parameter tuning. There is a meaningful number of hyper­

In this section, we are going to compare distributions between real and synthetic data,

Conclusion
This thesis has examined data­driven solutions for distribution grid reliability assessment,
providing an overview of the current techniques and the key challenges involved in utiliz­
ing data for machine learning tasks. In doing so, it has shone a spotlight on the current
techniques in the field while underscoring the challenges inherent in leveraging data for
machine learning tasks. The journey unfolded as a process of methodical experimen­
tation and in­depth investigation to tackle these data requirements. It culminated in the
formulation of a sophisticated workflow that brings together traditional statistical methods
and advanced deep generative models for data synthesis.
The journey led us to the application of our devised workflow to a case study of cur­
rent relevance: medium­voltage cable failures in Denmark, relying on a dataset furnished
by Green Power Denmark. The outcomes of our work clearly demonstrate the marked
improvement in data quality before and after the enrichment process, highlighting the
potential of the chosen algorithms for future research. Indications of substantial improve­
ment were observed in addressing key issues such as missing data imputation and class
imbalance for classification tasks.
Yet, in the process of generating synthetic data, a different facet of class imbalance in
our dataset posed a notable challenge. Here, the imbalance refers to the uneven repre­
sentation of various classes within the data, which can complicate the accurate learning
and replication of underrepresented class characteristics. Even as our efforts with Varia­
tional Autoencoders and Conditional Variational Autoencoders yielded promising results,
the class imbalance and related challenges presented us with an intriguing puzzle. Out­
lier removal, a common method in data processing, sometimes clashes with maintaining
class balance, thus adding an additional layer of complexity.
The findings from this research also illuminate areas of improvement in inferring categor­
ical variables, particularly those with less information density. The statistical properties
of these variables require further attention, as they are not captured as accurately as
desired.
While our investigation has been primarily oriented toward distribution grid reliability as­
sessment, we have identified a wealth of potential applications for our workflow. From
aspects of predictive maintenance in power systems to many of other fields, our approach
showcases remarkable versatility. Future work can delve deeper into the capabilities of
generative models, fine­tuning the current models, or even venturing into alternative ter­
ritories like Generative Adversarial Networks. Additionally, further reflections on the suit­
ability of workflow algorithms for specific scenarios will be of great importance. Questions
like: should outliers be eliminated, or are they vital for capturing specific behaviors such
as load spikes or extreme weather conditions, need to be addressed.
Overall, the work presented in this thesis opens up promising avenues for further advance­
ments in the realm of machine learning applied to power system reliability. By setting the
groundwork for a robust and versatile workflow for data enrichment, it opens up numerous
potential paths for further advancements. As we move forward, our work has the poten­
tial to make a significant impact on operational strategies and overall system reliability,
thereby transforming the landscape of power system management.
