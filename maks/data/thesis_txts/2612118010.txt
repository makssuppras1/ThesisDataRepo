Abstract  
 
Quality of indoor environment affects children’s health, physically and mentally. However, there is 
evidence for the relationship between health issues and CO2 concentration, but there are a few types of 
research in individuals’ experiences regarding indoor climate. Since 2014, the Ministry of children and 
education in Denmark provides national survey data for gauging well-being among pupils. This research 
tries to identify and quantify correlations between different indicators of well-being and indoor climate. 
Following our hypothesis, we expect a correlation between some of them and determine how strong 
they are rather than the others. As an example, CO2-concentration and “headache.” The value for 
“headache” is the answer to one of the well-being questions as “How often do you have a headache,” 
which could be chosen among some options: “Very often, Often, Sometimes, Infrequent and Don’t want 
to answer.” To further research, answers are ranked by a value, where “5” is the best, and “1” is the 
worse (0 for Don’t want to answer). The value used for the specific class is the average value for the 
answers given by the children of that class.  
Furthermore, other indicators than indoor climate factors are investigated. As an example, if the type of 
ventilation system will influence children’s feelings regarding their education area and if other indicators 
like income affect well-being more than the indoor climate indicators or not. Likewise, correlations 
between absence and social well-being and loneliness, and so on are also investigated. “Spearman 
correlation coefficient” is used for the purpose of this investigation.  
The sample data includes an average value of the answers to the chosen well-being question belonging 
to children in 5th grade for every school as well as the classroom’s CO2, Temperature, relative humidity 
in a period from 2nd to 13th November, between 8:00 a.m. and 5:00 p.m. This is the period that the 
classrooms are most likely to be occupied. To have better result, data for weekend days is removed. 
Our findings indicate a correlation between Headache and CO2, which can explain by a regression 
equation: “Headache_Satisfaction_rate = 3,7 – 7,1E-5 CO2”. Using a 95% confidence interval shows that 
reducing CO2 concentration from 4000 ppm to 1000 ppm increases the satisfaction rate minimum 4,1%. 
There is also a relatively strong correlation between “Loneliness” and Temperature, where higher 
Temperature causes less loneliness among our sample cases. Correlation between income and some of 
the well-being indicators are more significant comparing to the indoor climate factors. The calculations 
for this work are performed in RStudio.  
It’s expected that this research will notify decision-makers about the expected satisfaction gains by 
improving the indoor climate and calculate the result’s reliability.

Abstrakt  
 
Indeklima kvaliteten er altid en af de foretrukne faktorer for energisektoren i bygning design. Der er 
udført mange undersøgelser, der viser at der er en relation mellem sundhed og indeklima faktorer. En 
stor koncentration af CO2 vil føre til træthed eller nogle værre problemer. Dog de fleste af 
undersøgelserne beskæftiger sig med generaliseringer. Dvs. en persons oplevelser er som regel 
overskuet. Folk bliver normalt ikke spurgt om hvordan det føles, når indeklimaet forbedres via de nye 
ventilationssystemer eller lignende. 
Siden 2014 udfører Børne og uddannelse ministeriet en undersøgelse, hvor skolebørn skal svare på ca. 
fyrre spørgsmål om trivsel. På den anden side, måles indeklima indikatorer på nogle af skolerne til at 
have styr på luftkvalitet og energiforbrug i de danske skoler. Som et eksempel en gruppe fra DTU har 
gennemført en undersøgelse i november 2009, hvor CO2 koncentration, temperatur og luftfugtighed er 
målt for 88 skoler i løbet af en måned.  
I denne rapport, vil vi undersøge om der er en korrelation mellem nogle af indikatorerne af indeklima og 
de besvarelser som eleverne har givet til trivselsspørgsmålene. For eksempel, dette spørgsmål ”Hvor ofte 
har du hoved pine?” kan besvares med disse muligheder:  
”Meget tit, Tit, En gang i mellem, Sjældent, Aldrig, Jeg ønsker ikke at svare”  
Disse svare er blevet rangeret i forhold til tilfredsheds grad. DVS. tallene 1,2,3,4,5 er tilkoblet til 
svarmulighederne forholdsvis. Derefter anses den procentdel af klassen, som har valgt de forskellige 
svare, og til sidst gennemsnit svar for den bestemte klasse regnes ud.  
Al de udregnede værdier samt indeklimamålingerne bruges til at finde korrelation koefficienterne, hvor 
efter at undersøge dets troværdighed, den tilhørende ligning defineres.  
I eksemplet for korrelationen mellem CO2 koncentration og tilfredshedsgrad i forhold til hovedpine ser 
ligninger sådan ud: Tilfredshedsgrad _hovedpine = 3,7 – 7,1E-5 CO2. 
Korrelationen accepteres på grund af den store værdi for Spearmans korrelation koefficienten og en lille 
p-værdi, som indicier at der er en korrelation mellem de to faktorer. Med 95% tillid konkluderes at når 
CO2 koncentrationen reduceres til 1000 ppm, oppe fra 4000 ppm, tilfredshedsgrad stiges med mere en 
4%. Desuden findes også andre korrelationer, for eksempel mellem ”ensomhed” og ”temperatur”, hvor 
højre temperatur har en faldende effekt på ensomhed følelsen. Andre faktorer end indeklimafaktorerne, 
bland andet indkomst, fraværsprocent og ventilations type er også blevet undersøgt til at se 
påvirkningerne. RStudio coding-software er blevet brugt til at definere de lineær regressions, at tegne 
diagrammerne osv.  
Der forventes at denne undersøgelse hjælper myndighederne, bygnings designer, renovation eksperter 
og energidesign rådgiver til at antage tilfredsheds gevinster i deres beslutninger, og prøver at forbedre 
det samtidig med at forbedre indeklimakvaliteten. Der er også et behov for at udarbejde  
troværdigheden relateret til data og undersøgelse resultater.

1. Introduction  
 
When we work on some technical research in the field of technology or science, it seems that something 
is missing. It is not always easy to find out what. Is “Feeling” the missing link that our technology lacks in 
reality?! Do we make our technology for humans?! Do we ask people what they feel while using the tools 
or the equipment?! Maybe all is about “feelings”. What you and I feel about these words, this research, 
the situation today, the room you spent most of your time in! The answer to these questions will 
determine and influence the decisions.  
 
1.1. Background 
Energy-saving as the main factor related to the renovation of the buildings is a debatable topic these 
days. Whiles the municipalities have spent approx. DKK 18 billion on renovation and construction of new 
primary school buildings in 2007-2014, the mass experiments from resp. 2009 and 2014 showed that the 
Danish primary and lower secondary schools’ indoor climate had not improved in the intervening period.  
More than 85% of the Danish primary schools are built before 1995, while the requirement for having a 
ventilation system has been included in BR95 standards (Realdania, 2017). 
According to the OECD, in only one country, namely Australia, children in primary and lower secondary 
education spent more time comparing the Danish children. In their report from 2016, the number of 
compulsory instructions in Danish schools is more than 10 hours (Nordic Parenting, 2016). 
Ministry of children and education in Denmark surveys the well-being of the pupils every year. Questions 
about the experiences during school time will help the school authorities and other related institutions 
find the barriers to healthy growth and Well-Being (Børn Og Undervisning Ministeriet, Trivselsmåling, 
2014-2019). 
Based on several studies, there are several signs that school children’s well-being is declining. It is 
reflected in this fact that more of those who change schools do so because they thrive poorly (Vive, 
2018).  
Well-Being covers a broad range of definitions. Thus, the questions which will be identified must point to 
experiencing appropriate conditions to develop. For example, Vive, the center for research and analysis 
about Well-Being in Denmark, asked the 7-19 years old these questions about experiencing problems in 
their school within a year: 
1) Have you ever been sad and kept it? 
2)Do you have difficulty concentrating in class?  
3) Do you have had a conflict with your classmates? 4) Do you have had a disagreement with your 
teacher?  
Students could answer with “Yes,” “No,” and “I don’t know” (Vive, 2018).   
Tracing pupils’ development and well-being through surveys and exploring the link between indoor air 
quality and improvements will help educational building designers to improve their designs and using 
new devices through innovative thinking.

ventilation system compared with the manual ones (Realdania, 2017), satisfaction and comfort are still in

1.2. Report structure 
In this report, questions regarding well-being will be addressed by using the surveys carried out by the 
Ministry of children and education in Denmark from 2014 and so on, while indoor climate data refers to 
measurements made by a team from DTU in 2009. The focused group is 5th-grade pupils, whose school 
has participated in the research in 2009.  
Various literature works have been studied to be familiar with the topic and find out which studies have 
been done until now. The used methods related to this field are also researched. This work will combine 
two sets of data in two different areas, so the studies are about both topics: well-being research and 
measuring indoor climate effects.  
The method part will explain data gathering, data quality determination, the calculation to the further 
use, adjusting data to the different factors to combine analysis and get a result that makes sense. Static 
methods, models, and programming in RStudio are also a part of this section. To find out correlations, 
Spearman’s rank-order correlation coefficient uses to determine the strength and direction of the 
monotonic relationship between two variables rather than the strength and direction of the linear 
relationship between those, which is what Pearson’s correlation determines. (Laerd Statistics, 2018). To 
find the exact equation values, the simple or multiple regressions coded in RStudio, are used.  
The analysis and results section contains findings correlation coefficients between well-being survey 
questions, which has been interpreted into satisfaction rate values and indoor climate parameters. In 
addition, other parameters than indoor climate factors could affect pupils’ satisfaction rate. So, they are 
also be considered in the analyses. At the end of this section, the reliability of some results has been 
examined by using the diagram of 95% confidence interval and coding in RStudio. Improving IEQ (Indoor 
environment quality) is translated by using a specific type of ventilation system, and its impact is 
investigated as well.  
In the discussion part, we will look at the findings more profoundly and try to understand what causes 
them to occur. What do we need to make the study results more reliable? What can these findings be 
used for? Offer some suggestions, etc.  
Finally, the conclusion part is about what these results lead us to and how we can use them. It’s also 
about which factor influences well-being and satisfaction rate more than the others; while we are talking 
about the changes, how big of a difference do we expect for different aspects, and how much are the 
impacts. The topics that decision-makers can consider regarding the renovation projects, especially when 
it’s about school children.

2. Literature review (state-of-the-art) 
 
Nowadays, development is probably the most used phrase. People spend most of their time in an indoor 
environment. Without considering air quality and indoor climate comfort, the field of building design 
seems incomplete. In particular, poor indoor air quality (IAQ) can be harmful to vulnerable groups such 
as children, young adults, the elderly, or those suffering from chronic respiratory and cardiovascular 
diseases (Alessandra Cincinelli et al., 2017). In this article, variables that impact IAQ have been discussed. 
Some of the mentioned examples are carbon monoxide, fug, different gases like radon, bacteria, and 
particles caused by pets or people’s cough and sneezing, or any other resources. The authors refer to 
several studies like a study of 25 houses in Prilep, Republic of Macedonia, which emphasizes a link 
between IAQ with the factors like chemical-physical measurements and building characteristics like 
construction and renovation year. Moreover, among the factors, CO2 tracing as a guide can be used in 
the transient mass balance method as an instance. Referring to another study in this article, inhabitants, 
who their houses are equipped with a mechanical balanced ventilation system, have rated a higher 
quality of air, while the link between CO2 level and perceived stale air also observed. They conclude that 
even though there is some evidence to prove the link between health and indoor air pollution, for 
example, asthma, allergic rhinitis, and respiratory infections among students and dampness or mold, few 
studies are at workplaces schools. According to this article, there is a need to develop a practical 
diagnostic tool for proper IAQ management by focusing on improving air quality.  
 
Sergio A. et al., in his work in 2020, emphasize more research on well-being and operating it in a built 
environment. By evaluating ten questions, they try to notify that the conventional research methods 
focus on providing an average condition for people, while it is the inhabitants’ behavior, activity, 
psychology, etc. for every individual determine satisfaction rate. For example, they mention a study in 
U.S. commercial buildings, in which only 40% of occupants are satisfied with the thermal environment, 
even though the indoor environment comfort has been considered. They assume that well-being is 
supported when people are thermally happy and comfortable. Based on this article, a personal comfort 
system and some interdisciplinary research are required. Likewise, the authors mention some principles 
for well-being, links between air quality and well-being, the environmental effects on human psycho-
social and physical stressors, acoustic and thermal design, suggested research agenda to support well-
being, and so on. In this paper, the author mentions a need for adequate research and a mix of 
quantitative and qualitative design and non-linear analysis methods to consider the well-being and use it 
in environmental designs. While performance is essential, both referring to educational and office 
environments, rethinking the definition of health will be necessary. Instead of “absence of disease and 
infirmity,” well-being will be the central topic for Health performance indicators. On the other hand, in 
the authors’ opinion, to achieve a more sustainable future, as explicitly future in U.N. Sustainable 
Development Goals, well-being must be promoted.  
 
User satisfaction regarding using the different types of ventilation systems is one of the popular topics 
among researchers. A study about finding a relationship between perceived usability of building services 
and indoor environmental satisfaction in retrofitted low-energy houses showed that occupants who 
were dissatisfied with the indoor air quality and air supply wished more manual control on their 
ventilation system. Although the energy consumption reduced according to the set goal, problems such 
as less manually user-control devices, especially when the indoor air quality is not as expected by 
occupants, have caused dissatisfaction. Their article athers occupants’ experiences, for wwhomsome of 
their houses are retrofitted to low-energy houses, via surveys in printed form and on the Internet. They 
use some statistical methods to clean data in terms of clustering most related factors (questions), and 
then find a correlation between parameters like winter thermal comfort, Air supply by the ventilation 
system, and the perception of Usability and Control over Ventilation system. Satisfaction is the major

Another similar study belongs to the National Institute for Health and Welfare (THL) in Finland in 2014, 
conducted research among the 6th-grade students and asked the students and their parents about 
indoor environmental quality (IEQ) and any related symptoms like fatigue, stuffy nose, and headache. It 
investigates if a poor IEQ causes these kinds of problems or how strong the relationship is. They used 
questionnaires that included questions about students’: socioeconomic status, health and well-being, 
school environment, home environment, living habits (e.g., eating and sleeping), and 
advantages/disadvantages in learning. Moreover, parents could describe the child’s health status in a 
range between 1-4, where 1: Excellent, 2: Good, 3: Satisfactory, 4: Poor. The results showed that the 
most frequently reported IEQ factors causing daily inconvenience in classrooms were noise (11.0%) and 
stuffy air/low indoor air quality. Moreover, for classrooms with more than 15 respondents, the group-
level prevalence values most frequently above the 95% CI were weekly wheezing, cough with wheezing, 
and fever over 37 ◦C. (M. Turunen et al., 2014).  
 
By considering these articles, well-being, innovative research methods, performance, satisfaction, poor 
air quality, and the related factors seem to be keywords. Although performance is a central topic for 
school children, flourishing requires more. Well-being or satisfaction through school time will affect 
pupils’ future life. In the current definition of health, satisfaction is missing as a factor. Additionally, 
increasing the level of life quality requires redefining some of the phrases that we know traditionally.  
 
For this purpose, we have decided to investigate the link between air quality in some selected 
classrooms to the 5th grade students in Danish schools and the responded surveys regarding well-being. 
We will try to find the related questions about satisfaction and find out which one strongly is affected by 
the quality of air or the opposite.  
 
One of the issues in this field is the participation rate. In most cases, it is lower than expected, e.g., 
almost 17% for the research about environmental air satisfaction and perception of usability, and nearly 
52% for the Finnish schools. As the authors mention, it is difficult to assume that responses are 
representative. Nevertheless, the research can be used as a tool to measure well-being with some 
factors and link them to some of the quantitative parameters. While satisfaction is even a vague matter 
for oneself, the way to measure it and discuss parameters and findings will open a view to further 
research.  
 
In this research, we try to indicate if there is a correlation between the indoor climate factors provided 
by the technical system and what the pupils in the 5th grade feel and answer to the surveys. It will maybe 
help to focus on the crucial points when deciding about renovations.

stronger correlation, while the negative or positive sign represents an inverted correlation or the 
opposite. A correlation coefficient does not necessarily indicate that the variable has been affected by 
the factor. For example, CO2 cause headache in the classroom with a specific rate. Many other factors 
can play a role. It will show that if the factor has a probability of having an effect (Towards data science,

6. Discussion 
 
Indoor climate factors are maybe an extremely small category when the research is about the effects on 
children’s well-being. Although this work shows that in some points, correlations are more potent than 
the others, and the impacts can be concluded, but dissatisfaction can be caused by many other factors as 
well. A feeling to have an abdominal pain can have many reasons. Stress, having family problems, having 
health issues, feeling not fit, etc., can lead to abdominal pain. On the other hand, it’s challenging to work 
with the data and trust the measurements, because as an example, a ventilation system can be out of 
work due to technical problems. Another example is the difference between actual Temperature and 
what feels like Temperature in different seasons.   
The other issue is about personalized feelings. An appropriate temperature can feel very cold or very 
warm, depending on the child’s age, gender, clothing, etc. Therefore, with other samples or with a higher 
number of samples, the result can be different. The related well-being question that had a stronger 
relation to one factor can differ from one question to another.  
To perform this research more accurately, we need to adjust the measurement devices for this purpose. 
For example, change Temperature or humidity, or CO2 concentration for a specific period and then ask 
children about their feelings in different situations. It will be useful if we do the research for children of 
different ages to see if, for example, children in the various grades answer the questions differently 
under the same condition of the indoor climate factors.  
The indoor climate’s available measurement data was done in 2009, while the earliest data related to 
well-being was available from 2014. Although this was the best possible way to do this research with 
these data sets, there could be changes between 2009 and 2014; for example, the ventilation systems 
could be replaced between these years.

7. Conclusion 
 
Our findings indicate a correlation between some of the indoor climate factors and satisfaction rate 
regarding well-being questions. For example, headaches and CO2 concentration levels are correlated 
with a regression equation: “Headache= 3,7 – 7,1E-5 CO2”. We accept it because of the small p-value, 
0,0429, which is less than 0,05, and the high Spearman correlation coefficient as -0,3266. The negative 
sign also indicates the negative correlation; more CO2 concentration leads to less satisfaction rate. Using 
a 95% confidence interval shows that reducing CO2 concentration from 4000 ppm to 1000 ppm increases 
the satisfaction rate by more than 4%. There is also a relatively strong correlation between “loneliness” 
and Temperature, where higher Temperature among our sample cases causes less loneliness. Correlation 
between indicators other than indoor climate factors like income and professional well-being is strong 
when looking at the p-value (0,016). But, when we raise income by DKK 40 000, the satisfaction rate 
regarding well-being increases only by a maximum of 2,9%. 
About the other indicators, there are correlations between:  
“The type of ventilation” and “Social well-being.” 
Answer to the question “how often do you feel safe at school?” and “The type of ventilation.” 
“Professional well-being” and “Income.” 
As the results show by combining the effects of some factors like Temperature, satisfaction rate 
regarding loneliness, and absence rate, we can conclude that changing Temperature leads to a lower 
absence; in other words, a higher temperature gives a better result.  
Many factors influence well-being. Although the indoor climate factors play a role, other factors like 
income and even the type of ventilation system have an effect. Indeed, it does not mean that children 
notice the technical system directly. Still, that effect that provides the classroom condition will be a 
factor to feel better, at least regarding social well-being and feeling safe.  
Regarding renovation, by referring to the data about the schools’ renovation, in many cases, it’s not only 
about the specific classroom or even related to the indoor climate. So, we consider a mechanical 
balanced ventilation system as renovation at the school. As results show, the new ventilation systems 
influence pupils’ satisfaction.  
We expect that this research will inspire decision-makers to consider the predicted satisfaction gains 
while improving the indoor climate.
