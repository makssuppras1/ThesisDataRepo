Abstract
Starting in 2027 maritime shipping companies across the world are being required to
reduce greenhouse gas emissions, or face penalties [1]. In an effort to meet these emission
standards for environmental and monetary purposes, shipping companies have in recent
years been putting significant effort and resources into cleaner more efficient fuels and
engines. A recent transition to liquid natural gas (LNG), which is up to 90% methane,
emits far less CO2 due to the nature of the chemical reaction. However, this is only better
for emissions if the LNG is being burned efficiently. If the methane is not being burned
efficiently then the effects of methane on the environment are more than 28 times worse
than CO2.
This is why companies are turning to advanced modeling of their engines, in an attempt
to maximize the efficiency of these engines, and minimize the methane slip into the
atmosphere.
The model that will be described in this thesis is based largely on the
work published by Michael Jensen, Rasmus Cordtz, and Jesper Schramm [2], and later
further developed at Weel and Sandvig with the objective of modeling four stroke piston
combustion engines and attempting to limit methane slip while optimizing efficiency.
Part of this process includes parameter tuning for values that are not specifically known
in order to fit the model to real world data, a process which is not yet automated. In
this thesis the derivation and intuition of the model will be presented, and methods for
parameter optimization using observables from engine data will be detailed and applied.

1
Model Introduction
1.1
The Engine
The engine we wish to model is a four stroke piston combustion engine. A four stroke
piston combustion engine is a collection of cylinders, of bore (diameter) 0.34 meters.
Inside the cylinder a piston moves up and down under the direction of a crankshaft (0.2
meters) and a connecting rod (0.848 meters) as they pivot around the motor (Figure 2).
A four stroke piston combustion engine cylinder goes through four primary phases[3]:
вҖў Intake stroke - The piston goes down (volume goes up), and the inlet valve is
pumping a fuel/air mix into the cylinder
вҖў Compression stroke - The piston goes back up (volume decreases) and the fuel
restocked cylinder is compressed to create combustion conditions
вҖў Power stroke - Ignition of a pilot fuel sparks the combustion of the main fuel,
creating a rapidly expanding burning gas mixture that forces the piston back down,
generating power
вҖў Exhaust stroke - The remaining burned gas is expelled through the exhaust valve
as the piston comes back up
Figure 2: The four primary phases of the four stroke piston combustion engine [4]
1.2
Model Assumptions
Rather than time, this model will be dependent on the crank angle Оё. Four strokes of
the piston means that one full cycle of the engine process is a 720в—Ұchange in the crank
angle. Note that all angles will be in degrees.
The model will be divided into eight sections, with each section corresponding to changes
in the dynamics within the engine. These changes in dynamics are due to the exhaust
and inlet valves opening and closing, pilot fuel injection, combustion, and oxidation. A
detailed overview of each section will be provided during the derivation.
It is assumed that the contents of the mass entering the system via the inlet valve is a
mix of regular air (Nitrogen, Oxygen, Carbon Dioxide, and water vapor) and fuel. For
4

simplicity the fuel is assumed to be pure methane, although LNG is typically around 90%
methane. The main fuel reaction that is assumed to take place during the combustion
phase in the cylinder is
CH4 + 2 O2

The main fuel reaction is assumed to be sparked by the combustion of a pilot fuel.
The system of differential equations will therefore have five mass state variables to track
this: Methane (CH4), Oxygen (O2), Nitrogen (N2), Carbon Dioxide (CO2), and Water
(H2O). In addition the volume (V ), pressure (P), and temperature (T) will also be state
variables.
During the pilot fuel and main fuel combustion phases, in an attempt to be as accurate as
possible the model will split the cylinder into a burned zone and unburned zone, tracking
the state variables separately for each zone. Once the burn is complete it is assumed
that there is an oxidation phase where the reaction can still occur. More details will be
provided in the dedicated sections of the derivation.

2.2.3
Contents of Лҷmin
The contents of the mass coming through the inlet valve are assumed to be a mix of
atmospheric air and methane fuel. We use the air fuel ratio (AFR), and the excess air

adjusted accordingly). Note that the lower case yi implies a mole fraction of gas species

4.1
Model Initiation
The model is initiated by making a guess as to the mass contents of the cylinder, and
the temperature of the cylinder at Оё = 0. Depending on the initial guess it can take
some time for the model to reach a steady state. We can for example assume that the
contents of the cylinder is the standard inlet valve mix of fuel and air, with a normal
start-up temperature (300 K). This would represent the engine just starting up, and in
this scenario it would make sense that the engine would take some time to warm up
before reaching a consistent state. By tracking the total mass of the system over the
first 10 cycles (Figure 4) we get an idea that it takes some time to reach a steady state.
Reaching a steady state is defined as when the initial conditions of the state variables of
cycle N are within a certain tolerance of the initial conditions of the state variables of
cycle N вҲ’1.
Figure 4: Mass contents for the first 10 cycles using startup initial conditions
Running this model is also somewhat computationally expensive. A single cycle takes
roughly 3 seconds to complete. If the model must first run 5-6 times before reaching
a steady state, thatвҖҷs 5-6 times more computation time than if better initial conditions
were chosen. In later sections when the model must be run thousands of times, the choice
of initial conditions can make a significant difference in computation time. It is therefore
advantageous to look closer at how parameters influence the steady state, so that initial
conditions closer to this state can be chosen.
The analysis of initial conditions was done by taking the parameter space of interest
and taking a set of randomly chosen points within that parameter space. For each point
the model was simulated until a steady state was reached, and the steady state initial

In order for this model to be successful it must accurately represent what we observe
in the real world. We have observed from the model results that it seems to behave
according to the real world dynamics, and now it is just a matter of parameter tuning

said than done, and at Weel and Sandvig there is a lot of time and energy being put into

5.4
Optimization
5.4.1
Methods
A differential evolution algorithm is used for the optimization problem. Other methods
such as Nelder-Mead looked promising at first, but ultimately proved to be too sensitive
to local minima.
Differential evolution is designed on the idea of survival of the fittest, where the algo-
rithm considers many candidate solutions within the search space, implementing random
mutations in order to give the best chance of finding the global minimum [9]. It is also
good at handling non-differentiable problems such as our model. Though perhaps most
important for our interests, differential evolution can be done in parallel. This means
that we are not required to search over the entire parameter space in one optimization
29
==PAGE:31==
attempt, instead the parameter space can be split up into N different spaces and N dif-
ferent algorithms can search the respective subspaces simultaneously, saving a significant
amount of time - as mentioned earlier our model can be computationally expensive.
Parameters for the algorithm were chosen as population size = 15, mutation range =
(0.5, 1), and recombination = 0.7.

In this section we have introduced the importance of parameter optimization in fitting

6
Application to Weel and Sandvig Model
As more detailed data became available, the opportunity arose to apply the parameter
optimization methods to more realistic numbers and a more realistic model. A version
two model developed by Weel and Sandvig was used for this section.
6.1
Version Two Model
The biggest change in the upgraded model is the introduction of a crevice space around
the piston. The idea is that due to the nature of a pistonвҖҷs contact with the wall, and
the wear and tear over the lifetime of the engine, the cylinders will naturally have very
small pockets of space between the piston and the cylinder wall. Gas is able to find its
way into this space during the compression phase, and this means that the gas stuck in
the crevices will not burn during the combustion phase. Whatever gas is left unburned
at the end of combustion is then released into the environment when the exhaust valve
31
==PAGE:33==
opens. As previously mentioned, methane slip is far worse for the environment than CO2,
and against incoming regulations. If the model is going to fight to minimize the slip of
methane, it must take these crevices into account.
There are some other minor improvements to version two, but the overall dynamics of
the system are relatively unchanged. The full additions to the model will not be detailed
in depth (further interest can be directed to Jensen [2]), but the use of this model will
allow us a more accurate representation of the new data provided.

Analysis
It is important to note that the parameter estimates by Weel and Sandvig were likely
made on a more advanced model, as even the version two model we use here is not the
most up to date at the company. The companyвҖҷs estimates were also likely fit to match
power data, which would explain why their estimates outperform ours in the power data.
However, applying the companyвҖҷs fits to the max pressure, CH4, and CO2 data, we see
that the model is not accurately representing what we see in a real engine (at least with
the version two model).
Applying our optimized parameters, we see that in terms of max pressure the fit is still
a little too high, but a huge improvement from the original estimates. We see another
huge improvement in the CH4 emitted in the exhaust, where our fit actually performs
quite well. In terms of CO2 neither estimate is particularly good, but ours is again a
slight improvement. By far the optimized parameters worst performance is with respect
to power.
There could be several reasons as to why the fits were not better. We should always
be wary that perhaps this model may not be representing the processes occurring in the
engine well enough. However, there are a few things to consider before counting out this
model.
For example, if we look at the optimized values for Tw and Fcvinput (350.4 and 0.1077
respectively) we notice that these are essentially the minimum values allowed. If we recall
35
==PAGE:37==
the lower bounds we set for these two parameters during the optimization process, they
were set at 350 and 0.1 respectively. This would imply that the fit could possibly have
been better had we allowed the parameter values to go lower. We of course deemed lower
values to be unrealistic, but this begs the question that perhaps this behavior is over
correcting for another parameter we have assumed to be true and is not. This is most
likely the case, as we stated ahead of time that there currently just is not enough data
or experimental parameter information, and that such assumptions must be made.
Another reason for the imperfections in the fit could be from the fact that we know some
parameters will be dependent on the power level of the engine. In our optimization we
assumed that our optimized parameters were unchanging across different power levels.
Had we assumed they could, we would have been optimizing over 4 В· 11 = 44 different
parameters instead of just 4, and we simply do not have the data to do this at the
moment.
7
Final Discussion and Conclusions
Four stroke piston engines, common in maritime shipping, are at the center of a push
for greener emissions standards. While the switch to liquid natural gas produces far less
CO2 due to the combustion reaction, if allowed to slip out methane is over 28 times worse
for the environment. That is why research is being done to minimize the methane slip,
while maximizing the efficiency. A deep understanding of the physics of the cylinder,
and chemistry of the reactions is needed in order to create a mathematical description
of the engine that fits real world data. In this thesis we have outlined a derivation of
such a model, piecing together the different phases and their dynamics. We also highlight
the importance of parameter estimation in order to fit our modal to real world data, as
well as gain understanding for parameters that may not be able to be experimentally
verified. Optimization techniques using differential evolution were shown to be effective
at parameter estimation in a proof-of-concept example, and the application showcased
an improved fit to data, though still imperfect.
Despite the imperfection in the optimized fits, there were several bright spots, including
a very good fit for the amount of methane in the exhaust, arguably one of the most
important observables due to the ultimate objective of minimizing methane slip. We have
also through this work developed a very good idea of why the optimization is coming up
short, and what data should be necessary in the future to overcome this.
Through this work we learn that while the methods are promising, there is still a ways to
go before there is enough data to confidently expect realistic results. The hope at Weel
and Sandvig is that most parameters will soon be able to be known experimentally, and
enough data will be available to successfully apply these methods to the parameters left
unknown. However, there are still several things to do in future work in this direction.
One would be to look for improvements in the model, something Weel and Sandvig are
constantly doing. Another would be to perform a deeper analysis into the parameter
space and the effects that changes in parameters have on observables.
Overall, this thesis acts as a step toward more accurate fits modeling four stroke methane
engines, with the ultimate goal of minimizing methane slip without compromising effi-
ciency. The positive results showcased here provide confidence that the tools developed

here will soon be able to be put to effective use in the most up to date models at Weel
and Sandvig.
