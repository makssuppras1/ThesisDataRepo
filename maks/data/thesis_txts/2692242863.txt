Abstract 
MASH Energy ApS is a spin-out from the Technical University of Denmark, which has a focus in 
developing sustainable solution from biomass. In collaboration with MASH Energy ApS the main 
purpose of this master thesis is to develop a Techno-Economic feasibility analysis for sugar cane 
residue as biomass used in district heating and cooling in India.  
The business model is here. 
The plots shown below are the variable parameter that affect the business model, the operational 
time of the machine is calculated by considering that 300 Kg/h of biomass processing in the system 
as ideal 100% efficiency, based on which other variables are also calculated and plotted against the 
payback period (years), maximum/minimum start-up cost (INR) and cost percentage (cost % split up 
for biomass/tax/staff & factory/machine cost during the payback period)  
Assuming Operation of the 300 Kg/h Pyrolysis system with different downtime. Note lower feeding 
rate indicates, higher down time not a smaller machine. Thus the capex remains the same. And the 
payback is accordingly calculated. 
The payback period taken is 0-8 years and operational time is between 8.33 – 150 %. 
The parameters and the variance along with their units are list here: 
1. Sum (Other Operational Expense – Infrastructure & Staff Cost), Variance (1-2.5 % INR) 
2. Biomass Price, Variance (2-5 INR/Kg) 
3. Transportation Cost, Variance (0.5-2.5 INR/Kg) 
4. Biomass input, Variance (25-450 Kg/h) 
5. Moisture content, Variance (10-33 %wt/wt)  
6. HHV, Variance (12-19 MJ/Kg) 
7. Other additional revenues:  
 
Biochar (5-50 INR/Kg) 
 
Carbon Credit (0-15 INR/Kg biochar produced)

Immediate conclusions from the graph plots observation: 
1. Most parameter variable the machine should have at least an uptime of 83.33% which is 
when the machine process 300 Kg/h of biomass for it to have a reasonable payback period 
under 8 years. 
2. Price of biomass has a big influence in the payback, for biomass price at minimum of 2 INR at 
83.33% uptime gives a payback of 6.3 years and maximum of 5 INR under same uptime give 
a payback of 14.1 years which is a significant delay (7.8 years difference). 
3. Price of transportation has a less influence compared to biomass, where the transportation 
price at minimum of 0.5 INR at 83.33% uptime gives a payback of 7.3 years and maximum of 
2.5 INR under same uptime give a payback of 11.9 years which is a difference of 4.6 years. 
4. Moisture content beyond 16 %wt/wt exceed the payback year limit of 8 years when the 
machine uptime is 100%.  
5. HHV after 15 MJ/kg at 100% machine uptime gives a payback within 8 years. 
6. When we have a payback period of 83.33% of 8 years then typically 45% is spend on payback 
of the biomass cost, which is most commonly observed in all the parameters that tell that a 
huge amount of cost is spend only on biomass cost payback during profit so the initial capital 
expense is significant.  
7. Similarly machine payback also has a good share of cost percentage spend on its payback 
from the profits which is 35% when we have a payback period of 83.33% of 8 years. 
==PAGE:3==
8. The taxes and (Infrastructure & Staff Cost) have a very less significant percentage of cost 
spend during the payback. 
9. The biomass price and capital expenditure are the main two expenses when we look at the 
payback percentages. 
10. Biochar and Carbon credit have a really high positive influence in the payback period,  
11. Biochar at minimum of 5 INR/kg at 83.33% uptime gives a payback of 6.7 year and at 
maximum of 40 INR/kg gives 3 years when revenue from cooling is added and kept at a 
constant. 
12. Carbon credit at minimum of 1 INR/kg at 83.33% uptime gives a payback of 7.7 year and at 
maximum of 15 INR/kg gives 5 years when revenue from cooling is added and kept at a 
constant.
