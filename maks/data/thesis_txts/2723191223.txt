Abstract
This thesis investigates the dynamics of a vertical cylinder in oscillatory flow, with a
particular focus on the distributed wave load over the depth. The study combines
numerical simulations and experimental measurements to gain insights into the physics
of the problem and develop models for predicting the behaviour of the system.
In first part of the thesis, is a experimental analysis based on data provided from the
data report "Two directional force measurements on a sectional cylinder subjected to
regular and irregular waves" by Simone de Lemos (2020) [1]. The main focus is to find
and identity the Morison coefficients, for the inertia(CM) and the drag(CD). This is
done by analytic determine the surface elevation (η), by introducing a reflected wave.
By implementing this, a new η is found, this is used to calculated the velocity(U) and
the acceleration (∂U). The data provides the measured in-line wave load (Fin) on the
cylinder, and from the these 3 values, the 2 Morison coefficients are determined. The
calculated values will be compared to the values found by not introducing the reflected
wave, and know theory. The data also provide the measured transverse wave load which
will be analysed using the root mean squared Transverse force coefficients CTrms. The
effect of orbital motion is like wise being considered.
The second part of the thesis presents a numerical model preformed in the software
OpenFoam. The model takes into account the effects of the incoming and reflected
waves and predicts the motion and forces of the cylinder. The model is validated against
experimental data as it provides η, U, and the Fin.
Overall, the thesis contributes to the understanding of the dynamics of a vertical cylinder
in waves and provides useful tools for predicting the behaviour of the system.

Introduction
1
The introduction of this master thesis presents an overview of the research on wave
load distribution over the depth of a submerged cylinder, based on experimental data
and numerical analyses. The objective of this project is to investigate the wave load
distribution over the depth in regular waves, and from this estimate the force coefficients
for inertia (CM) and drag (CD) for the Morison’s force model. OpenFOAM is used to
analyse the distributed load and the waves behaviour, on the structure and compare to
experimental results.
Wave-induced forces on submerged structures are a complex phenomenon that have been
widely studied in the field of coastal and offshore engineering. The accurate prediction
of these forces is essential for the design and analysis of structures such as offshore
platforms, breakwaters, and coastal protection systems. Understanding the distribution
of wave loads over the depth of a submerged structure is important for the design of the
structure and the assessment of its stability and durability.
In this thesis a Wave reflected analysis (WRA) a computational method used to simulate
the behaviour of waves near structures. This method is a significant due to the need
to understand and predict the impact of waves on offshore structures such as oil rigs
and wind turbines. By modelling the reflection and diffraction of waves, it is possible to
obtain accurate predictions of wave heights, velocity, acceleration, wave loads, and other
important parameters.
One of the key advantages of reflected wave analysis is that it can be used to study a
wide range of different wave conditions, including regular, irregular, and extreme waves.
This makes it a valuable tool for both researchers and industry professionals who need to
ensure the safety and reliability of offshore structures in a variety of different operating
conditions. In this thesis only the regular waves have been analysed
Overall, reflected wave analysis is a powerful tool for studying the behaviour of ocean
waves and their impact on offshore structures. By combining laboratory experiments
with numerical simulations, it is possible to obtain a more complete understanding of
wave behaviour and design safer and more efficient offshore structures.

1.1 Purpose
The aim of this thesis is to estimate the values of CM and CD from experimental data
obtained from de Lemos (2020)[1]. This is necessary to gain a better understanding of
the distributed load, and potentially reduce these coefficients, resulting in a smaller wave
load distribution. When the structure is subjected to a smaller force, the design can be
made smaller, leading to a reduction in the amount of materials needed and hence, cost
savings. By estimating these coefficients accurately, it is possible to improve the safety
and efficiency of offshore structures, which is of great importance in the field of offshore
engineering.
1.2 Structure of the thesis
The thesis is structured into several sections, starting with the methodology that is used to
analyse the data. The method section includes a detailed explanation of the experimental
and numerical setup, as well as the data acquisition and analysis techniques.
The results section is divided into two parts: the analysis of experimental data and
the analysis of numerical data. The analysis of experimental data includes the results
obtained from the reflected wave analysis, the velocity/acceleration, the in-line force, and
the calculation of CM and CD coefficients. On the other hand, the analysis of numerical
data involves the results obtained from the reflected wave analysis, the velocity, the
in-line force, and the calculation of CM and CD coefficients.
The third part of the results section involves a comparison of the two analyses. The com-
parison is based on the differences and similarities found between the results obtained
from the experimental and numerical data analysis.
The discussion section interprets the results obtained in the previous sections and their
implications for the field. The discussion includes an explanation of the major find-
ings and their potential impacts, as well as a comparison of the results with existing
literature.
The final section of the thesis is the conclusion, which provides a summary of the major
findings of the research. The conclusion also discusses the limitations of the study and
suggests potential directions for future research in the field.

2.1 Linear waves theory
Linear wave theory is the most basic and widely used theory for describing the properties
and behaviour of ocean waves. The theory assumes that the waves are small compared
to the depth of the ocean, and that their amplitude and wavelength do not change
significantly as they propagate. The theory is used to describe various wave properties
such as wave speed, wavelength, period, and amplitude, and is based on a number of
fundamental principles.

2.2 Surface Elevation in Waves
In ocean engineering, the surface elevation of waves is an important parameter for
understanding the characteristics of ocean waves and their impact on structures such
as offshore platforms and ships. The surface elevation of water waves can be described
using mathematical models such as linear wave theory or higher order theories. In
general, the surface elevation of a wave can be defined as the vertical displacement of
the water surface relative to a reference level.
The surface elevation of waved can be described using the linear wave theory. According
to this theory, the surface elevation can be written as:
η(x, t) = a cos(kx −ωt + ϕ)
(2.7)
where η is the surface elevation, a is the wave amplitude, k is the wave number, ω is the
angular frequency, x is the horizontal distance, t is time, and ϕ is the phase angle. The
wave number and angular frequency are related by the dispersion relation:
ω2 = gk
(2.8)
where g is the acceleration due to gravity. For waves in deep water, the wavelength is
much greater than the water depth, and the wave motion is essentially two-dimensional.
In summary, the surface elevation of water waves is an important parameter for under-
standing the characteristics of ocean waves and their impact on structures. The surface
elevation can be described using mathematical models such as linear wave theory or
higher order theories.
2.3 Standing wave
Standing waves are a type of wave that occurs when two waves of the same frequency
and amplitude traveling in opposite directions interfere with each other. The resulting
wave pattern appears to be stationary, with certain points of the wave oscillating between
maximum and minimum amplitudes.
In fluid mechanics, standing waves can be created in a wave flume, which is a long,
narrow channel that is used to study the behaviour of water waves. In a wave flume,
standing waves can be generated by oscillating a wave paddle at one end of the flume,

2.6 Morison equation
The Morrison equation is a mathematical model used to predict the hydrodynamic forces
on offshore structures, such as floating platforms, under the influence of ocean waves. It
is based on the principle of linear potential theory and is valid for small-amplitude waves
(Morrison, F. A. (1976)[16].)

The hydrodynamic force on a structure can be decomposed into a steady (quasi-static)
force and an unsteady (dynamic) force. The steady force is due to the pressure distri-
bution around the structure, while the unsteady force is due to the added mass, added
damping, and added mass effects of the surrounding fluid.
The Morrison equation can be used to calculate the hydrodynamic forces on a submerged
body, such as a cylinder, subject to waves. The equation takes the form:
F = 1
2ρCDDU2 + ρCMAU′ + ρAU′
(2.15)
where F is the total hydrodynamic force, D is the diameter of the cylinder, U is the
relative velocity between the cylinder and the surrounding fluid, U′ is the derivative of
U with respect to time, ρ is the density of the fluid, CD is the drag coefficient, Cm is the
added mass coefficient, and A is the reference area.

2.9 Orbital motion
Orbital motion is the motion of a fluid particle in a circular or elliptical path, perpendicular
to the direction of wave propagation, caused by the passage of a wave. In the case of
ocean waves, the particles of water move in circular orbits as the wave passes through
them. The size of the orbital motion decreases with depth. The orbital motion can be
studied by The ellipticity (E) found by:
E = Vm
Um
(2.19)
Where Vm is the max vertical velocity, and Um is the max horizontal velocity. E vary
between 0 and 1, where 0 is 0 the particle moves in a straight line motion, and 1 the
particle moves in a orbital motion. The effect of the orbital motion is a discussed mater,
where Stansby et. al. (1983) [18] shows that E has no influence on the inline-force,
where Sarpkaya (1976) [19] reports a decrease in the total force when a high E is
obtained.

2.10 Equations in OpenFoam
OpenFOAM is a widely used open-source computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software
package that is capable of simulating the behaviour of waves. The software uses fi-
nite volume methods to discrete the equations of fluid motion, which are then solved
numerically.

including the design of coastal structures, the study of ocean currents and tides, and the
development of wave energy technologies.
When a wave meets a boundary, such as a wall, the wave can be reflected back into the
medium from which it came. The reflected wave can interfere with the incoming wave,
and this interference can cause changes in the wave velocity.
In the case when a wave meets a wall or other obstacle, some of the wave’s energy
is reflected back towards the source of the wave, while the remainder of the energy
continues to propagate forward. The reflected wave can cause a change in the wave

nature of the boundary with which it interacts. In general, the presence of a reflecting
boundary can cause the wave to slow down or speed up, change direction, or experience

3.2.1 OpenFoam
OpenFOAM solves the Navier-Stokes equations for fluid flow in a numerical way using
finite volume method. To generate the surface elevation and velocity, OpenFOAM uses
numerical algorithms to solve the continuity equation and momentum equations. The
surface elevation is calculated from the vertical velocity component at the surface, which
is set to zero. The velocity is calculated from the solution of the momentum equations,
which takes into account the pressure and viscous forces acting on the fluid.
The force component on the object is calculated using the pressure and viscous forces
on the object. The pressure force is calculated using the pressure coefficient and the
reference pressure. The viscous force is calculated using the velocity gradients near the
object.
The equations used in OpenFOAM to solve these problems are the continuity equation,
which describes the conservation of mass, and the Navier-Stokes equations, which
describe the conservation of momentum. The continuity equation is:
∂ρ
∂t + ∇· (ρu) = 0
(3.13)
where ρ is the fluid density, u is the fluid velocity, and t is time.
The Navier-Stokes equations are:
∂(ρu)
∂t
+ ∇· (ρuu) = −∇p + ∇· τ
(3.14)
where p is the pressure, and τ is the viscous stress tensor.
The pressure and viscous forces on the object are then calculated using the following
equations:
Fp = 1
2ρU2CpA
(3.15)
Fv = ρUAdU
dx
(3.16)

In summary, OpenFOAM solves the Navier-Stokes equations and the continuity equation
using numerical algorithms to generate the surface elevation, velocity, and force compo-
nent, which are calculated from pressure and viscous forces acting on the fluid and the
object.

Discussion
5
In this study, the implementation of using a modelled surface elevation from a reflected
wave analysis, could be a factor to reduce the inertia coefficients CM for a cylinder in
oscillatory flow according to Det Norske Veritas (DNV)[10]. The current suggested CM
can be seen in figure 5.1.
To arrive at these results, an analysis of experimental data was done to calculate the
surface elevation, the velocity, and the acceleration of the flow on the the cylinder in
oscillatory flow. This allowed us to explore a range of scenarios and make predictions
about the behaviour of the cylinder. By comparing the results of the experimental data,
to the simulated data we were able to validate our approach and gain confidence in our
findings.
It was found that the surface elevation of the fluid was a particularly important factor,
and that our approach of using a modelled surface elevation from a reflected wave
analysis was effective at reducing the inertia coefficients. This could lead to a smaller
estimation of the load distribution
Figure 5.1.: Suggested inertia coefficient values from DNV [10]
In Figure 5.2 We see that the estimated CM values is smaller than the suggested values
from DNV [10].

1-2 etc.
Overall, the results suggest that the use of a modelled surface elevation from a reflected
wave analysis may be a valuable tool for engineers working in the field of ocean engineer-
ing. By reducing the inertia coefficients for cylinders in oscillatory flow the estimation of
the wave load force could become smaller, and there reducing material in the construc-
tions of off-shore construction. The reduced CM coefficient can be seen in Figure 5.2
where it is compared to the suggested CM from DNV.
The simulated values for CM can be said to be in the tolerated interval from 1.7-2.1, but
the same decrease of the coefficient towards KC=8, could not be found as seen in figure

simulation. The reason that there where not more simulation was due to computational
time, a solution on this problem could have been to optimise the grid, so it was not that
heavy to run this would have made the simulation shorter and more could have been
carried out.
Another possible reason to not see the same decrease could be the opposite of the
problem described above, where the mesh is not fine enough. By making the mesh
finer the computational time will increase, but more accurate result could be found this
way. In this thesis a short sensitivity analysis was carried out for the case for T=1.2 and
H=0.110m, to see the change in results, based on the simulation. A finer mesh was

Conclusion
6
In this study, the various aspects related to the hydrodynamics of a vertical cylinder in
waves was investigated, through experimental data and numerical simulations. The
effect of the reflected wave on the cylinder,the inertia coefficient, and the wave load
distribution has been explored. In addition, an examination of the impact of orbital
motion on the cylinder, and the transverse force coefficient has been carried out.
The effect of using the model based ηmod including the reflected wave, from the equation
3.1, seem to have a positive impact when estimate the following hydrodynamic values:
• The calculation of the velocity based on both the ηmod and the simulated η from
numerical data, shows that a better fit i made with the model based ηmod as shown
in Figure 4.15b, where they are compared to the actual obtained velocity from the
simulation.
• The wave load distribution in Figure 4.8, shows a clear improvement of estimation
of the in-line force acting on the cylinder. This gives confidence in the calculation
of the coefficients on the background of the model based ηmod.
Based on these findings on model for η including the reflected wave, we conclude that
that the model based ηmod could be a effective tool for estimation of the force coefficient,
which lead to a god estimation of the wave load distribution.
• The Figure 4.13 shows that the deviation of the results when applying the calculated
ηmod are reduced, from the calculation of the measured η.
• Figure 4.19b and Figure 4.20b shows a good correlation between the calculated
CM from ηmod, and the simulated CM, this provides even more confidence in the
model based ηmod.
• From Figure 4.10c, a clear tendency for the inertia coefficient CM to start at ≈2 at
small KC-numbers<1 then the tendency decreases towards KC=8 obtaning a value
of ≈1.6
• All of the above leads leads to model dependent of the KC-number to suggest
inertia coefficient this is a reduction CM suggested by DNV as shown the figure5.2.
The new suggested CM when KC > 4 have a approximation of a reduction on 0.2.
50
==PAGE:59==
The cause of the decrease of the inertia coefficient was seek out by estimating the orbital
motion and the ellipsicity of the motion. This resulted in a conclusion that there where
no effect of this on the CM coefficient, this is shown in Figure 4.14
The wave load distribution was evaluated, and the results of this was that when applying
bigger waves, the distribution in the cell near the bed had a slightly bigger force acting
on it than the cell above as seen in Figure 5.4 A reason for this could be as explained in
discussion 5.1 the velocity as seen the Figure 5.5.
The transverse wave force was analysed by finding the root mean squared transverse
force coefficient CT rms, this was compared to the different wave heights as shown in
Figure ??. This shows that when the wave height increases the CTrms drops towards
zero.
Overall, the study provides valuable insights into the hydrodynamics of vertical cylinders
in waves, and the findings could have a practical applications in the design of offshore
structures.
