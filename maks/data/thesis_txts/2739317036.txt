Abstract
In this study we investigate various versions of both autonomous and non-autonomous quantum thermal
machines, and their capability to generate steady-state entanglement. The aim of this study is two-fold.
Firstly, to modify these systems such that the total number of physical systems present in the thermal
machine is reduced while retaining as high a measure of entanglement as possible. Secondly, to investigate
whether or not thermal machines which exhibit none or minimal measures of entanglement, can be modified
such that there is a higher measure of entanglement present. Using numerical and analytical methods,
master equations for these open quantum systems are formulated, following which they are solved in
both the steady-state and transient regimes. With the steady state density matrices found, a measure of
entanglement in these systems is optimized over for the numerous coupling parameters in the system, both
inside the local subsystems, and the ones coupling the local systems to the thermal reservoirs.
It is found that for some quantum thermal machines, it indeed is possible to simultaneously reduce the
number of physical subsystems and maintain a large measure of entanglement. This however comes at
the cost of changing the thermal machine from being autonomous, to non-autonomous. For other thermal
machines, it is possible to go from no entanglement to some small measure, by the same method. The
method involves modifying operators that constitute Hamiltonians and the dissipative terms in master
equations, by applying a unitary to them such that the spin-axis are oriented by a variable angle.

Introduction
Entanglement is a quantum physical phenomenon which has no classical analogue, and can be described
as non-classical correlations between physical subsystems [1, 2]. It is a crucial property required within
many topics inside quantum information theory, such as dense coding [3], teleportation [4] and quantum
key distribution [2]. The generation of quantum states which exhibit these non-classical correlations has
been investigated by using a plethora of systems, such as atoms in cavities [5] and spin qubits [6]. It has
been shown that the generation of entangled states can also be achieved via quantum thermal machines
(QTM), and these machines will be the main focus of this dissertation.
A QTM is a device which is made up of some local system, which could be a single or collection of
physical subsystems, to which there are at least two reservoirs coupled. These reservoirs are to take
separate temperatures, such that a net heat current can pass through the local system, and this heat current
acts as a source of free energy. The reservoirs considered here are macroscopic physical systems and
therefore have continuous energy spectrums. Since the local subsystems are connected to macroscopic
reservoirs, the evolution of the systems are considered to be governed by open system dynamics. The
physical subsystems which constitute the local system in the QTM here are quantized. If the interactions
between the local system and reservoirs are incoherent, and there is no external drive acting upon the
system, the device is considered autonomous. It has indeed been shown, that autonomous QTMs can
generate entanglement in the steady state regime [7], and it is from this work that the present dissertation
takes its jumping point.
In this work, we study various types of quantum thermal machines which have already shown promising
measures of entanglement. Systems which exhibit these high values of entanglement often consist of
ancillary subsystems which do not themselves exhibit entanglement, however they assist in the overall
system which contains subsystems that are entangled. Here, the goal is to reduce the number of total
subsystems, but retain as much measure of entanglement as possible.
Chapter 3 of the present thesis reviews theory concerning the closed and open dynamics of quantum
systems, and formulates the main workhorse of this dissertation, namely the quantum master equation. The
regimes in which these master equations are valid will be explained, and these restrict the bounds under
which the system can be realised. In chapter 4, the method by which these master equations are solved
will be reviewed, namely that the equation will be vectorized under the Choi-Jamiolkowski isomorphism,
such that the equation reduces to being a problem of finding the null space of a matrix equation, in the
steady-state regime. The transient case for solving the master equation is also touched upon. Chapter 5
presents the different ways in which entanglement will be quantified throughout the dissertation. Equations
are covered which calculate concurrence between two physical systems, and some of the difficulties
regarding entanglement between more than two physical subsystems will be highlighted, while a general
equation for quantifying said entanglement is presented.
Chapter 6 collects the previous chapters and applies the theory and method to a simple example of an
autonommous quantum thermal machine. The dynamics of the system is reviewed via exploring the
3
==PAGE:5==
fidelities of various states in the transient regime under time evolution. Chapter 7 introduces the first QTM
to be investigated, where the number of qubits in the system will be reduced while attempting to maintain
entanglement. The main modification to the systems presented in this dissertation is employed here, which
involves what is referred to as "rotating the qubit", and centers around rotating the operators which work
on parts of the subsystems. Chapters 8 and 9 investigate other quantum thermal machines, and how the
entanglement between their subsystems depend on this rotation of operators. These machines specifically
also exhibit entanglement between three qubits, and because there are different ways entanglement between
subsystems in this case can manifest, each of these are explored for these systems. Finally, chapter 10
discusses the main results obtained in this dissertation, and it reflects upon the fact that the operation of
rotating the qubit alters the system under observation such that it is no longer autonomous.
It must be stated that some of the results presented in this paper, due to limitations in parameter optimization
calculations, are not as accurate as we would like, and that it is mainly the tendencies of the result that
should be focused on.

Quantum Systems
Generally, when discussing quantum systems, we distinguish between two different types of evolution:
closed and open system dynamics. In a closed system, the dynamics of the system can be derived from the
Hamiltonian and can be fully described by a unitary time evolution described by the Schrödinger equation.
In a more realistic setting, quantum mechanical systems interact with the world around them and can
experience dissipation into the outside world. In general, the dynamics of open quantum systems make it
such that the evolution of the quantum system without the environment cannot be described by a unitary
time evolution as in closed systems, and one must instead formulate the dynamics by using an equation
generally referred to as a quantum master equation. The reason for this is outside the regular dynamics of
the systems density operator, one needs to account for dissipative terms in the equations of motion.
One can further distinguish open quantum systems into systems with local and global master equations.
The difference being that for a global master equation, one couples the environment to each subsystem
covered by the Hamiltonian, and for a local master equation one couples the environment to a smaller
subset of the systems. Here, the focus is on local quantum master equations. In the following, the dynamics
of closed quantum systems is reviewed as a jumping off point to describe the dynamics of open quantum
systems, the goal of which is to introduce the quantum master equations that will be used later on.

At this point we have described how to solve a master equation of the Lindblad type both in the steady

Discussion
So far we have implemented a method which we have denoted "rotating the qubit" for a variety of physical
systems, where in some cases it has been beneficial, and other cases detrimental with respect to increasing
a measure of entanglement between two or several qubits. However, even though the method has seen an
increase in entanglement for these systems, some insights have been gained with regards to the machine
that fundamentally alters the dynamics, which necessitate further work. In the following, this point and a
few others regarding the results of the previous chapters will be discussed.
10.1
Non-autonomous QTM
One result of employing the method of rotating the qubit, has been that the free Hamiltonian and interaction
Hamiltonian of the systems no longer commute. A consequence of this, is that in order for the unitary
acting upon the whole system to be energy-preserving, there needs to be some time-dependency on the
full Hamiltonian, making the device non-autonomous. Because the devices are no longer autonomous, an
external source of energy is required to drive the system. The systems which would be required to drive
the QTMs with rotated qubits have not been investigated, and are not expected to be thermodynamically
free. On the contrary, because the local systems investigated here are microscopic, the amount of energy
deposited into these external devices may be substantial [21]. Furthermore, the amount of energy required
to drive the QTM has not been investigated here, so further investigation into such driving sources could
be a way of realising systems such as the ones presented here.
For the three qubit chain model presented in chapter 9, it was found that rotating the qubit coupled to the
hot reservoir was responsible for the model exhibiting entanglement across bipartitions R|C,H and C,R|H.
During the investigations of this system, no entanglement was found across any bipartition in the case
where no rotations were performed. Crucially, however, it is stated now that it has not been shown that
entanglement can not be present in such a model with no rotations. However, changing the system from
an autonomous model to a non-autonomous model has shown that entanglement can be exhibited. While
violation of the witness was shown, this entanglement has not been directly linked to an improvement
in any physical process. Additionally, while a link was drawn between entanglement measures and heat
currents for similar models with 2 qubits instead of 3, no such link has been established here. In fact, the
net heat current in this system has remained zero, even in the presence of entanglement. Further reasearch
into this model could include adding a third thermal reservoir to the middle qubit, in the same vein as [19].
For the three qubit chain model, the coupling parameter values used when calculating the witnesses are
not values found strictly by some optimization process. When attempting an optimization process, the
calculations would never conclude, and sometimes run into systems of equations which were not solvable.
Therefore, the values inserted were ones found by trial and error with a jumping off point of similar values

for the two qubit model, and then the maximum values for the witnesses were sought. This is to accentuate
the fact that the found witnesses can by no means be asserted to be the maximal ones for the systems. If
one has more resources available to them such that the calculations can conclude, it may be possible to
attain a higher quantity of entanglement.
For the model presented in chapter 7, specifically the one with 4 qubits present, rotation of the qubits has
introduced entanglement between the two qubits, and in fact near maximal entanglement is present in
this case, up from zero entanglement when no rotation is implemented. This implies that there may be
an advantage to making some quantum thermal machines non-autonomous. In addition to entanglement
generation, it has also been shown that the entanglement makes rectification possible, meaning that the spin
current is blocked in one bias but flows in the other, meaning it can act as a type of thermal diode. However,
the external drivers that would be required to operate a system of this type has not been investigated in this
dissertation, so further research in this direction could shed more light on the feasibility of such a system.

Conclusion
For the purpose of generating entanglement in quantum thermal machines, several versions of such a
device has been investigated numerically.
For the 6 qubit chain which demonstrated maximal entanglement between two qubits in the system, it
was found that it was possible to remove a qubit from the chain, and still retain the maximal measure of
concurrence between the entangled qubits. Removing a second qubit from this device resulted in zero
entanglement present in the system. However, we proposed a modification to operators in the system
Hamiltonian, the result of which saw a return of a near maximal concurrence value, namely 0.995. This
value, however was at the expense of altering the temperature values of the system reservoirs such that
they were both close to zero. If the temperatures were kept the same as in the original model, the maximal
concurrence achieved by rotating qubits is 0.982. In this case it it also found that the rectification of this
system is sharply peaked around the maximal concurrence, however it is several orders of magnitude lower
than the original 6 qubit model. It was also found, that for fixed angle of rotation, the concurrence in the
system with a rotated interface qubit was dependent on the interaction strength parameter between the two
interface qubits.
For the three qubit model investigated in chapter 8, it was discovered that a rotation of the qubit coupled to
the cold reservoir could increase a measure of concurrence along two bipartitions of the system, namely
C|R,H and C,R|H. With parameters from the original paper [19], the concurrence values were found to
peak with a rotation of 0.88rad, and for the C|R,H partition the peak concurrence value was 0.00584,
while for the other bipartition the value was 0.00548. Additionally, when optimizing for these specific
bipartitions, even higher values for concurrence were found. Specifically for the bipartition C|R,H, peak
concurrence was found to be 0.01223, while for the other bipartition the value was 0.01010.
The system presented in chapter 6 was also investigated, to see if an increase in concurrence could be
achieved by this rotation of the qubits. It was found, that rotating either or both qubits would see no
increase in concurrence as a function of rotation angles, and that the concurrence would quickly go down
to zero with varying angles. This model was then extended to consist of three chains, each endpoint
connected to a thermal reservoir. Initially, without a rotation of any of the qubits, no concurrence along
any of the bipartitions was found. However, when the qubit coupled to the hot reservoir was rotated, it
was found that small measures of entanglement were found along the bipartitions {R|C,H} and {C,R|H},
with peak values along both of these bipartitions being 0.000025.
A fault present in the calculations for the 2 and 3 qubit chains, is that when optimizing over the space of
coupling parameters, one would run into unsolvable equations. Even sometimes, the calculations would
take an intractable amount of time, and not be able to be solved with the resources present, so values
for witnesses in these cases have often been found by trial and error. Specifically, inserting values for
these coupling parameters into the witness inequalities and manually seeing when this value would be the
highest. Therefore, we cannot state whether or not the values found in this dissertation are the maximum
possible given the parameter value space, satisfying the weak coupling regime.
