Summary
In this report we outline the procedure followed for creating a tool for modeling the life
cycle of pension savings account following the structure deﬁned contribution plan in Den-
mark. The tool serves as an educator for ﬁnancial planning as well as and an early anomaly
detection tool for accounts that behave abnormally when compared to their expected be-
havior. Overall it was found when it comes to the consumption curve drop the more money
you earn prior to retirement the more impact you will have in the transition. The major
contributor for balancing consumption throughout ones life is the contribution rate it signif-
icantly impacts both accumulation as well as deculmulation period. Inﬂation is an issue that
inﬂuences the results signiﬁcantly, and combating it can only be done by incorporating more
risk in your portfolio. Finally undertaking more risk is beneﬁcial to the lower class compared
to the other two as a major portion of their funds arise from the additional pension beneﬁt
which is risk free. As for the predictive capabilities of our model, it shows promise but the
data was inconclusive as the dataset could not be clearly separated into the representative
classes, especially the lower class. As for the other two classes it could be used suﬃciently
but it is not yet complete.

Introduction
It comes as no surprise that a considerable proportion of individuals throughout the
world ﬁnd their pension savings to be insuﬃcient for providing a prosperous elderly life
due to inadequate care during the saving period, low ﬁnancial literacy or due to the fact
that understanding tax and pension regulation is tedious and relatively complicated for an
individual to study suﬃciently [1].
Nevertheless, getting to know the basics and having
at least a surface level of understanding of how retirement planning functions, can have a
major eﬀect in the long term, rendering the later years of one’s life them most enjoyable and
carefree.
The scope of this thesis is to provide a tool with which an individual can build a
better understanding of the way a pension account grows during accumulation period and
how pensioners are receiving back their money during their retirement years. The study is
considering only deﬁned contribution plan type pension as these types of pensions require the
owner to make educated ﬁnancial decision to achieve higher payouts in the future. Besides
the growth and decumulation, the tool will act as an educator for an individual as it will
help them with understanding hard to grasp topics such as the risk that they are willing to
undertake, the notion and eﬀect of inﬂation in their life and standard of living and ﬁnally
how planning ahead will aﬀect their disposable income not just for the time being but further
into the future.
The tool was developed and examined using cases and tax regulation falling under the
umbrella of Denmark since due to the country’s pension system’s structure and regulation
almost everyone in the country has a pension savings account that falls under the deﬁned
contribution plan category. Note that although Denmark is put under the microscope, in
reality there has been a major shift in the preference of the deﬁned contribution plans over
deﬁned beneﬁt plans and numerous pension funds and countries are now operating utilizing
the main body of this schemes either in a major or minor degree[2]. Consequently, this
framework can be utilized under many diﬀerent pension policies that follow or contain the
deﬁned contribution plan scheme and can save millions if not billions of dollars around the

spending with varying contributions and payouts. A prime example of this problem is Greece,
which follows a pension system that is fully funded from taxpayers’ money, and it is handed

1.1.2
Structure of the Danish Pension System
The Danish pension system consist of three major parts rendering it a combination of a
state pension and occupational pension schemes and ﬁnally private pension. In this section
we will examine the structure of the Danish pension system, outlining the three pillars and
clearly deﬁning their goals[4][5][6].
First Pillar
The ﬁrst pillar is designed to provide the pensioners with the bare minimum amount of
income during retirement. This part of the pension is ﬁnanced through taxes that Danish
citizens have being paying throughout their life in a pay as you go manner.
It can be
broken down into three major parts, each given out to pensioners if they adequately satisfy
predetermined requirements.

The ﬁrst segment is called public old pension and to be eligible to receive this money
an individual must have spent at least 3 years residing in Denmark between the age of 15
and the retirement age for Danish and EU citizens. Note that in the case of people not
coming from said regions the criteria change to 10 years, of which at least 5 must be before
the retirement age. Furthermore, to receive the full beneﬁt one must have lived in Denmark
for at least 40 years during that period while percentages are given to the people that fall
into that range according to rules.
The second type of income that falls under the umbrella of the ﬁrst pillar is the Danish
Labour Market Supplementary Pension or ATP for short. ATP is a statutory, fully funded
collective insurance based deﬁned contribution scheme. Since the ATP is a contribution
based scheme the payment is dependent on the contributions the pensioner have made during
their time in the active workforce. The contributions are ﬁxed, meaning that the payments
are not a percentage of the individuals monthly income. In this scheme the employer is
also required to provide contributions with a 2/3 to 1/3 split between the employer and the
employee.
Concluding the ﬁrst pillar, the third section is the pension beneﬁt provided to individuals
with disabilities. In order to receive the full pension beneﬁt an individual must have spent
at least 4/5 of their life between the ages of 15 and retirement in Denmark. If otherwise
they would receive a proportion. A person can take this pension as long as they are over 40
unless they are completely unable to work.
Second Pillar
The second pillar is considered to be the backbone of the Danish pension system as it is
speciﬁcally design so that Danish citizens themselves are saving up for their future providing
a relief for the Danish economy. As described in the section above, in contrast with countries
like Greece who have a pension system completely relying on the government to provide for
its people, when faced with numerous problems in times of unpredictable economic turmoil
Denmark ensures that its population will manage to survive with the minimal potential loses
to their quality of life.

Modelling of a pension’s account life cycle
To suﬃciently model the pension account growth, simply assuming that funds ﬂow into
an account and receive some interest is a naive approach that is not reﬂecting of reality
that could potentially lead into misrepresentation of key concerns leading to wrong decision
making. Parameters such as current income, inﬂation, risk appetite, current tax laws, current
governmental beneﬁts personal preference and many more can aﬀect the growth of a pension.
Just examining a number of simple examples can illustrate why diﬀerent people may choose
diﬀerent paths for their pension savings, besides if it were that simple, we would all have
one plan to follow and it would be obsolete. Imagining a person who wishes to invest their
money in real estate for example buying up land and housing that can later rent to produce
funds a pension account might not seem quite as important to them as they would prefer to
take a risk now by investing their current funds in something that could yield higher returns
than a 4% yearly return. In another example one might be having a surplus of funds after all
their obligations are met meaning that their consumption is less than what they are earning
thus saving more in their pension account might sounds more attractive. Finally, a person
who is not familiar with notion of inﬂation and risk might put their fund in a low risk low
reward bracket thinking that if they save 3 million DKK they will be set for the next 20 years
of their life but they cannot associate inﬂation to that 3 million leaving them overexposed
in the future. Our purpose in this section is to outline the major factors that are proven
crucial when it comes to saving up in a suﬃcient way that insures a well being for a stable
retirement life.

2.4
Modelling risk and returns
In our tool, modeling the risk and return of our portfolio is crucial because it directly
impacts future planning for a pension account. The risk refers to the potential variability or
uncertainty in investment returns, while return represents the gains or losses on investments
over a speciﬁc period. As individuals plan for their retirement, understanding the risk and
return characteristics of their portfolio is essential for making informed decisions about asset
allocation and investment strategies.
The Monte Carlo method is particularly useful in modeling stock market outcomes
because stock prices are inherently unpredictable and subject to random ﬂuctuations and it
has been used in other studies such as [11, 12]. In our case, we can use the method to generate
a large number of possible future scenarios for returns of our portfolio based on diﬀerent risk
to reward brackets associated with pension savings account. By analyzing the outcomes of
these scenarios, we can obtain estimates of important parameters, such as expected returns
and risks, that are necessary for making investment decisions as mean values and variance
is clearly highlighted under diﬀerent circumstances. Overall, the Monte Carlo method can
help us make more informed investment decisions by providing a rigorous and probabilistic
approach to modeling complex and uncertain systems like the stock market.
When using the Monte Carlo method to model stock market outcomes, several assump-
tions must be made that can impact the accuracy of the results. One of the main assumptions
is that returns of our portfolio follow a particular statistical distribution, such as a normal
distribution or a log-normal distribution. However, in reality, these returns may not follow a
precise mathematical model, and may be aﬀected by unpredictable events such as economic
crises, natural disasters, or geopolitical tensions.
Moreover, the Monte Carlo method assumes that the simulated scenarios are indepen-
dent and identically distributed. However, in reality, market conditions can be inﬂuenced by
various factors such as macroeconomic events, news, and sentiment, which can lead to corre-

lated outcomes. This can aﬀect the accuracy of risk estimates and lead to underestimation

Finally, the Monte Carlo method is computationally intensive, and generating a large

In addition, the accuracy of the results can be aﬀected by the quality of the models and

Summing up, while the Monte Carlo method can provide valuable insights into stock
market outcomes, it is important to recognize its limitations and potential sources of er-

decisions.
For the purposes of our simulations on the distribution component, we have chosen to
use the normal distribution to model the returns of our portfolio. This assumption has both
beneﬁts and shortcomings.
One major beneﬁt of using the normal distribution is its simplicity and ease of imple-
mentation. It is a widely known and understood distribution, making it convenient to work
with in mathematical and statistical calculations. The normal distribution is characterized
by its bell-shaped curve, which allows for straightforward calculations of mean, standard de-
viation, and other statistical measures. This simplicity makes it easier to generate random
samples and conduct simulations using the Monte Carlo method.
Another advantage is that the normal distribution assumes that returns are symmetri-
cally distributed around the mean, implying that positive and negative returns are equally
likely.
This assumption can be reasonable for some assets or portfolios over short time
periods, as market eﬃciency and absence of signiﬁcant market shocks may support such
symmetry.
However, there are several shortcomings to consider when assuming a normal distri-
bution for portfolio returns. First, the assumption implies that extreme events or outliers,
such as market crashes or large gains, occur with very low probabilities. In reality, ﬁnancial
markets can experience infrequent but signiﬁcant events, known as fat-tailed or heavy-tailed
distributions, which are not accurately captured by the normal distribution. These extreme

events can have a signiﬁcant impact on portfolio returns and risk.
Additionally, the normal distribution assumes that returns are independent and iden-
tically distributed (i.i.d.), meaning that the returns of one period do not aﬀect the returns
of subsequent periods. However, in reality, ﬁnancial markets often exhibit correlation and
volatility clustering, where periods of high volatility or positive/negative returns tend to
persist. Ignoring these dependencies can lead to inaccurate risk and return estimates.
Lastly, the normal distribution assumes that returns have ﬁnite variance, meaning that
extreme events are bounded. However, empirical evidence suggests that ﬁnancial markets
exhibit volatility clustering, where periods of high volatility tend to be followed by other
periods of high volatility. This violates the assumption of ﬁnite variance and implies that
volatility can be persistent and unpredictable.
In summary, while the normal distribution oﬀers simplicity and convenience for modeling
portfolio returns, it is important to recognize its limitations. In real-world scenarios, consid-
ering alternative distributions that account for fat-tailed events, correlation, and volatility
clustering may provide a more accurate representation of risk and return characteristics.

Conclusions
4.1
Findings and discussion
Our ﬁndings can be separated into two categories, those who are important for pension
planning and those who are important for the predictive capabilities of the model. Firstly,
regarding pension planning attributes, our study reveals the critical role of individual choices
in shaping the outcomes of pension accounts. Speciﬁcally, the contribution rate emerges as
a key factor that signiﬁcantly impacts pension growth and the balance between current con-
sumption and future savings. By adjusting the contribution rate, individuals can eﬀectively
reduce their current consumption during the accumulation phase while simultaneously in-
creasing their potential retirement income. However, it is important to exercise caution and
strike a balance, as individuals tend to spend less during their retirement years compared
to their working years. This underscores the importance of carefully considering personal
ﬁnancial goals and lifestyle preferences when determining the optimal contribution rate.
Furthermore, we see the importance of inﬂation and how this should be managed. When
individuals ﬁnd themselves in an inﬂating market, it could be wiser to either spend their
money at the moment as they hold more value or invest it into higher-risk brackets as they
will combat inﬂation with higher returns. Usually, when inﬂation hits the markets, they
are inﬂated as well, and once it starts to get under control, then they should revert back to
lower-risk portfolios. Additionally, higher-risk portfolios by themselves can indicate a higher
income, but caution should be taken as before the returns start accumulating a signiﬁcant
proﬁt, a large amount of money should be implemented in the account beforehand. Thus,
this is relevant to higher classes mostly as their accumulation happens much quicker than
in the lower brackets of workers. Finally, when it comes to low earners, they seem to have
the smoothest transition when it comes to their lifestyle as a worker to their lifestyle as a
pensioner. The reason behind this is the fact that the government of Denmark ensures that
they will receive a supplementary pension on top of the basic one that ensures the standard
of living for the individual. With this in mind the risk they incorporate is more welcomed

can provide invaluable guidance to individuals with varying ﬁnancial goals, helping them
navigate the complexities of retirement planning and make informed decisions.

risk and reward in the context of retirement planning. Balancing risk levels in investment
