Abstract  
 
The Animal Defined Daily doses (ADD), determined by the Danish Veterinary and Food 
Administration were compared to the Used Daily Doses (UDDA) obtained from 20 Danish 
conventional integrated pig herds. ADD is a theoretical unit, for this reason, it is of interest to 
investigate if the measurement is reliable, e.g. when enforcing the “Yellow Card” intervention, a 
scheme to reduce antimicrobial usage by setting national thresholds for antimicrobial usage in 
Danish pig herds. 
Pig herds were visited between November 2014 and November 2015. Data was collected on 
biosecurity and antimicrobial usage. The latter was obtained retrospectively for all group treatments 
for the fattening pigs closest to slaughter for their whole lifetime. Herd sizes were extracted from 
the Central Husbandry Register. The dataset on ADDs were extracted from VetStat on the 19th 
August 2016. UDDAs and ADDs were determined per kg animal and per animal. Furthermore, the 
ratios UDDA/ADD-ratio-kg and UDDA/ADD-ratio-animal were calculated. Ratios above 1.2 were 
defined as overdosing and ratios below 0.8 were defined as underdosing. Age group, antimicrobial 
class, indication, route of administration, biosecurity level and herd size were included as 
explanatory variables in the analysis. Descriptive analysis of UDDA, ADD and explanatory 
variables was performed. Explanatory variables associated with over- and underdosing, 
respectively, were analysed by mixed logistic regression models for UDDA/ADD-ratio-kg and 
UDDA/ADD-ratio-animal, respectively. Hence four outcomes were evaluated. Herd and age group 
within herd were included as random effects. Only statistically significant models and models with 
the best fit for each outcome were presented.    
Determined by kg, 46% and 8% of the records represented over- and underdosing, respectively. Per 
animal, 23% and 66% of the records showed over- and underdosing, respectively. In the final 
models locomotive disorders (compared to intestinal disorders, OR: 3, 95% CI: 1.0-11.1, P = 0.04) 
and respiratory disorders (OR: 11, 95% CI: 3.3-39.8, P = 0.0001) were at higher risk for overdosing 
when UDDA/ADD-ratio-kg was used as outcome variable. If UDDA/ADD-anim. were included as 
outcome variable locomotive disorders (compared to intestinal disorders, OR: 8, 95% CI: 2.0-36.5, 
P = 0.004) were at higher risk for overdosing and sucklers (compared to weaners, OR: 79, 95% CI: 
9.8-644.7, P = 4.25e-05) were at higher risk for underdosing.  
The study was limited by a relative small sampling frame and a very low number of or no 
observations in some levels of some explanatory variables. Thus, it was not possible to run full 
models and include interaction terms.   
These findings suggest that estimation of the antimicrobial consumption as ADDs could be more 
accurate, and implicates that some herds potentially could receive the “Yellow Card” for rather 
technical than biological reasons. In addition, a separate age group for sucklers in VetStat would be 
desirable.

Introduction 
 
In production animals, antimicrobials are used to ensure animal health and welfare, and eventually 
for prophylaxy in order to improve production (Rushton, 2015; Ahmad et al., 2015). In Denmark, 
antimicrobial usage in the animal production is prescription only (DANMAP, 2015) and is only 
approved for therapeutic and metaphylactic treatments, while prophylactic treatments are illegal 
(Anonymous, 2015). Antimicrobial usage increases the risk of selecting for and spread of 
antimicrobial resistant bacteria and genes, ultimately increasing the risk of treatment failure of 
bacterial infections in humans and animals (EMA, 2013a; Aarestrup, 1999). Humans can be 
exposed to antimicrobial resistant bacteria originating from production animals either through food 
of animal origin, by direct contact with animals or through the environment (Rushton, 2015; EMA, 
2013a). The exposure can either be directly to bacteria pathogenic for humans or to bacteria non-
pathogenic for humans, which may transfer their resistance genes to the pathogenic bacteria 
(Aarestrup, 1999). The occurrence of antimicrobial resistant bacteria is associated with 
antimicrobial usage, and the number of antimicrobial resistant bacteria is correlated to the amount 
of antimicrobials used (Aarestrup, 1999), but the association is complex involving also other 
factors. A program for monitoring antimicrobial usage provides an important tool to control the 
emergence and spread of antimicrobial resistant bacteria and to evaluate interventions to reduce 
their level (EMA, 2013a).  
 
At the EU invitational conference, The Microbial threat, held in Copenhagen in 1998, it was 
recommended to implement national and European surveillance on consumption of antimicrobial 
agents in humans and animals (Stege et al., 2003; Anonymous, 1998; Mevius et al., 1999). In 
Denmark, this led to establishing the national database VetStat in 2000, containing information on 
all prescribed veterinary medicines. The vision of VetStat was to promote prudent use of 
antimicrobial agents for production animals, in order to minimize the emergence of antimicrobial 
resistant bacteria (Stege et al., 2003). Data are entered by the pharmacies, the veterinarians and the 
feed mills. Each prescription holds information on date, source, recipient and drug. Furthermore, 
information regarding the drug contains a specified product number, quantity of drug, animal 
species, age group and diagnostic category (Stege et al., 2003). VetStat has since its implementation 
been used both in many research studies and by the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration 
(DVFA) when estimating and analysing the antimicrobial usage for animals in Denmark (Temtem 
et al., 2016; DANMAP, 2014; Hybschmann et al., 2011; Fertner et al., 2015; Anonymous, 2016a, 
Jensen et al., 2014).  
 
Antimicrobial usage can be estimated quantitatively using different units of measurement (Chauvin 
et al., 2001). The simplest way of quantifying antimicrobials is total weight of active ingredient. 
However, this unit does not take into account the differences in potency between different products. 
Hence, incorrect trends, of either increased or decreased treatment frequency can be the result, if 
one product is substituted by another with a higher or lower potency (Chauvin et al., 2001; Arnold 
et al., 2004). Additionally, the therapeutic effect and the selection for resistant bacteria are 
dependent on the potency of the product and not only the amount of substance in terms of weight

In Denmark, from 2000 to 2009, the consumption of antimicrobials for animals increased by 62% 
measured in kg active ingredients. This was mainly driven by the consumption for pigs due to the 
size of and changes in the demography of the pig population in Denmark (DANMAP, 2015; 
DANMAP 2014). This has led to the implementation of the “Yellow Card” by the DVFA in 2010 
(Jensen et al., 2014), which determines thresholds for antimicrobial usage in pig herds defined as 
ADD per 100 animals per day for each age group (table 2). Thresholds are dynamic. Herds 
receiving the “Yellow Card” are subject to a number of actions by the DVFA and are obligated to 
reduce the antimicrobial consumption below the threshold within 9 months, which represents an 
economic burden to the farmers (Anonymous, 2014; Anonymous, 2016a; Jensen et al., 2014). 
 
From table 1 it can be seen that three different ADD values have been developed in Denmark: 1) 
old ADD, 2) new ADD and 3) DADD. The old ADDs were established in VetStat for each 
pharmaceutical product used for animals in 2001 (Dupont, 2016b). However, both the DVFA and 
DANMAP calculated new ADDs in 2014, new ADD and DADD, respectively, because 
antimicrobials marketed in Denmark in 2011 recommended higher doses, thus ADD’s, than similar 
products with identical active molecules, potency and pharmaceutical form. This made it possible 
for farmers to speculate using products which gave a lower number of ADDs when using the same 
doses (Dupont et al., 2014a). This could be an advantage as more antimicrobials could be used 
without exceeding the threshold for receiving the “Yellow Card”.   
 
The ADDs should not be considered the “true dosage” used for a treatment but a theoretical unit 
(Jensen et al., 2004). Therefore, it is of interest to compare ADDs with the truly administered daily 
dose, “used daily dose” (UDDA) (UNIVERSITEIT GENT, 2016) to investigate if those 
measurements are comparable, and if estimating the antimicrobial consumption using ADDs is 
reasonable, for instance when enforcing the legally provided thresholds for antimicrobial

Materials and methods 
 
Selection of herds  
Data collected as part of an EU project, EFFORT (Ecology from Farm to Fork Of microbial drug 
Resistance and Transmission), that deals with the consequences of antimicrobial resistance in the 
food chain (EFFORT against antimicrobial resistance, 2014) were used for this study. Additional 
information regarding EFFORT can be obtained from the website: http://www.effort-against-
amr.eu/.  
 
The sampling frame consisted of 20 Danish pig herds which had to fulfill the following selection 
criteria: 1) conventional integrated farrow-to-finisher and non-mixed pig farms, 2) batch and all-in 
all-out production at compartment level, 3) have at least 150 sows and 600 fatteners, 4) no contact 
between selected herds, 5) all herds should have different owners and 6) must have a track record of 
antimicrobial usage available. Herds fulfilling the criteria were extracted from the central husbandry 
register (CHR), which is a national database, where all herds are legally required to be registered 
(Anonymous, 2013). A number of herds were randomly selected using regional stratification. 
Owners were then contacted by letter and telephone between October 2014 and October 2015 to ask 
for their willingness to participate in the study. A total of 20 herds were finally included. 
 
Collection of herd data 
The 20 pig herds were visited once by 2 out of 5 veterinary Master students employed at the project 
and one assistant professor responsible for the project. The visits were conducted between 
November 2014 and November 2015 and spread equally over the seasons. Data on antimicrobial 
usage were collected for the group of fattening pigs closest to slaughter i.e. within the last two 
weeks before slaughter.  Data were collected retrospectively for group treatments with antimicrobial 
drugs applied to these pigs according to the farmer’s treatment records.  The usage data included 
their whole lifetime from birth to slaughter.  Usage in sows was not included. For each treatment 
registration information on age group, number of animals treated, product name, active substance, 
concentration, dose, route of administration, treatment duration, weight, age and indication for 
treatment was collected (appendix 1). Age groups were defined as 1) sucklers: from birth until 
weaning, 2) weaners: from weaning until the animals were moved to the fattening section and 3) 
fatteners: from movement to the fattening section and until slaughter. Route of administration and 
indication had the following levels, respectively: “injection”, “medicated feed”, “drench”, “feed 
mixed at herd”, “water”, “spray”, “oral solution”, and “respiration”, “intestines”, “locomotion”, 
“urogenital”, “nervous”, “dermatology”, “eye”, “general disorder” and “other”.  
 
Additionally, farmers and visitors filled in a questionnaire developed by EFFORT regarding herd 
demographics, production parameters and biosecurity. Answers of the questionnaires regarding 
biosecurity were entered in the web based tool biocheck.ugent (Biocheck) (Ghent University, 2016) 
to get a total score for the herd biosecurity level in the herds between 0 (“total absence of 
biosecurity”) and 1(“perfect biosecurity”). Biocheck is a risk-based scoring system including 6 sub-
categories of respectively internal and external biosecurity. Each sub-category is weighted in the 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
final score according to scientific knowledge and expert opinions on pathogen transmission and 
infectious risks (Laanen et al., 2013; Laanen et. al 2010).  
 
Information on herd size was extracted from the CHR on the 7th September 2016 (appendix 2, table 
8).  
 
Data extraction from VetStat 
The dataset on ADDs for all products calculated by the DVFA in 2014 was extracted from VetStat 
on the 19th August 2016. Specifically, ADDkg was obtained for the same products reported for each 
data record during the farm visits sorting on animal species, product name, concentration, 
formulation and cessation in Vetstat.  
 
Data on antimicrobial usage for the herds included in the analysis were extracted from VetStat on 
the 30th November 2016. All records entered by pharmacies, veterinarians and feed mills from 2015 
were extracted. The information for each record was supplemented with additional information 
from other VetStat tables to get number of ADDkg for each record. Likewise, the number of animals 
per herd for each age group (table 2) was retrieved from a datasheet extracted from the CHR on the 
9th December 2015 holding all information in the CHR available at that time.

Data checking, editing and construction of variables 
All data entries obtained at the herd visits were checked for errors in the specified concentrations, 
misspellings and outliers. Weight and age group were checked to see if there was agreement 
between those. All errors detected were corrected in accordance with datasheets filled in at the herd 
visits. One entry of age group was changed due to disagreement between age group and weight.   
 
UDDA per kg bodyweight (UDDAkg) was calculated through the following steps.  
 
1) Concentrations expressed as international units were converted to weight (mg) of active 
ingredient based on WHO standards (EMA, 2013b).  
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
2) Usage dosages expressed as mg active ingredient per kg feed were converted to mg active 
ingredient per kg animal using information from medicintildyr.dk5 
(MEDICINTILDYR.DK). 
3) Usage dosages expressed as ml per kg animal were converted to mg active ingredient per kg 
animal by multiplying the dosage expressed as ml per kg by the concentration expressed as 
mg per ml.  
 
The final unit for UDDAkg was thus mg active ingredient per kg animal.  
 
UDDA per animal (UDDAanim.) was calculated by multiplying UDDAkg by the weight of the 
animals expressed in kg.  
 
The final unit for UDDAanim. was thus mg active ingredient per animal.  
 
All ADDkg, values were checked twice and any errors detected were corrected.  
 
ADDkg expressed as ml per kg animal were converted to mg active ingredient per kg animal by 
multiplying the dose by the concentration as described for UDDAkg. 
 
The final unit for ADDkg was thus mg active ingredient per kg.  
 
ADDanim. was calculated by multiplying ADDkg by animal standard weights estimated for each age 
group as defined in Vetstat (table 2). Thus ADDs corresponding to age groups obtained in the 
primary data as sucklers, weaners and fatteners were multiplied by 200 kg6, 15 kg and 50 kg, 
respectively.   
 
In order to compare ADDs and UDDAs, the ratios UDDAkg/ADDkg and UDDAanim./ADDanim. were 
calculated. These ratios will following be referred to as UDDA/ADD-ratio-kg and UDDA/ADD-
ratio-anim, respectively. 
 
Data were cleaned and edited: Records with missing values for either UDDA and/or ADD were 
excluded; for the statistical analysis rows with indication for “nervous” and “other” and the 
administration route “spray” were excluded due to very few observations (appendix 2, table 10); 
observations for “feed mixed at herd” and “medicated feed” were put together in the new level 
“feed”, resulting in the variable administration route with 4 levels: “injection”, “feed”, “water” and 
“drench”; also, four levels for the variable antimicrobial class were created combining all other 
levels except “beta-lactam”, “tetracycline” and “macrolide” in an “other” category. This was done 
due to few observations for other antimicrobial classes (appendix 2, table 10).

Records not concerning pigs and antimicrobial substances were excluded. Additionally, two 
negative entries were subtracted from the corresponding positive entries. Negatives entries are due 
to corrections of errors or return of unused drugs (Dupont et al., 2016a). ADDkg were summed for 
each age group per herd. Treatment incidence (TI) for each age group per herd was calculated as:

This calculation quantifies antimicrobial usage as ADD per 100 animals per day for each age group

of antimicrobial usage in the study herds and all Danish pig herds.   
 
Data analysis  
Data analysis was carried out using R version 3.3.1 (2016-06-21) by the interface programme 
RStudio.  
 
Descriptive analyses 
The relationship between UDDA and ADD was visualized by plotting UDDAs against ADDs. This 
was done for both UDDAkg and ADDkg and UDDAanim. and ADDanim.. Additionally, UDDAs and 
ADDs were plotted against each other by age group, indication, antimicrobial class, route of 
administration, herd IDs, herd size and biosecurity to see how data were distributed and if there 
were any obvious correlations between the variables investigated (appendix 4).    
 
Statistical analyses 
Mixed effects models were chosen because data had a hierarchical structure. The effect of age 
group, indication, antimicrobial class, route of administration, herd size and biosecurity on the 
UDDA/ADD-ratio were evaluated.  
At first, linear mixed models were considered. However, the data did not meet the linear regression 
assumption of normal distribution of the outcome and it was not possible to transform the outcome 
to a suitable distribution. Therefore, the outcome variables were transformed into dichotomous data 
to fit a mixed logistic regression model. For both UDDA/ADD-ratio-kg and -anim. two new 
outcome variables were created: One where UDDA/ADD-ratio equal to 1.2 was the threshold and 
another where a ratio equal to 0.8 was the threshold (table 3). This was done to evaluate the effect

For categorical variables and continuous variables proportional plots and boxplots, respectively, 
were generated to visualize the relationship between the dichotomous outcome and the explanatory 
variables and get an impression of the expected outcome of the models (appendix 5).  
 
 
The general model can be described as:  
 
Logit (y) = β0 + β 1 x age group + β 2 x indication + β 3 x antimicrobial class + β 4 x route of 
administration + β 5 x herd size + β 6 x biosecurity + µherd + µherd_age group  
 
 
In the model two random effects were included to take into account that animals originating from 
the same herd and animals originating from the same age group within herd were expected to be 
more similar than animals originating from different herds and from different age groups within 
herds.  
 
The estimation of parameters in the models was done using maximum likelihood estimation.  
 
The models best fitting the data were chosen by forward selection procedure of variables using 
dummy variables. It was not possible to run the models with all variables at once (full model) due to 
the limited number of observations in each variable level. Models chosen had to be significant in 
the Likelihood Ratio Test and have a significant drop of residual deviance compared to the null 
model at a significance level of 5% as they had to provide a significantly better fit to data than the 
null model. Only significant explanatory variables were included. If two or more models were 
significantly better than the null model, those models were compared using Akaike’s Information 
Criterion (AIC) and Likelihood Ratio Test. The model significantly better in the Likelihood Ratio 
Test and with the lowest AIC was chosen as the final model. Any correlations between variables 
were evaluated using a correlation matrix between the fixed effects. Correlated fixed effects were 
excluded from the final model. It was checked if random effect quantiles were normally distributed 
by plotting random effect quantiles against standard quantiles. Furthermore models were checked 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
for overdispersion comparing the residual deviance with the residual degrees of freedom. If this 
ratio was considerably larger than 1, evidence of overdispersion was considered.  
 
For model 4 the outcome variable for sucklers was transformed from 1 to 0 for a single observation 
with the highest ratio e.g. equal to 0.20. It was done because all outcomes for sucklers otherwise 
were equal to 1 making it impossible to run the model.     
 
Estimated odds ratios (OR) and 95% confidence intervals (CI) were presented as final measures of 
association.

Discussion 
 
This study compared UDDAs and ADDs for pigs in Denmark by analyzing factors associated with 
over- and underdosing, respectively. The UDDA/ADD-ratio-kg and UDDA/ADD-ratio-anim. were 
used as outcome variables and a large proportion of the records showed deviations between the two 
measures. Determined by kilo, 46% and 8% of the records represented over- and underdosing, 
respectively. For UDDA/ADD-ratio-anim. 23% and 66% of the records showed over- and 
underdosing, respectively. All records of underdosing for UDDA/ADD-ratio-anim.  were related 
treatment of sucklers.   
 
Selection bias  
The selection of herds for the study was initially conducted by random selection of herds fulfilling 
the inclusion criteria. The final enrollment in the study was depending on the willingness of the 
farmers to participate, and influenced by the possibility of reaching the farmers by letter and 
telephone in order to invite them to participate in the study. The study could therefore be subjected 
to selection bias, e.g. volunteer bias and non-response bias (O’Connor et al., 2016). It was not 
attempted to investigate, if there were any systematic differences between herds enrolled in the 
study and those either declining to participate or those not reached. However, an indication of this 
can be given when comparing herds participating in this study with all Danish pig herds as done 
below. In addition, the potential application of the results from this study on the Danish pig 
population as a whole can be obtained through such a comparison.  
 
Representativeness 
The average herd size in the study was 3372 (median: 3400) pigs per herd compared to 2949 pigs 
per herd in Denmark in 2012 (Danmarks Statistik, 2014). Thus the herds included in this study were 
a little bigger than the average in Denmark in 2012. However the average herd size increases every 
year (Christiansen, 2015). The location of herds in the study was comparable to the distribution of 
pig herds in Denmark with most herds located in Region Midtjylland and Region Syddanmark 
followed by Region Nordjylland, Region Sjælland and Region Hovedstaden (Danmarks Statistik, 
2016).  
 
Only integrated pig farms were included in the study. Nationwide integrated pig farms composed 
34.5% of the total number of pig herds in 2015 (Landbrug & Fødevarer, 2016). The average 
biosecurity level at the herds was 0.61 compared to 0.77 in 140 Danish pig herds in 2015 (Johansen, 
2016). Thus the herds included in this study had an apparently lower biosecurity level than herds 
investigated by Johansen (2016). However, the estimate of biosecurity in the two studies may not be 
directly comparable as it was based on farm visits in the present study and telephone interviews in 
the study by Johansen (2016). The latter may be more prone to prestige bias, as the farmer could 
answer what he/she thought was most correct and not what he/she was actually doing (Nielsen et 
al., 2004). This was attempted to avoid in the present study as the interviewer observed what was 
the actual practice at the farm. However some questions were answered by the farmers as it could 
not be observed, and thus the present study could be subjected to prestige bias as well. In addition, 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
only integrated pig farms were included in the present study whereas Johansen (2016) included both 
integrated pig farms and herds with only sows and weaners. This could influence the comparability 
of the estimated biosecurity in the two studies as different production systems may have different 
levels of biosecurity. Production systems with younger animals are more vulnerable to diseases 
(Fertner, 2016), and it could therefore be argued that those systems may be more likely to 
implement biosecurity measures.  
 
Antimicrobial usage 
The antimicrobial class most frequently used for pigs in Denmark in 2015 was tetracyclines, 
followed by macrolides and pleuromutilins, and weaners consumed the highest proportion of 
antimicrobials (DANMAP, 2015). In this study, sucklers consumed most antimicrobials followed 
by weaners, and the antimicrobial classes administered most often were beta-lactams, tetracyclines 
and macrolides. In VetStat, treatment of sucklers is “hiding” in the group “pre-weaning pigs, sows, 
boars and bred gilts” meaning that a high number of treated sucklers only appear as treatment of 
one sow in the statistics as the consumption for this group solely is based on the weight of the sows 
i.e. 200 kg. Therefore, the antimicrobial usage in the group sucklers in this study is not comparable 
with the usage in the group “pre-weaning pigs, sows, boars and bred gilts” as reported in VetStat. 
The most often used antimicrobial class in this study for sucklers was beta-lactams, which is then in 
agreement with DANMAP (2015), where beta-lactams also were the most often used antimicrobial 
class for sows (including sucklers). Tetracyclines, macrolides and pleuromutilins were the second, 
third and fifth most used antimicrobial classes in this study, respectively, which is also in agreement 
with the general usage patterns as reported in DANMAP 2015.  
 
The most frequent indications for treatment in this study were intestines, respiratory, locomotive 
and dermal disorders, which is comparable to the most frequent indications for treatment on a 
national scale (Jensen et al., 2014). In the present study sucklers were most often treated for 
intestinal and respiratory disorders, which is not in accordance with treatment indications for this 
age group on a national scale (Jensen et al., 2014). However, this difference could be due to the 
different registration of sucklers in this study and in VetStat as elaborated earlier. Antimicrobials 
were most frequently administered by injection, whereas oral administration is most frequently used 
on a national scale (Jensen et al., 2014). This could be due to the relatively more treatments of 
sucklers as compared to oral treatments in other groups, as sucklers cannot be administered 
antimicrobials through feed and water. However, in the present study fatteners were also treated 
more often by injection than through feed or water revealing a possible different treatment strategy 
by farmers in the present study compared to a national scale.   
 
The antimicrobial consumption for pigs in Denmark in 2015 was 21 DAPDs (DADD per 1000 
animals per day) for sows and piglets, 101 DAPDs for weaners and 20 DAPDs for finishers (Høg, 
personal communication). For the herds included in the study the average antimicrobial 
consumption was 20 (median: 22) ADDs per 1000 animals per day for preweaning pigs, sows, 
boars and bred gilts, 88 (median: 69) ADDs per 1000 animals per day for weaners and 20 (median: 
21) ADDs per 100 animals per day for finishers and unbred gilts. Thus, in general, the antimicrobial 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
consumption for the herds included in the study is comparable to the consumption on national level, 
when taking into account the difference in the registration of sucklers in this study and on national 
level which explains the observed differences. However, the average consumption of antimicrobials 
for weaners in the study population was lower than on a national scale.   
 
These differences combined with a relative small sampling frame of 0.8% (19/2398) (Danmarks 
Statistik, 2016) of the Danish pig population are important to consider when interpreting the results 
of this study.   
 
On-farm antimicrobial recordings 
Data on actual antimicrobial usage doses was obtained during the farm visits. One of the inclusion 
criteria was that a track record of antimicrobial usage should be available, i.e. the farmer should be 
able to consult a written sheet with information on antimicrobial usage during the past 6 months. 
This means, that if treatments were noted correctly, the data collected during the herd visits would 
have a high accuracy (Houe et al., 2004).  
 
Large differences existed between the number of animals treated per record (range, 1-1760). 
“Group treatments” were not strictly defined in this study, which could lead to a different 
interpretation of “group treatments” by the farmers, and thus a considerable deviation between 
numbers of records per herd. This would influence the completeness of data. However, such 
deviations could also be due to different levels of diseases and/or treatment strategies in the herds 
where some farmers may be more prone to make group treatments and others individual treatments. 
An eventual difference between such strategies and disease levels were not investigated in this 
study. Hence it is not possible to conclude on the reasons for the difference in the number of records 
per herd. 
 
The data collection was conducted by five different interviewers. Interviewer bias might therefore 
have occurred, if questions were not put in the same way or answers were not perceived in the same 
manner by the interviewers (Nielsen et al., 2004). It was attempted to avoid this by having a start-up 
meeting before the sampling started, where questionnaires were reviewed to accommodate 
uncertainties by the interviewers and interviewers were asked to put the question in the same way, 
thus minimizing this type of bias as much as possible. Interviewer bias is, therefore, not considered 
to have influenced the study importantly giving the study a high precision (Houe et al., 2004).  
 
Data checking and editing 
During data entering and editing it was attempted to avoid misclassification bias by checking data 
(Nielsen et al., 2004). Erroneous entries are therefore considered a minor problem. For some 
analyses, records or levels of variables were either grouped or excluded due to few observations. 
For some records data was missing because of incomplete registrations by the farmers in the 
original herd records. The reasons for this were not investigated and were thus unknown. However, 
it is interesting to ask whether some herds were more prone to having missing records than others 
and how this influenced the final data used for the analysis. One herd was excluded entirely from 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
the final analysis and another herd had a substantial drop in the proportion of records included due 
to missing data. For the remaining herds, only minor changes in the proportion of records in the raw 
data and the data used for the analysis were seen. Whether the data exclusions had an impact on the 
distribution of levels within the variables in the data used for analysis compared to the raw data was 
not investigated.   
 
The results of the study were influenced by the very low number of or no observations in some 
levels of the variables e.g. dermatology. This made it difficult to obtain proper parameter estimates 
for some variables and necessitated that the outcome for sucklers were transformed for a single 
record. For the same reason, it was not possible to run full models or models including interaction 
terms. Such difficulties could have been avoided if the number of records had been larger. The 
number of records in this study was low compared to other studies in other countries comparing 
UDDAs and ADDs (Merle et al., 2014; Regula et al., 2009; Trauffler et al., 2014). 
 
Comparison of UDDAs and ADDs 
The comparison of UDDAkg and ADDkg showed that overdosing was far more prevalent than 
underdosing. This is in accordance with results from similar studies. Callens et al. (2012) found that 
respectively 79.5% and 29.4% of injectable and oral products were overdosed. Regula et al. (2009) 
found that 92% of treatments for adult pigs and 54.9% for fattening pigs were overdosed, 
respectively. It should be noted that definition of over- and underdosing varied with UDDA/ADD-
ratios between 0.8-1.2 and 0.9-1.1 across studies, and that the recommended dosages (i.e. ADDs) 
differ between countries (Postma et al., 2015). Jensen et al. (2004) compared the prescribed daily 
dose (PDD) for pigs in Denmark with the ADD, and found that, for 80% of the drug groups, the 
deviation between PDDs and ADDs was less than 10%. This contrasts the results from the present 
study inferring non-compliance to PDDs for farmers.    
 
Model 1 
The variable best explaining the outcome in model 1 was indication. The odds for overdosing were 
increased by a factor 3 and 11 for locomotive and respiratory disorders, respectively, compared to 
intestinal disorders. The descriptive statistics showed 100% overdosing for dermatology, but 
dermatology did not came out significantly in the model due to few records for dermatology that all 
were classified as overdosed. A plausible explanation for the results of model 1 could be the fact 
that gastrointestinal disorders are more often treated by oral application of antimicrobials i.e. 
multicollinearity was observed for gastrointestinal disorders and oral application, whereas 
locomotive disorders are more often treated by injection (Fertner, 2016). However, Fertner (2016) 
describes that respiratory disorders are also more likely to be treated by oral application of 
antimicrobials which may not the case in the present study. It is plausible that a more accurate 
dosage in general is applied for a large preparation of medicines in feed or water compared to 
injection, where you may be more prone to overdose few animals e.g. to be on the safe side when 
rounding dosages according to an estimated weight of the animal. This could especially be the case 
for suckles for whom it can be difficult to administer small amounts of drugs (Timmerman et al., 
2006). This is in accordance with the descriptive statistics in this study, where sucklers tended to be 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
more overdosed than the other age groups. This hypothesis is supported by that fact that the second 
best explanatory variables for model 1 were “route of administration” + “herd size” where the odds 
for overdosing were 24 times higher for injection compared to water. The descriptive statistics also 
showed that injection appeared to be more frequently overdosed compared to other administration 
routes. In other studies, similar results have been found, with injected antimicrobials more prone to 
be overdosed and oral treatments more prone to be underdosed (Callens et al., 2012; Timmerman et 
al., 2006; Trauffler et al., 2014). However, another explanation could be that ADDs are calculated 
based on the main indication, which means that if other indications than the main indication have a 
different treatment dosage, UDDA and ADD are not comparable even though the right treatment 
dosage is chosen. It is therefore of interest in future studies to investigate whether it is the indication 
and/or administration route that best explains the observed differences between UDDAs and ADDs. 
If such future studies show that the divergence is mainly explained by the administration route, it 
would be desirable to recommend farmers and veterinarians to be more precise when dosing 
injectable products. This would make it possible to calculate the antimicrobials usage based on 
ADDs more precisely both in the interest of the farmers, the authorities and for scientific purposes. 
On the other hand if it is due to treatment of indications with other dosage recommendations than 
the main indication, it is not desirable to change the ADDs as they by definition is based on the 
main indication (Jensen et al., 2004) and because changes in the ADDs makes it impossible to 
compare antimicrobial usage over longer periods (Chauvin et al., 2001). It is thus just an uncertainty 
which is inherent in ADDs.   
 
The implications of these results are that herds which have many problems with respiratory, 
locomotive and dermatological disorders may actually treat a lower number of animals than 
estimated in VetStat. The same is true for herds which are more prone to administer antimicrobials 
by injection. Ultimately, this can put those herds at a higher risk of receiving the “Yellow Card”, 
hence forcing them to reduce their antimicrobial usage for technical rather than biologically 
meaningful reasons. This could potentially affect animal welfare, health and productivity. Studies 
investigating the effect of the implementation of the “Yellow Card” on animal welfare, health and 
productivity show deviating results. Dupont et al. (2014b) showed that daily weight gain was 
decreased and mortality was increased as a possible effect of the reduction of antimicrobial usage 
due to the “Yellow Card” implementation in 2010. However, Alban et al. (2013) showed that some 
chronic lesions detected at meat inspection were increased whereas others were decreased, which 
was also show by another study (Dupont et al., 2016b). This decrease was probably due to increased 
usage of vaccines. Thus a restriction on antimicrobial usage can also motivate farmers to prevent 
infection thereby improving animal health (Alban et al., 2013). From a perspective of preventing 
antimicrobial resistance it is desired to treat few diseased animals by injection instead of 
administering antimicrobials to a large number of animals by either feed or water with the risk of 
treating healthy animals (Ahmad et al., 2015). Therefore, it is problematic that antimicrobials 
administered by injection give rise to a higher number of ADDs in Vetstat compared to oral 
administered antimicrobials when treating the same number of animals.  
 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
The results also showed that estimating the antimicrobial consumption for intestinal disorders as 
ADDs is mostly in accordance with the used treatments dosages. This is an advantage, as intestinal 
disorders are the most frequent indication treated with antimicrobials in weaners (Jensen et al., 
2014), which is the age group contributing to the largest proportion of the consumption of 
antimicrobials for pigs in Denmark (DANMAP, 2015). This makes the estimated treatment dosages 
quite accurate for this large group of indications. Hence, if the antimicrobial usage has to be 
reduced for intestinal disorders, it has to be done through improved disease prevention rather than 
through changes in the calculation method.  
 
As a final remark, it should be stated that over- and underdosing, respectively, were estimated 
according to comparability between UDDAs and ADDs. As previously noted, ADDs are based on 
recommended dosages. Studies show that different dosing regimens (duration, frequency, dose) 
have a different impact on the level of antimicrobial resistant bacteria. Ahmad et al. (2015) showed 
that longer duration of treatment with tetracycline in pigs increased the level of antimicrobial 
resistant bacteria, whereas different treatment frequencies did not had an impact on the resistance 
level. Thus, using recommended dosing regimens would not necessarily contribute to less 
antimicrobial resistance.  
 
Model 3 and 4 
The comparison of UDDAanim. and ADDanim. takes into account both the dosages administered and 
the weights of the animals. This means that if the relationships found for UDDAkg/ADDkg and 
UDDAanim./ADDanim. are similar then the standard weights defined in VetStat are in accordance with 
the actual weights at treatment.  
 
For model 3, the variable best explaining the outcome is indication. The odds for overdosing were 
increased by a factor 8 and 2 for locomotive and respiratory disorders, respectively, compared to 
intestinal disorders. However, respiratory disorders were non-significant. This is comparable to the 
results from model 1 meaning that the weight estimates for weaners and fatteners, who are more 
prone to overdosing, seem reasonable. Still, the odds are not the same for model 1 and 3 meaning 
that the standard weights are different from the weights estimated at treatment. However, this is to 
be expected as animals are treated through the entire production period and therefore almost never 
will weigh the standard average weight. For dermatology, the results are different from model 1 as 
no observations show overdosing. This is because all observations for dermatology relate to 
treatment of sucklers, for whom all treatments are underdosed when comparing UDDAanim. and 
ADDanim.. This is explained further in the next section dealing with model 4. In addition, the total 
proportion of treatments which were overdosed is lower for UDDAanim./ADDanim. than 
UDDAkg/ADDkg, meaning that a larger proportion of the treated animals had lower weights than the 
standard weights at treatment. A great part of this can probably be explained by the wrong estimates 
of standard weights for sucklers. In addition, it is plausible that animals are more diseased and 
hence more treated in the first period after movement into a new section, why the standard weights 
could have been estimated at a to high level. This has to be investigated further in future studies.  
 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
In model 4 underdosing was evaluated. From the analysis, sucklers appeared to be highly 
underdosed because the estimated standard weights for sucklers are wrong, which can also be seen 
as no such relationship appeared when underdosing was evaluated for UDDAkg and ADDkg. This 
result was expected since the standard weights defined in VetStat are not defined separately for 
sucklers, as sucklers are combined with sows, boars, and bred gilts giving them a standard weight of 
200 kg. These findings are in accordance with a study conducted by Merle et al. (2014), where 
deviations between UDDAanim. and ADDanim. were explained by wrong estimates of the standard 
weights. However, the results are not comparable between countries due to different production 
systems between countries; this means that the same standard weights cannot be used across 
different countries (Merle et al., 2014). As can be seen from the descriptive statistics, some levels of 
the other variables, e.g. drench and dermatology, have 100% of underdosing in model 4 revealing 
again multicollinearity between some levels of some variables in the data: antimicrobials 
administered as drench are only administered to sucklers, which are “technically” underdosed and 
coincidences, e.g. all age groups could be treated for dermatological disorders.   
 
This study reveals an obvious need for estimating a standard weight for sucklers in order to be able 
to estimate the antimicrobial consumption for this age group more precisely. Knowing the 
antimicrobial consumption is a prerequisite for surveillance of trends in the consumption of 
antimicrobials and a reduction of antimicrobial usage (EMA, 2013a) for this age group. This is 
important to reduce the resistance load nation- and worldwide which implies reduction of the 
antimicrobial usage (Ahmad et al., 2015), including at all production levels in the pig production.  
ESVAC also recommend reporting antimicrobial usage for pigs for four age groups: 1) suckling 
piglets, 2) weaners, 3) finishers and 4) sows and boars. ESVAC proposed a standard weight of 4 kg 
for suckling piglets (EMA, 2013a). However, this weight may not be applicable for the Danish 
reality and the right estimate has to be investigated before implementing it. In the present study, the 
average weight for sucklers at treatment was 2.6 kg.  
 
A drawback of a separate age group for sucklers will undoubtedly be more registrations for 
pharmacies, veterinarians and feed miles, and more work for farmers as they will need to have 
separate medicines for sucklers and sows. However, this could partly be reversed by implementing 
new technology saving time and reducing bureaucracy. Such new technology could also aid in 
improving the registrations to VetStat over all e.g. by record dosages, indications and age groups at 
treatment. An estimate of the number of sucklers per herd also needs to be implemented in and 
reported to the CHR, as this is needed for the calculation of antimicrobial consumption per day per 
number of pigs. This means that an implementation of such a change in the end will depend on a 
political decision.  
 
However, before any actions are taken, it is of relevance to see if the results found in this study are 
true on a larger scale. In addition, it would be of relevance to include data on sows, boars and gilts 
and for individual treatments in such a study to see if the same tendencies are observed in this age 
group and for individual treatments.  
 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
International context 
Last but not least, it should be stated that the estimation of antimicrobial consumption for animals in 
Denmark, as ADDs per animal species per age group, is exemplary in the international context, as 
most countries only have data on total sales figures available at present (Bondt et al., 2013). In 
addition, it is recommend by ESVAC to report antimicrobial consumption on species level as 
defined daily doses animal. However, ESVAC also recommends to report it as defined course dose 
(DCDA), this way taking into account the duration of treatment (EMA, 2013a). Estimating DCDAs 
could be a future improvement of the Danish system. WHO also states that antimicrobial 
consumption estimated as DDDs could never reflect the truth, but should be seen as “a rough 
estimate of consumption and not an exact picture of actual use (…) enabling the researcher to 
assess trends in drug consumption and to perform comparisons between population groups.” 
(WHO, 2016). This must necessarily be the same for ADDs and has to be taken into account when 
calculating antimicrobial consumption as ADDs, as illustrated by this study. 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
Conclusion 
 
A large proportion of records, both determined by kg animal and per animal, showed deviations 
between UDDAs and ADDs. Hence, both evidence of over- and underdosing were observed. 
Determined by kg, overdosing was far more prevalent than underdosing. The odds for overdosing 
were significantly higher for locomotive and respiratory disorders compared to intestinal disorders. 
On animal level, evaluating standard weights estimated in VetStat, 23% and 68% of the records 
showed over- and underdosing, respectively. The odds for underdosing were significantly higher for 
sucklers compared to weaners. This was to be expected as sucklers have a standard weight of 200 
kg in VetStat. In addition, a trend towards lower actual treatment weights than standard weights at 
treatment for weaners and fatteners was observed. These findings suggest that estimation of 
antimicrobial usage as ADDs per number of animals per day could be more accurate. Furthermore, 
it implicates that some pig herds could potentially be put at risk of receiving the “Yellow Card” 
because of technical rather than biological reasons. In addition, a separate age group for sucklers in 
Vetstat is desirable. The study was hampered by the relative small sampling frame, and the very low 
number of or no observations in some levels of some explanatory variables. Therefore, future 
studies are needed to investigate if the observed tendencies in this study are true on a larger scale 
too, and for age groups and individual treatments not included in this study. Furthermore, it has to 
be clarified whether indication best explains the observed overdosing. Despite the observed 
deviations, antimicrobial usage for animals quantified as ADDs per number of animals per day is 
exemplary in an international context.
