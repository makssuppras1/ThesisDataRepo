Abstract
For successful engineering of cell factories a good genetic design is essential. However,
a good design requires extensive knowledge of the native system. Much is still to be
learned and understood about these native systems. Our lack of knowledge often leads
to unexpected behavior or even failure of carefully designed cell factories. One aspect that
still needs to be investigated in deeper detail is the noisy nature of gene regulation and how
this affects gene expression, both in native and synthetic systems. Gene expression noise
causes populations of genetically identical cells to have different gene expression levels
and, therefore, potentially, show different phenotypes. This thesis particularly investigates
the noise that occurs when transcription factors are relocated, resulting in a change of
spatial distance to their target. The gene malT, which encodes the transcription factor
of the MalT regulon, was relocated into four new loci. The effect of these relocations
was investigated by studying the population fitness during growth on maltose. The malT
relocations had different effects on population fitness, depending on the locus for malT
expression. This is predicted to be a result of the gene expression noise that occurs
due to the change in distance between the transcription factor and its targets. This noise
in expression was found using single­cell measurements on strains with a GFP reporter
integrated into the four loci of interest. The exact mechanisms of how noise in gene
expression affects the population fitness still remains unclear.

Introduction
Cell factories offer a wide array of possibilities. From bio­based production of valuable
compounds for the chemical and pharmaceutical industry to applications in agriculture
and food industry. Synthetic biology offers the tools to engineer these cell factories for
almost any imaginable purpose. But for cell factory engineering to succeed, extensive
knowledge about the native systems of the cell is needed. Scientist strive towards full
control over their engineered cell factories, but are often challenged by the gaps in the
knowledge of the native mechanisms that cause unexplainable failures of the engineered
systems.
The engineering of cell factories involves genetic designs to control gene expression of
both native and recombinant genes. By introduction of inducible promoters, genes can
be turned on and off or even be finely tuned for protein expression levels. By adding
more genes under inducible systems, complex gene regulatory networks can be created.
These networks can be activated when adding a certain chemical inducer or can even be
designed to be automatically regulated, dependent on certain conditions. These synthetic
networks mimic the native ones in the cell that control its phenotypes throughout different
conditions.
Often these gene regulation networks are compared to electric circuits and scientists try
to utilize some of the same design principles. A key difference to this analogy is the
way signals travel. Instead of electric pulses traveling in organized wires, the signals
of gene regulatory networks are transcription factors and their inducers traveling in the
three­dimensional space of a cell. This means that for biochemical reactions to occur, as
the ones involved in gene expression, the reactants have to find each other in the cell.
The result of this is that gene expression underlies a certain degree of stochasticity. The
random processes involved in gene expression often lead to great cell­to­cell variations
in protein levels. This can ultimately result in genetically identical cells showing different
phenotypes. This biological concept is called gene noise [1], [2].
Gene noise is a challenge when it comes to the design of cell factories, especially since
not all mechanisms of the concept are completely understood. The goal is often to limit
the cell­to­cell variation as much as possible. For instance, in large fermentation pro­
cesses where heterogeneity in cell populations can be a hinder for optimal yields. Much
is possible to be won by understanding the mechanisms of gene noise, and utilizing this
knowledge when designing cell factories.

1.1
Aim
The overall aim of this thesis is to investigate the effect of gene noise when the spatial
architecture of a gene regulation network is disrupted. This investigation can contribute
to the knowledge of how the spatial architecture affects a gene regulation network. The
MalT iModulon [3], which is responsible for maltose utilization, was chosen as a case
study. The architecture of the iModulon was disrupted by relocation of the transcription
factor gene, malT. The effect of this relocation was evaluated by observing the effect on
cell population fitness when grown in maltose. Furthermore, single­cell measurements
were performed on strains where green fluorescent protein was expressed from a MalT
activated promoter. These measurements should help us understand whether the tran­
scription factor relocation indeed leads to increased noise levels. At last, the thesis also
includes the initial steps of testing a new approach to strain library engineering.

Background
2.1
Gene regulation in Escherichia coli
Gene regulation is crucial for proper functionality of the cell. Without regulation, resources
would be wasted on expression of genes that are not needed in the moment and the cell
would not be able exploit different phenotypes depending on conditions [4].
Many mechanisms for gene regulation in Escherichia coli are found in all stages of the
gene expression process. This includes mechanisms like regulatory molecules (DNA and
RNA binding protein and small regulatory RNA), riboswitches, messenger RNA (mRNA)
stability, DNA modifications, and σ factor competition [5]–[9]. The cell has many options
to control and regulate the levels of its proteins and their activity, all the way from initiation
of transcription to protein degradation [4]. In this thesis, the focus will exclusively be on
regulation of transcription initiation by transcription factors.
2.1.1
Regulation of transcription initiation by transcription factors
The majority of E. coli genes are regulated on transcriptional level during initiation by DNA
binding proteins called transcription factors (TFs) [4]. A TF can regulate transcription in
both a positive or a negative manner. A TF with a positive effect on transcription initiation
is called an activator. It can regulate the initiation by mechanisms like recruitment of
RNA polymerase and promoter remodeling [10]–[12]. A TF that affects the transcription
initiation negatively is called a repressor. Repressors can affect the initiation by hindering
RNA polymerase binding, blocking RNA polymerase during transcription elongation, anti­
activation, or deformation of the DNA binding site [13]–[17].
TFs are often allosteric proteins and their conformation can therefore be affected by the
binding of small molecules, called inducers or co­repressors, or by chemical modifications,
such as phosphorylation. This can change the TF’s affinity of DNA­binding and thereby
either hinder or activate binding of the TFs. This adds a level of regulation that allows gene
activation based on environmental conditions [4]. Examples of this include the extensive
regulation network for utilization of carbon sources and stress responses.
Overall, TFs play a crucial role in gene regulation and are one of the main factors in the
organization of gene regulation networks.
2.1.2
The gene regulation network of E. coli
Currently, the gene regulation network of E. coli is the best known regulation network for
any organism in terms of known network interactions. The network includes 221 TFs,
3937 regulatory interactions, and 4708 regulated genes that are currently registered at
RegulonDB (acessed on 21­01­2022) [18]. Despite this, it is still estimated that only 10­
30% of the TF­gene interactions are described [18]. Others report that up to 60% of the
E. coli promoters do not have any known regulatory input [4], [19].
Often smaller gene regulatory networks are described by characterizing and defining reg­
ulons. The term ”regulon” was first presented by Maas and Clark [20] for systems where

and their functions are found in table 2.1. Figure 2.1 illustrates the mechanisms involved

2.2
Gene noise
Gene expression is essentially a result of a series of biochemical reactions demanding
several reactants to meet in time and space. As some of these reactants are found in
a small number of molecules, DNA being the best example of this, the process of gene
expression is bound to have a stochastic nature. Despite this fact, scientists often neglect
this nature with the idea that large numbers average out. But the stochastic processes dur­
ing gene expression can cause great variation between genetically identical cells, which
should not be ignored [2], [34]. This phenomenon of cell to cell variation in gene expres­
sion is called gene noise.
The noisy nature of gene expression has been known since the late 1950s. Novick and
Weiner [35] found that in an induced population of β­galactosidase producing cells, not all
cells produced the protein in equal amounts. Despite this observation, it was first in the

past two decades that the basic mechanisms of gene noise has been investigated more
closely [34], [36]. This work has been helped by the development of methods to monitor
and measure cell populations on single­cell level, which is essential to properly observe
the cell­to­cell variation of a population. The following sections review the basic types and
causes of gene noise.
2.2.1
Intrinsic and extrinsic noise
A number of factors play into to the occurrence of gene noise. One of the early findings
was that gene noise occurred as a result of either intrinsic or extrinsic fluctuation. Intrinsic
fluctuations are defined as those that affect the gene expression of individual genes due to
the stochatic processes of the transcription and translation. This means that the intrinsic
fluctuations would cause two identical promoters to express their genes at different levels
within a cell. Extrinsic fluctuations are those that have a global effect on gene expression.
This could be the number of RNA polymerases, the number of ribosomes, gene copy num­
ber and DNA topology. Extrinsic fluctuations would therefore affect the expression from
the two identical promoters in the same cell the same way [39]–[41]. Figure 2.4.A illus­
trates, how the cell­to­cell variance looks in regards to the expression of two genes from
identical promoters, dependent on either intrinsic or extrinsic factors alone. Taniguchi, et
al.[42] found that gene noise occurs for both highly and lowly expressed genes. Noise
for highly expressed genes tends to be more affected by extrinsic fluctuations, whereas
noise for lowly expressed genes is mostly caused by intrinsic fluctuations.
2.2.2
Transcriptional and translational bursts
The bursts that occur during transcription and translation influence the cell’s gene expres­
sion and are one of the factors that contribute to cell to cell variance. Bursts are defined as
variations in the production of mRNA and proteins. mRNA and proteins are not produced
at equal levels over time but the production is affected by on and off stages, creating this
variation. Figure 2.4.B illustrates how transcriptional bursts could look. Ozbudak et al..
[43] showed that the level of noise was inversely dependent on the rate of transcription.
They found that at low rates of transcription and high rates of translation the variation in
gene expression was high. At this point, transcription was thought to occur in a constitutive
fashion, also called a one­state model, following a Poisson distribution of mRNA numbers.
This meant that the variation of gene expression was thought to be a result of translational
bursts from a constant number of mRNA transcripts [34], [44]. But as the later study from
Golding, et al. [45] showed, mRNA transcripts in E. coli are also produced in bursts. This
is a result of transcription following the dynamics of a two­state model, switching between
an on and off state, where mRNA is produced in bursts during the on state. It was later
found that the transcriptional bursts contributed to gene expression noise and that one of
the main parameters is the duration of the individual transcriptional bursts [46].
Mitarai et al. [47] showed that the transcriptional bursts could be a result of negative DNA
supercoiling. This is due to the open complex formation occurring at a higher rate. The
simulation they based this on matched the original findings of transcriptional bursts from
Golding, et al. [45]. The relation between transcriptional bursts and DNA topology has

2.2.3
Promoter dependent noise
In contrast to some of the previously mentioned causes of gene noise, which only seem
to affect noise in a gene­independent manner, Jones, et al. [51] showed that noise was
affected by the specific promoter features. By changing parameters, such as promoter
strength, transcription factor binding strength, and transcription factor copy numbers, it
was shown that it was possible to tune the gene noise levels. Silander, et al. [52] also
found that noise level were controlled by the specific promoter sequence. Promoter de­
pendent noise has also been observed in eukaryotic cells (yeast) [53], [54].
Recently, a study from Urchueguia, et al. [19] proposed that promoter dependent noise is
a result of how a promoter is regulated. They observed this by testing the noise levels of
approximately 2000 native E. coli promoters in a standardized plasmid­based setting for
8 different growth conditions. If the promoter dependent noise was purely mediated by
a promoters sensitivity to global factors, one would expect a promoter to have the same
level of noise across the different conditions. Instead, they found that noise levels of in­
dividual promoters were highly dependent on the conditions. The regulation of genes by
their TFs in the different conditions, therefore, seemed to have a great influence on the
noise levels. They found that genes that were expressed constitutively showed the least
expression noise across all conditions. In contrast to this, regulated genes show addi­
tional noise because of noise propagation from their TFs. As the expression level of a
certain regulated gene is a result of the binding and unbinding of its TFs, the noise in the
expression levels of the TFs will propagate to the noise level of the gene. An illustration
of noise propagation from TFs is found in figure 2.4.C. Urchueguia, et al. [19] showed
that the higher expression noise a promoter showed across the conditions, the more TFs
seemed to be involved in its regulation. The study suggests that the structure of a gene
regulation network is not only determining for the mean expression levels of genes but
also their expression noise due to the noise propagation from the TFs [19].
2.2.4
Genome architecture
Another parameter that influences gene noise is the spatial dynamics of the reactants in
the regulatory network. The physical distance between a TF and its targets can play a
role in expression noise [37]. In order to gain a full picture of the dynamics of a gene
regulatory network, it has to be considered that cells are three­dimensional systems. The
fact that TFs have to travel from their own site of expression to the promoters they target,
creates an additional level of stochasticity to a regulation network. As this way of travel
is not controlled, the diffusion from the expression site of a TF happens at random. This
means that the chance of a TF finding its target decreases when the distance increases.

it is hard, if not impossible, to predict the actual spacial distance between two genes. That
the structure of the nucleoid is also condition dependent only complicates the matter [55].
2.2.5
The purpose of noise
One might assume that gene noise is purely a disadvantage for a cell due to a waste of
resources. Following this thought, gene noise should be decreased as much as possible.
But research suggests that gene noise is not an necessarily unwanted side effect of gene
expression. Wolf, et al. [56] found through evolution of random promoter sequences, that
the promoters generally exhibited a low level of gene noise if they were selected for a
particular expression level. In fact, the noise level of these artificially selected promoters
was generally lower than that of native E. coli promoters. This suggests that natural
selection must have favored increased levels of gene noise for certain promoters in E.
coli. Others have found that highly conserved genes and genes with essential functions
show low gene expression noise [52], [57]. This suggests that there has been a selection
pressure to reduce cell­to­cell variation for fundamental cell functions. It also confirms that
gene noise is a function that can be selected for. Therefore, it has to be assumed that
the gene noise can have positive effects for a cell population or even be an indispensable
component for the proper function of certain regulatory networks. An important function
of gene noise could be to allow the individual cells to explore different phenotypes which
may increase the population’s chance of survival in specific conditions [2], [19], [56].

2.2.6
Measuring gene noise
As noise is defined as variations in expression levels between cells in a genetically identi­
cal population, single­cell measurements are needed for accurate measurements of gene
noise. This can be done by measurements of gene expression in flow cytometry and flu­
orescent microscopy [2], [19], [36], [37], [43], [45], [46], [58]. Gene noise from promot­
ers can be measured by expression of fluorescent proteins, as, for example, shown by
Urchueguia, et al. [19], who measured noise levels of native E. coli promoters based on
their expression of green fluorescent protein (GFP) from plasmid based constructs with
flow cytometry. A more direct approach could be measuring the mRNA levels directly by
binding of fluorescent RNA target probes as is done in methods like Fluorescence In Situ
Hybridization (FISH) [1], [45], [46], [58]. The FISH method was used for the discovery of
transcriptional bursts in E. coli by Golding, et al. [45]. They used the RNA bacteriophage
MS2 coat protein fussioned with GFP to measure the mRNA levels of an RNA reporter
consisting of 92 repeated binding sites to the MS2 coat protein. This way, they were able
to measure the mRNA levels of the RNA reporter by fluorescent microscopy. Later, pro­
tocols have been developed where the mRNA targeting probes are fluorescent labeled
synthetic DNA probes, allowing for a wider range of mRNA targets [58].
From these types of measurements, gene noise can be expressed as a coefficient of
variation (CV). CV is defined as the standard deviation (σ) relative to the mean (µ) of a
stochastic variable like mRNA copies or proteins per cell in a cell population [2].
In this thesis, gene noise will be evaluated based on single cell measurements of GFP

2.2.7
Noise and synthetic biology
In the above sections, the known causes of gene noise have been reviewed. Whether or
not gene noise is seen as an opportunity or a hindering when it comes to synthetic gene
regulation networks is up to the individual scientist to decide. But it is, without doubt, an
aspect that needs to be taken into consideration when designing these networks. Per­
spectives as promoter noise, TF noise propagation, gene location and TF­Target distance
should all be considered and incorporated in the design, no matter the purpose of the ge­
netic system. Gene noise seems to often be a concept that is neglected in genetic designs,
as scientist tend to only look at means of a population [34]. But maybe a more careful
consideration of gene noise and population heterogeneity could help making fermenta­
tion processes more robust. Possibly much is to be won by more careful considerations
of gene noise. The pros and cons of gene noise in synthetic biology will be elaborated on
in the discussion.
2.3
Genome Engineering of E. coli
Genome engineering is an important tool to study phenomenons like gene noise. It al­
lows scientists to alter the genome and, thereby, modifies cell functions by deletions and
insertions of genes, reporter cassettes and more. In this thesis, E. coli strains have been
constructed to study gene noise when the spatial distance between a TF and it targets
is altered. To perform these modifications, λ­Red recombination has been used together
with three different selection and counter­selection methods. The Standardized Genome
Architecture (SEGA) system by Bayer, et al. [59] has been used for the construction
of integration cassettes. Furthermore, this thesis includes the initial steps for testing a
high­throughput approach to genome engineering of strain libraries.
2.3.1
λ­Red recombination
The λ­phage derived recombination system allows for simple integration or gene deletions
based on homologous recombination. The system consists of the three proteins, Beta,
Exo, and Gam, that assist the homologous recombination of a single or double stranded
DNA insert (ssDNA or dsDNA). Figure 2.6 illustrates the proposed mechanism of this
process.
The system can both be used for insertion of genes, by providing a dsDNA gene cassette
with homology for the insertion region, as well as deletion of a gene, by providing a long
oligo (often 90­mers) with homology to each site of the gene [60].
In this thesis, two different plasmid systems for expression of the λ­Red proteins have
been used. For gene deletions an unpublished plasmid system from Brain Pfleger lab,
University of Wisconsin­Madison, has been used. The system allows for integration of
ssDNA by expressing the Beta protein and includes CRISPR/Cas9 for counter­selection.
CRISPR/Cas9 based counter­selection is reviewed in section 2.3.2. The expression of
the Beta protein is under control of the arabinose inducible promoter from the araBAD

guide RNA (gRNA) by the transformation of a gRNA plasmid. In this thesis, the system
will be referenced to as the Pfleger system. For gene insertions the pSIM19 plasmid

2.3.2
Selection and counter­selection
Selection markers, such as antibiotic resistance markers, have for long been essential
to allow selection of the successful recombination events. Unfortunately, the co­insertion
of these markers later often requires their removal to avoid the unnecessary burdens or
to allow for another round of genome engineering. Counter­selection markers, which uti­
lize the selection of cells that have lost the marker, have been used for these purposes.
This includes markers as the tetracycline resistance gene tetA. By integration of a gene
cassette with tetA, one allows for selection of modified cells by growing the transforma­
tion culture on tetracycline. For removal of tetA, one can select for cells that have lost
the gene by plating on a media containing nickel salts [59], [63]. As the removal of tetA is
done by recombination with a DNA oligo, scars can be avoided. Another counter­selection
marker used in this thesis is galK. galK encodes the galactokinase, which catalyzes the
phosphorylation of galactose to galactose­1­phophate. The galactokinase is also able to
catalyze the phosphorylation of the galactose analog, 2­deoxy­galactose (DOG). As 2­
deoxy­galactose­1­phosphate cannot be metabolised further, it leads to an toxic buildup

[64]. Thereby galK can be utilized as a selection and counter­selection marker in the
same manners as tetA, as long as the native galK gene is knocked out from the back­
ground strain.
Despite the possibilities that selection and counter­selection markers offer, the use of
them often requires multiple rounds of genome engineering, which can be very time­
consuming. Furthermore, the use of these markers is not ideal when it comes to recom­
bination of smaller DNA fragments, such as oligos, for gene deletion or smaller base­pair
changes, which do not allow for co­integration of a marker. Therefore the recent use of
CRISPR/Cas9 (short for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) for
counter­selection has been of great interest.
CRISPR/Cas9 can be utilized for counter­selection, as the Cas9 endonuclease mediates
dsDNA breaks at the sequence targeted by the gRNA. As a dsDNA break in the genome is
lethal for E. coli cells, CRIPSR/Cas9 can be used to kill the cells that have not undergone
the desired modification [65]. This is done by designing the gRNA to target the unmodified
sequence. This could be the gene for deletion or the codon that should be changed.
2.3.3
High­throughput approach to genome engineering of strain libraries
A proper design of the constructs within synthetic biology is important to avoid waste
of time and resources. Sometimes the lack of knowledge about a complex biological
system overrules the possibilities of perfect rational designs. In some cases it might,
therefore, be beneficial to explore a wide range of possible designs and screen for the
most optimal once. This could, for example, be relevant when choosing the best locus for
protein expression or, as in this thesis, exploring the effects of TF relocation to different
genome locations. In these cases it could be an advantage to construct many strains
and screen for interesting phenotypes. As both the design and the construction process
of gene insertion into multiple different loci is time consuming, it was decided to use the
Keio collection (see text box) for the construction of malT relocated strains. The Keio
cassette allows for an identical design of the malT reinsertion cassette across all Keio
strains, because the homology sequences used for the integration can be reused for all
targeted strains and thereby all genome locations. The Keio collection would therefore be
a great resource for exploring different loci for TF relocation in a more random and high­
throughput manner. By pooling the Keio collection strains, or fractions of it, one could
potentially integrate a gene cassette into multiple locations in the different strains at once.
The first steps to investigate this possibility of genome engineering on strain libraries,
were taken in this thesis. A small library of five Keio strains was used to test the coverage
of the library throughout multiple rounds of genome engineering.

The Keio collection
The Keio collection from Baba, et al. [66] is a collection of 3985 single non­essential

gene is replaced by a kanamycin resistance cassette. The kanamycin resistance
gene is flanked by two flipase recognition target (FRT) sites, which allows for easy
removal of the cassette only leaving an 81 base pair long scar. The Keio collection
has not only been a resource for basic knowlegde of the gene knock­out effect, but
can also serve as a possible resource for high­throughput strain engineering.

Media and growth conditions
For growth in liquid media, E. coli strains were inoculated in lysogeny broth (LB) [70] and

µg/µl ampicillin (Amp), 50 µg/µl kanamycin (Kan), 34 µg/µl chloramphenicol (Cm), 50µg/µl
spectinomycin (Spec) and 10 or 25 µg/µl tetracycline (Tet).
Super optimal broth (SOC) media, which were used for recovery after transformations,
contained 20 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, 10 mM NaCl, 2.5 mM KCl, 10 mM MgCl2,
10 mM MgSO4 and 20 mM glucose.

containing: 68 g/L Na2PO4, 30 g/L KH2PO4, 5 g/L NaCl, and 10 g/L NH4Cl. The M9 salts
solution was diluted 10 times in MilliQ water and was mixed with 100 µM CaCl2, 2 mM

at 10°C. Incubation was performed in C1000 Touch and S1000 Thermal Cyclers (Bio­Rad,
USA). Primers with USER overhangs were designed to have a melting temperature be­
tween 20°C and 25°C. USER reactions were transformed into chemical competent DH5α
as described in section 3.2.10

4.1
Construction of malT relocated strains ­ a pooled
approach for genome engineering
The MalT iModulon was chosen as a subject to investigate the effect of gene noise on
population fitness and phenotype. It was expected that gene noise would occur if the
gene for the TF MalT was relocated to more distant locations from the four operons and
genes, which it is regulating.
The five loci, atoB, lacY, melB, wbbL, and ykgH, were chosen for malT relocation and
their respective Keio strains, described in section 2.3.3, were used to construct the malT
relocated strains. The location of the five loci are shown in figure 4.1.A. The genome engi­
neering group has experience working with the specific strains. Most recently, the strains
have been used for the publication on the Standardized Genome Architecture (SEGA)
[59]. As a part of this research, the expression levels in the different loci were investi­
gated. The five loci cover a variety of expression levels as seen in figure 4.1.B. Lastly,
the loci are divided over the genome with two pairs located relatively close to each other.
Especially the lacY and ykgH loci are an interesting pair to investigate. Despite the fact
that they are so close to each other, they show very different levels of expression. The low
expression levels in the ykgH loci was found to be associated with high protein occupancy
[71], [72]. Table 4.1 contains a short summary of the functions of Keio knock­out genes.
Figure 4.1: A: Blue: Location of the loci for malT relocation on the E. coli BW25113 genome. Green: Loci of
the genes in the MalT iModulon B: Unpublished data obtained by Anja Ehrmann. GFP expression of the five
chosen loci normalized to OD measured after 7h (n = 3 biological replicates).
To construct these malT relocated strains, three steps of genome engineering were needed.
First, the malT gene was removed using λ­Red recombination and counter­selection with

4.2.2
Rates of maltose depletion
MacConkey plate assays were used to study the depletion rate of maltose for the malT re­
located strains. MacConkey agar contains a pH indicator that turns red when the present
carbon source is fermented. In this way, it is possible to identify colonies that can utilize
the carbon source of interest. From titration experiments of different maltose concentra­
tions in the MacConkey agar, it was found that the red color disappeared after a certain
amount of time when the plates had a lower maltose concentration (less than 0.4% w/v).
This loss of red color was interpreted as the maltose being depleted. It was therefore
hypothesized that it would be possible to estimate the rate of maltose depletion of a strain

OD600 normalized cultures were plated on MacConkey agar with with 0.2% w/v maltose.

Gene noise levels ­ Target relocation
The previous results presented are not direct measurements of gene noise, but rather
phenotypic differences as a result of the disruption of the MalT iModulon spatial architec­
ture when malT is relocated. One possible explanation for the observed differences could
be population heterogeneity of the strains, possibly due to noise in the gene expression
of the MalT iModulon genes. This next section will look more directly at gene noise by
measurements at single­cell level. This is done to test the hypothesis that TF­Target dis­
tance influences the gene expression noise in a regulatory network.
To test this, five strains were constructed by integrating a GFP expressing cassette into
each of the five Keio locations. The expression of GFP was under control of the MalT
activated promoter from the malPQ operon (PmalP). malT was thereby expressed from
the original locus, and the TF­Target distance depended on the Keio loci. Figure 4.15
show an illustration of the construction.

Discussion
Genome engineering of strain libraries
A small strain library of five Keio strains was tested to investigate the coverage of the
library throughout rounds of engineering. Two different plasmid systems utilizing λ­Red
recombination were used in combination with either CRISPR/Cas9 counter­selection or
tetA selection. Both systems resulted in most, if not all, of the screened strains to be
successfully engineered. The coverage of the library varied and big differences in strain
distributions were found between replicates. This is possibly caused by the different Keio
gene knock­outs as well as variations in integration efficiency. Bayer et al. [59] showed
that the integration efficiency can vary for different genome locations. They also observed
big variations between integration efficiencies in each replication.
In this thesis, it was observed that the coverage of a library decreased for each round of
engineering. Therefore, this library engineering approach should probably be limited to
one or two rounds of engineering before the individual strains should be recovered.
The presented library engineering approach is most likely not suited for designs where a
set of specific strains has to be constructed, as it should be expected that some strains
will be lost along the way. Furthermore, the work of identifying the strain after engineering
is tedious and requires a large setup for screening with colony PCR. One can easily use
the same time on identifying the strains as the individual constructions would have taken,
depending on the library size.
Despite these drawbacks, the library approach might be interesting for constructions that
do not require a set collection of strains. It might allow one to move away from the rational
design of choosing a smaller number of strains to test. If the construction can be limited to
one or two rounds of engineering a good coverage of the library could be achieved. The
approach should be combined with a good method for screening. It might not be feasible
to try to identify all colonies after transformation. Instead a screening for interesting phe­
notypes could be performed. A possible example of how this approach could be beneficial
would be to use it for the construction of more strains to test the gene noise dependence
of TF­Target distance as performed in section 4.3. With this approach a large Keio strain
library could be engineered by inserting the PmalP­gfp cassette targeted at the Keio cas­
sette. Colonies could be picked and screened for population variance and interesting
colonies could be identified. This approach of engineering on strain libraries could allow
for a screening of way more loci than possible through engineering of individual strains.
Relocation of malT causes phenotypic changes
Four malT relocated strains were constructed and investigated for phenotypic changes
compared to their respective parental Keio strains. The phenotypes were tested based on
growth rates (section 4.2.1), maltose depletion rates on MacConkey plate assays (section
4.2.2) and fitness benefits during competition assay (section 4.2.3).
Phenotypic changes were clearly observed for some of the malT relocated strains during
Exploring the spatial architecture of gene regulation networks with synthetic biology
49
==PAGE:60==
the MacConkey plate assays. The changes of the red color of the colonies over time was
interpreted as the maltose depletion rates. However, since there might be other ways of
interpreting these color changes, this interpretation should be validated by other experi­
ments. The observations of these color changes was made possible due to the imaging
system from Reshape Biotech. This added a time parameter to the MacConkey assay
and thereby allowed for phenotypic observations that have previously not been possible.
It was observed that the relocation does not automatically lead to a lower or higher deple­
tion rate, but that it seems to be dependent on the locus malT is relocated to. Relocation
to the atoB locus increased the depletion rate, whereas relocation to the lacY and wbbL
loci decreased the depletion rate. Furthermore, the relocation to the melB locus did not
seem to influence the depletion rate significantly. It should be noticed that the selection
marker tetA was not removed from the ∆lacY strain. The presence of tetA likely has a
strong influence on the observed outcome of the experiment and it is, therefore, hard to
conclude anything about the effect of relocation to the lacY locus.
The observations from the competition assay supported the observations of maltose de­
pletion rates, as the strains with higher depletion rates seem to have an competitive ad­
vantage. This seems to be logical, as a strain with a higher depletion rate should be
able to utilize the maltose faster and thereby outcompete a strain with a lower maltose
depletion rate. Interestingly, the observations of growth rates do not find any significant
differences between the malT relocated strains and the respective parental strains when
grown on maltose. The only exception to this is for the ∆atoB strains, where the malT
relocated strain has a lower growth rate than its parental strain. This does not match with
the previous findings which show that the malT relocated strain has a fitness benefit over
its parental strain. It might indicate that growth rates in the exponential phase are not a
key indicator for which strain has a higher depletion rate or a fitness benefit. Other growth
phases, such as the lag phase might tell more about where the mechanisms of the fitness
benefits occur. One could imagine that a strain with a shorter lag phase would show a
fitness benefit as it would be able to start the catabolism of maltose faster.
Protein abundance of MalT does not explain the phenotypic observations
The strains were analyzed by proteomics to verify the expression of MalT in the relocated
strains and to investigate possible differences in expression levels depending on the lo­
cus of MalT expression. No significant differences in MalT abundance were found. This
is surprising as the loci for malT relocation have previously been shown to have differ­
ent expression levels, as described in section 4.1. Furthermore, the negative control,
BW25113 grown in glucose, had the same levels of MalT as the strains grown in maltose.
This should not be the case as the Crp activation of transcription for malT is dependent
on cAMP, that should only be present in the absence of glucose [31]. It might indicate that
the levels of MalT observed present a baseline level and that full expression of MalT is
only activated at a later stage of the growth cycle. It would, however, be strange to imag­
ine that the strains would not utilize the available maltose during the exponential growth.
Despite the equal levels of MalT it was possible to see significant differences in abun­

dance of some proteins from the MalT regulon between the relocated strains and their
parental strain. This was most noticible for the ∆wbbL strains, where the malT relocated
strain shows a significant lower abundance of MalP. One could think that this might explain
the lower maltose depletion rate, as this would be a logical following of the lower protein
levels, but it does not seem to correlate with the findings for the other strains. It would,
for example, be expected that the malT relocated ∆atoB strain had a higher abundance
of MalP, however, this is no the case. This shows that the observed phenotypes cannot
simply be explained by the abundances of the MalT regulon proteins. Furthermore, it has
to be noted that the proteomics data is only a mean of the population’s protein abundance.
It, as also observed, cannot give an inside of how expression noise and, thereby, popu­
plation heterogenicity affects the phenotypic observations.
From the proteomic dataset presented in this work, there is likely much more information
to be extracted about the relocated strains.However, this would exceed the scope of this
thesis, and further analysis of the dataset is therefore not included. For further analysis it
would also be interesting to analyze samples from later growth phases to see if how the
MalT abundance changes.
Transcription factor to target distance affects gene noise levels
It was possible to observe how the TF­Target distance affects the expression noise using
a GFP reporter which expression was controlled by the malP promoter. The GFP reporter
was integrated into five different loci across the genome, after which multiple TF­Target
distances could be investigated. The observations were based on single­cell measure­
ments using flow cytometry and could therefore be used for direct observations of gene
noise. The gene noise levels were compared to strains with GFP reporters integrated into
the same loci, which expression was controlled by a constitutive promoter. The overall
noise levels were higher for the malP promoter, which was not surprising as Wolf et al.
[56] have shown that promoters regulated by TFs generally show higher noise levels. The
strains that expressed GFP from a constitutive promoter exhibited equal levels of noise
across all five loci. This is an important control which suggests that the differences in
expression noise, observed between malP promoter strains, is not a characteristic fea­
ture of the particular expression locus, but is instead dependent on the promoter. The
differences in expression noise are therefore expected to be connected to the difference
in TF­Target distance. As the amount of TF that is present around the target promoter is
dependent on the random diffusion of the TF from its expression site, the chance of having
a higher amount of TF around the target promoter increases as the TF­Target distance
decreases.
Similiar observations of the TF­Target distance effect on expression noise were made by
Goñi­Moreno, et al. [37]. They were able to measure very large variations in expression,
and explain these findings by including the spatial dynamics of the TF to their model. It
should, however, be noticed that their gene regulation network is very different from the
one studied in this thesis. An interesting note is, also, that both the TF in their regulation
network and the TF studied in this thesis are activators. The dynamics of repressors might

==PAGE:62==
affect expression noise in a different way when relocated.
In general one should be careful when measuring expression noise using flow cytometry,
as the system in itself carries a certain degree of noise. A thoughtful process for the data
analysis should therefore be preformed before a conclusion on small differences in noise
can be drawn. In this thesis, the noise from the flow cytometry system was accounted for
by only looking at the 95­percentiles of the fluorescent events. This way it was possible
to avoid outliers, which were likely due to technical noise from the flow cytometry system.
Galbusera, et al. [75] have created an R software package designed to remove the tech­
nical noise from flow cytometry datasets. They have also used it in their latest publication
Urchueguia, et al. [19] where they investigate the noise levels of native E. coli promoters
across different conditions. For further examinations on how TF­Target distance affects
expression noise, where the GFP reporter could be integrated into a wider range of ge­
nomic locations, a different solution to account for the technical noise from flow cytometry
should be considered. A possible solution could be the R package from Galbusera, et al.
[75].
Spatial architecture is an important aspect in gene regulation networks
It would be interesting to investigate whether the observed differences in expression noise,
based on the TF­target distance, could explain the phenotypic difference observed in the
malT relocated strains. A noticeable difference in maltose depletion rates and fitness
benefit was observed for the strain where malT was relocated to the wbbL locus. For
this locus the highest noise level was observed when GFP was expressed from the malP
promoter. Even though the two systems are not directly comparable, it can be speculated
whether the increased expression noise causes the phenotypic differences observed. It
would, in this case, be expected to see similar differences in expression noise for the
other three loci, which could explain the phenotypic observations. For example, it would
be expected that the ∆atoB strain had a lower noise level than the ∆melB strain, which
is not the case.
Despite the finding that the TF­Target distance does affect expression noise levels, it does
not tell us how the noise levels change for the individual genes in the MalT regulon. The
relocation of malT could very well affect the genes differently and, as it is not known which
genes are most important for the phenotypic changes observed, it is is hard to extract the
exact mechanisms. One thing that could possibly help, would be to find the noise levels of
the native regulation network. This could be done by measuring the noise levels of a GFP
reporter that could be fusioned after the MalT regulon genes. An alternative approach
would be to measure the noise levels directly on mRNA level. This could be done with
a method like Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH), as e.g. shown in Skinner, et
al. [58]. By targeting the mRNA transcripts of the MalT regulon genes with fluorescent
DNA probes, it would be possible to get a direct output of the noise levels across the
malT relocated strains compared to their parental strains. The first steps for applying this
method was taken during this thesis, but there was, unfortunately, not enough time to
optimize the protocols and obtain results from this.

Not only is it interesting that the disruption of a gene regulation network, by the relocation
of a TF, can lead to such visible changes in population phenotype, but it is also intriguing
that this disruption does not always seem to be a disadvantage. In the case of malT
relocation to the atoB locus, the relocated strain seems to have gained a fitness advantage
over its parental strain, possibly due to the higher rate of maltose depletion. The question
is whether the relocation overall results in an advantage for the cell compared to the native
systems. As the gene regulation networks are possibly more intertwined than expected,
the relocation of a TF might have effects during other conditions that can not be foreseen.
An example of this was found by Mitchell, et al. [76]. They showed that some of the MalT
regulon genes were expressed when the cells catabolize lactose. This can possibly have
an evolutionary explanation, where E. coli first meets lactose in the digestive system of
animals and later meets other polysaccharides such as maltose and maltodextrin. It once
more highlight how the lack of knowledge in these mechanism hinders our understanding
of the observations. This is why it crucial to obtain a deeper understanding of these
systems to improve the design of the synthetic networks.
Gene noise and synthetic biology
It is often argued how the expression systems within synthetic biology should be tightly
regulated to gain full control [4]. The noise in gene expression is therefore often thought
of as something that should be eliminated or minimized. Already early in the fundamental
studies on gene noise, it was found that expression noise could be limited by negative
feedback regulation [77]. This use of negative feedback regulation is also used in newer
designs of synthetic gene regulation networks to limit the noise levels [78]. Although many
efforts are laid into these feedback regulation system and they might decrease the varia­
tion of a population, they give no guarantee to how the individual cell responds in certain
conditions. Studies on how to control single cells within a population using optical or
chemical inputs were performed [79], [80]. These, again, try to push cells within a popula­
tion into showing the same phenotype. The question is whether, instead of undermining
the phenomenon of noise by only seeking to limit it, it could be embraced as a factor to to
help control cell factories and exploit a different set of phenotypes. Gene noise is, after
all, a cell function that most likely suits a purpose in the native systems. It is, for example,
the only way a population of unicellular organisms can display heterogeneity [34]. May,
et al. [81] have recently suggested a model for how noise can be used to drive cells in a
population to show different, but specific, phenotypes with a single input. This is a great
example of how noise within gene expression does not always have to be hindering a
good design, but can, in fact, be utilized to benefit it.

Conclusion
In this thesis, four malT relocated strains were constructed to study the effect of disrupting
the spatial architecture of the gene regulation network of the MalT iModulon. The effect
of these relocations was investigated through observations of the population phenotype
during growth on maltose. Three of the four malT relocated strains showed a different
phenotype from their respective parental strains. Interestingly, the disruption of the spa­
tial architecture of the MalT regulation network did not only seem to be a disadvantage.
The relocation to the atoB locus increased the rate of maltose depletion and the strain
showed to have a fitness benefit compared to its parental strain. Whether these obser­
vations are the same during other conditions still remains unclear. Therefore, an obvious
continuation of this study would be to investigate growth on maltodextrin. Additionally, it
would be interesting to repeat the competition assay and get a quantitative output. This
could help investigate whether the concentration of maltose has an effect on the fitness
benefit of the strains. Furthermore, a novel way to study phenotypic changes was intro­
duced by monitoring the MacConkey plate assay over time. This allowed for new insights
on how maltose is utilized by the strains. The image analysis became a useful tool and
potentially holds much more information on the dynamics of different phenotypes.
Direct measurements of gene noise were conducted using a GFP reporter activated by
MalT. This showed that the TF­Target distance had an effect on expression noise levels,
based on five genomic placements of the GFP reporter. Besides these not being direct
measurements on noise levels in the malT relocated strains, they gave the indication that
expression noise is involved in the phenotypic changes that were observed. This high­
lights that there is still much more to be resolved in the question of how TF­Target distance
affects expression noise and how exactly this noise influences the population phenotype.
Furthermore, this thesis includes the first steps of testing an approach for genome engi­
neering of strain libraries. The approach proved to be successful for engineering a small
library of five strains. Although this approach might not be optimal for all procedures in
genome engineering, it can possibly provide a useful method for further studying of the
gene noise mechanisms.
