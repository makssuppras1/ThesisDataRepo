Abstract 
Luminescence-based image inspection techniques are considered a very powerful and efficient 
characterization method for PV modules, that can provide accurate and effective information 
for PV cells & modules and basically identify most of the degradation modes around them. 
These luminescence techniques, Electroluminescence (EL) or Photoluminescence (PL), not 
only allow the identification of a variety of failures but also the assumption of causation and 
severity verification of these degradation modes. For this particular reason, these techniques 
are highly desirable to inspect solar PV modules to ensure reliable and high-power production 
as well as to provide accurate feedback to the solar PV industry for future technological 
decisions and improvements. 
Specifically for photoluminescence, either indoor or outdoor, has contributed significantly to 
the technological improvement of Operations & Maintenance (O&M), general failure detection, 
and the preventive maintenance of the solar PV industry, since it is capable to inspect the quality 
of solar cells throughout their whole value chain, from manufacturing until installation and 
operation.  
The main objective of this thesis is to implement a new novel approach, called Non-Invasive 
outdoor photoluminescence imaging for solar PV inspection, a method that takes advantage the 
ability of the state-of-the-art string inverter to sweep the I-V curve. Generally, this technique 
implies by switching between the module's two operating points, the bigger the difference in 
voltage between the two operating points, the bigger the difference in the photoluminescence 
(ΔPL) and the better the image quality for deeper and further investigation. This maximum ΔPL 
is obtained through string inverter modulation between open-circuit (OC) and maximum power 
point (MPP) conditions. This default function of the inverter to sweep the I-V curve results to 
the minimization of the acquisition time and the technical expertise required for PL imaging in 
solar PV panels inspection. The acquired PL images will be then processed through specific 
image processing techniques and the characterization of the final PL images will take place to 
find current or potential failures in the PV module which will be analyzed further in detail.  
Additionally, as long as the technique does not require external equipment or extra cables for 
the investigation on the module or cell level, innovative theoretical approaches will be 
mentioned for extended real-life scenarios, like outdoor PL inspections with unmanned aerial 
vehicles, or drones mounted with the appropriate inspection camera(s), for future applications 
in large scale solar PV farms. 
The thesis consists of five chapters. The initial two chapters provide an introduction and a 
comprehensive literature review, encompassing an overview of photovoltaic systems and the 
current situation in the global PV market, various maintenance approaches, common 
degradation modes and failures, and an exploration of existing module inspection techniques. 
The third chapter focuses on the experimental methodology, covering a detailed description of 
the methodology, the equipment used and the detailed experimental plan for each conducted 
experiment. Moving forward, the fourth chapter entails the data analysis and interpretation of 
results obtained from the acquired PL image datasets during the experiments conducted 
outdoor. Lastly, the fifth and final chapter summarizes the key conclusions and outcomes 
derived from the outdoor photoluminescence technique for preventive maintenance in solar PV 
systems with the aid of the installed on-field equipment. Additionally, it includes suggestions 
for future research and highlights the potential for applying aerial photoluminescence imaging 
of photovoltaic modules in extended real-life scenarios.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Current Situation in the Photovoltaic Market 
With the constant increase of countries that pledge to achieve net zero emissions in alignment 
with the Paris Climate Agreement, it becomes apparent that there is a great global urgency to 
reduce CO2 emissions and accelerate the green transition towards sustainable energy and fuel 
production. Paris Agreement is the first-ever legally binding international treaty on climate 
change which aims to keep global warming well below 2°C compared to pre-industrial levels 
while striving to further limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C [1]. Solar PV is a crucial pillar 
of clean energy transitions worldwide, underpinning efforts to reach international energy and 
climate goals. Putting the world on a path to reaching net zero emissions requires solar PV to 
expand globally on an even greater scale, raising concerns about the security of manufacturing 
supply for achieving such rapid growth rates – but also offering new opportunities for 
diversification [2]. 
Solar photovoltaic (PV) energy has become an increasingly important source of renewable 
energy in recent years due to its many benefits, including its ability to reduce greenhouse gas 
emissions, its potential for decentralization and its low maintenance costs. In fact, solar PV has 
become the fastest-growing energy source in the world, with the global solar PV capacity 
increasing from just 2.6 GW in 2000 to over 750 GW in 2020, 940 GW by the end of 2021, and 
the 1-Terawatt milestone already had been achieved one year ago, in May 2022. The Compound 
Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of cumulative PV installations was 30% between the decade 
2011 to 2021. PV energy is expected to play an increasingly important role in the global energy 
mix in the coming years, as countries continue their transition to more sustainable and 
renewable energy sources. According to the International Energy Agency, solar PV is set to 
become the largest source of electricity generation worldwide by 2050, with a potential capacity 
of up to 8,000 GW [3,4].  
Three years characterized by continuing COVID-19 waves and economic recovery programs, 
as well as an energy crisis that brought record-high electricity prices around the world, solar 
solutions have seen a constant rise concerning countries’ usage for their energy needs. Figure 
1 illustrates the net renewable power generating capacity installed in 2021.

The aim of the International Technology Roadmap for Photovoltaic (ITRPV) is to inform 
suppliers and customers about anticipated technology trends in the crystalline silicon (c-Si) 
based photovoltaic industry and to stimulate discussions on required improvements and 
standards. The global c-Si cell and PV module production capacity have further increased to 
over 470 GWp due to continued capacity expansions, and the market share of about 95% for 
the c-Si and about 5% for thin-film technologies is assumed to remain unchanged for the 
upcoming decades [6]. In 2021 producers from Asia count for 94% of total c-Si PV module 
production. China holds the lead with a share of 75%. Europe contributed with a share of 1%; 
USA/CAN with 3%. The Energy Payback Time of PV systems is highly dependent on the 
geographical location, for example PV systems produced in Europe and installed in Northern 
Europe need around 1.1 years to balance the input energy, while PV systems in the South equal 
their energy input after 0.9 years, depending on the technology installed and the grid efficiency 
[4].  
The growth of the solar PV market can be attributed to several factors, including improvements 
in technology, lower costs, and increasing awareness of the environmental and economic 
benefits of solar energy. Advances in PV technology have led to higher efficiency, lower 
manufacturing costs, improved durability, supportive policies and regulations, and increasing 
demand from consumers and businesses for clean energy, to make solar energy more accessible 
and affordable to a wider range of consumers. In addition, innovations in solar technology, such 
as bifacial modules, floating solar systems, and integrated energy storage, are expected to 
further boost the efficiency and competitiveness of solar PV systems in the following years 
[6,7,8].  
Despite these advances, however, solar PV systems can still experience performance issues and 
require maintenance to ensure optimal performance. This is particularly important in large-
scale PV power plants, where small efficiency losses can have significant economic and 
environmental impacts. As the solar PV market continues to grow, effective operation and 
maintenance strategies will become increasingly important to ensure the highest performance 
and reliability of PV systems. One approach to maintaining the performance of PV power plants 
is through preventive maintenance techniques, which involve regular monitoring and analysis 
of the system's performance to identify and address any issues before they become major 
problems [7]. Contactless acquisition of outdoor photoluminescence images during inverter I-
V sweeps is one potential method for monitoring PV system performance, allowing for the 
early detection of issues such as potential induced degradation (PID), cell cracks, and other 
defects that can impact energy production and system reliability. It has to be mentioned that 
nowadays, inverter efficiency for state-of-the-art brand products is 98% and higher, with string 
inverters conquering the market by holding approximately 65% of the market share [4]. 
Moreover, critical advances in digital technologies, such as artificial intelligence, machine 
learning, and big data analytics, are expected to play a major role in this regard, enabling more 
efficient and effective monitoring, analysis, and maintenance of PV systems.  
For example, predictive maintenance techniques, such as the use of digital twins and predictive 
analytics, can help identify and address potential issues before they occur, reducing downtime 
and maintenance costs [7]. In addition, new monitoring technologies, such as drone or 
unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV)-based inspections, are expected to further improve the 
accuracy and speed of PV system inspections and maintenance. 
In summary, the solar PV market is poised for continued growth in the coming years, driven by 
falling technology costs, supportive policies, and new innovations in solar technology [8]. 
Effective operation and maintenance strategies will be essential to ensure the long-term 
performance and reliability of PV systems. High volume and high-quality PV module

2.1 Photovoltaic Technologies: A General Review 
2.1.1 Photovoltaic Systems 
Photovoltaic (PV) systems convert sunlight directly to electricity using semiconductor 
materials – solar cells [9,10]. A photovoltaic system does not necessarily need clear and bright 
sunlight to function, but it can also generate electricity on cloudy or even rainy days from the 
reflected sunlight [10].  
Nowadays, is one of the most rapidly growing and important renewable energy sources, 
alternatives to conventional fossil fuel electricity generation, but compared to other electricity 
generating technologies, it is a relative newcomer with the first practical and usable devices 
demonstrated almost 70 years ago, in the 1950s. In the last decades, the increasing market for, 
and profile of photovoltaics, means that they have become a huge business throughout the 
renewable energy world with more than 100 GW to be deployed each year. Photovoltaic 
systems are now often deployed with batteries attached with the ultimate aim that the system 
can continue providing electricity even after sunset [9].  
Presently, various different types of PV technologies are industrially available, however, there 
are two major types that conquer the solar PV market, and these types are Crystalline silicon 
and Thin-Film PV technologies [11]. The current PV market is mostly dominated by crystalline 
silicon (c-Si). c-Si technology is further divided into monocrystalline Si (mono-Si) and multi-
crystalline (mc-Si) with a percentage of about 87% of the market share [6,11,12]. Because of 
the need for continuous reduction of cost, the mono-Si market increased to 75% whereas mc-
Si holds 25% of the share in 2022 [6]. It is widely assumed and predicted that silicon-based 
technology will continue to lead the solar PV market for the next decade. Figure 2 demonstrates 
the evolution of the PV technologies market share in the last 6 years. The market share of about 
95% for the c-Si and about 5% for thin-film technologies is also assumed to remain unchanged 
[6].

2.2 Degradation Modes & Failures in PV systems  
In the following subchapter, the most common and important PV module failures are described. 
In the PV dictionary, failure and degradation modes are used interchangeably to describe an 
irreversible effect impacting the performance of a PV module [9].  
Due to the fact that a PV system has no moving parts, a PV module’s operating life may be 
determined by the stability and the resistance to corrosion of the materials from which it is 
constructed [9]. There is a large variety of degradation and failure modes that could potentially 
affect fielded PV modules. A PV module failure is an effect that degrades the module power 
which is not reversed by normal operation or creates a safety issue [17]. Considering the 
relevance of PV technology in the power generation system, degradation and failure of 
photovoltaic modules are becoming particularly relevant [15].  
==PAGE:22==

Degradation mechanisms may involve either a gradual reduction in the output power of a PV 
module over time or an overall reduction in power due to the failure of an individual solar cell 
in the module. There are several failure modes and degradation mechanisms which may reduce 
the power output or cause the module to fail and most of them are influenced by temperature, 
humidity, altitude, thermal cycling, snow load, and air salinity [9, 15].  
2.2.1 Hot Spots 
One of the most common failures that occasionally appear in a PV solar cell are the hot spots. 
As stated before, solar cells in a PV module are connected in series. If one of these cells, or 
even a part of it, produces fewer carriers, the exceeding current produced by the other cells 
forces reverse bias [15]. If a solar cell is reverse biased with a higher voltage than is rated or 
designated for, it will probably result in hot spots. The hot spot phenomenon occurs in the solar 
cells of a PV module either in cases of defective material or due to partial shading and incorrect 
connection(s). Therefore, the phenomenon of the hot spot is not actually a failure on its own, 
but an effect that takes place due to the presence of other failures or DMs. Nevertheless, in all 
cases, a common symptom of the hot spot is the local overheating of the problematic cells, 
which brings as a result, always depending on the severity of the damage, extensive damage 
and/or in the worst cases, total failure and destruction of the PV frame [18].  
The increased temperature affects the materials and escalates other more serious degradation 
mechanisms like the browning of the encapsulation material of the PV module, solder bond and 
ribbon failures, or even burning spots. In order to avoid a solar cell to be reversely biased 
beyond its breakdown voltage, bypass diodes are embedded in PV modules, and short circuit 
less current producing for cell strings [15,17, 18]. Due to its simple detectability by a well-
established in-the-market inspection technique, named Infrared Thermography (IRT), a 
technique which will be analyzed further in the next sub-chapter, hot spots are often reported 
but not further investigated and therefore got the status of a stand-alone failure. Figure 6 
illustrates a visible hot spot in a PV solar cell.

2.2.2 Fractured Cells and Glass Breakages 
Cracks and breakage in solar cells result mainly from mechanical or a combination of 
thermochemical stresses. Specifically, in crystalline silicon cells, cell cracks are observed 
because of their structure and their small thickness while glass breakage can be caused by 
moisture ingress, expansion, or other effects as well as through mechanical loads, such as hail 
or storms.  
Most of the time, glass breakage will probably lead to other failures and degradation modes. 
Moisture ingress has an impact on the total power output of the PV module and creates an 
immediate safety risk which can result in a module failure. Glass breakage is most likely to 
appear in glass-glass modules due to the unprotected glass edges [19].

Due to the very small thickness of the solar cell panel (<0.5 mm), practically all solar cells will 
experience cracks or microcracks during their operational lifetime [18]. In most cases, these 
cell cracks are not visible and noticeable to the naked human eye, however, with the passage of 
time, these cracks tend to become bigger and wider and hence visible. Cell cracks can occur at 
various stages, on the one hand through external impact during the production phase, module 
manufacturing, transportation, installation, or through mechanical loads (snow, wind) during 
PV’s operation phase and on the other hand through internal processes like temperature or heat 
alternations during operation [17,19,20]. On top of that, cell cracks can be categorized

Production is reduced partially.

Depending on the severity of the crack, the component power loss (CPL) of a cracked PV 
module is rated between 1% to 15% and is correlated with the disconnected cell area [15].

result after 3-12 months of operations, depending always on the weather conditions of the

faster. It has been reported that snail trails can result in important performance losses

2.2.3 Encapsulation (EVA) Failures 
Encapsulant discoloration is one of the most manifest degradations found in PV fields. The 
yellowing and browning of the ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) or other encapsulation materials 
is an easily identifiable defect in the PV module area and can be easily visible to the human 
eye. Discoloration reduces the sunlight that penetrates the solar cell. The discoloration is mostly 
caused when the PV modules are exposed to ultraviolet (UV) radiation, high temperatures as 
well as humidity for a long period of time or if the quality of the encapsulating material is low 
[17,20,22]. For severe discoloration, reduced light transmission of the EVA can result in 
reduced short-circuit current and therefore power production, described with a component 
power loss of up to 45% for single solar cells [15]. Figure 9 shows a solar cell with EVA 
discoloration.

Delamination is caused by the adhesion loss and the consequent separation of the EVA 
encapsulant and the substrate film of the PV module. Delamination most of the time leads to 
corrosion and increased moisture ingress and is expected to occur more severely in humid and 
hot climates [17,19,21]. This failure particularly occurs near the edges and corners of the 
module, leading to water infiltration that accelerates, even more, the corrosion process.

2.2.4 Potential Included Degradation (PID)  
Potential Included Degradation has gained important attention from the PV research society. 
PID can potentially lead to catastrophic failure of the PV modules under outdoor conditions 
and is mainly caused by the different potentials between module frames and solar cells of a PV 
system under operation. Irrespective of the module type, PID can take place in any solar PV 
module due to the fact that it depends on the frame, glass, and encapsulation material and can 
be influenced by environmental factors [23].  
Commonly, as mentioned at the beginning of the chapter, solar PV modules are connected in 
series to form a PV system with a maximum power voltage. The frames of the modules are 
typically maintained at fixed ground potential to avoid harming the technicians with the 
electricity, through the operation maintenance processes. The difference in potential between 
the frame and the individual modules in the string, results in a leakage current and high voltage 
stress across the different module components, resulting in PID. This degradation affects 
severely mostly the cells that are found next to the module edge more than those ones in the 
center and the edge of the PV string [15, 17,20,22].  
In c-Si solar cells, the effect of the PID is highly dependent on the structure of the cell. If the 
investigated c-Si cell is p-type, a current is created through negative bias, which can flow in the 
p-n junction where the cell is shunted and cause losses in the efficiency of the system. On the 
other hand, if the investigated c-Si cell is n-type, it is more likely to experience a surface 
polarization under positive now bias, reducing both voltages as well as operating current [23].

In relation to the reduction in the output power and the module degradation rate for PID 
failure, yield losses of up to 20% and more within 1 year have been observed. PID-s can be 
considered to some extent a reversible polarization effect and can therefore be repaired if 
detected on time. Otherwise, if it has been detected too late the PV system can’t be repaired 
and nonreversible damages have to be considered. However, the way it can be repaired is by 
removing the potential and keeping the modules at room temperature. This procedure reduces 
the power loss and can be accelerated by thermal recovery [19].  
An alternative way to surge recovery is through the implementation of reversed bias, during

2.2.5 Defective Bypass Diodes 
As mentioned earlier, bypass diodes exist in solar PV systems in order to assist and avoid 
reverse biasing of solar cells beyond their breakdown voltage in case of shading or other serious 
or not material defects. Generally, a bypass diode is installed for a group of about 20-40 cells 
[15, 18,20]. The main failures of bypass diodes are either because they are undersized or 
because they are exposed to high voltages due to lightning strikes or other high-voltage events, 
these failures are called either short-circuit or open-circuit bypass diodes. In addition to these 
two reasons, the diodes have a certain possibility percentage of failure rate, that comes from 
the nature of the component. For diodes working constantly at high temperatures, this failure 
rate increases [19].  
For an open circuit, the failure occurs if the diode is damaged (e.g., strong electric current), not 
connected properly, or even missing, while for the short-circuit the failure occurs either if the 
diode is mounted inappropriately if it is physically shortened in the junction box or again has 
been exposed to strong and high voltages. These effects have a high possibility to cause hot 
spots which will lead to browning / brown marks or even in more severe situations, fire. In the 
majority, each PV module is usually equipped with 3 bypass diodes and so if a bypass diode is 
failed, the underperformance will be 1/3 of both the maximum power and the open circuit 
voltage and hence the total performance of the affected PV module will be reduced by 1/3 
[15,17,19,20]. Lastly, it has to be mentioned that the failure rate of bypass diodes in a PV system 
is generally high, so regular inspections prior to the installation test(s) have to be taken into 
consideration in order for more severe failures to be prevented.

2.2.7 Light Included Degradation Effects (LID/LeTID) 
There are two different light-induced degradation effects, and both have occurred at the cell 
level, these are known as light-induced degradation (LID) and light and elevated temperature-
induced degradation (LeTID), however, the physical mechanisms of these two degradations are 
very different. LID is related to the concentration of boron and oxygen in the cells, while LeTID 
is not fully understood yet, but up to now, it is considered that is related to the concentration of 
hydrogen in the cells. The main type of modules that are affected by these failures is p-type 
mono and multi c-Si, whereas cell technologies that use modern n-type wafers, such as n-type 
PERC, HJT, or n-PERT seem to be much less or not at all affected by them [15,17,19]. These 
degradations are recognizable as a drop in the standard test conditions (STC) power output as 
well as short circuit current and open circuit voltage, within the initial lifetime of a PV system. 
LID and LeTID do not cause safety problems but both of them have a negative impact on the 
lifetime of the energy yield and the financial return of the investment in the PV system [19].  
LID is taking place in the first week of exposure to the sun and experiences losses between 3 
and 6% in c-Si cells, whereas LeTID is characterized as a more serious and long-term light-
induced degradation mechanism. Performance losses can reach up to 10% and sum up with the 
LID loss, records have reached up to 16%. It occurs only at elevated temperatures above 50 °C 
and the more cells are affected the greater the power decline of the performance of the module 
[15,17,19]. Figure 13 presents two low-current electroluminescence images of a PV module 
before (left) and after (right) LeTID degradation. Finally, except module level degradation 
modes, failures can appear in other components like cables and interconnectors with failures 
like DC connector mismatch, insulation failure, and damage in the combiner box.

2.3 What is Maintenance and what types of it in Photovoltaic Systems  
Maintenance in general, can be used to define a set of tasks that have the purpose to maintain 
the equipment of industrial or construction enterprises in a desirable state of effective operation 
[24]. Maintenance can also be defined as efforts taken to keep the condition and performance 
of a system like the performance of the system when it is still functionally operated. 
Recently, the term maintenance has been used to refer to the set of actions that can detect 
failures at an early stage and hence help the system to maintain a good and efficient function 
by minimizing the downtime of the system and increasing its reliability and availability of it. 
==PAGE:29==
Periklis Aprilis 
 
Master Thesis Project 
 
[27] 
 
Maintenance activities basically can be divided into two parts: planned and unplanned 
maintenance activities. Planned maintenance is maintenance that is organized and carried out 
aiming for the short- or long-term future, while unplanned maintenance is maintenance that is 
carried out unexpectedly, without a formal schedule, and is usually the result of an unexpected 
failure or breakdown that has to be fixed in order the system to start function properly again. 
Finally, it includes the personnel (technicians) and the general services aimed at maintaining 
and restoring the operation of the system.  
In relation now to solar power plant maintenance, automation of maintenance tasks is becoming 
more and more prevalent, nevertheless, it is still in a primary developing phase and still not 
widespread [24]. The 3 main types of maintenance that are mainly applied in Solar PV arrays 
are described below: 
2.3.1 Preventive Maintenance  
The strategy of preventive maintenance is based on the execution of the planned work with the 
ultimate goal to eliminate the occurrence of failures that they are leading to unexpected 
stoppage times. Preventive maintenance also referred to as PM, is a proactive maintenance 
strategy that involves regular and routine maintenance of equipment to reduce the likelihood of 
failure which will further lead to unplanned downtime. The classical model of preventive 
maintenance involves a time-based approach with a scheduled set of tasks [3,24]. 
Preventive maintenance is the core element of the maintenance services to a PV plant. It 
compromises regular visual and physical inspections, as well as verification activities on all the 
key components of the solar PV park. This maintenance is carried out at predetermined regular 
intervals according to prescribed Operational & Maintenance (O&M) manuals that are usually 
included in the annual maintenance plan, which is provided with an established time schedule 
with a specific number of iterations for carrying out the maintenance. Furthermore, it must also 
maintain the equipment and component warranties in place and reduce the probability of failure 
or degradation [3,24]. All these preventive maintenance activities should follow and be 
implemented under manufacturer recommendations and always comply with respective legal 
issues (e.g., national standards for periodic inspection of certain electrical components). 
Specifically, solar panel preventive maintenance focuses on testing regularly electrical 
connections, controlling the status of the panel's fixations and their good general state of them 
[25,26]. 
There are multiple examples of preventive maintenance activities in solar PV parks, including 
cleaning the solar cells, inverter filters, and fans as well as ensuring that a site's draining system 
and civil works remain in good working order [3,24]. Another crucial and broadly used aspect 
of preventive maintenance in the solar industry is using image-based techniques. Imaging 
methods like infrared thermography (IRT), visual inspections (VI), or luminescence techniques 
(Electroluminescence or Photoluminescence) are able to visualize materials and material 
properties of a PV module [15]. 
These image-based inspection techniques are able to identify defective modules and cells on a 
PV plant and hence several categories of abnormalities like hot spots, cell cracks, moisture 
ingress, snail trails, open-circuited bypass diodes, soiling, etc., which potentially can occur 
noticeable reductions in the productivity of the whole solar array. The aforementioned 
inspections can take place either by operators with handheld special cameras or by using 
remotely piloted drones or UAVs equipped with special cameras for the obtaining of infrared 
or luminescence images. These inspection techniques are considered innovative technologies 
in the area of preventive maintenance of solar arrays and can significantly benefit power plant 
maintenance procedures as they can lead to time and cost savings as well as safety

2.3.2 Corrective Maintenance  
The basic principle of corrective maintenance is the planned execution of maintenance on parts 
of the system where failures have been identified and can no longer operate smoothly and 
efficiently. These failures are likely to lead to larger and even more serious damages or even 
lead to a sudden cessation of the system’s operation. It is known that corrective maintenance is 
the simplest form of maintenance policy and is increasingly limited in practice, mainly due to 
the modern systems/equipment that are becoming more and more complex and therefore the 
consequences caused when a sudden failure occurs are much more serious and cost effective 
[24,27]. With the implementation of corrective maintenance costs are greatly declined, due to 
the fact that maintenance activities take place only when they are necessary. The stake of cost 
compression is the lowest possible reliability that the system is operated on, until a failure 
arises.   
In relation now to the solar PV parks, corrective maintenance covers the activities performed 
by the maintenance team to restore a solar PV power plant system, equipment, or component 
to a status where it can perform smoothly again. It occurs after failure detection by remote 
monitoring or during an on-site inspection. Corrective maintenance includes three core 
activities [3,25]:  
1. Fault Diagnosis: Identify and locate the cause of the fault (troubleshooting). 
2. Temporary Repair: Restore the required function of a faulty item for a limited time, 
until a full repair is implemented. 
3. Full Repair: Restore permanently the required function. 
 
Moreover, a key aspect of corrective maintenance is to be able to identify and track failures to 
their root cause. Most of the time, this is a problem that comes from the manufacturer or the 
model/series number, but it may also be linked to errors during the installation or environmental 
conditions such as the temperature inside the enclosures [3,25].

functionality of a device. Table 1 illustrates these three levels together with their characteristics 
in detail. Finally, in cases where the PV plant or segments need to be taken offline, the execution 
of scheduled corrective maintenance during the night or low irradiation hours would be 
considered the ideal best practice, as the overall power generation is not affected.

that allow the effective diagnosis of the actual condition of the system while it is operated (non-

problems or failures occur. It is obvious that predictive maintenance combines the positive 
characteristics of preventive and corrective maintenance because it contains the element of 
damage prevention but at the same time, it uses the prognosis in order to intervene and correct 
the damage on time. It helps anticipating maintenance activities [24,28]. Figure 15 represents

Predictive Maintenance is a special service provided by O&M service providers who follow

2.4 Module Inspection Techniques  
In this sub-chapter, the most established and broadly used in the solar PV market inspection, 
and maintenance techniques for PV modules and systems will be presented. The on-site module 
characterization techniques which are used to evaluate the performance, quality, and durability 
of the PV module/system, are divided into visual-based inspection techniques and image-based 
inspection technique. On-site field inspections of PV systems are being implemented in order 
to give to the PV plant managers and operators, confidence that the PV power plant is working 
properly, performs at its current standards and provides the projected yield [29].  
On-site inspection methods have a variety of benefits and particular strengths. First and 
foremost, the inspection takes place without the need for dismounting and transport the 
investigated PV modules to the appropriate laboratory for testing, come through and avoiding 
transport risks and a long down time of the PV system. On top of that, the results are available 
for further investigation immediately and there is the chance to create a targeted failure analysis, 
by choosing combination of suitable inspection methods [29].  
In relation to inspection methods or combination of them, the inspection technique or 
combination of them have to be carefully chosen according to various external factors and 
aspects [29,30]. These factors can be: 
 
Focus on the inspection (PV string or module) 
 
Test Requirements (Daytime, Nighttime, Weather Conditions etc.) 
 
Throughput (Time required to inspect a certain number of PV modules or strings) 
 
Intervention in the PV System (Dismounting / Disconnection or not of PV modules or 
strings 
 
Infrastructure Requirements (Plain and paved roads for transport the necessary test 
equipment – power supply etc.) 
 
2.4.1 Visual Inspection  
Visual inspection is one of the most simple and fast module inspection techniques, without the 
usage of extra equipment, in the solar PV area. Visual inspection of the module is taking place 
before and after the module has been subjected to electrical & mechanical stress testing in the 
laboratory. The detectability of the method, as it is obvious, is very limited as the majority of 
the most common and typical failures happen internally inside the materials without any visual 
effect. Based on the IEC 61215 standard, visual inspection is mandatory after the PV module 
manufacture as well as in the field inspection. It requires illumination of more than 1000 lux 
during the visual inspection and only a few defects are detectable with the naked eye. The 
inspection is done from multiple angles to have a correct understanding of the fault and to avoid 
errors due to reflection. Degradation modes like discoloration, delamination, bubble formation, 
snail trails broken and burnt cells are detected and documented using the method.  Finally, 
visual inspection is not considered the ideal maintenance method for electrical failures in the 
PV module area, however, if these failures have become more severe and they produce 
consequences thar are visible with the naked eye like browning or corrosion, they can also be 
identified only through visual inspection [15, 17, 30,31]. 
2.4.2 I-V Curve Measurements 
Current – Voltage (I-V) curve measurements are also considered one of the simple and 
relatively not so complex inspection techniques in the solar PV area. I-V curve measurements 
are being implemented to extract important data for the condition of the electrical equipment 
of a PV module/string. These electrical parameters are short-circuited current (Isc), open-circuit

typical module I-V curve measurement system consists of a natural or artificial simulated light

monitoring facility, and a data acquisition system to measure the I-V curve when the voltage

The conditions that the I-V measurement can take place to differentiate and can be implemented

2.4.3 Ultraviolet Fluorescence  
Ultraviolet Fluorescence (UV-F) is an inspection technique that is applied mostly to identify 
degradation modes that are related to the encapsulation material in a PV module and can detect 
cell cracks and EVA-related failures like discoloration. The inspection with this technique is 
taking place on the operating PV plant, so there is no need for disconnection or transport in the 
laboratory of the investigated PV module, however, the measurements need to be performed in 
absence of light, in a dark environment. Fluorescence is considered a form of luminescence, 
which is the emission of light by an activated material that has absorbed light or other 
electromagnetic radiation. In a PV module, EVA is playing this role, EVA combined with 
different additives such as oxidation, stabilizers, and UV absorbers, can be built up 
fluorophores, which if radiated with photons of adequate energy, are able to emit light in the 
visible regime. A fluorophore is a fluorescent chemical compound that can re-emit light upon 
light excitation, fluorophores are created as degradation products during exposure to the UV-
light and reach an appropriate signal in which UV-F can be implemented to detect cell edges 
and cracks as they appear as dark areas in the UV fluorescence image [15, 29, 30]. 
The presence of oxygen is creating an opposite effect because it triggers photobleaching and 
reduces the fluorophores hence oxygen spreads through the back sheet into the encapsulant 
material and finally reaches the front side of the EVA through damaged cell gaps and cracks 
[15]. Nevertheless, the PV module has to be exposed to sunlight for a sufficient time, 
approximately 1 – 2 years, until it is ready to create fluorophores capable to emit a sufficient 
signal from UV fluorescence. This is the main reason why this method is typically avoided 
being implemented before or directly after the installation stage of the modules. On top of that, 
it is also preferred to avoid this method for PV modules that are operated for many  
years as the cell areas near the cell edges, after some years, stop to emit fluorescence due to 
photobleaching, hence it is restricting the detection of cell cracks there [20]. 
In conclusion, the main failures that UV-F is capable to detect are hot spots, cell cracks, 
distinction between older and younger cell cracks and failures related to used encapsulation and 
backsheet materials [29,30]. Figure 17 demonstrates the UV-F image of solar PV module with 
cell cracks.

2.4.4 Infrared Thermography (IRT) 
Infrared thermography (IRT) is a method which determines the spatial distribution and the time 
dependence of heat in the investigated objects / systems. The imaging system applied for the 
IRT inspection, transforms an infrared image into a visible image on the light spectrum. IRT 
imaging systems, visualize the invisible to the human naked eye infrared rays and provide 
efficient, accurate, non-destructive, and contactless temperature measurements. In a few words, 
infrared thermography does not measure the temperature of the object’s / system’s surface but 
the change of the infrared radiation on the surface [18].  
Concerning its applicability in solar PV systems, IRT has three different types of methods to 
detect degradation modes and failures in PV modules. These three types are thermography 
under steady state conditions, pulse thermography, and the lock-in thermography. The broadest 
and simplest way to amply is the thermography under state conditions as it allows the 
contactless analysis of the investigated modules under working conditions in the field, while 
the other two types provide a more detailed investigation into the PV module, however, they 
have to take place in the laboratory so the disassembly and transport of the PV modules to the 
lab is necessary and also the usage of an expensive, high speed and resolution infrared camera 
is mandatory. This analysis focus in the first type of method as it is the dominant and most 
common in the solar PV area.  
Infrared cameras, either as individual equipment or mounted on UAVs – Aerial Infrared 
Thermography (AIRT), equipped with the spectral range of 8 μm to 14 μm are mainly used to 
show PV module temperatures during operation. It can be used as a contactless method for 
diagnosing mostly thermal and electrical failures in PV modules. The measurements can be 
performed during normal operation for both individual PV modules and as a scan of large-scale 
systems, this the primary reason for the implementation of AIRT. On top of that, it has to be 
assured that the measurement is done under steady state conditions of the PV module [30].  
As stated before IR thermography detects defects by capturing the emitted infrared radiation 
(typically in the wavelength range of 1,000 nm or longer) from the systems, in this case the PV 
modules which are dependent on the temperature distribution across it, that means that even if 
the main focus to identify failures is on cell or module level, IRT is also able to detect 
abnormalities in other parts of the PV system like inverters, cables and combiner boxes [15].  
Infrared thermography is a safe and valuable tool for detecting and diagnosing degradation 
modes, abnormalities, unusual temperature profiles and general problems in many different 
fields. Based on that, infrared thermography can play a leading role in the maintenance and 
diagnosis of large-scale systems and installations. On the other hand, in relation to PV systems, 
although this inspection technique is contactless, cost and time efficient it is limited identifying 
only failures that cause heating of the modules, for example, hot spots. Furthermore, IRT is 
facing problems related to low sensitivity and resolution compared to other techniques like 
luminescence and its types, EL and PL that will be analyzed further in detail.  
In the aggregate, the possible failures which IRT can capture in outdoor on-site PV systems, at 
the module level are [15,18,29,30,32]:  
 
PID in the encapsulant material 
 
Broken cell interconnection  
 
Hot spots and shadowing effects

2.4.5 Luminescence Imaging  
Luminescence-based image inspection techniques are considered a very powerful and efficient 
characterization method for PV modules, that can provide accurate and effective information 
for PV cells and modules, to identify most of the degradation modes around them. These 
luminescence techniques, electroluminescence or photoluminescence, not only allow the 
identification of a variety of failures but also the assumption of causation and severity 
verification of these degradation modes. These degradation modes include cracks, series 
resistance effects, PID, LID as well bypass diode failure. For this particular reason, these 
techniques are highly desirable to inspect solar PV modules to ensure reliable and high-power 
production as well as to provide accurate feedback to the solar PV industry for future 
technological decisions and improvements.  
Generally, luminescence radiation is a form of light emitted by the radiative recombination of 
charge carriers in semiconductors. An image through luminescence techniques can be captured 
in the short-wave infrared regime at 1140 nm and is triggered externally. This image is able to 
demonstrate the properties (conductive) of the solar cells. A camera with an Indium–Gallium–
Arsenide sensor has a high and appropriate for these techniques’ quantum efficiency in the 
desired spectrum and is therefore needed for fast and efficient image capturing [15]. As 
mentioned before the luminescence which triggers the emission of luminescence radiation in 
solar cells, Si or not, is grouped into two categories, EL and PL. The main objective and main 
tool of this thesis is the engagement with PL and specifically with outdoor PL.

2.4.5.2 Photoluminescence (PL)  
  
The necessity for routine inspection of outdoor photovoltaic systems is getting bigger and 
bigger from an operation and maintenance point of view, in order to detect and identify failures 
before, or at an early stage after they occur. Generally, as mentioned before, luminescence 
imaging technique is widely used as an authentication method for defect and degradation modes 
detection in many cases for research and development - purposes, as well as PV inspections 
outdoors or indoors for preventive maintenance [38]. Cable handling and PV shading practices 
for on-site field inspections can correspond to 80% of the inspection time and also be 
responsible for the highest cost and safety concerns of the whole process. The main difference 
between PL and EL is the luminescence signal and how this signal is triggered. Luminescence 
refers to photons (light) emitted by material and is not related to the material temperature. In a 
semiconductor, the intensity of the emitted luminescence is directly related to the quality of the 
semiconductor. The luminescence intensity can thus be used as a very sensitive, temperature 
stable representative for the maximum voltage that can be obtained from a solar cell. 
Luminescence images are taken with a camera, with the images allowing direct assessment of 
the quality of the solar cell, revealing any imperfections or defects that affect the voltage of the 
cell. PL imaging is a type of luminescence imaging where the electron hole pairs are created by 
photon absorption, i.e., shining a light onto the sample. Upon absorption of a suitable photon, 
a charge carrier is excited from a lower energy electronic state into a higher energy electronic 
state. On the other hand, in EL the electron-hole pair is generated by electric carriers (electrons) 
injected into the device (external power supply) [30]. 
PL is applied by capturing radiative emission from the solar silicon cell. However, in this 
method the excitation is triggered by the exposure to the strong sunlight and not from back 
powering the module with an external power device. The method works from the simple fact 
that silicon’s photoluminescence band, for Si-manufactured PV modules, overlaps exactly to 
the point with a significant drop in the sunlight that finally reaches the surface of the earth. 
Remarkably, this occurs at a wavelength around 1130 nm and is a result of sunlight being 
strongly absorbed by water vapor as this pass through the atmosphere of the earth. Based on 
this phenomenon and in collaboration with the use of appropriate camera filters, it is becoming 
possible to limit the camera’s detection to this range and greatly improve the ratio of the 
desirable photoluminescence signal to unwanted noise of the sunlight. A typical filter for this 
application would be centered around 1130 nm and have a spectral bandwidth of about 25 to 
50 nm, with this camera filtering, and always depending on the outdoor weather conditions as 
well as the type of the investigated PV module technology and its quality, the detected PL from 
specifically a PV module at the open circuit is 1% of the detected sunlight. However, the 
majority of the time, it will need a higher camera sensitivity and hence a more expensive and 
lower resolution on the sensor InGaAs camera for outdoor photoluminescence inspections in 
solar PV parks during daytime [30].  
The necessary equipment needed for the implementation of this method is certainly much more 
lightweight than in EL, and can, in the simplest always implementation, even be fitted in one 
suitcase, which makes it even more approachable for large field on-site inspections of solar PV 
farms where mobility and ease of use are of highest priority and importance. The equipment 
used can be easily transported to different locations of a solar farm. A custom-built tripod

solution can additionally help transport all required components of the equipment and will make 
moving between different PV modules and different locations on the solar farm much more 
convenient [30].  
Based on the existing knowledge, since PL image is a relatively new and novel still method, 
there are many positive advantages as an outdoor method for preventive maintenance of solar 
PV modules. PL has the ability to contribute positively to: 
 Inspect solar parks that are shown underperformance, in order to identify the cause of 
them and replace the faulty PV modules. 
 A more detailed and to the point inspection method, by gaining better insights into PV 
module degradation modes on solar PV parks that have been initially inspected by IRT 
or AIRT and identified with high numbers of faults. 
 Examine and maintain solar PV parks after severe weather events (wind loading and 
hail) to inspect the damage and for insurance claims. 
 Scan solar PV parks before the change of ownership to ensure that the installed PV 
modules are still of sufficiently high quality and that the farm can be expected to long 
term future of operation. 
 Inspection of the PV modules production to guarantee the highest possible installation 
standard. 
 
On top of that, excluding the fact that it does not need an external power supply generator to be 
carried out on-site in the field, since the sunlight is being used as this source, it can detect major 
degradation modes in the surface of PV modules. In this context, outdoor PL imaging has a 
unique role to play as it can detect a large range of electric defects that cannot be identified with 
other methods with the same level of detail and throughput. The main failures that PL is able 
to detect can be summarized in [30]: 
 
Cell cracks (Type A, B & C) 
 
Bypass Diode failures (Open & Short Circuited) 
 
Degradations (PID, LID & LeTID) 
 
Snail Trails 
 
Inactive cell areas or disconnected PV sub-string 
 
EVA degradation 
 
Furthermore, PL can also be applied after the manufacturing phase in the production line or 
even after their installation to the field to check for further failures that probably took place 
throughout their transportation from the factory to the field, so again as in EL, throughout the 
whole value chain of the PV modules. Moreover, luminescence-based measurements are 
fundamentally robust to the factor of temperature differences as the PL intensity weakly 
depends on the temperature [30].  
Nevertheless, as the method of outdoor PL imaging is still considered a novel method some 
fundamental challenges still need to be addressed. First and foremost, the method requires 
specific outdoor conditions in order to be implemented and generate high-quality output 
images, cloudy days are not appropriate since they will cover the sunlight and it will 
automatically increase the on-site measurement time hence, the whole inspection will take more 
time, in conclusion, the weather conditions should be clear sky conditions. Even the smallest 
visible disturbances due to the form of the clouds will cause ruinous effects on the quality of 
the image. This is explained by the fact that the majority of the PV modules that have been 
installed and operated, and probably investigated in this research topic, are old technology types 
of PV modules with lower open circuit voltage compared to the modern state of the art PV

modules, like for example PERC modules, which will give certainly better and more accurate 
results [38,39]. Based on that, it is stated that applying outdoor PL inspection to older PV 
modules with lower open-circuit voltages is challenging as the luminescence intensity emitted 
will be significantly much lower [30].  
Up to now, a variety of different approaches have been tested and implemented for outdoor 
either daylight or in the dark photoluminescence mostly performed on module level. 
Photoluminescence has the potential to provide more detailed, in relation to the failure’s 
detection images, than infrared thermography images, while the imaging procedure has the 
potential to be faster than electroluminescence imaging simply because it does not require 
further electrical connection to the modules.  
Firstly, the most common is the Lock-In principle technique for daylight photoluminescence 
imaging, which in a few words, it implies by switching between the PV module’s two operating 
points. The lock-in technique is used, where the PV module is periodically electrically 
modulated with a known constant frequency while the camera collects a steady stream of 
images between the two operational points, however, this results in the need to connect 
additional electrical equipment dimensioned for the task and leads to production disruption for 
the investigated modules, like in the EL technique [40]. This method electrically modulates the 
operating point of the investigated PV modules, allowing separation between the weak PL and 
the strong sunlight, during the daytime inspection, the power used to modulate the PV string is 
taken from adjacent PV strings, while at night-time or low sunlight condition, an external 
generator is used to provide the necessary biasing condition for the PV modules. The method 
is reliable to detect serious failures like cracks, PID, shading and bypass diode failure, while 
the measurement time for PL images is ranging depending on the solar cell technology. For 
example, if the investigated PV modules belong to the category of PERC, HIT and IBC, the 
acquisition time ranges from 1 to 10 seconds while in older and yearly operated crystalline PV 
modules it reaches from 60 up to 120 seconds. Since the entire PV module string is modulated, 
then it is possible to take images of several PV modules in a PV module string [41].

Apart from the above-mentioned technique, a few more alternative methods have been 
developed the last years for outdoor PL imaging with specific goal the optimization on the 
switch between the operational points of the PV module(s).  
Another technique that has been applied in order to achieve control of the operational point not 
only of the PV module but of a PV sub-string is by the aid of a LED lamp. This LED lamp will 
be mounted in one specific cell (control cell) and will vary the illumination and hence the 
operating points of all the other remaining cells in the PV sub-string. Based on the same 
approach, the method has been moved forward also into a PV string scale. At the PV string 
level, multiple control cells are being used in different PV modules of the string with multiple 
as well LED lamps. The simplest implementation of the technique is by switching the LED(s) 
between different light intensities – in the simplest implementation this is between fully On and 
fully Off. While the LED is turned on the current in the investigated PV sub-string or string is 
maximized with almost 0 PL signal, while in a completely different position, when the LED is 
turned Off, the current of the investigated area is becoming minimum, and the PL signal reaches 
its maximum emission.  
Figure 26 shows the experimental equipment for this method. With this current procedure, 
about 100 solar PV modules can be imaged per hour. The image acquisition time for a single 
image is about 1 sec for PERC installed PV modules and this results in the fact that in one 
sunny working day it is possible to test around 1600 PV modules, an estimated power of 640 
kWp. The advantage of this method is that it can be very quickly and easily applied at any 
location on a solar power plant. However, the throughput of this method is fundamentally 
limited to about 1-2 PV modules per minute due to the requirement to move the optical 
modulator from module to module [30,39,42,43,44,45]. 
In a further step, several optical modules can be used based on the principle that in a large-scale 
solar PV park a lot of PV strings, with more than 20 series connected modules each, are 
connected in parallel, by such means if pinning the PV string’s voltage to a constant value even 
when one string in the array is rapidly switched from maximum power point to open-circuit 
voltage. With PV string modulation, the switch of the operating point of an entire string of 
about 20 modules is achieved with up to five optical modulators that are placed on the modules 
connected to one PV string. This enables rapid outdoor PL imaging of a single PV module or 
even the acquisition of PL images of many modules at the same time, in only 1 sec, and this is 
considered the main advantage of the method. The image acquisition is being implemented by 
synchronizing the optical modulators and the imaging camera via low-latency wireless radio 
frequency transmission [45]. Figure 27 demonstrates an experimental design of the method 
with three LED lamps mounted on three “control” cells, aiming to string modulation.

Concerning the outdoor PL methods mentioned before, it is easily understood that when it 
comes to outdoor, in field measurements, PL imaging is becoming physically connected with 
external equipment technique, similarly to outdoor EL. This fact makes the method even more 
complex in its applicability especially in large scale and isolated areas, as the size of the external

ground-based tripod from PV module to module in a large-scale solar PV park, most of the 
times on very rough and abnormal ground is very hard to carry and objectively limits the 
efficiency and effectiveness of outdoor PL imaging [45].  
In the current study of this thesis, the idea of performing PL during an IV sweep that has been 
already programmed to happen in a “smart” inverter is examined. The state-of-the-art inverter 
market has recently implemented I-V sweep in a string level for preventive maintenance and 
better maximum power point tracking. In relation to that, this collaboration with the inverter’s 
capability to sweep by default the I-V curve is considered an excellent strategy to reach high 
throughput for PV luminescence-based inspections on site without a significant additional 
demand in terms of cost and inspection complexity [48]. During the IV sweep of the inverter, 
the PV modules are in the different operational points required for extracting sunlight noise out 
for daylight PL imaging to be successful. Most PV string inverters have the hardware capability 
to measure at least part of the current-voltage (I-V) characteristic curve of the PV strings 
connected at the input. This functionality can be very useful for PV system monitoring 
purposes, to detect the presence and cause of power-loss in the PV strings due to shading, 
degradation of the PV modules or balance-of-system components through increased series 
resistance losses, or even shunting of the PV modules [49]. The strongest advantage of this 
approach is twofold. This is a contactless and non-invasive method, which only requires 
imaging apparatus and, therefore, allows for a flexible imaging procedure on the string, module, 
or cell level without having to consider additional external equipment [40]. 
Generally, outdoor PL implies by switching between the module's two operating points, the 
bigger the difference in voltage between two operating points, the bigger the difference in the 
photoluminescence (ΔPL) and the better the image quality for deeper and further investigation.

3.1 Introduction 
The performance of photovoltaic systems is critical to their efficient operation and maintenance 
as they are an important source of renewable energy. This chapter presents the experimental 
methodology used to evaluate the PL signal intensity of PV modules, with the goal of improving 
the understanding of PL signal intensity under different conditions, such as dissimilar camera 
settings, unalike sun intensities, and different investigated PV module technologies. The 
experiments aim to provide insight into the performance of PV modules and to evaluate the 
usefulness of PL imaging as a tool for monitoring and evaluating the functionality of PV 
systems. 
The chapter is divided into three main sections including, methodology, equipment and 
experimental setup. The methodology section details the overall approach and procedures used 
for conducting the experiments. This includes a description of the data collection methods as 
well as explain how the data will be validated and interpreted to draw meaningful conclusions

The equipment section provide a detailed list of all the equipment that has been used during the 
experiments. This includes information about the types of equipment, such as the chosen PV 
module technologies – samples, the PL imaging system, IV tracer, cameras, and the on-site 
installed inverter.  
The experimental plan section outlines the specific experiments that have been conducted and 
the purpose of each one of them. This section describes the different PV module technologies

for each experiment and outline any potential challenges or limitations that generally may arise 
during the experiments. The method and the experimental procedure are analyzed in detail. 
In the first experiment, the outdoor PL measurements are performed on a single healthy PV 
module using the two EKO IV tracer installed on field. The purpose is to assess the behavior 
of the installed EKO IV tracer and obtain the higher quality PL images. The second experiment

settings into investigated modules of the same technology by using again the same IV tracer.

3.2 Methodology  
The methodology used in this study is aimed firstly at evaluating how different ways of 
changing the PV panel operational points, through the IV tracer and the inverter’s IV sweep, 
can generate the PL signal, further assessing the PL image quality acquired with different 
methods, and secondly correlate and compare the PL signal intensity from different PV module 
technologies. 
The general methodology for conducting the previously mentioned four outdoor PL imaging 
experiments follows a structured approach to ensure accurate data collection and reliable 
comparisons. The methodology begins by measuring the irradiance at the location where the 
PL images will be acquired, accounting for variations in sunlight intensity. Next, careful 
positioning of the camera in the tripod is implemented to minimize reflections and unwanted 
interference from sunlight, thus optimizing the quality of the PL images captured. 
Once the camera is properly positioned on the tripod, the PL images are acquired using the 
respective equipment for each experiment. In that point, it also needs to be mentioned that the 
initial experiments with the IV tracers are used as baseline for the experiments, for the simple 
reason that there is a better and easier control of the experiments from the investigator’s side, 
through the IV tracer. More advanced experiments are considered the ones with the use of the 
installed on-field inverter.  
Throughout each experiment, the methodology emphasizes the importance of repetition. The 
process is repeated to ensure reliability, consistency, and representative results for accurate 
comparisons. By repeating the experiments, the impact of different variables, such as camera 
settings, can be properly assessed. 
Overall, this general methodology encompasses essential steps including irradiance 
measurement, camera positioning, PL image acquisition, and repetition of the process. By 
adhering to this structured approach, the experiments aim to provide comprehensive insights 
into the PL signal intensity, module performance, IV tracer’s and inverter’s behavior within the 
solar PV systems under investigation. This general methodology has been followed throughout 
the different experiments on-site, providing a standardized approach to evaluate the PL signal 
intensity of PV modules. 
In addition to the general methodology described above, there are several additional factors that 
must be considered to ensure accurate and reliable results: 
 
Safety Measures: It is important to take all necessary safety measures while handling 
equipment like solar panels. Precautions, like special boots, gloves and helmets had been 
implemented to avoid electrical shocks and to protect against accidental falls while working 
on-site. 
 
 
Timing and Weather: The specific time of day when the experiment is conducted can have 
a significant impact on the solar irradiance and ultimately the PL signal intensity. 
Additionally, weather conditions such as cloud coverage, wind speed, global horizontal 
irradiance (GHI), and other weather conditions can influence the PL results.  
 
 
Potential Limitations or Challenges: There may be limitations or challenges that could 
affect the experiment's results. For example, differences in the manufacturing process of 
PV modules or variations in their specifications may affect the PL signal intensity. To 
address these limitations, the experiments have been designed to measure and evaluate 
different PV module types with different power generation under identical conditions to 
minimize the effect of such variations. Additionally, technical difficulties such as camera

After the data acquisition of PL images using the respective methodologies, the acquired images

 I-V Tracer: 2 EKO IV Tracer  
I-V curve tracers are specialist types of test equipment that sweep an electrical load connected 
to the solar PV module or string and measure both the current and voltage at multiple points 
during the sweep. The pairs of current and voltage values are then used to plot an I-V curve 
directly or to calculate and plot a P-V curve. In this point, it has to be mentioned that a P-V 
curve, also known as a power-voltage curve, is a graphical representation of the relationship 
between the output power and the voltage of a PV device or solar cell. It illustrates how the 
electrical characteristics of the PV device change with varying voltage or current.  
The curve typically shows the power output on the y-axis and the voltage (or current) on the x-
axis. To overcome the influence of external factors such as temperature and irradiation, I-V 
curve tracers typically measure and record irradiance and temperature to allow measurements 
to be converted to STC to enable an accurate comparison to be made with the PV module 
specification. 
In relation to the 2-Channel EKO IV tracers’ system, it is a specialized instrument used for PV 
module testing and analysis. It is designed to measure the current-voltage (I-V) characteristics 
of PV modules under varying conditions, providing valuable insights into their performance 
and efficiency as well as time-saving evaluation. Regarding the measurement setup, the PV 
modules to be tested are connected to the system's channels using appropriate connectors. The 
system provides connections for measuring both the positive and negative terminals of the 
module [53]. 
Moreover, the 2-Channel EKO IV system has even more capabilities than just construct IV 
curves. It provides irradiance control, which allows for precise control of the irradiance level 
during testing, by controlling the irradiance, accurate comparisons between different modules 
or test conditions can be made. On top of that, the system collects and stores the measured 
voltage and current data for further analysis. This data can be used to calculate various 
parameters such as MPP, Voc, Isc, FF, and the efficiency. The system also provides reporting 
features to generate comprehensive reports summarizing the test results for all the measured 
parameters [53].

their electrical characteristics makes it a valuable tool for assessing module performance,

Methodology: 
1. Start the experiment by measuring the irradiance with a reference cell. In the context 
of solar photovoltaics, a reference cell is a calibrated device used to measure the 
intensity of sunlight or solar radiation at a specific location. The reference cell is 
typically a small photovoltaic device, with a known conversion efficiency, that 
produces a voltage or current output proportional to the intensity of the incident light. 
This proportional value should be calibrated with the highest accuracy at a certified 
lab. During the PL imaging experiment, the reference cell is used to measure the 
intensity of sunlight or solar radiation at the location, position plane-of-the-array, and 
==PAGE:58==

Master Thesis Project 
 
[56] 
 
 
moment in time where the PL images will be acquired. This measurement is necessary 
because the intensity of sunlight can vary due to factors such as cloud cover, time of 
day, and atmospheric conditions, which can affect the intensity of the PL signal. By 
measuring the intensity of the incident light on the PV measured module with a 
reference cell, the PL signal intensity can be evaluated, which means that the PL images 
can be compared between different times or locations, or between different PV 
modules, to evaluate the performance or degradation of the modules over time. 
2. Position the camera on the tripod with the lowest level of perspective as possible, 
avoiding reflection from sunlight. To avoid unnecessary reflections, the camera should 
be positioned in a way that minimizes the angle between the panel surface and the 
camera lens. Additionally, here is when the camera lens should be implemented, to 
further reduce reflections and improve the image quality. 
3. Acquire PL images. After positioning the camera, it is important to ensure that the 
module is connected to the IV tracer. The PL images should then be acquired for the 
investigated module after the oscilloscope measurements. 
4. Repeat the process for the investigated PV module, it is important to repeat the process 
to ensure that the results are representative and adequate for being evaluated. Figure

4. Data Analysis & Interpretation 
In this chapter, the acquired photoluminescence image data will be thoroughly prepared and 
organized for further analysis. The data preparation stage involves carefully structuring the PL 
image data obtained from the experiments conducted in Chapter 3. The collected PL images 
have been organized in a systematic manner, ensuring data completeness and quality. This 
includes verifying that all necessary information are present and accurate. By undertaking these 
meticulous data preparation steps, it can be ensured that the subsequent analysis is conducted 
on reliable and well­organized data, enabling to draw meaningful conclusions from the 
experiments. 
4.1 Data Preparation 
Following the successful implementation of the outdoor on­site PL experiments, the focus now 
shifts towards analyzing the acquired images. These PL images provide valuable insights into 
the state of the modules, as they cover the entire IV curve in a single sweep. By examining the 
photoluminescence images obtained at different current extraction levels, it becomes possible 
to investigate and assess the condition of the cells and modules. This comprehensive image 
series offers a powerful tool for evaluating and understanding the performance and 
characteristics of the photovoltaic system. 
To begin the image processing of the acquired datasets, the first step involves plotting the 
number of frames for each sequence in correlation with the mean pixel value. This initial 
analysis provides a general overview of the dataset's quality and whether the PL signal is visibly 
captured. For this purpose, the software ImageJ is utilized [50]. The original raw file image is 
imported into the software, adjusted to 16­bit for better analysis. Subsequently, the software 
generates a table displaying the mean pixel values for each frame rate of the image sequences. 
Additional parameters, such as histogram parameters (integrated density, skewness, and 
kurtosis), can be incorporated into the table for further analysis, as they may also relate to the 
PL signal. Consequently, the generated table is pasted in an excel sheet in which the calculated 
values regarding the mean pixel are plotted for visualization and hence to start selecting the 
clear visual levels that are able to be used for the ΔPL signal analysis. The next step involves 
determining the optimal number of images to be selected from the highest PL signal (Voc) and 
the lowest PL signal (Mpp) for each level displaying ΔPL in the mean pixel value plot. 
As mentioned in the previous chapter, for the experiments conducted with the Huawei inverter, 
the IV sweep is commanded to run while the current to the PV string is monitored using an 
oscilloscope to verify when the IV sweep had started. After estimated the inverter response 
time, the IV started in coordination with the camera acquisition. Figure 39 shows the

the fourth experiment in which 2 Trina cracked PV modules were investigated while Figure 40 
demonstrates the image acquisition plot of the same experiment in relation to mean pixel value

the current is going up, the luminescence signal is going down, so when the PL signal has its 
maximum value (Voc) the Isc has its minimum value and when the PL signal has its minimum

4.3 Photoluminescence IV Curve Analysis 
In the context of the experiments conducted using the installed inverter, an intriguing 
observation arises wherein the IV curve may manifest as a distinct level in the mean pixel 
value plot diagram. This phenomenon necessitates a deeper investigation to elucidate the 
relationship between the signal­to­noise ratio, current, and the photoluminescence signal. This 
particular analysis holds paramount significance and warrants dedicated attention, which 
justifies its inclusion as an independent sub­chapter in the thesis. In the forthcoming section, a 
comprehensive exploration of the interplay between SNR, current, and the PL signal will take 
place, unveiling valuable insights into the underlying dynamics of the photovoltaic system. 
As mentioned before, the IV curve is a graphical representation that illustrates the relationship 
between the current and voltage output of a PV module or system. It provides valuable 
insights into the electrical characteristics and performance of the PV device. As the load 
resistance changes, the current and voltage values correspondingly vary, and plotting these 
values on a graph produces the IV curve. Generally, the current at the top of an IV curve 
refers to the Isc. It represents the maximum current that can be generated by a PV module 
when the output terminals are shorted, effectively setting the load resistance to zero. At this 
point, the voltage across the terminals is minimal or zero, while the current is at its maximum 
point.  
In the aforementioned experiments and specifically the ones implemented with the Huawei 
inverter, it became apparent that the IV curve might also be visible through the mean pixel 
value plot of the acquired image sequences. Simultaneously, this became a very interesting 
reason to analyze and identify the behavior of the IV curve in relation to the SNR and the Isc. 
Generally, when IV curve measurements are implemented, higher currents in the IV curve 
typically indicate increased power generation, which can lead to a stronger PL signal. This, in 
turn, can result in a higher SNR. In order now to identify if the SNR and the ΔPL signal are 
decreasing proportionally with the Isc, a comprehensive analysis of the captured IV curve in 
the mean pixel value plot takes place. Figure 50 illustrates both the plots from the two 
different measurements of the experiment 3 in which the IV curve is clearly captured and 
further highlighted.

4.4 Interpretation of Results 
The final sub­chapter include the interpretation and the discussion of the acquired results 
analyzed throughout this chapter. The key outcomes below highlight the effectiveness of 
outdoor PL imaging for evaluating and understanding the performance and characteristics of 
photovoltaic systems. The choice of equipment, exposure time, and module technology plays a 
crucial role in obtaining high­quality PL image. These findings shed light on the reliability and 
effectiveness of these tools in capturing clear PL images and identifying various aspects related 
to the performance and degradation of bifacial and monofacial mono­PERC modules. 
Firstly, the study utilized the EKO IV tracer as a reliable tool for capturing clear PL images of 
healthy bifacial mono­PERC modules, even under medium irradiance conditions. The 
successful implementation of the tracer facilitated the acquisition of high­quality PL images, 
which played a crucial role in the accurate analysis and evaluation of module performance. 
Another important observation made during the experiments was the noticeable decrease in the 
quality of the final PL image as the measurement exposure time decreased. The identification 
of the optimal combination of exposure time and frame rate became crucial in order to capture 
high­quality PL images Striking the right balance enabled the achievement of PL images that 
effectively reflected the performance characteristics of the investigated PV modules. 
The study also demonstrated the effectiveness of outdoor PL imaging as a powerful on­site 
technique for the identification of degraded bifacial mono­PERC modules. Furthermore, by 
employing the Huawei inverter, which had the capability to sweep the IV curve, the experiments 
successfully conducted, concluded that outdoor PL imaging, proving its reliability in detecting 
PID in these modules. The correlation established between the PL signal levels and the IV curve 
allowed for the accurate identification of instances of PID, thereby providing valuable insights 
for maintenance and quality control purposes. 
Notably, the study found that different irradiance levels did not significantly impact the final 
PL image quality, as long as the exposure time remained constant. On top of that, the appearance 
of the defective and healthy cells was also clearly distinguishable throughout the experiments. 
This observation highlights the robustness of the outdoor PL technique, demonstrating its 
ability to capture accurate module performance data under varying environmental conditions. 
Consequently, the study further solidifies the effectiveness of outdoor PL imaging as an on­site 
evaluation method. The investigation from the first three experiments also revealed that bifacial 
mono­PERC modules emerged as an optimal PV module technology for outdoor PL 
implementation. These modules consistently provided fast, cost­effective, and precise results 
when subjected to PL imaging. Leveraging the capabilities of the Huawei inverter, it has been 
showcased the potential practical applications of bifacial mono­PERC modules in the field of 
PV module characterization. Moreover, the study conducted a comprehensive analysis to

5. Conclusions & Future Work  
5.1 Conclusions 
The present study aimed to interpret the results obtained from the investigation, which focused 
on the implementation and analysis of outdoor PL imaging for evaluating the performance and 
characteristics of photovoltaic modules. The study successfully achieved its objectives of 
developing more efficient and cost­effective ways for the inspection and maintenance of solar 
PV systems. It addressed the research questions and fulfilled the initial motivations behind the 
thesis. The key findings and outcomes of the research contribute to the development of 
sustainable energy solutions. By improving the inspection and maintenance practices of solar 
PV systems, the study contributes to the advancement of cost­effective PV inspections based 
on luminescence imaging for the O&M sector and ultimately to assure return on investment of 
PV plants and reliability of renewable energy sources, in the end contributing to a more 
sustainable future. 
The first two chapters provided the introduction including the main research objectives and the 
motivation of the thesis in conjunction with the literature review including a comprehensive 
overview of photovoltaic systems and the current situation in the photovoltaic market, various 
maintenance approaches, common degradation modes and failures, and an exploration of 
existing module inspection techniques. The upcoming third chapter centers on the experimental 
methodology followed on field, covering a detailed description of the methodology, the 
equipment used and the detailed experimental plan for each conducted experiment. It laid the 
groundwork for the data analysis chapter, where the interpretation of the collected data and its 
implications for the monitoring and maintenance of PV systems took place. 
In relation now to the key outcomes exported from the experiments, for the experiments 
conducted under different camera settings, a notable observation was the important decrease in 
the quality of the final PL image as the measurement exposure time decreased. To address this 
challenge, it became essential to determine the ideal combination of exposure time and frame 
rate. After careful analysis, it was determined that the optimal combination consisted of an 
exposure time of 2.5 ms, coupled with frame rates of 400 and 500 Hz. Striking the right balance 
allowed for the capture of high­quality PL images that effectively reflected the performance 
characteristics of the investigated PV modules. 
Furthermore, through the study it has been found that different irradiance levels did not 
significantly impact the final PL image quality, as long as the exposure time remained constant. 
This observation highlights the robustness of the outdoor PL technique, demonstrating its 
ability to provide qualitative diagnostic information, like identifying modules with PID, or 
verify that the investigated modules have cracked cells, under varying environmental 
conditions. Consequently, the study further solidifies the effectiveness of outdoor PL imaging 
as an on­site evaluation method. 
Moreover, the study demonstrated the effectiveness of outdoor PL imaging as a powerful on­
site technique for identifying degradations in bifacial mono­PERC modules and cracks in 
monofacial mono­PERC modules respectively. By employing the Huawei inverter and the EKO 
IV Tracer, which facilitated the sweep of the IV curve, the experiments successfully concluded 
that outdoor PL imaging is reliable in detecting failures like PID, degradation and cracks in 
these modules. The correlation established between the PL signal levels and the IV curve 
enabled the accurate identification of instances of PID and cracks, providing valuable insights 
for maintenance and quality control purposes. 
The investigation also revealed that mono­PERC modules, either bifacial or monofacial, 
emerged as very good PV module technologies for outdoor PL implementation and they show

a good response for this approach. These modules consistently provided fast, cost­effective, 
and precise results when subjected to PL imaging. Leveraging the capabilities of the Huawei 
inverter, the study showcased the potential practical applications of mono­PERC modules in 
the field of PV module characterization. 
On top of that, another significant observation from the experiments with the inverter, gave the 
chance to a comprehensive analysis in order to understand the relationship between the SNR, 
current, PL signal and hence the quality of the final generated PL image. By examining the IV 
curve alongside the PL signal levels in the mean pixel value plot diagram, a gradual decrease 
in both SNR, ΔPL signal, and final PL image quality, was observed as the current different 
decreased along the IV curve. After this outcome, it is confirmed that these three factors are 
dependent on a proportional relationship. For validation and confirmation purposes, a 
comparison between PL and EL images of an investigated PV module verified the correlation 
between current, image quality, and the PL signal. These results reaffirmed the previously 
established findings, supporting the notion that the PL technique provides valuable information 
regarding the module's performance.  
Overall, this study broadens and deepens the existing knowledge and understanding of the 
potential of outdoor PL imaging as a powerful tool for evaluating the health and general 
situation of PV systems. The analysis of the results from the conducted experiments highlights 
the robustness and effectiveness of outdoor PL imaging, facilitated by the utilization of the 
installed on­field equipment. It opens avenues for further research and development in the field 
of PV module characterization, paving the way for advancements in monitoring, maintenance, 
and quality control strategies.  
The main findings of this study have practical implications for industry professionals, providing 
them with valuable insights for failure identification, location and overall preventive 
maintenance of PV plants, detecting potential issues, and ensuring the long­term reliability and 
efficiency of PV systems. Future studies can build upon these findings to explore additional 
applications, refine measurement techniques, and explore the potential of outdoor PL imaging 
in further solar PV technologies and system configurations.

5.2 Future Work 
Operations and Maintenance stakeholders in the energy industry are increasingly embracing 
innovative and data­driven solutions to meet the evolving market demands. They recognize the 
importance of staying abreast of technological advancements to effectively address the dynamic 
needs and requirements of the industry. This shift towards more machine­driven and data­
centric approaches allows stakeholders to enhance their operational efficiency and 
responsiveness to changing market conditions. 
With the rapid growth of photovoltaic electricity generation, solar PV power plants are scaling 
up to the terawatt level, often encompassing millions of solar modules. In such large­scale 
installations, maintenance becomes a critical aspect, particularly in its predictive and preventive 
form. These maintenance practices are essential for optimizing cost efficiency and minimizing 
the time spent by technicians or engineers on­site. By proactively identifying and addressing 
potential issues, predictive and preventive maintenance strategies ensure the smooth operation 
and optimal performance of solar PV systems. Emphasizing these maintenance approaches 
enables effective resource allocation and contributes to maximizing the overall output of solar 
PV installations [8]. 
In the context of large­scale photovoltaic installations, it is crucial to recognize the unique role 
that outdoor PL imaging plays. As emphasized throughout the thesis, outdoor PL imaging 
stands out by its ability to detect a wide range of defects in PV modules and most importantly 
the invisible ones. Unlike other methods, it has the potential for exceptional levels of detail and 
throughput, making it an invaluable tool for comprehensive defect identification, however there 
are still some demanding dares regarding the engineering development and specifically the 
inverter­camera synchronization or the automatic image processing that need to be overcome. 
By taking advantage of  the capabilities of outdoor PL imaging, maintenance teams can uncover 
and address a broader spectrum of electric defects with unparalleled precision and efficiency, 
thereby ensuring the optimal performance and longevity of PV systems. In addition, the specific 
technique of outdoor PL imaging, which is extensively described in the report, offers several 
distinct advantages. By utilizing a contactless imaging approach for solar PV inspection, it 
capitalizes on the advanced capabilities of modern equipment like string inverters or advanced 
research tools installed on field like IV Tracers to perform I­V curve sweeping. This innovative 
approach not only reduces the acquisition time required for PL imaging in solar PV panel 
inspections but also minimizes the technical expertise needed. As a result, the benefits of 
outdoor PL imaging in terms of efficiency, ease of implementation, and accuracy are continually 
increasing, making it an increasingly valuable tool in the industry, however there are also a 
variety of challenges from the PV installation readiness point of view that need to be addressed 
in order to achieve the optimized outcomes of it. 
Despite the several advantages that latest explained technique provides, the deployment of 
outdoor PL imaging for routine inspection on solar power plants, even in the case when the 
only necessary equipment is just a camera apparatus, it is strongly dependent on the ease of use 
and throughput of this method. The throughput is currently limited by the need to move the 
image capture unit throughout the PV power plant on a custom­built tripod, which besides its 
apparent simplicity, this tripod is a heavy and delicate tool, adding to the complexity and 
fragility of the process. In a few words, manually moving a ground­based tripod from module 
to module in a largescale solar farm – often on very rough terrain – is cumbersome and 
fundamentally limits the throughput of outdoor PL imaging [45,57,58]. 
Based on the aforementioned, theoretical approaches for extended real­life scenarios like 
streamlined ground­based PL or automated aerial PV inspections through UAVs or drones have 
to be considered. The concept of aerial inspections for monitoring and maintenance purposes

In line with this concept, significant progress has been made worldwide in exploring the 
potential of aerial outdoor photoluminescence, research groups from universities in Australia 
and Germany specifically have shown some initial outdoor experiments during daytime with 
various research initiatives underway. The advantages of this approach are several, including 
no contact with PV modules and the ability to conduct inspections from the air, potentially 
enabling high­throughput inspections of large­scale installations. The experimental setup 
usually consists of the aerial platform (either a drone or an aircraft) equipped with the PL 
imaging system and finally in some research cases excitation source(s) are mounted on the 
aerial platform in order to stimulate the PV modules and induce photoluminescence emissions. 
The aerial platform utilized in aerial PL imaging is predominantly drones due to their versatility, 
maneuverability, and cost­effectiveness. As for the PL imaging system, it comprises specialized 
cameras and sensors tailored to detect and capture the emitted photoluminescence from the 
investigated PV modules. The specific cameras employed can vary across research groups. For 
instance, one research group utilize a combination of three camera types: visual, thermal 
infrared, and outdoor PL short­wave infrared cameras, all mounted on the aerial platform. 
Conversely, another group found that a near­infrared camera suffices for outdoor PL imaging. 
In certain cases, an excitation source is incorporated into the setup. The most commonly used 
sources are LED arrays or lasers that emit specific wavelengths. These excitation sources are 
positioned on the aerial platform and directed towards the PV modules during the imaging 
process [37,45,57,58].  
The PL imaging system captures images or videos of the PV modules while the excitation 
source illuminates them. The cameras and sensors record the emitted photoluminescence 
signals, along with any other relevant data such as GPS coordinates and timestamps. To 
accurately locate and map the captured data, the PL imaging system is often integrated with 
GPS technology. GPS data is synchronized with the captured images or videos, enabling precise 
geo­referencing of the photoluminescence data. This integration allows for the creation of 
detailed maps and spatial visualization of the PV installation and detected defects. Same as in 
ground outdoor PL, further image processing and analysis of the acquired data will be followed 
using specialized software and algorithms. The image processing algorithms enhance the 
quality of the images, while the analysis algorithms extract valuable information from the 
photoluminescence emissions, such as defect identification, spatial distribution mapping, and 
quantification of performance­related parameters [45,57].  
The aerial PL techniques implemented so far have showcased several notable advantages. These 
include the capability to detect a wide range of defects with a high correlation, ensuring accurate 
defect identification and assessment, these include cracks, fractures, cell mismatches, inactive 
areas, and other anomalies that affect the efficiency and reliability of the solar panels. 
Furthermore, the ability to perform contactless inspections from the air provides a significant 
advantage, enabling efficient and high­throughput inspections of large­scale PV installations. 
Another advantageous observation is that state­of­the­art aerial PL imaging techniques can be 
integrated with other inspection technologies, such as infrared thermography or 
electroluminescence imaging, to obtain a more comprehensive assessment of PV module 
condition. This integration allows for a multi­modal approach, combining different imaging 
modalities for a more detailed understanding of defects and degradation mechanisms [57,58]. 
However, there are also many limitations and challenges that need to be addressed in order to 
create a solid and robust technique and methodology that can be widely applied from the PV 
industry. These limitations include the partial and non­homogeneous illumination at low 
irradiance, which potentially can hinder the identification of PV module defects. Moreover, the 
equipment weight and payload capacity, aerial platforms, particularly drones, have weight and

payload limitations. This restricts the number and types of imaging equipment (cameras – 
excitation sources) that can be deployed, as well as the duration of flight time. Overcoming 
weight restrictions while maintaining the necessary imaging capabilities is a technical challenge 
that needs to be addressed. On top of that, the large volume of data generated by aerial PL 
imaging requires sophisticated image processing and data analysis techniques. Handling and 
analyzing the vast amount of data in a timely manner poses computational challenges, 
necessitating the development of efficient algorithms and software for effective interpretation 
and utilization of captured PL image. Here it needs to be mentioned that this obstacle can be a 
bottleneck for the UAV approach and even make the tripod approach more economically and 
technically viable. Furthermore, ensuring stable and robust equipment setup on the aerial 
platform is crucial. Factors such as vibration, wind, and platform movement can introduce 
motion artifacts and blur into the PL images, compromising their quality. Last but not least, 
conducting aerial inspections must comply with aviation regulations and safety protocols. 
Acquiring necessary permits, adhering to flight restrictions, and ensuring the safety of 
personnel and bystanders are critical considerations. Collaboration between researchers, 
industry stakeholders, and regulatory bodies is vital to effectively navigate these regulatory and 
safety challenges. Standardization efforts are necessary in order to enable meaningful 
comparisons and benchmarking between different aerial PL imaging studies and methodologies 
[8,45,57,58].  
Overall, aerial PL imaging demonstrates great potential for the future of photovoltaics systems. 
Despite the existing challenges and limitations, significant strides have been made in advancing 
this technique, showcasing its capacity for efficient and comprehensive inspections of large­
scale solar PV installations. The ability of aerial PL imaging to detect a wide range of defects 
with high correlation, facilitate contactless inspections from the air, and enable high­throughput 
assessments is highly encouraging. Ongoing research and industry collaboration are expected 
to lead further advancements in aerial PL imaging systems, improved data analysis techniques, 
and standardized practices. The technology has the potential to become the new standard for 
routine high­volume and high­quality inspection of PV modules on operating solar power plants 
by providing a cost­effective and efficient solution for widespread use in the PV industry 
[8,45,57,58].

trends and innovations that are shaping the industry. Some of the key trends and innovations

analyze data from PV installations. This enables real­time performance monitoring, 
predictive maintenance, and optimized decision­making. Hence, remote monitoring 
and control solutions are becoming increasingly prevalent in the O&M PV market.

technologies enable precise monitoring of individual panels, detection of 
underperforming modules, and identification of potential issues. Predictive and 
proactive maintenance approaches are gaining prominence. By analyzing historical

Sustainability and Environmental Considerations: The O&M PV market is placing 
more and more emphasis on sustainability and environmental considerations. Practices 
such as recycling PV components through well­structured Life Cycle Assessments 
(LCAs) analysis and reports, implementing eco­friendly cleaning methods, and 
reducing carbon footprint are gaining traction. On top of that, with the growth of energy

becoming a trend. This enables better management of energy generation and 
consumption, as well as providing backup power during grid outages.

improvement, further research, and aggregated collaboration will further propel the industry 
forward, unlocking new possibilities and ensuring the long­term success of solar PV
