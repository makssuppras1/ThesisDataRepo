Abstract
Tribology is important when describing the interaction between foot-
wear and surface. Shoe-soles nowadays are made of viscoelastic materials
which exhibit time-dependent strain, meaning that deformation varies
for diﬀerent load-time due to the viscous characteristics. Considering
the shoe-sole’s viscoelastic nature, current methods for friction meas-
urements are insuﬃcient for providing realistic results for the dynamic
shoe-surface friction behaviour. After literature study, a new device was
designed and manufactured to investigate the friction between diﬀerent
materials used for ﬂoors and shoes with the aim to do more dynamic
tests in later projects.
The test-setup consisted of a surface rotating
at constant sliding velocity and an arm-mechanism on which the shoe
was attached. The results showed that the rubber has generally better
grip and that can be correlated with its bulk properties compared to the
other two materials. The use of diﬀerent surface-materials did not have
a distinguishable impact on the coeﬃcient of friction values and that is
attributed to the surface roughness being the main contributor to friction
forces.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Sport’s equipment went through drastic transformations throughout history.
Especially, during the past few decades, technological advances in materials
science and design made it possible to increase athletes’ performance by max-
imizing their potential.
Correspondingly, footwear was evolved to provide protection to the the foot
from injury and to increase agility. It is proven that by increasing the avail-
able traction of athletes’ shoes on the ground systematically improved the
response time for both acceleration and sprinting [4]. Therefore traction be-
haviour has been extensively investigated mechanically, using various shoe and
surface types. The experiments showed that each shoe-surface combination
aﬀects the traction generated at the shoe-surface interface [5].
Modern sport indoor ﬂoors are optimized to have high friction against sport
shoes to maximize the desired traction. Nevertheless, this has been associ-
ated with two dangerous phenomenons for the athlete’s feet. First one is the
ﬁxation of the foot to the ﬂoor [6, 7] and second one is the presence of high
pressure on the lateral part of the foot, which could lead to an ankle sprain
[8, 9].
Hence, in this project an attempt will be made to investigate how the mater-
ials’ choice on the interface between shoe and ﬂoor in sport arenas aﬀects the
overall friction forces (Ff) which generated from the contact. The ﬁndings
could help to study the consequences on the biomechanics of the human foot
and ultimately help in reducing the ankle sprain occurrences.
1.1
Background
Practising sports is a good way for maintaining a healthy lifestyle. Physical
inactivity can be very dangerous and in fact, it is responsible for, around 7%
of all deaths in Denmark according to a research conducted by the Danish
Health Authority. However, injures are an inevitable part of physical exer-
cising [10].

Lateral ankle sprain is the most common musculoskeletal injury among people
who are physically active [11]. Only in USA it is estimated that there are more
than nine million ankle sprains annually and often they end up as a chronic
condition. Almost half of the sprain emergency incidents occur during sports
practising, and approximately half of these are related to indoor sports [10].
Ankle sprains cause a big ﬁnancial burden on the health care system of many
countries. In Netherlands alone, a study showed that the treatment for ankle
sprains costs 187 million euros per year [12]. There is a huge socio-economic
beneﬁt by preventing them happening in the ﬁrst place so the necessary meas-
urements should be taken. This way not only the national health care system
will be ﬁnancially relieved by reducing treatment costs, but also there would
be less need for emergency rooms. Also, the risk of developing long term ankle
problems, which can have a big impact in people’s productivity and quality
of life, is reduced [10].
Methods for preventing ankle sprains have been applied for indoor sports due
to their popularity and their high-risk injury proﬁle [13].
However, currently these methods aim only at protecting the already injured
athletes from future ankle sprains [14].
The beneﬁcial potential of those methods for athletes without previously in-
jured ankles is less clear. Therefore, there is a great need for new strategies
to prevent ﬁrst-time ankle sprains without interfering with the athletes’ per-
formance.

2.1
Materials for shoe-soles and ﬂoors
The primary parameter whose impact is investigated in this project is the
materials which come in contact during the sliding of the shoe onto the ﬂoor-
surface.
Shoe-sole materials
Shoes have evolved a lot through history with a lot of improvements and
much more variety of models to choose from. There are designs and materials
which are more suitable for each application. The use which the shoes are
intended for aﬀects their appearance, their ability for shock absorption, and
their durability. Most of the shoe-soles, especially the ones aimed for sport
activities nowadays, are made of viscoelastic materials (Section 2.1.) such as
natural rubber, polyurethane and polyvinyl chloride compounds [15].
Elastomers
Elastomers are really appealing materials for certain applications because of
their extremely small modulus of elasticity and high deformability. These two
signiﬁcant characteristics of elastomers are due to their structure, which com-
prises of polymer molecules which have weak interaction between them and
they are in a coiled state. While a mechanical stress is applied to the material
the molecules begin to untangle and when the stress is removed they return
back to their coiled state [16].
There two big categories of elastomers are: thermoplastic (TPE) and ther-
moset elastomers (TSE). TPE achieve their properties by a physical process

(solidiﬁcation); whereas, TSE achieve their properties with a chemical process
(cross-linking) [17].
Viscoelastic properties of Elastomers
In the range between the glass transition temperature (Tg) and the melting
temperature (Tm) many elastomeric materials exhibit viscoelastic behaviour.
Viscoelasticity is the property of materials with both elastic and viscous beha-
viors. A fully elastic material will return to its original position right after a
stress stops acting on it, given that the stress did not cause any plastic deform-
ation; whereas, viscoelastic materials such as polymers dissipate a portion of
the mechanical energy applied to them and they exhibit time-dependent strain
meaning that their deformation varies for diﬀerent load times [18].
Materials used in the experiment
For the current project the materials which were used are two TPE polyester
types of thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) with the code-names TPU03 and
SD27B and one TSE (vulcanized rubber) type with the code-name RU04.
TPU
TPU is elastic and melt-processable and can be processed using several manu-
facturing methods such as extrusion, injection, blow and compression molding
equipment. TPU has a big number of physical property combinations making
it an extremely ﬂexible material adaptable to many applications.
TPU is a linear segmented block copolymer with hard and soft parts. The
soft part can either be a polyether or polyester type, depending on the needs
of the application. For instance, a polyether-based TPU can withstand wet
environments while a polyester-based one has high resistance to oil and hy-
drocarbons. Also, the molecular weight, ratio and chemical type of the hard
and soft portions can be furtherly adjusted.
This versatility is due to the unique structure of TPU which gives high resili-
ence, good compression set, resistance to impacts, abrasions, tears, weather,
and hydrocarbons. It also oﬀers ﬂexibility without the use of plasticizers as
well as a broad range of hardness’s and high elasticity. Its range of physical
properties enables TPU to be used as both a hard rubber and a soft thermo-
plastic [19].
Rubber
Vulcanized rubber it is an elastomer which has its chains connected to each
other with sulfur bridges. By adjusting the amount of the added sulfur during
vulcanization, the rubber’s hardness can be modiﬁed as desired. Some of its

Floor-surfaces materials
There is a variety of materials that are being used for each ﬂoor applica-
tion. The two materials which were used for the experiments of this project
were not representative of realistic ﬂoor-surfaces but they were used for better

is the term which will be used in this report.
Teﬂon
Teﬂon is made from the gaseous monomer tetraﬂuoroethylene, using high-
pressure suspension or solution methods in the presence of free-radical initiat-
ors [21]. Some of its most important properties are: high ﬂexibility, chemical
resistance, thermal resistance, non-stick behaviour and electrical resistance.
It is used for coating parts, e.g. bearings and screws, to increase their lifetime
and for making tubes or liners for handling or storing corrosive chemicals.
Steel
Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon in which the carbon content varies and
reaching up to two percent.
Because of its high tensile strength and low
cost, it is a major component used in buildings, infrastructure, tools, ships,
automobiles, machines and appliances.

Chapter 3
Tribology
Tribology is the subject looking into friction, lubrication and wear of surfaces
being in relative motion. It has been a subject of investigation for scientists for
many years; some basic laws of friction, like the one stating that the friction
is proportional and perpendicular to the normal force, have already developed
by the end of the 15th century.
Friction is the resistance force to the relative motion of interacting surfaces
and it generates wear and loss of kinetic energy, wear causes material wastage
and decreases the mechanical performance and lubrication is used for friction
reduction and wear control.
Almost any hardware device is aﬀected by surface interaction in multiple
levels. Some estimations suggest that one-third of all the energy consumed
in the world is to counteract diﬀerent forms of friction. Therefore, it is clear
that huge amount of savings can be achieved with better friction control [32].
3.1
Contact mechanics
Contact mechanics is the science which focuses on how solids that touch each
other deform. Two solids with theoretically ﬂat surfaces that are in contact,
practically touch each other only in small areas and the sum of these areas is
exceptionally small compared to the nominal area of contact. These contact
areas can be envisaged as the asperities of the two solids rubbing on each other
[33].
The asperities can undergo elastical or plastical deformation depending on the
conditions and the materials which are in contact and this has impact on their
coeﬃcient of friction (COF) [34].
The real contact area can be increased up to one point by applying higher
pressure and for soft solids and smooth surfaces almost total interface contact
can be achieved [35].
The contact area has a great impact on the wear and sliding friction and
inﬂuences signiﬁcantly the adhesive force between the two solids [33].

Variables inﬂuence on friction behaviour
Generally a lot of the measuring parameters can inﬂuence the COF. The most
basic ones are the sliding speed, the materials’ temperature, the acceleration
rate, the contact pressure, their surface roughness and the wear rate of the
movement. Therefore, there is not a default value of COF for each material.
Consequently, it is not realistic to use tables with recorded COF values as
a source of design data. When a relatively big investment will be made and
precised data is required, is generally advisable to measure the Ff using a pro-
totype device simulating the real system’s operating conditions. A reason that
making a prototype is a better practise is that systems which are in motion
move in an unsteady manner because of mechanical dynamics and standard
test devices might not be able to simulate those dynamical conditions. [34].
COF measurement testing
The process of measuring COF can be complicated as the data almost always
ﬂuctuate up to a small percentage because of the diﬃculty of reproducing
exactly the same measuring conditions each time. This can sometimes cause
disagreement among scientists of which values are the most applicable ones.
Organizations like the International Organization for Standardization (ISO)
formulate standards for friction test methods so that users can have a better
understanding of the results and they can make comparisons of their values
with published ones. These standard methods are also useful when materials
and the device components have to meet speciﬁc requirements [34].
Polymer tribology
There is an increase in the use of polymers for tribological applications because
of their useful properties such as high elasticity, shock absorption, low friction
and wear resistance. Additionally, polymers’ surface and bulk material can be
modiﬁed, by either chemical or physical ways to better ﬁt a speciﬁc applic-
ation. The latter reason make them exceptionally interesting for tribologists
who do a lot of research on how to control the polymers’ friction and wear
performances [36].
However, tribology of polymers diﬀers up to a great degree than tribology of
other materials. The diﬀerence is due to their chemical and physical structure
and properties. They, also, have viscoelastic properties and very low surface
free energy which diﬀers them from other materials and are easily aﬀected
by the environment conditions. Moreover, some lubricants, which work well
for other types of materials, can be absorbed by polymers. Therefore, some

traditional experimental methods might not be applicable for use in polymer
tribology [37], [36].
Connecting viscoelastic properties of elastomers to friction
Elastic modulus of elastomers E(ω) is greatly inﬂuenced by frequency and
temperature. At very low frequencies, which correspond to high temperatures,
they are very soft with a really low E(ω) and likewise, at high frequencies,
which correspond to low temperatures, they are hard with high E(ω). The
latter can be changed by a factor of 103 or more, by changing the applied fre-
quency or temperature. For experimental purposes the frequency is reversibly
analogous to the contact time. Meaning that elastomers behave as soft solids
for long contact times and the contact area is large; while for short contact
times the opposite happens. It should be noted that a relatively large fre-
quency change is needed (order of three decades) to switch from the rubbery
to the glassy region [33].
Experiments with viscoelastic materials show that their COF exhibit the same
temperature dependence as that of their complex elastic modulus E(ω) (see
section 4.1).
More speciﬁcally, at high frequencies and low temperatures,
conditions which correspond to the glassy region of the material, the latter
behaves more as a plastic; it has a low-value COF. The foregoing show that
there is a relation between the Ff and the internal friction of the viscoelastic
materials, i.e. the Ff is a bulk property of the material.
Persson[1] gives a clear explanation of the mechanics at the interacting inter-
faces during the sliding. When the viscoelastic material slides with a speed
v on a hard surface where its asperities have wavelength λ, ﬂuctuating forces
will act on it with frequencies ω ∼v/λ. Since, there is always a wide dis-
tribution of length scale of the surface peaks, there is also a wide variety of
frequencies with which the surface forces acting on the sliding material. These
forces cause cyclic deformation on the latter and there is energy dissipation
due to its internal damping capability [1].

Dynamic mechanical analysis
In order to investigate the viscoelastic properties of each material an ad hoc
measuring technique called (DMA) is used. The DMA applies an oscillatory
force, which causes a sinusoidal stress to the sample, generating a sinusoidal
strain. From the amplitude of the deformation at the peak of the sine wave
and the time delay between the stress and strain sine waves, the E(ω) can be
calculated [2].

Complex modulus (E(ω))
The E(ω) is calculated from the ratio of the sinusoidal stress to strain:
E∗= σ(t)/ϵ(t)
as a function of time. The strain which is applied on the material under invest-
igation during the experiment is kept at low levels to maintain the material’s
response to stresses linear viscoelastic.
Therefore the stress and strain change linearly in relation to each other and
the complex modulus can characterize the material’s deformation behaviour.
It is assumed that the temperature is constant and thus it does not appear as
a variable.
There is no well-established theoretical model on what is the exact inﬂuence
level of temperature on material’s viscoelasticity. However there is an em-
pirical model which relates the time and temperature eﬀect on viscoelasticity
and it is called the time - temperature superposition (TTS) principle and it
will be discussed later in detail.
The complex modulus is comprised of real and imaginary components:
E∗= E′ + i ∗E′′
E′ is known as the storage modulus and it measures the energy that is stored
into the material and represents the elastic segment of it; E′′ is known as the
loss modulus and it measures the energy transformed to heat and it represents

the viscous segment of the material.
If the solid is fully elastic the generated strain and stress waves will be abso-
lutely in phase. In contrast, for a purely viscous ﬂuid, the generated strain
wave will have a π/2 phase delay in relation to the stress one [18].
For better understanding of their physical meaning, the E′ is described as the
level of the stiﬀness of the material; Whereas the E′′ shows the level of the ma-

Time - temperature superposition (TTS)
In order to describe the viscoelastic behaviour of a linear polymer for a broad
range of frequencies, TTS is used. The latter is a tool with which it is possible
to shift values acquired from various temperatures to a chosen reference one.
The three variables, frequency, time and temperature, have a closely interre-
lated eﬀect on polymers. By decreasing the time length of an experiment is
equivalent to decreasing the temperature, so decreasing the frequency of os-
cillation imposed on the material is equivalent to increasing the experiment’s
time length or temperature [39].
To apply the the TTS technique temperature - dependent shift factors are used
on the measured stress, time or frequency values. The vertical shift factor is
multiplied with a stress measured at a temperature to calculate a stress that
is the equivalent value at the reference temperature. Likewise, the horizontal
shift factor multiplies a frequency or divides a time to calculate a reduced
value equal to the equivalent value at a speciﬁc temperature.
TTS is based on the fact that the curves of the viscoelastic properties of a
material for various temperatures have similar shape when plotted in relation
to log(t) or log(ω). Thus, a curve can be constructed for a broad frequency
range using temperatures values over smaller frequency range or time and by
moving the curves on the log time axis so that they ﬁt each other and align
as a continuous curve [38].
The curve which is formed is named as a "master curve". The latter gives
information about the viscoelastic behavior for a large range of frequencies
or times [40]. Moreover, on a master curve the ﬁve diﬀerent characteristic
viscoelastic regions or physical states of the polymer can be observed. Listing
them from lower to higher temperature, these are the glass region, the leath-
ery region, the rubbery plateau, the elastic or rubbery ﬂow and the liquid ﬂow
[38].
Transitions between viscoelastic regions move to higher temperatures at higher
frequencies. This shift is because of the time - temperature equivalence. At
high frequencies, the timescale motions are short; so the polymer responds as
if it were at a lower temperature. Thus, for higher frequencies, higher tem-
peratures are needed to achieve an equivalent mechanical state, meaning that
the transitions happen at higher temperatures.
TTS is a very useful tool but it can not be applied in all cases. Many times
it can predict the viscoelastic behaviour of polymers accurately but there are
cases where its predictions do not match the actual behaviour of the polymers
being investigated.That is because some polymers do not comply with the
assumptions taken for the use of the time-temperature equivalence principle.
For instance there is the assumption that the temperature eﬀect on the re-
laxation time is constant for its whole period but that is not always the case.
Relaxations have diﬀerent activation energies at diﬀerent temperatures and
thus, they will not superpose with only one set of temperature values.
==PAGE:32==
4.2. DMA ANALYSIS OF THE THREE ELASTOMERS

In addition, there is the assumption of the homogeneity of the polymer. That
is not the case for block copolymers and multiphase blends. The foregoing
materials might have diﬀerent shift factors for a given reference temperature
since they will have several relaxations which are unrelated.
Finally, another assumption is the consistency of the polymer’s chemical struc-
ture and morphology. A chemical change of a polymer caused by oxidation,
additional polymerization, UV degradation or crosslinking will have its prop-
erties altered, aﬀecting the TTS validity. Likewise, a change in its morphology
caused by crystallization, annealing or partial melting will change its relaxa-
tion spectrum will not follow TTS.
Therefore, TTS should be used with caution for predicting a polymer’s prop-
erties for various times and temperatures, especially for non-homogeneous
systems.
Nevertheless, if there is good alignment between the curves while shifting the
data taken from a range of temperatures, then it is a good sign that TTS is
valid and it can be used for the speciﬁc case [41].

Chapter 7
Conclusion
From the project’s ﬁndings it is shown that the material used for the shoe-sole
notably aﬀects the COF. Speciﬁcally, the rubber generates higher Ff than the
two TPU’s.
Therefore, the correlation of the COF of elastomers with their bulk properties
is conﬁrmed. Particularly the correlation can be attributed to the diﬀerence
in the viscoelastic behaviour of the materials. The mastercurves which were
created for the three materials show that RU04 has higher internal stress
damping in the high frequency region and therefore higher COF compared to
the TPU’s.
On the other hand, the substrate’s material choice does not have an inﬂuence
on the COF; concluding that the roughness of the substrate is the main inﬂu-
encing factor on the Ff.
These results, however, should be interpreted with caution as the tests values
were not consistent enough due to the ﬂuctuation of the Ff values. Thus,
it was not applicable to do a statistical analysis that could show with more
conﬁdence which variables have a noteworthy impact on the COF value.

Chapter 8
Future work
A future modiﬁed version of the set-up could eliminate the vibration of the
device during its operation and this would give the possibility to acquire more
precise results. Speciﬁcally, the generated torque from the Ff should be elim-
inated with a new design, e.g. the Ff axis could coincide with the center of
the rotating bearing. Hence, the mechanism would be always in rotational
equilibrium and the readings from the experiments will be considerably more
consistent.
Moreover, to repeat the measurements with the operating conditions which
are deﬁned in the International Standard (ISO [31]), a new gearbox can be
installed on the motor with ratio 40:1 raising its torque capacity up to eight
times compared to the current one. The new maximum linear speed will be
v = ω ∗R = 2m/s which is suﬃcient for the tests.
