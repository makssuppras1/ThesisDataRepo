Abstract
About 10% of hot (OBA) stars host a stable magnetic field, often mainly dipolar but in­
clined with respect to the rotation axis, described by the Oblique Rotator Model (ORM).
Spectropolarimetry is a method aiming at measuring the polarisation state of the light as
a function of wavelength and can reveal the strength and topology of a magnetic field
present at the surface of a star. Such presence is observable through the Zeeman effect,
the splitting of spectral lines due to a magnetic field. 17 spectropolarimetric observations
of the hot star V352 Peg have been acquired with the ESPaDOnS spectropolarimeter at
the CFHT (Canada France Hawaii Telescope) between 2018 and 2019 and completed by
one archival ESPaDOnS measurement obtained in 2011. Using Least­Squares Deconvo­
lution, the total intensity (Stokes I) and circular polarization (Stokes V) state of the spectral
lines are extracted from the spectra and a clear Zeeman signature is detected, highlight­
ing the presence of a magnetic field. The high variability of Stokes I profiles suggests an
inhomogeneous distribution of chemical elements at the stellar surface. Using a spec­
trum synthesis program, chemical abundances of V352 Peg are computed and the star
is re­classified as a chemically­peculiar main sequence star (BVp). Magnetic and bright­
ness maps of the stellar surface are generated using a Zeeman­Doppler Imaging method.
The magnetic configuration of the star is mainly poloidal, dipolar, and non­axisymmetric,
with a polar field strength of ∼9 kG and a magnetic axis almost perpendicular to the ro­
tation axis. Chemical maps of the most abundant elements are also computed. Finally,
the magnetic topology of V352 Peg is compared to the one of the hot star i Car. The mag­
netic configurations are very different, highlighting the difference in the magnetic structure
between a chemically­peculiar star (V352 Peg) and a chemically­normal star (i Car).

Introduction
Magnetic fields are known to exist in about 10% of hot stars on the main sequence. Recent
projects and international collaborations in the 21st century have enabled researchers to
progress in the understanding of stellar magnetism.
For instance, between 2008 and 2013, the MiMeS (Magnetism in Massive Stars) project
recorded circularly polarized spectra of 560 stars of spectral types O and B (Wade et al.
2016) with the objectives to understand the origin of magnetic fields in massive stars and
its role in the interaction between the stars and their environment.
The magnetism of cool stars is different from the one arising in hot stars. The origin is
due to dynamo actions in the stellar convective envelope. Conversely, hot stars exhibit
a radiative envelope where no dynamo field can arise. The magnetic field present in hot
stars is believed to have a fossil origin, a remnant of the magnetic field of the molecular
cloud where the star formed (Neiner et al. 2015). The international program LIFE (the
Large Impact of magnetic Fields on the Evolution of hot stars) aims to study evolved hot
stars in order to understand the evolution of magnetic field with stellar age and its impact
on the evolution of the stars.
A high percentage of magnetic hot stars also exhibit an inhomogeneous surface distribu­
tion of chemical elements and are classified as chemically peculiar (CP). Through atomic
diffusion, chemical elements, channeled by the magnetic field lines, gather in spots dis­
tributed around the stellar surface.
Reconstructing the magnetic field and brightness
topology of such stars can help understanding the interaction between chemical elements
and the magnetic field.
The main focus of this thesis is to characterize the magnetic field of the hot star V352 Peg.
17 spectropolarimetric observations of the star have been obtained with the ESPaDOnS
(Echelle SpectroPolarimetric Device for the Observation of Star) at the CFHT (Canada­
France­Hawaii telescope) between August 2018 and June 2019 in the scope of the LIFE
project. The dataset has been completed by an archival observation from the MiMeS
project taken in 2011. All the observations show clear Zeeman signatures attesting of
the presence of a magnetic field. By an analysis and modelling of the data, a better
understanding of the magnetic structure is expected.
In Sect. 2, I first present the theory and methods used in this work. In Sect. 3, I present
the star V352 Peg and its observations. Then, I derive the magnetic results in Sect. 4.
The data will also allow the computation of the stellar parameters and determination of
the evolutionary stage of the star, presented in Sect. 5.
In Sect. 6, the Zeeman­Doppler­Imaging (ZDI) method is used to produce a map of the
stellar surface in order to map the magnetic configuration and check for the presence of
chemical inhomogeneities produced by the magnetic field.
Finally, in Appendix C, I compute the magnetic configuration of an other massive hot star
i Car using ZDI and compare the results with the one of V352 Peg in Sect. 7.
The results obtained will be presented in an article for the Monthly Notices of the Royal
Astronomy Society (Fréour et al. to be submitted).

2.1
Spectropolarimetry
By measuring the polarization as a function of the wavelength, spectropolarimetry pro­
vides detailed information about the polarization state of the light emitted by a source,
enabling for instance to characterize circumstellar disks surrounding a star or stellar mag­
netic fields.

2.1.2
Spectropolarimeter
A spectropolarimeter is a device made of a polarimeter and a spectrograph, combined to
obtain high resolution data. The most common way to make a polarimeter is by combining
a retarder and a beamsplitter as illustrated in Fig. 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Principle of a polarimeter (from Hébrard 2015). Module A is a wave plate also
called retarder. Module S is a beamsplitter.
Module A is a plane parallel plate, for example a quarter waveplate. The light polar­
ized along the plane propagates more slowly than the light polarized in the perpendicular
direction, thus introducing a phase shift. This enables the conversion between linearly po­
larized and circularly polarized light. Module S separates the light beam into two beams
with orthogonal polarization states. A Wollaston prism is the optical component commonly
used for the beamsplitter. By rotating the retarder and acquiring at least four measure­
ments, one can measure the four Stokes parameters. Usually, polarimeters are mounted
immediately after the Cassegrain focus of the telescope to avoid to introduce instrument
polarization (Semel et al. 1993).
Then, the two light beams usually travel through double optical fibers until reaching the
Echelle spectrograph.
Echelle spectroscopy enables to achieve very high resolution on a wide wavelength range
due to higher order of diffraction. The principle is illustrated in Fig. 2.3. First, the light
ray coming from the telescope reaches the Echelle grating, creating overlapping orders
(40 in total for ESPaDOnS) forming a spectrum. Then, a high dispersion cross­disperser
prism scatters the light in the orthogonal direction. The orders get separated. Conse­
quently, with two scattering elements (the Echelle grating and the cross disperser), one
can record a large portion of a spectrum, made of many separated orders, on a rectan­
gular detector format. ESPaDOnS spectropolarimeter enables a full spectral coverage of
the optical domain (in 40 echelle orders between 372 and 1029 nm, with only 3 very small
gaps: 922.4­923.4 nm, 960.8­963.6 nm, 1002.6­1007.4 nm) in a single exposure with a
resolution in excess of 65000, achieving very high resolution measurements.
2.1.3
Zeeman effect
The four Stokes parameters introduced previously are useful to quantify the magnetic field
of a star.
In the presence of a magnetic field, the light emitted by the star is polarized. This is
observable in the emission or absorption lines. The atomic energy levels are split into a
larger number of levels and the spectral lines are also split. The pattern and amount of
splitting indicate the presence and strength of a magnetic field. The splitting is associated

2.1.6
Weak field approximation
An other underlying assumption in the LSD technique is the weak field approximation
(WFA). It relies on the hypothesis that the magnetic splitting of spectral lines via the Zee­
man effect is smaller than their local Doppler broadening.
The weak field limit is appropriate up to about 1 kG for most metal lines, or up to at least
10 kG for Balmer lines according to Bagnulo et al. 2002.
The Polarized Radiative Transfer Equation (PRT) governs the interaction between matter
and radiation when a magnetic field exists. If valid, the WFA clearly simplifies the PRT
equation, allowing one to apply a Taylor expansion. This leads to the establishment of a
much simpler relation between the Stokes V signature and the derivative of the Stokes I
parameter, according to the following equation:

2.2
The oblique rotator model
The oblique rotator model was first proposed by Stibbs 1950 to model the magnetic con­
figuration of the star HD 125248, showing important line and magnetic variability with its
rotational period.
Nowadays, the model is broadly used to describe the magnetic configuration of magnetic
hot stars.

2.3
Zeeman­Doppler­Imaging method
The Zeeman­Doppler Imaging (ZDI) method was introduced by Semel 1989. It has been
inspired by the Doppler Imaging method (Vogt and Penrod 1983), applied to polarized
data.
Doppler imaging (DI) uses the Doppler effect, the broadening of spectral lines due to
stellar rotation. DI aims at matching the wavelength position of a given spectral line to
the spatial position across the stellar disk. This match is unique, which differentiates this
method from other imaging methods developped before 1983.
An inhomogeneous surface brightness affects the shape of the spectral lines. A darker
area produces a bump in the intensity line while a brighter spot generates a depression.
This modification is correlated to the rotation of the star (Doppler effect). Thus, as the star
rotates, the darker or brighter region propagates along the line­of­sight and the associated
bump or depression propagates across the line profile.
The principle of Doppler Imaging is illustrated in Fig. 2.7 (Kochukhov 2016).
Figure 2.7: Illustration of the main principles of Doppler imaging. The two left panels show
a single spot with reduced continuum brightness at two different times while the right panel
represents a spot at the same location as the middle panel but with enhanced continuum
brightness. The curve under each stellar surface corresponds to the Stokes I profile.

Through a modelling of these profiles, Zeeman­Doppler Imaging aims at reconstructing
the vectorial magnetic field at the stellar surface. Reconstructing a brightness or magnetic
map based on a set of line profiles is an ill­posed inverse problem that can be resolved by
several methods among which is the maximum entropy method (Skilling and Bryan 1984)
used here.
A stellar spectrum can be seen as a 1D map of the visible part of the stellar surface. To
reconstruct a 3D map, many observations at different rotation phases are required in or­
der to obtain time series of Stokes I and V spectra.
This section is divided into 4 parts. Firstly, the model used to describe the magnetic
field is introduced (Sect. 2.3.1). Then, the forward problem is presented, the notations
introduced (Sect. 2.3.2). The inverse problem, solved by the ZDI algorithm is explained
in Sect. 2.3.3. Finally, Sect. 2.3.4 presents the maximum entropy method, commonly
used to obtain a unique solution to inverse problems.

2.3.2
The forward problem: reconstruction of Stokes profiles from an
image
The image is divided into a grid of approximately 1000 surface elements. The coordinates
used are colatitude, longitude, and radius. The free model parameters are:
• 3 harmonic coefficients describing the magnetic field: αl,m, βl,m, γl,m
• A coefficient bj per surface element j which describes the corresponding brightness
of the surface
From the above spherical harmonic coefficients, the magnetic field at the element j is
evaluated to obtain the vector [Br(θj, ϕj), Bθ(θj, ϕj), Bϕ(θj, ϕj)]. Based on that vector
and the local brightness bj, the local line profile Fj = (Ij, Qj, Uj, Vj), gathering the 4 local
Stokes parameters, is calculated. Finally, using the set of Fj and integrating over the
visible hemisphere of the star, one can get the final line profile F = (I, Q, U, V ). Thus,
by knowing Fj, αl,m, βl,m, γl,m and bj and given a line profile model, one can obtain the
desired Stokes spectra: this is the forward problem.
The local line profile used to model Stokes I curve is a Voigt profile, a convolution of a
Gaussian and a Lorentzian profile. It allows to combine thermal broadening with the natu­
ral or collisionally broadened line profile and is illustrated in Fig. 2.9. It better reproduces
Stokes I profiles. A simple Gaussian fit is sometimes not enough to accurately recover
their shape, especially in the case of V352 Peg, where Stokes I profiles are distorted.

2.3.3
The inverse problem: mapping of the stellar surface from Stokes I
and V spectra
The inverse problem that one should solve in order to obtain magnetic field and brightness
maps of a star can be formulated as follows.
A first image is presented. Progressively, a magnetic configuration and brightness spots
are added and modified, based on the misfit between the observed Stokes I and V spec­
tra and the spectra derived from the configuration. The procedure is iterative. Once
the convergence criterion reached, the outputs are the three harmonic coefficients of the
magnetic field (αl,m, βl,m, γl,m) and bj describing the surface brightness. These quantities
enable to obtain a 3D map of the stellar brightness and magnetic field.
2.3.4
Maximum entropy method
The inverse problem is an ill­posed problem as there are usually more pixels to reconstruct
than observations. Moreover, a multitude of configurations can produce a same set of
spectra. In other words, the solution is not unique.

star?

V352 Peg or HD 221218
3.1
General information
With a right ascension of α=23:30:01.8793479800 and declination δ=+21:32:58.376823176,
V352 Peg is located in the Pegasus constellation, at a distance of 179.301±2.624 pc (Per­
ryman et al. 1997). Its apparent magnitude is mV = 7.12 ± 0.01 in the visible band (Høg
et al. 2000).
It is classified as an Alpha2 Canum Venaticorum star, a type of chemically peculiar vari­
able star of spectral type B8p to A7p. Such stars present variable magnitude and spectral
line intensities with periods believed to be equal to their rotation period. The variability
may be due to an inhomogeneous distribution of metals in the stellar atmosphere, there­
fore these stars are good magnetic candidates.
3.2
Previous work
V352 Peg has previously been observed in the scope of the Hipparcos mission (Perryman
et al. 1997). A total of 137 observations were recorded, enabling to plot the light curve of
the star (see Fig. 3.1). The light­curve shows a clear variability with the rotation phase.
From these measurements, the variability period has been estimated to be P = 2.64±0.01
days. V352 Peg being a α2CVn type of star, the variability period is equal to its rotation
period. A first estimation of the rotation period is thus available. Both Hipparcos and Skiff
(Skiff 2014) catalogues classified the star as a B8/9III type of star, so as a giant star, lying
above the main sequence on the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram. This classification justi­
fied the observation of V352 Peg in the scope of the LIFE (the Large Impact of magnetic
Fields on the Evolution of hot stars) project.
New observations were recorded in the scope of the TESS project and presented in the
TESS Input Catalog (TIC, Stassun et al. 2019), aiming to enable the selection of optimal
targets for the planet transit search, which is the main goal of the TESS mission. Addi­
tional parameters of the star could be obtained such as the effective temperature, gravity,
radius and mass. The parameters are gathered in Tab. 3.1. TIC parameters have been
computed using GAIA­2 colors and distance. These new data suggest a spectral type
less evolved than predicted by Hipparcos and Skiff catalogues. Given its effective tem­
perature and gravity, V352 Peg would be a main sequence star rather than a giant. This
result will be confirmed in Sect 3.4.

3.5
Chemical abundances
Chemical abundances have been computed by John Landstreet using his ZEEMAN spec­
trum synthesis program (Landstreet 1988, Wade et al. 2001). This program essentially
computes a synthetic spectrum for an assumed stellar atmosphere, using an assumed
initial abundance table and if necessary a simple model of the stellar magnetic field. The
program computes the predicted spectrum, compares it with an observed spectrum, and if
required, adjusts the abundance table, one element at a time, to find a best fit to a chosen
spectral window, typically about 100 Å long. The final chemical abundances of the star
are presented in Tab. 3.3.

3.6

stars have a life expectancy of a couple of million years, low­mass stars like our Sun can
live 104 times older.

intermediate­mass star, with a life expectancy around 400 million years.

have seen the development of multiple grids of stellar evolution. The characterization of
the stellar evolution of V352 Peg relies on models developed by Ekström et al. 2012, with

Characterization of the magnetic field
To model the longitudinal magnetic field as a function of the phase, a few steps of data
preparation are necessary.
4.1
Normalization of the spectra
The first part consists in normalizing the spectra. This is very important in order to aver­
age the spectral lines and compute the longitudinal magnetic field. The continuum should
be corrected and set to 1 in order to avoid the large scale variations seen in Fig. 4.2b.
As seen in Sec. 3.3, initially, each spectrum is divided into 40 echelle orders ranging from
370 to 1050 nm that are then combined to form the spectrum observed in Fig. 4.2.
The normalization is performed for each order of the 18 spectra. Thus, the first step is to
retrieve the 40 orders from a spectrum. This is done automatically by using a Perl script.
Then, a semi­automatic Python code SpeNT developed by Martin 2017 enables to nor­
malize each order. This is an interactive pipeline (see Fig. 4.1). A cubic spline is fitted
to the continuum. The user can modify several parameters such as the number of knots
defining the third order spline or the number of iterations, the objective being to adjust the
continuum value to 1.

4.2
Tailored mask
The second step is to design a line mask. A mask is an ensemble of Dirac functions de­
scribed by the chemical element, depth of the line and the Landé factor, characterising
the sensitivity of the spectral line to the magnetic field. This is a key part of the analysis.
From Eq. 2.1, the mask is used to compute the mean Zeeman signature. A non suitable
mask triggers errors on the extracted Stokes profiles, thus on the magnetic field charac­
terization.
Firstly, a mask with solar abundances, effective temperature of Teff = 11000 K and gravity
log g = 4 was used as a template. However, it was not fitting the spectrum in certain
wavelength ranges and lot of spectral lines were unidentified.
Consequently, either the star is chemically peculiar (with non solar­abundance elements)
or its temperature and gravity were erroneous as they remained an approximation, deter­
mined based on the spectral type of the star. After trying masks with different tempera­
tures and gravity, the first option appeared to be the most probable one, especially given
the high amount of chemically peculiar stars among magnetic stars.
Besides, the splitting of spectral lines can directly be observed in the spectra, suggesting
a strong magnetic field. The 18 spectra also showed different spectral lines. In particular,
spectra obtained when the magnetic equator is directed along the line­of­sight exhibited
considerable differences in the spectral features compared to when the star is ”pole­on”
(the magnetic axis pointing toward the Earth).
To test this conjecture, the chemical abundances, temperature, and gravity of V352 Peg
have been determined by our colleague John Landstreet as explained in Sect. 3.4 and
3.5. The line­of­sight velocity ranges from 35 to 40 km/s, depending on the spectrum.
A new template mask was extracted from VALD3 (Piskunov et al. 1995, Pakhomov et al.
2017) for the above temperature and gravity, using the chemical abundances determined
in Tab. 3.3 and a micro­turbulence velocity of 5 km/s. Many more spectral lines are
identified with this new tailored mask.
To obtain the final mask used for the deconvolution of Zeeman signatures, two more steps
have to be completed.
Firstly, the mask should be cleaned from hydrogen and telluric lines, and lines blended
with those. Hydrogen lines have a very typical shape, shown in Fig. 4.4, different from
the metal lines.

To confirm that the presence of V signatures is indeed linked to the existence of a mag­
netic field, one can check two quantities: N profiles and False Alarm Probability (FAP).
N profiles (see Fig. 4.6) are featureless, indicating the absence of spurious signatures.
The FAP is computed after the deconvolution, from the Stokes V and N profiles. This
statistical quantity is a detection probability based on the χ2 statistics. It is determined
both inside and outside the stellar lines and characterizes the presence of a Zeeman sig­
nature in the Stokes V profile, enabling to distinguish between real signature and noise.
A detection is classified as definite if the FAP is smaller than 10−5, corresponding to a de­
tection probability of 99.999%. The signature is marginally detected if the FAP falls in the
interval [10−5,10−3] and not detected for a FAP greater than 10−3. Inside the stellar lines,
the presence of a magnetic field is definitely detected for the 18 Stokes V observations
and not detected in N.

5
Characterization of the star
5.1
Determination of the rotational period
Two common methods are used to determine the stellar rotational period (Silva­Valio
2008). The first one consists in using Doppler broadening, the rotational broadening of
spectral lines. The second method relies on the rotational modulation of an observable
variable (chemical spots, light curve flux, Hα emission, longitudinal magnetic field,...). The
longitudinal magnetic field varies with the rotation period of the star as illustrated in Fig.
4.7, thus can be used to recover the rotation period. An advantage of this method is that
it does not rely on the knowledge of the inclination angle i and it can be used on stars
having long periods. It is especially useful for chemically peculiar stars showing chemical
spots at the stellar surface.
5.1.1
Frequency analysis of the magnetic field
In the following, we decided to exploit the variation of the longitudinal magnetic field to
extract the rotation period of V352 Peg. To compute the rotation period of the star, a first
idea was to use a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm on the longitudinal magnetic
field measurements. However, the data being unevenly spaced in time, the FFT did not
lead to any convincing results.
The Lomb­Scargle periodogram is another method to analyse time data in the frequency
domain. It deals very well with unequally spaced data points and relies on a least­squares
fit of sinusoids to the data points. Such method is limited if the mean of the model is non­
zero, which can happen in case of small amount of data.
To overcome this issue, a generalized Lomb­Scargle periodogram is computed instead
of a classical periodogram, better dealing with small datasets (only 18 observations are
available here) and accounting for non­zero mean.
The code I used relies on the astroML python library, presented in Ivezić et al. 2014, and
was developed by Jake VanderPlas and adapted to this purpose.
A significance level is calculated based on the bootstrap statistics, a random sampling
with replacement method used to estimate the accuracy of a measurement.
The results are presented in Fig. 5.1.

5.1.2
Refinement of the period
The longitudinal magnetic field for the rotation period of 2.63605 found using the GLS is
plotted in Fig. A.1. The fit seems to be in good agreement with the data points. How­
ever, using the Zeeman­Doppler Imaging code from Folsom et al. 2018 (see Sect 6.1),
I detected a small error on the rotation period thanks to the first data point from 2011.
For a small change in phase visible in Fig A.2, the observations from August 19, 2011
and December 21, 2018 have different magnetic signatures. Thus, we used the following
reasoning to refine the rotation period.
Between the first observation on the 19th of August 2011 and the mid day of all the ob­
servations, the star rotated N = 1012 times. Thus, a small error on the rotation period
will have a strong impact on the 2011 datapoint. Such an error can be estimated based
on the misfit between the phase of this point and the expected phase from the dipolar fit.
The expected period phase of V352 Peg ϕexp when the 2011 observation was recorded
can be calculated from the dipole magnetic field model given by Eq. 4.1. It is estimated
to be ϕexp = 0.08968.
The 2011 MiMeS point phase found with the rotation period of 2.63605 days is ϕobs =
0.90131. It represents a difference in phase of ∆ϕ = 1 −ϕexp + ϕobs, thus an error on the
rotation period of e = P×∆ϕ
N
= 4.9 × 10−5 where P = 2.63605 days. The final period is
Prot = 2.63654 ± 0.0006 days. In the following, all the observations of V352 Peg are thus
phased according to this period and the following reference date HJD0 = 2458348.4725.
The precision regarding the period determination might be surprising. Indeed, the rotation
phase is determined with a precision of about a few seconds, which is very uncommon.
As mentioned above, such precision can be achieved thanks to the spectrum from 2011
and the very high data quality available.

7
Discussion of the results
7.1
Abundance maps and correlation with the magnetic
topology
The last 40 years have seen the development of many theories and models regarding the
atomic diffusion of chemical elements. Michaud et al. 1981 suggested that the magnetic
configuration plays a key role in the formation and location of chemical spots. Ionized
elements interact with the magnetic field, spiraling along the magnetic field lines in the
absence of turbulence. If turbulence, thus collision, is dominant, this effect will cancel the
magnetic channeling. When both actions coexist, the size of chemical abundance rings
can be influenced by turbulence, with a widening of the rings.
According to Michaud et al. 1981, iron peak elements are expected to gather in rings or
spots at the magnetic poles. The maps of Cr and Fe in Fig. 6.7 are in good agreement
with theory, with chemical spots present where the magnetic field is stronger.
Cr I is mainly distributed around the magnetic equator.
Interpreting the Ti II map is more complicated. The magnetic equator is clearly depleted.
Two main spots and four other spots are visible, three in each magnetic hemisphere. Ti
II might gather around regions with a strong magnetic field.
However, these results should be considered carefully. Brown et al. 1991 suggested that
Stokes Q and U profiles should be taken into account with Stokes I and V in order to derive
reliable abundance maps. Yet, by mapping the magnetic field of α2 CVn, the prototype
of α2 CVn type of stars, using the four Stokes parameters and comparing the results to
previous articles that used only Stokes I and V parameters, Kochukhov and Wade 2010
found that total intensity and circular polarization are sufficient to accurately recover the
large scale structure of the magnetic field at the stellar surface. Linear polarization (Q and
U Stokes parameters) only enable to obtain more details in the magnetic maps.
7.2
Weak field approximation limit
Details about the weak field approximation (WFA) are presented in Landi Degl’Innocenti
and Landolfi 2004. The WFA is valid when the Zeeman splitting ∆λB is much smaller
than the typical width of the line profile, called Doppler width ∆λD. The condition is given
by:

where λ0 is the wavelength of the spectral line, B the magnetic field, T the kinetic tempera­
ture, µ the atomic weight of the atom producing the spectral line, and ξ the micro­turbulent
velocity.
For example, using the Cr II line at λ0 = 4588.199 Å, taking the mean magnetic field
strength B = 2994 G, and assuming thermodynamics equilibrium in order to use the ef­
fective temperature as kinetic temperature, one obtains vB = 0.89. Thus, the Zeeman
splitting would be of the same order of magnitude as the Doppler shift.
This estimate is corroborated by Bagnulo et al. 2002 suggesting that, for spectral lines
of iron­peak elements, the weak field approximation is valid for magnetic strengths less
than about 1 kG. Also, Bagnulo et al. 2012 highlighted that other hypotheses should be
considered when applying the weak­field regime. It applies to isolated, unblended lines.

taken relative to the continuum (as 1σ of the Gaussian distribution). Then, I treated the
line profiles as LSD profiles and used the ZDI code from Folsom et al. 2018 to recover the
magnetic field at the stellar surface. The reconstructed magnetic topology for the three
values of dipole field strengths is presented in Tab. B.1. The magnetic topology is almost
perfectly recovered for a dipole field of 500 G and 5000 G and very well recovered for the

the obliquity angle β is well estimated within ±2◦. In the other hand, the dipole magnetic
field strength is underestimated, even for a magnetic field of 500 G. This underestima­
tion increases from 7% (500 G) to 23% (5000 G) and 28% (10000 G). We can conclude
that the WFA still enables to recover the magnetic topology very well for a field of 10 kG.
The main effect appears to be an underestimation of about 30 % of the polar magnetic
field strength. Therefore, the value of Bdip = 6040+155
−261 G is underestimated around 30%,
leading to a polar magnetic field strength of about 9000 G. This value is in better agree­

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, I presented the first analysis of the magnetic chemically peculiar hot star
V352 Peg. With an effective temperature of Teff = 11400 K and gravity log g = 4.22 found
with Geneva photometry in good agreement with the TIC catalogue, this is an early main
sequence star. Chemical abundances and the projected rotational velocity have been
derived by John Landstreet using his ZEEMAN code (Landstreet 1988). Chromium, iron,
and titanium are found in large excess while oxygen and helium are depleted.
Stokes I and V profiles were obtained through a least­squares deconvolution of the spec­
tra. Zeeman signatures are clearly detected in all the spectra, with a clear modulation of
the signature with the rotation phase due to a misalignment between the rotation and mag­
netic axis of the star, predicted by the Oblique Rotator Model. Stokes I profiles also show
a variability revealing the presence of chemical spots at the stellar surface. The modula­
tion is also visible in the longitudinal magnetic field strength Bl, varying with the rotation
phase of the star. This variation has been used to refine the rotation period with a Lomb­
Scargle periodogram. Then, using the archival observation from 2011, the rotation period
could be refined even more, leading to the very good accuracy of Prot = 2.63654±0.00006
days.
The Zeeman­Doppler Imaging method implemented by Folsom et al. 2018 has been used
to obtain magnetic and brightness maps of the stellar surface as well as to refine the geo­
metrical parameters. We find i = 62.5◦+4.0
−3.5, v sin i = 35.0±1.5 km/s, and β = 100.5±4.0◦in
good agreement with the analytical computation. The polar magnetic field strength is esti­
mated to be Bpol = 6040+155
−261 G, different from the 9635+1309
−996 G predicted analytically. This
is due to the weak field approximation, underestimating the polar magnetic field strength
by around 30% for a 10 kG field. Indeed, we tested the limit of the weak field approx­
imation by using line profiles calculated for a dipole magnetic field with strength of 500
G, 5000 G, and 10000 G. I used these synthetic profiles as input for the ZDI algorithm
and recovered the magnetic topology. The magnetic configuration is very well recovered,
even for a magnetic field strength reaching 10 kG. The main effect of the WFA is an un­
derestimation by 30% of the polar magnetic field strength for an initial strength of 10 kG.
Thus, V352 Peg is a strong magnetic star with a polar magnetic field strength of around
9000 G.
The magnetic field topology is mainly dipolar, which is coherent with usual configurations
for magnetic Ap and Bp stars. The toroidal component is very weak and the magnetic
field highly non­asymmetric. Abundance maps of the three most abundant elements,
chromium, iron, and titanium have been derived. Abundant regions seem to occur where
the magnetic field is stronger.
The configuration of V352 Peg has then been compared to the one of i Car, an other more
evolved hot star. The two configurations are clearly different, highlighting the differences
between the magnetic topology of a chemically­peculiar main­sequence star (V352 Peg)
and a chemically­normal evolved star (i Car).
Additional observations in linear polarization (Stokes Q and U parameters) could enable
to complete the study of the chemically peculiar star V352 Peg.

C.1.1
General information
With an apparent magnitude in the visible band of V = 3.96, i Car is a bright star located in
the constellation of Carina, at a distance of 166 ± 3 pc. No agreement has been reached
regarding its spectral type. However, all studies suggest that it is a rather evolved hot star.

2015 suggested a period of about 3 weeks. The star has recently been observed in the
scope of the TESS mission, three times in 2019 and once in 2021. The light curve was
extracted and a period of 22.24 ± 0.03 days derived (Neiner et al., in prep).

C.2
Magnetic characterization
To characterize the magnetic field of i Car, the same procedure as in Sect. 4 has been
followed.
The 34 spectra have been normalized using the SpeNT code from Martin 2017. Then,
a mask with solar abundance, an effective temperature of 18000 K and log g = 3.5 has
been cleaned from hydrogen lines and lines blended with either hydrogen or telluric lines
and tailored to the spectra (Neiner et al. in prep).
Contrary to Sect. 3.5, where one mask per spectrum is tailored, the Stokes I profiles of
i Car do not show any sign of variability with respect to the rotational phase. Thus, one
single mask is used for the 34 spectra.

Least­Square Deconvolution of the spectra
The LSD method described in Sect. 2.1.4 enables to compute the Stokes V and I, and N
profiles. They are plotted in Fig. C.1.

have the same shape from one observation to another, confirming the absence of chem­
