Enhanced Index Tracking
Abstract
In this dissertation the Thesis Model, an enhanced index tracking model, is constructed
and back-tested. The Thesis Model is based on the Omega Ratio, an operations research index
tracking model presented in the paper by Guastaroba [Guastaroba et al., 2015] which tries to
mimic and beat a stock index. The Thesis model will try and expand the Omega Ratio model
by adding periodical portfolio rebalancing and transaction cost. Several model performance
measures are tested in order to observe the performance of the Thesis Model which shows no
signs of index tracking properties and several hypothesis’s are tested in order to compensate
for this though to no avail and suggestions are discussed to make amends.
Resumé
I dette kandidatspeciale konstrueres Tesemodellen, en "enhanced index tracking"-model, som
efterfølgende bliver "back-tested". Tesemodellen er baseret på Omega Ratio modellen, en en-
hanced index tracking operationsanalyse model, præsenteret i artiklen [Guastaroba et al., 2015],
som forsøger at replikere og slå et givent aktieindeks. Omega Ratio modellen udvides med
tilføjelsen af periodiske rebalanceringer af den konstruerede portefølje samt de tilhørende
transaktionsomkonstinger. Forskellige relevante præstationsmålende modeller bliver brugt i
specialet for at teste Tesemodellens formåen. Disse blotlægger Tesemodellens manglende in-
dex tracking egenskaber. For at kompensere for den manglede index tracking evne afprøves
forskellige ændringer til Tesemodellen - dog uden held. Det konkluderes efterfølgende, at in-
gen index tracking egenskaber blev overført fra den originale Omega Ratio model, og for at
rette op på dette diskuteres forslag til fremtidig implementering af Tesemodellen.

What’s the point
There is a lot of money to be earned on the stock exchange but alas, I am not an expert investor
but an engineer. Thus, my expertise is not centred around profound knowledge about investing
in stocks but designing models to solve problems with the help of a computer. So, what if I could
get the computer to design a portfolio which would be on par with what the wise investor would
create, or maybe even better? This paper will dive into the idea of making a portfolio with the
help of models, algorithms, constraints and data from the stock exchange in order to beat a given
index (like the Standard and Poors 500). We will see whether the automation of portfolio creation
is realistic and if the equity market, which is infamous for it’s unpredictability, can be "tamed".
If this turns out plausible, the investment market could be faced with drastic changes due to the
applicability of automation on the equity market which today is still dominated by high cost
employees.

Introduction
One could imagine lending out tools with interest (bonds) and joined ownership of a cave (assets)
has been part of the economies since the dawn of the ﬁrst trade markets. Since then things have
become a lot more elaborate and the global bond and equity market is now one of the biggest and
most inﬂuential economic forces on the planet - leaving wealth and debt in its wake. It is, indeed,
so topic, that it forms the foundation for the plot in numerous movies.
Thinking about the bond and equity market, the ﬁrst thing that comes to mind are the count-
less mutual- and investment funds eagerly wanting to invest your money in the market for a
small cut. The traditional investment funds are depicted as having big staﬀs trying their best to
ﬁnd the most promising investments. These investment funds are known as actively managed
funds and not before 1975 did the ﬁrst passively managed fund (Bogle on December 31 19751)
pop up. The passively managed funds stood out from the masses by using computer algorithms
in order to construct portfolios, hence the passive i.e non-human element. Most passively man-
aged funds will, through an automated process, construct a portfolio which tracks or mimics an
index. An index is a measurement of the value of a section of the stock market. This could e.g.
be the net-worth of the biggest 500 companies in the US also known as the S&P 500 index. If the
index rises, the portfolio should be constructed in such a way that it too rises and vice versa, i.e
tracking the behaviour of the index. This investment discipline is known as Index Tracking and
investment funds using this portfolio construction approach are known as Index Funds or Index
Tracking Funds.
The idea of trying to track the market instead of beating it could sound a bit silly, but it turns
out only a small margin of the active managed funds actually beats the index [Gruber, 1996] which
paves the way for the index tracking funds popularity. Having an algorithm to dictate investments
eliminates the need for big staﬀs of highly paid investors resulting in lower running costs. The
index tracking funds also have lower trading frequency2 compared to actively managed funds
thus, again lowering the running costs by having fewer transaction costs. The combiantion of
above factors justify the presence of the index tracking funds. However, the big downside of the
index tracking funds are the lack of ﬂexibility to quickly react to new market information which
are the primary "hunting grounds" for the highly ﬂexible actively managed funds and therefore
investment funds consisting of both passive and active management are commonly found.
The area of automatic portfolio construction has undergone intense research since its intro-
duction, bringing more robust and eﬃcient algorithms and risk measures to the table, but not until

Enhanced Index Tracking
recent years has the branch of what is known as enhanced index tracking (EIT) met its spring. EIT
tries to win a bit more if the market experiences growth and loose less during a recession, just
like the few top performing actively managed funds by combining elements from the actively and
the passively managed funds.
Enhanced index funds rely on models dictating which stocks a portfolio must consist of in
order to mimic a given index. A good model will have a small index tracking error, however, a
good model can be very complex and as complexity rises, the demand for processing power and
eﬃcient algorithm follows. Over-including information into a model will increase the complexity
of the solution to such an extend that the model will use too much time before arriving to an an-
swer. Hence the challenge; how does one create an algorithm which includes enough information
to create a portfolio which satisfyingly mimics or even outperforms a given index but at the same
time has a computation time short enough to make it viable? The index tracking literature oﬀers
an abundance of diﬀerent models, many claiming good tracking properties and low computation
times - and one will be chosen and tweaked to make the testing environment as close to real life
scenarios as possible and afterwards thoroughly tested.
The Holy Grail of enhanced index tracking models is, of course, a model that constructs a
portfolio, which always will do better than the index, no matter the underlying conditions of the
index be it a market boom or crash. A more humble wish is to create a model closely tied to
the market, say with a β-value of 0.8 or 0.9 and still achieve better than average performance
of the portfolio, namely with a positive α-value, a wish that maybe isn’t entirely implausible
[Frazzini and Pedersen, 2014]. To elaborate, a β-value of 1 means that the portfolio perfectly mim-
ics the market-volatility where a β-value of 0.5 will only be exposed to half of the volatility the
market is exposed to. As an example; if the stock prices of the market goes up by 20% a portfolio
with a β-value of 0.5 will only enjoy half of the upswing and therefore only rise 10%. But if the
market should suﬀer a 4% decline the portfolio with β = 0.5 will only suﬀer half of that decline
namely 2%. The opposite is true for a β-value. When it is above 1, the portfolio will have a higher
volatility than the market and will gain more value than the market during an upswing but also
loose more money during a downswing of asset prices. In this regard the β-value is used as a
risk measure to match a risk preference of a portfolio. The α-value is a measure of the individual
investors performance and is the excess return which is not explained by a performance measure
model like the capital asset pricing model (CAPM). The α-value is also commonly known as a
Jensen’s α.

The Index Tracking Model
How does one track an index? One could mimic an index by owning a proportion of all of the
stocks which makes up the index, but this will be impractical for several reasons;
• Some stocks may be so small in proportion of the market, that owning a minimum of 1
of this stock requires an ownership of ten thousand of another stock which might be very
expensive, making the creation of the portfolio too expensive to create.
• Companies will leave the index and new companies will enter. Forcing the index tracking
portfolio to renew time after time which can cause a deﬁcit in transaction costs alone.
Thankfully there exists better ideas for tracking an index. The approach in this thesis has it roots
in mathematical modelling and operations research.

3.2
The Enhancement to Index Tracking
Taking elements from both the passively managed funds which are trying to mimic the market
by minimizing the tracking error, and elements from the active managed funds which are trying
to beat the market, an enhanced index tracking model is constructed which will use the same
fundamentals as the passive funds i.e relying solely on an algorithm/model to construct portfolios,
but the model will be sacriﬁcing tracking precision to introduce risk in order to beat the index as
seen in the actively managed funds. The simplest interpretation of this formulation would be the
Markowitz inspired formula with a direct relationship between risk and return.
λ(Rate of Return) + (1 −λ)(Tracking Error)
The above model is of course a naive simpliﬁcation of the models used by index funds but the idea
of balancing risk with tracking error is a theme found throughout the index tracking literature.

requires investments in highly volatile assets, but at every occasion the portfolio underperforms
the benchmark index the denominator will grow and repeatedly underperforming the benchmark
will have the denominator, the downside risk measure, to accumulate to such a size that any excess
return otherwise achieved by the portfolio will dwarf in comparison to the accumulated portfolio
underperformance. This urges the optimisation model to invest in such a manner that the portfo-
lio underperformance will be minimized as controlling this factor will be crucial, but at the same
time it is paramount that the numerator, the excess return, is positive or else the whole term will
become negative.
Setting a high α can tip the balance, resulting in sub-optimal performance as too high α’s can
result in the numerator inevitable becoming negative and the optimisation model will choose as-
sets in such a way that the denominator will become as large as possible to minimize the impact
of the negative numerator.
The Lower Partial Moment (LPM) was ﬁrst deﬁned by Fishburn [Fishburn, 1977] and the LPM
risk measure can be shown to have second order dominance [Prigent, 2007] and proved to be co-
herent [Artzner et al., 1999], [Giorgi and Hens, 2006].
Having coherency on a risk measure is crucial as non-coherent risk measures can give mislead-
ing results in some situations [de Vries et al., 2005]. This is the case of the popular risk measure
Value at Risk (VaR) which lacks the sub-additivity property hence, the portfolios risk proﬁle can
be manipulated by dividing the portfolios into smaller sub-portfolios.
The four properties a risk measure must satisfy in order to be a coherent risk measure is
• Monotonicity
• Sub-additivity
• Homogeneity
• Translational invariance
Other important contributors worth mentioning for the development of the LPM are Bawa
([Bawa, 1975]) and Nawrocki ([Nawrocki, 1991], [Nawrocki, 1992], [Nawrocki, 1999])

The Omega Raio Model compared to the Thesis Model
The biggest change between the two models is the introduction of the transaction cost Gj. In
order to implement the transaction costs, the conversion of xj going from a percentage of assets

the demand of data used as the Thesis Model need to use stock prices whereas the Omega Ratio
can use rate of returns as well as stock prices which can easily be converted to rate of returns.
The transaction cost was implemented by subtracting it from the real return insuring the
model will try to limit total transaction costs. Other versions of the Thesis Model which did not

As earlier suggested, the model can be expanded by adding "real life" constraints which adds
more complexity to the model at the cost of computation time but prevents irrational behaviour
the model otherwise happily would indulge in.
The ﬁrst two real life constraint added was done in accordance with Guastaroba
[Guastaroba et al., 2015] and includes a cardinality constraint and a weight constraint, preventing
spreading risk too little or too much.
Adding the cardinality constraint which restrains the number of maximum allowed assets that
can be selected to a given portfolio indirectly lowers the transaction costs, as a portfolio with
many diﬀerent assets will be more vulnerable to changes in the index resulting in higher trading
frequency implying additional transaction costs as opposed to a portfolio consisting of fewer
assets.

causes division by zero error
In order to transform the Full Linearised Thesis Model from a non-linear model to a linear, the
variable v0 was introduced and multiplied on all existing variables. The model was then executed
and all variables needed to be converted back to their original form by dividing with v0 in order to
read the original results. As v0 was multiplied on all variables (with the exception of zj) including
the transaction costs, changing v0 would also change the value of ˜Gj. While executing the model
in order to ﬁnd an optimal portfolio the models breaks down as v0 is set to equal zero in order
to limit the impact of the transaction costs. This is unfortunate as after each period ˜xc
j is un-
linearised by dividing with v0 which is zero, and a division by zero error terminates the model.
The model setting v0 = 0 happens regardless of the datasets only diﬀerentiating in which period
v0 equals zero. Removing the minimum transaction costs does prevent this error from happening
in some data-sets, but if the price of the fractional transaction costs are increased to a point where
they are no longer neglectable, the model will break down again as it sets v0 = 0 in order to avoid
losses due to transfer costs. Adding a constraint which causes v0 to be strictly positive removes
this division by zero error, hence the the Full Linearised Thesis Model is added this last constraint
v0 > 0

7.4

One tool used to reveal the performance of the Thesis Model is the capital asset pricing model oth-
erwise known as CAPM. The CAPM is a simple regression model which makes it easy to use and is
why the CAPM is still is popular today. The simplicity of the CAPM is also the Achiles heel of the
model as the empirical records of the model is poor as a consequence [Fama and French, 2004].
Other more modern and better performing approaches to asset prices exist today such as Arbi-
trage pricing theory [Burmeister and Wall, 1986] and Merton’s portfolio problem [Merton, 1969].
But despite of newer and better performing models the CAPM still provides adequate insight into
the performance of the Thesis Model and will be the basis of the performance measures calculated
in this dissertation.

7.6
The Fama-French three-factor model
An expansion of the CAPM is the Fama and French Three Factor Model designed by Eugene Fama
and Kenneth French from the University of Chicago Booth School of Business and ﬁrst published
in 1993 [Fama and French, 1993]. The Fama-French three-factor model goes more in-depth than
the CAPM model in trying to describe portfolio returns by basing portfolio returns on three dif-
ferent factors namely company size, company price-to-book ratio and market risk, whereas the
CAPM only explains returns of the portfolio with a single variable hence the CAPM cannot pro-
vide the same amount of detail as the Fama-French three-factor model [Fama and French, 1996].
The Fama-French three-factor model for portfolio i at time t is given as
E(Rit) −Rft = βiM[E(RMt) −Rft] + βisE(SMBt) + βihE(HMLt)
(80)
E(Rit) −Rft is the expected return on a portfolio in excess of the risk-free rate which can be
explained by three factors;
1. The portfolios excess return on a market or index given as E(RMt) −Rft .
2. The diﬀerence between the return on a portfolio of small stocks and the return on a portfolio
of large stock given as SMB (Small Minus Big). SMB is the average return on the three small
portfolios minus the average return on the three big portfolios,
SMB = 1
3(Small Value + Small Neutral + Small Growth)
−1
3(Big Value + Big Neutral + Big Growth)
(81)
3. The diﬀerence between the return on a portfolio of high-book-to-market stocks and the
return on a portfolio of low-book-to-market stock given as HML (High Minus Low). HML
is the average return on the two value portfolios minus the average return on the two growth
portfolios,
HML = 1
2(Small Value + Big Value) −1
2(Small Growth + Big Growth).
(82)
E(Rit)−Rf, E(SMBt) and E(HMLt) are expected premiums and the factor sensitivities βiM, βis,
and βih are the slopes in the time-series regression shown below
Rit −Rft = αi + βiM(RMt −Rft) + βisSMBt + βihHMLt + ϵit
(83)
where αi is the intercept.

7.7
Drawdown
The drawdown of a portfolio is the percentage change in the peak price of the portfolio and the
low price of the portfolio, also known as the trough of the portfolio. The maximum drawdown
is the largest of these drawdowns and is used as a risk measure to expose the inherent risk in an
investment.
The drawdown can be split up three parts, the start, the trough and the recovery period. The
start of the drawdown is the exact time at which the price of the portfolio starts to decline, the
trough is the time at which the decline price of the portfolio has stopped, and the recovery period
is the period of time the portfolio uses to recover from the trough back to the original price when
the initial drawdown started. A visualisation of a drawdown is depicted in ﬁgure 4.
The drawdown is used as risk measure where a given value of a drawdown is a "cash out"
criteria at which the stock holder will sell aﬀected stocks to take on a cash position instead. A
drawdown of 20% are a rule of thumb for most investors as a cash out criteria.
To illustrate the usefulness of the drawdown as a risk measure an example could be comparing
two investors, investor A which will cash out at 20% drawdown and investor B which will cash
out at 50%. During the ﬁnancial crisis of 2008 a drawdown of 50% was observed. Investor A would
already sell his or hers assets when the drawdown reached 20% and investor B would sell his or
hers assets when the drawdown hit 50%. Investor A’s net-worth would now only be 80% of the
previous value and needs to recover 25% in order to regain the previous net-worth. Investor B on
the other hand has lost 50% of his previous net-worth now only having 50% left and therefore needs
to increase his net-worth with a 100% in order to regain the previous net-worth. The diﬀerence
between investors A’s 25% return requirement versus investors B’s 100% return requirement are
also reﬂected in the recovery time as investor B potentially needs 4 times more recovery time than
investor A hence, the bigger the allowed drawdown the longer the recovery time of the portfolio.

Discussion
In this section results and observations discovered throughout the dissertation will be discussed.
8.1
Drawdown
The indexes and the portfolios all experienced multiple drawdowns, some bigger than others,
but focus were on the maximum drawdown - a "worst case" perspective one could say - and the
smaller the maximum drawdown the better. Only the two smallest portfolios, namely the Hang
Seng portfolio and the DAX 100 portfolio, managed to achieve a smaller drawdown than their
given indexes and especially the DAX 100 portfolio with a maximum drawdown of 13.53% only
had a 15 weeks recovery time. The DAX 100 index on the other hand spent a whopping 90 weeks
to recover from its 18.59% drawdown.
Three other portfolios, the FTSE100, S&P 100 and S&P 500, all had drawdowns close to and
above twice of what their indexes experienced, whereas the Nikkei 225 portfolio also showed a
bigger drawdown than it’s index but not in the same magnitude.
Comparing the recovery times and maximum drawdowns, there is no clear link between the
magnitude of the drawdown and the recovery period which makes sense as the return of the index
can be considered stochastic and sudden upswings or downswings can end a drawdown as well
as prolong the drawdown period. The size of the index and the severity of the drawdown does
not show any coherency either and the recovery time versus the maximum drawdown are also
non-coherent. The diﬀerence in maximum drawdown for the indexes and the portfolios as well
as their recovery times are not surprising given the low CAPM β-values from table 6.
8.2
Data and Period
The data used in this dissertation was from March 1992 to September 1997 which, relatively speak-
ing, can be considered a rather stabile period. Just before 1992 two bigger crises which could aﬀect
the datasets settled; the United States Savings & Loan crisis ending in 1991 and the Japanese as-
set price bubble also ending in 1991 and both the Nikkei 225 index and S&P 100 and S&P 500
markets were partly normalized when data for the dissertation started. The Nikkei 225 was still
aﬀected by the major prior market crashes and is the only index with a negative rate of return as
a consequence of this. Two crises did, however, occur between March 1992 and September 1997,
i.e. Black Wednesday which was ignited by speculative attacks on currencies in the European Ex-
change Rate Mechanism and the economic crisis in Mexico, which was a result of a speculative
attack and default on Mexican debt. None of these seem, however, to echo in any of the datasets.

8.5
CAPM
There exists two common - but very distinctive - investment strategies. One strategy revolves
around maximizing proﬁts with little to no regard of how the index they are competing against
behave, other than the motivation of beating the index, which serves as a comparable benchmark.
This investment strategy is known as alpha trading. The other common investment strategy tries
to mimic the index and is the very basis of the Thesis Model (and hopefully very well explained
at this point) and is known as beta trading.
8.5.1
Beta
The results of the CAPM shown in table 6 do not align with the idea of an enhanced index track-
ing portfolio as the β-values which should, give and take 10% be around 100% if the portfolio did
indeed track the index. In reality, nearly all the β-values are negative and the average β-values are
around minus 7%. This does, however, not mean that the portfolios are negatively correlated to
the index, as the zero-hypothesis (H0) for the β-values suggests there are no correlation between
the β-values of the index and the portfolios with the exception of the Nikkei 225 portfolio. This
could make sense, as the return of the portfolio is aﬀected by the transaction costs, which are
embedded in the Thesis Model. This could be to such a degree that any tracking properties would
be obscured by the fees imposed on the portfolio through buying and selling, where the index in
comparison is unaﬀected by any such transaction fees.
Hypothesis 6 stripped the Thesis Model from any transaction costs and should as a result show
good tracking capabilities. However, the β-values obtained through hypothesis 6 did not show
any tracking properties suggesting that the Thesis Models does not qualify as an index tracking
model.
8.5.2
Alpha
Jensen’s α-values of the CAPM are all positive with the exception of the Nikkei 225 portfolio.
Active trade funds operates in the same manner as the Thesis Model with little regard to the
benchmark as other investments dominates the decision process resulting in a low β-value. For
the Thesis Model it looks like the aspect of loosing too much money with excessive trading dom-
inates the outcome of the model.
8.6
Transaction costs and index tracking properties
From the CAPM section it is clear that the transaction costs plays a major role in the Thesis Model
portfolio rebalancing. The Omega Ratio model presented in the paper by [Guastaroba et al., 2015]

which is the basis of the Thesis Model showed good tracking properties but with the introduc-
tion of transaction costs when rebalancing the portfolio the index tracking properties vanishes
maybe due to the construction of the Thesis Model. The Thesis Model tries to maximize excess
return over the benchmark but each portfolio rebalancing incurs a cost, and if the model deems
the cost of trading stocks too expensive compared to the gain of the transaction, the transaction
will not happen. This means if the portfolio is rebalanced a cost incurs which will obscure the

again suﬀer. The introduction of transaction cost cripples the index tracking capabilities but at
the same time reduces the spendings on transactions.
Previous versions of the Thesis Model disregarded trade expenditures resulting in a depletion
of wealth as it indulged in costly high-frequency trading. Including transaction costs in the de-
cision process is vital for the performance of the Thesis Model it makes the Thesis Model too
conservative restricting trade activities too much.

small percentage of the stocks in the portfolio would be sold or bought causing the model to stop
trading entirely.
Hypothesis 3 showed the same results as the hypothesis 1; all rebalancing seized.
A better insight of why portfolio rebalancing stops when the budget is too big was achieved
through the test of this hypothesis. It was clear that the costs of portfolio rebalancing even if only
aﬀecting the overall budget and not directly present in the objective function is a prevailing factor
for the outcome of the models rebalancing decision making.

Conclusion
The overall performance of the model is unconvincing and highly volatile behaviour has been
observed throughout the dissertation. The β-values of the CAPM were worrying and was tried
explained by the build-in noise the model produced as the transaction costs were embedded. The
two hypotheses with the purpose to remove this static and reveal the true tracking properties of
the model were rejected, as they did not show any increase in tracking performance. They both
suggest that it is most likely the model, and not the transaction costs, which result in a poor model
performance.
Besides total absence of the essential index tracking behaviour, the Thesis Models outper-
formed their respective indexes in all cases but one. The combination of insigniﬁcant β-values
and outperforming the market with high yields, shares investing behaviour similar to active trade
funds, which shows little regard to the index but sets high performance as a top priority. This re-
sult was not the premises on which the dissertation was build, but nonetheless, the undeniable
conclusion of the thesis.

Future Work
10.1
Transaction costs
A ﬂaw with the implementation of the transaction costs, and the whole Thesis Model in general,
is that the model navigates by only using historic data which is comparable to driving a car only
using the rear view mirror. But introducing a bootstrap method to generate future scenarios to
give weight to investment choices, could be hugely beneﬁcial to soften up the otherwise con-
servative transaction costs constraint, as the model would have a little bit more elbow room for
decision making as one part of the model says "this is how the index evolves, we should follow it"
whereas the transaction costs constraints at the moment says "No, the incurred transaction costs
would eat up any gains, we will not engage in any trading". With the introduction of bootstrap
scenario-generation, the transaction costs constraint would have a probabilistic input to ﬁgure
out if a portfolio rebalancing would be beneﬁcial.
10.2
Data
The used data for the thesis did not suﬀer any big shocks and would be regarded as insuﬃcient
"ﬁeld testing" as it would be highly relevant to see how a model, which solely relies on back-
wards information, would react to major market shocks. How quickly would the model react to
new information and what would the model do? As of now this is only a speculative matter but
in future research the model should be tested to market shocks.
10.3
Drawdown
All portfolios, with the exception of the DAX 100 portfolio, have drawdowns above 20% as seen
in table 13. A future implementation of the Thesis Model should include a cash-out criteria when
drawdowns reach a critical value and observe if any enhancement to the performance is achieved.
10.4
Diﬀerent Assets
The Thesis Model is constrained to only deal with stocks, excluding options of shorting or other
assets as short term bonds like the one-month T-bills, which could be regarded as risk free invest-
ments. Especially the T-bills would be a valuable addition to the Thesis Model in combination
with a cash-out drawdown criteria.
