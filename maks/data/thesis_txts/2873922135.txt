Abstract
Integration of a hybrid propulsion system that combines the conventional internal combustion en-
gine (ICE) with a vertically oriented wind rotor. The wind rotor is designed to operate in multiple
modes, each tailored to harness different aerodynamic principles for propulsion and energy recov-
ery. Specifically, we will analyze the performance of the rotor functioning in three distinct states:
Turbine, Propeller, and Autogiro.
The turbine mode leverages the rotor to generate power by converting wind energy into mechani-
cal or electrical energy, which can supplement or reduce the load on the ICE. The propeller mode,
on the other hand, utilizes the rotor to provide additional thrust, enhancing the overall propul-
sion efficiency of the system. Finally, the autogiro mode exploits the rotor’s ability to generate
lift and stabilize the system, potentially reducing fuel consumption and improving aerodynamic
performance.
By examining these modes, we aim to identify the optimal configurations and scenarios under
which the hybrid propulsion system delivers maximum efficiency. This study not only seeks to
advance the understanding of hybrid propulsion technologies but also addresses the broader context
of reducing emissions and improving fuel economy in transportation systems. The insights gained
from this research could pave the way for innovative applications in both land-based and aerial
vehicles, where sustainable and efficient propulsion systems are increasingly becoming a priority.

1
Introduction
Decarbonisation of international shipping is now a regulatory and commercial imperative. Con-
ventional measures slow steaming, fuel switching, and exhaust after treatment deliver only incre-
mental reductions and face diminishing returns. Wind-assisted ship propulsion (WASP) has there-
fore re-emerged as a credible pathway, with modern devices such as Flettner rotors, rigid wings,
and suction sails already undergoing commercial trials. These systems exploit well-understood
aerodynamic principles but differ markedly in cost, deck integration, and control complexity.
Horizontal-axis wind turbines (HAWTs), by contrast, have received limited attention in the WASP
literature despite their maturity in the offshore wind sector. Unlike sails or Flettners, a HAWT can
operate not only as a turbine but also as a propeller or in autorotation, depending on shaft torque
and inflow conditions. This multi-regime capability offers unique potential for hybrid propulsion
architectures, but it also complicates aerodynamic modelling and control. Classical momentum-
theory treatments, such as those by Bose (1983), highlight the autorotation principle yet fall short
of capturing realistic blade geometries, unsteady wake dynamics, and the broad operating envelope
relevant to ships.
Research Gap and Contribution
Wind-assisted ship propulsion concepts have been studied for decades, ranging from Flettner rotors
to modern wing sails. Horizontal-axis turbines, however, remain comparatively underexplored in
the naval-architecture literature. The classical analysis by Bose (1983) established the principle
of autogyro mode, but relied on heavy simplifications (uniform inflow, small-angle theory) and
provided only limited guidance for practical ship integration. More recent work has focused on
either steady BEM simulations in a narrow operating regime (turbine or propeller mode only) or on
high-fidelity CFD of isolated rotors, neither of which delivers the broad operating-state coverage
required for control design or real-time efficiency prediction at sea.
This creates a gap: there is currently no high-resolution aerodynamic database that spans all three
operating regimes turbine, propeller, and autorotation—and maps rotor coefficients consistently
into a propulsion efficiency metric relevant for ships.
The present report addresses this gap by:
1. Implementing and validating an unsteady Blade-Element–Momentum (BEM) solver across
yawed and oblique inflows for a realistic, megawatt-class rotor (DTU 10 MW reference
blade).
2. Constructing a large-scale aerodynamic database that tabulates 𝐶𝑃, 𝐶𝑇, 𝐶𝐹, and 𝐶𝑆over
pitch, yaw, and tip-speed ratio, enabling interpolation across the full operating envelope.
3. Embedding these coefficients into a new dimensionless power-balance metric Δ𝑃⋆, which
quantifies net engine off-loading or penalty for arbitrary ship states (𝜇, 𝛽).

Background and Theory
This chapter establishes the theoretical background and historical context of using rotors for wind-
assisted ship propulsion. It introduces the aerodynamic principles that govern turbines and sails,
reviews the historical development of wind-assist concepts, and synthesises practical lessons from
past and ongoing projects. By doing so, it provides the conceptual foundation for the Blade-
Element–Momentum (BEM) modelling and database construction that follow in later chapters.

2.1.3
Heeling Moment and Structural Load
For shipboard horizontal-axis rotors, the resultant aerodynamic force acts above deck and induces
heel and yaw moments that must be managed by hull form and control. Compared with soft
sails, a compact HAWT typically concentrates loads into a smaller tower base and benefits from
centrifugal stiffening, which limits blade deflection. Design considerations therefore emphasize
tower compression, overturning moment, and fatigue in accordance with offshore-wind standards
adapted for maritime operations.

Context and Background
3.1
Heeling Moment and Structural Load
For shipboard horizontal-axis rotors, the resultant aerodynamic force acts above deck and induces
heel and yaw moments that must be managed by hull form and control. Compared with soft
sails, a compact HAWT typically concentrates loads into a smaller tower base and benefits from
centrifugal stiffening, which limits blade deflection. Design considerations therefore emphasise
tower compression, overturning moment, and fatigue in accordance with offshore-wind standards
adapted for maritime operations.
3.2
Historical Development
The concept of wind-assisted ship propulsion (WASP) has roots in both rigid sails and rotary de-
vices:
Figure 3.1: E-Ship 1 leaving Emden, Germany, with four Enercon rotors.
• Flettner rotors. Anton Flettner’s Buckau demonstrated Magnus-effect thrust in the 1920s.
Later examples such as Barbara/Caird revived interest in the 1980s.
• Autogyro sails. Bose’s 1983 yacht experiments showed autorotating rotors could deliver
forward thrust analogous to a sail, providing the first theoretical framework for autogyro
mode [2].

• Modern retrofits. Norsepower’s Flettner installations and the Pyxis Ocean trial (2023)
show that commercial operators are again embracing mechanical sails, with measurable fuel
savings under favourable wind conditions.
Figure 3.2: WindWings® installed on the bulk carrier Pyxis Ocean.
3.3
Physical Parallels between Turbine and Sail
The aerodynamic forces on a rotor in autorotation resemble those on a high-aspect-ratio sail:
• Force decomposition. The relative wind produces lift and drag vectors on the blades. When
resolved, these yield thrust (aligned with ship axis) and side force (perpendicular to course).
• Induction factors. Just as sails accelerate and deflect air, rotors induce an axial velocity
deficit and tangential swirl. These induction factors are central to predicting net forces.
• Control authority. Collective pitch and yaw misalignment provide trimming functions
equivalent to reefing and sheeting in sailing.
3.4
Integration into Hybrid Propulsion Architectures
A deck-mounted rotor can couple into ship propulsion in two main ways:
1. Mechanical power take-off (PTO/PTI). Shaft power is transmitted to a swivelling propeller
or directly into the drivetrain.
2. Electrical integration. Rotor-generated power is routed into the onboard grid to offset
auxiliary loads or charge batteries.
Model-predictive control over 30–60 s horizons can coordinate turbine torque, engine load, and
battery state-of-charge, achieving incremental efficiency gains [3].
3.5
Regulatory Landscape
Classification societies and international bodies have begun formalising requirements for wind-
assisted propulsion:

4
Practicality of a Three-Bladed
Horizontal-Axis Rotor
4.1
Operating Principle and Unique Capability
A deck-mounted, three-bladed horizontal-axis wind turbine (HAWT) converts the apparent wind
into useful power. This power can either (i) be transmitted mechanically or electrically to a water
propeller, enabling direct headway even into the wind, or (ii) be fed into the ship’s electrical grid
to offset auxiliary loads [4, 5].
Order-of-magnitude assessments for 𝒪(150 m) concept tankers with ∼40 m rotors indicate shaft-
power relief of about ∼0.6 MW at 𝑈∞≈12 m/s on a beam reach. Voyage-scale simulations report
fuel-use reductions of 10–20 % under favourable conditions [4, 5].
4.2
Aerodynamic Considerations
Unlike conventional wind-turbine optimisation, which maximises extracted power near the Betz
optimum (𝑎= 1/3), marine integration prioritises forward thrust and main-engine offloading. The
optimal axial induction is therefore lower than the Betz value, and blade chord/twist benefit from
re-optimisation for ship duty [6, 4].
With fine collective-pitch settings 𝜃𝑝, the rotor can operate torque-free in autorotation. Here, the
tilted lift vector functions as a very high-aspect-ratio sail, as first shown by Bose [2] and later
confirmed by CFD with only single-digit discrepancies in the pre-stall envelope [7].
4.3
Structural and Control Aspects
Finite-element studies in the Swedish Wind Turbines on Ships programme suggest that a ∼1 MW,
∅40 m rotor of about 35 t mass remains structurally feasible on vessels ≥150 m LOA, producing
heel angles of order 1∘in gale conditions [4].
Marine durability is supported by ISO 12944 C5M coatings and root heaters [8]. Collective-pitch
dumps can shed lift within 1–2 s, while nacelle yaw keeps misalignment 𝛾within a few degrees
in steady winds. Electrical PTOs incur ∼5–8 % conversion losses but offer routing flexibility,
whereas mechanical shaft lines avoid these losses but become mass-critical above ∼1.5 MW.
Model-predictive control across 30–60 s horizons that co-optimises turbine torque, engine load,
and battery SOC has shown incremental gains in simulation [3].

4.4
Economic and Environmental Pay-off
Techno-economic comparisons on LR1-class tankers suggest that a ∅35 m HAWT can rival two
∼30 m Flettner rotors in net fuel saving, with ROI timescales of only a few years depending on
fuel and carbon prices. Inclusion of EU-ETS exposure further improves payback [5, 4]. Lifecycle
assessments indicate a GHG payback of only a few years, even when construction and end-of-life
are accounted for.
4.5
Operational Challenges and Mitigations
Marine coatings and de-icing mitigate salt and ice exposure, while tip-speed caps (e.g. ≤60 m/s)
address acoustic impact at bridge wings [9]. Telescopic or knuckle towers can reduce air-draft to
≲30 m within minutes for bridge clearances [9, 10].
Alternative blade counts have drawbacks: two-bladed rotors suffer higher 1-per-rev loads and
tower-shadow transients; four- or five-bladed designs add mass and cost with little thrust benefit
at ship-limited tip-speed ratios (𝜆≈4). Three blades therefore remain the pragmatic compromise
between aeroelastic stability, acoustics, and smooth thrust delivery [6, 7].
4.6
Summary and Transition
A three-bladed, deck-mounted HAWT can deliver meaningful (∼10−30%) fuel and GHG reduc-
tions on suitably large vessels with manageable implications for stability and operations. Chal-
lenges remain in blade optimisation for marine duty, tower integration, and supervisory control,
but none appear prohibitive.

5
Definition and Theoretical Basis of Autogyro
Mode
The previous chapter established why a three-bladed horizontal-axis turbine (HAWT) is a practical
candidate for marine propulsion. A unique capability of such a rotor is its ability to operate in
autorotation—the so-called autogyro mode—where it spins freely in the apparent wind without
applied shaft torque. Unlike a wind turbine, which extracts positive torque to deliver shaft power,
or a propeller, which consumes shaft power to generate thrust, the autogyro settles into a passive
equilibrium with net aerodynamic torque equal to zero. Despite producing no shaft power, the
rotor still generates a useful aerodynamic force vector that can be resolved into thrust and side-
force components, analogous to a soft sail.
5.1
The Bose (1983) Framework
Bose’s 1983 study [2] provided the first systematic, ship-oriented analysis of such rotors. His
model introduced three simplifying assumptions that made the mathematics tractable while still
capturing the essential autorotation physics:
1. Uniform induced velocity: the rotor disc is subject to a single constant axial induction.
2. Strip theory: each blade is divided radially; elemental lift and drag forces are computed
from 2-D coefficients using small-angle approximations.
3. Torque equilibrium: free spin is enforced by requiring zero integrated torque, leading to a
quadratic in the inverse tip-speed ratio whose positive root defines the autorotation point.
In this framework, a rotor is in autogyro mode when

Even with its simplifications, Bose’s work highlighted several enduring trends:
• Untwisted, constant-chord blades autorotate more readily and achieve higher driving coef-
ficients than twisted/tapered blades.
• Maximum driving force occurs just before stall (pitch −6∘to −8∘in Bose’s examples).
• Optimum incidence varies with apparent-wind angle, so active trimming is required for ef-
fective operation.
5.4

1. It provides a clear operational definition of the autogyro mode (𝑄= 0), which is used to
identify autorotation states in the BEM database.
2. It offers reference benchmarks for expected driving-force trends and pitch dependence, against
which the DTU 10 MW rotor results can be compared.

Blade Element Momentum Theory
Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory is the primary analytical framework used in this thesis to
predict the aerodynamic performance of rotating blades, including deck-mounted rotors on ships.
By combining one-dimensional momentum balance with sectional airfoil aerodynamics, BEM pro-
vides a fast, transparent, and extensible tool for estimating thrust, torque, and power under varying
operating conditions.
6.1
Principles of BEM Theory
BEM unites two complementary approaches:
1. Momentum theory. The rotor is modelled as an actuator disk that alters the momentum
of the incoming airflow. Conservation of mass and momentum yield relationships between
induced velocity, thrust, and power.
2. Blade element theory. Each blade is divided into radial elements, each treated as an in-
dependent airfoil. Sectional lift and drag are computed from aerodynamic polars, and inte-
grated across the span to determine overall loads.
By iterating between these two descriptions, BEM predicts performance in terms of dimensionless
coefficients:

Monte Carlo simulations (𝑁= 104) with perturbed input data show:
• 𝐶𝑃is most sensitive to lift-curve slope errors (𝜎𝐶𝐿= ±0.05) [6],
• Net thrust is dominated by uncertainties in axial induction 𝑎[11],
• Errors in wind speed (±0.2 m s−1) or pitch control drift (±0.25∘) have secondary influence.

Data analysis
The previous chapter established the validity of the unsteady BEM implementation for different
inflow and yaw conditions. Having confirmed that the solver produces physically consistent forces
and power, we now extend it to build a large-scale aerodynamic database. This database is the
cornerstone for later efficiency mapping: it translates the raw aerodynamics into a form that can
be interpolated and optimised in real time for ship propulsion scenarios. The following sections

Data analysis
A numerical tool such as the BEM-based database is only valuable if its outputs are internally con-
sistent and aligned with basic physical constraints. Before employing the database for efficiency
mapping and optimisation, a series of targeted checks were performed to validate its correctness.
These checks focused on simplified limit cases where the expected behaviour can be derived ana-
lytically, and where any inconsistencies would reveal implementation errors.
10.1
Assumed Efficiencies
All verification calculations were performed with the following set of representative efficiencies:
𝜂hull = 5.0,
𝜂transm = 0.6,
𝜂prop = 0.7.
These values were chosen for the following reasons:
• Hull factor (𝜂hull = 5). This parameter represents the effective scaling between aerody-
namic force and net engine demand. A value significantly larger than unity is appropriate
because the aerodynamic thrust generated by the rotor is applied above the centreline, and
the resulting contribution to net propulsion is amplified by lever arms and hydrodynamic
interactions. Setting 𝜂hull = 5 thus provides a conservative but realistic scaling consistent
with values reported in preliminary WASP (wind-assisted ship propulsion) studies.
• Transmission efficiency (𝜂transm = 0.6). The drive train between the air rotor and the ship’s
shafting involves mechanical and electrical conversions. Typical ranges in the literature for
combined gearbox and generator systems lie between 0.5 and 0.7, depending on component
quality and load point. The adopted value of 0.6 therefore reflects a realistic mid-range
estimate.
• Water-propeller efficiency (𝜂prop = 0.7). For a modern ship propeller operating near its
design point, open-water efficiency is typically 0.65–0.75. The chosen value of 0.7 is repre-
sentative of a well-designed but non-ideal propeller, consistent with merchant vessel prac-
tice.
Together, these assumptions ensure that the verification checks are carried out under realistic op-
erating conditions without biasing the results towards overly optimistic or pessimistic outcomes.

The previous chapter demonstrated, through idealised operating points, how the rotor transitions
between turbine, propeller, and autorotation modes. While such maps and case studies are valuable
for interpreting aerodynamic trends, the ultimate question remains: what practical benefit can
this provide for a ship? In this chapter, the theoretical findings are translated into vessel-scale
calculations to quantify potential reductions in propulsion power demand.
12.1
Reference Operating Point
To illustrate the implications, three representative cases are selected from the mode analysis:
1. Case B (Turbine): 𝜇= 0.30, 𝛽= −145∘— Efficient turbine operation at low ship speed
with strong forward force component.
2. Case F (Propeller): 𝜇≈1.97, 𝛽≈165∘— Propeller operation consuming shaft power

Conclusion
This thesis has examined the feasibility of employing a wind rotor as a means of auxiliary propul-
sion on ships, with a particular focus on the DTU 10 MW reference turbine. The work combined
a Blade Element Momentum (BEM) implementation, a systematically constructed aerodynamic
database, and an optimisation framework for evaluating rotor performance across a wide envelope
of operating conditions.
The developed database and optimiser allowed a classification of rotor states into turbine, propeller,
and autorotation regimes. This provided a coherent picture of how the same rotor can extract en-
ergy from the wind, deliver thrust to the vessel, or operate in mixed states depending on the relative
flow geometry. Scenario analyses demonstrated that both idealised turbine operation and autorota-
tion yield non-negligible reductions in effective ship propulsion power demand. At representative
operating points, potential fuel savings were shown to be of significant magnitude, highlighting
the practical relevance of such systems for wind-assisted ship propulsion.
Several key contributions of this work can be emphasised:
• A validated BEM-based database was established for the DTU 10 MW reference rotor, pro-
viding a reliable foundation for efficiency mapping in maritime applications.
• The methodology was extended to capture apparent wind and ship-relative inflow effects,
enabling realistic integration of aerodynamic rotor performance into vessel operating sce-
narios.
• Optimisation of pitch, yaw, and tip-speed ratio demonstrated clear operational strategies for
maximising power extraction or thrust augmentation in different sailing conditions.
The analysis also highlighted practical constraints. Transmission efficiency, hull–propeller inter-
action, and system-level integration substantially affect the net benefit. Assumed values for hull
(𝜂hull = 5), transmission (𝜂trans = 0.6), and propeller (𝜂prop = 0.7) efficiencies illustrate that real-
istic gains are sensitive to these factors, and further engineering refinement is required to quantify
their impact at full scale. Moreover, the omission of wave-induced ship motions was a neces-
sary simplification; future models should incorporate unsteady inflow to better capture operational
variability.
Overall, the results indicate that large-scale wind rotors, when mounted on vessels, have the poten-
tial to provide measurable propulsion assistance and reduce fuel consumption. The DTU 10 MW
rotor served as a practical reference for this investigation, but the methodology developed herein
can be applied to alternative rotor geometries and novel blade designs. Future work should focus
on experimental validation, inclusion of ship-motion effects, and techno-economic assessments to
establish whether such systems can be deployed effectively in commercial shipping.
In conclusion, this study has demonstrated both the aerodynamic feasibility and the potential ben-
efits of rotor-based wind-assisted ship propulsion. While challenges remain in terms of technical

A.1
Summary of Bose (1983)
The horizontal-axis rotor that spins freely in the apparent wind—an autogyro—has been suggested
since the 1930s as an alternative to conventional soft sails for generating propulsive force on ships.
Whereas a windmill extracts shaft power, an autogyro produces an aerodynamic force vector that
can be resolved into a useful driving component, but with the structural advantage of a compact,
rigid rotor. Bose’s 1983 paper [2] was the first systematic, design-oriented attempt to quantify
these questions for marine applications.
Aim and Scope
Bose set out to create a calculation method that would:
• predict lift, drag, torque, driving and heeling force coefficients for a rotor of arbitrary plan-
form, aerofoil section and twist;
• run fast enough for parametric design studies on 1980s computers; and
• clarify how pitch setting and geometry determine the balance between useful thrust and
adverse moments.
The analysis was deliberately aerodynamic: flapping dynamics, structure, and economics were
excluded to isolate feasibility and sizing guidance.
Theoretical Framework
Bose’s model extends classical autorotation theory through three simplifications:
1. Uniform induced velocity. The rotor disk experiences a constant axial velocity deficit. This
links thrust coefficient 𝐶𝑇to inverse tip-speed ratio 𝑥.
2. Strip formulation. Each blade is divided radially. Local velocities are expressed in terms
of 𝑥, azimuth angle 𝜓, and incidence 𝑖. Small-angle approximations give closed-form thrust,
torque, and side-force expressions.
3. Torque equilibrium. For a free rotor the net torque vanishes. At a given incidence and
pitch, the integrated torque yields a quadratic in 𝑥; the positive root fixes the autorotation
operating point.
Numerical integration (Simpson’s rule, 10 radial stations) then provides global coefficients and
identifies stall or reverse-flow regions every 5∘of azimuth.

Example Rotors and Findings
Two contrasting designs illustrated the model:
Autogyro A: Constant chord, untwisted. A two-bladed rotor (𝜎= 0.20) with no twist matched
Glauert’s drag curve closely; lift was higher due to a modern lift-slope assumption. The max-
imum driving-force coefficient occurred at pitch −6∘to −8∘. At low pitch, thrust dominated
side force.
Autogyro B: Tapered, twisted. A three-bladed, twisted, cambered rotor stalled earlier at the tip.
Lower solidity raised tip-speed ratio but reduced achievable driving force.
Bose also showed that optimum incidence varied with apparent-wind angle: active trimming is
essential for effective use across different points of sail.
Assessment of Practicality
• Driving vs. heeling. On beam reaches the autogyro exceeded soft-sail driving coefficients
at similar incidence, with a lower centre of effort (beneficial for stability).
• Rolling moment. Rigid blades produced significant rolling moment; flapping hinges could
mitigate this but complicate design.
• Pitch–twist conflict. Windmill duty requires twist; autorotation prefers untwisted blades.
Variable pitch is essential to reconcile this.
• Operating envelope. Theory is valid up to 𝑖≈45∘and for tip-speed ratios avoiding sus-
tained reverse flow.

Implications
