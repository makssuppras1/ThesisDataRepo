Abstract
The goal of this thesis was to study the performance of multiple linear regression (MLR),
Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) and Artificial Neural Network (ANN) models as a
function of the number of training observations, the experimental input noise and the
modeling bias inherent in the methods in the context of pharmaceutical process sim-
ulation and optimization. This was done by implementing a framework of workflows
consisting of model building and selection, uncertainty analysis, global sensitivity anal-
ysis and process optimization which were collected in a systematic framework called the
InstantEngine framework. The central idea was to have a mechanistic model based on a
validated process serve as an in-silico laboratory called the InstantLab from which either
noisefree or noisy synthetic data could be generated at will for training models.
The study found in general that while the MLR models were not significantly affected
by noise they were unable to be used for predictions and estimating optimal operating
conditions due to inherent modeling bias which could not be overcome with more training
observations. In addition, both the GPR and ANN models were severly affected by noise
which lead to a significant decrease in model performance which could not be overcome
with more training observations.
However, for two out of three cases, GPR models were in fact able to predict acceptable
operating conditions while the ANN models were severly affected by noise and therefore
unable to estimate acceptable operating conditions unless trained on noisefree data.
However, the findings and results obtained in this thesis are problem specific to the
acylation cube model and thus future work must incorporate more InstantLab models
to obtain more general results.

1 Introduction
1.1 Background
Mathematical modeling is a complex art which is essential in many fields of science and
engineering for describing natural phenomena. A mathematical model is a description of
some subset of reality known as a system and is written in the language of mathematics.
Examples include differential equations, statistical models, difference equations, graph
theory and optimization among other fields of study [Giordano et al., 2013]. Modeling
ultimately rests upon the hypothesis that the world is governed partially or completely
by rules which can be uncovered. Furthermore, different models and types of models can
describe the same system. However, the degree to which the models are able to predict
the same system is ultimately limited by the assumptions used to build the models.
Some models might be restricted due to simplifying assumptions while others might be
just simple enough to describe the underlying mechanics.
Mathematical modeling problems are often classified as white box or black box mod-
els. The former include mechanistic models which are built upon first principles of the
underlying phenomena. The latter is an input-output relationship in which the dynam-
ics governing the system are unknown hence the term black box. These types of models
include data-driven models such as statistical and machine learning models. There are
essentially three main types of models:
• Mechanistic white-box models.
• Data-driven black-box models.
• Hybrid models.
In this thesis, the focus is to study the performance of data-driven statistical and machine
learning models. A mechanistic model is used for generating data to be used by the
data-driven models. However, in this thesis since the focus is on the study of black-box
models, hybrid models are not considered and are beyond the scope of this thesis.
Data are units of information which can be used for fitting mathematical models
for the purposes of prediction. The predictions can be either done by interpolation or
extrapolation, the former of which is for estimating new data points based on the range of
known data used to fit the model. The opposite of interpolation is extrapolation, or the
estimation of new data points beyond the observed range [Steffensen, 2006]. However,
the true value derived from reality is not necessarily the value of the recorded data in
some database. This difference or distortion is known as noise and the data can thus be
described as a combination of the true value and noise:
data = true value + noise ⇔yobs = ytrue + ε
(1.1)
where yobs is the observed quantity, ytrue is the true quantity and ε could be either
a deterministic or random quantity which distorts the true value. The effect of noisy
data can have serious adverse effects on modeling predictions. [Zhu and Wu, 2004] argue
1
==PAGE:13==
that the origin of noise is due to the errors introduced by measurement instruments and
data processing and recording by the expert. Noise can also be added by corrupting
the input by random or systematic error. The noise introduced in data is due to two
main processes: Measurement errors introduced by measurement instrument and random
noise.
In this work, statistical and machine learning methods such as multiple linear regres-
sion (MLR), Gaussian process regression (GPR), and fully connected feed forward arti-
ficial neural network (ANN) were applied to model synthetically generated experimental
data in order to evaluate model performance in terms of quality of predictions and model-
based optimization. In the context of pharmaceutical process optimization the goal of
process optimization is to find near optimal factor settings in order to maximize the yield
of a product or minimize the cost of producing a product. Furthermore, the problem
could be constrained by the impurity of one or more waste products [Montgomery, 2017].
The setting for this thesis is pharmaceutical process simulation and optimization and
thus the synthetic data is generated with a mechanistic model that serves as a sub-
stitute for the true system which is a downstream acylation process at Novo Nordisk
A/S. Specifically, the generation of synthetic experimental data is achieved by having
a mechanistic model of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) based on first principles
modelling of an acylation fed batch process.
Since the data is generated in-silico the noise level of the input factors can be chosen
to study the effect of noise on the predictive performance of the MLR, GPR and ANN
models trained on noisefree and noisy data. Furthermore, the fitted models can be judged
based on their predictions on previously unseen data for which the ground truth based
on the underlying mechanistic model is known. Therefore, the models were compared by
calculating statistical measures such as coefficient of determination (R2), sum of squared
errors (SSE), and mean squared error (RMSE) among other statistical measures.

1.2 Problem statement
The aim of this thesis is to study the performance of statistical and machine learning
models impacted by possible experimental noise and modelling bias in terms of prediction
uncertainty and optimization results in the context of pharmaceutical process simulation
and optimization for an acylation fed batch process at Novo Nordisk A/S. This will be
done by fitting the models to synthetically generated experimental data and evaluating
the models’ performance on previously unseen test data and performing comparative

1.3 Thesis contribution
This subsection presents a short description of the main contribution of this thesis,
namely the InstantEngine framework.
The mechanistic model serves as the substitute for the true system being modelled
and is used for generating synthetic experimental data. The model is referred to as the
InstantLab and also functions as a benchmark for the fitted models since the ground
truth is known. Henceforth, the ground truth refers to the true observations generated
by InstantLab with no input noise. The fitted models can then be evaluated in terms of
their predictive performance on previously unseen test data generated with InstantLab.
The workflows of model building and selection, uncertainty analysis, global sensitivity
analysis and simulation-based optimization were collected in a systematic framework
called the InstantEngine framework. The proposed framework can be used for studying
and evaluating the performance and uncertainty of predictive models in the context
of pharmaceutical process simulation and optimization. It is a framework for building
a digital twin or the virtual representation that serves as the digital counterpart of
a physical object or process. The framework is a collection of scripts written in the
MATLAB programming language and will be described in further detail in section 3 of
the thesis. An overview of the InstantEngine workflow is depicted in Figure 1. Consider
the idea of the uncertainty navigator at the bottom left of the graphic. The uncertainty
of the predictions offered by the models fitted to data can be increased or decreased by
either toggling on or off the noise level, changing the number of observations used to train
the models and the modeling bias can be changed by switching from one mathematical
model to another. Therefore, in this thesis there are three major parts which affect the
uncertainty of model predictions, namely the noise, the number of observations used for
fitting the models and the modeling bias inherent in the mathematical model.
The MATLAB codes developed in this work can be found in the zip file included
with this thesis. However, selected scripts have been published in a public repository on
GitHub. See http://github.com/Thyssen1/InstantEngine
The goal was not to derive general knowledge about the performance of MLR, GPR
and ANN models since such an endeavour would be too comprehensive and beyond the
scope of a master thesis. Instead this thesis proposes a framework which could function
as a testbed for deriving knowledge of models applied to modelling process systems in
particular the acylation fed batch process at Novo Nordisk A/S.

2 Methods
2.1 Design of experiments
Statistical design of experiments (DOE) is a widely used tool for characterizing and
optimizing chemical processes. DOE is a means for establishing an empirical relationship
between process inputs, x, and responses, y, by the systematic variation of controlled
input factors. Thus a mapping of the following form is approximated:
f : x 7→y
(2.1)
which is done with multiple linear regression (MLR) models. The recorded response are
either used for screening out significant and insignificant variables or for optimizing one
or more response variables [Weissman and Anderson, 2015] [Montgomery, 2017]. These
methods stand in contrast to the use of mechanistic models which are developed on the
basis of first principles.
2.1.1 Screening designs
In factor screening, the results of DOE experiments are analyzed to find which fac-
tors have a significant effect on the variation of the output response. The number of
variables is sought to be decreased in order to model the relevant variables and to im-
prove the economical feasibility of optimizing a response variable as a function of the
significant factors [Montgomery, 2017]. One-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) and two-level full
factorial (FF2) designs are widely used in the pharmaceutical industry for the screening
of significant and insignificant variables.
In the OFAT method, a single factor is varied from from a setpoint value to the mini-
mum and maximum values such that for k factors, 2k datapoints are generated along with
repetitions of a center or set point. In comparison to other designs, OFAT is an obsolete
method which cannot capture the identification of several factor changes and thus cannot
be used for identifying interactive or other higher order effects [N. Politis et al., 2017].
The FF2 method is an experimental design able to account for interactive effects in
which variables are coded such that they are set to either their minimum value of -1
or maximum value of +1. Thus since there are k factors that can be one of two values
there are 2k possible permutations to choose from. Therefore, the combinations where
all values are at their minimum or at their maximum or any combination of the minimum
and maximum of the inputs [N. Politis et al., 2017]. In this thesis, MATLAB’s ff2n.m
function has been used for generating a FF2 experimental design.
2.1.2 Optimization designs
Once it is suspected that only explanatory variables are left following factor screening,
a second order MLR model is fitted to results defined by a more complicated design
which allows for the estimation of interactive effects and curvature. The fitted model
is then used to estimate the optimal factor settings subject to some constraints in or-
der to optimize some quantity. This optimization could be the minimization of cost,
6
==PAGE:18==
maximization of the yield of one or more components and/or maximizing throughput of
some process. Optimization designs include Box-Behnken design (BBD), Central Com-
posite Design (CCD) and three-level full factorial design (FF3) which can account for
higher order effects in contrast to the OFAT and FF2 designs but typically require sig-
nificantly more runs [Montgomery, 2017]. Furthermore, in industry, practitioners make
use of different methods for optimization. Some use experimental designs such BBD,
CCD, FF3 or similar designs for fitting higher order MLR models while others rely on
OFAT methods.
Box-Behnken designs were introduced in 1960 by Box and Behnken. These were de-
signed to be efficient in terms of the number of required runs for fitting MLR response
surface models for use in optimization of a response. Examples include the maximization
of a yield in a process. Is considered to be one of the most proficient designs compared
to FF3 and CCD [Montgomery, 2017] and is therefore included as one of the sampling
methods in this thesis. Furthermore, MATLABs bbdesign.m function was used to gen-
erate an experimental plan dictated by the BBD. See Figure 2 for an illustration of the
design with 3 factors with coded variables such that −1 is the minimum and 1 is the
maximum of some input factor.

2.2 Latin Hypercube sampling
Latin Hypercube sampling (LHS) is a commonly used statistical method for generating
quasi-random samples from a multivariate sampling space [McKay et al., 2000]. It is
widely used in computer simulations for machine learning purposes or for Monte Carlo
simulations in part due to its space filling properties and ability to achieve higher con-
verge rates of the Monte Carlo method in comparison with other sampling methods such
as simple random sampling [Smith, 2013].
The concept is to subdivide the input domain into squares in the case for two inputs,
cubes for three inputs or hypercubes in the case of a higher dimensional sampling space.
Then points are picked at random from within each square, cube or hypercube such that
exactly one point falls within each subinterval for each input.
Figure 3: Graphical depiction of Latin Hypercube Sampling in 2 dimensions with n = 5
subdivisions of the sampling space into squares. A random point is chosen
within each of the colored squares. Graphic was created by the author of this
thesis.

∀i ∈k and a, b ∈R. Then let n ∈N be the number of samples or subdivisions of each
interval. Each interval is subdivided into n subintervals and a random point is picked
from each. In 1 dimension, the interval [a1, b1] could be considered as [0, 1] without loss of
generality. The subdivision of [0, 1] into n subintervals yields [0, 1
n], [ 1

2.5 Stepwise regression
Stepwise regression is a method in statistics for fitting MLR models in which predictors
are added or removed from the model by use of an algorithm. The selection of which
variables to add or remove is based on forward, backward or both steps by evaluating
statistical criterions such as the p-value of an F-test of the change of the sum of squared
errors resulting from the addition or removal of a term, the change in the Akaike infor-
mation criterion, the change in the Bayesian Information criterion or an increase in the
R2 value [Efroymson, 1960].
A study by [Yamashita et al., 2007] advocate for using stepwise regression with AIC
instead of criterions based on the F-statistic and BIC. Furthermore, Harrell argues that
using the R2 statistic might lead to the R2 of the final model being too high and using
p-values might be too low [Harrell, 2001]. Therefore, using the criterions ’sse’ and
’rsquared’ could lead to overfitting and irrelevant factors could be included. In ad-
dition, as previously mentioned the AIC penalizes the number of variables included in
the model. Therefore, in this thesis an approach for stepwise regression with the AIC is
utilized.

overview of the algorithm used in this thesis.

2.8 Hyperparameter optimization of machine learning models
The tuning of hyperparameters is the problem of finding an optimal set of hyperpa-
rameters for a machine learning algorithm in order to improve predictive performance.
Hyperparameters control the learning process and thus have a significant effect on the
performance of the learning algorithm.
The tuning can be done by the simple trial-and-error method in which the analyst
changes the hyperparameters manually, trains the model and evaluates the results and re-
iterates until satisfactory results are obtained. However, the problem can also be solved
by automation since there are several methods for such as random search, grid search
and Bayesian optimization [Hutter et al., 2019]. In this thesis, Bayesian optimization
is used by utilizing the functionality offered by MATLABs fitrnet.m and fitrgp.m
algorithms which have the ’OptimizeHyperparameters’ name-field for toggling on the
tuning of hyperparameters [MATLAB, 2022]. Then the hyperparameter optimization
options can be set by passing a struct of name-value arguments which include the choice
of optimizer such as Bayesian optimization, grid search and random search, maximum
18
==PAGE:30==
number of objective function evaluations, choice of acquisition function in the case of
Bayesian optimization, and cross-validation options.
The objective function in Bayesian Optimization is the machine learning algorithm
where the loss is sought to be minimized. They keep track of previous evaluations and
select subsequent set of hyperparameters based on a probabilistic model. It makes use
of an acquisition function in order to decide on which set of hyperparameters to explore
next [Hutter et al., 2019].

2.10 Uncertainty Quantification
In the context of the predictive sciences, UQ can be broadly defined as the science of
identifying, quantifying and reducing uncertainties associated with models. In general,
there are two types of uncertainty [Smith, 2013]:
• Aleatoric uncertainty: Statistical, stochastic, or irreducible uncertainty. Uncer-
tainty inherent to a problem or experiment that in principle cannot be reduced by
additional physical or experimental knowledge although more knowledge can be
used to better categorize this type of uncertainty.
• Epistemic uncertainty: Systematic uncertainty due to simplifying model assump-
tions, missing physics or basic lack of knowledge. If numerical error are negligible,
then epistemic uncertainties are often termed model errors, model discrepancies,
or model inadequacies.
In this thesis, aleatoric uncertainty is in the form of random input noise which corrupts
the datasets used to fit mathematical models. For epistemic uncertainty, the modeling
bias and increase in knowledge as the number of observations used for training are
studied for the MLR, GPR and ANN models.
Uncertainty analysis and sensitivity analysis are broadly recognized as good modeling
practices to better understand model behaviour.
However, uncertainty analysis and
sensitivity analysis are often misunderstood [Saltelli et al., 2019]. The former allows for
the quantification of the variability of the output distribution due to variability on the
inputs. The latter provides insights into how much of the model output uncertainty can
be apportioned and attributed to the uncertainty in a specific factor.
In this thesis, uncertainty analysis will be based on the method of propagation of
uncertainty with Monte Carlo simulation, confidence and prediction intervals for the
relevant modeling methods. The linear error propagation method will be used for ap-
proximating confidence intervals of the predictions for the InstantLab mechanistic model.
Global sensitivity analysis will carried out by utilizing Sobol’s method and standardized
regression coefficients.

2.10.3 Global sensitivity analysis
Global sensitivity analysis (GSA) are a class of methods which view model behaviour
in a global fashion, allowing more than one factor to vary at the same time as opposed
to the OFAT method. Among two distinct classes of GSA methods are the variance
decomposition-based methods such as Sobol indices and the regression-based methods
such as standardized regression coefficients (SRC) [Saltelli et al., 2008].
The goal of sensitivity analysis can be largely viewed as the quantification of the
relative contributions due to individual parameters or inputs, and determining how vari-
ations in the parameters affect measured QoIs. A possible definition of SA is learning
how much uncertainty in the model output can be apportioned to different sources of
uncertainty in the model input [Saltelli et al., 2004]. Thus GSA is a tool for answering
the question:
• How can uncertainty in the output be divided and allocated to different sources of
uncertainty in the inputs?
The reasons for conducting sensitivity analysis according to [Smith, 2013] are:
• Ascertain whether a model is robust or overly fragile with respect to certain inputs
or parameters.
• Can be determined if the model can be simplified by fixing insensitive parameters.

3 Instant Engine framework
This chapter presents the proposed InstantEngine framework for evaluating and discrim-
inating between statistical and machine learning methods applied to modeling pharma-
ceutical process systems. It consists of four major components: 1) InstantLab, 2) model
building and selection, 3) uncertainty and sensitivity analyses, and 4) process optimiza-
tion.
The systematic framework is based on the idea of utilizing a mechanistic model as-
sumed to serve as the best possible approximation to a real system for generating syn-
thetic data. This allows one to overcome the problem of data scarcity and allows for
evaluating the performance of different models such as MLR, GPR and ANN in terms

3.1 InstantLab
The InstantLab mechanistic model is the heart of the framework and is a method for
generating synthetic experimental data in-silico on demand. Specifically, in this thesis
the mechanistic model known as the acylation cube model which is used as the Instant-
Lab can be found in appendix A. Different noise levels can be applied to the factor inputs
in the form of normally distributed random noise.
The custom instantlab.m function InstantLab accepts three inputs namely exp plan,
sigma input and seed:
• Experimental plan: Matrix of inputs, X ∈Rn×k, with n observations and k inputs.
• Standard deviations σε,x1, . . . , σε,xk for k inputs.
• Seed for controlling random number generator and for reproducibility.
The function exp plan.m was implemented for specifying an experimental plan. The
function accepts a structure array of inputs which specifies the experimental design
used, the number of observations, lower and upper bounds for the input variables. Ex-
perimental designs include OFAT, FF2, FF3, BBD and LHS.
Then the experimental plan, X, the input noise standard deviations, σ, and the seed
for controlling random number generation are passed to the instant lab.m function
which runs the mechanistic model for each of the rows in the experimental plan. Finally,
the relevant outputs are recorded and saved in a dataset used for 1) fitting the mecha-
nistic model itself as a benchmark and 2) training the statistical and machine learning
models to be used for further analysis.

3.2.3 Learning curves
Learning curves are diagnostic tools in machine learning for understanding if the trained
models are overfitting or underfitting the data and if models are suffering from high
variance or high bias errors. The training loss and estimate of the generalization error
are plotted as a function of a key quantitiy such as the number of observations used to
training the supervised learning model. Futhermore, the generalization error estimate
could be computed on a test set as will be done in this thesis. The behaviour of the
curves can be interpreted and give valuable information on the quality of the models. In
addition, the learning curves can be used to find out how many training observations are
sufficient for fitting the models and at which point no more observations are required
for achieving an adequate result.

3.2.4 Bias quantification
The modeling bias is the notion of the model discrepancy between the predictions of the
fitted model and the true underlying system. The discrepancy manifests itself as the
difference between the model output and the true values (ground truth). In the context
of fitting to data a model, M(x), is acquired:
f(x) = M(x) + δ(x) + ε
(3.4)
where f(x) is the ground truth, M(x) is the fitted model, δ(x) is the bias at the point x
and ε is the noise. Therefore, in the case of no noise, ε = 0 where the model completely
describes the system, the modeling bias is 0 for all x, that is δ(x) = 0 ∀x ∈Rn and
f(x) = M(x). See Figure 10 for an intuitive understanding.
Figure 10: Illustration of bias and variance. Left: Bias-variance in low variance, low
bias, high variance and high bias situations. The graphic was created by the
author of this thesis.
In the ideal case where no noise is present to corrupt the dataset, D = (X, y), for
which M(x) was obtained, the bias, δ(x), is left as the difference f(x)−M(x). Different
models have intrinsic properties due to their nature and underlying assumptions which
could limit their predictive performance. For example, consider the case of fitting an
MLR model to data sampled from a sine function. The linear model is biased since it
cannot capture the underlying non-linear behaviour of the ground truth. See Figure

specific case, R2 is 61% after using n = 100 and n = 1000 observations to fit the model.
For n = 1000 the average error is RMSE = 0.4436. Thus the model will not improve

3.4 Process optimization
A constrained optimization problem is defined and the workflow for evaluating the MLR,
GPR and ANN methods for use in process optimization is as follows:
• Compute estimate of global minimum with InstantLab mechanistic model to serve
as benchmark.
• Estimate minima with fitted MLR, GPR and ANN models.
• Comparative analysis of running estimted minima with InstantLab. Analysis of
the actual yields, actual impurities and actual costs.
First the constrained optimization problem to be solved is defined. Then estimates of
the global minimum are obtained with sampling-based optimization for which N samples
are generated with the mechanistic InstantLab model and the best initial guess for the
global minimum is found among the samples. The best initial guess is used as the initial
point when the estimate of the global minimum is obtained by using fminsearchcon.m
and fmincon.m with the mechanistic InstantLab model. This best estimate is referred
to as the ground truth estimate and will serve as a benchmark for the minima computed
with the MLR, GPR and ANN models.
The yield and impurity prediction functions obtained by fitting the MLR, GPR and
ANN models to data are passed as function handles to the solver in order to compute
the value of the objective function, f(x), and the nonlinear constraints.
Although the constraints of the estimated minima in the context of the MLR, GPR and
ANN prediction functions were satisfied this might not be the case when the estimated
minima are run with InstantLab. These actual yields, actual impurities and actual costs
are then analyzed.

4.3.3 Challenges of hyperparameter optimization in Gaussian Process regression
A further analysis which is presented here showed that some GPR yield models trained
on the datasets, DBBD and DBBD,ε, tended to overfit the data for some kernel functions
which was not the case when training on the LHS datasets, DLHS and DLHS,ε.
46
==PAGE:58==
The workflow for tuning hyperparameters outlined in section 3 was followed for the
GPR models. Specifically, for each kernel function the Bayesian optimizer was run with
at most 50 objective function evaluations and an estimate of the generalization error
was obtained with 5-fold cross-validation for each model. In order to gauge the true
performance of the trained models, a test set of n = 1000 observations was generated
with InstantLab using LHS as an experimental design and the MSE was computed for
the model predictions on the test set.

the models. However, in this specific case there is some performance to be gained when

5 Future directions and criticisms
There are several future directions of this project in this section:
• The performance of the MLR models might have been improved if different basis
functions were used to account for the nonlinearities of the underlying system.
• Other experimental designs other than BBD such as Central Composite Design
such as the ones described in [Montgomery, 2017] could be applied and used as
sampling methods to generate data for fitting the models.
• Although the instantlab.m function was written with measurement noise in mind,
in this work only the effects of input noise were studied. This could be studied in
a future work.
• Bayesian optimization has been applied for process optimization [Del Castillo, 2007]
and is also being applied at Novo Nordisk. Therefore such an approach could be
included in the optimization workflow of the proposed InstantEngine framework.
• MATLABs fitrnet.m only allows for the use of three activation functions namely
the ReLu, tangent hyperbolic and sigmoid functions as shown in the methods
section. Using other machine learning APIs might offer more functionality and
different neural network architectures might be explored for better performance.
• MATLAB’s fitrgp.m does not offer functionality for optimizing the initial kernel
parameters, θ0, which according to the results shown in the thesis and from the
theory [Williams and Rasmussen, 2006] are important to obtain kernel parameters
during training which do not cause the model to overfit or underfit the data.
Therefore, in a further development a function for testing different initial points,
θ0, would be implemented.
• Study on the use of different optimization algorithms for process optimization.
• Robustness of optimum in which the input noise is assumed known and Monte
Carlo simulations are run with InstantLab around the predicted minima.
• In the context of improving the performance of the ANN model, adding noise to
the inputs thus artificially creating a larger dataset [You et al., 2019]. The original
dataset, D, is augmented by artificially creating a larger dataset by adding noise
to the input, X + ε, such that the new dataset, Dε becomes:

Criticisms:
• Modeling bias is case specific and more mathematical models must be studied
by being used as the InstantLab mechanistic model in order to generate general
knowledge on the modeling biases of the MLR, GPR and ANN methods applied
to acylation process systems.
• The results obtained in this thesis are very specific to the acylation cube model
and more InstantLab models of the same system must be studied in order to obtain
more general results.
• The problem of training data-driven models on data and obtaining general models
might not be solvable in general due to the assumptions that the mapping f : x 7→y
is a continuous mapping. For example, consider [Wahlgreen et al., 2020] for which
the steady state of system of three ODEs is considered. The result is a system
of simple equations for which nonlinear dynamics are obtained. However, in the
paper the mapping f : x 7→y was not one-to-one since some x mapped to multiple
y. If a system can be shown to exhibit such dynamics then the MLR, GPR and
ANN models which approximate continuous mappings would likely fail to describe
the dynamics adequately.
In particular for developing new InstantLab models, the general methodology proposed
by [Ryde et al., 2021] could be applied. [Ryde et al., 2021] suggested a general model
for a fed batch fermentation in which the mass balances for a well-mixed fed batch
fermentation assuming constant densities can be compactly stated as:

6 Conclusion
The proposed InstantEngine framework in MATLAB was implemented to study the
performance of MLR, GPR and ANN models impacted by noise and modelling bias
in terms of prediction uncertainty and optimization results for an acylation fed batch
process at Novo Nordisk.
The study found that the MLR model was unable to capture all of the variability
in the system in contrast to the GPR and ANN models which were successful with a
sufficient number of training observations. Furthermore, while the impact of noise on
the MLR models was insignificant these types of models offered inadequate predictions
of the underlying system with high uncertainty. Both the GPR and ANN were adversely
affected by noise which lead to a significant decrease in model performance which could
not be overcome with more training observations. In general, models trained on data
generated by using BBD outperformed the corresponding models trained on LHS data.
However, for the MLR and GPR models trained on BBD the predictions had greater
uncertainty.
Furthermore, the global sensitivity analysis section showed that all the models were
able to identify the most important factors in the system.
The effect of noise was
significant but the relative importance of the factors remained unchanged.
For process optimization in all cases the MLR model was unable to find acceptable
operating conditions with actual impurity concentrations which satisfied the hard upper
constraint of 1%. In two out of three cases, the GPR models were able to find acceptable
operating conditions while the ANN model was only able to do so when trained on
noisefree data.
However, overall the results obtained in this thesis are problem specific to the acyla-
tion cube model. Therefore, for future work more InstantLab models must be applied
and used with the framework to generate more general results regarding the model per-
formance of the MLR, GPR and ANN models.
