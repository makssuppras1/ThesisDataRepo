Abstract
Massive harmful algae blooms causing ﬁsh kills have occurred worldwide. In 1988, a massive bloom of
Prymnesium polylepis occured in Kattegat, Skagerrak and at the southern coastline of Norway, causing
both wild and caged ﬁsh to die and causing loss in the Norwegian salmon production for million of dol-
lars. In 1989, a new ﬁsh killing HAB occurred again, this time it was caused by the algae Karlodinium
armiger. The toxic compounds of these algae have never been discovered and isolated. One compound,
karmintoxin, has been isolated in the algae K. armiger. Karmitoxin is in close relation to the compounds
karlotoxins from another specie of Karlodinium, Karlodinium veneﬁcum. Close to 200 L of both algae
species have been harvested. Two of the harvested biomasses of P. polylepis were extracted. A search
of known compounds from Prymnesium parvum was performed from a LC-MS sample of these fractions
from extraction. A previously discovered and isolated compound called Polylepin A was discovered in the
biomass extractions again. From LC-MS, it was determined that the structure was a penta-chlorinated
compound. The structure of Polylepin A was solved from a NMR data set.
Fractionation of the supernatant of K. armiger was performed. It gave three fractions with compounds
in 60%, 80% and 100% MeOH. Dereplication of the 80% MeOH fraction was performed. The dereplication
process showed that karmitoxin was present in the crude sample as well as one analogs of karmitoxin.
Explorative solid phase extraction was performed on all three fractions to ﬁnd the best way of separating
the compounds in the three fraction. Strong cation exchange was chosen to separate the compounds in the
80% MeOH fraction, because it provided a good separation of compounds with little overlap. Karmitoxin
and its analog were obtained in the retained fraction. HPLC was performed on both the retained and
the unretained fraction. Four compounds were isolated, karmitoxin and three unknown compounds, one
from the unretained fraction and two from the retained fraction. NMR analysis was performed on the
unknown compound from the unretained fraction. The compound was not completely pure and it was
therefore not possible to solve the structure, but parts of the structure were solved. It was not possible
to determine if these compounds belong to the compound or an impurity. One of the isolated compounds
from the retained fraction was a karmitoxin analog, diﬀering in the mass of 4 Da similar to two more
double bonds in the apolar chain of karmitoxin.

Prymnesium polylepis i Kattegat, Skagerrak og lang den sydlige del af Norges kystlinje. Dette udbrud af
algen P. polylepis for˚arsagede at massive mængder af vilde ﬁsk og ﬁsk i opdræt døde. Dette betød at den

stoﬀer der er skyld i ﬁske dødsfaldene er endu ikke blevet fundet og isoleret. Et stof er blevet isoleret
i K. armiger, nemlig karmitoxin. Karmitoxin har næsten samme struktur som karlotoxins, hvilket er

Introduction
Harmful algae bloom (HAB) is a recurring event. happening all over the world. When HAB occurs
it eﬀects a lot of marine life.
The last HAB of the algae Prymnesium polylepis occurred in 1988 in
the Scandinavian oceans[1]. HAB often occurs in brackish waters, meaning ocean with a salinity just
above that of fresh water. The 1988 bloom started in Kattegat and Skagerrak and moved towards the
southern Norwegian coastline past part of the Swedish coastline[1]. Another reported HAB of the algae
Prymnesium parvum related to P. polylepis occured in 1989 in the fjords of Norway, killing tons of ﬁsh.
Also another ﬁsh killing algae, Karlodinium armiger, has had major HAB with a big impact. The HAB
had fatal consequences for many ﬁsh both wild ones and caged ﬁsh. The wild marine life can often swim
away and are therefore not as aﬀected as the caged ﬁsh. Every time a HAB occurs close to ﬁsh farms,
million of dollars are lost due to these ﬁsh killings[1]. Even though these HABs often occurs, these algae
are relatively unkown.
In natural product chemistry, a lot of research has throughout the years focused on algae that are
toxic toward humans, but not toxic towards ﬁsh. When ﬁsh die they are not allowed to be sold to humans
beings, so people do not get sick from eating them. The algae where a lot of research has been performed
are algae found in shellﬁsh. These algae are not toxic towards the shellﬁsh, but are toxic for human
beings. Recently, more research has focused on the algae causing HAB. This is due to the high amount
of loss of ﬁsh, a major impact on the marine life and an extensive impact on the economy in ﬁsh farming
areas.
Algae blooms have always occurred and can be caused due to a higher amount of nutrition than usual,
change of current, big amounts of fresh water mixing with the sea water. A high accumulation of the
biomass is usually also related to these blooms. However, ﬁsh farms, also providing nutrients that could
stimulate local growth of the algae is a also a possibe source of accumulating blooms.
Studies of the 1988 bloom showed that, besides the death of the ﬁsh related symptoms were damage
to the permeability of the gill epithelia, discoloration, changes in behavioural patterns, the loosening of
organs[2].
Diﬀerent compounds have been isolated from all the above mentioned three algae, but none of the
compounds have been identiﬁed as being the toxic that causes the ﬁsh killings. It has been suggested that
in some cases, it can be a group of compounds that, combined, becomes the toxin. Since the toxins are not
yet discovered more research can certainly be performed on these algae. In this master project, the object
is to identify compounds in the crude extracts of both P. polylepis and K. armiger and work towards
isolating a compound and possibly identifying the compound through analysis of NMR spectroscopy.

Algae biology
Algae is a broad term that encompasses multiple aquatic organisms. Algae can live in sea water, brackish
water and in freshwater. Algae play a big role in the marine environment and are a part of the marine food
chain as the ﬁrst nutrition. Algae are usually classiﬁed in the kingdom of Protista, but can sometimes
also be classiﬁed in the kingdom of Plantae, plants[3]. The kingdom of Protista consists of unicellular
organism and unicellular organisms in colonies. Some algae are prokaryotes and others are eukaryotes. The
prokaryotic algae are cyanobacterias which are commonly known as blue-green algae[3]. The eukaryotic
algae vary a lot in size and consist of both macroalgae and microalgaes. Macroalgae are also known
as seaweed and can become up to severeal meters long. Macroalgae include the red algae, green algae
and brown algae.
Microalgae are diatoms, which are unicellular species.
Their size, apperaance and
structure and function diﬀer a lot from algae to algae. Some algae have lost their ability to perform
photosynthesis, but most algae perform photosynthesis.
Algae in general, but especially microalgae
contribute to the production of more than half of the global primary production of phptosynthesis[3].
Some algae species are mixotrophe which means they get energy from diﬀerent sources than, for example,
only from photosynthesis[4].
The focus of this master project has been to analyse K. armiger and P. polylepis. K. armiger is a
dinoﬂagellate which is a subgroup of the Protista kingdom. The structures of dinoﬂagellates consist of
continuous layer of ﬂattened vesicles, called cortical alveoli which supports the cell membrane. A lot of
the photosynthetic producing dinoﬂagellates is mixotrophe[4]. K. armiger can feed on several diﬀerent
prey types through an inconspicuous feeding tube. This allows for feeding of large prey. K. armiger can
only obtain a high growth rate by being mixotrophic, not only through photosynthsis[4]. P. polylepis
is a haptophyte which consists of two unequal smooth ﬂagella with a haptonema in the middle. The
haptonema is a little similar to the ﬂagellas, but is used diﬀerently, and also the microtubes are arranged
diﬀerently.
Microalgal phycotoxins are toxic compounds produced by photosynthetic algae. They are produced
in the secondary metabolic pathway. They are not toxic towards the producing algae, but toxic toward
other parts of the marine ecosystem. Microalgal is a big part of the marine ecosystem where they live
together with other marine prokaryotes and eukaryotes and have to compete against them[5]. Producing
toxins is therefore a survival mechanism.
There are two groups of toxins ichthyotoxins and shellﬁsh
toxins. Shellﬁsh toxins accumulate in shellﬁsh and are toxic to humans after consumption. Ichthyotoxins
are compounds that are toxic towards ﬁsh and can cause ﬁsh kills. In this project, it is the ichthyotoxins
produced by K. armiger and P. polylepis that are of interest, due to their ﬁsh killing eﬀects during HAB.

Algae chemistry
All natural products consist of primary metabolites and secondary metabolites. Primary metabolites are
molecules in the natural products which are directly involved in the growth, development and reproduction
of the natural product.
Secondary metabolites are organic molecules that are not necessary for the
survival, but can be very speciﬁc for the specie. Secondary metabolites can have diﬀerent mechanism,
but often some of the secondary metabolites produced by the the natural product have something to do
with protection. This can be toxic secondary metabolites which can work as a survival mechanism toward
their enemies.
7.1
Secondary algae metabolites
In algae research, diﬀerent secondary metabolites have been isolated and identiﬁed.
These diﬀerent
compounds all have diﬀerent structural properties, but some species and algae produce similar structures,
which have been divided into diﬀerent structural groups. During this part, diﬀerent phycotoxins will be
reviewed along with chlorophylls[5].
7.1.1
Tetrahydropurines
The tetrahydropurine group is one of the most studied groups of metabolites of all algal toxins because
they are very toxic towards humans. They are the principle metabolites responsible for paralytic shellﬁsh
poisoning (PSP). Saxitoxin, which can be seen in ﬁgure 1, is one of the ﬁrst metabolite found responsible
Figure 1: Structure of saxitoxin[6]
for PSP and subsequenty, lots of other analogs have been isolated and characterized[5]. Saxitoxin is a com-
pound formed from one tetrahydropurine group and has diﬀerent sidegroups. A Tetrahydroprine group is
the backbone of the compound which can be seen in ﬁgure 1. More than 50 derivatives and 25 analogs have
been structurally elucidated. These metabolites are responsible for potent neurotoxicity which works as
sodium ion channel blockers and is known collectively as paralytic shellﬁsh toxins (PST)[5]. These toxins
have predominantly been found in dinoﬂagellate species like the genera Alexandrium, Gymnodinium and
Pyrodinium, but it has also been shown that cyanobacteria and bacteria cultivated from toxic dinoﬂag-
ellates may produce these toxins. Even though a lot of research has been performed on these microalgal
and a lot of toxic compounds have been isolated, none of these toxins have been associated with ﬁsh kills.

7.1.2
Secondary Amines
Secondary amines are a group of compounds with either a secondary amine in a ﬁve-membered ring or
incorporated in a six-membered ring. From the genus Pseudonitzschia, a ﬁve-membered secondary amine
is produced named domoic acid, which can be seen in ﬁgure 2. This compound is a kainoid analog and
has three carboxylic acids[5]. This secondary amine is neurotoxic and accumulates in shellﬁsh feeding on
these diatoms and can therefore be the cause of amnesic shellﬁsh poisoning when eating seafood.
Figure 2: Structure of domoic acid[7]
The other types of secondary amines are called azaspiracids which is a six-membered secondary amine ring,
a carboxylic acid and three spiro-assemblies incorporated in a spiro-polyether backbone. The azaspiracid
toxins were ﬁrst shown to be produceded by the dinoﬂagellate Azadinium spinosum, but have later been
shown to be produced by other species of Azadinium[5].
7.1.3
Macrocyclic Imines
The macrocyclic imines are a group of compounds that are shown to be fast-acting neurotoxins when
injected into mice, but do not have any toxicity towards humans.
The macrocyclic imines all have
a macrocyclic scaﬀold containing an imine functional group, but diﬀer in the size of the macrocyclic
scaﬀold[5]. All these toxins are produced by diﬀerent species. An example of a macrocyclic imine is be
shown in ﬁgure 3. Spirolides are produced by the dinoﬂagellate A. ostenfeldii and A. peruvianum.

8
Analytical chemistry
Analytical chemistry is the overall name of the type of chemistry where the steps of sample collecting,
sample preparation, separation, purifying and analysing all ﬁts under. Analytical chemistry is used all
over the world to either determine which compounds are in your sample or to verify that you have made
the right compound.
8.1
Sample preparation
Extraction is an important part of sample preparation. It is the ﬁrst step to separate the desired natural
product from the sample. The most widely used method of extraction is solvent extraction. There are four
steps in the extraction: the solvent penetrates into the solid matrix, the solute dissolves in the solvents, the
solute is diﬀused out of the solid matrix and the extracted solutes are collected[13]. Selecting the solvent is
very important for solvent extraction. When choosing a solvent some of the things to consider is selectivity
and solubility. Some of the universal solvents in solvent extraction for phytochemical investigation are
alcohols. They often have a polarity close to the polarity of the solute so they are likely to work very
well. More than one solvent with diﬀerent polarities is often used because secondary metabolites can
have diﬀerent polarities[13]. This is for example used when extracting the secondary metabolites from
the algae biomass. If the diﬀusivity and solubility are enhanced it will facilitate the extraction. Some of
the properties that have an eﬀect on the eﬃciency of the extraction are the solvents, the particle size of
the sample, the extraction temperature, the solvent-to-solid ratio and the extraction duration[13].
Another type of extract is solid phase extraction. This is the technique used to extract the supernatant
of K. armiger from the water. This technique is described further in section 8.2.2.
8.2
Chromatography
Chromatography is a method used to separate and purify samples from complex mixtures to obtain a
pure natural product. The extracts mentioned in section 8.1 contain a lot of compounds and to obtain
only a selected compound, it is important to be able to separate and purify the compounds. Several
diﬀerent types of chromatography methods exist with the overall categories being gas chromatography
(GC) and liquid chromatography (LC). In this project, LC is used and described more in detail in the
next sections[14].
8.2.1
Liquid chromatography
Liquid chromatography (LC) is a very versatile method of separation where the mobile phase is a liq-
uid. There are several diﬀerent liquid chromatography methods. Most commonly LC is carried out in a
column[14]. All the diﬀerent LC methods used in this project are carried out in solid phase extraction
which is explained in section 8.2.2. Some of the techniques used are ion exchange, diol and size-exclusion,

8.2.2
Solid phase extraction
Solid phase extraction consist of a stationary phase and a mobile phase. The stationary and mobile phase
vary depending on which extractions are run. Solid phase extraction can be used simply as an extraction
method or as a chromatography technique to purify compounds. Diﬀerent solid phase extraction tech-
niques will be explained further down[15]. Like liquid extraction also solid phase extraction is based on
diﬀerent polarities and therefore reverse phase or normal phase extraction can be used and both of these
technique also use two diﬀerent solvents with diﬀerent polarities. In reverse phase and normal phase
extraction, the gradient of the two solvents changes over time and diﬀerent fractions will therefore be
collected and have diﬀerent analytes in them.
Explorative solid phase extraction
Explorative Solid-Phase Extraction (E-SPE) is a method used
to explore diﬀerent solid-phase extraction methods to ﬁnd the best methods to use to isolate the desired
compound. E-SPE is used in natural product chemistry because natural products often are very complex
and consist of a lot of products[15]. Natural products often have a very high chemical diversity and
are therefore possible to isolate by diﬀerent extraction methods. When the examined compounds are
unknown and the yield is not very high, E-SPE is a good method to use because a very small amount of
the crude is used.
Reverse phase separation is a very used methods in natural product chemistry because it is easily repro-
ducible and has a high capacity, but it also has its limitations when extracts contain a lot of compounds.
When there are a lot of compounds in the extract some of them tend to elute at the same time and the sep-
aration will not be pure. A new method using a matrix system of extraction methods will therefore make
it easier to isolate the compounds without them eluting at the same time. Diﬀerent separation methods
can be used in E-SPE and depending on whether the compounds desired are known or unknown, diﬀerent
methods can be used[15]. In this project, the compounds were unknown and therefore the functionality
and composition of the compound were unknown. We therefore chose several diﬀerent methods to cover a
range of functionalities like diﬀerent sizes of molecules, diﬀerent functionality groups like carboxylic acids
and amines and diﬀerent polarities. The diﬀerent methods are described further in the next sections.

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is a very used chromatography method in separation

8.3.1
Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy
If a compound contains conjugated double bond it will absorb light in the ultraviolet or visible (UV-vis)
range in the electromagnetic spectrum. The wavelenght of the compound depends on the unsaturation and
conjugation of the compounds. A lot of compounds have conjugated double bonds and would thereby be
detected. The part of the compounds with an absorption is called the chromophore. These chromophores
absorb speciﬁc wavelengths above 210 nm and therefore have instinct absorption patterns. These patterns
can be used in the dereplication process described in section 9, by matching the spectrum with library
spectra to help identifying peaks. A diode-array-detector (DAD) is used to record the spectrum of each
solute as it elutes. The DAD consists of several detectors. All these detectors are responsible for collecting
light of a small wavelength interval. A light source of a deuterium and a tungsten lamp sends light to a
mirror. Here, it is reﬂected into eluent[14]. The elute runs in a zig-zag system coming in to a straight
tube close to the detector, and runs to where the light enters and bends oﬀinto another tube in the
zig-zag formation[14]. The light therefore runs against the elute, before hitting a slit and thereafter a
mirror that bend the light down to a grafting polychromator and spread the light to be detected in all
the diode-array-detectors. Ultraviolet detectors are good for gradient elution with nonabsorbing solvents
which is used in a HPLC system[14].

according to their mass-to-charge ratio. In this project, a mass spectrometer with a quadrupole and a
time-of-ﬂight detector is used as illustrated in ﬁgure 17. A diode-array-detector is also used as a detector

Ion source
Electrospray ionization (ESI) is the most common method to convert molecules into ions. It is a soft
ionisation method and a good way of transferring the analytes from high pressure conditions in UHPLC
to high vacuum conditions in MS. The ionisation method is illustrated in ﬁgure 18, part 1. The analyte
enters the steel nebulizer capillary as a solvent from the liquid chromatography along with a coaxial

compared to the spray chamber[14]. Because of the strong electric ﬁeld, the droplets are repelled towards
the sampling cone and it is in the space between the nebulizer capillary and the sampling cone that the

ion transmission over a wide mass range. A lens directs the ions and molecules from the octopole to the
quadrupole where the mass range becomes more narrow than in the octopole.

Quadrupole
The quadrupole is a common mass separator because of its low cost and because it focuses the ions or
molecules into the mass analyser. The quadrupole consists of four parallel metal rods placed in a square as
illustrated in ﬁgure 19. To these metal rods, a constant voltage and a radio-frequency oscillating voltage
are applied[14]. The oscillating electric ﬁeld is generated between the rods and this electric ﬁeld deﬂects
ions in complex trajectories. Only ions or molecules with one particular mass-to-charge ratio that are
very stable will exit the quadrupole towards a collision cell. The other ions that do not have a stable
trajectory will collide with the rods before they reach the mass analyser. The voltage are varied so that
only one selected precursor ion reaches the next stage which is a collision cell[14] as illustrated in ﬁgure
17. The collision cell is a radio frequency ion guide that sends all ions of all m/z on to the next stage.
When entering the collision cell, the precursor ions collide with N2 or Ar at a very low pressure and break
into fragments called product ions before they reach the detector.

Time-of-ﬂight
Ions from the collision cell are directed into the source region of the Time-of-Flight (TOF). These ions are
injected orthogonally from the collision cell to the TOF because it reduces the spread of kinetic energy
and thereby reduces the width of a peak reaching the detector[14]. In the source region of the TOF, there
are a backplate seen in ﬁgure 20 as the ion pulser. On this backplate, approximately 20,000 V is applied
Figure 20: an illustration of a Time-of-Flight mass analyser[27]
about 3,000 to 20,000 times per second to accelerate ions up into the drift region. There is no electric
or magnetic ﬁeld in the drift region so the ions will not be further accelerated. All ions have the same
kinetic energy which is expressed in the equation
Ekin = 1
2mv2
where m is the mass and v is the velocity[14]. When the ions of diﬀerent masses have the same kinetic
energy the lighter ions will travel faster than heavier ions. All the ions do not have the same kinetic
energy, though, because in the source region, not all the ions are accelerated close to the backplate, some
of them can be accelerated closer to the grid. The ions formed close to the grid will not have as high a
kinetic energy as the ions formed close to the backplate, but those formed close to the grid will be expelled
ﬁrst[14]. There is a space of s in the source region between the backplate and the grid. The faster ions
will have caught up to the slower ion when they reach the space focus plane with a distance of 2s[14].
Therefore all ions of the same mass reach the space focus plane at the same time. For the rest of the ﬁrst
drift region the faster ions will overtake the slower ones before reaching the reﬂectron. The reﬂectron is
a series of hollow rings with an increasingly positive potential with a grid at the end. This grid has a
potential more positive than the grid on the backplate in the source region. The ions are slowed down
and stopped and thereafter reﬂected back towards the right side of the TOF and the detector. Depending
on the kinetic energy of the ions when penetrating the reﬂectron, they will penetrate further in to the
Page 17 of 69

reﬂectron before they are turned around. There is a grid just before the detector. All ions with the same
mass will reach this grid at the same time, regardless of their initial kinetic energy. In this way, it is

is approximately 2 ppm. TOF therefore provides high resolution and high mass accuracy. Combining

Dereplication process
Dereplication is a process used to identify already known compounds and new compounds from a crude
extract.
Dereplication is a very important process after the initial extraction because it saves time
and resources. It allows you to eliminate the already known compounds and to focus on only isolating
new compounds, so no rediscovery is performed.
Dereplication cannot stand alone and is reliant on
chromatography and spectroscopy together with database searching[28]. Dereplication is performed on
analysis of crude extracts data from LC-MS combined with database searches. The data provided from
LC-MS give a total ion chromatogram (TIC). TIC is the sum of all intensities of all peaks from the same
scan plotted against retention time. A base peak chromatogram (BPC) is then extracted which gives a
more smooth baseline because it is only the sum of the ions with the highest abundance from each scan
that is showed against retention time. From here, it is possible to assign molecular ions to the diﬀerent
peaks in the BPC. By clicking on a certain peak at a speciﬁc retention time a mass spectrum will show
with various molecular ions. There are two diﬀerent patterns to look at, the isotope pattern and the
adduct pattern.
An isotope pattern is the pattern around the [M + H]+ peak.
This often also gives
some extra peaks [M + H]+ + 1, [M + H]+ + 2, diﬀering in how many peaks with a space of 1 mass unit.
One of the things you can see from this isotopic pattern is if the peak is protonated [M + H]+ or double
protonated [M + 2H]2+. The mass in the mass spectrum is given as a mass-to-charge ratio, m/z. The
mass displayed in the mass spectrum is the monoisotopic mass. The monoisotopic mass is the sum of the
accurate masses of the primary isotope of each atom in the molecule. Monoisotopic mass is expressed in
Dalton (Da). If the space between these peaks are 1 mass unit it is charged once, z = 1. This means that
if z = 2 the [M + H]+ + 1 has a space 0.5 mass units from [M + H]+ and it is double charged. Another
important thing to be revealed from the isotopic pattern is the presence of any halogens. Halogens all
have diﬀerent isotope abundancies that will show in this part of the mass spectrum. An example is 35Cl
which has a +2 isotope 37Cl with a natural abundance of 31.98%. This shows in the A+2 peak being
32% of A if there is one chlorine molecules present in the compound. 62% of A if there is two chlorine
molecules present in the molecule, etc. This also shows for 81Br which is a +2 isotopes and has a natural
abundance of 50%.
An adduct pattern is the mass diﬀerence ∆m/z between known molecular ions in the spectrum. Most
natural products are ionised in ESI+, but some molecules cannot ionise in positive mode which means
that they cannot be detected in ESI+. If a natural product is not detected by ESI+ negative electrospray
Page 18 of 69
==PAGE:27==
ionisation can be used, ESI−. The most common adduct diﬀer from ESI+ and ESI−. The most common
adducts for ESI+ is [M + H]+ and [M + Na]+, which has a mass diﬀerence of 22Da. [M + Na]+ is a very
stable molecular ion and therefore occurs quite often in the mass spectrum. Other common adducts are
[M + K]+ with a mass diﬀerence of 38Da from [M + H]+ and [M + NH4]+ with a mass diﬀerence of 17Da.
Another adduct arises from the mobile phase used. Acetonitrile is a neutral molecule, but can bind to
[M + H]+ and the mass diﬀerence between [M + H + MeCN]+ and [M + H]+ 41Da. The compounds can
also have neutral losses. A common loss is the loss of water [M + H −H2O]+ with a mass diﬀerence of
-18Da. These adduct patterns do not all have to be present to ﬁnd an exact mass, but it helps conﬁrming
the exact mass. If the mass has been found to be double charged through the isotopic pattern the adduct
pattern will be double charged as well and show in halves as well, so the adduct pattern between [M + H]+
and [M + Na]+ will have a mass diﬀerence of 11Da.
After investigating the isotope and adduct pattern it can be discovered that more than one compound can
elute at the same retention time which will show in the same peak. Deconvolution is a technique used to
diﬀerentiate between compound eluting at the same time. Extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) is used for
this. To use EIC, the molecular ions in question will be extracted. This will give two smaller peaks and the
retention time of these two peaks is then possible to determine. Based on all the information collected from
the mass spectrum, a possible molecular formula can be calculated. The program Qualitative Analysis
(Masshunter) has a function that can do this for you.
The next step is to search databases to see if the compounds have previously been described in literature.
The exact mass can be used to search in diﬀerent databases like Antibase 2017 and Reaxy or other online
or inhouse databases.

Identiﬁcation by NMR spectroscopy
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is a very important technique used to identify natural
products. NMR is an analytical method used to solve chemical structures in organic chemistry and nat-
ural products, by determining how the diﬀerent atoms are located by looking at the chemical shifts of
1H-atoms and 13C-atoms.
When a compound has been isolated NMR spectroscopy is run to try to solve the structure. There
are various experiments that can be run in both one dimension (1D) and in two dimensions (2D). It is
important to decide which experiments to run, depending on the amount of sample isolated. For small
compounds with less than 500 MW, the amount needed to run a NMR experiment is less than for larger
compounds with a higher than 500 MW.
The ﬁrst spectrum that is acquired for all samples is a 1D 1H. In a 1D 1H, only one time domain is
obtained and it is the simplest experiment to run. The spectrum contains information about the number
Page 19 of 69
==PAGE:28==
of protons in the compound. The signal intensity is proportional to the number of protons in the com-
pound. Integration of the peaks in the proton spectrum will therefore provide the total number of proton
in the compound. The chemical shifts of the proton spectrum provide information about the environ-
ment around the nuclei. Oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur and other atoms will force the chemical shift downﬁeld.
Looking at the multiplicity of the peak will provide information of the neighbour to the proton. If there
are no neighbouring protons the peak will only give one peak. The peak will split up in one more peak
than the neighbouring protons. The proton spectrum also indicates whether the compound is pure. If
the compound is not pure there will be more exchange of protons. The baseline of the spectrum will
therefore not be as clean. The more complex the compound is the more the protons will overlap. The
NMR experiments of this project have all been run in the solvent CD3OD. When using CD3OD, the
protons in alcohol groups will exchange with deuterium and therefore not all protons can be seen in the
1D 1H spectrum.
Another 1D experiment that can be run is 1D 13C −NMR. When the amount of sample is limited 1D
13C −NMR experiment is skipped and a 2D experiment of Hetero Single Quantum Correlation (HSQC)
is run instead because the HSQC is more sensitive than 1D 13C −NMR. 2D experiment uses a second
time domain providing a higher resolution. HSQC provides the correlation between carbon and proton
within one bond. The sensitivity of this experiment becomes much lower than for 13C −NMR because
the proton becomes attached to the 13C. In this spectrum, all protons are designated to a carbon and it is
also possible to assign diastereotopic protons to the same chemical shift, assigning protons with diﬀerent
chemical shifts to the same same chemical shift, making it a CH2 group. A disadvantage of HSQC is that
quartinary carbons cannot be seen.
Another experiment which is prioritised is Double Quantum Filter Correlation Spectroscopy (DQF-
COSY). This is a simple 2D experiment which can provide information of the correlations in the proton
coupling systems. The DQF-COSY spectrum consists of a proton spectrum in both time domains and
when two nuclei couple to each other it gives rise to a cross peak.
The couplings are proton-proton
coupling and the couplings can be either 2JHH, 3JHH or 4−5JHH couplings. The DQF suppresses singlets
that cannot have a cross peak and it makes the spectrum easier to interpret.
The last experiment which is prioritised for almost every compound is Hetero Multi Bond Correlation
(HMBC). HMBC shows correlations between carbon and protons that are separated with 2, 3 or 4 bonds.
The 4 bond correlation is weak and cannot always be seen. The correlation between carbon and protons
of one bond like in HSQC is suppressed. An advantage of HMBC is that it allows coupling between
quartinary carbons and protons. When a 1D 13C is not run the combination of running both HSQC and
HMBC provides all the same information as the 1D 13C would provide without using as much sample
and without taking as long time as a 1D 13C and at the same time providing more information, because
Page 20 of 69
==PAGE:29==
correlations to protons are provided in the these two spectra.
Two other experiments that can be run are Heteronuclear 2 Bond Correlation (H2BC) and Total
Correlation Spectroscopy (TOCSY). H2BC provides signals between carbon and protons that are 2 bonds
away. H2BC essentially provides the same information as DQF-COSY and HSQC spectra can provide.
DQF-COSY and HSQC are therefore prioritised and H2BC is only run if there is enough sample. TOCSY
can also provide information about the correlations in the proton coupling system. In a TOCSY spectrum,
the protons can only couple to protons as long as the carbons can transfer them, and it is still only
carbons with a proton on that can transfer the signal. TOCSY can also be mixed with a HSQC, making
a Heteronuclear Single Quantum Coherence-Total Correlation Spectroscopy (HSQC-TOCSY). Here the
second dimension is the 13C chemical shifts in stead of proton shifts. The 13C chemical shift often has a
better resolution than the chemical shift of 1H and it is therefore a useful tool to determine overlapping
protons. If both a DQF-COSY and a HMBC spectrum provide enough information to solve the structure,
then H2BC and TOCSY are usually not prioritised when the amount of sample is little. If there is enough
sample, then all these these experiments can be run.

Conclusion
The algae P. polylepis and K. armiger were chosen as the subject of this project. They both produce
ﬁsh killing toxins, but very little is known about the chemistry of these algae. The purpose is therefore
to isolate compounds to know more about their chemistry. Extraction of the P. polylepis biomass was
performed. LC-MS results showed various compounds for dereplication. None of the compounds gave
any hit in database searches. An EIC of [M + H]+ with m/z=676.0833 was acquired. [M + H]+ with
m/z=676.0833 was a compound called Polylepin A that has been isolated in a previous project in this
Natural Product group at DTU Bioengineering, but has not yet been published. The EIC gave a peak
with low intensity of [M + H]+ with m/z=676.0833. A NMR data set of Polylepin A was acquired and
the structure was solved.
Through dereplication of K. armiger, it was determined, that the previously isolated compound kar-
mitoxin was present in the crude sample. Also analogs of karmitoxin were present in the crude sample.
E-SPE was used to determine which method of solid phase extraction would separate the compound best
and supplement HPLC for isolation of compounds. SCX was used to extract the positively charged com-
pound from the neutral compounds. Karmitoxin was present in the retained fraction of positively charged
compounds together with the analogs. HPLC was performed on both fractions. From the neutral fraction
[M + H]+ with m/z=341.2323 was isolated and NMR experiments were acquired. From the 1D proton
NMR, it was determined, that the compound was not pure. It was not possible to solve the structure, but
small fractions of a structure was solved. It was not possible to determine if these fractions of a structure
belonged to the [M + H]+ with m/z=341.2323 or if they belonged to another compound.
From the retained fraction three compounds were isolated, karmitoxin being one of them. The two
unknown compounds isolated were [M + H]+ with m/z=1382.8871 and [M + H]+ with m/z=750.5903.
NMR data sets were acquired for both of these compounds, but have not yet been solved. From the
mass spectrum it is thought that the karmitoxin analog can have the formula of C73H121NO23. It has
a mass diﬀerence of ∆m/z 4 which could indicate the presence of 2 more double bonds. Karmitoxin
shows a resemblance to Karlotoxins. Karlotoxins and several analogs have been isolated and they diﬀer
in the apolar chain. It is therefore thought that the 3 double bonds can be placed in the apolar arm in
karmitoxin.
