Abstract
Today, prefabricated reinforced concrete elements are central to most construction projects
in Denmark. Compared to in-situ construction work, precast elements are generally made
to ensure better quality and precision. However, the connections between the elements
introduce weakness in the system, and failure is inclined to happen here. It is therefore
important for a practical assessment of structural robustness to be able to model the post-
cracking behaviour of such shear connections. This thesis seeks to do so.
Experimental data on a shear connection subjected to shear was analyzed using the soft-
ware GOM Correlate to obtain surface strains and response curve. The data were analyzed
in detail using diﬀerent software settings to ensure the accuracy of the results. It was found
that changing the standard settings did not provide any signiﬁcantly diﬀerent information.
Decreasing the facet size by 50% revealed some additional details in the strains, but not
of signiﬁcance for the general behaviour, and the results contained considerably more noise.
To model the post-cracking behaviour of a shear connection consisting of a single key, a
model combining the upper bound theory with the tension chord model was derived. Re-
sults showed that the model did not capture the ductile response of the shear connection,
as deformation was not accounted for.
To capture the ductile response a second-order plasticity model was formed. The model
combined contributions from dowel and catenary action in the reinforcement with the dis-
sipation in the yield line. But this did not model the response adequately. The response
curve was greatly aﬀected by the decreasing contribution from the yield line.
Combining dowel and catenary action with friction produced a response in better agree-
ment with experimental results. Here the curve for the total load increased continuously.
The modelled response curve only slightly underestimated the measured response.
The model was then used to model the response of shear connections consisting of multi-
ple shear keys. Here it was found that it did not capture the behaviour. The measured
response showed a signiﬁcant brittle behaviour for the connection as the mortar cracked
extensively, and the model was not formed for such a mechanism.

Introduction
Concrete is the most widely used material in the world. The material is central to most
construction projects, and the assessment of the behaviour of the material is therefore of
great importance. Today, the most common use of concrete in construction in Denmark
is in the form of prefabricated elements. Prefabricated reinforced concrete elements are
produced by casting concrete in reusable formwork and curing it in a controlled environ-
ment. The elements are transported to the construction site and placed as a part of the
structure. This is in sharp contrast to the traditional way of construction where concrete
is cast in forms and cured on site. The fact that precast elements are made in a controlled
environment improves the quality and precision, and using precast elements is generally
considered more economical and less time-consuming.
However, because the behaviour of precast elements is not comparable to that of in-situ
cast structures, the use of precast elements must be accounted for in the design. Precast
elements are highly inﬂuenced by the construction joints, i.e. the joints between the precast
elements (Sørensen, 2018). These construction joints normally introduce weakness in the
structure, and failure is therefore inclined to take place here.
Extensive research has been performed by Sørensen (2018) to estimate the capacity of the
structural joints. Using a derived upper bound solution, the ﬁrst peak load of a shear
connection can be calculated with reasonable accuracy. However, to asses the overall ro-
bustness of a structural system made from precast elements, it is important to form a model
that captures the post cracking behaviour of shear connections. Some shear connections
show a large ductile response after cracking, and to utilize this, a model accounting for it
must be formed. The main objective of this thesis is to capture this behaviour.
First, the use of the Digital Image Correlation (DIC) software GOM Correlate is examined.
The software is widely used by researchers to determine deformation on the surface of en-
tire test specimens and not just at speciﬁc points. Using diﬀerent settings in the software,
an experiment on a concrete specimen is analyzed and the optimal settings are determined.
The focus is then moved to the post cracking behaviour of shear keyed joints. To begin
with, a joint consisting of a single key is evaluated. A model based on upper bound theory
combined with the tension chord model is derived and compared to the test results. Ad-
ditionally, in an attempt to capture the ductility of the joint after cracking, models based

1.1
Structure of thesis
The thesis contains three main parts: Investigation of optimal settings in GOM Correlate,
derivation of a model for estimating the post cracking behaviour of shear joints, and the use
of the model in connection with multiple keyed joints. Each chapter contains the following:
Chapter 2
The theory that forms the basis for the thesis is presented. This includes,
among others, the normality condition, the failure criterion for concrete, and
the tension chord model.
Chapter 3
GOM Correlate as a software to estimate strains on the surface of a test
specimen and the load-displacement curve is presented. How the software
processes images is explained and the optimum settings in GOM Correlate
for experiments of this type are presented.
Chapter 4
The experiment used for Chapters 5 and 6 is presented. This includes an as-
sessment of the displacement ﬁeld of the test specimen, and a approximation
introduced to describe the displacement ﬁeld.
Chapter 5
A formula to calculate the eﬀectiveness factor as a function of crack spacing
is derived. The tension chord model is used to determine the stress in the
reinforcement at a certain crack width. This is combined with the upper
bound theory in an attempt to describe the post cracking behaviour of the
joint.
Chapter 6
A second-order plasticity model is formed to account for the ductile response
of the joint by assuming that the reinforcement is subjected to dowel and
catenary action. This is ﬁrst combined with the dissipation along the yield
line in the mortar and later with friction.
Chapter 7
The second-order plasticity model based on dowel and catenary action com-
bined with friction is used to describe the response of a shear connection
consisting of multiple shear keys subjected to both pure shear and N-V in-
teraction.

2
Theory
This chapter presents the theory that forms the basis for the work done throughout the the-
sis. The ﬁrst part of the chapter describes perfectly rigid-plastic materials. Such materials
do not experience any deformations until a certain point - i.e. the yield point fy. After this
point, arbitrary large deformations are induced without a change in stress level. In reality,
a perfectly rigid-plastic material does not exist, but it is an acceptable model for materials
where the strains are much larger than the stresses (Nielsen and Hoang, 2011). Structural
steel is generally considered to behave similarly to rigid-plastic, whereas concrete has more
of a quasi-brittle behaviour. However, if suﬃcient reduction parameters are introduced,
concrete can be assumed to behave rigid-plastic.
The load-carrying capacity of a structure made of perfectly rigid-plastic material is tra-
ditionally determined with a limit analysis. Within the limit analysis, the upper bound
theory exists. This is presented here and is applied in later chapters in an attempt to model
the response curve of shear joints after cracking. A formula to determine the dissipation
along a yield line in concrete is presented.
2.1
The yield condition
The yield condition is a mathematical description of a combination of stresses that causes
a material to yield. It is assumed that the yield condition is convex and encloses the point
of no stresses. Given by Nielsen and Hoang (2011), the yield condition is deﬁned as

2.3
Principles of extremum
Based on the extremum principles formulated within the theory of plasticity, the capacity
of a structure can be determined. These principles are based on the assumption that the
yield condition is convex and the fulﬁllment of the normality condition. They may be
formulated as the following:
Lower bound theorem
”A load, for which a safe and statically admissible stress distribution can be found, is less
than or equal to the yield load.” (Nielsen et al., 1978)
A stress ﬁeld is considered safe when it corresponds to stresses within or on the yield surface
(f ≤0). Furthermore, it is considered statically admissible when it satisﬁes the equilibrium
equations and the static boundary conditions.
Upper bound theorem
”A load found by the work equation for a failure mechanism is greater than or equal to the
yield load.” (Nielsen et al., 1978)
A failure mechanism is deﬁned as a displacement ﬁeld which is consistent with the geomet-
rical boundary conditions. This implies that the stress ﬁeld does not have to comply with
the static boundary conditions.

Failure criterion for concrete
Uniqueness theorem
When the highest lower bound solution and the lowest upper bound solution are equal, the
yield load of the structure is found. This is called an exact solution.
2.4
Failure criterion for concrete
A complete yield condition for concrete and other cementitious materials has not yet been
formed, but Coulomb’s frictional hypothesis is generally accepted as a failure criterion for
concrete. Coulomb observed that for concrete, failure often happens as sliding along certain
planes. It was observed that the actual sliding depends on the cohesion c and the internal
friction, where the friction depends upon the normal stresses in the plane. The condition
for sliding failure is given as
|τ| = c −µσ
(2.4)

the friction angle ϕ is related to the friction coeﬃcient by tanϕ = µ (Nielsen and Hoang,
2011). A material that follows Equation (2.4) is deﬁned as being a Coulomb material.
A related failure called separation failure occurs when the tensile stress reaches the tensile
strength, ft

Structural concrete
The tensile strength of concrete is often taken to be zero in plastic models. This is because
the tensile failure of concrete is far from ductile (Nielsen and Hoang, 2011). Furthermore,
concrete in tension cracks even at small loads, and this is long before the reinforcement
yields. As the reinforcement yields, the tensile strength of concrete is zero.
2.5
Structural concrete
Concrete in structures will often crack, and because of cracking and size eﬀects, concrete
has a signiﬁcant softening behaviour after reaching the peak strength, i.e. the stresses
decrease as the deformation increases. This is unlike steel where the ductile behaviour can
be modelled as being rigid-plastic with good accuracy. To be able to model concrete using

GOM Correlate
When deforming concrete, it is diﬃcult to tell exactly where a crack will develop on the
surface, making it diﬃcult to place extensometers on the test specimen to measure the
width of a crack. Here Digital Image Correlation (DIC) comes in handy. DIC can mea-
sure the strain on the entire surface of the specimen at any given time, avoiding the need
to estimate where a crack will develop before initiating an experiment. Furthermore, the
software can also be used to detect the propagation of small cracks, which often would not
have been prioritized.
This section describes the DIC software GOM Correlate, what settings can be adjusted,
and what the optimal settings are for processing pictures of loading of keyed shear joints.
To determine the optimal settings and potentially extract more information from the data,
results with diﬀerent settings are produced and evaluated.
3.1
Digital Image Correlation
When using DIC to collect data on the behaviour of a test specimen, the software requires
a stochastic pattern. The pattern must be applied to the surface of the specimen prior
to testing using spray paint in order to track the movement. After applying the paint
and taking a picture, all the pixels in the picture have a grey value between 0 and 255.
This grey value can be the same in thousands of pixels, making it diﬃcult to distinguish
a single pixel from another across pictures. Therefore, DIC uses the area around the pixel
to determine the position of speciﬁc areas in diﬀerent pictures (Schreier et al., 2009). In
GOM Correlate, these areas are called facets, and they are square.
The chance of a random distribution of grey values being the same within two facets in an
image is low. However, the software needs a clear signal to identify an area. The signal
is detected as the maximum contrast between grey values. As factors like blurriness and
distortions can aﬀect the pictures, it is recommended in GOM (2016) to have between
three and four contrast points within one facet to get a clear signal. An illustration of
such a facet and the clear contrast in colour within the facet is shown in Figure 3.1. After
identifying an area, the software assigns coordinates to it. By tracking the coordinates of
an area across pictures, the software determines quantities such as strains.
11
==PAGE:32==

Figure 3.1: Example of facet on test specimen.
The user can choose the facet size and the distance between facets. The standard facet
size is 19x19 pixels. According to GOM (2018), increasing the size causes a longer compu-
tation time, as the facets contain more points. At the same time, this also gives a poorer
detection of local eﬀects. Choosing a smaller facet size decreases the computation time
and gives a better detection of local eﬀects. However, too small facets may result in more
noise because they are more diﬃcult to track precisely. The facet should contain at least
three contrast points in order to trace the deformation of the specimen. The optimal facet
size will be evaluated in the coming sections.
The point distance is the distance between the center of adjacent facets, and the stan-
dard point distance is 16 pixels. Changing the point distance aﬀects the measurement
point density of the surface. It is advised by GOM (2018) to have an area of overlapping
between facets of between 20 and 50%. Decreasing the point distance produces a higher
measurement point density, and thereby a longer computation time. Increasing the point
distance will, on the other hand, lower the measurement point density and give a shorter
computation time.
The software also operates with three diﬀerent types of computation for the surfaces. These
are the following:
– Standard: GOM (2018) recommends the standard computation. Here GOM Cor-
relate interpolates the pixels bicubically and only patterns of high quality is used.
– High Accuracy: When choosing this computation type, the software only use facets
of very good quality. This option is useful when a very high quality of the computed
component is necessary.
– More points: Here the software accepts more points when computing the compo-
nent. Areas with reduced pattern quality are accepted, and the software uses more
facets to compute the component. The interpolation is bilinear.

Experimental setup
The main diﬀerences between the computation types are the levels of acceptance in pattern
quality and the interpolation. With the bilinear interpolation, missing values are deter-
mined from a weighted average of the four closest pixels. Using bicubic interpolation, it is
determined from a weighted average of the 16 closest pixels. This makes the picture a bit
sharper and more smooth, but the interpolations are also more time-consuming.

Upper bound theory combined with tension

Here a model to reproduce the post-cracking behaviour of test specimen T40d is derived.
For this, the simpliﬁcation of the displacement ﬁeld presented in Section 4.3.2 is utilized,
where the change in the displacement ﬁeld at each load stage is taken into account.
The model rests upon the upper bound theorem presented in Section 2.3, and therefore
the equations presented in Section 2.6 is applied. As mentioned, the problem is considered
to be a plane strain problem, as out of plane strains are more or less prevented in shear
connections.
However, some modiﬁcations are made to the traditional use of the upper bound theorem.
The reinforcement is not assumed to behave rigid-plastic, but elastic-plastic with hardening.
The changing stress in the reinforcement is therefore included in the model, and it is
estimated using the tension chord model. Furthermore, the eﬀectiveness factor is assumed
to account for the loss in cohesion of mortar due to the crack and is therefore varying.

satisfying degree.
The dissimilarity between the model and the test results arise from the ductile response
the shear key joint displays. A possible way to account for this ductility is to make a
second-order model where plastic hinges develop in the reinforcement to accommodate the
displacement ﬁeld. However, combining plastic hinges and the tension chord model entails
some conﬂicts as the basis of the models does not match. The tension chord model is an
elastic-plastic model whereas deformation in plastic hinges is purely plastic. Therefore, a
second-order plasticity model based on dowel and catenary action is formed in Chapter 6.
5.5
Concluding remarks
In this chapter, the upper bound theory was combined with the tension chord model in an
attempt to model the post cracking behaviour of a single keyed joint. To use the upper
bound theory, an expression for the eﬀectiveness factor as a function of the crack width
was derived. The ﬁndings are summarized as follows:
• The eﬀectiveness factor as a function of the crack width has an initial value depending
on the constant k and then decreases towards zero at maximum crack width.
• The eﬀectiveness factor was initially derived in order to model the capacity of a
structure with failure along an existing crack. The use of it to model post cracking
behaviour is therefore debatable.
• Based on the tension chord model, the stress in the reinforcement due to crack width
was calculated, and it was seen that the reinforcement reached a stress-free level
within the borders of the test specimen.
• It was observed that the contribution to the total calculated force from reinforce-
ment decreased once the plastic regime was reached due to a decreasing increment of
transverse displacement together with a small inclination of stress in reinforcement.
• The contribution to the total calculated force from the yield line in mortar followed
the change in the eﬀectiveness factor.
• The behaviour of the modelled total load curve did not match the behaviour experi-
mentally obtained. The calculated forces were well below the measured.

6
Second-order plasticity model
Combining contributions from the yield line and the stress in the reinforcement in Sec-
tion 5.4 did not produce a load-displacement response similar to the one obtained from
experiments due to the ductile behaviour of the key after cracking. To capture this duc-
tile behaviour, the change in structural geometry during loading is taken into account by
forming a second-order plasticity model.
The second-order plasticity model is formed, assuming that the load-carrying mechanism
of the reinforcement is a combination of dowel and catenary action. This is chosen based
on promising results presented by Sørensen et al. (2016) for displacements parallel to the
interface. Here a similar model is formed, but one that accounts for a displacement ﬁeld
with a transverse displacement. This is combined with a contribution from the yield line,
to model the response of the entire connection.
In the end, a model accounting for the friction between the interfaces is developed. The
model is combined with the dowel and catenary action in the rebars, and comparisons
between the model and test results are presented.
6.1
Dowel and catenary action
As mentioned, a second-order plasticity model is formed based on dowel and catenary
action in the rebars. To determine the combination of dowel and catenary action, it is as-
sumed that two plastic hinges are formed in each rebar due to the displacement ﬁeld. The
deformation in the hinges is determined from kinematic relations. The kinematic relations
provide a connection between the rate of deformation and the displacement ﬁeld. A link
between the sectional forces and the rate of deformation is then found from the normality
condition. Combining the two, the sectional forces are determined for a changing displace-
ment ﬁeld.
Using the upper bound theory, the response of the shear joint is estimated. The internal
work consists of a contribution from the energy dissipated in the plastic hinges and from
the yield line. The estimated response is then compared to the experimental results.

6.4
Concluding remarks
In this chapter, two second-order plasticity models was presented. Both was accounting
for a combination of dowel and catenary action in the reinforcement. The diﬀerence lied in
the fact that one used the dissipation of energy in the yield line, and the other was based
on friction. The ﬁndings are summarized as follows:
• Using kinematic relations, a link was established between the shear displacements
and the rate of elongation and rotation.
• Based on the normality condition, a link between the sectional forces and the rate of
elongation and rotation was introduced.
• Combining the two expressions for the rate of elongation and rotation, the sectional
forces depending on the shear displacement were derived.
• Using the work equation, an expression to determine the load-displacement curve
was found based on dowel and catenary action combined with the dissipation along
the yield line. However, this did not produce a load-displacement curve suﬃciently
in agreement with the measured loads.
• A model based on friction and the combination of dowel and catenary action was
presented.
• The friction model showed an acceptable match with the measured forces.
The
model slightly underestimates the available plastic energy but is still a strong tool
for a robustness analysis.

Discussion of results
7.3
Discussion of results
The presented experimental results show a large inﬂuence of the external normal pressure.
The measured loads increase signiﬁcantly due to the normal pressure, and the transverse
displacements become smaller. The oﬀset in the measured loads due to increasing normal
pressure supports the choice of adding this contribution to the model. In the model, the
contribution from the external normal pressure constitutes much of the total calculated
force. Despite this, the estimated capacity is still signiﬁcantly lower than the measured.
The large diﬀerences show that the model with the current assumptions is not adequate
to represent the shear connection and will produce a large underestimation in a robustness
assessment.
The low contribution from the internal mechanisms could be part of the reason for the
large diﬀerence between measured and calculated loads. The model has been shown in
Chapter 6 to underestimate the capacity at low shear displacements. However, in all the
cases presented here, the modelled loads do never reach the measured capacity of the con-
nection - only after partly failure of the system. In the model, the plastic deformation of
three U-bars is used, but this seems to provide a very small contribution. The locking bar
has not been included, but accounting for this is not expected to make up the diﬀerence.
This points towards the internal mechanisms being more complex than modelled.
Furthermore, the experimentally obtained load-displacement curve shows a brittle be-
haviour. Wind (2018) presented, that for test specimens B1, B2 and B3, only one U-bar
and the locking bar had ruptured at the end of the experiment. Sørensen (2018) com-
mented that for experiments conducted on test specimens with this type of reinforcement
layout, the deformation of the U-bars was found to be little after the experiment. At the
same time, the mortar around the U-bars was greatly cracked. The model is based on
the assumption that the connection is designed for the rebars to yield, and that failure
of the mortar around the U-bars does not govern failure of the connection. However, in
light of the small deformation of the reinforcement, this might not be the case. It is de-
batable whether plastic hinges develop in the rebars. This concern is supported by the
load-displacement curves showing a large softening response after the ﬁrst peak.
Sørensen (2018) conducted experiments on test specimens with a diﬀerent layout of rein-
forcement than the conventional one. For this new design, the orientation of the U-bars
was rotated. This produced a much more ductile response of the connection and a larger
deformation of the U-bars. Comparing this to the deformation found for the conventional
design and the more brittle response, this also points towards the failure not being gov-
erned by yielding of U-bars.
Furthermore, it is up for discussion, whether it is correct to assume that the plastic hinges
forms at a length l, found by Equation (2.11), on each side of the crack. For the test
specimen subjected to external normal pressure, the concrete under the reinforcement is
most likely aﬀected by the external pressure. This is expected to aﬀect the enhancement
factor c, and thereby change the length l.
For the modelled curves, the angle of friction is estimated at 30° and from this, the friction

Concluding remarks
This chapter presented the results from applying the model based on friction, on test
specimen containing multiple shear keys. Here assumptions were formed on the number
of keys subjected to plastic deformations. When comparing the modelled and measured
response curves, it became evident that a more accurate modelling of the failure inducing
mechanisms should be formed to represent the behaviour. The main ﬁndings are as follows:
• Increasing the normal pressure gave a higher measured capacity for the shear con-
nection.
• The transverse displacement was reduced and the measured loads raised for increasing
normal pressure.
• The normal pressure was accounted for in the model by multiplying the external
normal force with the roughness.

Concluding remarks
• The model based on friction did not produce load-displacement curves similar to the
measured response curves.
• The measured load-displacement curves showed a very brittle response, and it had
been reported that only a small deformation of the U-bars was detected after the
experiments. This indicated that plastic hinges might not have formed in the U-bars.
• Increasing the coeﬃcient of friction increased the capacity found by the model.

Conclusion
Firstly it was assessed how the software GOM Correlate evaluates pictures using DIC. It
was found that changing the facet size had no inﬂuence on the obtained load-displacement
curves - facet being a speciﬁed area of pixels. Increasing the facet size by 50% only entailed
small diﬀerences in the major strain distribution of the test specimen. Decreasing the facet
size by 50% produced a considerable amount of noise which likely originated from a light
source.
Only between loading stage 55 and 56, using the small facet size produced signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent results than the other facet sizes. Here a large increase in strains were visible in
the lower right corner of the test specimen, indicating localized movement of some contrast
points. In conclusion, the smaller facet is inconvenient to use because it is too sensitive to
noise. Furthermore, changing the computation method did not have any inﬂuence on the
obtained results. Based on these results, it is recommended to use the standard settings
in GOM Correlate for the analysis of similar experiments.
The post-cracking behaviour of a shear connection consisting of a single key was examined.
First, upper bound theory combined with the tension chord model was applied. For this
a formula for an eﬀectiveness factor accounting for the change in material properties due
to the crack was derived. From the expression, it was found that a higher constant k gives
a higher value of the eﬀectiveness factor at a crack width of zero. The eﬀectiveness factor
then decreases towards zero at w0.
Plotting the modelled response curve, it was seen that the contribution to the response
from the yield line in mortar showed the same decreasing behaviour as the eﬀectiveness
factor until a constant crack width was reached. It was observed that the contribution
to the response curve from the reinforcement increased at small displacements, but then
decreased towards zero at a constant crack width. These contributions put together en-
tailed that the modelled response curves showed a decreasing capacity at increasing shear
displacement. This was unlike the experimental results, which showed the opposite be-
haviour. The model is therefore not found to accurately model the response, as the model
did not account for structural deformations and therefore did not capture the ductility of
the system.
Because of the dissimilarity, the deformation of the reinforcement was accounted for in
model.
Based on promising results presented by Sørensen et al. (2016), this was done
forming a second-order plasticity model where the reinforcement was assumed to be ex-
65
==PAGE:86==
posed to dowel and catenary action. Using kinematic relations and the normality condition,
a link between the sectional forces and the shear displacement was established. Based on
this, it was seen that the contributions from moment and crushing concrete decreased while
the normal force increased at an increasing shear displacement.
First, contributions from the sectional forces in the reinforcement were combined with the
dissipation along the yield line in mortar to model the response. Here, the total force
decreased at small shear displacements because of the strong inﬂuence of the contribution
from the yield line. At larger shear displacements, the curve increased because of the con-
tribution from the normal force. Large diﬀerences were still seen between the modelled
and measured forces. The measured curve continuously increased until failure, and the
model did not capture this behaviour because of the signiﬁcant inﬂuence from the yield
line. Here problems with this contribution were brought into focus, as the use of the ef-
fectiveness factor is debatable and the constrictions on the displacement angle were not met.
Dowel and catenary action was then combined with friction on the interface. Compar-
ing this model with the experimental results showed a good agreement. The model now
showed an increasing behaviour at small shear displacements because of the contribution
from friction. The model only slightly underestimated the response. A particular good
agreement was observed using a decreasing friction coeﬃcient. Here the friction coeﬃ-
cient was assumed to be 1.0 at cracking and decreasing toward 0.7 at the point of pure
catenary action. However, whether this is an acceptable assumption remains to be veriﬁed.
For the third part, the model based on friction was applied to shear connections consisting
of multiple shear keys. The model was extended to account for normal pressure. For all
of the shear connections containing multiple shear keys, the model did not reproduce the
response curve accurately. The experimentally obtained response curves showed a brittle
response, while the model produced a ductile behaviour. Furthermore, the modelled re-
sponse was signiﬁcantly below the measured. After the experiments it was seen that the
assumptions, which formed the basis for the model, had not been met; the U-bars had
not deformed and the mortar was extensively cracked. The model was thereby not fully
capable of modelling the mechanism.

Recommendations for future work
The second-order plasticity model based on friction only accounts for a joint consisting of
one material. To get a better representation of the real situation, it is recommended to
extend the model to account for the diﬀerence in material properties on each side of the
interface. A model including diﬀerent material properties is expected to produce larger
loads, as the compressive strength of concrete is then accounted for, which is higher than
the one for mortar used in this thesis. It would be interesting to see if the modelled re-
sponse curve, which now underestimates the capacity, then would reproduce the response
more accurately.
The model which uses for a higher coeﬃcient of friction right after cracking shows a good
match with the measured loads (see Figure 6.10). It could be interesting to conduct a
series of experiments to conﬁrm or refute the idea of a changing coeﬃcient of friction. If
conﬁrmed, it should be incorporated in the friction model permanently.
As mentioned in Section 7.3 is debatable whether the U-bars form plastic hinges, as the
mortar between the U-bars fails before the rebars. Sørensen (2018) conducted experiments
on a new design of reinforcement for shear connections between precast elements. Here
the U-bars were twisted, and the connection showed a more ductile response after the ﬁrst
peak. After testing it was observed that the U-bars had deformed signiﬁcantly more than
with the conventional design. It was not possible for Sørensen (2018) to determine if the
U-bars had deformed gradually since the initiation of plastic deformations. However, it
is still interesting to examine if the second-order plasticity model based on friction and
assuming the formation of plastic hinges could reproduce the ductile response.

This appendix contains a Matlab script formed to model the response curves presented in
Chapters 5 and 6. This includes the upper bound theory combined the tension chord model,
where the stress in the reinforcement and the eﬀectiveness factor are calculated for each
shear displacement. Furthermore, it also includes the second-order plasticity models, which
ﬁrst includes the dissipation along the yield line in mortar, and later friction.

% Script to plot the modelled load-displacement curves
clear all 
 
%% Material properties 
%Reinforcement 
fy=607.5; %Yield strength
fu=705.2; %Ultimate strength
d=6; %Diameter
n=4; %Number of rebars
As=n*pi*(d/2)^2; %Area
epsu=0.05; %Ultimate strain 
Es=200000; %Modulus of elasticity 
epsy=fy/Es; %Yield strain
Esh=(fu-fy)/(epsu-epsy); %Hardening modulus
Np=(1/4)*n*pi*d^2*fy; %Plastic tensile capacity

dg = 4; %Maximum aggregate size
w0 = 2; %Maximum crack width
A = 40*200; %Key area

taub0=2*ft; %Bond shear stress
taub1=ft; %Bond shear stress
 
%Constant for effectiveness factor
