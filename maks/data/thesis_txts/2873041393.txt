Abstract
As transport infrastructure becomes increasingly critical to economic growth and social connectivity,
governments face growing pressure to deliver projects efficiently despite fiscal constraints. Public-Private
Partnerships (PPPs) have been promoted as a solution to these challenges by enabling the mobilisation
of private capital and expertise. However, the real-world performance of PPPs remains contested, partic-
ularly in the transport sector where projects are complex, capital-intensive, and carry long-term public
service obligations.
This thesis explores the continued relevance of PPPs as a delivery model for transport infrastructure
in the 21st century. The research adopts a mixed-methods approach, combining a structured literature
review, semi-structured stakeholder interviews, and a comparative analysis of eight case studies from
Denmark, Sweden, Uganda, and India. The central methodology is a Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis
(MCDA), using the REMBRANDT method, which enables a structured comparison between PPP and
traditional public procurement projects. Projects were assessed on four performance criteria: adherence
to budget, adherence to time, delivery of promised benefits, and long-term partnership sustainability. The
relative importance of these criteria was derived from stakeholder input and applied through sensitivity
analyses representing government, investor, and public user perspectives.
The analysis reveals that PPPs do not consistently outperform traditional public projects. The find-
ings suggest that the success of PPPs is highly context-dependent, influenced by institutional capacity,
financing mechanisms, and project type.
Based on these findings, the thesis presents a decision-support framework to assist public authorities in
determining whether a PPP is an appropriate model for a given transport infrastructure project. The
framework integrates the results of the MCDA and stakeholder perspectives, offering a practical tool to
support structured, evidence-based decision-making. It emphasises the need to assess political, financial,
and institutional conditions alongside project-specific goals to determine the most effective procurement
strategy.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation of the Research
Transport infrastructure is a cornerstone of economic development, social connectivity, and environ-
mental resilience. With cities continuously growing, delivering such infrastructure requires substantial
investment, long-term planning, and coordination among diverse stakeholders. As public budgets face
increasing pressure, public-private partnerships (PPPs) have become an alternative to finance, deliver,
and manage large-scale transport projects. Advocates of PPPs argue that they can enhance efficiency,
promote innovation, and shift certain risks to the private sector. However, the actual performance of
PPPs remains contested, with mixed evidence regarding their ability to consistently deliver projects on
time, within budget, and aligned with public interest goals.
While there is a substantial amount of literature evaluating PPPs across various sectors, relatively few
studies focus specifically on transport infrastructure, an area that presents distinctive challenges not typ-
ically encountered in sectors such as healthcare or education (Roumboutsos 2015). Transport projects
often involve high capital intensity, complex engineering requirements, long construction and operational
periods, and high visibility in the public domain.
Furthermore, they rarely generate significant rev-
enues from the project. These characteristics complicate performance evaluation and require a nuanced,
context-specific approach.
Existing assessments of PPPs frequently emphasise limited criteria or quantitative insights, overlooking
the full complexity of success in infrastructure delivery. There is a need for a more comprehensive and
transparent framework to assess and compare infrastructure projects, particularly in the transport sector,
where decisions have long-term implications for both economic productivity and social equity.
This study seeks to address these gaps by applying Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) along with
stakeholder interviews to assess and compare the performance of transport infrastructure projects deliv-
ered through PPPs and traditional public procurement. By incorporating multiple success criteria and
stakeholder perspectives, the research aims to contribute to a more robust and practical understanding of
when, and under what conditions, PPPs present an effective delivery model for transport infrastructure.
1.2
Hypotheses
This study examines the continued relevance of PPPs as a model for financing and delivering transport
infrastructure projects. While PPPs have been widely adopted to leverage private investment and ex-
pertise, their outcomes have varied across different contexts and project types. To contribute to a better
understanding of when and where PPPs are most suitable, this research raises the following question:
To what extent do Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) remain a viable model for financing
and delivering transport infrastructure projects in the 21st century?
To determine this, the thesis investigates in what settings PPPs are relevant, focusing on three key
questions:
1. Whether PPPs generally perform better than traditional public projects?
2. Whether PPPs are more effective for rail or road infrastructure?
3. Whether PPPs perform better in developed or developing countries compared to public projects?
The formulation of the three research questions guiding this study was grounded in both conceptual
reasoning and early insights from the interviews conducted for the thesis. The first question, on whether
PPPs generally perform better than traditional public projects, addresses a fundamental concern. If
PPPs do not demonstrably outperform public alternatives in terms of cost, time, or benefits delivered,
then the rationale for adopting them becomes significantly weaker. Establishing a comparative perfor-
mance baseline is therefore essential before deeper analysis can proceed.

INTRODUCTION
The second and third questions emerged from the interviews conducted early in the research process. One
interviewee highlighted that PPP roads in Europe are often toll roads. This observation raised impor-
tant sectoral distinctions and suggested that PPPs may be more suitable for certain types of transport
infrastructure, where revenue generation is more feasible. Conversely, drawing from experience in the
rail sector and the common wish for governments to expand rail infrastructure, it became obvious to
explore whether PPP effectiveness varies systematically between rail and road projects. With sustain-
ability becoming a central objective in infrastructure planning, mass transit is frequently promoted as
a key solution. However, such projects typically involve substantial upfront costs for construction and
long-term maintenance, costs that can be challenging for governments to cover through traditional public
financing alone.
Furthermore, the same interview pointed to differences in institutional capacity and market maturity, es-
pecially in countries struggling with corruption. This led to the third research question, which considers
whether PPPs are more likely to perform in certain national settings. Together, these three questions
aim to unpack some of the conditions under which PPPs are likely to be viable and effective.

Methodology
This study adopts a mixed-methods approach that integrates a structured literature review, semi-
structured stakeholder interviews, and Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) to evaluate the per-
formance of public-private partnerships in the delivery of transport infrastructure. These methods are
combined to enable a holistic comparison between PPP and traditionally procured projects, grounded in
both empirical evidence and stakeholder perspectives. Lastly, the insights are synthesised into a decision-
support framework designed to assist policymakers and practitioners in determining whether a PPP
model is appropriate for a given transport infrastructure project. Figure 1 depicts this methodology and
how each part is approached in this thesis.
Figure 1: Flowchart of methodology used in this thesis
2.1
Literature review
The literature review serves as a foundation for situating the study’s findings within the broader academic
and policy discourse on PPPs. Rather than prescribing evaluation criteria directly, the review is used to
identify how the core research questions, regarding the general effectiveness of PPPs, and their relative
performance across project types and contexts, have been approached and answered in the literature.
This helps establish a baseline of expectations against which the empirical results of this study can be
compared. In particular, the literature highlights divergent views on the value for money, risk-sharing,
and long-term performance of PPPs in infrastructure delivery.
2.2
Stakeholder interviews
To complement the literature and integrate practical experience, semi-structured interviews are conducted
with key stakeholders, including public officials, private sector participants, and infrastructure experts.
These interviews serve three purposes:
1. To validate and refine the study’s performance criteria
2. To capture the relative importance assigned to each criterion by different stakeholder groups
3. To provide interpretive insights into specific project outcomes
The stakeholder input plays a critical role in shaping the MCDA framework, particularly through the
assignment of weights in the sensitivity analysis.

2.3
Case studies
The core of the empirical analysis consists of a structured comparison of transport infrastructure projects
delivered via PPP and traditional public procurement models. A sample of case studies is selected to
capture variation across project types (rail and road), geographies (developed and developing countries),
and procurement models.
Project performance is assessed using MCDA, specifically the REMBRANDT method, which enables the
integration of both qualitative and quantitative data.
Criteria weights are derived from stakeholder input, allowing the analysis to reflect real-world priorities,
while the input data are derived from several sources from governments and media. The results are
synthesised into composite performance scores, enabling direct comparison between PPP and non-PPP
projects. A sensitivity analysis explores how the outcomes vary under different weighting scenarios rep-
resenting the views of various stakeholder groups.
The selected cases consist of transport infrastructure projects, specifically roads, and public transporta-
tion systems, from both the public and PPP sectors. The following criteria were used to choose the
cases:
1. Geographical Relevance: Projects were chosen from the same region to ensure similar economic,
political, and regulatory conditions, thereby minimising external influences on project outcomes
2. Project Type: Only transport infrastructure projects were considered, as they are the primary focus
for both PPPs and public sector projects
3. Completion Status: Only completed projects were included to ensure the availability of relevant
performance data, such as budget adherence, timeline compliance, and benefit realisation
4. Data Availability: Projects with sufficient data on budget, timeline, and stakeholder feedback were
selected to allow for a thorough comparison
The findings of this analysis aim to contribute in the determination of the research questions defined in
section 1.2
2.3.1
Comparison metrics
Before doing the analysis, it must be decided what metrics the projects are compared on. The overall
aim is to assess whether PPPs perform better or worse than traditional publicly funded transport in-
frastructure projects. Therefore, we need to assess each projects success. The ”Iron Law of Megaproject
Management” by Bent Flyvbjerg states that megaprojects are ”Over budget, over time, under benefits,
over and over again” (Flyvbjerg 2017). A successful project is a project that delivers on all three factors.
For PPP projects, several researchers have tried to map out the essential success criteria. The paper
”Critical Success Criteria for Public-Private Partnership Projects: International Experts’ Opinion” (Osei-
Kyei 2016) assesses which success criteria are most crucial for assessing the effectiveness of a PPP. From
this assessment 15 criteria have been identified as well as their level of criticality. The 15 criteria include,
adherence to time, adherence to budget, and quality/meeting output specifications, but since PPPs have
the special partnership aspect, this is also assessed among several other criteria.
The reasons why PPPs, or any project, succeeds or fails is complex and can be due to several factors,
not necessarily known to the stakeholders. Therefore, a more in-depth analysis of each project is needed
to determine the exact causes of the failures and successes. Such analysis would have to consider all 15
success criteria. This would require an extensive amount of data not readily available. Furthermore, the
analysis does not seek to evaluate the specific PPP in detail, but rather to compare it with similar public
projects. The criteria are intended to capture broader trends and outcomes, rather than the specifics
of the individual PPP. Therefore, the projects will be evaluated on the following four criteria from the
paper (Osei-Kyei 2016):
• Meeting output specifications
• Adherence to time
• Adherence to budget

• Long-term relationship and partnership
”Meeting output specifications” assesses how well the project meets the needs and expectations of its
users. This measure is based on public reporting on each project, and a binary score is given on how
well it performs, where 1 means it meets expectations and 0 means it does not. Since the analysis will
be done using a pairwise comparison, apart from the score, the two projects will be compared based on
the reporting to assess which project is preferred.
The second criteria is ”Adherence to time”. Here advances and delays in the project are taken into
consideration, compared to the expected delivery time of the project. This measure does not take into
account operational time considerations. Adherence to time is determined based on years of delay to the
project in the analysis.
The next criteria is ”Adherence to budget”. Similar to time, this measure considers whether the project
stays within the expected budget, or if overruns become necessary. This is assessed by the extent of
the overrun or the absence of one. The value of $1 in one country, is different than the value of $1 in
another. The value of the same $ in the same country also changes throughout the years due to inflation.
Therefore, we use relative cost as a measure, to ensure a fair comparison across different countries as well
as years, calculated as:
Relative cost =
Actual cost
Estimated cost
This approach also prevents favouring projects that initially included a larger contingency, ensuring a
more precise comparison base.
The last criteria is ”Long-term relationship and partnership”. This criterion aims to capture the nuances
behind why certain PPPs may underperform or fail over time.
Rather than attempting to evaluate
partnership quality through subjective or varied indicators, it takes a practical and observable approach:
whether or not the government has had to intervene in the project after its implementation.
Public intervention can indicate several underlying issues. It may reflect ineffective risk allocation, chal-
lenges in maintaining a productive long-term relationship between the public and private sectors, or the
failure of the partnership to deliver the intended public service effectively. It may also signal problems
related to profitability, excessive public administrative burden, or operational shortcomings in terms of
service reliability and quality.
By using the need for public intervention as a proxy, this criterion allows for a comparable and prac-
tical measure of partnership sustainability across PPP and traditional public projects. It assesses not
just whether the partnership has formally persisted, but whether it has functioned as intended without
requiring additional government involvement.
2.3.2
Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis is done to ensure a robust analysis and enhance the credibility, since the gradation
index is decided on a preference scale and thus may be subject to bias.
Sensitivity analyses can be
performed using several different methods, such as One-at-a-Time variation, where the weight of one
criterion is raised with a pre-decided percentage, and the other criteria are reduced proportionally. The
sensitivity analysis can also be done using different personas that weigh the criteria differently from the
baseline, which is how it is done in this thesis. The weights are given on a scale from 0-100, where 0
is not important and 100 is very important. This method is chosen, since the decision of doing a PPP
contract depends on many factors from several stakeholders.
The baseline weights, table 1, form the foundation of the comparison. While they are derived through a
systematic process, they inherently reflect subjective judgement. These weights are intentionally designed
to place greater emphasis on the success of the partnership itself, rather than solely on the project’s
outcomes.

ment, Private Investors, and Public Users. For each persona, a set of weights have been chosen based

The Government persona value the budget the most. The reason for doing PPPs can often be financial,

responsibility for unexpected risks and budget overruns. The government can tolerate time delays, as
long as they do not affect the other criteria. Lastly, the government place significant importance on the
delivered benefits. The primary goal of transport infrastructure is to enhance mobility for society. If this

study analysis. The purpose of the framework is to support public authorities and decision-makers in de-

The framework was designed for practical use, allowing easy integration into existing project assessments,
especially those using MCDA. It aligns with typical decision points and encourages structured reflections
on whether conditions for a successful PPP are in place. Rather than giving a yes/no answer, it translates
research insights into a clear, actionable guide to support strategic, evidence-based decisions in transport
infrastructure planning.

("Public-private partnerships" OR "PPP") AND ("performance") AND ("traditional

This provided a total of 56 results, 27 on OKR searched on the 30-05-2025 and 29 on DTU findit searched

screening resulted in 3 papers from OKR and 3 from DTU findit which are included in this final review.
This process ensured a focused and thematically relevant evidence and research to lay base to the subse-
quent comparison of the literature and the research done in this paper.
3.1
Economic Theory and Procurement Design
The paper ”Procurement in Infrastructure What Does Theory Tell Us?” by Estache et al. (Antonio Es-
tache 2009) explores how economic theory can inform better decision-making in public infrastructure
procurement. The paper emphasises the inherent complexity and long-term nature of infrastructure in-
vestments, which necessitate carefully designed procurement mechanisms to ensure both efficiency and
accountability on the involved parties.
A central theme in the analysis is the role of incentivised contracts. Estache et al. argue that high-
powered incentives, such as fixed-price contracts, can promote cost efficiency when project outcomes are
easily measurable and uncertainty is low. On the other hand, low-powered incentives, like cost-plus con-
tracts, may be more appropriate in contexts of high uncertainty or when the output quality is difficult
to verify or measure.
Furthermore, the paper also explores the implications of ownership structures.
Public ownership is
favoured in projects with significant positive social externalities or redistributive goals, while private
ownership may provide efficiency gains when profit motives align with service delivery objectives.
Lastly, Estache et al. address the issue of contract renegotiation in infrastructure procurement. Given the
long-term nature of such projects, the initial contracts often require adjustments. The paper discusses
how to design contracts that minimise the risk of opportunistic behaviour and renegotiation induced
inefficiencies. The authors recommend designing contracts with built-in flexibility, such as adjustment
mechanisms and clearly defined performance metrics. Furthermore, they emphasise the importance of
institutional safeguards, including independent regulatory oversight and transparent renegotiation pro-
cedures. Finally, they suggest that task bundling can reduce the need for renegotiation by minimising
failures in coordination across the project phases.
The paper ”Measuring the value for money of transport infrastructure procurement: An intergenerational
approach” by Penyalver et al. (Domingo Penyalver and Williamson 2019) introduces a novel framework
for evaluating value for money (VfM) in transport infrastructure procurement by incorporating an in-
tergenerational perspective. Penyalver et al. explain that traditional VfM assessments often focus on

3.2
PPP Promise and Political Reality
The paper ”Public-Private Partnerships Promise and Hype” by Michael Klein (Klein 2015) offers a crit-
ical and historically grounded perspective on the evolution and performance of PPPs in infrastructure.
Klein argues that while PPPs are often promoted as innovative solutions to public sector inefficiencies
and fiscal constraints, they are in fact a recurrent response to cyclical political and economic pressure
rather than a fundamentally new model.
The paper states that many infrastructure services originally began under private ownership, but were
later nationalised, and are now increasingly being re-privatised through PPPs. This cycle is driven largely
by fiscal stress and the politics of pricing. When governments face budgetary constraints, they turn to
PPPs to mobilise private capital. However, once fiscal conditions improve, political resistance to cost-
reflective pricing often resurfaces, undermining the financial sustainability of PPPs.
Klein emphasises that there is no universal rationale for choosing PPPs. Their effectiveness depends
on context-specific factors such as institutional quality, regulatory capacity, and the design of incentive
structures. While PPPs can outperform traditional public projects under the right conditions, particu-
larly when sound governance and incentive alignment are in place, well-functioning public systems can
achieve similar outcomes.
Klein also criticises the over-enthusiasm often surrounding PPPs, warning against viewing them as a
solution to all problems. Klein stresses that PPPs are not inherently superior; rather, they are tools
that can be effective when used appropriately. He underscores the importance of realistic expectations,
transparent evaluation, and a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs involved.
In contrast, the paper ”Public Private Partnerships in Urban Transport” by Engel et al. (Engel and
Galetovic 2014) argues that PPPs can indeed improve infrastructure delivery, if properly structured
and supported by strong institutions. They frame the urban transport dilemma: As cities grow and
agglomerate, the demand for mobility increases, yet public infrastructure often fails to keep pace due
to fiscal constraints and institutional inefficiencies. Furthermore, they identify three core challenges in
infrastructure provision:
• Deciding what to build and when
• Ensuring cost-effective construction
• Maintaining service quality over time
They suggest that PPPs can help address these challenges by introducing market discipline, aligning
incentives, and leveraging private sector expertise.
However, the paper also highlights several pitfalls that commonly undermine PPP performance, namely:
• Poor contract design, which can lead to misaligned incentives or excessive renegotiations

• Weak institutional frameworks, which fail to enforce contracts or regulate monopolistic behaviour
• Lack of transparency and accountability, especially in politically sensitive projects
The paper finds that PPPs are not a one-size-fits-all solution. Their success depends heavily on institu-
tional capacity, particularly the ability to design, monitor, and enforce long-term contracts. Similarly to
Estache et al., Engel et al. emphasise the importance of flexibility in contract design to accommodate
changing circumstances without undermining the integrity of the agreement.
The paper also discusses the ”PPP premium”, defined as the additional costs associated with private
financing and risk-bearing. While PPPs can deliver efficiency gains, these must be weighted against the
higher cost of capital and the complexity of managing long-term contracts.
While both Klein and Engel et al. ultimately converge on a cautious and context-dependent view of
PPPs, their tone and emphasis differ notably. Klein presents a more critical and historically sceptical
perspective, warning against the cyclical hype and unrealistic expectations often associated with PPPs.
He stresses that PPPs are not a novel solution, but rather a recurring response to fiscal and political
pressures, and highlights the risks of over-reliance on them. In contrast, Engel et al. adopt a more
optimistic stance, recognising the potential of PPPs to enhance infrastructure delivery—particularly in
urban transport—when they are carefully structured and supported by strong institutions. Despite this
difference in outlook, both papers agree that PPPs are not inherently superior to public provision and
that their success depends on sound governance, robust institutions, and well-designed contracts.
3.3
Performance of PPPs in Infrastructure Delivery
A paper ”Do public–private partnerships achieve better time and cost performance than regular con-
tracts?” by Stefan Verweij and Ingmar van Meerkerk (Verweij and Meerkerk 2021) provide an extensive
empirical assessment of the performance of PPPs in infrastructure, focusing on Dutch projects. The
central question is whether PPPs deliver better outcomes in terms of cost and time performance.
Verweij et al. ground their analysis in principal-agent theory, a concept from economics that explores how
relationships work when one party delegates work to another, which highlights the challenges of informa-
tion asymmetry and misaligned incentives in contractual relationships. They argue that PPP contracts
create stronger incentives for private partners to manage risks efficiently and avoid cost overruns, since
design, construction, financing, and maintenance have been bundled. In contrast public projects often
lack these integrated mechanisms, potentially leading to opportunistic behaviour and higher costs.
Using data from 65 Dutch highway infrastructure projects, the study finds that PPPs significantly out-
perform public projects in terms of cost performance. On average, PPPs incurred additional work costs
amounting to just 6.24% of the initial contract value, compared to 24.72% for public projects.
In terms of time performance, PPP projects also showed better average outcomes, with shorter delays or
even early completions. However, Verweij et al. caution that while the trends favour PPPs, more data
are needed to draw firm conclusions about time efficiency.
Importantly, the study contributes to the literature by relying on objective performance data rather than
perceptions or forecasts. This addresses a common limitation in PPP research, which often depends on
previous evaluations or subjective assessments.
3.4
Sustainability in Public-Private Partnerships
The paper ”Investigation of factors affecting sustainability in public–private partnerships for infrastruc-
ture projects” by Ahmadi et al. (Loqman Ahmadi and Laali 2024) addresses the growing concern that
while PPPs are widely adopted to bridge the infrastructure financing gaps and enhance service delivery,
they often fall short in aligning with sustainability goals. Ahmadi et al. argue that the prevailing PPP
frameworks tend to prioritise short-term financial returns over long-term environmental and social out-
comes, creating a structural conflict with sustainable development principles.

4.1
Procurement models in construction
All transport infrastructure projects, publicly or privately procured, are implemented through a variety
of approaches, influenced by factors such as national standards and procurement methods. Despite these
variations, most projects generally follow six main phases (Thuesen 2023), with each phase progressively
adding more detail to the project (boligministeriet 2017):
Phase 1 Preliminary investigation/Concept development
Phase 2 Decision-making
Phase 3 Initial design
Phase 4 Detailed design
Phase 5 Construction
Phase 6 Operations and maintenance
In Phase 1, preliminary investigation, feasibility studies, cost-benefit analyses, and stakeholder consulta-
tions are conducted to determine whether the project should proceed. A formal decision to initiate the
project is made.
In Phase 2, a more thorough investigation of the project’s feasibility and implications is carried out. This
typically includes obtaining necessary building permits, engaging with relevant regulatory authorities,
and often conducting Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs) to evaluate potential environmental
effects and ensure compliance with legal and sustainability requirements.
In Phase 3, the basic technical solutions and design alternatives are developed. The goal is to define the
project’s scope and overall layout to guide further planning.
In Phase 4, comprehensive technical drawings, specifications, and construction plans are produced, en-
abling accurate cost estimations and preparation for implementation.
In Phase 5, the physical infrastructure is built according to the detailed design. This phase involves
coordination of contractors, supervision, and quality control.
Once completed, the infrastructure is put into use. Phase 6 includes routine maintenance, monitoring,
and potential upgrades to ensure continued functionality and performance.
Responsibility for each project phase varies depending on the chosen procurement model. Procurement
methods in transport infrastructure projects vary significantly depending on factors such as project size,
complexity, regulatory context, and risk allocation preferences.
However, a number of standardised
models are widely used across countries and sectors. These models primarily differ in how responsibilities
for project phases—such as design, construction, financing, operation, and maintenance—are distributed
between the contracting authority and the delivery entity. While there are several approaches, the four
most prominent models are:
• Design-Build (DB)
• Design-Build-Operate (DBO)
• Design-Build-Operate-Maintain (DBOM)
• Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Maintain (DBFOM)
In the Design-Build (DB) approach a single contractor is responsible for both the design and construction
of the infrastructure (U. D. o. Transportation 2025a). This model promotes integration and can shorten
the project timeline by overlapping design and construction phases. However, the public authority typi-
cally retains responsibility for financing, operating, and maintaining the asset.
12
==PAGE:21==

THEORY
In the Design-Build-Operate (DBO) model, operational responsibilities are added to the contractor’s
scope (Mastt 2025). This allows the authority to benefit from the contractor’s operational expertise
while still maintaining control over financing and long-term maintenance. This is often used when rolling
stock is involved, to ensure it is working properly.
The Design-Build-Operate-Maintain (DBOM) model goes a step further by assigning both operational
and maintenance duties to the contracted entity (U. D. o. Transportation 2025b). This integrated ap-
proach aims to ensure lifecycle efficiency and long-term performance, with the public sector usually
remaining responsible for project financing. Furthermore, it adds extra incentives for the contractor to
build higher quality.
A more comprehensive model is Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Maintain (DBFOM), where the contracted
entity assumes full responsibility for designing, building, financing, operating, and maintaining the infras-
tructure over a long-term concession period. This model is commonly associated with a Public-Private
Partnership (PPP), where the public sector leverages the private sector expertise and capital to deliver
complex infrastructure projects.
Each of these procurement approaches offers different advantages and trade-offs in terms of cost control,
risk allocation, innovation, and public accountability. Figure 2 shows an overview of responsibilities in
each procurement model against the project phases. The choice of model depends on project objectives,
available resources, and the institutional framework within which the project is developed.

4.2
Public-Private Partnerships
Public-Private Partnerships are collaborative agreements between government entities and private sector
companies aimed at financing, designing, implementing, and operating projects and services that are
usually provided by the public sector. These partnerships leverage the strengths and resources of both
the private and public sectors to achieve common goals, improve efficiency, and deliver public services.
PPPs have been used for centuries and can be traced back to the Roman Empire, where a network of
postal stations was developed to accompany the vast expansion of the cursus publicus, which was the
transportation system of the time. Postal stations were constructed and managed by a private partner for
a 5-year period, sometimes including maintenance of the associated highway, under the contract (PPIAF
2009).
There is not one specific definition of what entails a PPP. The World Bank defines PPPs as:
“A long-term contract between a private party and a government entity, for providing a public
asset or service, in which the private party bears significant risk and management responsibil-
ity, and remuneration is linked to performance.” (T. W. Bank 2020)
The distinction between public and private entities is not strictly defined and varies according to the legal
framework of the country in which the partnership is established. According to the World Bank Guidance
on PPP Legal Frameworks (T. W. Bank 2022), both public and private entities can include state-owned
enterprises (SOEs). This is particularly evident in China, where SOEs have become the primary partners
13
==PAGE:22==
4
THEORY
of local governments, rather than traditional private sector entities (Xinhua 2017).
In this paper PPP is defined as:
”A long-term contractual arrangement between a public authority and a non-governmental
delivery partner, publicly or privately owned, tasked with the provision of infrastructure or
services traditionally delivered by the public sector.”
Key features of a PPP in this study include the transfer of significant risk to the partner, payment linked
to performance, and a degree of independence in how the project is managed and financed.
The primary distinction between the definition used in the thesis and that of the World Bank lies in the
treatment of SOEs. While the World Bank allows SOEs to be considered private partners more broadly,
this study includes SOEs only when they operate with genuine independence from the state. That is, they
must bear financial and operational risks, make autonomous business decisions, and compete fairly in the
market without relying on direct government control or subsidies. This narrower interpretation aims to
ensure that the “private” aspect of the partnership entails real managerial and financial accountability.
Even in a PPP, the public sector typically retains responsibility for defining service standards, monitor-
ing performance, and ensuring alignment with public policy goals. This distinguishes PPPs from full
privatisation.
A common point of confusion arises from the question: “If the government ultimately pays for the project,
how is it still a PPP?” This question reflects a misconception that private financing alone defines a PPP.
In reality, the defining feature of a PPP is not who pays, but how risks, responsibilities, and performance
incentives are allocated between the public and private partners.
4.2.1
Financing of PPPs
PPPs can take many forms depending on how responsibilities are allocated. Common models include
Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Maintain (DBFOM), Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT), and Design-Build-
Finance-Operate (DBFO), each reflecting different levels of private sector involvement.
While PPPs involve the private partner taking on responsibility for financing the project upfront, this
does not mean the project is entirely funded by the private sector or that it comes at no cost to the
public. In many PPP models, the government ultimately pays for the infrastructure or service, just over
time, rather than as an upfront capital expense (W. Bank 2020).
For projects financed by the public sector, an initial investment is made by government funds and later
realised by the community through the benefits provided by the project. In contrast, with privately
financed projects, the community bears the cost by making payments to the private sector during the
project’s operational phase. These payments can come from government funds or be collected directly
from users through a toll or service fee.
The advantage of having the private partner can for example be that the project cost for the public can
be spread over a longer time period, which is more in line with the expected benefits of the project, as
illustrated in figure 3. It can help governments spread out large costs over time, improve budget pre-
dictability, and transfer construction and operational risks to the private sector. However, it also creates
long-term fiscal commitments that must be carefully assessed to avoid hidden public liabilities (PPIAF
2022).

The government financing of PPPs can take many forms such as, but not limited to (W. Bank 2020):
Availability payments, where the government makes regular payments to the private partner based

Shadow tolls, where the government pay the private partner based on usage levels (e.g., number of

The main difference from public project financing is that, in privately financed projects, the private part-

of the financial risk during both construction and operation. However, the public sector often remains
the primary source of revenue, particularly when user fees do not cover the full costs or are politically
infeasible.
4.3
MCDA
Evaluating infrastructure projects is often done by combining a Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) and a
Multiple-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA). The CBA is used to assess the economic feasibility of the
project by monetising costs and benefits. Accurately monetising the costs and benefits is one of the
fundamental challenges of CBA, as there are many aspects, such as environmental impact, societal im-
pacts, and long-term operational efficiency, that are difficult to quantify. Since this study focuses on a
broader set of success criteria, and aims to assess whether PPPs perform better than public projects,
relying solely on CBA would provide an incomplete evaluation. The MCDA allows for the inclusion of
qualitative criteria such as project performance and stakeholder satisfaction, making it more suitable for
assessing the overall success of the project, which the CBA does not allow for, since it has a clear focus
on the monetary values. Therefore, the analysis will be done using only the MCDA.
The MCDA will be done using the ”Ratio Estimation in Magnitudes or Deci-bells to Rate Alternatives
which are Non-Dominated” (REMBRANDT) method. REMBRANDT is a type of pair-wise comparison
using multiplication. The method assesses ratios, rather than differences to ensure a fair comparison
between otherwise very different projects that can be hard to compare (M. Barfod 2014).

Findings from stakeholder interviews
This section presents key insights gathered from semi-structured interviews conducted with various stake-
holders involved in transport infrastructure projects. For each stakeholder, a set of questions regarding
the relevance of PPPs and which long-term sustainability and risk considerations should be taken into
account when doing PPPs. Furthermore, questions regarding the Kliplev-Sønderborg PPP were consid-
ered for the interviewees who had worked with that project.
The interviewees included a senior market director from a private consulting firm, a technical project
manager from a private consulting firm, who previously worked on a PPP in Denmark, and a director of
the Danish Road Directory (Vejdirektoratet). The purpose of these interviews was to gather qualitative
perspectives on the use of PPPs, and get insights on what benefits and challenges PPPs may offer. The
interviews are not meant to generalise, but rather to enrich the analysis and discussion conducted in this
thesis.
5.1
Theme 1: Profit Orientation and Efficiency
The senior market director highlighted that PPPs benefit from the private partner’s commercial per-
spective, remarking that “the private partner has a better eye for making a profit.” This was framed
as a positive feature, implying that a profit-seeking approach can drive more efficient and financially
disciplined overall project. This perspective aligns with the rationale for incorporating the private sector
in infrastructure development, to leverage its incentive structures for improved outcomes.
As an example the Copenhagen metro stations were built without a private partner. The stations are
built only to serve the purpose of transportations, with a typical Scandinavian aesthetic in mind. The
senior market director argues that if a private partner had been involved in the project, the station
design would likely have incorporated commercial spaces, such as shops, to generate additional revenue.
He also noted that maintenance considerations would probably have been given greater priority. As an
example, he pointed out that the current station design requires several workers and a lift just to replace
a light bulb, due to their placement high in the ceilings, something a profit-oriented partner might have
optimised to reduce operational costs.
5.2
Theme 2: Financial Trade-offs in PPPs
In contrast, the director of the Danish Road Directory offered a more critical perspective, describing
PPPs as akin to a ”forbrugsl˚an” (consumer loan) for the government. He argued that while PPPs pro-
vide up-front financing, they can ultimately increase project costs due to higher interest rates compared
to what the government will be able to get. His comment reflects concerns about the long-term financial
burden of PPPs compared to traditional public procurement, where government borrowing costs are typ-
ically lower. This insight speaks directly to the cost-efficiency dimension of infrastructure financing and
underscores the importance of evaluating total lifecycle costs in project appraisal.
Furthermore, he adds that an important factor is the cost of operations compared to the cost of construc-
tion. For traditional road projects the cost of operations are fairly low compared to the construction cost.
The yearly cost of operations are well under 1% of the construction cost. This means that the private
partners opportunity to affect the cost of operations is not very large. Although the full life cycle cost
(LCC) should always strive to be the lowest as possible.
5.3
Theme 3: Time Sensitivity and Financial Incentives
The project manager, who had experience both managing and bidding on PPPs, emphasised that strict
adherence to time schedules is central to the financial logic of such projects. He noted: “The shorter
time it takes to build, the faster you can start making a profit on it, making the interest lower”. Ac-
cording to him, this focus on rapid delivery shifts priorities away from cost-saving on technical aspects,
an approach more common in classical public projects, toward minimising time-to-revenue. This insight
illustrates how private partners may structure their project strategies to optimize financing conditions

Kliplev-Sønderborg Highway insights
Both the director and the project manager had worked on the Kliplev-Sønderborg Highway, why they

This section presents a series of case studies comparing public- and PPP infrastructure projects across
different countries and sectors. The aim is to evaluate how procurement models influence project outcomes
such as cost performance, time efficiency, the need for public intervention, and delivery of promised
benefits. Each pair of projects has been selected based on similarity in scope, function, and context,
allowing for meaningful comparisons. The analysis applies an MCDA framework to systematically assess
project performance from multiple stakeholder perspectives.
6.1
Case 1: Denmark
Denmark is a small Scandinavian country with a generally well-functioning transport infrastructure.
Denmark’s transport infrastructure is primarily managed by the government, often through state-owned
enterprises (SOEs). While public-private partnerships (PPPs) are used in some cases, the government
generally prefers SOEs for major projects. This approach ensures public control and long-term planning
stability. A notable SOE is Sund & Bælt A/S that manages and own many of the larger bridges and
tunnels among other things. Denmark use SOEs to reduce the need for private financing and thus al-
lowing the government to fund infrastructure projects directly. This way the infrastructure projects can
economically sustain themselves through state-guaranteed loans and user charges, both through taxation
and direct charges.
In Denmark there is one PPP project on transport infrastructure, namely the Kliplev-Sønderborg High-
way. The project was meant to test out the contract type, to give basis for deciding if it is providing the
benefits Denmark needs. At the time of deciding to do a PPP, the government was liberal.
Although the project generally can be considered a success, the Danish government have not utilised
PPPs since, and has no publicly known plans to do so in the near future.
6.1.1
Motivation
The comparison will consist of the PPP project and a similar public project. The aim is to understand
which is preferred in a Danish context.
The Kliplev-Sønderborg highway is the first and only highway in Denmark built as a PPP. It is a 26
km long highway, connecting the southern part of Denmark to the European route 45. The highway
opened in 2012, more than a year earlier than expected. The partnership consist of the Austrian Strabag
and the Danish Road Directory, with the contract running over a 30 year period.
The second project chosen for this case analysis is the Holstebro motorway.
It is a 29 km highway
built by the Danish Road Directory, as a public project.
The highway connects the western city of
Holstebro to the primary network, with the purpose of improving the mobility in western Jutland. The
highway has proved to provide a higher time saving than expected, giving a socio-economic benefit of
11.1% compared to the expected 5.1% (D. M. o. Transportation 2023).
The two projects are comparable in both physical scale and overall scope, with similar timelines. Addition-
ally, both were designed to improve regional mobility in areas that previously lacked strong connections
to the primary road network. The two projects allows for a meaningful evaluation of how different pro-
curement models impact project efficiency, cost effectiveness, and long-term benefits, in a Scandinavian
context.

6.2
Case 2: Sweden
Sweden has taken a distinctive approach to transport infrastructure procurement, characterised by a
cautious engagement with PPPs. Although it maintains a well-functioning transport system and a strong
institutional framework, its experience with PPPs has been limited. The most notable example is the
Arlandabanan rail project, launched in the 1990s to connect Stockholm with Arlanda Airport. Designed
to attract private investment and transfer certain risks to the private sector, the project encountered
significant challenges, particularly around demand forecasting and long-term revenue stability. These
issues dampened enthusiasm for expanding the PPP model.
In the years since, Sweden has largely returned to a public model for infrastructure delivery. Major
projects are typically overseen by Trafikverket, the national transport agency, with funding drawn pri-
marily from the state or state-owned enterprises. The emphasis remains on public control, long-term
planning, and financial stability, with limited reliance on private capital. As a result, PPPs continue
to play only a minor role in Sweden’s transport sector, and no major new partnerships are currently
planned.
6.2.1
Motivation
The purpose is to assess how the choice of procurement model influences project performance and long-
term outcomes within a Swedish context.
The Arlandabanan rail link stands as Sweden’s primary PPP in transport infrastructure. Completed in
1999, the 41 km line connects Stockholm Central Station to Arlanda Airport. Developed under a 45-
year concession with the private company A-Train AB, the project transferred significant financial and
operational responsibilities to the private sector. Although the line was delivered on schedule, it faced
financial difficulties partly a result of overestimated passenger numbers.
As a public counterpart, the Citybanan project offers a fitting comparison. Delivered by Trafikverket
and completed in 2017, Citybanan is a 6 km commuter rail tunnel beneath central Stockholm (Wikipedia
2025), designed to increase capacity and reduce congestion in the capital’s rail system. Funded entirely
through public investment, the project was delivered within its revised budget and schedule, with strong
public control over planning, execution, and integration with the broader transport network.
Although differing slightly in physical scope as well as financial burden, both projects aimed to address
critical capacity and connectivity issues in the Stockholm region. Together, they provide a valuable basis
for analysing how PPP and traditional public procurement models affect cost control, risk allocation,
delivery reliability, and long-term value in a Swedish setting.
Furthermore, it enables comparing a
closed-system PPP with a rail project that needs to connect to the surrounding system.
6.2.2
Comparison Metrics
The Arlandabanan was completed on schedule in 1999, aligning with the timeline outlined in its conces-
sion agreement. However, the project experienced a cost overrun of approximately 14%, with the final
cost reaching SEK 6.8 bn, compared to the expected SEK 5.95 bn (Centre 2014). While the line has
operated without major public intervention, its long-term performance has been affected by lower-than-
anticipated passenger numbers, largely due to high ticket prices and limited competition on the route.
These issues have raised concerns about the PPP model’s ability to manage demand risk and deliver
long-term value for money (Office 2016).
In contrast, the Citybanan project, was completed in 2017, also on time. The project was delivered
totalling SEK 16.8 billion in 2007 terms (Levy 2022), compared to the estimated cost of SEK 16.3 bn
(Wikipedia 2025). As a fully public venture led by Trafikverket, the project benefited from close co-
ordination with the national rail system and tight public oversight throughout its development. Since
opening, Citybanan has been widely regarded as a success, enhancing network capacity and reducing

Case 3: Uganda
Uganda, a landlocked country in East Africa, has made significant strides in infrastructure development
as part of its broader strategy for economic growth and poverty reduction. The country’s infrastructure
needs span critical sectors such as transportation, energy, water, and urban development. Historically, the
Ugandan government has been the principal driver of infrastructure projects, but fiscal constraints and the
need for increased efficiency have led to a growing interest in PPPs. Over the past two decades, Uganda
has adopted PPPs as a means to mobilise private capital, transfer risk, and enhance service delivery.
While the PPP market in Uganda is still emerging compared to larger economies, the government has
established a supportive legal and institutional framework, including the Public Private Partnerships Act
of 2015 (Uganda 2015).
6.3.1
Motivation
The comparison will consist of a Chinese-funded PPP road project and a similar fully government-funded
public road project. The aim is to understand how financing structure and contractor involvement influ-
ence project outcomes in a Ugandan context.
The Buhimba–Nalweyo–Bulamagi & Bulamagi–Igayaza–Kakumiro road is a 93 km road project located
in the Western Region of Uganda. It connects oil-rich areas of the Albertine Graben to the national
transport network. The project was financed through a loan from the China Exim Bank and constructed
by China Wu Yi Co.
Ltd.
Construction began in 2018 and was completed in 2021.
The road is a
strategic infrastructure link, expected to support Uganda’s emerging petroleum industry and improve
rural accessibility in the region.
The second project selected is the Musita–Lumino–Busia/Majanji road, a 104 km government-funded
road in the eastern part of Uganda. This road connects multiple trade-important towns to the Kenyan
border, aiming to enhance cross-border mobility and local economic activity. It was implemented by the
Uganda National Roads Authority and fully financed by the Government of Uganda. Although initially
scheduled for completion in 2017, the project faced delays and was completed in 2019. The road now
serves as a key trade corridor and has improved market access and transportation efficiency for surround-
ing communities.
The two projects are comparable in both physical scale and intended impact and benefits. Both aimed to
improve regional mobility and stimulate economic development in underserved areas. Their similarities
make them well-suited for evaluating how different funding models affect cost control, schedule adherence,
and delivery of long-term development benefits in a Sub-Saharan African context.

6.4
Case 4: India
India, the world’s largest democracy and one of its fastest-growing economies, has a vast and complex
infrastructure network spanning transportation, energy, water, and urban development. Traditionally,
infrastructure projects were managed by the central and state governments, but in recent decades, India
has increasingly turned to PPPs to improve efficiency and share risks. India has approximately 2000
PPP projects across sectors (Economic Affairs 2023), making it one of the largest PPP markets globally.
Notable examples include the Delhi Airport modernisation, the Mumbai Metro projects, and the National
Highways Development Project, where private partners play a major role in financing, building, and
operating infrastructure.
6.4.1
Motivation
The comparison will consist of a PPP project and a similar public project in India.
The aim is to
understand which procurement model is preferable in the context of a developing country.
The Hyderabad Metro Rail project is one of the largest metro projects in the world developed through
a Public-Private Partnership. The project covers approximately 72 kilometres across three corridors in
Hyderabad, the capital of Telangana. The project was awarded to Larsen & Toubro (L&T) on a Design-
Build-Finance-Operate-Transfer (DBFOT) basis, with an initial contract period of 35 years. Although
initially scheduled for completion in 2016 (Toubro 2011), the project faced delays, with the first phase
opening in late 2017 and final phases finished by late 2023. The project experienced significant cost
overruns, largely due to land acquisition and right-of-way issues (Infrahub 2023).
The second project chosen for this case analysis is the Delhi Metro Phase II. This expansion added around
125 kilometres to the existing metro network in the National Capital Region. Unlike the Hyderabad
Metro, it was delivered through traditional public sector procurement, managed by the Delhi Metro Rail
Corporation (DMRC). Phase II was largely completed by August 2011, with only minor delays compared
to the planned finish date of March 2011. Despite facing substantial budget overruns, the expansion
delivered major improvements in urban mobility and integration of satellite cities around Delhi (Kumar
2012).
The two projects are comparable in terms of physical scale and overall purpose, both aiming to improve
urban transport capacity and reduce congestion in major Indian cities.
Additionally, both projects
were undertaken during a similar period, making them suitable for analysing how different procurement
methods influence project timelines, cost control, and the delivery of promised benefits in a developing
country setting.
6.4.2
Comparison Metrics
The Hyderabad Metro Rail project was initially expected to be completed by 2016 but faced delays,
with phased openings beginning in late 2017 and final segments completed by late 2023. The project
experienced a substantial cost overrun, increasing from an estimated at approximately RS 14,100 crore

7.1
Evaluation of case studies
This study aims to evaluate the performance of PPP and traditional public projects within the transport
infrastructure sector across four key criteria: Cost performance, time performance, long-term relationship
(i.e., the need for public intervention), and whether the project delivered its promised benefits. The goal
is to enable comparison between the PPP and the public projects across these dimensions.
The selected case studies are both in the rail and road sectors and include examples from both developed
and developing countries. This broader scope makes it possible to explore whether project outcomes
are influenced not only by the procurement model but also by the surrounding economic, institutional,
and political contexts. The analysis included four case study countries: Denmark, Sweden, Uganda, and
India, and incorporated the perspectives of three key stakeholder groups: government, investors, and
users. A baseline score was also included, representing a neutral weighting across all criteria.
To synthesise the country-level results from the MCDA, figure 9 presents a visual summary of the rela-
tive preference for PPP versus public procurement across the four countries studied. The vertical axis
represents the relative preference score for PPP over public procurement, with values on the top of the
graph presenting preference for PPP, and the lower part of the graph presenting preference for public
procurement. Each column represents the stakeholder perspectives: baseline, government, investor, and
user.
Figure 9: Relative preference across all case countries and stakeholder perspectives
In Denmark, results are relatively balanced, but it is clear the preference is towards public procurement.
Comparing all personas, the investor has the highest favour towards the PPP model, see figure 9, both
government and user perspectives indicate a more definite preference for the public project. The baseline
score reflects a more even preference, still leaning toward public delivery, as seen in figure 9, suggesting
that in high-capacity systems with strong institutional frameworks, public procurement can perform just
as well, if not better, than PPPs.
Although, the Danish Road Directory decided to do a ”light”-version of PPP by paying the full cost of
construction immediately after it had finished 5.4, this has not had a direct impact on the score. This is
due to the comparison metrics used, as they do not take into account the payment scheme.
In Sweden, the results clearly favour public procurement, with high scores across all stakeholder per-
spectives, as shown in figure 9. This could reflect dissatisfaction with the specific PPP implementation
or a broader public and institutional scepticism toward privatisation of infrastructure in the Swedish
context (Office 2016). Interestingly, there is a fairly large difference in performance of the PPP vs public

procurement between Denmark and Sweden—countries that are otherwise very similar. This may be due
to the transportation mode of the infrastructure, rather than the performance of the PPP itself.
By contrast, in Uganda the PPP model strongly outperforms public procurement across all perspectives,
with higher scores from the investor and government viewpoints, see figure 9. This may reflect limitations
in public sector delivery capacity, where PPPs bring in needed expertise and financial resources, thereby
improving performance on both time and benefit delivery.
India presents a complex case where the results are highly sensitive to the weighting of the public inter-
vention criterion. This complexity comes from the PPP project dealing with public intervention, which
the other projects did not. Under the default weighting used here, where public intervention is considered
less critical compared to other criteria like cost and time performance, the PPP model performs better
overall.
From the government and investor perspectives, the PPP scores higher, see figure 9, while users remain
neutral, showing no clear preference. The baseline score also favours PPP slightly. These results indicate
that, when public intervention is not seen as a major concern, PPPs in India can deliver comparatively
better outcomes, especially in terms of budget and schedule. However, when the importance of avoid-
ing public intervention is increased—that is, when public interference is seen as a sign of weakness or
failure—the performance of PPPs appears significantly worse. Under this alternative weighting, public
projects outperform PPPs in every perspective except the government’s, and even there, the advantage
is reduced. This sensitivity reveals that stakeholder preferences and contextual priorities have a strong
influence on project evaluations.
These results suggest that contextual factors, such as institutional capacity, financing needs, and public
sector capability, play a central role in determining which procurement model performs best. In the de-
veloping countries, PPPs tend to outperform public procurement, particularly in cost, time, and benefit
delivery. This likely reflects PPPs ability to fill gaps in capacity and funding. By contrast, in devel-
oped countries such as Sweden and Denmark, public procurement can be more efficient and more closely
aligned with stakeholder expectations—particularly for users and governments.
The findings also underscore that procurement success is not only context-dependent but also influenced
by stakeholder priorities. In some cases, such as Uganda and India, PPPs received higher preference
scores when evaluated from the investor or user perspective, suggesting that the perceived effectiveness
of a procurement model can shift depending on whose priorities are emphasised and how performance
criteria are weighted. This is an important factor to consider, when weighing the criteria in the MCDA,
and why a baseline aiming for objectivity is important.
Overall, the findings challenge the notion that PPPs are inherently superior or inferior to traditional
public delivery. The case studies reveal no universal advantage of one model over the other; instead,
performance varies significantly by context. In Uganda and India, PPPs outperformed public delivery
across several criteria, particularly in cost and time performance, and in the delivery of promised bene-
fits. Conversely, in Sweden and Denmark, public projects performed better, especially from the user and
government perspectives. This variation reinforces the idea that PPPs are context sensitive rather than
universally superior or inferior. It highlights the importance of a contingent approach to infrastructure
delivery: The most suitable model depends on the local context, the specific project characteristics, and
the performance criteria prioritised by stakeholders. For instance, in India, investors and governments
favoured PPPs, while users were neutral, demonstrating how outcomes and preferences shift depending
on stakeholder perspectives and the weighting of criteria such as public intervention. These findings align
with Verweij et al. (Verweij and Meerkerk 2021), who observed that while PPPs often performed better
on time and budget, more comprehensive data is needed before drawing general conclusions about their
overall superiority.

Sensitivity analysis insights
The sensitivity analyses showed that project rankings can shift considerably depending on the weight
assigned to each criterion, illustrating how different stakeholders’ values influence the perceived success
of a project. In the Indian case, for instance, the PPP model initially appeared more successful. How-
ever, when greater weight was assigned to the ”public intervention” criterion, the advantages of the PPP

project diminished, and in some perspectives, disappeared entirely.
This pattern underscores the importance of clearly articulating whose priorities are being considered in
infrastructure evaluations. Government stakeholders, investors, and users may each prioritise different
outcomes and criteria, leading to different conclusions about what constitutes success.
The REMBRANDT method is helpful in structuring the comparison across perspectives and in allowing
for both a baseline (neutral) and stakeholder-weighted view. However, it also highlights that no ranking
is truly objective; every outcome reflects the weight of underlying values and assumptions. This reinforces
the case for using MCDA as a decision support tool, rather than a definitive verdict, when comparing
PPP and public procurement models.
7.2
Key findings
To interpret the findings from the case studies, this section relates the results to existing literature and
the qualitative insights drawn from expert interviews. The results are evaluated based on the research
questions considering whether PPPs generally perform better than public projects, whether they perform
better in rail or road infrastructure, if they perform better in developing or developed countries, and
lastly if PPPs are still a viable model for the 21st century infrastructure delivery.
7.2.1
Do PPPs generally perform better than traditional public projects?
As stated earlier, the case studies show that there is no universal superiority of PPP projects over pub-
lic projects. The findings from the interviews support this ambiguity. From the interviews, the senior
market director emphasised that PPPs “have a better eye for making a profit,” enabling more efficient
designs and stronger maintenance planning 5.1. This reflects Klein 2015’s argument that profit motives
can incentivise better performance, especially in operations. However, the director of the Danish Road
Directory warned that PPPs can function as a “consumer loan” for governments, causing higher long-
term costs. A concern Engel and Galetovic 2014 shares, who argue PPPs often obscure true fiscal costs
and may burden governments with expensive long-term commitments.
Overall, PPPs can outperform public projects in certain settings, but this depends on institutional ca-
pacity, stakeholder priorities, and project-specific dynamics.
7.2.2
Do PPPs perform better in rail or road infrastructure?
The data does not show a clear performance gap between rail and road projects. However, the qualitative
insights suggest that PPP suitability may vary by sector due to differences in lifecycle cost structures
and commercial potential.
For example, the Copenhagen Metro (publicly delivered) was criticised by the market director for ne-
glecting commercial opportunities, such as integrating retail into station design. He argued that a PPP
model would have prioritised revenue generation and operational efficiency. This supports the view from
Antonio Estache 2009 that PPPs bring value not just from financing, but through operational expertise
and commercial orientation.
By contrast, the Kliplev-Sønderborg highway PPP involved relatively low operational costs, as noted by
the director of the Danish Road Directory. This limits the potential for PPP partners to optimise LCC
(life-cycle cost). This suggests PPPs may add more value in rail projects, where long-term operations
and maintenance play a larger role. This observation reflects Domingo Penyalver and Williamson 2019’s
findings that PPPs are most useful when projects have high long-term operational complexity.
While this study did not directly assess revenue generation, road PPPs may offer greater financial ad-
vantages compared to rail projects due to differences in how income is typically collected. Rail projects
mainly depend on ticket sales, which are often limited by the need to keep prices affordable for everyday
users. This puts a cap on the revenue that can be earned during operations. In contrast, roads, and
especially highways, commonly use toll systems. Either in the form of direct tolls in the form of user
charges or in the form of shadow tolls. These tolls are often higher than rail ticket prices, making it easier
for road projects to generate greater revenue over time, which can enhance their financial performance
under a PPP model.

7.2.3
Do PPPs perform better in developing countries than in developed countries?
The findings indicate that PPPs tend to perform better in developing countries, such as Uganda and
India, where public sector delivery capacity is more limited. In Uganda, PPPs were rated highly by
all three stakeholder groups in the MCDA. Interviews also suggest that in these contexts, PPPs bring
technical expertise, financing, and execution capacity that the public sector may lack. This supports
Loqman Ahmadi and Laali 2024, who argue that in developing countries, PPPs can contribute to sus-
tainable infrastructure delivery by compensating for public sector weaknesses.
In high-capacity, developed settings, PPPs can also play a valuable role, if they are designed with explicit
environmental and lifecycle goals in mind.
For example, the Danish industry’s recent infrastructure
strategy (DI 2025) emphasises that while green investments may initially raise the climate accounting
burden, they are indispensable for achieving long-term climate targets.
“It impacts the climate footprint to invest in infrastructure, but if we are to achieve the green
transition, doing nothing is simply not an option. Denmark must, in the coming years, invest
massively in both the expansion and maintenance of infrastructure with a focus on mobility,
energy security, and climate transition. Therefore, when we build the infrastructure of the
future, it is crucial that we follow the greenest possible path.” (Winther 2025)
In practice, this means crafting PPP contracts with clear environmental performance indicators, lifecycle
carbon budgets, and climate-resilient design requirements. Such provisions could help ensure that private
partners drive innovations in energy efficiency, material usage, and emissions reduction, supporting na-
tional sustainability goals. This approach extends the sustainability logic; not just filling capacity gaps,
but actively delivering green infrastructure in line with industrial and climate policies.
However, in developed countries, where institutional frameworks and technical competence in public
agencies are stronger, public procurement outperformed PPPs as seen in the case studies. The Swedish
results in particular reflect broader public scepticism toward privatisation and suggest PPPs may be
less aligned with national preferences and governance styles. This is very common for the Scandinavian
countries and may differ for developed countries with different political tendencies. This aligns with Klein
2015, who notes that ideological and institutional resistances are highly influential in defining the success
of the PPP project.
This distinction supports the argument that PPPs may be more viable in environments with weak public
sector delivery capacity, where the private sector can fill critical gaps.
7.2.4
Are PPPs a viable model for 21st-century infrastructure delivery?
The findings affirm that PPPs remain a viable model, but not universally so. Their effectiveness depends
on contextual fit, including the country’s institutional strength, financing needs, and sector specific dy-
namics.
The sensitivity analysis in India demonstrates how changing the importance of one criterion, public in-
tervention, can reverse the preferred procurement model. This underscores the importance of aligning
procurement strategies with stakeholder priorities. As discussed by Antonio Estache 2009, a PPP project
must adapt to the surrounding institutional realities and stakeholder concerns, which can be done through
incentivised contracts.
A frequently overlooked factor of PPPs in transport infrastructure is their potential to provide political
and institutional stability over the long term. These projects often span multiple electoral cycles, which in
many countries introduces uncertainty due to changing political leadership and evolving policy priorities.
In such contexts, the long-term and contractually binding nature of PPPs can serve as a stabilising force.
Unlike traditional public procurement, PPP agreements are more difficult to alter unilaterally, offering
protection against policy reversals or politically motivated shifts. While political turnover can pose risks,
such as renegotiation or project delays, PPP contracts can also insulate infrastructure delivery from po-
litical fluctuations. Particularly in democracies where infrastructure is a politically sensitive domain, this
contractual rigidity can help ensure continuity and consistency, turning what might otherwise be a risk
into a strategic advantage.

However, this same rigidity can also have negative implications. If the political or economic context
changes significantly, the public sector may find itself locked into long-term obligations that are no longer

while PPPs can enhance project stability, they also require careful long-term assessment of risk allocation

Insights from the interviews highlights the trade-offs inherent in choosing PPPs for transport infrastruc-

costs and start generating revenue sooner. In settings where speed and discipline are essential, this can
be a key advantage, but where LCC and affordability are paramount, public procurement may be more

against. Engel and Galetovic 2014 argue that governments must avoid being seduced by speed alone. The
financial structure of PPPs can conceal long-term costs unless rigorously assessed through a thorough
LCC analysis. In this light, while PPPs may offer advantages in certain time-sensitive contexts, public
procurement may remain more appropriate where stability, flexibility, and lower long-term costs are the
priority.
Ultimately, PPPs should not be promoted as a one-size-fits-all solution. They are best understood as
one tool in the infrastructure delivery toolbox, well-suited to certain context but less so in others. Their
continued viability in the 21st century depends on strategic, case-by-case deployment, grounded in robust
stakeholder analysis and contextual awareness.

8
Analysis results and implications
This section draws on the findings from the analysis and explores their implications for the use of PPPs
in transport infrastructure. Drawing on the case studies, literature review, and stakeholder interviews, it
examines the underlying drivers behind PPP adoption and evaluates when the model is most appropriate.
The section concludes with a practical diagnostic tool designed to support more context-sensitive and
strategically grounded procurement decisions.
8.1
Determining when PPPs are relevant
The decision to deliver infrastructure through a PPP is rarely made on purely technical or financial
grounds. While the PPP model is often promoted for its ability to mobilise private capital, share risks,
and improve efficiency, the empirical findings in this thesis do not indicate that PPPs consistently lead
to better project performance. Across the case studies examined, there was no clear evidence that PPPs
delivered superior outcomes in terms of cost control, time performance, or long-term public value com-
pared to traditional public procurement. The literature review and the interviews did also not show clear
evidence of this improved performance.
This observation raises a critical question: If PPPs do not inherently perform better, why are they chosen?
To address this, the chapter reflects on insights from the case studies, the literature review, and the
interviews to identify four recurring drivers that help explain the continued reliance on PPPs:
1. Lack of public financing
2. Gaps in institutional capacity
3. Political considerations
4. Ideological orientation
The chapter explores each of these drivers in depth, examining how they shape infrastructure decision-
making in practice and what risks and trade-offs they introduce.
Rather than presenting PPPs as
inherently good or bad, the aim is to provide a more nuanced understanding of when and why PPPs are
used. The chapter concludes by proposing a framework to support decision-makers in evaluating whether
a PPP is a strategically sound choice in their specific context, rather than a default response to systemic
constraints or political pressures.
8.1.1
Main drivers for PPPs
8.1.1.1
Lack of public financing
A central driver behind the adoption of PPPs is the public sector’s
limited fiscal capacity to fund infrastructure projects directly. Governments often face tight budgetary
environments and competing spending priorities that restrict their ability to undertake capital intensive
projects through traditional public procurement. Projects in transport infrastructure are rarely cheap,
but often necessary to drive the economic development of a country. In this context, PPPs emerge as an
appealing tool to deliver infrastructure without immediate recourse to public budgets.
Evidence from both the literature review and the interviews confirms this motivation. Antonio Estache
2009 and Domingo Penyalver and Williamson 2019 argue that PPPs are often adopted not because they
offer superior value for money, but because they enable infrastructure delivery despite fiscal pressures.
The interviews reinforced this finding. The interviewed project manager described that the PPP model
was chosen simply to enable the county to deliver the highway needed to enhance mobility in the area.
However, while PPPs can provide temporary fiscal relief, they often come with long-term financial impli-
cations as described by the interviewed director of the Danish Road Directory. Repayment obligations,
typically spread out over 20-30 years, can accumulate into significant fiscal commitments as well as higher
cost due to growing interest rates. This can lead to higher LCC compared to public delivery.
Given the long-term financial commitments involved, it is vital that decisions to pursue PPPs in response
to financing constraints are supported by a thorough assessment of LCC. While a PPP may appear more

affordable in the short term due to the deferral of public spending, this must not overshadow a project’s
total economic cost over its full lifespan, including financing costs, maintenance, and risk premiums
embedded in private bids. A robust CBA is therefore essential to determine whether the anticipated
social and economic benefits of the project truly outweigh its long-term costs. Without this discipline,
there is a risk that PPPs chosen primarily for fiscal expediency could result in poor value for money,
with governments committing to expensive obligations that crowd out future spending or burden future
taxpayers.
8.1.1.2
Gaps in institutional capacity
Another key driver of PPP adoption is the perceived or
actual lack of technical and/or managerial competencies within the public sector to plan, procure, and
deliver complex infrastructure projects. Large-scale transport projects often involve sophisticated risk
allocation, performance-based contracting, and long-term service delivery components, areas where many
public agencies may lack sufficient expertise or experience. In such cases, PPPs are promoted as a way to
access private sector efficiency, innovation, and technical know-how that might otherwise be unavailable
or difficult to develop in-house.
Domingo Penyalver and Williamson 2019 and Verweij and Meerkerk 2021 argue that governments often
turn to PPPs not simply to outsource construction, but to gain access to better project design, lifecycle
cost management, and operations expertise. In theory the PPP model can incentivise private partners to
deliver infrastructure more efficiently, maintain it to a higher standard, and optimise performance over
time, all of which are benefits that may be difficult to achieve under fragmented public delivery. This
rationale was also highlighted by the interviewed senior market director. As he described how the private
partner can have a better understanding and incentive for making maintenance more cost-effective in the
long run.
However, relying on the private sector to deliver competence does not absolve the public sector of its
responsibilities. Effective PPPs require strong contract design, procurement oversight, and especially con-
tract management, all of which rest with the public partner throughout the project lifecycle. Weaknesses
in these areas can seriously undermine the expected benefits. For instance, if performance standards are
poorly specified, or if enforcement mechanisms are weak, private operators may underperform without
facing consequences. Furthermore, if the public sector lacks the skills to monitor risks, renegotiate terms,
or manage disputes, the balance of the partnership can shift in favour of the private party, potentially
leading to cost escalations or service failures.
In this sense, the promise of private sector efficiency is contingent on a capable and informed public sector
counterpart. Without adequate capacity to structure and manage the partnership, the very rationale for
using a PPP, to compensate for internal limitations, can become a source of new vulnerabilities.
8.1.1.3
Political considerations
The choice to pursue a PPP is often shaped as much by political
considerations as by technical or financial factors. In politically volatile or fragmented governance con-
texts, PPPs can offer strategic advantages by shifting risk, cost, and responsibility away from the public
sector’s immediate control. For elected officials, PPPs may allow them to initiate and deliver visible
infrastructure projects during their time in office, while deferring costs and operational responsibility to
future administrations. This can make PPPs politically attractive, particularly in systems with short
electoral cycles or frequent government turnover.
Moreover, by embedding long-term contractual obligations, PPPs can ”lock in” projects, helping to pro-
tect them from shifting political priorities and avoid corruption. In some cases, this can ensure continuity
and project completion in otherwise unstable environments. However, when projects are pushed forward
using PPPs to avoid public debate over large upfront expenditures, the result can be reduced fiscal visi-
bility and diminished public accountability.
This means PPPs can be used strategically to limit short-term political exposure, particularly in contexts
where decision-makers anticipate resistance to major spending or debt accumulation. This use of PPPs
as a political risk management tool can reinforce their appeal, but also highlights the need for robust
governance frameworks to guard against opportunistic use. Without such safeguards, there is a danger
that projects will be selected or structured more for political expediency than for long-term public value.

A further layer of complexity emerges in how infrastructure responsibility is distributed between different
levels of government. In decentralised systems, such as those where counties or municipalities are tasked
with delivering transport infrastructure, the possibility of PPPs can become essential. When counties
are responsible for infrastructure but are unable to fund major capital investments, PPPs may appear to
offer a solution, but they also risk entrenching uneven governance outcomes, particularly if accountability
mechanisms at the local level are weak. Local governments may lack both the fiscal capacity and the
technical expertise required to develop and manage PPPs effectively, leading back to two previous drivers
of choosing PPPs.
These dynamics underline the importance of political and institutional context in shaping whether PPPs
are appropriate or beneficial. In decentralised governance structures, where counties bear responsibility
for delivering transport infrastructure, PPPs can serve as a pragmatic tool for overcoming local financ-
ing constraints and enabling projects that might otherwise be delayed or cancelled. In such cases, a
well-structured PPP can help deliver essential infrastructure and support local economic development,
especially when public funding mechanisms are limited or unpredictable. However, this potential benefit
hinges on the existence of adequate governance capacity at the county level. Without, PPPs may expose
local governments to long-term obligations they are ill-equipped to manage.
Conversely, in more centralised or fiscally capable state-level contexts, the justification for using PPPs
becomes more conditional. When the state already has the budgetary resources and in-house competen-
cies to deliver projects efficiently, the use of a PPP may introduce unnecessary complexity, risk transfer
costs, and private sector premiums. In such cases, the appeal of PPPs may rest more on political than
practical considerations. This underscores the need for careful, context-sensitive assessment of PPPs, not
only in terms of project economics, but also in light of the governance structures and political incentives
that shape public infrastructure decisions.
8.1.1.4
Ideological orientations
The final driver behind the choice to pursue PPPs lies not in
technical needs or institutional constraints, but in ideological orientation. Specifically, beliefs about the
appropriate role of the state versus the market in delivering public services.
In many contexts, the
adoption of PPPs is underpinned by broader public sector reform agendas. These reform movements
prioritise efficiency, competition, and privatisation, leading to a normative preference for involving the
private sector in the delivery of public infrastructure. This can lead to the notion that PPPs are not just
one option among many, they are instead perceived as the default model for achieving better outcomes
through market-based mechanisms. While this can drive reform and innovation, it also creates a risk
that PPPs are adopted without a clear strategic rationale, or in contexts where they may not be the
most suitable approach. Specifically for transport infrastructure, that is meant to be a public service,
privatisation can lead to less accessibility for the general public due to higher cost, defeating the purpose
of said infrastructure.
This perspective was evident in the case studies. In all four case countries at the time of the projects, the
governments were leaning towards privatisation to some degree with a pro-market orientation. While not
the sole determinant, the political climate at the time of decision-making appears to have reinforced a
predisposition toward private sector involvement. This suggest, that ideological beliefs, rather than purely
financial, technical or political considerations, played a role in determining the procurement choices.
When PPPs are driven primarily by ideology, there is a risk that contextual fit, public value, and long-
term accountability are subordinated to a generalised belief in private sector superiority. This can lead
to inappropriate project structuring, overlooked public sector capabilities, or inflexible contracts that
prioritise investor security over adaptability. It may also result in missed opportunities to strengthen
internal capacity or improve traditional procurement models.
To avoid these pitfalls, it is crucial that the choice of PPP is made not as an ideological commitment,
but as a strategic, evidence-based decision. Governments must assess whether the PPP model aligns
with the specific objectives, constraints, and governance context of each project. They should resist the
temptation to treat it as a one-size-fits-all solution. At the same time, governments must continuously
develop their capabilities to deliver projects for cases where that model is appropriate.

Decision tool
While the previous section have unpacked the individual drivers that often lead to the adoption of PPP,
there remains a need to synthesise these insights into a format that can support structured reflection
and informed decision making. To address this, the following section introduces a diagnostic matrix
that brings together the four key drivers into a single framework. This matrix is not intended to offer
a mechanical decision rule, but rather to help practitioners and policymakers recognise the contextual
pressures influencing the PPP choice and critically assess the suitability of PPP for the specific case. By
identifying both the rationale for choosing PPP and the associated risks of doing so, the matrix aims to
promote more transparent, accountable, and strategically grounded decisions.
Figure 10: Diagnostic matrix outlining key considerations for evaluating the appropriateness of PPPs in
different institutional and political contexts
The matrix in figure 10 structures these four drivers into a practical diagnostic tool by posing a key
question for each, accompanied by critical considerations and contextual guidance. Rather than offering
prescriptive answers, it aims to support informed judgement. For each driver, the tool highlights not
only why PPPs might be considered, but also under what conditions caution is warranted. Therefore,
the matrix is not a decision rule but a structured prompt for early-stage PPP consideration, helping
decision-makers ask better questions before committing to a delivery model.
For example, consider a regional authority planning to develop a new rail line to serve a growing com-
muter corridor. Faced with constrained public budgets, the authority may initially view a PPP as an
attractive funding mechanism. The matrix would prompt them to interrogate this rationale: Is the fund-
ing constraint due to a temporary shortfall or deeper structural limitations? Will a PPP commit the
government to long-term payments that exceed the project’s value? They then examine institutional
capacity: If the public sector lacks experience managing complex contracts, can this gap be addressed
before procurement? Politically, the project may be used to signal progress ahead of an election, which
raises concerns about long-term accountability. Finally, if PPP is being considered simply because it
aligns with a pro-market ideology, the matrix urges decision-makers to reflect on whether alternative
models were seriously considered. In this way, the matrix supports more reflective, evidence-based deci-
sions about whether PPP is genuinely the most suitable delivery path.
The matrix is not intended to replace established project appraisal methods such as CBA and MCDA,
but rather to complement them by introducing an institutional and contextual perspective. It is best
used in the early stages of project development, particularly during option appraisal or initial screening,
where fundamental questions about delivery models are first posed. By considering the key drivers the
matrix encourages reflection on whether a PPP is being considered for the right reasons. Importantly,

DISCUSSION
9
Discussion
This thesis sets out to evaluate the suitability of PPPs versus traditional public procurement in trans-
port infrastructure using multi-criteria performance evaluation. Drawing on eight case studies and a
stakeholder-informed MCDA, the analysis revealed that PPPs do not consistently outperform public pro-
curement across the selected success criteria. While certain PPPs demonstrated a strong performance in
time or cost control, others showed weakness in delivering promised benefits or required public interven-
tion soon after completion. Conversely, public procurement projects were not inherently underperforming,
challenging some conventional assumptions about private sector efficiency or risk management.
These findings align with the increasing academic view that PPPs are not inherently superior. While
much of the literature emphasises the potential efficiency gains of PPPs (e.g. Penyalver et al., Estache et
al.), this study aligns more closely with critical accounts (e.g. Engel et al., Verweij et al.) that highlight
uneven performance and the influence of contextual factors. The results suggest that the effectiveness of
PPPs is highly case-dependent, shaped by sector characteristics, project design, and institutional context
rather than the procurement model alone.
Furthermore, the diagnostic matrix developed in this thesis provides a novel way to map the rationale
for PPP adoption across different projects. It offers a structured lens to distinguish between ideological,
pragmatic, and capacity-driven motivations, with potential for use in both academic analysis and policy
evaluation. This approach, while exploratory, may serve as a useful starting point for developing better
tools to assess PPP suitability before procurement decisions are made.
9.1
PPPs in a changing policy landscape
While the empirical findings of this study focus on past project performance, they also invite broader
reflection on the evolving role of PPPs in public policy.
In countries like Denmark as of 2025, a growing share of public budgets is being redirected towards de-
fence due to changing geopolitical realities (DR 2025). As governments face fiscal constraints, long-term
infrastructure investments, for example transport infrastructure, risk is being postponed or underfunded.
In this context, PPPs may re-emerge as a pragmatic policy tool to mobilise private investment in essential
infrastructure to ensure economic growth, even when public resources are limited.
Furthermore, the urgency of sustainable transport adds another layer to the equation. Rail systems, for
example, are more environmentally sustainable than road transport but require high upfront investment.
The benefits, including lower emissions, improved connectivity, and long-term modal shift, often unfold
over decades. Here, PPPs could play a key role in enabling governments to finance and deliver such
projects while sharing risks and maintaining budget flexibility.
This study’s findings suggest that while PPPs do come with challenges, they may be particularly suited
to contexts where long-term investment goals must be balanced against short-term fiscal pressures. Used
selectively and with strong institutional oversight, PPPs could support not only financial efficiency but
also broader strategic objectives, from climate goals to regional development.
9.2
Limitations
While the analysis offered a structured insight into how PPP and public projects compare, several limi-
tations constrain the generalisability and precision of the results.
The qualitative grading, used in the REMBRANDT method when comparing the cases, introduced sub-
jectivity into the evaluation. Criteria such as ”delivered promised benefits” required researcher interpre-
tation often based on a mix of official evaluations, media reports, and sometimes expert inputs. Although
care was taken to apply consistent standards, these assessments can still be influenced by framing effects,
uneven data availability across projects and countries as well as general subjectivity.
Furthermore, the analysis was limited to four criteria: Cost performance, time performance, public inter-
vention, and delivery of promised benefits. These were selected for their relevance and data availability,

but they do not cover the full range of possible success dimensions as shown in the paper by Osei-Kyei
2016. Several other important factors such as environmental impact, social impact, quality of service,
and so on were excluded. This limits the scope of conclusions and may omit trade-offs that matter to
certain stakeholders.
Another limitation of this study was the case study sample. While it intentionally varied across geogra-
phy and sector, the sample remains small. The limited number of cases means that the findings are best
seen as indicative rather than universally generalisable. Moreover, the focus on completed projects with
available data further excluded more recent or ongoing PPPs where lessons are still emerging.
Lastly, while REMBRANDT provided a manageable and stakeholder-sensitive weighting structure, it
is not immune to critique. Like all MCDA methods, it depends on input assumptions and simplified
representations of complex trade-offs. These choices, while methodologically justified, can still shape
outcomes in subtle but important ways. Furthermore, the weighting of criteria was determined by the
researcher rather than by actual stakeholders for each case project. This approach was necessary due to
the unavailability of consistent stakeholder input across all studies. However, it inevitably introduced a
degree of bias into the analysis. Relying on different stakeholders for each case would also have posed
challenges, as it could lead to inconsistent and potentially incomparable weighting schemes across projects.
Overall, the study presents a structured comparison that opens space for more informed debate, but its
findings should be understood within these empirical and methodological constraints.
9.3
Future research
This study has offered insights into the performance of PPP and public procurement models across a
small sample of transport infrastructure projects. However, several opportunities exist to extend and
strengthen this study.
First, expanding the number of case studies would greatly enhance both the robustness and the generalis-
ability of the findings. A larger and more diverse dataset, both geographically and across more sectors in
transport infrastructure, would allow for stronger conclusions and help identify patterns that may not be
visible in this limited sample. This would be particularly valuable for drawing firmer distinctions between
performance in developed and developing country contexts. It would also allow looking into what type
of projects PPPs fits best into. For example for rail projects, one could look into if it works best for
greenfield projects, for rolling stock projects, for closed system projects and many more.
Second, future studies should pursue splitting into more sector-specific analyses, for example by sepa-
rating road and rail infrastructure into distinct categories. While this thesis considered both modes, a
deeper dive into each could help identify sector-specific dynamics, such as differences in revenue models,
operational complexity, and stakeholder engagement. It would also be valuable to explore other transport
infrastructure options, such as airports and bus systems, to broaden the analysis and assess whether the
observed patterns hold across different sectors.
Thirdly, incorporating long-term outcomes more explicitly into the evaluation of case projects as well as
other criteria would provide a more comprehensive view of project success. While this study included
a proxy for long-term performance through the “public intervention” criterion, future work could track
post-completion outcomes over 10–20 years, such as maintenance quality, operational sustainability, or
long-term benefit delivery to users.
Fourth, given the highly political nature of the decision to pursue PPPs, future research should incor-
porate extensive qualitative data through interviews with a range of stakeholders. The strategic use
of PPPs is often shaped by political, institutional, and experiential factors that are difficult to capture
fully through quantitative criteria alone. Stakeholder perspectives are critical to understanding project
complexity and the nuances that MCDA frameworks may miss, making their inclusion vital to developing
a more holistic and robust assessment.
Fifth, future research should also focus on real-world application and testing of the decision tool devel-
oped in this study. Integrating the tool into live case evaluations and further embedding it within MCDA

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
This thesis examined the suitability of PPPs for transport infrastructure by applying an MCDA frame-
work. The analysis was done by using REMBRANDT to compare PPP projects to publicly procured
projects across multiple countries and sectors. To complement the quantitative assessment, stakeholder
interviews were conducted to provide context, interpret performance outcomes, and explore perceptions
of PPP effectiveness from both institutional and practitioner perspectives.
The findings addressed four key research questions: Whether PPPs generally perform better than tra-
ditional public projects, whether PPPs are more effective for rail or road infrastructure, whether PPPs
perform better in developed or developing countries compared to public projects, and to what extent do
PPPs remain a viable model for financing and delivering transport infrastructure projects in the 21st
century. First, PPPs did not systematically outperform public projects; their success depended on con-
text, including institutional capacity, economic conditions, and contractual structures, as illustrated by
the differing outcomes across Denmark, Sweden, Uganda, and India. Second, while results were mixed
across sectors, there is some indication that PPPs may offer more value in road projects than in rail,
due to higher operational complexity and commercial opportunities, as seen in particular in the Scandi-
navian cases. Here the rail project success varied more than for the road projects. Third, PPPs showed
stronger relative performance in developing countries, where they helped address public sector capacity
and financing gaps, as seen with the cases in India and Uganda. In contrast, public procurement often
performed equally well or better in developed countries with strong institutions, as seen with Denmark
and Sweden. Finally, both the MCDA results and interviews supported a contingent approach: PPPs
are not universally better, but can be effective when aligned with the specific needs, constraints, and
priorities of a given context.
These findings have important implications for policymakers and practitioners involved in infrastructure
development. Rather than relying on assumptions about the superiority of one procurement model over
another, decision-makers should adopt a context-sensitive approach that accounts for institutional, eco-
nomic, and political conditions. To support this, the thesis developed a practical decision-support matrix
based on four key drivers of PPP adoption: Lack of public financing, gaps in institutional capacity, po-
litical considerations, and ideological orientation. The matrix helps assess whether PPPs are appropriate
by guiding users through targeted questions and context-aware guidance, encouraging transparent and
evidence-based decision-making.
This study has some limitations. The number of case studies was limited, and while REMBRANDT
structured the weighting process, it remains partially subjective due to the use of the semantic scale,
which is also the part that allows for context-specific and stakeholder based preferences. Future research
could expand the scope of cases, incorporate more stakeholder perspectives in weighting the criteria and
in understanding the complexities and experiences PPPs come with.
Overall, this thesis showed that while PPPs can offer advantages under specific conditions, their success
remains contingent on the alignment between project characteristics, institutional capacity, and gover-
nance frameworks, underscoring the importance of context-sensitive decision tools like MCDA.
