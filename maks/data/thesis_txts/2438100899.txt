Abstract
This master thesis consists of three major parts. In the ﬁrst part, a thorough investigation on different design ap-
proaches for calculating wave loads for offshore wind turbines’ substructures are made. Comparisons are made
between using Morison approach and Radiation-diffraction approach. It has been found that the Radiation-
diffraction approach can best capture radiation and diffraction effects while Morison approach can somehow
also capture these effects if the ratio between dimension of the structure and wave length is smaller than 0.2.
In the second part of this thesis, the inﬂuences of the ﬂoater ﬂexibility to the system structural responses are
addressed. In the current design practise, only rigid modes are considered for the ﬂoater. While when the ﬂoater
becomes bigger and bigger, the inﬂuences of ﬂoater ﬂexibility might well change the system global natural fre-
quency within the 1p or 3p range, or close to the peak wave frequency. The dynamic responses of the whole
system might be greatly inﬂuenced if the ﬂoater ﬂexibility is signiﬁcant. Craig-Bampton method is used to
equip selected possible ﬂoater ﬂexible modes into the whole structural system. It has been found the current
chosen ﬂoater model with the ﬁrst two eigenmodes included have little inﬂuences to the structural dynamic
responses.
In the last part of this thesis, a simpliﬁed, frequency domain analysis method, QuLAF has been proposed. The
method is based on four in-plane modes (surge, heave, pitch, tower fore-aft mode) and possible ﬂexible modes in
the ﬂoater. Aerodynamic damping are extracted to compensate missing information from time domain analysis.
It has been found that if only wave loads are considered, the structural responses can be very well captured based
on QuLAF model. While, when turbulent wind is considered, the accuracy is of lower order. But in general, the
frequency contents of the structural responses are comparable with non linear time domain analysis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction to ﬂoating wind turbines
This chapter consists of two main parts. In the ﬁrst part of the chapter, some basic concepts of offshore wind
turbine foundations will be addressed. In the second part of the chapter, the coordinate systems used in this
thesis will be introduced. Readers should be aware that, due to different simulation tools used in this project, it
is very important to keep in mind which coordinate systems are used when checking the results.
1.1
Introduction to offshore wind energy
Wind energy prevails in the ﬁeld of the green energy production [GWEC, 2014]. A drastic cost reduction at
2014 for offshore wind energy makes offshore wind energy becoming one of the most favorable green energy.
The 2011 Fukushima disaster tenders more and more people believing that nuclear power is not safe. Counties
like Taiwan has proposed policies that the nuclear power plant will be completely replaced by wind and solar
energy in next few years. The Taiwanese government has setup a target to generate 3000MW through wind
power by 2025. Due to relatively small size of the land, offshore wind energy might be the only choice for
Taiwanese government to develop green energy.
1.1.1
Types of offshore wind turbine foundations
Offshore wind turbine foundations are used to transfer loads from substructures and superstructures into soil.
These loads might from the structural self weight, environmental loads, such as wind and wave loads, and self
excitation forces due to rotor imbalanced responses. There are many types of wind turbine foundations as shown
in Figure 1.1. Among these, the load transfer mechanisms of three types of offshore wind turbine foundations
are brieﬂy introduced in here, namely monopile, jacket, and spar-like ﬂoating foundation.

Monopiles and Jacket foundations
Monopile and jacket foundations are the two dominant foundations which are suitable for water depth up to ap-
proximately 80m to 100m. Monopile is most suitable for shallow or medium water depth. Due to its simplicity
in fabrication, monopile is the most commonly used foundation type in offshore wind turbines. When the water
depth goes to deeper, the requirements for the whole system’s stiffness will inevitably increase the thickness of
the pile wall and thus increase the difﬁculties of the fabrication. The wind and wave loads will mainly transfer
as shear force and overturning moment to the foundation pile and will then transfer to the soil. The soil lateral
capacity will determine the maximum shear and overturning moment the foundation can resist. The vertical
loads, such as selfweight of the structure, and the torsional moment, will transfer to soil by the pile skin friction
and tip bearing compression.
A more economically feasible solution for deep water foundation type is the jacket foundation. Typically, four
legs jacket structure with piles embedded at the bottom of the legs is used to support and transfer loads from
other part of the structural components. Since the jacket structures are much transparent in hydrodynamics, the
jacket foundations are less sensitive in wave loads when compared with monopile foundations. It should be
noticed that the load transfer mechanism is quite different with monopile load transfer mechanism. The over-
turning moments will transfer as torsion and compression to the embedded piles. The shear force and torsional
moment will transfer as later loads to the embedded piles.
ﬂoating foundations
When moving to an even deeper water, the ﬂoating foundation will be the only feasible foundation type. Based
on the stability mechanisms, the ﬂoating wind foundations can be divided into three groups, the ballast, tension
leg, and buoyancy types [Veritas, 2011]. For ballast type of the ﬂoating foundation, the rotational stability is
mainly achieved by the restoring moment aroused from the centre of buoyancy and the centre of gravity. The
stability of the tension leg ﬂoating foundation is mainly achieved by the mooring lines of the system. For the
buoyancy type, the stability of the ﬂoating wind turbine system is achieved by the stiffness contributed from the
water plane. A typical example of this ﬂoating foundation is the semi submersible platform.

2.1
Introduction
In the way of designing offshore wind turbine structures, wave loads play a detrimental role for offshore wind
turbine support structure design. Calculation of wave loads are complex in the sense that usually one needs
to consider different design methods in one design project. This chapter will deal with the different design
methods for calculating wave loads on offshore wind turbine support structures. Discussions will be given for
below aspects,
• Ways of modeling wave kinematics
• Ways of calculating wave loads
• Ways of modeling structural response
Theories of above aspects will be ﬁrst presented in a concise way. Readers should refer to [Journée and Massi,
2001], [Jorgen and Mutlu, 1997] for detailed explanations. A generic example will be given for addressing
some important features for different design methods used in this project.
2.2
Modeling of wave kinematics
For calculating wave loads, it is necessary to obtain wave kinematics ﬁrst. Typical ways of modeling wave
kinematics can be generally considered as two groups. The linear wave model, such as the Airy wave model
and NewWave theory, and the nonlinear wave model, such as stream function theory and Stokes law. The
detailed presentations of these wave theories are not feasible. Only some of the main features will be addressed
below.
2.2.1
The linear wave theory
George Biddell Airy, a British engineer proposed a relatively easy wave model to describe prorogation of grav-
ity based waves. The method is distinguished by its easy applications and accuracy in some purposes for many
practical offshore problems. While the accuracy of the Airy wave theory really depends on the purposes and the
5

6

situations of using and it is under the assumptions that the ﬂow is inviscid, incompressible and irrotational and
small amplitude of waves, the error of the Airy wave theory accumulates quickly when the wave amplitudes are
large relative to the water depth. As a consequence, in most of the situations, the linear wave theory cannot be
used for extreme wave load when checking ULS of the offshore wind turbines.
The small amplitude requirement indicates the wave kinematics can only be calculated until mean water level.
This limitation can be somehow compensated by stretching wave kinematics accordingly to the real wave ampli-
tude positions. The existing stretching techniques includes Wheeler stretching, delta stretching, linear stretch-
ing, etc, Among these, Wheeler Stretching is most widely used in offshore wind area. The illustration of the
wheeler stretching is shown in Figure 2.1
Figure 2.1: Illustration of the Wheeler Stretching [Bredmose, 2017]
As one could ﬁnd from Figure 2.1, Wheeler Stretching modiﬁes the horizontal velocity proﬁle for whole
water depth. While for other stretching methods, the modiﬁcations usually are done only above the mean water
level which would lead to too conservative results if the water goes deep.
Besides the simplicity of using Airy wave theory, this wave theory is also prevailing when dealing with stochas-
tic waves. Its nature of linearity guarantees the use of superposition principle. The irregular stochastic waves
can thus be presented in frequency domain and each wave can be treated individually.
2.2.2
The nonlinear wave theory
Many nonlinear wave models and their extensions exist and they receive extensively attentions among re-
searchers. While due to their implementation complicity and computation time in real projects. Only few
nonlinear wave models are included in current design codes.
One of the prevailing nonlinear wave theory is the stream function theory. This wave theory is fully nonlinear
and thus can only deal with regular waves. While since the stream function is a numerical solution, it can handle
wave heights up to near breaking wave height [Henderson and Zaaijer, 2004].
Another prevailing nonlinear wave theory is the Stokes wave theory. The Stokes waves are weakly nonlinear
and can be used for regular, irregular and directional waves. While only the lower order Stokes wave (usually
second order) can be used for irregular and directional waves. For regular waves, it is common to use ﬁfth order
Stokes wave theory.

When calculating extreme wave loads for offshore wind turbines, the design extreme wave height is the 50
year and 1 year unconditional value of wave height. The design wave height usually well beyond the limit wave
height for linear wave model. It is necessary to use nonlinear wave model for extreme wave loads.
In general, the importance of employing nonlinear wave model is less for the fatigue load calculations. While
due to the nonlinear wave structural interaction, the higher harmonic resonance might happen and thus increase
damages. As a consequence, using of linear wave model might underestimate fatigue loads due to waves.
2.3

As already described in the previous sections, there are no universally true wave theories for offshore wind
turbine structures. The same goes true for methods of calculating wave loads. In general, three different
methods can be employed for calculating wave loads on offshore wind turbine support structures,
• The Morison equation
• The radiation-diffraction solver
• Using of CFD

2.3.1

2.3.2
Morison equation
Morison equation has been widely used when calculating wave loads on slender structures. Basically, this
equation (with some modiﬁcations) can be used to calculate wave loads for all kind of offshore monopile
foundations. This equation was proposed by a graduate student, J.R. Morison and other three people in 1950.
Morison et al., 1950 found this equation by mixing empiricism in experiments with a little potential ﬂow theory.
Actually, in their original paper, they only use potential ﬂow theory for obtaining wave kinetics. By doing
experiments, they found the recorded force of the cylinder can be grouped into two, one in phase with oscillation
acceleration and one in phase with oscillation velocity. More investigations are made with different acceleration
and velocity, they were able to see that the force in phase with velocity is in quadratic relation with the oscillation
velocity and the force in phase with acceleration is linear with the oscillation acceleration. Further more, by
changing the size of the cylinder diameter, they found the force in phase with acceleration has linear relationship
with the size of the square of diameter and the force in phase with velocity is linear to the diameter. Based on
these observations, they obtained following equation,

2.3.3
Comparisons between radiation-diffraction approach and Morison approach
So far the main theory of the radiation-diffraction solver and Morison equation are presented. The next step
would be to ﬁnd out under which conditions the methods should be chosen in a appropriate way.
For bulk offshore structures (D/λ > 0.2), where the KC number, a parameter inverse propositional to the diam-
eter, is small, the radiation-diffraction approach can be well used for calculating wave loads. The explanation
can be made based on the factor that under very small KC number and without wave breaking, it is very unlikely
that ﬂow would separate and ﬂow behaves just like potential ﬂow. Since the radiation-diffraction approach is
entirely based on the potential ﬂow theory, it will give the highest accuracy as long as the ﬂow is potential ﬂow.
For slender offshore structures (D/λ < 0.2), the KC number is larger in general, the pressure drag should be
considered when calculating wave loads. By employing Morison equation, the drag force can be considered for
the whole transverse/inline force.
Based on above discussions, limitations for both methods are clear. For radiation-diffraction solver, the drag
force cannot be directly considered in the solver and the nonlinear wave model cannot be used. For Morison
equation, the radiation and diffraction effects, especially for the wave potential damping, cannot be considered.
Also, due to the quadratic drag term, the Morison equation cannot be solved in frequency domain and many
computational efforts are needed if the problem is solved in time domain.
It is possible to ﬁx some of the limitations indicated before. One have to add the drag term after using radiation-
diffraction solver in order to consider wave and current drag. For Morison equation, MacCamy-Fuchs correction
can be used for better considering diffraction effects, although this correction only works for simple shapes of
structures. For the difﬁculty of solving problems in frequency domain, the concepts of linearlizing drag term
can be used based on energy conservation.

bines with ﬂexible modes included. Discussions about how accurate of the procedures will also be given. A
case study will then be presented. Comparisons are also made by checking the inﬂuences of ﬂexible modes.
4.1
Procedures for HAWC2-WAMIT coupling analysis
In this section, procedures about how to conduct HAWC2-WAMIT coupling analysis will be given. It should be
mentioned here that the idea presented here is based on [Borg, A. Hansen, and Bredmose, n.d.]. The accuracy
of these procedures will be given later in this chapter.
4.1.1
Pre-processing procedures
Table 4.1 shows the required Pre-processing procedures. In order to consider the effects of the ﬂexible modes
in WAMIT, it is necessary to ﬁrst be sure how many ﬂexible modes are needed and then how these generalized
modes can be obtained. The answers to these two questions requires conducting a full FE model of the ﬂoater.
HAWC2 is able to do FE analysis of whole wind turbine system with ﬂoater modeled as beam element. By
checking natural frequencies of the global system, one can choose how many ﬂexible modes are required by
comparing the interested frequency range in the system. The system reductions happen in here for gathering
all possible interesting static and eigen modes. The interested modes will be used to map WAMIT to ﬁnd
the generalized deformation for each panel deﬁned in WAMIT geometry ﬁle. Until now, all the necessary input
information for WAMIT has deﬁned. The excitation forces can thus be calculated as standard procedures shown
in[Newman, 1977].

Once the pre-processing has been done, the dynamic analysis of the whole ﬂoating wind turbines can be done

the case study.

In order to calculate the added mass in the system, hydrodynamic loading model should be built in HAWC2.
As discussed in the previous chapters, two commonly used hydrodynamic loading models can be used, namely
Morison approach and Diffraction-radiation approach.
The Morison approach can be done directly inside of the HAWC2. The added mass coefﬁcient should be deﬁned

cies requires iterative procedures coupling with WAMIT and HAWC2 at the ﬁrst two steps shown in Table 4.1.
For each frequency, iterative procedures are required until the added mass achieve the same value. Figure 4.7
shows how the iterative procedures implement.

six diffraction IRFs will be generated. In this case study, only zero wave heading will be considered and thus

It should be mentioned here that the radiation IRF cannot have nonzero force at negative time while the diffrac-
tion IRF can. This notice can also be found from Figure 4.7 and Figure 4.8. Since the radiation force can only
be generated after the structure is actually displaced, a nonzero radiation force before the structure moves does
not physically reasonable. While the diffraction force depends on the wave elevation which is not decided by
the responses of the structure, a nonzero diffraction force in negative time is natural.

Conclusions
This chapter has investigated the aerodynamic damping mechanism of the ﬂoating wind turbines. Two receipts

damping in cross wind directions. These limitations can only be captured by running fully coupled wind turbine
simulations in SoA software.

Frequency domain analysis has a wide application in vibration analysis. While when it comes to solving a

The limitations come to two aspects. First, the transient responses of the system will lose due to the incapability
of the frequency domain approach that can only capture steady response. Second, solving system’s EoM can
only be done if there are no non-linearities in the system. The ﬁrst limitation is really not an issue for the offshore
wind turbine foundation design since merely are transient responses given attentions. The second limitation,
which is believed to be the major drawback for the frequency domain approach, requires efforts to linearlize
equation of motions for the system. For design of offshore ﬂoating wind turbine foundation, nonlinearities
mainly come from the mooring system, drag force and the aerodynamic damping due to the relative motion of
the turbine. More detailed discussions will be presented in the following sections where a step by step, detailed
case study will be given.
It is also worthwhile to mention that the idea of the frequency domain analysis has in fact indirectly introduced
in previous chapters where the hydrodynamic coefﬁcients are calculated in frequency domain by using WAMIT.

ﬂoater modes. It should be noticed that more ﬂexible modes can be easily added by expanding the size of the

peaks are found at heave natural frequency when using QuLAF.
The big discrepancy in heave response is mainly due to the inherent limitation of the QuLAF. Since QuLAF
is a frequency domain approach, the transient responses cannot be calculated. Compared with surge and pitch
motions, radiated waves generated by heave motion is small and thus the radiation damping in heave motion
is less prominent. Although the responses of the ﬁrst 3000s have been neglected, the relatively low damping
in heave requires much longer time to damped out the transient responses. The author has tried to neglect
the responses for the ﬁrst 4000s, the energy contributions at heave natural frequency decrease signiﬁcantly.
So as long as the transient responses are neglected, the two approaches will yield the same responses at this
environmental condition.

damping. A possible reason that causes the discrepancy is that the aerodynamic load used in QuLAF is wrong
in heave direction. It has been found that the aerodynamic loads in heave direction is the vertical projection of
the thrust force. The angle ✓between global vertical direction and thrust force direction changes with time and
the aerodynamic loads in heave direction are the function of cos ✓. This non linearity cannot be captured by the
QuLAF model and thus causes a low accurate result in heave motion.

to the bad design of the ﬂoater and mooring line systems. More ballast can be added or a deeper draft can be
placed to reduce the pitch rotations. While redesigning of the ﬂoater might change all the structural properties
and thus all simulations done before need to be re-run again, the author has decided to keep the above results

6.4
Limitations and conclusions
This chapter proposes a new frequency domain method QuLAF for calculating structural responses of offshore
ﬂoating wind turbines with ﬂexible ﬂoater. Several assumptions are made in order this use this method. These
assumptions are concluded and listed below,
1. The wave loads can be calculated based on potential ﬂow theory
2. The aerodynamic loads and aerodynamic damping can be precalculated
3. The mooring system can be modeled as linear springs
4. Only tower ﬁrst fore-aft bending mode is considered for the tower modes
5. Only the in-plane rigid modes and two ﬂexible modes of the ﬂoater are considered
6. The RNA system is modeled as a concentrated mass block
7. The transient responses of the system are neglected
All these assumptions decrease the accuracy of QuLAF but increase the speed of the calculations. It is not
difﬁcult to increase the accuracy of the QuLAF. For example, in some cases, the second fore-aft mode of the

to provide a quick look of the structural response at the preliminary design stage, a relatively low accuracy is
acceptable in this stage.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions and further work
Conclusions of this master project will be presented here. Some suggestions will be given for the future work.
7.1
Conclusions
This master thesis has been devoted to investigate the inﬂuences of ﬂexible modes of the spar-like offshore
ﬂoating foundation. An in-house load calculation tool ’QuLAF’ has been further developed to include the extra
ﬂexible modes from the ﬂoater. Several comparisons have been made in order to check the inﬂuences of ﬂexible
modes and the accuracy of the load calculation tool ’QuLAF’. These comparisons are listed below,
1. Structural responses of the rigid ﬂoater and ﬂexible ﬂoater calculated based on nonlinear time domain
analysis tool HAWC2.
2. Structural responses of the rigid ﬂoater and ﬂexible ﬂoater calculated based on frequency domain tool
QuLAF.
3. Structural responses of the rigid ﬂoater calculated based on HAWC2 and QuLAF.
4. Structural responses of the ﬂexible ﬂoater calculated based on HAWC2 and QuLAF.
It has been found that under the current design of the ﬂoater, the inﬂuences of the ﬂexible modes in ﬂoater is
negligible. The maximum discrepancy for the tower top acceleration is less than 0.6%. The reason is that the
ﬂoater is too rigid to be excited by the environmental loads. For the accuracy of the QuLAF, it has been found
that the QuLAF results are accurate where only wave loads are applied. While when turbulent wind loads are
applied, the accuracy of the QuLAF decreases. The major reason for low accuracy when wind loads are con-
sidered is that QuLAF cannot capture any nonlinearities in the system. These nonlinearities mostly come from
the aerodynamic load and its effects in structural responses. The effects include aerodynamic damping and also
P-∆effects due to gravity and sudden change of the shear force from wind. For compensating aerodynamic
damping effects, free decays are made to calculate representative aerodynamic damping for each wind speed.
While the accuracy of the aerodynamic damping is unknown, discrepancies are inevitable. Although discrepan-
cies exist, the results coming from QuLAF are still comparable with HAWC2 results. The QuLAF model can
still be a good tool to give a overlook of the structural responses in the early design stage.
