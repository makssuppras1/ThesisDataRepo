Abstract
Earth’s ground is subject to subsidence (vertical motion).
Subsidence can be the
result of human activities, such as mining or extraction of oil. The occurrence of
subsidence can damage infrastructures and result in ﬁnancial losses. It is therefore in-
teresting to monitor subsidence to detect these phenomenons. The development and
launching of Earth Observation satellites in the past decades brought new convenient
tools, such as Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar, InSAR, to monitor ground
motion remotely.
This thesis focuses on the implementation of a PSInSAR method. PSInSAR is a pow-
erful technique developed to overcome the limitation of classic InSAR, namely the
sensitivity to decorrelation and atmospheric inhomogeneities. This method is based
on the study of Persistent Scatterers, which backscatter radar waves in a stable way
through time.
The diﬀerent steps of the PSI method are presented. Then, the implemented PSI al-
gorithm is tested on a simulated dataset, with known deformations and known noise
level. The ability of the algorithm to retrieve the deformations from the simulated
interferometric phase is assessed. The validation also aims to assess the strengths and
weaknesses of diﬀerent PSs networks and inversion methods.
The PSI processing chain is used to study the area of Aarhus, and in particular to
monitor the subsidence of CERES Panorama and Aarhus Harbour. The quality of the
results is assessed by looking at the inversion residuals, comparing the ﬁnal velocity
to the previously approximated ones, comparing with SqueeSAR data provided by
SDFE, and levelling measurement provided by GEO.

Introduction
1.1
Ground subsidence
The surface of Earth is constantly sub-
ject to motion, as the underground
properties and the loading on the sur-
face evolve through time. Subsidence
designates the sinking of the ground
surface with little or no horizontal
movement (which makes it diﬀerent
from landslides for example).
Subsidence can be the result of natu-
ral processes, such as karst phenom-
ena (dissolution of rocks such as lime-
stone, dolomite, and gypsum), melting
of permafrost, moderate crustal warp-
ing, consolidation and/or oxidation of
organic compounds, ejection of lava
from magmatic chambers. It can also
be the result of human activity, such as
extraction of underground water, ex-
traction of oil, mining, construction of
infrastructures or buildings.
Ground
subsidence
can
result
in
strains in the surface that can cause ﬁs-
sures to appear. An example is the ﬁs-
sures in Lucerne Lake landscape, Fig-
ure 1.1.
When occurring in inhab-
ited areas, subsidence can damage the
structure of buildings.
It is there-
fore important to be able to monitor
ground subsidence to prevent extreme
cases, such as the Crooked House ex-
ample, Figure 1.2.
Figure 1.1: Lucerne Lake, San Bernarduno
County, Mojave Desert California U.S.:
ex-
traction of groundwater resulted in ground
subsidence which caused ﬁssures on the land-
scape

1.2
Monitoring ground subsidence
A wide range of solutions exists to monitor ground subsidence, from on-ﬁeld surveys
to remote observations. The diﬀerent techniques may require a team of operators
to do the survey on the ﬁeld (optical levelling, GPS levelling) or can be done from
satellite images directly (InSAR monitoring for example).
1.2.1
Optical levelling
From far the most common technique to monitor land subsidence, optical levelling
is both economical and reliable. It uses optical systems to determine the relational
elevation diﬀerences between diﬀerent points, as can be seen from Figure 1.3, and
allows creating a network of elevation points. The accuracy is expected to be within
±3 mm, Fergason et al. (2015). The time series measurement spacing is determined
by the frequency at which the geometers go on the ﬁeld.
Figure 1.3: An example of optical levelling: the spiral levelling method, picture from The
constructor, civil engineering (n.d.)
1.2.2
GPS levelling
Diﬀerent studies, such as Nagi Zomrawi Mohammed (2012) and Kenyeres (2016),
underlined the possibility of using GPS data for levelling surveys. This technology
requires a sparse GNSS levelling network and provides aﬀordable cm-accuracy results.
The height derived from this method is sensitive to the surrounding environment and
is impacted by atmospheric state.

1.2.3
InSAR
Repeat-pass InSAR interferometry is a powerful tool to monitor ground subsidence.
The basic concept of InSAR interferometry is presented in Hanssen (2002). An inter-
esting method using the stability of Persistent Scatterers (PSs) have been developed
and is presented in Ferretti, Prati, et al. (2001).
It enables to detect millimetre-
accurate terrain motions on a grid of sparse PSs. PSInSAR ability to monitor subsi-
dence has been demonstrated with the monitoring of crustal deformations in Hooper
et al. (2007) or subsidence caused by mining activities in Raucoules et al. (2009).
Each of the previously mentioned methods has its strength and weaknesses. Optical
levelling provides economical and accurate measures at desired locations but requires
the intervention of a team of workers on the ﬁeld. GPS levelling provides aﬀordable
accurate measurements but is quite sensitive to the surrounding environment and
atmospheric state. InSAR is a convenient tool that provides accurate measurement
with dense time series: as often as the satellite ﬂy above the area.

CHAPTER 2
SAR interferometry
Synthetic aperture radar interferometry is a powerful and convenient imaging tool.
It allows measuring the topography of a surface and/or its deformation over time, by
studying the phase evolution of a coherent backscattered signal through time, usually
looking at images acquired at diﬀerent times.
This thesis is about implementing
a PSI technique, which is a speciﬁc Diﬀerential Interferometric Synthetic Aperture
Radar (DInSAR) technique. The basic principles of SAR interferometry will be brieﬂy
reminded here. More information can be found in Rosen (2000), Simons and Rosen
(2015) and Hanssen (2002).
2.1
SAR Theory
2.1.1
SAR image properties
Synthetic Aperture Radars (SAR) has
been developed in order to answer the
limitations of real aperture radars.
A
small antenna is mounted on an air-
craft/spacecraft which is moving along-
side its ﬂight path, as can be seen from
Figure 2.1. While ﬂying, it emits a signal
whose pulses are reﬂected by scatterers.
The individual signals are then recorded
by the antenna and combined to create
the synthetic aperture, improving greatly
the azimuth resolution. The structure of
the echo changes because of the Doppler
eﬀect as the radar moves along its track,
which is later used to focus the image.
Figure 2.1: SAR geometry during one pass,
point target response from Cumming, Ian G.
(2005)

2.1.2
Point target response
While ﬂying alongside its path, the sensor continuously sends pulses. A point target
backscatters several pulses while it is illuminated by the SAR antenna. During one
sampling interval, the radar receives echos from diﬀerent targets. Therefore, focusing
the image is necessary to distinguish the contribution of each target. The focusing
is done ﬁrst in range and then in azimuth taking into account the Doppler centroïd
frequency, and the SAR image is focused to generate the Single Look Complex (SLC)
image. Considering the acquisition geometry described in Figure 2.1, the response of
a point target, like a corner reﬂector or a Persistent Scatterer in a SAR SLC image
is:
zSLC(τ, η) = pr(τ −2R0
c )·pa(η−η0)·exp(−j 4π
λ R0)·exp(−j2πfcτatm)·exp(jϕb) (2.1)

approach, η0 azimuth time at which the Doppler shift is equal to zero, λ the radar
wavelength, fc the carrier frequency, τatm related to the atmospheric propagation, ϕb
the phase contribution of resolution cell elements such as speckle and c the speed of
light. pr and pa describes the amplitude of the signal. The response of a point target,
without side-lobes ﬁltering, is usually described by a sinc-like function:
pr(τ −2R0

The shape of the sinc

misinterpreted as targets or hide weaker targets. Therefore, it is necessary to reduce
the side-lobes and apply side-lobe ﬁltering, presented in Sec. 3.4.

and the eﬀect of the processing system which focused the image. Hence, the phase of
the SLC image itself cannot be used directly for analyzing deformations.

technique
From Eq. 2.3, the principal limitations of DInSAR are due to temporal and geomet-
rical decorrelations, ϕdecorr, and atmospheric inhomogeneities, ϕatm. The Permanent
Scatterer Interferometry SAR (PSInSAR) method is an eﬃcient way to overcome
these limitations and has been developed at ﬁrst at the Polytechnical University of
Milano. The basic principles are presented in Ferretti, Prati, et al. (2001) and Fer-
retti, Prati, et al. (2000). PSInSAR overcomes the sensitivity to decorrelation by
studying only targets that are backscattering in a stable way through time, Persis-
tent Scatterers, Sec. 3.1. It overcomes the sensitivity to atmospheric inhomogeneities
by retrieving the interferometric phase from the arc phase diﬀerence deﬁned by a
network, Sec. 3.5, and solving the inverse problem deﬁned in Sec. 3.7. Moreover,
studying a large number of interferograms reduces the impact of atmospheric artefacts
and enables accurate estimations of deformations regardless of decorrelation.

3.5
Network generation
As explained in Subsection 2.2.2, diﬀerent sources contribute to the phase. When
looking at ground subsidence, ϕdefo is of interest and other contributions such as
ϕatm are detrimental for our ϕdefo estimation. Permanent Scatterer Interferometry
overcome this by retrieving the phase evolution of the PSs from the phase diﬀerence
with the surrounding PSs. The local undesired contributions such as ϕatm are then
averaged out. An important step of the PSI algorithm is therefore the generation of
the network linking all the PSs.
Figure 3.5: Diﬀerent kind of networks, from Kampes 2012
Figure 3.5 shows diﬀerent kinds of networks. The star network (a) cannot be used
in the present study as it links each PS to the reference PS. The resulting distance
between two PSs can be considerable and the atmospheric contribution is not ho-
mogeneous anymore and does not average out. The minimal network (b) overcome
this issue, by creating a path between each PS to the reference PS while minimizing
the distance between each pair of PSs. Nevertheless, this network is not suitable
because of the lack of redundancy: a corrupted/not coherent through time pixel in a
branch will propagate errors. The redundant network (c) overcome all the aforemen-
tioned issues. It both links the PSs with minimal distance between two and provides
redundancy to limit the propagation of errors. Two methods to generate redundant
networks will here be presented: the method based on the Delaunay interpolation and
the method developed by Kampes for the STUN algorithm, as presented in Kampes
(2012).

When the PSs network has been created, the phase diﬀerence ∆ϕk,l between two
connected PSs k and l is then calculated and is linked to the relative motion between
the two PSs. As explained in SubSec. 2.2.2, Eq. 2.4, the interferometric phase ϕint
is wrapped between −π and π. Before deriving the relative velocity of all PSs from
the phase diﬀerence evolution, it is necessary to unwrap the phase diﬀerence for each
couple of PSs. This can be done easily if the phase diﬀerence to unwrap has no phase
jump greater than |π|. In presence of phase jumps greater than |π|, the unwrapping
becomes ambiguous. An eﬃcient way to prevent this situation is presented in Ferretti,
Prati, et al. (2000): the relative velocity and height diﬀerence between two PSs is
estimated and their contribution to the phase diﬀerence is subtracted, hence phase
jumps greater than |π| are less likely to occur.

lead to a model that can predict this data value correctly. Therefore, if the kth data

From Figure 4.72 and Table 4.6 can be seen that the use of robust LS with both
Huber weights and Tukey’s biweights improves the quality of the velocity results: the
distribution of the errors is more narrow and more centred on 0. The mean value
of the errors is 2 to 5 times smaller with respectively Huber weights and Tukey’s
biweights and the value of the median is almost 5 times smaller with both weights
compared to the simple LS method. The use of a robust method slightly improves
the height results. The calculation time, on the other hand, increases consequently
by the use of a robust method: it takes 256 times longer using Huber compared to
simple LS and more than 400 times longer when using Tukey’s biweights.
4.4.5
Discussion on the impact of the integration method
As underlined in SubSec. 4.4.3, the use of a robust method in presence of low noise
does not improve the quality of the results. Moreover, the calculation time is increas-
ing consequently because of the iterative reweighting. As explained in SubSec. 3.7.3
the robust method has been developed in order to solve inverse problems where noise
and outliers are present in the data. Therefore, these results were to be expected.
The simulation on a noisy data set, presented in SubSec.
4.4.4, underlined that
the use of robust method improves the quality of our velocity results, but do not
improve so much the quality of the height results. The cost of this velocity result
improvement is a consequent increase in the calculation time.
Finally, the use of Tukey’s biweight in presence of noise gives better quality results
than Huber weights, but also comes at a higher calculation time cost. Also, Tukey’s
biweight might exclude too much data (0 weighting data whose residuals are too high)
when Huber weights just down-weight them.
4.4.6
Validation of the processing chain
The simulation carried out in the present chapter validates the viability of the PSI
processing chain implemented and its ability to retrieve velocities and height error.
The displacement simulated was only linear, but the inversion of the interferograms
being done for each interferogram independently, the ability to retrieve displacement
extends from linear motion to more complex motion.
Section 4.2 clearly shows the superiority of high connectivity Kampes networks. A
Kampes network with Nconn = 10 will therefore be used on the real dataset. The use
of a robust LS inversion rather than simple LS inversion also improves the quality
of the results, especially the velocity results, but comes with more calculation time.
The real data might be noisy, therefore a robust LS inversion using Huber weight will
be used.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
The results presented in Chapter 6 and Chapter 7 are now discussed. First is discussed
the choice of the chain parameters and then the quality of the results. The results
from the two diﬀerent areas are jointly analysed to understand them better.
8.1
Influence of the choice of parameters
8.1.1
Amplitude threshold and sidelobe filtering coherence
threshold
The urban area presented in Sec. 6.1 is a convenient area to process with the PSI
chain: the urban environment provides several PSs spread quite homogeneously, with-
out isolated parts. Therefore, it enables freedom on the choice of the parameters: on
the amplitude dispersion threshold Da0 and the threshold on the sidelobe coherence
ξ0. The only limiting fact has been the detection of our two points of interest, the
reference point, Aarhus DomKirke, and the building to monitor, CERES Panorama.
The investigation of the detection of CERES Panorama as a PS, in Appendix B,
shows that CERES Panorama PS is not a PS on the ﬁrst fraction of the observation
time, which results in a low amplitude dispersion parameter Da = 0.354. The am-
plitude dispersion threshold is selected as relatively conservative, Da0 = 0.36, just
above CERES Panorama’s. This allows selecting the point of interest as a PS with-
out selecting aberrant PSs, such as ones in the water. The threshold on the sidelobe
coherence has been chosen relatively low, ξ0 = 0.20, to totally get rid of the sidelobes
(circled in blue on Figure 6.4). The combination of these two parameters leads to the
selection of a relatively large number of PSs. From there, the network is created using
Kampes network with connectivity Nconn = 10, to improve the quality of the results,
as discussed in Sec. 4.2.
The resulting network, as can be seen from Figure 6.7,
is dense, well connected and with relatively short connections (limiting atmospheric
inhomogeneities, SubSec. 2.2.2).
On the other hand, the harbour area presented in Sec. 7.1 shows more challenges:
the two harbour peninsulas are naturally less connected zones. Moreover, the PSs in
the harbour area might be less persistent through time or might be spread scatterers
(wave breakers for example).
The choice of the parameters plays there a crucial
==PAGE:96==
84
8 Discussion
role, as too constraining threshold results in poorly connected PSs and not selective
enough parameters might result in the selection of wrong pixels, not persistent in
time and whose results will deteriorate the quality of the ﬁnal results. A conservative
amplitude dispersion parameter is therefore chosen, Da0 = 0.36, as for the urban area.
To prevent the sidelobe ﬁltering from discarding valid PSs, the value of the threshold
on the sidelobe coherence is increased to ξ0 = 0.50. Even with these conservative
thresholds, it can be seen from Figure 7.2 that the middle peninsula of the harbour
has an area with a lower density of PSs. The network created, using Kampes with
Nconn = 10 and shown on Figure 7.3, is not optimal in the middle peninsula zone as
the connections are relatively long (without exceeding the threshold on the distances
between two pixels, set to prevent too high atmospheric inhomogeneities) and as the
PSs at the extremity of the peninsula are poorly connected and isolated.
8.1.2
Regularization parameter
Thanks to the use of General-Cross Validation the optimal regularization parameter
is found. Considering the urban area, Sec. 6.6, the regularization parameters for
the phase inversion is α2 = 2.31 and for the height inversion is α2 = 1.05 · 10−2, as
can be seen from Figures 6.15 and 6.16. These can be interpreted as the relative
weight will be given to minimizing the data misﬁt relatively to the model misﬁt:
in the case of the phase inversion, the model misﬁt norm has a relatively higher
inﬂuence compared to the height inversion. When considering the harbour area, the
regularization parameters for the phase inversion is α2 = 1.29·10−1 and for the height
inversion is α2 = 1.15 · 10−2, as can be seen from Figures 7.4 and 7.5. Therefore, the
model misﬁt for the phase inversion has a sensibly lower inﬂuence.

Regarding the harbour area, as can be seen from Figure 7.18, the distribution is

The diﬀerence with the ﬁrst-order estimation approximation is higher in the harbour

ﬁrst-order estimation of the PSs velocities might be wrong.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
Within the framework of this thesis has been implemented a PSI processing chain, as
presented in Chapter 3, which takes as input a stack of N interferograms and gener-
ates deformation time series and height error estimation for the selected Persistent
Scatterers (PSs) within the image. PSInSAR method overcomes traditional InSAR
limitation such as the sensitivity to decorrelation in time and the sensitivity to at-
mospheric inhomogeneities. This is achieved respectively by studying only Persistent
Scatterers (PSs) for which the scattering is stable through time and by working on
the phase arc diﬀerences between couples of PSs, from which the ﬁnal interferometric
phase is retrieved. This complex processing chain is built from several blocks:
• PS selection: determines the pixels in which PSs are located
• Sidelobe ﬁltering: ﬁlters and discards the pixels previously selected as PSs but
that are in fact sidelobes of a strong scatterer
• Network creation: creates the diﬀerent arcs between PSs, links them together
• ﬁrst-order approximation: optional step, approximates the diﬀerence in veloci-
ties between two PSs by a linear velocity, and estimates the height error between
them. Their contribution to the phase diﬀerence is then removed to facilitate
the phase unwrapping
• Phase unwrapping: unwrap the phase diﬀerence which is wrapped between −π
and π.
This step is made easier by the removal of the contribution of the
ﬁrst-order approximated relative velocities and height errors
• Spatial integration: the phase arc diﬀerences are linked to the interferometric
phase evolution of each PSs through an inverse problem. Solving this inverse
problem enables to retrieve the interferometric phase evolution which can then
be translated into a deformation time series.
From the deformation can be
calculated the average velocities of each PS, by ﬁtting a linear curve to the
deformation.
The PSI chain, as previously described, as then been implemented. The validation
section of this report, in Chapter 4, proved the ability of the chain to retrieve defor-
mations from a stack of N interferograms. The chain has therefore been tested on
a simulated stack of N interferograms, which recreates a realistic situation with the
==PAGE:100==
88
9 Conclusion
implementation of noise on the simulated interferometric phases. The second aim of
the validation section of this report was to assess the superiority of diﬀerent methods.
Therefore, the use of a Delaunay triangulation compared to Kampes algorithm to
generate the network has been compared, as well as the use of a simple Least Square
(LS) inversion compared to more sophisticated robust LS inversions, and that for dif-
ferent noise levels. This section concluded on the ability of the chain to retrieve the
interferometric phase evolution and hence the deformation time series and concluded
on the superiority of Kampes algorithm with a high connectivity. Results are also
improved by the use of a robust LS method in presence of noise.
The PSI chain has then been used to retrieve the deformation in two sub-areas:
Aarhus urban area, Chapter 6 and then Aarhus Harbor area, Chapter 7. The results
have been presented in these two chapters and discussed in Chapter 8. The validity
of the retrieved results has been assessed by comparing selected points of interests,
where both time series generated by the implemented PSI processing chain and time
series provided by SDFE are available (generated using SqueeSAR algorithm). Both
methods agree in the deformation found to the order of a millimetre. Moreover, the
deformation time series for the considered points matches the before-hand expecta-
tions presented in Chapter 1, namely the sinking of CERES Panorama due to the
important loads caused by the 75m high building on the ground, and the sinking of
Aarhus Harbor due to the presence of soft soils and the heavy loads in the area.
Further investigation regarding the validity of the deformation time series has to be
carried out. So far, it has only been possible to compare the generated times series
with SqueeSAR measurement on a limited amount of PSs that were found by both
processing chains. The intent to validate the results with the levelling measurement
provided by GEO had a limited success: important extrapolations and approxima-
tions need to be made to compare the results, as the measurement from the levelling
data and the found PS are not located at the same place. Therefore, it would be
interesting to cross-validate the results with other sources of measurements, such as
levelling measurement at known PSs locations. Running the PSI processing chain
on other Sentinel-1 tracks might also provide interesting complementary results: as
other tracks use other viewing directions, the PSs found would be diﬀerent. Using
diﬀerent tracks would enable to retrieve the displacement of more PSs and map better
the deformation of the ground.
