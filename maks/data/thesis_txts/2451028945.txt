1. Abstracts 
EN: 
The iron-catalysed acceptorless dehydrogenative synthesis of quinolines from alcohol substrates has been 
investigated. Dehydrogenation is a reaction type that is gaining in frequency of use because it facilitates the 
transformation of common and wide-spread substrates, alcohols, to the corresponding carbonyls which 
have a more comprehensive synthetic transformation library. 
Acceptorless dehydrogenation using iron complexes as catalysts eliminate the production of stoichiometric 
quantities of toxic chemical waste. As green chemistry is gaining popularity in the recent years, these very 
advantages comes more into focus. 
Using Fe(III)(salen) as the catalyst for a dehydrogenation of 2-aminobenzyl alcohol and subsequent reaction 
with 1-phenylethanol, 2-phenylquinoline was successfully synthesised by a modified Friedländer synthesis. 
The reaction conditions are optimised and thus a plausible synthesis of substituted quinolines relying on 
acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols is presented.

2. Introduction 
The objective of the research presented herein has been to investigate the catalytic dehydrogenation of 
alcohols by the iron complexes Fe(III)(salen) and Fe(III)(salan) and to further advance the atom-economical 
method of utilising first-period transition metal complexes to facilitate the catalytic dehydrogenation of 
alcohols. While dehydrogenations have long been known, most of the procedures for such reactions are 
waste-intensive, low-yielding or rely on rare, expensive noble metals (Azizi & Madsen, 2018). These 
drawbacks in concurrence with growing environmental concerns are a large part of what has driven the 
recent push towards developing catalytic dehydrogenative procedures that rely on abundant and 
environmentally benign metals, like iron. 
 
The traditional methods for the production of carbonyl compounds and the like from alcohols by 
dehydrogenation suffer from the abovementioned drawbacks. By current chemical and environmental 
standards these methods would seem unappealing, but they have been necessary since the removal of 
hydrogen atoms from adjacent atomic centres in saturated organic molecules is highly unfavourable 
(Pandey, Dutta, & Bera, 2016). Ideally, the good dehydrogenation is then an acceptorless dehydrogenation. 
Being able to liberate the hydrogen as gas has several advantages. Not only is molecular hydrogen 
essentially non-polluting, but it is also valuable as a chemical feedstock and fuel in its own right. Developing 
dehydrogenation reaction procedures that are acceptorless and reliant on a cheap, non-toxic metal is only 
half of the picture, however. A suitable catalyst to incorporate the metal and facilitate the dehydrogenation 
reaction is also required. This catalyst would also have to be non-toxic, but also be able to support 
dehydrogenation under suitable reaction conditions and, preferably, with a large tolerance of a range of 
functional groups. 
 
2.1. Iron in catalytic systems 
Iron is a promising candidate for the acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols. Iron is one of the most 
abundant metals in the crust of the Earth; both by mole fraction and mass fraction, and this abundance 
makes iron an excellent choice to base future catalysts on as price is likely to remain low due to the large 
supply available (Ashby, 2005). The widespread use of iron in many other technologies and applications not 
involving synthetic chemistry will also assist in ensuring iron remains available. The most well-known use of 
iron in a chemical or biological context is most likely as the centre in the oxygen-carrying protein 
haemoglobin. Iron as a fulcrum for catalysis in biological systems is well known, several enzymes containing 
iron centres have been discovered, many of them dehydrogenases. Dehydrogenases are a class of enzymes 
that carry out catalytic dehydrogenations – exactly the process this project aims at reproducing 
synthetically. 
Iron is found in group 8 of the periodic table, alongside ruthenium and osmium, both metals well known in 
the field of chemical reagents and catalysis (Bauer & Knölker, 2015; Makarov, Fristrup, & Madsen, 2012; 
Mazziotta & Madsen, 2017). Some of the most common current catalysts for dehydrogenation are 
ruthenium-based. A range of iron-catalysed chemical reactions have been published, thoroughly 
demonstrating that iron, like its heavier, more expensive and more toxic fellow transition metals is a viable 
choice for metal catalysis of organic reactions.

An example of iron complexes used in organic chemistry is the

transfer hydrogenation is a hydrogenation where the source of the 
hydrogen to be added to the substrate is not gaseous hydrogen, 
but rather hydrogen transferred from a suitable donor, like tetralin,

2.2. Dehydrogenation 
In the simplest of terms, dehydrogenation is the removal of hydrogen molecules from a substrate. This 
hydrogen is usually, elegantly released as gaseous hydrogen. The removal of a product is a powerful force 
in shifting the chemical equilibrium, thus driving the reaction further towards completion. Hydrogen can 
also be produced by a dehydrogenation reaction and utilised in situ to hydrogenate a viable substrate. The 
use of a compound to "soak up" the hydrogen has been common (Sudhakar & Luh, 2002) and a lot of work 
has been done on making these hydrogen scavengers - or acceptors - as efficient as possible (Annen & 
Grützmacher, 2012). 
Dehydrogenation reactions can be used to synthesise a wide range of functionalities from alcohols, as well 
as form C-C bonds, usually by alkylation of an α-C. Alcohols in general are a well-known feedstock for many 
chemical syntheses and transformations, despite their relative low degree of chemical reactivity. Being able 
to easily and in good yields dehydrogenate and thus formally oxidise alcohols into the corresponding 
carbonyls would greatly increase the applicability of alcohols in all areas of chemical synthesis (Balaraman, 
Nandakumar, Jaiswal, & Sahoo, 2017). This wide range of reactions and functional group interconversions 
made possible by dehydrogenative processes have increased interest in these catalysts in many fields of 
synthetic organic chemistry, including the synthesis of pharmaceuticals (Roughley & Jordan, 2011). 
Examples of the wide range of utility of dehydrogenative processes include the extrusion of gaseous 
hydrogen from alkanes (Fuse, Kojima, Mitsunuma, & Kanai, 2018) and the formation of amides from 
primary alcohols and amines (Makarov et al., 2012). 
When proposing that a reaction proceeds through catalytic dehydrogenation, acceptorless or otherwise, 
one must consider other possibilities (Xu, Chen, & Liu, 2013). One way to confirm that a reaction path is 
truly dehydrogenative is to collect the evolved hydrogen and compare the volume of it to the stoichiometry 
of the reaction or measure the concentration of the hydrogen scavenger, depending on whether the 
reaction is thought to be acceptorless dehydrogenation or otherwise. A reaction often mistaken for a 
proper dehydrogenation is the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction (Kabadwal, Das, & Banerjee, 2018; 
Liang et al., 2013). This reduction is a reduction of a ketone or aldehyde to the corresponding alcohol in the 
presence of a catalytic amount of alkoxide and an alcohol that serves as a sacrificial source of hydrogen. 
The opposite reaction of the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction is the Oppenauer oxidation; the 
oxidation of an alcohol to the corresponding carbonyl by means of a carbonyl acting as a hydrogen 
scavenger. The cycle of Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction and Oppenauer oxidation is usually mediated

by the cheap and non-toxic aluminium isopropoxide and either isopropanol or acetone, respectively, as the 
sacrificial component (Smith, 2010). There are multiple advantages to this strategy; like the availability and 
price of both isopropanol and acetone, both of which can be utilised as the solvent for the reaction further 
driving the equilibrium towards formation of product. The salen ligand has been shown to improve yields 
from the MPV reduction as well (Tsygankov et al., 2016). 
More common terms describing the cycle of MPV-O chemistry is transfer hydrogenation or hydrogen 
borrowing chemistry. These terms are used to make it clearer what happens during the reaction, e.g. that 
hydrogen is transferred from one substrate somewhere else or that hydrogen is borrowed from a 
hydrogen-rich substrate and passed to a hydrogen-poor substrate. Mechanistically, the transfer 
hydrogenation reactions usually involve the contribution of a proton from the R-OH and a hydride from the 
CH carrying the hydroxyl group to a metal complex or catalyst, which then either link the proton and 
hydride to form hydrogen or pass the proton and hydride to the carbonyl substrate. The hydrogen transfer 
can take place by two different overall mechanistic pathways; an inner-sphere and an outer-sphere. The 
principal difference between the two being that in an inner-sphere mechanism, the alcohol oxygen forms a 
covalent bond to the catalysts metal by an oxidative addition. Meanwhile, in an outer-sphere mechanism, 
coordination via hydrogen bonds occurs between the metal and the CH, and OH and a ligand lone-pair 
donor. After the catalyst and alcohol substrate have bonded, β-hydride elimination removes the required 
hydride from the primary carbon, thus forming the carbonyl (Nielsen, 2015). 
 
2.2.1. Acceptorless dehydrogenation 
Several strategies for acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols to yield a wide range of different 
functionalities have been proposed end expanded upon in the literature (Annen & Grützmacher, 2012; 
Azizi, Akrami, & Madsen, 2019; Azizi & Madsen, 2018; Bottaro & Madsen, 2019; Fristrup, Tursky, & Madsen, 
2012; Gunanathan & Milstein, 2013; Makarov et al., 2012; Monda & Madsen, 2018; Nielsen, 2015). The 
crucial part in an acceptorless dehydrogenation is the lack of a hydrogen acceptor or scavenger. The 
hydrogen must be released as gaseous hydrogen, which opens up the possibility of utilising acceptorless 
dehydrogenation not just for synthetic transformations but for the production of hydrogen gas in an 
efficient manner. The acceptorless dehydrogenation catalysts developed recently has made renewable 
sources of alcohol functionalities highly attractive as substrates to be important parts in a future hydrogen 
economy. Many traditionally petrochemical-based alcohols have also been produced from renewable 
sources (Bull, 1994). Furthermore, it has been shown, that iron-based catalytic systems are on par with 
precious metal-based catalytic systems (Balaraman et al., 2017). 
When a dehydrogenation reaction is acceptorless, no hydrogen scavenger is present and so acceptorless 
dehydrogenations produce far less chemical waste. Essentially, acceptorless dehydrogenations carried out 
by non-toxic catalysts are among the greenest and most atom-economical reactions. Atom-economy is a 
term describing the overall economy of atoms in a reaction; a good atom economy means that many, if not 
all, of the atoms present in the reactants, must also be present in the product(s) of the reaction. The 
concept is predicated on the thought that every atom that is put in to a reaction represents an investment 
and those of the atoms that are not part of the product represent a net loss. In an acceptorless 
dehydrogenation reaction, every atom present in the reactant is also present in the product, including the 
liberated hydrogen.

The Madsen group has previously demonstrated that a Mn(III)(salen) complex can facilitate the catalytic 
acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols (Samuelsen, Santilli, Ahlquist, & Madsen, 2019). Mechanistic 
studies have shown that the catalytically active species does not in fact contain the Schiff bases present in 
the salen complexes, but rather the reduced species. It was shown, however, that the Schiff base 
functionality was crucial to the reactive properties of the metal complexes. The activation of Mn(III)(salen) 
to form the active C-N form is shown in appendix A and the proposed catalytic cycle of the Mn(III)(salen) 
complex in a dehydrogenation cycle is shown in appendix B. It is fully expected that the mechanism for 
Fe(III)(salen) is analogous, though studies have not yet been carried out. 
 
2.3. Quinoline synthesis 
Quinolines are organic compounds containing the quinoline motif, an N-heterocyclic version of 
naphthalene. Quinolines have been known for a long time and many quinoline alkaloids have been 
discovered in plants, bacteria and fungi (Michael, 1999). Heterocycles in general are a common occurrence 
in pharmaceuticals and quinolines are no exception. The pharmaceuticals primaquine, chloroquine, 
amodiaquine and quinine are just a few examples of quinoline-based antimalarial drugs. All four are 
present on WHOs list of essential medicines. Quinolines are widely regarded as a synthetic motif that many 
future pharmaceutical drugs will be based on, especially since the quinoline-based drugs already approved 
have shown antiprotozoal and antimalarial properties and been effective in the treatment of diseases only 
few other drugs treat effectively and that are expected to grow in patient number over the coming years, 
including hospital-acquired pneumonia (American Thoracic Society, 2005; Egan, 2001). 
Synthetically speaking, a range of different pathways can be taken to yield quinolines. The aromatic 
heterocycle has been an attractive target structure for many synthetic procedures and many different 
routes have been discovered (Madapa, Tusi, & Batra, 2008). Quinoline derivatives remain desirable 
synthetic targets as many compounds containing the quinoline motif have been shown to possess 
antimalarial, antibacterial, antifungal and anticancer properties (Diaz, Miranda, & Diaz, 2015). 
Using a dehydrogenative synthetic route to quinolines represents significant progress. Many of the 
standard routes for the synthesis of substituted quinolines involve either harsh reagents or reaction 
conditions or several possible side reactions. These obstacles are largely removed when utilising 
dehydrogenation to generate the required precursors in situ, as this project has aimed to do.  
 
2.3.1. Friedländer quinoline synthesis 
The Friedländer synthesis is one of the more widespread syntheses of quinolines. Published in 1882, it is a 
reaction between 2-aminobenzaldehyde and a carbonyl compound (Friedlaender, 1882). The Friedländer 
synthesis tolerates a broad spectrum of functionalities, so the spectrum of possible products is equally 
broad. As substitutions on the 2-aminobenzaldehyde or the carbonyl will be carried through into the final 
quinoline, this protocol will also be able to produce substituted quinolines. Research has produced 
protocols for the green synthesis of polysubstituted quinolines under mild reaction conditions (Arcadi, 
Chiarini, Di Giuseppe, & Marinelli, 2003). Reacting a benzaldehyde with a ketone under basic conditions 
will, of course, rapidly lead to the formation of several side products stemming from aldol condensations. 
==PAGE:9==
Page 9 of 32 
 
This is one of the reasons a dehydrogenative protocol would be advantageous not only for the reasons 
already mentioned in this report, but also because the reactants would then be an alcohol and 2-
aminobenzyl alcohol, thoroughly eliminating aldol reactions. At least until an appreciable amount of alcohol 
has been dehydrogenated as the reaction progresses. A palladium-catalysed protocol has been developed 
for the condensation of 2-aminobenzyl alcohol and suitable ketones, like acetophenone, to form 
substituted quinolines (Cho & Ren, 2007). 
The Friedländer synthesis can proceed through two different paths, shown hereunder. The first path 
proceeds first by the formation of the aldol product between the two carbonyls. Elimination of water 
follows to form the α, β-unsaturated carbonyl, which rapidly loses another unit of water through imine 
formation and at the same time cyclisation and aromatisation. This final loss of water yields the final 
quinoline. The second reaction path opens with the imine formation to give the anil intermediate. This 
intermediate then gives the aldol product by forming a ring. In the end water is lost to yield the quinoline. 
 
 
Fig. 2.3-1 - Reaction paths of the Friedländer synthesis 
 
In the dehydrogenative pathway to quinolines by a Friedländer synthesis, 2-aminobenzyl alcohol and 
another alcohol is used as the substrates. The two alcohols are then dehydrogenated to form the carbonyl 
compounds employed in the traditional synthesis (Azizi et al., 2019). The use of catalysts that facilitate 
dehydrogenation does not however, preclude the formation of any of the traditional side products formed 
by undesired side reactions. 
One of the side reactions expected in the synthesis of quinolines from alcohols by dehydrogenation is the 
Guerbet reaction. The reaction involves the conversion of a primary aliphatic alcohol into the β-alkylated 
alcohol. The carbon chain from one unit of the alcohol will become the alkyl chain in the finished product 
(Miller & Bennett, 1961). The predicament of this side reaction in a catalytically active reaction involving 
hydrogen is, that several side products can be formed. If using 1-phenylethanol to form a phenyl-
substituted quinoline, as done in this project, there are several side products than can form.

3. Results and discussion 
This project has focused on the formation of quinolines by dehydrogenating suitable alcohols with 
Fe(III)(salen)-based catalysts in the presence of 2-aminobenzyl alcohol. The well-known and thoroughly 
documented salen complexes have been shown to have catalytic properties, in particular for 
dehydrogenations (Nishinaga, Yamazaki, & Matsuura, 1988; Samuelsen et al., 2019). The Madsen group has 
been investigating the salen-ligand and its derivatives in coordination with several different metals to 
expand the knowledge of the catalytic dehydrogenation capabilities of these systems. The drive to use 
more abundant, less toxic and cheaper metals have led to the investigation of iron complexes. The iron 
complexes of salen and the reduced ligand, salan, was chosen as the primary focus of this project.

The synthesis of imines has long been used as the standard reaction to investigate acceptorless alcohol 
dehydrogenation reactions as imines are useful synthetic products. This project has however, focused on 
the application of acceptorless alcohol dehydrogenation in the synthesis of quinolines, instead.

3.1. Quinoline synthesis by dehydrogenation 
The synthesis of quinolines by the Friedländer synthesis has been adapted to the purposes of investigating 
dehydrogenation of alcohols to form the carbonyl substrates. 2-aminobenzyl alcohol was a given as the 
starting material which, upon dehydrogenation would yield the required 2-aminobenzaldehyde. As alcohol 
substrate 1-phenylethanol was chosen and thus the product of successful quinoline synthesis would be 2-
phenylquinoline. 
The first parameter that was investigated was the choice and stoichiometry of bases. As shown in the 
mechanism for the Friedländer quinoline synthesis, equilibriums involving loss of water are ubiquitous. 
Using KOH as base would serve to increase the water content of the reaction mixtures, due to hydroxide 
forming water when deprotonating a substrate. This water would serve as a chemical force driving the 
reaction to the detriment of quinoline formation. Using tBuOK as a base instead would form tbutanol and 
not water and thus by not impacting the reaction equilibrium, lead to higher yields.

3.1.1. Optimising the reaction conditions 
In the process of optimising the reaction conditions, two criteria were established. The yield of quinoline 
was chosen as the first criterion; it is easily discernible from thorough GC analysis and reportable in 
scientific publications. The second criterion chosen for the evaluation of the reaction conditions was the 
propensity to form unwanted side products. 
It was thought, that a weaker base would assist in limiting the number and amount of side products. 
Eventually, phosphate was selected along with potassium and sodium hydride. It is known that using 
phosphate tends to limit aldol condensations due to the relative weakness of the base strength and it has 
been common to use hydrides as bases for quinoline synthesis in the literature (Padwa & Pearson, 2002). 
Along with altering the base and the alcohol substrate, the reduced catalyst was also tried. Since the active 
catalyst does not contain the Schiff base functionalities of the salen ligand, it was thought that the salan 
ligand might be an effective catalyst for these reactions as well.

The choice of using an imine formation instead of a quinoline synthesis to demonstrate the efficacy of the

synthesis and if perhaps larger alkyl substituents are more deactivating to the catalytic cycle. 
It is important to note, that the N-C bond in the alkylated group is labile under the reaction conditions. It 
was shown by TLC of the reaction mixture after the end of the reaction that both salen, salan and Me2salan 
ligands were present. This points to a more complicated scenario than originally theorised. If the alkylated 
catalyst somehow is dealkylated over the course of the reaction, it no longer follows that the catalytic 
activity seen in the reaction is caused by the alkylated catalyst. It is clear, that further research is required 
to promote the understanding of these catalytic systems. 
Another key point to note is that no attempts were made at collecting the formed hydrogen gas or run 
reactions with a hydrogen scavenger to confirm a dehydrogenative pathway. Optimally, that would have 
been attempted as well. The experiments conform to the expectations to the catalysts activities and the 
products that should form. The elimination of any aldol products after using phosphate as base certainly 
indicates, that Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley-Oppenauer chemistry is not a significant contributor to the 
synthesis of quinolines developed over the course of this project. 
It is unfortunate, that higher yields quinolines could not be achieved. It is thought, that adding a small 
amount of molecular sieves to the reaction will increase the yield significantly. In the work the group has 
done with several different catalytic dehydrogenation schemes, 4 Å molecular sieves generally increase 
yields of processes that involve the loss of water, of which the quinoline synthesis unquestionably is one. 
The use of sieves was precluded early on as the long reaction time in a high-temperature, highly caustic 
reaction mixture degraded the sieves into a useless sludge and even damaged the glassware over the 
course of several runs. But by using phosphate, a significantly weaker base, this may no longer be a 
problem. No pitting was observed in fresh glassware used for phosphate reactions and glass and molecular 
sieves are chemically similar, so it is possible that the addition of molecular sieves to the synthetic protocol 
for the dehydrogenative synthesis of quinolines from alcohols will increase yields appreciably.

4. Conclusion 
Iron is among the most abundant, accessible and cheap metals on Earth - the development of efficient, 
robust and adaptable iron catalysts is imperative. The acceptorless dehydrogenation of alcohols is a highly 
atom-economical and versatile transformation that affords a more environmentally benign synthetic 
approach to a broad spectrum of organic compounds. 
Dehydrogenation reactions are clearly a powerful tool to synthetic chemists as it opens up entirely new 
avenues of functional group conversion and reactions. As alcohols are such ubiquitous substrates and are 
often easy to manufacture and often from renewable sources, in the future, the green and atom-
economical acceptorless dehydrogenation of renewable alcohols, like sugars or carbohydrates could play 
an important part in manufacture, storage and transport of hydrogen that would change the energy sector 
worldwide. 
Over the course of this project, the formation of substituted quinolines from 2-aminobenzyl alcohol has 
been investigated and the reaction conditions optimised significantly, leading to a simple catalytic system 
that yields quinolines of good purity in decent yields. 
Quinolines are a good target moiety to improve synthetic protocols for as they are important substrates in 
the pharmaceutical industry. Designing a cheap, efficient catalyst could potentially impact the cost of 
manufacture for quinoline-based drugs significantly when compared to current-generation iridium or 
platinum catalysis. This is especially true since many of the current quinoline-based drugs are used in the 
treatment of diseases on the increase. 
This report presents syntheses of quinolines in low to moderate yields. It is clear, that further research is 
needed to improve the iron-catalysed dehydrogenative synthesis of quinolines. An extension of the 
substrate scope to encompass more alcohols and more varied substituents in the aromatic rings would 
yield a greater variety of quinolines. The continued amendment of the reaction conditions should also be 
considered. Particularly the addition of 4 Å molecular sieves holds promise for increasing yields as their 
hygroscopic properties should drive the chemical equilibrium towards formation of greater quantities of 
quinolines, while not imparting the risk of new side reactions.

5. Experimental 
5.1. General experimental methods  
All chemicals and solvents used were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich and used without further purification, 
with the following exceptions: Both Fe-catalysts were synthesised by Ph.D. candidate Fabrizio Monda 
before this project was started and purity was confirmed with LCMS before use. Mesitylene was degassed 
by freeze-pump-thaw cycling and dried and stored over molecular sieves (4 Å) while dry THF was obtained 
as needed by using a PureSolv™ MD7 Solvent Purification System. The water content of the solvents was 
measured on a Karl-Fischer apparatus before use. All experiments run under N2-atmosphere were carried 
out under a nitrogen flow using Schlenk-line techniques. 
NMR spectra were recorded in appropriate deuterated solvents on a Bruker Ascend 400 MHz 
spectrometer. GC-MS analyses were carried out on a Shimadzu GCMS-QP2010S instrument fitted with an 
Equity 5, 30m×0.25mm×0.25μm column. Mass spectra were recorded using ESI with TOF detection using 
helium as the carrier gas. 
Yields of reactions were measured by GC-MS using tetradecane present in the reaction mixture as an 
internal standard. Tetradecane standard calibration curves were made by Ph.D candidate Fabrizio Monda. 
Sample preparation consisted of sampling a few drops of the reaction mixture, filtering through a syringe 
filter and diluting with diethyl ether to 1 ml sample volume. 
 
5.1.1. General procedure for quinoline synthesis  
Catalyst (0.05 mmol) and base (1 mmol) and 2-aminobenzyl alcohol (1 mmol, 123 mg) was placed in an 
oven-dried tube along with a stir bar. The tube was placed in a Radley carousel, a vacuum was applied and 
the tube was then filled with nitrogen gas. This cycle of alternating evacuation with nitrogen refill was 
repeated three times to sufficiently ensure removal of oxygen. Anhydrous, degassed mesitylene (2 ml), 
tetradecane (0.1 ml, 0.4 mmol as internal standard) and secondary alcohol (1 mmol) were added by 
syringe, and the reaction was refluxed with stirring under a flow of nitrogen for 24-96 hrs. 
 
5.1.1.1. Optimised reaction conditions for quinoline synthesis 
Fe(III)(salen)Cl (0.05 mmol, 20.6 mg) and potassium phosphate (1 mmol, 212 mg) and 2-aminobenzyl 
alcohol (1 mmol, 123 mg) was placed in an oven-dried tube along with a stir bar. The tube was placed in a 
Radley carousel, a vacuum was applied and the tube was then filled with nitrogen gas. This cycle of 
alternating evacuation with nitrogen refill was repeated three times to sufficiently ensure removal of 
oxygen. Anhydrous, degassed mesitylene (2 ml), tetradecane (0.1 ml, 0.4 mmol as internal standard) and 
secondary alcohol (1 mmol) were added by syringe, and the reaction was refluxed with stirring under a flow 
of nitrogen for 24-96 hrs.

5.1.2. Procedures for ligand synthesis 
5.1.2.1. (1R,2R)-N,N’-Bis(salicylidene)-1,2-cyclohexanediamine (salen) 
  
(1R, 2R)-(+)-1,2-diaminocyclohexane L-tartrate (2.0 g, 7.6 mmol) and K2CO3 (1.05 g, 7.6 mmol) was dissolved 
in water (5 ml) and stirred until complete dissolution. Then methanol (40 ml) was added. The reaction 
mixture was heated to reflux and a solution of salicylaldehyde (1.6 ml, 15.2 mmol) in methanol (16 ml) was 
added in portions over the course of 30 min. The reaction mixture was refluxed for an additional 2 hrs, after 
which the reaction should be complete. Reaction progression was checked with TLC (20% diethyl 
ether/heptane). The reaction mixture was then cooled to room temperature and concentrated in vacuo. 
The residue was dissolved in ethyl acetate (30 ml), washed with water (2 x 10 ml), dried over sodium 
sulphate and concentrated in vacuo to afford the desired ligand as a yellow viscous oil. 
Yield: 2.426 g (98.5%). NMR(CDCl3) data are in accordance with literature values (Balsells, Carroll, & Walsh, 
2001; Samuelsen et al., 2019). 
 
5.1.2.2. (1R,2R)-N,N’-Bis(2-hydroxybenzyl)-1,2-cyclohexanediamine (salan) 
 
Sodium borohydride (605 mg, 16 mmol) was added over 30 min to a solution of (1R,2R)-N,N’- 
bis(salicylidene)-1,2-cyclohexanediamine (2.4 g, 7.5 mmol) in methanol (30 ml) at room temperature and 
the reaction mixture was then stirred under reflux for 1 hr. After cooling to room temperature, water (30 
ml) was added and the mixture was extracted with dichloromethane (3 x 30 ml). The combined organic 
extracts were dried over anhydrous sodium sulphate and evaporated to dryness. The residue was purified 
by column chromatography (1% dichloromethane/heptane) to afford the desired ligand as a slightly 
yellowish solid. 
Yield: 2.228 g (91%). NMR(CDCl3) data are in accordance with literature values (Samuelsen et al., 2019). 1H 
and 13C spectra are shown in appendix E.

5.1.2.3. (1R,2R)-N,N’-Bis(2-hydroxybenzyl)-N,N’-dimethyl-1,2-cyclohexanediamine  
 
Under nitrogen atmosphere, 3.6 ml of n-BuLi (2.5 M solution in hexane, 9 mmol) was added to a solution of 
salan ligand (650 mg, 2 mmol) in 15 ml dry THF slowly at 0 °C. The yellow solution is allowed to warm to 
room temperature while stirring. Iodomethane (0.59 ml, 9 mmol) was added dropwise and stirring 
continued for 2 hrs. Upon completion, the reaction was quenched with water (15 ml) and extracted with 
dichloromethane (5 x 15 ml). The organic extracts were combined and dried over anhydrous sodium 
sulphate. Evaporation of the solvent in vacuo gave the crude product, which was further purified by flash 
chromatography (20% ethyl acetate/heptane) to afford the desired ligand as a white solid. 
Yield: 452 mg (63%). NMR(CDCl3) data are in accordance with literature values (Dai et al., 2005). 
 
5.1.2.4. (1R,2R)-N,N’-Bis(2-hydroxybenzyl)-N,N’-diethyl-1,2-cyclohexanediamine  
 
Under nitrogen atmosphere, 1 ml of n-BuLi (2.5 M solution in hexane, 2.48 mmol) was added to a solution 
of salan ligand (200 mg, 0.6 mmol) in 5 ml dry THF slowly at 0 °C. The yellow solution is allowed to warm to 
room temperature while stirring. Iodoethane (0.1 ml, 1.24 mmol) was added dropwise and stirring 
continued for 2 hrs. Upon completion, the reaction was quenched with water (5 ml) and extracted with 
dichloromethane (5 x 5 ml). The organic extracts were combined and dried over anhydrous sodium 
sulphate. Evaporation of the solvent in vacuo gave the crude product, which was further purified by flash 
chromatography (25% ethyl acetate/heptane) to afford the desired ligand as an off-white solid. 
Yield: 23.8 mg (10%). Note: It is likely that increasing the amount of iodoethane to a x4 eq. excess in the 
reaction will increase yield without significant alkylation of the phenol groups (Dai et al., 2005).

5.1.2.5. (1R,2R)-N,N’-Bis(2-hydroxybenzyl)-N,N’-dibenzyl-1,2-cyclohexanediamine  
 
Under nitrogen atmosphere, 3.2 ml of n-BuLi (2.5 M solution in hexane, 8 mmol) was added to a solution of 
salan ligand (600 mg, 1.84 mmol) in 15 ml dry THF slowly at 0 °C. The yellow solution is allowed to warm to 
room temperature while stirring. Benzyl bromide (0.45 ml, 3.8 mmol) was added dropwise and stirring 
continued for 2 hrs. Upon completion, the reaction was quenched with water (15 ml) and extracted with 
dichloromethane (5 x 15 ml). The organic extracts were combined and dried over anhydrous sodium 
sulphate. Evaporation of the solvent in vacuo gave the crude product, which was further purified by flash 
chromatography (1% ethyl acetate/heptane) to afford the desired ligand as an off-white solid. 
Yield: 749.7 mg (80.4%). Note: It is important to control the amount of benzyl bromide in the reaction 
rigorously, as excess benzyl bromide will yield significant alkylation of the phenol groups. The first attempt 
at benzylating the ligand led to benzylation at both N-Hs and both phenols. 
 
5.1.3. General procedure for the synthesis of iron complexes 
Equimolar quantities of FeCl3 hexahydrate and ligand in EtOH (40 ml per mmol ligand) were refluxed for 2 h 
in the presence of air. The reaction mixture was allowed to cool to room temperature and concentrated 
under reduced pressure yielding a dark solid, which was dried under vacuum on a Schlenk-line to yield the 
desired product. Pure according to TLC using 10% EtOAc/heptane as eluent. 
 
5.1.3.1.  (1R,2R)-N,N’-Bis(2-hydroxybenzyl)-N,N’-dimethyl-1,2-cyclohexanediaminoiron(III) 
chloride 
 
Following the general procedure for synthesis of iron complexes using ferric chloride, the desired catalyst 
was obtained as dark violet, fine crystals. LCMS analysis was run to confirm catalyst molar mass. 
Yield: 245.2 mg (95.6%). Note: The yield is effectively quantitative, in that mechanical losses are the only 
significant source of lost yield.
