Abstract
As the global transition to low­carbon energy accelerates, green hydrogen is increasingly
recognized as a crucial facilitator for integrating renewable energy sources (RES) and
facilitating deep decarbonization. By utilizing renewable electricity, hydrogen production
through electrolysis can reduce variability in the power grid and improve energy inde­
pendence, making it suitable for integration into both connected and autonomous energy
systems. Grid­connected systems benefit from energy dispatch and revenue optimization
through the grid. In contrast, off­grid systems offer strong independence and flexible de­
ployment, especially in remote areas or locations with limited grid access. Alkaline Elec­
trolyzers (ALK) and Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzers (PEM), two major elec­
trolysis technologies, each exhibit distinct advantages in terms of cost­effectiveness, op­
erational flexibility, and energy efficiency. Although their combined application has been
explored in some studies, large­scale simulations and systematic modeling efforts remain
limited.
This study investigates an off­grid green hydrogen production system and proposes an
integrated modeling and simulation framework incorporating both ALK and PEM elec­
trolyzers. Implemented in Python, the framework simulates the dynamic response of both
electrolyzers under startup and steady­state operation. To handle the intermittency of
renewable energy sources, a tailored power allocation method is implemented, utilizing
actual wind and solar generation data as inputs. A high­capacity system rated at 300 MW
is constructed, capturing the dynamic operational characteristics of the electrolyzers with­
out relying on fixed power assumptions.
The simulation results include hydrogen yield and the calculated Levelized Cost of Hydro­
gen (LCOH). A multi­objective genetic algorithm is employed to simultaneously optimize
LCOH, renewable power curtailment, and electrolyzer shutdown frequency. Through this
process, a set of Pareto­optimal solutions is obtained, enabling the identification of the
optimal capacity configuration between ALK and PEM electrolyzers. Furthermore, sys­
tem performance is assessed under three distinct renewable input conditions—wind­only,
solar­only, and combined wind­solar—to provide reference for the seasonal operational
scheduling of electrolyzer systems in engineering practice.
The results provide the optimal capacity configuration of the hybrid electrolyzer under spe­
cific wind­solar conditions, offering reference and validation methods for the engineering
practice of hybrid electrolyzer deployment. This study also validates the advantages of
the proposed two­level power allocation logic, as well as the superiority of utilizing a com­
bination of wind and solar resources over relying on a single RES.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Background
Cutting carbon emissions represents a straightforward and impactful approach to ad­
dressing the increasing global focus on environmental sustainability. Various countries
and regions have proposed specific development plans to gradually reduce emissions
within their jurisdictions. Since the introduction of the European Green Deal (EGD) in
2019, the European Union has enacted a series of legislative measures, such as the Eu­
ropean Climate Law—effective from 2021—and the “Fit for 55” package [1]. The objective
of these initiatives is to cut carbon emissions by 55% by 2030 and to reach net­zero emis­
sions by 2050 [2]. As of 2025, these policies are gradually entering the implementation
stage [3].
Recognized for its leadership in climate policy, Denmark adopted the legally binding Cli­
mate Act in 2020. The legislation sets ambitious targets: reducing greenhouse gas emis­
sions by 50–54% by 2025 and achieving a 70% reduction by 2030, with the overarching
aim of reaching climate neutrality by 2045 [4, 5]. Supported by its advanced deployment
of onshore and offshore wind and solar power—expected to exceed 100% of national
electricity demand by 2030—Denmark launched its national Power­to­X strategy in 2021.
The strategy aims to install 4–6 GW of electrolysis capacity to produce green hydrogen for
export and use in hard­to­abate sectors [6, 7, 8]. As one of the world’s leading economies,
China outlined explicit carbon reduction objectives in its 14th Five­Year Plan for National
Economic and Social Development and the Long­Range Objectives for 2035, published
in 2021 [9]. The plan designates 2030 as the target year for reaching peak carbon emis­
sions, after which a steady reduction is to be pursued [10].
The Renewable Energy Statistics 2024 released by the International Renewable Energy
Agency (IRENA) [11] indicates that the global share of renewable energy has experienced
a significant increase from 2013 to 2023. Consequently, enhancing the utilization effi­
ciency of renewables and driving down related costs have emerged as central priorities.
Power­to­X (P2X) technologies represent a vital approach to harnessing renewables by
transforming wind, solar, and other variable energy sources into hydrogen or alternative
energy carriers collectively referred to as “X” [12, 13, 14]. Hydrogen is considered a
promising solution, as it facilitates the buffering and delivery of variable RES. Hydrogen is
commonly categorized into green, blue, and gray types based on the production pathway
and related carbon footprint. Green hydrogen is generated through electrolysis of water
using electricity from renewable sources, resulting in minimal or zero carbon emissions.
Blue hydrogen is produced from natural gas, with carbon dioxide emissions partially mit­
igated through carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies. In contrast, gray hydro­
gen originates from fossil fuel reforming without emission mitigation, leading to significant
1
==PAGE:14==
greenhouse gas output. Other classifications, such as brown and pink hydrogen, are also
defined based on different energy sources [15, 16].
Green hydrogen stands out as a zero­emission energy carrier, with no carbon emissions
during either production or use, making it highly relevant for decarbonization. In con­
trast to hydrogen produced from fossil fuels, green hydrogen provides several benefits,
including carbon­free generation, operational flexibility, and wide­ranging application po­
tential [15, 16]. It can be used not only in transportation and industrial sectors, but also as
a long­duration energy storage medium to address supply­demand imbalances caused
by renewable energy variability [17, 18].
However, the variable and uncertain nature of renewable energy sources, driven by fac­
tors such as wind speed fluctuations and changes in solar irradiance, leads to fluctu­
ating power outputs. When directly used for electrolysis, these fluctuations can cause
frequent start­stop operations, reduced efficiency, and increased equipment wear. En­
suring efficient and stable hydrogen production under such conditions remains a major
challenge [19].
Hydrogen production configurations are commonly divided into grid­connected and off­
grid systems, depending on their deployment context. Grid­connected systems operate
in coordination with the power grid and are suitable for areas with mature infrastructure.
In contrast, off­grid systems provide more independence and flexibility, making them ideal
for remote regions rich in renewable resources but lacking grid access. These systems
enable effective use of site­specific renewable energy while minimizing grid extension and
dispatch­related costs, contributing to the progress of the energy transition [20, 21, 22].
This study focuses on off­grid green hydrogen systems, exploring hybrid electrolyzer con­
figurations and power allocation strategies using wind and solar inputs. The goal is to
provide a methodological foundation for optimizing hydrogen production in highly variable
renewable energy environments.
1.2
Green Hydrogen Production Technologies
Among the available electrolyzer technologies, Alkaline Water Electrolyzers (ALK) have
seen widespread industrial deployment, attributed to their high level of technological de­
velopment and comparatively low capital requirements [23]. Proton Exchange Membrane
(PEM) electrolyzers have also been extensively studied and applied; however, their higher
cost compared to ALK limits their large­scale deployment [24, 25]. Solid Oxide Electrolyz­
ers (SOEC) operate at elevated temperatures (typically above 700 °C), making their ap­
plication scenarios more complex and increasing installation and maintenance costs [26].
Anion Exchange Membrane (AEM) electrolyzer technology is still under development and
has not yet reached maturity [27]. Therefore, both SOEC and AEM remain at the stage of
small­scale demonstration projects [28, 29]. Table 1.1 presents a comparative overview
of various electrolyzer technologies.

Although ALK offer advantages in cost­effectiveness and technological maturity, they op­
erate at relatively low current densities, resulting in larger system sizes. In addition, ALK
exhibits limited load­following capability, with dynamic response times typically ranging
from several seconds to minutes. When integrated with highly variable RES, ALK may
exhibit lower energy utilization efficiency and lead to resource wastage [30]. Further­
more, to prevent safety issues such as uneven gas evolution, membrane damage, and
hydrogen­oxygen ratio fluctuations, ALK must operate above a minimum load threshold—
typically not less than 20% of the rated power [31]. Its slow startup time further restricts
its range of applications.
In contrast, PEM electrolyzers feature high operating current density, compact structure,
and fast startup response, making them more suitable for integration with intermittent
RES [32, 33]. However, their higher cost and shorter operational lifespan result in lower
overall economic competitiveness compared to ALK [34, 35, 36, 37].
This study investigates the optimal capacity distribution between ALK and PEM electrolyz­
ers and proposes a control strategy for their coordinated integration within a unified elec­
trolysis system.
1.3
Challenges of Renewable Utilization
RESs are inherently intermittent and uncertain, with their output significantly influenced
by natural conditions. Their power generation fluctuates considerably across various time
scales, including minutes, hours, days, and seasons. Such variability introduces difficul­
ties in maintaining supply–demand equilibrium and undermines the stability of the overall
system.
Although renewable energy offers low generation costs, part of its output is frequently
curtailed to preserve system stability. With the growing share of wind and solar in the en­
ergy mix, the amount of unused or curtailed renewable power continues to increase [38].
Furthermore, when wind or solar output changes rapidly, the electrolyzer system cannot
always respond in time due to inherent parameter constraints, resulting in energy waste.
In green hydrogen production systems, directly powering electrolyzers with renewable en­
ergy leads to frequent start­stop operations. However, frequent cycling can negatively im­
pact the performance and stability of the electrolyzer, causing reduced efficiency, degra­
dation, and equipment fatigue [39, 40, 41]. In addition, since electrolyzers require time to
start and stop, some electricity may be inevitably wasted during these transitions, reduc­
3
==PAGE:16==
ing system availability and economic efficiency. Therefore, this project will also consider
the number of shutdowns as a key factor.
Although wind and solar power appear complementary—wind power performing better
at night or in winter, and solar power contributing more during the day, each source still
experiences periods of high variability under changing environmental conditions, making it
difficult to maintain a stable power supply. Thus, this project will simulate three scenarios:
wind power alone, solar power alone, and a hybrid wind­solar system. To assess the
effectiveness of the hybrid strategy, comparisons will be made across different cases in
terms of hydrogen output, levelized cost of hydrogen (LCOH), energy curtailment rate,
and overall system stability.
1.4
Hybrid Electrolyzer Systems
Various studies have explored the improvement of hydrogen production efficiency and
economic viability in ALK and PEM electrolyzers through control optimization [42, 43, 44,
45]. Shi et al. [45] examined the role of capacity sizing in determining system performance
and demonstrated that applying PI control can enhance the economic aspects of ALK
electrolyzers.
Other works [43, 44, 42, 46, 47, 48] have investigated control strategies for optimizing
PEM operation in renewable energy systems. For instance, Qiu et al. [48] proposed a
hybrid approach combining forecasting and real­time control: wind power fluctuations
are predicted before the scheduling period, while real­time control is applied during the
scheduling phase to optimize power response. Results showed that predictive control
significantly improves wind power utilization. Another study [42] successfully integrated
supercapacitors with PEM electrolyzers to mitigate wind power fluctuations and enhance
electrolysis efficiency.
These findings suggest that ALK and PEM, as two mature electrolyzer technologies, offer
distinct advantages and can complement each other to some extent. As a result, recent
studies have explored the joint operation of ALK and PEM systems [49, 42], aiming to
leverage the cost benefits of ALK and the performance advantages of PEM.
However, few existing studies have modeled the full operational stages of electrolyzers,
including startup and shutdown processes, while incorporating long­term real­world wind
and solar data for simulation. In addition, few studies have investigated hydrogen pro­
duction systems that operate entirely on renewable energy sources (RES), where all
subsystems—including balance­of­plant (BoP) components—are fully powered by wind
and solar energy. Comprehensive evaluations that simultaneously consider key indica­
tors such as economic performance, curtailment rate, and number of shutdowns are also
scarce.
Therefore, this project aims to conduct detailed simulations under relatively accurate mod­
els, taking into account multiple evaluation metrics. The objective is to determine optimal

1.5
Research Objectives and Contributions
The primary aim of this study is to develop a high­fidelity and dynamically adaptive model
for green hydrogen production in a completely off­grid configuration. The system exclu­
sively utilizes wind and solar energy and integrates both ALK and PEM electrolyzers for
joint hydrogen generation. The model fully considers the time­based changes in renew­
able energy and includes detailed modeling of different operating stages of the electrolyz­
ers, such as cold start, hot standby, minimum load operation, and frequent on­off cycles.
The key objectives and contributions of this study are outlined below:
1. Complete dynamic techno­economic simulation model:
A hydrogen production model with minute­level time resolution and long­term simu­
lation ability is built. It provides an accurate representation of the off­grid operational
behavior of ALK and PEM electrolyzers. The model separates the power used for
electrolysis and for the BoP systems, and clearly simulates energy use and hydro­
gen production at different operating stages. It supports real wind and solar data
input and is suitable for practical engineering design and deployment.
2. Batch hydrogen yield calculation based on time series:
An efficient and precise approach is proposed for estimating hydrogen production,
utilizing fitted current–voltage (I–V) characteristics, Faraday efficiency, and the re­
versible cell voltage equation.
It supports batch calculation and analysis under
changing power inputs.
3. Stepwise power allocation strategy:
A two­level power allocation method is proposed. First, it ensures that the stacks
meet their standby and minimum power needs. Then, it gradually increases the
power to each stack up to its maximum capacity, using the remaining power. This
helps reduce curtailment and improves system stability.
4. Multi­objective optimization for scheduling and capacity planning:
A four­objective optimization model is built using the NSGA­II algorithm. The vari­
ables are the number of ALK and PEM stacks. The four sub­objectives are:
• Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH);
• Total power curtailment;
• Number of ALK shutdowns;
• Number of PEM shutdowns.
5
==PAGE:18==
The model gives a set of Pareto­optimal solutions, which allows balancing between
cost, energy use, and system reliability.
5. Analysis of how energy mix affects system performance:
Three energy input cases—pure wind, pure solar, and mixed wind­solar—are stud­
ied through simulations. The model evaluates key results such as best capacity
sizes, curtailment, and stability, giving guidance for system design under different
renewable energy conditions.
6. Deeper understanding of off­grid dynamic operation:
This study focuses on the system’s behavior during dynamic changes and star­
tup/shutdown stages in off­grid conditions. This is different from traditional static
sizing methods. The work provides useful insights and engineering suggestions for
designing green hydrogen systems powered by variable RES.

Renewable Energy Input (wind and solar) Profiles
The SOLETE dataset from the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) [50] is employed
in this study. The dataset was recorded by the SYSLAB experimental facility and contains
15 months of meteorological and renewable energy data, covering the period from June

The dataset comprises measurements of air temperature, relative humidity, atmospheric
pressure, wind speed and direction, global horizontal irradiance (GHI), irradiance on a
tilted surface, and the active power generation from wind turbines and photovoltaic (PV)

3.1.3
Operating Modes of ALK Stack
The transition between switching­off and normal operation(NOR) of the ALK electrolyzer
involves multiple intermediate stages. To accurately estimate hydrogen production and
other key metrics over long time periods, this study implements a detailed operating state
transition logic.
First, the system initiates a nitrogen purging phase to remove residual gases and en­
sure a safe, inert internal environment. This process brings the electrolyzer into a cold
standby state (SBcold). During cold start­up, the system gradually increases temperature
and pressure until the designated operational thresholds are reached, at which point the
electrolyzer enters the NOR.
During NOR, if the available input power becomes insufficient, the system transitions to
21
==PAGE:34==
a warm standby state (SBwarm), in which key components are maintained in a ready con­
dition to enable rapid resumption of operation. However, if the input power drops below
the minimum load threshold, the electrolyzer initiates a switching­off sequence.
In the switching­off state (SWO), the system enters an irreversible deactivation process. It
cannot be restarted immediately and must first complete a full cooldown and switching­off
cycle before accepting a new start­up command.
This start–standby–switching­off mechanism ensures safe operation and effective lifetime
management of the electrolyzer system. The complete mode transition logic is illustrated
in Figure 3.5.Each operating mode is discussed in detail in this section.
Off to Cold Standby State
This state represents the first transitional phase as the electrolyzer moves from an off
condition into operational readiness. During this stage, the system is powered on and
nitrogen purging is initiated to remove residual gases and ensure a safe and inert internal
environment. Simultaneously, heating of the electrolyzer begins [61]. The required power
in this stage is minimal [61] and is set to 1% of the total rated power in this study. The
transition is completed when two conditions are met: (1) the available input power exceeds
the cold standby threshold, and (2) the nitrogen purge timer reaches its preset duration.
In this study, the purge time is set to 30 minutes [61]. These conditions correspond to
Condition (2): power ≥cold standby power & timer done?. Upon fulfillment, the system
enters the cold standby state SBcold.
Cold Standby State
SBcold is a low­power steady state, indicating that the system has been activated but
electrolysis has not yet begun. It maintains internal temperature and pressure at control­
lable levels while minimizing energy consumption. The power consumption in this state
is fixed at 5% of the rated electrolysis power [62]. If the input power increases beyond
the minimum working threshold (Condition (3): power > min working power?), the system
transitions into the Cold Start­up phase to begin ramp­up.
However, if the input power drops below the standby threshold, or if the system remains
in cold standby beyond the allowed duration (Condition (7): power < standby power or
standby timer reached?), it enters the SWO state and begins safe shutdown procedures.
Cold Start­up State
Cold Start­up is the key transitional phase between SBcold and NOR. In this state, the
system gradually increases temperature and pressure; parameters such as electrolyte
temperature and stack pressure progressively approach nominal operating ranges.
Upon entering this state from SBcold, the input power rapidly rises to the minimum working
power within approximately 300 ms, as reported in [61]. Subsequently, due to thermal and
pressure limitations, the power is ramped up at a limited rate—no more than 2% of the
rated electrolysis power per minute, as derived from [61].

NOR State
NOR denotes the system’s main hydrogen production mode. In this mode, the supplied
power is kept above the minimum operational limit (set here as at least 15% of the rated
ALK electrolysis power [63]). The Faraday efficiency is considered constant, approxi­
mately 0.98.
The system permits a maximum ramp­up rate of 10% and a ramp­down rate of 20% of
the rated electrolysis power per minute [64]. If the power input drops below the minimum
working threshold but remains above the standby level (Condition (5): standby power <
power < minimum working power?), the system transitions to SB_warm, allowing thermal
conditions to be maintained for rapid recovery.
If power drops further below the standby threshold (Condition (6): power < standby
power?), the system enters SWO and begins the shutdown process.
Warm Standby State
SB_warm serves as a buffer state to handle short­term power fluctuations. The system
suspends hydrogen production while maintaining temperature and pressure, enabling a
rapid return to NOR once power is restored. The power consumption in this state is fixed
at 5% of the rated electrolysis power [62].
If power rises back above the minimum working threshold (no longer satisfying Condi­
tion (5)), the system returns directly to NOR. Conversely, if the power remains below the
standby threshold or if the standby time exceeds the maximum allowable limit (18 hours
in this study), the system initiates a shutdown sequence (Condition (7)) and enters SWO.
Switching­off State
SWO is an irreversible transitional state indicating that the electrolyzer is executing a safe
shutdown procedure. During this process, electrochemical reactions are halted, current
is cut off, and system pressure and temperature gradually decrease to safe levels.
Once the shutdown timer reaches its preset duration (set to 5 minutes in this study), the
shutdown is considered complete (Condition (8): reached switching­off time?), and the
system reverts to the Off state, completing a full operational cycle.
While in SWO, the electrolyzer cannot accept any start­up commands. It must complete
the shutdown process before transitioning again into the Off to SBcold sequence.

During normal operation, the PEM stack dynamically adjusts its load in response to fluc­

Cold Start­up State
Cold Start-up serves as the transition phase from the Off state to Normal Operation.
During this phase, the stack power increases at a controlled rate not exceeding 10%
of the rated electrolysis power per minute [32] to mitigate thermal stress on the system
components.
Unlike ALK systems, which require nitrogen purging and preliminary heating in Cold Standby,
the PEM system has a much shorter preparation time [32, 73, 74]. For modeling purposes,
this duration is neglected, and the system is assumed to begin ramp­up directly based on
the available input power. This characteristic makes the PEM stack particularly suitable
for highly dynamic renewable energy scenarios.
As in the ALK model, the progress of the Cold Start­up is evaluated by integrating the
stack power over time until the required thermal and pressure conditions are assumed to
be met (Condition (2): power condition reached). During the start­up phase, the system
transitions to the SWO state whenever the input power drops below the minimum operating
threshold.
Normal Operation State
The NOR state corresponds to the normal operating mode of the PEM electrolyzer. In this
mode, the stack operates under stable thermal and pressure conditions, with input power
exceeding the minimum working threshold. In this study, the threshold is set at 0% of the
rated electrolysis power, meaning that the input power must surpass the BoP demand.
Compared to ALK, the PEM stack responds much more rapidly to power variations. Pre­
vious studies have reported PEM ramping capabilities of up to 10% per second [75] [76],
which is significantly faster than the maximum power variation in this study (100% per 5
minutes). Therefore, no ramping limit is imposed in the NOR phase.
Additionally, PEM electrolyzers are capable of operating under overload conditions. In
this study, the allowable operational power range is set between 0% and 160% of the
rated electrolysis power [73, 77]. However, extended overload can accelerate membrane
degradation and increase the likelihood of side reactions, thereby shortening the system’s
operational lifespan [78].
To mitigate this, overload operation is limited to a maximum duration of 30 minutes per
cycle. If this threshold is exceeded, the stack power is restricted to no more than 100%
of the rated electrolysis power. The system can resume overload operation only after it
has remained within the non­overload range for an additional 30 minutes.
If the input power drops below the minimum working threshold but remains above the
standby level (Condition (3)), the system transitions to SB_warm. If power continues to fall
33
==PAGE:46==
below the standby threshold (Condition (4)), it enters the SWO.
Warm Standby State
SB_warm is a thermal standby mode used during short periods of insufficient input power.
In this mode, the stack halts hydrogen production but maintains internal temperature and
pressure. The power consumption is fixed at 2% of the rated total power [79]. This state
significantly reduces the recovery time required to return to full operation.
If the system remains in standby without sufficient power input, or if the standby duration
exceeds the allowed limit (6 hours [79] in this study), it transitions to the SWO to begin
shutdown procedures.
Switching­off State
SWO represents the shutdown sequence of the PEM electrolyzer. In this mode, the system
gradually reduces stack power and controls the decline in temperature and pressure to
ensure material safety. This process is irreversible—once initiated, the system cannot be
restarted until shutdown is fully completed.
In this study, the shutdown duration is set to 2 minutes [80]. However, due to the system’s
temporal resolution of 5 minutes, this behavior is modeled identically to the ALK shutdown
procedure. Upon reaching the defined shutdown time (Condition (6): reached switching­
off time), the system transitions back to the Off state.

3.4
Power Allocation Strategy and Assumptions
In this study, a hybrid electrolyzer system is constructed using two types of electrolyzers—
ALK and PEM—each consisting of multiple stack­level controllable units. Therefore, de­
signing an effective power allocation strategy is essential. A two­layer allocation approach
is developed and evaluated. The impacts of three different strategies—uniform allocation,
single­layer allocation, and two­layer allocation—on system performance are compared
in this section.
3.4.1
Intra­stack Allocation Strategy
If the available power is equally distributed across all stacks, all units would participate
in power fluctuations simultaneously. This increases the number of shutdown events,
shortens equipment lifespan, and leads to a higher rate of power curtailment. Moreover,
during periods of low input power, the system may not have sufficient energy to maintain
standby conditions or reach the minimum operating power for all stacks, resulting in mass
38
==PAGE:51==
shutdowns or extended standby phases where no hydrogen is produced.
To mitigate these issues, a priority­based intra­stack allocation strategy is implemented:
1. Power is first assigned to activate as many stacks as possible by providing standby
power: 5% of rated total power for ALK and 2% for PEM.
2. If additional power remains, it is sequentially allocated to these activated stacks to
bring them up to the minimum working power: 15% of rated electrolysis power for
ALK and 0% for PEM.
3. Any remaining power is then equally distributed among the active stacks until they
reach their maximum operational power: 100% of rated total power for ALK and
160% for PEM.
3.4.2
Inter­electrolyzer Allocation Strategy
If power is distributed between ALK and PEM stacks solely in proportion to their installed
capacity, the operational inflexibility of ALK—such as its longer startup time and limited
ramp rate—can result in substantial power curtailment. This underutilizes the PEM units,
which are designed to complement ALK under dynamic power conditions.
To address this, a two­step inter­electrolyzer allocation strategy is proposed:
1. Based on the current total available power, the system evaluates ALK’s state and
ramping constraints to determine a feasible power reference for ALK. This reference
power is then allocated to the ALK group.
2. The remaining power is assigned to the PEM group as its total input power.

• Equal distribution: Power is allocated proportionally to the capacity of each elec­

• Single­layer allocation:

• Two­layer allocation: Both inter­electrolyzer and intra­stack allocation strategies

current is derived from the voltage–power relationship, providing a basis for subsequent

4.3
Simulation­based Evaluation Procedure
In this study, a Python­based simulation model is developed to evaluate the techno­
economic performance of different ALK­to­PEM system configurations. This model forms
the core evaluation module used within the multi­objective optimization framework. For
each configuration, defined by the number of ALK and PEM stacks, the model processes
the renewable power data to calculate key performance indicators, including LCOH, hy­
drogen yield, and curtailment rate. The detailed evaluation procedure is illustrated in the
flowchart shown in Figure 4.1.
The overall calculation process is structured as follows:
1. Priority­based Power Allocation
At each time step, all available renewable power is first allocated to the ALK system.
Any remaining power, after ALK consumption, is then allocated to PEM.
2. Overload Protection and State Transitions
Overload protection is applied to prevent stacks from operating above their rated
capacity for extended periods. Additionally, based on input power and stack status
(e.g., Off, SBcold, Cold Start­up, NOR), state transitions are simulated. This step
determines actual power consumption and records stack shutdown events.
3. Reference Power Calculation and Operational Constraints
For each electrolyzer type, the total allocated power is distributed across stacks
based on the number of units, rated power, and boundary conditions such as mini­
mum and maximum allowable operating power. This ensures that each stack oper­
ates within its permissible range, avoiding underload or overload.
4. Hydrogen Production Calculation
Hydrogen yield is computed at each time step based on the actual operating power
and stack state. The model accounts for current density, stack efficiency, Faradaic
efficiency, and temperature effects, enabling accurate estimation of hydrogen out­
put.
5. Curtailment Rate Evaluation
At each time step, unused power is recorded as curtailed energy. The curtailment
rate is calculated by comparing this value against the total available input. Both
instantaneous and cumulative curtailment rates are evaluated to assess system uti­
lization and flexibility.
6. Cost Evaluation
CAPEX, discounted replacement cost, and OPEX are computed separately. CAPEX
and replacement costs are annualized using the CRF, enabling a unified annual cost
representation.

4.4
Performance Indicators and Decision Variables
In the optimization framework, it is essential to clearly define the performance indicators
(i.e., objective functions) and the decision variables. In this study, four performance indi­
cators are selected as the objectives for multi­objective optimization, and two key system
design parameters are chosen as decision variables:
• LCOH: The average life­cycle cost per unit mass of hydrogen, expressed in USD/kg.
It reflects the economic performance of the system. A lower LCOH indicates higher
cost­effectiveness, and thus it is minimized in the optimization process.
• RES curtailment rate: The percentage of available renewable energy that cannot
be utilized due to capacity limits or operational constraints of the electrolyzers. This
indicator represents the efficiency of renewable energy utilization, and minimizing it
helps reduce wasted energy and improve overall system performance.
• Number of ALK shutdowns: The total number of times the ALK units undergo
shutdown during the optimization horizon.
Frequent start­up and shutdown cy­
cles induce thermal and mechanical stress on the system, accelerating degrada­
tion and increasing maintenance requirements. This indicator should therefore be
minimized.
• Number of PEM shutdowns: Similarly, this represents the total number of shut­
down events for the PEM units. Excessive switching negatively affects system dura­
bility and reliability and is undesirable from both a technical and economic perspec­
tive.
All the above performance indicators are treated as objectives to be minimized in the
optimization model.
The corresponding decision variables are:
• Number of ALK stacks (NALK): This integer variable determines the capacity of the
ALK portion of the system. It directly influences hydrogen production and CAPEX
48
==PAGE:61==
and is treated as a tunable parameter in the optimization.
• Number of PEM stacks (NPEM): This integer variable defines the capacity of the
PEM portion. Similar to NALK, it affects production potential and investment cost.
In this study, both decision variables are bounded within feasible engineering limits (e.g.,
NALK ∈[0, 100], NPEM ∈[0, 1000]) to ensure the search space remains realistic and com­
putationally tractable.
4.5
Multi­objective Optimization Method
To address the optimization problem involving the four aforementioned objectives, a multi­
objective optimization approach is adopted. Unlike single­objective optimization, multi­
objective optimization does not yield a unique global optimum. Instead, it generates a set
of trade­off solutions known as the Pareto­optimal set [86], in which no solution is strictly
better than another across all objectives.
In other words, within the Pareto set, improving one objective would necessarily deterio­
rate at least one of the others. These non­dominated solutions collectively form the so­
called Pareto front in the objective space, which represents the optimal trade­off boundary
of system performance. The Pareto front offers decision­makers a range of balanced and
feasible system designs.
To efficiently obtain the Pareto­optimal set, this study employs the well­established Non­
dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA­II). The original version, NSGA, was intro­
duced by Srinivas and Deb in 1994 [87], and was significantly improved by Deb et al. in
2002, resulting in NSGA­II [88], which remains one of the most widely used algorithms in
multi­objective optimization.
NSGA­II incorporates several key enhancements that offer distinct advantages over its
predecessor. First, it uses a fast non­dominated sorting method to rank individuals in the
population efficiently. Second, it introduces a crowding distance metric to maintain diver­
sity and ensure a well­distributed Pareto front. Third, it implements an elitism mechanism,
which preserves high­quality solutions by combining parent and offspring populations be­
fore selection. These mechanisms collectively improve convergence speed, solution di­
versity, and computational efficiency, making NSGA­II particularly well­suited for solving
engineering problems with conflicting objectives [88, 89].
Compared with classical scalarization techniques such as the weighted­sum method,
NSGA­II has the advantage of not requiring predefined weight coefficients among ob­
jectives. This feature makes it more robust and practical when optimizing systems with
heterogeneous and potentially incomparable objectives [89]. NSGA­II directly explores
the objective space to construct the Pareto front, offering system designers or investors
a comprehensive set of optimal configurations reflecting different trade­offs in cost, per­
formance, and operability.

4.6
Optimization Implementation in Python
To solve the multi­objective optimization problem described above, the NSGA­II algorithm
is implemented using the DEAP library in Python. The complete procedure includes the
following steps:
4.6.1
Problem Definition and Initialization
Each individual in the population is encoded by two integer­valued decision variables,
NALK and NPEM, representing the number of ALK and PEM stacks, respectively. Initial
individuals are randomly generated within predefined bounds. For reproducibility, a fixed
random seed is set (42 in this study).
4.6.2
Fitness Evaluation Function
Each candidate solution (i.e., a pair of NALK and NPEM values) is evaluated using the
system­level techno­economic simulation model described previously. The model out­
puts several indicators, from which four objective metrics are extracted as the fitness
vector (f1, f2, f3, f4): LCOH, curtailed RES ratio, ALK shutdown count, and PEM shut­
down count. If any returned value is invalid (e.g., NaN or infinity), it is penalized with a
large value to ensure meaningful comparisons among individuals.
4.6.3
NSGA­II Parameter Configuration
Key parameters of the genetic algorithm are set as follows: population size of 256, number
of generations 120, crossover probability 0.8, and mutation probability 0.25. A uniform
crossover operator for integer encoding is used to ensure moderate recombination, along
with uniform integer mutation to introduce diversity. These operators allow the offspring
to effectively explore the search space.
4.6.4
Evolution Process
The evolutionary loop proceeds as follows in each generation:
• Selection and Reproduction: Parents are selected based on non­dominated sort­
ing and crowding distance. Elitism is applied by retaining the best individuals across
generations.
• Crossover and Mutation: Selected parents undergo crossover with a probability
of 0.8 to generate new offspring, followed by mutation on individual variables with a
probability of 0.25 to maintain diversity.
• Fitness Evaluation: The newly generated offspring are evaluated by running the
simulation model. To reduce computation time, the evaluation is parallelized using
multiple CPU cores.
• Population Update: Parents and offspring are merged and sorted using non­dominated
sorting. The next generation is selected from the combined pool based on rank and
crowding distance, ensuring preservation of Pareto­optimal solutions and diversity
along the front.

4.6.5
Output and Post­Processing
After reaching the predefined number of generations, the final non­dominated population
is extracted as an approximation to the Pareto­optimal set. Each solution includes its
corresponding NALK and NPEM values, as well as the four performance metrics. This
solution set is exported (e.g., to a CSV file) for further visualization and analysis.
Through this process, a diverse set of hydrogen system configurations is obtained, each
representing a different trade­off among economic performance, RES utilization, and sys­
tem stability. These results provide a comprehensive basis for informed decision­making
in electrolyzer capacity design.

6
Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Summary of Contributions and Findings
This thesis focuses on off­grid green hydrogen systems and develops a hybrid modeling
and optimization framework combining ALK and PEM electrolyzers. Based on real wind
and solar data, a 300 MW­scale RES­powered hydrogen production system was simu­
lated in detail. Specifically, individual operational models for ALK and PEM electrolyzers
were established, along with a power allocation control strategy. An evaluation method for
LCOH and a multi­objective optimization model were also developed. The proposed op­
timization model yields a Pareto­optimal set of solutions, enabling a systematic trade­off
analysis among key performance metrics.
Simulation and optimization results indicate that the combination of wind–solar hybrid in­
put and ALK+PEM electrolysis shows clear advantages over systems relying on a single
energy source or a single electrolyzer type. Economically, the hybrid setup significantly
reduces LCOH, improving cost­effectiveness. In terms of operational stability, the com­
plementary nature of wind and solar energy results in smoother and more balanced power
input, reducing the number of on/off cycles. More electrolyzers are kept in the hydrogen
production state simultaneously, enhancing overall reliability. Moreover, proper power
allocation ensures that ALK and PEM units operate more frequently within their optimal
operating ranges, thereby mitigating overload and frequent start/stop operations, which
in turn helps extend system lifespan. In summary, this study demonstrates the superiority
of the hybrid strategy in terms of cost, stability, and durability compared to conventional
single­technology approaches.
6.2
Limitations of this Work
Despite the promising results, the proposed framework and analysis still have several
limitations. First, thermal dynamics are not considered in the current model, particularly
the effects of temperature variation during cold start­up on efficiency. Second, pressure
dynamics are also neglected—pressure fluctuations during cold start­up and their impact
on performance and energy consumption are not accounted for, limiting the comprehen­
siveness of the system assessment.
Additionally, at the system level, the model lacks representation of stack degradation and
replacement strategies, i.e., the long­term aging of electrolyzers is not modeled, which
may affect the reliability of economic evaluations over extended operation. Finally, the
temporal resolution of the model also limits accuracy. Due to hardware constraints, simu­
lations were conducted using a 5­minute time step, which may result in some inaccuracies
in capturing instantaneous system states and outputs. These aspects constitute key limi­
tations of the current study and deserve attention in future research, including the absence
63
==PAGE:76==
of sensitivity analysis and the extended computation time of the optimization process due
to hardware limitations.
6.3
Suggestions for Further Research
To address the above limitations and further enhance system performance, future re­
search can be extended in the following directions:
First, additional modules such as energy storage and fuel cells can be integrated to form a
more complete “wind/solar–storage–hydrogen/power” system. Under surplus generation
conditions, storage units (e.g., batteries) can absorb excess energy and release it during
shortages, stabilizing RES output. The feasibility of incorporating reversible electrolyzers,
which combine electrolysis and fuel cell functionality, can also be explored. This would
enable hydrogen­to­electricity conversion on the demand side, forming a closed energy
loop. Such integration improves system flexibility and broadens the role of hydrogen in
both generation and consumption, offering a more efficient and robust solution for off­grid
scenarios.
Second, to improve the accuracy and predictive power of the model, future studies may
adopt higher time­resolution simulations. Although the input data has a 5­minute inter­
val, internal dynamic models can be developed with millisecond­level resolution. This
would better capture fast system behaviors such as load fluctuation and start/stop tran­
sitions, significantly improving the accuracy of key parameters like hydrogen yield. High­
resolution modeling also provides a better foundation for control strategy design and real­
time system management.
Lastly, to enhance the comprehensiveness and long­term accuracy of the model, thermal
and pressure modeling should be included to reflect their influence on efficiency during
cold start­up. A degradation model for electrolyzer stacks should also be developed,
along with stack replacement strategies incorporated into the optimization routine. This
will enable the system to better represent realistic operating conditions and improve the
reliability of long­term performance evaluations. With these enhancements, the proposed
modeling and optimization framework will be more versatile and applicable to real­world
engineering deployments.
