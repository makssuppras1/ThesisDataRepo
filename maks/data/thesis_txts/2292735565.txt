Abstract 
Municipal solid waste comprises a significant portion of food waste which can be a source of 
renewable energy and resources when treated appropriately. In 2014, Singapore generated 
788,600 tons of food waste and only 12.9% were recycled. Currently, municipal food waste in 
Singapore are sent to incineration plants and anaerobic digestion is a feasible alternative 
treatment method for food waste. This study intends to assess the impacts, using LCA 
methodology, on the entire waste management system due to the introduction of anaerobic 
digestion as an alternative treatment for food waste, including waste cooking oil, in Singapore. 
The EASETECH software was used for model construction and life cycle impact assessments. 
Seven scenarios, each with different treatment technologies and final use of products, were 
created for investigation. The treatment technologies employed include incineration, 
anaerobic digestion (using the anaerobic digestion of organic slurry (ADOS) or mesophilic 
process) and biodiesel refinery. The use of final products include electricity generation from 
combustion in incineration plant and biogas from anaerobic digestion plant, upgrading of 
biogas for use in vehicle, use of digestate on land as soil conditioners and biodiesel as vehicle 
fuels.  
The life cycle impact assessment showed that there was not a single scenario that performed 
better than all other scenarios in all impact categories. All scenarios achieved environmental 
savings in global warming potential and depletion of abiotic fossil resources. In terms of 
global warming, anaerobic digestion of food waste followed by upgrading of biogas for use in 
vehicle proved to be the best solution. All the alternative scenarios achieved greater savings in 
global warming potential than the baseline scenario in which all waste was incinerated. 
The scenario in which biogas is upgraded for use in vehicle also performed best in 
carcinogenic human toxicity, photochemical oxidant formation, freshwater eutrophication, 
depletion of abiotic fossil resources, terrestrial acidification, terrestrial eutrophication and 
particulate matter emission. This was due to savings from avoided production of petrol and 
emissions related to car operations due to substitution by biomethane. The production of 
electricity from biogas proved to be inferior as there is significant energy loss in the 
electricity production process due to the low conversion efficiency of gas engines. 
Contribution analysis showed that use-on-land was the dominating process in non-carciogenic 
human toxicity, ecotoxicity, freshwater eutrophication and marine eutrophication in all 
scenarios that involved anaerobic digestion. Anaerobic digestion and incineration were also 
major contributors to impact potentials, each of them being the dominant process in different 
impact categories. Transport and landfill were found to be not influential to the results and 
their contributions were relatively small.  
==PAGE:6==
iv 
 
Perturbation analysis showed that water content of food waste was the most sensitive 
parameter as it significantly influenced the energy recovery of incineration. Other important 
parameters include electricity conversion efficiency, methane yield of anaerobic digestion 
plant and energy content of food waste. Scenario analysis showed that the marginal electricity 
production and food waste content had significant influence on the results while soil profile 
had negligible influence. 
Uncertainty analysis identified key parameters that have significant influence on the final 
results. These include composition of waste input and technical parameters related to 
important treatment processes such as anaerobic digestion and incineration. Hence, accuracy 
and representativeness of these data are crucial in ensuring reliability and robustness of LCA 
results. Data collection should be focused on these parameters for future studies.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Modern societies generate increasing quantities of waste which pose immense challenges in 
terms of proper management and treatment of these waste to all nations. At the same time, 
environmental concerns such as global warming and depletion of resources have prompted 
nations to look at innovative ways of handling municipal solid waste such as recycling and 
utilizing waste as resources. 
Municipal solid waste comprises a significant portion of food waste which could create 
problems such as odor, hygiene issues, pollution and other detrimental environmental 
problems if not handled properly. On the other hand, food waste can be a source of renewable 
energy and resources such as fertilizers when treated appropriately. Technologies such as 
anaerobic digestion and composting of organic waste were utilized as early as in the 19th 
century (Penn State College of Agricultural Sciences, 2015) but are not widely adopted in 
most nations currently. 
Waste in Singapore 
With a current population of 5.47 million but only 718.3 km2 of land size, land is very scarce 
in Singapore (Singapore Department of Statistics, 2014). As such, there is very limited land 
area that can be used as landfills. The Semakau landfill, an offshore landfill, is the only 
operating landfill in Singapore and is estimated to be able to handle Singapore’s waste 
disposal for the next 20 to 30 years (NEA, 2014). The Semakau landfill only accept 
incineration ash and non-incinerable waste and therefore all incinerable waste are handled by 
other waste treatment facilities in Singapore. 
The amount of waste generated in Singapore had increased by 56.9%, from 4.79 million tons 
in 2004 to 7.51 million tons in 2014, in the past decade (National Environment Agency of 
Singapore, 2014) which outpaced the population growth of 31.3% in the same period 
(Singapore Department of Statistics, 2014). Hence, there has been an increase in the amount 
of waste generated per capita in Singapore. The rapid increase is largely due to increasing 
affluence, population growth and rise in tourist arrivals (The Straits Times, 2013). 
Wastes are classified into three categories, which are domestic, industrial and institutional, in 
Singapore. Domestic sources include households, markets, food centers and commercial 
premises such as restaurants, hotels and shops. On the other hand, industrial sources are 
industries but excluding toxic and hazardous waste that requires special handling, treatment 
and disposal. Lastly, institutional sources include various government and statutory board

installations, hospitals, schools, recreational facilities and public development projects (Bai & 
Sutanto, 2002). 
Approximately 10% of total waste generated in Singapore are food waste. In 2014 alone, 
Singapore generated 788,600 tons, equivalent of 144kg per capita, of food waste (NEA, 2015). 
While Singapore has achieved a 59.5% recycling rate of total waste generated in 2014, there 
were still several waste types which had low recycling rate of 20% and below, as seen in 
Table 1. Among these is food waste which only had a recycling rate of 12.9%. As such, 
Singapore has failed to meet her target of achieving 30% recycling rate of food waste by 2012, 
set in the Singapore Green Plan 2012 in 2002 (Zero Waste Singapore, 2013).

The low recycling rate of food waste was largely due to the failure to recycle domestic and 
institutional food waste. These two sources contributed the majority of food waste produced 
in Singapore in the form of uncooked kitchen food and post-meal leftovers (Siau, 2014). 
Currently, only industrial food waste are recycled. These include clean food waste such as 
spent grains from beer brewing, bread waste and soya bean waste which are converted into 
animal feed (The Straits Times, 2013).  
==PAGE:15==
3 
 
According to the Singapore Sustainable Blueprint 2015, Singapore aims to become a ‘Zero 
Waste Nation’ by reducing consumption and improving reusing and recycling (MEWR and 
MND, 2014). Food waste possess immense potential to be recycled and there is huge room of 
improvement for such recycling in Singapore. Indeed, the National Environment Agency 
(NEA) of Singapore has been looking at recycling methods of food waste that are cost-
effective, practical and sustainable. These methods include production of fertilizers and 
biogas, which can be used to generate electricity, from food waste (The Straits Times, 2013). 
Currently, all of the food waste that are not recycled are sent to incineration plants (MEWR & 
NEA, 2015). 
On the other hand, waste cooking oil possesses great potential to be recycled into biodiesel 
which can be used by vehicles without engine modification. Currently, more than 100,000 
tons of cooking oil is consumed in Singapore in a year. It is estimated that 18.5% of this 
cooking oil consumed can be recovered as waste cooking oil while the remaining 81.5% is 
adsorped by food. Hence, up to 20,000 tons of waste cooking oil is disposed of every year in 
Singapore (Alpha Biofuels, 2015). This amount occupies approximately 2.5% of Singapore’s 
food waste generated. 
This waste cooking oil usually ends up in the municipal solid waste or the sewage system 
which leads to problems such as clogging of sewage pipes and higher stress on wastewater 
treatment plants. In this study, alternative treatment of waste cooking oil is also explored. 
Solid Waste Management in Singapore 
Singapore’s solid waste management system incorporates recycling, collection and disposal. 
Recycling is done at homes and businesses. Recyclables are sorted at the sources and 
collected separately for processing. Besides, companies have to pay for the collection and 
disposal of waste. This has helped to improve the recycling rate in the industrial and 
commercial sectors. The recycling industry in Singapore is able to recycle the following 
waste categories: electronic waste, food waste, wood waste, horticultural waste, used copper 
slag, construction and demolition waste, ferrous waste and plastic waste (Teo, 2007). On the 
other hand, NEA also launched the National Recycling Program (NRP) in 2001 to encourage 
recycling in the community. Under the program, recycling bags or bins are distributed to 
households. Contracted recycling companies collect recyclables from households bi-weekly 
for further processing. Under the NRP, the household participation rate reached 56% in 2005 
(Teo, 2007). 
Incineration has been adopted as the disposal option for non-recycled solid waste in 
Singapore. The incinerable solid waste collected are sent to the 4 waste to energy plants 
located at Tuas, Senoko and Tuas South, as seen in Figure 1. All of the plants are equipped 
==PAGE:16==
4 
 
with advanced air pollution control equipment to treat the flue gas generated. Heat generated 
from the combustion process is used to produce steam in boilers which drives turbines to 
produce electrical energy. Ash from the four plants and non-incinerable solid waste are first 
delivered to the Tuas Marine Transfer Station and unloaded into long barges. The covered 
barges are then transported to the Semakau landfill on a 30km-long sea route by tugboats 
(National Environment Agency of Singapore, 2015). 
 
Figure 1: Solid waste disposal infrastructure, including 4 incineration plants and a landfill, in Singapore 
The Semakau landfill is an offshore sanitary landfill that only accepts inert inorganic waste. 
The landfill is formed by a rock bund that enclosed a part of the sea between Pulau Semakau 
and Pulau Sakeng. The landfill is lined with impermeable membrane and marine clay to 
prevent leaking of leachate. The wastes transported to the landfill site are loaded onto dump 
trucks which then transport them to the designated landfill cells. When filled, landfill cells are 
covered with a layer of earth that allows trees and grass to grow. The Semakau landfill is also 
designed to serve recreational purposes.  
Anaerobic Digestion 
Even though food waste has high calorific value, it is not an ideal fuel for incineration due to 
its high water content. As Singapore aims to achieve an overall recycling rate of 70% for all 
waste by 2030 (Siau, 2015), anaerobic digestion serves as a feasible alternative treatment 
method for food waste in Singapore. 
Anaerobic digestion of food waste utilizes the recycling potential of food waste to produce 
digestate and biogas, which can then be used to generate electricity. The environmental 
==PAGE:17==
5 
 
benefits of using anaerobic digestion as an alternative treatment for food waste include 
recycling of nutrients and carbon, production of renewable energy and digestate and reduced 
burden on landfill. 
A previous attempt at recycling food waste in the form of an anaerobic digestion plant was 
constructed and operated by IUT Singapore Pte Ltd in Singapore in 2008. The digestion plant 
aimed to treat 800 tons of food waste per day in two phases. However, the plant closed down 
in 2011 as the actual food waste collected of 120 to 130 tons per day was far below the 
targeted amount of 800 tons per day. Besides, up to 40% of the food waste collected 
contained impurities such as plastic bags and other inorganics which made the food waste 
unsuitable for digestion. The contaminated food waste had to be sent for incineration instead. 
These factors led to high operating cost and losses at the plant which eventually led to its 
closure (The Straits Times, 2011). 
Moreover, there are several other obstacles that are inhibiting recycling of food waste in 
Singapore. These include scarcity of agricultural land to utilize digestate, high operating costs 
and cultural restrictions on application of digestate on agricultural land such as the halal 
guidelines as the produced digestate is very likely to contain non-halal contents. The latter 
also implies that the sale of digestate to neighboring Muslim countries, Malaysia and 
Indonesia, would be impractical. 
The government, however, has come up with a new plan to reemploy anaerobic digestion to 
recycle food waste. A new demonstration plant housed within the Ulu Pandan Water 
Reclamation Plant will co-digest a mixture of used water sludge from the water reclamation 
plant and collected food waste from the Clementi district. The biogas produced will be used 
to generate electricity. However, the use of digestate was not mentioned. This demonstration 
plant, which will be completed in September 2015, will be used to assess the ‘efficacy and 
cost-effectiveness of co-digestion implementation in Singapore’. Successful implementation 
could mean implementation of co-digestion in future water treatment plants and waste 
management facilities (Tan, 2015).  
1.2 Goals 
With the challenges of treatment and handling of food waste in Singapore in mind, this study 
intends to assess the impacts, using LCA methodology, on the entire waste management 
system due to the introduction of anaerobic digestion as an alternative treatment for food 
waste, including waste cooking oil, in Singapore. The goals of this study are to identify and 
quantify where the environment loads and savings are in the waste management system and 
which treatment technology is preferable for food waste, including waste cooking oil, in 
Singapore.  
==PAGE:18==
6 
 
The potential of application of anaerobic digestion to treat food waste in Singapore was 
quantified based on the quantity and waste content. Parameters that are crucial for the 
introduction of anaerobic digestion in Singapore were also assessed and quantified. The 
environmental impacts between anaerobic digestion and incineration, the current treatment for 
food waste, were compared. Besides, the potential final use of the digestate generated from 
anaerobic digestion, such as use on local parks and farms or agricultural land in neighboring 
countries and the impacts generated from such usage were investigated.  
This study also aims to identify key parameters that are sensitive to uncertainties and have 
significant influence on the results through sensitivity analysis. This would help to identify 
key areas whose data collection should be focused on in the future to produce reliable and 
accurate results. 
The results could be used by the Singapore government as a decision-supporting tool to 
decide on how to improve treatment of food waste in Singapore. The technology proposed 
would also help Singapore to reach her target of higher recycling rate on food waste.  
1.3 Scope 
Scenarios 
3 main scenarios were designed to be examined in this project. The baseline scenario 
(scenario 1) represents the current state of treatment for municipal food waste which is 
incineration. Scenario 2 includes a large size anaerobic digestion plant which can handle 36.5% 
of Singapore’s food waste while the remaining 63.5% are incinerated.  
Scenario 2 comprises five sub-scenarios (2a to 2e), each with slight variations. They are 
explained in more details in chapter 4. Scenario 3 includes an additional biodiesel refinery 
plant which processes the waste cooking oil in food waste into biodiesel. In scenario 3, the 
waste cooking oil is collected separately from other food waste.  
Functional Unit 
The functional unit for this project is the collection, management, treatment and disposal of 1 
ton of food waste, including waste cooking oil, generated within the geographical boundaries 
of Singapore.  
System Boundaries 
This project focuses on the handling of food waste, including waste cooking oil, generated in 
Singapore. Hence, the activities involved are collection, transport, treatment, disposal and 
final use of final products. The collection and sorting of wastes are done by contracted waste 
==PAGE:19==
7 
 
collectors. The sorted wastes are transported to the different treatment facilities based on their 
waste type.  
In the scenarios constructed for this project, the treatment activities produce secondary 
materials and energy. These functional products were considered in the model as substitution 
for products of the same functions. 
Lifecycle 
In this project, the lifecycle considered starts at waste generation at the sources and ends at 
the disposal or uses of the final products from the treatment facilities.  
Approach 
The system was modelled using the consequential approach as the interest of the study is in 
analysing the consequences of changes in the system. The marginal technologies for energy 
production and other technologies affected by changes in the system were modelled. The 
marginal electricity production in this study would be natural gas-based power plants in 
Singapore.  
Assessment Criteria 
The ‘ILCD recommended – 2013 NR’ life cycle impact assessment method was used to 
assess the impacts. ILCD stands for the International Reference Life Cycle Data System and 
is a handbook developed by the Institute for Environment and Sustainability in the European 
Commission Joint Research Centre, in co-operation with the Environment DG (European 
Commission, 2014). The impact categories investigated in this study include the following: 
- 
Global warming, carcinogenic human toxicity, non-carcinogenic human toxicity, 
photochemical oxidant formation, freshwater eutrophication, marine eutrophication, 
ecotoxicity, depletion of abiotic fossil resources, terrestrial acidification, terrestrial 
eutrophication and particulate matter 
Temporal Scope 
This study modelled the present waste management system and several additional treatment 
facilities that do not currently exist in Singapore. Newest available data of the existing waste 
management system were used, where possible, in the scenarios construction. However, it 
was unavoidable that some old data had to be used. The alternative treatment options were 
modelled based on latest or best available technologies taken from various literatures. 
Therefore, this study is assumed to be valid for at least the next 2 decades.  
==PAGE:20==
8 
 
Technological Scope 
The technologies used in the model are the current one employed for incineration and the best 
available for the anaerobic digester. The incineration process modelled was based on the 4 
operating incineration plants in Singapore. The anaerobic digester modelled was largely based 
on a default anaerobic digestion plant in EASETECH which was based on a plant in 
Karpalund, Sweden. Modifications were made based on the AD plant operated by IUT 
Singapore from 2008 to 2011 to better reflect the conditions in Singapore.  
Working Approach 
The data needed for this project were sourced from literature and relevant authorities. The 
main reliable source for Singaporean data was the National Environment Agency of 
Singapore (NEA). Certain parameters with unknown values were estimated based on best 
knowledge. 
EASETECH was used to model the system. The data obtained from relevant sources were 
supplemented with the default data in EASETECH and the Ecoinvent database where 
necessary. 
Limitations 
The main challenges for anaerobic digestion in land-scarce Singapore are the costs and space 
needed. Anaerobic digestion is a space and time-consuming process as compared to 
incineration which is a quick and efficient way of treating and reducing the volume of waste. 
This, coupled with the high costs involved, have been the main obstacle that has inhibited 
successful application of anaerobic digestion in Singapore.

2. Literature Review 
LCA of Waste Management Systems 
The application of LCA in waste management provides new perspectives and it is expected to 
become an important decision support tool in all aspects of waste management (Hauschild & 
Barlaz, 2011). Waste-LCA are different from product-LCA in several ways. While a product-
LCA generally conducts LCA on a single product from cradle-to-grave, a waste-LCA 
conducts LCA on a heterogeneous flow of material consisting various waste fractions from 
end-of-life to grave or reuse (Clavreul, et al., 2014). As such, as seen in Figure 2, a waste-
LCA has a different system boundary from that of a product-LCA. While a product-LCA’s 
system boundary typically encapsulates all processes related to the product from raw material 
extraction to disposal or final use of the product, a waste-LCA’s system boundary include all 
activities involved in the collection or handling of waste from generation of wastes to disposal 
or final use of the final products from these wastes.

(horizontal analysis). (McDougall, et al., 2001) 
LCA on Treatment of Organic Waste 
Bolin (2009) did a similar study on environmental impact assessment of energy recovery from 
food waste in Singapore using the LCA methodology.  The study examined three scenarios in 
addition to the baseline scenario using Microsoft Excel as the modelling tool. A big, medium 
and small biogas plant was modelled respectively in each of the scenario while two different 
ways to utilize the biogas produced, which were generation of electricity or use in heavy 
vehicles, were also studied. The impact categories examined in the study were global 
warming potential, acidification, eutrophication, energy use and land use. The study 
concluded that ‘from an environmental perspective production of biogas is a better way to 
treat the organic fraction of municipal solid waste (OFMSW) than incineration’. The 
scenarios produced improvements in global warming potential, acidification and 
==PAGE:22==

eutrophication. The study also noted that leakage of biogas ‘will lead to a loss of all the gain 
in saved greenhouse gas emissions’. However, one shortcoming of Bolin’s study is that the 
composition of the food waste used in the study was not specified. Bolin cited a lack of such 
data as the reason and the emissions of different treatment facilities were based on data of 
facilities in other countries. This might lead to inaccuracy of the results. As such, the author 
aimed to overcome this shortcoming in his study. This was done by gathering data from 
Singapore as much as possible. For data that proved to be scarce, the author aimed to identify 
key areas in which crucial data were not available. This would provide direction for future 
work in terms of important aspects to gather data for. 
Another study done by Khoo et al. (2010) came to similar conclusions. The study concluded 
that implementation of anaerobic digestion to recycle food waste produced significant 
reduction of global warming impacts. This study also examined aerobic composting of food 
waste which produced favorable results compared with incineration. However, anaerobic 
digestion was a more preferable technology than aerobic composting in terms of carbon 
dioxide and ammonia emissions. The impact categories examined were global warming 
potential, acidification, eutrophication, photochemical oxidation and energy use. Khoo et al. 
also recommended that the values of nitrogen and phosphorus nutrients in the compost 
produced be investigated further to improve the study. 
These two studies coincide with the objectives of the author’s project. However, they did not 
examine several impact categories such as ecotoxicity and human toxicity which could be 
important factors for decision makers. Hence, the author aimed to fill this gap in his study. 
Bernstad and Jansen (2011) used the EASEWASTE LCA-tool to compare environmental 
impacts from incineration, decentralized composting and centralized anaerobic digestion of 
organic household waste based on a case study in southern Sweden. They discovered that the 
results were highly dependent on energy substitution and emissions during biological 
processes.  
Naroznova et al. (2013) did a LCA study on pretreatment technologies for anaerobic digestion 
of source-separated organic household waste. The two technologies examined were screw 
press and waste pulping. Screw press was more commonly used but resulted in great loss of 
organic matter in the reject and affected the biomass quality as well. Waste pulping is a new 
technology developed that uses cold water and transforms organic waste into a volatile-solid 
rich biomass fraction and an inorganic reject fraction. The study concluded that there were 
minimal differences in overall results between the two pretreatment technologies. The waste 
pulping technology led to less organic matter loss which resulted in higher biogas output and 
nutrient recovery. The environmental benefits lost due to higher loss in organic matter from

provided in this study was also adopted in the author’s modelling work. 
Anaerobic Digestion 
De Baere and Mattheeuws (2012) reported on the status, experience and prospects of 
anaerobic digestion of the organic fraction of municipal solid waste in Europe. Anaerobic 
digestion is becoming more common in Europe and is already handling more than 25% of the 
biological treatment in Europe. The long term success of anaerobic digestion has made it the 
favorable technology for treatment of organic waste. De Baere and Mattheeuws mentioned 
that anaerobic digestion will continue to increase on a steady basis. They also stated that 
anaerobic digestion plants should receive at least 30,000 tons per year in order to be 
economically viable. The current state-of-the-art anaerobic digestion technologies in Europe 
were also reported and this has helped the author to design scenarios for his study. 
Solid Waste Management in Singapore 
Bai et al. (2002) reported on the practice of solid waste management in Singapore and also 
examined the challenges faced. Incineration has been the dominant solid waste treatment 
option and is currently handled by four incineration plants. Up to 87% of the total waste 
disposed of in 2000 was incinerated which is in stark contrast to other developed countries 
such as the Netherlands (27%), Denmark (23%), the US (16%) and Canada (5.48%). On the 
other hand, landfill is the least preferred method to handle solid waste in Singapore although 
it is the most important option in many other countries. These differences in preferences are 
due to the land constraint faced by Singapore. Bai et al. also mentioned that Singapore needs 
to improve on recycling of household and commercial waste. Biological treatment was 
recommended to recycle food waste. 
Food Waste in Singapore 
Wang et al. (2013) indicated a lack of food waste research in Singapore. Detailed composition

conducted food waste composition analysis based on food waste collected in Nanyang

study was used as a reference for the author’s designed model.

3. Methodology 
3.1 Life Cycle Assessment 
Life cycle assessment (LCA), also known as cradle-to-grave analysis, is a technique to assess 
potential environmental impacts and potential aspects associated with a product or service. It 
is done in several steps, including ‘defining the goal and scope of study, compiling an 
inventory of relevant inputs and outputs, evaluating the potential environmental impacts 
associated with these inputs and outputs and interpreting the results of the inventory and 
impact phases in relation to the objectives of the study’ (ISO 14040:2006, 2006), as shown in 
Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3: The 4 stages of Life Cycle Assessment 
The goal definition describes the objectives of the study while the scope definition describes 
several issues such as the functional unit, the system boundaries and assessment criteria as so 
on. This was done and shown in chapter 1.  
Life cycle inventory is compiled after scope and definition and involves collection of data on 
emissions and resource information on the input and output of the processes involved. The 
compilation relates the input and output to the functional unit of the study. This is crucial for 
subsequent impact assessment to evaluate the environmental impacts of the service provided 
specified by the functional unit (Hauschild & Barlaz, 2011). 
The life cycle impact assessment phase interprets the inventory data into impact potentials on 
areas of protection of interest. Examples of areas of protection include human health, natural 
environment and natural resources. The life cycle impact assessment done in this study 
involves 3 steps: 
1. Selection of impact categories and classification: exchanges from the inventory are 
assigned to defined impact categories by their contribution to different problem areas. 
==PAGE:26==
14 
 
2. Characterization: the contributions of the exchanges are used to generate the 
environmental impact potentials within each impact category. Results are shown in 
characterized values in their respective units corresponding to the impact categories 
they belong to. 
3. Normalization: the impact potentials generated are related to a set of common 
references to aid comparisons across impact categories. This step is explained further 
in 3.3. 
The last step of LCA is the interpretation of results, taking into account the goal and scope 
defined. The outcome could be recommendations to intended audience and decision makers. 
This phase could also provide input for further iteration, review of scope, collection of data 
for the inventory and impact assessment. Thus, LCA is overall an iterative process. A good 
interpretation addresses sensitivity analysis and limitations of the study.  
3.2 EASETECH 
EASETECH stands for Environmental Assessment System for Environmental Technologies. 
It is a LCA-model built for assessment of environmental technologies and is developed at the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU, 2015). EASETECH is a specialized tool which 
focuses on material flow modelling and is designed to allow easy construction of scenarios to 
perform LCA of complex systems. It is suitable for LCA of solid waste management systems 
as it is designed to handle complex systems with heterogeneous material flows (Clavreul, et 
al., 2014).  
EASETECH was used in this study to model the waste management system and calculate the 
impacts of the scenarios. Uncertainty analysis was also done using EASETECH. The results 
were exported to Microsoft Excel for further calculations and processing into plots. 
3.3 Normalization 
The results of LCIA are presented in both characterized values in their respective units and 
person equivalents, which are normalized values. Often it is difficult to decide which 
alternative has the best performance based on the characterized results. Besides, it is also 
difficult to interpret the significance of the characterized results based on their values alone 
(Hauschild & Barlaz, 2011). 
Hence, normalization is done to support the interpretation of the potential impacts from the 
different impact categories. The normalized results present the relative share of the impact of 
the examined system in the total impact of this category per average citizen in a year. 
Through normalization, the results of all impact categories are presented in the same unit 
which makes for easier comparison. 
==PAGE:27==
15 
 
In the LCIA method used in this study, the normalization references are based on European 
citizens’ impacts. While a European citizen’s impacts are likely to be different from those of a 
Singaporean citizen, which means that the normalized results might not be representative of 
the case in Singapore, it does not affect the interpretation of the results as these values are 
used as basis for comparison. Since this study is done using a consequential approach, the 
interest is to analyse the consequences of changes in the modelled system. Therefore, the use 
of European values as normalization references is still valid for the study.

3.4 Uncertainty Analysis 
‘All models are wrong, but some models are useful.’ – George Box, 1979 
All models are flawed. The assumptions and data used in the modelling of the scenarios are 
subjected to various extent of uncertainty. LCA modelling is essentially an approximation of 
the real system and are susceptible to uncertainties brought by variability of inputs and 
epistemic uncertainty. Hence, to improve the reliability of LCA results, uncertainties should 
be minimized by using good and updated data and they should be assessed and quantified if 
possible. Uncertainty analysis is a useful tool to examine the robustness of LCA results. 
Uncertainties can be grouped into 3 types (Lloyd & Ries, 2007): (i) parameter uncertainties 
which are uncertainties in values due to insufficient data, measurement errors and so on; (ii) 
scenario uncertainties due to choices and assumptions made in scenario constructions and (iii) 
model uncertainties due to the mathematical models involved in LCA calculations. 
The methods used to conduct uncertainty analysis were adopted from the study done by 
Clavreul et al. (2012) and are explained in more details in the following sections. 
3.4.1 Sensitivity Analysis 
Perturbation analysis and scenario analysis were performed in this study to identify sensitive 
parameters and test different options individually and observe the effect of these options on 
the final results. Parameters and options were changed one at a time to observe their 
consequential effects. 
Perturbation Analysis 
Perturbation analysis identifies sensitive parameters whose variations have huge influence on 
the final results. The parameters tested in this study include those related to treatment 
technologies such as energy conversion efficiencies and energy consumptions as well as 
parameters whose values were assumed such as fertilizer substitution. The list of parameters 
tested is available in Table 19 in Appendix A4. 
The sensitivity ratios of these parameters were calculated in several steps. The original values 
of these parameters were first increased by 10% and then the model was run to obtain the 
impact values. The impact values were exported to Microsoft Excel for further calculations.

3.4.2 Uncertainty Propagation 
Uncertainty propagation was conducted to calculate the results’ uncertainty by propagating 
input uncertainties. In this study, the probability theory was adopted and all the uncertainties 
were assumed to be represented by normal distributions. This arbitrary assumption, however, 
could lead to underestimation of the likelihood of outlier results but is more practical for this 
study (Clavreul, et al., 2012). An example of definition of parameter’s distribution done in 
EASETECH is shown in Figure 4. 
==PAGE:30==
18 
 
 
Figure 4: Example of parameter definition in EASETECH. In this figure, ND means Normal Distribution, first value in 
the parenthesis is the mean value while the second value is the standard deviation of the parameter 
Monte Carlo Simulation 
Monte Carlo simulation was used in this study as the propagation method because it is the 
most commonly used and can be done rather quickly. It relies on repeated random sampling 
the probability distribution of each uncertain parameter to obtain numerical results. In this 
study, the procedure was done 10,000 times and the results generated were used to calculate 
the mean value and standard deviation using Microsoft Excel. The results were also used to 
construct relative frequency histograms and cumulative relative frequency plots to show the 
probability distribution of the results. These were done by sorting the results in bins first 
before constructing the plots. The model parameters were assumed to be independent. 
The parameters assessed in Monte Carlo simulation are listed in Table 24 in Appendix A4. 
The parameters concerning waste compositions and waste properties could not be carried out 
in EASETECH using the parameter table currently and hence had to be left out of Monte 
Carlo simulation. Hence, the parameters tested were all technical parameters and energy 
consumptions. 
Discernibility Analysis 
Discernibility analysis was conducted to obtain the probability distribution of the differences 
between scenarios. Some parameters, such as electricity consumption of anaerobic digester, 
contribute to uncertainties of the scenarios’ results but not to the difference between them. 
Hence, these parameters that were used in both scenarios were chosen to run the Monte Carlo 
simulation to generate numerical results. The difference in results between the 2 scenarios 
was then calculated and shown in relative frequency histograms and cumulative relative 
frequency plots. The plots exhibit the relative predominance of one scenario over another.

4. Scenarios 
Three main scenarios, each with different combination of treatment technologies, were 
created for examination. Scenario 1 serves as the baseline scenario and represents the current 
practice of food waste management in Singapore. Scenario 2 includes an anaerobic digestion 
plant and has 5 sub-scenarios each representing a different anaerobic digestion technology or 
use of produced digestate and biogas. Scenario 3 has an additional biodiesel refinery that 
converts waste cooking oil into biodiesel. 
The waste input in the model was divided into 2 fractions: ‘Singapore food waste’ which 
represents the generic food waste produced in Singapore and ‘waste cooking oil’ which 
represents waste cooking oil that can be source-separated and collected separately from the 
other food waste. As there is no available quantification on the amount of waste cooking oil 
that ends up in the sewage system or municipal solid waste, it was assumed in this study that 
all the waste cooking oil generated ends up in municipal solid waste for simplification. The 
waste cooking oil makes up approximately 2.5% of the total food waste input in the model. 
The incineration plant modelled in all scenarios are based on the four existing incineration 
plants in Singapore. They are represented as a combined incineration process in the model. 
The values of emissions and operating processes of each incineration plant were taken from 
the data obtained from IUT Singapore’s Project Design Document (see chapter 5). These 
values were then calculated in proportion to each plant’s operating capacity, in weight, and 
used in the incineration process in the EASETECH model.  
Heat energy produced from combustion of waste is recovered and used to generate electricity. 
The electricity conversion efficiencies of the incineration plants range between 15% to 20% 
due to high moisture content of waste, typically in the range of 40-65%, part of which is 
contributed by food waste which has more than 80% moisture (IUT Singapore Pte Ltd, 2006). 
In this study, an average efficiency of 17.78% was calculated according to the capacity of 
each incineration plant.  
In the EASETECH model, the collection and transport of food waste were represented by 
transport processes already available in EASETECH. These transport processes were created 
using results of measurements done in Denmark. The collection of municipal food waste is 
represented by the process ‘Collection truck, residual waste, multi-family, Aarhus, Euro 3, 
DK, 2007’ while transport of end products from the treatment facilities to their respective 
final destinations are represented by ‘Road, Truck, 28t-32t, Euro 5, on highway, DK, 2009’. 
The transfer of bottom ash from the marine transfer station to the Semakau Landfill is 
represented by the process ‘Sea, Bulk carrier, 2000t, domestic, DK, 2010’. 
==PAGE:32==
20 
 
Currently, petrol kiosks in Singapore offers ultra-low sulphur diesel (ULSD) which is 
equivalent to Euro4 standards (Shell, 2015), a slightly inferior grade than the Euro 5 grade 
diesel in the modelled transport processes but slightly superior than the Euro 3 in the 
modelled collection processes. Thus, the collection and transport processes used might have 
introduced less and extra environmental savings respectively in the final results. Nevertheless, 
previous studies (Gaztelu & Chew, 2015) (Clavreul, et al., 2012) have indicated that transport 
processes have little influence on the final results and hence the difference in grade of diesel 
should not have significant impact on the results. This will be verified in contribution analysis 
in the results section.  
Bottom ashes from the incineration plant are transported 30km via sea route to the Semakau 
landfill. The Semakau landfill is represented by a bottom ash landfill which was already 
available in EASETECH. This default process fits the actual condition as the only end 
product from treatment of food waste that ends up in the landfill in Singapore is in the form of 
bottom ash. This is because the Semakau landfill only accepts inorganic and non-incinerable 
waste. 
In this study, the electricity mix in Singapore is based on marginal electricity production from 
natural gas and is represented by the process ‘electricity production, natural gas, combined 
cycle power plant, MY’ which is taken from the Ecoinvent database. As the data of electricity 
production in Singapore was not available, the process based on Malaysia was used. In 2014, 
natural gas makes up 95% of Singapore’s fuel mix in power generation. The remaining 
portion is contributed by diesel and fuel oil (0.9%) and other energy products such as biomass, 
coal and municipal waste (3.9%) (Energy Market Authority, 2015). Thus, the marginal 
electricity substitution due to renewable energy generated by the modelled processes are 
attributed to savings in production of electricity using natural gas as fuel. 
Consumption of water in the treatment technologies were modelled by a combined water 
production process which simulates the mix of water production technologies in Singapore. 
Currently, Singapore’s water supply comes from 3 major sources: local catchment and 
imported water (45%), NEWater (30%) and desalination (25%). NEWater and desalination 
employ the reverse osmosis technology while the local catchment and imported water is 
treated using conventional technology. These processes were represented by similar water 
production processes that utilize the same technologies from the Ecoinvent database.

Scenario 1 represents the current management system for municipal food waste which is 
incineration. Food waste is collected with residual waste and transported to firstly a material

4.2 Scenario 2 
Scenario 2 has an additional anaerobic digestion plant to treat collected food waste, in 
addition to incineration. The food waste is collected in compostable liners distributed to 
households. This can be done together with the recycling bags for other recyclable fractions 
which are distributed to households in Singapore under the National Recycling Program. The 
compostable liners were not included in the waste input as material composition of these 
liners were not available. Nevertheless, the weight of these liners, approximately 6g for a liner 
of 2 gallon capacity (BioBag, 2015), would make up an insignificant fraction of the waste 
input. Therefore, omission of the liners in the waste input is extremely unlikely to have much 
influence on the results.

All six sub-scenarios in scenario 2 have an anaerobic digestion plant each but with different 
technologies or different use of produced digestate and biogas. The anaerobic digestion plants 
in all sub-scenarios are capable of handling 36.5% of Singapore’s total food waste. This 
designed capacity is based on IUT Singapore’s anaerobic digestion plant of 800t food waste 
per day and represents state of the art technology (IUT Singapore Pte Ltd, 2006). 
In the EASETECH model, the anaerobic digestion plants were created based on a template of 
an existing anaerobic digestion process ‘Biogas plant, Karpalund, Sweden, 2008’. The 
operating values such as resources consumption are adjusted accordingly based on scenario 
assumptions and collected data from literatures to simulate the conditions in Singapore.  
For scenarios 2a, 2b, 2c and 2e, the AD plant by IUT Singapore was simulated. Key data were 
taken from IUT Singapore’s project design document provided by the National Environment 
Agency of Singapore upon request by the author. IUT Singapore employed state of the art 
technology for its biogas plant in 2008. The biogas plant was also designed to suit 
Singapore’s conditions. Hence, these data helped to improve the reliability and accuracy of 
the model’s simulation.  
The plant employs an ADOS (anaerobic digestion of organic slurry) process which is in 
between the wet and dry processes. This unique technology has several benefits such as 
adaptation to varying water contents during operation, high biogas yield and insensitivity to 
variances in feedstock composition. The process is thermophilic, which is preferred due to 
hygiene regulations and also its faster reaction rate. The faster reaction rate allows a shorter 
residence time in the digester which also means the plant can be of smaller size.  
In the AD plant, the collected food waste first undergoes a mechanical pre-treatment to 
remove inorganics and plastic bags used to contain the food waste using a screener. The 
screened waste is then fed into the ADOS mill, a key part of the ADOS process, which 
conducts a size reduction on the organic solids while removing the remnant of inorganics. The 
organic waste is then shredded further and mixed with circulate material in a digester which 
contains anaerobic microbes. The sealed digester undergoes a 21-day anaerobic digestion 
process on average.  
The technology operates under thermophilic conditions at a temperature between 52oC and 
55oC which is maintained by some of the heat generated from combustion of produced biogas. 
Such technology produces biogas with an average of 62% methane. The biogas is 
continuously fed into a gas engine for combustion after being withdrawn from the digester. 
The produced electricity is used to power the plant’s operation while the excess is sold to 
national power grid. The process flow diagram is illustrated in Figure 6.

In scenario 2a, the digestate produced, which has a water content of 97%, is transported 20km 
away and used as soil conditioner in local parks, landscaping and agricultural land. This 
substitutes the uses of N, P and K fertilizers. Carbon sequestration was also accounted for. 
Scenario 2b has the same anaerobic digestion technology as scenario 2a, but the produced 
digestate is dewatered and shipped to China for use on agricultural lands. China was chosen 
instead of neighboring South East Asian countries due to cultural consideration as the use of 
digestate on farmlands would breach the halal guidelines since the digestate would very likely 
contain pork, alcohol and other non-halal substances. As all South East Asian countries either 
have a large Muslim population or exports agricultural products to Muslim countries, the 
fertilizer made from digestate in Singapore is very unlikely to be widely accepted in South 
East Asia.  
In scenario 2d, the anaerobic digestion plant employs a mesophilic process instead of a 
thermophilic one. Scenario 2d was designed to examine the differences in environmental 
impacts between different AD technologies. The AD technology modelled in scenario 2d was 
based on the Karpalund biogas plant in Sweden which also employs a mesophilic process. 
However, the energy needed to maintain the temperature at 38oC has been lowered as

Lastly, scenario 2e adopts the same anaerobic digestion technology as scenario 2a, but it also 
includes upgrading of biogas into biomethane for use in vehicles instead of electricity 
generation. The biogas produced by the AD plant contains approximately 62% methane and 
38% carbon dioxide and possibly trace amounts of other compounds such as ammonia, 
nitrogen and hydrogen sulfide (H2S).  
The upgrade of biogas is done in two steps. Firstly, a cleaning process to remove unwanted 
substances such as moisture, hydrogen sulfide, ammonia, particles etc. Removal of these 
substances are crucial to prevent corrosion and mechanical wear of vehicle engines. The 
second stage removes carbon dioxide to improve the energy content of the biogas. One of the 
most common technology employed commercially to remove carbon dioxide is pressurized 
water scrubber (Petersson & Wellinger, 2009). A schematic representation of the water 
scrubber is shown in Figure 9. 
 
Figure 9: Schematic representation of the pressurized water scrubber technology for biogas upgrade (IEA 
Bioenergy Task 37, 2014) 
As carbon dioxide has higher water solubility than methane, especially at lower temperatures, 
it dissolves to a larger extent in the scrubber column, leading to an increase in the methane 
==PAGE:38==
26 
 
concentration in the gas. The water leaving the column is transferred to a flash tank where the 
gas is released and transferred back to the raw gas inlet. The off-gas leaving the upgrading 
process often contains small amount of methane. Approximately 0.1-3% of methane is lost 
and an average value of 1.5% is used in this study (Jensen, 2009). In order to prevent the 
methane from leaking to the atmosphere, catalytic oxidation using platinum, palladium or 
cobalt as catalyst is used. Upgraded biogas typically contains approximately 98% methane 
(IEA Bioenergy Task 37, 2014). In this scenario, the upgraded biogas is assumed to be stored 
in the compressed natural gas (CNG) form, which is a common form for use as vehicle fuel.

4.3 Scenario 3 
In scenario 3, in addition to the incineration and AD plants, a biodiesel refinery plant which 
can convert waste cooking oil into biodiesel was added. The AD plant employs the same 
technology as scenario 2a. The source-separation efficiency of municipal waste cooking oil 
was assumed to be 70% while the remaining 30% was assumed to be disposed of together 
with other food waste. 
In the biodiesel refinery plant, the waste cooking oil first undergoes a pre-treatment process to 
remove unwanted substances such as solids and inorganic materials. It then undergoes 
transesterification, a process in which the triglyceride in the oil reacts with methanol in a base 
catalyzed reaction to produce methyl esters and glycerin, as seen in Figure 11. This reaction 
allows the waste cooking oil to have a high conversion rate of up to 98% (National Biodiesel 
Board, 2012). The catalyst commonly used is potassium hydroxide. A separation process 
follows the transesterification process to remove glycerin from the solution. The remaining 
==PAGE:39==

solution is biodiesel which can be used as vehicle fuel. The removed glycerin is sent for 
incineration.

As a component of LCA, the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) is the quantification of relevant 
inputs (fuels, resources, materials) and outputs (emissions or wastes) of an LCA system. Data 
used in this study were collected from the National Environment Agency of Singapore (NEA) 
and online resources such as journal articles, LCA studies, the Ecoinvent database and 
company websites.   
Singapore Food Waste and Waste Cooking Oil 
In this study, the waste input to the waste management system comprises the food waste 
fraction and the waste cooking oil fraction. The food waste fraction occupies 97.46% of the 
waste input and the waste cooking oil fraction occupies the remaining 2.54%. 
Data of food waste were based on IUT Singapore’s project design document (IUT Singapore 
Pte Ltd, 2006) provided by NEA upon request and previous study done in Singapore

food waste, however, were taken from the average values of animal food waste and vegetable 
food waste in EASETECH as such data for Singapore’s food waste were not available.

the various studies the author has seen. Besides, the volatile solids content is low at 5%.

6. Results and Analysis 
The first part of this chapter presents the results of life cycle impact assessment on all the 
scenarios. The impact assessment results are shown in both characterized values in their 
respective units and person equivalent which is dimensionless. The positive values are 
environmental loads, while the negative values are environmental savings. The impacts of the 
scenarios are compared to assess the consequential changes due to the different technologies 
and assumptions involved. Each scenario is also analysed individually. Contribution of the 
different processes are also presented to identify key processes that had the largest impacts.  
The second part of this chapter presents the results of uncertainty analysis on selected 
scenarios and impact categories.  
The following bullet points can be used as a guide for easier understanding of the 
interpretations: 
- 
Scenario 1: incineration 
- 
Scenario 2a: ADOS process, digestate used in Singapore 
- 
Scenario 2b: ADOS process, digestate dewatered and shipped to China 
- 
Scenario 2c: ADOS process, digestate incinerated 
- 
Scenario 2d: mesophilic process, digestate used in Singapore 
-

6.1 Life Cycle Impact Assessment Results 
6.1.1 Overall Results 
As seen in Table 10, in terms of magnitude in person equivalent alone, marine eutrophication 
had the largest values in all impact categories followed by non-carcinogenic human toxicity 
and ecotoxicity. Their magnitudes were significantly larger than other impact categories such 
as global warming potentials, carcinogenic human toxicity and particulate matter emissions. 
All scenarios achieved beneficial global warming potential and depletion of abiotic fossil 
resources. On the other hand, all the scenarios contributed environmental loads to 
photochemical oxidant formation, marine eutrophication, terrestrial acidification and 
terrestrial eutrophication. In other impact categories, some scenarios contributed to 
environmental savings while other contributed to environmental loads.  
In the global warming potential impact category, the largest saving, -75.50 kg CO2-eq/t waste, 
was achieved in scenario 2e while scenario 1 had the least saving at -16.43 kg CO2-eq/t waste. 
Scenarios 2a and 2d had similar savings (approximately 5% difference), -35.29 kg CO2-eq/t 
waste and -37.11 kg CO2-eq/t waste respectively. Scenario 2b achieved the fourth most 
==PAGE:47==
35 
 
savings at -27.85 kg CO2-eq/t while scenario 3, at -19.17 kg CO2-eq/t waste only had slightly 
more savings than scenario 1. Scenario 2c achieved the second least savings at -17.45 kg 
CO2-eq/t. 
It is hard to conclude from the characterized values if the savings or loads achieved are 
significant or not. Besides, it is also difficult to compare the performance of the scenarios 
across impact categories. Hence, these results were normalized and presented in Figure 13 for 
further interpretation. 
In Figure 13, the introduction of anaerobic digestion in scenarios 2a to 2e and scenario 3 led 
to improvements in global warming, photochemical oxidant formation, freshwater 
eutrophication, fossil resources depletion, terrestrial acidification, terrestrial eutrophication 
and particulate matter as compared to scenario 1. However, they performed worse than 
scenario 1 in non-carcinogenic human toxicity, marine eutrophication and ecotoxicity. 
The savings from incineration was low mainly due to the low conversion efficiency in 
recovering electrical energy. As the energy produced from combustion of waste was used to 
produce electricity only, unlike in many other countries where it is also recovered for heating, 
the savings from incineration were not as significant. Besides, a significant portion of energy 
produced from combustion of waste was used to evaporate the water in the waste. One 
significant contributor to this high water content was food waste which had 84% water 
content. Thus, when other municipal waste types are taken into account, channeling the food 
waste away from the incinerators to anaerobic digestion plants would help to reduce the 
overall water content of the waste delivered to the incinerator plants. This in turn would help 
to raise the energy recovered in the incineration plants on a per weight basis. 
Scenarios 2a and 2b had rather similar results in all impact categories which suggests that the 
transport of digestate to China did not have significant influence on the environmental 
impacts. This could mean that shipping the digestate to China could be a viable alternative for 
use of digestate from a LCA point of view. 
Scenario 2c, in which the digestate was incinerated instead of being used as soil conditioner, 
exhibited large similarity with scenario 1 in impact categories in which other AD scenarios 
had larger impacts (larger than 0.02 PE/t waste in absolute value) in. Scenario 2c performed 
significantly better than the other AD scenarios in non-carcinogenic human toxicity, marine 
eutrophication and ecotoxicity but performed worse in freshwater eutrophication. The 
differences were mainly due to the absence of use of digestate on land which had large 
impacts especially in the toxicity categories. Incinerating the digestate eliminated these 
impacts due to use-on-land and improved savings in the toxicity categories. However, the 
characterization factors employed by USEtox in the calculations of impact potentials have 
==PAGE:48==
36 
 
huge uncertainties and hence the actual environmental loads due to use-on-land could be 
much lower than suggested (USEtox, 2015). Nonetheless, the significant differences in results 
between scenario 2c and other AD scenarios could still suggest that the final use of end 
product from the AD plant has large implications on impact potentials. 
Scenario 2d, in which the mesophilic process was employed, had very similar results as 
scenario 2a, which employed the thermophilic ADOS process, in all impact categories. Hence, 
the results suggest that the employment of either ADOS or mesophilic process did not seem to 
have significant influence on the LCA impacts. When contribution analysis was conducted on 
the scenarios, it was realized that the anaerobic digestion process did not contribute 
significantly to the impact potentials. Most of the impact potentials were generated by use-on-
land and, in certain scenarios, incineration. Therefore, the technology employed in the AD 
plant does not have substantial influence on the results. In order to further verify this, a 
different biological treatment technology, the combined aerobic-anaerobic digestion, was 
tested in scenario analysis. The results can be seen in section 6.2.2.2. Nevertheless, the plant 
would benefit from the thermophilic process in terms of less plant size which would be 
significant if costs involved are taken into account. 
Scenario 2e, in which biogas was upgraded to biomethane and used in vehicles, performed 
significantly better than the other AD scenarios in global warming potential, carcinogenic 
human toxicity, ecotoxicity and particulate matter emission. It performed very similarly as the 
other AD scenarios in all other impact categories. It was found out that these extra savings 
due to use of biogas in vehicle was found to be due to the substituted petrol production. The 
production of biomethane from biogas is a much cleaner process than production of petrol 
from crude oil. Besides, biomethane contains much less chemical compounds than petrol 
hence the emissions from combustion of biomethane are cleaner as well. Moreover, the 
engine used for electricity production in the AD plant only had a conversion efficiency of 
36.4% which means a significant portion of heat energy was lost in electricity production 
from biogas. Therefore, upgrading of biogas to biomethane was more efficient in conserving 
the energy content and as such scenario 2e had much larger savings than other AD scenarios 
in the abovementioned impact categories. 
Lastly, scenario 3, which had an additional biodiesel refinery to treat waste cooking oil, did 
not exhibit any advantage over the other scenarios. Among the anaerobic digestion scenarios, 
scenario 3 performed worst in almost all impact categories. Hence, the results seem to suggest 
that converting waste cooking oil into biodiesel does not appeal in terms of LCA impacts. 
This is mainly because in general, waste cooking oil is a better input material for anaerobic 
digestion than food waste as it has much higher anaerobically digestible biogenic carbon 
==PAGE:49==
37 
 
content and hence greater biogas generation potential than food waste. Similar conclusion was 
also drawn by Long et al. (2012). Therefore, the environmental savings from conversion of 
waste cooking oil into biodiesel was found to be inferior to that from anaerobic digestion of 
waste cooking oil. However, anaerobic digestion of lipids could pose challenges such as 
‘inhibition of acetoclastic and methanogenic bacteria, substrate, and product transport 
limitation, sludge floatation, digester foaming, blockages of pipes and pumps and clogging of 
gas collection and handling systems’ (Long, et al., 2012). These problems would lead to more 
resources needed in maintenance and repair which were not considered in the model. Hence 
in real life, the environmental savings due to anaerobic digestion of waste cooking oil are 
very likely to be lower than that achieved in this study. As such, conversion of waste cooking 
oil into biodiesel could still be a viable alternative.

Global Warming Potential 
Figure 21 displays the sensitivity ratios of parameters tested for scenarios 1, 2a and 3 for 
global warming potential. Water content of food waste was found to be the most sensitive, 
with sensitivity ratios between -6.18 and -20.83, in all 3 scenarios. This means that, when the 
water content of food waste was increased by 10%, the benefits of the scenarios in terms of 
global warming potential decreased by 208.3% in scenario 1, 61.8% in scenario 2a and 89.9% 
in scenario 3. This parameter’s influence was especially large in scenario 1 as savings in 
incineration were very dependent on water content of the waste input. When water content 
becomes higher, more energy is needed to evaporate the water and hence less energy 
recovered. It would even lead to environmental loads instead when the water content reaches 
a certain value. Besides, the more water there is in the waste, the less the solids available for 
combustion will be.  
After the influence of food waste’s water content on the impact values was identified, the 
author tried to search for more precise data. Such data proved to be rare and eventually the 
water content of food waste specified in IUT Singapore’s project design document was used 
in this study. It was chosen as the author considered it to be the most representative for 
Singapore food waste on a national level. 
In scenario 1, there are 4 other parameters that had sensitivity ratios larger than 1: energy 
content of food waste (3.76), electricity conversion efficiency of incinerator (2.48), energy 
content of waste cooking oil (1.21) and ratio of waste cooking out of waste input (1.17). In 
scenario 3, ratio of food waste out of waste input (1.08) and electricity conversion efficiency 
of AD plant (1.07) had sensitivity ratios higher than 1. This means that a change in their value 
leads to a larger relative change in the scenarios’ global warming potential. 
In scenario 2, the next highest sensitivity ratios were from electricity conversion efficiency of 
AD plant (0.94) and methane yield of AD plant (0.85).  
The identification of these sensitive parameters are crucial as it provides information on 
where data collection should be focused on to ensure reliability of results.

7. Discussion 
Limitations 
As previously mentioned, detailed data of food waste in Singapore were rare and hence 
missing data such as chemical composition had to be estimated based on the default values in 
EASETECH. The lack of data meant that it was difficult to decide which parameter values 
were the most representative of the food waste in Singapore. If more of such data was 
available, the choice of parameter values could be done based on the average of different 
sources. Besides, even data of food waste from countries such as Taiwan, which has quite 
similar style of cuisines as Singapore, had significant differences in certain waste composition 
values with the values used in the model. The lack of accurate data on Singapore food waste 
could have impacted the impact values significantly as parameters such as water content and 
zinc content were found to be very influential to the results. 
Several processes such as marginal electricity production from natural gas was based on the 
technology in Malaysia as such data were not available for Singapore in the Ecoinvent 
database. This might have introduced some inaccuracy due to technological differences 
between the two countries. Besides, the marginal electricity production was a key process to 
the impact potentials which further highlight the importance of its accuracy. 
The use-on-land processes employed in the model did not represent the soil profile of 
Singapore and China well as the default process which represented Danish soil was used. 
While the author tried to simulate the use-on-land processes of Singapore using the DNDC 
model, it was cancelled in the end due to lack of data to construct precise model and time 
constraint. The construction of precise model in DNDC required huge amount of data ranging 
from crop area to flooding and irrigation which the author could not collect in time. 
Thus, the accuracy and robustness regarding impacts due to application of digestate on land 
could be low. While use-on-land was the dominating processes in non-carcinogenic human 
toxicity and ecotoxicity, it was found that the large contributions were due to the large 
characterization factors of the heavy metal content used in the calculations. Besides, the 
results of scenario analysis suggested that the soil profile did not play an influential role in the 
results. Hence, the uncertainty related to the soil profile used in the use-on-land processes in 
the model did not impact the impact values significantly. 
On the other hand, the distributions of parameters were too simplified when Monte Carlo 
analysis was conducted as all the parameters tested were arbitrarily assumed to be normally 
distributed. There was not enough data to substantiate these distributions in a statistical sense 
and the assumption was made based on personal judgement. Hence, this could have 
introduced inaccuracy to the results of uncertainty analysis. 
==PAGE:72==
60 
 
Future Recommendations 
In order to ensure high accuracy of LCA results, more data collection on Singapore’s food 
waste composition has to be done. The detailed composition of food waste that is 
representative at the national level should be of the highest priority of data collection for 
future studies on Singapore’s food waste. This could be possible with the upcoming 
biological treatment facility, which will co-digest sludge from water treatment and food waste 
collected from the Clementi district, that is commencing operation in late 2015. The food 
waste collected for treatment in this facility can be used for composition analysis which will 
provide valuable data that is representative of Singapore’s food waste. 
The accuracy and robustness of the results, especially those related to use-on-land processes, 
could be further improved by modelling soil behaviour and reactions using software such as 
DNDC and Daisy. Large quantity of data relating to Singapore’s soil profile, climate and 
plantations will be needed to construct a precise model. 
There are other tools available that can be used to represent expert judgements better than 
single probability distributions in uncertainty propagation. These include min-max intervals, 
fuzzy sets and imprecise probabilities (Clavreul, et al., 2012). Such tools could reduce the 
underestimation of the likelihood of outlier results.

8. Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to assess the impacts on the waste management system due to 
introduction of anaerobic digestion as an alternative treatment option for food waste in 
Singapore. The life cycle impact assessment showed that there was not a single scenario that 
performed better than all other scenarios in all impact categories. All scenarios achieved 
environmental savings in global warming potential and depletion of abiotic fossil resources. 
In terms of global warming potentials, introduction of anaerobic digestion led to greater 
savings than incineration alone. Upgrading of biogas to biomethane for use in vehicle fuels 
proved to achieve even greater savings than utilizing biogas for electricity production. This 
was found to be due to the huge energy loss in electricity production from combustion of 
biogas. On the other hand, conversion of waste cooking oil into biodiesel did not lead to 
significantly more savings overall as diversion of waste cooking oil from anaerobic digestion 
and incineration led to reduction in savings from these 2 processes. This is due to its low 
water content, high energy content and high anaerobically digestible carbon content which 
makes waste cooking oil an ideal input material for incineration and anaerobic digestion.  
The scenario 2e in which biogas is upgraded for use in vehicle also performed best in 
carcinogenic human toxicity, photochemical oxidant formation, freshwater eutrophication, 
depletion of abiotic fossil resources, terrestrial acidification, terrestrial eutrophication and 
particulate matter emission. This was due to savings from avoided production of petrol and 
emissions related to car operations due to substitution by biomethane. The production of 
electricity from biogas proved to be inferior as there is significant energy loss in the 
electricity production process due to the low conversion efficiency of gas engines. 
Contribution analysis showed that use-on-land was the dominating process in non-carciogenic 
human toxicity, ecotoxicity, freshwater eutrophication and marine eutrophication in all 
scenarios that involved anaerobic digestion. Anaerobic digestion and incineration were also 
major contributors to impact potentials, each of them being the dominant process in different 
impact categories. Transport and landfill were found to be not influential to the results and 
their contributions were relatively small.  
Perturbation analysis showed that water content of food waste was the most sensitive 
parameter as it significantly influenced the energy recovery of incineration. Other important 
parameters include electricity conversion efficiency, methane yield of anaerobic digestion 
plant and energy content of food waste. Scenario analysis showed that the marginal electricity 
production and food waste content had significant influence on the results while soil profile 
had negligible influence. 
==PAGE:74==
62 
 
Monte Carlo simulation showed significant overlap in the distribution of global warming 
potentials of scenario 1 and scenario 3 while scenario 2e was found to be certain to have 
larger savings in global warming potentials than other scenarios. Discernibility analysis 
showed that scenario 3 performs better than scenario 1 in global warming potentials 88% of 
the time. 
Uncertainty analysis identified key parameters that have significant influence on the final 
results. These include composition of waste input and technical parameters related to 
important treatment processes such as anaerobic digestion and incineration. Hence, accuracy 
and representativeness of these data are crucial in ensuring reliability and robustness of LCA 
results. Data collection should be focused on these parameters for future studies.
