Abstract
Acrylamide (AAM) is a presumed carcinogen in humans and have been detected
in table olives by high concentrations (up to 2326 µg/kg) related to heat treat-
ment in the production.
No investigation have been performed regarding for-
mation of AAM in olives after cooking, where the olives are exposed to furhter
heat treatment.
Thus, the aim of the study was to describe the formation of
AAM during domestic cooking of olives.
The objective was examined by con-
ducting a preliminary baking experiment, a full factorial baking experiment and
a boiling experiment, using two types of olive with low and high initial content
of AAM respectively. Samples were analyzed by Liquid Chromatography-tandem
mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) to determine the concentration of AAM. High
concentrations of AAM was found by the baking experiments, with up to 18307
µg/kg during baking at 220 °C in 21 minutes. A signiﬁcant eﬀect of tempera-
ture and time on the formation of AAM during baking was discovered, as well
as the interaction between temperature and time. The study suggests that for-
mation of AAM during baking is following a low to moderate increase at short to
medium baking durations (7 and 14 minutes) with increased temperature, whereas
it reaches a maximum followed by a decrease with increased temperature during
long baking times (20 - 21 minutes). Furthermore, the study showed that AAM is
extracted from olives or formed in olives during boiling, depending on the type of
olive.

Formålet blev
undersøgt gennem et forudgående bageforsøg, et "full factorial" bageforsøg og et

1 Introduction
High levels of AAM have been found in table olives produced by principles, which
applies sterilization during processing [1–3]. This is of concern due to the presumed
carcinogenic eﬀects of AAM in humans [4]. Sterilization is not a traditional proce-
dure in table olive production, with Spanish style, and Greek style olives being the
most common traditional processing methods [5]. However, in modern productions
sterilization may be used and is known as California-style olives, which is widely
practiced in the US, but also in European countries, especially Spain [1,2].
AAM content in olive products are related to large variation within countries,
but diﬀerences between countries are not supported by a statistical signiﬁcance.
Olives from the Swiss marked have been analyzed, yielding AAM concentrations
from not detected to 2326 µg/kg [3]. Levels found by the U.S. Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) range from 226 to 1925 µg/kg in olives. In both cases the
concentrations range higher than some products that have attracted more attention
in relation to the concern of AAM exposure. This includes e.g. french fries, for
which concentrations have been detected by up to 1325 µg/kg [6].
Olives do not contain AAM in its raw state. The mechanism behind AAM for-
mation is not fully understood. Studies in the ﬁeld suggest that it is not related
to maillard reaction, which is noramally associated with AAM formation in food.
The most conclusive results about AAM formation in olives are related to pro-
cessing methods, with sterilization being the main step responsible for high AAM

heat treatment. Although olives can be consumed directly, the product ﬁgure in

date, the formation of AAM in olives after cooking have not been investigated.
1.1 | Composition of olives
Olives are single-stone fruits grown on the roboust olive tree, which can grow even
in poor soils due to its alkali and salt tolerance. It is mainly grown in climates
with warm dry summers, where the tree is exposed to sunlight during prolonged
time periods. The olive fruit comprise the stone (endoacrp), pulp (mesocarp) and
outer skin (exocarp). It takes 6 to 7 months for the fruit to grow until the olive is
fully developed [11]. Olives contain relatively high amounts of fat, with 9.8 g/100g
for black olives, as seen in table 1.1.1, compared to 2.9 g/100 g carbohydrates and
1 g/100 g protein [12]. Other sources have found up to 23 g/100 g fat in Spanish
style olives, 18-31 g/100 g in Greek style olives and 9-21 g/100 g in California-style
olives [13].

1.2 | Table olive production
The commercial preparation of table olives can be divided into three types: Treated
olives, natural olives and California style olives, for which speciﬁc principles in the
production are maintained. However, a variety of diﬀerent processing methods
are applied in table olive production, and the main purpose is to remove the
natural bitterness in olives caused by phenolic compounds, especially the glucoside
oleuropein. This aim applies in all table olive productions [1,5,11].
Olives are produced by using either green olives, olives turning color or black olives.
During harvesting, the olives are selected by color and ﬁrmness of the fruit, which
criteria is determined by the speciﬁc production method and purpose of the ﬁnal
product. A ﬁrm product is obtained in the end when using green olives or olives
turning green, whereas the ﬁnal product will be soft when using black ripe olives.
It is important for the quality of the ﬁnal product, that only undamaged fruits are
used in the production. Originally, olives were picked by hand to prevent damaged
fruit, but today’s technology allow mechanical harvesting, which can be even more
eﬃcient to ensure undamaged fruits [1,5].
1.2.1
|
Treated olives
The most common type of treated olives is Spanish style olives, which are pro-
duced by chemically removing bitterness with lye, a 2.0-5.0 % sodium hydroxide
solution, which facilitates hydrolysis of oleuropein. The lye treatment is essential
to remove the bitter taste, but if it preceeds for too long (usually >3 hours), it
can cause peeling of the skin of the fruit. Some cultivars are more susceptible to
break the skin than others. To avoid this issue, the olives are sometimes let to
rest for a period of 24-48 hours, which reduces the risk of damaging the fruit in
the lye treatment. After the lye treatment, some excess of alkali will have pene-
trated into the ﬂesh of the fruit. Olives are therefore washed thoroughly in water
to remove excess alkali. Next processing step is fermentation in brine, which is

acidic compounds, which decreases pH of the solution from higher than 10 to val-

1.2.2
|
Natural style olives
Natural style olives are most commonly associated with Greek style olives, where
only ripe olives are used, because they provide a desirable color of the ﬁnal product,
although green olives or olives turning green can also be used in natural olive
production [5].
The production of Greek style olives begins directly in the brining step without
alkali treatment beforehand. The concentration of the brine is temperature depen-
dent with regards to the production area. Normally a soultion between 8 and 10
% NaCl is used, but in cold areas the solution can be about 6 %. The debittering
is happening by obtaining equilibrium of dissolved oleuropein in the brine. It is
initiated by fermentive compounds inside the olive fruit, which diﬀuse from the
skin, and it is a slow process that last for 8-12 months without the facilitation by
lye treatment. Where the fermentation of treated olives are dominated by lactic
acid bacteria, natural olive fermentation is dominated by yeast, which are present
in the brine throughout the whole process [5].

California-style olives
California-style olives are also known as black ripe olives. The production uses
unripe olives, often green, but through air oxidation the color will turn black.
California-style olive productions are typically large, and the total harvest of olives
are picked one time per year. Since the capacaty of production sites are therefore
not big enough to produce all at once, and the olives are stored in brines of acidic
solution to avoid spoilage. The production begins with lye treatment in a 1-2
% sodium hydroxide.
solution.
The treatment last for 3-7 days in total, but
for 2-24 hours at a time each day. Inbetween treatment with lye, the olives are
oxidized with air in water. This is when the color of the olives turn from green to
black. Olives are furthermore treated with ion salts, because the pigmentation of
black color is easily deteriorated, and the ion salts have a stabilizing eﬀect on the
pigments. The alkaline solution from the brining is then neutralized with either
lactic acid or carbon dioxide, but no fermentation is involved in the production of
California-style olives. Finally, the olives are packed into cans and heat treated in
the last processing step by sterilizing at 115.6 °C for 60 minutes or at 121.1 °C for
50 minutes [1].
1.3 | Acrylamide
AAM is an organic compound in a three carbon structure of one carbon in double
bond and one carbon linked to an amide functional group (carbon attatched to a
double bond to oxygen and an amide, NH2), as illustrated in ﬁgure 1.3.1. Thereby
it is given the formal name, 2-propenamide. The molecular weight is 71.08 g/mol,
and it is an odorless, white crystalline solid form with a melting point of 84.5
°C [14].
AAM is highly soluble in water, but supposedly not very volatile in
water [15,16].
AAM is considered as probably carcinogenic for humans by the International
Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) since 1994, which is well established by

1.4 | Exposure assessment
Because AAM is considered probably carcinogenic for humans, it is not approri-
ate to establish a tolerable daily intake (TDI). There does not exist a maximum
limit of how high a concentration a type of food may contain, but there are rec-
ommendations by European Food Safety Authority (EFSA). Some food items are
recommended to be monitored on a yearly basis for AAM concentrations. Those
could be products like breakfast cereals, coﬀee products or potato crips, which are
known to contain high concentrations and constitue a great proportion of food
types consumed in the human diet [14]. Instead of an establishment of TDI the
assessment of AAM is based on margin of exposure (MOE). The MOE is calcu-

lated by an estimated daily intake of a food stuﬀknown to contain AAM and
Benchmark Dose Level (BMDL)s or no observed eﬀect level (NOEL)s. BMDLs
and NOELs are determined based on animal studies are have been carried out in
relation to diﬀerent neoplastic (tissue cancers) and non-neoplastic eﬀects like re-
productive and developmental eﬀects (non-neoplastic). Depending on which type
of risk is considered, the respective BMDL or NOEL is applied to determine MOE.
In a lot of cases Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA)
rises concern about AAM in food based on the MOE [14]. Based on JECFA evalu-
ations on AAM from 2005, the daily intake of AAM was 1 µg/kg b.w. per day for
the general population and 4 µg/kg b.w. per day for consumers with high intake
in European countries. The numbers included also children. The daily intake was
based on AAM exposure from diﬀerent sources of food [14]. Consumption data
available from DTU, gives an estimate of the intake of olives alone in the Danish
population. For children in the age of 4-14 years, the mean estimated intake of
olives is 1.8 g/day and 5.7 g/day for children with high intake. Adults consume 3.9
g/day based on the mean and 10.9 for high intake. The data is based on consumers
of olives and does not regard people who does not eat olives which constitutes 35
% of the Dansih population [24]. This can be used to determine the MOE by
considering concentrations of AAM in olives. The MOE is calculated by dividing
BMDL with estimated daily intake over a life-time. If the MOE is less than 10000,
it is an indication of toxicological concern.
1.5 | Acrylamide formation and precursors
AAM formation in heat treated food is usually connected to the maillard reaction
between reducing sugars and free amino acids (especially asparagine). This is well
established for starch rich product and supported by several studies [21,22,25]. The
reaction occurs typically at temperatures above 120 °C and with low moisture, and
investigation has shown that AAM is not formed in boiled food [19,23].
Investigational studies of AAM in table olives, shows no evidence of correlation be-
8
==PAGE:26==
Dagnæs-Hansen

tween AAM formation and the maillard reaction, even though it has been studied
with regards to several diﬀerent free amino acids and glucose [2,26]. In addition
to this, table olives does not necessarily contain sugar, because it should be con-
sumed during fermentation in the processing. However, residual glucose have been
detected in some cases, as the sole reducing sugar found in table olives. Several
amino acids have been found in olives, although in low amount. They include
asparagine, glutamate, arginine, serine and glutamine among others [2].
How-
ever, analyzes of AAM in raw and extracted olives during diﬀerent types of heat
treatment indicates, that AAM formation in olives is not initiated by the gen-
erally recognized precursors (i.e. free asparagine and reducing sugars) for AAM
formation. In these analyzes, concentrations of AAM were measured after heat
treatment in relation to initial levels of free asparagine and reducing sugars. The
underlying study showed that raw olives contained 30 mg/kg free aparagine and
10500 mg/kg reducing sugars (glucose and fructose) before sterilization, whereas
extracted olives had low contents of about 1.4 mg/kg and 180 mg/kg respectively.
After heat treatment in GC oven at 120 °C for 40 min, respectively 4680 µg/kg
and 3107 µg/kg AAM was formed in raw and extracted olives. After sterilization
in a pilot sterilizing unit (heating in water in closed glass jar in a pressure cooker)
for 10 min at 121 °C, the AAM formation was 2100 µg/kg and 1090 µg/kg for raw
and extracted olives respectively [3].
It is well established, that water activity is an important factor related to AAM
formation in foods. Studies have shown that low water activity leads to higher for-
mation rate [27–29]. However, AAM can also form in large quantities at high water
activity (aw close to 1.0) in olives during heat treatment of moderate temperatures
(maximum 120 °C) [3].
Even though AAM formation occur during heat treatment, it does not develop in
a linear relationship with increased temperature. This is found by several stuides
on coﬀee roasting, where results show that medium roast coﬀee contain higher
concentrations of AAM than dark roasted coﬀee [30, 31]. In addition to this, it
is well established that AAM degradation also occur in food processing, and that

initiatives can be made in a food processing plant to reduce the concentration of
AAM in ﬁnal products [29,32].
Latest research on AAM precursors in table olives shows promising results of the
inﬂuence of small peptides on the formation of AAM after sterilization, although
the exact mechanism is not fully understood [26]. It is moreover discovered that
levels of glucosamines in olives before sterilization is strongly correlated to AAM
formation after sterilization in California-style olives [33].
1.6 | Inﬂuence of processing on acrylamide
formation
In the production of table olives, the individual processing steps have great impact
on the level of acrylamide in the ﬁnal product. Sterilizaion is the main processing
step, where AAM is formed, during production of black ripe olives (i.e. California-
style olives) [34]. Besides the inﬂuence of sterilization, other processing steps seems
also to play an important role. During lye treatment, the pH level is correlated
with AAM contents of the ﬁnal product of sterilized olives. High pH above 12 can
reduce the level of AAM in the ﬁnal product [33].
AAM concentration in olives changes throughout the production line, with highest
concentraion in the raw fruit at the beginning of the process, and then decrceased
AAM levels are observed to the soaking step and furhter in the brine, which is
especially a reducing step, due to the extraction of precursors and the eﬀect of low
pH. Values of AAM have been detected in in the brine of some canned olives to 540 -
910 µg/kg [3]. It is suggested that prolonged storage in brine before processing into
California-style table olives has a positive eﬀect in decreasing AAM concentration
of the ﬁnal product, although a short storage in brine (1 month) leads to increased
AAM concentration of the end product compared to olives that are processed fresh.
The composition of brine solution does also have an impact on the AAM level.
Calcium chloride is a common ion salt to apply in the brining process, because

of its stabilizing eﬀect on the cell wall of the fruit, which keeps a ﬁrm texture.
However, there might be a side eﬀect from this beneﬁt, that AAM precursors
are retained in the fruit when the cell wall is strengthened [1]. Furthermore, air
oxidation is known to increases the concentraion of AAM in the ﬁnal product
signiﬁcantly, whereas lye treatment signiﬁcantly decreases AAM concentration [1].
Process time does also play a role of AAM formation, since it has been shown that
concentrations increase with time, when heat treated by pressure cooking in water
in glass jar. Moreover, it is evident that AAM extracts from the olive into the
water, when undergoing this type of treatment [3]. During storage of table olives,
the level of AAM does not change, thus it can be considered stable in this product
as in opposition to other products known to contain AAM [2].
1.7 | Aim of study
The aim of the study is to investigate AAM formation during domestic cooking of
black olives.
Since AAM is already known to form in the production of table olives due to heat
treatment, it is likely that heat treatment during domestic cooking will also have
an impact on the formation.
Simple cooking experiments of baking and boiling will be performed to simulate
domestic cooking. LC-MS/MS analysis will be used to quantify the concentration
of AAM in the olives.

The origin of olives might diﬀer in each batch, since delivery can be from diﬀer-
ent manufacturers. This is more likely to explain the large lot to lot variation
discovered for "Budget" olives.
In all the analyzed brands of table olives, the aw was high (0.972 - 0.998). This
should be a limiting factor of AAM formation found by several studies [27–29],
although not related to olives. When a high concentration is detected in two of
the table olive products in the current study, it is however an indication, that
AAM can be formed even in conditions of high aw. This observation is moreover
supported by another large study, investigating the AAM formation in olives during
heat treament [3].
By the investigation of AAM in the brine of "Budget" and "Vores" table olives,
which were selected for the experimental work related to cooking, the concentration
was found to be remarkably higher in the brine compared to the olives.
This
result supports the ﬁndings of other studies, and reinforce the fact that brining is
a reducing step of AAM formation in olives during production, by indicating that
AAM is extracted from the olives into the brine [1,3].

Preliminary baking experiment
The preliminary baking experiment of 20 minutes baking yielded high concentra-
tions of AAM in the olives, especially during a temperature of 210 °C (9490 µg/kg
and 8163 µg/kg for "Budget" and "Vores" respectively). The concentrations ex-
ceeds the levels found by other studies (up to 2326 µg/kg), which investigated
AAM concentration in olives during heat treatment related to the production of
olives [1–3]. The high concentration found by the present study, is an indication
that domestic cooking of olives causes further formation of AAM in olives, than is
already formed during production.

From the preliminary baking experiment, some interesting patterns in the forma-
tion of AAM was observed in the two type of olives. The formation in "Budget"
olives was higher at the middle tempearture than the highest temperature, which
is in agreement with investigations of coﬀee beans, where AAM formation is de-

factor than temperature [31]. When the opposite pattern is observed for "Vores"
olives, it might be explained by the diﬀerence in composition of the olives rather
than the initial concentration of AAM in the table olives, since both type of olives
has a high formation of AAM at the middle temperature (210 °C) in the experi-

The results from the preliminary baking experiment indicate that there is an upper
limit to how much AAM is formed during a baking time of 20 minutes. With three
levels of the temperature, only a limited pattern of the concentration is presented.
It would be necessary to include more data points, to determine the maximum
concentration of AAM during baking of olives and how it behaves mathematically.
The decrease of aw in the olives from 150 to 210 °C could indicate, that the
extreme high formation of AAM is observed in both olives at 210 °C is facilitated
by lower aw. However, this is contradictory to the observation of baking at 270
°C, especially for "Vores" olives, since the AAM content decreases, while the aw
also decreases quite radical. In addition, the examinations from ﬁgure 3.2.1 also
supports the proposition, that high amounts of AAM can be formed at high aw,
becuase the concentration of AAM is high (789 µg/kg) for "Budget" olives even at
150 °C, where the aw is high. The aw does therefore not seem suitable to explain
the formation of AAM, bacause it does not show a certain pattern.

and "Budget" olives baked in 20 minutes at 210 °C) had a aw below the reference
value (0.500) for powders (prescribed by the Aqualab Pre Water Activity Analyzer
(Meter Group Inc., WA, USA)). This is an indication, that some baking conditions
(270 °C, 20 minutes) lead to dry products, possibly not suitable for consumption.

With regards to a domestic concern of AAM formation in olives, it is noteworthy
to consider the sensory state of the product. Even if the concentration of AAM
is high at some conditions related to cooking, it does not matter from a domestic
point of view, if the olives are not edible. Then, the high AAM concentration is
not a problem, because the food would not be consumed anyway. Meanwhile, the
observation of a high AAM content in olives is interesting from a scientiﬁc point of
view, because it provides information of how much AAM can be formed in olives
at certain parameters during heat treatment.
4.2 | Baking experiment
By comparing the full factorial design with the preliminary baking experiment,
the results indicate that the pattern of AAM formation varies between olives in
relation to diﬀerent baking conditions. The study indicates that increased baking
time does not necessarily lead to increased formation of AAM. It is increased
with temperature at baking times of 7 and 14 minutes, but when baking at 20 -
21 minutes, the concentration decreases at high temperatures (260 - 270 °C). The
present study is not able to explain the diﬀerence in patterns, but factors that could
be investigated is those known to be diﬀerent from product to product as described
in the litterature [3,26,33]. Such factors could be composition diﬀerences, initial
AAM content or conditions related to the processing if the olives are produced by
diﬀerent principles.
Moreover, it was found by the full factorial experiment that patterns of AAM for-
mation during baking vaires a lot within the same type of olive, when prepared
by diﬀerent baking conditions. During baking times of 21 minutes, the formation
of AAM seems to decrease in olives, based on observations from olives of low and
high initial content of AAM. At shorter baking time, i.e. 7 and 14 minutes, the
formation seems to increase by increased temperature. In general, these observa-
tions indicate that the temperature could be a reducing factor on the formation

of AAM in olives. Whether or not this would be a useful initiative in reducing
the exposure of AAM from domestic cooking, depends on the sensory state of the
olives when prepared by prolonged and more extreme parameters during cooking
as discussed in section 4.1.2. Also in the full factorial baking experiment, some
olive samples yielded water activites below 0.500, indicating that the product is

The statistical analysis of the results obtained by the full factorial design showed
that temperature and time has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the concentration of AAM in
olives during baking. The eﬀect of interaction between temperature and time was
even more signiﬁcant. This means that it would be possible to control the forma-

pattern was explored.
Since "Budget" olives does not contain carbohydrates as stated by table 3.2.4, the
results suggest that carbohydrates may not act as precursor for AAM formation
in olives, because high concentrations are formed during the experiments of the

This observation is contradicting with several studies of AAM
formation in other food matrices [21,22].
4.3 | Boiling of olives
The boiling experiment showed that AAM is not formed in large amounts in boiled
olives, even at long boiling times (up to 3 hours). It might be due to the lower
temperature of boling compared to baking, since 100 °C is only a third of the
lowest temperature applied in the baking experiments of the present study.
The stability of AAM concentration in "Budget" olives (43 - 56 µg/kg) during
boiling corresponds well to the observations of stable water content and aw. The
detected concentrations were lower than the initial content of "Budget" table olives
(373 µg/kg), as presented in table 3.2.3. This indicates that AAM is extracted from
the olives to the water during cooking. Another study found similar observations,

but showed also that AAM extracted from olives to the water is correlated with
time during heating [3]. The present study found also that the concentration of
AAM in boiled water increases, but it is more likely to occur as a consequence
of evaporated water, since the AAM concentration in "Budget" olives is stable
throughout the three boiling time, and "Vores" olives only shows an increase of
AAM from 6 µg/kg (initial content as seen in table 3.2.3) to 252 µg/kg during
boiling. The increase of AAM in the water is therfore not only related to the
extraction from the olives.
To avoid AAM to extract from the olives to the surrounding food in domestic
cooking, e.g. when preparing a stew, an option could be to boil the olives in water
to extract the AAM before adding them to the stew. Similar to when quinoa is
washed to extract bitter compounds.
4.4 | Conclusive remarks
The study suggests that the formation of AAM in olives could be even more pro-
nounced during domestic cooking of olives, than is already discovered from the
conditions during processing of table olives where heat treatment is applied. The
highest amounts were detected in Greek olives after baking, where the concentra-
tion reached up to 18307 µg/kg. The formation in Greek olives were generally
higher than Spanish olives, for which the highest detected concentration was 9490
µg/kg after baking. Throughout the experiments simulating domestic cooking of
olives, the concentration of AAM ranged between 20 µg/kg and 13374 µg/kg, apart
from the highest detected concentrations.
During baking times in the area of 20 minutes, the study indicates that the forma-
tion of AAM in black olives reaches a maximum concentration at temperatures of
210 - 220 °C. This observation requires conﬁrmation by investigation of additional
temperatures to determine, at which temperature the maximum formation occur.
The study suggests that the combination of temperature and time is a determining

of these factors are very signiﬁcant. The eﬀect of the single factors, i.e. tem-
