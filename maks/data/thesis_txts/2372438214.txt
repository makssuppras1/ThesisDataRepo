Abstract 
i 
 
Abstract 
Spatial localization (i.e., “imaging”) of geochemical heterogeneities is crucial for improved 
understanding and effective management of a number of groundwater contamination problems. This 
master thesis focuses on the study of pyrite oxidation under controlled experimental conditions and 
aims at demonstrating the possibility of imaging pyrite inclusions from distributed measurements of 
water quality parameters. 
The investigation is carried out at various scales including batch experiments, 1D column experiments 
(14.5 cm) and a 2D tank experiment (80 × 40 × 0.5 cm). The pyrite oxidation rate with oxygen as 
electron acceptor is defined based on the study of the batch and column experiments. The calibration 
of the kinetic parameters is run through the coupling of the geochemical code IPhreeqC and Matlab. 
Spatially distributed measurements of oxygen and pH are performed using high-resolution non-
invasive optical sensors inside the porous medium and iron, sulphate and sulphur concentrations are 
analyzed for validation of the stoichiometry of the reactions. Models for the 1D column and 2D tank 
setups are built by combining the finite-difference modeling tool MODFLOW and the 
multicomponent reactive transport code PHT3D. The reactive model is validated based on two 
column experiments with distinct locations and concentrations of pyrite inclusions, as well as on a 
2D tank experiment. Forward modeling of both experimental setups reveals good fits between the 
observations of steady state oxygen concentrations and the simulations, which suggests the 
calculation of the pyrite oxidation kinetic parameters is accurate. The steady state spatial profile of 
oxygen in the column experiments allowed for a successful estimation of the location and 
concentration of pyrite inclusions along the columns using the Principal Component Geostatistical 
Approach (PCGA) for inverse modeling.

1. Introduction 
1 
 
1. Introduction 
The spatial distribution of physical and geochemical heterogeneities in the subsurface is of key 
importance for assessing groundwater contamination. The localization and quantification of reactive 
minerals is critical in areas where redox conditions may be altered, and thus influencing the release 
of contaminants. Since the early 80’s, researchers have focused on the spatial distribution estimation 
of aquifer physical properties such as hydraulic conductivity or specific storage (Cardiff et al., 2012; 
Cardiff et al., 2009; Zhu and Yeh, 2005; Revil et al., 2012; Slater, 2007; Kowalsky et al., 2004), 
however only very few studies have addressed the spatial distribution of geochemical heterogeneities. 
In 2016, Fakhreddine et al. proposed using reactive transport modeling with the goal of imaging 
geochemical heterogeneities in the subsurface, specifically focusing on the spatial distribution of 
arsenic-bearing minerals. This Master thesis uses the same novel approach, by combining water 
quality measurements with forward and inverse reactive modeling for identifying natural sources of 
contamination in the subsurface.  
The need to image physical and geochemical heterogeneities in the subsurface has risen with the 
awareness of mobilization of metalloids and heavy-metals from geogenic inclusions and human-
based activities. An example is the natural mobilization of arsenic (As) in the subsurface, a problem 
of enormous relevance, which affects the health of more than 100 million people worldwide 
(Worldbank, 2005). The problem is of greater importance in poor regions of south Asia where the 
local population relies exclusively on local drinking water wells due to the lack of alternatives. 
Chronic arsenic toxicity in regions where the arsenic content in drinking water wells is higher than 
the World Health Organization (WHO) limit (50 g/L) was analyzed statistically by Mazumder and 
Dasgupta (2011). Symptoms of arsenic poisoning appear after 6 months to 2 years or more of 
exposure: hyperpigmentation, arsenical keratosis or skin cancer symptoms.  
The number of peer-reviewed articles on arsenic contamination in groundwater has significantly 
increased in the last two decades, elucidating important mechanisms of arsenic release and transport 
(e.g. Postma et al., 2007; Sharma et al., 2010; Sharma et al., 2014). Arsenic is naturally occurring in 
the subsurface and it is released from the dissolution of As-bearing minerals, which are geogenic 
sources of arsenic pollution (Figure 1). The oxidative dissolution of As-bearing reactive minerals 
(such as arsenopyrite) has been identified as one of the main causes of arsenic release in natural and 
managed aquifers (e.g., Appelo and Postma, 2005; Kocar et al., 2008). This process is especially 
studied where groundwater management strategies such as Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) or

the impact of arsenic release at bench scale depending on the principle anions present (chloride or

As mobilization did not decline with subsequent cycles.

Another widely studied heavy-metal mobilization in groundwater is nickel. Larsen and Postma (1997) 
studied nickel mobilization in a pyritic soil in Aarhus, Denmark, where it appeared that the high 
concentration of Mn(II) accentuated the release of nickel in the groundwater. Nickel would bind to 
the manganese oxides and be further released by dissolution of the oxides due to the presence of 
Fe(II). 
Localization of reactive minerals such as pyrite and As-pyrite is, thus, crucial for a number of 
subsurface contamination problems. The present work focuses on the mineral pyrite (FeS2). Pyrite is 
a ubiquitous mineral in Earth and one of the most common sulfide mineral. The oxidation of pyrite 
plays an important role from an environmental perspective. It is directly related to the release of 
metalloids in groundwater aquifers (Walker et al., 2006; Neil et al., 2014).  The oxidation of pyrite 
can also play an important role in denitrification in sandy aquifers of agricultural areas (Jessen et al., 
2017). Williamson and Rimstidt (1994) compiled data from the literature to propose a valid rate law

for pyrite oxidation with dissolved oxygen and thoroughly studied oxidation in presence of ferric iron 
(Fe3+) under both oxic and anoxic conditions. The reactivity of pyrite inclusions in the subsurface has 
been studied and assessed in cases where groundwater resources management have been 
implemented. Prommer and Stuyfzand (2005) successfully modelled the transport of compounds 
released from the pyrite oxidation which occurred when intentionally injecting an oxic solution at 
300 m depth into a previously anoxic environment. 
1.1. Reactive transport and inverse modeling 
Reactive transport modeling is an essential tool for the analysis of a variety of processes affecting 
Earth systems including contaminant retardation in the subsurface, fluxes between the major Earth 
reservoirs, or even the treatment of deep Earth processes such as magma transport (Steefel et al., 
2005). Reactive transport modeling have been applied to a wide number of groundwater 
contamination problems including: petroleum hydrocarbons and chlorinated solvent spills, landfill 
leachate contamination, heavy metals release and arsenic contamination (Prommer et al. 2009; Rolle 
et al. 2008; Li et al. 2010; Li et al. 2011). Combined with the appropriate inverse modeling tool, good 
approximates of the subsurface parameters can be obtained. The inverse modeling procedure adapted 
to this Master thesis problematic is the Principal Component Geostatistical Approach, first introduced 
by Kitanidis and Lee (2014) for large-dimensional inverse problems. This method is based on the 
geostatistical inverse method, which is widely used for property calibration problems (Kitanidis, 
1997; Kitanidis, 1995; Snodgrass and Kitanidis, 1997). The PCGA inversion is a fast large-scale 
inversion approach that avoids direct evaluation of the Jacobian matrix, reducing by a factor of 10 
the number of simulations needed for the inversion in most cases (Kitanidis and Lee, 2014; 
Fakhreddine et al., 2016). 
1.2. Objectives 
The overall objective of this project is to prove the capability of imaging geogenic pyrite inclusions 
in the subsurface, using measurements of common water quality parameters perturbed by the 
geochemical reactions. The present work is based on a tight coupling between laboratory experiments 
and modeling investigation. The laboratory setups aim at simulating the oxidative dissolution of 
pyrite in high-resolution flow-through experiments while introducing an oxic solution into a 
previously anoxic system. The experiments are performed in 1D column and 2D tank setups, packed 
with sand in which one or two pyrite inclusions of varying location and concentrations are embedded. 
The monitoring of the reaction is performed with non-invasive sensor foils of oxygen and pH based

on an optical innovative technique (PreSens GmbH). These sensors allow for hundreds of distributed 
measurements of both oxygen and pH within a flow-through system (Haberer et al., 2015; Rolle et 
al., 2010), which carry the information relevant to identify the location and magnitude of the pyrite 
inclusions. Besides the non-invasive sensors, conventional sampling and analyses are also performed 
at the outlet ports of the setup. A PCGA-based approach (Kitanidis and Lee, 2014; Fakhreddine et 
al., 2016) for inverse reactive transport modeling will be developed to integrate the distributed sensor 
measurements performed in the laboratory setup and the forward geochemical model. 
Pyrite oxidation kinetics are studied precisely to ensure a correct implementation of the reactions in 
the transport modeling. Batch experiments are performed to observe the oxygen depletion and obtain 
a quantitative description for the kinetics of pyrite oxidation. Analyses of the chemical composition 
of the batch experiments over the time also allow confirming the occurrence of the reactive processes.

2. Materials and Methods 
Bench scale experiment setups were used in this master thesis to simulate pyrite oxidative dissolution 
in groundwater aquifers. 1D column and 2D tank experiments were run with one or two pyrite 
inclusions of different location and magnitude. Estimation of the mineral inclusions was performed 
with the coupling of a reactive transport model with a PCGA-based method for inverse modeling and 
the development of both models was based on and validated with the outcomes of the flow-through 
experiments. The reactive transport model included kinetically-based reactions and chemical 
equilibrium. In this chapter, the experimental setups, forward and inverse modeling as well as the 
model-based design of the experiments are presented. 
2.1. Experimental setup 
The cleaning of pyrite, analytical methods and experimental setups are described in this section. The 
methodology summarizes the knowledge gained from performing batch, column and tank 
experiments. 
2.1.1. Pyrite preparation 
The pyrite used in the following experiments is provided by Ward’s Science.  The mineral was 
crushed with a mortar to obtain a grain size between 125 and 250 m. Approximately twice the 
amount needed for the experiments was crushed as more than half of it was lost in a finer grain size 
through the sieve. For a better contact between the surface of the mineral and the cleaning solutions, 
a conical flask of 1L was used. The cleaning was realised before packing the column or tank 
experiment, to prevent the contact of the mineral with the atmosphere. The methodology was initially 
based on Parthasarathy et al. (2014). 
The pyrite was rinsed five times with Ethanol 50% (v/v) with sonication cycles of 3 minutes each. 
After each sonication, the supernatant was removed by decantation. The pyrite was dried under a slow 
flow of N2. Afterwards, the mineral was treated with HCl (37%) for 5 minutes, with suitable agitation 
to allow a better contact between the grains and the concentrated acid. It was rinsed three times with 
distilled water (3 minutes each) and with Ethanol 50% (v/v) for 1 minute. The pyrite was then dried 
under a constant N2 flow, and left under anoxic conditions until used in the experiments.  
2.1.2. Analytical instrumentation 
The ability to monitor water quality of an experiment in areas where the water is not accessible with 
a conventional electrode is very attractive for this research case. A high-resolution non-invasive

optode technique based on O2 and pH sensitive sensors was used to perform distributed measurements 
within the porous medium with a resolution of up to 2.5 mm. Both types of sensor foils were provided 
by the company PreSens, which is specialised in sensor systems since its creation in 1997. 
Conventional analyses of iron, sulphate and sulphur provided additional information on the reaction 
pathways occurring in the setups. Analyses of iron for the batch experiments were performed with 
Atomic Absorption Spectroscopy (AAS). Analyses of iron and total sulphur for column and tank 
experiments were performed with Inductively Coupled Plasma - Optical Emission Spectroscopy 
(ICP-OES). Both instruments required samples with 1% HNO3, and approximately 15 mL of sample. 
Sulphate analyses were performed with conventional Ion Chromatography (IC). 
2.1.2.1. 
The oxygen sensor foil 
Optical sensing of oxygen is based on the effect of dynamic luminescence quenching by molecular 
oxygen. The excited state of the luminophore will be attenuated by the collision of molecular oxygen 
at the surface of the sensor. Energy is transferred from the sensor to the molecular oxygen, 
deactivating emission of light (Klimant and Wolfbeis, 1995). This effect is called collisional or 
dynamic quenching (Figure 2). 
 
Figure 2: Illustration of the dynamic quenching mechanism with molecular oxygen (1): Absence of oxygen; (2) 
Deactivation of the signal by transfer of energy to the molecular oxygen. 
The oxygen concentration in a sample is related to the luminescence intensity, as well as the 
luminescence lifetime (Klimant and Wolfbeis, 1995). Monitoring of oxygen spatial profiles using 
oxygen sensor foils in porous media has already been used to investigate the impact of fluctuating 
capillary fringes on oxygen transfer (Haberer et al., 2015a) or for the study of transverse dispersion 
experiments with pore-scale modeling interpretation (Rolle et al., 2012).

2.1.2.2. 
The pH sensor foil 
The measurement of pH with the sensor system is based on fluorescence intensity. A phase value is 
measured by the optical signal going to the sensor foil which is then converted to a pH value. The 
pH-sensitive materials are deposited over a transparent polyester support. A cross section of the 
membrane is shown in Figure 3. The upper part of the membrane consists of a phosphorescent 
luminophore ruthenium (II)-4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline (± 10 m particles) added to a sample 
containing pH-sensitive fluorescent indicator. The fluorescent indicator consists of a 
poly(acrylonitrile)-based core. The mixture is deposited on the polyester support by the means of a 
hydrogel shell (Liebsch et al., 2001; Werner et al., 1999). Current pH foils are at the stage of a 
prototype β version, and a collaboration with PreSens is in place for testing and assessing the pH 
foil’s applications. 
 
Figure 3: Schematic representation of the transverse profile of a pH foil (Liebsch et al., 2001). 
2.1.3. Batch experiments 
The reaction framework in the reactive model required information about the kinetics of pyrite 
oxidation, and batch experiments were proposed to obtain a reliable estimate of the pyrite oxidation 
rate as well as monitoring the reaction products. A batch experiment consisted of up to 15 small 
bottles with a total volume of 26.8 mL (Figure 4). Each bottle was prepared with 1.0 g of pyrite and 
20.0 mL of deionized water. For comparative purposes, a blank sample was run at the same time. The 
bottles were hermetically sealed with butyl/PTFE “Pharma Fix” 20 mm caps. The systems were left 
under constant agitation until oxygen depletion was observed. Oxygen concentration was measured 
at a constant rate of 5 minutes for one bottle, and every 24 hours for all other bottles. Oxygen values 
were measured three times for reliability.

The sampling for sulphate, iron and pH measurements implied to remove and loose two bottles per 
day. With a syringe and a filter of 0.2 mm pore size, 1 mL was sampled for sulphate analysis and 
around 15 mL were taken for iron analysis. The pH of the sample was measured before adding 1 mL 
HNO3 (68%) for iron analysis. 
2.1.4. Column experiments 
Column experiments for simulating pyrite oxidative dissolution were carried out in glass columns of 
length 14.5 cm and diameter 1.7 cm (Figure 5). 1D flow through conditions were established with a 
high-precision peristaltic pump (Ismatec IPC-N) and one needle at the inlet of the columns. 
Homogeneous flow was obtained between the chemical phases using sand as porous media, sieved at 
the same grain size than the pyrite inclusions (125-250 m). The sand was left 24h in a pH 4 solution 
(HCl based, Sigma-Aldrich) and washed 10 times with distilled water prior to use. Monitoring of the 
oxygen and protons transport was performed with high-resolution sensor foils (PreSens) placed inside 
the porous media, and water samples for analysis of iron and sulphur were taken at the outlet. Inlet 
solutions of the experiments were prepared with 5 mM NaCl. The experimental work was performed 
in a thermostatic room at 20°C.

2.2. Modeling 
Two tools were used for simulating the experiments performed in the laboratory. The geochemical 
code PhreeqC was used for both batch and 1D reactive transport simulations (Parkhurst and Appelo, 
1999) whereas 1D and 2D reactive transport models for the inversion were built using the finite-
difference groundwater modeling tool MODFLOW (Harbaugh, 2005) combined with the 
geochemical capabilities of PHT3D (Prommer and Post, 2010). The reaction framework for pyrite 
oxidation is described in the next section, followed by the two reactive models implemented. The 
PCGA-based inverse modeling procedure is described at last.

2.2.2. Batch and 1D reactive transport modeling in PhreeqC 
Batch experiments were modeled in PhreeqC using a simple batch calculation, considering a closed 
system at constant volume and oxygen present in both gas and liquid phase. The PhreeqC code was 
implemented in Matlab using the module IPhreeqC-COM (Charlton and Parkhurst, 2011), which 
allows for the parametrization and calibration of the pyrite oxidation kinetic parameters in Eq. 6. The 
minimization was performed with the calculation of the RMSE for all measurements available.  
Additionally, column experiments were also modeled using the geochemical code PhreeqC as a 
reactive transport of the oxygen through a 1D column. The column length was discretized in 145 
cells. The code was also implemented in IPhreeqC/ Matlab allowing for calibration of the kinetic 
parameters. The outlet oxygen concentration of a column was set as optimizing condition for the 
parameter estimation, using the RMSE value as objective function.

2.2.4. Inverse modeling 
The inverse modeling procedure allows for an estimation of the pyrite inclusions location and 
magnitude using steady state concentrations of oxygen from the experimental setups. Fakhreddine et 
al. (2016) successfully assessed the inversion of 1D and 2D flow-through modelled data using the 
Principal Component Geostatistical Approach (PCGA) for large-dimensional inverse problems, first 
introduced by Kitanidis and Lee (2014).  PCGA was also implemented for performing the inversion

of the experimental setups in this project, as it improves the computational requirements of the 
conventional Geostatistical Approach (GA). The observation equation is the core of the GA and 
PCGA methods, and it is described as: 
𝑦= ℎ(𝑠) + 𝑣 
[Eq. 16] 
where 𝑦 is the data vector, ℎ is the mapping from Rm to Rn, 𝑠 is the vector of unknown and 𝑣 is 
Gaussian term accounting for the error between the experimental data and the model. This last 
parameter accounts for the random data generated in a first place through the covariance matrix. In 
this research, 𝑦 is referred as the oxygen data, ℎ is the reactive transport model and 𝑠 is the 
concentration of pyrite spatially distributed. An iterative Gauss-Newton method is repeatedly 
changing the initial guess of pyrite distribution in the model, allowing for the fitting of the reactive 
forward model results to the experimental data. To enforce the positivity in the estimation, the square 
root of the pyrite concentration was taken as the vector of unknown s.

calculation of the full covariance matrix and using it precisely around the area of interests. The

3.1. Batch experiments 
Batch experiments were performed to study the kinetics of pyrite oxidation by dissolved oxygen. 
Figure 10 shows the time-dependent concentrations of oxygen, pH, iron and sulphate during the 
reaction. Oxygen depletion in all four batch experiments was observed. Batch experiments 1, 2 and 
3 reached anoxic conditions after 4 - 4.5 days. For the same experiments and at the same time, steady 
state concentrations of iron and sulphate were reached at approximately 65 mg/L and 35 mg/L 
respectively. The pH decreased drastically in the first day of the experiments, as expected from the 
release of protons in the pyrite reaction with oxygen (Eq. 1). The stoichiometry of pyrite reaction 
with oxygen as electron acceptor would predict a higher concentration of sulphate in the batch system 
(Eq. 1). Quantitative assessment of the stoichiometry was made with the simulation of the batch 
experiments in PhreeqC (See section 4.1.1.). Another interesting feature observed was the increase 
of pH occurring after the three batch solutions have become anoxic. It is reasonable to think at this 
stage that sulphate was subject to other physico-chemical pathways in the system, and most likely 
when the systems reached anoxic conditions.

3.3. Tank experiments 
To visualize the effect of different pyrite inclusion sizes and concentrations on the shape of the oxygen 
depleted plume, several reactive transport simulations were performed. A single inclusion of 16 × 4 
cm with a concentration of 12.85 mol/L of pyrite was found optimal for this first trial of the 2D tank 
setup. The seepage velocity was set to 1.5 m/day to be consistent with the velocity of the column 
experiments. Oxygen and pH sensor foils were used for a high-resolution measurement for both

4.2. Inverse modeling

5. Conclusions 
36 
 
5. Conclusions 
The present work focused on obtaining an estimate of spatial distribution of pyrite inclusions in 
controlled laboratory experiments combining water quality measurements with reactive transport 
modeling and a recently introduced PCGA-based inverse modeling technique. The experiments 
simulated oxidative dissolution of pyrite inclusions in a 1D column and a 2D tank setup with high-
resolution monitoring of oxygen and pH in the porous media and conventional sampling for water 
characterization.  
The kinetics of pyrite oxidation were defined based on batch and column experimental setups, using 
the coupling of IPhreeqC and Matlab for parameter estimation. The stoichiometry of iron and oxygen 
was successfully simulated, and sulphate concentrations in the batch experiments revealed a probable 
co-existence with intermediates of the pyrite oxidation reaction. Reactive transport models were built 
combining MODFLOW and PHT3D to simulate the pyrite dissolution in the 1D column and 2D tank 
experiments performed in the laboratory. Three column experiments (14.5 cm) were prepared with 
different locations and magnitudes of pyrite inclusions and allowed for calibration and validation of 
the reactive transport model built. A 2D flow-through chamber (80 × 40 × 0.5 cm) was used to 
experimentally simulate the oxidation of a single pyrite inclusion (16 × 4 cm). High-resolution non-
invasive sensor foils for oxygen and pH delivered spatially distributed profiles along the columns and 
tank at transient and steady state conditions. The simulated oxygen profiles with the reactive transport 
model fitted well the observed data at steady state conditions in both 1D and 2D setups. pH sensor 
strips appeared to be extremely sensitive to long-term use at low ionic strength solutions and could 
only provide qualitative information with the spatial profiles. 
The PCGA-based inverse modeling of the three column experiments, coupled with the validated 
reactive transport model, was applied to the experimental data obtained for estimation of the spatial 
distribution of pyrite inclusions. Inclusions’ location and magnitude were successfully estimated for 
all three experiments, capturing the true inclusion within the 95% confidence interval of the estimate. 
The oxygen profiles obtained with the estimates reproduced very well the experimental observations. 
Sharp boundaries of the experimental inclusions were hard to capture in the inversion, as the 
algorithm uses a Gaussian-like distribution in its calculations. The continuation of this research will 
focus on the inversion of the spatially distributed oxygen data obtained in the 2D tank experiment, as 
well as on improving the imaging of sharp boundaries of mineral inclusions.
