Abstract
This study investigates the use of Direct-Quadrature-Zero (DQ0) transformation and
Data-Driven Reference Modelling in detecting faults in a bearing-rotor interface in a
test rig when only using phase currents. The methods are purely data-driven with-
out cumbersome and computationally heavy models seeking to exploit advances in
computational capabilities. A series of healthy (non-defect) baseline runs are used
to determine unique model thresholds that allow the methods to detect failures as
they are introduced to the system. Possible implementation of real-time adapta-
tions of the methods has been explored for each approach. The DQ0 transformation
method was able to differentiate healthy from defect operation solely from phase
current input, while the Data-Driven Reference Model was not. Combinations of
other signals are tested for the Data-Driven Reference Model with some combina-
tions enabling more sensitive defect detection. The possible applications of both
methods are discussed and the pros and cons are extensively explored. The DQ0
transformation method is effective and inexpensive in detecting severe defects and
shows promising trends with varying operating conditions. The Data-Driven Ref-
erence Model approach is not well suited for rapidly propagating defects but offers
increased sensitivity in applications where the economic expense is irrelevant.

Introduction
In increasingly competitive markets, reducing costs to stay competitive in the in-
dustry is an ever-present factor. Thus, minimising downtime and operational costs
is essential. Knowing the state of rotating equipment is important for minimising
plant downtime and ensuring optimal utilisation of parts.
Generally, maintenance strategies have developed from reactive maintenance through
scheduled maintenance to predictive maintenance. Having a reactive maintenance
scheme, where maintenance is initiated after an equipment failure, can result in long
unplanned plant shutdowns, periods with no production output or loss of product
[1]. Changing to a scheduled maintenance scheme, where the equipment life-time
expectancy is the limiting factor for equipment replacement, can reduce the risk of
unplanned shutdowns. It can, however, also lead to sub-optimal parts utilisation as
some parts may be retired from service while still in working order [2]. Predictive
maintenance has been introduced to mitigate the drawbacks of both the reactive
and scheduled maintenance schemes. The maintenance concept relies on continuous
machine health monitoring and performance assessments to determine when parts
or pieces of equipment are fit for an overhaul or replacement [3]. Knowing which
part to replace and when is essential to secure near uninterrupted production.
Increased levels of vibrations are typically a good indicator of deteriorating machine
health in rotating machinery. Imbalances, leading to increased vibrations, can be
introduced to a machine through external factors such as mishandling, dropping,
etc. or internal factors such as bearing wear or misalignment, etc. Vibrations can
be monitored acoustically, with accelerometers, or through other means [4]. The
common denominator for conventional machine health monitoring is capital expense,
data processing, and technical expertise for correct installation of the sensors.
Faults in rotating machinery introduce harmonics in the phase currents.
These
harmonics can be analysed to indicate equipment health [2]. If it were possible to
monitor and detect the state of a bearing without installing sensing equipment at
the physical location of the rotating equipment, the monitoring would be less cum-
bersome and more economically feasible. This can be achieved through electrical
load monitoring which uses inexpensive sensing equipment installed on the machines
electrical supply, e.g. in a plant control room [5]. Detecting a fault through electri-
cal load monitoring, however, is considerably easier than diagnosing it, due to the
multitude of possible defects that can cause irregularities in the current draw [6].
A study by Irfan et al. has utilised direct-quadrature-zero transformation to trans-
form 3-phase alternating current into 2-phase d-q currents that can be analysed
visually and statistically. This analysis type has yielded good results and has shown
that it is possible to distinguish defects in an electrically driven centrifugal pump
[7].

Another study, conducted on wind turbines by Tcherniak and Mølgaard, has shown
that a data-driven reference model based on acceleration data can be utilised to
detect defects in the wind turbines wings [8]. This model will be explored with a
multitude of physical signals such as temperatures, pressures, etc. gathered from a
test rig. Ideally, the model should work with only phase current signals as input.
This master thesis will investigate the applicability of the two statistical methods’
ability to detect faults in a test rig with a journal bearing. The methods will be
driven purely by data without cumbersome and computationally heavy models, seek-
ing to exploit advances in computational capabilities. A test rig at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) will be used to gather data of different running con-
ditions for the rotor-bearing interface to investigate if the methods can determine
a defective state in the test rig. A series of healthy (non-defect) baseline runs will
determine unique model thresholds that should allow the methods to detect failures
as they are introduced to the system. The applicability of the methods will not be
explored in real-time as the data will be post-processed. Possible implementation
of real-time adaptations of the methods will be discussed for each approach.

Experimental Setup
The test rig used in this project was already designed, constructed and assembled in
the workshop in building 414 at DTU. The test rig is a 1 degree of freedom rigid rotor
setup originally designed by Santos and Nielsen [9]. It has been used for multiple
experiments concerning bearing lubrication control in the past. This chapter will
introduce the existing test rig, its operation, and the modifications made for it to
be suitable for this project.
Figure 2.1: Test rig with electrical load monitoring additon
Due to the experimental nature of this project and to ease handover for future
experimental work, the test rig, how it is operated, and the modifications made in
connection with the project will be described. The management and handling of the
data files generated by the data acquisition systems will also be described.
2.1
Test Rig
The Active Lubrication Bearing test rig used to perform the test runs has been
used extensively for previous research at the Department of Mechanical Engineering,
Section of Solid Mechanics at DTU. It consists of a structural base also serving as
an oil catch for the lubricant. The base has an elevated pivot point for a pivoting
frame and a housing for a journal bearing and rotor. A U-frame consisting of two

arms and an endplate is mounted in the elevated pivot point allowing it to pivot in
one end (point O) and be free in the other (point P), see Figure 2.2. A solid rotor
is mounted on an axle supported by the U-frame. A static load can be applied to
the free end to load the rotor. The rotor, and thus the U-frame, is supported by a
tilting pad journal bearing that is lubricated with high-pressure hydraulic oil. The
high-pressure oil to the upper and lower bearing pads is regulated with a servo valve.
The surface of the rotor is lubricated with low-pressure hydraulic oil. An electrical
motor controlled by a frequency transformer drives the rotor through a belt drive.
The rig is heavily instrumented and can be set up to run a multitude of experiments.
Not all of the instrumentation will be used in this project due to channel limitations
in the existing data acquisition platform. The existing instrumentation that will
be used to monitor the test rig for the experiments is: temperature and pressure
(combined) sensors in both journal bearing pads, flow meters in the high-pressure
oil lines that feed the upper and lower bearing pads and an encoder attached to the
rotor axle. The seven signals are fed to a dSPACE board and computer with the
required hard- and software to acquire the signals. The servo valve that controls the
flow of high-pressure oil is controlled through dSPACE.
All electronics for the instrumentation are gathered in a single cabinet. Power to
the pumps, motor and instrumentation cabinet is supplied from a switchboard on
the wall that is powered by mains power.

Essential Components
The following sections will contain descriptions of the components most essential
to the project. The structural parts of the test rig will not be described in further
detail.
Drive Motor
The motor is of the type VLT® FCM 330 manufactured by Danfoss and equipped
with a frequency converter (in Danfoss terminology and from hereon a VLT). It is
a 3 phase 4 pole motor rated for 380 to 480 V AC mains supply with a rated peak
power of 3 kW. The drive motor is connected to the rotor through a belt drive with

a 2.85 ratio between the motor and rotor pulleys. The operating parameters of the
motor can be changed through the VLT controller. The operational range of the
motor is 1 to 60 Hz and the voltage in the mains supply is 400 V.
Rotor
The solid rotor has a diameter of 99.384 mm, a length of 110 mm and a mass
of 6.88 kg [10]. It is mounted on an axle that is supported by two tapered roller
bearings, one mounted in each arm of the U-frame.
Journal Bearing
The upper and lower journal bearing pads are of the mechatronic tilting pad type.
Both pads have three injection nozzles for high-pressure oil. The pads are made of
brass and have an inner radius of 49.892 mm [10]. In this project, the central nozzle
(middle of the pads) will be used for injection of the high-pressure lubrication oil to
ensure uniform lubrication of the pads. The journal bearing components are shown
in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Journal bearing components, single pad. Picture courtesy of Thomas
G. Pedersen in [10].
High-Pressure Lubrication System
The high-pressure lubrication system supplies the upper and lower journal bearing
pads with high-pressure oil. The high-pressure oil is supplied by a constant speed
pump with a manual pressure regulator located at the pump discharge port. The
flow to the pads is regulated with a servo valve. The servo valve is controlled with
dSPACE ControlDesk 5.1 control software. The software can either send a constant
voltage signal or an alternating voltage signal (sine signal) to the valve. With a
constant voltage signal flow is regulated to either the upper or lower bearing pad.
With an alternating signal, flow is rapidly switched between the upper and lower
bearing pads securing an essentially constant flow of oil to the bearing pads. The
magnitude of the alternating control signal determines the valve opening and thus
the magnitude of the flow. Alternatively, the valve can be centred, such that only
the valves leakage flow is passed to the bearing pads.
Low-Pressure Lubrication System
The low-pressure system sprays the rotor surface with lubrication oil through a
single nozzle. Low-pressure oil is supplied by a pump with a frequency converter,
allowing the low-pressure oil flow to be varied.
Lubrication Oil
Both lubrication systems are fed from the same 20 L oil reservoir containing ISO
VG22 hydraulic oil manufactured by Mobil. A scavenge pump that scavenges the
oil from the oil catch is connected to the reservoir.

The pressure and temperature sensors are Kistler type 4618A2, which is a

The flow meters are manufactured by GHM Honsberg and are of type LABO-

Encoder
The encoder is manufactured by BEI Sensors and is of the type GHM-406-

2.2
Electrical Load Monitoring Addition
Additional instrumentation has been installed to acquire the phase currents of the
electrical drive motor and make electrical load monitoring of the test rig possible.
This has been done by clipping a non-invasive current transformer on to each phase
lead. The three output signals from the current transformers are acquired using
National Laboratories’ FlexLogger software and their cDAQ suite of acquisition
hardware. dSPACE is not used due to a lack of free input channels on the dSPACE
board.
Significant effort was put into sourcing the correct current sensor type for this appli-
cation. To make the setup as user-friendly and versatile as possible a non-invasive
sensor variant was chosen. This has the added benefit of showcasing the flexibility
and ease with which electrical load monitoring can be retrofitted and implemented.
The chosen sensor is rated for a relatively high ampere draw of 30 A.
2.2.1
Current Sensors
The current sensors are manufactured by Tokin (sold as Kemet) and are designated
C/CT-0306.
They are of the non-invasive snap-on type and have an applicable
measurement range of 0.1 to 50 A. The sensor works by transforming the current
down by a factor of 3000 and transmitting the lower current in the two output wires.
Three sensors will be installed resulting in three acquired signals. The datasheet for
the current sensors can be seen in appendix C.1.

2.3
Acquisition Setup
As described in sections 2.1 and 2.2 two separate data acquisition systems are used.
The unmodified test rigs instrumentation signals are acquisitioned through dSPACE
in a ControlDesk 5.1 interface developed by Thomas G. Pedersen [10].
The additional instrumentation signals i.e. the current sensor signals are acquired in
FlexLogger. The FlexLogger interface has been set up for this project. An example
of data structuring, handling and alignment of the two acquisition platforms in
Matlab has been provided in Appendix B.
The sampling frequency was set to 1 kHz after analysing an FFT plot of a phase sig-
nal sampled at 2 kHz, see Figure 2.8. Setting the sampling rate at i.e. 200 Hz would
arguably not have changed the resulting data as it would still satisfy the Nyquist
criteria while reducing the size of the sampled data considerably and muffling the
high-frequency noise.

4.1
Direct-Quadrature-Zero Transformation
Direct-quadrature-zero (DQ0) transformation converts a three-element vector to a
tensor and rotates the tensors reference frame. The transformation is typically done
to simplify 3-phase calculations. DQ0 transformation is the product of the Clarke
and Park transforms and can be used to indicate the state of an electrical motor by
visualising the DQ-patterns created by the DQ components of the transformation
[7], [12]. An addition to the method is proposed by Irfan et al., where a parameter,
the splitting factor, can be calculated based on the DQ patterns. This parameter
is shown to be unique for two types of defects in a centrifugal pump with varying
severity [7].

4.2
Data Driven Reference Modelling
The data-driven reference model used in this project is a statistical method presented
by Tcherniak and Mølgaard [8]. In their research, the authors have developed an
artificial intelligence model that can detect defects in a wind turbine wing. The
model utilises multiple inputs from accelerometers to calculate a single parameter,
the Mahalanobis distance, for each sample. A threshold, defining the limit that
separates a healthy (undamaged) wing from a damaged wing, is defined by training
the model with healthy data to create a healthy reference data set. Subsequent
measurements are then compared to the healthy data set and a defect is detected
if the calculated Mahalanobis distance, the distance between the two data sets, is
greater than that defined by the threshold.

Applicability During Ramp-up
The data has been acquisitioned with the rotor running at or trying to maintain
constant speed. This means that when the threshold is defined for constant speed
operation, the motor ramp-up phase will produce DQ-patterns with splitting factors
greater than the threshold due to the increase in current required to accelerate the
rotor. A ramp-up, starting from 5 Hz, DQ-pattern with the rotor lubricated is shown
in Figure 5.10. It visualises the increasing frequency, and current draw under ramp-
up resulting in a splitting factor of 0.1150, which is nearly the same magnitude as
the largest splitting factor from the critical defect. This means that the method will
wrongly indicate that the system is malfunctioning while the motor is ramping up.
Figure 5.10
A DQ-pattern of ramping up the motor from a standstill is presented in Figure 5.11.
The analysis yields a splitting factor for this pattern of 58.6015. The splitting factor
becomes this large because the parameter ∆σavg is calculated by subtracting the last
half of the sample from the first half and subsequently dividing with the mean of
the complete sample. Thus this method is not well suited to cover ramp-up or slow
applications of electrical motors, as the statistical premise is violated when only a
few - in this case approximately 1.5 - revolutions are used to calculate the splitting
factor.

5.7.5
Other Obtainable Metrics
The splitting factor is not the only state-indicative metric obtainable from the data
used in DQ0-transformation. Investigating other parameters derived from the data
could lead to enhanced fault detection.
Examples of obtainable metrics
Mean value of 3-phase current
– A shift in the phase symmetry plane is observed in some of the presented
DQ-patterns. Calculating the mean value of the current draw over a set
period could be indicative of a defective condition if the mean deviates
from 0 more than a defined limit, e.g. ±2 %.
Discrepancy between the magnetic field and shaft frequencies
– The motor used in this test rig regulates the shaft speed through slip
compensation.
This means that the motor will increase the field fre-
quency (magnetic field) when it registers a slip between the field and
shaft frequencies. A slip that surpasses a defined threshold could be in-
dicative of a defect or rapidly increased resistance in the application that
the motor drives. The magnetic field frequency can be obtained from the
VLT controller.
Area of DQ-pattern
– The area of the DQ-pattern is related to the magnitude of the current
draw in the 3 phases. The area of the pattern can be calculated and if it
surpasses a predefined limit it could be indicative of a defect.
If these metrics can be shown to be indicative of the bearing state, they could be
used to enhance the detection capabilities of the DQ0-transformation approach.

5.7.6
Comments on the Splitting Factor
The splitting factor (SF) indicates how smoothly an electrically driven machine
operates and it is observed that the splitting factor drops as the test rig nears
steady-state operation. The DQ-patterns produced for this test rig are relatively
smooth and circular, due to the way the defect condition manifests itself when the
bearing has no moving parts. If, say, a roller type bearing with a defect in one of the
races or a rolling element, supported the rotor, there would be a cyclic disturbance
in the DQ-patterns as the motor would have to work slightly harder to overcome the
defective point as it carries the load in the rotation. This cyclic disturbance would
manifest itself as a larger SF as found by Irfan et al. [7].
The influence of the length of the time-series used to create the DQ-pattern and
corresponding SF is unclear. Using a span too short will probably run into the
same statistical issues as for ramp-up, where increasing the span risks muffling the
variance in the pattern. The performance of the method, when varying the span, is
shown in Figure 5.15.

Electrical Supply and Uncertainties
The stability of the electrical mains supply has not been investigated. As the ex-

5.8
Method Conclusion
Monitoring the bearings operational state by analysing the DQ-patterns of the elec-
trical motor has proven an effective and cheap way of determining whether a severe
fault is present in the system when it is in continuous operation. The method lacks
sensitivity, but crucially it is able to detect critical defects as they develop. It is a
computationally cheap method, that clearly indicates the machine state with a sin-
gle easy-to-interpret metric. The method requires little data and therefore should
be simple to implement in a real-time application.
The method has shown good performance when the load on the motor is varied,
indicating that it may be possible to cover electrical machines with a variety of op-
erating conditions. However, a clear drawback of the method lies in its applicability
in slow speed applications or ramp-up, where the splitting factor has been shown to
be misleading.
Analysing an entire time series has shown the development of the splitting factor
for various operating conditions. Analysing a time-series increases the sensitivity of
the method for the minor defect, while the serious defect is still undetectable. This
approach resembles a post-processed real-time implementation of the method for a
machine removed from the controlled workshop environment, where only snapshots
of data are analysed. This gives a clear indication of the performance to be expected
in an industrial application of the monitoring method in real-time.

6.2
Model Implementation
The physical signals are normalised to ensure that all signals are in a uniform range.
This also ensures that the model is not biased due to scaling in the magnitude of the
signals. The data is normalised by centring it to have a mean of 0 and then scaling
it to have a standard deviation of 1. The normalisation causes the signals to loose
their physical meaning.
The experimental data signals used for training the model are approximately 3 h
long, while the signals for the runs with a defect introduced are approximately 1 h
long. The model is restricted in that it requires the matrix size in the calculation of
the Mahalanobis distance to be consistent for both training and defect data. This
is accommodated by segmenting the training data into ten equally spaced blocks
and dynamically adjusting the size of the blocks such that the entire length of the
defect data is covered. The block-size of the training data is defined as a tenth of
the length of the defect signals. An example of the data segmentation is shown in
Figure 6.1. Further, the segmentation reduces the computational expense of the
method, while retaining information across the full range of the training signals.

6.8
Discussion
6.8.1
Operational Span
The wide model does not respect the healthy testing in any configuration. It seems
that training the model in a wide operational regime results in a model that is unable
to detect defects. On the contrary, the narrow model respects the healthy testing
in most configurations and is able to differentiate the healthy testing from defective
operation. Thus it can be said that the model is not usable for equipment with a
range of different operating settings. However, this could possibly be mitigated by
using multiple models that each cover one mode of operation.
6.8.2
Mathematical Limitations
A significant limitation of this method is that the matrices used to calculate the
Mahalanobis distance have to be the same size. This means that an equal amount
of data points must be used for training the model as well as for determining defects.
When the defects are severe, the monitored machine may not be able to run long
enough for the size criteria to be fulfilled, thus it could be investigated to train the
model with data sampled at a relatively slow frequency, i.e. 1000 Hz, and monitoring
the operation with data sampled at a significantly higher frequency, i.e. 10 000 Hz,
such that the defect data signals only need to be sampled for a tenth of the training
data length.
The statistical model uses principal component analysis, which requires the input
matrix to be two rows or more as it reduces the matrix dimension by one. This
means that the test-data, whether healthy or defect, has to be provided in pairs
as a minimum. This, especially in combination with the required sampling length
discussed earlier, poses a problem when trying to detect defects in an electrical
machine.
There may not be enough time for two consecutive measurements of
sufficient length to be made before the machine experiences catastrophic failure.
A different approach to detect potential defects more rapidly could be to simply
duplicate a single run. This, however, would not work due to the computation of
the covariance, that requires the samples to be nonlinear.
These mathematical constraints combine to make a rigid model that will be hard
to implement in any practical pseudo-real-time configuration for rotating electrical
equipment in an industrial application.
6.8.3
Signal Combinations
It was expected that combinations of signals with large deviations from healthy op-
eration would be detected, however, this was not the case and as written previously
the physicality of the signals is lost in mathematical operations of the method.
There are many combinations of filtered signals, rate of change in the signals, as
well as signals derived from the acquired signals that can be used as input for the
model. However, it is not practical to review all possible combinations in the time
frame of this project.

Defect Isolation
Isolating a critical defect in the two critical defect test runs proved effective for the
model using a combination of 5 signals (torque, flows and pressures). If this can be
shown to be consistently reliable for defects of less critical nature, that is defects
that do not destroy the machine initially, the area of potential applicability in the
industry for this model is increased.
6.8.4
Statistical Significance
The conclusions and comments made on the model are not based on statistically
significant amounts of data. Before potential implementation of the model, it would
be prudent to confirm the initial trends found here with a stronger statistical foun-
dation. To reduce the time required for each experiment to near steady-state, a
heating element could be introduced in the lubrication system.
6.8.5
Environmental Influence
The measurements used for training the model may be influenced by environmental
factors such as season, temperature fluctuations and operation of machinery around
the monitored equipment. Therefore, the training data should be gathered in the
setting that the given machine will operate in, to ensure that the model is as precise
as possible and that no false alarms occur. It is not, however, a given that the model
will work when trained to cover a wider span in environmental changes. The impact
of environmental differences would have to be researched before trusting the model.
6.8.6
Potential Application
The data-driven reference model may be applicable in plants with custom electric
machinery with long supplier lead times. The additional precision would be a great
benefit while the cost would be negligible in such an application.
6.9
Method Conclusion
Data-driven reference modelling has a lot of cons and few pros when the application
is monitoring rotating electrical machinery.
The approach is not well suited for monitoring rapidly propagating defects as the
mathematical constraints in the method require vast amounts of data in pairs that
cannot be acquired before a defect manifests itself in catastrophic ways. The vastness
of the data requirements also manifests itself in significant computational cost, that
is not ideal for real-time implementation.
Very few of the tested combinations proved somewhat effective and even fewer proved
to be fully reliable.
Only the combinations trained with data from the similar
experiments turned out to be reliable in detecting all three defect conditions and
could have a use in detecting the less severe defects as they develop.
Crucially
the desired combination with only phase current signals did not work and thus the
main objective of the project is not fulfilled by this method. Additional sensing
equipment is required for this method to work and thus it would make sense to
pursue a different approach for electrical load monitoring.
This method may have its uses when monitoring custom electric machines of high
monetary value, where the cost of additional sensors is negligible, however, it is not
well suited for equipment that has short lead times or is easily kept in stock on site.

Conclusion
The applicability of two statistical models used for monitoring of electric machines
has been thoroughly reviewed and investigated.
The simplest of the two, DQ0-transformation, has been shown to be a cheap and
effective way of detecting severe defects in continuous operation when these are
introduced to the test rig. The method only requires three-phase current inputs and
little data and computation for analysis. This makes it well suited for low-cost real-
time monitoring of electrical machines. Further, the model has shown promising
results for varied operating conditions increasing the flexibility of the monitoring
method.
Reference driven data modelling is a computationally heavy and mathematically
constrained method requiring vast amounts of data. The combination work is cum-
bersome and time-consuming, and due to the nature of artificial intelligence models,
qualified guesses in signal combinations are not necessarily fruitful. This model is
best suited for slowly propagating defects in expensive electrical machinery where
the cost of additional sensing equipment and computational effort is dwarfed by the
monetary value of the machinery.
Both methods have been tested in a controlled environment, however, in an uncon-
trolled environment, we cannot be sure that the detected defect is related to the
bearing. It may just as well be related to a defect elsewhere in the system.

B
Data Handling in Matlab
B.1
Scope
This short appendix will describe how data acquired from the test rig is used in
Matlab with special focus on the National Instrumemts .TDMS file format used in
FlexLogger and how to align data acquired with multiple platforms. The appendix
will be based on the setup used for this project.
B.2
Prerequisites
It is essential to be familiar with or familiarise oneself with how structs are used to
store data in Matlab.
B.3
Introduction
As a result of using two different acquisition systems to acquire the signals from
the test rig, handling the data that is produced by the two programs is not trivial.
dSPACE is set to export the acquired data in a .MAT file, which can easily be read
and manipulated in Matlab. FlexLogger on the other hand outputs a file in .TDMS
format, which poses an issue when trying to read the file in Matlab - this cannot be
done. The FlexLogger software is able to split the .TDMS file into .CSV files that
can be read by Matlab. These .CSV files are, however, limited in the maximum
number of rows they can consist of.
For short time series this is not an issue,
however, when longer durations are logged, the file needs to be split into several
separate files, which is not convenient. Thankfully a program has been written to
allow data extraction from .TDMS files.
B.4
How-to
To read TDMS files in the same manner as .MAT files it is necessary to download
TDMS Reader made by Jim Hokanson. It is a program package that extracts a
struct from the .TDMS file that can then be manipulated as a .MAT file.
The
program package can be found at the following link working in June 2020:
https://www.mathworks.com/matlabcentral/fileexchange/30023-tdms-reader.
B.4.1
Aligning Data
The data is aligned with a voltage signal from a portable laboratory power supply
unit (PSU) that is sampled on both platforms. The PSU is set to deliver a voltage
of 5 V. The test duration is then initiated by turning on the PSU and ended when
it is turned off again. The voltage jumps are instantaneous and thus easily located
in the data.
To ease the following data processing it can be beneficial to save the extracted from
both systems to a shared struct. A small guide will be provided as an example of
how the data can be handled.

Handling Precautions
Precautions for Product Storage
Current sensors should be stored in normal working environments. While the sensors are quite robust in other 
environments, exposure to high temperatures, high humidity, corrosive atmospheres, and long-term storage 
degrade solderability.
KEMET recommends that maximum storage temperature not exceed 75°C, and that atmospheres should be free of chlorine 
and sulfur-bearing compounds. Temperature fluctuations should be minimized to avoid condensation on the parts. Avoid 
storage near strong magnetic fields, as they can magnetize the product and cause its characteristics to change.
The stock of current sensors should be used within 24 months of receipt.
Before Using High Alternating Current Sensors, Snap-on Type
•	 Do NOT drop or apply any other mechanical stress, as such stresses may change performance characteristics.
•	 Conduct a preliminary study when heating by current conduction (required).
•	 Do NOT use the high alternating current sensors, snap-on type, opened between secondary output terminals. Heat build-
up in the magnetic core may occur, resulting in damage to the parts by coil melting.
• Install at room temperature. Open/close operation at below 5°C may break hinge of the case.

Amplifier Circuit
Fig. 2 shows a schematic of the amplifier circuit. The connec-
tor wiring for sensor and signal output resp. power supply can 
be gathered from Fig. 3. Temperature output can be obtained 
from any piezoresistive pressure sensor with constant current 
excitation (Types 4065/67, 4090/91 and 4085). 
The tempreature signal is obtained from the thermal resistance 
change of the piezoresistive Wheatstone bridge. There is no 
need for a temperature sensor. The temperature signal must 
be calibrated at the works.
Calibration of the Amplifier
Standard pressure output is UA = 0 ... 10 V or IA = 4 ... 20 mA 
for FS. Smaller pressure ranges are available on request, e.g. 
UA = 0 ... 5 V or IA = 4 ... 12 mA for FS. 
Temperature compensation, signal linearization, and coarse 
adjustment of zero and range are provided by resistors mount-
ed in standardizing  plugs Type 4958A0. The resistor values 
may readily calculated or read from tables. When ordering a 
replacement pressure sensor for the amplifier Type 4618A, a 
new standardizing plug will be included in the delivery.
Zero fine and range fine are adjusted by internal potentio-
meters. Zero can be readjusted after opening the screw cap.
For replacing the pressure sensor, the standardizing plug must 
be also replaced. The values for the sensitivity and the zero 
of the sensor are given in the calibration sheet and can be 
adjusted with the built-in calibrator.
The temperature signal, however, can only be calibrated by 
Kistler.
Two additional internal potentiometers are available for adjust-
ing the limit switches. The switching levels may be adjusted 
with a variable pressure or with a voltage taken from the elec-
tronic circuit. With the cover of the amplifier housing open, a 
schematic of the electronics can be found inside which shows 
the positions of the potentiometers and slide switches.

Characteristics
The VHZ gearwheel flow meter measures the flow by a volumetric 
principle, in which a pair of gearwheels is moved proportional to the 
flow rate. The movement of the gearwheels is measured through 
the enclosing housing wall by a sensor. The devices are suitable for 
viscous, fluid, self-lubricating media, as well as for aqueous fluids 
such as soaps, pasts, emulsions etc. which have a non-abrasive 
character.  Because  of  the  volumetric  functioning  principle,  the 
devices are almost completely independent of viscosity.
The LABO electronics make various output signals available:
●
Analog signal 0/4..20 mA (LABO-VHZ-...I)
●
Analog signal 0/2..10 V (LABO-VHZ-...U)
●
Frequency signal (LABO-VHZ-...F) or 
●
A value signal Pulse / x Litres (LABO-VHZ-...C)
A model with switching output is also available.
If desired, the range end value can be set to the currently existing 
flow using "teaching".

Handling and operation
Installation
The VHZ flow measurement device can be installed anywhere in 
the pipework system. A run-in section is not required. The direction 
of flow may be freely chosen. 
It should be ensured that no dirt particles (thread cutting swarf) can 
get into the flow space, as this could cause the blockage of the 
gearwheels.  It may therefore be  necessary to  install  filters up-
stream of the flow measurement device (mesh size 30 µm).
Note
The metering range end value can be programmed by the user via 
"teaching". Requirement for programmability must be stated when 
ordering, otherwise the device cannot be programmed. The ECI-1 
device  configurator  with  associated  software  is  available  as  a 
convenient option for programming all parameters by PC, and for 
adjustment.
The teaching option is not available for the pulse output version.
Operation and programming
The teaching process can be carried out by the user as follows:
●
The flow rate to be set is applied to the device.
●
Apply an impulse of at least 0.5 seconds and max. 2 seconds 
duration to pin 2 (e.g. via a bridge to the supply voltage or a 
pulse from the PLC), in order to accept the measured value.
●
When the teaching is complete, pin 2 should be connected to 
0 V, so as to prevent unintended programming.
The  devices  have  a  yellow  LED  which  flashes  during  the 
programming  pulse.  During  operation,  the  LED  serves  as  an 
indicator of operating voltage (for analog output) or of switching 
status (for frequency or pulse output).
In order to avoid the need to transit to an undesired operating 
status during the teach-in, the device can be provided ex-works 
with a teach-offset. The teach-offset point is added to the currently 
measured value before saving. The offset point can be positive or 
negative.
Example: The end of the metering range should be set to 80 %. 
However, only 60 % can be achieved without problem. In this case, 
the device would be ordered with a "teach-offset" of +20 %.. At a 
flow rate of 60  % in the process, teaching would then store a value 
of 80  %.
There are many more parameters which can be programmed by 
the ECI-1 device configurator if necessary.
