Summary
In hearing aids, the conventional wisdom has been to reduce low-frequency
(LF) gain in the octave bands centered at 125, 250, and 500 Hz in order to
minimize upward spread of masking. However, pitch cues extracted from
LF resolved harmonics are considered important for speech intelligibility (SI)
in competing voices situations. Furthermore, the sound quality is typically
described as tinny when lowering the LF gain. In the current work, the effects
of modifying LF gain on SI and sound quality for a female target voice masked
by either a same-sex or different-sex masker voice were assessed.
Ten young normal-hearing (NH) listeners and 11 older hearing-impaired (HI)
listeners with moderate LF hearing loss participated. Two conditions with
an increase in LF gain of either 4 dB or 8 dB relative to the prescribed-gain
condition were tested. In the third condition with prescribed-gain, the HI
listeners were ﬁtted individually on both ears using a linear CAMEQ rationale,
while no gain was applied for the NH listeners. A self-scored closed-set
Danish matrix-type SI test (Dantale II) was used. The speech stimuli were
presented co-located via headphones. The percentage of correctly identiﬁed
words was evaluated at 4 ﬁxed signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). All NH listeners
were measured at the same absolute SNR while all HI listeners were measured
at constant SNRs relative to their individual speech reception thresholds.
For NH listeners, no signiﬁcant effect of increased LF gain on SI was found. At
0 dB SNR in the prescribed-gain condition, the HI listeners scored signiﬁcantly
lower with the same-sex versus different-sex masker, whereas the scores were
==PAGE:4==
ii
similar for the two maskers at SNRs different from 0 dB. The HI listeners did
not beneﬁt either from higher LF gains in terms of SI. However, an interaction
between LF gain and masker type (same sex versus different sex) was found.
This is because a 4-dB increase in LF gain was beneﬁcial in the different-sex
masker condition but detrimental in the same-sex masker condition.
A subjective quality rating of the different LF gain conditions was also per-
formed by the same HI listeners using the speech and masker material from
the SI test at 0 dB SNR. The quality ratings were signiﬁcantly higher with
increased LF gain compared to the prescribed-gain condition.
Overall, the results suggest that a higher LF gain in hearing aids does not affect
SI but may be beneﬁcial in terms of sound quality.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Conversations are an important part of social interactions. When listening
to a person one has to focus on this target talker. Often, conversations take
place in environments with more than one sound source. In these sound
environments, the speech intelligibility is not only dependent on the properties
of the target talker and the listener but also on possible background noises.
If the background sound is speech, the condition is called a competing voices
situation. A competing voices situation is characterized by the number of
interfering talkers and their relation to the target talker like same sex, different
sex.
Especially, hearing-impaired listeners report difﬁculties understanding target
speech in competing voices situations. Furthermore, wearing a hearing-aid
could even lead to reduction of speech intelligibility and sound quality in
competing voices situations. This work describes possible mechanisms and
solutions to improve speech-intelligibility and sound quality in competing
voices situations.
This chapter describes the background and the goal of the project. Section 1.1
gives an overview of low-frequency (LF) gain in hearing aids and its effect on
speech intelligibility and sound quality. In Section 1.2, the research question is
presented.

2
Introduction
1.1
Low-frequency gain
In this work, LF gain describes the gain in the octave bands centered at 125, 250,
and 500 Hz. In the ﬁrst section, the factors for reduced LF gain in hearing aids
(HAs) are described. The second and third section explain possible beneﬁts
and drawbacks of higher LF gain on speech intelligibility. Finally, the inﬂuence
of LF gain on sound quality is reviewed.
1.1.1
Factors for reduced low-frequency gain in hearing aids
Open domes or vents are often used in modern HAs. They have openings to
connect the ear canal to the outside. Many users of HAs use open domes or
earmoulds with vents to prevent the occlusion effect. The occlusion effect arises
from closing the ear canal which results in amplifying the listener’s own voice
(Winkler et al., 2016). For example, the ear canal is closed when using an ear
mould in a closed ﬁtting. Fittings with open domes or vents are called open
ﬁttings and they have a reduced LF gain compared to closed ﬁttings because
of a natural high-pass effect.
The high-pass effect has a physical explanation. The air in the vent acts as a
moving mass at low frequencies because the wavelength is long in comparison
to the dimensions of the vent. This lowers the impedance of the vent for lower
frequencies (Dillon, 1991). Therefore, low frequency sound is going through
the vent which results in a lower low-frequency level at the ear drum. The
internal response (outgoing sound) of a large vent with diameter 2.3 mm is
shown in Figure 1.1. At low frequencies sound is attenuated by 15 dB per
octave starting with no attenuation at 500 Hz.
However, inserting a vent in the earmould can also increase the low-frequency
sound at the eardrum since LF sound can enter the earcanal through the
vent. Figure D.1 shows incoming and outgoing sound through a large vent
in comparison to a closed vent. Dillon (1991) found that the net effect of the
vent depends on the gain of the HA. If the direct sound is larger than the
ampliﬁed sound, the external vent response (incoming sound) is dominating. If
the ampliﬁed sound is larger than the direct sound, the internal vent response
is dominating and the vent is acting like a high-pass ﬁlter.
Directional HA settings are commonly used since they can improve the speech

However, they could not ﬁnd a better SI for normal-hearing (NH) listeners or
people with mild LF hearing loss (HL).
A higher LF gain may also be detrimental regarding SI. Cook and Bacon (1997)
described two assumptions why a reduced LF gain is used in current HA
settings: A high LF gain would amplify LF background noise and it could lead
to upward spread of masking. However, their results showed that hearing-
impaired (HI) listeners did not beneﬁt from a lower LF gain in a more realistic
environment.

1.1.2
Beneﬁts of higher low-frequency gain
In the auditory system, speech is decomposed by auditory ﬁlters into signals
consisting of a slowly varying temporal envelope superimposed on a more
rapid temporal ﬁne-structure (TFS) (Moore, 2012). The voice pitch is related to
the fundamental frequency (F0) of a talker and can be extracted from the TFS of
the speech signal. Therefore, talkers with different fundamental frequencies
can be segregated by the use of TFS. A higher LF gain may emphasize the pitch
cues of different talkers which makes it easier to separate them in competing
voices situations.
Harmonic complex tones are often used as stimuli to investigate how F0
is extracted in the auditory system. The harmonics can be split into two
classes when it comes to temporal processing in the auditory system: Resolved
harmonics at low frequencies with one harmonic in a single auditory ﬁlter or
unresolved harmonics at higher frequencies with more than one harmonic in a
single auditory ﬁlter. The auditory nerve can follow TFS of resolved harmonics
(Palmer and Russell, 1986) and therefore extract F0 information. Furthermore,
it was shown that F0 can be extracted from envelope ﬂuctuations of unresolved
harmonics (Moore (2007), p.167). HI listeners have broader auditory ﬁlters
(Moore (2007), p.91, Glasberg and Moore (1990)) and therefore the access to
temporal ﬁne structure could be limited because the amount of unresolved
harmonics is higher than in the NH system. However, Hopkins and Moore
(2011) found that the broadening of auditory ﬁlters can only partly explain
insensitivity to TFS.
In a competing voices scenario, F0 information can be crucial to focus on a
target talker (Assmann, 1999; Ba¸skent and Gaudrain, 2016; Darwin et al., 2003).
Talkers can have different F0 and may be differentiated by this cue. It was
shown that talkers are easier to separate if their F0 differ. The mechanism of
extracting F0 may be the same as for harmonic complex tones. Christiansen et al.
(2013) showed that F0 information from resolved and unresolved harmonics
was beneﬁcial. The same ﬁndings had Oxenham and Simonson (2009) who
measured the same performance for high-pass and low-pass ﬁltered stimuli.
However, Oxenham et al. (2009) states that pitch information is more accurate

from low-frequency resolved harmonics, which is supported by Schubotz et al.
(2016), who could not ﬁnd beneﬁts from using HF envelope information for
vowel identiﬁcation in unmodulated masking noise.
Speech is not stationary but a signal with temporal modulations. Therefore,
modelling a masker talker with stationary noise is not appropriate and a
ﬂuctuating masker should be used instead. When replacing a steady-noise
masker by a ﬂuctuating masker, the speech reception threshold (SRT) is most
likely increasing. This advantage is called masking release (MR).
The main advantage by replacing a steady noise masker with a ﬂuctuating
masker could be the listening in the dips. Hereby, it was shown that HI listeners
have less MR than NH (Bronkhorst (2000); Festen and Plomp (1990)). However,
there is some discussion why MR is less in HI listeners. Some studies proposed
that TFS processing deﬁcits in HI listeners could account for the reduced
MR (Moore, 2008; Hopkins and Moore, 2009; Jespersgaard and Dau, 2012).
However, other studies found that TFS processing may be not so important for
MR (Christiansen et al., 2013; Moore, 2012; Stone et al., 2011; Bernstein and
Brungart, 2011; Hopkins and Moore, 2010; Bernstein and Grant, 2009; Oxenham
and Simonson, 2009). For example, Oxenham and Simonson (2009) found no
differences in MR when low- or highpass ﬁltering speech with one competing
talker. This suggests that TFS is not crucial when separating target and masker
talker.
One should bear in mind that in most of the experiments speech processing
may have affected the results. For example, envelope processing can introduce
errors and impair performance in general (Apoux et al., 2013; Moore, 2012).
Furthermore, speech ﬁltering may have reduced speech redundancy (Oxenham
and Simonson, 2009).
Two types of masking could inﬂuence the performance in competing voices situ-
ations: energetic masking and informational masking (Brungart, 2001). Energetic
masking means that masker energy masks the signal voice and therefore, target
speech information is missing. Informational masking can occur in competing
voices situation when the informational content of the masker is drawing the
listener’s attention from the target to the masker voice and therefore, target
speech information is missing. Informational masking is also related to target-
masker confusion (Kidd et al., 2008) which results in difﬁculties to separate
target and masker voices in competing voices situations.
TFS could reduce the effect of informational masking by providing cues for

1.1.3
Drawback of higher low-frequency gain
When presenting two tones which are close in frequency, it may be that a
tone is masked by another tone, which means one tone is not audible. With
increasing level, high-frequency sounds are masked by a greater extent than
low-frequency sounds. Therefore, masking is spread out more over high
frequencies than low frequencies. This effect is called upward spread of masking
(Moore (2007), p.60). When applying a higher low-frequency gain, the level
of the low-frequency sounds in the signal is increasing and upward spread of
masking may occur.
Upward spread of masking might be of particular interest in HI listeners (Moore
(2007), p. 229). Therefore, even though a higher LF gain could provide better
access to TFS, it could mask low-level high-frequency consonants resulting in a
reduced SI. However, Cook and Bacon (1997) found no relationship between
upward spread of masking caused by increased LF gain and speech recognition
in stationary noise.
1.1.4
Sound Quality
Changing the gain in different frequency bands can have an inﬂuence on the
perceived sound quality of a signal. Moore and Glasberg (1998) report that a
lower low-frequency gain makes speech quality ’tinny’ or ’harsh’ because high-
frequency components are more prominent. Also the perceived naturalness
of a bandlimited speech signal is degrading when the cut-off frequency is
increased from 123 Hz to 208 Hz (Moore and Tan, 2003).
Subjective quality improved with increased LF gains in listeners with signiﬁcant
low-frequency hearing losses (Freyaldenhoven et al., 2006). However, subjective
sound quality with higher LF gains may be negatively affected by microphone

noise (Ricketts and Henry, 2002) and background noise (Freyaldenhoven et al.,
2006).
In general, it is a trade-off between a a higher low-frequency gain to have better
sound quality and a lower low-frequency gain to attenuate background noise
(Cook and Bacon, 1997) and minimize upward spread of masking.
1.2
Goal of the project
For hearing-impaired listeners, the conventional wisdom has been to reduce
low-frequency gain in order to minimize upward spread of masking. However,
temporal ﬁne structure and associated pitch cues are considered important
for speech intelligibility (SI) in competing voices situations, and these cues are
most easily accessible in the LF (Oxenham and Simonson, 2009). Furthermore,
the sound quality is described as tinny when lowering the LF gain (Moore and
Glasberg, 1998).
The main goal of this project was to assess the effect of modifying LF gain on
SI and sound quality of a female target voice masked by either a same-sex or
different-sex masker voice.
The following research question was investigated:
Does a higher low-frequency gain improve speech intelligibility and
sound quality in a competing voices situation?
This research question was addressed by means of a speech intelligibility test
for NH and HI listeners and a subjective quality rating for HI listeners.

Chapter 2
Method
This chapter describes the methods used to evaluate the effect of low-frequency
gain on speech intelligibility and sound quality. The main test was the speech
intelligibility test which is explained in Section 2.1. Section 2.2 is about the
subjective quality rating which was only done by hearing-impaired listeners.
The methods used for statistical analysis are explained in Section 2.3.
2.1
Speech Intelligibility Test
2.1.1
Participants
Ten young normal-hearing (NH) listeners and 11 older hearing-impaired (HI)
listeners participated in the experiment. All participants were native Danish
speakers.
The NH listeners were students from the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU), for whom the mean age was 23.5 years (standard deviation: 2.3 years,
range: 21-25 years) and they got an hourly wage. Most of the NH listeners
had not participated in a listening test before. Their pure-tone thresholds were

2.1.2
Stimuli
The speech intelligibility test was a closed-set Danish matrix-type speech intelli-
gibility test (Dantale II, Wagener et al. (2003)). The target talker was a female

voice. The target speech was mixed with a single talker masker. The single
talker masker was either a female or male voice resulting in a simultaneous
mixture of two same-sex or different-sex voices. The masker talker was reading

1 second on each side.
2.1.3
Processing and Equipment
The tests took place at the facilities of the Hearing Systems Group at the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in Lyngby.
The test person was
sitting in a double-walled sound-treated booth. The sound was presented
via headphones (Sennheiser HDA 200) which were connected to a sound
card (Fireface UCX) and a headphone ampliﬁer (SPL phonitor mini). The
stimulus generation and the test procedure were implemented in Matlab using
a sampling frequency of 48 kHz.
Target and masker were co-located and frontal head-related transfer functions
(HRTFs) were applied so the sound should have been perceived as coming
from the front. Speech and masker signals were summed before applying side-
speciﬁc frontal HRTFs to the signals on both ears. The HRTFs were obtained
with a binaural measurement in the blocked ear canal opening of a head and
torso simulator (B&K HATS) at 0 degrees azimuth and 0 degrees elevation
angle.

low-frequency (LF) gain. The reason is that it should reduce upward spread of
masking since the important frequency range for SI is ranging from 500 Hz
to 4 kHz (Moore and Glasberg, 1998). However, the frequency range between
125 Hz and 250 Hz could be important for separating different talkers in a
competing voices situation because the fundamental frequencies of human
voices are within this range. Therefore, a LF gain is applied in this study which
is the same as increasing the INT values. Figure 2.3 shows the INT values
when a LF gain of 4 dB and 8 dB is applied. Applying the CAMEQ insertion
gain is like high-pass ﬁltering the signal and therefore speech information is
reduced. Applying a higher low-frequency gain results in a more constant gain
over frequency which might restore speech information.

2.1.5
Procedure
Psychometric functions (PMFs) are expected to be less steep when replacing a
steady-noise masker with a single talker interferer (Oxenham and Simonson,
2009; Festen and Plomp, 1990) and adaptive procedures are only valid if the
PMF is sufﬁciently steep (Hey et al., 2014). Therefore, it was chosen to evaluate
the percentage of correctly identiﬁed words (PC) at ﬁxed signal-to-noise ratios
(SNRs). An advantage of this procedure is that test subjects are not confused
by level changes like in adaptive procedures. However, measurements at ﬁxed
SNRs need usually more time than adaptive procedures.
The masker level was held constant at 70 dB SPL and the speech level was
changed for different SNRs. The gain of the sound ﬁles was adjusted so the
output of the headphone (Sennheiser HDA 200) in the Artiﬁcial Ear (Brüel
& Kjær Type 4153) read 70 dB SPL when playing the calibration sound. The
calibration sound was generated by summing up all sentences of the Dantale II
speech intelligibility test. The calibration was done before applying the ﬁtting
gain for hearing impaired (HI) listeners.
All NH listeners were measured at the same absolute SNR because the variation
between NH listeners was small. The range of SNRs was found in pilot tests
with three NH listeners. The SNRs were -36, -28, -20, -12, dB for all NH
listeners. The three different gain conditions (Section 2.1.4) were measured
with the two different masker conditions (Section 2.1.2). Therefore, six different
conditions were measured in total (Table 2.1). A two-hour session was needed
for the speech intelligibility test and an audiometry.
All HI listeners were measured at constant SNRs relative to their individual
speech reception threshold (SRT). Since the variation in performance was

Self-scored Dantale II
The test person could enter the answer by pressing buttons on a screen showing
a matrix with all the words used in the Dantale II speech corpus (Figure
B.1). The ﬁve columns of the matrix represented the syntactical structure of
the Dantale II test (name, verb, numeral, adjective, object) and the ten rows
contained the possible word alternatives. The words were sorted alphabetically
in each column except the numerals were in ascending order. The test person
had to choose a word in each column for each sentence because it was not
possible to use a don’t know button. Therefore, the chance level could be
calculated.
The overall chance level of the test was 10% because the test subject had to
choose a word out of ten alternatives. Therefore, the probability of guessing a
word correct is 1/10 = 10%. The procedure is equivalent to a 10-AFC (alterna-
tive forced choice) task. Therefore, the psychometric function is ﬁtted between
the ﬂoor performance level 10% (guessing rate) and ceiling performance level
100% (Pedersen, 2007).
While the sound was played no answer could be given. The test person could
enter the answer by pressing the word in the shown matrix with a mouse or
the touchscreen after the sound stopped.
Twenty training sentences were run prior to testing to reduce the training
effect in the actual test to less than 1 dB (Wagener et al., 2003) and to practice
the self-scoring. The SNR of the training sentences for NH listeners and HI
listeners in the ﬁrst session was the highest SNR used in the actual test. In the
training trials, the SNR was set to -6 dB for NH listeners and to 12 dB for HI
listeners in the ﬁrst session. The SNR of the training sentences in the second
session for HI listeners was set to 6 dB.
Ten sentences were used to calculate the PC at a ﬁxed SNR for a given gain and
masker condition. Therefore, one word score was evaluated with 50 datapoints
(ﬁve words times ten sentences).

2.2
Subjective Quality Rating
The sound quality of a challenging listening situation with different LF gains
was another point of interest besides the SI in HI listeners. Since a differen-
tiation between sound quality and SI in a challenging listening situation is
difﬁcult, the term subjective quality was chosen. It combines the parameters
sound quality and SI. The term subjective quality was used to evaluate tele-
phone services based on spoken dialogue systems (Bech and Zacharov (2006),
p.350).
A subjective quality rating was done after the speech intelligibility (SI) test
with the same hearing-impaired listeners who did the SI test. The ﬁtting and
the sound samples were the same as in the SI test. All sound samples were
ﬁxed at 0 dB SNR to investigate the subjective quality at a condition where
some listeners confused target and masker voice. Therefore, the ratings of
some listeners may be inﬂuenced by sound quality and of other listeners by SI.
The listeners could listen to the same sound sample with the three gain con-
ditions (prescribed gain, 4-dB and 8-dB LF gain). Same-sex and different-sex
condition were repeated once resulting in four rounds. The listeners were
told to focus on the target voice which they knew from the SI test. Then they
were asked to rate how good the different gain settings were. A 0 to 100 point
continuous quality scale which was divided into ﬁve intervals was used in
the test (Bech and Zacharov (2006), p.335). The intervals had the categories
bad - poor - fair - good - excellent and were shown in Danish language. The
Graphical User Interface (GUI) used in the test is shown in Figure B.2.
Between the SI test and the sound quality rating was a short break to start the
program. It was approximately ﬁve minutes long and was included in the total
test duration of two hours in the second session for HI listeners.

Statistical Analysis
For all statistical tests, a 5% signiﬁcance level was used. The statistical analysis
was done in MATLAB.
2.3.1
Speech Intelligibility Test
The percentage of correctly identiﬁed words (PC) was calculated by counting
the words rated correctly and dividing it by the total number of words. For
example, having 50% of correctly identiﬁed words means that 25 words were
scored correctly because 50 words were presented for one word score (Section
2.1.5).
The mean and standard deviation of PC was calculated using the raw data.
To check for statistical signiﬁcance an analysis of variance (ANOVA) was
conducted. The data was arc-sine transformed for the ANOVA to account for
ﬂooring and ceiling effects and to make the data more normally distributed
(McDonald (2009), p.248). The applied arc-sine transformation was:

A mixed-model 4-way ANOVA is used which accounts for ﬁxed and random

freedom of the error source in Matlab.

In this chapter, the results of the speech intelligibility test and the subjective
quality rating are discussed. The main focus lies on understanding the results
by looking for correlations and relating the ﬁndings to the literature.
4.1
Speech Intelligibility Test
Section 4.1.1 describes general ﬁndings of the speech intelligibility test. In
Section 4.1.2 and Section 4.1.3, the results for NH and HI listeners are discussed
and compared.
==PAGE:44==

Discussion
4.1.1
General Discussion
Comparison with the literature
No signiﬁcant effect of LF gain on SI in NH and HI listeners was found. This
agrees with the ﬁndings of two studies (Freyaldenhoven et al., 2006; Keidser
et al., 2007) but not with the ﬁndings of Ricketts and Henry (2002). The average
LF HL in the current work according to the deﬁnition in the studies was 50 dB
HL.
Keidser et al. (2007) did not ﬁnd a signiﬁcant effect of LF gain for speech
recognition in noise for listeners with LF hearing loss ranging from mild to
severe. This agrees with the ﬁndings in the current work.
Freyaldenhoven et al. (2006) found SI beneﬁts from higher LF gain in a multi-
talker environment only for an average LF HL greater than 60 dB HL, which
is higher than the LF HL in the current work. Therefore, it agrees with the
ﬁndings in the current work, that NH and HI listeners do not show a beneﬁt
of LF gain.
However, Ricketts and Henry (2002) found an effect of LF gain in a multi-talker
environment for LF HL greater than 40 dB HL which is lower than the average
LF HL in the current work. Therefore, a SI beneﬁt from increased LF gain
would be expected but it was not found in the current work. One has to bear in
mind that in the studies real HAs were used with focus on directional beneﬁt.
Furthermore, the masker types differed.
Speech material and beneﬁt from temporal ﬁne structure
One reason for no effect of LF gain in the current work may be that the closed-
set matrix type SI test was not sufﬁcient to show signiﬁcant differences for
increased LF gain conditions. It is well known that SI scores are dependent
on the speech material and test procedure. For example, Lunner et al. (2012)
found that young NH listeners got higher beneﬁts from TFS information using
open-set material than closed-set material. However, no differences in beneﬁt
depending on the speech material were found for old HI listeners. A closed-set
matrix type SI test was chosen in this study because self-scoring was possible.
In the present work, neither NH listeners nor HI listeners beneﬁted from LF
gain suggesting that the speech material had the same effect on NH and HI
listeners in terms of SI beneﬁt with increased LF gain.

Speech reception thresholds
In the present work, the SRTs were lower than one would expect in real-life
situations. The reason for these unrealistic low SRTs may be that Dantale II
speech is more predictable than, for example, open-set material or target speech
with random starting points because the Dantale II test has a small set-size
and ﬁxed temporal strucure (Lunner et al., 2012). Furthermore, the target and
masker voices were the same in all trials and Brungart et al. (2001) showed that
performance was better when listeners were exposed to the characteristics of the
target voice before testing. The same effect might occur with prior knowledge
of the masker voice characteristics. When designing the experiments in the
current work, a linear relationship between SNR and the effect of increased
LF gain on SI was assumed. Since the effect of LF gain was deﬁned as the
difference in performance between increased LF gain condition and prescribed
gain condition, it was expected that this relation is the same at unrealistic low
SNRs and at more realistic higher SNRs. However, since no signiﬁcant effect of
LF gain at low SNRs was shown, it may be that the relationship between effect
of LF gain and SNR is non-linear and a larger effect might be found at higher
SNRs.
Audibility
It is difﬁcult to differentiate between masking effects and audibility problems.
The low SNRs in the test might have caused audibility problems. The lowest
SNR for NH listeners was -36 dB and the corresponding speech level at this
SNR was 34 dB SPL (because the masker level was ﬁxed at 70 dB SPL and the
speech level was changed according to the given SNR; Section 2.1.5). Low-level
normal speech has sound pressure level around 40 dB SPL (Oticon, 2013) and
therefore, a speech level of 34 dB SPL may be difﬁcult to understand even
without background noise. Choosing a masker level of 70 dB SNR was the
trade-off between the limit of audibility on the one side and the limit of sound
pressure level exceeding the maximum exposure limit on the other side.

Low-frequency gain
Another reason for no signiﬁcant effect of LF gain may be that the LF gain was
too low. It can be observed in Figure 2.3 that a signal after CAMEQ ﬁtting
was still attenuated in the low-frequencies at 125 Hz and 250 Hz by -3 dB and
-2 dB with a 8-dB LF gain. Therefore, some spectral speech information is still
missing which could be restored by using more LF gain to get a ﬂat insertion
gain over frequency.
4.1.2
Normal-hearing listeners
The reason why NH listeners did not show an effect of LF gain may be that the
full spectral speech information was already available in the prescribed-gain
condition. The fact, that no signiﬁcant reduction in SI was measured could
imply that upward spread of masking is not a problem with higher LF gain in
NH listeners.
In the prescribed-gain condition, the speech reception threshold (SRT) for NH
listeners was -25.5 dB SNR for the different-sex masker and -24.1 dB SNR for
the same-sex masker. The reference SRT for NH listeners in the Dantale II test
is -8.4 dB SNR and the slope of the PMF around the SRT is 13.2% per dB for a
stationary speech-shaped noise (Wagener et al., 2003). Therefore, the masking
release (MR) when replacing the stationary noise by the single competing
talker used in the present work was approximately 16 to 17 dB depending on
the masker condition. The results do not comply with the ﬁndings of Festen
and Plomp (1990) who found a MR of 6-8 dB for a single interfering voice
compared to a steady noise. However, the two studies agree that the slope
of the psychometric function (PMF) is less steep when replacing steady-state
masker noise with a speech masker. The difference in MR may be caused
by different speech material or test procedures. For example, the amounts of
pauses in the masker signals could be different.

4.1.3
Hearing-impaired listeners
Prescribed-gain condition and comparison with normal-hearing listeners
In the prescribed-gain condition, the mean SRTs were -9.3 dB SNR for the
different-sex masker and -8.4 dB SNR for the same-sex masker. Compared to
the group of NH listeners, the SRT was 16.15 dB higher for the different-sex
and and 15.76 dB for the same-sex masker condition. Therefore, there was a
huge difference in SRTs between NH and HI listeners. It agrees with the results
obtained in some studies (Bronkhorst, 2000; Festen and Plomp, 1990) that HI
listeners beneﬁt less from masker ﬂuctuations in competing voices situations
than NH listeners which explains the large difference in performance.
Another reason for the large difference in performance between NH and HI
listeners may be that the audibility was not matched between the two groups.
In the prescribed-gain condition, NH listeners had 0 dB insertion gain whereas
HI listeners had the CAMEQ insertion gain. Therefore, NH listeners had
full spectral speech information in the prescribed-gain condition whereas HI
listeners missed low-frequency cues because applying the CAMEQ insertion
gain was like high-pass ﬁltering the signal. To match the audibility for NH and
HI listeners, the stimuli for NH listeners could be high-pass ﬁltered by using
the INT-values of the CAMEQ formula as gain (Section 2.3). For example, a
similar procedure was done in Glyde et al. (2015). The reason for not matching
audibility for NH and HI listeners was to avoid losing any spectral speech
information or speech redundancy, which is inherent to meaningful speech
(Rhebergen and Versfeld, 2005), by ﬁltering.
Performance in different-sex and same-sex masker condition with
prescribed gain and comparison with normal-hearing listeners
The difference in performance between different-sex and same-sex masker
condition was similar in NH and HI listeners in the prescribed-gain condition.
The difference in SRT between different-sex and same-sex masker condition was
0.9 dB for HI listeners and 1.3 dB for NH listeners. The results are not consistent
with the ﬁndings of Brungart (2001) who found substantial differences between
different-sex and same-sex condition of 15%-20% at negative SNRs for NH
listeners with beneﬁts in the different-sex condition. Here, the differences do

A major difference between the two studies is the speech material. Brungart
(2001) used a speech corpus based on the coordinate-response measure (CRM)
(Bolia et al., 2000) which is supposed to be insensitive to energetic masking
effects. Therefore, the level differences between target and masker might have
more impact on SI in the present work than in the study of Brungart (2001)
which might explain why the differences between different-sex and same-sex
masker condition were different in the two studies. Another difference between
the two studies is the spectral matching of target and masker stimuli. In
the current study, target and masker voice were spectrally matched which
might have reduced the differences between performance in different-sex and
same-sex condition which was not the case in the study of Brungart (2001).
The reason for spectrally matching the stimuli was to miminize all effects
not related to TFS and F0 information. For example, if the spectra were not
spectrally matched, the male voice would mask low-frequencies but the female
voice would not (Pressnitzer et al. (2005), p.311).
Performance at 0 dB SNR
Great differences in performance of 20.4% correctly identiﬁed words between
different-sex and same-sex masker at 0 dB SNR were found, even though the
differences at SNRs lower than 0 dB were small. At 0 dB SNR, target and
masker voice were presented with the same sound pressure level. Therefore, it
seems likely listeners used level differences to separate target and masker voice
at SNRs different from 0 dB. The results agree with the ﬁndings of Samson
and Johnsrude (2016) that listeners beneﬁt from level differences in two-talker
mixtures. When the cue of level differences is missing, differences in talker
characteristics get more important as observed at 0 dB SNR. Our results comply
with the ﬁndings in Brungart (2001) that the PMF plateaus at SNRs below 0
dB for same-sex maskers. However, in this study the plateu was only ranging
from -6 dB SNR to 0 dB SNR. Decreasing the SNR from 0 dB to -6 dB reduced
SI only by 7.4% compared to a reduced SI of 26% when decreasing the SNR
from -6 dB to -12 dB. The reason might be the missing cue of level differences
at 0 dB SNR.

Correlation analysis using the word score difference
To investigate the effect of LF gain on SI, the word score difference is used. The
word score difference is the percentage of correctly identiﬁed words (PC) in
the prescribed-gain condition subtracted from the PC in the increased LF
gain condition. Both word scores were obtained with the identical masker,
either different-sex or same-sex target and masker voices. Therefore, a word
score difference equal zero means equal performance in the increased LF gain
condition and the prescribed-gain condition. A positive word score difference
means a beneﬁt in SI with higher LF gain and a negative word score difference
means a reduction in SI with higher LF gain.
A correlation analysis was done to understand why some HI listeners beneﬁted
from increased LF gain in terms of SI but others did not. The following
attributes of the HI listeners were used in the analysis: age, the year the
person started wearing hearing aids (HA start), low-frequency hearing loss (LF
HL) and high-frequency hearing loss (HF HL). More details can be found in
Appendix E.
The beneﬁt from LF gain in terms of SI was signiﬁcantly correlated with two
attributes of HI listeners: high-frequency HL (HF HL) and the year they started
wearing HAs (HA start). The two attributes were also correlated in the group
of HI listeners who participated in the experiments. Furthermore, individual
differences in performance between different-sex and same-sex condition were

It can only be hypothesized that listeners with higher HF HL miss cues to

beneﬁt more from a higher LF gain than listeners with lower HF HL. Oxenham

ﬁltered speech at 0 dB SNR, that HF information like formant frequencies,
which are determined by the vocal tract length, could be more important than
LF information (Figure D.3). More studies found that F0 information and
information from formant frequencies contribute to segregate different-sex
target and masker (Darwin et al., 2003; Ba¸skent and Gaudrain, 2016). The
results suggest, that if information in one spectral region is missing the other
spectral region becomes more important.
4-dB LF gain in different-sex and same-sex masker condition
In HI listeners was found that a 4-dB increase in LF gain was beneﬁcial in
the different-sex masker condition but detrimental in the same-sex masker
condition. The effect is mainly observed in listeners with HF HL between 50
and 70 dB (Figure E.2). These listeners beneﬁted mainly from a 4-dB LF gain
in the different-sex condition but worsened performance with the same gain in
the same-sex condition. It may be that these listeners could use LF information
from an increased LF gain in the different-sex condition to segregate talker and
masker by different F0s. However, in the same-sex condition it may be that the
LF gain deteriorated information to segregate target and masker voice which
worsened the performance. Also for NH listeners at -20 dB SNR, 4-dB LF gain
worsened performance in the same-sex condition but not in the different-sex
condition. It looks like the 4-dB LF gain could be used to segregate different-sex
talkers but it degrades performance in same-sex conditions. Oxenham and
Simonson (2009) showed that HF information might be more important in same-
sex conditions. It can only be hypothesized that a higher LF gain deteriorated
this HF information in same-sex conditions, possibly due to upward spread of
masking.

Conclusion
The effect of increased low-frequency gain on speech intelligibility and sound
quality in a competing voices situation was of interest. A two-talker mixture
with different-sex and same-sex target and masker voices was used. No signiﬁ-
cant effect of increased low-frequency (LF) gain on speech intelligibility (SI) in
normal-hearing (NH) and hearing-impaired (HI) listeners was found with a
closed-set matrix type speech test. However, higher LF gain signiﬁcantly im-
proved sound quality in HI listeners compared to the prescribed-gain condition
(linear CAMEQ gain).
Based on the experiments the following conclusions can be made:
• Level differences between target and masker voices were identiﬁed as a
major segregation cue. When the level difference was absent, differences
in voice characteristics of target and masker talker became more impor-
tant. Therefore, the different-sex condition had signiﬁcantly higher word
scores than the same-sex condition at 0 dB SNR in HI listeners. Hereby,
large individual differences in performance between different-sex and
same-sex condition were found which might be related to the degree of
low-frequency hearing loss.
==PAGE:58==
48
Conclusion
• A higher LF gain did not affect SI signiﬁcantly in NH and HI listeners.
Therefore, upward spread of masking or better access to temporal ﬁne-
structure and F0 information could not be identiﬁed as beneﬁcial or
detrimental when applying higher LF gain.
• The individual high-frequency hearing loss (between 1kHz and 4kHz)
and the experience with wearing hearing aids were correlated with the
effect of LF gain on SI. HI listeners with HF HL greater than 60 dB HL
or people wearing hearing aids for more than 20 years mainly beneﬁted
from higher LF gains. The two attributes HF HL and the year people
started wearing HAs were correlated in our group of participants.
• With a 4-dB LF gain, HI listeners beneﬁted in the different-sex condi-
tion but performance worsened in the same-sex condition. It might
imply that increased LF gain helps in different-sex conditions because
LF information might be important, but deteriorates performance in
same-sex conditions because LF information might be less important and
segregation cues in other spectral regions might be more important.
• Subjective sound quality was improved with increased LF gain in HI
listeners with the target and masker voice at the same level. Hereby, a
8-dB LF gain was rated better than a 4-dB LF gain. Older HI listeners
gave higher ratings for increased LF gains than younger HI listeners.
Overall, the results suggest that a higher LF gain in hearing aids does not
improve SI but may be beneﬁcial in terms of sound quality.

Future Work
The closed-set matrix type test was chosen in the current work because self-
scoring was possible. It might be that the closed-set matrix type test was not
suited to show signiﬁcant effects of LF gain on SI. The SNRs were lower than
one would expect in real-life scenarios. Smeds et al. (2015) found only few
real-life situations with SNRs lower or close to 0 dB which was the range of
SNRs in the current work. Therefore, experiments could be replicated with
speech material measuring at higher SNRs. For example, the Danish HINT
(Nielsen and Dau, 2009, 2011) or DAT corpus (Nielsen et al., 2014) could be
used.
Correlation analysis was done with only ten listeners. To show more signiﬁcant
correlations the tests have to be replicated with more test subjects. In this
work, regression analysis was done to relate listener’s attributes to the beneﬁt
of LF gain. With more test subjects, it may be possible to observe clearer
patterns that may be useful to classify the listeners based on their proﬁles. It
would be interesting to investigate which factors inﬂuence beneﬁt of LF gain.
Furthermore, beneﬁt from a higher LF gain could be predicted based on the
listener’s proﬁle.
In general, no signiﬁcant effect of LF gain on speech intelligibility in a two-
==PAGE:60==
50
Future Work
talker mixture was found. The effects of increased LF gain on suprathreshold
processing remain unknown. It would be interesting to investigate if low-
frequency gain is correlated with access to F0 information and upward spread
of masking. It could be tested in the following ways:
• Perform F0 discrimination tasks with different low-frequency gain settings.
It might show that with higher LF gain F0 discrimination is improved
which might be beneﬁcial in separating talkers.
• Measure temporal ﬁne structure sensitivity with different LF gains. Use
a monaural TFS test like the phase-jitter detection test (Kortlang et al.,
2016).
• The effect of LF gain on upward spread of masking could be investigated by
measuring masked thresholds or consonant confusions, for example with
the Danish speech corpus PiTu (Christiansen and Henrichsen, 2012).
• Simulations: Use speech intelligibility models which account for upward
spread of masking but not for informational masking or streaming effects.
For example, the speech intelligibility index SII (ANSI, 1997) could be
used.
Better sound quality without signiﬁcant effect on speech intelligibility was
found with increased LF gain. It could be interesting to implement higher LF
gain settings in hearing aids (HAs) to provide better sound quality which re-
sults in better acceptance of hearing aids. A possible implementation in hearing
aids needs further investigation and solutions of the following problems:
• Technical improvements in HAs to realize higher low-frequency gains
when using vents or open domes: High LF gains are needed to com-
pensate for the LF sound which is leaving the ear canal through larger
vents and open domes. Current HAs cannot provide sufﬁcient LF gains
because the power of the ampliﬁer is limited. Therefore, more powerful
solutions have to be found. One could also think about shifting low
frequencies to higher frequency regions. A similar mechanism called
frequency lowering is already used in current hearing aids. It means that
high frequencies are shifted to lower frequencies.
• Higher LF gain deteriorates sound quality because microphone noise
becomes audible (Ricketts and Henry, 2002). In current HAs, the problem
arises when a LF boost is applied to compensate for the directional roll-off.
Therefore, solutions with reduced microphone noise should be found.

Appendix E
Correlation analysis: HI
listeners
The correlation analysis was done with the data from the speech intelligibility
(SI) test and the subjective quality (SQ) rating. The analysis included only data
from the HI listeners.
The correlation analysis was done in MATLAB. R is the Pearson correlation
coefﬁcient of two random variables. In the tables, the coefﬁcient of determination
R2 is shown which describes the strength of the relation between variables
(McDonald (2009), p.207). The p-value indicates if a correlation is signiﬁcant.
In this work, a p-value smaller than 0.05 indicates a statistically signiﬁcant
correlation and is indicated with a star. A star in brackets indicates a close-to-
signiﬁcant correlation.
The percentages of correctly identiﬁed words (PC) from the SI test were sub-
jected to a rationalized arcsine transform (RAU) prior statistical analysis. In
the ﬁgures, the raw data is shown. In general, the results were collapsed across
SNRs.
The SQ results are processed as described in Section 2.3.2 .
==PAGE:78==
68
Correlation analysis: HI listeners
The variables of the experiments were:
• ∆PC or word score difference: Difference between PC in the increased
LF gain condition (4LFG and 8LFG: 4-dB and 8-dB LF gain) and the
prescribed gain condition in the corresponding masker condition (SI test)
• ∆PCmasker: Difference between PC in the different-sex condition and the
same-sex condition in the prescribed-gain condition (SI test)
• Relative rating: Ratio of the rating in the increased LF gain condition
and the rating in the prescribed gain condition in the corresponding
masker condition (SQ test)
The attributes of each HI listener used in the correlation analysis were:
• Age in years
• HA start: Year the person started wearing hearing aids
• LF HL: Low-frequency hearing loss (average across 125, 250, and 500 Hz)
• HF HL: High-frequency hearing loss (average across 1k, 2k, and 4kHz)
