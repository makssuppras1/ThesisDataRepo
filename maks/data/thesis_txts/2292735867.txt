Summary (max. 2000 characters):
In this study, numerical model of the 1:60 scaled DTU 10MW ﬂoating
wind turbine on a TLP platform is created. The validation of the
numerical model is performed against the experimental test data. The
numerical model is simulated in regular and irregular waves under
various environmental conditions. Additionally, the eﬀect of wind and
the eﬀect of wave misalignment on the numerical model are examined.

Abstract
Floating wind turbine concepts are getting more attractive as the wind industry
tends to increase the size of wind turbines and goes towards deep water projects.
Remarkable amount of studies have been done for the ﬂoating concepts in order to
use them in the wind industry. However, there are some challenges to be solved.
One of the challenges for the ﬂoating wind turbine concepts is the validation of the
existing aero-hydro-elastic codes that investigate the complete coupled behaviour of a
ﬂoating wind turbine [14]. It is therefore necessary to validate numerical tools against
measurements for the ﬂoating wind turbine models.
The purpose of this study is to validate HAWC2, the aero-hydro-elastic code, for the
ﬂoating wind turbine concept. DHI & DTU are currently working on an experimental
testing 1:60 scaled model of the 10 MW ﬂoating wind turbine on a TLP platform. In
the present study, numerical model of the tested wind turbine is created in HAWC2
and the validation of the numerical model is performed against the measurements.
Model validation is carried out under 10 diﬀerent environmental conditions.
The
model is simulated in regular and irregular waves with and without wind conditions.
Waves are divided into two cases which are aligned and 30◦misaligned to the wind
direction.
The analysis of the numerical model mainly focuses on the model displacements, the
mooring line tensions and the nacelle acceleration. It is shown that the surge motion
is closely related to the wave amplitude. As the wave amplitude grows, the surge
response gets bigger. On the other hand, the mooring line tensions, heave motion
and nacelle acceleration are primarily linked to the surge motion. They vary in the
bigger ranges as the surge gets higher values.
Including wind entails an increase
in the surge and heave motion. Also, the upwind tendons experience more tension,
while the downwind tendons take less loading when including wind. What is more,
misalignment of the wave results in an asymmetry in the mooring system and the
response of the tendon tensions changes signiﬁcantly.
In conclusion, the numerical model demonstrates the ability of estimating the response
of the ﬂoating platform under the selected environmental conditions.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Wind energy is getting more and more desirable, as the world is moving towards
green and sustainable energy sources. At the moment, onshore wind turbines have the
major share of installed wind energy capacity in the world. However, recent research
is mostly focused on oﬀshore projects, particularly deep water oﬀshore projects.
Foundations are recognized as the main challenge in the oﬀshore wind industry due
to their cost. As the water depth increases, costs of foundations increase. Figure 1.1
illustrates the diﬀerent types of bottom ﬁxed foundations, typically used in the oﬀ-
shore wind industry. Since the monopile foundation is simpler and cheaper at design,
fabrication, and installation stages, it is much more preferable. It has been widely
used in oﬀshore wind projects as most of these projects have been installed in shallow
waters (nearly 75% share for online wind farms by the end of 2012) [2].
Figure 1.1: Diﬀerent types of bottom ﬁxed oﬀshore platforms: monopile, tripod,
jacket, gravity based [10]
Large majority of oﬀshore wind farm projects in Europe are located in the North Sea

and the Baltic Sea. There are currently no oﬀshore wind farms in the Mediterranean
Sea as the water is deep. Ongoing commercial foundations are limited to 40m to 50m

Figure 1.2 displays the average water depth and distance to shore for online, under

order to unlock market potential for water depths of over 50m, ﬂoating substructures
will be the most economical alternative [15].

1.1
State of the art of oﬀshore ﬂoating platforms
The concept of ﬂoating platforms has been used in the oil and gas industry but it is
quite new for the wind industry. Currently, three main types of ﬂoating substructures,
adapted from the oil and gas industry, have been used in the wind industry. These
substructures are the spar buoy, the tensioned leg platform (TLP) and the barge
concepts (see Figure 1.3).
2
==PAGE:15==
1
INTRODUCTION
Figure 1.3: Diﬀerent types of ﬂoating platforms [10]
The TLP achieves the stability primarily through the pretension in the tension legs.
The spar buoy maintains the stability through deep draft combined with ballast by
lowering the centre of gravity. The barge consists of a large water-plane area and
shallow-drafted platform which achieves the stability.
Even though the ﬂoating wind turbine concept has existed since the early 1970s, the
industry started researching in the mid-1990s. In recent years, studies in ﬂoating wind
turbines are expanded. In 2008, Blue H technologies installed the ﬁrst test ﬂoating
wind turbine with a rated capacity of 80 kW in Italian waters [2].
In 2009, the world’s ﬁrst large scale grid connected ﬂoating wind turbine, Hywind, was
installed oﬀNorwegian coast with a 2.3 MW Siemens wind turbine (see Figure 1.4a).
It is located at a water depth of 200m and a spar buoy concept was used. After the
inspections, no signs of damage or deterioration have been reported and turbines in
the 3 MW and 7 MW range are expected to be deployed as a next step [2].
Next, the second largest scale ﬂoating wind turbine equipped with a 2 MW Vestas
wind turbine, WindFloat, was installed in Portugal in 2011 [2].

1.2
Deep water challenges
Cost reduction is one of the main challenge in the oﬀshore wind industry. The plat-
form and the anchoring system constitute the main cost of the ﬂoating wind turbine
projects. These costs are similar to ﬁxed bottom oﬀshore platforms. However, the
design and installation costs are expected to be cheaper for ﬂoating systems in the
following years. Overall, ﬂoating concepts are expected to generate more power as
bigger turbines can be mounted and the ﬁnal cost per MWh will be reduced [2].
Anchoring is a key issue for ﬂoating projects. Depending on load carrying capacity,
there are diﬀerent anchoring systems available such as gravity based, driven pile,
drag embedded, suction, drilled. Soil conditions determine the load capacity of the
anchors. Therefore, the right anchoring system must be selected based on the soil
conditions [14].
Installing mooring lines is another important issue for ﬂoating projects. Hooking up
mooring lines to the ﬂoating platform is one of the challenging phase of the installa-
tion. In order to reduce design and installation risks, experience of the oil and gas
industry is being transferred [14].
Moreover, modelling tools combining the turbine and the substructure must be im-

1.3
Description of the main loads of the system
The loads on a ﬂoating wind turbine are dominated by aerodynamic and hydrody-
namic eﬀects. The main loads exciting the ﬂoating wind turbine are written as wind,
waves, mooring system, buoyancy, gravity and sea ice (see Figure 1.6). Eﬀects of sea
ice loadings are not included in the model as it is not the scope of this study.
Figure 1.6: Loads on a ﬂoating wind turbine [11]
Air ﬂowing over the airfoil of the blade generates the aerodynamic lift and drag forces.

The mean wind speed generates the steady aerodynamic forces on the wind turbine.
When including the wind shear, rotor rotation and tower shadow eﬀects, periodic
aerodynamic forces are produced. Next, randomly ﬂuctuating aerodynamic forces
are generated by gusts, turbulence and dynamic eﬀects. One of the main diﬀerence
between a ﬂoating wind turbine and a ﬁxed bottom wind turbine is that the wind
seen by the blade is not only dependant on the angle of attack but also dependant
on the motion of the ﬂoating platform.
Wave loads are generated by irregular waves. Irregular waves can be represented
by sum of multiple linear wave components. Basically, multiple linear waves with
various frequencies and their random phase angles are summed with respect to their
probability spectral density function.
Mooring forces prevent the wind turbine from drifting and sinking. Tensioned legs
react as springs and provide the stiﬀness in order to obtain the stability of the system.
Mooring lines are attached to the platform at fairlead connections and anchored to
the sea bed.
The bodies in water are under the inﬂuence of a buoyancy force. Buoyancy force is a
lifting force aﬀecting on the ﬂoater through the buoyancy point.
Gravitational force acts on the centre of gravity of the system. This must be taken
into account in the ﬂoating system as vertical separation between the structure’s
centre of gravity and the structure’s centre of buoyancy provides restoring.
1.4
Purpose and description of the model
Considering industry’s tendency to increase the size of the wind turbines and to go to
deep water projects, ﬂoating foundation concepts are becoming even more important.
As it was mentioned in Section 1.2, one of the challenge for the deep water projects
is validation of the numerical software tools.
In recent years, noticeable amount of studies have been performed to extend the exist-
ing aero-elastic codes to compute bottom-ﬁxed oﬀshore wind turbine loads. However,
there are limited numbers of codes modelling the complete coupled behaviour of a
ﬂoating wind turbine [14]. For this reason, it is necessary to validate numerical tools
against measurements for the ﬂoating wind turbine models.
DHI & DTU are currently working on a testing 1:60 scaled model of a 10 MW ﬂoating

1.5
Description of the software used: HAWC2
The numerical simulations in this study have been carried out using HAWC2 aero-
elastic code which was developed by DTU-Risø. Basically, HAWC2 models the wind
turbine and the wind turbine response in time domain is calculated.
Figure 1.8
illustrates the structure of HAWC2.
Figure 1.8: Structure of HAWC2 (Bjarne S. Kallesøe and Anders M. Hansen of
DTU-Risø)
The aerodynamic part of the code is based on the Blade Element Momentum (BEM)
theory. Additionally, shear eﬀects on the induction, tower shadow eﬀect, dynamic in-
ﬂow, dynamic stall, skew inﬂow, eﬀects from large deﬂections and tip loss are included
[6].
The structural part relies on a multi-body formulation. The main structure of the
wind turbine is divided into a number of bodies and these bodies are composed of
Timoshenko beam elements to account for ﬂexibility and inertia. Then, algebraic
constraints are used to connect these bodies.
The wind conditions are included as deterministic and stochastic wind. The determin-
istic wind is mean wind speed, wind steps and ramps, while the stochastic wind refers
the turbulence provided outside the HAWC2 code.
The Mann turbulence model,
generated by the code of WAsP engineering, is used to produce the turbulence.
The Morrison equation is used in order to model the hydrodynamic forces acting on a
ﬂoating members due to the waves. The water kinematics describes how the structure

Control of the wind turbine is provided by deﬁned DLLs. It is an open source DLL

1.6
Description of the coordinate system
The ﬂoating system is exposed to translations and rotations motion because of the
wind and wave loads. The simulations in HAWC2 are based on the global coordinate
system seen in Figure 1.9.
The global coordinate system is located with z=0 in
the mean water level. Surge and sway are horizontal motion respectively along y
and x axes. Heave motion is along z-axis and positive z-axis is directed downward
through the centerline of the ﬂoater. The rotation motions about x,y and z axes are
represented as pitch, roll and yaw respectively.

Wind turbine design
The wind turbine model is provided by downscaling the DTU 10 MW reference wind

2.2.2
Nacelle design
The nacelle design is simpler in its composition compared to a full scale nacelle, and
the number of components is reduced for the scaled model. In the experimental setup
the hub is assembled to the nacelle. The target mass for the combined hub and nacelle
was 2.49 kg. According to [7], dimensions and mass properties of the nacelle could

weighted 3.48 kg. However, it was considered as an admissible divergence from the
scaled design as the aerodynamic drag is insigniﬁcant along with the inertia of the
nacelle [7].
The supporting plate, where the nacelle and the main shaft are located, is tilted
5◦in accordance to the actual shaft tilt angle for the prototype. The design of the
prototype speciﬁes that the rotor is upwind preconed of an angle of 2.5◦, but due to
a considerable increase in design complexity by applying this, it was decided that tilt
angle would be admissible to avoid the blades from hitting the tower [7].
2.2.3
Tower design
When designing the wind turbine tower for the small model, it is important to match
the scaled natural frequency of the tower as the structural frequencies of the tower
can be excited by 1P and 3P frequencies [7]. An experimental study of a 5 MW TLP
model was performed in DHI in 2012 [16]. The tower that was used in [16] is quite
similar to the scaled tower values of this model with respect to the mass and height.
This tower is not conical as the full scale tower, but this was considered of secondary
importance as the cross sectional geometry of the tower mainly has an eﬀect on the
aerodynamic drag on the tower [7]. Consequently, this tower is used in the present
study.
The tower, made of PVC, consists of a cylindrical column with a ﬁxed diameter of
80 mm and a ﬁxed thickness of 3 mm. From the decay tests which are presented in
Section 3.3.1, tower fore-aft natural frequency is determined as 2.0 Hz which does not
coincides with any critical excitation frequencies. More detailed information about
the tower can be found in [7].

located above the centre of buoyancy. Due to this, the platform is insuﬃcient against
overturning moment. The main contribution in restoring is provided by the mooring
system for keeping the stability. Therefore, it is crucial to provide the required tendon
pretension.

the cables. The pretension is equally distributed to four cables with a magnitude of

Numerical model
In this section, the numerical model is created in HAWC2. In HAWC2, proﬁle coef-
ﬁcient input (the pc ﬁle) and blade aerodynamic layout input (the ae ﬁle) must be
provided to show the airfoil characteristics. These data are obtained from [12], which
outlines the aerodynamic downscaling of the DTU 10 MW wind turbine blade for
model testing of 1:60 scale.
The pc ﬁle in HAWC2 deﬁnes lift, drag and moment coeﬃcients of the airfoils as
function of angle of attack, AoA, (Re = 40000). In [12], the data for the airfoil set
is deﬁned for AoA within the span of [-5◦, 20◦]. However, the pc ﬁle in HAWC2
must be provided for AoA within the span of [-180◦, 180◦]. For this reason, 360◦
interpolation for the airfoil set was carried out by using QBlade [9], which is an open
source software for wind turbine blade design.
The ae ﬁle in HAWC2 provides the aerodynamic properties depending on geometric
characteristics which are span-wise position, chord length and relative thickness. Both
pc ﬁle and ae ﬁle are found in Appendix A.2 and A.1, respectively.
What is more, elastic center and shear center of the blade are set 0 as the data is not

Therefore, it is also very important to

For the thrust measurements, the mean turbulence intensity is around 5% for all wind
speeds. The thrust force is logged for 30 s for each wind speed case and the mean

intensity of TI = 0.05 is used in HAWC2.

corresponding scaled rotational speed values are set and the simulations are performed
with a constant rotational speed.
It is important to mention that single point design for the new blade is done for the
thrust matched rotor design for the wind speed of V = 10 m/s for full scale which
corresponds to 1.3 m/s in model scale [7]. It means that when designing the new
blade for the small model, the aim was to match the downscaled thrust force for the
wind speed of 1.3 m/s in model scale with the same pitch angle as the DTU 10MW
reference wind turbine.

the bottom of the wind generator and the lowest blade tip position was around 15cm.

Irregular wave analysis
Irregular sea states are the most appropriate way to represent the real sea conditions
since the real wave ﬁeld consists of a mix of diﬀerent wave heights and lengths [1].
The response of the numerical model in eight diﬀerent irregular sea states without
wind is investigated. In addition, the eﬀect of wind and wave misalignment are taken
into account, respectively.
Since using random seeds in HAWC2 for generating irregular waves would result
in inaccurate comparison against the measurements, the same surface elevation is
provided in HAWC2 for each sea state from the corresponding measurement data.

Each simulation state has a duration of 10 minutes as it is in the measurements. For
the irregular wave cases, empirical exceedance probability is introduced as it is not
favorable to compare the model responses in time series. It enables to present of a
large amount of data in a brief and applicable manner. The exceedance probability
P is determined as follow
P = p(X ≥xi) = 1 −i −1
N
(5.4)
where xi is the i’th peak response sorted in increasing order and N is the number of
peaks. The exceedance probability is illustrated on a logarithmic scale in a way that
the extreme events in the response are magniﬁed.
When presenting the extreme values statistically, it is important that the observations
are uncorrelated. To ensure that the peaks are not correlated, a down-crossing analy-
sis was performed. A wave is deﬁned between two down-crossings. Note that the ﬂow
is inertia dominated as it was mentioned in Section 2.3.1. Therefore, hydrodynamic
force is in phase with the water particle acceleration.
From linear wave theory it is known that the particle acceleration is in phase with
the time derivative of the surface elevation [1]. The ﬂoater is expected to experience
the maximum acceleration at event where the slope between the trough and crest is
highest within the corresponding down-crossing period [7].
The maximum displacement for the six modes motion is expected to occur a short
moment after the wave impact. For this reason, the peak of all six modes of motion
are determined within a period that is shifted half a period forward in time series.
The tendon tension is mainly dependent on the surge motion of the model. Maximum
tension in the upwind tendon and minimum tension in the downwind tension is related
to the maximum surge motion whereas the opposite case appears as the following wave
is approaching. Thus, the maximum tension in the upwind tendon and the minimum
tension in the downwind tension are determined within a period shifted half a period
forward in time while the minimum tension in the upwind tension and the maximum
tension in the downwind tension are determined within a period shifted a full period
forward in time.
To generate similar surface elevation in the numerical model is necessary to estimate
the dynamic response of the ﬂoater more reliable. Figure 5.14 illustrates the surface
elevation in numerical model and test model. In the logarithmic scaled exceedance
probability, the part between 100 and 10−1 represents the 90% of the signal while the
values seen in the bottom of the ﬁgure represent the extreme events. Therefore, the

CONCLUSIONS
6
Conclusions
In this thesis, the numerical model of the 1:60 scaled DTU 10MW ﬂoating wind
turbine on a TLP platform is created and the validation of the numerical model has
been performed against the test results. The ﬂoating wind turbine was tested in the
oﬀshore-basin at Danish Hydraulic Institute (DHI) as part of a previous study [7].
For the purpose of the numerical model validation, the test results that were obtained
from the previous study was used in this study.
The numerical model of the tested ﬂoating wind turbine is created in HAWC2. Pa-
rameters that were used in the numerical model are obtained from the tested model.
A new blade was used in the numerical model as it was in the test model in order
to give the right thrust in model scale. This was done by applying the slim airfoils
SD7003, SD7032 and SD7062 that perform well at low Reynolds numbers. Design of
a 1:60 scaled model of the DTU 10MW wind turbine results in a model with a rotor
diameter of 3m and a hub height of 2m. The turbine was mounted on a four legged
TLP in a water depth of 3m.
Some parameters may vary in the experimental setup due to the uncertainties. In
order to estimate the dynamic behavior of the current model more accurately, some
parameters in the numerical model are calibrated. Calibration was carried out by
using trial and error method, meaning that varied attempts have been done until
matching the target value. In this sense, total mass of the system, centre of gravity
and tendon tensions are validated.
Validation of the natural frequencies and the
damping ratios are essential to have a representative numerical model. For this reason,
model decay tests are carried out. The model decay tests consist of the tower decay
test and still water decay test. Then, the rotor thrust is calibrated by changing the
design pitch values and the right thrust is delivered when the turbine operates.
The calibrated model is simulated in regular and irregular waves under the diﬀer-
ent environmental conditions. The environmental conditions for the analysis in the
numerical model were selected based on the experiments. Additionally, the eﬀect of
wind and the eﬀect of wave misalignment on the numerical model are investigated for
both regular and irregular wave analysis.
64
==PAGE:77==
6
CONCLUSIONS
In the analysis of the numerical model, the main focus was to match the surge, heave
and pitch responses with the test model responses. Therefore, only these motions are
taken into account in this study. However, it was found that pitch motion results
from the experiments are not reliable. Therefore, pitch motion is also disregarded.
Instead, the tendon tensions and the nacelle accelerations are considered.
It was found that the surge motion is closely linked to the wave amplitude. As the
wave amplitude increases, the surge response gets higher magnitudes. The other pa-
rameters which are heave, tendon tension and nacelle acceleration are mainly related
to the surge motion. Heave motion is mostly invisible in the environmental conditions
with the small wave amplitudes. Big wave height result in a more surge motion and
it makes the heave motions noticeable. The same behavior is observed for the tendon
tensions and the nacelle acceleration as well. As the platform displaces more, the
range of tendon tension and nacelle acceleration increases.
Under the eﬀect of the wind, aerodynamic thrust is taken into account. The presence
of an aerodynamic thrust leads to an increase in the surge and heave motions. In
addition, it entails an increase in the mean upwind tendons and a decrease in the
mean downwind tendons.
The misalignment of wave by an angle of 30◦reveals the sway motion and reduce the
surge motion. The remarkable point of changing the wave heading angle is that two
upwind and two downwind tendons do not share the loading equally. The tendons
being more parallel to the incoming wave direction take more of the loading.
In general, the present work has demonstrated a successful performance creating the
numerical model. It has mostly proven the ability of estimating the dynamic behavior
of the TLP wind turbine. The presence of wind results in a better match between
the numerical and the test model. When including wind, the eﬀect of uncertainties
in the experimental setup reduces and this may provide more reliable test results.
For the future studies, in order to improve the numerical model it is recommended to
• Calculate the nacelle mass moment of inertia more precisely,
• Include the elastic center and the shear center of the new blade.
It is also recommended to use at least 6 diﬀerent turbulence seeds to have more
representative results when including the wind in the simulations.
All in all, the present work has contributed to the development of the numerical
models that analyze the complete coupled behavior of the ﬂoating wind turbines.
