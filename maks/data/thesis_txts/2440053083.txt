Abstract
This project contributes to the development of an open source Powder Bed Fusion
(PBF) machine developed at the Mechanical Engineering department at the Techni-
cal University of Denmark. The current PBF machine was initially designed in the
aim to give the operator full control over the metal additive manufacturing process.
This work reviews the principles behind PBF and describes in detail the specifica-
tions of the current open source PBF machine. An experimental powder bed chamber
setup is designed in the aim to enable the creation of a powder bed with different
layer thicknesses and to ensure sufficient air-tightness of the chamber. The accuracy
of the printer system is improved by means of a calibration process that includes the
discussion of the F-θ lens fundamentals, the determination of the Rayleigh length, the
adjustment of the scanning distances and the determination of a an equation system
to correct the intrinsic distortions caused by the lens. The project ends with the
characterization of the quality of the output components, containing an analysis of
the scanning speed limits of the system, a repeatability analysis to test the experi-
mental powder bed chamber setup, a study on the energy density and the melt pool
characterization and a method to reduce the spatter of the PBF machine. Finally,
the defects found during experimentation are discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
This project contributes to the further development of an open source Additive Man-
ufacturing (AM) machine which is being developed at the Mechanical Engineering
Department at the Technical University of Denmark. The manufacturing process of
the current setup is known as Powder Bed Fusion (PBF) which consists of selectively
melting thin layers of metal powder material by means of an energy source.
Additive manufacturing technologies emerged 30 years ago and they have gained
popularity during the past years. Back in the 20th century, Henry Ford introduced
the concept of mass production, mainly based on the manufacture of large amounts
of identical products. At that time, there was a need calling for high productivity
and most of the industries decided to focus in producing large amounts of products
at a reduced cost. Although this perception is still present today, the demand for
product customization has considerably increased during the recent years, favouring
the research for additive manufacturing techniques.
Manufacturing components by using AM technologies permits to create individ-
ualized and customized products with complex structures in a reduced number of
manufacturing steps.
This essentially minimizes the production costs of intricate
structures and at the same time, it reduces the amount of tools and machinery needed
to create the components. Nevertheless, the process involves several disadvantages
compared to conventional manufacturing methods in terms of achieving shorter pro-
duction times and acquiring high mechanical properties of the pieces.
Powder Bed Fusion is one of the most recent methods to produce high strength
metal components. However, most of the investigation performed within this field
remains inaccessible and unknown for the end users, as the information is property
of the printers manufacturers. Besides that, the quality of the components will de-
pend on a large variety of factors such as the powder, the parameters utilized and
the printer configuration, which makes the transmission of knowledge difficult. As a
result, an open source PBF machine was developed by the additive manufacturing
team in DTU in the aim to give the operator full control over the printing process.
This printer will be used during this project.
==PAGE:24==
2
1 Introduction
In the current PBF machine, all the elements composing the printer are connected
to a computer and the operator can easily select and modify the parameters. This
printer is being continuously renewed and improved and the existing setup has been
proven capable of producing parts. However, the quality of the components is still very
far from that of commercial printers and very rough surfaces are obtained. Besides
that, the parameters influencing the final quality of the components are unknown,
given the number of factors involved.
1.2
Project scope
The aim of this project is to optimize the quality of the output components obtained
with the current PBF machine and to determine the influence of the manufacturing
parameters in the final quality of the pieces. That involves designing and constructing
a new powder-bed system that fits in the actual setup to enable experimental testing.
During this work several questions will be answered:
• How does the the current additive manufacturing process work?
• Which processes are involved during PBF?
• Is it possible to construct an experimental powder-bed chamber setup to acquire
successful prints?
• Which parameters have an influence in the quality of the prints?
• Can the accuracy of the current setup be improved?
• Is there any relation between the input parameters and the output quality of
the components?
• Can the current quality of the prints be compared to other commercial additive
manufacturing machines?

2.1
Additive Manufacturing
Additive manufacturing (AM) is a process involving the creation of physical compo-
nents from a three dimensional digital model by means of the consecutive addition of
material, as described in the ISO/ASTM 52900 [1]. As explained by Duda et al. [2],
this process differs from most of conventional manufacturing methods characterized
by a series of substractive techniques.
AM technologies are frequently used for rapid tooling, rapid prototyping and
reparation of components. Among the advantages of additive manufacturing are the
flexibility of design, the reduced cost of complex designs, the elimination of tooling,
the elimination of manufacturing steps and the short time to market of complex single
components. However, there are some drawbacks which are considerable slow building
rates for mass production purposes, high production costs, considerably low dimen-
sional accuracy and surface finish, need of post-processing, anisotropy and limited
component size. Information from Duda et al. [2].
Figure 2.1: Cost versus complexity of the pieces for conventional methods and for
additive manufacturing [2].

AM methods can be performed in a broad range of materials, being polymers,
metals, ceramics and composites good examples. However, the material properties
must be taken into account when selecting a specific material for a particular additive
manufacturing process. Some of the characteristics governing the different processes
are adhesion, viscosity, melting temperature, photo sensitivity, material purity, bond
type, wettability, castability, affinity with the atmosphere constituents, thermal con-
ductivity and cross-linking ability, among others. Explained by Bourell et al. [3].
2.2
Metal Additive Manufacturing
The origin of Metal Additive Manufacturing dates back to 1986, when a technique
called Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) was invented. As explained by Sames et al. [4],
SLS consisted in selectively fusing or sintering layers of polymer using a laser beam
as energy source. This method is still used today in a broad range of raw materials,
like metal. After the apparition of SLS, a process based on inkjet printing to deposit
binder was invented. Back then, it was named three dimensional printing.
Over 30 years, metal additive manufacturing technologies have started gaining
importance, especially in applications like biomedical implants, tooling or aerospace
components [4]. Today, an extensive number of metal 3D printing technologies are
being investigated and they all compete to face the main challenges of metal additive
manufacturing: limited mechanical properties, presence of defects, heat transfer along
the components, solidification and post-processing.
According to the ISO/ASTM
52900 [1], additive manufacturing technologies can be classified into seven categories,
as it can be observed in Figure 2.2. Among them, there is Powder Bed Fusion (PBF).

2.3
Powder Bed Fusion (PBF)
The term Powder Bed Fusion (PBF) is generally used in printing techniques involving
an energy source that selectively melts the powder bed region, as explained in the
work of Duda et al. [2]. The PBF printing process is composed by several steps, as
it can be observed in Figure 2.3. The process starts with a 3D CAD model, which is
sliced into layers. After that, a layer of powder material is deposited on top of the
build surface, by means of a roller or a scraper. The surplus of powder will fall into
a container, located just after the powder bed. Subsequently, the laser beam will be
directed to the mirror scanning system, whose function will be to deflect the path
of the beam, according to the information transmitted. The laser beam is focused
through a scan lens (called f-θ lens) and it will finally melt the powder, forming a melt
pool. Once it starts solidifying, the build cylinder moves down to a distance equal
to one layer thickness and a new layer of powder is deposited on top. The process is
cyclically repeated until the 3D structure is built. This information was explained by
Bremen et al. [5].

2.3.1
PBF technologies
The technologies involving the fusion of particles in a powder bed are commonly
named differently depending on the printers manufacturer. Besides that, every tech-
nique can introduce slight variations, such as the energy source. The most common
processes are laser cusing, electron beam machining, selective laser sintering and se-
lective laser melting.
Laser Cusing consists of three processes integrated in one single machine, as de-
scribed by Herderick [7]. These processes are machining, marking and laser melting.
The laser source is commonly a fiber laser of about 200W. Laser Cusing printers are
characterized by the incorporated drive motors and mirrors that change the beam di-
rection. This technology is used in micro-scaled printings and one of the advantages
is the improved surface finishing of the built structures [7].
Electron Beam Machining (EBM) is a process based in a thin layer of metal
powder which is melted by means of an electron beam energy source. As explained
in the work of McGeough [8], a high voltage is applied between the cathode, made
out of tungsten, and the anode, at ground potential. As a result, the electrons are
accelerated and subsequently directed towards the work-piece. As described by Herd-
erick [7], the powder bed chamber is in a vacuum environment and the build chamber
is commonly heated up to 700-1000◦C. The increased temperature contributes to
achieving lower internal stresses. One of the advantages of EBM is high build rates
compared to other laser powder bed technologies. However, it is characterized by its
high surface roughness, which leads to post-machining.
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) consists in partially melting layer by layer the
metal powder located in the powder bed system. As explained by Herderick [7], the
atmosphere is usually argon or nitrogen and the energy source is commonly a fiber
laser system of 200-400W. One of the main advantages of SLS systems is the improved
surface finish, compared to other technologies like EBM. However, the building rates
are quite slow and given the low temperature of the powder bed, high stresses can
appear during the printing process [7].
Selective Laser Melting (SLM) consists in selectively melting the metal pow-
der located in the powder bed system, also layer by layer. As in the Laser Cusing
technology, the main characteristic of this method is the introduction of a series of
drive motors and mirrors which change the path of the laser beam. The environment
of an SLM machine is usually nitrogen or argon and the energy source tends to be
a fiber laser of 100-400W. The powder bed chamber in SLM AM machines can also
be depressurized, achieving really low oxygen concentrations and good surface finish
levels. Described in the work of Herderick [7].

2.4
Principles of PBF
The manufacturing process behind PBF requires the understanding of a set of prin-
ciples. In this work, the principles behind PBF have been divided into three main
groups according to the principal components composing a PBF machine: energy
source, optical system and powder bed. Given that in the current PBF machine a
laser source is used as an energy source to fully melt the metal particles, the en-
ergy source described in this work will be a laser source. Thus, the main principles
described will be finally divided into:
• Laser source
• Optical system
• Powder bed
2.4.1
Laser source
The laser of a PBF machine provides the source of energy to fully melt the metal
particles laying in the powder bed system. The melting process can be explained as
consecutive micro-weldings taking place when the laser, in form of intense coherent
light, is moving along the powder bed. Described by Meshede [14].
Generally, lasers can be classified into several groups according to its wavelength.
Specifically, for micro-welding purposes, the wavelength becomes of great importance
given that large wavelengths will diminish the spot size of the laser, improving the
beam quality. As described by Naeem [15], for micro-welding purposes, the wave-
length will be commonly located in the infrared range, being higher than 700nm.
In conventional PBF machines, two types of lasers are frequently used: Neodymium
doped Yttrium Aluminum Garnet (Nd:YAG) and fibre lasers; being the latter known
for its higher beam quality and its wall plug efficiency, offering small spot sizes and
achieving an excellent ratio between input electrical power and output optical power,
respectively. This information was explained by Naeem [15].
The laser beam is characterized by a Gaussian distribution, following a symmetri-
cal ”bell-curve” shape, as described by Hetch [16]. This curve depicts the irradiance
versus the radial position from the beam axis, as it can be seen in Figure 2.5. The
irradiance is defined by Hetch as the ”amount of light illuminating a surface” or in
other terms the ”average energy per unit area per unit time” [16].

2.4.2
Optical system
Before being delivered to the powder bed system, the laser beam undergoes a series
of optical elements: an optical scanner system and a lens.
As explained by Sugioka et al. [18], the scanner system is frequently composed
by a pair of galvanometers which change the path of the incident beam. Each of the
mirrors changes the beam direction along one of the x and y axis. The galvanome-
ter is composed by three main elements, the closed loop servo control electronics,
the positioning actuator and the position detector. The author also describes three
scanning modes: step mode, vector mode and raster mode. In the ”step mode”, the
galvanometers move at maximum acceleration from one point to another. The ”vector
mode” is used to achieve accurate trajectories given that single vectors are processed
individually at controlled speed. Finally, in the ”raster mode”, the scanning is per-
formed line-by-line, the first galvanometer moves in straight lines at constant speed
and the second increments the position between lines [18].
Once the laser beam is deflected by the scanner system, the beam usually goes
through a series of lenses. Typically, a flat-field scan lens, also called F-θ lens is used
[18]. F-θ lenses are composed by an assembly of positive and negative lenses with
different curvatures in such a manner that the scanning distance in the image field x
is proportional to the product of the effective focal length, f, and the scanning angle,
θ, as described by Thompson [19]. In Eq. (2.8) this relation can be observed [19].
x = Kfθ
(2.8)
where x the scanning distance in the image field, K a constant of proportionality
and θ the scanning angle.

2.4.3
Powder bed
The powder bed chamber is the subsystem containing the metal powder to form a
three dimensional structure. As described by Bourell et al. [3], the range of materials
in commercial PBF machines is rather limited. The list of materials is mainly re-
duced to pure titanium, Ti6Al4V, 316L stainless steel, 17-4PH stainless steel, 18Ni300
maraging steel, M2 high speed steel, AlSi10Mg, CoCrMo, Iconel 718, Iconel 625 and
precious materials like gold, silver and platinum [3]. A proper powder deposition will
affect the quality of the final components. Three factors must be considered regarding
the metal powder utilized:
i) Flowability
ii) Particle size distribution
iii) Thermal conductivity and reflectivity
i) Flowability
The metal powder of conventional PBF systems is usually water/gas atomized,
providing spherical shapes, as described by Spierings et al. [21]. Spherical shapes
result in improved melt flow, which becomes essential for additive manufacturing
processes. Spierings describes a method to evaluate flowability. The method is gener-
ally known as Hall Flow Testing and it corresponds to the method recommended by
ASTM to assess the suitability of metal powders for additive manufacturing purposes.
The process is described in ISO-4490/ASTM B213 [22] and it consists in determining
the angle of repose by pouring the material on a flat surface. The angle of repose
ranges from 0◦to 90◦, an angle of 0◦will correspond to a highly flowing powder and
90◦to a highly cohesive powder.
Figure 2.8: Angle of repose of metal powder [23].

ii) Particle size distribution
The distribution of the metal particles plays an important role in determining the
amount of porosity of the components. As described by Panwisawas et al. [26], dif-
ferent combinations of packing density, particle size distribution and layer thickness
will result in distinct packing configurations. As explained by the author, most of
the commercially distributed powders are composed by different particle sizes, which
usually range from 20 µm to 50 µm in diameter.
The spherical nature of the particles leads to a considerable efficient packing of
the powder. However, voids are still present between particles of same diameter. As
described in the work of Spierings [25], these voids are commonly filled with finer
grains of smaller dimensions.
The density of the powder bed is directly related to the final density of the manu-
factured components. As described by Karapatis [27], the highest density values are
achieved when the powder is composed by 30 wt% of finer particles and 70 wt% of
coarser particles with a size ratio between fine and coarse particles of 1:10.
iii) Thermal conductivity and reflectivity
Alloys with high reflectivity and high thermal conductivity become bad candidates
for PBF. The main reason is that due to the materials low absorption it becomes very
difficult to create an effective melt pool. High laser powers of up to 1000W are com-
monly used to increase the absorption levels. Aluminum, silver and gold are good
examples [3].

In addition to the powder requirements, several conditions in the powder bed cham-
ber can severely affect the quality of the final components:
i) Balling effect
ii) Affinity for atmosphere constituents
iii) Residual stresses
i) Balling effect
One of the defects affecting the quality of the printed components is ”balling”,
identified in the form of spheres formed by the melted liquid. As explained by Kruth
et al. [12], this phenomenon originates due to poor wettability of the molten mate-
rial laying on the build plate. As the author describes, balling phenomenon results
in very rough surfaces and reduced density values, affecting most of the mechanical
properties of the printed components.
Poor wettability can be explained by the surface tension term. As described by
Kruth et al. three states need to be considered: a non-reactive liquid (L), a smooth
and homogeneous solid surface (S) and the presence of vapor (V). Then, if the liquid
doesn’t entirely cover the solid, a contact angle θγ can be defined between the solid
surface and the liquid, as seen in Figure 2.9. This contact angle θγ corresponds to
the mechanical equilibrium of a droplet laying on a solid surface under the action
of three different surface tensions (γSV , γSL and γLV ), defined between the different
surface interfaces, as explained by Young [28] by means of the Young’s equation in
Eq. (2.11). The molten pool formed during the scanning process of a single line can
be approximated to half a cylinder, as explained by Kruth et al. Then, if the total
surface of the cylinder becomes larger than that of a sphere of the same volume, the
balling effect occurs in the aim to minimize the surface free energy.

The ”balling effect” can be prevented by reducing the oxygen contents, given that
oxide products prevent chemical reactions to occur. Another possibility is remelting,
in order to break down the oxide films formed. Besides, certain alloying elements like
Phosphorous (P) have been proved to improve wettability, as well as very high pulse

ii) Affinity for atmosphere constituents
Some metals form stable oxides when they are in contact with oxygen, especially
powders containing iron. As described by Rombouts et al. [29], most Fe powders
initially contain oxygen as an impurity (0.1 wt %). In addition to this percentage,
the residual oxygen content of the atmosphere is absorbed during the PBF process.
At high temperatures, the oxygen diffuses into the metal and the amount of oxygen
dissolved in liquid Fe increases (at 1700 ◦C it is 0.4 wt%). Then, when the liquid
metal starts to solidify, the concentration of oxygen exceeds the maximal dissolved
oxygen content and metal oxides are formed. Li et al. [30] recommend an oxygen con-
tent in the atmosphere below 1%. Bourell et al. [3] also describes a similar behavior
for aluminum and aluminum alloys forming an oxide layer of Al2O3 on the surface of
the components.
iii) Residual stresses
When the laser melts the powder, a localized heat affected zone (HAZ) is created.
The HAZ is in contact with a large cold area, involving high thermal gradients. As a
result, high residual stresses arise. The printed structures commonly have compres-
sive stresses in the center and high tensile stresses in the contours. Residual stresses
are mainly caused by the printing process. On the other hand, cracks tend to be
dependent on the material. While some materials exhibit macroscopic cracks, such as
Ti6Al4V and M2 high speed steel, others are characterized by microscropic cracking,
like in the case of nickel based alloys.

4 Design of a powder bed chamber
4.1
Definition
The construction of the powder bed chamber aims to accomplish one of the main
purposes of the project which is optimizing the quality of the output components.
The printing ability of the current PBF printer was validated in previous projects.
However, the printing quality was far from that of commercial printers. Therefore,
the product design perspective in this project will be to design a powder bed chamber
prototype to execute the additive manufacturing experiments.
The concept of the product design is to build a product that enables to gain
control of the printings process. Then, besides ensuring high quality printings, the
system needs to enable the deposit of at least one layer of powder material and permit
an easy control of the layer thickness.
4.2
Design specifications
The powder bed chamber needs to meet several requirements to guarantee the success
of the additive manufacturing process.
In first place, the product needs to ensure that the printings can be effectuated
under an inert gas atmosphere and an oxygen content <1%. Thus, the product re-
quires to be completely air-tight and contain an inlet for the inert gas to enter and
an outlet for the oxygen to escape. In addition, the product must have a place to
incorporate an oxygen sensor and it must prioritize the safety of the users.
The design needs to be adapted to handle the powder bed particles. A mechanism
to form the powder bed will be needed as well as a compartment to keep the surplus
of powder. Besides that, the oxygen sensor needs to be protected of the micron metal
particles.
The small size of the powder particles leads to another requirement, the easiness
to clean the chamber. Given that every printing process will require the formation
of a new powder bed, the particles need to be easily removed to achieve successful
printings.
During the manufacturing process, the particles melt and weld to the build plate
which means that every print requires a new build plate. Thus, ensuring an easy re-
moval of the build plate is an essential requirement. The chamber needs to be easily
accessible to be able to exchange the build plate and at the same time it needs to
ensure air-tightness once the build plate is in place.
Overall, the product must be stiff and resistant and all components need to with-
stand high temperatures, thus a high melting temperature of the materials is also

required. The chamber also needs to ensure that the laser can enter the chamber and
it must include the possibility to see through the powder bed chamber to enable easy
monitoring.
Finally, the size of the powder bed chamber must be big enough to comfortably
handle the metal particles and light enough to be able to move it. It is important to
note that the dimensions of the product will be limited by the current setup dimen-

The deposition system was tested by pouring metal powder on the build plate and

magnetic moments of the atoms become randomized instead of aligned. At Tc, the
spin-coupling forces are destroyed and the material loses its magnetism. As it can be
observed in Figure 4.19 (b), most of the magnetism was eliminated but some zones
were still magnetized. As it is shown in Figure 4.20, the quality of the powder bed
was very poor and three lines could visibly be observed. Finally, a new blade made

5.1
Methodology and equipment
5.1.1
Methodology
The calibration of the PBF machine is performed by engraving steel plates of 2 mm
thickness, which were previously coated with black spray paint. The black coating
will evaporate due to the high energy levels provided by the laser source. As a result,
a trace corresponding to the trajectory of the laser will be generated.
The engravings performed can be classified into two types depending on the
method used to send the information to the galvanometers.
The first method in-
volves writing the coordinates (in degrees) directly into the “Gcode Sender” whereas
the second method consists in using the “Slicer” program to convert the STL distances
from mm to degrees. A good understanding of the different methods is convenient to
successfully calibrate the PBF machine.
The conversion employed in the Slicer program utilizes a tangent approach to
transform the distances from mm to degrees. That involves that the height from the
galvanometer mirrors to the build plate must be set to conduct the conversion. Per
contra, the F-θ lens establishes a linear relation between the scanning distance and
the product of the focal length and the scanning angle, as it can be observed in Figure
5.3. The linear and the tangent approach are represented in Figure 5.4, where the
relation between the scan angle (θ) and the scan distance (x) can be observed. This
graph shows that at low scanning angles, the relation between the scanning angle
and the scanning distance can be assumed to be linear. Thus, given that the working
scan angles range from 0◦to 20◦, the error due to the Slicer conversion method can
be assumed to be minimal.

In order to explain the origin of the distortion occurring before the calibration
performed by the manufacturer technical service, it is important to understand the
function principle of the galvanometers. As it can be seen in Figure 5.22 (a), given a
scanning angle θx, the motor of the galvanometer x is activated. As a consequence,
the mirror x rotates and a line is drawn in the x axis direction. A similar behavior
occurs when a certain angle θy is sent to the galvanometer y. As a result, a line is
drawn in the y axis direction. It is important to note that in the Figures it has been
assumed that the two galvanometers are completely perpendicular.
The distortion phenomenon can probably be caused by the rotation of the entire
pair involving the motor and the mirror. When the motors are correctly mounted in
the mount frame, the laser beam centering process consists of setting the mirrors to
their respective relaxed positions, corresponding to the coordinates (10,10), expressed
in degrees, as it can be observed in Figure 5.22 (a). However, if the entire pair motor-
mirror is rotated, the centering process will be effectuated in the same manner, by
setting the mirrors to be in their relaxed positions (10,10). Then, when the laser beam
is centered to fit the hole of the centering plate, the laser beam could be slightly tilted,

According to the literature, the experimental cross-sections obtained during this
project should show a keyhole porosity, given the high energy densities. However,
they show much smaller melt pool depths, differing from the expected keyhole geom-
etry. Nevertheless, a behavior similar to the lack of fusion geometry is commonly
observed, especially at low laser powers (P=100W). This behavior is caused by the
balling effect, usually appearing at low energy densities and at very low laser powers.
It is important to note that the energy densities utilized are extremely high due to
the scanning speed limitations. Thus, it can be concluded that high laser powers are
required to fuse the metal particles and create a proper melt pool depth to enhance
good wettability between the melted powder and the build plate.
Another possible assumption to explain the differences in the melt pool geometry
is that the large energy densities are causing instability in the melt pool during the
printing process. An indication of this behavior is the misalignment between the melt
pool and the line track cross-section, as it can be observed in Figure 6.12. When high
energy densities are applied, the metal powder particles can exceed their melting tem-
perature (2857◦for Fe) [52] and vaporize. The vapor is formed by molecules moving
at high speed, generating an increase of pressure in the melt pool. This pressure is
known as recoil pressure [52].

6.5
Spattering reduction
The recoil pressure becomes determinant in the formation of spatter. At low energy
densities, the recoil pressure will be low, flattening the melt pool. In contrast, high
energy densities will result in high recoil pressures, causing material removal of the
melt pool in the form of droplet spatter. Besides that, the vaporization of metal
can cause the surrounding metal particles to disperse. The spatter behavior can be
observed in Figure 6.13. In the work of Liu et al. [52] they classify the spatter types
regarding the size of the jetted particles. The spatter coming from the melt pool is
identified in the form of oversized clusters, while the spatter coming from the raw
metal powder maintains its original size. However, it is very likely that the jetted
powder particles removed from the powder bed interact with the laser beam, as the
laser beam moves forward, altering the particle size.
Figure 6.13: Spatter representation in the form of melt pool droplets and powder.
The spatter particles accumulate in the other zones of the powder bed still con-
taining raw powder bed material to be fused, creating uneven surfaces. In order to
reduce the effect of the spatter particles in the rest of the powder bed, a fan was in-
corporated to the powder bed chamber design. The fan would be functioning during
the manufacture of the line tracks and the air flow created would set aside the jetted
particles once they separate from the build plate.
An experiment was performed to analyze the effect of the fan introduction. In or-
der to assess any differences, a total of 4 build plates were utilized with 21 line tracks
of exactly the same parameters (P=150W, v=40mm/s, t=50µm, hd=d=100µm). Two
of them were printed with a fan incorporated in the setup while the rest were man-
ufactured without any ventilation. The incorporated fan can be observed in Figure
6.14.

Parameter investigation
The parameter analysis was performed by printing line tracks with different setting pa-
rameters. In the experiments, the layer thickness was maintained constant (t=50µm)
while the power and the scanning speed were tested at different levels. As it can be
observed in Figure 6.16, the roughness tends to increase with the scanning speed. Par

shown in Figure 6.16, two repetitions were used for each of the setting parameters
and that can easily lead to misleading conclusions. However, this observation might
give an indication for further experimentation.
The results obtained can be compared to those encountered in the melt pool
characterization. At high laser power (P=200W), the energy applied is sufficient to
fuse the powder material and subsequently create a melt pool that welds the powder
material to the build plate. At lower powers (P=100W), the energy applied might
be not enough to fuse the metal particles. As a result, the melt pool can’t even be
noticed. Regarding the scanning speed, lower scanning speeds lead to higher energy
densities given the increased amount of time that the energy source strikes into a
particular point. As a consequence, a wider melt pool might be created which can
result in an improved adhesion of the powder material to the build plate.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This project aimed to contribute to the project development of an accessible open
source PBF machine created to give the operator full control over the entire metal
additive manufacturing process. This work has focused in the final stage of the manu-
facturing process based on the quality characterization of the final output components.
In order to satisfy the objectives, research has been made to understand the work-
ing principles under PBF of metal and to determine the specifications of this particular
setup. The design of an experimental setup has enabled to perform the experiments
under appropriate conditions. Besides that, the accuracy and precision of the setup
has been improved and a system of equations has been proposed aiming to correct
intrinsic distortions. The work concludes with the characterization and investigation
of the parameters affecting the quality of the components.
The main characteristics of the open source PBF machine were explained in detail
in Chapter 3: Printer system, with an overview of the software and hardware
utilized and a description of the connection between them. A description of the com-
ponents specifications and the operational method was performed to give the reader
a good understanding of this specific printer and, at the same time, document the
main working principles that could serve for further investigations. This chapter also
serves as a basis for the upcoming sections.
The development of the experimental setup explained in Chapter 4: Design of
a powder bed chamber described the iterative process in order to satisfy all the
design specifications described. The design concept evolved from a preliminary de-
sign based on achieving very tight tolerances to a less vulnerable and more economic
design characterized by the use of easily machinable rectangular build plates and an
adjustable recoater. Many design characteristics were ameliorated through the iter-
ative process leading to a design incorporating intricate walls and sealing bands to
avoid the escape of air. The final design was proved to maintain very low oxygen
contents of around 0.1% and capable of deposit a uniform powder bed.
The calibration phase described in Chapter 5: Calibration examined the dif-
ferences between the “linear approach” of the F-θ lens, ensuring a linear relation
between the scanning angles and the scanning distances and the “tangent approach”,
==PAGE:124==
102
7 Conclusion
utilized in the Slicer software. Both methods showed a linear relationship between
the scanning angles and the scanning distances at low scanning angles (±20), validat-
ing the Slicer conversion method from millimeters to degrees. The Rayleigh length
and the confocal parameter were experimentally determined and compared to their
theoretical values. Both methods indicated a focus region of around 14 mm. The
height of the breadboard was adjusted to ensure that the laser beam was focused at
the build plate height. After that, the height set in the Slicer software to convert
from millimeters to degrees was adjusted and the offset between the experimental val-
ues obtained and the theoretical values sent to the printer was significantly reduced.
The maximum offset registered was 0.353 mm in the y axis direction. At the end of
this chapter, an equation system was determined to correct the default barrel effect
distortion caused by the F-θ lens.
The experiments performed in Chapter 6: Optimization of 3D metal print-
ing to optimize the quality of the output components started with an analysis of
the scanning speed, where the maximum speed was encountered, corresponding to
80 mm/s. This value was compared to that of a commercial PBF machine and it
was found to be an order of magnitude below the industrial printing parameters.
The reduced scanning speed was found to limit the experimentation given that it
was causing an enormous increase in the energy density values. At the maximum
scanning speed, the laser power needed to satisfy the energy densities of commercial
PBF machines was calculated. This value corresponded to 50W which in most cases
wouldn’t be enough to fuse the metal particles. A method was proposed as a solution
to maintain the energy density and achieve better adhesion of the metal particles,
based on increasing both the scanning speed and the laser power.
The melt pool was also examined in Chapter 6: Optimization of 3D metal
printing, which could only be identified at very high energy densities and consid-
erably high laser powers (P>150W). Besides that, the melt pool geometry showed
possible signs of instability, probably originated by the high energy density values
and a consequent vaporization of the metal, causing an increase of pressure. The
spatter was proved to be affecting the quality and this phenomenon was significantly
reduced by incorporating a small fan inside the experimental setup to set aside the
jetted particles during manufacture. A parameter analysis was also performed, indi-
cating a tendency of low roughness at low velocities and high powers. Finally, an
analysis of the defects encountered during the experimentation was performed, where
the defects of balling, heat affected zone, spatter, worsening and widening of scan
tracks and debonding were identified.

CHAPTER 8
Future work
The future work of this project would involve in first place, further research and con-
sequent installation of a heat sink in the galvanometer mirror system that helps to
easily dissipate the heat. A suggestion would be a heat sink from the same manufac-
turer, model GHS003/M from Thorlabs. Another recommendation would be checking
the output power of the SPI fiber laser and verify that there aren’t any picks of power
when running the laser in CW. Besides that, mounting a pressurized enclosure around
the galvanometer system to prevent the metal particles from depositing on top of the
mirrors.
Once the galvanometer mirrors are secured, an experiment to detect the barrel
distortion effect at high scanning angles (±20) should be performed.
In the case
of identifying this type of distortion, a new holeplate involving larger distances and
larger scanning angles should be engraved and the correction equation system should
be determined. Then, a new holeplate should be engraved to test if the barrel effect
disappears.
Another suggestion is to implement a new galvanometer system capable of mov-
ing at higher scanning speeds, in order to reduce the energy density values. Once
done, a test should be performed at higher scanning speeds and higher power. When
the manufature parameters are comparable to those of commercial PBF machines, a
study on the melt pool characterization should be done in order to be able to identify
any trends between the printing parameters and the melt pool depth.
A test involving a certain percentage of oxygen should be performed to test any
differences between the experiments performed in a almost oxygen free environment
(0.1%) and the new tests involving different percentages of oxygen. If the experiments
look alike, the oxygen should be discarded as a factor taking place in the balling for-
mation. Besides that, experimentation with another metal powder material would be
advised.
Finally, it is highly recommended to be very cautious with the metal particles, to
handle the metal particles outside the printing cabinet and protect all the elements
of the PBF machine from dust.

APPENDIX B
Programming
The programming language that reads and interprets the coordinates and the diam-
eters of the holeplates acquired by the DeMeet optical microscope was developed in
Matlab. This code was utilized to acquire the data and to create the Figures showing
the differences between the experimental and the theoretical values. The code can be
observed below.
==PAGE:148==
%Clear all the previous data 
clear all; 
close all; 
clc; 
  
% Read excel Experimental coordinates 
A=xlsread('holeplate_experimental.xlsx'); 
  
%Read excel Theoretical coordinates 
B=xlsread('Holeplate_theoretical.xlsx'); 
  
%Create two matrices with the experimental coordinates 
Xcoord=[]; 
Ycoord=[]; 
N=size(A,1); 
for k=1:1:N 
    if k==2 || rem(k-2,3)==0 
        Xcoord=[Xcoord A(k,1)]; 
    end 
    if k==3 || rem(k-3,3)==0 
        Ycoord=[Ycoord A(k,1)]; 
    end 
end 
 
%Center the experimental coordinates 
Mx= 40*ones(1,25,'uint16'); 
My= Mx; 
Xcoord= Xcoord-Mx; 
Ycoord=Ycoord-My; 
 
%Create two matrices with the theoretical coordinates 
Xcoord_th=[]; 
Ycoord_th=[]; 
N=size(B,1); 
for k=1:1:N 
    if k==2 || rem(k-2,3)==0 
        Xcoord_th=[Xcoord_th B(k,1)]; 
    end 
    if k==3 || rem(k-3,3)==0 
        Ycoord_th=[Ycoord_th B(k,1)]; 
    end 
end 
  
%Center the experimental coordinates 
Xcoord_th= Xcoord_th-Mx; 
Ycoord_th=Ycoord_th-My; 
  
% Locate the center cercle in the center Dif_x=Xcoord(13)-Xcoord_th(13); 
Dif_y=Ycoord(13)-Ycoord_th(13); 
for k=1:1:25 
        Xcoord(k)=Xcoord(k)-Dif_x; 
        Ycoord(k)=Ycoord(k)-Dif_y; 
end 
  
  
 
 
==PAGE:149==
% Rotation matrix to align the point P 
P=12; 
XYcoord=[]; 
if Ycoord(P)~=Ycoord_th(P) 
    if Ycoord(P)*Xcoord(P)>0 
        Angle= -tan(Ycoord(P)/Xcoord(P)); 
        Rotation_m=[cos(Angle) -sin(Angle); sin(Angle) cos(Angle)]; 
    else 
        Angle= tan(Ycoord(P)/Xcoord(P)); 
        Rotation_m=[cos(Angle) -sin(Angle); sin(Angle) cos(Angle)]; 
    end 
end 
  
for i=1:1:25 
    XYcoord= [XYcoord Rotation_m*[Xcoord(i); Ycoord(i)]]; 
end 
Xcoord=XYcoord(1,:); 
Ycoord=XYcoord(2,:); 
 
%Create a matrix with the 25 experimental diameters 
Diam_exp=[]; 
for k=1:1:N 
    if k==1 || rem(k-1,4)==0 
        Diam_exp=[Diam_exp A(k,1)]; 
    end 
end 
 
%Create a matrix with the 25 theoretical diameters 
Diam_th=[]; 
for k=1:1:N 
    if k==1 || rem(k-1,4)==0 
        Diam_th=[Diam_th B(k,1)]; 
    end 
end 
 
%Calculate the diameter mean 
Mean_diam=mean(Diam_exp); 
Mean_diam_th=mean(Diam_th); 
  
std_diam=std(Diam_exp); 
std_diam_th=std(Diam_th); 
  
%Calculate the number of circles 
NXYcoord=size(Xcoord,2); 
  
%Create matrices to connect adjacent circles.  
Link_x_1=[]; 
Link_x_2=[]; 
Link_y_1=[]; 
Link_y_2=[]; 
  
Link_x_1_th=[]; 
Link_x_2_th=[]; 
Link_y_1_th=[]; 
Link_y_2_th=[]; 
  
hipot= sqrt(2)*abs(Xcoord(1)-Xcoord(2)); 
==PAGE:150==
  
for i=1:1:NXYcoord 
    for j=1:1:NXYcoord 
        if abs(Xcoord(i)-Xcoord(j))+ abs(Ycoord(i)-Ycoord(j))< hipot && 
i<j 
                Link_x_1=[Link_x_1  Xcoord(i)]; 
                Link_x_2=[Link_x_2  Xcoord(j)]; 
                Link_y_1=[Link_y_1  Ycoord(i)]; 
                Link_y_2=[Link_y_2  Ycoord(j)]; 
                 
                Link_x_1_th=[Link_x_1_th  Xcoord_th(i)]; 
                Link_x_2_th=[Link_x_2_th  Xcoord_th(j)]; 
                Link_y_1_th=[Link_y_1_th  Ycoord_th(i)]; 
                Link_y_2_th=[Link_y_2_th  Ycoord_th(j)]; 
    end 
    end 
end 
  
  
Xs=Link_x_1'; 
Xd=Link_x_2'; 
Xf=[Xs Xd]; 
  
Ys=Link_y_1'; 
Yd=Link_y_2'; 
Yf=[Ys Yd]; 
  
Xs_th=Link_x_1_th'; 
Xd_th=Link_x_2_th'; 
Xf_th=[Xs_th Xd_th]; 
  
Ys_th=Link_y_1_th'; 
Yd_th=Link_y_2_th'; 
Yf_th=[Ys_th Yd_th]; 
  
  
%Calculate the difference and the error 
X_error=[]; 
Y_error=[]; 
X_diff=[]; 
Y_diff=[]; 
for i=1:1:25 
    X_diff(i)=Xcoord(i)-Xcoord_th(i); 
    X_error(i)=abs(X_diff(i)/Xcoord_th(i)); 
     
    Y_diff(i)=Ycoord(i)-Ycoord_th(i); 
    Y_error(i)=abs(Y_diff(i)/Ycoord_th(i)); 
end 
Xcoord_t=Xcoord'; 
Ycoord_t=Ycoord'; 
 
% Plot the experimental and the theoretical centers 
p1=plot(Xf',Yf','g-'); 
hold on 
p2=plot(Xf_th',Yf_th','b--'); 
hold on
