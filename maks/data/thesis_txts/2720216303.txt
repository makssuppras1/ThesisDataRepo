Summary
Patient relocation is a potential solution to alleviate overcrowding in hospi-
tals by matching patients’ demands and available resources. However, patient
relocation also introduces internal interactions, which bring challenges in the
allocation problem of medical resources.
We consider an N-department hospital where patients arrive in departments ac-
cording to independent Poisson processes and are independently cured after an
exponential amount of time, while the patients abandon if they do not get beds
immediately. An agent-based simulation model of hospitalization and relocation
is developed to validate the performance of bed distributions in such a hospital.
This study aims to minimize the number of patients rejected by their preferred
wards by allocating beds in different wards optimally. Inspired by convergent
infinite series, we estimate the performance differences between neighborhood
points and the current point. Granting bounty and penalty to estimators based
on their sign correctness, the adaptive search algorithm applies the results of
estimation by probability, when it searches the neighborhood.
The experimental results show that compared to local search, the adaptive
search can find a better solution in limited time, and take less time to terminate
with a local optimal; For 20-ward cases, the average runtime saving over
local search is 91.2%.

1. Introduction 
Overcrowding of hospital wards is a well-known problem in the healthcare sector. Some 
Danish hospitals are implementing patient relocation to balance the wards’ capacity and 
cope with overcrowding. Unmet demands of patients and the idle medical resources can 
be matched by the patient relocation, alleviating the overcrowding and reducing the 
expensive cost due to waiting and inter-hospital transferring (Sikkert, 2015). In these 
hospitals, daily capacity meetings and dedicated staff support patient flow coordination, 
and patients will be relocated from overcrowded wards to wards with resources available 
for treatment.  
 
However, patient relocation also introduces 2 problems for the hospital. First, although 
relocations can alleviate overcrowding, they also require considerable effort. This is 
because the relocations will significantly affect the quality of care and the hereto-related 
costs in the following weeks; decision-making of relocation, therefore, must be 
thoroughly considered. Second, patient relocation also brings challenges in allocating 
medical resources. When patient relocation is enabled, a specific ward may accept its 
corresponding patients and relocated patients from other wards, and the arrivals of 
relocated patients are affected by the resources allocated in other wards. Thus, the 
overcrowding in a ward is related to not only the resources in the ward, but also the 
resources in other wards.  
 
This study aims to optimally allocate medical resources in a centralized hospital with 
multiple departments such that minimize the needed cost related to relocation is 
minimized. Resource allocation problem is frequently discussed in operations research, 
and its application in health care area can date back to Arrow, K. J. (1972). However, 
patient relocation introduces internal interactions to the hospitalization system and 
separates our study from those well-researched bed allocation problems. 
 
In this study, we propose a simulation-based optimization approach to optimally allocate 
medical resources among different wards. The scale of solvable problems is sufficient to 
support decision-making in hospitals. More specifically, we use a simulation model to 
estimate the performance of given resource distributions, and utilize a heuristic to find an 
optimal distribution effectively. 
 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
The main contributions of this study are as follows: 
1) We develop an agent-based simulation model of the hospitalization system with 
patient relocation, and this simulation model can evaluate the performance of a 
feasible solution in polynomial time. 
2) We utilize Erlang-B formula and the gradient decrease method to generate a good 
initial solution, reducing the time required to find a local optimal solution. 
3) We propose an estimation method to pre-estimate the performance of neighborhood 
points, so that the number of validations for every iteration is expected to be reduced 
and the heuristic is speeded up. 
4) Compared to local search, our algorithm can guarantee a local optimal in shorter time 
and return a better solution in limited running time, demonstrated by the numerical 
results based on the data. 
5) Our algorithm can terminate and guarantee a local optimal in around 12 hours for a 
20-ward case, which is sufficient for almost all hospitals.  
 
In section 2, we review relevant research. In section 3, we describe the problem and build 
a mathematical model based on the problem. Section 4 illustrates the simulation model of 
the hospitalization system with relocation and implements the model testing. Section 5 
explains our heuristic algorithm (adaptive search based on estimation). We also present 
numerical experiments and discuss our solution approach in section 6. Lastly, we present 
our conclusions in Section 7.

2. Literature review 
Hospital bed utilization is a big and recurrent topic in healthcare management, dating 
back to Newsholme (1932). We restrict our review to validation of bed distributions and 
optimization of allocating beds.  
 
The papers usually validate the performance of a certain bed distribution using either: (1) 
simulation (Goldman et al. 1968; Kokangul 2008; Troy et al. 2009; Mohamed et al. 2021; 
Hajłasz & Mielczarek 2021); (2) queueing theoretic approaches (Gorunescu et al. 2002; 
Pinto et al. 2014). 
 
Goldman et al. (1968) investigate levels of bed utilization under various bed allocation 
policies by means of a simulation model. Kokangul (2008) gets the distributions of the 
daily accepted, rejected, transferred arrivals, discharges, and length of stay with a 
simulation model. Troy et al. (2009) uses a simulation model based on the Monte Carlo 
technique to find the start and end times of hospitalization. In Mohamed et al. (2021), 
several key performance indexes (KPIs), including utilization level, admission rate and 
service level, are estimated with a discrete event simulation model. Hajłasz & Mielczarek 
(2021) use a simulation model to predict hospital admissions and bed utilization in a 
pediatric ward and a geriatric ward with population data as input. 
 
In the area of queueing theoretic approaches, Gorunescu et al. (2002) utilizes the M/PH/c 
queue to indicate the progress of treatment, and dismissal of patients who have gone 
through sufficient stages. Pinto et al. (2014) uses a queueing model to plan the bed 
capacity and develops a simulation model to evaluate the results obtained by queuing 
model and to check its usability. This research is also an example for exploiting the use 
of both simulation and queueing theory. 
 
As for the optimization of allocating beds, existing optimization models differ from one 
study to another. There are various objective functions depending on the goal of the 
study, but most studies focus on three major objectives: (1) facility performance (Boaden 
et al., 1999), (2) reduction in costs (Kim et al., 1999), and (3) patient loss, wait and 
satisfaction (Torabipour et al., 2016.; Claret et al., 2016).  
 
Much of the recent studies involve the rich context of sub-units of hospitals (Levi et al., 
==PAGE:13==
4 
 
2018, Cardoen et al., 2010; McClean & Millard, 1998), which can include the intensive 
care unit (ICU), emergency department (ED), operating room (OR), transitional care unit 
(TCU) and other departments, while other models (Andersen et al. 2019; Schäfer et al. 
2017) introduce the characteristics of patients, such as gender, age, expected 
hospitalization, isolation requirements, etc. On the other hand, some very simplified 
models or analysis of a single ward utilize shortest path model in network optimization 
(Akcali et al.2006), mixed integer programming (Bachouch et al. 2012), and linear 
programming (Zhou et al. 2012). 
 
Although our model lacks the contextual richness of some of the metioned models for 
wards and patients, the most distinctive feature of our model is the generality of the 
patient admission, which allows us to grasp the essential problem, that we need to solve a 
resource allocation problem with sufficiently large size in feasible time. 
 
The most closely related paper is Andersen et al. (2017), which also considers a hospital 
enabling relocation of patients. Compared to our study, they use a homogeneous 
continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC) to calculate the probabilities of system status. 
They also mentioned the size of state space |𝑆| of the CTMC is in billions even if the 
number of wards is only 3. Although they use the truncation procedures to reduce the size 
of the state space and make the model applicable for small cases, this approach cannot 
solve a sufficiently large problem in acceptable time.

3. Problem description and modeling 
3.1 Problem background and target 
At the moment when medical resources are tight, allocating limited resources to different 
wards is an important tactical decision for a comprehensive and multi-department 
hospital. The scarcity of resources causes overcrowding, and the mismatch of resources 
and demands exacerbates the overcrowding; the mismatched resources are idle, while 
patients whose demands are not met have to wait for the fewer resources simultaneously. 
A typical example mentioned by Li (2021) is that the medical resources were over-
allocated in obstetrics and pediatrics in China, whereas hospitalizations in cardiovascular 
and oncology are very tight and overcrowded.  
 
Another argument to support the importance of the resource allocation decision is that it 
is difficult for a stable hospital to reallocate resources among different wards in the short 
term. Patient relocation is a possible solution to balance capacity without changes in the 
distribution of resources. In this study, we consider a centralized and comprehensive 
hospital with multiple wards, where the total amount of available medical resources and 
the division of wards will remain stable for a certain period. Also, dedicated staff and the 
coordination mechanism of different wards for relocation have been fully implemented, 
and relocation is the main method to cope with overcrowding.  
 
Selecting proper alternative wards for patients who are rejected by the preferred wards is 
the basic for relocation. The relocation decisions are important, because improper 
alternative wards will lead to the mismatches between patients and wards, impacting the 
quality of care and leading to potential costs. However, allocating patients beds is never 
an easy decision. Schäfer (2017) believed the patient-bed allocation needs to concern 
three stakeholders: patients, medical staff, and hospital management. Wang (2020) 
concerned the patient-bed allocation as a bilevel problem where decision-makers need to 
consider the optimal countermeasures of patients to the tactic of hospitals.  
 
Based on above reasons and related studies, choosing an alternative ward is a complex 
decision and requires unignorable effort. Thus, we hope to reduce the number of 
relocation decisions by assigning medical resources optimal. When the distribution of 
medical resource is proper, more patients can be accepted by their preferred wards and 
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
doctors can discuss less cases of relocations. 
 
Based on the solid advantages and cost saving of relocation within a hospital (Sikkert, 
2015), we rationally assume that the staff of hospital make efforts to assign an alternative 
ward for every patient who is rejected by the preferred ward. Thus, our goal can be 
transformed to minimizing the number of rejections at preferred wards by changing the 
distribution of resources. 
 
For the remaining of this paper, a patient hospitalization at a preferred ward, will be 
denoted as a primary hospitalization. A patient relocation to an alternative ward, will be 
denoted as a secondary hospitalization. In the same way, patient blocking at a preferred 
ward, is denoted a primary rejection, as well as patient blocking at the alternative ward is 
denoted a secondary rejection. 
 
3.2 Assumptions and simplifications 
When building the model, we need to make some assumptions to abstract the problem to 
a model rationally and achieve the goal more efficiently. We mentioned that relocations 
are complex decisions, and that the hospital has sufficient staff, organizational structure, 
and coordination to support these decisions. We, therefore, focus on the relocation 
probability of patients from the preferred ward to another ward. For specific cases, we 
can get these average relocation probabilities among different wards. Then, we simply 
assume that the patient has a chance of being relocated to another ward and that the 
probability of relocation equals the average relocation probability of the type.  
 
We also assume that the staff of the hospital will make efforts to assign an alternative 
ward for every patient who is rejected by the preferred ward. This assumption allows us 
to consider the objective function as minimizing the number of primary rejections. 
What’s more, an intuitive assumption is that no patients will be relocated when there are 
idle resources in the preferred ward type. 
 
Another factor that we may abstract and simplify is the medical resources in this 
problem. There is a range of medical resources in a hospital, but we abstract all resources 
that support a hospitalization into a “sufficiently” staffed and equipped bed. Meanwhile, 
we ignore that hospitalization may occur when staff or equipment is insufficient. 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
 
 
Furthermore, we assume that we can allocate all beds among different wards. This is 
because, if an available hospitalization in a ward is restricted by the lack of resources, 
these resources always exist in another ward.

4. Simulation model of hospitalization with 
relocation 
We consider a case with 𝑁 patient types, 𝑖∈{1,2, … , 𝑁}, and 𝑁 wards, 𝑗∈
{1,2, … , 𝑁}. For the convenience of subsequent discussion, when the type of the patient 
equals to the label of ward (𝑖= 𝑗), we define that the ward is the preferred of the patient. 
In this case, different patients arrive according to independent Poisson processes. When a 
patient arrives, he may be allocated a bed in his preferred ward or alternative ward, or he 
may not get a bed and leave the hospital directly. If a patient is allocated in a bed, it will 
stay in the hospital, get treatment and leave after getting cured.  
 
Taking the above context of this problem into account, we need to consider some 
characteristics of the simulation model. 
1. The ward-blocking model must be stochastic. This is because the arrival interval 
and treatment time are not deterministic but follow certain distributions covered 
in several empirical studies; the relocations of patients are not deterministic yet. 
The model, therefore, needs random variables to simulate these stochastic factors.  
2. The model must be dynamic, because we generally think of a hospital as a 
dynamic entity. The patients subsequently arrive the hospital, and the hospital 
cannot foresee future patients.  
3. The status of the model is discrete, and discrete events in continuous time can 
change it. In the real world, the events changing model status include arrival, 
relocation and departure of a patient. These events occur in real-time. 
 
We use MATLAB to develop an agent-based simulation model of hospitalization system. 
The performance analysis algorithm, as well as the comparison of running time, is 
carried out on this basis.

5. A heuristic optimization algorithm 
This research aims to solve a bed allocation problem in a centralized hospital, and the 
scale of the model should be sufficiently large to use in most hospitals. The bed 
allocation problem is a generalization of resource allocation problem. However, this 
problem is unique in resource allocation problems because each term of the objective 
function, 𝑓𝑗(𝑩), is related to the number of beds in all wards. Thus, existing methods for 
resource allocation problems cannot solve this problem.  
 
In this section, we consider the bed allocation problem as a nonlinear integer 
programming. There is no general efficient solution for nonlinear integer programming, 
but the number of feasible solutions is finite. Same with many applications and 
generalizations of resource allocation problems known to be NP-hard, the feasible 
domain of this problem is very large, so enumeration is not time feasible. In contrast, a 
heuristic optimization algorithm can be suitable to solve this problem effectively. 
 
5.1 Local search and neighborhood 
Local search is a quick and brief algorithm. Several advanced heuristic optimization 
algorithms are based on local search. If time is enough, local search will terminate with a 
local optimal solution, which can be good enough for solving an actual problem.  
 
Local search starts with an initial solution (section 5.2). This initial solution is the current 
position (or current point) where the algorithm starts. Local search explores points 
around the current point, and the collection of points which are able to be explored from 
a current point is defined as the neighborhood of this current point. As the algorithm 
runs, the current point can move to another point in its neighborhood, according to a pre-
set rule. Each time the current position moves from one position to another is defined as 
an iteration. If points in neighborhood are all explored but none of them can be the new 
position for the next iteration, the algorithm terminates.  
 
The neighborhood points of the discreate resource allocation problem are generally 
generated by redeploying a unit of resource from one activity to another. In a bed 
allocation problem with 𝑁 wards, a proper neighborhood generator is an 𝑁-element 
==PAGE:37==
28 
 
vector, 𝑁𝐺= [0, … ,1, … , −1, … ] to the current solution. 𝑁𝐺 consists of one 1 element, 
one -1 element, and 𝑁−2 0 elements. There are many 𝑁𝐺s because the positions of 
non-zero elements are different. For 𝑁𝐺𝑖,𝑗, its 𝑖𝑡ℎ element is 1 and 𝑗𝑡ℎ element is -1. 
We enumerate all 𝑁𝐺 by changing the indexes of -1 and 1. A point is generated by 
adding 𝑁𝐺 to the current point, but the definition of a neighborhood point also requires 
that the point is a feasible solution (𝑏𝑖≥1, ∀𝑖∈{1,2, … , 𝑁}). Thus, we need to discard 
infeasible points to finish the neighborhood generation. 
 
In heuristic, the movement occurs between the current position and a point in its 
neighborhood. There are two common strategies for local search to move the current 
point. The first strategy is to explore all points in the neighborhood and move to the point 
which has the biggest improvement. The second one is to move as soon as it finds a point 
that brings improvement. In this problem, we use the second strategy. This is because we 
use a simulation model to evaluate the performance of bed distributions (points) and it 
takes unignorable time for each simulation. 
 
To avoid repetitions and speed up the optimization, our heuristic optimization algorithm 
also includes a memory list, which is commonly used in local search. The memory list is 
empty when the algorithm starts to work, and it will record every point the algorithm 
validated, regardless of whether the performance of this point is better than the current 
objective value. Before using simulation model to estimate the performance of a point, 
we check if this point is in the memory list. If the point is already a member of the 
memory list, we do not estimate its performance with simulation model, but directly 
mark it as “validated”. Thus, we can avoid validating the same point more than once. 
 
Once we find an improving point, we will end the current iteration and accept the 
improving point as the new point for the next iteration. The next iteration will be carried 
out in the neighborhood of the new point. At the beginning of every iteration, all points 
are not validated. In the process of iteration, more and more points are marked validated 
after we estimate their performance with simulation model or find they are members of 
the memory list. Suppose we have validated all points in the neighborhood but cannot 
find an acceptable point. In that case, we denote the iteration as the terminating iteration,

5.3 The estimation method (before validation) 
In local search, validations of neighborhood points are typically in random order. 
However, in this simulation optimization problem, every validation will cost considerable 
time. To speed up the optimization, we hope to have a higher probability of validating 
solutions that are more likely to have improved objective values. Thus, estimations are 
incorporated into the algorithm, and we can estimate a solution’s performance before 
validating the solution with the simulation model. For the convenience of discussion, we 
denote the change that a ward adds or subtracts one bed from the existing number of 
beds, as marginal change in the ward. When the number of bed increases, the change is 
positive. In contrast, the change is negative. 
 
When we discuss the method to estimate the performance of points in the neighborhood, 
we have already validated the current point utilizing the simulation model and known the 
number of arrivals and rejections in different wards, 𝑵𝑨 and 𝑵𝑹. According to our 
definition of the neighborhood, we can decompose the difference between a 
neighborhood point and the current point, 𝑁𝐺𝑖,𝑗, into a positive marginal change of the 
number of beds in ward 𝑖, and a negative marginal change in ward 𝑗.  
 
We consider the negative marginal change of the number of beds in ward 𝑗, and discuss 
its influence in combination with the internal interactions of this system. First, because 
ward 𝑗 has one less bed, more patients will be rejected. These rejections may include 
primary rejections and secondary rejections. Patients who meet secondary rejections, will 
leave the system immediately (Scenario 3), and have no influence on the value of 
objective function. However, some patients who meet primary rejections, may be 
relocated and allocated beds in another ward. During the time these relocated patients are 
treated, the number of beds available to serve corresponding patients will be reduced. 
Therefore, the number of primary rejections in this ward will increase. Then, these new 
primary rejections will be relocated, leading to more primary rejections in other wards. 
 
In this way, the marginal change in ward 𝑗 has effect on the whole system. We can 
divide this effect into several stages. In the first stage, compared to previous bed 
distribution, more type 𝑗 patients are rejected in ward 𝑗, and the number of primary 
rejections in ward 𝑗 increases. In the second stage, these added type 𝑗 patients are 
relocated to other wards, and some of them may be allocated beds. As a result of the 
second stage, the numbers of primary rejections in other wards will increase. Meanwhile, 
==PAGE:42==
33 
 
 
 
the adding primary rejections also hint more patients need to be relocated. These extra 
patients who need to be relocated will result in the next stage. In this way, the result of 
each stage is the cause of the next, a spreading process similar to ripples on the surface of 
water.  
 
Theoretically, we can describe the effect of the marginal change as such a series of 
stages, and number of stages is infinite. However, the result of a stage is expected to keep 
decreasing as the process continues. This is because these extra patients who need to be 
relocated may be rejected by alternative wards (Scenario 3), or cannot find a suitable 
alternative ward (Scenario 4), and thus disappear in the system. After infinite 
propagations, the expectation value of a propagation tends to 0. What’s more, the sum of 
the effects of an infinite number of stages is finite, because the numbers of primary 
rejections under the bed distributions before and after the marginal change are always 
finite.

validate the 𝑩𝒏𝒆𝒘 and see if 𝑩𝒏𝒆𝒘 has a better performance than 𝑩𝒄; if so, 𝐸𝑖, 𝐸𝑗 and 
their coordinate index will get awarded by increasing the credence (e.g. multiped by 
1.1), otherwise it is penalized by decreasing the credence (e.g. multiped by 0.9). On the 
contrary, when we discredit 𝐸𝑖,𝑗 whose sign is positive, we validate the 𝑩𝒏𝒆𝒘 and 
compare its performance with the performance of 𝑩𝒄. However, in this scenario, we 
award the estimation credibility when the performance of 𝑩𝒏𝒆𝒘 is worse than 
==PAGE:48==
39 
 
 
 
performance of 𝑩𝒄, but penalize it when 𝑩𝒏𝒆𝒘 is better, because we discredit the 
estimation but it is right. If we trust a positive result or discredit a negative result, we do 
not validate this point, but skip to the next one.

7. Conclusion and future work 
Table 7.1 Results from cases of different number of wards 
 
 
With a view to optimizing the distribution of bed resources, we presented a solution 
approach consisting of two main components. The first was an agent-based simulation 
model used to estimate the primary rejection rate of given bed distribution. The second 
incorporated the simulation model in a heuristic to optimize the distribution of bed 
resources. In addition, inspired by the infinite series, we divided the influence of a 
marginal change of bed number in a ward into infinite stages, and estimated the influence 
due to a marginal change in short time. Based on the estimation method and the analysis 
to its precision, we developed the adaptive search algorithm. 
 
For a real case from a Danish hospital, our approach was used to find a 37.8% reduction 
in number of primary rejections – that is, the number of patients rejected on first arrival 
to the hospital.  
 
The adaptive search algorithm was evaluated using a range of different tests and 
==PAGE:59==
50 
 
compared to local search. Firstly, the iteration path of both algorithms for the case-
hospital were compared. Here, we found adaptive search algorithm terminated in shorter 
time. On the condition that runtime is limited, adaptive search algorithm returned a better 
solution. Second, we considered the same parameters with some extra constraints from 
real case, and these constraints make the feasible domain is non-convex. The results 
showed that the adaptive search algorithm saved a greater percentage of time if we set the 
runtime of local search as 100%. We also compared the two algorithms on cases of 
different scales, and we found the adaptive search algorithm has a more obvious 
advantage in runtime saving when the scale of cases is big. For cases of 20-ward, 
adaptive search algorithm saves an average of 91.23% of the running time compared to 
the local search. 
 
Future Work 
For future work, global optimization should be considered. We already proved that the 
simplified problem, where relocation is invalidated, has only one local optimal. In 
several numerical experiments, except the one with extra constraints, we can find only 
one local optimal. In the future, we may be able to prove this problem is a convex 
optimization, so its heuristic solution is in fact the global optimal. 
 
Second, we have notice that we can estimate the results of first two stages of a marginal 
change in short term, but it takes much more time to estimate the influence of stage 3 and 
following stages. On the other hand, we may speed up the optimization with the higher 
precision of estimation. In the future, we need to consider how many stages we should 
consider so the heuristic algorithm performs best.  
 
Lastly, this model has the potential to be used in other relocation problems, such as 
relocating charging piles, building public house and allocating computing resource of 
servers.
