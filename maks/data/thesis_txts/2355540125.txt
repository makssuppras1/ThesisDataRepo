Chapter 1
Introduction
Hemolysis is a worldwide concern in the health care industry due to its impact on pa-
tient care. Clinical laboratory test results are adversely aﬀected when blood undergoes
hemolysis. Hemolysis is the rupture of red blood cells leading to cellular content in the
plasma. Hemolysis can be broken down into two main categories:
In vivo hemolysis which is usually caused by a disease. It is unavoidable and cannot
be helped by the health care provider.
In vitro hemolysis, on the other hand occurs by mishandling of the blood by the
care provider.
Cellular content in the plasma can lead to inaccurate test results causing health care
specialists to use the wrong diagnosis. This can lead to the wrong treatment which can
damage the reputation of the hospitals and laboratories.
Hemolysis in labs has been reported to be 3.3% of all samples taken, and of those,
40% to 70% percent of all unsuitable samples have proven to be the primary cause of
erroneous blood test results. The level of misdiagnoses can have a serious eﬀect on
patient care, therefore there is a need to verify blood samples to ensure the validation
of the test results [1].
The goal of this thesis is to characterize the optical components of a hemolysis
detector that is based on OWLS (Optical Waveguide Lightmode Spectroscopy) [2] lab on
a chip (LOC) design for label free detection with rapid response time. The LOC device
is to be integrated into a portable Radiometer ABL analyser with the given dimensions
of 450x249x249 cm. The hemolysis detector will act as a validation measurement for
samples test results.

1.1
Research Approach
The accepted marker by healthcare specialists to measure hemolysis is to detect free
plasma Hemoglobin (Hb) [3]. Hemoglobin is an intracellular protein that is mostly
found in the red blood Cells (RBC). Interference from hemolysis is approximately lin-
early dependent of the free Hb concentration. Our approach to detect hemoglobin is
based on optical waveguide lightmode spectroscopy which will give us a label free with
real time monitoring of hemolysis [2]. The biosensor is a transparent lab on chip poly-
mer optimized for optoﬂuidic spectroscopy. The lab on the chip device can be broken
down into two components:
Optical Component consisting of a planar waveguide grating coupler design for spec-
troscopy measurement and a
Microﬂuidic Component that consists of a micro-channel ﬁlter to separate cellular
content from intact whole blood. See ﬁg.1.1 for a simple model of the Hemolysis Sensor.
Figure 1.1: Basic model of Research approach for hemolysis detection. Microﬂudic housing
inlet is where plasma enters and Hemoglobin separates from the blood. Grating is used to couple
light into the device. The spectroscopy takes place between the two gratings.

Chapter 2
Background
Hemolysis is derived from the Latin word Hemo (blood) and lysis (to break open). It
is the leakage of intracellular constituents (e.g. potassium, lactase, and dehydrogenase)
by erythrocytes into the surrounding plasma. Hemolysis in the blood can be separated
in to two categories:
In vivo hemolysis - the blood cell is ruptured inside the patient’s body but the
contents inside the cell are reabsorbed through various biochemistry methods, if the
amount is small. There is also the case that in-vivo hemolysis is so great that the body
cannot reabsorb cellular content leaving an excess of analyte in the plasma. In this
case the patient care provider should be informed immediately. An excess amount of
cellular content in the blood is an indication of disease.
In vitro hemolysis - The blood cell is ruptured outside of the patients body by
something other than a disease that originates internally. The intracellular content is
not reabsorbed. The cellular volume of the organism is too small to conduct enough cel-
lular uptake to rectify analyte, leaving an excess of biochemical content in the plasma.
The excess of cellular content like potassium is far greater in the blood than what is
found in the plasma. This increase of cellular content causes interference in test ana-
lyzer. One of the markers in the cellular content to detect hemolysis is hemoglobin[3].
2.1
State of the Art
Currently the most common way to detect hemolysis is to withdraw large amounts of
blood from the patient in order to send it to a centralized lab for centrifugation. Then
analysis of the blood is conducted by a visual assessment, spectrometic method, or a
combination of the two[4].
The visual assessment is done by taking a picture of the sample then comparing
16
==PAGE:18==
the species plasma with a color chart reference guide. The reference guide gives an
estimation of the hemolysis level. Visualization assessment of hemolysis can take a long
time and results are subjective since determining small color variation can be diﬃcult
for the human eye. It is also not guaranteed that the patient’s health care provider will
perform the visual assessment test but instead invoke intuition[5]. It has been shown
that small amounts of hemolysis can inﬂuence test results because it becomes diﬃcult
to compare the sample with the reference guide 2. As of 2015 visual assessment of
hemolysis accounted for 43% of cases, according to the College of American Pathologists
(CAP) Quality Practices Committee. Other spectrometric methods such as the scale
harbor method or some other automated analyzers were used in 11% of the cases and
a combination of visual and another method was used in 41% of cases[5] [1].
The international standard for measuring blood is by using the Drabkin’s cyan-
methemoglobin method and spectrometric method[1]. Drabkin’s cyanmethemoglobin
method indirectly measures Hemoglobin by diluting the blood with potassium ferri-

2.2
The Optical Biosensor
What exactly does the term Optical Biosensor mean in this thesis? Optical Biosensors
are devices that use light to interact with biological substances which in turn give out
a signal that can be quantiﬁed in some form. The optical biosensor that was developed
for this project is based on a optical waveguide lightmode spectroscopy (OWLS)[2].
The waveguide sensor was ﬁrst introduced by Lukosz and Tiefenthaler who used it to
sense gas and humidity by a change of the refractive index of the cover medium[7].
Variations of the same underlying principle of their waveguide sensor are found in
countless research papers. The basic principle being that refractive index of the cover
layer is sensed by an evanescence wave coming from the core of the waveguide. The
ﬁrst commercial product using the waveguide sensor concept was an immunosensor
created by MicroVacuum Ltd., a Hungary based company that later further developed
a chemosensor[8]. These sensors are label free and work on the principle of binding
17
==PAGE:19==
the analytes on the surface of the sensing layer using antibodies or some other form
of chemical mechanisms that cause binding. The bounded analyte is detected by light
and quantiﬁed. The sensor design is similar to the Hemolysis sensor discussed in this
thesis, where it uses a grating couple conﬁguration to in-couple light, then detects
the analyte, and then out-couples light for quantiﬁcation. The main diﬀerence is that
immobilization of the analyte is not attempted, rather a microﬂudic ﬁltration technique
is used instead, see illustration in ﬁg.2.1a.
2.3
A description of the Hemolysis Sensor
The Hemolysis sensor is based on a polymer planar waveguide with two grating couplers
with microﬂudic housing on top. The planar waveguide is a three layer system. The
core layer has a higher refractive index then the substrate layer and the cover layer
in order to create a waveguide. The guiding of the light can be explained by total
internal reﬂection. The core layer is a thin ﬁlm and the refractive index contrast is
small allowing an evanescent decaying electric ﬁeld to occur away from the core. The
evanescent electric ﬁeld is what we use to perform absorption spectroscopy on our
sample.
The complete process is illustrated in ﬁg.2.1 The cover layer is where the
sample undergoes ﬁltration separating free hemoglobin from whole blood as shown in
ﬁg.2.1a. Plasma is to be pumped into the inlet and enters ﬁltration and is sucked out
from the outlet by a pump, this can be seen in ﬁg.2.1b In order to excite an electric wave
we need a way to couple light into the waveguide. Simply shining light on the detector
does automatically couple light into the waveguide due to a mismatch of momentum
between the air and core layer. One of the key rules of physics is that momentum
must always be conserved. In order to achieve the conservation of momentum we must
add a light coupling mechanism to the waveguide. There are several coupling methods
to achieve conservation of momentum. For instance using a prism, butt coupling and
grating coupling are some of the more common methods. Grating coupling was chosen
because it is easy to manufacture and integrate into a lab on a chip system. Furthermore
it is the most robust design and one of the least expensive methods for coupling light.

3.1
Waveguide
Planar waveguides are basically a three layer dielectric structure. The three layers have
diﬀerent refractive indexes making it an asymmetric waveguide as shown in ﬁg:3.1.
The core being the highest index layer and the substrate and cladding making the
lower index layers. To achieve light guidance the outer materials have to have a lower
refractive index than the core material, this is the principle of Total Internal Relﬂection
(TIR). The core layer, also called the thin ﬁlm, essentially traps the light between the
two lower refractive indexes and guides the light through the structure. The light that
is guided through the waveguide is termed modes. Modes are electro-magnetic waves
that can propagate a homogeneous material[9].

3.2
Waveguide coupling
The propagation constant of light in free space and the waveguide are diﬀerent therefore
the phases do not match. The boundary condition requires continuity of the electric
ﬁeld at the interface of two diﬀerent materials. In order to couple light into a waveguide
momentum must be conserved at the interface. To achieve the phase matching condition
the longitudinal component of the propagation constant in free space must be adjusted
so that the transverse component can match that of material 2. There are various
methods to achieve phase matching conditions which would allow coupling into the
waveguide. Some of the most popular are the coupling prism, parallel waveguides, and
grating[9]. All of these diﬀerent mechanism have its advantages and disadvantages. The
method used for in-out coupling into the waveguide for this sensor is the grating coupler.
The grating coupler, although not the most eﬃcient way to couple light into waveguide,
over-comes its ineﬃciency by being robust, simple to manufacture and compact[12][13].
The grating is a periodic structure that diﬀracts light causing waves to travel in
diﬀerent directions. We can use this diﬀraction of light which is essentially a change
of momentum to match the momentum of our waveguide by using the grating as an

intermediate. In order to model and get an understanding of the physics of grating and
interaction of light we now turn to the grating equation.

3.5.1
Waveguide Model
The main points to consider in designing a waveguide are the core width, refractive
index, and sensitivity. Also, the cladding width (i.e substrate) should be wide enough
where analytically considerations for thickness can be approximated to inﬁnite in com-
parison to the core width. The core refractive index should be higher than the sur-
rounding material to ensure light guidance. In order to achieve single mode waveguide
guidance the refractive index contrast and core thickness have to both be considered.

In this section design considerations for the waveguide sensor will be discussed. First
there will be a presentation of the general design consideration. Then the focus will be
put on waveguide materials and physical parameters. This is followed by the grating
structure and dimensional parameters. The microﬂudic component also has an inﬂuence

4.1
General Design Considerations
The lab on chip design was chosen because the biosensor has to be integrated into
the Radiometer ABL analyzer within a compartment with dimensions of 450x249x249
cm.
The optical component of the lab on chip system uses an asymmetric planar
waveguide structure with a nano-stamped grating on both sides of the core/substrate
layer interface. A cover layer with multiple channels will be placed on top of the core
but for the angular measurement of this experiment the ad-layer will be absent. The
conﬁguration of the hemolysis detector allows for a durable, mobile, robust compact
device that it easy to fabricate.
The planar slab waveguide materials have to be bio-compatible because the sensor
will be dealing with human blood and it is necessary to avoid cell damage from toxi-
city to obtain an accurate hemolysis reading. The optical waveguide goal is to detect
hemoglobin after it has been separated from whole blood. The separation of hemoglobin
from whole blood will take place in the cover layer by a ﬁltering process which will leave
hemoglobin in the bottom layer of the microﬂuidic chamber while the blood cells will
be higher up in the layer as illustrated in ﬁg:2.1a. After separation the hemoglobin will
interact with an evanescent wave that will perturb the hemoglobin while avoiding the
36
==PAGE:38==
red blood cells. The reason we want to avoid the red blood cells is because hemoglobin
is found in large amounts inside of them. Therefore light interaction with red blood
cell will cause a false reading for the hemolysis measurement.
To ensure the constituents will be viable enough to be measured the material that
will interact with the plasma is made to be bio-compatible[22]. The material should also
be transparent (non absorbing) in the wavelength between 380nm to 4nm in order to
avoid interference. Operating wavelength is dependent on hemoglobin and the available
light source. Hemoglobin has high absorptions at wavelengths of 380nm to 460nm and
520nm to 580nm. This gives us a range of light sources for the design that can be used.
For reasons of cost eﬀectiveness a laser diode at 406nm was chosen. It can be seen in
ﬁg:4.1b that suﬃcient resonance of hemoglobin around the ranges of 390 nm to 430 nm
can be achieved. A laser diode was chosen over an LED to ensure that there is enough
power to measure angular dependency. Once angular measurements are achieved it
may be wise to revisit LED for a more cost eﬀective option.
Index contrast and core thickness should be considered since both parameters have
an eﬀect on the amount mode the system can hold and the evanescent wavelength.
Substrate thickness is also critical due to the limitation of fabrication technologies to
create a grating on the nanometer scale.
The numerical models discussed in sec: will be implemented to formulate dimen-
sional parameters for the design. Using these models and keeping in mind the parameter
restraints the hemolysis sensor is designed.

Chapter 5
Fabrication
In order to have an understanding of the limitations of mathematical models and the
design of the waveguide-grating coupler it is advantageous to have a basic understand-
ing of the fabrication process. In this chapter the fabrication process of the optical
component of the hemolysis detector will now be explained. The chapter will start
oﬀwith a brief introduction to Nanoimprint Technology (NIL). Then the fabrication
process of the stamp followed by the fabrication process for the optical component of
the hemolysis device will be covered.
The hemolysis detector is fabricated using Nanoimprint Technology (NIL). NIL tech-
nology has been applied to nanophotonoics, opto-electronics, biological application, etc
for many years. The basic principal of NIL is to create a pattern on a template that
can be transferred to a polymer by a Ultra Violet (UV) curing process or a thermal-
mechanical process. NIL allows for a high resolution on the micro and nanometer scale
structures. It is easy to fabricate allowing for mass production and rapid replication at
a lower cost than other fabrication processes. There are many NIL fabrication meth-
ods but the most fundamental are the Hot Embossing Lithography (HEL), Thermal
Nanoimprint Lithography (TNIL), and the Ultra Violet Based Nanoimprint Lithogra-
phy (UV-NIL). All of these methods have been able to achieve grating in the 10nm
sub-wavelength range.
5.1
Stamp Fabrication
The stamp also known as the master is a physical structure that has the pattern design.
It is one of the most critical aspects of the fabrication process because it determines the
resolution of the device design. The material chosen for the stamp has an eﬀect on the
life-time and how accurate the pattern can repeat. Stamps are typically broken into
44
==PAGE:46==
two categories: hard material and soft material. Hard material tends to last longer and
is typically made of Silicon, Quartz, Nickel and Silicon Nitride. Soft materials can make
the stamping process simpler and are typically made of various polymeric materials such
as polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), polyvinyl chloride (PVC),
and polyurethane acrylate (PUA). These materials are UV transparent. The reason for
this will be explained later[13] in the device fabrication section.
Some of the most common methods used to create the stamps are electron beam
lithography (EBL), photo-lithography, and phase mask[12]. The EBL is the method
used to create patterns on the silicon based stamp. The key advantage of using the
EBL over the other lithography methods is that there is no need for a mask. A mask is
a template typically used on other lithography methods such as photo-lithography to
transfer patterns on a structure, and it has a better pattern capability on the nanoscale.
Patterns are generated on CAD ﬁles allowing for rapid design modiﬁcation which is
excellent for research.
There are two main phases of the fabrication process for the grating coupler waveg-
uide. The ﬁrst phase involves the creation of the master(stamp) and the second phase
uses the master to imprint the design pattern on a polymer material[13].

6.1
The Plan
The light guiding of the waveguide process can be broken down into three sections:
In-coupling of light, waveguide propagation, and out-coupling of light. Light is incident
on the in-couple grating (see ﬁg:6.1) at an angle that achieves guided mode resonance
(GMR) as shown in ﬁg:3.7. For the particular system shown in ﬁg:6.1 this can occur
at an incident angle of 30o causing a 2nd order diﬀraction (m=2) or at 23o to get a
1st order diﬀraction (m=1). We take the example of an angle of incident at 30o where
two modes are guided in the in the waveguide. Remember the waveguide design allows
for two modes, each propagating at its own propagation constant; therefore it can be
expected that two modes are to be coupled out of the waveguide. The momentum of
the waveguide for the fundamental (mode 0) and higher order (mode 1) is equal to
incident momentum. Once again by symmetry one of the out-coupling angle will be
the same as the incident angle. Also by symmetry we know that the second mode
coupling out should be at the same incident angle as the diﬀraction order that was not
used for in-coupling, this is shown as a dotted line arrow for kinc in ﬁg:6.1. This can
also be expected if the angle of incident at 23o (m=1) was used instead. Although the
out-coupling angle would be the same, the power coming out will not be.

6.4
Algorithm Implementation
The algorithms that execute the motors and spectrometer play a key role in the ability to
detect intensity from the waveguide at a given angle. Implementation of the algorithm
comes from a theoretical understanding of the model as discussed in sec:6.1. If the
algorithm to acquire angular intensity is wrong then the measurements we acquire will
not accurately describe the angular dependency of the hemolysis detector; therefore a
presentation of the algorithm is in order. Input parameters are related to the calibration,
steps of the motor, and the desired angle of measurement commencement. The detector
angle is ﬁxed relative to the angle of the waveguide. So the detector stays at a ﬁxed
angle following the steps of the rotational stage until completion. Once the sample goes
through all the steps the detector then moves one angular step and the rotational stage
starts all over again. For each rotational stage of the angular step a call to the jaz
spectrometer is made to measure the spectral intensity at that angle.
Data acquisition
The data for each spectral intensity is stored in a column(m). When the rotational
stage goes through the range of steps an MxN matrix is created, where M is the total
intensity taken for a spectrum and N is the total steps taken. Each matrix relates to
55
==PAGE:57==
the photo detector angular step-size and is saved to a ﬁle to free up computational
storage space. The total data for one experiment will be MxNxK (3d matrix), where K
relates to the total amount of steps taken by the detector. Data is then processed; this
will be discussed further in chapters 7 and 8. The program that is used to implement
the algorithms is given in appendix 9.4. The algorithm for the automatic automated
transmission setup shown in ﬁg:6.5 is similar to that used for in/out coupling angle
measurements, the diﬀerence is caused by the detector being stationary.
Therefore
there is no need for a double loop in the algorithm.
A full automatic automated setup would allow for detailed measurements at many
points using diﬀerent samples with the design parameters and fabrication process. The
purpose of repeating angular measurements for diﬀerent samples is to ﬁnd the optimal
incident angle. There will be a variation in the fabrication process for each grating, but
by identifying an angle which consistently overlaps one can choose an optimal angle
that will resonate for all samples and enable the overcoming of fabrication variations.

8.5
Results Summarized
This is a summary of the main results of this thesis:
• The grating angular sensitivity is less than 0.4 degrees with the assumption that
grating is the sole cause of angular dependency.
• The highest intensity occurs at symmetric angles, the highest being approxi-
mately: θinc = 30o and θout = 30o
• It has been conﬁrmed that incident light couples to the waveguide by visual
conﬁrmation.
• Angular sensitivity appears to be the dominant issue when attempting to measure
the out-couple intensity.
• The automated systems are capable of measuring the intensity at a given angle.
• Automatic measurements can be performed with the transmission setup with a
step size of 0.1 degrees and ﬁts closely with visual, numerical and theoretical
models.
• For 3D automatic measurements the system does not have the capability to give
consistent and repeatable results because of the sensitivity of the grating.
To take continuous automatic measurements it is best to use the transmission setup
at this stage. Results from the transmission setup ﬁt the GMR model with consistency
and repeatability.

9.1
Future Work
Future work relating to the device will be left to the designer. But, there are some
experimental designs that were not completed or were not anticipated for. They are as
follows:
• Beam characterization:
In-coupling beam-Measuring the angular intensity proﬁle of the incoming beam
ﬁxing the laser and changing the angle of the photo-detector
Out-coupling intensity- scan the out-couple grating with a camera to obtain
the intensity proﬁle.
• New algorithm for the opto mechanics should be evaluated in comparison to the
current algorithm (see sec: 6.4. Where the photo-detector stays at a ﬁxed angle
while the incident angle changes. For example:
Step 1: The detector starts at a ﬁxed angle of 30 degrees while the incident
angles moves from 10 to 45 degrees at a step-size 0.2 degrees.
Step 2: Photo detector moves to 29 degrees and again the incident beam goes
from 10 to 45 degrees at step-size 0.2 degree.
This action repeats until a range of desired photo-detector angles is measured.
This algorithm is less intense on the motor and reduces the possibility of motor
failure, which did occur during experiments.
78
==PAGE:80==
• Optimization for the mode overlap of incident Gaussian beam compared to out-
couple beam should be evaluated and implemented experimentally, possibly using
methods indicated by Harper K. at BYU[14][15]
• There is a need to be able to adjust the angular sensitivity of the optics indepen-
dent of the grating. By decoupling the optical setup sensitivity from the grating
sensitivity we can better account for the angular dependency of the grating and
eliminate any notion that sensitivity may be due to the optical setup. Out-couple
angular sensitivity can be reduced optically by moving the detector further back
or adding curve mirrors which is typically done inside spectrometry devices to re-
solve angular sensitivity. This will cause a spreading of the out-couple beam thus
reducing the angular sensitivity of photo-detector. Furthermore, an investigation
of reducing incident angle by beam divergence as done by Vivan Co. should also
be considered.
• In the transmission measurement a couple of extra dips in intensity occurred
which may indicate a second grating layer. Also, experimental results seem to
indicate that the grating period may be longer than the 368nm that were use for
the theoretical model. Therefore, Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) or AFM
characterization needs to be performed to validate these assumptions.
9.2
Conclusion
The Hemolysis detector is able to in-couple light and out-couple light. From visual
inspection it is evident that the electric ﬁeld does penetrate the cover layer which is an
indication that the detector will be able to perform absorption spectroscopy. The in-
couple angular measurement ﬁts with the GMR resonance for 1st order diﬀraction but is
less reliable for ﬁrst 2nd order diﬀraction.The intensity measurement for the higher order
is substantially lower than what was measured for the fundamental mode. Indicating
power eﬃciency may need to be shifted to 1st order diﬀraction if a spectroscopy using
a higher order mode is desired.
The automated measurement does appear to be able to measure the angular depen-
dent intensity proﬁle of the Hemolysis sensor and follows the theoretical model as well
as the experimental models. The down-side of the automated measurement is that a
proper map of the angular dependency will be a time consuming process.
The systems is capable of automated automatic measurement using the transmission
setup with a step-size of 0.1 degrees. For 3D automatic automated measurement the
79
==PAGE:81==
system fails. Intensity at a known angle is not recorded or does not ﬁt any of the
experimental or theoretical models. Possible issues may be angular sensitivity of the
grating making it diﬃcult to align the detector with the out-couple intensity. Also,
algorithm for motor movement and intensity measurement may need to be adjusted for
optimal use of motor movement and data acquisition.
The system’s current capability is a manual automated measurement and automatic
transmission measurement. Both measurement regions are adequate to obtain an angu-
lar dependence of the mode. Because of the reciprocal nature of the hemolysis detector
the transmission automatic automated method can establish the angular dependency
of the hemolysis detector on a large scale and at a rapid rate. The measurement for the
angular dependency took roughly 4 to 6 hours depending on the step-size selected while
transmission measurement took a maximum of 20 to 30 minutes. Finally, transmission
measurements give a higher resolution (step-size) of 0.1 than the 3D measurements,
which at best were at 0.3, making this option more precise.
