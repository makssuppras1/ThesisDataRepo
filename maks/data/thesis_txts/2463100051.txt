Abstract
English:
In this thesis, I will look into and compare different visualization techniques for col-
lected time series data, with the purpose of enabling knowledge and self-reﬂection.
The goal of this, being to highlight some of the common issues that arise when visu-
alizing time series data.
I will then describe the process of designing and implementing a prototype applica-
tion that enables users to view and learn from their own self-tracked data, in order
to understand more about their habits or illnesses.
This prototype was designed with a PTSD case study in mind, and has been tested by
5 different test participants, over two separate tests, in order to further develop the
application towards a simple and accessible tool for users without extensive knowl-
edge in the ﬁeld of data visualization.
Dansk:
I min afhandling vil jeg beskrive, samt sammenligne, nogle forskellige teknikker til
at undgå nogle af de problemer der opstår ved visualisering af personlige tidsdata.
Derefter, vil jeg beskrive min design- og udviklingsproces af et prototype system,
udviklet med fokus på et PTSD tilfælde, som kan anvendes til at se og navigere
i personlige tidsdata, med det formål at promovere selvreﬂeksion. Formålet er at
give brugeren et redskab til at kunne få indsigt i sine vaner og eventuelle lidelser, og
dermed lære mere om sig selv.
Prototypen er blevet testet af 5 almindelige personer i to forskellige brugertests, for
at forbedre prototypen og eliminere nogle af dens problemer i forhold til forståelse
og enkelhed. Disse brugere var uundværlige for at designe en prototype som kan
anvendes af almindelige brugere, uden særlig ekspertise inden for visualisering af
data.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction
With the rise of technology and especially the widespread use of a variety of smart
devices, people now have access to several everyday devices that can track and com-
pute massive amounts of data. Whether it’s Smartphones, Smartwatches, or a va-
riety of ﬁtness devices, most people have some sort of device capable of tracking
them going about their everyday life. Self-tracking has long been immensely pop-
ular among ﬁtness and health enthusiasts, where tracking your calorie and protein
intake can facilitate muscle growth. Athletes track heartbeats, weight loss, running
paths and several other things that help give an edge over the competition, but this
data, and these methods, have plenty of usages outside of ﬁtness and sports. Moni-
toring and documenting your own life can lead to a great deal of self discovery. This
is something that has been practiced for decades in something as simple as a diary
or a notebook.
While there’s a huge commercial interest in using this data for advertising and prod-
uct developing, another less commercially motivated movement, called Quantiﬁed
Self, has surfaced. Gary Wolf and Kevin Kelly are generally credited with starting
this movement, which focuses on self-tracking and self logging to promote knowl-
edge of oneself.
My supervisor, Jakob Eg Larsen, has previously done plenty of research within the
area of Quantiﬁed Self and self-tracking. One of his papers, [LEC17] focused on us-
ing a simple one-button device to generate temporal data to facilitate PTSD (Post
Traumatic Stress Disorder) treatment in a case study.
PTSD is a terrible mental illness that affects roughly 10% of Danish veterans 2.5 years
after deployment in Afghanistan[MAK14], and an estimated 1 out of 11 Americans
who have never been deployed contract PTSD at some point in their lives 1. PTSD
can affect people of all ages and types, and comes in many different forms.
1.2
My Goal
The goal of my thesis changed throughout its runtime. Initially, I primarily aimed to
develop a prototype application that would enable users to handle their self-tracked
time series data and enable exploration of the dataset. I hoped that the prototype
would encourage users to engage in a self-motivated learning process that could
help alleviate issues arising in every day life with mental or physical illnesses. To do

this, I developed an early prototype of this application, which enabled users to easily
view the temporal data they had logged. The tool was being designed with veterans

However, during development, or more speciﬁcally during early user testing, it be-

how the user was to interact with it, as well as the ease of setting up and using the
application.
Therefore, throughout this report, I will thus describe the process of designing and
implementing this application, as a means to explain how we got to ﬁnd the actual
problem we needed to solve, and then follow up with my attempt at bringing light
to the issue of visualizing time series to regular users without a mathematical back-
ground, as well as which models proved successful during my own user testing.
A common issue within the ﬁeld of software engineering and development lies in
trying to solve a problem that the designer sees, while the user has a completely dif-
ferent problem in mind. Don Norman, one of the greatest experts on usability and
cognitive science, says it best, in what became a sort of mantra for this me during
this project:
"A brilliant solution to the wrong problem can be worse than no solution at all:
solve the correct problem." Source: [Nor88] p. 218
My hope is that this thesis will show some key points that need to be considered
when presenting time series data to common users, and that it can prove useful for
further application development for users suffering with PTSD or other issues in
their every day life. Even if we manage to make an amazing application that has
a variety of features and tools to navigate and handle data, it will be useless, if the
people we design it for are unable to understand what we are delivering.

Related Work
There has been plenty of prior research regarding self-tracking, and a variety of mo-
bile applications that focus on the tracking procedure. These are some of the more
interesting examples I came across during my research.
2.1
PTSD case study
The main motivation for this thesis came from my supervisor Jakob Eg Larsen.
Along with Kasper Eskelund and Thomas Blomseth Christiansen, he previously
worked with self-tracking and how to use it for facilitating life with a variety of
conditions. One of his research papers [LEC17] has been on using self-tracking as a
tool for therapy. In this case study, they equipped a military veteran suffering from
PTSD with a wearable device which could be pressed to generate a timestamp. The
veteran would wear the device, and then press the button whenever he started feel-
ing precursors to hyperarousal. The paper covers the ﬁrst 100 days the veteran used
the device for tracking, and concludes that they managed to identify key areas in the
veterans life where hyperarousal would appear. This data proved highly beneﬁcial
to the therapist when trying to assess what would trigger his patients PTSD. This
showed that something as simple as a timestamp can yield highly valuable informa-
tion, and can make a difference for someone who struggles with PTSD every day.
Another takeaway from the research came in the therapy sessions themselves. By
looking at the frequency of the datapoints, the therapist could see weekly patterns,
for which the patient could then retrieve events in his memory, that he had pre-
viously not necessarily correlated to his symptoms. The data acted as an ofﬂoading
device for his memory, which he could later use to ﬁnd the exact moments he pressed
the buttons. Together they managed to identify stressful encounters at work, as the
main source of his hyperarousal. This then prompted the veteran to consider other,
less stressful career paths.
2.2
Quantiﬁed Self
Gary Wolf and Kevin Kelly are generally credited with having started the Quanti-
ﬁed Self movement back in 2007 [QSi] [Wol11] while writing for the Wired Magazine.
The ﬁrst time they ofﬁcially wrote about it seems to have been in 2009 [Wol09]. They
started the movement to enlighten people to the beneﬁts of self-tracking for reﬂec-
tion and self-learning, and more speciﬁcally encourage others to start self-tracking
for the beneﬁt of everyone. In [Wol09] they focus on how data gathering is becoming
a macroscope. A way to learn more about yourself, by comparing your data to the
data of others. This is already being done in so many other faucets of life. Athletes

self-track to learn about their own body, as well as to compare it to competitors or
previous athletes. So why shouldn’t the average Joe beneﬁt from seeing his own

Quantiﬁed Self is in essence a movement that exists to explore and gather as much
knowledge about life as we can, by using tools that are no longer just physical diaries

2.3.2
Other visualization studies
Furthermore, [AMST11] contains a survey of 101 different techniques for visualizing
time-oriented data. Among the techniques are a wide variety of visualizations that
work with different types of time data while focusing on different types of use cases.
These four techniques are the ones I’m most interested in for this project. The ﬁrst
two are very simple all-round visualization techniques that most people would have
learned in school and are thus very common. They pop up every day through news
articles or other types of media. The latter two are much more common for Time
series visualization purposes.
Point Plot
FIGURE 2.2: An example of a Point plot. Source: [CPL14] p.152
Perhaps one of the simplest plotting techniques, a point plot is fairly simple to un-
derstand and easy to implement. If the data has obvious patterns, the point plot
might have valuable uses for conveying them to the user. One thing the point plot
excels at, is showing values for speciﬁc data instances, as it is extremely easy to ﬁnd
the exact value corresponding to the time given.
Bar/Spike Graph
Spike graphs are very similar to the previous point plots, however they offer a bet-
ter overview when comparing across several data instances. While the spikes are
simply an extension of the point plot, it gives a better overview of the values and
how they progress when following the x-axis. At the same time, the spike graph still
retains most of the features of the point plot. It’s very simple, common, and useful
across a wide variety of ﬁelds of study.
Calendar
The ﬁrst less-commonly used graph type is a Calendar-based visualization. It’s men-
tioned in both [CPL14] (p.547) and [AMST11] (p.177). The idea is fairly simple. Most

Tile Maps

Tile maps are very interesting for this project as they are generally used to show
how time data is divided throughout a time period. It is essentially very much like
the aforementioned calendar, except it’s more condensed. With a tile map, we take
away some of the organisation of the calendar heatmap. We condense the days into

a bigger matrix and plot the days based on their corresponding week and weekday.
As can be seen in Figure 2.5, we have the months of the year shown along the x axis,
but since we throw away any information about the dates, ﬁnding out exactly which
square represents the ﬁrst day of a month isn’t immediately obvious. The tilemap
interface does show the days of the week and the months along the axes, but the
month doesn’t line up perfectly with it’s corresponding tiles, since the month can
change 1 row before the corresponding month is shown on the axis.
Froelich also has a large element of his visualization that functions similar tile maps
and calendar heatmaps. The main part of his visualization, as seen in Figure 2.1, is
a timeline plotting the different times of day with tiles that scale based on the den-
sity of data at the given intervals. The main difference to his implementation, and
the ones detailed above, is the separation from the matrix style calendar focusing on
an overview of how a whole month or year looks. Froelich chose to have a more
timeline style calendar view, where you can see the distribution of button presses
throughout the individual days, giving a much more information dense visualiza-
tion.

3.1
Problem Deﬁnition
Developing the application itself, comes with many problems, some directly related
to time series visualization, and others more rooted in the usage and setup of the
application for the intended users.
The problem itself is fairly simple, even though there are many viable solutions.
In essence we want a tool that can create value by conveying the most essential in-
formation to the user in the most simple way possible. This is generally the goal of
any user interface. For my interface, I am building upon the research of my super-
visor [LEC17], and his former student [Fro18]. With that, my intention is to create a
simpler visualization than what Johan Froelich did in his thesis. His solution was a
very intricately detailed and complex graph which visualizes the Time Series incred-
ibly well. The main issue with it however, is its complexity and how overwhelming
it can seem to users without prior knowledge of statistics. From Cognitive Science
we know to consider the users Mental Model [Nor88], and try to design our Con-
ceptual Model to match. As impressive as Froelichs work is, I argue that most users,
maybe more speciﬁcally users with a proﬁle like the previously mentioned PTSD
case [LEC17], will need a simpler interface to keep them engaged with the data col-
lection process. The main user proﬁles I am targeting aren’t academics, with knowl-
edge of statistics, nor are they highly motivated researchers who are paid to use the
program.
Furthermore, the platform that the data is accessed on is of great importance. The
user needs to be able to access and view the data from a device that’s convenient and
accessible. If setting up the program is too much of a hassle, then the user is unlikely
to want to use it.
As such, my goal is to solve the problem of getting the users acquainted with the
data they gather. I hope to create a simple and easy-to-use visualization, which en-
ables the user to have an overview of their temporal data. This would hopefully
encourage user engagement, and support self-discovery both for recreational and
therapeutic usage. Generally, as soon as the program seems overly complex to use,
or is unable to convey the data to the user in a way they can understand, this would
be a major issue.
However, even if we fulﬁll all of the technical requirements. The application is easy
to setup, and runs on commonly used hardware. It supports all the necessary ac-
tions, and is perfectly in line with how a user would navigate it. All of these require-
ments are irrelevant, if the user doesn’t understand the data from the visualizations

3.2
Time Zones
One of the biggest issues in dealing with visualizing time data, is to manage time
zones and daylight saving in an appropriate way. Anyone who has ever had to
handle time zones knows how complicated it can be, and even though there’s been
decades of research on how to do it, so far no one has managed to ﬁnd a perfect
solution.
3.2.1
The problem with Time Zones
The problem with Time Zones shows itself in many ways. We have several differ-
ent time zones. At a base level, it’s fairly simple. If you need to convert between
time zones, you add or subtract a few hours here or there, right? Well yes, but some
countries have half or quarter hour differences. Countries close to the equator, in
particular some countries in Africa, have a lot of unusual time zones. This stems
from the sheer size of the African continent, and the therefore wildly different sun-
sets that can appear from one side of a country to another. In and of itself, this
would be alright to deal with. Whether you add an hour or an hour and ﬁfteen min-
utes, isn’t really a deal-breaker. However, we also have to deal with Daylight saving
times. Across the globe, different countries have different days for when they turn
back the clock an hour, and some countries only turn the clock back half an hour.
Additionally, some countries have adopted daylight savings within the last decade,
or completely abolished it, permanently or temporarily. In general keeping track of
all the speciﬁc rules for each country is one big mess.
Luckily, the core issue here is not so much that we have different time zones with
a wildly complex set of rules on how to navigate them. In fact, plenty of libraries
exist to keep track of these things for us. The main issue for this project comes when
the user switches between two time zones in the middle of the data set. This means
we suddenly have to make a decision on how to show this, and how to show this
change of reference. It’s especially important when trying to infer any meaningful
correlation from the data, as faulty data can easily cause false patterns. Consider the
following examples.
Our user is an average working adult who goes to work every day at 8.00. The
user is using self-tracking in order to track his stress levels during the day. For the
last few weeks he’s been going to work every day, consistently noting his high stress
levels during morning trafﬁc. Now we add daylight savings, and turn the clock
back an hour. Our user still goes to work every day at 8.00, but now we have a
problem. How do we represent that there’s been a change? If we ignore the fact that
a change in time has happened, then suddenly the data will show that our user is
being stressed around 9.00, when he is in the early hours of his work day, and the
==PAGE:18==

data will in general be an hour off, until daylight savings is reversed. This would un-
doubtedly leave to a lot of confusion when viewing the data, and would inevitably
lead to erroneous correlations between the timestamp occurrences and real world
context.
Alright then, so we simply change the internal timer to match the local time. This
would solve some problems, but introduce other issues. Suddenly we have an over-
lapping hour during the hour in which the time is turned back. This gives that hour
far more signiﬁcance than every other hour, as it contains the data of two consecu-
tive hours combined. At the end of the year, an hour has completely disappeared as
all the data has been shifted forward an hour. In and of itself, that might not seem
overly important to some. It will create some data issues, but only at the overlap-
ping hour, once every half a year, right? For fairly short self-tracking projects that
could very well be insigniﬁcant, and easily ﬁxed.
But what if we now take travelling into account. Suddenly this 1 hour change every
half a year, can turn into several 4 or 5 hour time changes over the course of a day
or two. Now you’ve got a large bundle of scrambled data throughout the day. What
if the user is tracking medication intake, and suddenly the data is showing rampant
intake of medication during the ﬂight? And if we’re modelling a watch or calendar
interface, how does the user know how much time was between each intake?
So what do we do, and how do we visualize it? So far there’s been a lot of dif-
ferent ideas, and it generally depends on the program and how important accuracy
and overview of the data is to the user.
3.2.2
Possible Solutions
UTC
One solution is to use UTC time internally. UTC (Coordinated Universal Time), or
GMT as some know it, is the standard timezone for which me measure time. Ev-
ery other timezone is then expressed as UTC plus or minus some amount of hours
and minutes. UTC doesn’t change when daylight savings come into effect, which
means there are no overlapping hours for the data to get tangled, nor does UTC
time change as you travel across time zones. This means we can express our data
in UTC time, and then just keep track of what the offset is. This solves the issue of
storing and representing the data, but it still doesn’t solve the issue of visualization.
Users shouldn’t be burdened with having to convert the time they see, into the time
zone they are currently in, especially with daylight savings changing the UTC off-
set. And what do we do when they travel? No, we need a way to show the different
time zones, and which timezone the different time stamps were created in. Looking
at how different applications handle it, might show some interesting results.
Google Calendar
Google Calendar is perhaps the most used Calendar system available, and they have
an elegant way of showing the time zones when looking at any given week. This
means the user always has the time zone information readily available to them. This
is the same solution that [Fro18] decided to use in his project.

Design
In this chapter I will brieﬂy explain the data that was used in the project, and will
then go on to describe the design processes I went through. The data collection sec-
tion provides valuable Real World context, which is relevant to deﬁne the types of
users the project is aimed at helping. Additionally, the design processes are essen-

for developing the application itself, and then I will explain the design process for
the additional visualizations I ended up testing.
4.1
Data
All of my data was generated using the 1-button device mentioned in [LEC17]. In
short, the device is a wristband worn microcontroller, with a button that, when
pressed, generates a timestamp instance. The device is small enough, that it can
be worn in many different subtle ways like in a belt or around the neck, or whatever
suits the needs of the user. The timestamps generated are put into a .csv ﬁle, which
can then be extracted via USB.
4.1.1
Symptoms Data
The Symptoms data set, as mentioned earlier, was collected by a DTU professor. This
is the data used by Johan Froelich in [Fro18] to generate his plotting webtool. Com-
bined with contextual data about pollen count in the periods corresponding to the
tracked Symptoms data, Johan used it to conclude what type of allergy the professor
had, since the pollen count matched up accurately with the dataset. The data itself
stretches from February 2017 to December 2017 and consists of 5742 timestamps
spread across several different timezones.
4.1.2
PTSD Data
The PTSD dataset, also used in [LEC17], is much smaller in comparisons. It ini-
tially consisted of 928 timestamps where the only timezone change happens due to
daylight saving time happening in late October. The timestamps symbolize the pre-
cursors to the patients hyperarousal. In other words, the patient pressed the button
whenever he could feel his PTSD ﬂaring up. In this study, the patient would press
the button twice in succession, to rule out any accidental presses from simply hav-
ing the device laying around in a container, or from everyday activities. The ﬁltered
PTSD dataset (containing only the intended button presses) will serve as the primary
data set and user proﬁle for this project.

4.2
Design Goals
The design of my tool came in several iterations. The ﬁrst step was to ﬁgure out what
information I wanted to convey, or far more importantly, what information the users
needed me to convey, and what speciﬁc options the users needed to have available to
them. [RPZMB12] describes a framework with three main support dimensions that
are essential when combining Quantiﬁed Self strategies with Reﬂective Learning.
Tracking cues: capturing and keeping track of certain data as basis for
the whole reﬂective learning process.
Triggering: fostering the initiation of reﬂective processes in the learner,
based on the gathered data and the analysis performed on it.
Recalling and revisiting experiences: supporting learners in recalling
and revisiting through the enrichment and presentation.
The ﬁrst part is covered by the one button wearable device, which was created be-
fore this thesis. The metrics that are being collected are determined by the individual
user and their therapist. The second and third parts are what my design needs to
accommodate.
With this framework, we now have some of the functionality the application needs
to accommodate in order to be useful for reﬂective learning purposes. Combining
this with the following four heuristics described in [CPL14],
1. Make Data interpretable at a Glance.
2. Enable Exploration of Patterns in Time Series Data.
3. Enable Discovery of Trends in Multiple Data Streams.
4. Turn Key Metrics into affordances for Action.
yields a good overall framework for how to go about designing any visualization
application for reﬂective learning.
4.2.1
Users
The main users in the case of the PTSD study, is the veteran and his/her thera-
pist. This gives us two different proﬁles to develop for. To get an understanding of
the functionality I needed to construct, I brainstormed on the needs of the different
users, and the possible use cases they might have for the application.
The ﬁrst user is the veteran. A user who might not have the highest educational
Background, and who might not have a lot of patience for yet another test or pro-
cedure as part of their therapy sessions. According to [WKN+19], there might be a
variety of other conditions that could overlap, which puts an even greater mental
strain on the user. One of the things that [LEC17] put weight on was the memory
recall bias that the data visualization can help greatly with. Not only can the visu-
alization help remind the user of the times the button was pressed, but being able
to go back to speciﬁc times and noting them, can help preparation for the next ther-
apy session. This purpose of facilitating the therapeutic process is a core part of the

project.
The second user proﬁle then focuses on the therapist working with his patient, and
the functionality he would like to have available. The therapist is someone who usu-

4.3.1
Designing the ﬁrst visualization models
In the ﬁrst design I created, the timestamp data was plotted into their corresponding
weekdays and hours of the day. This was only a simple point plot, but was an early
attempt to try and infer a meaning from the days of the week or the time of day in
which the data was generated, and functioned mostly to get some quick value out
of the datset. This plot, as well as the rest of the initial prototype is seen in Figure 4.1.
Initially, this type of plot made most sense for the PTSD case, as it in some cases
can be heavily correlated with the patients daily schedule or sleep patterns. Whereas
the Symptoms data is more likely to be related to pollen numbers which are more re-
lated to the time of year, than any given day of the week. This plotting of the PTSD
data showed a pattern, in that there were far more occurrences during weekdays,
which [LEC17] had previously used to conclude that the data was likely related to
the patients work habits.
This plot had a lot of issues. The main concern was how overly cluttered it felt.
While you can deﬁnitely make out some red areas, the different shades of yellow
and red make comparisons of speciﬁc times of day very difﬁcult. Additionally, while
the idea of plotting the entire dataset in terms of weekday and hour makes sense for
smaller periods of time, it likely wont work well for datasets like the Symptoms data
where the data stretches over longer periods of time. This is due to the fact that over
longer periods of time, your weekly schedule and work patterns could be changing.
If the data is collected while in therapy, hopefully the therapy should have an impact
on the occurrences of the data. As such it would be much more convenient with a
model that would enable the user to see their data as it progresses over time.
My second design was based on a calendar. We argued that most users would have
at one point used a calendar, or have a minimum amount of understanding of how
to use one. Looking back at how Google calendar and other calendars work, I started
creating a calendar application that would visualize the data as a Calendar heatmap.
This design is good for ﬁnding correlations between the data and the days of the
week. It can quickly give an overview of whether the data could be related to your
weekly schedule, and as can be seen in Figure 4.2 it’s fairly easy to see that there are
barely any occurrences during the weekend. This interface is what I kept working
on for quite a while, as a calendar seemed as the most intuitive way to present and
navigate the dataset.

4.3.2
Adding an annotation interface
Building on the Calendar heatmap as a basis for my application, I wanted to support
annotating speciﬁc data instances, and thereby hopefully support the functionality
of recalling past events through annotation. The intent for this interface is that it
would help with therapy through reﬂective learning in two different ways. The ﬁrst
intent is that the process of sitting down and annotating events would have a ther-
apeutic effect in and of itself. When writing down why or where you are during a
speciﬁc button press, you are more likely to remember that speciﬁc event. Secondly,
it will facilitate recalling the event, since the event is no longer just represented by
a time stamp, but also by the users own words and contextualization. Furthermore,
the user could potentially provide some degree of severity to the events by classify-
ing the event as a number between 1 and 10. Enabling annotation adds a whole new
level of interaction with the software and data.

My version of such an interface can be seen in Figure 4.3. The idea behind the de-

The idea behind the design was based on other calendar applications. Calendars
like Google Calendar, or most popular smartphone calendars, show an overview for
the current week or month, and then by pressing any individual day, you get an
overview of the plans or notes you have set for that day. This is essentially the same
mechanic, the main difference being that events are not plotted unto a timeline, but
are instead ordered in order of the time of day they happened. The delete function
enables users to ﬁlter out the events that matter, and will remove the unimportant
notes from the list, while still keeping them in the dataﬁle. This can be used to re-

This idea of annotating the different events enables the user and therapist to cus-
tomize the data collection to their advantage. The user can be instructed to write
different contexts or information depending on the situation, or they can choose to
go through it during the therapy sessions. In general, it opens up a lot of options for
reﬂective learning.

4.3.3
Post User Test Changes
The result of the ﬁrst user test showed many problems with the application. Leav-
ing aside the bugs that the users found, there were a few mismatches between my
conceptual model, and the users mental model. As for the visualizations, the users
had different issues with trying to use the application. The calendar was fairly suc-
cessful. Most users understood it intuitively, and the one that didn’t, immediately
understood how to use it once she realized it was just a calendar. The main issue was
that so far, I had been scaling the heatmap colors for each month separately, so that
the color values of each month, had no correlation to the colors of any other month.
This was a problem, as users were confused when comparing one month to another
while going through the entire dataset.
The annotation interface had a lot of issues. While the basic idea worked and made
sense, users found it cumbersome but interesting and worth looking into. As for the
plot of the weekdays and their times of day, it was confusing to all the users, with
one user being unable to understand it even after a small tutorial.
After the user test, the annotation layer was put on hold, as creating a more ac-
cessible visualization of the dataset took priority.

4.4.1
Updated Weekday Plot
The initial weekday plot had one glaring issue. Due to the crosses used for plotting
the point on the graph, and the colorscheme used, it was hard to compare different
points on the graph. Additionally, it wasn’t always clear which time of day had
the most dense cluster of button presses. To ﬁx these issues, I ﬁrst added a scaling
factor to the plotted crosses. This adds some visibility by making sparse areas of
the graph small to signify their lack of relevance. However, this doesn’t work well
with crosses, as they don’t really scale well when used for plotting points. As such, I
chose to change my point to a circle. This had the effect I was searching for, and the
resulting plot can be seen in Figure 4.4. This new design makes the important areas
stand out far more than the old one, and should hopefully give better performances
during user tests.

4.5
Design Goals in retrospect
Taking a look back at our design goals, some of them are fulﬁlled, while some are
still left for future work. Looking at the three points from [RPZMB12], all three are
fulﬁlled in one way or another. Obviously tracking cues exist, as the sheer nature
of the project is based on the self-tracking actions of the user. As for triggerend and
recalling past experiences, this is where it get’s a bit more interesting.
For triggering self-reﬂection in the user, my application primarily focuses on pas-
sive triggering. Most of the data is simply being plotted as is, and very little data
analysis is being done. The user is therefore urged to navigate and explore their
data themselves, in order to understand more about themselves. Where we can start
discussing active triggering, comes in the weekday plot. The weekday plot bins the
data together, and highlights areas of interest. This is not done through contextual-
ization or looking at any severity ratings, but simple through looking at the amount
of events within certain frames. This, while only a slight analysis, does attempt to
direct the users attention at speciﬁc times of day, as they are more likely to contain
the cause of the users button presses as a whole. With this, the visualization inter-
faces therefore fulﬁlls my ﬁrst design goal framework.
As for the second framework from [CPL14], my application only fulﬁlls the frame-
work partially. For the ﬁrst heuristic of making data interpretable at a glance, it can
be argued that parts of the application fails to do this. While the heatmap, tilemap,
and weekday plot, all show their data fairly simply, and with emphasis on areas of
interest. No numerical goals or statistics are shown. There is no indication of the
amount of button presses within the currently viewed time frame. This option for
for simple statistics would perhaps be of value to the user, if the goal was to reduce
the amount of events that the user encountered. In the case of the PTSD patient,
hopefully the amount of button presses should go down, as the therapy progresses,
but in other cases like with the professor, it might not always be the goal to reduce
the amount of data collected, but to learn from it. That being said, not having button
presses listed for the selected time period is an oversight on my part. More inter-
esting data to have listed would be amount of events classiﬁed as each different
context in the annotation layer. This statistic would be highly useful, as it would
both highlight the amount of important events, and could include the statistics for
overall button presses. This would have been a great feature, had I continued with
the annotation layer.
The second heuristic entails enabling exploration of patterns in the data. For this
heuristic, both my tile map and calendar heatmap focus on showing the days and
the density of events on the given days. As has previously been detailed, the visual-
izations I designed heavily focus on visualizing the data as days and months, which
enables the user to ﬁnd patterns based on their schedule. However, it would have
been nice to also have an interface showing individual days, more so than the list of
events supplied in the annotation layer. This interface currently functions as a list
of timestamps, but gives no indication of how close or far these are from each other,
besides the numerical value of the time stamp. A time line or daily view to that
of a standard calendar interface, would possibly bring a lot of value to the annota-
tion layer.Furthermore, taking the monthly accumulated data and plotting it in the
weekdays plot can give a great overview of how the users weekly schedule impacts
their pressing of the button, but an additional option to show just the weekday view
==PAGE:30==

for a given week, would be useful to discover patterns in weeks that stand out from
the rest of the weeks of the chosen month. For example, looking at 4.2, it could be
interesting to isolate week 3, and look solely at the patterns for that week, as it seems
to contain the majority of the data for that month.
The third and fourth heuristics are a bit more complicated. To fulﬁll the third heuris-
tic, it implies that there are multiple variables to track simultaneously. In the case of
the one button wearable, it simple tracks the time at which the button is pushed, so
there is only a single variable to consider, which is the time at which it is pressed.
However, with the addition of the contextualisation supplied by the annotation
layer, the user can deﬁne a classiﬁcation context for any given button press. This
adds an extra variable to consider, and it could be beneﬁcial to have a visualization
of how the different contexts are scattered across days or times of day. This didn’t
make it into my prototype, but would be an interesting addition for future work, as
applying context to data is an important part of self-reﬂection, and being able to see
the distribution of those contexts, more so than just the amount of each, could be a
valuable asset to the application.
The fourth, and last, heuristic is sparsely fulﬁlled. While the application does sup-
port having smaller slices of the overall data, in that, any given month is shown
in the calendar heatmap, the entire year is shown in the tilemap, and the individual
days can be seen in the annotation layer, it doesn’t support more detailed time frame
slicing to support more intricate user exploration. The application also doesn’t allow
for changing what data is being plotted. For example, changing the heatmaps to fo-
cus on amount of events classiﬁed as speciﬁc contexts would give a great overview
of which days the different contexts are more prevalent. While a Thursday might
contain the most events, Monday events might be almost entirely due to public
transportation usage.
As such, not all design goals were met. Some were partially met, and others were
kept as future projects.

5.1
Platform
When deciding on which platform to use, the main concern was the accessibility to
the user. The ﬁrst choice was to decide which device to develop for. On one hand
smartphones are abundant, and have become more and more attainable without
paying a hefty price. But they’re also tedious to develop for, as you are develop-
ing for a variety of different hardware. In particular the screen size and resolution
could prove difﬁcult to work with across a wide variety of devices. While Android
owns most of the marketshare (albeit different versions and phone manufacturers),
I didn’t want to limit myself to a single target device.
This lead me to developing natively for the PC, and since I focus primarily on Linux
and Windows (while acknowledging that Linux systems are still very much niche
among consumers), I decided on developing for Windows. As for Programming
Language, Python ended up being my language of choice for a variety of reasons.
5.1.1
PyInstaller
My goal was to provide the program in a package that was as easy to open and use as
possible. This means the program needs to be available without a lot of preinstalling
of various programs. Python generally is run through an interpreter, which means
you’d need to install that, and often a lot more to make it run. The user might need to
setup pip, Anaconda would help greatly, dependencies could be an issue. In general
it could be a mess. I ended up using PyInstaller, which is a great tool for building
essentially Python Applications, without the need for a Python Interpreter, or any
setup really. PyInstaller bundles Python code, along with it’s dependancies into a
neat package with a .exe ﬁle. PyInstaller was essential for my goal of making the
program as accessible as possible.

5.2.1
Data Parser
The data is currently being parsed in a very crude way. The different data ﬁles, while
all using the same time format, contained different delimiters and differing amounts
of variables per button press. This is largely due to the different projects they have
been used for, but it stands to reason that a more optimized parser should be writ-
ten, once a standard has been settled on for the one-button device data format. Thus,
optimizing the parser at this point wasn’t a priority. As it stands currently, the parser
works as follows.
Once the ﬁle has been chosen, the ﬁlepath is sent to the parser, and the parser is
initialized. The different mplwidgets are then each updated separately, calling their
individual methods for obtaining the data they need from the parser. The Calendar
for example, needs both knowledge of the month it is currently looking at, as well
as the day of year with the maximum amount of events, in order to scale color of the
heatmap correspondingly. While the weekdays only focuses on any given month, as
it simply accumulates the data instances based on the weekdays and times of day
they represent.

5.3
Calendar and Tilemap
The Calendar heatmap is the most complicated visualization I did. The calendar
representation itself is constructed from the ground up, with a simple 6-by-7 matrix
representing the possible months of the year. Even though the longest month is gen-
erally 31 days, due to the time of week the month can start and end, extra days are
padded on either side, so that when the visualization updates to a new month, the
days of the week match the previous one. The visual matrix is then lined with an ar-
ray of action listeners, that send click events back to the UI layer to handle updating
the window, and open up the annotation interface.
The tilemap is essentially just an array of the calendar heatmap but without the
click events, transposed so that it ﬁts the style of github, and scaled to ﬁt. The most

5.4
Annotation
The annotation interface itself is fairly simple in essence. The most interesting part
of it is probably the interaction with the data ﬁles that the context and notes are
stored in. Currently, the application doesn’t create any ﬁles unless the user speciﬁ-
cally chooses to generate them. When the user then chooses a date in the calendar,
the option to generate all the notes for the day is available. If the user chooses to
press the button, they then get the option to annotate any given notes for that day.
These ﬁles are just stored as simple text ﬁles in a folder, with an integer representing
the classiﬁcation (used for the pictures associated with the note), and the text the
user has inputted for the note. Each event has it’s own .txt ﬁle, but only the notes
that the user has chosen to actually create are saved.
There are a variety of things that could be done with the annotation layer, in or-

the location context classiﬁcation, to add an additional layer to the data, but it didn’t
have priority over the visualizations.

Chapter 7
Discussion
Visualizing time series data is not a simple matter. This has been shown time and
time again by people with more experience than I, but there are deﬁnitely some steps
that can be taken in order to facilitate understanding and exploring of the data. My
goal with this thesis was to create an application that could help users gain an un-
derstanding of their data without having a large background in statistical modelling
or mathematical principles.
The application I created enables self-reﬂection of simple self-tracked data through
exploration and navigation. However, it is not very thoroughly optimized, espe-
cially not from a Usability standpoint. At one point improving the usability of the
application itself was put on hold, in favor of focusing on the data visualizations be-
ing presented to the user. This leaves the application is a very ﬂawed state, and far
from optimally implemented. On the other hand, this gave me more time to focus
on the visualizations, and the overall interface design, which became the focus of
my thesis.
The application and it’s usefulness was tested by 5 test participants across two dif-
ferent user tests. This is deﬁnitely less participants than would be ideal, and all of
them being students, hardly makes them representative of the user proﬁles I tried
targeting the application at. However, due to convenience and time constraints, their
assistance was highly valued, as it would have been problematic to procure for ex-
ample PTSD veterans as part of the user testing group.
Additionally, while the objective of the application is to enable self-reﬂection, the
participants weren’t looking at their own data, which results in a separation be-
tween the test participant and the data. Ideally, the user test would be performed
with someone who had been collecting their own data, and then the purpose of the
test could include trying to make sense of that data, and what tools are needed to
facilitate that process. Instead the test participants ended up being mostly reviewers
on the applications usability, as well as the accessibility of the visualizations.
Despite these limitations, the application proved successful in conveying the data
to the test participants, without any prior explanation of the application itself. Users
were able to draw valuable conclusions about the context to the data, from simple
visualizations of the timestamps. This enabled the participants to give valuable in-
sight into the visualizations and the application as a whole.

Chapter 8
Future Work
There are plenty of things that still needs to be done, both things that could’ve been
done as part of my project, and things that needs to be done within the subject of
self-reﬂection in Quantiﬁed Self as a whole. For my project, this section shows the
things I would have liked to touch more upon.
8.1
Visualize time changes and timezones better
Timezones are always a mystery when it comes to visualization and time series man-
agement. As described earlier, handling Timezones is far from trivial, and in my
project I chose the easy way out and represented my time data in local time. This
might work for smaller research or therapy projects where the user generally doesn’t
move across timezones a lot, but would be insufﬁcient for users like the DTU pro-
fessor from the Symptoms data, who ﬂies across the globe to conventions and other
appoints frequently. It would be beneﬁcial to his self-reﬂective process to have either
a form of separation of the data depending on time zone and usecase. In his case, it
might be relevant to continue using local time, but show that there has been a change
of location / timezone. One could easily imagine a situation where the event being
tracked was related to the time of day and users daily schedule. As such, the way
time zones should be handled, can vary greatly depending on use scenario. An in-
teresting solution to this could be to show when time zones had been changed, and
then continue to represent data in local time, but with the added annotation that the
data was generated in a different time zone than previously on the timeline. This
is in stark contrast to the Google Calendar standard, where you can freely change
between which time zone you want to convert to.
8.2
Progress
An important part of the therapy process is looking at how the patient has pro-
gressed over time. Initially, I always aimed to look more into facilitating self-reﬂection
through progression by showing views of how the button press frequency had pos-
sibly changed over time. This was down prioritized a bit, when my focus changed
to simplifying the initial calendar and weekday visualization. Ideally, while the cur-
rent calendar heatmap and year tilemap can show progress across a month or year,
taking two different time periods and comparing them side by side, with a detailed
numerical analysis isn’t feasible. The user can compare different months of the year
by navigating through the year, and can use the tilemap to get a vague understand-
ing of how the frequency has changed, but a more detailed analysis tool would add
great value. The tilemap vaguely supports having the a progress overview, in that,

you get an overview of the entire year, but it’s not detailed enough, and doesn’t give
a good enough progression overview.
8.3
Show more relevant data
One of the things I strived to do, was to keep the visualizations simple, and with a
minimum amount of clutter and information overload. In doing this, a lot of possible
value was lost from not giving users access to more numbers and information. Some
of the data that could be beneﬁcial to the user include:
1. Frequency of button presses over time (as mentioned above).
2. How the button presses are distributed across the different user selected con-
text classiﬁcations.
3. Amount of events annotated, compared to overall amount of events.
Having this information available could reap huge beneﬁts. In the case of the PTSD
patient, it could be very useful to the therapist to see how the patient contextualize
or classify different days more speciﬁcally. The distribution of events falling into
the work category during weekdays, compared to how the classiﬁcation looks dur-
ing weekends, would for example make the process of discovering the root to the
patients much easier. Having evolution of the amount of events over time plotted
meaningfully could also lead to interesting results. From conversations with my su-
pervisor, I’ve understood that normally there are plenty of common patterns that
occur with different subjects, as the data collection frequency tends to scale with
user engagement and point in the therapy process. This would likely be apparent in
the users willingness to annotate events as well. Finding a meaningful way to show
this data could prove of great use to the therapist.
8.4
Manage multiple data ﬁles
Currently the burden of managing data ﬁles is on the user, as the application does
not support using multiple data ﬁles at the same time, or even merge them together
into one. This could potentially be useful, both for comparing the data of different
datasets (and possibly different users), but also for just merging several dataﬁles to-
gether in case the user for example changed his device halfway through the project.
This was deﬁnitely not a priority for my project, but at least the ﬁrst point, of being
able to compare several ﬁles, can yield great results for self-reﬂection, and especially
as a part of the macroscope mentioned in [Wol09]. Being able to compare your own
data, to the data of similar users could have beneﬁcial effects for a multitude of dif-
ferent user proﬁles trying to make sense of their own situation and data.
8.5
More and more user tests
One of the core aspects of developing applications for users, is involving and con-
sulting users frequently. While I discussed the project and the visualizations with
former test subjects and colleagues from time to time, extensive user testing left
much more to be desired.

8.6
Annotations stored locally
Currently, I store the user annotations locally on the users machine. This makes
sense, and was the most obvious ﬁrst choice for a prototype. This could be cumber-
some to the user though, as they would have to bring their laptop or home machine
to his therapist. Alternatively, the user would need to know how to extract the data
from the folder structure, and manually bring it to the therapist via for example a
USB stick. This would be highly unacceptable, as the whole point of the application
is to facilitate the process for users who are not necessarily accustomed to working
with software already. Having the data be somehow exported to a ﬁle, and then
either sending that or bringing it to the therapist could be an option. In general,
storing the data locally leads to some issues in regards to the therapy process. While
the time series data can easily be brought to therapy sessions via the one-button
wearable, so far the annotation data can’t.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
In this paper, I have detailed the development process I engaged in, in order to de-
velop an application for self-reﬂection purposes. I have detailed my motivations
and goals, as well as the thoughts that went into designing said application, based
on some of the ﬁndings of a previous Master’s student and the valuable input of my
supervisor, Jakob Eg Larsen.
I have described the testing of an early prototype of said application, and detailed a
variety of issues and improvements that should be made to it, following the results
of the user testing. Furthermore, I’ve described the future work that needs to go into
similar applications in order to bring further value to potential users.
Unfortunately, I didn’t manage to get good comparison of my application to the
one of Johan Froelich through user testing. This would have been valuable data, to
conﬁrm whether or not users valued my prototype as simpler to understand. As it
stands, my user tests primarily validated my prototype in a vacuum.
There is still plenty of work to be done, both within the ﬁeld of Personal Informat-
ics, and in terms of my application and research speciﬁcally. The Quantiﬁed Self
movement, as mentioned in Chapter 2 and [Wol09], calls for people to start gather-
ing more and more date about themselves, so that as a whole, we can gain an insight
into our daily lives. To this end, more simple tools need to be available to normal
users, so that not only professors and researchers and up being the ones collecting
the data.
Hopefully my work will help shed some light on some of the design choices that
need to be considered when designing a visualization tool for this kind of purpose,
even if the application itself probably won’t be of much research use to anyone other
than myself.
