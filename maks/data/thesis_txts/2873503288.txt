Abstract
The growing reliance on Long Range Wide Area Network (LoRaWAN) in Internet
of Things (IoT) applications introduces new security challenges, particularly with
regard to Denial of Service (DoS) attacks. This thesis investigates the feasibility of
such attacks using low-cost hardware and proposes detection and mitigation strate-
gies at the device level. Several strategies—including Retry, Dynamic, Listen Before
Talk (LBT), and Probe Aware Listen Before Talk (PALBT)—were implemented and
tested against both static and dynamic jamming conditions using real-world hardware.
Evaluation metrics such as message delivery rate, energy consumption, and mitiga-
tion accuracy were analysed in detail. Additionally, a server-side uplink monitoring
microservice was developed to passively detect jamming based on network traﬀic
patterns. The results show that while no single strategy performs best in all scenar-
ios, certain combinations like PALBT and Retry offer a balanced trade-off between
robustness and energy eﬀiciency. Limitations in hardware and protocol compliance
were also explored, providing a foundation for future research into more adaptive and
scalable LoRaWAN defences.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the evolution toward smart cities and modern infrastructure, the reliance on wire-
less communication has become more pronounced than ever. From energy distribution
to transportation, modern systems depend on seamless, real-time data exchange be-
tween distributed devices.
However, as this reliance grows, so do concerns about
reliability and resilience. Wireless communication, particularly in unlicensed spec-
trum, is inherently vulnerable to interference and disruption, raising challenges that
demand robust, low-power, and long-range communication protocols.
1.1
Motivation
One of the key drivers of modern infrastructure is the growing presence of wirelessly
connected physical devices, commonly known as the Internet of Things (IoT) [1].
The IoT refers to a network of everyday objects embedded with sensors, software,
and wireless connectivity, allowing them to collect, exchange, and respond to data
without direct human input. These connected devices underpin systems across a wide
range of domains:
• Smart energy grids, for example, use smart meters to monitor consumption and
dynamically adjust power distribution.
• Precision agriculture involves devices like soil moisture sensors and automated
irrigation systems to optimise crop yields.
• Industrial automation includes predictive maintenance systems that monitor
equipment health and remote monitoring tools that reduce downtime.
A defining feature of many IoT deployments is the need to support devices that are
located in remote or varied environments, power-constrained and expected to operate
unattended for extended periods. This necessitates communication technologies that
are not only energy-eﬀicient and long-range but also robust enough to handle real-
world interference and signal degradation.
To meet these requirements, various low-power wireless communication protocols
have emerged. Among them, Long Range (LoRa) stands out for its ability to transmit
data over long distances while consuming minimal power [2]. LoRa achieves this by
using Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation at the physical layer (described in
==PAGE:14==

1 Introduction
Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) model), where digital data is encoded into radio
signals using chirps (frequency-modulated signals that increase or decrease over time).
This modulation technique enhances LoRa’s resistance to natural interference and
background noise, allowing it to operate reliably in low-power, long-range scenarios.
On top of the LoRa physical layer, the Long Range Wide Area Network (LoRaWAN)
protocol provides Medium Access Control (MAC) and network management capabil-
ities, such as addressing, acknowledgements, and encryption. Together, they enable
robust communication between constrained devices and central servers, even in chal-
lenging radio environments. It is now a central component in applications such as
smart agriculture, utility metering, environmental monitoring, industrial control sys-
tems, and other low-power networks in critical environments [3].
However, LoRaWAN’s simplicity and reliance on unlicensed spectrum come at
the cost of reduced resilience to intentional disruptions. In particular, it is vulnerable
to jamming, a form of deliberate radio interference where an adversary transmits
conflicting signals to disrupt communication [4]. These attacks exploit fundamental
characteristics of the protocol, such as its open transmission medium.
Furthermore, since the spectrum used by LoRaWAN is unlicensed, other wireless
protocols, such as Sigfox or Wi-Fi-based devices, are legally allowed to operate in
the same frequency bands. This coexistence introduces ambiguity, as it can become
diﬀicult to distinguish between legitimate cross-technology interference and malicious
jamming.
This added uncertainty complicates detection and mitigation efforts in
practice [5].
While several studies have investigated LoRaWAN security, particularly in areas
like key management and encryption, the detection and mitigation of physical-layer
attacks such as jamming appear to be less thoroughly explored. This is particularly
concerning for critical IoT deployments, where communication reliability is essential.
This thesis aims to address this gap by experimentally evaluating the impact of
jamming on LoRaWAN networks and by exploring practical detection and mitigation
techniques. By simulating jamming attacks in a controlled environment and analysing
their effect on communication performance, the project seeks to contribute insights
into how these threats manifest at the Physical (PHY) and MAC layers, and how
they may be identified or counteracted in practice.
1.1.1
Research Question
To scope and angle the thesis in the direction of Denial of Service (DoS) attacks on
LoRaWAN, following research questions has been formulated:
• General
1. What types of Denial of Service (DoS) attacks target LoRaWAN, and how
do they impact network performance?
2. How can a DoS attack be executed at the LoRaWAN network layer, and
what existing countermeasures can be implemented?
==PAGE:15==
1.1 Motivation
3
• Detection
1. Can missing acknowledgements reliably indicate a DoS attack?
2. Can Listen Before Talk (LBT) detect DoS activity or ongoing transmissions
on the end device’s channel?
3. Can abnormal or invalid packets serve as indicators of DoS attacks?
• Mitigation
1. How effective is dynamic spreading factor adaptation in mitigating tar-
geted jamming or flooding?
2. How effective is LBT as a mitigation strategy when up against reactive
jamming?

CHAPTER 2
Methodology
This chapter will outline the methodological approach used in this thesis to handle
detection and mitigation of Denial of Service (DoS) attacks on Long Range Wide Area
Network (LoRaWAN). The chapter will begin with presenting the overall research
strategy, followed by a high-level threat model to demonstrate the adversaries and
the attack that will be focused on in this study. Subsequently, the methodology for
conducting the attack is described and how it is has been decided to conduct a DoS
attack.
2.1
Research methodology
The research took a practical and experimental approach, combining a review of
existing work with real-world testing to explore jamming attacks in LoRaWAN. From
the gained knowledge, experiments were conducted to identify the most practical,
eﬀicient and feasible technique to detect and mitigate jamming attacks.
The process began with a broad search using general search engines (e.g., google.com),
where keywords such as LoRa, LoRaWAN, and jamming were used to identify intro-
ductory material and gain a general overview of the technological landscape. This
initial phase produced several thousands informal hits, of which roughly 50 sources
were reviewed in more detail to understand the core concepts of Long Range (LoRa)
and LoRaWAN, with particular focus on the Physical (PHY) and Medium Access
Control (MAC) layers.
To ensure academic rigour, a more systematic literature search was conducted
using databases such as DTU Findit and Google Scholar. The initial queries focused
on LoRaWAN, returning approximately 4900 results on Findit and 37000 results on
Google Scholar.
These were then refined using combinations of keywords like Lo-
RaWAN jamming and LoRaWAN DoS, which narrowed the pool to around 500 rele-
vant papers. Abstract screening reduced this number to roughly 50 articles, of which
20 were selected for close reading and referencing in this thesis.
To better understand how jamming is addressed across different communication
technologies, complementary searches were performed on related standards, including
Wi-Fi, Zigbee, and Bluetooth. These searches revealed that while jamming is a well-
known issue in wireless communication, real-time detection can be challenging even
in more mature protocols.
==PAGE:17==
2.2 Context and Scope of the Study
5
The aim of this initial phase was to build a strong foundational understanding
of the subject matter and the underlying technologies. It also enabled us to identify
commonalities and differences in how jamming attacks are conducted and detected.
Through the review, we found that there are multiple approaches to carrying out
jamming attacks, each with varying levels of complexity and effectiveness.
Based on the gathered information, known jamming strategies were evaluated
in terms of practicality, effectiveness, and relevance to the scope of this thesis. A
specific strategy was then selected for implementation in a controlled experimental
environment. The focus of this phase is on observing the impact of the attack and
assessing how effectively such activity can be detected and mitigated.
2.2
Context and Scope of the Study
To frame the relevance of this research, it was important to consider the real-world set-
tings where LoRaWAN was deployed, and where disruptions due to DoS attacks could
have caused tangible impact. LoRaWAN is increasingly used in critical applications
such as smart metering, agriculture, supply chain monitoring, and environmental
sensing [2,6]. These deployments typically operate in constrained environments with
battery-powered devices that rely on long-range, low-power wireless communication
to transmit infrequent updates.
For example, in smart utility metering, sensors periodically report energy or wa-
ter consumption data to the utility provider. A successful jamming attack on such
systems can lead to billing inaccuracies, resource mismanagement, or delayed fault
detection [7].
Similarly, in precision agriculture, sensors monitor parameters such
as soil moisture and crop health.
Here, disruption in transmission may result in
crop damage due to untimely irrigation or treatment, demonstrating the potential
operational impact of even short-term communication outages [2].
These environments are typically characterised by:
• Sparse deployments: Devices are distributed over large geographic areas.
• Resource constraints: End devices are low-power, with limited ability to perform
security checks.
• Unreliable backhaul connectivity: Gateways may operate in rural or industrial
settings with weak infrastructure.
• Public unlicensed spectrum: Communications occur in open spectrum (e.g., 868
MHz in EU), making physical-layer attacks both feasible and hard to prevent.
Given this environment, the threat model must account for an adversary who does
not require sophisticated tools or access privileges to disrupt communication. It is
often suﬀicient to use a simple LoRa-enabled device to inject noise or mimic legitimate
transmissions, exploiting the broadcast nature of LoRa radio and the unencrypted
MAC and PHY headers [8,9].

This practical context directly informs the adversarial capabilities defined in the
following section. By understanding the operational landscape of LoRaWAN, we can
more accurately assess what an attacker could realistically know and do - ranging
from identifying Device Extended Unique Identifier (DevEUI)s in plaintext headers
to transmitting on overlapping frequencies and exploiting limitations in duty cycle
compliance [10].
2.3
Threat model
Having established the practical deployment scenarios and the environments in which
LoRaWAN systems operate, we now define the threat model that frames this thesis.
The following section outlines the adversarial assumptions, capabilities, and objectives
based on realistic constraints derived from common LoRaWAN use cases.
The model is designed to reflect a realistic but limited adversary capable of dis-
rupting LoRaWAN communication using commonly available hardware. The focus is
on external adversaries with physical access to the radio environment but no control
over the LoRaWAN infrastructure or end devices. The adversary’s goal is to disrupt
message delivery or prevent device activation through jamming attacks.
2.3.1
System Overview
The system under evaluation consists of a single LoRaWAN end device that transmits
data packets to a network server via a gateway. Communication between the device
and the gateway occurs wirelessly using LoRa modulation, while the gateway forwards
the received data to the network server over a standard Internet Protocol (IP)-based
connection.
An external adversary attempts to disrupt this communication using a LoRa-
capable device configured for interference. The adversary’s device is connected to a
laptop that provides control and power, allowing flexible deployment and repeatable
execution of jamming behaviour. This setup enables unauthorised transmissions to
be injected into the radio channel in an attempt to degrade the network’s availability.
2.3.2
Adversary
The adversary is an external entity with physical access to the LoRa radio environ-
ment but no access to the backend infrastructure, end devices, gateways, or servers.
As mentioned in the previous Section, the adversary is equipped with a LoRa-capable
device connected to a laptop, configured to interfere with legitimate communication.
The adversary is assumed to be motivated and technically capable, but does not
possess encryption keys or credentials that would allow access to authenticated ser-
vices within the network. Instead, the adversary focuses on degrading communication
availability by injecting interfering signals into the channel.

Adversary capabilities
The following capabilities are assumed:
• The adversary can transmit using the same frequency bands, spreading factors,

• The adversary can eavesdrop on unencrypted PHY and MAC layer headers.
• The adversary can generate and transmit valid-looking LoRaWAN frames (e.g.,

• The adversary may use a high-gain directional antenna and/or external power

Adversary objectives
The adversary’s primary goal is to degrade or deny the
availability of LoRaWAN communication. This includes:
• Preventing the successful delivery of uplink messages from end devices to the
network server.
• Exploiting unencrypted header fields to selectively interfere with legitimate traf-

• Wasting gateway airtime and bandwidth by flooding it with syntactically valid
but semantically irrelevant packets.

Despite these capabilities, the adversary faces several prac-

• No access to session keys: The adversary cannot decrypt payloads or forge

session keys and the ability to generate correct MICs.
• Hardware constraints: The attacker’s device may have limitations in power

are half-duplex, restricting simultaneous transmission and reception.

2.3.3
Justification of adversary choice
This thesis adopts an external adversary model focused on PHY layer jamming rather
than insider threats, malware injection, or protocol exploitation. This choice reflects
the practical feasibility of launching jamming attacks using low-cost, commercially
available LoRa hardware, without requiring backend access or credential compromise.
External jammers represent a realistic and critical threat, especially in unlicensed
spectrum environments where transmissions are open and easily observable. Further-
more, focusing on this adversary allows the experimental evaluation of observable,
protocol-compliant interference patterns and their detection feasibility under con-
strained conditions.

3.2
Denial-of-Service
As IoT systems become increasingly integral to critical infrastructure, threats like
DoS attacks have emerged as a major concern. A Denial of Service (DoS) attack is a
malicious attempt to disrupt the normal functioning of a targeted server, service, or
network. It does this by overwhelming the target with a flood of illegitimate requests.
As a result, the system becomes slow, unresponsive, or completely inaccessible to
legitimate users [16].
Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks are more advanced and often make
use of the fact that using multiple systems simultaneously increases the impact of
a DoS attempt.
In a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack, the malicious
traﬀic typically comes from a large number of compromised devices, forming what’s
known as a botnet. These devices can include computers, servers, IoT devices, or
even smartphones. They are typically infected with malware and controlled remotely
by an attacker, often without the owner’s knowledge.
Because the attack originates from many sources, it’s much harder to block or
mitigate. Traditional security measures like IP blocking or firewall rules are often
insuﬀicient. Malicious traﬀic can appear to originate from legitimate users across

various geographical locations. Over the years, DDoS attacks have grown in both
scale and sophistication. They are now capable of generating terabits of traﬀic per
second, overwhelming not just individual servers but entire networks or data centres.
3.2.1
Layers of DoS Attacks
While DoS attacks are commonly associated with digital services, the concept also
extends to physical scenarios or any of the 7 layers of the Open Systems Interconnec-
tion (OSI) model. For example, an attacker disabling the lighting in a store denies
service to customers and staff. Similarly, puncturing a car’s tire prevents it from
being used, effectively denying the service it is meant to provide. These physical
analogies highlight that a DoS attack is ultimately about obstructing access to a
resource, regardless of whether it is digital or physical.
In the digital realm, the open nature of internet communication has amplified the
threat of DoS attacks, particularly against widely used protocols such as Hypertext
Transfer Protocol (HTTP), Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure (HTTPS), Secure
Socket Shell (SSH), and File Transfer Protocol (FTP).
These attacks can cause
significant disruption by exploiting vulnerabilities at various layers of the OSI model.
The OSI model [17] is a conceptual framework that divides network communica-
tion into seven layers, ranging from physical transmission (Layer 1) to application-
level interactions (Layer 7). While application-layer attacks, such as those involving
HTTP, HTTPS, SSH, FTP, or other application layer protocols, are the most widely
recognized, this report focuses specifically on the Physical (PHY) layer and the data
link layer.
The physical layer (Layer 1) deals with the transmission of raw bits over the
communication medium. In the case of Long Range Wide Area Network (LoRaWAN),
this includes radio frequencies, chirp modulation, and signal timing. The data link
layer (Layer 2), on the other hand, handles how devices identify each other on the
network and how data is framed and verified. In LoRaWAN, this corresponds to the
Medium Access Control (MAC) layer, which manages addressing, frame counters, and
acknowledgements. Since the LoRaWAN stack omits the Logical Link Control (LLC)
sublayer, the MAC layer serves as the entirety of Layer 2.
At the PHY layer, DoS attacks target the radio spectrum itself [18–20], regardless
of whether the data conforms to any specific protocol. This includes jamming by
transmitting Long Range (LoRa)-like or arbitrary noise on the same frequency and
spreading factor as legitimate traﬀic. Additional methods include chirp interference,
which uses LoRa-style chirps to confuse receivers. Another is carrier flooding, where
an attacker keeps the channel occupied with long preambles or continuous tones.
For instance, a rogue device transmitting random LoRa chirps using the same
frequency and spreading factor as a legitimate end device can cause packet collisions,
resulting in lost uplinks. These attacks are inexpensive to execute, requiring only
basic hardware such as the SX1276/SX1262 and minimal technical expertise. Their
effectiveness is amplified by LoRa’s use of the unslotted Additive Links On-line Hawaii
==PAGE:24==

Area (ALOHA) protocol [21] at the MAC layer, which does not coordinate access to
the channel and performs poorly under high collision rates. Additionally, gateways
have a limited ability to demodulate multiple simultaneous signals, making them
more susceptible to interference. Beyond disrupting communication, these attacks
also increase the energy consumption of end devices, potentially accelerating battery
depletion [22].
At the data link layer (corresponding to the MAC layer in LoRaWAN), attack-
ers may exploit the protocol’s logic using syntactically valid but malicious frames.
Several techniques fall under this category. One is the replay attack [23], where pre-
viously captured packets are resent to confuse the network. Another is confirmed
uplink flooding, which forces gateways to issue acknowledgements, consuming down-

Attackers may also send fake join requests from fabricated Device Extended Unique
Identifier (DevEUI)s to overload the server’s processing capacity. Finally, some ad-

to trigger unnecessary synchronisation attempts and error handling procedures.
For example, an attacker may flood a gateway with bogus Device Address (DevAddr)s.
They might also continuously send confirmed uplinks to exhaust the gateway’s down-

problematic in constrained bands such as EU 868 MHz.
These data link-layer attacks are particularly dangerous because they use syntac-

they exploit LoRaWAN’s inherent airtime and timing limitations.
3.3
LoRaWAN
Before discussing specific attack and mitigation strategies, it is necessary to establish
a foundational understanding of the LoRaWAN protocol. This section provides an
overview of both the physical layer (LoRa) and the MAC layer (LoRaWAN), along
with relevant transmission characteristics, security mechanisms, and operational con-
straints. This background is essential for understanding the vulnerabilities and de-
fence strategies addressed later in the thesis.
3.3.1
The LoRaWAN stack
LoRaWAN builds on top of LoRa by defining how devices interact within a network.
To provide context for the attack surfaces and countermeasures discussed later, it is
first necessary to understand the division of responsibilities between the physical and
MAC layers in the LoRaWAN stack [24].
At the physical layer (PHY), LoRa handles the modulation of data into radio
signals using Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) techniques (explained in section 3.3.2).

operates at the MAC layer and specifies addressing, encryption, acknowledgements,
and device classes. In other words, LoRa determines how data is transmitted over
the air, while LoRaWAN determines when, what, and to whom the data is sent.

The LoRaWAN specification is widely adopted in IoT applications due to its ver-

data intermittently. As its name suggests, Long Range (LoRa) is capable of transmit-

useful for devices located in remote areas.
LoRa operates in license-free radio frequency bands [27, 28], which may vary by
region due to local regulations. However, the practical use of LoRa remains consistent
across different regions.
3.3.2
LoRa
LoRa [28] is a physical radio communication technology designed for long-range, low-
power wireless communication. It operates at the PHY layer and uses spread spectrum
modulation to encode data for transmission. In contrast to narrowband modulation
(like Amplitude Modulation (AM) or Frequency Modulation (FM)), spread spectrum
techniques distribute the signal energy over a wider frequency band. This enhances

resistance to interference and allows for longer transmission distances, as the signal
becomes harder to jam or corrupt.
Specifically, LoRa employs Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS), a modulation scheme
in which information is embedded into chirp signals, which are waveforms whose
frequency increases (up-chirp) or decreases (down-chirp) linearly over time. These
chirps are highly resilient to noise and can still be demodulated even when received
below the noise floor. This is because the signal continuously sweeps through a wide
range of frequencies, making it less susceptible to narrowband interference.
As a

levels of radio noise.

3.3.3
Packet Transmission
LoRa packet transmission involves complex interactions between multiple parame-
ters that affect range, data rate, power consumption, and reliability. Understanding
these relationships is crucial for optimizing network performance and ensuring reliable
communication.
Packets are categorized as uplink (from end-devices to the server) and downlink
(from the server to end-devices).
Typically, a transmission includes the Received
Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI), Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) [30], and data, with
the first two occupying 4 bytes each, followed by the actual payload.
LoRa supports two packet formatting modes: implicit mode and explicit mode. In
implicit mode, the transmitting and receiving devices must pre-agree on parameters
such as payload length, coding rate, and presence of a Cyclic Redundancy Check
(CRC). As a result, the packet omits header fields to reduce overhead. In contrast,
explicit mode includes a physical header (Physical Header (PHDR)), a CRC for the
header (Physical Header Cyclic Redundancy Check (PHDRCRC)), and the payload
==PAGE:29==
3.3 LoRaWAN
17
(Physical Payload (PHYPAYLOAD)) with an optional final CRC. This provides more
flexibility, especially in dynamic or heterogeneous networks. Uplink transmissions in
LoRaWAN typically omit the final CRC [31].
Additionally, LoRaWAN includes beacon frames, which are special downlink pack-
ets periodically broadcast by gateways. Beacons are primarily used for time synchro-
nisation in Class B devices (described in Section 3.3.5), enabling them to schedule
receive windows and conserve energy. Beacon packets contain fields like a time stamp,
a beacon-specific payload (Beacon Payload (BCNPAYLOAD)), and optional gateway

Transmission Parameters and Trade-offs
Spreading Factor Impact: The spreading factor fundamentally determines the
trade-off between data rate and sensitivity. Each increase in SF doubles the trans-
mission time while improving receiver sensitivity by approximately 2.5 dB. Different
spreading factors enable orthogonal signal transmission, allowing receivers to success-
fully receive distinct signals sent over a given channel simultaneously. This relation-
ship has profound implications for network capacity and battery life [32].
These trade-offs are clearly illustrated when comparing the transmission time
of a fixed payload across different spreading factors.
For a 20-byte payload with
different spreading factors: SF7 provides a higher bit rate than SF12, with larger
spreading factors providing higher receiver sensitivity but longer transmission times.
For example, spreading factor 7 requires -7.5 dB SNR to decode information, while
higher spreading factors require progressively lower SNR thresholds [30].
Bandwidth Considerations: LoRaWAN uses bandwidth values of 125, 250,
and 500 kHz, with increasing bandwidth providing higher effective data rates but
reduced sensitivity due to reduced time-on-air [33]. The bandwidth determines the
chip rate of the LoRa signal modulation, with one chip sent per Hz per second. The
choice of bandwidth affects both transmission time and interference susceptibility.
Power Control Strategies: LoRaWAN devices can adjust transmission power
with regional limitations, such as +16 dBm Effective Isotropic Radiated Power (EIRP)
in Europe and +30 dBm in the United States of America [34, 35]. Adaptive power
control algorithms can optimize power usage while maintaining link quality, extending
battery life by 20-40% in networks with good coverage.
Channel Access and Duty Cycle Constraints: In addition to spreading
factor, bandwidth, and power settings, LoRaWAN devices must operate within strict
regional duty cycle regulations, particularly in the unlicensed Industrial, Scientific,

are typically limited to a 1% duty cycle per sub-band, restricting the maximum

3.3.3.2
Error Correction and Reliability
Forward Error Correction: LoRa implements Forward Error Correction (FEC)
using Coding Rate (CR) of 4/5, 4/6, 4/7, and 4/8. A CR of 4/5 means that for every
4 bits of real data, 1 redundant parity bit is added. These additional bits enable the
receiver to detect and correct certain errors without requiring retransmission. Higher
CRs (e.g., 4/8) offer better resilience to interference and noise by increasing redun-
dancy, but they also result in longer airtime and reduced effective data throughput.
The coding rate can be configured statically by the end-device or dynamically adapted
by the network using Adaptive Data Rate (ADR), depending on link conditions and
reliability requirements [37,38].
Cyclic Redundancy Check: Uplink messages include CRC protection to detect
transmission errors. The CRC is a checksum appended to the end of the payload, cal-
culated based on its contents. Upon reception, the receiver recalculates the checksum
and compares it to the transmitted value. If a mismatch is detected, the message is
discarded, ensuring that corrupted data is not processed. While CRC cannot correct
errors, it is essential for verifying message integrity. Downlink messages also include
CRC fields, enabling bidirectional error detection.
Automated Repeat reQuest: LoRaWAN supports a basic retransmission mech-
anism using confirmed uplink messages. When a message is sent as “confirmed,” the
network server must send an Acknowledgement (ACK) back to the end device. If
an ACK is not received within the specified timeout period, the device attempts
to retransmit the message, up to a defined maximum. This mechanism, known as
Automated Repeat reQuest (ARQ), increases delivery reliability but also consumes
airtime and energy. Therefore, it must be used judiciously, especially in dense net-
works or regions with strict duty cycle regulations. Studies have shown that optimal
performance depends on careful packet interval management and selective use of con-
firmed messages.
3.3.3.3
Channel Access and Collision Avoidance
ALOHA Protocol: ALOHA is an early random access protocol developed for com-
puter networks, first implemented in the ALOHAnet at the University of Hawaii in
the 1970s. The protocol operates on a simple principle: devices can transmit data
==PAGE:31==
3.3 LoRaWAN
19
whenever they have a message to send, but if two or more devices transmit simul-
taneously, their messages collide and become corrupted. When devices detect such
collisions, they wait for a random period before attempting retransmission.
The protocol exists in two main variants. Pure ALOHA allows devices to transmit
at any time without coordination, while Slotted ALOHA divides time into discrete
slots and restricts transmissions to slot boundaries, thereby reducing collision proba-
bility [21].
Despite its simplicity, ALOHA faces significant eﬀiciency limitations that directly
impact modern implementations like LoRaWAN networks. LoRaWAN relies on pure
ALOHA [24] for MAC, which creates substantial performance constraints as network
traﬀic increases. The theoretical maximum throughput of pure ALOHA reaches only
18.4% of total channel capacity, but practical LoRaWAN deployments achieve even
lower eﬀiciency due to packet capture effects and timing inaccuracies inherent in
real-world wireless environments. This fundamental limitation becomes increasingly
problematic as LoRaWAN networks scale and device density grows.
Listen Before Talk Protocol: LBT works by listening to a channel before
actually transmitting its message. This way it can avoid collisions and smartly choose
channels based on less activity. It is widely used as an alternative to the ALOHA
protocol for dense networks or regions where duty cycle is not allowed. It is often
paired up with Adaptive Frequency Agility (AFA) which means that it will keep
scanning for a free channel until it finally can transmit [39]. This means that LBT
consumes more power and is less simple in implementation compared to ALOHA.
Capture Effect: When two LoRa signals with the same spreading factor arrive at
a gateway simultaneously, the stronger signal may be successfully decoded if the power
difference exceeds approximately 5 dB [40]. This capture effect can improve network
throughput in scenarios with varying signal strengths but may create fairness issues
between devices. Research has shown that the capture effect allows for concurrent
reception of signals with different SFs, as they are orthogonal.
Collision Probability: The probability of packet collision depends on network
traﬀic density and transmission duration. Research has shown that longer transmis-
sions (higher SF) increase collision probability in dense networks, with collision rates
affecting overall network performance [41].
3.3.3.4
Duty Cycle Management and Compliance
Regional Regulations: LoRaWAN uses unlicensed radio bands, which are regulated
differently depending on the region. In Europe (EU868), most channels have a 1%
duty cycle limit.
This means a device can only transmit on a given channel for
1% of the time - around 36 seconds per hour. Some channels allow up to 10%. In
the United States of America (US915), there’s no strict duty cycle limit, but each
transmission must stay under a maximum dwell time (like 400 ms). In the AS923
region, a 1% duty cycle usually applies, but there may be exceptions depending on
the country [27,33,36].

3.3.4
Packet Reception
Packet reception in LoRaWAN involves sophisticated signal processing techniques
and protocol mechanisms to extract data from weak signals in noisy environments.
Understanding these processes is essential for optimizing network performance and
troubleshooting connectivity issues.
3.3.4.1
Signal Detection and Synchronization
Preamble Detection: LoRa receivers continuously monitor for preamble sequences
consisting of upchirps. The preamble detection algorithm calculates correlation be-
tween received signals and expected preamble patterns, with detection sensitivity
depending on the number of preamble symbols (typically 8-12) and the receiver’s
noise floor [31].

Symbol Synchronization: After preamble detection, receivers must synchro-

3.3.4.2
Signal Quality Metrics
Received Signal Strength Indicator: RSSI measures the total received power
within the configured LoRa channel bandwidth, including both signal and background
noise. RSSI is expressed in dBm and provides a coarse estimate of signal strength
at the antenna input. However, unlike SNR, it does not differentiate between the
desired signal and interference or thermal noise, making it a less reliable metric for
determining successful demodulation [30,33].
In LoRa devices, RSSI is typically computed internally by the radio chip (e.g.,
Semtech SX127x/SX126x series [44,45]) and is approximated using:
RSSI = Ptotal (dBm) = Psignal + Pnoise
(3.6)
where Psignal is the power of the desired signal and Pnoise is the combined back-
ground noise and interference within the receiver bandwidth. The actual calculation
may vary depending on the chip architecture, and some chips apply offset corrections
to align RSSI readings with real-world dBm values.
For example, in the Semtech SX1276 datasheet, RSSI is estimated as [45]:
RSSI (dBm) = −157 + RSSIraw
(for 125 kHz bandwidth)
(3.7)

3 Theory
Here, RSSIraw is a register value returned by the chip. The offset changes with
bandwidth; for wider bandwidths, different constants are used.
Because RSSI includes noise, a high RSSI does not necessarily imply a strong
usable signal—especially in noisy environments. Therefore, SNR is a more accurate
predictor of successful demodulation, particularly in LoRa systems where signal de-
coding is still possible well below the noise floor.
Signal-to-Noise Ratio: SNR is the critical metric for LoRa reception, repre-
senting the ratio between signal power and noise power, expressed in dB. Unlike
traditional modulation schemes, LoRa can demodulate signals even when the signal
power is below the noise floor, thanks to its significant processing gain. Research
shows that typical LoRa SNR values range between -20 dB and +10 dB, with success-
ful reception occurring at SNR values as low as -20 dB depending on the SF [30,33].
Different spreading factors correspond to different minimum required SNR values.
For instance, SF7 requires approximately -7.5 dB, SF10 requires -15 dB, and SF12 can
tolerate SNRs as low as -20 dB. The relationship between the received signal strength
and the ambient noise level is given by the following equation:
SNR (dB) = Preceived signal (dBm) −Pnoise (dBm)
(3.8)
Here, Preceived signal is the power of the received LoRa signal in dBm, and Pnoise is
the noise floor level, also in dBm. The noise floor represents the minimum detectable
signal level and is determined by thermal noise and receiver characteristics. It can be
approximated using:
Pnoise = −174 + 10 log10(B) + NF
(3.9)
where:
• −174 dBm/Hz is the thermal noise power density at room temperature,
• B is the bandwidth in Hz,
• NF is the receiver noise figure in dB.
For example, with a bandwidth of 125 kHz and a receiver noise figure of 6 dB, the
noise floor is:
Pnoise = −174 + 10 log10(125 000) + 6 ≈−111 dBm
(3.10)
Understanding and calculating SNR and noise floor is essential for evaluating
link performance, receiver sensitivity, and overall network reliability in LoRaWAN
deployments.
Link Quality Metrics: Modern LoRaWAN gateways provide additional quality
metrics including frequency error (indicating crystal oscillator accuracy), timing error
(showing symbol synchronization quality), and channel activity measurement.

3.3.4.3
Multi-Gateway Reception
Gateway Role: To communicate with a server, an end-device relies on a gateway
to act as an intermediary, capturing LoRa transmissions and forwarding them to the
server [46, 47]. This is where raw LoRa signals are converted into the LoRaWAN
protocol for network transmission. While we will discuss security in more detail later,
it’s important to note that once an end-device joins a network, LoRa packets are
already encrypted at the PHY layer, leaving little to no plaintext visible.
Diversity Reception: LoRaWAN’s architecture allows multiple gateways to
simultaneously receive the same packet [47], providing spatial diversity that improves
reliability. The network server selects the best quality reception or combines multiple
receptions to recover data that might be corrupted in individual receptions. Research
has demonstrated that antenna diversity techniques can significantly improve LoRa
reception performance, particularly for weak packets in challenging environments.
Gateway Selection Algorithms: Network servers use various criteria to select
the optimal gateway for message processing, including highest SNR or RSSI, best
long-term link quality statistics, geographic diversity for location services, and load
balancing across gateway infrastructure. Uplinks can be picked up by any gateway
and forwarded to the network server, whilst downlink typically will be sent via the
gateway that received the best reception during uplinks and not through multiple
gateways [46,47].
Packet Deduplication: When multiple gateways receive the same packet, the
network server must identify and deduplicate them using DevAddr, Frame Count
(FCnt), Message Integrity Code (MIC), and reception timestamp correlation.
3.3.4.4
Reception Challenges and Mitigation
Near-Far Problem: Strong signals from nearby devices can desensitize receivers,
making it diﬀicult to detect weak signals from distant devices [48]. This is particu-
larly problematic in dense urban deployments where gateway coverage areas overlap
significantly. Mitigation strategies include automatic gain control with wide dynamic
range, interference cancellation algorithms, multiple receiver paths with different gain
settings, and spatial separation of gateway antennas.
Interference Sources: LoRa operates in unlicensed ISM bands shared with
various other applications including WiFi (2.4 GHz band overlap in some regions),
Bluetooth and Zigbee devices, industrial scientific and medical equipment, other
LoRaWAN networks, and unintentional radiators from electronic devices [28]. Re-
search has shown that LoRa exhibits varying immunity to interference and multipath
propagation depending on the configuration parameters used.
Capture Effect Optimization: The capture effect allows stronger signals to
override weaker ones when transmitted simultaneously [40]. Network optimization
can leverage this effect through power control to create appropriate power differen-
tials, strategic gateway placement for optimal coverage, and scheduling algorithms to
minimize simultaneous transmissions.

3.3.5
Device Classes
The LoRaWAN specification defines three device classes: A, B, and C, with all devices
required to implement Class A. Classes B and C are extensions that enhance Class A
capabilities. Each class supports bi-directional communication (uplink and downlink)
[49].
1. Class A - Devices are the most power-eﬀicient, operating mostly in sleep mode
with long intervals between uplinks. After sending an uplink, they open two
short receive windows to listen for downlink messages. They have the highest
downlink latency, requiring an uplink before receiving any downlink. Use cases
include environmental monitoring, asset tracking, and smart parking.
2. Class B - Devices extend Class A by periodically opening receive windows
called ping slots, synchronized with network beacons. This reduces downlink
latency compared to Class A, but with higher power consumption. Class B
devices are suitable for use cases like utility meters and street lights.
3. Class C - Devices are always listening, keeping their receive windows open
except during uplinks, resulting in the lowest downlink latency. However, they
consume the most power and are typically mains-powered. They are ideal for
applications requiring instant downlink responses, such as utility meters and
alarms.
3.3.6
Security and Activation
LoRaWAN is a proprietary system owned by Semtech. However, despite this, its
specifications are fully public and open for scrutiny. This transparency allows for
thorough security evaluations without any guesswork. While the LoRa modulation
itself remains closed-source, making it diﬀicult to assess potential improvements on
that front, the LoRaWAN protocol is well-documented and accessible.
3.3.6.1
ABP vs. OTAA
The LoRaWAN specification defines two primary methods for connecting devices to
a network: Activation By Personalization (ABP) and Over-The-Air Acti-
vation (OTAA).
These methods differ in both their setup and network-joining
processes.
OTAA has become the preferred approach due to its advantages in security and
practicality. The key differences between ABP and OTAA include [10,50]:
1. Device Address Constraints – In an ABP setup, the device address is fixed,
meaning the device can only operate within a predefined network. OTAA, on
the other hand, dynamically assigns addresses, allowing greater flexibility.
==PAGE:37==
3.3 LoRaWAN
25
2. Session Security – ABP uses a fixed session configuration, which poses a
security risk. If an ABP session key is leaked, there is no simple way to update
it. In contrast, OTAA renegotiates session keys and frame counters at the start
of each new session, significantly improving security.
3. Frame Counter Management – Since ABP devices use hardcoded session
keys, their frame counters should never be reset. Resetting them can lead to
message rejection, effectively ending the session. OTAA avoids this issue by
generating new session keys for each activation.
Additionally, OTAA devices benefit from dynamically assigned network parame-
ters, whereas ABP devices rely on fixed parameters, further limiting their adaptabil-
ity.
3.3.6.2
Specification
LoRaWAN security [10, 51, 52] relies on a set of 128-bit encryption keys, using the
Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)-128 algorithm. The key system consists of
session keys, an application key, and frame counters to ensure secure communication.
Session keys are generated when a device joins a network.
These include the
Network Session Key (NwkSKey) and the Application Session Key (AppSKey). The
NwkSKey is shared with the network and ensures message integrity through a Message
Integrity Code (MIC), preventing tampering. The AppSKey, which remains private,
encrypts and decrypts message payloads, ensuring end-to-end confidentiality. Devices
using OTAA generate new session keys for each activation, whereas ABP devices use
fixed session keys, which pose a security risk if compromised.
The Application Key (AppKey) is known only to the device and the application.
In OTAA, it is used to derive the session keys during activation. Networks such as
The Things Network (TTN) allow either a default AppKey for all devices or custom
keys per device.
Frame counters (Frame Count Up (FCntUp) and Frame Count Down (FCntDown))
help prevent replay attacks by tracking message sequence numbers. If a message ar-
rives with a lower counter than the last recorded one, it is discarded. This mechanism
can cause issues in ABP devices since resetting the device also resets the counters,
leading to message rejections unless the device is re-registered.
LoRa uses Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) for data transmission. Originally de-
veloped for military communications, spread spectrum techniques such as Frequency
Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS) and Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS)
enhance signal robustness.
CSS provides benefits such as interference immunity,
eﬀicient channel sharing, and resistance to radio reflections, improving operational
reliability rather than serving as an anti-surveillance measure.

3.4
Jamming
Jamming, in essence, is quite simple. You have one device transmitting something on
a specific frequency, and then there is another device that tries to interfere with said
transmission by introducing additional signals, effectively creating collisions in the
radio waves that cannot be deciphered when they reach their destination. The term
initially came from radar jamming, which has been used as Electronic Countermeasure
(ECM) in electronic warfare since the invention of radar systems. Today, many devices
rely on wireless communication, which means that most every device is susceptible
to these kinds of jamming attacks [53]. In Section 4, will we discuss more of the
researched jamming attacks and countermeasures.
In the context of LoRa, the technology is particularly vulnerable to jamming due
to several inherent characteristics [54, 55]. LoRa operates in unlicensed ISM bands
with relatively low transmission power (typically 14-20 dBm), making legitimate sig-
nals weak and easier to overwhelm with interference [2]. The protocol uses predictable
frequency channels and lacks sophisticated anti-jamming measures like frequency hop-
ping, as implementing such features would contradict LoRa’s core design goals of low
cost, low power consumption, and simplicity [22].
Jamming attacks against LoRa can take various forms, including reactive jam-
ming [56, 57], where an attacker monitors the channel for legitimate transmissions
and immediately transmits interfering signals upon detecting activity. This approach
is more energy-eﬀicient than continuous jamming and harder to detect [58]. Attack-
ers often employ Gaussian noise, a type of random signal with a normal statistical
distribution. as it effectively disrupts the chirp spread spectrum modulation that
LoRa uses [59]. Since LoRa’s collision-prone ALOHA-based medium access doesn’t
include coordination between devices, even modest interference can significantly in-
crease collision rates and degrade network performance [4,55]. If not using ALOHA,
Listen Before Talk (LBT) is also an option for LoRaWAN networks, although still
vulnerable to reactive jamming [56].
3.4.1
Countermeasures
While LoRaWAN networks are inherently vulnerable to jamming attacks due to their
operation in unlicensed spectrum and relatively low transmission power, several coun-
termeasures could be implemented to mitigate these threats. These countermeasures
operate at different layers of the protocol stack and range from simple detection mech-
anisms to sophisticated adaptive techniques. In this section, some possible counter-
measures are proposed and which have been talked about. In Section 4.3.1 more
researched countermeasures are discussed.
The first line of defence against jamming is the ability to detect when it is occur-
ring. Several indicators can signal the presence of jamming attacks:
Signal Quality Monitoring involves continuous assessment of RSSI and SNR
values across the network. A sudden degradation in signal quality across multiple
==PAGE:39==
3.4 Jamming
27
devices or gateways may indicate jamming activity [55, 59].
Network servers can
establish baseline measurements and trigger alerts when values deviate significantly
from normal patterns. Employing strategies such as Listen Before Talk (LBT) may
help indicate the presence of jamming, especially if channels consistently appear noisy
when they are expected to be idle [56]. Furthermore, by modifying LBT to transmit
before listening, the approach could also be leveraged to detect reactive jammers.
Packet Loss Analysis tracks delivery rates and identifies unusual patterns [60].
While packet loss can occur naturally due to interference or poor coverage, jamming
typically produces distinctive patterns such as complete communication blackouts in
specific geographical areas or time periods, or synchronized packet loss across multiple
devices using the same spreading factor and frequency.
Duty Cycle Anomalies can also indicate jamming. Attackers may not respect
regional duty cycle regulations, leading to unusually high channel occupancy rates
that legitimate devices cannot achieve while remaining compliant.
3.4.1.1
Network-Level Countermeasures
Gateway Diversity is one of the most effective countermeasures against localized
jamming. By deploying multiple gateways with overlapping coverage areas, the net-
work can maintain connectivity even if one or more gateways are targeted [58]. This
approach leverages LoRaWAN’s inherent support for multiple gateway reception,
where packets from a single device can be received by several gateways simultane-
ously. Additionally, depending on the relative positions of the device and the jammer,
the device’s transmission to the gateway may be more reliable if the jammer is not
positioned between them.
Frequency Diversity utilizes the multiple channels available in each regional
frequency plan [27]. While LoRaWAN devices typically use all available channels
in a pseudo-random manner [52], network servers can implement intelligent channel
selection algorithms that avoid consistently jammed frequencies. This can be achieved
through adaptive channel hopping (effectively blacklisting), where frequently jammed
channels are temporarily removed from the available channel list [53].
3.4.1.2
Device-Level Countermeasures
Adaptive Transmission Timing can help devices avoid predictable jamming pat-
terns. Instead of using fixed transmission intervals, devices can implement random-
ized delays [61] or listen-before-talk mechanisms to identify clear channels before
transmitting [62]. However, this approach must comply with regional regulations and
device class constraints.
Spreading Factor Adaptation provides another layer of resilience. Different
spreading factors have varying susceptibility to jamming, with higher spreading fac-
tors generally offering better resistance due to their increased processing gain. Devices
can dynamically adjust the spreading factors based on observed jamming patterns,
though this must be balanced against the resulting changes in data rate and power
==PAGE:40==
28
3 Theory
consumption. The network can define frequency plans, but these are typically con-
strained by regional regulations [34, 36], making them less effective for countering
jamming - unless unregulated frequency plans are employed.
Power Control Mechanisms allow devices to increase transmission power when
interference is detected, up to regulatory limit [48]s. While this increases power con-
sumption, it can help overcome moderate jamming attempts and maintain connectiv-
ity during attacks. Similarly, the gateway could also increase its power output when
sending downlinks/ACKs.
Message Redundancy involves transmitting critical messages multiple times
using different channels, spreading factors, coding rates, or time slots. Possibly with
retries and awaiting acknowledgements. This increases the probability that at least
one transmission will successfully reach the network, though it also increases spectrum
usage, power consumption, and can flood the network with duplicate messages [32,37].
3.4.1.3
Advanced Techniques
Collaborative Jamming Detection leverages the distributed nature of LoRaWAN
networks. By correlating data from multiple devices and gateways, network servers
can build a comprehensive picture of jamming activity across the coverage area [63].
This information can be used to identify the likely location of jammers and coordinate
appropriate responses. In general this could also be used to find out if there are faulty
devices or bad placement of the device, because of bad transmissions.
Machine Learning Techniques are increasingly being employed to distinguish
between legitimate interference and malicious jamming [64, 65]. These systems can
learn normal network behaviour patterns and identify anomalies that may indicate
attacks. Features such as signal characteristics, temporal patterns, and geographical
distribution can be analysed to improve detection accuracy.
Spectrum Monitoring Infrastructure involves deploying dedicated spectrum
monitoring equipment [66] alongside LoRaWAN gateways. These devices can provide
detailed analysis of the radio environment, including identification of jamming signals
that may not conform to LoRa modulation patterns.
3.4.1.4
Trade-offs and Limitations
It’s important to acknowledge that perfect jamming resistance is not achievable in
LoRaWAN networks due to fundamental physical and regulatory constraints. If a
jammer does decided to bombard the used channels constantly with noise, it would
be diﬀicult to get legitimate messages through, although detecting such a jammer
would be trivial in turn.
All countermeasures involve trade-offs between security,
power consumption, network capacity, and cost. For instance, increasing transmission
power or message redundancy improves jamming resistance but reduces battery life
and increases spectrum usage.
Furthermore, sophisticated attackers may adapt their techniques to circumvent
implemented countermeasures.
Reactive jamming, where attackers only transmit

interference upon detecting legitimate signals, is particularly challenging to counter
as it minimizes the attacker’s detectability while maintaining effectiveness.
The most practical approach typically involves implementing multiple complemen-
tary countermeasures to create layered defence. This strategy increases the cost and
complexity for attackers while providing network operators with multiple options for
maintaining service during attacks.

4.1
Wireless Security Threats in Other Protocols
As the focus of this thesis lies in the detection and mitigation of such attacks, it is valu-
able to first consider how similar wireless-layer threats appear in other communication
protocols. Technologies such as Wi-Fi, Zigbee, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), and

4.1.1
Wi-Fi
Wi-Fi is a widely used wireless communication protocol based on the IEEE 802.11
standard, primarily designed for Local Area Network (LAN)s.
It operates in the
unlicensed 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz frequency bands, which are part of the Industrial,
Scientific, and Medical (ISM) radio spectrum [68]. To avoid transmission collisions
between devices, Wi-Fi uses a method called Sense Multiple Access with Collision
==PAGE:43==
4.1 Wireless Security Threats in Other Protocols
31
Avoidance (CSMA/CA), which checks whether the communication channel is free
before sending data [69].
In Wi-Fi, jamming typically disrupts communication by exploiting the physical or
Medium Access Control (MAC) layer. At the physical layer, an attacker may transmit
noise or structured signals on the same frequency as the legitimate transmission,
reducing the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) below a usable threshold. At the MAC
layer, jammers can interfere more selectively, for example by corrupting control frames
such as Request to Send (RTS), Clear to Send (CTS), or ACK packets, which are
critical for coordinating access to the channel. Since Wi-Fi uses CSMA/CA, even
brief interference can mislead devices into believing the medium is busy, effectively
causing a denial-of-service without constant transmission.
However, this reliance on a shared and open medium also makes Wi-Fi inher-
ently susceptible to interference. Despite its maturity, Wi-Fi (Wireless Local Area
Network (WLAN)) remains highly vulnerable to jamming attacks across both single-
and multichannel environments. Vadlamani et al. (2016) [70] present a taxonomic
survey categorizing jamming strategies such as constant, deceptive, reactive, and
random jammers, and show how reactive jamming in particular poses a significant
energy-eﬀicient threat. While Vadlamani et al. (2016) do not conduct original ex-
periments, they compile extensive evaluations of jamming attacks from the literature,
including constant and reactive jamming implemented on software-defined radios, as
well as MAC layer attacks like RTS/CTS spoofing.
To complement this broader taxonomy, Pelechrinis et al. (2011) [18] focus specifi-
cally on Denial of Service (DoS) attacks in Wi-Fi, offering a detailed analysis of jam-
ming techniques targeting the MAC layer. These include unfair channel acquisition,
virtual carrier sensing manipulation, and back-off window abuse - techniques that
exploit the very mechanisms intended to manage fair medium access in CSMA/CA-
based systems.
Foundational work by Xu et al. (2005) [20] and Tague et al. (2008) [71] further
demonstrates that even mature Wi-Fi systems are highly susceptible to selective and
intelligent interference, especially in multichannel environments. These findings estab-
lish the practical relevance of jamming threats and motivate continued development
of robust detection and mitigation strategies.
In summary, Wi-Fi’s reliance on a shared, contention-based channel access mech-
anism renders it particularly vulnerable to both physical and MAC layer jamming
attacks. While taxonomic overviews such as Vadlamani et al. (2016) outline a wide
spectrum of jamming strategies, works like Xu et al. (2005), Pelechrinis et al. (2011),
and Tague et al. (2008) demonstrate the real-world feasibility of both simple and in-
telligent jamming in practice. These insights underscore the need for robust, adaptive
defence mechanisms, many of which remain active areas of research.

4.1.2
Bluetooth
Bluetooth is a short-range wireless communication protocol operating in the 2.4 GHz
ISM band, typically used for point-to-point or small networked device interactions. It
is designed for low-power, low-data-rate applications such as peripheral connections,
audio transmission, and short-range data exchange. Bluetooth uses Frequency Hop-
ping Spread Spectrum (FHSS), where the transmitter and receiver switch frequencies
in a pseudo-random pattern over 79 different 1 MHz channels. This mechanism is in-
tended to improve resilience against interference and make eavesdropping or jamming
more diﬀicult [72].
However, Bluetooth is still vulnerable to targeted jamming attacks, particularly
when an adversary can predict or synchronize with the frequency hopping pattern.
By either following the hop pattern or flooding enough channels simultaneously, an
attacker can degrade communication quality or trigger connection loss.
Reactive
jamming is also possible if the attacker listens for bursts of activity and responds
with interference in real time. These forms of jamming are particularly relevant for
Bluetooth Classic (BR/EDR), but BLE is also susceptible due to its reduced hopping
range and advertising-based communication model [73].
While Vadlamani et al. (2016) [70] focus more extensively on WLAN and sensor
networks, their taxonomy does note FHSS as a defence mechanism, which Bluetooth
relies on. Still, as shown by studies like Xu et al. (2005) [20], frequency hopping alone
may not be suﬀicient against adaptive or reactive jammers. Additionally, Pelechri-
nis et al. (2011) [18] discuss how narrowband jamming in Bluetooth can be highly
effective when the attacker uses power-eﬀicient, selective targeting of active channels.
In summary, although Bluetooth incorporates anti-jamming principles at the phys-
ical layer, such as FHSS, it remains vulnerable to adversaries capable of exploiting
predictability or system limitations. These weaknesses are particularly concerning in
environments with high device density or critical applications (e.g., medical, indus-
trial), where communication disruptions can have cascading effects. With Bluetooth
forming a key component in many IoT systems, understanding its jamming vulner-
abilities helps further contextualize threats in wireless ecosystems before turning to
LoRaWAN-specific countermeasures.
4.1.3
Zigbee
Zigbee is a low-power, low-data-rate wireless protocol based on the IEEE 802.15.4
standard. It is commonly used in mesh-based IoT applications such as home automa-
tion, industrial monitoring, and sensor networks. Like Wi-Fi and Bluetooth, Zigbee
operates in the unlicensed 2.4 GHz ISM band, although regional variants also use
sub-GHz bands.
Zigbee employs Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS) along
with CSMA/CA to manage channel access and reduce interference [53].
Despite these mechanisms, Zigbee remains vulnerable to both physical- and MAC
layer jamming. Because it uses fixed channels and relatively low transmission power,

even low-energy constant or reactive jamming can cause significant disruption. At-
tackers can target control packets such as beacons, acknowledgments, or route dis-
covery frames to destabilize the network. In mesh configurations, jamming a few key
nodes or links can result in route failures or degraded performance throughout the
system.
López‑Vilos et al. (2021) [74] examine jamming in IEEE 802.15.4, specifically
analysing how both constant and reactive jamming disrupt CSMA/CA-based commu-

degrade Packet Reception Rates (PRR) and goodput—and that reactive jammers
consume far less energy than continuous jammers while inflicting similar or greater

In summary, Zigbee’s design choices for low-power, low-throughput communica-
tion make it a strong fit for distributed sensing applications—but also create avenues
for targeted jamming. Its use of a small number of channels and broadcast-based
discovery procedures exposes it to both denial-of-service and network partitioning
attacks. Understanding these vulnerabilities reinforces the need for context-aware,
protocol-specific defences across wireless ecosystems, including LoRaWAN.
4.2
Countermeasures in Other Wireless Networks
Although Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and Zigbee differ in their design and purpose, they all rely
on unlicensed radio spectrum and shared channel access. This makes them vulnerable
to jamming attacks. Over time, researchers have proposed different ways to detect,
reduce, or work around jamming in these technologies.
One common strategy is jamming detection. In Wi-Fi, this can involve monitoring
for unusual drops in packet delivery, sudden changes in signal strength, or long delays
in accessing the channel [20]. Similar detection methods are used in Bluetooth and
Zigbee, such as looking for failed connections, high retransmission rates, or irregular
timing in communication.
Another set of defences focuses on changing the transmission frequency. Bluetooth
uses built-in FHSS, which switches channels quickly to avoid interference. However,
as mentioned earlier, this is not always enough against reactive or wideband jammers.
Wi-Fi includes Dynamic Frequency Selection (DFS) in some versions (e.g., IEEE
802.11h), and Zigbee devices can re-select channels when jamming is detected.
More advanced defences take place at the MAC layer. For example, Wi-Fi can
use randomised back-off times, padded frames to hide timing patterns, or adaptive
sleep cycles. These changes make it harder for an attacker to predict when and where
to jam [18] [20]. In Zigbee mesh networks, devices can also reroute traﬀic to avoid
jammed parts of the network.
Some countermeasures combine signals from multiple layers of the protocol. These
cross-layer methods may use machine learning to detect unusual patterns or allow

devices to share jamming alerts with each other. This creates a bigger picture of
what is happening and helps improve detection and response.
In summary, no single method can fully prevent jamming. However, combining
detection and defence techniques at different layers of the system has proven effective
in improving security for wireless networks. These lessons are useful when thinking
about how to protect LoRaWAN, which faces similar risks but operates under stricter
power and bandwidth limits.
4.3
Security Threats in LoRaWAN
LoRaWAN is increasingly used in critical IoT applications, such as smart cities, agri-
culture, and industrial monitoring. As a result, understanding its security posture
has become more important. While the LoRaWAN specification includes mechanisms
for end-to-end encryption, message integrity, and mutual authentication, the protocol
remains exposed to several key threats. These arise both from its design constraints
such as limited bandwidth, low duty cycle, and fixed channel parameters and from
its reliance on unlicensed spectrum.
Among the most prominent and practical threats is jamming. However, LoRaWAN
is also susceptible to other types of attacks, including replay attacks, where previously
captured packets are resent to the network in an attempt to exploit frame counter
weaknesses or trigger unnecessary responses from constrained devices.
This section outlines the primary wireless-layer threats in LoRaWAN, with a
particular focus on jamming, which is examined both conceptually by performing a
literature review in the following subsection.
4.3.1
Jamming
Jamming in LoRaWAN, as discussed in Section 3.4, refers to Physical (PHY) layer
interference where an adversary transmits overlapping signals to disrupt legitimate
communication. Jensen et al. [9] showed that jamming LoRaWAN is possible using
low-cost hardware such as ESP32 boards and HackRF One devices. Their experi-
ments included single-channel jamming, Gaussian noise, and reactive jamming. They
found that even users with limited technical skills could completely block LoRaWAN
communication. Reactive jamming was especially effective, achieving a 100% block
rate while using much less energy than constant jamming.
Bertelsen [66] focused on how such attacks could be detected.
Using SODAQ
Explorer nodes, an ESP32-based jammer, and a Dragino gateway, they tested how
jamming affects metrics like Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI), SNR, FPort,
and frame counters. The study found that no single metric was reliable on its own.
Instead, multiple indicators had to be analysed together. Tools like Power BI and
Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)-based spectrum analysis helped visualise and confirm
interference after it happened
==PAGE:47==
4.4 Countermeasures in LoRaWAN
35
These results show that LoRaWAN is vulnerable to jamming, even with its energy-
eﬀicient and long-range design. Because attackers can use cheap, simple tools to cause
serious disruption, it is important to explore countermeasures.
4.4
Countermeasures in LoRaWAN
Given LoRaWAN’s susceptibility to jamming attacks, researchers have proposed a
range of countermeasures to improve network resilience.
These approaches target
different layers of the protocol stack and vary in complexity, effectiveness, and com-
patibility with LoRaWAN’s low-power, low-bandwidth constraints.
4.4.1
Signal-Based Detection and Classification
Several studies propose detecting jamming by monitoring changes in signal-level met-
rics such as RSSI and SNR. For instance, Bertelsen [66] demonstrated that sharp
or sustained deviations in these values may indicate interference. However, relying
on any single metric can lead to false positives. For example, RSSI may rise due to
legitimate transmissions or environmental noise. Therefore, multi-metric correlation
and statistical trend analysis are often recommended to improve detection accuracy.
4.4.2
Frame Counter and Metadata Analysis
Another class of countermeasures focuses on analysing LoRaWAN metadata, such as
frame counters and port numbers. Because jamming often causes packet loss or forces
retransmissions, irregularities in frame counter progression can serve as indicators of
interference. Kalokidou et al. [19] suggest combining frame counter patterns with end-
device behaviour to distinguish between natural packet loss and targeted disruption.
However, these methods require access to gateway-side logs or backend platforms
capable of tracking per-device state.
4.4.3
Spectrum Monitoring and Post-Processing
Hou et al. [59] present a reactive detection framework that uses spectrum monitoring
and FFT analysis to visualise and classify jamming signals. Their approach identifies
unique energy patterns across frequency plans to detect both wideband and narrow-
band jammers.
While this method offers high detection accuracy, it depends on
spectrum sensing capabilities not typically found in standard LoRaWAN gateways.
==PAGE:48==
36
4 Related Work
4.4.4
MAC and PHY Layer Adaptations
Some countermeasures adapt LoRaWAN’s communication behaviour to make jam-
ming less effective.
For example, Adaptive Data Rate (ADR) can be disabled to
prevent predictable modulation patterns.
Additionally, frequency hopping or dy-
namic channel allocation, though not natively supported in the LoRaWAN spec, can
be implemented in customised stacks to increase unpredictability. Jensen et al. [9]
suggest that even basic channel rotation or randomisation can lower jamming success
rates, particularly for reactive jammers.
4.4.5
Redundancy and Multi-Gateway Deployments
Network-level strategies such as deploying multiple gateways or overlapping coverage
zones can reduce the impact of localised jamming. If one gateway experiences interfer-
ence, others may still receive the same uplink packet due to LoRaWAN’s star-of-stars
topology. Studies like Bertelsen [66] and Dossa et al. [57] highlight how redundancy
in coverage improves packet reception rates during attacks and enables better trian-
gulation of interference sources.
4.4.6
Limitations and Trade-offs
Despite promising results, many of these countermeasures come with trade-offs. Signal-
based detection can be noisy, spectrum sensing may be expensive or impractical, and
proactive methods like frequency agility may break LoRaWAN compatibility. More-
over, some defences increase energy usage or backend complexity, which may be
unacceptable in low-power or cost-sensitive deployments.
In summary, while LoRaWAN faces real and demonstrated jamming threats, a
combination of lightweight detection, backend analysis, and selective protocol adapta-
tions can improve resilience. Future work may focus on standardising these defences
or integrating them more seamlessly into LoRaWAN-compatible hardware and net-
work servers.

Approach
Since this thesis focuses on the detection and mitigation of Denial of Service (DoS)

5.1.1
Jamming
To conduct meaningful detection and mitigation experiments, we implemented two
types of jamming strategies: a static-spreading-factor jammer and a dynamic jammer.
The static-spreading-factor jammer maintains a constant spreading factor (e.g.,
SF9) while sequentially sweeping across all EU868 uplink frequencies.
From this
point onward, this strategy will be referred to as the static jammer. This approach
provides wide coverage over the LoRaWAN band with consistent demodulation char-
acteristics, making it well-suited for baseline interference studies.
The dynamic jammer, by contrast, operates in cycles. It begins by sweeping all
uplink frequencies using SF9, then repeats the same process with SF10, SF11, and
SF12. After completing a full cycle, it restarts at SF9. This approach more closely
resembles an adaptive adversary and allows us to explore the effectiveness of detection
strategies against a broader range of interference patterns.
5.1.2

Detecting such attacks is not trivial, as disruptions may stem from a range of issues
including server outages, hardware malfunctions, excessive environmental noise, or
deliberate jamming.

The LilyGO LoRa ESP32 development board was used to carry out jamming

disrupting the reception of valid packets at the gateway.

Evaluation Criteria
To assess the effectiveness of our proposed jamming detection and mitigation strate-

Experimental Scenarios

2. Attacked, Unprotected: Jamming enabled, no detection or mitigation.
3. Detection/Mitigation: Jamming enabled, with detection and mitigation ac-

by timing-related factors, such as gaps in jamming activity during the probe or slight
delays in jammer activation. A more aggressive jammer or tighter timing between
probe and transmission might have worsened performance further.
Nonetheless, the strategy still outperformed both the Standard and LBT strategies
under static jamming. This resulted in a message delivery ratio of 35%, correspond-

improvement over the Standard strategy (31%).

The Retry strategy, while less adaptive, demonstrated solid resilience through its

For dynamic jamming, most strategies still achieved respectable delivery rates

to pre-emptively detect potential interference, intentionally triggering the dynamic
jammer before actual data transmission. This behaviour effectively buys time, as the
jammer then must cycle through all frequencies and spreading factors (9–12), allowing
the legitimate transmission a better chance to succeed.
6.3.6.2
Detection Accuracy
To reliably detect transmission failures, it’s crucial to identify both false positives and
true negatives. False positives occur when the device mistakenly assumes a message
was successfully received, while true negatives indicate that the device correctly rec-
ognized a failed transmission. When a device knows that a message did not arrive, it
can infer that interference, such as jamming, is likely the cause.

Mitigation Accuracy
To truly assess whether a strategy is effectively mitigating jamming, it’s not enough
to look solely at how often messages are successfully delivered (as shown in Section
6.3.6.1). One must also verify whether the device is aware of the delivery outcome.
This is where true positives become important. In case a device doesn’t recognize
a failed delivery, any success might simply be due to chance rather than strategic
resilience.
Figure 6.4 shows that under no jamming, both the Standard and PALBT strate-

delivery rates. However, when jamming is introduced, the picture changes signifi-
cantly. As shown in Figure 6.2 and discussed in Section 6.3, some strategies exhibit
more false positives or identify transmission failures through true negatives. This
distinction matters: to actively mitigate jamming, a strategy must not only maintain
delivery rates but also detect when delivery fails. Only then can it take corrective
actions such as retries or frequency changes.
Based on the combination of true positives and true negatives, the Retry and
Dynamic strategies stand out.

experiment was capped at sending 51 messages regardless of whether they were suc-

Unlike device-side detection mechanisms, this server-side approach focused on analysing
message statistics over time to identify patterns of abnormal behaviour. The system
aggregated metrics across fixed windows of up to 50 messages per device and stored
the results in CSV files for later analysis. This enabled temporal tracking of network
behaviour and allowed comparison between normal and potentially disrupted con-
ditions, where manual triggers were possible to save the data, when for example 50
messages were received from TTN and calling the endpoint /save on the microservice.
In our tests, we followed a structured evaluation flow, starting with No Jamming
(used as a baseline for normal behaviour), followed by static Jamming and then
dynamic Jamming, after which we saved the data per execution.
We also had a
singular timestamp with 1 message, as the end devices send 51 messages and not 50
at a time.
The server recorded timestamps for each 50-message window, allowing us to ob-
serve how performance evolved across different conditions. As shown in Figure 6.8,
all messages were successfully received during the initial (baseline) period. However,
subsequent windows revealed varying degrees of message loss - clear indicators of
potential interference or jamming activity.

Discussion
The Long Range Wide Area Network (LoRaWAN) specification imposes significant
constraints on device behaviour, especially concerning duty cycles, coding rates, and
acknowledgment timing.
Throughout this thesis, some proposed mitigation tech-
niques, such as dynamic coding rate adjustment or Listen Before Talk (LBT), con-
flicted with these constraints. For instance, modifying coding rates dynamically on
the SODAQ device was infeasible due to hardware and backend restrictions (e.g., The
Things Network (TTN) requiring CR 4/5), limiting practical applicability. Similarly,
LBT proved ineffective because the built-in radio API did not provide general chan-
nel noise measurements, as it only detected valid LoRaWAN messages. This raises
a broader question: should effective anti-jamming strategies remain fully compliant
with the LoRaWAN specification, or is deviation acceptable if it improves resilience.
In practice, real-world deployments must strike a balance. While full compliance
ensures interoperability and certification, localized deviations may be justified in high-
risk environments where reliability is critical. For instance, the Probe Aware Listen
Before Talk (PALBT) strategy-although it introduces additional overhead-could still
be viable, as it uses preambles to sense interference more proactively. Likewise, Retry
and Dynamic strategies might be preferred in networks where message loss is unaccept-
able, as they can detect the absence of acknowledgments and initiate retransmissions.
However, acknowledgment-based strategies are not without limitations. If a down-
link ACK is jammed, devices may incorrectly assume the transmission failed, leading
to unnecessary retries and increased airtime usage. This underlines a key limitation
of relying solely on ACKs to assess communication success. A more robust approach
could involve hybrid intelligence, where both the server and the end-device share
responsibility for reliability decisions-without violating protocol constraints.
During evaluation, strategies like LBT and PALBT underperformed significantly
under static jamming conditions. In the case of LBT, the system listens before trans-
mitting, but because it cannot detect general interference (only valid LoRa packets),
it fails to recognize that the channel is jammed, leading to wasted transmissions.
Both with LBT and PALBT, performance was better under dynamic jamming but
still degraded under static conditions. This likely stems from how they samples RSSI
and SNR values, which may be done at suboptimal times or in a way that fails to
reflect actual jamming. It also highlights that raw signal strength alone might not be
enough to detect interference.
To improve detection, future strategies could use simple frequency-based analysis

like Fourier Transforms. This technique breaks a signal into its frequency components
and can reveal hidden patterns, such as repeated bursts from reactive jammers or

A similar approach could help PALBT

could better tell the difference between actual jamming and normal background noise,
without adding too much complexity.
Finally, the dynamic jammer’s behaviour suggests that adversarial models could
be improved further. Even basic jamming cycles can cause considerable disruption
unless detection strategies become more adaptive. Smarter jammers might prioritize
certain time windows or channels based on observed behaviour, which means defences
must evolve in parallel.
6.4
Further Enhancements
Building on insights from our evaluation, several enhancements can improve the ro-
bustness and flexibility of tested systems. On the Heltec device, increasing the number
of probe packets per detection cycle may help PALBT gather more reliable interfer-
ence metrics-though this comes at the cost of energy and airtime. Adjusting when
and how these probes are transmitted (e.g., synchronised with known idle periods or
bursts) could also reduce missed detections.
For the SODAQ platform, lack of low-level radio control remains a limitation.
Using more configurable platforms like the Heltec or STM32-based boards would pro-
vide better access to coding rate and sensing logic. Similarly, switching to alternative
backend infrastructure (e.g., ChirpStack) could facilitate more dynamic adaptations
and gateway-level logging to support smarter mitigation strategies.
The modular strategy codebase for SODAQ enables not only new strategies but
also combinations of existing ones. Future research could explore compound strategies-
e.g., combining Retry with adaptive spreading factors, or PALBT with acknowledg-
ment feedback-to reduce false positives and increase resilience. Such combinations
might offer a stronger defence than any single technique alone.
For the gateway, one potential improvement involves increasing the transmission
power of acknowledgements or downlink messages in general. This could enhance
the likelihood of successful reception by end-devices, particularly those located at
the edge of the gateway’s coverage area or equipped with small, low-gain antennas.
In such cases, weak signal strength may prevent the device from reliably receiving
downlinks. Additionally, in scenarios where a jammer is present, it is possible for the
uplink message to reach the gateway successfully while the corresponding acknowl-
edgement is blocked or disrupted. This would cause the end-device to mistakenly
assume that the message was lost. By boosting the gateway’s transmission power,

these challenges could potentially be mitigated, improving overall communication re-
liability. This approach warrants further investigation, especially in environments

The jammer itself could also be improved. Instead of sweeping all frequencies

most likely to be in use-perhaps based on prior knowledge or real-time learning. The
dynamic jammer’s latency when switching channels or SFs could also be reduced to

Finally, future work should test a broader set of spreading factors and consider
temporarily disabling duty cycle enforcement (where legally permitted) to evaluate
worst-case performance under repeated transmissions. More advanced defences could
include cooperative jamming detection, where devices or gateways share observations
to localize interference and respond accordingly.
By continuously building on these insights-from the limits of spec compliance to
smarter detection heuristics and jamming strategies-we can better understand how to
defend LoRaWAN networks against both basic and advanced threats, ensuring robust
performance in even the most constrained environments.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the feasibility of Denial of Service (DoS) attacks on LoRaWAN
networks and evaluated practical strategies to detect and mitigate their impact, using
real-world hardware and controlled experiments. The study began by addressing the
fundamental research questions related to the nature of DoS attacks in LoRaWAN,
their detection using embedded or server-side mechanisms, and the effectiveness of
various mitigation strategies.
Research Questions Revisited
• What types of DoS attacks target LoRaWAN, and how do they im-
pact network performance?
The study confirmed that both static and dynamic jamming attacks can signif-
icantly degrade message delivery rates in LoRaWAN networks. Static jammers
cause consistent disruption, while dynamic jammers adapt their interference
patterns, making detection and avoidance more complex.
• How can a DoS attack be executed at the LoRaWAN network layer,
and what existing countermeasures can be implemented?
Using low-cost, commercially available hardware, jamming attacks were exe-
cuted through broadcast interference. Countermeasures including Retry, Dy-
namic Spreading Factor adaptation, Listen Before Talk (LBT), and Probe
Aware Listen Before Talk (PALBT) were implemented and evaluated.
• Can missing acknowledgements reliably indicate a DoS attack?
While missing ACKs can indicate potential interference, they are not definitive
indicators of malicious activity. However, they are practical and lightweight for
initiating fallback mechanisms like retries.
• Can LBT or abnormal packets serve as indicators of DoS?
LBT showed limited utility under reactive jamming, where-as with anomaly
detection of packet patterns it proved to be more effective, especially in server-
side implementations.
• How effective are the evaluated mitigation strategies?
The evaluation showed varied performance depending on the attack type:

Summary and Wrap-up
This thesis demonstrated the practicality of launching jamming attacks on LoRaWAN
systems using inexpensive hardware and also showcased the effectiveness of device-side
and server-side mitigation strategies. A Flask-based uplink monitoring microservice
was developed to enhance detection from the network backend.
The main contributions of this work are:
• A comprehensive, hardware-based experimental framework for evaluating LoRaWAN
under jamming conditions.
• Implementation and performance comparison of 4 detection and mitigation
strategies and one baseline.
• A detection system combining device behaviour and server-side monitoring.
Final Thoughts
LoRaWAN is a key enabler for critical Internet of Things (IoT) applications, yet it
remains vulnerable to physical-layer attacks due to its open nature and reliance on un-
licensed spectrum. This thesis provides practical insights into both the vulnerabilities
and the possibilities for securing LoRaWAN systems against jamming.
While limitations in hardware configurability and protocol compliance presented
some challenges, they also point to promising directions for future research: smarter
adversary models, cross-layer mitigation strategies, and collaborative defence mecha-
nisms across gateways and devices.
In conclusion, the detection and mitigation of DoS attacks in LoRaWAN is not
only feasible but also critical for ensuring the resilience of future IoT infrastructures.
This work lays the foundation for building more robust, adaptive, and secure low-
power wide-area networks.
