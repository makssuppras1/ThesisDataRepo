Abstract 
Anaerobic digestion is one of the most energy-efficient and environmentally beneficial 
technologies, which can reduce the extent of dependence on fossil fuels and eventually 
decrease GHG emissions. However, some of the most commonly used substrates 
contain high amounts of ammonia, which can be proven inhibitory for anaerobic 
digestion in full-scale reactors. A great number of assays have been accomplished, 
assessing the inhibition caused by ammonia. In the majority of these studies ammonium 
chloride was used as an ammonia source. 
In the present study, the impact of two basic ammonia sources (i.e. ammonium chloride 
and urea) was investigated on four different methanogenic strains. Moreover, 
hydrolysis of urea under the presence or absence of enzyme as well as the pH variation 
after the hydrolysis of urea or the dissolution of ammonium chloride were tested. 
Therefore, several sets of batch reactors were applied to different levels of ammonia 
and pH. 
The results clearly showed that urea was statistically a more inhibitory source (P > 0.05) 
than ammonium chloride regardless the tested strain, while M. barkeri and M. 
bourgensis were the most ammonia sensitive and tolerant strains respectively. In 
addition, the importance of the urease enzyme during hydrolysis reaction was 
confirmed, since the absence of enzyme led to a conversion no higher than 20%, while 
the presence of urease led to a hydrolysis percentage of at least 80%. Finally, pH almost 
reached the value of 9 after the hydrolysis of urea, while there was a pH decrease of 0.3 
units after dissociation of ammonium chloride, confirming the significant influence of 
urea’s hydrolysis in the weak buffer systems. 
These findings underline the fact that a further investigation on the different ammonia 
inhibition mechanisms should be implemented.

1 Introduction 
Nowadays, an issue of major concern is the problem of the greenhouse gasses (GHG) 
and the related climate change, caused by the increasing temperature on the planet. The 
impacts from climate change could be harsh, such as heat waves, droughts, floods, 
cyclones, and wildfires, causing major hazard to societies and ecosystems exposed to 
these extreme phenomena (Field et al., 2014). It is clear that consumption of fossil fuels 
is the main source of GHG emissions and the need of promoting renewable energy 
sources (RES) such as biomass, wind, solar, hydropower, and geothermal is of crucial 
importance (Herzog et al., 2001). 
Currently, 14% of the total world energy demand is supplied by RES, with this share to 
be expected to increase significantly during the next 20 years due to technological 
development and socio-political awareness (Panwar et al., 2011). One of the most 
energy-efficient and environmentally beneficial technologies is bioenergy production, 
which can diminish the use of fossil fuels. Unlike solar or wind, which are variable 
sources of energy solely dependent on weather conditions, anaerobic digestion can 
produce a stable amount of energy utilizing waste as feedstock and reducing their 
organic load (Weiland, 2010; Lakatos et al., 2011). Among the waste materials that can 
be used as feedstock are organic waste from households and the food industry, energy 
crops, agricultural waste by-products such as crop residues and manure and waste 
activated sludges (Panwar et al., 2011). 
1.1 Anaerobic digestion 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a multistep process, in which biodegradable organic matter 
is anaerobically degraded by microorganisms under mesophilic or thermophilic 
conditions. The end-product of this process is biogas, composed mainly of methane and 
carbon dioxide, while the by-product is called digestate (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2010). 
The four steps of AD are hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and methanogenesis 
and are summarized in Figure 1.

1.1.1 Hydrolysis 
Hydrolysis process involves the depolymerization of complex organic polymers into 
soluble oligomers (Angelidaki et al., 2009). The three main compounds that can be 
hydrolysed to dissolved substrates, such as monosaccharides, amino acids, long-chain 
fatty acids (LCFA) and glycerol are carbohydrates, proteins and lipids. The degradation 
of these insoluble complex materials is conducted extracellular by enzymatic action of 
different groups of fermentative bacteria (Angelidaki et al., 2011). Cellulases and 
xylanases hydrolyse cellulose and xylan (polysaccharides) to their monosaccharides, 
glucose and xylose respectively. Moreover, proteases degrade proteins to amino acids 
whereas lipases catalyse the hydrolysis of lipids to glycerol and LCFA (Neurath & 
Walsh, 1976; Ahring et al., 1996; Svendsen, 2000).

==PAGE:11==
1.1.2 Acidogenesis 
Acidogenesis is an intracellular process, in which fermentative bacteria are absorbing 
the monosaccharides, the amino acids and the glycerol produced during hydrolysis step 
and convert them into volatile fatty acids (VFA), hydrogen, alcohols and carbon dioxide 
(Angelidaki et al., 2009). On the contrary, LCFA cannot be utilized by fermentative 
bacteria and transformed into VFA (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2010). Acidogenesis is a 
quick and high energy yield process, and when system runs under stable operating 
conditions the main part of organic substrate is transformed to acetate, hydrogen and 
carbon dioxide. However, under inhibited system operation higher concentrations of 
propionate, butyrate and ethanol are produced (Angelidaki et al., 2011). This happens 
due to the fact that acidogenesis process is following branched metabolism and is able 
to change metabolic pathway in case of high hydrogen partial pressure or extreme pH 
level (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2010). 
1.1.3 Acetogenesis 
Acetogenesis process involves the anaerobic oxidation of VFA and alcohols, produced 
during acidogenesis, alongside with LCFA, produced during hydrolysis step, to acetate 
(Angelidaki & Batstone, 2010). The degradation of the organic acids and alcohols is 
conducted by obligate hydrogen producing acetogens. However, hydrogen, which is 
concentrated from the oxidation, renders the hydrogen-releasing reactions energetically 
unfavourable (ΔG0>0) as can be seen from the Table 1 (Angelidaki et al., 2011).

1.1.4 Methanogenesis 
The final step of anaerobic digestion is methanogenesis, in which methanogenic archaea 
generate methane from acetate, hydrogen and carbon dioxide, formate and alcohols 
following three distinct metabolic pathways; aceticlastic, hydrogenotrophic and 
methylotrophic methanogenesis. In the first acetate is transformed into methane and 
carbon dioxide (Reaction A), in the second hydrogen and carbon dioxide is converted 
into methane (Reaction C), whereas in the third methyl-group compounds are reduced 
to methane (Reaction E) (Angelidaki et al., 2011).

Methanogenesis from acetate follows two different pathways; the direct acetate 
transformation to methane and carbon dioxide (Reaction A) and the syntrophic acetate 
oxidation, consisting of a two-step reaction, where acetate is oxidized to hydrogen and 
carbon dioxide and subsequently converted to methane (Reactions B & C). The reason 
for the two-step process is that Reaction B by itself is energetically unfavourable 
(ΔG0>0) and therefore hydrogenotrophic archaea reduce hydrogen partial pressure 
through interspecies hydrogen transfer (Fotidis et al., 2013).

Even though 60-70% of the biologically generated methane derives from acetate, 
Methanosarcinales is the only order where aceticlastic methanogens, such as 
Methanosarcinaceae spp. and Methanosaetaceae spp., can be located (Wang et al., 
2015). It is worth mentioning that Methanosaetaceae spp. is a superior acetate utilizer 
in comparison to Methanosarcinaceae spp. and is capable of using acetate at 
concentrations 5–20 μM, while Methanosarcinaceae spp. requires concentrations 
higher than 1mM (Karakashev et al., 2006; Liu & Whitman, 2008). Thereafter, 
Methanosaetaceae spp. was observed to be most abundant in the seed sludge, but the 
population declined when acetate concentration increased with a simultaneous increase 
in Methanosarcinaceae spp. (Demirel & Scherer, 2008). The remaining 30-40% of the 
methane comes from the other methanogenic substrates like hydrogen and carbon 
dioxide, formate and methanol. 
Among all steps of anaerobic digestion process, methanogenesis is proved to be the 
most sensitive to a group of inhibitory factors, which will be discussed afterwards, and 
therefore is considered to be the rate-limiting stage of the whole anaerobic digestion 
process (Wang et al., 2015). 
1.2 Factors affecting anaerobic digestion process 
The overall performance of an anaerobic digester is affected by several physical and 
biochemical parameters such as the nature of the substrate, nutrients, temperature and 
pH. 
1.2.1 Substrate 
The type of substrates used in anaerobic digestion includes livestock manure, food 
waste, municipal solid waste, lignocellulosic waste (agricultural by-products) and waste 
activated sludge. The main factor affecting biogas yield is the kind of substrate used; 
the volatile solids (VS) concentration in the substrate and its composition (Table ) 
(Mata-Alvarez et al., 2000).

1.2.2 Nutrients 
The amount of macronutrients required for anaerobic biological treatment processes is 
much lower in comparison with the requirements for aerobic biological treatment 
processes. This exists due to lower cell yield compared with aerobic processes under 
the degradation of equal quantities of substrate (Gerardi, 2003). 
Two of the most important macronutrients are nitrogen and phosphorus, which become 
accessible to anaerobic bacteria in the soluble form of ammonium nitrogen (NH4+-N) 
and orthophosphate phosphorus (HPO4--P) respectively. Nitrogen is one of the main 
ingredients in organic materials alongside with hydrogen, oxygen and carbon, whereas 
phosphorus is structural element of DNA and RNA (Gerardi, 2003). The amount of 
nitrogen and phosphorus in the digester should be determined from the amount of COD 
in feedstock. More specifically, for high strength feedstocks ratio COD:N:P of 1000:7:1 
should be used, while for low organic content feedstocks the 350:7:1 (Gerardi, 2003). 
Nevertheless, the optimum C/N ratio has been reported to be 25 and 30 at 35°C and 
55°C, respectively. In case of a higher ratio, insufficient nitrogen may be provided to 
the system to maintain the cell biomass, while lower ratio may cause ammonia 
inhibition to methanogenesis process (Mao et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2014). Other 
nutrients such as calcium, magnesium, and potassium are necessary co-factors for 
enzyme activity and finally sulphur is required for synthesis of amino acids (Angelidaki 
& Batstone, 2010). 
In addition to the macronutrients, methane-forming bacteria have also micronutrient 
requirements. Nickel and cobalt are the most important for growth of anaerobic 
organisms, since they activate enzyme systems in methane-forming bacteria 
(Angelidaki & Batstone, 2010). Finally, iron facilitates the degradation of organic 
matter (Mao, et al., 2015). It is worth mentioning that the forenamed micronutrients are 
usually present in sufficient quantities in domestic animal and manure waste as well as 
municipal wastewaters (Gerardi, 2003).

Even though methane production occurs in such a wide temperature range, most 
reactors operate at either mesophilic or thermophilic temperatures, with optimum at 
37°C and 55°C respectively, since the majority of methane-forming microorganisms 
are mesophiles and thermophiles (Gerardi, 2003). Between these two reactors, the 
thermophilic one may have an increased microbial activity comparing with the 
mesophilic. This allows thermophilic reactors to operate under lower hydraulic 
retention times or with higher organic loading without compromising their 
performance. However, thermophilic digesters have a higher demand of operational 
energy for heat exchange, are more susceptible to free ammonia inhibition and 
demonstrate a larger degree of temperature instability (Leven et al., 2007). 
Temperature is one of the most important factors affecting microbial activity and any 
sudden change is capable of causing an irreversible damage to the whole anaerobic 
digestion process. On the one hand, even a small drop in temperature of five degrees 
may reduce biogas production rate by 14-17% (Chae et al., 2008). On the other hand, 
temperature’s increase above the methanogens cardinal curve might cease methane-
forming bacterial activity. At the same time, hydrolysis rate remains stable, as 
hydrolytic bacteria are not sensitive to temperature variations, leading to an organic 
overload (Gerardi, 2003).

1.2.4 pH and alkalinity 
The optimum pH varies between the different microbial groups. Methanogenesis 
process can be accomplished in a pH range between 6 and 8.5, with most methanogens 
to be neutrophiles and have a pH optimum around 7 (Angelidaki et al., 2011). The 
growth rate of methanogens could be greatly reduced below pH values of 6.6, whereas 
an excessively alkaline pH can lead to an increase of free ammonia concentration, which 
is toxic for methanogens (Ward et al., 2008). Furthermore, hydrolytic enzyme activity 
has been reported to be the highest at pH 6, while the optimum pH of acidogenesis 
process was between pH 5.5 and 6.5 (Mao et al., 2015). Taking into consideration all 
the above, it is concluded that the ideal pH range for anaerobic digestion is very narrow; 
between 6.8 and 7.2. 
Georgacakis et al. (1982) reported that the pH is a result of the equilibrium between the 
three primary buffers of VFA, bicarbonate and ammonia. When VFA concentration 
increases the reaction with the bicarbonate ions forms carbonic acid and decreases the 
alkalinity or the buffering capacity of the system (Reaction 1). When organic 
compounds are degraded, carbon dioxide is released shifting the bicarbonate buffer 
system to bicarbonate and carbonate production until a new equilibrium is reached 
(Reaction 2). On the contrary, proteinaceous wastes, which release ammonia during the 
degradation of amino groups, enhance the alkalinity of the system by binding the 
carbonic acid and producing bicarbonate ions (Reaction 3).

As it is mentioned before, methanogens are considered to be the least tolerant and the 
most likely to cease growth due to ammonia inhibition (Chen et al., 2008). However, 
there are conflicting results about ammonia sensitivity of aceticlastic and

ammonia inhibition compared to hydrogenotrophic methanogens, which tend to be the 
prevailing point of view (Angelidaki & Ahring, 1993; Borja et al., 1996; Chen et al., 
2008). Same studies showed that specific growth rate for the aceticlastic was halved at 
a twofold TAN concentration in comparison with hydrogenotrophic methanogens.

1.3.2 Volatile fatty acids – Long chain fatty acids 
VFA are a key intermediate in the process of anaerobic digestion despite inhibiting 
methanogenesis in high concentrations. Inhibition of methanogenesis process will result 
in slowing down VFA utilization, leading to organic acids accumulation, pH decrease 
and a total system failure (Ward et al., 2008). Even though, acetic acid is usually present 
in higher concentrations than other VFA (85%), propionic acid is considered to be the 
most toxic one, causing inhibition at concentrations above 3000 mg·L-1 (Ward et al., 
2008). 
LCFA are the acids that contain carbon chains of 13 to 21 units and comes from the 
hydrolysis of lipids by lipases, as was stated previously. Angelidaki and Ahring (1992) 
claimed that all steps of a thermophilic biogas reactor can be inhibited by low 
concentrations of LCFA. More specifically, the reactor was fully inhibited at 
concentrations of 0.5 g oleate·L-1 and 1 g stearate·L-1. However, the physical 
characteristics of the feedstock determine LCFA toxicity level, with suspended and 
flocculent sludges to suffer greater inhibition than granular ones, due to the higher 
specific surface area (Chen et al., 2008).

2 Aim and objectives 
As mentioned earlier, ammonia inhibition is a highly debated topic, and hence a great 
number of assays have been conducted both in continuous digesters and batch reactors 
testing pure methanogenic strains (Hendriksen & Ahring, 1991; Angelidaki & Ahring, 
1993; Hajarnis & Ranade, 1993; Angelidaki & Ahring, 1994; Cuetos et al., 2008; 
Rajagopal et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016). 
2.1 Ammonium chloride and urea 
In all these studies ammonium chloride was used as an ammonia source. As a salt, 
ammonium chloride can be dissociated in water releasing chloride anions and 
ammonium cations and acting as a weak acid due to hydrogen cations described by 
Reaction 6. The dissociation chemical reaction is presented below: 
 
NH4Cl (s.) + H2O (l.) → NH4+ (aq.) + Cl- (aq.) 
 
ΔG0= -7.65 kJ·mol-1  (6) 
 
Nevertheless, an inadequate number of experiments have been conducted using a totally 
different ammonia source besides ammonium chloride (Sterling et al., 2001; Wang et 
al., 2013; Sutaryo et al., 2014). Since chloride anions might also inhibit the anaerobic 
digestion process, it is unfeasible to differentiate the inhibitory effect originated by 
ammonia and chloride. Moreover, no studies have been performed evaluating the 
inhibition caused by the source of the ammonia itself; for instance, the molecules of 
ammonium chloride or urea. 
A totally different source of ammonia could be used, such as an organic compound like 
urea, in order to isolate the inhibition caused by ammonia (Sutaryo et al., 2014). Urea 
can be enzymatically hydrolysed into carbonic acid (H2CO3) and free ammonia 
(Reaction 7) by the presence of urease, one of the most proficient known enzymes (Dai 
& Karring, 2014): 
 
CO(NH2)2 (s.) + 2H2O (l.)

Moreover, the uncatalyzed decomposition of urea has been observed in aqueous 
solutions under a negligible reaction rate constant of 6.3·10-9 s-1 and a half-life of 3.5 
years (Krajewska, 2009). Instead, thermal hydrolysis of urea can be attained, with the 
reaction to start slowly at 110oC and become rapid at around 130°C (Figure 3a). After 
20 minutes of heating at 140oC of 10% w/w urea solution, conversion percentage 
reached 20% (Figure 3b). As reported by Sahu et al. (2008), at lower temperatures (110-
130oC) conversion percentage was slightly affected by the initial concentration of urea. 
 
 
Figure 3: (A) Effect of temperature on conversion at different concentrations, (B) Effect of time on 
conversion at different temperatures for a constant 10% w/w solution (Sahu et al., 2008). 
 
Regarding the enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis of urea (Reaction 7), urease (EC 
3.5.1.5) is a well-known nickel metalloenzyme. This process is performed 1014 times 
faster than the uncatalyzed decomposition, with half-time in the order of microseconds 
(Ciurli et al., 1999). Urease is widely distributed in plants, algae, fungi, and 
microorganisms with the primary environmental role to be the production of ammonia, 
so that organisms utilize it as a nitrogen source (Zaborska et al., 2002). 
Nonetheless, hydrolysis product (NH3) can cause a sudden pH increase, capable of 
bringing instability to the AD process (Mobley et al., 1995; Ciurli et al., 1999). Study 
from Sterling et al. (2001) is in agreement with the previous conclusion proving that the 
A) 
B)

final pH values for each of the anaerobic digesters increased proportionally to the 
amount of urea added. 
Finally, regarding all the experiments conducted with urea as an ammonia source, none 
of the researchers mentioned how the hydrolysis of urea occurred, even though results 
have shown that the desirable TAN concentrations were achieved. This could be 
explained if it is taken into account that ureases are enzymes widely spread in nature 
such as soils, plants and animal feces (Dai & Karring, 2014). 
2.2 Aim of the study 
All the above leads to the conclusion that there is lack of scientific background 
regarding the action of urease and its effect on pH as well as the inhibition caused by 
the different sources of the ammonia. Therefore, the aim of this thesis was to investigate 
the effect of two basic ammonia sources (i.e. ammonium chloride and urea) on different 
methanogenic strains. Different gradient levels of TAN, pH and temperature were set 
in order to test the influence of the total and free ammonia concentration. Meanwhile, a 
secondary objective was set in order to investigate hydrolysis rate of urea under two 
different TAN and pH levels with the addition or the absence of urease. Finally, a third 
objective of the study was the investigation of pH variation after the hydrolysis of the 
urea or the dissolution of the ammonium salt in the media. 
Thus, three experimental procedures conducted in order to elucidate the lack of 
scientific background concerning the inhibition caused by the different sources of 
ammonia as well as the action of urease and its effect on pH during urea’s hydrolysis. 
In Assay I, hydrolysis rate of two methanogenic strains under different TAN and pH 
levels with the addition or the absence of urease was investigated. In Assay II, the effect 
on pH after the hydrolysis of urea or the dissociation of ammonium chloride was 
examined. Finally, Assay III had as a goal to assess the impact of two ammonia sources 
on methanogenesis process under different TAN and pH levels.

3 Materials and Methods 
3.1 Pure strains and growth media 
Four pure methanogenic strains (Methanosarcina thermophila TM-1 DSM No.: 1825, 
Methanosarcina barkeri MS DSM No.: 800, Methanoculleus bourgensis MS2 DSM 
No.: 3045, and Methanoculleus thermophilus CR-1 DSM No.: 2373) were purchased 
from DSMZ Company and used throughout the study. The first two strains were 
classified as acetotrophic methanogens, while the other two as hydrogenotrophic 
methanogens. Moreover, two of the strains were mesophilic (M. barkeri and M. 
bourgensis) incubated at 37±1oC, while the other two were thermophilic (M. 
thermophila and M. thermophilus) incubated at 55±1oC. 
Before the experiment, the pure strains were cultivated in specific growth media 
suggested by literature (Weimer & Zeikus, 1978; Balch et al., 1979; Zinde & Mah, 
1979; Hutten et al., 1980; Rivard & Smith, 1982; Zinder et al., 1985; Ollivier et al., 
1986; Bryant & Boone, 1987; Maestrojuan et al., 1990), in order to grow the requisite 
biomass. In particular, there were used media 120 for M. thermophila, 120a for M. 
barkeri, 332 for M. bourgensis, and 141 for M. thermophilus. According to the growth 
media, M. thermophila was consuming acetate and methanol while M. barkeri only 
methanol. For hydrogenotrophic strains, M. bourgensis was consuming formate and 
hydrogen stored in the batch reactor’s headspace while M. thermophilus only hydrogen. 
It is worth mentioning that ammonia source in all growing media was NH4Cl. The full 
composition of them can be found in Appendix A. 
3.2 Enzyme 
Urease (Type IX, CAS No: 9002-13-5) from Canavalia ensiformis (jack bean) seeds 
was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co as a lyophilized powder with a specific activity 
of 84 U·mg-1, where 1 U corresponds to the amount of enzyme that liberates 1 μmol of 
ammonia from urea per minute at 25 °C and pH 7. Furthermore, free ammonia was less 
than 0.1 μg per unit (Sigma-Aldrich, 2014). A buffer solution was prepared for the 
dissolution of the enzyme just before use. The buffer solution consisted of 0.2 M sodium 
phosphate with pH equal to 7.3 as indicated by the company (Sigma-Aldrich, 2014).

4 Results and Discussion 
4.1 Assay I: Enzyme experiment 
For both methanogenic strains the presence of enzyme was crucial for the hydrolysis of 
urea. As it can be seen from Figure 5, conversion percentage overpassed the 85% in 
batch reactors with enzyme, while it remained steady constantly at 20% for those 
without. These findings correspond to similar results in studies investigating both the 
uncatalyzed and catalysed decomposition of urea (Ciurli et al., 1999; Krajewska, 2009). 
 
 
Figure 5: Effect of urea concentration, pH and enzyme addition or absence, on urea conversion at 
different temperatures, (A) for M. thermophila at T=55oC and (B) for M. bourgensis at T=37oC. 
 
More specifically, the uncatalyzed decomposition of urea was found to be a very slow 
process with a reaction rate of 6.3·10-9 s-1, independent of pH between 2 and 12 
(Krajewska, 2009). Instead, thermal hydrolysis of urea can be succeed over 110oC 
(Sahu et al., 2008; Krajewska, 2009). During autoclave procedure batch reactors were 
26 
==PAGE:35==
sterilized, imposed to two bars pressure of saturated steam at 121oC for around 20 
minutes. These conditions with the specific duration were capable of converting 20% 
of urea into ammonia (Figure 3a). 
Hydrolysis rates of M. bourgensis were significantly higher (P < 0.05) at pH 7 than pH 
8 regardless ammonia concentration, while for M. thermophila the tested pH levels had 
no significant effect (P > 0.05) on hydrolysis rate for the ammonia concentration of 5 g 
NH4+-N·L−1. On the contrary, for ammonia level of 3 g NH4+-N·L−1 hydrolysis rate at 
pH 7 was significantly higher (P < 0.05) than pH 8. Jack bean urease has a pH optimum 
at 7.4 (Cesareo & Langton, 1992), which in general was in accordance to experimental 
findings. 
Moreover, hydrolysis rates of the thermophilic strain (M. thermophila) were statistically 
higher (P < 0.05) compared to the mesophilic one (M. bourgensis), regardless ammonia 
concentration and pH level. These results were consistent with those obtained by Moyo 
et al. (1989), in which an increase in temperature could affect the relative activity of the 
enzyme positively accelerating hydrolysis. The only exception observed for ammonia 
and pH level of 3 g NH4+-N·L−1 and 8 respectively, was that the difference in 
temperature had not any statistical difference (P > 0.05) on hydrolysis rate between the 
two methanogenic strains. 
Nevertheless, none of the investigated parameters affected the hydrolysis outcome of 
the batch reactors containing the enzyme. The final hydrolysis percentage was not 
statistically affected (P > 0.05) by temperature, pH or urea concentration.

stabilized to non-viable levels for microorganisms’ growth according to literature

decrease in pH after dissociation of the gradient levels of salt, without the maximum 
difference overpassing the 0.3 units. Results from this assay verified completely the 
behaviour of ammonium chloride and urea after the dissociation and hydrolysis of these 
chemicals respectively (Mobley et al., 1995; Ciurli et al., 1999).

4.5 Inhibitory mechanism of different ammonia 
sources 
As was stated previously, urea has a more significant influence on methanogenesis than 
ammonium chloride, causing greater inhibition, with the explanation of this 
phenomenon to be still unknown. However, it is possibly related to the different reaction 
products. On the one hand, ammonium chloride is instantly dissociated in water, 
releasing ammonium cations (Reaction 6) and slightly decreasing the pH, according to 
Assay II. On the other hand, urea’s hydrolysis is a gradual phenomenon with the kinetics 
of the reaction to be fully affected by the presence of urease as it was proved in Assay 
I. Moreover, the product of this hydrolyzation is the free ammonia (Reaction 7), which 
was confirmed to be the most toxic form of ammonia in Assay III. Finally, another 
effect from urea’s hydrolysis is the sudden increase of pH, especially in solutions with 
weak buffer capacity (Assay II). From all the above is concluded that for ammonium 
chloride there is only one parameter that can shift the NH4+/NH3 equilibrium (Reaction 
4) towards free ammonia; the increase in ammonia concentration. Unlike ammonium 
chloride, for urea there are two affecting parameters; the increase in ammonia 
concentration and pH. 
In this study, same experimental conditions for both ammonia sources were applied, by 
practicing a pH strategy, as was stated in experimental setup of Assay III, and ensuring 
pH of 7 and 8. The final FAN concentration from the two ammonia sources would be 
the same and hence a justification for the significant difference in the inhibition of the 
two sources should be sought deeper into the distinct mechanisms of ammonia 
generation. For ammonium chloride, Reaction 4 shifts towards free ammonia, while for 
urea shifts towards ammonium ion, with the final outcome to be the re-establishment of 
a new equilibrium, which eventually would have the same free ammonia concentration 
for both strains. 
41 
==PAGE:50==
Nonetheless, even temporarily, until the re-establishment of equilibrium, FAN 
concentration from urea is higher than free ammonia concentration from ammonium 
chloride. Moreover, the duration of this effect could last up to 24 h, since hydrolysis is 
a progressive reaction depending on the concentration and the relative activity of the 
enzyme. Therefore, microorganisms would be exposed at higher free ammonia 
concentration until the end of hydrolysis and the new equilibrium. 
Undoubtedly, this theory must be further confirmed by implementing a new 
experimental procedure, most likely investigating a different ammonia source and 
correlating the findings with previous results.

5 Conclusions 
The present study investigated the impact of two basic ammonia sources (i.e. 
ammonium chloride and urea) on different methanogenic strains. Undoubtedly, the 
experiment confirmed that urea was statistically a more inhibitory source (P < 0.05) 
than ammonium chloride regardless the tested strain. 
A possible explanation for this phenomenon could be extracted by the distinct 
mechanisms of ammonia generation. Ammonium chloride releases ammonium cations, 
while during urea’s hydrolysis free ammonia is produced. Hence, for ammonium 
chloride FAN concentration is increasing until reaching the new equilibrium, while for 
urea FAN concentration starts from a much higher level and gradually decreasing as the 
new equilibrium is reached. This prolonged exposure to higher free ammonia 
concentration is assumed to be the key factor for the higher inhibitory effect of urea. 
Two more findings, which were in accordance to the literature, have been deducted 
from the same experimental procedure. Free ammonia is confirmed to be more toxic for 
methanogenic strains than ammonium ions. Moreover, the two aceticlastic 
methanogens seemed to be more sensitive to ammonia inhibition than the two 
hydrogenotrophic ones. 
In addition, the impact of urease in hydrolysis of urea, as well as the factors that could 
be influence hydrolysis rate were tested. The conclusion was that enzyme was crucial 
for hydrolysis of urea, in the absence of which conversion never surpassed 20%. 
Furthermore, hydrolysis rate was affected positively by the increase of temperature, 
while it decreased by the corresponding increase of pH. Nonetheless, the final outcome 
of the hydrolysis was not significantly affected (P > 0.05) by parameters such as 
temperature, pH and urea concentration. 
Finally, pH variation after the hydrolysis of urea or the dissociation of ammonium 
chloride was examined. Results pinpointed the fact that the growing media had weak 
buffer capacity concerning urea’s hydrolysis and hence a pH adjustment strategy was 
necessary to ensure same pH conditions for all the batch reactors. For ammonium 
chloride there was a slight decrease in pH corroborating the fact that after dissociation 
ammonium ions act as weak acid.

6 Future perspectives 
The findings of this study offer an opportunity for further research, focusing on the 
inhibition mechanism of the two different ammonia sources. It is of crucial importance 
implementation of an experimental procedure verifying author’s assumption about the 
inhibition mechanism. The most promising idea is the application of a new ammonia 
source and correlation of the findings with previous results. 
Regarding the existing experiment, methanogens that was not fully inhibited under the 
maximum applied ammonia concentration could be further examined. Specifically, 
higher TAN levels (above 10 g NH4+-N·L−1) could be set for the two hydrogenotrophic 
strains, until the system is completely inhibited. 
Finally, a next step for the development of the study is to investigate the inhibitory 
effect of the sources in a continuous reactor. The differences from batch reactors can be 
focused on the stronger buffer capacity of a manure-based reactor as well as the 
existence of urease inside the feedstock.
