Summary
This M.Sc. Thesis presents a workﬂow for creating a computational ﬂuid dynamics
three-dimensional fully non-linear numerical wave tank for the modelling of six degrees of
freedom wind turbine ﬂoating foundations using ®OpenFOAM. It uses the DeepCwind
foundation from the Oﬀshore Code Comparison campaign OC3 to OC6. The aim of
this Thesis is to validate the use of CFD for the high ﬁdelity modelling of oﬀshore wind
turbine ﬂoating foundations.
The open source CFD toolbox OpenFOAM version 1912 is used throughout this work,
with the interFoam solver for multi-phase ﬂow solving using the fraction of ﬂuid method.
Its extension developed by Jacobsen, Fuhrman, and Fredsøe, 2012 for waves generation
and absorption waves2Foam is used for generating and absorbing uni-directional irregular
waves within the CFD domain.
The online CAD software ®OnShape, and ®SALOME 9.4.0 geometry software were
used to prepare geometry ﬁles input to the meshing library. The cfMesh meshing utility
developed by Juretic, 2010, and most particularly its cartesianMesh library was used to
mesh the 3D domain and the ﬂoater. A geometry workﬂow is described as a guideline to
go from CAD ﬂoater ﬁle to high quality mesh using cfMesh.
Validation of the interFoam and waves2Foam solvers, and of the solvers parameters
and discretization scheme was made for wave and current generation in a two dimensional
empty domain generated with blockMesh. This validation serves as a calibration of
settings and boundary conditions for wave and current generation within OpenFOAM
The DeepCwind foundation was meshed within a 3 dimensional domain corresponding
to the OC6 experimental setup at the MARIN basin. And the numerical setup was used
as to simulate the ﬂoater in diﬀerent physical conditions, to compare with the load cases
deﬁned within the OC6 framework. The load cases from 1 to 5 deﬁnes experimental
conditions in which the DeepCwind foundation should be modelled by the participants
of the OC6 projects, to be compared to experimental results.
The ﬁrst simulations feature a ﬁxed foundation, subject to current and waves. It
was modelled using the described setup, and the output was compared to the OC6
experimental results, which validated the stability and accuracy of the numerical wave
tank. The CFD results showed good accordance on the forcing and moment due to waves
on a ﬁxed ﬂoater.
Moored decay test where conducted, that validated the dynamic mesh handling and
rigid body motion solving coupled with ﬂuid interaction. They comprise the modelling
of OC6 taut spring mooring lines using the interFoam dynamic mesh dictionary. The
comparison of obtained natural periods validate the correct modelling of added mass for

the ﬂoater with CFD, with some discrepancies in the added mass in the surge direction
to be further investigated. The damping observed in CFD simulations was compared
to the damping observed experimentally, and was found close but over estimated for

Finally, the six degrees of freedom moored ﬂoating foundation in regular and irregular

running of such a complex simulation represents the main achievement of this Thesis,
and paves the way for the use of CFD for oﬀshore wind foundation design.
The integration of turbulence modelling and more complex mooring models is then
discussed for a widened and more accurate use of CFD for foundation design. The
guidelines presented are only validated by the running cases and their good accordance to
experimental results but more thorough and systematic comparison of interFoam solvers
settings, diﬀerent meshing tools and time stepping settings should be done to further
validate this work.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
In the recent years, the global discussion about climate change has continuously increased.
Climate change now represents a global scientiﬁc consensus, and its anthropogenic origin
is globally accepted. Despite the uncertainties due to the limited understanding of the
Earth’s geophysical system, more and more scientiﬁc publications stress the potential
dramatic eﬀects of anthropogenic global warming on the biodiversity, land livability,
food stocks, water supplies, human health etc. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate
Change (IPCC), a global scientiﬁc committee responsible for summing up the scientiﬁc
community’s ﬁnding concerning climate change, and formulating guidelines for potential
climate change mitigation strategies, stated that the global average temperature had
raised by 1°C from pre-industrialized levels, and presented guidelines and scenarios
limiting global warming to 1.5 °C (Rogelj et al., 2019). In December 2015, the Paris
Agreement made the 160 signing countries, responsible for 97% of the worldwide emis-
sions, accountable for their greenhouse gases emissions. Every country has prior to
the conference, submitted Nationally Determined Contributions, mapping the way they
would reduce their emissions, and those contributions are to be revised in 2020.
However, the Nationally Determined Contributions ratiﬁed by the Paris Agreement
would lead to an annual global emissions of 52 −58 GTCO2.yr−1, whereas the IPCC’s
estimations of the needed emissions target for 2030 in order to stay within a global
warming of 1.5°C are of 25 −30 GTCO2.yr−1 (Rogelj et al., 2019). Moreover, zero net
emissions must be reached by 2050. In order to succeed in limiting global warming to
1.5°C, electriﬁcation of energy end-use should be achieved globally, combined with demand
reductions and decarbonization of the electricity production. Despite the warnings of
scientiﬁc evidence, energy related emissions reached an historical peak in 2018. Electricity
accounts for 19% of the global total energy consumption, and 64% of which is produced
from coal, gas or oil, which are carbon intensive energy sources (IEA, 2019b).
In this context, the development of renewable energy sources paves the way to
decarbonized, decentralized electricity production. The recent dramatic drop in the
Levelized Cost of Energy of renewable energy sources now makes them competitive with
coal and oil. Solar energy and wind energy are reaching industrial maturity and present
a potential big enough to cover the global energy consumption. Now the wind turbines
are moving oﬀshore, driven by strong political will from Northern European countries
such as Denmark, Germany, the UK, the Netherlands, Sweden and Belgium. There the
wind is steadier and stronger, the land use impact is smaller with communities, and the
visual and acoustic impact of wind turbines is almost nonexistent.

1.2
Oﬀshore wind energy background
Oﬀshore wind energy is one of the biggest potential in renewable electricity production.
It is a very dynamic market, that has grown 30% per year for a decade. It is on track
to become the largest renewable energy source in Europe according to the IEA energy
outlook (IEA, 2019b). Europe alone, the biggest market for now, has installed 22GW of
capacity, most of it in the North Sea and the Baltic Sea. The main countries being the
UK, Germany, Denmark, the Netherlands and Belgium. Even though the conditions in
the Baltic Sea and the North Sea are particularly suitable for the development of oﬀshore
wind, it is only a small part of the global potential, which is estimated to 11 times the
global consumption in 2040 by the IEA.(IEA, 2019b).
Figure 1.1: Oﬀshore untapped potential worldwide, estimation from (IEA, 2019a).
Every indicator related to oﬀshore wind increases, be it the turbine size, capacity
rating, the installed capacity per year or the distance from shore. As the technology
reaches maturity and the market diversiﬁes with several experienced players at every
level, the prices of the produced electricity dramatically drops. In 2019, all the bidding
processes in Europe were in the range of 40 −50e/MWh, getting closer to the levels of
solar energy and onshore wind energy (Walsh, 2019).

1.2.1
Floating wind energy - context
Despite the favorable conditions for the development of bottom ﬁxed oﬀshore wind in
the North Sea and the Baltic Sea, most of the potential development areas worldwide
have bathymetries superior to 50m. It is the case for major high potential development
areas such as Japan, Korea, the US west coast, etc. At the end of 2019, 65MW were
installed worldwide, including 45MW in Europe, notably including the ®Equinor 30MW
HyWind Scotland wind farm. Floating wind can harvest the wind further out in the sea
were it is stronger, but also unlocks development areas such as the Atlantic Ocean, the
Mediterranean sea, the Eastern Paciﬁc Ocean, were the bathymetry increases quickly
from the coast. It is a less mature technology than bottom ﬁxed wind energy due to
higher cost traducing higher technological challenges, including transmission, stability
and loads. There is no general consensus on the optimal design for ﬂoating foundations,

There are four main groups of ﬂoating foundations, the semi submersible platform,

Floating wind is not as mature as bottom ﬁxed wind, but it has a huge potential.
Its cost has not yet dropped as bottom ﬁxed has, but it expected to do so, as the wind
farms move further oﬀshore on deeper sites. Key drivers for cost reductions expected
for ﬂoating wind are the technological development of ﬂoaters, moorings, and dynamic
cables, the port adaptation for ﬂoating foundation for installation processes, and the
diminution of the risk associated with it. As a general rule, a better understanding
of the loads on the structure would lead to an optimization of the foundation’s design
and a reduction in risk hence facilitating the investment and development of ﬂoating
wind energy. The development of accurate modelling tools is crucial to the design of
foundations, that are can be over-designed due to high uncertainties in the modelling.
1.2.2
Dynamics of ﬂoating wind
Floating wind installations are to be situated oﬀshore, several kilometers from land. The
wind turbines are erected on ﬂoating foundations, themselves moored to the seabed. The
electrical connection is made through dynamic array cables. The system is then subject
to aerodynamic loading on the rotor, nacelle and tower, hydrodynamics forcing on the

foundation and mooring due to wave and current. The foundation itself is subject to
station keeping loads from the mooring system, and turbine related loads. The correct
modelling of the system’s dynamic response can be challenging. In this paper, the
aerodynamics will not be considered, and the foundation will be modelled alone moored
to the seabed. The electrical connection is not considered. The goal being to understand
the modelling capabilities of the hydrodynamic loads on the ﬂoating structure.
1.2.2.1
Foundations
The foundation system has 6 degrees of freedom pictured in Figure 1.4. The terminology
is the same as the one used in aeronautics and shipping industry. In all that follows,
a wave of zero angle will propagate along the x-axis. x-axis motion will be referred to
as surge motion, y-axis as sway motion and vertical motion along the z-axis as heave
motion. The rotation around the x-axis is referred to as roll, y-axis rotation is pitch and
z-axis rotation is yaw. The same terminology applies to the rotor system.

terminology. Figure from (Tran, Kim, and Nguyen, 2015).

The ξ vector contains the 6 degrees of freedom of the ﬂoating wind turbine system.
The right hand side member of 1.1 contains the aerodynamic loads that are not considered
in this study, the hydrostatic force, viscous eﬀects, wave excitation loads and radiation.

in the sway, surge and yaw degree of freedom. Moorings are designed to provide restoring
moments in those degrees of freedom.
The stability of a ﬂoater derives from the stiﬀness matrix. There is basically three
main types of stabilization for ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines. Spar buoys are ballast

1.4
Litterature review
The modelling of ﬂoating wind turbines is a subject of increased interest. State of the art
methods for the modelling of loads on ﬂoating oﬀshore structure, and for the design of
ﬂoating foundations use blade element momentum theory (BEM) for aerodynamics, and
linear potential ﬂow based tools for hydrodynamics, as described in (A. N. Robertson
et al., 2017) and (Cordle and Jonkman, 2011). The main used algorithms are notably
the NREL’s FAST model, or the DTU’s HAWC2 models, but there exist a wide variety
of models. Time domain approaches contain fully-coupled non-linear simulation codes
have often been adapted to oﬀshore wind by adding hydrodynamics models to existing
wind turbines codes (Lupton, 2014). Some partially decoupled time domain code oﬀer a
signiﬁcant gain in computational cost by neglecting some eﬀects and hence allow for a
decoupling of codes. It is the case for the widely used HAWC2 or DNV GL’s Bladed codes.
Eventually, some linearised models have been developed, and showed good eﬃciency by
working in the frequency domain. The Nowitech’s SIMO-RIFLEX, Orcina’s OrcaFlex
models can be cited.
Various results obtained with such models can be found in (Lupton, 2014), (Guignier
et al., 2016), (Cheng et al., 2017), (Sethuraman and Venugopal, 2013), (M. A. Bhinder
et al., 2015), (Driscoll et al., 2016). In the OC5 campaign, all those models showed
good overall assessment of the lifetime equivalent loads due to wave on the foundation.
However, all models using Boundary Element Method hydrodynamic approach showed
==PAGE:26==

some under estimation of the non-linear low frequencies excitation of waves on the ﬂoater,
especially on the pitch and surge natural frequencies (A. N. Robertson et al., 2017).
It is expected that the use of Computational Fluid Dynamics for the computation of
hydrodynamics loads on ﬂoating structure would enlarge the scope of modelling of such
structures, by better capturing viscous eﬀects. In (M. A. Bhinder et al., 2015), it is
shown that the adding of a viscous damping term in the Morison equation yields to
better agreement for BEM method. CFD would then be helpful for pre-processing of
coeﬃcients, and calibration of faster models. (Liu et al., 2017) also shows that the fully
coupling in CFD can also help better understand the aerodynamics of wind turbine under
ﬂoater’s motion. Indeed, in pitch motion, the rotor interacts with the incoming wind,
and this can be really challenging to model. The emphasis is put on the growing interest
for CFD modelling in ﬂoating wind turbines.
The use of CFD for modelling of ﬂoating structure has been widely investigated in
the context of Wave Energy Converters (WEC). In this ﬁeld, the interaction between
ﬂoating structures and wave is crucial. (Ransley, 2015) uses the OpenFOAM 6 degrees
of freedom solver for investigation of focused waves on wave energy converters. The
diﬃculties linked to mesh deformation notably is mentioned. However, (M. a. Bhinder
et al., 2011), (Islam, Mohapatra, and Guedes Soares, 2018), (Lataillade et al., 2017),
(Windt, Davidson, and Ringwood, 2018), (Palm et al., 2016) emphasize the high ﬁdelity
of CFD models, and the increased capabilities and stability of such models. In most
cases, some degrees of freedom seem to be more troublesome than others, depending
on the ﬂoater’s geometry. It is emphasized that the viscous eﬀects modelled in CFD
widely depend on the geometry, the turbulence model and the wall functions, and that it
yields to over or under prediction of viscous damping. CFD being highly computationally
expensive, it is seen as a ’valuable bridge between simpliﬁed approaches and costly
experimental investigation’ (Palm et al., 2016).
Concerning ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines, the use of CFD is more recent. It’s been
a recommended solution for the OC6 campaign, in order to understand the discrepancies
between linearised and time domain models and experimental results. CFD for wind
turbines was ﬁrstly found useful for the modelling of wake aerodynamics, by resolving the
boundary layers on the blades (Sanderse, Pijl, and Koren, 2013). Then coupled models
were used to assess hydrodynamics on ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines (Beyer, Arnold, and
Wen Cheng, 2013), (Quallen et al., 2014). In (Kara et al., 2016), the added value of CFD
is shown for the vortex induced motion for ﬂoating foundation with current. In (Paci,
2018), the use of CFD for the modelling of ﬂoating platform is proved to be a high ﬁdelity
tool, oﬀering good stability. It is applied to a simpliﬁed spar foundation model. The
OOStar platform was also modelled using OpenFOAM in (Sarlak, A. Pegalajar-Jurado,
and Bredmose, 2018), performing decay test with 1 degree of freedom. The DeepCWind
foundation was also recently modelled with CFD, and its dynamics is assessed through
decay tests. In (Burmester et al., 2017), a CFD model of the DeepCwind ﬂoater is
described, and the challenges that it raises are described, including the diﬃculty of
meshing the complex semi-submersible structures, in opposition with simple wave energy

2.1
CFD modelling
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) regroup computational techniques that solve
non linear equations by the discretization of the system in space and time. It was ﬁrst
introduced by Lewis Fry Richardson in 1922 (Lewis Fry Richardson, 1922) for numerical
models in meteorology (Hunt, 1998), where the discretization of the physical space was
ﬁrst introduced, paving the way for CFD. Those techniques later developed with the ﬁrst
computers in the 1940s, initially in solving easy 2-dimensional problems, then extended
to more complicated 3-dimensional problems.
The starting point of CFD problems is often the non-linear Navier-Stokes equation.
The spatial discretization process is called the meshing, and then the transport equations
are solved for each cells in the domain’s mesh. The CFD models can solve any transport
equation. For this Thesis, the transported quantity is the ﬂuid velocity. The Navier
Stokes equation are discretized and the set of algebraic equations resulting from the
space-time discretization is solved at every point in time.
2.1.1
OpenFOAM
®OpenFOAM stands for Open Source Field Operation and Manipulation. It is a C++

and it gathers an active community within research and development. It has no limitation

2.1.3
CFL number
The Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condtion was described in 1928 in (Courant, Friedrichs,
and Lewy, 1928), it is a numerical condition for the stability of solving hyperbolic partial
diﬀerential equations. In one dimension, the CFL condition writes:
C = ux∆t
∆x ≤Cmax
(2.9)
with C being the Courant number, ∆x the space discretization step, ux the ﬂuid’s
velocity along x, and ∆t the time step. It gives the ratio between the time taken
by the ﬂuid to cross a cell, and the time step. The CFL condition states that the
Courant number should be as small as possible across the domain to achieve stability
and accuracy. It is a limit to the amount of information crossing the cells in one time step.
The CFL condition links the size of the cells and the time stepping. Hence the
time step should be decreased for too reﬁned meshes, increasing the computational cost.
OpenFOAM proposes a control option of adjustable time step to make sure that the
Courant number stays below a maximum value by decreasing the time step on the ﬂy.
2.2
Meshing tools and techniques
2.2.1
Structured/Unstructured mesh
OpenFOAM proposes various libraries for meshing the experimental domain. In the
diﬀerent meshing algorithms available, there is two main categories:
• The structured meshes: They are deﬁned by the regularity of their cells’ connectivity.
In these algorithm, full control is given to the user. All the cell’s size or grading
are to be deﬁned by the user. It is the case for the widely used OpenFOAM library
blockMesh. The domain is often deﬁned within the blockMesh dictionnary. The
user is to describe blocks formation through manually entered vertices, then each
block is meshed given the number of cells on each direction and the grading. It is
very useful for meshing simple geometry with full control on the cell size.

• The unstructured meshes on the other hand are deﬁned by the irregularity of their
connectivity. The unstructured mesh algorithms are designed to mesh around
complex geometries that can’t be reduced to vertices manually. They take geometry
ﬁles as input, and allow for great control of the geometry’s surface meshing,
reﬁnement levels, boundary layers etc.
The OC6 case, comprise of a ﬂoater within a numerical wave tank, is a very complex
geometry that requires the use of unstructured meshing. OpenFOAM has an unstructured
mesh utility for automatic meshing called snappyHexMesh (CFD Direct Ltd, 2018).
However, the open source meshing utility developed by ©Creative Fields, ©cfmesh can
easily generate various type of meshes, cartesian, hexahedral or tetrahedral, and requires
less manual eﬀort than snappyHexMesh. The boundary layer generation seems better,
and it is way faster and works good in parallel. For all those reasons, the cfmesh
library will be used all along this thesis, knowing that similar results could be used with
snappyHexMesh albeit some more extensive manual eﬀorts.

2.3.2
Wave modelling
The wave modelling in OpenFOAM requires special treatment for the boundary condi-
tions, both at the inlet an the outlet, for generation and absorption of the wave.
The boundary conditions at the inlet for generating waves need to be deﬁned ana-
lytically according to the wave model chosen. Only wet cells, i.e. cells with α > 0 are
to be concerned by this boundary condition. The interFoam solver has an integrated
wave model, originally IHFoam, developed in (Higuera, Lara, and Losada, 2013), that
is part of the OpenFOAM release since the 1612 version. It has integrated boundary
conditions that model the wave generation and absorption. It uses active absorption
at the outlet, which analytically creates a velocity boundary condition that cancels the
incoming wave signal. It has the advantage of not adding computational domain for
passive wave absorption, but also creates signiﬁcant reﬂection at the outlet.
On the other hand, an OpenFOAM toolbox was developed for wave generation and
absorption using relaxation zones (Jacobsen, Fuhrman, and Fredsøe, 2012). In this case,
computational domains several wavelength long are added at the inlet and outlet.
Within the inlet and outlet domain, a relaxation function αR is applied to the variables
of the system as follows:
φ = αRφcomputed + (1 −αR)φtarget

relaxation zones despite adding a signiﬁcant computational cost to the simulation, are

2.4
Rigid body motion
In order to model the interaction of a rigid body with the ﬂow, a rigid body motion
solver needs to be used. The rigid body motion solver will compute the response of the
body to external forces. The mesh around the body also needs to be deformed when
the body moves. The interFoam solver includes the possibility to account for rigid body
motion and mesh deformation.
==PAGE:37==
2.4 Rigid body motion
27
2.4.1
Mesh morphing
The mesh morphing in OpenFOAM is controlled by two parameters, the inner distance
and outer distance. Basically, only the cells between the outer distance on the inner
distance from the body will be deformed in the process. Anything within the inner
distance will move rigidly with the body.
The setting of inner and outer distances can change the ﬂow around the structure,
and aﬀect the stability of the simulations. Small cells near the surface of the ﬂoater are
to be protected from morphing to avoid numerical issues, and the outer distance needs
to be big enough to distribute the morphing among a high number of cells.
2.4.2
Degrees of freedom and relaxation
The dynamic mesh dictionary in OpenFOAM also provides some control for constraints
and restraints of the rigid body. The constraints are used to remove degrees of freedom
for the ﬂoater. For constrained ﬂoater simulations, i.e. towing tests and wave forcing on
ﬁxed foundations, the 6 degrees of freedom of the ﬂoater are frozen. The releasing of
degrees of freedom for ﬂoating structures can be troublesome and source of numerical
instabilities. High accelerations can create instabilities, since they will trigger high ﬂuid
forces. Acceleration relaxation is used throughout this report to stabilise the simulations.
The acceleration relaxation simply applies a coeﬃcient to the body’s acceleration a∗= ra,
with r ∈[0, 1]. Low acceleration relaxation coeﬃcient can lead to eﬀects on the accuracy
of the body motion.
Throughout this report, unless stated otherwise, an acceleration relaxation of 0.7 is
used. It proved not to impact the ﬂoater’s natural frequencies, and improve stability. All
simulations of free ﬂoater are performed with 6 degrees of freedom.
While restraints are reactions forces that turn oﬀdegrees of freedom, the integrated
interFoam dynamic mesh dictionary also allows for the implementation of some forces on
the rigid body. The moorings for the OC6 campaign as described is Section 3.2 being
linear springs, they can be directly modelled as added forces in the interFoam dynamic
mesh dictionary. The sixDofRigidBodyMotionRestraint linearSpring creates a lin-
ear spring restoring force between an anchor point and an attachment point, which moves
with the body. The resting length and stiﬀness of the spring are given, and there is
a possibility to add a damping coeﬃcient. Three linear springs are then set with the
parameters described in Section 3.2. Unless stated otherwise, those spring forces are
used for the free ﬂoater simulations.
More complex and realistic moorings can also be implemented through the library
waves2Foam (Jacobsen, Fuhrman, and Fredsøe, 2012), it is the case for catenary lines.
In this case, the dynamic of the mooring can be integrated in the simulation. It will not
be covered in this work.

2.5
Solvers
2.5.1
Numerical schemes
The numerical schemes speciﬁed in the dictionary fvSchemes deﬁne the discretization
schemes for the diﬀerent members of the governing equations. A vast amount of diﬀerent
schemes are available. 1st order accurate schemes are more stable and bounded, but tend
to be diﬀusive. On the other hand, second order accurate schemes are more accurate but
can be oscillatory and diverge.
2.5.1.1
Time schemes
The time discretization schemes are of two types, Euler (1st order) and Crank-Nicolson
(2nd order). 1st order schemes are too diﬀusive but 2nd order schemes can become
oscillatory. The Crank Nicolson scheme is unbounded, so OpenFOAM oﬀers the possibility
to add a blending coeﬃcient φ ∈[0, 1] between Euler and Crank-Nicolson schemes, to
help stabilise the simulation. For 6 degrees of freedom simulations, it was necessary to
use an blending coeﬃcient of 0.5.
2.5.1.2
Gradient schemes
The sub-dictionary gradSchemes contains the discretization schemes for the gradient
terms. Throughout this project, the Gauss linear gradient schemes is used, with no
limiter. The scheme is second order accurate.
2.5.1.3
Divergence schemes
Convective terms are discretized with the sub-dictionnary speciﬁed divSchemes. The
used methods for this study are the vanLeer and linear methods, which are second-order
accurate.

2.6.1
Control parameters
The control dictionary deﬁnes the control parameters of the simulation, including the
time step, write interval etc. Special attention will be given to the time step handling.
As it’s been discussed above, it is of special importance both for the simulation accuracy
and stability and for the computational cost.
Unless stated otherwise, the time step used in this work is dt = 5e −4, to balance
with the existence of very small cells in the mesh. It is way below the recommended
time step in the OC6 campaign, reﬂecting the diﬃculties faced for handling the complex
geometry.
OpenFOAM can also adjust the time step on the ﬂy, complying with a maximum
Courant number and interface Courant number. In this study, the complex geometry
creates high Courant numbers for the 6 degree of freedom simulations, and adjustable
time step tend to make the simulation crash due to divergence of time step. While,
when keeping a ﬁxed time step, simulations showed good stability and robustness despite
having high maximum Courant numbers of more than 10, where they should stay inferior
to 0.5. It is likely that the very high Courant numbers are located in some very small
cells, and that those few cells do not impact the stability and accuracy of the solution.

2.6.2
Output
The writing of the output in OpenFOAM can be of several types. functions objects
can be added to the control dictionary, for post processing purposes. The forces library
applied to the patch of the ﬂoater computes the pressure and viscous forcing on the
surface of the rigid body and the moment relative to the centre of mass. All forces and
moment output in this study are obtained from this function object.
It can be somehow challenging to output the surface elevation. For this purpose,
probes are added at a specif (x, y) location, evenly distributed on the z axis around the
free surface. For each time step, the fraction of ﬂuid is output for each point of the probe,
and is integrated over z, which gives the free surface elevation. This technique has the
advantage to output the free surface elevation at each time step, and to limit the size of
the output ﬁle.
All other output parameter, ﬂoater position, orientation, velocity, Courant number,
etc. are read from the output ﬁle of the solver.

4.5
Conclusion for a ﬁxed ﬂoater
The simulation of the ﬁxed DeepCwind ﬂoater subject to current and regular wave,
compared to the experimental results from OC6 phase I campaign, validate the modelling
of the hydrodynamic forcing on the ﬂoater with interFoam and waves2Foam. The wave
generation and current generation was calibrated in an empty tank before carrying long
simulations to estimate the forcing an moment on the foundation due to current and
waves. The CFD results showed very good accordance with experimental results for the
hydrodynamic forcing due to regular waves. The ﬁxed ﬂoater subject to current proved
diﬃcult to model due to the complexity of the ﬂow around the structure, and second
order discretization schemes were found to create big discrepancies in the forcing due
to the build up of small numerical oscillations. Finally, those simulations proved the
stability of the mesh and numerical schemes, allowing for reasonably computationally
expensive simulations and yielding to accurate results.
From the results of this chapter, the degrees of freedom of the ﬂoater are realeased
and the moorings implemented for free moored ﬂoater simulations.

In this chapter, the DeepCwind ﬂoater is released, and modelled as a six degree of freedom
rigid body. The mooring system described in Section 3.2 is implemented in interFoam,
and the dynamic behavior of the ﬂoater is assessed through decay tests in the heave,

Load cases 4 explored the settings for dynamic mesh modelling
in OpenFOAM, alongside with the 6-DoF/Navier Stokes coupling,testing the stability of
the simulation when releasing degrees of freedom. Decay tests are performed in a moored
situation, needing the design of moorings as deﬁned in the OC6 documentation. They
reveal the ability of OpenFOAM to capture non linear eﬀects causing damping during
the decay tests, and also the added mass eﬀects induced by the ﬂoater’s motion.
5.1
Heave decay test
The heave decay test is used to carry sensitivity analysis on the 6 degree of freedom
solver, and on the dynamic coupling between ﬂow and structure. Indeed, the natural
frequencies in surge and pitch motion being very low, it is computationally expensive to
obtain results on several periods. The decay test corresponds to Load Case 4.3, with an
initial displacement of 1.57m from the free equilibrium. The OC6 heave results for Load
case 4.3 starts at -1.06m from the equilibrium. The delta between the prescribed and
given initial displacement is equal to the diﬀerence in equilibrium draft between the free
and moored conﬁgurations in the CFD results. This validates the used mooring model
as described in Section 3.2.
5.1.1
Numerical setup
The base case numerical setup uses the interFoam solver, with wall boundary conditions
on every face of the numerical wave tank, assuming that the reﬂection of the radiated
waves on the walls would have little eﬀect on the movement.
The rigid body motion is done with a Newmark solver, with six degrees of freedom in
heave, surge, sway, pitch, roll and yaw. The moorings use the interFoam dynamic mesh
subdictionnary restraints to deﬁne linear springs with similar properties as deﬁned in
Section 3.2. The fairlead positions are computed given the initial heave displacement. The
simulation is computed with an acceleration relaxation of 0.7 to stabilize the simulation.
It was observed that the acceleration relaxation factor had little eﬀect on the movement

5.1.3.1
Mesh sensitivity
It is not straightforward to compare diﬀerent meshes and their results. As described
in Section 3.4, the base mesh for the presented simulations is 3.8 million cells, and is
already a trade oﬀbetween stability and number of cells. In order to study the impact
of mesh reﬁnement on the results, a ﬁner mesh and a coarser mesh are generated. Ín
cfMesh, the accessible sizes for the mesh cells are discrete values, dividing the maximum
cell size by a power of 2. Hence, increasing the reﬁnement level by 1 level for a given
volume would multiply the number of cells by 23.
On the other hand, the found conﬁguration around the ﬂoater is not very ﬂexible in
terms of mesh reﬁnement, since the meshing of the cross braces and joints is critical for
stability. The cells in the troublesome areas i.e. cross braces and joints, are deﬁned by
a maximum cell size. Their actual size is the highest reﬁnement level lower than this
maximum cell size. In this way, the surface mesh around the ﬂoater and reﬁnement at
the vicinity of cross braces and joints do not vary too much when changing the maximum
cell size.
The deﬁning of coarser and ﬁner meshes for sensitivity analysis is hence not straight-
forward, since the target meshes should ensure simulation stability, total a reasonable
amount of cells (< 10M cells), and keep a meshing around the ﬂoater similar to the base
mesh. The sensitivity presented for the mesh mainly represent the sensitivity of the
simulation to reﬁnement of the water, and around the ﬂoater, but not the surface meshing
of the ﬂoater nor the boundary layers. The coarser mesh is obtained by increasing the
maximum cell size while keeping the reﬁnement levels. Seemingly, the ﬁner mesh is

7.1
Discussion
7.1.1
Meshing tool cfMesh
The library cfMesh was used all along this Thesis, and proved to be robust and modu-
lar. Only the cartesianMesh utility using hexahedral cells was used, since it proved to
produce meshes with less cells, which was critical for computational cost. The cfMesh’s
cartesianMesh utility, combined with the described geometry preparation workﬂow using
a CAD software and the SALOME tool, showed good robustness. The smooth handling
of group of faces from the initial geometry, along with the including of edges within the
FMS ﬁle format considerably smoothed the workﬂow.
However, the use of hexahedral cells as base cells in cartesianMesh, is problematic
when meshing diagonal surfaces, such as the cross braces or joints in our case, where
the use of (x, y, z) hexahedral cells fail to easily mesh the surface. It comes out of this
the use of very small cells around the cross braces and joints, that increase the total
amount of cells but also the complexity of the mesh, its non-orthogonality, skewness, and
the presence of very small cells, eventually aﬀecting the stability of the simulations and
quality of results.
It is believed that the use of polyhedral cells or tetrahedral cells with cfMesh’s
pMesh and tetMesh utilities, would capture such a complex geometry more easily. The
comparison of those three utilities, and with the widely used snappyHexMesh utility,
would be a continuation of this work.
7.1.2
Moorings
The mooring model implemented in this Thesis in very simpliﬁed, since it is only an
linear stiﬀness model. It is comparable to the guidelines for the OC6 phase I in terms
of response, but for further studies, and in most cases of ﬂoating foundations, a more
complex mooring model should be implemented, using the catenary equations and consid-
ering the dynamic eﬀects of the mooring lines. The use of the MoorDyn utilities within
the waves2Foam library should be investigated.
However, the linear spring model implemented in the CFD compared well in terms of
response to displacement compared to the mooring models used in HAWC2 for the OC6
campaign, which validates the use of interFoam linear spring mooring in this campaign.
==PAGE:100==
90
7 Recommendations and conclusion
7.1.3
Dynamic mesh
The mesh morphing handling proved to be quite complex, and its eﬀects on the dynamics
of the ﬂoater and on the stability of the simulation was not fully captured through
the validation cases. From the extensive work that was done on the DeepCwind very
complex geometry, the recommendation would be to set medium distances for the mesh
morphing. An inner distance that protects the most complex parts of the mesh, say the
whole reﬁned area around the ﬂoater, should be used, while the outer distance should be
as big as possible but not interfering with the domain boundaries. This way, the mesh
deformation is diﬀused through the whole domain, the sensitive areas are protected and
the boundary cells stay untouched.
7.1.4
Schemes, solvers and control parameters
The choosing of solvers, schemes and control parameters can be somewhat confusing
with OpenFOAM. A stable numerical setup giving satisfying results and showing good
robustness for a reasonable computation cost was used for the simulation in this Thesis.
There surely is more investigation to do to study the impact of ﬁrst or second order
schemes, and time discretization schemes, as it proved to impact the quality of results in
the towing tests simulations. Moreover, the PIMPLE corrector was not used to its full
potential and is likely to improve the quality of results by more accurately modelling the
coupling between rigid body motions and ﬂuid motions. The time and computational
power limitations experienced in the realisation of this Thesis did not allow to increase
the number of outer correctors for the PIMPLE corrector, and some good accordance was
found between CFD results and OC6 experimental results using the PIMPLE corrector
in PISO mode. The correct prediction of natural periods in heave and pitch degrees
of freedom, and the stability of the six degrees of freedom under irregular waves were
achieved with only one outer corrector. Sensitivities carried out for heave and surge
decays showed a very slight diﬀerence in the response for a higher number of outer
correctors. The dramatic increase in computational cost associated with the increase
number of outer correctors is to be balanced with the gain in accuracy, which was not
fully studied here. The outer loops of the PIMPLE algorithm did not add stability in this
case, they rather increased the instabilities by increasing the coupling between the rigid
body and the ﬂuid, which is believed to amplify numerical oscillations at the body surface.
Concerning the time stepping and Courant number constraint, all the simulations
presented in this Thesis showed high Courant numbers. Given the complexity of the
mesh and the very small volume cells found at the vicinity of the cross braces joints, it
was not reasonable to limit the Courant number with an adjustable time step at levels
recommended for the OC6 CFD campaign i.e. CFL < 0.2. A ﬁxed time step was used
most of the time to prevent the divergence of the simulation, as it was observed with
adjustable time step. It was concluded that for the mesh used in these simulations, one
should accept to work with high Courant numbers between 5 and 10, as they are only
==PAGE:101==

representatives of some very small cells that proved not to impact the stability nor the

The ﬁxed time step with high Courant number and PIMPLE algorithm with only one

and showing robust stability.
7.1.5
Turbulence modelling
No turbulence modelling was used throughout this work. Despite the justiﬁcation of
laminar ﬂow modelling considering the low Reynolds numbers involved at model scale,
it is possible that a proper turbulence modelling, using for example the k-omega SST
Stab model, would improve the accuracy of results, by dissipating kinetic energy and
improving the body-ﬂuid interaction. Further investigations on the diﬀerent turbulent
models and their impact on the hydrodynamic forces on the ﬂoater should be carried out.

Stability of the numerical wave tank
The implementation of a stable numerical irregular wave tank with a moored 6 degrees
of freedom ﬂoater, with such a complex geometry is the achievement of this work, and

the design of ﬂoating foundation for oﬀshore wind energy. The meshing process and the
numerical setup presented led to a robust and stable numerical wave tank. The moored

7.2
Conclusion
This work serves as a proof of concept, showing the feasibility and robustness of a fully
non-linear wave tank for the modelling of a six degrees of freedom moored ﬂoating
foundation. It was validated on the OC6 phase I experimental results, and compared to
the DTU HAWC2 results. It showed good accordance, validating the mooring modelling,
the wave generation and absorption, and the ﬂuid-structure interaction modelling and
forcing on the structure. The following conclusions are drawn from the presented work:
• The use of interFoam solver and waveFoam extension within OpenFOAM version
1912 allows for eﬃcient modelling of a fully non linear 3D wave tank for ﬂoating
foundation modelling
• A satisfying linear stiﬀness model in line with the OC6 phase I experimental taut
spring set up can be realized within interFoam
• cfMesh meshing tools proved to be an interesting alternative to the mainstream
snappyHexMesh utility for unstructured mesh creation
• CFD computations show very satisfying accordance on the natural frequencies
obtained with free decay tests, showing the correct modelling of added mass
and validating the coupling between rigid body motion and ﬂuid motions within
interFoam

7.3
Further development
The CFD workﬂow in OpenFOAM can be tedious, and very time consuming, due to
the high number of parameters. A workﬂow and some guidelines leading to a stable
conﬁguration was described throughout this Thesis, but further work should be done to
better understand the impact of diﬀerent parameters on the ﬂow, and the forces on the
ﬂoating body.
The meshing tool cartesianMesh from the cfMesh toolbox proved to be quite modular,
leading to a high quality meshing around the extremely complex DeepCwind geometry.
Its stability and output should be compared with other unstructured meshing utilities
such as snappyHexMesh, and the cfMesh tetMesh and pMesh.
The DeepCwind ﬂoater used within the OC3 to OC6 project proved hard to mesh, due
to very diﬃcult areas at the cross braces joints. Geometry work could be carried out
to simplify the geometry of the joints within a CAD software. It would signiﬁcantly
reduce the amount of cells needed to mesh the ﬂoater’s surface, and increase the quality
of the mesh, hence the quality of results and stability of simulations, while reducing the
computational cost.
The impact of turbulence modelling should be assessed, to justify the use of laminar
simulations or not. Moreover, a more thorough sensitivity analysis should be carried out
on the various solvers and discretization schemes settings.
Using the described numerical wave tank that was shown operational within this work, a
more detailed study on the non-linear eﬀects of the ﬂow should be done to assess the
gain from engineering models.
More complex mooring models should be investigated to widened the scope of use for
CFD modelling. Catenary based restraints, and coupled FEM models should be applied
to ﬂoating foundations to prove the feasibility of using CFD for foundation and mooring
design.
