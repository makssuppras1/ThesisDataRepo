Abstract
This thesis addresses three current challenges: housing shortage, a stagnant construcôÙ
tion sector, and environmental pollution from this sector. The research utilizes BioôÙPolymer
Concrete (BPC) as an experimental material, allowing additive manufacturing with a maxôÙ
imum inclination of 80 degrees to the horizontal. Drawing inspiration from historical strucôÙ
tural optimization and bioôÙinspired concepts, the aim of the design is to fully exploit the
materialãs capabilities. The significant achievement of this study is the successful realizaôÙ
tion of a prototype through 3DôÙprinting using a multiôÙaxial robotic arm, incorporating inôÙ
clined layers, based on the defined design. The application of multiôÙaxial inclined layering
presents potential opportunities for utilizing other materials in the 3DôÙprinting construction
industry.

1.1
Research Background and Motivation
The research project aims to investigate the combination of a bioôÙbased composite maôÙ
terial and additive manufacturing (3DôÙPrinting) for the construction of strong and durable
structures. The motivation behind this research lies in the potential to create optimized
structures by strategically placing the material within a structure or structural element usôÙ
ing this new building method. The idea behind the composite material used in this project
was to use a natural binder, derived from renewable and locally available resources such
as food waste or production byproducts.
While the strength of the bioôÙpolymer composite (BPC) may not match that of standard
cementitious concrete, the use of topologyôÙoptimisation and formôÙfinding can compensate
for this by creating structures with significant robustness. This concept draws inspiration
from natureãs structural design, where millions of years of evolution have resulted in efôÙ
ficient solutions using ordinary and natural materials. Biomimicry, the study of natureãs
solutions and their application in technical contexts, serves as a parallel aspect to this
research.
The central focus of this project is to explore the possibilities of optimized structural shapôÙ
ing using the newly developed BioôÙPolymer Concrete in combination with multiôÙaxial adôÙ
ditive manufacturing. By investigating the doability of this technology in form finding and
manufacturing, the research aims to identify suitable case studies that encompass historôÙ
ical and modern approaches to structural optimization, as well as draw inspiration from
biomimicry.
By utilizing the material and manufacturing methodãs flexibility in shaping both the exterior
and interior of structures and structural elements, the research seeks to determine the
practical limits of this approach. With the current means available, the project aims to push
the boundaries of this technology and assess its capabilities and limitations. It will further
evaluate whether any limitations can be attributed to the material itself, the manufacturing
process, or other factors. The practical knowledge gained from a number of case studies
will help optimize the production process.
The outcomes of this study will provide valuable insights for the advancement of multiôÙ
axial additive manufacturing and its potential applications in the construction industry, as
well as for bioôÙpolymer concrete.
The practical aspects of this masterãs thesis were carried out in collaboration with Matej
Durcik [152], a student in Civil Engineering at DTU. His own masterãs thesis is closely
related to this research, with a specific focus on the structural aspect and the prediction

stress would affect extrudability and potentially clog the printing nozzle. Thus, a good
balance between both extremes is required to ensure easy pumping of fluid while firmly
adhering to other layers when extruded.

4.2
Comparing 3DCP with Masonry
Masonry constructions have a long history dating back 10,000 years when civilizations
began utilizing stacking stones or sunôÙdried mud to provide shelter. Over time, firing
techniques for brick production have continued to advance, culminating in their standardôÙ
ization during the Industrial Revolution. Although concrete block structures have largely
replaced brick masonry in modern times, the principle of stacking is still widely employed
in the construction industry [79]. Within this section, a comparison will be drawn between
traditional masonry structures and 3DôÙConcrete Printing with horizontal layers. These
similarities have been stated already by pioneers in the 3DCP world.
Applying the principles of unreinforced masonry to the design of 3D printed structures
using a MARA and BPC can offer several benefits. Firstly, it allows for the precise placeôÙ
ment of concrete only where necessary along the compressive flow of forces, thereby
reducing the overall amount of concrete needed. Secondly, it can reduce the amount of
tensile strength required by designing the structure to be compressionôÙappropriate, which
is strongly depending on the overall shape of the structure [59].
A masonry structure is traditionally built by placing discrete elements, such as bricks, in
three dimensions and binding them together with a mortar for stability. On the other hand,
3DôÙprinting with concrete creates a structure using a continuous layer defined by only
two dimensions (height and width), with bonding between layers provided by the material
itself. The stability of the 3DôÙprinted structure depends on its geometry, gravity and the
properties of the material. [76]

The Voronoi pattern can be mathematically defined as ãthe complete separation of space,
which is determined by a distance criterion with a group of generatorsã [70]. The tesselôÙ
lation is created from a predetermined set of points n, or ãseeds,ã scattered on a defined
surface. These n points then subdivide the surface into n cells, each of which contains
points that are the closest to the seed from which they grew. The boundaries of all the
cells are located exactly in the middle of two neighboring seeds. In other words, it can
also be understood as a set of points growing towards each other simultaneously and
stopping at the midway point between them.
The design of the Voronoi diagram is influenced by the regularity of the seed grid. Its dual
is the regular Delaunay disposition of seeds, which are arranged in equilateral triangles
onto the surface. When the Voronoi diagram is generated from this regular set of points,
a regular hexagonal pattern is produced, as depicted in the figure below. In contrast, the
nonôÙregular randomized seed disposition leads to a nonôÙuniform Voronoi structure forôÙ

5.2.2
Mechanical Properties ôÙ Literature Review
To support the investigation of a Voronoi diagram optimized shell structure, several studies
have analyzed this nonôÙuniform pattern with mechanical testing. However, it is essential
to note that this pattern may not necessarily offer structural advantages to the design
outcome of this research. It would be necessary to compare its structural performance
with other conventional patterns applied to shell design, which is discussed in Section 5.3.
Sehat S. et al. [67] conducted a study where various 2D beams were designed, consisting
of Voronoi cell areas with equal tubular crossôÙsections. The study also examined the
effect of altering the uniformity of the Voronoi cells on the structure. The uniformity of
the cells was evaluated by controlling the difference between the maximum and minimum
cell sizes, with bigger differences resulting in lower uniformity. The study concluded that
more uniform Voronoi structures should be used for higher loadôÙbearing capacity and
Exploration of Architectural Building Design for multiôÙaxial 3D Printing with BioôÙPolymer Concrete.
51
==PAGE:71==
lower deflection. Increasing the cell area would reduce the loadôÙbearing capacity and
lower the structureãs overall weight, while decreasing the cell area would increase the
loadôÙtoôÙdisplacement ratio. In their conclusion, the authors stated that Voronoi structures
can bear loads as well as conventional structural beams while allowing for aesthetically
pleasing geometries in modern structures. [67]
Another paper states that long Voronoi cells could potentially improve mechanical perôÙ
formance.
Nevertheless, the stretching of the cells would also show higher levels of
anisotropy in the mechanical properties [62].
A third scientific article [68] aims to create a 3D Voronoi pattern in a cube composed
of interconnected tubular components. The goal is to optimize the model to achieve a
structure that is both lightweight and rigid, which reduces material usage while maintainôÙ
ing stability. The optimized Voronoi structure was found to be lighter and stiffer than a
triangular grid structure. [68]
Mele E. et al. [65] and Angelucci G. et al. [63] conducted a study on the mechanical
properties of nonôÙconventional structural patterns arranged in regular (Hexagrids) and
irregular (Voronoi tessellation) arrays by perturbating the regular honeycomb pattern. This
pattern was applied to the outer shells of highôÙrise buildings and evaluated according to
lateral displacement. The effectiveness of these patterns for stiffness was assessed and
the study concluded that the mechanical response of Voronoi patterns varies significantly
depending on the level of irregularity and density. The best design outcome according
to stiffness is the gradually increasing Voronoi density towards the base of the building
with constant crossôÙsection. The authors suggest that analyzing these nonôÙconventional,
irregular patterns for weight and loadôÙbearing efficiency is worthwhile, but note that the
economic aspect remains a challenge due to the use of various shapes. [65], [63]
Previously mentioned research papers donãt conclude that the Voronoi structure is the
most efficient method for subdividing a surface to achieve improved structural properties.
Knowledge regarding its structural properties has been primarily limited to theoretical reôÙ
search, due to the recent adoption of AM in the construction industry. According to Mele
E. et al. [65], the practical application of VoronoiôÙoptimized structures still presents ecoôÙ
nomic challenges due to manufacturing limitations. That is why there has only been limited
exploration of their mechanical properties.
As a conclusion to the literature review, it is evident that Voronoi structures offer enhanced
rigidity and reduced weight when implemented in a structure. However, this outcome is
not exclusive to Voronoi structures but applies to various, more conventional forms of
structural optimization. To address these doubts, Section 5.3 will conduct experimental
tests on different structural tessellations.

6.2
Preparation for manufacturing of the designed structure:
Slicing Protocol
Before extruding the BPC hemispherical dome, it is necessary to divide the 3D model into
layers of a specific thickness. This process, known as slicing, can be performed using
either planar layers or nonôÙplanar layers. Slicing a 3D model involves extracting points or
planes with a defined origin and converting that information into a code that can be utilized
to give motion instructions to the RA. The most common code used for slicing models into
horizontal layers is GôÙcode, which is compatible with computer numerical control (CNC)
machines like 3ôÙaxis 3D printers, milling machines [148], but also gantry 3DôÙConcrete
Printers.
The GôÙcode is made of a number of commands with single letters followed by a number
providing the specific value. An example of this kind of command in AM is: G1 F500
X25.00 Y33.33 Z23.00 E1.80 which is instructing a motion, with positions XYZ, linear
movements G1, spindle speed (S), feed rates (F) and extrusion height (E). Many software
options are available that allow for the import of an STL file and automatic horizontal
slicing, generating readyôÙtoôÙuse GôÙcode. However, slicing 3D models with nonôÙplanar
layers presents more challenges.
The first subsection will introduce the method used by Leusink S. [10] for slicing a model
specifically for the KRA using KUKA Robot Language (KRL). Following that, the differôÙ
ences between KRLôÙcode and the RAPID Language used by the ABB RA will be exôÙ
plained. Additionally, reasons for not utilizing Leusinkãs slicing method will be provided.

General Slicing Protocol to KRL
The underlying steps of the slicing protocol are the following. A 3DôÙmodel (CADôÙfile) needs
to be exported to an STLôÙfile. The STLôÙfile contains a number of interconnected meshes
(triangular surfaces) that describe a 3DôÙsurface. These meshes are then contracted to a
skeleton mesh that can be used to fit a curve, resulting in a skeleton spline. [10]
Figure 6.4: Spline fitting process. Left to right: original mesh, mesh contraction, skeleton
mesh, spline fitting, dividing the spline in a number of nonôÙplanar planes with N (Normal)
and P (Origin) [10]
This skeleton spline would then be split into a certain amount of points with a given disôÙ
tance in between those points giving the desired layer thickness. This process resulted in
defining the number of layers needed and this was translated to a number of planes. The
slicing operation was then finalized by calculating the intersection of the meshes from the
STLôÙfile with the planes extracted previously. The input planes are defined by the plane
origin P = (x0, y0, z0) and a normal vector to the plane N = [A, B, C]. Figure 6.4 shows
all the previous steps. The intersections result in a set of lines that define the path that
the RA should follow. The following steps include postôÙslicing which consists in reordering
and connecting the line elements. [10]
The orientation of the planes has to be defined with ABC angles based on its normal
vector. The tool attached to the KRA head defines the A (rotation around zôÙaxis) and B
(angle to the horizontal plane) angles. Angle C is used to define the rotation of the RA with
respect to the extruder. These angles should all be kept between +180ô¯ and ôÙ180ô¯. After
all these operations, the print path can be stored as a GôÙcode. Afterward, this GôÙcode
should be translated to the KRL with specific syntaxes. This is how the coordinate in KRL
looks like for a ãpointôÙtoôÙpointã (PTP) motion: PTP : X10.0, Y 20.0, Z0.0, A20, B7.5, C90.
[10]
Specific Slicing Protocol to KRL for Domes
According to Leusink S., the slicing of the dome is more complex as the result consists
of nonôÙplanar layers following the curvature of the dome on all sides. In the case of a
dome these contracted meshes wouldnãt describe the curvature of the wall. This is why
the dome should be split into smaller elements which would result in better modeling of
the curvature of the dome in the contracted skeleton meshes. While the details of the next
steps of the slicing protocol are not relevant to mention here, Leusink S. has developed
a specific example that demonstrates the feasibility of nonôÙplanar slicing for domes. [10]

The oven was located near the printing setup to facilitate the transfer of heated bags

cartridge. When multiple bags were introduced simultaneously in the oven, the material
heated up slowly. It is advisable to have a good rotation principle of bags being in the
oven, starting with five bags and replacing one bag with a new one after use.
Meanwhile, the extruder unit had to be heated with a heating coil until it reached an internal
temperature of 60ô¯C.

air remained in the bag, it would significantly affect the consistency and flow of the layers
during printing, resulting in many holes in the layers. These holes result in smaller crossôÙ
sections, potentially compromising the structural integrity. This kneading process left the
bag partially filled, containing less material than expected. This should be considered
when estimating the required amount of material for a specific structure.

the bags were not completely filled to allow for the material to spread inside the bag to

This research aimed to explore the design possibilities of optimized structural shaping
using BioôÙPolymer Concrete (BPC) in combination with multiôÙaxial additive manufacturôÙ

7.1
Conclusion
Summary of the obtained results.
After an extensive introduction of the employed methods in this practical implementation
of AM and BPC, a detailed analysis was conducted to select the most suitable construction
considering the given boundary conditions. Domes were deliberately chosen as the optiôÙ
mal structures to push the limits of the material and construction technique. This design
choice not only reflects a continuation of the historical optimization of structures, where
domes have been extensively utilized as shelters or for covering large areas, but also
allows for thorough testing and exploration of the capabilities of the chosen material.
The concept of bioôÙinspiration, specifically the idea of efficiently reducing material usôÙ
age through intelligent reinforcement, led to the exploration of Voronoi tessellation, a freôÙ
quently observed phenomenon in nature. Several case studies were conducted to comôÙ
pare the compressive strength of structures employing Voronoi tessellation with more
conventional grid structures. Although the results did not demonstrate the superiority of
Voronoi tessellation over conventional grids, the investigation provided valuable insights
into how this reinforcement approach could be adapted for practical extrusionôÙbased techôÙ
niques, potentially extending its applicability to conventional reinforcement methods.
The research successfully demonstrated the feasibility of using BPC in the construction
of a prototype structure using a RA. The printing setup designed for this purpose deôÙ
livered satisfactory results, although it became apparent that additional experience and
fineôÙtuning of manufacturing methods and setup usage were necessary for more precise
outcomes. Additionally, the use of BPC as mortar between elements proved to be highly
efficient, offering potential for future research.

Final Thoughts and Closing Statements.
In conclusion, it is evident that the widespread implementation of BPC 3DôÙprinting in the
construction sector is not an immediate possibility. Certain crucial aspects such as waterôÙ
resistance, fireôÙresistance, and reinforcement methods still require further exploration and

vidual elements that could be subsequently assembled using bioôÙpolymer mortar. This
method would eliminate the need for extensive formwork and allow for the creation of opôÙ
timized bricks. For instance, envisioning the construction of the ãStriatusã bridge by Zaha

Lastly, it is worth noting that the 3DôÙprinting process proved to be more demanding and
laborôÙintensive than initially anticipated. The constant engagement in overseeing the robot

Grasshopper and Karamba 3D Code Explained: Catenary Dome and
Voronoi Catenary Dome
To create compression only structures based on a given base shape, the Rhino plugôÙin
RhinoVault 2 can be used. In this case, the plugôÙin has been used to calculate a catenary
compressionôÙonly dome based on a circular base shape. The following images show the
process of how this has been done with the help of RhinoVault2.
1. Initialize RV2 on Rhino.
2. Draw the base shape with a curve and patch the curve to create a surface. Use
quadremesh to make a mesh of the given surface with a target quad count of around
10000 (create as many meshes as possible for more accuracy). In this case a circle
with a diameter of 3 meters has been drawn.
3. Use createpattern from RV2 and select the created mesh. This will create points at
every intersection of the given mesh.
4. Then with Define Boundary Conditions, select the option AllBoundaryVertices to
define all the points at the boundary of the given circle as supports.
5. A FormDiagram must then be created, this happens automatically by clicking on the
button.
6. A ForceDiagram should then be calculated automatically by clicking on the button.
This calculates force concentrations on the defined mesh.
7. The last step of the process is to click on VerticalEquilibrium which will calculate the
final compressionôÙonly structure. A TargetHeight of 2,3 meters has been given in
this case.
The following Figures D.26 and D.27 show the process and the final result of the catenary
dome modeling calculation with RhinoVault2.
