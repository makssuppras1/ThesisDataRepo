Abstract
This work investigates the secondary metabolites production of Aspergillus flavus, a species
well known for producing aflatoxins, some of the most potent natural carcinogens. However,
beyond aflatoxins, the broader chemical diversity of A. flavus secondary metabolites has
received less attention, despite its potential biotechnological applications. This study
expands on understanding A. flavus metabolic capabilities by employing a combination
of advanced analytical techniques to identify and structurally characterize novel compounds.
Four different A. flavus strains were cultivated on various agar-based media following
the One Strain Many Compounds (OSMAC) approach to maximize metabolite diver-
sity. High-performance liquid chromatography coupled with diode-array detection and
quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (HPLC-DAD-QTOF-MS) was used for prelim-
inary metabolite screening, with a targeted mass selected for further purification. Among
the tested conditions, A. flavus IBT 23106 grown on yeast extract agar (YES) exhibited
the highest production of an unknown compound.
To isolate and characterize this metabolite, liquid-liquid partitioning was performed,
yielding three distinct phases, of which the medium-polar fraction was further purified by
flash chromatography. The most promising fraction underwent preparative reversed-phase
liquid chromatography coupled with quadrupole mass spectrometry (RP-LC-QMS). The
purified compound was subsequently analyzed using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR),
including two-dimensional experiments.
The unknown compound was identified as a hybrid polyketide-nonribosomal peptide
belonging to the pyrononigrin family. Notably, this is the first report of this compound
being produced by a non-genetically modified A. flavus strain.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Aspergillus flavus is widely known for its ability to produce aflatoxins, which are highly
carcinogenic and hazardous to human health. While extensive research has been conducted
on aflatoxins, less is known about the other secondary metabolites produced by this species.
Within our research group, three different strains of Aspergillus flavus have been identified
that do not produce aflatoxins. Spectroscopy studies have shown that these strains produce
a blue fluorescent compound, suggesting the potential presence of novel or previously
unreported metabolite, that could potentially be a unique biomarker distinguishing A.
flavus from taxonomically closely species such as A. minisclerotigenes.
1.2
Aim
The primary aim of this thesis is to purify and elucidate the structures of secondary
metabolites produced by non-aflatoxin-producing strains of Aspergillus flavus, with a focus
on identifying novel and unique biomarkers for this species. However, other metabolites
produced by these strains will also be evaluated. To achieve this, a One Strain Many
Compounds (OSMAC) approach will be employed, wherein the strains will be cultured on
various media to induce diverse metabolite production. The optimal strain and medium
combination will be determined through a Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry
(LC-MS) metabolomics study.
The purification process will involve a combination of separation techniques, includ-
ing centrifugation, extraction, and chromatography. Structural elucidation of the purified
compounds will be carried out using Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy.

Background
This chapter provides the necessary background on Aspergillus flavus and its natural
products. It highlights some of the key compounds identified in the literature and it
discusses the chemical features commonly encountered in fungal metabolites. The chapter
will also cover the main analytical techniques used to study these natural products,
with a focus on Ultraviolet-visible (UV-vis) spectroscopy, Mass Spectrometry (MS), and
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR). Additionally, recent advancements in the field of
metabolomics, particularly the use of bioinformatics and data mining alongside traditional
analytical methods, will be introduced.
2.1
Fungal natural products
2.1.1
Mycology of Aspergillus flavus
Filamentous fungi are eukaryotes with chitinous cell walls and spores, and they are immotile
throughout their life cycle. Lacking an embryonic stage, they develop directly from spores,
which germinate into hyphae. The hyphae of an individual fungus form the mycelium.
Spores produced asexually are called conidia [1]. The different fungal phyla are shown in
Figure 2.1.
Aspergillus flavus belongs to the phylum Ascomycota, genus Aspergillus, section Flavi. Its
cell wall is mainly composed of glucans, chitin, and lipids [2]. Classified as a mold, it has a
characteristic fuzzy appearance due to hyphae that absorb nutrients. A. flavus reproduces
asexually through conidia, produced on conidiophores.
A saprophyte, A. flavus feeds on decaying organic matter. It is commonly isolated in
agriculture and medicine due to its ability to produce aflatoxins, which contaminate crops
and pose health risks. A. flavus is closely related to A. oryzae, a non-toxigenic species
used in food fermentation, and can be considered its domesticated form [3].
Distinguishing between A. flavus and A. oryzae is based on minor anatomical differ-
ences in the spores, and misclassification between the two has been reported in literature.
A. oryzae conidia are smoother, larger, and produce more amylase. The conidiophore stipes
are longer, the mycelium is more floccose, and the conidium color is light brownish-green,
while A. flavus conidia are yellowish grass-green. A. flavus also produces large, globose

2.1.2
Structural diversity of fungal natural products
Primary metabolism serves as the foundation for all organisms. In contrast, secondary
metabolism involves compounds whose function to the producing organism is not fully
understood. Secondary metabolic pathways can be switched off as the compounds are
not necessarily produced under all conditions by the organism. These compounds are
structurally diverse and they include polyketides, terpenoids, non-ribosomal peptides,
alkaloids and hybrid compounds. Each class of compounds will be briefly described in the
following sections.
Research conducted in the 1980s revealed that individual strains of filamentous fungi can
biosynthesize a wide variety of secondary metabolites. This diversity becomes particularly
evident when fungi are cultivated in different growth media, as each medium can induce

(OSMAC) by Bode et al. OSMAC highlights the remarkable capacity of a single fungal

2.1.3
Polyketides
The polyketide backbone is biosynthesised by a series of Claisen condensation reactions
(Figure 2.2). The Claisen reaction results in the formation of a bond between two carbon
atoms and is base catalysed. The base deprotonates an acid alfa proton between two
carbonylic carbons. The deprotonated carbon nucleophilically attacks to the electrophilic
carbonylic carbon of another molecule. This condensation reaction eliminates carbon
dioxide. Two units can be distinguished here, the starter unit and the extender unit.
The starter unit is typically activated acetic acid in the form of a thioester, known as
acetyl-CoA, while the extender unit is typically malonyl-CoA [6].
Figure 2.2: Claisen condensation reaction [7].
Polyketide biosynthesis is catalysed by specific enzymes, called polyketide synthases (PKSs).
There are different types of PKS. Type I PKSs are very large multifunctional proteins with
individual functional domains, while type II are complexes of individual mono-functional
proteins. Iterative type I PKSs use their functional domains repeatedly while non-iterative
PKS have a distinct active site for every step. Type I PKSs and type III PKSs are found
in fungi [8]. The enzyme activities for a modular PKS are reported below in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Enzyme activities from modular PKS [7].

2.1.6
Hybrid polyketides-nonribosomal peptides
Fungal PKS-NRPS hybrids produce a variety of structurally complex bioactive metabolites.
These enzymes consist of an N-terminal iterative PKS module and a C-terminal NRPS
module. In the NRPS module, the adenylation (A) domain activates and transfers an
amino acid to the thiolation (T) domain, while the condensation (C) domain facilitates
polyketide-amino acid fusion, forming an amide bond. Examples of metabolites synthesized
by these hybrids include isoflavipucine and pseurotin A [6].

2.1.8
Secondary metabolite production in Aspergillus flavus
Aspergillus flavus is a prolific producer of secondary metabolites. As mentioned before, A.
flavus can produce aflatoxins, a group of polyketide-based compounds with hepatocarcino-
genic properties. These compounds have been detected in various strains of A. flavus.
In addition to aflatoxins, A. flavus produces several other secondary metabolites with
distinct chemical structures and biological activities. For instance, cyclopiazonic acid is a
tetrameric hybrid polypeptide, a non-ribosomal peptide that incorporates a dimethylallyl
terpene unit. Another important compound produced by A. flavus is nitropropionic acid
and is known for its inhibitory effects on mitochondrial function. The structures of these
compounds are represented below (Figure 2.10). Aflatrem and β aflatrem, on the other
hand, are indoloterpenes, a class of metabolites derived from tryptophan and terpenoid
precursors and they have been detected in the sclerotia of A. flavus [4].

2.2
Analytical methods for natural product discovery
2.2.1
Sample preparation for chromatography
Sample preparation is the process of transforming a sample into a form that is suitable for
analysis. The preparation of secondary metabolite samples typically involves extraction
and chromatography, and is followed by sample analysis (Figure 2.11). The primary
goal of sample preparation is to stop all metabolic activity immediately and to leave all
metabolites intact. An effective preparation method should be simple and efficient.
Figure 2.11: Typical workflow for metabolite sample preparation and analysis.
Extraction is the physical transfer of analytes from one phase to another. For fungal
metabolites, organic solvents such as acetonitrile, methanol, and isopropanol are commonly
used. These solvents are used for liquid-liquid extraction (LLE), which relies on the
different solubility of metabolites in two immiscible phases, typically an aqueous and an
organic phase [12].
Solid-phase extraction (SPE) is an alternative to LLE and it exhibits reduced organic
solvent usage, shorter processing times, and enhanced selectivity for targeted analytes. A
main limitation of SPE is that it is less suitable for untargeted analysis, and its experimen-

tal setup generally requires more careful consideration compared to LLE. SPE uses a solid
sorbent material that selectively retains target compounds based on their physicochemical
properties. Common sorbents include C8, C18, and silica gel. In an SPE workflow, the
sample is loaded onto the column, where the target analytes are selectively retained on
the sorbent, while unwanted matrix components are washed away. The retained analytes
are then eluted using an appropriate solvent and collected for further analysis [13]. The
extract containing the target still contains many other interfering compounds, which can
be separated using chromatography.
2.2.2
Chromatography
Essentially, chromatography consists of a mobile phase, stationary phase and the sample.
Liquid chromatography is performed with a liquid mobile phase. Gas chromatography
uses instead a gas mobile phase and it is typically used for volatile organic compounds.
Since many compounds are not volatile, liquid chromatography (LC) dominates and is
more commonly used.
There are different mechanisms of interaction by which the analyte interacts with the
stationary phase and the mobile phase (Figure 2.12). These mechanisms include adsorption,
partition, ion-exchange, size exclusion, and affinity.
Figure 2.12: Overview of chromatographic techniques and their interaction mechanisms
(Pavel N Nesterenko, 2019) [14].

Adsorption chromatography uses a solid support and a liquid or gas phase. The solute is
adsorbed onto the surface of the solid stationary phase. More strongly adsorbed compounds
are retained longer on the column. In partition chromatography, a liquid stationary phase
is bonded to a solid surface, the solute equilibrates between the solid and stationary phase
and the separation is based on the different solubility in each phase [14].
Ion-exchange chromatography separates ions and ionizable molecules based on their
electrostatic interactions with a charged stationary phase. It operates in two modes:
cation-exchange, where the stationary phase is negatively charged and attracts positively
charged analytes, and anion-exchange, where the stationary phase is positively charged
and interacts with negatively charged analytes. Separation depends on the strength of
these interactions, influenced by analyte charge, density, and surface distribution. The
ionic strength of the mobile phase is gradually increased to displace bound analytes, with
elution times determined by the strength of their interactions [15].
Size-exclusion chromatography separates molecules based on their size by employing
differential pore sizes in the column’s packing material. The separation relies on the
relationship between the analyte’s size and the pores of the stationary phase within the
column. Larger molecules, which cannot enter the pores, are eluted first, while smaller
molecules penetrate the packing material’s pores and are eluted later, requiring a greater
elution volume. A commonly used gel material in SEC is Sephadex, a medium consisting
of cross-linking dextran with epichlorohydrin [16].
Affinity chromatography separates molecules based on specific biological interactions
between a target molecule and an immobilized binding agent. The stationary phase is
typically an antibody, enzyme, or receptor that selectively binds its complementary partner.
This selective and reversible binding allows the purification of the target compound from
highly complex mixtures. Molecules that do not interact with the immobilized ligand
are eluted first, while the retained target is recovered later using an appropriate elution
method. Agarose-based resins and synthetic polymers are commonly used as supports in
affinity chromatography columns [17].
Chromatography can be classified based on the composition of the mobile phase, which is a
critical component of the separation process. The mobile phase can either remain constant

throughout the separation or vary over time, leading to two primary modes: isocratic elu-
tion and gradient elution. In isocratic elution, the composition of the mobile phase is kept

consistent retention times. Isocratic elution is often used in simpler separations or when
analyzing samples with a narrow range of polarity or size. On the other hand, gradient
elution involves a systematic variation in the composition of the mobile phase during the
separation. This approach is especially beneficial when dealing with complex mixtures con-

2.2.3
High performance liquid chromatography
High-Performance Liquid Chromatography employs high pressures to send solvent through
columns containing fine particles, enabling high-resolution separations. For analytical
purposes, particles of 2-5 µm are common. HPLC coupled with MS has become the most
employed analytical platform in metabolomics. An HPLC system has an autosampler, a
solvent delivery system, a sample injection valve, a high pressure column, a detector and a
computer for data visualization (Figure 2.13).
Figure 2.13: Diagram of HPLC system components [14].
The most common stationary phase support is high purity microporous silica (normal
phase), often modified with bonded agents like octadecyl and therefore operated in reverse
phase. Reverse phase LC often employs a gradient of water and organic solvents, such as

==PAGE:22==
acetonitrile and methanol for efficient metabolite separation [20].
Different types of columns can be used, analytical columns are used for trace analy-
sis, while preparative columns handle higher concentrations. Smaller particle sizes increase
efficiency by improving mass transfer and theoretical plate numbers but necessitate precise
pumps for high-pressure operation. Guard columns, containing the same stationary phase,
protect the main column from blockages and impurities. The sample ultimately reaches
the detector, which identifies analytes based on their distinct physical-chemical properties
and include UV detectors, fluorescence detectors, and mass spectrometers. Each type
offers unique strengths, and are often used in combination. The overall system is controlled
by a computer which will display a chromatogram, which is a graph showing the detector
response as a function of retention time [21].
2.2.4
Ultraviolet visible spectroscopy
Spectrophotometry is any technique that uses light to measure chemical concentrations.
The application of UV-vis spectroscopy is vast and can be employed to evaluate the disso-
lution of active pharmaceutical ingredients from formulations, the stability of compounds,
reaction kinetics, equilibria, metal or ligand binding, and quantitative analysis.
A schematic UV detector is reported in the figure below, consisting of a light source
that covers the UV-vis spectrum, a diffraction grafting, a cell containing the sample and
the diode array component (Figure 2.14).
When a specific molecule absorbs a photon, its energy increases, and it is promoted
to an excited state. Conversely, when a molecule emits a photon, its energy decreases,
returning to a lower energy state. The lowest energy state of a molecule is referred to
as the ground state. Ultraviolet and visible light promotes electrons to higher energy
orbitals. When light is absorbed by a sample, the irradiance of the light beam decreases [22].
Irradiance P is defined as the energy per second per unit area of the light beam. To select
one wavelength, light passes through a monochromator, producing monochromatic light,
which then travels through a sample along a path length b. Some of the light is absorbed
by the sample, so P < P0, where P0 is the irradiance of the incident light [23].

2.2.5
Mass spectrometry
Mass spectrometry provides information about the molecular mass of compounds, which
can lead to the generation of the molecular formula of a compound. The mass spectrometer
can separate and detect ions based on their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z), allowing for the
distinction of isotopes, which are atoms with the same number of protons but differing
numbers of neutrons [26].
The instrumentation consists of an ion source, ion lenses, a mass analyzer, a detec-
tor, and a data system to process the data. The system operates under high vacuum to
prevent ions from colliding with each other or with carrier gas molecules. Such collisions
could disrupt electron movement during the ion formation step and stop the ionization
process.
In liquid chromatography (LC), the sample enters in its liquid phase. The most commonly

(Figure 2.15). A high voltage applied to the droplets causes them to become charged [27].

adducts from salt impurities are possible as well as dimers due to the high energy of
MS [28]. The MS1 spectrum can provide the exact mass of a compound and also the
isotopic pattern, which helps in quickly identifying the presence or absence of a few atoms,
particularly Cl, Br and S.

Detector saturation and ion suppression are two significant challenges that impact the
accuracy and dynamic range of mass spectrometry analyses. Detector saturation occurs
when ion intensities exceed the capacity of the detection system, causing signal distortion
and inaccurate quantitation. This is particularly problematic in high-abundance species,
where the mass spectrometer fails to register the true intensity of the peaks. Saturation

tion. This phenomenon is common in electrospray ionization (ESI), where high-abundance
compounds can reduce the ionization of lower-abundance species, leading to diminished

A quadrupole consists of four rods, two positively charged and two negatively charged

the ion trajectories, while the DC voltage stabilizes oscillations for specific m/z ions. The
quadrupole functions as a mass filter, allowing only ions with a specific m/z to pass through
[32].
TOF analyzers use a long tube under high vacuum to separate ions based on their m/z
and velocity. The instrument measures the time required for ions to travel through the

Nuclear magnetic resonance
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is an analytical technique that uses the
magnetic properties of atomic nuclei to study the structure and dynamics of molecules.
When certain nuclei, like 1H and 13C, are placed in a strong magnetic field, they align
Purification and structural elucidation of natural products from Aspergillus flavus

==PAGE:28==
with or against the field due to their intrinsic spin. Short bursts of radiofrequency energy
are applied to disrupt this alignment, exciting the nuclei to higher energy states. As the
nuclei relax back to their equilibrium positions, they generate a free induction decay (FID)
signal, which is a time-dependent oscillation of radiofrequency energy. This signal is de-
tected and then processed using Fourier transformation to produce an NMR spectrum. [34].
The superconducting magnet is the core of the NMR instrumentation, providing the
stable, high-intensity magnetic field (Fig. 2.18). Surrounding this core, a probe houses the
sample. The RF transmitters generate precisely controlled pulses, while the RF receiver
captures the resulting free induction decay (FID) signal, which is then amplified by the RF
preamplifier to enhance sensitivity. Modern setups integrate analog-to-digital conversion
(ADC) and digital signal processing, enhancing precision. The processed data is ultimately
sent to a host computer, where it is transformed into an interpretable NMR spectrum [35].
Figure 2.18: NMR instrumental components [35].

but can be limited in resolving complex structures due to overlapping signals. Two-

information over two frequency dimensions, enhancing resolution and enabling the identifi-
cation of correlations between nuclei. Among the various 2D NMR experiments, homonu-

2.2.7
Advancements in metabolomics and open source tools
Metabolomics is the study of metabolites within biological matrices, offering valuable
insights into identification of e.g. fungal secondary metabolites as in this Thesis. The
metabolome can be analyzed using two primary approaches, targeted and untargeted
metabolomics, each tailored to distinct research objectives [38].
Targeted metabolomics focuses on the precise analysis of specific metabolites, typically se-
lected based on prior knowledge or biological relevance. This hypothesis-driven approach is
particularly advantageous for validating specific biomarkers or studying well-characterized
pathways with high sensitivity and accuracy. In contrast, untargeted metabolomics pro-
vides a comprehensive overview of all detectable metabolites within a sample. By avoiding
predefined targets, untargeted analysis facilitates the discovery of novel metabolites and
metabolic signatures [39].
Some metabolomic studies lack standardization, prompting initiatives to establish clearer
guidelines. Figure 2.20 illustrates an example of a proper metabolomics workflow.

The sample size determination is a balancing act, as a low sample size can lead to errors
and lack of precision and a high sample size can lead to a waste of labour and consumables
for minimal information gain. Different numbers of replicates have been used, with 3 being

Dereplication is a critical process in metabolomics and natural product research, aimed at
identifying previously discovered compounds in new samples to avoid redundant efforts in
discovering already known metabolites. This is particularly important in the exploration
of complex natural sources, where the structural diversity of bioactive compounds is vast.
Without dereplication, the identification of novel metabolites may be hindered by the pres-
ence of known compounds, which can obscure the discovery of new bioactive substances [40].
In metabolomics studies LC-MS is one of the most widely used analytical platforms.

However, no single platform can comprehensively cover the entire metabolome due to
the vast diversity and complexity of metabolites. The challenge of annotating metabo-
lites arises because many of the peaks observed in chromatograms remain structurally
unknown. To address this, the Metabolomics Standardization Initiative has outlined four
levels of confidence for metabolite annotation: identified compounds, putative annotated
compounds, putative compound classes, and unknown compounds. The process of derepli-
cation significantly enhances the annotation process by enabling researchers to compare
experimental data against online databases and libraries [40].
In this context, libraries such as MassBank of North America, GNPS, MZMine play
a crucial role [41]. They contain high-resolution mass spectra of known metabolites,
and by matching experimental spectra with those in these libraries, compounds can be
rapidly identified. This approach helps in distinguishing novel substances from previously
characterized ones, saving time and resources. However, for accurate dereplication, it
is essential that the conditions under which the spectra were acquired-such as the mass
analyzer, collision energy, and other experimental parameters—match the conditions under
which the data is being investigated [42].
One of the most effective ways to accelerate dereplication is the combination of UHPLC-
DAD-QTOFMS with comprehensive MS/HRMS libraries. Ultra High-Performance Liquid
Chromatography (UHPLC) enables high-resolution separation of metabolites, facilitating
the detection of compounds that may co-elute in traditional chromatography methods.
The Diode Array Detector (DAD) provides complementary UV-Vis absorption spectra,
aiding in the identification of known bioactive metabolites based on their UV signatures.
Coupled with Quadrupole Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometry (QTOFMS), which offers
accurate mass measurements and structural information, this setup enables the detection
of a wide range of metabolites, from small to large, contributing to a more detailed and
comprehensive metabolomic profile [40].
NMR spectroscopy has several key advantages that make it a useful tool for metabolomics
studies. In particular, it is a non-destructive technique; it provides structural information,
and it does not require sample separation, setting it apart from MS analysis. However,
some limitations have hindered this technique from expressing its full potential in the field,
including limited spectral resolution and high-field instrument availability [43].

Significant progress has been made in recent years to overcome these challenges. Pure
shift methods have been implemented in the field of metabolomics to improve resolution,
thus facilitating peak assignments [44]. A common experiment is the pure shift yielded
by chirp excitation (PSYCHE), where homonuclear proton couplings are removed. This
experiment consists of a hard 180 degree pulse followed by two, low power small angle
pulses. Simultaneously, a weak pulse field gradient to suppress unwanted transfer path-
ways [45, 46]. Furthermore, there have been improvements in NMR hardware. Ultra-
field instruments allow higher sensitivity and resolution, increasing the coverage range of
metabolites. More than ten 1.2 GHz instruments have been deployed or are due in Europe,
including in Florence, Italy and Utrecht, Netherlands. [47]. Concerning data analysis, it
has been reported that there is a lack of uniformity in the application of both univariate
and multivariate statistical methods. The diversity of statistical approaches used can lead
to inconsistent results, making it difficult to compare findings across different studies. In
addition to this, there is a disparity in the software used for data processing, with some
of the most commonly used platforms being Chenomx and TopSpin. The absence of a
standardized workflow complicates cross-study comparisons. This has reinforced the need
for common framework for sample preparation, data collection and data processing [48].
Structure elucidation in metabolomics plays a crucial role in identifying unknown metabo-
lites, especially those not present in existing databases. Traditional methods require
extensive purification and isolation of metabolites for detailed analysis using NMR and
other spectroscopic techniques. However, this approach is often time-consuming and
impractical for low-abundance metabolites. As an alternative, structure elucidation can be
performed directly in complex mixtures, such as crude extracts or partially fractionated
samples, enabling high-throughput analysis. Three main strategies have been proposed
for this purpose. The first approach relies on LC-MS and LC-MS/MS techniques, where
experimental MS/MS spectra of unknown compounds are compared with predicted frag-
mentation patterns of candidate structures using computational tools like MetFrag and
CSI:FingerID. The second strategy utilizes multidimensional NMR spectroscopy, where
experimental chemical shifts are sequentially assigned and verified through quantum
NMR chemical shift predictions. These modern approaches significantly improve the
efficiency and accuracy of metabolite identification, overcoming limitations associated with
traditional isolation-based methods [49].

Preparative LC
The selected sample, fraction 5 of 20 from the Isolera fractionation, corresponding to
internal code AROMERO00275, was purified using preparative LC on an Agilent 1260
Infinity II system equipped with a ThermoPrep column (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 150 mm
× 20 mm, particle size 5 µm). The mobile phase consisted of two HPLC-grade solvents,
acetonitrile and water, both containing 0.1% formic acid. Solvents were ultrasonicated and
degassed before use. A linear gradient was applied, increasing from 10% to 80% acetonitrile
over 60 minutes, with a flow rate of 9 mL/min,corresponding to a system pressure of 42 bar.
The column was maintained at room temperature, and dry loading was performed without
injection. The sample was dissolved in a mixture of methanol and dichloromethane (DCM),
preadsorbed onto C18 silica phase, dried, and then loaded onto the sample cartridge.
Detection was carried out using both UV and quadrupole mass spectrometry. Purified
fractions were collected using a 60-slot fraction collector.
Preparative LC was employed to isolate the target compound efficiently while mini-
mizing interference from kojic acid. Due to its high water solubility, kojic acid was rapidly
eluted using a fast gradient with a high percentage of water, effectively removing it early

5
Conclusion
This study contributes to the broader effort of linking natural products to their corre-
sponding biosynthetic gene clusters in fungi. By focusing on non-aflatoxin-producing
Aspergillus flavus strains and employing an OSMAC approach, the metabolite produc-
tion was investigated. While several compounds were automatically annotated and set
aside, a specific unknown metabolite was targeted for structural elucidation. Through
large-scale cultivation, liquid-liquid extraction, flash chromatography, and preparative
chromatography, the compound was obtained in sufficient purity and quantity. NMR
analysis revealed a structure characterized by a long aliphatic chain, an oxygen-containing
ring, and a lactam. While structurally similar compounds have been identified in other
microorganisms, this is the first report of such a metabolite in Aspergillus flavus.
Future studies should include detailed fluorescence characterization of this compound.
Additionally, further exploration of the biological activity and potential applications of
this newly identified metabolite is to be investigated. Expanding its structural analogs,
evaluating its bioactivity, and performing genome-mining approaches could uncover its
broader biotechnological relevance.
