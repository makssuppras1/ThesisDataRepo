Abstract
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate different natural ventilation strategies and their
effect on ventilation and indoor environment in a generic representation of a classroom, while
simultaneously varying various parameters assumed to affect the indoor climate. The aim was
an evaluation of the effect of a given strategy or parameter variation, as seen in regard to
ventilation, flow and indoor climate parameters. As a mean of validation, the results were
compared to a real-life case study and similar theoretical expressions.
The study was split in three sections, with the main one concerning the process of building,
simulating, analysing and discussing results from a total of 28 computational fluid dynamic
models made to resemble a generic model of a typical danish classroom. The process was
carried out through the use of the program Ansys Fluent Workbench. Similarly a comparison
was made to measurements of velocity and air change rate carried out at a physical location,
and theoretical expressions for the volume flow rate through openings.
The main study concluded that neither of the four ventilation strategies in themselves could
be said to provide an overall adequate base for an acceptable indoor environment at steady
state conditions. At the same time the study indicated, that a combination of the strategies
with other methods of ventilation might be beneficial for the overall design and renovation pos-
sibilities. This might also be seen in relation to the possibilities of combining the single- and
cross side ventilation strategies in situations where the tolerated level of potential discomfort
could be disregarded to various degree. The investigation of the parameter variations showed
a tendency for the indoor environment to mainly be affected by changes in wind speed and
direction, while the remaining parameters did not lead to noticeable changes. The comparison
with the case study and theoretical expressions did not results in large differences, but neither
did it show a overall uniform tendency in the differences. It was assumed that the CFD models
thus to a degree were able to describe broad tendencies across the four ventilation strategies
and parameter variations.

Introduction
Living in today’s society, an increasingly recurring topic in almost every aspect of our daily life
is considerations towards the environment and a more sustainable future. An important part of
this concerns our generation and use of energy, which inevitably is due to experience nothing
less than a revolution through the coming years. In accordance with both international climate
obligations and high national ambition, Denmark has committed to a number of actions that
regulates and reforms the energy sector towards a more sustainable future. The overall target
is to transform Denmark into a low-emission society before the year 2050, which amongst
other includes: lowering the emission of greenhouse gasses from buildings, agriculture and
transportation; increasing the fraction of renewable energy in the total energy consumption; and
increasing the overall energy efficiency [1]. In a fact-sheet from 2017 concerning the distribution
of the building stock in the EU (based on data from roughly 23 of the total european floor area),
Buildings Performance Institute Europe (BPIE) estimates that 75% of the building stock in the
union is to be considered energy inefficient, and that 97% of the building stock needs to be
upgraded, in order to achieve the EU’s 2050 decarbonisation vision. With a share of highly
efficient buildings of 7%, Denmark is one of the leading countries on this area.
Nevertheless, we still have a long way to go. An area that have thus received much attention
the recent years, concerns the condition and state of our schools, which often consist of older
buildings ripe for energy renovations. At the same time, it is a simple fact that many older
and un-renovated buildings more often than not relies on a principle of natural ventilation, as
the primary mean of ventilation. As the process of energy optimization often relates to an in-
creasingly more airtight facade system, due to extra insulation and energy savings on heating,
concerns have been expressed for the general state of the indoor environment, as the process
thus naturally affects the effectiveness of the ventilation in such buildings. Through the years
2009, 2014 and 2021, Clausen and Toftum et al. carried out three separate mass-studies of
the indoor environment in danish schools, with the general aim of gaining more knowledge and
data about the development and current state of the indoor environment and its subsequent
impact on the students [2, 3, 4]. Measurements were made in a total of 700 to 800 classrooms
that was distributed between 200 to 300 schools/gymnasiums. The study concluded, that the
percentage of measured CO2 concentrations below 1000 ppm (recommended highest concen-
tration) was 44, 41 and 47 percent in 2009, 2014 and 2021 respectively, and thus not very
different from each other even though some active measures had been undertaken since the
first investigation. Furthermore, more than half of the measurements were above the recom-
mended max value across all three studies, indicating that the indoor environment of danish
schools not only have remained relatively unchanged through the last twelve years, but also
generally still can be said to be of a quite poor quality. Likewise, Sadrizadeh et al. investigated
the interaction between classroom air quality and the pupil’s performance, comfort and health.
The study concluded that most schools worldwide has a basic naturally ventilated system that
is inadequate for meeting the needs of their users, and thus leading to negative consequences
for the pupils. This included a reduction in cognitive performance, negative consequences for
progressive learning, an increase in short-term sick leave and an increased exposure to pollu-
tants. As children inhale a larger volume of air compared to body weight, exposure to pollutants
risks severe damage to pupil’s health, which is especially important, as many included studies
reported of higher pollution levels in schools than in residential or commercial buildings [5].
Overall the indoor climate of schools and energy consideration thus cannot be viewed entirely
separate, and we should take great care when implementing new solutions. In [6], Wargocki
1
==PAGE:9==
argues that providing better thermal environment and air quality conditions in schools is actually
costs efficient for society as a whole, as the health and education of our children is one of the
most important ways of benefiting our future generations. As such, Wargocki argues that the
indoor environment in general should be prioritized first, and that energy considerations in this
context must be viewed as a secondary consideration. As the sustainable agenda in general
seems unlikely to simply pass by the school sector, other ways must thus be found for energy
and environmental improvements, without compromising the indoor environment. In this, a
better utilization of natural ventilation might prove a part of the solution. It seems reasonable
to assume, that the renovation and construction of mechanical systems in both new and older
buildings requires a substantial investment in both cost, time, energy and resources in general.
While these system, perhaps rightfully, often are viewed as better in terms of the quality of
the indoor environment, savings on all points might be found in a better and more efficient
utilization of natural ventilation. As such, a new approach is necessary, where solutions within
the area of natural ventilation is considered first, followed by a subsequent implementation of
mechanical equipment to account for the remaining, until a sufficient indoor environment is
achieved. However, natural ventilation is by nature unpredictable and depends highly on wind
and thermal forces. While the concept in itself has been around for a long time, more knowledge
is thus required in regards to how we can optimize the use of natural ventilation in schools in
a modern context, especially as many schools are not designed and optimized with natural
ventilation in mind.
The purpose of this study is thus to investigate different natural ventilation strategies and their
effect on ventilation and indoor environment in a generic representation of a classroom, while
simultaneously varying various parameters assumed to affect the indoor climate. The aim is
mainly to gain knowledge about how the strategies and parameter variations seem to affect the
indoor environment of the classroom, and to evaluate whether tendencies can be found, which
might be used as a broad starting point for the design of future investigations and studies.
As a mean of validation, the results are partly compared to a real-life case study and similar
theoretical expressions.

Background
In this chapter various background information and supplementary material necessary for the
execution of the study in general is listed. The aim is to provide general information on how the
study is carried out, and to set a base for the origin and considerations of the information used
in the subsequent simulations.
2.1
Literature search process
The literature referenced in this study was found though the use of different search engines,
mainly DTU Findit and Google Scholar. Different combinations of keywords was used including
(amongst others); Natural Ventilation, Indoor Environment, Indoor Air quality, Schools, Energy
and Review. The desired type of literature was mainly review articles and subsequently direct
searches on the referenced articles, journals or other relevant material. The literature was ini-
tially sorted based on the information gained through the reading of abstracts and conclusions,
to see if the literature contained information relevant to the subjects in this thesis. These in-
cluded investigations of: the importance of having a good indoor environment and air quality in
schools; the current status of the quality of indoor environment and air quality in Danish schools;
regulations and goals in regard to environmental actions; and the newest research within the
field of naturally ventilated buildings and the relation to schools in today’s society. An attempt
was made to find the newest review articles to gain knowledge about the state of the art within
the respective fields. A specific age limit was not set in the search of referenced articles.
2.2
Natural ventilation strategies
As a key element in the study, four main strategies for natural ventilation is used and referenced
repeatedly throughout the report. The strategies are based on the concepts of single-side (SSV)
and cross-side (CSV) ventilation, and thus relates foremost to whether the air entering the build-
ing can be said to flow in and out of the same facade only, or whether flow through the building
is possible. The two concepts are further split into situations where a vertical difference in the
facade(s) is introduced, which overall leads to the four natural ventilation strategies outlined in
fig. 2.1.

2.3
Modeling process
As a starting point for the investigation, a process is devised for the modeling and variation
of the four natural ventilation strategies though the use of computational fluids dynamics. In
addition to this, two simultaneous studies are carried out, as a way of validating and relating the
obtained results to real-life data and theory. Below the general approach to the three studies is
outlined, along with a description of the conceptual idea and reasoning behind the CFD study.
The aim of this section is thus to provide an overview and to clarify the reasoning behind some
of the applied method and choices.
2.3.1
Approach
The main approach to the task is threefold, with the aim of both generating and validating the
results obtained through the CFD study. The studies are carried out separately, but may overlap
or rely on some of the same data at times. A schematic representation of the three studies are
shown below, followed by a general description of the approach to each task.
Figure 2.2: Schematic representation of the approach.
The case study is carried out as part of a validation process of the simulated CFD models,
by generating a set of simulated data that can be directly compared against a similar set of
measured real life data. The aim is to document how accurate the CFD modelling can be
said to represent reality, and is thus to be seen as a measure of the quality of the simulations.
Measured values of velocity at a given height is used for this purpose, along with measurements
of the air-change-rate. Both are carried out in a classroom at a physical location in Denmark.
More information about the location can be found in section 2.5.
The theoretical study is carried out based on expressions found in literature, by calculating
and comparing a theoretical air-change-rate with data obtained from the simulation in the CFD
study. The aim is to document how accurate the CFD modelling can be said to represent
the theoretical expressions that traditionally have been used when performing calculations of
natural ventilation in buildings. More information can be found in section 3.3.
The CFD study follows the process of the phases outlined in the schematic of the conceptual
idea in section 2.3.2, which consist of a gathering of information, modelling, simulation and
generation of results through computational fluids dynamics. The process is elaborated below.

2.3.2
Conceptual idea (CFD study)
The conceptual idea is based on the assumption, that a generic model can be used to generate
a range of simulation results, that to some degree of accuracy can be said to represent a typical
classroom in Denmark. Thus, the aim is not to simulate results for a specific type of classroom,
but rather to create a comparable set of generalised data, that can be used as an indicator
towards the effect of a given combination of a parameter and ventilation strategy. In this regard,
the conceptual idea is to be perceived as an outline of the steps necessary to achieve this. On
fig. 2.3 below a schematic representation of the conceptual idea is shown.
Figure 2.3: Schematic representation of the conceptual idea.
As can be seen from the figure, the schematic is split in four overall phases. The Generic
classroom specification consist of all the information necessary to construct a generic model
of a classroom, and thus serves as the starting point from which all the subsequent modelling
and simulation is carried out. This information is combined with the four natural ventilation
strategies forming the Base-models. Note that these models are thus identical in many ways,
with the sole exception of settings related directly to the choice of the ventilation strategy itself
(e.g. placement of windows). Simulations are carried out for the four base-models, and used
as a reference of comparison in the subsequent Parameter variation. In this phase, chosen
parameters are varied across all the base models, in order to generate a comparable set of data
on the effect of the combination of a given ventilation strategy and parameter change. The initial
parameter setting of the base-models is hence referred to as the base-line parameter setting.
Note that the values in the parameter variation are varied one at a time, and that all other input
in a simulation should thus be considered either a part of the generic specification of the base-
line parameter setting. The last phase consist of the ordering and analysis of the generated
Output data, that is used in the subsequent discussion.

Classroom dimensions
From the data it is evident, that the most typical values for floor area and room height is given
as 60m2 and 3m respectively, with the median and mean of both datasets being exactly or
very close to these values. Furthermore it can be seen, that 50% of the measured data lies
within the intervals [52;68] and [2.80;3.40] respectively, and thus the data appear relatively
centered around these values. From this, it is assumed justifiable to define the overall size of a
generic classroom as above. As no data for the length and width of the rooms area available,
dimensions of 6x10x3 meters (WxLxH) are assumed appropriate.
Window area
From the data it is evident, that the typical window area of a classroom lies within an interval
of 8-14m2, with a median of 12m2. However, this value represents the total glass area of the
walls, and thus likely more useful for estimations of the amount of daylight entering the room.
In regard to ventilation, it can thus only be used as a measure of a (theoretical and extreme)
maximum area for the window opening. No specific value is clearly specified in standards and
regulations (e.g. [7, 8, 9]) either, as these are based on requirements to the total ventilation
rate, rather than the size of the window opening. Thus a value is assumed, based on the above
and measurements of the windows at the physical location of the case study. A total openable
window area of 4.2m2 is chosen, corresponding roughly to a third of the median found above.
Note that this value is to be seen as an initial value, and thus it is only directly applicable to
windows in the SSV-Cen base-model (as it is considered the simplest case). Also note, that all
base-models are modelled with three pairs of identical window configurations, and that this e.g.
corresponds to an opening area of 1.4m2 pr. window for the SSV-Cen base-model. The initial
width of each individual window is set to 1.0m. Variations in the window size due to the chosen
natural ventilation strategy is described in section 2.4.2.
Occupants
From the data it is evident, that the typical number of occupants in a classroom (measured as
the number of students) lies within an interval of 16-22, with a median of 19. Danish legislation
[7] states, that the maximum number of occupants in a classroom is limited in such way, that the
room must be able to accommodate a volume of 6m3 of air pr. occupant (below a height of max.
4 meters). For a room of the dimensions given above (6x10x3), this equals a total of exactly
30 occupants, or as assumed in this project: 28 students and 2 teachers. As can be seen from
the Me2021 [4] data, only a smaller number of classrooms reach this level. It should be noted
though, that the data does not represent every classroom in Denmark, and thus the actual
value might be higher. Nevertheless, the generic model is assumed to include the maximum
number of occupants, in order to better simulate appropriate conditions. This is done, as the
occupants of the classroom are amongst the main contributors to the contamination of the
indoor environment, and thus is is deemed more suitable to generate data for a worst-case
scenario situation.
In the generic model the occupants are roughly spaced out throughout the room, with some
minor adjustments to better imitate the setting of an actual classroom. Thus the students are
placed in one end of the room, with a distance of approximately 1m between. The teachers are
placed in the other end of the room and are given slightly more floor area to accommodate for
an entrance zone, a teachers table and a blackboard area. The situation is illustrated in fig. 2.5.

Thermal environment and emissions
For the thermal environment in general, it is assumed appropriate to set a wall temperature
corresponding to 20°C, as this somewhat adequately represents a value that might be expected
as a set-point for the classroom and its surroundings. Similarly it seems evident, that many
classrooms would include a heat source and that this often would be placed near the windows.
As such, a radiator with a capacity of 55 w/m2 is deemed suitable, and thus this is included in
the generic model. The heat generation from the occupants is set at approximately 40 w/m2.
Likewise, emissions from the occupants were based on an assumed generation rate of CO2 of
18 L/s. This value was later reshaped based on the volumes of the occupant representations,
to its current form 0.0011 kg/s for the student volumes and 0.0022 kg/s for the teachers.

Methodology
In this chapter, the procedure and methodology applied during the study is presented. The
chapter is split in sections in correspondence with the areas of investigation outlined in the
conceptual idea and approach, as described in chapter 2.
3.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics simulations
The CFD simulations were carried out through the project platform accessible in the program
ANSYS Workbench 2021 R2. More specifically, the Fluent Flow (Fluent) analysis system was
utilized, which contains a component-based project schematic interface, that includes geometry
creation (ANSYS DesignModeler), model meshing (ANSYS Meshing), generation of solution
data (ANSYS Fluent) and post processing (ANSYS CFD-Post)1. For more information on the
utilized ANSYS products, please refer to the program manuals. On fig. 3.1, an example of the
Fluent Flow (Fluent) analysis system can be seen.

the project schematic interface of ANSYS Workbench 2021 R2.
In the following, the procedure for the CFD simulation is elaborated. As the parameter variation
models described in chapter 2 are treated similarly in many ways, a general approach is applied
in the description. Thus the following should be assumed valid for all models, unless otherwise
noted. To illustrate the process, graphic material from the SSV-Cen base-model is displayed
throughout the sections as a visual guide to the process.

dimensions can be found in table 2.2. The geometry is shown in fig. 3.2.

3.1.2
Domain sizing
In an attempt to best simulate conditions for a natural flow of wind, the model was placed
inside a computational domain considerably larger than the actual size of the model. This was
done in order to ensure sufficient space for an more accurate prediction of turbulence and eddy
generation around the model. The size of the domain in the vertical, lateral and flow direction
was based on values obtained from a Best Practice Guideline for CFD flow simulation in urban
environment [11]. In the guideline the domain dimensions are set in relation to the height [H] of
the modelled geometry, an is thus given as 3H to the side of the model, 3H towards the inlet,
15H towards the outlet and 5H from the roof to the top of the domain. The situation is illustrated
on fig. 3.4.
Figure 3.4: Applied domain dimensions in relation to the height of the geometry

3.1.3
Model decomposition and grid sizing
In order to ease the meshing process and the simulation time, an attempt was made to generate
a grid that was as close to being fully structured as possible, while also considering the total
amount of generated cells. This was done by a decomposing of the geometry into a number of
meshable sub-volumes, which for the main part of the model was of a rectangular nature (all
beside the window regions). As the main point of interest was confined to the bounds of the
classroom geometry it was further assumed, that the remaining parts of the domain would not
require as dense a mesh, and thus could consist of a decomposition with larger sub-volumes.
However, due to the nature of the meshing process this would require a ”conversion” of the
14
==PAGE:22==
structured mesh from the classroom to the domain geometry, which might easily lead to a poor
quality in large areas of the mesh. To avoid this, the model was split in two parts, meaning
that the sizing of the classroom (inner part) and domain (outer part) mesh could be carried out
more independently of each other. However, This would also cut the mesh- and computational
connection through the windows (and thus the possibility of air entering the classroom). To
retain this, identical sizing of the window regions was applied for both parts and a manual
mesh merge was carried out subsequent of the meshing process. This process is elaborated
in the meshing section. Also note that the generated mesh visualised in the meshing section
often follows the grid sizing outlined in the section below, and thus may serve as an visual guide
for this.

3.1.5
Simulation setup
The utilization of the fluent solver setup was limited in accordance with the tools necessary
to simulate the required flow through the domain and classroom. As the setup contains an
abundance of settings and possibilities, these should be assumed inactive or set at a standard
value, if it is not otherwise mentioned in this section. The following goes through the outline view
of the setup interface and comments on the selected values and settings. A basic knowledge
about the setup interface and the significance of the chosen settings is assumed. A picture of
the outline view and the general settings tab is shown in fig. 3.10 below.
Figure 3.10: Outline view of the fluent solver setup with the general tab open.
General tab
As can be seen in fig. 3.10 the solver was set to perform a pressure based steady state simu-
lation with an absolute velocity formulation. As the Mach number at the inlet velocity was low
and the pressure variations through the domain were not expected to fluctuate much relative
to the static pressure, a simplification was made to the governing equations by assuming the
fluid as an incompressible ideal gas. Thus the density of the fluid was only dependent on the
temperature and not the local pressure fluctuations, and from this a pressure based formulation
was deemed most appropriate. The reference pressure was set in the operating conditions tab
(obs. not visible on fig. 3.10) to the static atmospheric pressure value of 101325 Pa. Likewise
gravity was enabled at a constant value of -9.82 m/s2 in the y-direction, amongst other things
to account for the species modelling as elaborated later.
Model tab
In the model tab the energy, viscous and species transport models were turned on to account
for temperature, fluid movement and pollutant generation respectively in the simulation. The
viscous model was chosen as a k-epsilon RNG model with a standard wall treatment and no
modifications of the default model constants. As the simulations were run based on a generic
and quite unspecific model, it was deemed a reasonable approach to chose this viscous model,
as it is often described as one of the most popular and commonly used models in various
sources, including the model description in the Ansys Fluent Theory Guide. According to the
18
==PAGE:26==
guide, the standard k-epsilon model is valid for fully turbulent flows only and has certain lim-
itations, which the k-epsilon RNG model amongst other things is an attempt to improve. As
such, and based on discussions with external guidance of this project, this was deemed an
appropriate choice.
The species transport was set up to model the generation and flow of pollutants (assumed
only to be CO2) from the occupants. As such, a mixture material of CO2 and air was defined
based on standard material properties for CO2 and air obtained from the Fluent Database. A
mixture template for the fluid species was created containing only the imported CO2 and air
species, and with the later defined as the last species. Properties of the species were keep
at the default values. Mass fractions for the CO2 species (and thus also for the remaining air)
were later specified for the inlet volume flow at 400 ppm, while the pollution was defined in
smaller volumes created at the occupant head in correspondence with the generation rate of
18 L/h as listed in chapter 2.
Material tab
The creation of the mixture material for the fluid is described above, and will thus not be repeated
here. Note that the model was generated based on fluid zones and walls only, and thus no other
material was used in the simulation. While the occupants were generated during the geometry
creation, they were later suppressed as it was assumed that only the occupant surface body
was needed to affect the flow. It was likewise assumed, that a standard wall in this scenario
was sufficiently representative of a human body, although it is acknowledged that the properties
of a wall and the way a fluid acts around this may not be completely comparable. As it was
mainly the general flow patterns of the classroom that was investigated, this was deemed an
acceptable simplification.
Cell zone conditions tab
Cell zone conditions was applied to the listed fluid bodies consisting of the fluid elements for
the two parts (domain/nearzone and classroom) and the volumes created specifically for the
species generation at the occupant heads for students and teachers respectively. The former
was kept at the standard values and no species source terms was added. For the volumes at
the student heads a CO2 species source term of 0.0011kg/m3/s was added for both the mass
and species terms, while it was set at 0.0022kg/m3/s for the volumes at the teacher heads.
Boundary conditions tab
The inlet was specified as a velocity inlet, with a magnitude as specified by the parameter
variation of the specific model. The magnitude was set as normal to the boundary, with the sole
exception of the models for the parameter variation of the wind direction. For these models the
direction was specified by the use of a Cartesian coordinate vector to originate at an angle of
15° to the right. It is acknowledged that a possibility exist for this to not comply completely with
the way the sizing of the domain, and that ideally the whole domain would have been likewise
rotated. A small angle was thus chosen in an attempt to account for this, which was assumed
an acceptable approach. Similarly to this, a inlet temperature was specified in accordance with
the parameter variation, and a species mass fraction of 400 ppm was added to account for
the background concentration of co2. Turbulence intensity and other values were kept at the
default. For the walls, heat fluxes were defined for all the occupant surfaces, in accordance
with the value (40 w/m2) assumed in chapter 2. In the same way, constant wall temperatures
(20°C) were applied for all the wall of the classroom, with the exception of the open window
surfaces and the floor, which were set as adiabatic (0 heat flux). The surface immediately below
the window zone of the facade was likewise set to a heat flux of 55 w/m2 to imitate a radiator
below the windows, as this was assumed appropriate to the generic model in general. For the
outlet, only the temperature was specified in accordance with the parameter variation. Interior
surfaces and symmetry was not edited.

3.1.6
Data processing
After running the simulations, data was extracted either directly from the fluent solution file
(solution component) or from the results post processing component. See fig. 3.1 for a graphical
reference of the component systems. This data was either inserted directly into this report or
processed through the use of the native solutions in the fluent solution component, the post
processing component or via extraction and processing in Microsoft Excel. Below follows a
general description of the applied approach for the results generation. For more information,
please refer to the project files listed in appendix B.1.
Volume Flow Rate and Air Change Rate
The volume flow rate through the windows was calculated directly in the fluent solution com-
ponent. The aim was to get separate values for the volume flow rate in both the positive and
negative direction of the axis perpendicular to the window-surfaces. As built-in solutions for
this sort of calculation was found not to account for the direction of the flow, but rather reported
the net flow through the surface, named expression was created and used for the calculation.
The calculation was based on the definition of volume flow rate, as given in the Ansys Fluent
Theory Guide. An example of such an expression is shown in fig. 3.13.
Figure 3.13: Example of the named expression tool and the surface integral tool
from the solution component of the Ansys Fluent (fluent) Analysis System.
Note that the aim of this expression was to isolate the product of the z-component of the velocity
vector and a corresponding facet area, based on the direction of the z-component of the velocity.
Afterwards a surface integral of the sum of the named expression was calculated from the
window surfaces in the facade, thus resulting in a volume flow rate in a given z-direction. A
similar method was used to calculate the volume flow rate in the other direction and for other
windows and facades. The total volume flow rate in a given direction was found by summing the
results from all the surfaces. Note that the calculation surfaces were based on the internal fluid
for the intersection of the two parts (classroom and domain/nearzone), and the different areas
were separated by cutting these planes with the iso-clip tool, until only the desired window
surface was left. Values for air change rate were calculated based on the usual expression
(multiplied with 3600 and divided with the total volume of the room).
21
==PAGE:29==
Facade air flow patterns
The analysis of the facade air flow patterns were based mainly on images exported directly from
the post processing component. The images were generated based on the z-component of the
velocity vector, and thus only showed tendencies for this specific direction. Likewise, section
cuts through the width of the classroom and placed in the middle of the window surfaces were
exported. The analysis was only curried out for the base-models. From the calculated volume
flow rats listed above, the percentage of air passing through a specific window surface, as seen
in relation to the total volume flow rated reported for the facade as a whole, was calculated and
supplied. Except for the exported volume flow rates described above, this process was carried
out through the use of Microsoft Excel.
Indoor climate parameters
The data for the visual and statistical reporting of the indoor climate parameters was exported
through the post processing component, and was based on the fluid-volume (cell zone condition
in the fluent setup) for the classroom geometry. The export menu is shown in fig. 3.14. Note that
the export thus does not contain the volumes in the classroom used for the pollution generation
at the occupant heads, which is deemed a minor deviation.

volume for the classroom geometry.
The data was transferred to Microsoft Excel, and cumulative sum charts were created for all

tively. The cumulative sum charts were gathered in groups corresponding to the investigated

3.2
Case study
The case study was conducted on 7 December 2022 at Skt. Klemensskolen near Odense.
In order to achieve a data-set that could be used in the comparison with the simulated data,
measurements of air velocity and air change rate were carried out. Both experiments were
conducted in the same classroom with none of the regular occupants present. Note that the
three adults who conducted the experiments were still present in the room. Furniture and other
items in the classroom was left as they were, except when it got directly in the way of the
measuring equipment. Windows were left at an open position corresponding to the 30° used
in the CFD modeling, in order to make the case as comparable as possible.
3.2.1
Measurements of velocity
The measurements of the air velocity was carried out with four anemometers mounted vertically
above each other on a stand, in heights corresponding to the standard measuring heights of 0.1,
0.6, 1.1 and 1.6 (1.5) meters respectively. Note that the top anemometer was placed slightly
lower (at a height of 1.5m), due to this being the maximum mountable height on the stand. The
stand was then moved around between a total of 6 pre-selected locations in the room, where
measurements were carried out every 30 seconds for a total of 3 minutes (6 measurements pr.
location). The positions in the room can be seen on fig. 3.15. Additional picture of the used
equipment and the room in general can be found in appendix A.1.

3.2.2
Measurements of air change rate
The measurement of air change rate was carried out as a tracer gas experiment, and calculated
backwards from the measured values trough a decay approach. A quantity of the gas was
introduced in the room and movement in the air was created in order to mix the gas with the
surrounding air volumes. Note that this was done manually as no mechanical equipment was
at hands at the time. At the time of the measurements start, the air and gas was assumed
sufficiently mixed. From this, the air change rate was calculated using a general simplification
of the decay formula as given in the equation below:
C(t) = C0 · e−n·t
(3.1)
where Ct denotes the concentration after a given time, C0 denotes the initial concentration, n
denotes the air change rate and t denotes time.
3.2.3
CFD modeling
The CFD modeling of the case study geometry closely followed the process outlined in sec-
tion 3.1 for the SSV-Cen base-model. As such, the process from the geometry creation to the
meshing of the model can be assumed identical, with the sole exception of the occupants and
the corresponding classroom sub-volume division not being modelled in the Case study simu-
lation. Instead the classroom is simply modelled as one rectangular volume of 6x10x3 meters
(WxLxH). The meshing follows the same procedure as above, and in the simulation setup only
a few values were changed. Below follows an overview of the changes made from the SSV-Cen
Base-model in the creation of the Case study model:
• The information listed in the general tab was not changed.
• For the model tab, the species model and all the corresponding settings done in relation
to this was disabled, as no pollutants were assumed to be generated duo to the lack of
occupants. Only air change rate and velocity were measured for the comparison, and as
such the mixture model was likewise changed back to consisting solely of air.
• No additional materials were defined in the materials tab.
• The cell zone conditions was reverted to the standard with no source terms, as the species
model and the volumes related to the former was no longer modelled.
• For the boundary conditions the inlet velocity was set to 3.5 m/s, while the temperature
was changed to 2°C. (measurements from the location were erroneous, and thus values
were estimated based on data from the Danish Meteorological Institute [13]).

5
Discussion
In this section the results outlined in chapter 4 are discussed. The aim is to investigate what
the results can tell us about the the different natural ventilation strategies and their effect on
ventilation and the indoor climate in a generic classroom, wile simultaneously varying a num-
ber of pre-selected parameters. Likewise, some of the results are held up against data from a
real world case study and theoretical expressions. Thus this chapter discusses limitations and
uncertainties of the applied methodology, validation against case study and theoretical expres-
sions, investigations of volume flow rates and air change rate, investigations of facade air flow
patterns and lastly an investigation of the overall effect on selected indoor climate parameters.
Each section is summed up with a sub-conclusion, highlighting the most important takes of the
discussion. Likewise a section on possibilities for continued work and development is included.
5.1
Limitations and uncertainties
As is the case for all, this study is conducted within a limited time frame and with a basic initial
knowledge about the subject, corresponding to what might be expected of a typical univer-
sity master student, and what might reasonably have been learned from earlier projects and
courses. The Ansys Fluent software package is of a quite complex nature and contains a wide
range of possibilities and an even larger range of equivalent mathematical theory needed to
properly understand and perform an adequate simulation. Even with the allocated professional
help from more experienced professors, access to the various Ansys theory/user manuals and
guidance found online in general, the author does by no means claim to be an expert on the
use of the software or the subsequent interpretation of the output data. The report should be
viewed with this in mind, as there might very well exist a more suitable approach to the mod-
elling process and interpretation as a whole, within the bounds of the software. Below, a few
of the known limitations and uncertainties are pointed out, in an attempt to narrow down the
applicable space of conclusions that might be drawn from the results.
By the authors best knowledge, the Ansys Fluent software package is not initially designed
to perform simulations of naturally induced air-flows into a building, as is the main objective
of this study, but should (amongst other things) rather be associated with flows in pipes and
other geometrical volumes with a set of closed and well-defined boundary conditions. While
the applied method is it not assumed to be wrong (amongst other things because numerous
examples of experiments exists with this particular use or something similar), it does add a
degree of uncertainty throughout the modelling process as a whole, as it may be less certain
whether a specific function or method performs as intended under the applied conditions.
On a general note, the starting point for the simulations is based on a generically designed
model of a classroom, and thus the boundary conditions and other applied set-points are at
best a good estimation of what could be said to be representative of a typical danish classroom.
Opposed to a more traditional use of the software, where a specific real life case is imitated best
possible and conclusions are drawn from this, one should be careful to compare the results
of this report directly with any real-life cases, unless they happen to resemble the modelled
generic geometry well (as is the case for the case study model of this project), and in this
case one should still be wary. Rather, the results of this report should perhaps be viewed as
a way of hinting possible effects of e.g. a specific parameter variation, which could be used
in the process of developing and conducting the appropriate simulations for a given real life
geometry.

5.2
Natural ventilation strategies
One of the main areas of interest in this study was an investigation of the four natural ventilation
strategies as outlined in chapter 1. More accurately, the strategies were classified as either
single-side- or cross-side ventilation, depending on the individual placement of the windows
in the two facades perpendicular to the initial flow direction (front and back). As such, the
single-side ventilation models consisted either of a set of windows placed at a centered position
in the front facade (SSV-Cen), or a set of windows placed vertically above each other in the
front facade (SSV-Ver). Similarly the cross-side ventilation models consisted either of a set of
windows placed horizontally in the same position in the front and back facade respectively (CSV-
Hor), or a set of windows positioned at a centered position in the front facade, and at a higher
position in the back facade (CSV-Ver). In the following the four natural ventilation strategies
and their effect on the ventilation, flow and indoor environment in a generic classroom are
discussed, based on the results obtained from the study.
Considering only the minimum levels and recommendations given for the volume flow rate in
the danish building code and DS/EN-16798, all of the reported values initially seem to comply
with the necessary ventilation rate, as seen in relation to the number of occupants. As may be
expected the single-side ventilated models (SSV) had a volume flow rate much lower than the
cross-side ventilated models (CSV), with the former being within a rather narrow general range
of approximately 0 to 1 m3/s across the parameter variations, and the later being within a much
wider general range of approximately 2 to 10 m3/s. Locally within the Single-side ventilated
model group, the models with vertically distributed window placement (SSV-Ver) generally re-
sulted in larger volume flow rates than the centered models (SSV-Cen). Locally within the cross
side ventilated model group, the models with a horizontally distributed window placement from
the front to the back facade (CSV-Hor) similarly resulted in a larger volume flow rate than the
models with a vertical distribution from the front to the back facade (CSV-Ver). Even so, the
numerical difference between the pairwise natural ventilation strategies across the parameters
variations did not seem to differ much relatively seen, and as such the largest differences were
found between the use of a single-sided or cross ventilated strategy in itself rather than in the
local window placement variation (although the later should not be completely neglected).
Considering only the minimum levels and recommendations given for the volume flow rate in
the danish building code and DS/EN-16798, all of the reported values initially seem to comply
with the necessary ventilation rate, as seen in relation to the number of occupants. However,
even though they seem to comply with the standards, none of the four ventilation strategies
appear really ideal, as the reported volume flow rates either tends to fluctuate close to the
minimum requirements or high above them, with the single-side ventilation (SSV) potentially
leading to a poor ventilation rate in general and cross-side ventilation (CSV) likely leading to
various degrees of discomfort. SSV would thus require a constant open state of the windows,
while CSV would require ventilation in intervals.
In continuation of this, the above only enlightens half of the issue, as the ventilation rate through
a window surface in itself does not reveal much information about how the air in the classroom
behaves, or whether the ventilation thus to a sufficient degree can be said to have achieved
its desired effect. The investigation of the facade air flow patterns and the z-velocity sections
cuts through the base-models revealed a tendency for the air to enter mainly through the middle
windows for the single-side ventilated models (SSV), followed by a shift in the flow-path towards
and along the floor, within the first few meters.
Combining the fact that the flow-path was mostly evident for the center windows, the fact that the
air-speeds in the room was generally reported as low and the nature of the vertical distribution
of hot and cold air, the mixing of air could probably be assessed as quite poor throughout
57
==PAGE:65==
large parts of the classroom, perhaps with the exception of a local zone near the windows.
Furthermore it could be theorized, that a portion of the in-going air from the centered windows
is circulated more or less directly out of the remaining windows again, and thus the credibility of
the already quite low reported volume flow rate and air change rate might be questioned. The
ventilated zone near the windows was found to be larger for the vertical case (SSV-Ver), and
as the vertical window distribution seemed to result in a more clear distribution of the in- and
outgoing air between the windows, this might have increased the uniformity and magnitude of
this effect. For the cross-side ventilated base-models (CSV), the z-velocity sections cuts of the
base-models revealed a tendency for the air to enter quite equally distributed between all the
windows. The sections cut showed a tendency for the in-going air to reach the opposite (back)
wall of the classroom, thus resulting in a large amount of backflow and presumably turbulence
throughout mainly the bottom part of the classroom. Opposed to the single-side ventilated
models (SSV) the z-directional flow was obvious and clearly seen, with a general flow-path
going from the front windows and along the ceiling of the classroom. The effect was found
to be smaller for the vertical models (CSV-Ver), as the higher placement of the back-windows
seemed to provide a more direct route for the air to exit the classroom. It should be noted, that
for the pattern to be this evident on the section cuts (as only the z-component of the velocity is
shown), both the initial and backward flow were presumably quite dominant. Although it could
likely be theorized that a large amount of air went directly out of the opposite windows, due
to both airspeed and increased negative pressure on the outside of the back facade, it should
probably be assumed, that the whole classroom to some degree is ventilated. The mixing of the
air was thus assessed to be quite effective. As for the single-side models (SSV) the credibility
of the reported volume flow rates and air change rates might still be challenged however, but
rather as seen in relation to whether a certain volume of air going more or less directly through
a space can be said to contribute well to the ventilation of the space as a whole, or whether
this effect should rather be ascribed mainly to the presumably smaller amount of backflow and
turbulence.
Similar to the investigations outlined above, the results of the indoor climate parameter investi-
gation indirectly revealed and perhaps confirmed some of the same tendencies. For the single-
side ventilated models (SSV), the level of CO2 did not comply with the maximum concentration
of CO2 in the air from the building code and standards, as the medians revealed that roughly
50% of the reported values were still higher than this level at steady state conditions. Consid-
ering a subsequent continued build-up of CO2 after the end of an assumed ventilation period,
the CO2 concentration would thus quickly rise to higher levels again, which would likely lead
to a higher rate of necessary ventilation periods in general or otherwise influence the health of
the occupant negatively. The median was found to be somewhat lower for the vertical models
(SSV-Ver), which again suggest a slightly better ventilation rate, but presumably not enough to
account for the issue above. Opposed to this, the level of CO2 reached levels rather close to
the background concentration for the cross-side ventilation models (CSV) at steady state con-
ditions, and thus did not constitute any issues in regard to the building code and standards on
this point. The horizontal (CSV-Hor) model reported values slightly closer to the outdoor con-
centration. Depending on the time needed before reaching a concentration close to the steady
state conditions, this might thus prove an effective ventilation method, e.g if considerations to
local discomfort can be disregarded temporarily.
For the single side models (SSV), the temperature median was not found to have diverged
much from what might have been otherwise expected, as seen against e.g the DS-16798 rec-
ommended value for the minimum operative temperature in the heating season (20°C) and
assuming the room would normally be heated to about this temperature before ventilating. Like-
wise a general lowering of temperature would likely often be expected while utilizing natural ven-
58
==PAGE:66==
tilation, (assuming a colder outdoor climate) and thus a lowering of temperature at a smaller
rate is not considered an issue in regard to the thermal environment in general. If the ventila-
tion period were to be assumed of a shorter overall time frame, the temperature of walls and
furniture would also not be expected to decrease much, and thus the classroom would quickly
return to the state is was in (temperature-wise) before the ventilation period was initialized. On
the other hand some temperature variation would be expected due to the mixing of presumably
colder outside air and the inside air, and a difference of only a few degrees (as was the case
for the single-side models (SSV)) might just as well hint of a generally poor mixing of the air.
For the cross-ventilated models (CSV), the temperatures across the classroom was generally
found to be much closer to the outdoor temperature, which combined with the generally high
velocity rates may cause some discomfort in terms of the thermal environment. Even with an
expected general lowering of temperature due to the general utilization of natural ventilation,
this might prove an issue for the comfort of the occupants, if the steady state conditions were
reached over longer periods of time with open windows. If the ventilation period were to be
assumed of a shorter overall time frame, the temperature of walls and furniture might again
not be expected to decrease much, thus lessening the long term consequences to the thermal
environment.
The pressure build up across both single-side ventilated models (SSV) and cross-side ven-
tilated models (CSV) was generally found to be positive, which corresponded to what may
logically be assumed when air is pushed into an otherwise closed enclosure (for the SSV) or
similarly when backflow and turbulence is created within the space (for the CSV). For the for-
mer, this may further have contributed to the generally low ventilation rates, as pressure was
thus applied to the in-going air from both sides of the windows, and thus may be assumed to
have affected and perhaps accelerated the local flow pattern around the windows, thus creating
more direct outflow. For the later, the pressure might be presumed to exist mainly outside the
main flow-path through the classroom, it may thus further have contributed and enlarged the
effect of air going directly through the classroom, without much occurrence of mixing.
For the single-side ventilation models (SSV), the velocity was found to generally be low across
all models, further emphasizing the tendencies above. The reported maximum velocity was
generally quite high as seen in relation to e.g. the median, which may likely be contributed to
a local velocity field within or close to the windows. As such, the local window regions might
thus result in a higher degree if dissatisfied occupants than other pars of the classroom. In
general, close to no difference was seen in the reported velocity pattern between the centered
(SSV-Cen) and vertical (SSV-Ver) model. For the cross-side ventilation models (CSV), the
velocity measurements in the classroom was found to generally be high across all models,
further underlying aforementioned tendencies. Both the median and the third quartile of the
reported velocity measurements was found to be quite a bit lower than the reported max value,
perhaps again suggesting that the main part of the classroom did not experience the same level
of extreme velocity as may have been found at the windows and in the flow-path from the front
to the back facade. That being said, the reported velocities were in general quite high and may
have resulted in quite a bit of discomfort. The velocity in general was found to be slightly lower
for the vertical models (CSV-Ver), which might be a result of the smaller pressure and more
direct flow-path, as discussed above.

5.3
Parameter variation
Another main interest of the study was an investigation and simulation of a range of parameter
variations, as seen in respect to the models simulated with the base-line parameter setting
as outlined in chapter 1. Amongst other things, the aim was to gain knowledge about how a
change in a given parameter would effect the simulated results, and what this would mean in
terms of the ventilation and indoor climate of a classroom. In the following the six parameter
variations and their effect on the ventilation, flow and indoor environment in a generic classroom
are discussed, based on the results obtained from the study.
Note, that the facade air flow patterns across the majority of the models experienced little
change in general, as seen in regard to where the air was found to enter and leave the class-
room respectively (see tables 4.4 to 4.7. Thus only the parameter variations that caused a
notable change in the pattern has been included in the discussion below. Sections cut through
the room were only produced for the base-models, and as such these do not otherwise form
part of the analysis below.
5.3.1
Variation of temperature (2°)
The change of the bases-line parameter setting to a lower temperature seemed to result in a
slightly larger volume flow rate for the single-side ventilated models (SSV), while the cross-side
ventilated models remained more or less unchanged. The former thus appear more exposed
to changes in temperature, which might be attributed to a presumably larger dependency on
principles of thermal buoyancy. Similarly the vertical single-side ventilated model (SSV-Ver)
showed tendencies towards a slightly larger change in the volume flow rate.
For the single-side ventilated models (SSV), the parameter change did show tendencies to-
wards an improvement of the general conditions of the indoor environment in terms of the con-
centration of CO2, but the improvement was not large in general and overall did not contribute
to the median of the data achieving levels much below the recommended max concentration.
At the same time the temperature showed a tendency of having dropped several degrees at
steady state conditions (as seen compared to a an assumed indoor temperature of 20°), and
thus neither the level of CO2 or the thermal environment seemed to benefit much from this. On
the other hand the cross-ventilated models (CSV) did not seem to be effected by the variation
in terms of the level of CO2, but this might also be due to the level already being close to the
background concentration, and thus a given change due to the parameter variation might not be
measurable under the given conditions. Similar to the above, the models showed a tendency
to result in a general worsening of the thermal environment, with the median temperature drop-
ping even more than for the single-side ventilation models (SSV). Neither pressure or velocity
seemed much effected by the parameter change across all models. For shorter periods of
time, where the long term temperature can be assumed not to be affected much and where a
discomfort amongst the occupants can be tolerated, the cross-side ventilation strategies (CSV)
thus might be an acceptable method of ventilation. The same cannot be said of the single-side
ventilated models (SSV) under the same conditions, as the time needed for the ventilation to be
effective would result in both a deterioration of the long term thermal environment and require
a presumably longer period with no occupants to allow for the ventilation to take effect.
5.3.2
Variation of temperature (18°)
The change of the bases-line parameter setting to a higher temperature seemed to result in a
slightly lower volume flow rate for the single-side ventilated models (SSV), while the cross-side
ventilated models remained more or less unchanged. Not surprisingly, the tendency was thus
more or less opposite to the variation with the lower temperature. The former thus again appear
more exposed to changes in temperature, which might again be attributed to principles of ther-
mal buoyancy. Similarly the vertical single-side ventilated model (SSV-Ver) showed tendencies
towards a slightly lower change in the volume flow rate.

==PAGE:68==
For the single-side ventilated models (SSV), the parameter change showed tendencies towards
an worsening of the general conditions of the indoor environment in terms of the concentration
of CO2, and overall contributed to the median of the data achieving levels further above the rec-
ommended max concentration. The temperature showed a tendency of having actually risen
a few degrees at steady state conditions (as seen compared to a an assumed indoor temper-
ature of 20°), which is assumed caused by a minor oversight, as the radiators had not been
switched off in the simulation as would likely have been the case at this outdoor temperature.
As the temperature can thus probably be assumed the same as the outdoors, neither the level
of CO2 or the thermal environment seemed to benefit much from this, as the occupants would
presumably prefer a lowering of the temperature in this case. On the other hand the cross-
ventilated models (CSV) did not seem to be effected by the variation in terms of the level of
CO2, but this might again be due to the level being close to the background concentration. In
terms of the thermal environment the models resulted in roughly the same temperatures as the
outdoors temperature. Neither pressure or velocity seemed much effected by the parameter
change across all models. Opposed to before though, this might be beneficial for the cross-side
ventilated models (CSV), as they would thus provide a slight draft at approximately outdoors
temperature, which depending on the occupants might improve the experienced indoor envi-
ronment. If the draft rate from the cross-side ventilated models (CSV) can be tolerated, all
ventilation strategies could in principle be used indefinitely without worsening the thermal envi-
ronment. The single-side ventilated models (SSV) would however not prove very effective in
terms of the concentration of CO2, except if the classroom was left unoccupied for presumably
a longer period of time, which might however not be completely unrealistic. The cross-side
ventilation models (CSV) would prove much more efficient, but caution should still be taken in
respect to the velocity and experienced indoor environment.
5.3.3
Variation of wind speed (10 m/s)
The change of the bases-line parameter setting to a higher wind speed seemed to result in a
much higher volume flow rate for both the single-side ventilated models (SSV) and the cross-
side ventilated models. The effect on the later corresponded to roughly a doubling of the volume
flow rate, while the effect on the former was a little less. This suggests, that a variation in the
wind speed affects the single-side ventilated models (SSV) to a large but not complete degree,
while the effect for the cross-side ventilated models (CSV) appears to be directly proportional.
This corresponds well with what might be expected, and seems to confirm the earlier sugges-
tions about the pressure build up and general flow-path tendencies in the classroom.
For the single-side ventilated models (SSV), the parameter change showed tendencies towards
an improvement of the general conditions of the indoor environment in terms of the concentra-
tion of CO2, and overall contributed to the median of the data achieving levels quite notably
below the recommended max concentration. The temperature showed a tendency of having
dropped a few degrees at steady state conditions (as seen compared to a an assumed in-
door temperature of 20°), but the effect was not large and might be expected during periods
with utilization of natural ventilation. The pressure median roses to roughly four times of the
base-models, while the velocity median was a little more than doubled, but still not very high
relatively seen. The cross-side ventilated models (CSV) did not seem to be effected by the
variation in terms of the level of CO2, but this might again be due to the level being close to the
background concentration. In terms of the thermal environment the models resulted in slightly
larger deviation (as seen compared to a an assumed indoor temperature of 20°), while the pres-
sure median again was roughly four times the base-models and the velocity median roughly
was doubled. Opposite before, this resulted in the median of the velocity moving towards what
might be considered a too high value (1 m/s), which while considering the corresponding max
velocities being even higher, could result in quite a bit of dissatisfied occupants. Overall the
single-side ventilated models thus seemed to improve notably with the wind speed and as a

mean of ventilation during occupancy, although it should be noted that potential effects on e.g.
the acoustic environment has not been investigated. On the contrary, the cross-side ventilated
models showed a clear tendency of extremes within both temperature deviations and velocity
in general, and thus can not be said to perform well under these conditions. The later might still
be used in periods with no occupancy, given that the indoor climate deviations can be tolerated.
5.3.4
Variation of wind direction (15°)
The change of the bases-line parameter setting to a different wind direction at a glance seemed
to result in some of the least uniform results overall. However, it should be noted that the sim-
ulation of the vertical singe-side ventilated model (SSV-Ver) is suspected to be erroneous, as
practically no air (0.04 m3/s is reported to enter the room. Thus it is omitted for the following
discussion, and only the centered single-side model (SSV-Cen) is considered for this ventila-
tion strategy group (SSV). As such, the parameter still showed clear signs of primarily effecting
the single-side ventilated model (SSV-Cen), while the cross-ventilated models (CSV) remained
almost unchanged. This corresponds well with what might be expected, as the angle of the flow
would thus result in a more direct route into the classroom, which for the former would result in
presumably more skewed turbulent motion of the air within the classroom, while the air would
still be able to leave through the back facade in the later. The results likewise seem to suggest,
that the left and right side of the classroom are more exposed to changes in the wind direction,
as compared to the center.
For the single-side ventilated models (SSV), the parameter change showed tendencies towards
an improvement of the general conditions of the indoor environment in terms of the concentra-
tion of CO2, and overall contributed to the median of the data achieving levels quite notably
below the recommended max concentration. The temperature showed a tendency of having
dropped moderately at steady state conditions (as seen compared to a an assumed indoor
temperature of 20°), which might cause some discomfort even considering the what might be
expected during periods with utilization of natural ventilation. As the only model the pressure
changed sign, which might again be ascribed to flow now going more directly in through one
end of the classroom and out through the other, thus resembling conditions similar to the cross
ventilation. Likewise the median of the velocity was effected towards a higher value, presum-
ably due to the same change in the general flow-pattern. For the cross-side ventilation models
(CSV) no major effect of the parameter variation was found, as compared to the base-model in
respect to the indoor climate parameters.
5.3.5
Variation of opening degree (15°)
The change of the bases-line parameter setting to a different opening degree seemed to result
in a much lower volume flow rate for both the single-side ventilated models (SSV) and the
cross-side ventilated models (CSV). This might also be expected, as the parameter change
directly effects the effective opening area of the windows. The trend suggest that the reported
medians were highly influenced by a change in the opening degree, which appeared to be more
important, when the volume flow rate was initially high rather than low.
For the single-side ventilated models (SSV), the parameter change showed tendencies towards
a deterioration of the general conditions of the indoor environment in terms of the concentration
of CO2, and overall contributed to the median of the data achieving levels quite a bit higher than
the recommended max concentration. The temperature showed a tendency of having dropped
only a few degrees at steady state conditions (as seen compared to a an assumed indoor
temperature of 20°), while the pressure and velocity did not change notably. For the cross-side
ventilation models (CSV) no major effect of the parameter variation was found, as compared to
the base-model in respect to the indoor climate parameters.

5.3.6
Variation of window width (1.2m)
The change of the bases-line parameter setting to a different window width seemed to result
in an increase in the volume flow rate for both the single-side ventilated models (SSV) and the
cross-side ventilated models (CSV). This might also be expected, as the parameter change
again directly effects the effective opening area of the windows. The effect seemed larger for
the former, which might be due to the flow of air having to both go in and out of the same open-
ing area, and thus increasing this would allow for a larger flow.
For the single-side ventilated models (SSV), the parameter change showed mixed tendencies,
with the centered model (SSV-Cen) hinting of a slight deterioration of the general conditions
of the indoor environment, while the vertical model (SSV-Ver) showed tendencies of a slight
increase of the same, in terms of the concentration of CO2. Overall both models contributed to
the median of the data achieving levels around or a bit lower than the recommended max con-
centration. The temperature showed a tendency of having dropped moderately at steady state
conditions (as seen compared to a an assumed indoor temperature of 20°), while the pressure
and velocity did not change notably. Overall this suggested that the indoor environment was
snot notably changed for the parameter. For the cross-side ventilation models (CSV) no major
effect of the parameter variation was found, as compared to the base-model in respect to the
indoor climate parameters.
5.4
Comparison with case study
The comparison of the case study CFD model and the physical case study location was car-
ried out in an attempt to provide the overall study with some degree of relation to an actual
classroom, that could be said to resemble the simulated ones. Overall the comparison showed
a tendency for the simulated velocities to resemble the measured values quite well, although
with some differences. The tendency was most clear for the values towards the back, while
the ones at the front varied more. It should be recalled, that the case study CFD model at best
resembles a very broad version of reality, and as such variations should be expected in general.
Numerous uncertainty could be the included, and as both the measured and simulated values
in general are quite low, they might easily be affected by outside parameters. To mention a
few, this could include variability in the wind speed and pattern or qualities of the surrounding
areas not taken into account in the CFD model. As mainly the values at the left front windows
seem to deviate, the pattern resembles the findings above for the parameter variations with a
change in wind direction, and thus it might be that the wind simply might not have been oriented
directly perpendicular to the facade at the physical location. Likewise the tracer gas experiment
resulted in a volume flow rate and corresponding air change rate rather close to their simulated
counterparts. As such, the CFD modeling seems a reasonable simplification of the physical
case study location, given the various uncertainties and the generic nature of the case study
CFD model.
5.5
Comparison with theoretical study
As for the case study, the theoretical comparison was carried out to provide some degree of
relation between a selection of expressions that might usually be used to calculate volume
flow rates and air flow rates in a building, as seen in relation to the simulated results. The
results for the comparison of the simulated SSV-Cen values and the values calculated through
the expression provided by De Gids and Phaff, showed a general tendency for the calculated
volume flow rates to be slightly higher than their simulated counterparts. The opposite case
was evident for the comparison of the simulated CSV-Hor values and the expression provided
by Karava, which showed a general tendency for the calculated volume flow rates to be slightly
lower than their simulated counterparts. Overall the difference did not represent a very notable
difference, meaning that the values seemed to within the same approximate order of magnitude.

Various reasons might exist for the difference, including the selection of the Cp values in the
expressions or inaccuracies in the simulations. As a whole the results seem to suggest, that
the simulated results to a reasonable degree can be said to be comparable with the theoretical
expressions, given the various uncertainties and the generic nature of the case study CFD
model.

6
Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to investigate different natural ventilation strategies and their
effect on ventilation and indoor environment in a generic representation of a classroom, while
simultaneously varying various parameters assumed to affect the flow. The aim was to gain
knowledge about how the strategies and parameter variations seemed to affect the indoor en-
vironment of a classroom, and to evaluate whether tendencies could be found, which might be
used as a broad starting point for the design of future investigations and studies. As a mean of
simplified validation, the results were compared to a real-life case study and similar theoretical
expressions.
The investigation of the four natural ventilation strategies showed, that neither of the strategies
in themselves could be said to provide an overall adequate base for an acceptable indoor
environment at steady state conditions. All four initially complied with the minimum volume
flow rates from regulations and standards, but an analysis of the facade air flow patterns of
the base-models and similar section cuts through the windows suggested, that this effect was
most noticeable locally within the flow-stream, and that the credibility of the reported volume
flow rates cannot unambiguously be used as a measure for the effectiveness of the ventilation
in the classroom as a whole. This was backed by the analysis of the effect on indoor climate
parameters. While some variation was found within the single- (SSV) and cross-side (CSV)
ventilation strategy groups respectively, the most notable difference was between the groups
themselves. The single-side ventilated models (SSV) showed tendencies of being ineffective
in the removal of CO2, but generally stayed within reasonable temperature variations and low
velocities, thus amplifying the suspicion that the majority of classroom was not ventilated very
well. The cross-side ventilated models (CSV) showed a tendency of the opposite, with an
effective removal of CO2, but with large variations in temperature and general high velocities,
thus proving an effective mean of ventilation but with risk of potential discomfort. A subsequent
discussion of the results as seen in relation to the setting and use indicated, that a combination
of the strategies with other methods of ventilation might be beneficial for the overall design and
renovation possibilities. This might also be seen in relation to the possibilities of combining
the single- cross side ventilation strategies in situations where the tolerated level of potential
discomfort could be disregarded to various degree, which might depend on the season or time
of the day.
The investigation of the parameter variations showed a tendency for the indoor environment to
mainly be affected by changes in wind speed and direction, suggesting a dominant nature of
the pressure difference generated by the wind induced force regardless of the strategy. While
variations in temperature showed some contribution in terms of increased buoyancy effect, it
was small and did not contribute to large improvements of the indoor environment. Neither
the variation of opening degree or window width lead to noticeable changes to the indoor en-
vironment, and mainly contributed as measure of controlling the magnitude of the volume flow
rate.
The comparison with the case study and theoretical expressions did not results in large differ-
ences, but neither did it show a overall uniform tendency in the differences. Given this however,
it seemed evident that the generic CFD models to some degree of accuracy could be said to
represent a similar real-life or theoretical scenario, and thus the results of the CFD study in gen-
eral might be assumed able to describe broad tendencies across the four ventilation strategies
and parameter variations.
