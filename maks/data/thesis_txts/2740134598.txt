Abstract
This thesis investigates the use of sewage sludge ashes in alkali activated materials (AAMs). The properties
of the sewage sludge ashes are examined in their raw form and after having undergone phosphorous extraction
treatment. Three sewage sludge ashes are examined, SSA from Avedøre wastewater treatment plant, SSA-ED,
SSA after having undergone electrodialytic phosphorous extraction treatment, and silica sand, from Lynetten
wastewater treatment plant subjected to Ash2Phos phosphorous extraction. These sewage sludge ashes and
inert quartz are incorporated in geopolymers with MetaMax metakaolin (MK) as precursor at 0, 10, 20, and
40 % addition or replacement. Inert quartz is used as a control to determine whether the change in the AAMs
properties is due to volume changes or the effects of the sewage sludge ashes. The properties of the metakaolin
is then compared to an excerpt of those used in literature. To characterize the materials used, a series of different
experimental techniques were used: acid digestion, leaching, XRF, XRD and compression test. Through the
characterisation of the raw materials it is found that the sewage sludge ashes are high in allumino-sillicates, with
the treated sewage sludge ashes having the highest relative content and thereby closer in make up to metakaolin.
The SiO2/Al2O3 ratio of the metakaolin, SSA, SSA-ED and silica sand is found to be 1.16, 2.62, 4.43 and 5.95
respectively. The setting for the AAMs was 21 days, and as such testing on these were done at 28 and 90+
days. The 28 day series with differing replacement percentages did not develop sufficient mechanical strength
to be compression tested, and the integrity test performed shows a lack of environmental stability at this stage
as well. The compression tests show that addition of quartz aids in the mechanical strength of AAMs. Between
the ashes, AAMs with silica sand added at 20 - 40 % yields results comparable to the pure MK specimen.
At 90+ days SSA-ED yields results suggesting high pozzolanic activity when partially replacing metakaolin
in the AAMs. The integrity tests of the AAMs show a high degree of heavy metals from the sewage sludge
ashes being immobilized in the geopolymers, except in the case of silica sand where the Ash2Phos treatment
has already greatly reduced the heavy metal content. Through the comparison with literature, it is found that
the used metakaolin yields AAMs with a lower compression strength despite being chemically similar. The
insights present in this thesis provide basis for further investigation into application of sewage sludge ashes in
geopolymers.

Sammenfatning
Denne afhandling undersøger brugen af spildevandsslamaske i alkaliaktiverede materialer (AAM’er). Spilde-
vandsslamaskernes egenskaber undersøges i rå form og efter at have gennemgået en fosforudvindingsbehan-
dling. Tre spildevandsslamasker undersøges, SSA fra Avedøre renseanlæg, SSA-ED som er SSA efter at have
gennemgået elektrodialytisk fosforudvinding, og silica sand, en spildevandsslamaske fra Lynetten renseanlæg,
der har været udsat for Ash2Phos fosforudvinding. Disse spildevandsslamasker og inert kvarts er inkorporeret
i geopolymerer med MetaMax metakaolin (MK) som forløber ved 0, 10, 20 og 40 % tilsætning eller erstatning.
Inert kvarts bruges som kontrol til at bestemme, om ændringen i AAMs egenskaber skyldes volumen ændringer
eller virkningerne af spildevandsslamasken. Egenskaberne af metakaolin sammenlignes derefter med et uddrag
af dem, der er brugt i litteraturen. For at karakterisere de anvendte materialer blev der brugt en række forskel-
lige eksperimentelle teknikker: oplukning, udvaskning, XRF, XRD og trykstyrketest. Gennem karakteris-
eringen af råmaterialerne viser det, at spildevandsslamasken har et højt indhold af allumino-silikater, hvor de
behandlede spildevandsslasasker har det højeste relative indhold og dermed tættere på metakaolin. SiO2/Al2O3
forholdet mellem metakaolin, SSA, SSA-ED og silicasand er fundet at være henholdsvis 1,16, 2,62, 4,43 og
5,95. AAM’erne stod i 21 dage før de kunne afformes, og der blev derved først testet på disse efter 28 og
90+ dage. 28-dage serien med forskellige erstatningsprocenter udviklede ikke tilstrækkelig mekanisk styrke til
at blive trykstyrketestet, og den udførte integritetstest viser også mangel på miljøstabilitet. Trykstyrketestene
viser, at tilsætning af kvarts fremmer den mekaniske styrke af AAM’er. Ift. askerne, giver AAM’er tilsat 20
- 40 % silica sand resultater, der kan sammenlignes med den rene MK-prøve. Ved 90+ dage giver SSA-ED
resultater, der tyder på høj puzzolanaktivitet, når den delvist erstatter metakaolin i AAM’erne. Integritetstesten
af AAM’erne viser at en høj grad af tungmetaller fra spildevandsslamasken immobiliseres i geopolymererne,
undtagen i tilfælde af silicasand, hvor Ash2Phos-behandlingen allerede har reduceret tungmetalindholdet bety-
deligt. Gennem sammenligningen med litteraturen viser det sig, at det anvendte metakaolin giver AAM’er med
en lavere trykstyrke på trods af de kemisk sammelignelige egenskaber. Den indsigt givet i dette speciale, giver
grundlag for yderligere undersøgelse af anvendelse af spildevandsslamaske i geopolymerer.

Introduction
Construction and the surrounding industry is one of the world’s global sinners in regard to CO2 emissions,
accounting for 37% of global emissions (United Nations Environment Programme 2023). Approximately three
quarters of this is accounted for by the operational costs of a building: lighting and temperature regulation, and
it is projected that this will be reduced to 50% within the next decades (United Nations Environment Programme
2023). However, this still allows for classic construction materials and industry to account for 9% of global
CO2 emissions, dwarfing both the shipping and aviation sectors which are commonly referred to as the culprits
when referencing climate change (United Nations Environment Programme 2023). Because of this, interest
has been awoken in finding less environmentally taxing alternatives to cement and concrete in the construction
industry, either by substituting for a greener product and/or repurposing waste products.
In 2015 the UN’s 17 Sustainable Development goals were adopted by all member countries presented as The
2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development (UN n.d.). In relation to construction industry, and the subject mat-
ter of this master thesis, there are three sustainable development goals that are exceptionally relevant, and are
shown in figure 1. Goal 11 is to make cities and settlements inclusive, safe and sustainable; next is goal 12 with
sustainable consumption and production patterns; and lastly goal 13 taking urgent action to combat climate
change and mitigate its impacts (UN n.d.).
Figure 1: UN Sustainable Development Goals 11, 12 and 13 (UN n.d.).
A method by which the carbon emissions from the materials in the construction industry can be reduced is by
using geopolymers, thereby decreasing the need for cement. Geopolymers are typically made using an alkali
activated material, such as metakaolin. Unfortunately metakaolin is also environmentally costly as it requires
the firing of kaolin (Garcia-Lodeiro et al. 2015). However, the carbon footprint can be reduced further by
adding repurposed waste products, in the form of sewage sludge ash. Sewage sludge ash is the waste product
formed after sewage and wastewater from cities and other human settlements have gone through the processes
at a wastewater treatment plant and the clean water has been filtered off. The leftover sludge is then incinerated,
often used for district heating or electricity production, and the remaining ash has historically been relegated
to landfills. Using ashes as secondary materials has been done for decades in the construction industry, namely
coal fly ash in concrete. This use is however dwindling as coal use as an energy source is in decline. Due
to its high aluminum and silica content, like metakaolin, it is proposed that sewage sludge ash could act as a
secondary alkali activated material in geopolymers.
In this master thesis project, the properties of sewage sludge ashes are examined, in both their raw form as
well as after phosphorous extraction via two different methods, compared to metakaolin. Furthermore the
sewage sludge ash is mixed into geopolymers, with increments of 10%, 20% and 40% replacement or addition
to metakaolin. Thereby the environmental and mechanical stability of sewage sludge ash in alkali-activated
materials is studied. Lastly, a litterature study is conducted in order to determine the essential characteristics of

metakaolin and the chemical composition of the most effective geopolymers.
Based on these findings, the use of sewage sludge ash in the construction industry, whether pre-treatment of the
sewage sludge ashes has been beneficial, and the importance of the characteristics of metakaolin is discussed.

Theory
This section provides the necessary theory for a basic understanding of the concepts and methods applied in this
thesis. Firstly alkali activated materials, AAMs, also commonly known as geopolymers are presented. Mainly
focusing on metakaolin as precursor and the reactions mechanisms in a metakaolin based AAM. Following
this, sewage sludge ashes are introduced, with focus on their production, composition and further treatments
applied to the ashes. Lastly the incorporation of sewage sludge ashes in geopolymers is presented, where the
environmental quality requirements to be used in building materials are also ascertained.
6.1
Alkali-activated materials
Geopolymers are cementitious materials containing aluminosilicates activated by alkaline solutions, where
these agents form covalent bonds with high tensile strength (Kuenzel et al. 2014). Geopolymers can be syn-
thesised from a variety of different precursors, both natural or synthetic, such as slag, fly ash, and metakaolin
Garcia-Lodeiro et al. (2015). Crucial for these materials is their content of aluminum oxide, silicon dioxide and
calcium oxides as these are the main indicators of a given material’s pozzolanic activity. The distribution of
precursors often used in alkali activated materials are shown in an Al/Si/Ca-oxide pyramid are shown in figure
2.
Figure 2: Common composition range for precursors in alkali activated materials in a Al2O3-SiO2-CaO dia-
gram. Figure found in Crucial insights on the mix design of alkali-activated cement-based binders (Garcia-
Lodeiro et al. 2015).
In figure 2 the industrial byproduct silica fumes, SF, is also included as an example of a precursor in an AAM,
and all the precursors are sat in relation to ordinary portland cement, OPC.
6.1.1
Metakaolin as precursor
As seen in figure 2 the pozzolanic activity of metakaolin is mainly due to its Al2O3 and SiO2 content, as it has
close to no CaO the main constituent of cements binding abilities.
Metakaolin is an amorphous aluminosilicate produced by the calcination of kaolin, found in naturally occurring
DTU Sustain
Page 9 of 55
==PAGE:11==
kaolinitic clay. It is a highly reactive pozzolanic material which majority oxides consist of 20-35 % Al2O3 and
40-70 % SiO2, with <1 % of iron oxide, Fe2O3 and TiO2 and other materials such as Na2O (Garcia-Lodeiro
et al. 2015). Firing kaolin and kaolinitic clay typically also produces minerals such as illite and quartz, which
may also be found in metakaolin (Feldthus 2023).
A commonly used activator in alkali activated materials is a sodium hydroxide, NaOH, solution, providing Na+
ions to neutralize the negative charge on the aluminum oxide ion in the tetrahedral SiO4 and AlO4 groups in
geopolymers (Kuenzel et al. 2014). The minerals typically present in metakolin based geopolymers after curing
are quartz, anatase, hematite and different sodium carbonates, also known as trona (Kumar & Revathi 2020)
(Zibouche et al. 2009).
6.1.2
Reaction mechanisms
The journal article New cements for the 21st century: The pursuit of an alternative to Portland cement explains
the geopolymer reaction mechanism based on Gluhhovsky’s earlier works (Shi et al. 2011). A flow diagram of
the reaction mechanism is shown in figure 3.
Figure 3: Geopolymer raction mechanism. Figure found in New cements for the 21st century: The pursuit of
an alternative to Portland cement (Shi et al. 2011).
The first step encompasses the mixture of precursor and activator, where the dissolution of aluminosilicates by
alkaline hydrolysis begins immediately. This dissolution is rapid at a high pH, and results in the precipitation
of aluminosilicates, and once saturation occurs it forms the bi-phasic N-A-S-H gel, with water filling the pores
of the gel. This first gel has a high Al concentration and is only pseudo stable (Garcia-Lodeiro et al. 2015). A
secondary gel phase follows where the N-A-S-H precipitation forms a continuous gel, and begins to reorganize
and form a high-connectivity network. This gel is high in silicon and drives the development of the geopoly-
mer’s mechanical properties (Shi et al. 2011). The time in which the continuous gel is produced varies greatly,
and some systems may never gel. After this, the gel begins to polymerize as nucleation occurs. This formation
is dependent on both kinetic and thermodynamic factors. Lastly growth occurs, as the nuclei reach a critical
size, and a crystalline structure develops. This crystalline structure determines many of the physical properties
of the geopolymer (Duxson et al. 2007).
DTU Sustain
Page 10 of 55
==PAGE:12==
Crucial to the strength development of the geopolymer and the reorganisation of the N-A-S-H gel is the
SiO2/Al2O3 ratio. The initial SiO2/Al2O3 ratio in materials that exhibit highest mechanical strength is from 2
to 4 (Garcia-Lodeiro et al. 2015). These concentrations allow for structural reorganization and are linked with
the porosity of geopolymers with metakaolin as precursor. If the concentration of soluble silicon is too high,
it hinders the reorganization of the N-A-S-H gel in the second phase and contributes to a weaker crystalline
structure with a higher porosity (Duxson et al. 2007). While too little aluminum retards the process from gel
1 to 2, if the SiO2/Al2O3 ratio is too low and subsequently the aluminum content is elevated the aluminosili-
cate dissolution rate raised, decreasing the initial gel production and likewise causes lower mechanical strength
(Garcia-Lodeiro et al. 2015).
6.2
Sewage sludge ash
Sewage sludge ash is produced from sewage sludge from wastewater treatment plants. The wastewater treat-
ment plants connected to this projected are both located in urban areas, and the sludge collected is reflective
of this and mainly consists of organic materials. The sludge is stored in large tanks resulting in an anaerobic
process creating bio gas. The bio gas is later used for either electricity or district heating. The remaining sludge
is dried using a centrifuges, and the low moisture sludge is then incinerated (Vandetsvej.dk n.d.).
Biofos treats wastewater for 1.2 million inhabitants in the greater Copenhagen area in their three wastewater
treatment plants: Lynetten, Avedøre, and Damhusåen (Biofos n.d.). Wastewater treatments plants Lynetten and
Avedøre both incinerate the collected sludge, creating sewage sludge ash.
At the Avedøre treatment plant chemical precipitation with iron is used to remove phosphorous from the
wastewater, resulting in the sludge having a high iron content. After the sludge is fired in a fluid bed incinerator
at 850 ◦C, the sewage sludge ash has a vibrant red color due to the iron oxides contained within (Ottosen et al.
2019). Lynetten wastewater treatment plant likewise incinerates their sewage sludge ash at 850 ◦C (Ottosen
et al. 2022).
The sewage sludge ash is categorized as a waste product and is in Denmark set for temporary disposal, until
possibilities for reuse arise (Kirkelund 2023). The typical chemical composition of sewage sludge ash are
shown in table 1.

6.2.1
Treatment of sewage sludge ash
Phosphorous is on the European Union’s list over critical raw materials as it is essential to the economy in
Europe (European Commission n.d.). Phosphorous is widely used in fertilizers for agricultural use and currently
all phosphorous in Europe is imported (Kirkelund 2023). Since sewage sludge ash is an under utilized waste
product, interest in extracting phosphorous from this resource has gained traction. Consequently Denmark aims
to reuse 80 % of phosphorous from sewage sludge and sewage sludge ashes (Kirkelund 2023).
Sewage sludge ashes from Avedøre and Lynetten wastewater treatment plants have had phosphorous extracted
through two distinct methods, which are further described below.
Electrodialytic treatment of sewage sludge ash
Electrodialytic treatment of sewage sludge ash for the extraction of phosphorous is conducted in a two com-
partment cell as shown in figure 4 (Ottosen et al. 2019). The anode compartment on the left contains sewage
sludge ash suspended in demineralised water, where a constant electric current of 50mA is passed through to
recover phosphorous. The cell simultaneously removes heavy metal to ensure clean phosphorous production.
After the treatment is concluded, the extracted heavy metals are contained in the catholyte and the phosphorous
is contained in the anolyte (Ottosen et al. 2019).
Figure 4: Electrodialytic cell (Ottosen et al. 2019).
The phosphorous and treated sewage sludge ash is separated through filtration and and dried at 105 ◦C.
Easymining Ash2Phos
This phosphorous extraction technology is developed by the Swedish company EasyMining. The main purpose
of the company is extracting phosphorous from sewage sludge ash for use in mineral fertilizer (EasyMining
n.d.a). As this is a commercial company the specifics of their extraction technology is proprietary information,
however the main components of their process is depicted in figure 5.

6.3
Sewage sludge ash in geopolymers
Valorisation of sewage sludge ash has been studied for decades by scientists across the world. So far the general
consensus is that there are five main uses depending on the characteristics of the specific sewage sludge ash:
pozzolanic addition to cement, light aggregrate production, bricks and ceramics production, in cement clinker
production, and as a source for phosphorous (Kappel et al. 2018).
As the pozzolanic possibilities of sewage sludge ashes have already been established by using them in cements,
incorporating them in other cementitious materials is the logical next step. In alkali activated materials the
allumino silicate content is essential which is generally one of the major components in sewage sludge ash.
This suggests that their incorporation alongside metakaolin in geopolymers could have a positive effect (Garcia-
Lodeiro et al. 2015). Furthermore, sewage sludge ash in alkali activated materials have garnered positive results
in multiple cases both on cost and environmental friendliness (Istuque et al. 2019).
There are currently no standards for the use of geopolymers containing sewage sludge ash in construction as it
is still on a research level. However there is a statute that classifies the use of waste products for geotechnical
purposes, Statute on the use of residual products, soil and sorted construction and construction waste BEK nr
1672, and this can be used as a guideline for the environmental class of geopolymer (Miljø- og Fødevaremin.
n.d.). The categorization is as shown in figure 3.

7.3
Characterization
Several different methods are used to characterise both the raw materials and the geopolymer experimental
samples.
7.3.1
Particle size distribution
The particle size of the samples were measured using a laser diffractometer, the Malvern Mastersizer 2000. It
was measured on wet samples of the SSA, SSA-ED, silica sand and quartz using the ethanol method. The par-
ticle size distribution for SSA was performed by the laboratory technicians at DTU, while the distribution for
SSA-ED was performed by Frederik Højsager and Joachim Hermansen, bachelor students at DTU (Hoejsager
& Hermansen 2024). The particle size distribution for silica sand and quartz were likewise performed by Minna
Vedel, also a bachelor student at DTU (Vedel 2023).
7.3.2
Digestion
Microwave assisted acid digestion is applied to determine the content of acid soluble metals in the additives.
The acid digestion is carried out in accordance with American standard EPA 3051A (U.S. EPA 2007). 10 mL
nitric acid (HNO3) is added to 0.5 g sample, which is mixed and placed in an microwave for approximately 20
minutes. Afterwards the sample is processed through an 0.45 µm filter, and demineralised water is added to
the solution until a total volume of 50 mL. This is then ready for an Inductively Coupled Plasma, ICP, analysis
performed by the laboratory technicians at DTU. Acid digestion on SSA and silica sand were likewise per-
formed by laboratory technician whilst digestion on SSA-ED and quarts was performed by Frederik Højsager
and Joachim Hermansen (Hoejsager & Hermansen 2024). The analysis measures the concentration in mg/mL
which is then converted to mg/kg through the following equation 1:
mg/kg = A · C
Bkg/g
(1)
In equation 1 A is the metal concentration from the ICP analysis, B is the mass of the additive (0.5 g), and C is
the dilution factor, 50 mL (Arktisk Teknologi og Bæredygtige Løsninger 2016).

7.3.3
Leaching
Determining the amount of water soluble heavy metals is done in accordance with the AshCycle method of
ash characterization and categorization (AshCycle 2023) and in collaboration with the laboratory technicians
at DTU. A batch leach test was done on the dry sample and demineralised water mixed in a plastic bottle
with a liquid/solid, L/S, ratio of 10. The sample was agitated for 24 hours whereafter the solution was filtered
through a ≤45 µm filter syringe. Thereafter the pH and electrical conductivity was measured using radiometer
electrodes. The test sample is then ready for an ICP analysis where results are given in mg/L. The test was
performed on the raw materials as well as one of the four produced samples of each geopolymer mix.
Furthermore, in connection with this test, the mass of the AAMs were measured before and after the leaching
test as a measurement of the integrity of the samples.
7.3.4
X-ray fluorescence
X-ray fluorescence, XRF, analysis is done to determine the elemental and chemical composition of the ashes
and quartz. A sample is excited by X-ray radiation, resulting in the sample emitting characteristic x-ray fluores-
cence in accordance with the elements contained within (AshCycle 2023). These signals are used to determine
the composition of the test sample, with a wide range of elements included. These have been done on the
raw materials previously and were supplied by Ebba Cederberg Schnell or through previous bachelor theses
(Hoejsager & Hermansen 2024) (Vedel 2023).
7.3.5
X-ray difraction
An x-ray diffraction analysis, XRD, was used to determine the main crystalline phases in the alkali activated
materials. The geopolymer cubes used for compression test, were ground with a mortar to a size of ≤63 µm,
and placed in a PANalytical X’Pert Pro to perform the analysis. The PANalytical X’Pert Pro analysed the
samples using Cu K α radiation from 20◦to 100 2 θ at an accelerating voltage and current of 45 kV and 40
mA in accordance with the AshCycle program (AshCycle 2023). The major peaks were then identified using
Highscore Plus software and the ICDD-2 database.
7.4
Compression test
The compression test was meant to be performed on the geopolymers after curing for 7 days and 28 days,
however, due to the paste lacking integrity until 21 days, compression tests were instead performed at 28 days
and 90+ days where the geopolymers are no longer developing and the compression strength is final. As such
there were samples for both addition an replacement of the different additives at 28 days, and 90+ day addition
samples were done at 268 days and the replacement samples at 190 days. The compression tests were performed
using the SHY-064-Matest in the materials lab at DTU. The experimental setup is shown in figure 7.

The strongest geopolymer at 28 days of curing was produced by addition of 20 % inert quartz, followed closely
by MK and by 20 % and 40 % silica sand addition. During the curing process of metakaolin based alkali
activated materials extensive drying shrinkage can be mitigated by the addition of sand to the geopolymer
(Kuenzel et al. 2014). Although the particle size of inert quartz is much smaller than that of sand, it is plausible
that this has the same effect as using sand and creates a network within the geopolymer that reduces shrinkage
and thereby increases mechanical stability, and causing the quartz A20 to be stronger that the MK sample.
The AAMs containing 20 % and 40 % silica sand addition were next in line after MK. Silica sand A20 has
an incredibly high standard deviation compared to A40, however the results are very similar. One of the
A40 specimens was excluded due to it not having sufficient mechanical stability to register a break on the
machine, and the A20 had one specimen that was markedly lower than the others. Surprisingly silica sand A10
also resulted in the geopolymer with the lowest compression strength, whereas the other AAMs were more
consistent across the different addition percentages, perhaps because the pozzolanic activity to the other ashes
is more similar to that of metakaolin.

Crystalline phases
Although an XRD analysis was performed on all geopolymers dry enough to be crushed to less than or equal
to 63 µm, mineralogy was only determined on the mix ratios that led to the strongest and weakest geopolymers
in the 28 day and 90+ day series.
Throughout the different XRD analyses, five different mineral phases were identified: quartz, hematite, anatase,
whitlockite, and sodium carbonates as shown in figure 14. 15, 16, and 17. This is in agreement with the gen-
eral expectations based on literature (Kumar & Revathi 2020) (Ottosen et al. 2019) (Ottosen et al. 2022). The
reference codes, scores and semi quantities for the minerals for each of the series can be found in appendix E.
Figure 14: Crystalline phases of addition/replacement ratio that yielded the highest long term compression
strength. Q=quartz, H=hematite, A=anatase, W=whitlockite, T=sodium carbonates.
The geopolymer with the highest mechanical stability was 90+ day quartz at 10 % addition. The XRD analysis
for the 90+ d A10 series is shown in figure 14. In this series, the addition of quartz led to the strongest
AAM followed by SSA-ED, silica sand and SSA, although there is a marked gap between quartz and the
sewage sludge ashes. Quartz and sodium carbonate peaks were found across all samples, and hematite across
all sewage sludge ash samples, which is what gives them their red color. The weakest sewage sludge ash in
this series is SSA, the only AAM containing whitlockite. Whitlockite is a mineral containing phosphorous,
which the other two ashes having undergone phosphorous extraction treatments did not produce. The treated
sewage sludge ashes also both performed better than the untreated, perhaps because the existence of whitlockite
retards strength development in geopolymers or because with phosphourous extracted, the remaining elements
in whitlockite, Ca and OH, are free to interact in other bond or raises the alkalinity, promoting pozzolanic
activity (Garcia-Lodeiro et al. 2015).

An excerpt of alkali activated materials in literature
In this section the alkali activated materials in the six different articles shown in table 7 will be evaluated. First

8.3.1
Overview of articles
Strength and leachability of geopolymers with the addition of municipal solid waste ashes (Łach et al. 2019)
studies immobilizing waste from municipal solid waste incineration (MSWI) plants. The geopolymers were
based on 30 % metakaolin and 70 % fly ash (FY). Three different types of fly ash were used, FY1 from a
MSWI plant in Klaipeda, Lithuania, and FY2 and FY3 from a MSWI plant in Konin Poland from 2013 and
2015 repectively. The activator consisted of solid sodium hydroxide dissolved in a sodium silicate solution,
and the ashes included in this overview were washed before compression testing. The geopolymers were cast
and heated for 24 hours at 75 ◦C, then cooled, demolded and set to cure for 28 days at ambient temperature
(Łach et al. 2019). No XRF analysis was done on either precursor or secondary material, so the oxide ratios are
unknown. The results produced in this article are designated as MFYx in table 19 and figure 18.
A metakaolin and steel slag based geopolymer is included in this study from Strength of Geopolymer Cement
Curing at Ambient Temperature by Non-Oven Curing Approaches: An Overview (Wattanachai & Suwan 2017).
The AAM consists of 80 % metakaolin and 20 % steel slag, cured at ambient temperature for 28 days. Unfor-
tunately no XRF was performed, but the activator consisted of a waterglass and NaOh solution (Wattanachai
& Suwan 2017). This sample is marked as MKS in table 19 and figure 18 where the compressive results are
displayed.
The articles Effect of sewage sludge ash on mechanical and microstructural properties of geopolymers based
on metakaolin (Istuque et al. 2019) and Evaluation of the long-term compressive strength development of the
sewage sludge ash/metakaolin–based geopolymer (Istuque et al. 2021) both use precursor metakaolin from
Metacaulim do Brasil and use sewage sludge ash as a secondary precursor produced from a wastewater treat-
ment plant in in Sao Jose do Rio Preto city, Brasil. The characteristics of the metakaolin and sewage sludge ash
are shown in table 17 and 18.
Effect of sewage sludge ash on mechanical and microstructural properties of geopolymers based on metakaolin
(Istuque et al. 2019) investigates the effects of sewage sludge ash in geopolymers and with two different
SiO2/Na2O ratios, 0.8 and 1.6, and two different temperature curing conditions. Only the results for ambi-
ent temperature curing will be included in this study. An 8M NaOH solution was used as activator and were
cured for 28 days. The geopolymers produced in this study are named xSSA-y in table 19 and figure 18, where
x is the percentage sewage sludge ash used and y is the SiO2/Na2O ratio (Istuque et al. 2019).
The geopolymers in Evaluation of the long-term compressive strength development of the sewage sludge
ash/metakaolin–based geopolymer (Istuque et al. 2021) used a similar methodology as the previous article
by Istuque, but the focus is instead on the long term strengt development of the geopolymers. Compression
tests were performed between 3 and 720 days curing at ambient temperature, however only the specimens cured
for 28 days will be included in this thesis. A 1.6 SiO2/Na2O ratio was used and 8M NaOH activator solution
(Istuque et al. 2021). The samples from this paper are denominated MKSSA-x in table 19 and figure 18, where
x indicates the sewage sludge ash percentage.

Furthermore, it is made apparent that although SiO2/Al2O3 is essential in predicting the mechanical stability

The SiO2/Na2O ratio was investigated briefly Effect of sewage sludge ash on mechanical and microstructural
properties of geopolymers based on metakaolin (Istuque et al. 2019), finding that a ratio of 1.6 resulted in a
stronger geopolymer than a ratio of 0.8, as also depicted by the xSSA-y series in the graph in figure 18. This
consequently suggests that the SiO2/Na2O molar ratio is also a determining factor in the reaction mechanisms
leading to strength development in alkali activated materials.

sludge ash found in literature further confirms that sewage sludge ashes are extremely variable, as is the perfor-
mance of metakaolin despite much smaller chemical variation.

Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate sewage sludge ashes in their raw form as well as after phosphorous
treatment. Three different ashes were studied, SSA, SSA-ED and silica sand. SSA-ED and silica sand had
both undergone different phosphorous extraction treatments. The phosphorous extraction treatment caused not
only the phosphorous contents in SSA-ED and silica sand to be lower, but also their relative allumino-silicate
content to be higher, as well as a generally lower metal content when acid digested.
The true difference between untreated, electrodialytically treated, and Ash2Phos treated became apparent dur-
ing the water soluble metals test. The untreated ash SSA, had a high amount of water soluble metals, SSA-ED
even more as a potential consequence of insufficient extraction after separating the phosphorous, and lastly the
As2Phos silica sand had a markedly low amount of water soluble metals, causing it to be less environmentally
harmful.
Furthermore, alkali activated materials with 0, 10, 20 and 40 % either replacement or addition of the three
different sewage sludge ashes to a metakaolin based geopolymer were investigated. After 24h in a mold, all
samples were not yet set sufficiently to be demolded and this did not happen until day 21. The late development
of basic mechanical stability, suggests that these geopolymers would not be fit for use in industry. This was
common for all of the alkali activated materials, and is therefore not a reflection of the sewage sludge ashes’
suitability but rather that of the metakaolin.
The mechanical and environmental stability of the produced geopolymers were likewise studied at 28 days
and 90+ days. Based on the compression test results a few conclusions can be drawn. The alkali activated
materials containing silica sand generally exhibited higher mechanical stability when silica sand was added at
a high percentage, compared to when used as replacement for metakaolin. The addition of quartz produced
the highest performing geopolymer, despite it remaining inert. This suggests the quartz provided a stabilizing
network during the strength development of the specimen. Moreover, there is evidence of SSA-ED aiding in
strength development when the metakaolin amount is reduced, as replacement with SSA-ED in the geopolymers
cured over 90+ days produced markedly better compared to the others within this series. The geopolymers
containing untreated SSA, performed at a generally mediocre level compared to the other sewage sludge ashes,
placing consistently in the mid-range.
The environmental stability of the geopolymers at 28 days and 90+ days of curing, except for the 28 day
replacement series. In this case the geopolymers generally lost mass when agitated in demineralized water
for 24 hours and the electrical conductivity was increased, indicated unreacted alkalis leaching out into the
solution. The geopolymers were however generally successful at immobilizing heavy metals in the case of
SSA and SSA-ED, whereas the heavy metal content in silica sand was already low due to its phosphorous
extraction treatment.
Lastly, through the minor literature study it was found that the experimentally used metakaolin is chemically
similar to others on the market but with a higher than average LOI. It performed remarkably worse than the
other metakaolin geopolymers, even when using the same activators.
Based on these things it is still not possible to determine whether sewage sludge ashes could be used in the
construction industry. However, it is definitely still of great interest to continue investigating their possible use
within this sector, due to their chemical similarity to known binders in construction materials. Pre-treatment
of sewage sludge ash is concluded to be beneficial, not due to an extreme positive effect on the mechanical
DTU Sustain
Page 42 of 55
==PAGE:44==
properties of the resulting geopolymer, moreso because there were no directly surmised negative effects, as
well as the fact that phosphorous is a critically necessary element.
Further on, it would be of great interest to study the effect of different SiO2/Na2 ratios on alkali-activated
materials, as well as investigating the effect of the sewage sludge ashes in a geopolymer based on another
metakaolin that is known to facilitate higher mechanical stability.
