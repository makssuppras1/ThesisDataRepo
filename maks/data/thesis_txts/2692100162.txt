ABSTRACT
1
Abstract
As the world becomes more digitalized the reliability and correctness of computer hardware
become increasingly essential. SystemVerilog can be used to verify this, in a design and
veriﬁcation phase, before a physical chip is produced.
This project reviews the simulation semantics by which SystemVerilog achieve veriﬁcation.
The focus has been on clarifying the semantics specifying simulation of parallel processes.
A subset of SystemVerilog constructs are applied to the project and used to give an imple-
mentation of the Dining Philosophers problem. At this point a design of a simulator, that
implements the reviewed semantics, is given and it is used to simulate the Dining Philosophers
problem.
Conclusively, the ﬁndings in this thesis suggest that additional reviews of the semantics
should be made. For the applied subset the simulation semantics were found to target a
superset of needed requirements. Speciﬁcally, that it is idea of parallelism that SystemVerilog
wants – and not its behaviour.

INTRODUCTION
2
Introduction
The world of hardware design and veriﬁcation engineers have come closer together after
the establishment of SystemVerilog, a language that enable one to express the workings
of electronic circuits. The core of the language’s scope was for many years focused on the
capacity to describe hardware designs. Though, with the increased need to push new, ever
increasingly advanced products to market its scope needed expansion with new veriﬁcation
functionalities. The development cycle of hardware products consequently required a more
elaborate veriﬁcation phase during the design of the product. In SystemVerilog this is
predominantly achieved by simulation. Hardware design and veriﬁcation engineers can rely on
SystemVerilog in their work for as long as they can trust that designs are simulated correctly
according to their description and veriﬁcation attained as intended.
The original intent with the project was to review the semantics of the SystemVerilog sim-
ulation engine with an eye for implementing it into a simulator tool. The tool should be
able to compile source ﬁles written in a subset of SystemVerilog into Java byte code and be
semantically correct simulated in a Java runtime environment. The intention with basing the
simulator on Java is that it would be cross-platform compatible. This makes it ﬂexible to
decomposition since it is assured that at least this component can work on any platform.
Instead, due to to the general complexity and at times uncertainty of the semantics, a
considerable eﬀort of the project went with researching designs of simulators that could meet
the requirements. This came at the cost of never getting to generate java byte code. The
semantics, which are deﬁned in the industry standard, request diﬀerent requirements to a
simulator depending on how the before mentioned uncertainties was interpreted. Simulation
of a speciﬁc case under the various interpretations became the focus of the project. During
information searches it was not found that other sources had done a similar attempt to
investigate the SystemVerilog simulator.
The ﬁrst chapters of the project will introduce required background knowledge of SystemVer-
ilog. Initially the language constructs and the applied subset of those are introduced. Next oﬀ
the discrete-event model used by SystemVerilog to simulate is described. Finalizing the ﬁrst
of the report SystemVerilog’s requirements to a compiler is detailed. This includes how one of
such can be designed to ﬁt this project. The next part, chapter 4, focuses on the interaction
between language constructs and simulator. It shows how the state of the simulation model
changes when simulating speciﬁc constructs. At this point the base has been described and
the focus will turn to the experiments. The case used for experiments is given in chapter
5 followed by how a semantically correct, proof-of-concept simulator can be implemented.
Finally the results of the experiments are presented, compared and their applicability in the
real world discussed.

BACKGROUND
3
Background
In this chapter the included subset of SystemVerilog (SV) constructs and their interaction
are explained. In short, SV looks and writes like other programming languages such as C
and Java in how e.g. statements are deﬁned. Yet, its execution is not exclusively set to be
handled by the running OS of the executing machine. Instead, to mimic the behavior a digital
circuit, the language is to be simulated in a discrete-event based manner. The semantics of
this simulator as deﬁned per the industry standard and language reference manual (LRM) is
the second subject to be presented in the ﬁrst half of the section.
The second half of this section will provide an overview of what parts make up a compiler
and compare it against what has been done in the project.
3.1
SystemVerilog
SystemVerilog is both a hardware description language (HDL) to describe hardware designs
and speciﬁcations as well as a hardware veriﬁcation language (HVL) that enable one to
achieve validation of designs with simulation. Speciﬁcally, a HDL is used to express the
intended structure (registers, wires, etc.) and behaviour of electronic circuits and components.
SystemVerilog has not always been and actually its core hardware description features are
inherited from Verilog, the language that came before. Verilog is a sole HDL and is (mainly)
used to apply a design abstraction called register-transfer level (RTL) to describe and model
digital circuits [13]. Various constructs such as modules, processes and data operations are
used to achieve this. Verilog is now a subset of SV and speciﬁes the majority of the HDL
available in SV.
SV mainly brought new constructs to the veriﬁcation domain and most importantly a new
paradigm, object-oriented programming (OOP). In combination with Verilogs structured
paradigm this advances the users ability to specify and model testbenches (TB) which are
modules that specify the environment, e.g. provided input stimuli, under which the device
under test (DUT) is tested. Among the new features are class, program and interface declara-
tions. These allow the user to focus on modeling and providing a proper testing environment
by using e.g. the factory design pattern[4] rather than specifying concrete hardware details [6].
To distinct the various features in SV from one another ﬁgure 1 is used to categorize them.

3.1.1
Concurrency
SV deﬁnes itself as a parallel programming language since many of the constructs are simu-
lated as concurrent processes [6]. It is not to be compared with parallel programming used
to achieve performance gains on e.g. data processing with SIMD. Rather it is a consequence
of mimicking the behavior of all components contained in the ﬁnal hardware product.
An example of a concurrent construct is the process shown in listing 1. A module can deﬁne
multiple processes and these, alongside processes in every other modules, are concurrent with
one another.
Two types of processes exist: initial and always processes. ”initial” processes are processes
which start their execution without prerequisite when the simulation is started. Contrary to
this, ”always” processes are synchronized on some change to a variable before they start their
execution. In this project two types of synchronization conditions, called event controls,
exist, posedge and negedge. An event control is always associated with a variable and is
evaluated every time that variable is updated. The posedge condition is satisﬁed if the value
of the monitored variable is incremented while negedge tracks a decremental development.
Once the condition is satisﬁed the process can start its execution. Finally, ”always” processes,
unlike ”initial” processes, are executed repeatably every time their synchronization condition
is met.

The body in processes are most often made up by a block statement which is a construct

used to group multiple statements since it deﬁnes the order they are executed.
Just as for processes two types of block statements exist. So far the sequential block
which starts with a ”begin” and ends at ”end” has been seen. Statements put herein are to
be executed sequentially, i.e. in declared order. This deterministic behavior is in the LRM
given as:

Statements within a begin-end block shall be executed in the order in which they
appear in that begin-end block. Execution of statements in a particular begin-end
block can be suspended in favor of other processes in the model; however, in no
case shall the statements in a begin-end block be executed in any order other

A sequential block thus ensures sequential execution of statements as known from the struc-

The other type of block is a fork-join block statement. It follows the traditional fork-join
model[10] in that each nested statement is spawned as their own process. Then, only once all
the new spawned processes have ﬁnished their execution, the parent process can continue on
its own. A similar block to this is the fork-join any block. Each statement is still spawned as
a child process but the diﬀerence is the applied type of join-barrier. join any only suspends
the parent process until any - not all - child process ﬁnishes their execution.
3.1.2
Chosen subset
The main constructs of the SystemVerilog language have been presented and to concretize
further the subset used in this project is presented. The set of constructs included in the
subset have been chosen to enable one to write a simple testbench. This is based on two
main concepts:
• Concept of parallel processes
• Concept of time
Every other construct in the subset are bound together on the terms of these concepts. The
syntax is given as a context-free grammar[5]:
Module :
M ::= module x; D P endmodule
Declaration :
D ::= int I; | D1 D2
Identifiers :
I ::= x | x, I
Process :
P ::= initial S | always EC S | P1 P2
Statement :
S ::= x = a; | while(b) S | if(b) S | # a S | EC | begin BS end
| fork BS join | fork BS join any
Event control :
EC ::= @(posedge x) | @(negedge x)
Block statements :
BS ::= S | S1 S2
Arithmetic expressions :
a ::= n | x | a1 + a2
Boolean expressions :
b ::= a1 == a2 | b1 && b2 | !b1 | a1 < a2

x is a variable reference and n a decimal number to be thought of as a signed integer.
A series of new constructs have yet to be introduced and are all located in the ”Statement”
production rule. Apart from the block statements are four new constructs included:
• Assignment statements (x = a) are used to assign the value at the right hand side
to the variable deﬁned on the left hand side. Diﬀerent types of assignments exist in
SV and diﬀer in how code is allowed to proceed once such a statement is met. The
supported statement is a blocking assignment meaning that it must be executed
before any following statements.
• Delay control statements (# a; S), where a is the time period to delay, are used to
postpone the execution of the statement S - and therefore the process - to a later
simulation time.
• Control ﬂow statements such as while loops and if conditions. Their behaviour is equal
to those found in other programming languages.
• Event control statements so far have been seen used only as synchronization conditions
in ”always” processes. They can also be applied in the body of a process. This causes
the process to suspend its current execution and suspend until the speciﬁed event has
been satisﬁed.
Note, it is allowed for a process to deﬁne a single statement as body instead of using a block.
It will have the same behaviour as a sequential block with a single statement.
The case used for experiments apply constructs as if it could have been a testbench. This
is done with the intention of enabling us to review how the constructs simulate. The scope
of the subset is too narrow for the applied case to showcase a hardware description - both
separately or in attachment with a testbench. Simulation of designs would not have added
more insight to the simulation features of SV as they are subjected to the same simulation
semantics.
3.2
SystemVerilog simulation
So far the language constructs used to express programs in this project have been introduced.
This section will present how simulation of such SystemVerilog programs has been deﬁned
per the LRM. It clariﬁes how processes are simulated to appear concurrent using a speciﬁc
discrete-event simulation model. Snippets from the LRM will be included, examined and put
into context to elaborate the model’s components. Initially a short introduction is given to
what discrete event simulation is.
page 10 of 91
==PAGE:12==
3.2
SystemVerilog simulation
3
BACKGROUND
A system simulated as a discrete-event system evolves with the evaluation of a sequence of
time ordered events. Each event is associated with some time T and has some action A.
When evaluated it may 1) change the state of the system and/or 2) spawn new events. To
maintain the order of events a type of queue that sorts by time is usually applied. When
no more events can be found to some time T the simulation advances to the next time slot
containing events. A simulation terminates when no more events exists in the queue [9]. The
pseudo code below encapsulates the simulation technique:
while events in queue
pop top item from queue
perform action and propagate changes to system
Check if new events are to be spawned (to current or future time)
Compared to this simple introduction SV uses a more advanced model. First of all it utilizes
two diﬀerent types of events. The type will depend on the SV statement being simulated.
Secondly it sorts events not only by time but also by so called regions. Both of these
concepts are detailed in the following sections.
3.2.1
Events
Every process in a simulation is represented by an event. Then, when the event is dequeued
from the event queue, the process can execute its body. The before mentioned action of
an event is the next statement in the represented process to be simulated. The process is
responsible for maintaining the state of its progress while an event is the entity telling it
when to proceed. If the represented process terminates the associated event can not reoccur
in the queue.
As determined, processes can be sensitive to changes in the state of variables. When a process
performs an assignment statement and updates a variable the event is referred to as an
update event. This is so because other processes might be sensitive to the change that took
place. If they happen to be sensitive they are added as new events to the event queue in the
current time slot. In any other case are events called evaluation events. They evaluate
the next statement in the process and determine what should happen to the process and
event. This includes scheduling the event for a later time slot if the statement is a delay
control statement or update the state of the process to follow a speciﬁc branch in case of a
control ﬂow statement. More on this will follow later.
3.2.2
Time
In SystemVerilog the time associated with events refer to the actual time it takes for the
DUT to be simulated:

==PAGE:13==

The term simulation time is used to refer to the time value maintained by the
simulator to model the actual time it would take for the system description being
simulated. [6]
Yet again, SystemVerilog increases the complexity of the given discrete-event simulation
example by using more than one generic time unit. As seen, it is possible to provide a delay
before some statement should occur with e.g. #5ms. A typical use case for this is a clock
generator to simulate the clock cycles of the DUT. In terms of simulation, a delay statement
will push the event to a later time slot in the event queue. The complete set of time units
available in SystemVerilog are:
• Second (s)
• Millisecond (ms)
• Microsecond (us)
• Nanosecond (ns)
• Picosecond (ps)
• Femtosecond (fs)
A simulator should be able to distinguish between these time units such that, if scheduled at
time 0, events delayed with some femtoseconds are dequeued from the queue before those
delayed with e.g. pico- and nanoseconds. The concept of time units is discarded from the
project to simplify requirements. A single, generic time unit is applied.
3.2.3
Regions
For the usual discrete-event simulator events are sorted solely by time. An event is associated
with a single simulation time and it contains a value of the currently simulating or a future
time slot. SystemVerilog further divides time slots into an order of so called regions. Each
event is then in additional to time also associated with a region.
The use of regions is to ensure that the simulated design and testbench code interact in a
certain way with the veriﬁcation constructs, e.g. to prevent race conditions among other
things. This is important because samples collected during simulation must be trustworthy
when veriﬁed against with e.g. assertions. Otherwise the whole process of veriﬁcation is
worthless.
What region an event belongs to depends on the action being simulated and its concurrent
semantics. The total hierarchy of regions consist of 17 regions. Yet, the constructs in the

To a given time slot T events can be ordered by the regions Active and Reactive.
The simulation engine works by emptying one region at a time while respecting the shown
order. Once a region is empty it continues on to the next. The active region is iterative in the
sense that events are moved up to it for processing. The processing of all events in the region
is referred to as a simulation cycle[17] and the processing of a single event an iteration.
For example, when a simulation cycle has been ﬁnished for the Active region it is checked
for if Inactive contains any events. If non-empty all its events are moved to Active where
they are processed in a new simulation cycle. A single time slot can have multiple simulation
cycles if events are moved to the active region more than once. After both regions have
been emptied the simulator continue on to the next time slot containing events. [6] further
provides a reference algorithm that deﬁnes this behavior - this can be seen in appendix A.
3.2.4
Scheduling
Concurrent and parallel processes are always executed according to some scheduling semantics.
A decision how to perform such scheduling is made by the simulator when multiple processes
are in the Active region to the same time slot. The implemented iteration algorithm dequeuing
events from the queue is what must accommodate to the chosen design. Usually two types
of semantics are considered when scheduling concurrency and parallelism: preemptive and
page 13 of 91
==PAGE:15==
3.2
SystemVerilog simulation
3
BACKGROUND
non-preemptive scheduling. Processes scheduled preemptively are not ensured to reach
termination when starting their execution. They may while being executed be suspended in
favor of another process. On what basis a suspension can happen varies but could be based
on some type of priority. Non-preemptive scheduling does it diﬀerently by running a single
process at a time until termination before scheduling the next. When multiple events are in
the active region in the same time slot, i.e. multiple processes are running at the same time,
the LRM speciﬁes:
One source of nondeterminism is the fact that active events can be taken oﬀthe
Active or Reactive event region and processed in any order. Another source of
nondeterminism is that statements without timecontrol constructs in procedural
blocks do not have to be executed as one event. (...) At any time while evaluating
a procedural statement, the simulator may suspend execution and place the
partially completed event as a pending event in the event region. The eﬀect of this
is to allow the interleaving of process execution, although the order of interleaved
execution is nondeterministic and not under control of the user. [6]
The quote suggest that two mechanisms must be implemented in the way processes are
scheduled.
• Events can be taken of the Active (Reactive is one of the omitted regions) in any order.
• Processes may have their execution suspended at any time
These distinct responsibilities can be separated to the iteration algorithm and simulation
engine. The iteration algorithm handles the ﬁrst of the responsibilities and suggest that
it must have a non-deterministic dequeuing behaviour. Processes are therefore not ranked
according to some priority hierarchy. The second responsibility states that executing processes
may be suspended at any time and be returned to the Active region in the event queue. This
suggest a preemptive approach though it is not speciﬁed on what basis an executing process
can be suspended. The responsibility of the iteration algorithm ends after having picked
an event to simulate and instead it is the responsibility of the simulation engine when to
suspend an executing process. If a process applies a delay statement greater than zero during
simulation, the simulator engine must abort it as it moves to a future time slot. If any events
remain a new event is picked from the queue.

A consequence of the parallel nature in SV and preemptive scheduling is that race conditions
are a possibility. This should not be put in relation to atomicity issues as the blocking
statement by itself is atomic. Rather it poses a challenge when e.g. a module’s output
signal rely on its input signals. If these signals are in a race condition the output from the
module might seem arbitrary. Consider if the processes contained in E1, E2 and E3 all change
the value of a variable x. Since all events run in the same time slot it causes x to change
value multiple times during the time slot. This will happen in a non-deterministic order for
each simulation. Further, if several ”always” processes - representing our input signals - are
sensitive to x it cannot be ensured what order they are awakened in.

approaches can be used to implement the simulation framework.

Back end
Typically the back end of a compiler is responsible for traversing the ﬁnal IR with target
for generating some machine code that continues to maintain the semantics of the source
program. This is not necessarily the only approach for SV because of the required interaction
with a simulator. The alternative is that the simulator interprets the design.

is executed. Compiled code uses the instruction set of the machine code to be executed
directly by the machine. The whole IR is traversed up-front and the source code is compiled
in its entirety before it can be executed. Interpreters instead execute the IR while traversing
it. While traversing the tree the interpreter translates the source code into the high-level
language of the simulator. This provides interpreters with a performance overhead when
compared to compiled code because a miniature compilation takes place for each interpreted
construct.
The challenge for SystemVerilog lies in how the compiled and interpreted code hereafter
is simulated correctly. In case of compilation a precompiled library that implements the
simulator can be incorporated. Some commercial simulators like Synopsys VCS chooses this
approach. When compiling, a precompiled library of some type, is needed to implement the
simulator. As the IR only contain information about the processes it says nothing about
how to simulate them. They therefore need to interface with the library which then become
responsible for invoking them correctly. If the simulator is an interpreter the semantics can
e.g. be incorporated in the algorithm used to traverse the IR.

5.1.1
Critical section
A critical section is a point in the program where a process updates e.g. a variable that
is shared among multiple processes. Timing is what needs to be controlled as multiple
concurrent changes to the same variable can lead to unanticipated behaviour. A critical
section for the given problem is represented by a philosopher wanting to pick up a fork. It
must be ensured that when one philosopher has made an intention to pick up a fork that
another philosopher is neither stealing or bestolen of a fork. If a fork is already picked up the
reaching philosopher must be made waiting until the fork is available again.
One algorithm to solve this has already been introduced in Peterson’s mutex algorithm. The
algorithm is designed to work as a mutex between two processes. It resolves around the
use of two variables, flag and turn. These are used in combination to indicate interest in
and allowing entrance to the critical section. The flag variable shows a process’ interest
in a critical section. On the other hand, turn is an indication of which of the processes
has priority to enter the critical section. Access is granted to a process P if P itself wants
access while the other process P ′ does not or if P has priority over P ′. The application of
this algorithm can be seen in listing 4 and will too be seen in the SV implementation of the
Dining Philosopher Problem.
5.1.2
Deadlock
A deadlocked system is one where every process is waiting on some other process to perform
an action such as releasing a shared variable. Since every process is in a waiting state only
capable of proceeding after the action of another process the system will halt indeﬁnitely.
Consider an algorithm that has every philosopher pick up their right-hand fork ﬁrst and the
left-hand second. If every philosopher wants to eat at the same time and all acquire their
right-hand fork the system deadlocks. At this point every philosopher holds a fork in their
right hand and now look to pick up their left hand fork. It is at this point the deadlock
occurs because their left neighbour used their right hand to pick up that exact fork. This
will be the case the whole table around leading to every philosopher eternally waiting for
their left neighbour to ﬁnish eating.
A solution to this does not require any specialized algorithm but rather a change to the order
of resource acquisition. In the given example all philosophers are right handed resulting in
no competition on picking up their initial fork. If just two philosophers compete on picking
up the same initial fork the chain will be broken. Consider the case the all right handed
philosophers pick up their ﬁrst fork. This leaves the left handed philosopher waiting for the
availability of his left fork and thus leaving his right fork on the table. This allow his right
neighbour to pick up what is their left side fork and eat. Once ﬁnished he then again enables
his right neighbour. Figure 12 illustrates this:

number of iterations to go ”wasted” as philosopher 4 (Ep4) initially need an iteration to
pick up the fork plus any subsequent iterations before it puts down the forks again. All of
these iterations compete with the four other events in being picked from the event queue.
When Ep4 ﬁnally gets to leave the event queue the issue reappears with Ep3. This is mostly a
consequence of the busy-wait mechanism but illustrates that the non-deterministic scheduling
enables the situation.
5.1.3
Starvation
Starvation is an issue of processes never being assigned any processing time, i.e. they never
proceed their execution and ultimately never terminate. This can be caused by some priority
ranking system where higher ranked processes are executed ﬁrst. If high priority processes

up a fork a philosopher can starve if either of its neighbours always pick up the fork before him.
Algorithms such as those by Lehmann and Rabin[8] solves this issue by introducing a fairness

and that can cause starvation. Lehmann and Rabin introduce fairness between philosophers
by making them aware of their neighbours desire to eat and who most recently used the fork.
So, between two philosophers, if only one is hungry he can take the fork freely. If both are
hungry the one that used the fork last to eat must show courtesy and leave the fork for the
neighbour.
Peterson’s algorithm is arguably achieving the same fairness with the turn variable. When
a process wants to enter a critical section it uses turn to show courtesy to the competing
process by letting them go ﬁrst if they too wishes to enter the section. Yet this courtesy is
only shown to the neighbour of the fork currently being reached for. Take that the algorithm
used to solve the deadlock situation applies Peterson’s to manage the picking up of forks.
In this case philosopher 1 and 5 are sharing a turn variable to show courtesy for the grey
fork. Yet, for a 5-man table, while philosopher 1 and 5 are being gracious to each other
about the grey fork philosopher 2, 3 and 4 can starve them by repeatedly picking up the
remaining forks. This lasts until someone picks up they grey fork and now seeks their 2nd
fork. The fairness with Peterson’s is therefore not complete but partial to the degree that
once a philosopher shows intention to pick up a fork they always eventually get to.

Section 4 clariﬁes how the semantics deﬁnes the behaviour of SV constructs and how they
relate with the simulator’s components. This section will describe how a proof-of-concept
simulator can be implemented to satisfy those semantics. The approach applied is inspired
by [12] and is implemented as a translator that constructs an internal data structure that
navigate the statements of the given processes. The overall design of the tool is shown in
ﬁgure 20:
Figure 20: Design of the simulator tool decomposed into a front and back end
The front end of the simulator is implemented according to the applied language subset using
Haskell and relevant scanner, parser libraries. The libraries use the formal grammar in 3.1.2
to generate the two components scanner and parser.
The back end is made up by 4 components:
• The simulator manages the control ﬂow of the simulation engine. It is responsible
for invoking the iteration algorithm on the event queue and schedule the simulation of
dequeued processes.

6.1
Front end
The front end is implemented with Haskell and uses the libraries Alex[1] and Happy[2] to
generate respectively scanner and parser. Alex is a Lexer generator and uses a domain
speciﬁc language (DSL) to specify the terminal symbols in the subset. The module deﬁning
the scanner is then referenced by the parser module that uses Happy’s DSL to specify the
context free grammar, i.e. Happy is a parser generator. When a production rule is found to
be satisﬁed by the source text the parser ﬁnally saves it in the appropriate AST construct.
The scanner, parser and abstract syntax tree constructs can be found in appendix D, E and
F. The AST parsed from the applied Dining Philosophers implementation is seen in appendix
G. Recall, this would not be the ﬁnal input to the back end as it needs to be transformed by
an elaboration phase.
6.2
Back end
[12] originally targets the domain of program analysis but its inspiration comes to realization
in the back end in terms of how it is designed. The back end consists of four components:
simulator, process representation, actions and an environment. The process representation is
a data structure that supposedly is generated from the elaborated AST and is responsible
for representing the ﬂow of a process. The simulator is responsible for interfacing with this
data structure when it wants to simulate the processes. When a process is simulated it
executes the next action in the process’ ﬂow. This action may change the system’s state, i.e.
environment, according to the semantics of the interpreted statement.
Petri nets and program graphs were considered as process data structures but only program
graphs ended up being used for the implementation. How these correlate with the simulation
semantics and why program graphs were chosen over Petri nets will be the focus of the ﬁrst,
next sections. Afterwards possible semantics of the simulator with focus on the iteration
algorithm is discussed.

Such a name space is represented by an environment Env that maps variables to their current

expressions read current values from Env to be able to evaluate. Only the blocking assignment
may update the values in Env. Each action thus have a corresponding semantic function that
takes the current environment and return a possibly altered one. Such a function is invoked
when the simulated statement is an action and they have the same semantics regardless of
process representation. Delay and event control statements do not need such a function as
they indicate a change to the state of the simulator’s event queue rather than that of the
process. The semantic functions can be declared separately from the chosen representation

be notiﬁed as suspended processes might be sensitive to the eﬀect of the change. This has
been implemented with the observer pattern where the simulator component has the role
of observer and the environment as observable[3]. The environment will, before updating
a given value, check if either the posedge or negedge condition is met and if so notify the
simulator accordingly.

6.2.2
Petri net
Petri nets is a modelling technique used for many years among various domains to model - in
particular parallel - systems. The modelled system is in our case the ”initial” and ”always”
processes. A petri net consists of 4 main components: places, transitions, arcs and
tokens. A place p represents a state the system can be in. Tokens are contained in those
places and indicate the state that is currently active. Transitions t evolve the state of the
system by moving one token from each place p in its set of source places to each place p’
in its set of target places. A transition t can have multiple source and target places and
move tokens only, said to be fireable, when all source places contain at least one token.
The actions of the process are attached to these transitions and their respective semantic
function is invoked when it is ﬁred. In Petri nets this is referred to as a labeling. The
relation between transitions and their set of source/target places is given by the set of arcs.
They deﬁne the connectivity between places and transitions and are given as pairs of the
form (P, T) or (T, P). The entire net structure is given as N = (P, T, F) where P is the set
of places, T the set of transitions and F ⊆(P × T) ∪(T × P)[16]. The full set of processes
to be simulated is then given as a set of nets {N}.
The initial state of a petri net depends on the type of process it models. As ”initial” processes
are executable without prerequisite their initiative place automatically contain a token while
”always” processes start with none. If a process contains a ﬁreable transition is an indication
to the simulator if the process is active or suspended/terminated. This gives in case of an
event control statement that the moved token instead is temporarily removed from the net
and put back when the condition has been satisﬁed.

(source), pt (target) are two unique places and S the statement to be modelled. Each function
returns a set of pairs according to the elements in F. How places and transitions should be

6.2.3
Program graph
A program graph is a directed graph which consist of a set of nodes Q and a set of edges E.
Every node q is distinct and is used in directed edges e (qs, a, qt) to capture what action a
takes place between source node qs and target node qt. The full set of edges then constitutes
the entire control ﬂow of a program (or process). Formally the complete program graph
is deﬁned as (Q, E) where Q is ﬁnite and E ⊆Q × Actions × Q. The entirety of program
graphs is given by {(Q, E)}. A process monitors its state by keeping a pointer to which
current node the program is at in the graph. Future paths are then found by looking at
which edges sources from the current node.
The construction rules for program graphs resemble those of petri net except transitions
no longer connects the nodes (places). A new construction function edges(qs, S, qt) can be
deﬁned for each SV construct. It returns a set of edges e. Consider the graphic in ﬁgure 23
of how processes are turned into program graphs:

6.2.4
Simulator
With the distinction between process representation and simulator it remained the responsi-
bility of the simulator to dequeue events to simulate, move events to new regions and time
slots and manage processes as consequence of event control statements or fork-blocks. The
order of simulated events is determined by the iteration algorithm, movement of events by
which statement is executed and the state of processes by the above described approach.
The movement of events has been strictly deﬁned by the LRM. Therefore the core simulator
implementation is based on the reference algorithm (appendix A) provided by the LRM. It
is only slightly altered as a consequence of the reduced set of regions. However, a variety
can be found in how events are handled after being picked through the iteration algorithm.

The number of reachable simulation sequences increase with the number of elements recorded

state space that becomes unsuited for application. The other approach, particular to this
case, is to store the sequence philosophers get to eat. This approach shadows all statements
simulated and is the approach used in the experiments.

7.3
Discussion
7
EXPERIMENTS & DISCUSSIONS
So, why is the LRM deﬁned to target a superset of behaviours of what is actually needed?
Interleaving of processes (should) have no inﬂuence on the outcome of a module and could
be skipped as requirement to the simulator. The simulator could in every simulation cycle
instead choose to simulate all processes by a certain order until they terminate or delay.
It would also make working with waveforms more consistent if the engineer could expect
statements to happen according to a speciﬁc order.
Yet, the concept of time seems to be why SV needs the concept of parallelism. The work with
writing testbenches includes specifying how and when in time stimuli is provided. Especially
the sequential and fork block constructs are used in this speciﬁcation. The fork-block’s
interaction with delay statements is particular useful if the forked processes have varied
delays. Without it separate ”initial” processes with diﬀerent delays would need to be written
for each forked process.
The subset of SV that is not expected to be synthesized, e.g. fork-join statements, control-ﬂow
statements and ”initial” processes, but to a large degree applied by veriﬁcation engineers,
pursue semantics that is akin to the semantics applied to similar constructs in the software
world. You want to be able to reason about the code as if it was executed as parallel software
code. This is achieved by applying well known parallel principles and constructs. On the
other hand, the actual parallel behaviour is essentially unwanted for because it worsen the
working conditions of SV. This make it seem like the SV standard deﬁnes two subcategories of
requirements, that of hardware veriﬁcation and design engineers, that fundamentally conﬂict
with one another.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
The objective of the thesis was to review the simulation semantics of SystemVerilog with
intention of implementing a complementary simulator. Due to the vague and uncertain
descriptions provided by the IEEE standard the work on the simulator got neglected in favour
of extended analysis and modelling of the semantics.
The thesis performs an extensive analysis of the Dining Philosophers problem and is imple-
mented with the selected SystemVerilog subset. Before this could take place, the parallel
simulation semantics used by SystemVerilog to mimic a behaviour as if the code ran on a
digital chip was unfolded. This outlined the extended discrete-time event simulator used to
make design and veriﬁcation code collaborate intentionally. It also introduced the concept
of concurrent processes that compete with the non-deterministic, non-preemptive simulator
engine to get simulated. Next, the expected behaviour of the statements placed in the body
of processes were speciﬁed. It was especially the interaction between discrete-event model,
iteration algorithm and statement constructs that occupied the extended work done with
understanding the semantics.
With the subset in place various implementations of the Dining Philosophers problem was
examined. The traditional synchronization issues of race conditions, deadlocks and starvation
was solved by applying relevant algorithms. Algorithms applying busy waits were shown to
be potentially marauding for the performance of the simulator, because it has no knowledge
of if it is simulating a busy waiting process. On the other hand, insertion of event controls,
meant to optimize the busy waits, reintroduced a starvation issue as suspended processes
risked never being awakened again.
The uncertainties of the scheduling semantics lead to the deﬁnition of three scheduling
conﬁgurations. Two of these were applied to the implemented case with intent of testing their
capability in reaching as many unique simulation sequences as possible. The interpretation
less strict in its interruption of simulating processes showed the best results. It simulated few
invalid simulations with starving philosophers while still managing to ﬁnd a high number of
unique sequences in the remaining valid simulations.
Conclusively, future work could be put in constraining the simulation semantics into a more
concrete form before continuing any work on the simulator. In general, SystemVerilog wants
to apply the concept of parallelism to its users. This is particularly useful in veriﬁcation where
concurrent constructs, such as fork-join, have been added to SystemVerilog to accommodate
for the increased need of veriﬁcation functionality. Yet, hardware modules are expected to
be designed to behave deterministic in any parallel scenario. Any interleaving of processes
should thus make no diﬀerence in behaviour. This suggest that the parallel simulation model
