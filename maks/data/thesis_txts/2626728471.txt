Summary
In recent years there has been an increased focus on the lowering of the CO2 emissions from concrete
production by replacing part of the cement with a supplementary cementitious material (SCM). This
thesis addresses that topic and aims to investigate mineralogical composition of three selected mine
tailings. It is investigated in relation to the leaching process of both the mine tailings and the cement
paste samples containing the same mine tailings. Three different solutions of NaOH are used for the
leaching test of the mine tailings in order to create conditions similar to those in the pore solution inside
concrete. The leaching solution for the cement paste samples is deionised water.
The mineralogical composition of the raw mine tailings is investigated microscopically and by use
of X-ray fluorescence (XRF). The mineralogical compositions are then linked with digestions from
previous work. Furthermore, both the mine tailings and the cement paste samples are analysed using
thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and X-ray diffraction (XRD).
The tests are performed by placing 5 g of solid sample in an airtight plastic container with 25 mL of
leaching solution. The solutions used to leach the mine tailings are 1.0 M, 0.1 M and 0.01 M NaOH
solutions with pH value 13.0, 12.9, and 12.1, respectively, and the cement paste samples are leached
in deionised water. As reference material, Normsand is used to compare with the mine tailings and
a cement paste containing Normsand, was produced as a reference to compare with the cement paste
samples containing mine tailings.
After leaching, the samples were filtered and the content of the leaching solutions was measured with
an inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OES).
Based on the results, it can be concluded that all of the three raw mine tailings contain high enough
amounts of heavy metals to be categorised as polluted to a certain extent.
Overall, the mine tailings seem unaffected by the leaching process and the leaching of the mine tailings
show that the largest amount of heavy metals tend to leach in the 1.0 M NaOH solution. Furthermore,
the results indicate that most of the expected content will have leached on the final day of leaching.
The leaching amounts of heavy metals from the cement paste samples containing mine tailings are found
to be approximately the same as the leached amount from the reference cement paste. Based on this
the cement paste is presumed to be able to retain the heavy metals to some extent.
The heavy metals are presumed to be retained in the C-S-H phase, but a final conclusion on this theory
would require further research. If the heavy metals are able to be retained in the cement paste it will
relevant to further investigate any possible issues related to the high calcite and sulphate content of the
mine tailings in relation to durability of the concrete.

CHAPTER 2
Introduction
There are several advantages to using supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs). First of all, the
production process of cement emits significant amounts of carbon dioxide (CO2) and on a yearly basis,
cement production accounted globally for about 6% in 2006 [1] and about 7% in 2018 [2]. For compari-
son the global emission from aviation account for about 2% [3].
By replacing an amount of the cement with an alternative material, the total CO2 emission from the
concrete will be lowered, making the concrete more sustainable. The most common SCMs are silica
fume, fly ash and blast-furnace slag from coal combustion. The mixing of SCMs and cement can result
in changes in the properties of the concrete, which may have a positive effect on the final product. Some
of the affected properties could be a lower permeability of the concrete when adding silica fume or an
increase in compressive strength when adding fly ash [4][5]. Besides the environmental benefits and the
effects on the concrete properties by using SCMs, the concrete can also be a way to store or deposit
toxic waste like e.g. waste from mine operations. This thesis focuses on a certain part of mine waste
called mine tailings.
In mine production, in order to extract the desired minerals or metals, both mechanical and chemical
processes are used. The non-valuable materials from the ore are called mine tailings. During the ex-
traction of valuable minerals a time will come where further extraction is no longer profitable due to
the decreasing concentration of valuable minerals in the material. This means that the mine tailings
are a combination of metals, minerals, chemicals, organics and process water [6].
The mineralogical composition of the chemical and physical properties of the mine tailings vary depend-
ing on the origin of the ore and the mining process. Some mine tailings may contain heavy metals or
other hazardous materials that should be contained when used in vulnerable environments.
This thesis is meant to investigate whether it is possible to neutralise the heavy metals from the mine
tailings by mixing them into cement paste. The presumption is that the mine tailings may react chemi-
cally with the cement or be encapsulated, rendering the heavy metals inactive. By performing leaching
experiments on both mine tailings and cement pastes containing mine tailings, it might be possible to
examine what leaches and what is assumed to be contained.
Given that the mine tailings will react with the cement, mine tailings could possibly be a future replace-
ment of fly ash for example. Other than that, concrete could be a possible way of depositing the mine
tailings. If so, further examination of the concrete properties will have to be investigated.
There are two main reasons why mine tailings could be profitable to use in concrete productions as
SCMs. First of all the amount of fly ash available is getting smaller, generating a need for new SCMs.
Across the United States alone, approximately 40% of the coal-fired power stations have been taken out
of service within the last 10 years. The same tendency is seen in the United Kingdom. This has been
leading to a reduction of the local availability of standard-compliant fly ash [7]. In 2017, the Danish
government joined the Global Alliance to Power Past Coal. The goal is that there will be no more
coal-fired power stations after 2030 [8]. This means, that Denmark must either import fly ash from
other European countries or find another SCM to replace it [9].
Secondly, depositing mine tailings is a major environmental issue. The environmental concern is associ-
ated with the oxidation of both sulphide minerals and other minerals which are susceptible to oxidation.
The oxidation processes release elements such as heavy metals, which then leach to the water, flowing
through the mine waste and polluting the groundwater. Due to the relatively low groundwater veloci-
ties and the large area covered by the mine tailings, a delay of measurable environmental degradation

closed [10]. Therefore, a lasting solution to isolate the mine tailings from the environment is desirable

2.1
Project statement
If it is possible to replace part of the cement with a SCM, the CO2 emitted from concrete production
will be reduced. If the heavy metals and other impurities in the mine tailings can be contained in the
concrete, the mine tailings could be used as a SCM.
This thesis will investigate the mineralogical composition of selected mine tailings and their leaching
process in sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solutions to imitate concrete pore solutions. The leaching process
of the selected mine tailings will be investigated in order to determine any possible environmental risks
due to the leaching of heavy metals and other impurities.
In addition, the aim is to determine whether cement paste is able to retain hazardous elements from
the mine tailings by reacting chemically with the cement or by being physically bound. This will be

3.1
Supplementary cementitious materials
The most common types of SCMs are silica fume, fly ash and granulate blast-furnace slag (GBFS) which
all have different properties. Silica fume and fly ash react only when added to a cementitious binder
and the cement hydration product portlandite has formed. GBFS on the other hand reacts slowly with
water. There are several advantages to using SCMs. When replacing part of the cement with SCMs,
which requires no additional clinker process, there is a significant reduction in CO2 emission [11]. The
specific advantage of the SCM silica fume is a high early strength and a lower permeability [4] and the
use of fly ash leads to an increase in strength and density [5]. When using GBFS as a SCM, the setting
time of the concrete is prolonged, the density of the concrete is increased, and the resistance against
sulphide attack and chloride ingress is improved [12].
The addition of SCMs can influence the hydration process of the concrete. For example, if the SCM is
rich on silica it will affect the kind and amount of hydrates formed. As a result the volume, the porosity,
and the durability of the binder are also influenced. The major changes, when adding silica rich SCMs
are the consumption of portlandite and the decrease in Ca/Si ratio in the calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-
H) phases [13]. When the SCM is rich on alumina, the uptake of alumina in C-S-H increases resulting
in more hydrates containing alumina [13].
Other articles have investigated the effect of calcined clay used to as a cement replacement. One of the
calcined types of clay used, is metakaolin, which is the produced by heating the kaolin clay to about
600 - 800 ◦C [14]. The metakaolin consists primarily of silica and alumina and it will react with the
Ca(OH)2 and produce C-S-H gel [15]. The metakaolin is found to contribute to the early age strength
of the concrete and contribute less to the strength after 14 days of curing. Given that the mine tailings
contain clay minerals, it could be relevant in future work to investigate the possibility of calcination.
The effect the metakaolin has on the strength of the concrete is assumed to be a result of the filler
effect.
The filler effect occurs when inert materials are blended with the cement. It has a significant effect on
the hydration of the cement which should not be confused with the reactivity of the SCMs themselves.
When mixed, the water-to-solid ratio is not meant to change, even when adding the SCM. Since the
SCM does not produce hydrates, the water to clinker ratio increases. The filler effect increases the
reaction of the clinker phases since there is more available space for it to happen [13].

area located somewhere below the surface, which has a higher concentration of valuables in the form

surface.

3.3
Portland cement
Portland cement mainly consists of clinker minerals and it often contains gypsum. Sometimes lime
stone filler, fly ash, silica fume, puzzolans, or blast furnace slag is added as SCMs. The clinker minerals
in Portland cement consists of alite (C3S), belite (C2S), ferrite (C4AF), and aluminate (C3A).
Alite constitute about 50 - 70% of the clinker minerals while the content of belite is about 15 - 30%.
Alite reacts relatively quick with water compared to belite which reacts more slowly with water. When
looking at the strength development, belite contributes little to the increasing of strength within the
first 28 days, whereas alite and belite, up till the first year of age, have almost equal contributions for
the further increase in strength.
The remaining clinker minerals in Portland cement is aluminate and ferrite, which constitutes about 5
- 10% and 5 - 15%, respectively. Aluminate reacts very rapidly with water which can cause unwanted
early settings whereas it varies a lot how fast ferrite reacts with water.
Gypsum can act as a set-controlling agent, where it reacts with aluminate and forms ettringite. Gypsum
makes sure that the hydration of aluminate happens after alite, which is why it is sometimes added.
The cement used for the experiments for this thesis is RAPID CEMENT, produced by Aalborg Portland,

3.3.1
Hydration of Portland cement
The hydration of the cement happens when the cement comes into contact with water and several
chemical processes begin to take place.
When looking at the solid volume of completely hydrated cement, the main constituents are C-S-H,
portlandite (CH) and calcium sulfoaluminate hydrates (ettringite). C-S-H constitutes about 50 - 60%
of the solid in completely hydrated cement and provides the majority of the long term strength and
durability. 20 - 25% of the solid hydrated cement consists of portlandite and 15 - 20% consists of
ettringite, which both, compared to C-S-H, contributes relatively little to the long term strength [26].
Within minutes of mixing cement and water, ettringite will begin to form and within the first few hours,
it will be possible to see ettringite formations in the X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis. Ettringite is
formed by calcium, sulphate, aluminate and hydroxyl ions and appear as needle shaped crystals. The
ettringite formation will peak within the first 24 hours and contributes to the setting and stiffening as
well as early stage strength development [26].
The hydration of alite and belite primarily produces portlandite and C-S-H. Considering the stoichio-
metric calculations for a complete hydration of alite and belite, alite is found to generate 61% C-S-H
and 39% portlandite, and belite is found to generate 82% C-S-H and 18% portlandite. A cement with
a high content of belite, compared to a cement with a high content of alite, will have a higher total
strength based on the final C-S-H content. Furthermore, it is expected to have a higher durability in
relation to acidic and sulphate environments due to the final portlandite content [26].
After 24 hours, gypsum and other calcium sulphate phases are usually no longer detectable. Since
alite and aluminate phases react quicker than belite and ferrite, the ratio between belite and alite will
increase steadily. Furthermore, after 90 days alite and aluminate phases are almost not detectable [27].
3.4
The leaching process
In general, the leaching process dissolves any soluble components causing the ions of the components to
be completely or partially released to the liquid surrounding or passing through the material in question
[28]. The quantity of dissolved solubles and the rate of dissolution, dependent, among other things, on
the amount of water available and the rate at which the water passes through the material [29].
In relation to mine tailings deposits, the process of leaching is relevant when water passes through
the mine tailings and dissolves any solubles the mine tailings contain, releasing potentially hazardous
chemicals to the surrounding environment [6]. The potential environmental risk from the mine tailings
is one of the reasons why it is relevant to investigate if the cement paste is able to retain the heavy
metals.
For the leaching process, this thesis uses a liquid-to-solid ratio of 10. The samples are stirred regularly
and kept sealed in order to prevent any water from evaporating, altering the amount of liquid in the
sample.

3.5.2
X-ray diffraction
The XRD analysis is used to characterise materials as well as their mineralogical composition. It is a
non-destructive test method and is used to examine all solid samples in this thesis. The purpose of the
XRD method is to determine what the mine tailings consists of, examine what happens when the mine
tailings are added to the cement paste, and how the mine tailings react when exposed to leaching.
The X-ray source emits radiation which is reflected by the internal planes of the material. If the planes
in the material are parallel with the same internal distance, the radiation will be reflected similarly.
This is called constructive interference. The equipment will detect the similar reflections and display
a peak with a specific intensity and the angle at which it is detected. The intensity of the peak is
influenced by the amount of material and the crystal structure as well as the orientation of the grains
in the sample. The opposite of positive interference is called destructive interference. This occurs when
the distance between the internal planes of a material varies and the reflected X-rays cancel each other
out.
The peak patterns, for both the mine tailings and the cement pastes, can then be compared. Based on
this, it will be possible to see if any components has disappeared or changed when exposed to leaching.
Preferred orientation may occur depending on the way the sample is prepared. Preferred orientation is
when the grains obtain the same orientation and, as a result, the intensity of the peak may be amplified
and misleading.
It has previously been investigated, that to obtain repeatable intensities in the XRD analysis the grains
must be ground to a maximum particle size of 15 µm. Therefore an insufficient grinding can become
a source of error.
In A Practical Guide to Microstructural Analysis of Cementitious Materials, an
experiment has been made where the same material has been ground in two size intervals. The sample
ground for the longest time has a more representative and regular peak profile which matches the
analytical peak formation [31].
The materials used for this thesis have been ground by hand until the sample could be pressed into the
sample holder and remaining intact when turned around. The material has not been sieved in order to
control an adequate grain size for the sample and the Coarse fractions may not have been ground as
fine as the Fine fractions.

When mixing the cement paste, the cement and mine tailings are dry-mixed for 30 seconds at speed
1 using a Bear Varimixer Teddy with a capacity of 5 L. Afterwards the water is added (over a time
period of 30 seconds), followed by 1 minute and 30 seconds of mixing at speed 1, then 3 minutes of
rest and an additional minute of mixing at speed 2. The grains of the cement and mine tailings have
a tendency to agglomerate. During the 3 minutes of rest, the mixture can be stirred by hand to even
out the agglomerates in the mixture, making it more homogeneous. After mixing, the cement paste
is stored in an air tight container, and rotated during the first 24 hours of hydration. This is done to
avoid bleeding and segregation of the mixture. The paste is then placed at room temperature to harden
until 28 days of age.

Leaching tests

paste containing mine tailings. By testing the leaching process of the mine tailings it is possible to
get an idea of what to expect when leaching the cement paste containing the mine tailings, as well as
assess if the cement paste is able to contain the heavy metals or change the chemical composition of
the elements leached from the mine tailings. Furthermore, it is possible to investigate whether there is
a change in composition during leaching when the mine tailings are used as a SCM. When the leaching
experiments are stopped, the dry samples are stored in zip-lock bags in a desiccator with silica gel

4.8
Stoppage of cement hydration by solvent exchange
After leaching, the hydration of the cement paste must be stopped. This is done by solvent exchange
using isopropanol and diethyl ether according to the method for double solvent exchange in A Practical
Guide to Microstructural Analysis of Cementitious Materials [31]. The reason for using double solvent
exchange is that it is the most gentle way of drying. Drying at high temperatures may cause a change
in the compositions of the mine tailings and cement paste which is unwanted in relation to the TGA.
The advantages of solvent exchange is that it is easy to perform, it preserves the chemically bound
water in the hydrate phases; it preserves the microstructure; and it removes the soluble ions from the
pore solution [31].
The leached cement paste sample is filtered the same way as the mine tailings as mentioned earlier
in Section 4.7.1. The liquid sample is saved for further analysis as mentioned in Section 4.7.2 and
the solid sample is placed in a glass flask. An amount of isopropanol, corresponding to 16.667 mL
times the weight in g of the solid sample, is added to the flask. In this case, for cement paste samples,
approximately 85 mL isopropanol is used to replace the free water in the sample. The flask is stirred
for 30 seconds and set to rest for 5 minutes. After the 5 minutes, the isopropanol is carefully decanted
into a waste container for isopropanol, and a new 85 mL of isopropanol is poured into the flask. Again,
the flask is stirred for 30 seconds followed by 5 minutes of rest. The sample is then filtered using the
==PAGE:31==
4.8 Stoppage of cement hydration by solvent exchange

experimental set up shown in Figure 4.8, using the vacuum filter, and the filtered sample is put into
another flask. The excess isopropanol is poured into the corresponding waste container.
Diethyl ether, corresponding to 3.333 mL times the weight in g of the solid sample, is added to the
flask. Approximately 20 mL is used in this case. Diethyl ether is used since it is more volatile than
isopropanol and therefore evaporates faster. The flask is then stirred for 30 seconds and set to rest for
an additional 5 minutes. The sample is vacuum filtered the same way as before and afterwards placed
in an oven at 40◦C for 8 minutes. The excess diethyl ether is poured into the corresponding waste
container, and the dry sample is placed in a zip-lock bag in a desiccator for further examination using
XRD and TGA. In between each sample being filtered, the equipment is thoroughly cleaned to avoid
contamination of the samples.

In general, the DTG graphs in Figure 5.13 show a larger content of CaCO3 in the Fine samples compared
to the Coarse samples. It can be due to the fact that the surface-to-size ratio is larger for the Fine grains
than for the Coarse grains. This makes it easier for the Fine grains to become carbonated, resulting in
a smaller content of portlandite and a larger content of CaCO3.
Since the amount of portlandite is approximately the same in both fractions, and not decreasing, the
probability of the Fine fractions being carbonated is not as likely.
Another explanation is that the difference could be a result of the grinding, when the cement paste
is broken down into the Fine and Coarse fractions. When adding CaCO3 to the cement paste, the
compressive strength of the concrete is lowered [39]. The places in the binder where CaCO3 is present
will therefore be weaker than the remaining part of the binder. When breaking down the cement paste
samples the fractures will occur where CaCO3 is located in the binder resulting in CaCO3 being present
on the surface of the grains that are broken off. This means that the proportion of CaCO3 percent in
each grain, is most likely larger for the Fine fraction compared to the Coarse fraction.
When grinding the material, mechanical activation of the material will take place, causing the surface of
the grains to fracture and small fractions will break off. The fracture will take place where the material
is weakest and because of that, the Fine grains that break off, are expected to have more surface damage
than the Coarse grains. Since the fractures are most likely located on the surface of the Fine grains, the
Coarse fraction is more difficult to grind, when preparing the samples for the XRD. The Coarse samples,

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This section covers the further discussion of the results presented previously in the thesis. The discussion
will among be a follow-up on the leaching tendencies seen for the mine tailings and the cement pastes
as well as the possibility of the heavy metals being retained in the cement paste. The leached amounts
of cement related chemical will be discussed as well, in relation to other studies.
6.1
Leaching of minerals from the mine tailings and cement paste
samples containing mine tailings
6.1.1
Leaching of microcline
In relation to the leaching of the cement paste containing Zinkgruvan mine tailings in Figure 5.23, the
peak of the carbonate mineral microcline seems to decrease approaching 28 days, whereas it seems
to increase in intensity approaching 32 days of leaching for the mine tailings as seen in Figure 5.19.
Assuming that the microcline leaches, it is not expected to create hydrate phases since the water
content measured from the hydrate phases seems stable in the different samples, as seen in Table 5.3.
After 28 days of leaching of the cement paste sample, the pH of the liquid was measured to be 12.94
and the pH of the liquid from the leaching of mine tailings after 32 days was measured to be 13.11.
The measured pH values for the leaching solutions can be found in Appendix B. Studies show that the
reaction rate of microcline increase as the pH increases [42]. Since the pH of the two leaching solutions
are approximately the same, it can be assumed that other factors than the pH must be considered
when investigating the leaching of microcline; and it could seem that the surrounding conditions in the
cement paste enable the microcline to leach.
6.1.2
Leaching of hornblende and plagioclase
The XRD results for the leached samples of Nalunaq mine tailings show variations in the intensity of
the hornblende and plagioclase peaks in Figure 5.22. The variations show no specific pattern for the
variation, but may to some extent be caused by leaching.
Previous work shows that plagioclase dissolves preferentially in solutions with a pH lower than 7 [43].
The liquid used to leach the Nalunaq mine tailings is 1.0 M NaOH solution and the pH of the solution
was to be approximately 13. Since, the plagioclase in the Nalunaq mine tailings is not expected to be
affected by leaching under high pH conditions it has not been possible to determine the specific reason
for the variations, but they might be a result of the heterogeneous composition of the mine tailings.
The dissolution of amphibole is highly pH dependent in alkaline solutions and the dissolution rate
varies for different types of amphibole [44]. Literature on the subject of dissolution of the amphibole,

hornblende in alkaline solutions have not been found. Based on this it is not possible to determine
whether or not the hornblende in the Nalunaq mine tailings are affected by the leaching.
6.1.3
Leaching of CaCO3
Due to the 30% cement replacement, the cement paste samples containing mine tailings from Tian-
baoshan can contain up to 19% CaCO3. 14% from the mine tailings and 5% from the limestone filler
from the RAPID cement.
This amount of CaCO3 can effect the final compressive strength of the
concrete. Looking at the European Standards DS 197 for the composition of common cement types,
a Portland-limestone cement is permitted to contain up to 35% limestone [45]. For comparison, this
amount of CaCO3 added via the 30% cement replacement, is within the permitted amount for a lime-
stone cement. To be clear, the cement with added mine tailings is not assessed as a limestone cement.
Studies show that a 10% cement replacement with CaCO3 will increase the early age strength, due to
the filler effect, but the final strength will be lower compared to a concrete with 100% Ordinary Portland
Cement [46]. This could suggest that the mine tailings from Tianbaoshan, used as SCM, might result
in a lower final compressive strength of the concrete.
The high content of CaCO3 in the Tianbaoshan mine tailings, could also be an issue in relation to cor-
rosion of the reinforcement in concrete. A high CaCO3 content results in a lowered pH of the concrete,
making the steel reinforcement less resistant to corrosion [47].
Based on this, it could be relevant in further work to investigate the strength development of cement
pastes containing the mine tailings. As to where to use concrete containing Tianbaoshan mine tailings,
it should be assessed with the possible strength reduction and the corrosion risk in mind.
6.2
Leaching of heavy metals
In Section 5.3.3.1, the leaching of heavy metals from the mine tailings and cement paste samples
containing mine tailings were compared. It was found that cadmium and nickel did not leach from
the cement pastes. Furthermore, approximately the same amount of arsenic, copper, and zinc leached
from the cement pastes containing mine tailings compared to the reference cement paste. Only the
leached amount of lead was slightly more comprehensive from the cement paste containing mine tailings
compared to the reference cement paste.
The greatest amount of leached heavy metal was found to be the lead leaching from the cement paste
containing mine tailings from Nalunaq. Compared to the expected total content, the leached amount
was found to constitute 1.01%.
Earlier studies have examined the retainment of heavy metals as well [48]. Leaching Behaviour and
Immobilisation of Heavy Metals in Solidified/Stabilised Products used the method S/S and the experi-
ments investigated the effect of different dried waste-to-binder ratios. The samples were cured for 28
days and subjected to leaching for 64 days. The leaching solutions were originally pH 4, but the alkaline
nature of the S/S matrix changed the pH of the leaching solutions so the pH became much higher at
the end.
The study found that the leaching of lead seemed to be controlled by the pH system and when the
leaching system reached pH 12, the leaching of lead increased. When the pH is high, the lead can form
hydroxide precipitates, which react with the hydrating cement paste. Thereby the lead is incorporated
as part of the C-S-H. The study assumes heavy metals in general to be either adsorbed or encapsulated
in the C-S-H. Furthermore, the study found that zinc has a good fixation to the cement matrix when
the pH is above 8. Compounds such as copper and iron may react with calcium and form more complex
compounds in the S/S matrix [48]. This could be the case in relation to the cement pastes containing
==PAGE:73==
6.3 Leaching of cement related chemicals
65
mine tailings, compared to the reference cement paste, no additional amount of neither copper nor iron
leaches. This can be seen in relation to the cement paste containing Zinkgruvan mine tailings in Figure
5.28b for cobber and Figure 5.40b for iron.
When the cement paste is placed in liquid, the portlandite will begin to decompose, releasing calcium
into the solution until equilibrium is reached. If all of the portlandite is able to dissolve, the C-S-H will
afterwards begin to dissolve. Therefore, the heavy metals adsorbed/encapsulated in the C-S-H may be
retained from leaching as long as the C-S-H phases are intact [48].
Leaching Behaviour and Immobilisation of Heavy Metals in Solidified/Stabilised Products concludes
that the main reason for the heavy metals to be retained is the alkaline nature and buffering capacity
provided by portlandite and C-S-H [48].
Since the TG values in Table 5.3 show no sign of a decrease in the content of portlandite, the lead
may be retained by the C-S-H. Further investigation would need a leaching experiment where all the
portlandite can be dissolved in order to investigate the dissolution of C-S-H and retainment of lead.
The study, Effect of Calcite on Lead-rich Cementitious Solid Waste Forms has compared four different
cement pastes, with and without addition of either/both calcite and Pb solids. It was found that in the
cement paste containing both 5% calcite and 10% Pb solids, the C-S-H phase possesses a large quantity
of lead and sulphate. It was though found that the cement pastes with no calcite and 10% lead solids,
had great accumulations of precipitated lead solid, rich in sulphate [49].
This study also used the S/S process, and like the previous study, showed that lead is very pH dependant.
The concentration of lead might change within the first few minutes after mixing, since dissolution and
re-precipitation processes of lead can occur.
The study has found that in the early stages of curing the precipitated lead will encapsulate some of
the calcium silicate phases which then will prevent the hydration of the cement clinker. Even as small
amounts of lead as 0.15 wt.% can effect the hydration and act as a retarder.
The presence of calcite accelerates the hydration process.
The acceleration occurs even when high
amounts of lead is added. It is likely that the rapid hydration of the clinker insures the S/S of lead in
the cement paste. Furthermore when calcite is present, it has been observed that C-Pb-S-H develops
and the structure becomes very insoluble.
To summarise, lead acts as a retarder, which the calcite counteracts, with the increase in rate of the
hydration [49]. It should be investigated further which effect lead has on the hydration when it acts as
a retarder.
Since all three mine tailings contain calcite and the RAPID CEMENT used contains 5% lime filler, any
retarding effect caused by the content of lead is assumed to be counteracted. Based on this and the
previous studies, the lead content from the mine tailings may be contained in the C-S-H in the cement
paste samples.

solution is NaOH with pH of 13 and a liquid-to-solid ratio of 10. The leached amount of SO4 is 106.00

It is assumed that ettringite acts as a buffer for sulphur, but it is not possible to further discuss the

previously performed by the laboratory at DTU Civil Engineering.

A previous study has tested the leaching of hydrated ordinary Portland cement in deionised water with
different liquid-to-solid ratios [53]. It was observed that the liquid-to-solid ratio should be above 500 in
order for the C-S-H to dissolve in deionised water. As mentioned earlier in this section, the dissolution
of C-S-H is relevant to investigate in order to confirm/reject the assumption of the heavy metals being
retained in the C-S-H.
After four weeks of leaching with a liquid-to-solid ratio of 10, the article found a calcium concentration
of approximately 20 mM and a silicon concentration of approximately 48 µM. In the article, the concen-
tration of calcium seems to have stabilised after four weeks of leaching but the concentration of silicon
has a decreasing tendency.
Comparing the values presented in this thesis with the values in the article, the concentrations of cal-
cium seem to coincide well, while the concentration of silicon from this thesis seems to be lower after 4
weeks of leaching, than expected based on the results in the article.
Since a liquid-to-solid ratio of 10 was used, it could be the reason why the results obtained in this thesis
do not coincide with other studies. It would be relevant to further investigate, what effect a higher
liquid-to-solid could have on the leaching process of the cement paste. Based on this, it was difficult to
deduce anything from the ICP data for the leached amounts of cement related chemicals.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Based on the results presented in Section 5.1, all three mine tailings in their raw state are estimated to
be polluted to an extent that makes them hazardous to the environment.
The portlandite content of the cement paste is found not to be affected by adding mine tailings but the
cement paste seems to contain a higher amount of water after 28 days of leaching and an explanation
for this has not been found.
The leaching of the mine tailings showed signs that some minerals in the mine tailings to some extent
might be affected by the leaching.
The microcline feldspar from the Zinkgruvan mine tailings could be expected to dissolve during leaching
of both mine tailings and cement paste samples due to the high pH of both leaching solutions. However,
based on the XRD results, the microcline seems to leach from the cement paste and not from the
mine tailings. Therefore there could be conditions in the cement paste which makes it possible for the
microcline to leach. It is not possible to make any further conclusions on the leaching of microcline.
The plagioclase and hornblende minerals in the Nalunaq mine tailings show variations in the intensity
measured by the XRD, but based on the conditions for which plagioclase and hornblende (amphibole
family) dissolves, the two minerals are not expected to be affected by the leaching process carried out
in this thesis. The variations in intensity of the plagioclase and hornblende peaks in the XRD date for
the Nalunaq mine tailings can therefore not be explained.
All three mine tailings are to some extent found to contain calcite and the highest amount was found in
the mine tailings from Tianbaoshan. The high CaCO3 content of the Tianbaoshan mine tailings could
be an issue in relation to using the mine tailings as a SCM. The CaCO3 content could result in both
a lower final compressive strength of the concrete, and a lower pH of the concrete, causing any steel
reinforcement to be at risk of corrosion.
The mine tailings from Zinkgruvan and Nalunaq contain considerably less CaCO3 compared to the mine
tailings from Tianbaoshan. Based on this, the CaCO3 content of the Tianbaoshan mine tailings are not
suspected to be a problem of significance.
The leaching of heavy metals, from the mine tailings, was found to be greatest in the 1.0 M NaOH
solutions. In general, the heavy metals seemed to some extent to leach from the mine tailings in most of
the solutions, and in particular in the 1.0 M and 0.1 M NaOH solutions. Still, the cement paste samples
seemed to be able to retain the heavy metals from leaching. Earlier studies, concerning heavy metals
being retained by the hydrate phases, found that some heavy metals are either adsorbed or encapsulated
in a C-Pb-S-H phase. This thesis found no decrease in the portlandite content (see Section 5.2.2) and
therefore it is not possible to reject the possibility of the lead being adsorbed on the surface of C-S-H.
Additionally, the calcite content in all three mine tailings would counter any retarding effect caused
by the lead when the mine tailings are added to the cement paste. Since the hydration process of the
cement pastes has not been investigated, it is not possible to reject that lead could be incorporated in
a C-Pb-S-H phase.
Concerning the remaining heavy metals investigated in this thesis, the results show no significant leached
amount and therefore there might be a tendency towards the heavy metals being retained in the cement
paste. Studies ([48], [49]) found that other heavy metals show the same tendencies as lead in relation to
retainment. Therefore the remaining heavy metals investigated in this thesis may possibly be retained
==PAGE:78==
7 Conclusion
70
in the C-S-H phase as well or in other complex compounds in the cement paste.
Common for the cement pastes containing mine tailings is that the leached amount of calcium is almost
identical to the leached amount of calcium from the reference cement paste.
Since the leaching is
approximately the same, it is assumed that the portlandite is acting a buffer in the solution. Since the
portlandite is not fully dissolved, leaving the C-S-H intact, it has not been possible to fully investigate
the leaching of the cement pastes containing mine tailings.
In relation to the phase diagram in Figure 6.1 the leaching of CaO is expected to be above approximately
24 mM, placing the samples in the Aqueous Phase + Ca(OH)2. The reason for this is that all the cement
paste samples contain high amounts of portlandite. Since the CaO concentration is lower than expected,
equilibrium in relation to calcium might not yet have been reached for the solution.
Other works [53] found that the liquid-to-solid ratio must be above 500 for the C-S-H to be able to
dissolve and the data also showed that the liquid-to-solid ratio influences the leached amount of both
calcium and silicon.
Based on the results in the article, the concentration of calcium measured in
this thesis might have stabilised after 28 days of leaching but the concentration of silicon still has a
decreasing tendency. Additional samples should have leached for a longer time period than 28 days in
order to investigate whether the samples have reached equilibrium.
In relation to the liquid-to-solid ratio, the amount of liquid used might have been inadequate for enough
calcium to dissolve. Among other things, the influence of the liquid-to-solid ratio, in relation to leaching,
should be examined in order to further investigate if the heavy metals are retained in the C-S-H phase.

CHAPTER 8
Further work
The test methods used in this thesis made it clear that some changes might have been necessary in
relation to the procedure of testing and how many samples were tested. During the analysis of the results
obtained in this thesis, it became clear that it might be necessary to produce triple determination of
the test samples. Furthermore, the order of the testing in the XRD and particularly in the TGA, could
be of importance in relation to the carbonation and moisturising of the samples. The error is assumed
to have the greatest significance for the TGA results. The extraction, amount of samples, and the order
of testing for the TGA should be consistent in future work. For the XRD data, the background could
be examined by use of the PONCKS technique in order to determine the presence of any amorphous
hydrate phases and their composition.
Concerning further work on the topic of mine tailings used as a SCM, it could be relevant to investigate
the strength properties of the cement paste and later on the strength of the concrete. Furthermore,
the amount of cement replaced with mine tailings should be investigated. Maybe the effect of smaller
replaced amounts of cement should be investigated, if the strength decreases with the addition of the
mine tailings. Furthermore, no significant activation of the mine tailings was found to occur in the
cement paste and it could therefore be relevant to investigate whether activation of the mine tailings is
possible as it is for calcined clay.
In relation to the increased water content in the cement paste sample after 28 days of leaching, it
could be due to moisturising of any clay minerals present in the mine tailings. It should therefore be
investigated further if any clay is present in mine tailings and whether it can be calcined. If the mine
tailings can not be activated it should be investigated whether the mine tailings could be used as filler.
When mixing the cement paste, it was hard to create a homogeneous sample due to a to small batch
size. If incomplete mixture resulted in a heterogeneous cement paste, it could be difficult to obtain
consistent results and further XRD and TGA tests would be necessary.
For this thesis, the materials were leached in alkaline solutions and the cement paste samples were
leached in deionised water with the same liquid-to-solid ratio used for all samples. It could be relevant
to investigate the leaching process under different conditions. For example the time of leaching could be
prolonged or the liquid-to-solid ratio could be varied. By increasing the liquid-to-solid ratio, it would be
possible to investigate whether the heavy metals are retained in the hydrated phases. It could also be
relevant to investigate the leaching of both the mine tailings and the cement paste samples containing
mine tailings under conditions where the amount of liquid continuously is changed or added to during
the time of leaching.
It should be investigated to what extent the lead affects the hydration of the cement, since lead is found
to have a retarding effect on the hydration.
Some mine tailings contain sulphur and therefore it could be relevant to investigate whether sulphate
related problems would occur. It was found that the mine tailings from Tianbaoshan contain sulphur
in the form of pyrite which can create pop-outs on the surface of the concrete. The issue with pyrite
related pop-outs should therefore be investigated further. In general, the resistance to sulphate attack
for the concrete containing mine tailings should be examined and the durability in relation to AKR and
corrosion as a result of the chloride content should also be considered in subsequent studies.

C.1
Determination of particle size distribution of Normsand
In order to create PSD of the non-reactive reference material similar to the mine tailings PSD, the PSD
of the mine tailings had first to be determined. Prior to this, the mine tailings needed to be dried in
an oven at 50◦C for approximately 24 hours. The oven was set at 0% open fan and 50% open flap.
To determine the particle size distribution a Mastersizer 2000 laser diffraction particle size analyser
from Malvern Panalytical is used. The mine tailings were analysed as dry ash, and the analysis program
SOP dry-ash was used. After cleaning the sample tray in the Mastersizer 2000 the sample was placed
according to the manual. After closing the safety flap the sample is sucked through the machine via
vacuum. In between samples, the tray is cleaned thoroughly. In lack of a vacuum cleaner the sample
tray was rinsed using ethanol and dried with clean paper towels. The analysis was repeated until three
similar results were achieved. The accumulated undersizes and sieve sizes were used to generate average
particle size distributions for each of the three mine tailings.
A possible error may have been caused based on the materials being kind of chunky after drying. This
may have caused material residue to settle in the sieve design to retain any larger particles.
Figure C.1 to C.3 shows the three PSDs for the different mine tailings.

C.5
Mixing of reference cement paste
The Normsand was dried for 24 hours and 8 minutes at 50◦C with 0% fan and 50% flap open. Then the
sand was crushed with a FRITSCH PULVERISETTE 0 Vibratory Micro Mill and sieved until a sand
sample achieved a similar PSD as the average PSD of the mine tailings. For 30 seconds the dry sand
and cement were mixed at speed 1. Due to complications the mixing was paused and then resumed
after 2 min. Then the paste was mixed for 1 minute and 30 seconds at speed one, set to rest for 3
minutes before mixing again for 1 minute at speed 2. (RPM for speed 2 is 2 RPM less per minute that
the standard prescribes). After mixing, the sample was poured into a 1000 ml clean airtight container
and placed in a Heidolph Reax 20 at speed 5 in order for the sample to be in movement during the first
24 hours of curing. This is done to avoid, bleeding and segregation of the sample.

APPENDIX K
Preparation and testing of
leaching experiment
K.1
Leaching of mine tailings
K.1.1
Procedure of leaching
The 25 mL basic NaOH solution was added to the dry samples using a glass pipette. When moving the
pipette from the container with the solution to the sample container, drops would be spilled along the
way if not careful. During the processing of the sample the pH strips used, caused some problems due
to inaccurate readings. Based on this, the type of pH-strips was changed 2 times. The first strips used
were a roll of pH-indicator paper with a scale from 1-14 with intervals of 2, which made it difficult to
get an accurate reading of 11 and 13 for the highly basic solutions. The second type op strips had a
single indicator area as well but a scale with single intervals, making it easier to read the values of 11
and 13. The third type of strips had 4 indicator areas, making the pH-reading much more accurate.
K.1.2
Stoppage of hydration
The stoppage of the hydration for the cement paste which did not leach, did not happen after 28
days for the reference cement paste, the cement paste sample containing Zinkgruvan mine tailings and
the cement paste sample containing Tianbaoshan mine tailings. The hydration stoppage by solvent
exchange for those was performed after 31, 30 and 29 days, respectively.
