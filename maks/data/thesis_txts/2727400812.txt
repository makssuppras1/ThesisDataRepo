Abstract
Complex networks appear everywhere around us and the ability to model them mathe­
matically is highly requested in a wide range of research fields, including neuroscience.
Modelling complex networks, such as the human brain, involve representing the network
as a graph consisting of nodes and links between nodes. Noninvasive neuroimaging tech­
niques such as diffusion­weighted MRI (dMRI) or functional MRI (fMRI) can be used to
derive structural and functional connectivity graphs, respectively. Different computational
methods can then be applied to discover patterns within these graphs. Most analysis
of functional resting state networks (RSN) focus only on within subject­differences. Mod­
elling across subject/population­differences holds great potential for revealing biomarkers
related to neurological diseases, which are currently diagnosed from non­objective crite­
ria. A prominent example is schizophrenia, which affects approximately 24 million people
worldwide and thus poses a significant public health problem.
This thesis proposes a new unsupervised Bayesian Stochastic Block Model (SBM), the
multinomial SBM (mSBM), for characterizing prominent differences across multiple graphs.
The thesis investigates the merits and limitations of the mSBM based on synthetic data
as well as uncovering the main type of variability across a population for two different
datasets: the Human Connectome Projet (HCP) or the Decoded Neurofeedback (Dec­
Nef) consortium. Using cluster­link probabilities obtained from the mSBM framework as
features, potential variability types in each dataset were attempted classified using four
different classifiers: LR, KNN, RF and SVM.
Based on a general high NMI between expected and MAP partition, it was found that the
mSBM is fairly robust wrt. random initialization, varying cluster sizes and class imbal­
ance. However due to the stochastic nature of the model, users should draw conclusions
across multiple initializations. The analysis of the HCP data showed that the most promi­
nent type of variability is caused by intra­cluster differences between structural and func­
tional connectivity. More specifically, the cluster containing mainly occipital and parietal
lobe areas were highly functionally linked and less structurally linked whereas a cluster
containing mainly frontal and temporal lobes were highly structurally linked and less func­
tionally linked. Future avenues should explore differences within functional and structural
graphs separately. The analysis of the DecNef data did not reveal the most prominent
type of variability even though multiple variability types were attempted classified. Over­
lapping connectivity patterns are likely to have caused ambiguous cluster­link probabil­
ities, thus leading to poor classification results. Future work should explore and refine
the model using alternative inference methods as well as consider additional preprocess­
ing and other graph construction approaches. Doing this can lead to the development
of important methodological tools that can help us gain a better understanding of brain
connectivity networks and possibly improve clinical assessment in the future.

Introduction
Complex networks are everywhere around us and there is a great need to model them
mathematically in many fields, such as physics, biology, sociology and psychology. Ex­
amples of complex networks are transportation systems in cities, communication paths in
the brain, social interactions between people as well as the Internet itself[1]. These net­
works are called complex, since their collective behavior cannot be predicted from their
individual components[2]. The study of complex networks, also known as network science
or graph representation learning, is a highly interdisciplinary research field, which aims
to improve our understanding properties of each network using theoretical and practical
techniques[3].
Modelling complex networks involves representing the network as a graph composed
by elements called nodes and connecting links (node interactions). Mathematical and
computational tools can then be used to explore the properties of the graph[2]. In general,
these tools involve grouping nodes based on similar properties/features. This is also
known as community detection, clustering or unsupervised classification. Clustering can
be based on either structural (separated regions) or functional (coordinated integration)
features, which can be derived at different levels/resolutions of the network[4].
There exists different methods for clusterings. When the number of clusters to be found
by a model is predefined (as in most cases), the model is referred to as parametric. On the
other hand, if the model is completely data driven, meaning there is no predefined number
of clusters, it is referred to as nonparametric. In order to evaluate similarity between
clusters, different similarity measures can be used. The most common similarity measure
is distance between features, where lower distance indicates higher similarity and vice
versa. One of the best­known and simplest clustering algorithms is the K means algorithm,
which finds centroids for each of the K clusters. Although K means is popular it has several
limitations including the need to predefine the number of clusters and sensitivity to outliers
and noise.
Improving methods for uncovering patterns within complex networks can be of great im­
portance in the field of neuroscience. The brain is estimated to have 1011 neurons and
1015 connections and thus constitutes a highly complex network, where many properties
remain unknown[5][6]. Brain networks can be defined based on either structural or func­
tional connectivity. Structural connectivity refers to the anatomical organization of the
brain through white matter tracts. It can be derived from diffusion­weighted MRI (dMRI),
which measures diffusion­driven displacements of water molecules over time. Since tis­
sue will hinder and/or restrict molecular diffusion, the resulting dMRI signal will reflect
tissue properties and thus allows for visualization of the microstructural organization[7]
Functional connectivity refers to the statistical dependence between time series reflecting
neural activity[7]. Neural activity can be measured directly using electroencephalogra­
phy (EEG) or magnetoencephalography (MEG) and indirectly using Functional Magnetic
Resonance Imaging (fMRI). EEG measures electrical activity generated by the various
cortical layers of brain and MEG measures the magnetic field produced by synchronously
activated neurons. Both EEG and MEG signals are directly related to neural activity with
high temporal resolution, but they have issues with sensitivity, localization, and spatial
resolution. fMRI has low temporal resolution, but high spatial resolution and is not lim­
ited to cortical areas. Functional brain imaging research are therefore often based on

fMRI[8][9][10].
fMRI is an indirect measurement of neural activity, since it uses a proxy signal known as
blood oxygenation level­dependent (BOLD). The BOLD signal is related to neural activ­
ity through metabolic demands of neurons during firing/spiking which is supplied by the
vascular mesh. The BOLD signal is measured as the difference between local concen­
tration of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood, which has different magnetic properties
depending on whether oxygen is bound to the hemoglobin or not. When there is a change
in behavioral and cognitive demands, there will be an increase in neural activity, which
leads to changes in the blood properties (called a hemodynamic response). The blood
flow increases and the oxygenated blood will displace the deoxygenated blood, which can
be observed as an increase in the BOLD signal[8].
When measuring the BOLD signal at rest, it is known to reflect baseline functional net­
works[11]. Most analysis of functional resting state networks (RSN) have been based on
a correlation analysis between different brain regions within subjects, for example using
a Stochastic Block Model (SBM) to cluster the brain regions that are linked[11]. In order
to do this, the fMRI data is first divided into brain regions using a predefined parcella­
tion atlas. Time series from each brain region can then be extracted and the correlation
matrix can be computed, resulting in a correlation­weighted graph. In the case where
the SBM models binary graphs, the correlation matrix is thresholded. A limitation of this
type of model is that it only finds connectivity patterns within individual subjects instead
of generalizing across a population consisting of multiple subjects.
The ability to discover connectivity patterns across a population and potentially reveal
biomarkers related to neurological diseases can have substantial impact on society. One
in eight people in the world live with a mental disorder, which is characterized by significant
disturbance in cognition, emotional regulation or behavior. These type of disorders can
be highly disabling and are profoundly associated with increased mortality[12].
Approximately 24 million (or one in 300) people worldwide are affected by schizophrenia,
which is characterized by impairment in perception and behavior changes. People with
schizophrenia have a life expectancy which is 10­20 years below the general population
and thus pose a significant public health problem[12]. The exact cause of schizophrenia is
unknown, but it is believed to be a combination of genetic and environmental factors[13].
There is no single test for diagnosing schizophrenia. Instead, the diagnosis is given after
assessment by a specialist in mental health, which is guided by diagnostic criteria such
as the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM)[14]. While diagnostic
criteria serve as useful tools for identifying mental disorders, the presence of overlapping
criteria across different conditions has compromised their validity. It has therefore been
suggested, that a new framework for objective diagnosis using biomarkers is needed in
order to avoid inappropriate and/or lack of treatment[15].
It has been found that people with schizophrenia have a functional dysconnectivity be­
tween components of large­scale brain networks. In other words the brain connectivity
pattern found in schizophrenia patients differs from that found in healthy individuals. This
functional dysconnectivity can be seen as abnormal brain activation on fMRI[16]. Mod­
eling the dysconnectivity in the brain network would be a great step towards building the
framework for objective diagnosis.
Project statement
The aim of the project is to develop unsupervised Bayesian stochastic block modeling
tools for the characterization of individual differences in brain connectivity data. The model

used in this project will be an extension from the model from [17] and we will refer to it
as the multinomial SBM (mSBM). The project is divided into three sub parts based on the
type of data used: synthetic data, Human Connectome Project (HCP) data including dMRI
and fMRI data for the same subjects, and lastly The Decoded NeuroFeedback (DecNef)
data including fMRI data for healthy and schizophrenia subjects. The first part aims to
explore the general properties of the mSBM by varying parameters used for the synthetic
data construction. The last two parts aim to explore what underlying variability drives the
extracted individual differences found in the respective MRI data. These research ques­
tions will be answered by analyzing the mSBM output and classification performance when
classifying the respective target type, including connectivity type (structural or functional)
in HCP data and diagnose (schizophrenia or healthy) in DecNef. The most prominent dif­
ference across the population found using the mSBM will be the target type related to the
best classification performance. The second part of the project works as a sanity check
of the mSBM to see if the model is able to find the clear difference between dMRI and
fMRI data. The research questions answered in this thesis are outlined below:
Research questions
1. What are the merits and limitations of the multinomial SBM for detecting
systematic differences in a population of graphs?
2. What type of variability drives the extracted individual differences in
the HCP data found using multinomial SBM?
3. What type of variability drives the extracted individual differences in the
DecNef data found using multinomial SBM?
In summary, this thesis aims to contribute to the fields of graph representation learning
and neuroscience by developing an unsupervised feature extraction model (mSBM) to
analyze functional connectivity differences across a population. A specific focus will be
put on diagnosing schizophrenia using graphs based on the noninvasive imaging tech­

2.1.1
Human Connectome Project data
The first dataset is the open access dataset from the WU­Minn Human Connectome
Project (HCP), which can be accessed from [18] by requesting access and accepting data
use terms. The goal of the HCP is to map the healthy human connectome by collecting
and freely distributing neuroimaging and behavioural data[19]. The used dataset contains
high­resolution in­vivo whole­brain diffusion­weighted and resting­state functional mag­
netic resonance imaging (dMRI and rs­fMRI) from 250 normal/healthy young adults, aged

Structural and functional connectivity can be derived from dMRI and fMRI, respectively.
In dMRI, the white matter trajectories across cortex are tracked and then used to form
structural networks, which are thus based on brain anatomy. In fMRI the time­dependent
neural activity within small brain regions (voxels) is indirectly measured through the blood
oxygenation level dependent (BOLD) response (described in Chapter 1) and then used
to form functional connectivity networks based on correlated activations[4]. For a review
of methods used to estimate the functional connectivity from fMRI, see [20]. Additional
details about the data collection in the HCP can be found in [19].
Multiple spatial and temporal artifacts in MRI data can occur due to MRI hardware/soft­
ware, subject motion and/or physiological noise such as cardiac and respiratory cycles.
In order to remove these so called confounds, the HCP fMRI was preprocessed and de­
noised using ICA­FIX (not performed by author). This pipeline uses independent compo­
nent analysis (ICA) to detect consistent spatial components and use it to separate signal
from noise. For further details see [21]. The dMRI data was preprocessed using the HCP
pipeline, which includes the removal of spatial artifacts, such as spatial distortions caused
by gradient nonlinearity, head­motion correction, cross­modal registration and alignment
to standard space. For more details see [22].

Graph construction
In order to construct the graphs, the MRI data (for each dataset) was parcelled into pre­
defined brain regions using the Schaefer atlas from 2018. This atlas is widely used in
neuroscience research and available through Nibabel and Nilearn librares in Python[26].
The Schaefer 2018 atlas is a a so called deterministic brain parcellation scheme, mean­
ing that it performs a hard parcellation of the brain into non­overlaping regions. The atlas
divides the brain (the cerebral cortex) into a set of anatomically defined regions (parcels),
based on either 7 or 17 yeo networks obtained from resting­state functional MRI (fMRI)

data. The default setting is 7 yeo (large­scale) networks, which was used in this thesis.
These networks include visual, somatomotor, dorsal attention, ventral attention, limbic,
frontoparietal and default network[27]. These parcels are created by grouping together
voxels (3D pixels) with similar functional connectivity profiles. The atlas comes in differ­
ent resolutions defined by the number of regions of interest (nrois). The parameter nrois
varies between 100 to 1000 in steps of 100. Using higher nrois yields finer parcellations
(smaller brain regions) and thus a higher resolution. Each parcel is assigned a unique la­

After parcelling an MRI data, the time series of each brain region is extracted and the
global confound signal is regressed out, which is elaborated in [29]. We then compute
the correlation between the time series of the respective brain regions and the result is a
correlation matrix. The correlation matrix itself corresponds to an undirected continuous­
weighted graph, which will be elaborated in Section 3.2.1. However, since the multinomial
SBM (mSBM) which we will use to extract features from the data, only tolerates discrete
graphs, e.g. binary or integer­weighted (elaborated later in ), we need to perform some
kind of thresholding on the correlation­weighted graphs.
Choosing a threshold can be difficult, since the same threshold might not be good for all
subjects[30]. Using a suboptimal threshold to create discrete graphs might result in dis­
carding valuable information in the graph (important links) as well as yield an unbalanced
graph density across subjects. To manage this problem we use the correlation percentiles
of each subject to threshold the graphs. Each subject graph will consist of integer­values
based on the thresholding with these percentiles. This way the density of each subject
graph will be the same (across subjects) and allow for a better comparison, i.e. the model
will be able to look beyond the inter­subject­variability and hopefully find ”more important”
differences such as population­based differences related to diagnose.
The natural follow­up question is now: what percentile should be used as threshold?
Even though using subject­specific percentiles as thresholds handles the issue of unbal­
anced link­density across subjects, we can still end up choosing a suboptimal percentile­
threshold, which (as mentioned earlier) might discard value information in the graph. By
using a high percentile as threshold, e.g. for the 90th percentile, the binarization of the

3.1
Introduction to Bayesian framework
In the Bayesian framework model parameters are defined as random variables, where
the randomness reflect our lack of knowledge about them[1]. The framework is often
divided into a modeling and an inference part, where modelling involves specifying the
relevant probability distributions for each model parameter. Bayesian inference refers to
the process of estimating the model parameters θ based on observed data X and prior
knowledge (initial belief about model parameters). More specifically, our prior beliefs are
updated using Bayes’ rule:
p(θ|X) = p(X|θ)p(θ)

prior distribution of the parameters and p(θ|X) denotes the updated distribution of the
parameters, which we refer to as the posterior distribution. The posterior represents our
uncertainty about the parameters after taking the observed data into account. The de­
nominator p(X), which is the normalization or marginalization constant, is given by

3.2.3
Infinite Relational Model
The Infinite Relational Model (IRM) is the non­parametric version of the SBM or in other
words; it is a SBM where an infinite number of clusters is allowed. In many cases it
is reasonable to assume that as we observe more data, new clusters will appear. It is
therefore undesirable to fix the (upper limit on the) number of clusters. Instead we prefer
a model that allows the number of clusters to grow if needed. The IRM does this using a
prior known as the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP)[36].

bilistic behavior in an intuitive way. Imagine a Chinese restaurant with an infinite number
of tables (clusters). The seating arrangement of the customers (nodes) corresponds to a
random partition matrix, Z, see visualization in Figure 3.7.

3.2.4
Multinomial Stochastic Block Model
The model used in this thesis is an extension of the model used in [17], which is based on
the concept of SBM and IRM. The proposed model uses a multinomial likelihood, which is
discrete and thus allows modelling of both binary and integer­weighted graphs. A natural
choice of prior on the link­probabilities is the Dirichlet distribution, since it is conjugate.
In the following section, two versions of the model will be explained: a parametric and
a non­parametric version, which we will refer to as the parametric and non­parametric
multinomial SBM (mSBM), respectively. From a modeling perspective, the only differ­
ence between the two models, is the prior distribution on the clustering: the parametric
model prior is given by the Dirichlet­Multinomial distribution in Equation (3.13) and the
nonparametric model prior is given by the CRP in Equation (3.25). To complete the two
model formulations, we need to specify the Dirichlet prior on the cluster­link probabilities
as well as the multinomial likelihood of node­links. These two distributions are the same
for both the parametric and nonparametric model.
First of all, we seek to discover clusters of brain­areas at subject­level based on connectivity­
differences present at population­level. In order to capture these population­based dif­
ferences, we need to distribute the likelihood of the links and prior on link probabilities
across the subjects (over the population). Since the clusters of brain areas are found at
subject­level, the node partitioning is distributed across clusters (and not subjects). In the
following, random variables distributed over subjects will be marked using a bar notation.
The concept of the mSBM with the 3D arrays A and η and vectors aij and ηlm is visualized

3.3.1
Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is a method used to approximate the posterior distri­
bution using the joint distribution. As mentioned earlier, the denominator in Bayes’ rule is
intractable, but since it is just a constant, the shape of the two distributions will be the same
(just at different scales). The idea is therefore to sample from the joint distribution, which
under the special conditions will end up being samples from the posterior distribution[31].

If the Markov chain satisfies the detailed balance condition, then after a certain number
of iterations, it will converge to a stationary/invariant distribution corresponding to the
target distribution (posterior distribution). Given two states θs+1 and θs+1, the detailed

space of the target distribution using random walk where the high density regions are
visited more frequently than areas with low density.

5
Discussion
This chapter is divided into two main parts: discussions of synthetic and MRI data re­
sults, which follows the order of the research questions. First the merits and limitations
of the mSBM will be discussed based on the experiments for the synthetic dataset. The
last section analyzes the type of variability captured by the mSBM when conducting the
different experiments for the HCP and DecNef data, respectively.
5.1
Synthetic data results
Research question: What are the merits and limitations of the multinomial SBM for de­
tecting systematic differences in a population of graphs?
Based on the findings in Table 4.1 and Table 4.2, the mSBM generally scores a high NMI
indicating that the model is able to find the expected partition. The vast majority of the syn­
thetic experiments showed consistency over the 10 random initalizations, meaning that
both the parametric and nonparametric mSBM may be considered fairly robust. However
due to the stochastic nature of the model, conclusions should always be drawn on results
obtained across multiple random intializations. Compared to the nonparametric model,
the parametric model appears less robust for a low initial number of clusters and identical
cluster link probabilities. In this case, the only variability across the population will be the
synthetic subject variability. Thus, the differences will be individual­ rather than group­
based, which seems to confuse the model. This is a clear limitation of the parametric
model in cases where it is used to discover if there are group­based differences. How­
ever if the model is used to discover which differences exist in data already known to hold
group­based differences, such as healthy and sick subjects, this would not be an issue.
Furthermore, a clear benefit of the model is that no significant change in performance was
observed for varying cluster sizes and class imbalance, indicating that the model is robust
against these as well.
It should be noted that the synthetic data generation does not strictly follow the generative
story of the model. We could have done this by using ancestral sampling, i.e. start by
sampling the partition, then the cluster­link probabilities and finally the links. However,
by constructing the synthetic data as done in this thesis, it is easier to adjust the different
parameter settings and thus carry out the experiments under more controlled conditions.
Moreover, it is noted that the conducted analysis of the model properties is limited to the
predefined parameter settings, which were not chosen rigorously. For further analysis of
the model properties, other approaches for synthetic data generation with other param­
eter settings could be used, e.g. varying the total number of nodes (corresponding to
data resolution) and/or implementing ratios of class imbalance and similarity between the
cluster­link probabilities. This might lead to a better understanding of the exact relation­
ship between groups similarities and the ability to extract potential differences.
5.2
MRI data results
When analyzing the results for the single experiment outputs for both MRI datasets (HCP
and DecNef), it should be kept in mind that the resulting partition should not be considered
as the true partition, but rather as the outcome of that specific experiment/parameter set­
ting. For visualization purposes, the partition obtained using the lowest parameter values
of nrois (resolution) and initial noc (number of clusters) were used. Using a different reso­
lution and initial number of clusters could lead to a different partition. Determining whether
70
Modeling Individual Differences in Functional Brain Connectivity using Stochastic Block Models
==PAGE:80==
these are true partitions, will rely on the actual classification performance of the target in
question. Even if it is possible to classify the target perfectly using the cluster­link prob­
abilities of the respective model, the discovered clusters will only reflect the true partition
for that particular parameter combination. To achieve a consensus partition, future work
should conduct experiments using many more parameter combinations and compare their
respective partitions.
Furthermore, Figure 4.15 and Figure 4.16 showed that the nonparametric model would
simply explode in the number of clusters when increasing the graph density throughout in­
ference. Since we seek to find subparts of the network rather than recovering the original
network (number of clusters = number of nodes), this is undesired behavior. By applying
the parametric model instead, it enables explicit control of the scale at which prominent
differences should be derived from. Thus, when applied on neuroimaging data, the para­
metric version of the model should be preferred over the nonparametric version. However,
future avenues should aim to develop alternative sampling methods for the parametric
model allowing more efficient search of the parameter space, similar to the split­merge
sampler used for the nonparametric model.
HCP data results
Research question: What type of variability drives the extracted individual differences in
the HCP data found using multinomial SBM?
Functional connectivity is known to be prominent across left and right hemispheres (in­
terhemispheric connectivity)[4], but mapping interhemispheric structural connectivity has
issues with tracking through the corpus callosum, which is the largest white matter struc­
ture connecting the two hemispheres[46].We would therefore expect the most prominent
differences between structural and functional connectivity to be interhemispheric. The
findings in this thesis suggest otherwise. Based on Figure 4.1, Figure 4.2, Figure 4.3 and
Figure 4.5, it was found that most prominent differences across the healthy subjects in
the HCP data was within­cluster­differences. More specifically, the first cluster, including
areas from occipital and parietal lobe, were highly functionally linked and less structurally
linked. The second cluster, including areas from frontal and temporal lobes, were highly
structurally linked and less functionally linked.
Based on the findings in Figure 4.6, Figure 4.7 and Table A.3, the type of variability caus­
ing the most prominent difference is the connectivity type. Only two clusters were required
to reach perfect classification using both LR, RF and KNN, implying that this difference
occurs at a macro­level of the brain. In order to get a better understanding of the connectiv­
ity patterns within these clusters, future work should look into differences within structural
and functional graphs, separately. Another way to explore differences between structural
and functional connectivity is to collapse all respective graphs into one functional and one
structural graph. This corresponds to assuming same cluster­link probabilities for all func­
tional and structural graphs, respectively, and imposing a functional and structural prior
on the cluster­link probabilities. This way the model will only look at differences between
the two types of connectivity, and not subject­variability as we have included now.
Since the cluster­link probabilities (features) clearly reflect connectivity type differences,
lower scores were expected when predicting gender. This was also the case. Even though
the gender­classification performance was not perfect, we still achieved an accuracy of
0.78 (with baseline 0.57) and F1­score of 0.71 using LR, which is relatively good. This
implies that the connectivity patterns are dissimilar to some level and they can therefore
coexist. This allows for prediction of different targets based on the same feature set.
Modeling Individual Differences in Functional Brain Connectivity using Stochastic Block Models
71
==PAGE:81==
Based on Figure 4.8 and Figure 4.9, it was found that after a certain point, increasing
the initial number of clusters will not improve the classification performance. To improve
the performance, one should rather increase the resolution. In future work, it should be
investigated whether there exists a similar saturation behavior for the resolution parameter
as well, indicating that after a certain point, increasing the resolution will not improve
performance. In other words, this could imply what level of detail is required for classifying
the given target as well as testify to what scale the relevant underlying processes of the
given target exist at.
DecNef data results
Research question: What type of variability drives the extracted individual differences in
the DecNef data found using multinomial SBM?
Based on Figure 4.10, Figure 4.11, it was found that similar brain areas were clustered
together in general. Therefore, future work should investigate whether setting the initial
number of clusters to 7 would result in clusters corresponding to the 7 networks (visual,
somatomotor, dorsal attention, ventral attention, limbic, frontoparietal and default) which
the used atlas is derived from. Based on Figure 4.12 and Figure 4.14, it was found that the
most prominent differences across the healthy and schizophrenia subjects in the DecNef
data was between­cluster differences. More specifically between cluster pairs 7­8, 3­8
and 0­6. Since cluster 7 and 3 both consist of mainly visual areas, it is not surprising that
both would be related to cluster 8, which mainly consists of the default network.
Without good classification performance, it is difficult to reason about the cluster­relations
in terms of variability type. The highest F1­score (0.66) achieved using cluster­link proba­
bilities as features was when predicting gender with KNN. However the accuracy was only
0.59, but this is still higher than the zero­rule accuracy. Due to the overall poor classifica­
tion performances, it is difficult to determine what type of variability that is most prominent
across the population. None of the tested target­types (diagnose, gender, handedness
or site) yielded convincing results, which might be because their respective connectiv­
ity differences, and thus feature patterns, overlap. If the variability signals from different
targets are equally strong/prominent, then the model appears to be unable to distinguish
these. In future work, one could try to divide the dataset in smaller parts, eliminating all
but one source of variability. For example keeping only young right­handed males from
scanner site 1, who are either healthy and have schizophrenia. If it is then possible to
classify schizophrenia, then the same procedure could be done using the corresponding
left­handed subjects. Finally the two classification results could be compared and if the
same feature pattern is found in both cases, then it could indicate that this pattern is con­
sistent over handedness. Continuing this way, it would be possible to see how many and
which features that can be included in the dataset without disrupting the diagnose­feature
pattern.
Another possible explanation of the poor classification could be insufficient preprocessing
steps. Including subject motion parameters, artifacts, respiratory and cardiac signals as
confounding factors during preprocessing have been motivated in [29] and should be
considered in future work. Furthermore, the design choices during graph construction
is important to get meaningful results. In this thesis, the graphs were constructed using
subject­percentiles for thresholding the correlations, but the choice of the exact percentile
was somewhat arbitrary. Thus, some important links might have been excluded from the
resulting graph. Future work could experiment with lower percentiles than 6.25% to see if
classification performance would improve.

Further extension of the mSBM should also be considered. First of all, the model only
works on binary or integer­weighted graphs, which is a limitation that comes from using
a multinomial likelihood. Future work could try to apply a multivariate continuous distribu­
tion instead, however this would likely be at the expense of conjugacy and thus complicat­
ing inference. Furthermore, since the within/intra­cluster differences were so small (std.
and KL divergence close to 0), an idea is to experiment with restrictions on the cluster­
link probabilities. This could be done by using priors that distinguish between inter­ and
intra­cluster­link probabilities, which is introduced in [1] for Bayesian community detection
(BCD) model.
Moreover, other inference methods than Gibbs sampling should be considered such as
split­merge sampling for the parametric mSBM or perhaps variational inference. Gibbs
sampling is known to have bad mixing ability for larger networks[47]. Using a convergence
criteria, the inference chains were limited to around 30 iterations, which is not enough to
proper sample the posterior distribution. However, even though the number of samples
might not result in a representative posterior distribution, the interesting aspect is whether
the inferred clustering are meaningful/useful in classification tasks. Doing inference, we
are interested in finding the partition that is most likely given the data, i.e. obtain a high log
likelihood. However, this is only true up to a certain point: as seen in in the Figure 4.18,
Figure 4.19 and Figure 4.20), the log likelihood will only be increased if the model com­
plexity (number of clusters) increases as well. Getting the highest possible number of
clusters would correspond to the worst case scenario where each node (brain area) has
its own cluster and the resulting partition will thus be useless. Therefore, the choice of the
optimal parameters for the feature model should be based on the classification results. If
the goal is target prediction and the target is perfectly classified for each feature model
instance (respective parameter setting), then the preferred feature model, would be the
simplest one, i.e. lowest resolution and initial number of clusters. On the other hand, if
the goal is also to explain the population differences at multiple levels/scales of the brain
network, the classification performances of each feature model would simply serve as a
confirmation that their respective features reflect the same target. In this case, the out­
come is not necessarily choosing a single preferred feature model, but rather comparing
the connectivity patterns of the respective feature models.
Finally, many other applications could benefit from using the mSBM. In this thesis, we
considered differences across a population, but the framework could easily be used for
discovering temporal differences, such as task­related connectivity changes or perhaps
effect of medication.

Conclusion
This thesis focused on developing and analyzing the properties of the unsupervised Bayesian
model, the multinomial Stochastic Block Model (mSBM), for detecting prominent differ­
ences in populations of graphs derived from brain connectivity data. The properties of the
model were analyzed using three different datasets: one synthetic dataset and two open
source MRI datasets from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) and the Decoded Neu­
rofeedback (DecNef) consortium, respectively. Using cluster­link probabilities obtained
from the mSBM as features, potential variability types in each dataset were attempted
classified using four different classifiers: LR, KNN, RF and SVM.
The first part of the analysis focused on the benefits and limitations of the mSBM. The syn­
thetic data results demonstrated that both the parametric and nonparametric version of
the mSBM performed well in identifying expected partitions, as indicated by high Normal­
ized Mutual Information (NMI). For the vast majority of experiments, both model versions
showed consistency over 10 random initializations, thus implying model robustness. The
parametric model showed reduced robustness in cases with a low initial number of clus­
ters and identical cluster link probabilities. This limitation suggests that the model is more
suitable for discovering and explaining population differences already known to exist in a
population rather than uncovering potential differences that might not even exist. Notably,
the model’s performance remained consistent for varying cluster sizes and class imbal­
ance. Future work should however conduct additional experiments with varying number
of nodes and/or using ratios of class imbalance and similarity between cluster­link in order
to further improve our understanding of these properties.
The second part of the analysis focused on identifying the type of variability that drives
the prominent differences across the subjects in the HCP data extracted using the mSBM.
Perfect classification performance (accuracy and F1­score of 1) was obtained when clas­
sifying structural and functional connectivity, indicating that the connectivity type is the
most prominent difference across the population. Classifying gender yielded an accuracy
of 0.78 and F1­score of 0.69, implying that the connectivity patterns of gender and connec­
tivity type overlap and can therefore coexist in the cluster­link probabilities, which allows
for prediction of both targets. Besides providing useful features for classification pur­
poses, the mSBM also enables feature explainability. It was found that only two clusters
were required to obtain perfect classification of connectivity type and that the prominent
difference is within the two clusters. The first cluster, which included areas from occipital
and parietal lobe, were highly functionally linked and less structurally linked. The oppo­
site observation was made for the second cluster, which included areas from frontal and
temporal lobes.
The third and last part of the analysis focused on identifying the type of variability that
drives the prominent differences across the healthy and schizophrenia subjects in the
DecNef data extracted using the mSBM. None of the attempted targets (diagnose, gen­
der, handedness and site) yielded convincing classification performance, i.e. using the
format ”accuracy (zero­rule accuracy) and F1­score” the best scores (based on F1­score)
obtained across the different classifiers for each target was 0.87 (0.84) and 0.55 for di­
agnose, 0.59 (0.55) and 0.66 for gender prediction, 0.34 (0.93) and 0.25 for handedness
and 0.58 (0.22) and 0.57 for scanner site. It is therefore not possible to conclude which
variability type was the most prominent. The poor performance might be explained by

==PAGE:84==
overlapping connectivity patterns, which would result in ambiguous cluster­link probabili­
ties. Further investigations, such as dividing the dataset into smaller subsets to eliminate
certain sources of variability may enhance classification performance. Prominent differ­
ences were found between three cluster­pairs, which mainly involved areas within the
visual, default and dorsal attention networks as well as limbic system. However to prop­
erly reason about the connectivity changes between these areas, better classification
performances are required.
The ability to extract prominent differences across multiple graphs at multiple scales as
well as explaining what type of cluster­relation (intra­ or inter­cluster relations) that drives
these differences, makes the mSBM a promising framework in various applications be­
yond population differences and the field of neuroscience. When applied on neuroimag­
ing data, the parametric version of the model is preferred over the nonparametric version,
since it enables explicit control of the scale at which prominent differences should be de­
rived from. Further exploration and refinement of the model, use of alternative inference
methods, along with careful consideration of data preprocessing and graph construction,
will contribute to its effectiveness and usefulness in uncovering meaningful insights in
graph­based datasets.

A.2
Split­Merge sampling
Split­Merge sampling was introduced in [48] and later the the extension with sequential
allocation (SAMS algorithm) was proposed in [49]. A further refinement was proposed in
[50], which speeds up computation time by avoiding the evaluation of unnecessary tran­
sitions probabilities of proposals that will be rejected anyways. The split­merge sampler
can be more efficient than other MH samplers in certain situations, such as when the pos­
terior distribution has a multimodal shape or when the target variable has a large number
of dimensions. The Gibbs sampler can become stuck in local modes, where a given clus­
ter is supposed to be split, but the steps towards splitting the cluster would decrease the
joint distribution.
Split­Merge sampling is an extension of the MH algorithm. The basic idea behind the
split­merge sampler is to propose new states for the target variable by splitting or merging
existing states. In this case, the target variable is the partition, meaning that clusters can
either be split into separate clusters or merged into one, thus resulting in a new state of the
partition. The sampler works by randomly drawing two nodes (uninformed/independent
of the current partition state) and then computing their respective cluster assignment. If
their cluster assignments are the same, then a split is proposed and otherwise a merge
is proposed. The sampler then accepts or rejects the proposal based on the likelihood of
the resulting partitions. A simple outline of the split­merge sampler steps is given below:
Split­merge sampler steps
1. Start with an initial state for the target variable, Z.
2. Propose a split or merge of the current state
3. Compute the acceptance probability for the proposed split or merge
4. Accept proposal with probability equal to the acceptance probability, other­
wise reject proposal.
5. Repeat steps 2­4 for a desired number of iterations.
The used split­merge sampler performs three restricted Gibbs sampling sweeps (restricted
to only sample nodes from the current cluster). The first sweep performs sequential allo­
cation, where each node is assigned to either the original cluster or a new cluster. The
second sweep is refinement of sequential allocation, making it more likely and thereby
increase acceptance ratio. The last sweep leads to the final state, from which the joint
probability is evaluated.

A.4
Kullback­Leibler divergence
The Kullback­Leibler (KL) divergence, also known as relative entropy, is an asymmetric
nonnegative metric used to measure the difference (in information) between two proba­
bility distributions (a baseline/reference and a sampling distribution), thus indicating how
different the respective distributions are. A Kl divergence of 0 implies that the two distribu­
tions are the same. The higher the KL divergence, the more dissimilar the two distributions
are. The formula for continuous distributions are given by
KL(p(x)||q(x)) =
& ∞
−∞
p(x) ln p(x)
q(x)dx
(A.13)
where p(x) is the sampling distribution and q(x) is the reference distribution. It is noted that
swapping the two distributions, will yield a different value. This is known as the asymmetry
property. The KL divergence is implemented in the entropy function from the python library
scipy.stats. For more elaborate explanation of the KL divergence, see [31].
