Abstract
Sampling points eﬀiciently and intelligently from implicit representations such as Signed
Distance Functions (SDFs) remains a key challenge in geometry processing. This the-
sis presents ASAP (Adaptive Sampling via Approximate Projections), a novel method for
adaptive point sampling from SDFs, designed to focus sampling density on geometrically
complex regions. ASAP leverages curvature estimates – specifically the Laplacian norm
of the SDF gradient – to guide a curvature-aware sampling distribution, followed by pro-
jection onto the learned surface. A self-occlusion heuristic further ensures coverage in
partially hidden or undersampled regions. The method is evaluated using DeepSDF as
the underlying implicit representation and Robust Surface Reconstruction (RsR) for mesh
extraction.
Quantitative and qualitative results across several 3D shapes show that ASAP outper-
forms the classical baseline Marching Cubes, producing smoother, more watertight re-
constructions with fewer artifacts. The modular structure of ASAP supports alternative
curvature metrics and sampling shapes, and can generalise to other SDF backbones. These
findings underscore the value of informed, geometry-aware sampling in neural surface
reconstruction pipelines, and suggest promising directions for future work in adaptive
sampling and the formalisation of geometric complexity.

Explicit- and Implicit Surface Representation
An explicit surface represents geometry through discrete elements such as vertices, edges,
and faces (e.g., meshes). Connectivity and topology are explicitly stored.
An implicit surface, however, is defined as the level set of a continuous function f(x),
typically the zero level set {x | f(x) = 0}.

Laplacian
The Laplacian of a scalar field f is ∇2f = ∇· (∇f). For SDFs, its magnitude correlates
with local curvature, with high values indicating fine geometric detail.
Manifold
A 2D surface is a manifold if every point has a neighbourhood locally homeomorphic to an
open disk. Manifold meshes avoid non-manifold edges and vertices, ensuring consistent
normals and topology.
Mesh
A mesh M = (V, E, F) is an explicit polygonal representation of a surface, consisting of
vertices V, edges E, and faces F, usually in the shape of triangular polygons.
Point Cloud
A point cloud P = {pi ∈R3 | i = 1, . . . , N} is an unordered set of 3D points without
connectivity information.
Signed Distance Function (SDF)
An SDF d : R3 →R assigns to each point x the signed distance to the surface: d < 0 inside,
d > 0 outside, and d = 0 is exactly the surface.

3
Introduction
3.1
Motivation
Surface reconstruction is a fundamental problem in geometry processing, with applica-
tions in computer graphics, computer vision, and manufacturing. Traditional explicit
representations, such as meshes and point clouds, are widely used due to their intuitive
geometric interpretation. However, they suffer from several limitations when used for
high-quality reconstruction.
Firstly, achieving high-resolution reconstructions with explicit representations incurs sig-
nificant memory requirements. Meshes require storing all vertices, faces, and connectiv-
ity information, and voxel grids grow cubically with resolution. Secondly, while meshes
provide explicit topology once constructed, inferring this topology from raw data such
as point clouds is challenging. Reconstruction algorithms must explicitly decide on con-
nectivity, handle non-manifold cases, and manage topology changes such as creating or
merging components. Finally, surface continuity is not guaranteed: reconstructed meshes
may contain cracks, irregular triangulations, or non-manifold edges, particularly in re-
gions of fine detail.
Implicit representations, in contrast, model surfaces as the level set of a continuous func-
tion, typically d(p) : R3 →R, with the surface given by the zero level set {x | d(x) = 0}.
Signed distance functions (SDFs) are a common choice, encoding the signed shortest dis-
tance to the surface. They are continuous and differentiable almost everywhere, which
allows the direct computation of normals (∇d(p)) and curvature estimates, and they nat-
urally handle topology changes. Moreover, implicit functions offer compact represen-
tations, as the surface is stored only through function parameters rather than discrete
vertices. Finally, their continuous nature enables flexible sampling strategies, which can
adapt to varying levels of geometric complexity.
3.1.1
Background
Recent advances in neural implicit representations have significantly improved the recon-
struction quality of SDF-based methods. DeepSDF [2] introduced a continuous, neural-
network-based SDF representation conditioned on latent codes, enabling compact shape
storage, smooth interpolation, and high-resolution reconstruction. Other neural implicit
approaches, such as Occupancy Networks, follow a similar principle but predict binary
occupancy instead of distances.
The accuracy of neural implicit surface reconstruction depends heavily on the sampling
strategy used during training. Uniform sampling within the bounding volume ensures
global space coverage but wastes many samples far from the surface, providing limited
gradient information. Consequently, DeepSDF introduced a near-surface biased sam-
pling strategy, which prioritises points close to the surface to improve reconstruction
quality. However, this approach often leads to under-representation of regions with high
curvature or intricate geometric features, causing over-smoothing and a loss of sharp de-
tails.
3.1.2
Problem Formulation
The reconstruction of fine geometric details remains an open challenge for neural implicit
surfaces. Sampling strategies must balance two competing objectives: (1) dense sampling

==PAGE:14==
in areas of high geometric complexity to preserve sharp features, and (2) suﬀicient global
coverage to avoid undersampled regions.
Most existing methods prioritise the first objective but lack explicit mechanisms to ensure
that the entire 3D space is adequately explored. A measure of “full exploration” would
provide valuable diagnostics for assessing and improving sampling strategies. Devel-
oping such a feature-aware yet globally reliable sampling method is essential for high-
quality surface reconstruction.
3.2
Contributions
This thesis proposes an adaptive, feature-aware sampling strategy for SDFs to improve
the reconstruction of fine geometric features while maintaining adequate global coverage.
The method builds on DeepSDF and leverages its differentiability to identify high-detail
regions through curvature estimation. In brief, points are first projected to the surface
using predicted normals and SDF values. The Laplacian of these points is then computed
to approximate local curvature, and regions with high Laplacian magnitude are resam-
pled more densely. Finally, the new points are projected back to the surface, resulting in
a refined, feature-aware training point set.
The primary research questions addressed in this work are:
1. Does adaptive, curvature-aware sampling improve reconstruction quality compared
to standard near-surface sampling?
2. How does the proposed method affect the preservation of fine geometric features
and overall surface accuracy?

Methods
4.1
Overview of the Pipeline
The proposed pipeline for surface reconstruction consists of three main stages:
1. Training a DeepSDF model to learn a continuous implicit signed distance function
(SDF).
2. Sampling points adaptively from the SDF to create a high-quality point cloud of
varying density depending on feature level.
3. Reconstructing the final mesh using Rotation Systems for Surface Reconstruction
(RsR).
The primary contribution of this work is the adaptive sampling strategy described in sec-
tion 4.5.

4.3
Continuous SDF representation using DeepSDF
4.3.1
Theory
DeepSDF (Park et al., [2]) is a neural implicit representation designed to model contin-
uous signed distance functions (SDFs) of entire classes of 3D shapes. Unlike classical
discretised SDFs stored on voxel grids, DeepSDF learns a continuous function dθ that
predicts the signed distance value for any 3D query point p ∈R3, conditioned on a latent
shape code z. The surface of a shape is implicitly represented as the zero-level set of this
function, while the sign of the output indicates whether the point is inside (negative) or
outside (positive) the surface, as per the most common SDF definition.
By representing shapes as a continuous volumetric field, DeepSDF inherits several ad-
vantages over mesh- or voxel-based methods. It eliminates discretisation artefacts, pro-
vides high-fidelity surface normals via analytic gradients ∇dθ(p), and requires signifi-
cantly much less memory compared to voxel grids, especially when representing a large
dataset of classes, e.g. thousands of planes. Moreover, as a learned generative model, it
can interpolate between different shapes in a smooth latent space, enabling shape com-
pletion and synthesis.
The DeepSDF model is trained as a conditional decoder (an auto-decoder, see figure 4.4),
where the decoder weights θ and a latent vector zi for each shape are jointly optimised.
This differs from the typical auto-encoder framework, where zi is created by the encoder
itself, from the input — instead, zi is the input for the auto-decoder. Each training sample
consists of a pair (p, s), where p is a 3D location and s = SDF(p) is the signed distance
value. The network is trained to minimise the difference between the predicted and true
SDF values using a clamped L1 loss:
L(dθ(z, x), s) = |CLAMP(fθ(z, x), δ) −CLAMP(s, δ)| ,
(4.1)
with CLAMP(p, δ) = min(δ, max(−δ, p)), essentially restricting the output and target to ±δ.
The parameter δ limits the spatial range over which metric distances are enforced, with
an enhanced focus on the surface the lower δ is, while sacrificing accuracy of SDF values
further from the surface.

4.3.2
Data Preparation
For training, meshes are first normalised to fit within a unit sphere. DeepSDF requires
dense sampling of points both near and far from the surface to learn a smooth signed
distance field. Following Park et al. [2], spatial points are sampled with a strong bias
towards the surface: in practice, 90% of points are sampled close to the surface (using
small perturbations along surface normals) and 10% are distributed uniformly within
the bounding volume. The signed distance for each point is computed as the distance to
the closest surface point, with a negative sign for points inside the mesh.
dθ(z, p) will henceforth be reffered to as either dθ(p) or dθ(·) (to describe the function
itself) for quicker notation. Once trained, the surface can be extracted by evaluating dθ(·)
across a set of spatial points and reconstructing the zero-level set using marching cubes
or by raycasting. Additionally, surface normals can be obtained analytically as

4.3.3
Advantages and Limitations
DeepSDF produces smooth, high-resolution surfaces and provides a compact latent space
for shape interpolation and completion. However, optimisation over the latent vector
during inference can be computationally expensive, as it requires solving a non-linear op-
timisation problem for each new shape. Furthermore, it assumes canonical alignment of
the training data, which limits direct applicability to real-world scans without additional
registration steps.

4.4
Baseline Reconstruction Strategy
For baseline surface reconstruction, we compare our method to:
1. Uniform Projection: A uniformly distributed set of random points in the bounding
box is projected to the surface using SDF values and gradients, without adaptive
resampling (described in Section 4.5).
2. Marching Cubes: The trained SDF is sampled on a regular grid, and the zero iso-
surface is extracted using the standard Marching Cubes algorithm (Lorensen &
Cline (1987), [6]). This provides a reference reconstruction, albeit often oversmoothed
and resolution-limited.
4.5
Proposed Adaptive Sampling Method
The proposed adaptive sampling method aims to generate a point cloud from the learned
DeepSDF that captures sharp geometric features and suﬀiciently samples self-occluded re-
gions, as to fully explore the shape, while avoiding excessive oversampling in smooth
areas. By self-occluded regions, we refer to areas of the object, where rays casted onto the
surface have trouble reaching due to partial or complete obstruction by parts of the object
itself — an example could be under the wings of the duck (figure ??).
The method proceeds in five stages: grid sample and initial surface projection, curvature
estimation, adaptive resampling based on a sigmoid mapping of the Laplacian, occluded-
region refinement, and a final surface projection.

The Laplacian is a fundamental differential operator that measures the rate at which a
quantity diverges from the gradient in an infinitesimal neighbourhood. For a scalar field
f : R3 →R, the Laplacian can be written as the sum of unmixed 2nd order partial deriva-

In the context of a signed distance field (SDF), dθ(·), the Laplacian quantifies how rapidly
the local SDF values deviate from being locally planar, i.e. the curvature in all directions.
In areas where the 0-level isosurface is approximately flat, ∇2dθ ≈0. Usually, this cor-
reponds to very simple areas of the shape, which can be adequately described using few
vertices.
In contrast, regions of high curvature or intricate geometric detail tend to exhibit rapid
variations in the SDF, which manifest as large positive or negative Laplacian values. In-
tuitively
• Sharp convex features: ∇2dθ is positive, because the scalar field is expanding out-
wards.
• Sharp concave features: ∇2dθ is negative, because the scalar field is contracting
inwards.
Thus, by prioritising points with high |∇2dθ| (both convex and concave), we can adap-
tively allocate sampling density to geometrically complex regions where uniform sam-
pling would otherwise undersample, leading to loss of detail in the reconstructed surface.
Smooth, nearly planar regions naturally receive fewer additional points, ensuring eﬀicient
use of the sampling budget.

Surface Reconstruction Using Rotation Systems
4.6.1
Theory
Surface Reconstruction Using Rotation Systems (Cui et al., [3]) (RsR) is a graph-based,
combinatorial method designed to reconstruct watertight surfaces from point clouds, with
a particular strength in preserving sharp features. Unlike volumetric methods such as March-
ing Cubes, which approximate the zero-level isosurface of a scalar field on a voxel grid,
RsR operates directly on the input points as vertices of the output mesh.
Volumetric methods are typically very robust and excel at closing holes, but they tend to
introduce excessive smoothing and often fail to preserve fine, sharp features. In contrast,
RsR is designed to preserve such details and offers significant control through its parame-
ters, making it particularly suitable for high-quality reconstructions of objects with sharp
edges.
The main idea of RsR is to first construct a minimum spanning tree (MST) from the input
points, and then iteratively add edges to form a mesh while explicitly maintaining genus 0
topology. This is ensured through two checks performed for each candidate edge, namely
the topology test and the geometry test, described in the following section.
18
Surface Reconstruction Using Rotation Systems
==PAGE:27==
The method represents the reconstructed surface as a rotation system (RS), a combinato-
rial structure that assigns a cyclic ordering to the edges incident to each vertex. Initially,
the MST corresponds to a trivial RS with a single face. As edges are added, the RS is
updated to represent additional faces while respecting the fixed circular ordering (CO),
which encodes the true geometric ordering of edges around each vertex.
Optionally, RsR can introduce handles to reconstruct higher-genus surfaces, but when
setting the argument genus = 0, only genus-0 surfaces are allowed, making the method
particularly well-suited for watertight objects.
4.6.2
Algorithm Description
The algorithm begins by constructing a proximity graph Γ = (V, E) from the input point
cloud P = {pi} using k-nearest neighbours (kNN), and the three user defined parame-
ters, r, k, and θ. k simply defines the amount of neighbours used for each vertex in the
creation of Γ, while r is a pruning of edges r times the average edge length of Γ. θ ad-
ditionally prunes edges if the associated normals of the endpoints, nu and nv, if they are
angled significantly differently, i.e. if nu · nv < cos θ.
Furthermore, if the points are assumed to be noise-free, they are used directly as vertices of
Γ, whereas for noisy inputs, RsR can optionally project points along their corresponding
normals N = {ni} onto a locally estimated surface to reduce noise. In this work, the
points are assumed to be noise-free, as only the converged sample points are returned, as
mentioned in section 4.5.
Now, for each connected component in Γ, a minimum spanning tree (MST) is computed.
While Γ may contain multiple connected components in the general case, our data typi-
cally produces a single dominant component representing the full watertight surface. A
crucial step in RsR is establishing a rotation system (RS) of incident edges around each
vertex, which defines the ordering around the vertex, so that the faces of the mesh can be
traversed. The initial RS, called a cyclic ordering, is constructed using the input normals, N,
and is trivial, as the MST consists of just a single face, which can be traversed by following
the half-edges around from an arbitrary point in the tree.
The remaining edges in Γ are processed iteratively, sorted in ascending order of length
(i.e., shortest edges first). For each candidate edge, two tests are performed before inser-
tion:
1. Topology test: Ensures that adding the candidate edge preserves the genus, g, of
the current reconstruction. For a candidate edge {u, v}, this is achieved by verifying
that:
(a) both endpoint vertices u and v belong to the same existing face, f, in the current
RS, and
(b) the two corners that would be split by the new edge also belong to f.
These two conditions guarantee that the new edge will split one face into two, in-
creasing both |E| and |F| by one while leaving the Euler characteristic 2(1 −g) =
|V | −|E| + |F| unchanged. If either of these conditions fails, adding the edge could
merge two distinct faces or introduce a handle, violating the genus-0 constraint.
2. Geometry test: Ensures geometric consistency by preventing edges that would lead
to local self-intersections or incorrect face adjacencies. For a candidate edge {u, v},
a local neighbourhood is defined as a ball centred at the midpoint of the candidate

6
Discussion
This chapter critically evaluates the results presented in chapter 5, with particular focus on
whether the objectives of this project were met. We then discuss the observed outcomes
in more detail, guided by both quantitative and qualitative assessments, and reflect on
the strengths and limitations of the methods used. Where relevant, we suggest possible
extensions and improvements to the current, leading up to the proposal for future work
on the method and solution to the main area of this thesis – quantifying complexity.
6.1
Achievement of Problem Statement
The primary goal of this project was to develop an adaptive point sampling strategy for
signed distance functions (SDFs), with emphasis on capturing intricate geometric features
through curvature-based sampling. This goal was largely met. The proposed sampling
algorithm, ASAP, successfully distributed sample points non-uniformly over a surface,
favouring geometrically complex regions as indicated by the Laplacian of the predicted
SDF. The reconstructions generated from these adaptively sampled points yielded high-
quality, watertight meshes, demonstrating that the method met its design objectives in
practice for the given problem and mesh complexity.
This is particularly evident in the results presented in figures 5.7, 5.9, 5.11, and 5.17, where
the sampling densities clearly concentrate around features of high curvature. Specific ex-
amples include the antlers of the moose (figure 5.13b), the stinger and segmented legs
of the scorpion (figure 5.14b), and the duck’s wings and feet (figure 5.15b). These high-
detail areas were appropriately favoured by ASAP’s curvature-aware sampling scheme,
validating the design of the sigmoid-mapped Laplacians shown in figure 5.9. Moreover,
the RsR reconstructions derived from these samples were nearly watertight, with a num-
ber of boundary edges comparable to the original RsR results. For instance, while ASAP
meshes contained hundreds of thousands of faces, the boundary edge count remained a
fairly modest |Eb| ≤322 (see table 5.1).
Even the self-occlusion heuristic, which aimed to ensure sampling completeness in poten-
tially undersampled regions, which could break RsR, performed admirably. As shown in
figure 5.10, the additional occluded points were well-distributed across structurally im-
portant but partially hidden areas, such as the scorpion’s claws and legs, the duck’s feet
and under its wings, and the moose’s antlers. When the heuristic failed to precisely isolate
occluded regions, the fallback behaviour still proved effective: the occluded points typi-
cally coincided with complex regions, thereby supporting the primary goal of maximising
reconstruction fidelity in challenging surface areas.
6.1.1
Comparison with baselines
The grid-based sampling baseline serves more as a lower bound than a competitive method.
As shown in figure 5.12, the reconstructions obtained from uniform grid sampling were
notably poor: sparse, fragmented, and lacking surface detail. The Chamfer distances in
table 5.1 confirm this, with values exceeding 1.0 for the moose and 0.4 for the duck. Ad-
ditionally, the voxel grid resolution required for acceptable coverage (ν > 120) was ex-
tremely memory intensive – so much so that it led to multiple kernel crashes on CPU1.
1In practical usage, uniform grid sampling with large grid resolutions is only viable on high-memory

Finally, the low number of sampled points meant that RsR reconstructions were fast, but
of little practical value due to their lack of geometric fidelity.
More illuminating is the comparison between ASAP and the classical Marching Cubes
(MC) algorithm. MC is known for its robustness and simplicity, and indeed it performed
reasonably well in terms of runtime (total time: 28.21 s), compared to ASAP (33.58 s).
However, ASAP reconstructed meshes with significantly lower Chamfer distances across
all shapes, demonstrating its superior fidelity to the ground truth geometry. Notably, the
ASAP reconstructions featured nearly twice the number of vertices and faces as MC (ta-
ble 5.1), yet did so within a similar runtime. This highlights the eﬀiciency of the sampling
and reconstruction pipeline as a whole.
Qualitative inspection further reinforces ASAP’s advantage: Figures 5.13 to 5.15 demon-
strate how RsR reconstructions from ASAP-sampled points produce smoother, more co-
herent surfaces. In contrast, MC results appear fragmented, with “island” artifacts (es-
pecially visible in the moose antlers), jagged surfaces (see duck wings), and significant
mesh noise from the discretised voxel grid. These issues are reflected in the Euler char-
acteristics reported in table 5.1, where MC exhibits non-zero χ values and even negative
ones, suggesting disconnected components or non-manifold edges. While some of these
shortcomings could be alleviated with post-processing (e.g. bilateral smoothing [10] and
hole-closing), ASAP offers high-quality reconstructions directly from point clouds, by-
passing the need for mesh denoising while barely needing hole-closing.
The only exception to ASAP’s dominance occurs in the reconstruction of the scorpion’s
claw (figure 5.14). Here, MC appears to better capture the local geometry of the pincers.
This could be attributed to stronger DeepSDF performance in this region, enabling MC
to perform well with uniform sampling, or to limitations in curvature-based sampling in
areas where curvature is not aligned with visually prominent geometry.
Insights from DeepSDF training and inference
The success of ASAP is tightly coupled to the performance of the underlying DeepSDF
model. As seen in the SDF slice visualisations in figures 5.2 to 5.4, the learned signed
distance functions appear continuous, smooth, and define a clear, closed zero-level iso-
surface for each shape. These results confirm the model’s ability to generalise across com-
plex geometries, with the isosurfaces remaining watertight and well-defined. The use of
a higher resolution ν = 120 for visualisation purposes (as opposed to ν = 25 or 30 during
sampling) further underscores the quality of the learned function.
However, some aspects of training required careful tuning. As shown in figure 5.1, a
marked increase in loss variance occurs after reducing the truncation parameter δ from
0.1 to 0.02 and changing the triangle sampling CDF to follow √area instead of a uniform
area distribution. While this jittery behaviour reflects higher gradient sensitivity, it was
ultimately beneficial. As shown in qualitative comparisons (e.g. figure 5.13b vs. ear-
lier versions in the appendix), the revised model captures finer detail: more pronounced
antlers on the moose, a sharper stinger for the scorpion, and improved definition of duck
wings, including visible separation.
These improvements are directly attributable to the refined sampling and loss configura-
tions. The decision to restart training from an earlier checkpoint (prior to overfitting and
convergence) was also pivotal. As evidenced by continued training to 200,000 epochs, the
model eventually began ”cutting corners” – literally. In later stages, it trimmed features
such as the moose’s antlers in an apparent effort to minimise global loss, at the cost of
34
Surface Reconstruction Using Rotation Systems
==PAGE:43==
geometric fidelity. By reverting and retraining with a tighter δ and improved sampling
focus, the model retained crucial details while avoiding excessive smoothing.
This behaviour highlights a known limitation of DeepSDF: while powerful, the network is
not suﬀiciently plastic to model highly concave or spiky features unless explicitly steered
to do so. As seen in figure 5.17, some expected high-curvature regions – e.g. the top part
of the antlers – were undersampled, despite their apparent complexity. This can be traced
back to the only_converged filtering step in algorithm 23, which discards sample points
where |dθ(p)| > ε. In other words, if the DeepSDF output is too uncertain, the projected
point is excluded from Pfinal.
This effect is quantifiable. For instance, only 86.32% of Pinit converged for the moose, com-
pared to 98.44% and 97.02% for the scorpion and duck, respectively. Since the moose’s
antlers are both highly complex and poorly represented in the training data (see fig-
ure 4.2), this explains their inconsistent sampling and the jagged MC reconstructions in
figure 5.13a. Interestingly, it may also help explain why MC slightly outperformed ASAP
on the scorpion claw, where DeepSDF’s predictions were likely more reliable across the
sampled region.
6.1.2
Limitations of Laplacian curvature sampling
While Laplacian curvature was an effective proxy for surface complexity in this work,
its use revealed limitations in some scenarios. For example, as highlighted in figure 5.17,
ASAP tends to oversample the outermost regions of narrow, capsule-like structures – such
as the ends of the scorpion’s legs and the moose’s dewlap2. This phenomenon stems from
two issues:
Curvature bias: The Laplacian norm increases where the SDF’s gradient vector field curves
in multiple directions, which is more pronounced at the tips of narrow structures than in
their midsections.
Projection bias: Disks sampled just outside the surface are more likely to converge to
nearby endpoints when projected, as these are closer to the initial sample locations.
The second effect could be mitigated by several alternatives: reducing the disk radius
rdisk (with a possible trade-off in coverage), switching to spherical sampling (which for-
feits normal-based sampling eﬀiciency), or using a cone-shaped sampling region aligned
with surface normals. This latter idea – sampling inside a cone of revolution (i.e. the
shape formed by rotating a right-angled triangle around one of its legs) – offers a promis-
ing compromise. It maintains alignment with Nproj while introducing a tunable angular
spread (ϕ) and length. This idea is visualised in figure 6.1, and its implementation would
allow samples to be both spatially focused and surface-aligned. Mitigating the first issue
– the limitations of Laplacian-based curvature – requires a deeper reconsideration of how
curvature is estimated. Another fairly simply alternative is the angular deviation:

worked well in this setting, the use of angular deviation or even more sophisticated mea-

ticularly for datasets where sharp edges play a more dominant role than rounded features.
A final notable point about the design of ASAP is its generality. As mentioned, it could
support different curvature estimators, but its structure is also modular in terms of the
projection method, sampling distribution, and even SDF model. While this implementa-
tion used DeepSDF for shape representation, future instantiations of ASAP could easily
swap this out for a different continuous representation – such as NeRFs, 3D Gaussian
splats, or other neural implicit surfaces. In that sense, the method proposed here is better
seen as a framework for guided sampling on SDFs, rather than a monolithic algorithm.
This modularity is not just convenient – it is a strength, enabling future work to explore
richer curvature proxies, smarter reprojection heuristics, or more expressive SDF models
within the same overarching architecture.
Still, despite its generality and flexibility, several limitations must be acknowledged. One
of the most significant is the lack of an ablation study to evaluate the effect of different
components of ASAP – for example, the contribution of occlusion handling, the role of

the sigmoid mapping, or the sensitivity to the number of samples in the disk. While some
visual comparisons suggest their utility (e.g., figures 5.9 and 5.11), a systematic study
would strengthen the claims.
Another limitation lies in the choice of baselines. While Marching Cubes provides a well-
understood, classical baseline – and one that performs reasonably well given the compu-
tational budget – it is arguably outdated. Poisson Surface Reconstruction (Kazhdan et
al., [kazhdan2006poisson]) or more recent deep learning-based reconstruction methods
could have offered a stronger comparison point. However, incorporating such methods
was ultimately sacrificed in favour of a deeper qualitative inspection of the RsR meshes,
which aligned more directly with the goals of the project.
Moreover, while RsR was a fitting choice for surface reconstruction – with its ability to
resolve sharp features and cope with varying density – it is not without shortcomings. Its
reliance on local triangulation and the assumption of consistent normals means it can still
fail in under-sampled or ambiguous regions. That said, the RsR reconstructions here were
overall very promising, and the outputs of ASAP matched the algorithm’s assumptions
fairly well.
From a reproducibility perspective, the flexibility of ASAP can also be a weakness. With
many hyperparameters – such as disk radius rdisk, projection convergence thresholds, sig-
moid steepness, and occlusion angle cutoffs – the performance of the method can vary
significantly depending on these choices. While this flexibility enables powerful adap-
tation to different settings, it also raises the barrier for replication and tuning. A future
version of ASAP could benefit from more principled defaults or adaptive parameter se-
lection strategies.
Perhaps the most significant bottleneck of this pipeline, however, lies in the accuracy of
the SDF representation itself. While DeepSDF provided a suﬀiciently faithful model for
the scope of this project, the quality of the resulting mesh reconstructions – and indeed the
sampling densities – are heavily dependent on the accuracy of the learned signed distance
values. In regions where DeepSDF fails to model fine features (e.g., antler tips or claw
gaps), projection and curvature estimation both suffer. This is ultimately a constraint of
the model, not the sampling strategy. As discussed next, improving the SDF would open
the door to significantly better performance.
6.2
Future Work
As outlined above, several aspects of this project present compelling opportunities for fu-
ture research. One of the most obvious extensions is the integration of a more accurate
or expressive signed distance function. While DeepSDF was seminal at its time, mod-
ern alternatives – such as NeRFs (Mildenhall et al., 2021 [14]), Diffusion models, or 3D
Gaussian Splattings (Kerbl et al., 2023 [15]) – may provide significantly better fidelity to
complex surfaces. By leveraging a stronger SDF backbone, ASAP could operate on a more
reliable field, especially in high-frequency or occluded regions.
An intriguing possibility would be to extract second-order information – like Laplacians
– directly from the neural SDF model, rather than estimating them via finite differences
on point samples. Much like how surface normals can be computed from gradients using
∇dθ(p),
backpropagation could, in principle, be extended to yield the Laplacian by computing the
divergence of the gradient field:
∆dθ(p) = ∇· ∇dθ(p).
Surface Reconstruction Using Rotation Systems
37
==PAGE:46==
This would allow for an entirely differentiable pipeline for curvature-aware sampling,
potentially improving runtime and precision in complex regions. It would also remove
the dependency on KNN_SMOOTH, simplifying the pipeline.
Another promising extension is to shift focus from SDFs to directly learning the point
cloud distribution. Approaches such as PointFlow [pointflow] or more recent genera-
tive models could potentially be adapted to include an ASAP-like sampling procedure
within the generative loop. Although merging these paradigms is non-trivial, the idea of
combining learned geometry and learned sampling strategies is appealing, especially if
trained end-to-end.
The sampling strategy itself also has room for innovation. The current use of disk sam-
pling – while intuitive and effective – is somewhat rigid. Exploring alternate support
shapes for sampling (as hinted at in section 5.2) like cones of revolution, defined as the
surface obtained by rotating a right-angled triangle around one of its legs, may provide
improved coverage in regions where the surface geometry naturally follows a directional
curvature (e.g., along narrow limbs or tapering features).
In practice, such cone sampling could be implemented by tilting the normal vector n at a
small angle ϕ, generating rotated samples along a swept surface whose radial component
is scaled by a second parameter rcone. The control over the angular spread and sampling
depth may allow for better exploitation of high-curvature areas while still preserving pro-
jection quality.
Additionally, rethinking the disk radius rdisk to be adaptive – perhaps based on local
curvature or proximity to boundaries – could further enhance eﬀiciency. This would al-
low ASAP to place more points in areas that “need” them while avoiding oversampling
smooth or uninformative regions.
Another key direction is the refinement of curvature metrics. While this project focused
on Laplacians, we already discussed angular deviation as a viable alternative – especially
useful in areas where Laplacians may be noisy or ambiguous, such as corners or edges of
polyhedral shapes. Extending this even further, one could envision combining multiple
metrics into a composite measure, possibly using a learned function to assign sampling
density based on a feature vector of local geometric descriptors.
Finally, a particularly exciting direction lies in the formalisation of complexity. While this
project uses curvature as a proxy for local complexity, the broader question of what it
means to fully explore an isosurface remains open. These are both very challenging prob-
lem statements. Defining and quantifying complexity could be approached through in-
formation theory, topological analysis (e.g., mesh saliency or persistence homology), or
even visual attention models – any of which might provide a better theoretical grounding
for adaptive sampling. Separately, measuring the completeness of a sampling strategy
might draw inspiration from coverage theory, entropy models, or topological descriptors
that reflect both quantity and diversity of sampled surface regions.
Combining all of these directions, a long-term goal might be a general-purpose, modular
adaptive sampling framework that leverages a modern SDF backbone, flexible curvature
measures, and a learned projection strategy – all under a unified architecture. The present
work lays a foundation toward this vision, offering a practical demonstration of how even
simple curvature measures and heuristics, when combined thoughtfully, can yield signif-
icant improvements in surface reconstruction.

Conclusion
This thesis introduced ASAP, an adaptive sampling strategy for signed distance functions
(SDFs) designed to prioritise geometrically complex surface regions. By leveraging cur-
vature using Laplacians of a learned SDF model (DeepSDF) and projecting samples onto
the surface through disk sampling, the method successfully concentrated points in high-
detail areas while preserving overall surface coverage.
Through evaluation on three diverse meshes, ASAP was shown to outperform the clas-
sical method Marching Cubes in terms of both quantitative metrics (e.g., Chamfer dis-
tance, Euler characteristic) and qualitative surface reconstruction. The integration of a
self-occlusion heuristic further improved sampling completeness, particularly in partially
hidden or concave regions.
Key to ASAP’s effectiveness was its tight integration with DeepSDF, from which smooth
and watertight reconstructions were generated via Robust Surface Reconstruction (RsR).
Despite some limitations – including the dependency on model accuracy, and the use of
fixed heuristics – the method proved modular and adaptable, laying the groundwork for
future extensions.
Ultimately, ASAP demonstrates that a relatively simple curvature-guided approach can
yield high-quality surface reconstructions from sparse SDF samples. The results highlight
the importance of smart sampling in neural implicit representations and open new av-
enues for future work in curvature estimation, adaptive strategies, and complexity-aware
geometry processing.
