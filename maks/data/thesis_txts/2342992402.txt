Summary 
Secondary metabolites from plants and microorganisms have been used as drugs 
since ancient times and nowadays, they are the center of attention of many 
chemists and molecular biologists, as they represent one of the most promising 
research areas for drug discovery. With exponentially growing sequenced 
genomes, the fungal kingdom is still a valuable target for secondary metabolite 
pathway studies. As a result, the gene cluster origins and the biosynthetic 
pathways of several accessible SMs have been elucidated. However, it seems that 
there are many SMs produced by multi-domain enzyme synthases and the 
tailoring enzymes of gene clusters that are cryptic or barely expressed. Revealing 
the silent pathways could lead to the discovery of many cryptic and possibly 
bioactive compounds with potential medical features. 
The present project aims to investigate the potential of the ubiquitous products 
of secondary metabolism, with focus on polyketides, in Aspergillus nidulans, a 
sequenced and well-studied model organism. More specifically, this MSc thesis 
explores the silent orsellinaldehyde biosynthetic pathway, which is thoroughly 
presented, as a primary goal to characterize the putative polyketidic compound 
that is presumed to interfere with this pathway and synthesize nidubenzals A 
and B, two novel compounds that were recently discovered.  
In order to reveal the orsellinaldehyde pathway, the cclA gene was deleted, a 
gene responsible for the histone methylation and thus, the chromosome DNA 
coiling. Furthermore, the orsellinaldehyde polyketide synthase gene pkfA was 
both deleted and overexpressed in an effort to enhance the putative nidubenzal 
production. The chemistries of all mutants (cclAΔ, cclAΔpkfAΔ and cclAΔOexpkfA) 
and wildtype (NID3) were analyzed through RP UHPLC-DAD-HR MS. A possible 
nidubenzal producer wildtype, IBT22600, was also used, in order to obtain UV 
spectra of asperugins A and B. All the strains were cultivated at two different 
temperatures (30 and 37˚C) and extracted through a new plug extraction 
method. Three plugs were collected only from the colony rims, as there were 
almost no traces of SMs when using the traditional approach.  
The pkfA pathway was not revealed under any of the above conditions. As a next 
step, the strains were fed with putative nidubenzal precursors (3-HBA, 4-HBA 
and 3,4-DHBA) at 30˚C. The compound feeding revealed the expression of pkfA 
pathway in NID1487, but did not lead to the production of nidubenzals. 
Consequently, chemical analysis of the above strains resulted in the rejection of 
the main hypothesis of the project. However, other major conclusions on 
secondary metabolism of A. nidulans could be made. The temperature conditions 
had a strong impact on secondary metabolite production. Wildtypes NID3 and 
IBT22600, as well as the OexpkfA mutant produced more compounds at 37 ˚C,

while cclAΔ and cclAΔpkfAΔ thrived at 30 ˚C. The chemical profiles of the fed 
cultures showed a major influence on OexpkfA when fed with all three HBAs and

1. Background 
1.1 Filamentous Fungi and their importance 
The Fungal kingdom is very diverse and consists of unicellular, simple eukaryotic 
microorganisms, such as yeasts, and multicellular higher fungi, such as 
filamentous molds or mushrooms. Fungi can be found on soil, dead matter, and 
they can be symbionts, but also parasites of animals, plants or other fungi [1]. 
They are nature’s major decomposers, as they secrete various enzymes in the 
environment which breakdown polymeric molecules of organic, and mostly dead 
(saprophytes), matter (lignin, insects, fruits, etc.) in order to absorb nutrients, 
such as carbon or nitrogen, and transform them into energy, as a result of their 
heterotrophic feeding process. Most fungi grow as filaments (∼80% of fungal 
species [2]), which is a form of eukaryotic cell differentiation that allows easy 
expansion in the environment through hyphal formation. Hyphae are small 
tubular filamentous structures, which may contain multiple nuclei and extend by 
growing at their tips through or within food (Figure 1.1.1). The mycelium 
consists of a very dense mass of hyphae, used for maximizing the surface area of 
fungi, an important process for nutrient absorption. 
 
Figure 1.1.1 Aerial growth of Penicillium [3]. 
With over 1.5 million members [4], fungi are commonly known for the infectious 
diseases that are causing, together with other eukaryotic pathogenic microbes. 
Fungal diseases have been sharply increasing nowadays, due to the organism’s 
rapid fungal and genetic revolution that results in drug resistance. These 
diseases are among the most dangerous threats to humanity. Indeed, fungi can 
have detrimental consequences due to mycoses, plant diseases, and mycotoxin 
production [2], [5], [6]. 
However, until today, some fungi have been used as a food source, such as 
Boletus edulis (porcini mushrooms), in fermentation processes, such as yeasts for 
leavening effects on bread or for alcohol and carbonation in beer and as cell 
factories for the recombinant protein production. Fungi are also being used 
nowadays as valuable sources of natural products, known also as secondary 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
metabolites, which are small bioactive molecules of scientific interest due to 
their protective functions (UV radiation [7]), but most importantly due to their 
medicinal abilities towards infectious diseases, cancer and their role as a source 
of innovative drug discovery [8], [9]. Lovastatin and antibiotics such as penicillin, 
cephalosporins, and cyclosporine are some of the pharmaceuticals produced by 
SMs of fungal species. SMs have been used as medicines since ancient times [10], 
but only since the 20th century have we began identifying, characterizing and 
distributing them commercially. These metabolites are produced in response to 
the environment, allowing the microorganisms to adapt to challenging 
conditions in order to ensure their survival and reproduction.

 Aspergillus species 
The role of fungi in SM and drug discovery is of great importance. With 
exponentially growing sequenced genomes, the fungal kingdom owns one of the 
widest sampling of genomes from any eukaryotic kingdom, making them an easy 
target for biological and evolutionary studies [11]. Among the numerous 
Aspergillus species, many of them have been completely sequenced, as shown in 
Table 1.1. A. fumingatus, a toxin producer important for agriculture, A. flavus, two 
species used in industrial processes, A. niger and A. oryzae and finally, a model 
organism, A. nidulans, are some of the Aspergilli which genome has been 
encoded. The genes, chromosome sequence information and proteins, but also 
the description of the species biological role are some of the information 
available in AspGD for the above mentioned Aspergillus species [12]. The 
availability of genome sequences has contributed to the discovery of the gene 
origins of novel secondary metabolites and, as a result, to the elucidation of 
biosynthetic pathways [13]. The genome sequence of the Aspergilli species has 
offered a wealth of predictive information about natural product biosynthesis. 
For example, asperfuranone, a suppressor of proliferative activity of human lung 
cancer cells, was discovered in A. nidulans by Chiang and co-workers by 
examining the fungal sequenced genome [14].

1.1.1.1 Aspergillus nidulans  
Aspergillus nidulans has been used for classic genetic studies since the 1950s by 
Guido Pontecorvo [27] and for cell biology and secondary metabolism since 1980 
[28], [29], establishing its’ role as a model organism. Finally, its’ genome 
sequence has been published at 2005 by Galagan [20], making it a great target 
for genetic engineering studies until today, such as genome mining and deletion 
studies [30]–[32]. 
Furthermore, A. nidulans is cultivated easily and inexpensively. It is a member of 
the filamentous fungi phylum of Ascomycota, like all Aspergilli, but it is one of the 
species that can reproduce both sexually and asexually. It is able to create sexual 
spores, called conidia (Figure 1.1.3), through a process called meiosis (from the 
Greek word μείωση which means reduction), which allows the cell to divide 
through reduction of the chromosome number by half, after the chromosomes 
duplicate and exchange information (crossover). Their rapid growth is a result of 
the asexual sporulation and it is a major advantage for laboratory experiments or 
industrial use.

Aspergillus nidulans, like all fungi, produces a wide range of enzymes and 
bioactive secondary metabolites with possible antimicrobial, insecticidal, 
cytotoxic, and anticancer activities.

1.2 Biosynthesis of Natural Products 
 Secondary metabolite production 
Secondary metabolic compounds are produced by certain (groups of) organisms 
as means of defense, protection, survival and reproduction, but also for reasons 
that are so far unknown. Almost 63% of the small molecule drugs that reached 
the market between 1981 and 2006 were derived by or simulated natural 
products, known as “natural product mimic” or NMs [33]. It is thus very 
important to isolate and characterize all possible metabolites, as well as to find 
the genes responsible for their biosynthesis, that are normally clustered in fungi 
[34]. Despite the accessible ones that have already been isolated, there are 
numerous others produced from gene clusters that are silent or barely 
expressed. For example, nidulans contains as many as 32 polyketide synthases 
(PKSs) of which, until recently, only nine gene clusters have been identified and 
linked to metabolites [35]. This has led to collaborations between molecular 
biologists and natural product chemists, in order to build advanced techniques 
for silent gene activation [36]. 
The main building blocks involved in the secondary metabolism (SM) are derived 
from products and intermediates of primary metabolism, which are Acetyl-
coenzyme A (Acetyl-CoA), shikimic acid, mevalonic acid, and methylerythritol 
phosphate, but also from building blocks based on amino acids. The classification 
of secondary metabolites depends on their scaffold, which could either be 
derived from polyketides (PKs), terpenoids, shikimates, non-ribosomal peptides 
(NRPs), alkaloids or a combination of the above, making a hybrid metabolite 
[37].  
 
1.2.1.1 Polyketides  
Polyketides are the most abundant secondary metabolites in microorganisms. 
They are synthesized through a Claisen condensation facilitated by the molecules 
participating, which consist of an Acetyl-CoA (electrophile) and Malonyl-CoA 
(nucleophile) molecules (Dewick, 2009; p.17-18, fig. 2.9) as shown in paragraph 
1.2.4.1.2. The driving force of the polyketide synthesis is the nucleophilic attack 
on the Acetyl-CoA carbonyl carbon with simultaneous loss of a Malonyl-CoA 
carbon dioxide CO2. The process is repeated by adding up more extender 
(Malonyl-CoA) units until the poly-β-keto chain that is produced reaches to the 
desired number of carbons. For example, orsellinaldehyde, an 8-carbon 
molecule, needs one Acetyl-CoA and three Malonyl-CoA molecules for its 
synthesis (1.2.4.1.2). 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
Next, the polyketide backbone may undergo dehydration, cyclization or 
reduction. During the synthesis of orsellinaldehyde, which is an aromatic 
polyketide, the polyketide undergoes cyclization. A multi-domain enzyme, 
polyketide synthase (PKS), stabilizes the reactive poly-β-ketoester before the 
cyclization begins by loading the molecule to the different enzymes it contains 
[38]. 
 
1.2.1.2 Polyketide synthases 
Polyketide Synthases (PKS) can be divided into types I, II, and III based on their 
catalytic process. However, fungal PKSs normally belong to the iterative type I 
PKS (iPKS). iPKSs are large proteins with multiple domains and functions, that 
repeat (iterative type) the same cycle of reactions, until the growing chain 
reaches the desired length. The domains are namely acetyl carrier protein (ACP), 
Malonyl-CoA/Acetyl-CoA-ACP-transacylase (MAT), β-ketoacyl synthase (KS), β-
ketoacyl reductase (KR), dehydratase (DH), β-enoyl reductase (ER) and 
thioesterase (TE).  
The polyketides are typically converted into enzyme-bound thioesters (Malonyl-
ACP and acyl-enzyme thioester); the reaction is catalyzed by MAT (a Malonyl-
acetyl transferase; AT can also be used as an acetyl-tranferase instead of MAT) 
(Dewick, 2009; p.40, fig. 3.2). ACP is the acyl-carrier protein subunit to which the 
growing chain is attached. It is bound to the iPKS protein, which is structurally 
identical to the mammal protein FAS (fatty acid synthase), which also contains 
an active-site cysteine residue in the KS (ketosynthase) domain (Dewick, 2009; 
p.130); [39]. Both building blocks are transferred from Coenzyme-A esters, to 
Cys thiol groups (where the Claisen condensation occurs) and finally to the ACP 
thiol groups, as shown below. The ACP residue allows different chemical 
reactions to take place without releasing any intermediates from the enzyme, by 
providing a long flexible “arm” from the phosphopantetheine group that it 
carries. The repeated use of these domains will produce a non-reduced 
polyketide with length equal to the number of iterations plus 1. For the synthesis 
of aromatic polyketides, such as orsellinaldehyde, a non-reducing iPKS (NR-
iPKS) is necessary, which is a iPKS with two extra domains, a starter unit-ACP 
transacylase (SAT) domain to specifically select the starter unit, and a product 
template (PT) domain to direct the cyclization [38].

For the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, a series of chemical reactions take 
place catalyzed by tailoring enzymes that are expressed from the 
microorganism’s genome. 
 
 Secondary metabolite gene clusters 
The fungal genome that expresses the tailoring enzymes responsible for the 
biosynthesis of SMs is separated in clusters, which makes it easier for genetic 
mining as the genes are usually physically associated with the PKS or NRPS gene 
in the cluster [35]. In A. nidulans, for instance, the biosynthesis of the non-
ribosomal peptide Emericellamide A initiates from the type I polyketide synthase 
EasB expressed by the gene AN2547.3, which belongs to the AN2547 gene cluster. 
The intermediate is then tailored by the Acyl-CoA ligase EasD (AN2549.3) and 
loaded onto the acyltransferase EasC (AN2548.3), before it is transferred to the 
NRPS EasA (AN2545.3) to become Emericellamide A [40]. All this information 
was derived through gene targeting techniques, where one by one the gene 
cluster’s genes were deleted and the chemistry of each mutant strain was 
analyzed for the function of each gene to be revealed.  Developing A. nidulans as a 
platform for pathway discovery of secondary metabolites is still promising.  
 
1.2.2.1 Genetic manipulations  
Genome mining and validation through screening of metabolites is a common 
strategy for elucidation of SM production. This approach involves gene deletions, 
point mutations and overexpression of genes that are producing putative 
tailoring enzymes that participate in biosynthetic pathways of certain 
compounds, or of regulatory ones, such as the cclA. As an example, Figure 1.2.2 
indicates the chemical differences between a wildtype strain and an AusAΔ

austinol and dehydroaustinol, compound products of the AusA pathway, in 
overlaid mode (Agilent MassHunter). It is obvious that the compounds are no 
longer produced when the AusA gene is deleted, proving that it is the polyketide 
synthase gene that initiates their biosynthesis.

1.2.2.1.1 Overexpression strategies for activation of SM gene cluster  
Overexpression of a secondary metabolite gene cluster can take place through 
genetic engineering of transcriptional factors, which either activate or repress 
the gene members. If such a transcriptional regulator is present in a cluster, it 
offers an easy way of controlling the whole gene cluster’s expression with only 
one genetic operation [35]. As an example, Chiang and co-workers, noticed two 
PKS genes located very close to each other on a chromosome, but individual gene 
deletions did not provide information about the production of any secondary 
metabolites from the PKSs. However, replacing the promoter of the putative 
transcription activator, located in vicinity to the PKSs, with an inducible 
promoter, PalcA [14] led to the discovery of asperfuranone [41]. This simple 
technique proves to be efficient for identification and activation of otherwise 
cryptic pathways. 
Overexpression of a TF or a gene, heterologous as well as endogenous, can take 
place with integration of an expression construct into the fungal genome, either 
at a specific locus or randomly. The construct, made by cloning methods, should 
contain the gene of interest flanked by a promoter and terminator, a selection 
marker and, in addition, two targeting sequences in the case of specific targeting 
via homologous recombination [42], [43].

1.2.2.1.2 Loss of cclA  
The possible production of SMs from silent gene clusters in condensed areas of 
chromosomes (e.g. near telomeres) suggests that chromatin modifications can 
lead to SM cluster regulations. Through the cclA deletion, it has been shown that 
the production of secondary metabolites in A. nidulans is increased. However, the 
growth rates are decreased and the mutant becomes sensitive [44]. The cclA 
locus is required for methylation of lysine 4 of histone 3 (H3K4) and its’ deletion 
has led to the revealing of two silent gene clusters which catalyzed the 
biosynthesis of monodictyphenone and several emodin analogs (mdpG), as well 
as F9775-A and F9775-B (orsA) [45]. Klejnstrup and co-workers have proved 
that in a strain where the cclA gene has been deleted, the production of 
asperugins and nidubenzals was activated [26]. Using these researches as a 
background, the cclA deletion was deemed necessary for the present project. 
 
1.2.2.1.3 OSMAC 
Another way of activating the production of SMs is the OSMAC (One strain many 
compounds) approach, where the growth conditions vary. Rather than using the 
standard laboratory growth conditions (optimized growth conditions), Bode and 
co-workers suggested to expose the microorganisms to various environments by 
changing the temperature, pH, media composition, light intensity, cultivation 
time, aeration and culture vessel. This approach, in combination with genetic 
manipulations, has been successfully applied in the present project by growing 
the A. nidulans strains in two different temperatures (30 and 37˚C) [46].

through genetic engineering techniques. Inglis and his team have manually 
curated 266 secondary metabolite gene clusters from the Aspergilli species 
available in AspGD, in order to facilitate the investigation of novel Aspergillus 
secondary metabolites [34]. Table 6.2, Table 6.3 and Table 6.4 of the Appendix 
include the list of secondary metabolites that were identified during the present 
project.

 Orsellinic acid& derived compounds 
Structurally, polyketides are complex molecules, such as aflatoxin B1, a very 
carcinogenic and mutagenic compound [47], [48]. Orsellinic acid, an aromatic 
polyketide produced from a NR-iPKS, is possibly the simplest non-reduced 
aromatic polyketide produced from the condensation of a starter and three 
extender units and it is the backbone for a lot of other secondary metabolites.  
 
 
Orsellinic acid structure 
 
In 2009, it was reported by Schroeckh et al. that AN7909.4 (orsA) is the 
polyketide synthase gene responsible for orsellinic acid, but also for F-9775 A 
and B synthesis [49], [50]. Orsellinic acid synthase was one of the first 
discovered fungal PKSs, isolated from Penicillium madriti and reported in 1968 
[51]. In drug discovery, such small bioactive molecules can be good candidates 
for drug-like compounds that can be easily developed into preclinical and later 
clinical products, to become marketed medicines [52]. 
The biosynthesis of orsellinic acid can lead to a range of other metabolites, 
violaceols I and II, gerfelin, lecanoric acid, F-9775 A and B, orcinol, diorcinol, C10-
deoxy gerfelin and cordyol C. The orsA pathway has been extensively studied and 
its’ compounds are quite often appearing in A. nidulans strains [26]. A short 
version of the biosynthetic pathway of most of the orsA pathway-derived 
compounds is shown in Figure 1.2.3.

1.3 Analytical Chemistry 
The methods for separation, comparison, classification and identification of 
single components in a sample is the core of Analytical Chemistry. For the 
sample analysis of the present project, a RP UHPLC-DAD-HR MS QTOF system 
was applied. Reversed-Phase Ultra-High-Performance Liquid Chromatography 
(RP UHPLC) was used for the separation of different fractions according to 
polarity, Diode-Array Detector (DAD) was applied for the identification of 
chromophores according to their unique UV spectrum and positive Electrospray 
Ionization (High Resolution) Mass Spectrometry (ESI HR MS) Quadrupole Time-
Of-Flight (QTOF) was used for the detection of the accurate mass-to-charge ratio 
(m/z) corresponding to different compounds. 
The instruments are connected in the sequence HPLC, DAD and finally MS, as the 
first two are non-destructive methods in contrast to the latter. MS ejects the 
samples onto the detector, thus they cannot be reused. Furthermore, HPLC and 
DAD are used as an important introduction for the MS analysis, as the MS 
instrument is not capable of separation. Finally, DAD and MS are serving as 
detectors of the HPLC eluent, therefor they must be placed after the latter. HPLC 
separates the different fractions according to their polarity, generating a 
fingerprint for each compound and DAD offers the unique UV spectrum of each

 Liquid Chromatography (Reversed Phase) 
Modern, instrumental methods of Analytical Chemistry use Chromatography to 
separate samples that consist of thousands of different compounds into 
individual components for further investigation and often for drug discovery. 
Chromatographic techniques are one of the most widely used methods for 
sample separation. There is a large variety of different chromatographic 
techniques used today, e.g., gas chromatography, ion-exchange chromatography, 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
liquid chromatography, affinity chromatography and thin layer chromatography, 
classified in relation to the principle behind separation of the different 
components. Depending on the sample type that is analyzed, the mobile phase 
can contain a liquid mixture (LC, Liquid Chromatography and CEC, Capillary 
Electrochromatography), gas (GC, Gas Chromatography) or a supercritical fluid 
(SFC, Supercritical-Fluid Chromatography) that will carry the analytes in a 
definite direction through the chromatography column (stationary phase). The 
samples are separated into fractions based on their relative affinity towards the 
stationary, and solubility towards the mobile phase, depending on their polarity. 
The fraction with higher affinity to the stationary phase travels slower, and the 
fraction with higher solubility to the mobile phase will dissolve and move faster 
through the column, and vis versa. 
The most commonly used type of liquid Chromatography (LC) is the High-
Performance LC (HPLC), which separates the molecules according to polarity and 
size through a multifaceted and wide-polarity separation. HPLC contains a liquid 
mobile phase (LC) and a column with granular material, which is the stationary 
phase. Reversed Phase Ultra-High Performance Liquid Chromatography (RP 
UHPLC), a method applied in the experiments of this project, forces the eluent to 
go through a tightly packed, apolar or weakly polar column, with very high 
velocity due to the high pressure applied by the pump. The separation of small 
molecules is increased with (RP) UHPLC instrumentation, by using superficially-
porous particle columns and by operating up to 8,700 psi (600 bar).  
Figure 1.3.2 briefly indicates the instrumentation of RP UHPLC. The mobile phase 
comprises of 100:1 Water (H2O): Formic Acid (FA) and 100:0.1 Acetonitrile 
(AcCN): FA. In a process called gradient elution, the solvents of the mobile phase 
will gradually elute the column from the most polar to the less. So, for example, 
at the initial stage 85% H2O:FA and 15% AcCN:FA, shifting to 100% AcCN over a 
span of 20 minutes. The column, consisting of an apolar phase, will retain the less 
polar solvent (AcCN) by creating stronger bonds and, as a result of the apolar-
apolar interactions, will be eluted last from the column [68]–[71]. 
More in depth, water will be injected in the column and will be bound first to the 
stationary phase, which is packed with large carbon molecules, such as octadecyl 
(C18) bound on small silica beads. The samples, which are dissolved in methanol, 
are also injected and since methanol is less polar than water, it will be longer 
retained in the column. Finally, acetonitrile is injected and, as the least polar one, 
it will create the strongest bonds with the non-polar stationary phase and will 
“wash” the column from all the previous solvents. This way, water will be eluted 
from the stationary phase first, as it is the smallest molecule and the one with the 
highest polarity. The fungal metabolic compounds diluted in methanol will elute 
in between (middle polarity) and the acetonitrile will elute last. The 
chromatogram indicates this information as shown in Figure 1.3.3 [68]–[70].

 Spectroscopy 
Spectroscopy is defined as the interaction between electromagnetic radiation 
and matter. The majority of human knowledge about the structural properties of 
atoms and molecules is due to spectroscopic studies. The interaction of matter 
and electromagnetic spectrum of a certain region offers different information 
about the sample. Nowadays, the most popular spectroscopic techniques are 
Infrared Spectroscopy, Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (NMR) and 
Ultraviolet-Visible Spectroscopy (UV-Vis). There are several different 
instrumentations used for each technique, according to the demands of each 
experiment. The UV-Vis Spectroscopy is the one used during this project as one 
of the methods for LC detection and, in this case, a Diode-Array Detector (DAD) 
was applied as a method for revealing the chemistry of the fungal metabolic 
compounds according to their conjugated double-bond systems [73], [74]. 
 
1.3.2.1 Diode-Array Detector (DAD) 
The aim of the Diode-Array Detection is to characterize the electromagnetic 
radiation-sample interactions through measuring the emitted wavelength over 
small photosensitive diodes with equal width, and reveal the functional groups 
or conjugated chromophores that each sample’s compound contains.  
A Diode-Array Spectrophotometer records a very broad spectrum at once, and it 
consists of several sensitive equipment that contribute to the generation of a UV 
fingerprint. It is capable of displaying 3-D chromatograms, which consist of time, 
response and a wavelength axis. The third axis provides a way of confirmation or 
rejection of the identity of an unknown compound peak. 
The wavelength absorbed from the eluted sample depends on the chemistry of 
each component. Chromophores are referred to certain structures that are 
responsible for the color that arises when a 
molecule absorbs certain wavelengths of visible (Vis) or ultraviolet light 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
(UV) and transmits others. A conjugated pi-bond chromophore molecule consists 
of alternating sing and double bonds, mostly in aromatic rings, which provide 
stability to the molecule. The longer the conjugated molecule is, the longer the 
wavelength it absorbs. The maximum absorption wavelengths of a molecule’s 
DAD chromatogram are used in order to confirm or reject the initial hypothesis 
about the molecule’s structure. UV spectra could be found in databases, such as 
ACD/Antibase version 2012 (Wiley-VCH, Weinheim, Germany), which contains 
41,000 recorded compounds. 
Figure 1.3.4 describes the main instrumentation of a DAD. Normally, a DAD 
contains a Tungsten lamp and a Deuterium lamp as light sources. The first 
produces visible light (Vis), emitting in a wavelength between 350 nm < λ < 2000 
nm and the latter produces ultraviolet light (UV), emitting in a wavelength 
between 180 nm < λ < 350 nm. After the emitting light, there is normally a 
focusing lens placed before the flow cell, which narrows down the wavelength up 
to ≈700 nm. Then, the light emits through the containing sample flow cell (a 
fused silica capillary column that allows UV/Vis light to interact with the analyte 
stream) and the sample absorbs light depending on the chemistry of its 
components. The light that is transmitted from the flow cell is directed onto the 
polychromator and then dispersed into its component’s wavelength and through 
a holographic diffraction grating, directed onto the Diode-Array Detector, where 
a different wavelength will strike each diode. Through DAD, information is 
translated and stored. 
 
Figure 1.3.4 A simple depiction of the main instrumentation that a Diode Array Detector consist of.  
So far, the complexity of the sample, the quality of separation and the sensitivity 
of the Diode-Array Detector combined with experimental skills are very 
important factors for a successful or fruitless experiment. However, 
spectroscopy is not a method that generates results, but a rather theoretical 
approach of science. Spectrometry is the method used to acquire a quantitative 
measurement of the spectrum. A UV spectrum could help us confirm or reject a 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
hypothesis about a compound’s structure, only if the compound has previously 
been identified and the UV spectrum is already available in a database. If the 
compound of interest is unknown, its elucidation is very time consuming. 
Furthermore, if two compounds are similar in structure and have the same 
number of conjugated double bonds and functional groups, but differentiate in, 
for example, a methyl group, or any group that does not absorb UV or Vis light, 
the UV spectra would be impossible to distinguish. Thus, other detection 
methods are necessary, such as the combination of DAD and MS which took place 
in this project and is recognized to be a powerful tool in classification and 
identification of compounds [75], [76]. 
 
 Spectrometry  
Spectrometry could be defined as an analytical technique for observation and 
measurement of the interaction between electromagnetic radiation and matter. 
Thus, a spectrometer is sequential to a spectroscopic instrument and could refer 
to Ion-mobility, Mass, Rutherford backscattering, Neutron triple-axis and Optical 
spectrometer. In this case, a Mass Spectrometer (MS) was applied, in order to 
measure the amount and type of chemicals present in the fungal samples. More 
accurately, MS measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of the separated and 
charged different metabolic compounds of the fungal extracts. The 
instrumentation that followed the DAD, was an Electrospray Ionization High-
Resolution MS Quadrupole-Time-Of-Flight detector (ESI HR MS QTOF), which 
provides a highly accurate isotopic pattern and a very high possibility for 
identification of the elemental compositions, for compounds below 400-600 Da 
[77]. 
 
 
 
1.3.3.1 Electrospray Ionization 
Electrospray Ionization is a soft ionization technique for molecular masses 
higher than 300 Da. The technique was first reported in 1984 by Dr. John Fenn 
and Masamichi Yamashita, who were rewarded with the Nobel Prize in 
Chemistry in 2002 for their attribution to the analysis of biological 
macromolecules [78], [79].  
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
The main role of ESI is to produce ions from macromolecules without being 
necessarily fragmented, as they would normally do. ESI instruments apply a high 
voltage to the liquid sample that continuously flows through a capillary tube 
ending in a thin needle, in order to release it as a fine spray of charged droplets 
of the same polarity as the voltage. The droplets are pushed into the evaporation 
chamber where, through a process called nebulization, a nitrogen (N2(g)) flow in 
the pump gradually dries out the solvent from the droplets and the sample is 
divided into single charged molecules. The ions are later sampled by a skimmer 
cone to accelerate towards the mass analyzer. In the present project, a positive 
voltage was applied to the capillary (+ESI). The presence of an acidic buffer 
enhances the generation of proton ions and the presence of elements in the 
sample, such as sodium and potassium, is necessary for the generation of the 
corresponding ions [M+Na]+, [M+K]+, etc. [80].  Figure 1.3.5 shows a simplified 2-
D scheme of a charged droplet creation through a positive ionization source. 
 
Figure 1.3.5 A simple depiction of a positive Electrospray Ionization source (+ESI). 
Fragmentation and adduct formation are highly dependent on the ESI settings, as 
a high potential difference between the skimmers offers a better ion 
transmission, fragmentation and higher abundance of [M+Na]+ and [M+K]+ ions, 
which are more stable and harder to be fragmented compared to [M+H]+ [76]. 
For each compound, the known or suspected major adducts are registered as: 
[M+H]+ , [M+Na]+, [M+NH4]+ , [M+K]+ , [M+H+CH3CN]+ , [M+Na+CH3CN]+ , 
[M+H−H2O]+ , [M+ H−2H2O]+ , [M+H−H2]+ (sterols), [M+H−HCOOH]+ , [M+ 
H−CH3COOH]+ , [M+2H]2+, [M+Na+H]2+ or [M+2Na]2+, but the most frequent 
adducts identified are [M+Na]+ and [M+K]+ ions as well as reveal losses of H2O, 
CH3COOH, HCOOH, and CO2 [71].

1.3.3.2 HR MS QTOF 
The main principle of ion separation in Mass Spectrometry (MS) is the affect that 
the mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio of each ion has on their movement, when 
travelling through a magnetic or electrical field. 
High-Resolution MS Quadrupole-Time-Of-Flight detector (HR MS QTOF) is a 
powerful tool for accurate metabolite detection. High-Resolution MS offers a 
better peak separation and, as a result, easier-to-read data. 
A Quadrupole is the most commonly used analyzer in MS instrumentation in 
clinical laboratory. In a Q system, four metal rods are strictly parallel and at 
equal distance. Each opposite pair of rods is connected electrically, and the two 
diagonally opposite pairs have equal, but counter voltages. The field that is 
created between the four rods causes the positive ions, generated from +ESI, to 
travel forward with an oscillatory motion. This motion is related to the m/z ratio 
and can be controlled by changing the voltages simultaneously, so that the ions 
of interest travel through the quadrupole without hitting any rod and on the 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
other hand, the undesirable ions hit the metals, get neutralized and thus, do not 
reach the detector. A simple quadrupole analyzer is shown in Figure 1.3.6 [67], 
[80]. 
 
Figure 1.3.6 Quadrupole instrument. The ions enter from the source, then they go through the 
focusing lenses and the quadrupole cylindrical rods for separation. The rods are circular, but ideally 
they should be hyperbolic. The idea of the image was based on [67], but it was modified.  
Next, typically follows a second Quadrupole, the collision cell, responsible for the 
ion fragmentation (addition of sodium, ammonium, potassium, loss of water, 
etc.), which will be later useful for adduct identification [80], [81]. 
The separated and fragmented ions enter the Time-Of-Flight analyzer. The 
already filtered ions that manage to get through the Quadrupoles pass through 
focusing and acceleration lenses. The pulser generates a high electric potential 
that transfers kinetic energy to the ions, which then travel through the TOF tube 
and are reflected back to the detector by the reflectron, as shown in Figure 1.3.7. 
The ions are separated based on their m/z at the quadrupole, whereas the 
detector is able to measure the m/z ratio of each ion based on the time it takes 
for each one to fly through the tube [80], [81].

 Data analysis and dereplication  
Dereplication takes place as a rapid method of recognizing and eliminating 
natural products that have previously been identified and it is a crucial method 
for fast discovery of novel drugs and natural products in general. Nowadays, it is 
mostly performed by LC-MS data analysis of extracts combined with searching 
for specific ions with a defined monoisotopic mass into databases. In this case, 
the most suitable library for microbial compounds is Antibase (Wiley-VCH, 
Weinheim, Germany), version 2012, which contains 41,000 recorded 
compounds, usually containing their structure, formula and other useful 
information (UV spectrum, RT, etc.). Antibase contains data for secondary 
metabolites from microorganisms and higher fungi, including yeasts, 
ascomycetes, basidiomycetes, lichens, algae, and cyanobacteria. The high 
accuracy of mass measurements that are obtained by LC-MS reduce the number 
of possible elemental compositions, and, together with a very limited mass 
tolerance of ±0.005 Da, the fewest possible candidates are given. In 2006, it has 
been shown that compounds within a 0.5−5 ppm mass range have very different 
compositions and that a useful way to narrow down the possible elemental 
composition candidates is to filter the isotopic masses [76], [77], [82]. 
Figure 1.3.8 shows the steps we used for data analysis and dereplication, using 
the example of Violaceols I and II, two compounds that are frequently appearing 
in the Aspergillus nidulans strains. First, looking at the Base Peak Chromatogram 
of the NID351 extract (30˚C), we notice two peaks that have the same mass 
spectrum, but different retention times, a case which is a result of an identical 
formula, but different structures. Searching for fragments, it is easy to identify 
the [M+H]+=263.0912, [M+Na]+ and [M+K]+. With a mass tolerance 0.05 Da, 
Antibase gives 90 hits for this monoisotopic mass. However, if we decrease the 
tolerance to 0.005 Da, the candidates are down to 17, from which 14 are derived 
from fungi (SOURCE [F]). The formula given in all cases is C14H14O5, which agrees 
with the Isotopic Distribution Calculator’s Isotopic pattern and the one derived 
from the experimental data. Extracting a DAD chromatogram will allow us to 
eliminate some of the molecules or even conclude to the identity of the natural 
product, if there is a UV spectrum given in Antibase between the hits, that is 
identical with the experimental one. Indeed, the DAD indicates the appearance of 
two bioactive molecules with identical UV spectra, which are also identical to the 
database one. As a conclusion, the peaks correspond to the compounds 
Violaceols I and II.

2.2.1 Reagents 
A UHPLC chromatographic column UHPLC Agilent 1290 Infinity LC System 
(Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, US) instrumentation was connected to an 
Agilent 1290 Infinity Diode Array Detector (DAD) which has a wavelength range 
between 190 and 640 nm and very high accuracy. Next followed the instrument 
of MS-QTOF Agilent 6545 with a Jet Stream Ionization system attached initially, 
for positive or negative ESI of the compound stream travelling through the DAD 
to the MS.  
Compounds were separated on a Poroshell 120 phenyl-hexyl column (Agilent, 
250 mm × 2.1 mm, 2.7 μm) at 60 °C with a water-acetonitrile (AcCN) gradient 
(both buffered with 20 mM formic acid (FA)) running from 10-100% AcCN over 
20 min followed by a 4 min wash (100% AcCN). The gradient was then returned 
to 10% AcCN for a total gradient time of 26 min. Formic acid was added to attain 
a low pH for better compound separation and reliable results. The data were 
recorded in positive ionization (ESI) mode during the present project and were 
acquired in the m/z 100-1,700 Da range with a sampling rate of 2 Hz. The 
instrument was tuned and calibrated using the Agilent ESI-L tuning mix solution 
for a proprietary Agilent calibration algorithm. A lock mass solution containing 
ions m/z 186.2216 and 922.0098 was constantly infused during the 
instrumental operation [71], [86].

2.2.2 Fungal extraction 
Throughout all the sample preparations for RP UHPLC-DAD-MS metabolome 
analysis, plugs from each colony were extracted and analyzed on the 7th day of 
incubation. The method of micro-scale agar plug extraction and preparation was 
inspired by the research of Jørn Smedsgaard [87]. Adapted from the cited 
published paper to the demands of the present project, culture extracts from 
agar plates were prepared by cutting 6mm diameter plugs from three different 
spots of the culture, and from three colony-rim spots. In the first case, the agar 
plugs were cut from the middle (inoculation point) and from the rims of one or 
different colonies, as shown in Figure 2.2.1, for reasons of variability. The plugs 
were inserted in 2ml disposable Eppendorf tubes and to each tube, 1ml of Ethyl 
Acetate (EtOAc) containing 1% (v/v) Formic Acid (FA) was added. The plugs 
were extracted Ultrasonically for 60min, in order for the cells to break and exert 
all the metabolic compounds. After the extraction, the fluid containing the extract 
was transferred into new vials, in order to be separated from the plugs. The Ethyl 
Acetate was evaporated to dryness under a gentle Nitrogen stream (35˚C, 
60min), as it needs to be replaced by Methanol before the sample analysis, in 
order for the sample injection to be compatible with the UHPLC mobile phase. 
The dried extraction residues were re-dissolved in 400 μl Methanol and the 
solution was centrifuged, as an alternative to filtration, for 2-3 minutes, in order 
to clear the solution from unwanted material (cells, particulates, etc.) that are 
insoluble and could interfere with the instrumental accuracy. The extract was 
transferred in clean vials and 200 μl of the supernatant were transferred by 
using a Pasteur pipette into HPLC vials [87], [88] before being injected into the 
chromatogram (1 μl).

The differences between the traditional and the colony-rims plug extractions in

As a result of the fast growth of Aspergillus nidulans wildtype strains, during the 
7th day of growing, the fungi have already reached the stationary phase, where 
the value of OD (biomass) remains stable, the growth rates are very low, the 
nutrients of the media are limited and the creation of new spores for 
reproduction is in low rates. The lack of nutrients inside the colony (e.g. 
inoculation point) could lead to the degradation of secondary metabolites, as a 
means of absorbing nutrients for survival and reproduction. In a simple 
explanation, when the resources are over, the fungus turns from primary to 
secondary metabolism, breaking down e.g. Nidulanin B in order to receive 
nitrogen for amino and nucleic acid production.

3.5 
Analysis of fed cultures 
This part of the project aims to identify the compound that interferes with the 
tailoring enzymes of the orsellinaldehyde pathway for the production of 
nidubenzal molecules. As mentioned before, the core of the project’s hypothesis 
is that the precursor molecule uses tailoring enzymes from the AN3230 (pkfA) 
gene cluster, but neither the precursor’s structure, nor the nidubenzal 
biosynthetic pathway have yet been elucidated. A short version of the principle 
of the project’s hypothesis considering the nidubenzal production is shown in 
Figure 1.2.5. 
In order to elucidate the biosynthetic pathway of nidubenzals and to understand 
the cross-chemistry that takes place and results to these compounds, the 
Aspergillus nidulans strains IBT22600, NID3, NID351, NID981 and NID1487 were 
fed with pure compounds that are putative nidubenzal precursors. More 
specifically, simultaneously with the inoculation, the cultures were fed with 50μL 
of 5 mg/ml of each compound diluted in distilled water and inserted in small 
wells that were created (the same way as plug extractions) in YES agar plates 
which included the usual supplements (uridine, uracil and arginine), precisely 
2cm away from the single-spot inoculation. The method that was used is similar 
to the agar diffusion test for antibiotic testing by Kirby-Bauer [89], as the 
compounds will diffuse throughout the media and the fungi will uptake them 
during growth. The compounds that correspond to the possible nidubenzal 
precursors were 3-hydroxybenzaldehyde (3-HBA), 4- hydroxybenzaldehyde (4-
HBA) and 3,4-dihydroxybenzaldehyde (protocatechuic aldehyde; 3,4-DHBA).

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) 
Principal component analysis (PCA) was applied in order to emphasize the 
variation between the data and to visualize them in a simple manner. We used 
projections of data in two-dimensional spaces, PC-1 with PC-2 and PC-3 together 
with PC-4, in order to cover as much data modelling as possible. PC-1 axis is the 
one with the most variation (41%), PC-2 (18%) the second most, and so on. The 
dataset is plotted as points in a plane and by selecting “bi-plot” we merged the 
two plots of the objects (fungal strains in different conditions) and variables 
(secondary metabolites) to have a better overview of the correlation between 
them. Also, a hierarchical clustering algorithm is used to generate a 
differentiation between the data [90]. More specifically, average linkage variety 
of hierarchical clustering is used, where the distance between clusters is defined 
as the average distance between pairs of patterns that belong to the two clusters 
[91]. This type of clustering has been used before in gene expression data 
analysis [92], [93]. The major results are clearly shown in the plots below, after 
the list of abbreviations that was used in PCA.

4. Conclusions 
The main hypothesis of the present project suggests that the nidubenzals, two 
novel secondary metabolites derived from nidulans, are synthesized from the 
interference of a polyketide precursor with the tailoring enzymes of the 
orsellinaldehyde pathway. 
In order to prove or reject this hypothesis, selected strains were initially 
analyzed through LC-MS. The strains were reference wildtype NID3, the mutant 
NID351 with a cclA deletion for revealing the cryptic orsellinaldehyde cluster, 
mutant NID981 with an extra deletion of the polyketide synthase of the AN3230 
gene cluster (cclAΔpkfAΔ) and finally NID1487, which, instead of deleted, pkfA 
was overexpressed. For the LC-MS sample preparation, a new plug extraction 
method is suggested, involving the collection of three plugs from the colony rims. 
The method is proved to be more efficient than the traditional one, suggesting 
that the fungi are utilizing the secondary metabolites as food through 
breakdown, when there is lack of nutrients.  
Initially, the chemical profiles of the strains were compared at two different 
growth temperatures (37˚C and 30 ˚C), but none produced pkfA pathway-derived 
compounds, except the wildtype IBT22600, which still did not produce 
nidubenzals. Taking advantage of the IBT22600 chemistry, the UV spectra for 
asperugins Α and B were obtained.  
The mutants NID351 and NID981 seemed to be very temperature-sensitive and 
the weakest ones chemically. When grown at 30˚C they both had much stronger 
chemical profiles, in contrast to the rest of the strains. The temperature 
preferences of NID351 and NID981, but mainly the lack of pkfA pathway-derived 
compound production, led to the next experimental step. Aiming to trigger the 
nidubenzal production, all strains were fed with putative nidubenzal polyketide 
precursors (3-HBA, 4-HBA and 3,4-DHBA) and were grown at 30˚C. NID1487 
revealed the pkfA pathway, but, once again, it did not produce the compounds of 
interest, resulting to the rejection of the initial hypothesis.  
Looking at all the data in overall, the strains needed to be “tricked” (fed) in order 
to synthesize more than two polyketide biosynthetic pathways, and most 
importantly in order to produce pkfA pathway-derived compounds. The limited 
production of Malonyl-CoA in fungal secondary metabolism and the cross-talk 
chemistry that takes place could be two of the main reasons responsible for this 
outcome. It is proposed that the nidubenzals are either undetected because of 
the above reasons, or that the molecules are originating from a different 
biosynthetic pathway (e.g. shikimate), rather than pkfA. A. nidulans cclAΔ mutant 
strain with an additional PKS deletion (e.g. cclAΔorsAΔ) could reveal the pkfA 
==PAGE:85==
84 
 
pathway and is suggested for future experiments, in order to attain stronger 
liability of the results of the present project (rejection of initial hypothesis). 
Nevertheless, the outcome of this experiment was far from insignificant. The 
wildtype NID3, when fed with 3-hydroxybenzaldehyde, and the mutant NID1487, 
when fed with any of the three HBAs, had almost identical chemical profiles and 
produced a high and very abundant number of peaks. The 
hydroxybenzaldehydes triggered the production of more many polyketidic 
pathways simultaneously, more than two for the first time during the 
experimental project, including the pkfA in NID1487. The hydroxybenzaldehydes 
also triggered the secondary metabolism in producing compounds from 
otherwise possibly cryptic clusters, such as UK44, etc., serving as a small-
molecule tools capable of inhibiting certain DNA and histone posttranslational 
modifying proteins. As a result, the hydroxybenzaldehydes used in this project, 
and especially 3-HBA, could be tested in the future for the possibility of being 
good candidates for the manipulation of fungal genome in chemical epigenetic 
studies.

1.3.4.12 
C15H26O4 (12-Hydroxy-6-epi-albrassitriol) 
Peak investigation 
Looking closer to one of the unknown peaks of the BPC, among the highest MS 
spectrum peaks there are adducts identified ([M+H] +, [M+Na]+ and  [M+K]+) for a 
compound with [M+H] +=271.2898. On the next step, we focus on dereplication by 
Antibase 2012, finding a compound formula that could give such mass-to-charge 
numbers. The highest accuracy (Isotope Distribution Calculator confirmation) was 
given for the formula C15H26O4, which according to the database corresponds to 12-
Hydroxy-6-epi-albrassitriol, FR-65814 or Trichocarane-D. 12-Hydroxy-6-epi-
albrassitriol is more likely to be the compound produced by the strain, as it has 
been previously derived from Aspergillus sp. FH-A 6357, and also because it is very 
close structurally with the compound presented at the following section. In 
addition, there is no UV detection for the peak and 12-Hydroxy-6-epi-albrassitriol 
does not have conjugated double bonds. 
In order to confirm the formula, we examine the different monoisotopic masses of 
the compound elements. Looking closer to the [M+H]+ peak, which corresponds to 
the monoisotopic mass of the highest abundance, we get exactly what is expected 
from the Isotope Distribution Calculator MS database. The highest peak 
corresponds to the formula (12)C15(1)H26(16)O4, with the numbers in the parentheses 
showing the monoisotopic masses of each element. The second peak is only 1 mass-
to-charge unit higher than the previous one and it is around 15.50% the height of 
the first peak. In theory, since there are 15 carbons in this molecule, a monoisotopic 
mass change of one carbon from (12)C to (13)C should be (1.07% abundance)×15 ≈ 
15.05% abundant in comparison with the previous case, which is very close to what 
was calculated in this case. Thus, the formula of the compound would now differ in 
monoisotopic masses and would correspond to (12)C14(13)C1(1)H26(16)O4. The 
compound formula of the peak that was investigated is now confirmed. This 
process is repeated in every case of peak elucidation.
