Abstract
Sound ﬁeld design and control in automotive applications requires extended knowledge of
the acoustic properties of the surfaces constituting a vehicle compartment. This project
examines the capacities of a new methodology to retrieve absorptive properties of car seats.
A pressure-particle velocity (P-U) probe is used in combination with surface impedance
reconstruction methods, analytically developed.
The overarching goal is to achieve an
accurate and repeatable characterisation of the normal incidence impedance and absorption
coeﬃcients of any relevant boundary inside a vehicle in situ.
The project assesses the
agreement with laboratory measurements and simulations.
The results highlighted advantages and limitations of the P-U probe and reconstruc-
tions methodology. Meaningful surface impedances and absorption coeﬃcients were ob-
tained in laboratory conditions over a broad frequency range for relatively absorptive mate-
rials. The in situ measurements showed a better agreement with their anechoic counterparts
than expected, providing a solid argument in favour of the methodology investigated. In
both acoustic environments, recommendations to approach ideal experimental conditions
were formulated.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and motivation
Automotive acoustics is a ﬁeld of engineering sciences that must consistently adapt to the
steady evolution of the automotive industry. Sound ﬁeld control inside car compartments
is one of the greatest modern challenges of acoustic engineering applied to industrial prob-
lematics. Music played in a vehicle should be perceived as the musician intended. The
constantly increasing knowledge about the behaviour of sound makes the following fact
seem rather obvious to an acoustic engineer – using identical audio systems does not suﬃce
to predict the behaviour of the interior sound ﬁeld. The unique combinations of geometrical
features and constitutive materials in each vehicle changes radically the listener’s experi-
ence. To improve the latter, it is crucial to be able to predict the behaviour of acoustic
waves inside any vehicle compartment . Acknowledging the speed at which the automo-
tive industry creates new car models using new materials and geometries, experimental
approaches must be replaced by more eﬃcient methods .
Wave-based algorithms such as the Boundary Element Method (BEM) and the Finite
Element Method (FEM) have been broadly developed over the past decades in application
to sound propagation [1, 2]. Such models require three main building blocks . First, a
precise deﬁnition of the geometry must be set through three-dimensional modelling and
meshing. Then, an adequate numerical modelling of the sound sources must be applied.
Last but not least, a thorough knowledge of the characteristics of all boundaries acoustically
interacting is critical. This last item is the core interest in this project.
Acoustical characteristics of boundaries refer to the capacity of a given surface to ab-
sorb and reﬂect incoming sound waves. The surface impedance is a physical concept that
incorporates those properties. Theoretical models based on known material characteristics
can occasionally be used to obtain valid estimates of this quantity under speciﬁc assump-
tions [3]. Unfortunately, these models can hardly be used for most constitutive materials
of a vehicle compartment. Alternatively, laboratory approaches represent a mean of deter-
mining the absorptive properties of a given material, under assumptions as well. Two main
standardised methods exist: the reverberation room (ISO 354:2003 [4]) and the impedance
tube (ISO 10534-1:1996 and 10534-2:1998 [5]) method. The ﬁrst makes use of the ideal
diﬀuse ﬁeld assumption to derive an estimate of the material’s “diﬀuse incidence” absorp-
tion coeﬃcient, but does not permit obtention of impedance values. The second assumes
a plane wave propagating in a rigid tube, and uses two closely spaced microphones to es-
timate pressure and particle velocity at a certain distance from the material terminating
the tube. From these can be derived a relatively accurate estimate of the material’s sur-
face impedance under normal plane wave incidence. Besides, extracting a suﬃcient amount
of material from its original environment to place it under laboratory conditions is often

where constitutive elements are numerous, intricate, combined, and constantly varying from

original environment to place it under laboratory conditions is often cumbersome, costly,

Besides, the surface impedance can rarely, if ever, be considered unique for a given
boundary. The incidence angle of impinging waves, the materials underneath the surface
exposed to the sound, and the potential mechanical links the boundary has with other
elements are susceptible to inﬂuence the surface impedance. For example, an air cavity
behind a membrane changes radically the surface impedance of the latter compared to a rigid
backing scenario. In this case, the cavity is indirectly interacting acoustically by impacting
the behaviour of the membrane, which itself interacts directly. Therefore, one should refer to
the “resulting acoustic impedance” of a boundary, when stepping out of idealised laboratory
scenarios.
This metric embeds the intrinsic material acoustical properties appearing in
any conditions as well as potential acoustical features resulting from interactions of the
material with its current environment.
Introducing this metric in simulations of sound
ﬁelds accounts for eﬀects that could not be translated using the straightforward deﬁnition
of acoustic impedance. To estimate this parameter, the focus must be placed towards the
experimental determination of acoustic properties in situ, in the actual conditions of use.
The challenge therefore resides in the ability to experimentally retrieve an accurate
value of surface impedance through an in situ approach, non-destructive, and as little
invasive as possible. This must be accurately achievable for any boundary, any combina-
tion of material, impinging sound, and environmental conditions, and must be repeatable.
Such knowledge can enable accurate numerical simulations of the sound ﬁeld inside con-
ﬁned spaces, creating opportunities for sound ﬁeld control techniques applied to automotive
acoustics.
1.2
State of the art
The determination of the acoustical properties of surfaces has received increasing attention
in the recent decades. An important part of this focus is due to the fact that the results
retrieved can serve crucial roles in sound propagation modelling, a valuable tool in numerous
ﬁelds of acoustic sciences. The computerised modelling of acoustic waves and their variations
in time and space was ﬁrst proposed in 1962 by Schroeder et al. [6] at the 4th International
Congress of Acoustics. It was put in practice later in the 1960s by Krokstad et al. [7]
then by Schroeder himself in 1973 [8]. The ﬁeld of room acoustics simulations particularly
beneﬁted from this tool, useful for the acoustic design of opera houses, classrooms, houses,
car compartments, or even for virtual reality purposes, as recently summarised Vorländer in
his 2009 feature article [9]. To obtain useful results from computerised sound propagation
models, accurate boundary conditions must be applied. Some laboratory approaches to
measure the latter have been standardised in 1985 [4, 5] and are still used to this day.
However, they do not provide an answer to the challenge of eﬃcient, non-intrusive surface
impedance measurements.
The focus became more recently oriented towards the experimental determination of
acoustic properties in situ. Two main arguments behind this shift of interest were (i) the
practical limitations of laboratory conditions and (ii) the realisation that acoustic properties
obtained under speciﬁc wave incidence assumptions do not suﬃce to accurately simulate
complex scenarios.

In his 2015 review of existing in situ impedance and sound absorption measurement
techniques, Brandão [10] exhaustively lists methods involving either temporal separation
(some of the most recents [11, 12, 13]) or acoustic ﬁeld representations ([14, 15, 16]). More
than eighty references are mentioned in Brandão’s review, most of which describe methods
that rely solely on pressure measurements and signal processing techniques to determine
the characteristics of a material. No reliable particle velocity sensor was available before
the late 20th century. Still today, many in situ measurement techniques rely on the sole use
of microphones, e.g in 2019 [17], with a microphone array combined to near-ﬁeld acoustical
holography.
In 2003, De Bree presented a sensor capable of measuring the particle velocity of a sound
ﬁeld in a given direction, using the temperature diﬀerence between two closely-spaced heated
wires [15]. This device, originally created in 1994, is referred to as “Microﬂown”. It can
be combined with a microphone to create a pressure-particle velocity sensor, or PU-probe.
Such device allows measurement of the ﬁeld impedance at any point. The sensor was used
in several recent publications [16, 18, 19], conﬁrming the validity of its outputs. Aretz &
Vorländer applied used it in 2012, along other in situ methods, to measure the absorption
characteristics of some constitutive elements of a car compartment [1]. The authors aimed
at using the measured outcomes in room acoustic simulations of audio systems in passenger
compartments. Their conclusion mentioned the many limitations encountered and hinted
towards a preferable use of two-port network models. Nevertheless, promising results were
obtained using the Microﬂown near a car seat, for a limited number of frequencies and
experimental conditions. These participated in launching the motivation for this project.
1.3
Problem formulation
Accurately measuring the acoustic surface impedance of any material in situ is an ambitious
objective, far from reached by the current state of art. Consequently, it had to be sized
down to speciﬁc, well deﬁned cases in order to ﬁt for this six-months research project.
First, remaining within the frame of automotive acoustics, it was chosen to investigate solely
materials from a typical car seat. In fact, windows or hard plastic panels display particularly
strong interactions between their mechanical and acoustical properties - as underlined in
[1] - whereas seats less so, making their investigation supposedly more accessible.
It was chosen to investigate the usability and validity of a P-U probe, generally brought
into play for intensity measurements, in the case of ﬁeld impedance retrieval.
Surface
impedance reconstruction methods proposed by Alvarez in 2008 [20] are chosen as complet-
ing tool to retrieve surface properties from ﬁeld data. The research question to be answered
through this thesis work is therefore: can a pressure-particle velocity probe estimate
the acoustical properties of compartment materials in situ when combined to
analytical reconstruction methods?

2.1

In this section are presented the concepts orbiting around the acoustic characterisation of
boundaries, as used throughout the rest of this document.
From here on, j = √−1 and ω = 2πf, where f is the frequency.
The impedance is a physical concept that can be generally described as the amount of
“cause” necessary to produce a certain unit amount of “eﬀect”, and is commonly denoted
Z. The acoustic impedance is deﬁned as the ratio between pressure p and volume velocity

is considered when no surface comes into play, as simply pressure over particle velocity. The
surface impedance of a material is deﬁned as the wave impedance found on its surface. A
general one-dimensional deﬁnition of the surface impedance for a boundary located at the

Any physical boundary between two media can be seen in terms of impedance change.
When a travelling sound wave encounters a new impedance, it is partly absorbed into the
new medium and partly reﬂected back into the original medium. The degree of absorption
(resp. reﬂection) can be quantiﬁed as a function of the impedance of each medium.

2.5
P-U probe principle
A pressure-velocity (P-U) probe is a device composed of two transducers: a microphone and
a Microﬂown. It can be used to measure sound intensity, and is even occasionally referred
to as an “intensity probe”. The microphone transforms a pressure input into an electrical
signal output through mechanical transduction – principles behind its functioning can be
found in exhaustive details in [34, 35] –. The Microﬂown serves a similar purpose for the
particle velocity. It was developed by De Bree in 1994, who detailed its principle in [15].
The Microﬂown is today considered as one of the few reliable particle velocity sensors on
the transducers market.
The Microﬂown principle distinguishes itself greatly from that of a microphone. Two
closely spaced heated wires serve as temperature sensors, relating an increase in tempera-
ture to an increase in electrical resistance. The presence of an acoustic wave introduces a
temperature gradient between the wires, which linearly quantiﬁes the local particle velocity
along the dimension perpendicular to the wires. This way, particle velocity is related to
an electrical signal. The asymmetry of the temperature proﬁle ensures distinction between
positive and negative particle velocities.
The unavailability of other reference devices to measure particle velocities makes the
calibration of a P-U probe rather complex, and relying on assumptions. Some methods
involve an impedance tube, while other require large anechoic chambers. Details can be
found in [36].

Digital signals and DFT
– The experimental study of acoustic phenomena nearly sys-
tematically involves the use of digital systems.
Physical quantities such as pressure or
particle velocity are captured by transducers which convert them into digital time signals.

each sample is assigned a value representative of the quantity measured at the corresponding
time instant.

– A transfer function is a function in the frequency

output signal, y(t). In the time domain, the corresponding concept is the impulse response.

3.6
Conclusive points
Several conclusions of practical interest for the following experimental studies could be
drawn from this series of simulations.
All simulated results tallied on the fact that the measurement point should be chosen
as close as possible to the surface of interest in order to ensure a value Zfield close to Zsurf.
This was highlighted visually by the low ∆values on the lower part of each h-f graph.
Focusing on the inﬁnite plane approximation, the octave bands centred from 125 Hz to 4
kHz provided the best match between Zfield and Zsurf. This conclusion notably relates to
the frequency range focused on in [17], where a similar experimental procedure was followed.
Experimental results outside of this range should be analysed with additional care.
Observation of the eﬀect of the surface impedance itself revealed that accurate results
are supposedly easier to retrieve from absorptive materials than reﬂective surfaces. Notably,
reconstruction methods behave best for high surface absorption coeﬃcients. Background
noise, as in any acoustic measurement procedure, is crucial. Measuring in situ imposes a
higher limit on the achievable SNR, with respect to anechoic conditions. However, SNR
above 60 dB should ideally be aimed at, avoiding additional acoustic disturbances while
measuring. A silent car compartment environment still remains an in situ environment.
Observing the impact of source-surface distance, it is a priori recommendable to choose
a large hs when the range of interest includes frequencies below 500 Hz. In a cramped
environment where only a small source-surface distance is possible, the method’s validity
should not be impacted signiﬁcantly above 500 Hz. It is systematically better to place the
measurement point as close to the surface as possible, for any source-surface distances.
Comparing a cuboid in free ﬁeld to an inﬁnite plane scenario showed that ﬁniteness
is also expected to signiﬁcantly impact the results, especially for reﬂective samples. Low
frequencies imply larger spatial regions where diﬀraction eﬀects can disturb the sound ﬁeld.
For mildly or highly absorptive materials, the inﬁnite surface approximation remains rela-
tively fair if the ﬁeld impedance is measured less than 5 mm above the surface.

Chapter 6
In Situ Measurements
In this chapter, the measurements of surface impedances inside a car compartment are
presented. They were conducted in the Harman facilities of West Jutland (Peter Bangs Vej
15, 7600 Struer).
6.1
Experimental setup
The vehicle used was a regular ﬁve doors city car. All of its seats were covered by few
millimetres thick perforated leather. The perforation size was similar to that of sample
A, with a diameter neighbouring 1 mm. The upholstery pattern was not uniform, instead
composed of a 4x4 cm2 leather squares tiling, each held together at its edges. The inner
composition of the seat was unknown, yet assumed to be partly composed of foam, backed
by springs or some air cavity.
The surface impedance of only one of the seats was measured. It is acknowledged that
the surface impedance can vary across seats even within a single compartment, yet this was
not investigated in this case. The rear part of the car was focused on as it provided a larger
space for the setup. The whole compartment was fairly symmetrical along the main axis of
the vehicle, allowing choice between left and right side. The right rear seat was selected due
to the larger space available in front of it when shifting the front passenger seat forward.
The setup used in anechoic conditions, as presented in chapter 4, could not ﬁt inside
the compartment. The probe was instead ﬁxed to a goose neck microphone holder, allowing
ﬂexible yet less precise placement in front of the back rest. Two diﬀerent excitation sources
were used: an electrodynamic mid/high frequencies baﬄed directive loudspeaker (shown on
Figure 6.1.1a); a low/mid frequencies baﬄed driver (Figure 6.1.1b).

6.2
Procedure
The PU Match was held in place at a known distance h from the surface under study, its
sensor perpendicularly facing the material. The source was placed at a certain distance
hs from the surface, with hs ≫h, such as the impinging wave was directed onto the
back rest. The loudspeaker was elevated to align its acoustic centre with the probe’s axis.
The loudspeaker baﬄe plane could not be placed perfectly parallel to the back rest of the
seat, unlike in the anechoic chamber measurements. The best resulting conﬁguration used
an incidence angle roughly 10◦oﬀnormal axis, also when measuring the samples placed
onto the back rest.
The probe itself was oriented perfectly perpendicular to the back
rest, capturing the normal particle velocity. Nevertheless, having a non-normal incidence
represents an additional diﬀerence between laboratory and in situ measurements.
As a
result of its simplicity, this setup unavoidably included more potential uncertainties than
the anechoic room counterpart. A photographic example is shown on Figure 6.2.1.

This underlines the sensitivity of the method to surrounding conditions, as this
low frequency range neighbours the dimensions of the whole compartment. Another clear
diﬀerence between open or closed doors scenarios is consistently noticed between 600 and

that region when the doors are closed. The divergence disappears when opening the doors.
A weak standing wave pattern is indeed able to form along the axis perpendicular to the
two rear doors when they are closed. This explains the consistent velocity drop creating
the impedance divergence at this frequency. A low particle velocity point between two rigid
boundaries is expected to appear at λ/2. At 750 Hz, λ/2 ≈23 cm, which is indeed similar to
the distance separating the inner part of the left door from the probe itself while measuring
doors closed.
These results underline the beneﬁt of reducing the amount of surrounding surfaces
when possible. However, no obvious reason to choose the directive loudspeaker over the
non-directive alternative seems to appear. The directive nature of the electrodynamic loud-
speaker kicks in above 2 kHz. Yet, it seemed to yield only a very slight improvement of
coherence at high frequencies. A better coherence could have been obtained by either (i)
increasing the output excitation level or (ii) increasing the number of FFT averages.
The absorption proﬁle of Figure 6.3.1 appears reasonable for a perforated leather car
seat under 6 kHz. Steadily increasing absorption until 1 kHz, highest absorption range
between 1 and 2 kHz, and decrease above, with α > 0.5 all along. Discarding the clear high
frequency noisiness, this measurement seems to provide a good insight on the acoustical
behaviour of this boundary in these speciﬁc in situ conditions, between 200 Hz and 6
kHz. Unsurprisingly, the absorption proﬁle diﬀers from those of sample A and B. This
can be explained by the inner constitution of the complete seat, which most likely diﬀers
tremendously from a simple rigidly backed foam.

This deviation is rather accurately made up for using Alvarez’s [20]

Naturally, the match is

Reconstructed surface impedance proﬁles are more likely to accurately

when it is placed against the back rest of a seat. This can be considered as an unexpected
yet welcomed advantage of measuring in situ.

7.1
Laboratory conditions
Experimental procedure
– The experimental procedure adopted in anechoic conditions
yielded meaningful ﬁeld impedance data. The empty setup conﬁguration revealed notable
deviations from the theoretical ﬁeld impedance of a spherical wave in free ﬁeld, some of
which could be compensated due to their consistency – subsection 4.2.1. Other disturbances
caused by the setup itself unpredictably also depended on the probe position, making them
unavoidable – subsection 4.2.2. The mere presence of solid objects in the ﬁeld neighbouring
the probe noticeably aﬀects the results. From this point on, accurate retrieval of surface
impedances with ﬁne frequency resolution was decidedly labelled as unattainable. Never-
theless, correct trends were obtained on speciﬁc benchmark cases over a broad frequency
range. Being able to measure accurate surface impedances under octave or one-third octave
bands resolution is still highly useful for application in simulated acoustical environments.
Benchmarks were set achieved via reference absorption data for rigidly backed samples of
Ecophon Modus porous absorber – subsection 5.1.1. This conﬁrmed the meaningfulness
and fair accuracy of retrieved surface impedances under anechoic conditions in the case of
relatively absorptive materials. Earlier this year, authors of [17] used near-ﬁeld holography
to show that a rigidly boxed sample exhibited diverging near-ﬁeld impedances at speciﬁc
frequencies. These characteristics, due to destructive interferences caused by the rigid box,
were correctly captured using the single P-U probe.This diverging feature was the ﬁrst to
experimentally hint at a limitation of the method, discussed in the next paragraph.
Rigid materials
– Interferences and standing wave patterns appear close to reﬂective
surfaces. These create diverging impedances at speciﬁc positions in the ﬁeld, varying as a
function of frequency. Consequently, the retrieval of surface impedance from measurement
of ﬁeld impedance was found to be unattainable for rigid materials at high frequencies
using a single measurement point. This limitation was conﬁrmed through the study of a
rubber membrane absorber, which also showed diverging near-ﬁeld impedance values at
speciﬁc frequencies depending on the probe-surface distance – subsection 5.1.2. The latter
were indeed related to a standing wave pattern, where low velocities appeared for sensor-
surface distances equal to multiples of a quarter wavelength. This eﬀect could possibly
be circumvented by sequentially placing the probe at several distances from the surfaces
and extracting the non-diverging frequency ranges across measurements. Further study of
this rubber absorber nonetheless permitted to correctly locate its theoretically predicted
membrane resonance, conﬁrming the usability of the method down to 50 Hz for materials
absorbing in that region. However, an important point of discussion in the interpretation
of this result is the limitation imposed by the local reaction assumption, discussed further

down in this section.
Non-porous, non-rigid materials
– This project participated in a broadening of the
usual in situ test material spectrum. While most previous in situ studies so far focused on
porous absorbers (e.g [17, 16, 42]) – with the exception of Aretz & Vorländer [1] on a limited
frequency range –, this project included the broadband in situ experimental investigation
of materials covered by perforated leather upholstery. They revealed absorption proﬁles
showcasing a bell-shaped peak of absorption in the mid-frequencies region. Such behaviour
was found to mimic that of perforated panels [23] relatively well. This investigation proved
that the P-U probe method enables extension of the spectrum of measurable absorptive
materials.
Local vs.
extended reaction
– No surface reaction models were considered in this
project. All test materials were assumed to be immovable and under normal incidence, a
strong yet necessary assumption to narrow the scope of the study. In in situ problems,
perfectly normal incidence hardly ever occurs. An experimental incidence angle study is
recommendable in order to observe the resulting changes in estimated surface impedances
using the P-U probe. The local reaction assumption holds fairly well in the case of porous
materials with high ﬂow resistivity and thick absorbers [43], but its use becomes debatable
for a membrane absorber. For instance, the assumption of immobility is too strong in the
case of the large rubber membrane, which could vibrate at low/mid frequencies due to the
presence of an air cavity behind it. Similar extended reaction materials can be found in car
compartments, e.g on the back rest of a seat where a leather membrane would be stretched
above a soft backing, creating a small air cavity behind it. Some surfaces such as windows,
headliner, door linings, or dashboard, combine rigidity and extended reaction, which makes
their in situ investigation a priori impossible with a P-U probe method. This project has
not demonstrated nor disproved the validity of the P-U probe measurement procedure for
an absorptive material with extended reaction properties. Further study must be brought
forward in order to assess this point.
7.2
Surface impedance reconstruction
The usefulness of reconstruction methods proposed by Alvarez [20] has been proved on
repeated occasions throughout this project. First entirely theoretically, then experimentally
in laboratory and in situ conditions.
Alvarez’s third method (assuming spherical incidence and spherical reﬂection) is the
closest to reality, yet its use was discarded due to witnessed instabilities and an implemen-
tation requiring large computational times. It is thought that a more optimised implemen-
tation, notably adapting the starting point of the inherent minimisation algorithm, could
solve both issues. Although, this was not explored further. Method 2 (spherical incidence
and plane wave reﬂection factor) yielded identical data at mid/high frequencies. Its simu-
lated results revealed a much better match than Method 1 (planar incidence and reﬂection)
over the whole frequency range, nevertheless still including a mismatch at low frequencies
compared to Method 3, due to a misrepresentation of near-ﬁeld reﬂections. In a trade-oﬀ
between speed, stability, and accuracy, Method 2 was chosen as reference reconstruction
method. A unexplored possibility could have been to combine Method 3 for low frequencies
with Method 2 for mid/high frequencies. Method 2 alone was experimentally proved to
yield meaningful results – section 5.3.
The use of reconstructions was found to be crucial when the wavelength becomes smaller
than the spacing between sensors and investigated surface. In this case, the ﬁeld impedance

is not a fair approximation of the surface impedance. The impact of reconstructions was
more noticeable in low absorption frequency regions, where the ﬁeld impedance is brought
away from the surface impedance due to the inﬂuence of reﬂected waves in the near-ﬁeld.
However, reconstructions are based on models which validity is limited to locally reacting,
plane, uniform surfaces. To extend the applicability of the method to other types of sur-
faces, it is necessary to either (a) develop extended reconstruction methods accounting for
these new surfaces characteristics, or (b) minimise the need for the use of reconstructions.
The latter was achieved in this project by reducing the sensor-surface distance as much as
possible. A counter argument to this recommendation could be that, as a result of this dis-
tance reduction, diﬀractions around the probe’s body might become less predictable hence
less possible to systematically compensate. The ideal scenario would be represented by an
acoustically invisible pair of sensors inﬁnitely close to the surface of interest.
The combination of ﬁeld impedance measures using a P-U probe and surface impedance
reconstruction methods deﬁnes the complete method investigated in this project.
Both
elements taken individually were found to be insuﬃcient for good accuracy over an extended
frequency range, but their combination allowed obtention of meaningful and exploitable
broadband results.
7.3
In situ applicability
The procedure was applied on a fully in situ case. Field impedances were measured with
the P-U probe inside a car compartment. As expected, the results included more artefacts
than those obtained in anechoic conditions due to the presence of neighbouring reﬂective
surfaces. The investigation of experimental parameters such as source-surface distance, car
doors conﬁgurations, and speaker directivity permitted the formulation of several discussion
points – chapter 6.
First, disturbances by neighbouring surfaces can be reduced by opening nearby doors
or windows.
This seemingly obvious conclusion nevertheless remains within the target
framework.
As explained in section 1.3, the objective of this project was to assess the
potential of a method permitting measurement of surface impedance in situ.
This was
motivated by the arguments of (i) practicality compared to laboratory methods and (ii)
respect of the material’s natural mounting conditions, expected to inﬂuence its surface
impedance. Both arguments remain satisﬁed when opening doors, since the investigated
material stays part of the compartment. One could argue that the mechanical stress imposed
on a constitutive element is not the same whether a door is open or closed, potentially
modifying its impedance characteristics. However, the corresponding changes observed fell
under the threshold of measurement uncertainties.
Another way to limit the presence of unwanted reﬂections was found by reducing the
source-surface distance. This has the additional beneﬁt of naturally increasing the SNR
while keeping the same input level. However, near-ﬁeld eﬀects can appear at higher fre-
quencies. This notably requires more accurate reconstruction methods to safely reconstruct
the lower end of the impedance spectrum. Interestingly, the opposite observation was made
through simulations of an inﬁnite impedance plane, where large source-surface distance
seemed recommendable. Naturally, the scenarios diﬀer greatly, which can explain such a
shift in observations.
No particular interest was found in using a directive source instead of a point source-
like excitation. Nevertheless, this conclusion should be taken rather lightly as results were
rather above 2 kHz, where the loudspeakers start diﬀering in directivity patterns.
The
interest of directivity could be investigated in anechoic conditions, with a fair SNR at high
frequencies. This was attempted in the early stages of the project, but was not thoroughly

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The principal focus of this project was to assess the advantages and drawbacks of an exper-
imental methodology involving a pressure-particle velocity (P-U) probe to recover surface
impedances in situ. This was applied to the ﬁeld of automotive acoustics. The study was
mainly directed towards car seats. Analytical and numerical modelling techniques came
in completion to a great deal of experimental work. Measurements were conducted using
various relevant test materials, in laboratory conditions as well as inside a car compartment.
Analytical surface impedance reconstructions methods proposed by Alvarez [20] were used
in combination to the experimentally retrieved data.
Preliminary simulations assumed a point source at normal incidence over an impedance
surface and hinted at a sound ﬁeld signiﬁcantly depending on many parameters. These
included source-surface distance, receiver-surface distance, frequency, background noise,
and surface impedance itself. All were found to inﬂuence the ﬁeld impedance present in
the proximity of the surface, driving it either closer or further away from the actual surface
impedance.
The importance of these parameters was ﬁrst conﬁrmed experimentally, through labo-
ratory measures on two benchmark cases. The P-U probe combined to surface impedance
reconstruction method yielded good results over the complete 100-10k Hz range in the case
of absorptive boundaries in laboratory conditions, providing yet another tool to estimate
the surface impedance of porous materials. The probe’s backwards orientation created the
need for a particular compensation applied to the probe’s sensitivities in order to account
for impinging wave diﬀraction around its own body. This compensation, naturally probe-
dependent, is considered crucial in such experimental investigation. It can be related to
the sensitivity calibration of a microphone intended for free ﬁeld application. Omitting
diﬀraction eﬀects can result in strong misinterpretations at high frequencies.
A strong drawback was discovered as more rigid materials were brought into play.
The method displayed a seemingly unavoidable sensitivity to low particle velocity values,
yielding diverging impedances. Such phenomena were found to appear due to standing wave
patterns and other interferences between incident, reﬂected, and diﬀracted sound waves. In
scenarios of simple geometry, e.g single plane surface, these eﬀects could be predicted, but
divergences can hardly be systematically compensated.
Consequently, the methodology
used in this project was unsuitable to measure the broadband surface impedance of rigid
materials, even in anechoic conditions.
A meaningful broadband surface impedance was successfully retrieved for perforated
materials, neither completely rigid nor porous. The comparison of laboratory results with
their counterparts measured in a car compartment revealed a good applicability of the
methodology in situ. Even in the supposedly “worse” experimental conditions, with im-

surface impedance features were correctly recovered. Practically, small source-surface dis-

The use of reconstruction method proved its importance throughout the project, mak-

Further Research
9.1
Future work
Measurement of rigid surface impedances
– In the case of rigid materials with simple
geometrical features, it is thought possible to recover a broadband surface impedance. Since
the recovered spectra seemed unaﬀected in between frequencies showing diverging ﬁeld
impedances, a fully undisturbed broadband spectrum could be retrieved by measuring at
several distances from the surface along the same normal axis. This way, the diverging
impedance values would be obtained each time at diﬀerent frequencies, shifting through the
standing wave patterns. Somehow extracting the unaﬀected frequency regions, it would be
possible to piece them back together into a full reconstructed surface impedance spectrum.
Accuracy of reconstruction methods
– As mentioned on several occasions, the sec-
ond reconstruction method proposed by Alvarez was used for practical reasons despite its
approximation of planar reﬂection. The analytical assessment of the methods respective
performances showed a lack of accuracy of Method 2 at low frequencies. On the contrary,
Method 3 yielded nearly perfect results down to 20 Hz, as it models the full sphericity of
the problem. It would be preferable to use Method 3, requiring a computationally eﬃcient
implementation of its algorithm, notably including an optimised starting point for the min-
imisation problem. A possibility would be to combine Method 2 for high frequencies with
Method 3 for low frequencies.
Incidence angle
– This project focused entirely on normal incidence. As mentioned in
chapter 7, this is rather limiting in a car compartment where incidence is much more likely
to be oblique. Hence, a future investigation at diﬀerent incidence angles is advised.
Compartment elements
– While it is valid to state that the seats are important consti-
tutive elements of a vehicle compartment in terms of acoustics, all other elements are also
inﬂuencing the shape of the sound ﬁeld. They too demand boundary condition character-
isation. Using the method presented in this project, it would be possible to also measure
the surface impedance of the ceiling, the lower part of the seats, and the head rests, all
supposedly similarly absorptive. For the remaining elements, alternatives in situ methods
must be developed.
Frequency range
– The project was restricted to frequencies under 10 kHz due to the
probe’s limitations, although reconstruction methods are still applicable at higher frequen-
cies. More broadband devices could be used in future work to extend this working range,
ideally up to the limit of human hearing, 20 kHz.

Moving vehicles
– In most cases when the vehicle is moving, the local reaction assump-
tion (as deﬁned in chapter 2) is not veriﬁed for many constitutive elements. Vibrations
from ground contacts, engine, and other components propagate inside the compartment
and induce mechanical deformations of all elements. This occurs mostly at low frequencies,
where mechanical vibrations are the strongest. These inevitably induce changes in the local
acoustic characteristics of the surfaces involved. Additionally, moving passengers or objects
can locally modify the seats acoustical properties. Therefore, while assuming local reaction
enabled narrowing of the study’s scope, it remained a strong statement that can conﬂict
with practical scenarios in automotive acoustics. Future works could consider the impact
of vibrations.
9.2
Test case in a controlled environment
The end-of-line interest behind recovering the surface impedance of constitutive materials is
its incorporation into numerical sound propagation models. Eﬃciently obtainable, accurate
boundary condition knowledge would enable the development of sound ﬁeld simulations
inside vehicle compartments. This section covers a small study conducted on a well-deﬁned
in situ environment, where the eﬀect of a change in local boundary condition could be
studied experimentally. The measured results could then be compared to those simulated
in FEM, using the surface impedance recovered by the P-U probe as boundary condition.
This represents a test for the applicability of experimental results in simulated studies.
An air-tight wooden “ﬁsh tank” was used as controlled environment in the Harman
facilities of West Jutland (Peter Bangs Vej 15, 7600 Struer). It was composed of a main
inner cavity of dimensions 119x50x38 cm3, on ﬁve sides of which were present 3 cm deep
rectangular open spaces. A 21 cm diameter driver was placed ﬂush on one side, facing the
side where a sample can be mounted. A pressure sampling inside the tank was conducted
using a 60-channels 1/4" microphones array.
9.2.1
Methodology
The microphones were arranged on two 10-by-3 grids with constant 50 mm spacing between
each of them. The two grids were themselves separated by a 100 mm gap. A log-sweep
excitation was produced by the driver.
The other end of the tank was either left rigid
(“empty” conﬁguration) or was replaced by sample A or B. The output of each microphone
was recorded and the transfer functions with respect to the input signal were computed.
Sixty spectra were obtained, covering the range of interest, 100 Hz to 10 kHz. A picture of
the setup is shown on Figure 9.2.1.
