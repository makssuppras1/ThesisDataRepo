Abstract
The following paper constitutes our thesis presented for the degree of MSc Eng in Human-Centered
Artiﬁcial Intelligence (recently renamed from Digital Media Engineering). The paper describes
common problems of video game developers and discusses how open development may be able to
help solving these problems. Furthermore, it describes our conducted experiments and the respective
ﬁndings. The paper was submitted as a conference paper to the European Group for Organizational
Studies (EGOS) for the 36th EGOS Colloquium 2020 [1], taking place at University of Hamburg,
July 2–4, 2020. The submitted conference paper differed from this paper, by being shorter and not
containing an appendix, due to strict word limit requirements of 3000 words. The current version of
this paper resembles a journal paper in terms of structure and length, e.g. CHI papers are required to
span a maximum of ten pages excluding references and appendixes [2]. Although this is a joint thesis,
the work has been clearly divided between us, which is speciﬁed in appendix H.

Abstract—Open development (OD), is a relatively new
development technique borrowing concepts from agile and
lean development, revolving around building a community
by iteratively releasing new versions of a digital product
before it has ﬁnished development. Based on the idea that
the community provides feedback through comments and
analytics, the developers can mature the product, save time
by focusing on developing things that provide value, and
avoid crunch time towards the release. While prior research
has informed us about the effect of agile development
methods for product quality, much less is known on the
effect of these methods on actual development time and
employees’ stress and well being. Yet, particularly stress
is a common reason for long term absence of employees
and can harm organizations in the long run. In this paper,
we are investigating how OD can inﬂuence crunch time,
stress, motivation, engagement, and happiness, working
under the supposition that OD will decrease both crunch
time and stress towards the end of production periods,
while increasing motivation, engagement, and happiness.
Following a six-week game production we compare six
teams out of which three utilize OD and three follow
more standard waterfall-based development techniques.
Our results, based on both quantitative data and qualitative
data, reveal that the teams using OD saw a signiﬁcant
decrease in stress levels by 0.84 points on a ﬁve-level scale
throughout the production, and that NOD teams in the end
of the production generally worked 35.36% more hours per
week than in the beginning, and 37.28% more than OD
teams. Additionally, our results suggest that OD increases
motivation, engagement, and happiness.
Author Keywords—Open development, Agile develop-
ment, Lean development, Early access, Open alpha, Game
development
I. INTRODUCTION
In the video game development industry, it is still
the norm for most companies to use a waterfall-based
development approach, i.e. an approach that entails only
little interaction with engaged players until the release
of the game. It is very common to have up to several
months of crunch time (periods with high workload
demands requiring compromises between work time and
delivered quality – refer to appendix A for an in-depth
description), with up to 80 ours work weeks, leading up
to tight deadlines [3]. During these periods, developers
will usually experience intense stress, which in the long
run is bad for their physical and mental health [4].
Meta analysis [5] indicate that the main causes of work-
related stress, physical and psychological health, and
sickness absence include work demands and long hours.
According to a 2014 survey [6], 81% of developers had
experienced crunch at some point the past two years,
while 50% felt that crunch was a ”normal part of their
job”. As a result, more and more studios are trying to
develop and adapt alternate development techniques [7].
Rather than using waterfall approaches, where crunch
tends to be lurking at the end of the project, software and
game developers have been adapting agile development
methods since 2001 [8], and with great business results
according to research [9]. Today, agile development is
commonly used e.g. via scrum, which distributes the
work into sprints, and hence, emphasising on evaluating
and improving after each iteration [9]. In 2003, lean
software development concepts were introduced [10]
helping developers focus their resources on things that
provide value, minimizing the amount of time wasted on
features not providing value for users, and potentially
reducing crunch time [11]. Another method that has
become widely used in the game development industry
the past few years is ’early access’ (also referred to
as ’open alpha’). It revolves around game developers
making their games accessible before the ofﬁcial release,
==PAGE:6==
usually with the motivation of bug and stress testing their
game and related systems to avoid disastrous surprises
at launch [12] [11]. Based on all of these concepts, an
approach called open development (referred to as ’OD’)
[13], has recently emerged.
OD is a relatively new process that appeared around
2014, and is mostly used by small independent teams.
Accordingly, the research on this topic is very scarce.
In his seminal paper (2018), Luke Thominet conducted
a content analysis on 23 texts from developers describ-
ing their development processes, and found three major
common points between all of them: Giving users access
to internal media during development, being transparent
about the current state and plans of development, and
getting and using feedback from users during the entire
process (refer to appendix B for a more thorough walk
through of the deﬁnition). We use this deﬁnition of OD
in this paper, with an emphasis on aspects of agile and
lean aspects.
With OD, developers use early access to not just get
bug reports, but also to iterate through regularly released
builds and evaluating what provides value to actual users
via their game-play data and comments. By regularly
uploading builds, announcements, etc. online through
social media platforms, they can build a community.
By doing this, they get more valuable feedback in each
iteration from expert user/developers [14]. This both
helps reducing the amount of work after the ﬁnal release,
as the game has already been bug tested, and additionally
reduces the game’s risks of being a commercial failure,
as the developers have already ensured that the game
provides value to players [15].
Another problem that is not just a plague for game
developers, but also for other types of companies, is that
disengaged employees tend to be more unhappy [16],
and in some cases turn into ’corporate terrorists’ or ’the
organisation’s silent killers’ – employees acting out their
unhappiness, undermining what their engaged coworkers
accomplish, and discouraging others from joining the
organisation [17]. According to the Gallup Employee
Engagement Index based on large scale studies with
42,000 randomly selected participants, 33 percent of
workers are engaged at work, 49 percent are not engaged
and 18 percent are actively disengaged, costing U.S.
businesses up to 350 billion US Dollars per year [18].
Apart from engagement, low motivation is also an
issue and smaller independent game development teams
(referred to as ’indie developers’ or ’indies’ [19]). In the
great majority of the cases they end up falling apart and
canceling their game even before it ﬁnishes production
[20], with one of the biggest reasons being lack of
motivation [21] [22] [20].
According to studies [23], high motivation has a
positive direct effect on engagement, and engagement has
a positive direct effect on the performance of employees
as well as their self-efﬁcacy and happiness. It has been
described how higher levels of happiness and psycholog-
ical well being has high positive impacts on productivity
and performance at work [18]. Therefor, by maintaining a
high motivation for their workers, companies can expect
more engaged, happy and productive workers.
Through community building and co-creation, OD
supplies the indie developers with an audience, who will
cheer them on and expect regular releases, giving a clear
motivation to keep working and showing new features
to the community [21] [22]. Through increasing the
motivation, OD also should increase engagement [23],
happiness [16].
In this paper, we are investigating the impacts of OD
on developers, speciﬁcally keeping an eye out for its
inﬂuence on crunch time, stress, motivation, engagement,
and happiness. We are working under the supposition
that OD will decrease both crunch time and stress
towards the end of production periods, while increasing
motivation, engagement, and happiness when compared
to the baseline of NOD teams.
II. METHOD
For our study, we teamed up with The National
Academy of Digital, Interactive Entertainment (DADIU)
[24]. DADIU comprises six teams consisting of around
14 people including a project manager (referred to as
PM) and a quality assurance manager (referred to as QA).
During the last six weeks of the DADIU semester, the
teams develop their ﬁnal game. One of the requirements
is to produce a motion matching system for characters
of the game. According the main teacher at DADIU
[25], the qualiﬁcations and competences of the teams
are as equal is possible. Refer to appendix C for more
information about DADIU.
We brieﬂy introduced the teams to the concepts of OD,
and three of them agreed to utilize it. As OD is not part
of the DADIU curriculum, and as it normally is not used
[25], we are assuming that the three teams not agreeing
to use OD are actually not using it – or at least that they
are using it to a lower degree than that of the other teams.
We describe these teams as non-open development teams
(NOD teams).
To monitor the effects of OD, we sent out ques-
tionnaires to the teams with the NOD teams working
as a joint control group and the OD teams as a joint
intervention group. The questionnaires were distributed
via a URL to an online google form [26] in an email
to a PM or QA of each team, who then distributed
==PAGE:7==
it to their respective team (refer to appendix I for the
questionnaire). The questionnaire was sent to the teams
three times spread across the production period. One
questionnaire was sent out at the beginning of week two,
one in week four, and one at the end of week six (in
appendix F-C, we discuss the number of questionnaires
to use).
The questionnaire collects data through voluntary self-
reporting. It asks for the number of days the developer
worked on the game project the past week, how many
hours per day in average during those days they were
working, respectively how stressed, motivated, engaged,
and happy the developer was the past week using ﬁve-
level linear Likert scales, and it allows surveyees to
describe their answers and enter any other comments
via free text forms. In line with [27], we presented
the options of the Likert scale as 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 only
displaying the explanations of the edge responses (1 and
5), to communicate that the scale should be considered
linear, with answers between the edge responses being
linear interpolations of the edge responses. With this
distinction, one can meaningfully compute and compare
means of data points co-existing in the space.
To better understand the underlying rationals behind
the measured data, (in line with [28]) we attempted
gathering additional qualitative (descriptive) data by con-
ducting interviews with members of the teams about
how they would describe their workﬂow and experiences
through the production (refer to appendix N for the
interview questions and responses). We pursued both
interviewing PMs, QAs as well as other members of the
teams such as programmers, from both OD and NOD
teams. The interviews were semi-structured and followed
the guidelines described in [29], [30], and [28].
III. RESULTS
A. Descriptive Results
As the questionnaires were anonymous, we denote the
open development teams by OD-A, OD-B, and OD-C,
and NOD teams by NOD-A, NOD-B, and NOD-C in an
arbitrary order respectively.
In total we received 83 questionnaire responses, of
which 41% originated from the ﬁrst questionnaire, 32.5%
from the second, and 26.5% from the third questionnaire,
indicating substantially more responses in the beginning
than in the end. The majority (67.5%) of the responses
came from OD teams in the three questionnaires respec-
tively. Speciﬁcally, in total, 38.6% of the responses came
from team OD-A, 12.0% from OD-B, 16.9% from OD-
C, 2.4% from NOD-A, 14.5% from NOD-B, and 15.7%
from NOD-C (refer to appendix J for all the numerical
questionnaire responses, appendix L for more data on
the distribution of the numeric responses, and appendix
K for the textual questionnaire responses).
B. Qualitative Results
We interviewed a QA from OD-A, two OD-C pro-
grammers, and a NOD-B PM and programmer. Through
the interviews and the comments from the questionnaires,
it became clear that all the teams used some concepts
of OD, and that the development methods varied from
team to team (refer to appendix N for the interviews
with the DADIU team members and appendix K for the
textual responses of the questionnaires). Additionally, the
NOD teams used OD to a lower degree than the OD
teams. For example the NOD-B PM described that their
ﬁnal game ”did not differ much from their initial design
documents”, and that they were ”generally not very
agile” and ”did not pivot much”, due to that they ”had
a clear vision from the beginning”. Contrary, the QA
of OD-A described the great length they went through
to conduct ”appreciation, play-through, mechanics, and
user experience tests”, which ”ended up inﬂuencing their
game a lot”. In addition to this, an OD-B programmer
described how they ”used player analytics and heat maps
to better understand the users, their choices and strug-
gles” and that their game was picked by two independent
video game live streamers with 535 thousand YouTube
subscribers combined a week before the deadline, giving
a relatively large online exposure to the game. The
programmer described how ”it was very motivating”.

D. Effect on Crunch Time
Fig. 1 shows the responses of work days and work
hours per day from the OD and NOD teams throughout
the three questionnaires as well as the computed total
hours of work per week in the rightmost sub-ﬁgure. From
a ﬁrst glance ignoring statistical analysis, it appears that
OD teams generally worked more days a week and more
hours per day than NOD teams in the beginning and
middle of the production and that both OD and NOD
teams generally worked a bit more in the middle than in
the beginning. In the end of the production, it appears
OD teams then, worked fewer days and hours per day
than they did previously, while NOD teams rise, passing
OD teams in both variables.
When observing the p-values of work days at table I
in conjunction with the graphs of ﬁg. 1, it can be seen
that the two-way ANOVA of the interaction yields a p-
value of 0.138 hinting at an inﬂuence of development
type on work days through the production period, in line
with what the ﬁgure shows. The one-way ANOVA of
OD also suggests that OD has an inﬂuence on days of
work, and the t-tests that there is a difference between
==PAGE:9==
OD and NOD work days in the end of the questionnaire
and that OD teams indeed do seem to work fewer days
in the end than in the beginning. The two-way ANOVA
show signiﬁcant indications that development types do
indeed have an inﬂuence of development type on both
hours worked per working day and hours worked the past
week. The one-way ANOVA signiﬁcantly indicates that
NOD teams work more hours per day over time, while t-
tests signiﬁcantly indicate that OD teams worked fewer
hours per day and week than NOD teams, as well as
signiﬁcantly indicating a rise in hours worked from the
middle to the end of the period for NOD teams.
In conclusion, OD teams generally worked fewer days
and hours the last week of production (21.33% decrease
in hours of work per week from questionnaire 2 to 3),
and NOD teams oppositely worked more hours per day
(up 29.16% (signiﬁcantly) from questionnaire 1 to 3
and 17.43% (signiﬁcantly) from 2 to 3) and per week
(up 35.36% from questionnaire 1 to 3 and 25.24%
(signiﬁcantly) from 2 to 3) the last week of production.
In the end of the production period, when compared to
OD teams, NOD teams generally worked signiﬁcantly
more hours per day (25.21% more) and hours per week
(37.28% more).
These results were generally conﬁrmed and explained
by the qualitative feedback. For example, a programmer
from NOD-B expressed how they had to ”crunch the last
two days up to the deadline”, and that they especially
”spent a lot of time on preparing and uploading the game
to Google Play, which ended up taking much longer
than expected - especially as we realized that the game
crashed on most devices”. Contrary, OD-B programmer
described how it became clear that ”early feedback saves
time later on” and OD-A QA explained how ”there was
not much to do the last week” and ”the last Friday
we didn’t really do much”. Although we did get some
contradicting feedback from both OD and NOD teams,
the feedback had a tendency of conveying what we have
quoted above.
E. Effect on Stress
The upper left sub-ﬁgure of ﬁg. 2 shows the self
reported stress levels of OD and NOD through the three
questionnaires. NOD appears to be rather stable, while
OD teams appear to have decreased stress levels over
time. From table I, it can be seen that while the two-
way ANOVA does not signiﬁcantly show the inﬂuence
of development type on stress over time, t-tests show a
signiﬁcant drop by 0.73 points on the ﬁve-level linear
likert scale in stress from the beginning to the end of
the production period of OD teams. However, if treating
the scores of NOD teams as a baseline, we observe an
OD drop by 0.84 points. Similarly, the one-way ANOVA
of OD’s inﬂuence of stress over time, with a p-value of
0.060, almost to a signiﬁcant degree, indicates a decrease
in stress, and the t-test between OD and NOD stress in
questionnaire three suggests that stress was lower for OD
teams.
The qualitative data supports this well. An anonymous
OD comment after the production stated ”It was stressful
when I still had user tests to do” and OD-A QA explained
that they were ”less stressed in the end compared to
other teams as we had already released and tested the
game online before, and as we ﬁnished early and did
not crunch”. The anonymous NOD comment after the
production stated ”Too much crunch. Feeling motivated
as I see everybody working hard, but at the same time it
is very stressful”. This indicates that members of NOD
teams were more stressed due to the crunch. Additionally,
most teams ﬁnished their motion matching system half
way through the development, hence giving less of a
burden on the teams and in turn giving them less stress,
as stated by an anonymous comment ”Motion matching
took a lot of resources from our team and was stressful
until it was ﬁnished [...] midway through”. These two
factors cancelling out each other could possibly explain
why the NOD stress levels stayed the same and did not
rise in the end.
F. Effect on Motivation, Engagement, and Happiness
On ﬁg. 2, the upper right sub-ﬁgure shows self-
reported motivation, lower left engagement, and lower
right shows self-reported happiness levels of OD and
NOD through the three questionnaires. At a glance,
the three of them appear to follow similar patterns (in
line with the ﬁndings of [23] and [16] that motivation,
engagement, and happiness impact each other) with NOD
teams showing higher levels than that of OD teams in
questionnaire 1, and then NOD levels falling and OD
levels increasing such that in questionnaire 2, OD scores
are higher than those of NOD. The scores then appears to
be relatively stable, but also to come closer to each other,
between questionnaire 2 and 3. Another thing that the
three sub-ﬁgures have in common, is the relatively large
conﬁdence intervals across the board. This was however
somewhat expected as we are dealing with subjective
responses from different individuals.
When also taking table I into consideration, it can
be seen that p-values of motivation, engagement, and
happiness each suggest different aspects of what was
observed from the corresponding graphs, although no
statistically signiﬁcant conclusions can be made. The
two-way ANOVA suggests that development type may
have an impact on motivation over time, as observed

The interviews and the comments on the question-
naires gave insights into the data. Firstly, it became
clear that there were many factors inﬂuencing motivation,
engagement and happiness, as expected. Especially group
dynamics were mentioned a lot, e.g. an anonymous OD-
A team member wrote ”Became very tired of certain
people. [...]” another member wrote ”Good spirits, we
==PAGE:11==
weren’t behind and we were happy with what we had
made”, and a third wrote ”During the beginning and end
it was ﬁne but in the middle I was uncomfortable due to
game director”. In an interview, an OD-B programmer
explained how ”The game director didn’t listen to the
game designer, leading to low motivation”, while a
NOD-C programmer explained how they were ”Very
motivated due to a good team, who had fun hanging out
together” and that it was ”motivating since everybody
liked the game”. There are also several contradictions in
the feedback, such as some expressing how the expert
sessions increased motivation (e.g. according to a OD-
B programmer) and some how it decreased it (e.g.
according to a OD-C programmer).
Although the interviews and questionnaire comments
were generally noisy, there still seemed to be evidence
backing the numerical data (which accordingly had high
conﬁdence intervals). For example in an interview, an
OD-B programmer explained how ”It motivated a lot
in the end when they got YouTube views”. A NOD-
C PM observed that ”Team members were happy in
the beginning”, but then that after a couple of weeks
”Happiness fell [...] People came late, were absent for
1-2 days a week, and didn’t take the work seriously”.
Another NOD-C team member expressed how ”The
director didn’t want to listen to the QA and the rest of
the team and we ended up pivoting too late and too little.
Overall it felt very underwhelming that we didn’t have
any say in the direction of the game – we have not been
making a game for ourselves or for players, but for the
director. This is the main reason why I have continuously
submitted low scores in engagement, motivation and
happiness” and ”Two members of our team were very
demotivated and didn’t show up that often because they
didn’t feel like they were part of the decision making
process and because it felt like we were not making a
game for actual people, but just to satisfy the director”.
Contrary, the member also expressed ”Too much crunch.
Feeling motivated as I see everybody working hard, but
at the same time it is very stressful”.
IV. DISCUSSION
A. Does OD Really Save Time?
OD-A QA reported that his team ”did not use open
development to its full potential, but rather used a light
version, as we felt like it would require too much time
to manage”. This raises the question if OD actually
saves (crunch) time at all, since it does indeed require
time to regularly upload material and to communicate
with the community. A counter argument to that, is that
the lean aspect of it helps cutting down on unpopular
features, as exempliﬁed by a OD-B programmer ”things
that we didn’t need were cut [...] based on the feedback
that our QA gathered”. Additionally, its agile iteration-
based nature helps spreading the work across the entire
project reducing the expected ﬁnal crunch time. Another
argument that OD in fact does save time, is that its
community-based nature would generally decrease the
burden of required marketing efforts around launch, as
an engaged and motivated community will generally help
spread the word [15].
Indie game developer, David Wehl, described in his
GDC talk [22], that he found that, as a solo developer,
the optimal amount of time to spend on sharing content,
such as game play via animations (GIFs), to online com-
munities, such as reddit, and building a community, was
around one third of his total time budget. He stretches
that if he had been spending less time than that he would
never have been able to achieve commercial success.
B. Inﬂuence on Motivation, Engagement, and Happiness
Although our data does not show a statistically sig-
niﬁcant impact of development type on motivation, en-
gagement, and happiness over time, it does show some
positive impact on the three variables, which was also
expressed in the qualitative data, and is consistent with
[21] [22] [20]. Additionally, OD-A QA expressed how
”We did not manage to get a big community [...] a bigger
community would deﬁnitely have increased motivation
more”.
Additionally, we did observe that the three variables
followed each other up and down across development
type and across time, in line with [23] and [16], although
more analysis is needed to determine if they actually
correlated.
C. Is Better Planning More Important than OD?
One thing that was mentioned a few times in the
qualitative feedback was the fact that project managers
affected the amount of crunch, e.g. as mentioned by an
OD-C programmer ”We didn’t crunch as our project
manager was against the concept of it”. One could
argue that this would be the main factor behind the
quantitative data. However, comments like these were
present both for OD and NOD teams, and some of the
teams reporting that their project manager was against
crunch, simultaneously reported levels of crunch at the
end of the development, indicating that unforeseen things
showed up. As described already, this was especially
the case for NOD teams, indicating that, while project
managers do have a great impact on the amount of
crunch, the exposing, agile, and iterative aspects of OD
helps reducing the crunch at the end. Just as planning
and work time and crunch policies have an impact on the
==PAGE:12==
results, so may work culture. Refer to E for an analysis
of this phenomenon.
D. Correlation, but not Causation?
Conﬂicts and miss-management between members of
a group can inﬂuence reported responses, as described
by an OD-B programmer ”Last minute changes by the
director were stressful” and that the biggest struggles
were ”due to the game director”. This highlights that
other factors than OD naturally has an effect on the
experiences of the team members. As a result of that,
one may ask if we actually just observed a correlation
between development type over time and crunch time and
stress, but not an actual causation. Through interviews
and questionnaire comments, we have decreased the risk
of us drawing conclusions based on correlation without
causation. We have ensured that the dynamics of the
data are indeed to some degree caused by the impact
of the development method on developers over time.
Additionally, according to a meta analysis [5] main
causes of work-related stress, physical and psychological
health, and sickness absence, are work demands, long
hours, lack of control over work, lack of participation in
decision making, and poor management. The ﬁndings of
the meta analysis align with the ﬁndings of our paper,
serving as a counter argument to claims that other factors
dominated our results.
Based on his many years in DADIU, where he taught,
listened to, observed and guided the students, the main
teacher at DADIU [25] described his take on causes of
stress, which align well with our ﬁndings and the meta
study: ”Stress can occur from pressure from superiors
or peers, when you are not in charge of your own
schedule, or when the management department fails”
he added that ”when your job position is not stable or
secure one may also experience stress”. He also expresses
that ”Everybody is an individual with unique problems
and sometimes the complexity of life and reality has
impacts that are hard to measure”. We argue that the
many random individual factors are expressed in the large
conﬁdence intervals of the data, and that this can be
considered white noise fading with an increased sample
size.
E. Does OD Work in the Real World?
Another question that arises with this study, is whether
its conclusions generalize and can be applied by stu-
dios in the real world. An argument for that, is that
experienced professionals in the established software,
management and game development industries alike,
argue that people indeed should incorporate agile [10]
[9], lean [11] [10], and open concepts [15] [7] [12] into
their procedures for the same reasons as we argue for.
Additionally, one could argue that OD would have an
even bigger impact in the real world than what it had in
this study, as ﬁrstly, the teams using OD reported that due
to the limited amount of time, with the production only
lasting six weeks, they ”didn’t have enough time to build
a big community” and stated that ”open development
needs time”. Second, none of the interviewed teams had
commercial ambitions with their games, and none were
going to continue the development after their ﬁnal hand-
in, meaning that they were not able to truly experience
the self-driven community-based business model that OD
provides [15].
V. LIMITATIONS AND IMPROVEMENT SUGGESTIONS
A. Small Sample Size
One limitation of this paper is the rather small sample
size of the quantitative data of just 83 questionnaire
responses spread across three questionnaires, and across
two categories, namely OD and NOD respectively. Each
of the questionnaires got between 13 and 25 responses
from OD teams and each got just nine NOD responses.
This is however already somewhat taken into considera-
tion through the relative large conﬁdence intervals.
B. Delayed Responses
Another limitation of our experiment is that the teams
were only answering the questionnaires three times over
a period of six weeks. In the questionnaire, they were
asked to answer based on their experiences the past week.
Moreover, although the questionnaires were handed out
to the PM or QA of each respective team at the same
time via email, arbitrary delays were introduced, since
the PM/QAs in the majority of the cases did not im-
mediately share the questionnaires with their respective
team, leading to us sending out reminders introducing
further delay. This means that the data has rather low
resolution and that it covers three disjoint periods spread
roughly across the production period, potentially skewing
the data and possibly omitting details in the structure of
the underlying ground truth, hidden outside the covered
periods.
C. The Limitations of Voluntary Self-Reporting
In order for the sample data to perfectly represent the
ground truth, either all team members from all teams
would have to ﬁll out every questionnaire, or question-
naires should be ﬁlled by uniformly randomly selected
people. Clearly, not all people ﬁlled questionnaires, and
additionally, the sampling of the data cannot truly be
assumed to be sampled uniformly randomly from the
population, which is evident with OD-A representing
39% of the total responses and NOD-A just representing
==PAGE:13==
2%. It is likely that the people taking the time to
respond potentially were less busy, less stressed or had
another reason to be more likely to respond, meaning
that speciﬁc people may be over-represented in the data.
Additionally, the PMs or QAs in charge of distributing
the questionnaires are directly exposed to it and therefor
more likely to be amongst the respondents.
Another limitation of self-reporting is that it adds a
layer of bias - conscious or unconscious - onto the data.
Future papers may explore ways of resolving this by e.g.
exploring different, more objective ways of measuring
data of participants such as logging what times during
the day things were pushed to the development pipeline
etc.
D. Hard to Guarantee a Leveled Field
Another limitation that is hard to get around, is the
difference in competence amongst the teams. Although
the main teacher of DADIU [25] expresses to attempt
putting the teams together such that each team is of
equal footing, one could argue that it is impossible to
guarantee a completely leveled ﬁeld. Apart from the
different team qualiﬁcations, the different teams are pro-
ducing different games. An OD-B programmer explained
that ”People got demotivated as they didn’t like the
game [...] I was demotivated due to the game director’s
vision”. Additionally, some games take longer to design,
build, tune etc. than others due to genre, ambitions, etc.
However, the main DADIU teacher [25] argues that even
the smallest of games pose their own challenges, and
that teams developing games in genres such as open
world exploration games, that under typical conditions
would take at least half a year to produce, are aware
of the enormous amounts of work that such a game
would require to make, and thus plan, scope, design,
and cut corners accordingly. Additionally, he argues that
several major elements such as game user interfaces
and motion matching systems are big tasks that DADIU
games require regardless of game genre.
E. Each Team used Different Development Processes
One other limitation of this paper is the fact that each
team used a different variant of OD and to a different
degree. Even the teams categorized as NOD teams did
have a QA, conducting user tests to a certain degree,
and additionally each team had combined weekly pre-
sentation, feedback and meeting sessions with industry
experts arranged by DADIU. Furthermore, although just
three of the teams had decided to utilize OD, the three
other teams had been exposed to the concepts of OD
from the beginning and throughout the production, as
some degree of communication between the teams most
likely will take place. Therefor, in future works, one
could attempt to make sure that the NOD teams were
as closed as possible and the OD teams were as open
as possible, or at least that each category has more
ﬁxed guidelines for the development ﬂow. Alternately,
one could attempt measuring openness and investigating
the correlation between openness and crunch, stress,
motivation, engagement, and happiness respectively.
F. T-Tests vs. Post Hoc Tests
For our statistical analysis, t-tests were utilized to
determine signiﬁcant differences between groups of data.
As described in appendix D, a 95% conﬁdence level,
means that we expect 5% of the observations to be
false positives. With many parallel t-tests, it becomes
very likely that a negative has falsely been classiﬁed as
a positive. To resolve this, instead of pure t-tests, one
could utilize post hoc tests [31] [32] that via adjusted
p-values or simultaneous conﬁdence intervals can keep
the expected amount of false positives down regardless
of the amount of tests.
VI. FUTURE WORK
A. Potential Found
In the previous chapter, several limitations of our
experiment were discussed. Accordingly, one may argue
(as done by [33]) that the ﬁrst logical step in the name of
scientiﬁc research should be to point out problems and
potential solutions via small experiments like ours, and
then if potential is found, to conduct large scale studies
thereafter. We argue that potential indeed has been found
in that OD appears to have the same impacts as expected,
namely that it decreases crunch time and stress possibly
increases motivation, engagement, and happiness.
B. Establishing Explanatory Questions and Norms
In future works, one could (in line with [27]) change
the questionnaire such that participants indicate how
much e.g. stress a speciﬁc item caused. Such items
could for example be ”Overtime demands”, ”Dealing
with coworkers” etc. This would help clear the fog
covering the causations of the data. Such items could
also help separate the concepts of intrinsic and extrinsic
motivation, as these are being measured as one in our
questionnaires, although they have different implications,
as intrinsic motivation, according to research [34] [35],
increases engagement and extrinsic motivation oppositely
decreases it.
Other future works could (in line with [27]) attempt
establishing norms and cut-off values for these measured
values. This would allow developers, companies, and
researches to be able to conduct surveys and via the
==PAGE:14==
normative values, providing a baseline (i.e. being able
to determine if stress levels of 2.5 are a good or a bad
sign), track the well being of the developers as well as
what to focus on improving, and evaluating effectiveness
of the attempted improvement strategies.
C. Comparing Success and Process of Video Game
Despite its interests, our experiment does not begin
to answer the most important question an indie studio
wondering whether or not to use Open Development
might have : will this lead to a more successful game?
This is the question we set out to answer when starting
to think about this study, but it turns out that it is way too
complex for a 6 month study. However, while researching
for this paper, we found a possible research subject that
could give interesting results to teams willing to try Open
Development.
As discussed in appendix B, it should be possible
to compare two different games on a three dimensional
scale of openness (although roughly), the three axis being
Access, Transparency and Feedback. With that in mind,
a team with enough time might want to contact as many
studios as possible, to interview them and get data related
to their development process and the success of their
games. The ideal result would be a 3D set of points
where each point represent a game placed on the three
axis, and contains data related to the game’s success. This
would be invaluable for any developer, as it would help
them ﬁnd out where to focus their effort, depending on
which parameter seems to be bringing the most value.
One might ﬁnd out for example that there is a point
of diminishing return on Transparency, where giving
more information to the community does not actually
help improve its engagement, or that the size of the
community tends to grow the further you go on the
Feedback axis.
There would of course be many difﬁculties to over-
come – the conﬁrmation bias for example, as it might be
harder to contact developers of unsuccessful games, or
the deﬁnition of this openness scale, since it seems very
qualitative and subjective – but it would undoubtedly be
very valuable for any studio wondering which process
to chose and how far to go on each aspect of Open
Development.
VII. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we presented our study in which we
compared three video game development teams using
aspects of open development (OD) with three using more
standard waterfall-based development techniques. Quan-
titative data was gathered through online standardized
questionnaires, shared with all of the teams three times
throughout the production period. It showed that NOD
teams in the end of the production generally worked
35.36% more hours per week than in the beginning,
and 37.28% more hours per week than OD teams. It
also showed that OD teams saw a signiﬁcant decrease
in stress levels relative to NOD teams throughout the
production by 0.84 points on the ﬁve-level linear likert
scale. Additionally, our results suggest that OD increases
motivation with up to a 1.01 points, engagement up to
0.47 points, and happiness up to 0.56 points, although
these three ﬁndings are statistically insigniﬁcant when
using 95% signiﬁcance levels. Qualitative data was gath-
ered through interviews (in line with [28]), and largely
backed the quantitative data. We discussed the limitations
of the paper, with one of the most profound ones being
the small sample size of our quantitative data with a
total of 83 questionnaire responses. We argued (as done
by [33]) that small studies like ours should be utilized
as the foundation of future large scale studies.

APPENDIX A
DEFINING CRUNCH TIME
Crunch time is a widespread term and phenomenon in the game development industry. Being an industry term,
it does not have a formal deﬁnition, however, there is broad consensus that it means time periods at which workers
are expected to perform more work than possible if working with the same speed and the same amount of hours
per day as expected under normal conditions. Crunch time, usually occurs as a result of milestones not being met
or deliverables not ﬁnished on time, leading to workers having to cram more work into less time to be able to
deliver on future milestones, while resolving tasks from missed milestones piling up [3] [36] [37]. If managers fail
to resolve the problem, developers are forced to either work more hours than normally (e.i. more hours per day
and/or more days per week) or to cut corners in their work, ultimately resulting in lower quality. During crunch
time, being under pressure and being forced to make such compromises is stressful and can inﬂuence the well being
of the employees [5] [4] [3].
APPENDIX B
DEFINING OPEN DEVELOPMENT
Although the term ”Open Development” has become the most used to describe a certain kind of process in the
video game industry, many teams would argue that they are not actually doing ”Open Development”, but rather their
version of it, using terms such as ”Community informed development” [38], ”Transparent Development” [39], or
”Performative Development” [40] to name a few. Regardless, a study by Luke Thominet [13] was able to determine
that despite having different ways of describing their processes, and being adamant that their version was different,
all these teams used processes with three major common points :
• Access : these teams all gave access to typically internal media during their development, mostly early versions
of their games, but also concept arts, music, design documents etc.
• Transparency : they also presented typically internal information about their development process, such as the
current state of development and future steps, the game design vision and goals of the team, or even information
about the video game industry and the general process of making a video game.
• Feedback : ﬁnally, they all presented ways for their community to give them feedback during development, be
it active (e.g comments about the game), passive (gathering anonymous data from every player), and even in
some cases allowing the community to develop parts of the game (music, factions, tutorials)
The tools used to achieve these common points differed from team to team (blogs, websites, various social-medias
or twitch, to give a non-exhaustive list) as well as how far they went in each category (for transparency for example,
some teams limited themselves to releasing their current version regularly and having a ﬁnal goal for everyone to
see, while others went so far as to stream their entire development process) but the fact remains that these three
points are cited by every team talking about their open development process.
A hard deﬁnition of ”open development” seems therefore difﬁcult to ﬁnd, and we can more sensibly talk about a
three dimensional scale of openness, with the three axis being the degree of access, of transparency, and of feedback
that each team used. Even then, it seems hard to deﬁne a gradation system for each axis, or even to compare
quantitatively two similar processes : does a team sharing their backlog of tasks with their community have a higher
degree of transparency than one sharing their bug tracker ?
Despite all these limitations, it seems possible to qualitatively, roughly compare two different processes on any
of these axis, and therefore place it on this three dimensional graph of openness, if only relatively to other games
and with a high margin of error. It also stands to reason that any process placed sufﬁciently high on all of these
three axis could be qualiﬁed of ”open development”, although the exact limit seems hard to deﬁne, and any process
close to this fuzzy limit might be hard to categorize strictly one way or another.
For the purpose of this study three of the six teams we compared where made aware of this open development
process, and tried to apply it their way, while the three other never heard of it. We made sure afterwards that the
three unaware teams had not accidentally implemented anything close to open development, and that they had stayed
sufﬁciently low on those three axis, so that any advancement by the three open team on any of those scale would be
enough to differentiate them, and that these three processes would qualify as open, insofar as they are more open
than the three base ones. Admittedly they might not qualify as ”open” on a scale that would include all the teams
used in Luke Thominet study, but this could not be avoided considering the small scale of this study, and the very
limited time frame these teams worked with compared to professionals.

APPENDIX C
DADIU
The National Academy of Digital, Interactive Entertainment (DADIU) is a Danish academy that, in collaboration
with universities and art schools in Denmark, educates students in game development over the period of a four
month semester [24].
DADIU comprises six teams, each around 14 people strong consisting of a project manager (referred to as PM),
quality assurance manager (referred to as QA), one to two audio designer(s), game designers, game directors, a
graphics team of around three artists, and a programmer team of around ﬁve programmers. Each team has a lead
team consisting of the project manager, game director, lead programmer, and more. The main teacher at DADIU,
Thomas Howalt [25], is responsible for putting the teams together, and has explicitly expressed his intentions of
putting the teams together based on competence and personal dynamics, such that each team ends up being at equal
footing.
Throughout the semester, the teams go through different workshops and mini-game productions. Based on the
role in their respective team, members join different workshops. For example PMs attend workshops, where they
are guided to encouraged their team to follow a 37 hour week structure with no to limited crunch time, and also
to decide on these rules together with their respective teams. Meanwhile, QAs attend workshops, with a big focus
on bug tracking, but where they also learn about different interview and testing methods, including user experience
testing, as well as hermeneutics (user experience interpretation). The second half of the semester is allocated for the
ﬁnal/graduation game project with one week of pre-pre-production and one week of pre-production, followed by the
production period of six weeks. Each team have the same tasks to fulﬁll and the same requirements, frameworks
and tools at their disposal. Throughout the productions, the teams have weekly presentations, feedback sessions,
and meetings with industry veterans and experts arranged by DADIU.
We followed the teams during their graduation game. The teams made a mobile phone game using the Unity
game development engine, and had an extra requirement of producing a motion matching system for character(s)
of the game.
APPENDIX D
HYPOTHESIS AND P-VALUES
In the two-way ANOVA, the null hypothesis is that the questionnaire number and development type and their
interaction have no impact on the scores. The alternate hypothesis is that they do in fact have an impact. In the
one-way ANOVA, the null hypothesis is that the three questionnaire groups are equal to one another for OD or
NOD respectively. The alternate hypothesis is that not all the groups are equal. In the t-test, the null hypothesis is
that two groups, using different development methods and/or sampled at different points in time, are equal to one
another. The alternate hypothesis is that the two groups not are equal.
A p-value ≤0.05 means that we with a 95% certainty can reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternate
hypothesis, while a p-value > 0.05 means that we fail to reject the null hypothesis. Working under 95% certainty,
means that we expect 5% of the observations to be false positives.
We see that two-way ANOVA only factoring in the questionnaire number and treating both development types as
one group, generally does not see signiﬁcant changes over time. This is to be expected, as OD and NOD oftentimes
go in opposite directions. Likewise, we see that when only factoring in development type and ignoring the concept
of time, we are not getting anywhere near any signiﬁcant values. However, when both factoring in development
type and questionnaire number, we see drastically lower p-values, indicating the signiﬁcance of development types
throughout the production period [31].
APPENDIX E
THE INFLUENCE OF WORK CULTURE
An unaddressed variable that inevitably has had some inﬂuence on the results, is the work culture, as this has
impacts on the stress and well being of developers etc. For example, Denmark often takes the ﬁrst place on Forbes’ list
[41] of best countries for business, and it is often pointed out [42] [43] that the Danish work culture is characterized
by a ﬂat, non-hierarchical structure, an informal environment, ﬁxed working hours that still allow ﬂexibility and
autonomy, a healthy work/life balance, team work, and a compassionate management style, ultimately leading to
less stressed employees [44]. Furthermore, the danish work culture leads to high levels of satisfaction and more
==PAGE:18==
committed workers driven by self-motivation [44] [43]. One should not assume that all teams had the same working
culture, since the culture is both determined by the individuals of the group, thereof especially the managers of the
group, and also by cultural differences of the group, especially as the students are not just Danish, but from other
countries as well. The different work cultures of the groups may have inﬂuenced the data. Oppositely, one may
argue that development type may inﬂuence work culture, as OD helps reducing the necessity of crunch, as already
discussed.
APPENDIX F
PROCESS
A. Motivation
Before starting this project, we were already both interested in the video games industry. Indeed, we had both
taken part of the DADIU program [24], which taught us a lot about the traditional way of making a game, and
we had both produced a few small games of our own. But we had also come to realize that the entrepreneurship
principles that we were taught at DTU in courses like Hardtech Entrepreneurship [45] – the agile methodology, the
lean principles, the necessity of having user feedback and user testing among others – had not really been used
during our time at DADIU.
When looking into it a bit more, it appeared that smaller indie studios where actually experimenting a lot on the
process that they were using, being way less rigid than triple A studios. One talk in particular, from the developers
of Desktop Dungeon [46] retained our attention : they were explaining the ”open development process”, that they
had used with great success to release a proﬁtable game. Other developers where also talking about very similar
processes, that where giving great results, while applying these concepts we had been told about in class.
With that in mind, we decided to look more in depth into this process, and in the indie video game industry in
general, to ﬁnd out what problems could be found in this industry, and whether or not Open Development could
address them. We later on had to limit our goals to something achievable in the time frame of a master thesis, but
our motivation was always to learn from successful indie studios, to maybe one day do it ourselves.
B. Finding a Project
When we started this master thesis, we had in mind to prove that the open development process was better than
the traditional waterfall one for indie studio, because it allowed them to release more commercially viable games.
Our theory was that because of the high feedback rate from this process, developers would be able to quickly discard
ideas that were not resonating with their customers, and focus on the ones that showed promises, exactly like the
agile methodology. They would then further improve these viable ideas during the development process still using
their customers feedback, while at the same time growing their user base organically by word of mouth from their
original players being happy to share a game where they could impact the direction of the development. And when
the game ﬁnally ends up being released, there is already lots of people ready to buy it, because they grew attached
to it during the development process, and they already know that they will like the game. This is the kind of success
story told by developers using open development, and this is why we think at the moment this process is better than
waterfall for indie studios : there is way less uncertainty at release. It is not about developing a game for one year
and hoping that it will sell enough to cover your costs anymore, there are signs during the whole process that shows
you if you’re on the right track and if your game will proﬁtable, and there is way less time wasted on unproﬁtable
ideas.
Unfortunately our initial thesis (for indie studios, Open Development is better than Waterfall to release
commercially viable games) was way to strong, and would have required way more time than a master thesis
would allow. By talking with different teachers, we realised that we could not even think of an experiment that
would help us prove it. The best one we could think about was to do a huge review of games developed by indie
studios, and comparing the proﬁt rates of those using open development versus waterfall. But even this experiment
has three clear pitfalls :
• There is no clear cut between open development and waterfall, more of a sliding scale where some games are
more open than other, meaning that we could not have two clearly differentiated population.
• Overcoming the conﬁrmation bias would be really hard, as it is way easier to ﬁnd and contact successful
developers rather than unsuccessful ones. Finding a representative sample would be a huge challenge.
==PAGE:19==
• This would take way longer than 6 month : since there is no easy way to say if a game was developed with
open development or not, it would mean contacting every studio from our representative sample and through
an interview determining the process they used.
In the end, this idea remains promising for a larger study, but was way outside of our scope.
At that time we also toyed with other experiment ideas : creating two games ourselves with the same initial pitch,
but with the two different process, and comparing their success. We could either develop one each for 5 month, or
one then the other, or even ﬁnd a game that was already developed with one of the process and try starting from
the same pitch with the other process. But all these ideas had obvious pitfalls :
• Two different teams have two different skill sets, and the quality of the resulting games can be entirely dependent
on that instead of the process used.
• Deﬁning the ”success” of a game is really hard : are we talking about the quality ? As deﬁned by players ?
Professional testers ? Or maybe the commercial success ? In terms of net proﬁt ? Proﬁt ratio ? And even then
there is a lot of noise involved in the success (whatever deﬁnition you end up choosing) of a game. Some
games with a really low quality end up making millions (Flappy Bird being an obvious example : at its peak
it was making more than 50 000$ per day [47] despite a score on metacritic of 52/100 [48] and only a few
days of development by one person) while other gems never get discovered.
• Having the same team work twice with the same pitch means the second game is inﬂuenced by the ﬁrst one.
• According to lots of talks from developers having used open development, 5 month is too short to see the
beneﬁts from the process, as you do not have enough time to gather a player base.
But despite none of our ideas being good enough for a master thesis, we found one thing that was promising :
DADIU. in the DADIU program, each year, 6 teams of similar levels make 6 games in the same amount of time,
with the same constraints. This looked a lot like a good experiment ground for us, as it was controlling lots of
variable better than we could ever do (size of the teams, level of the developers, time of the project, target platform),
the team sizes were more realistic (teams of 16 people with various skills on each team, which is closer to the
average indie studio than the two of us working on a game), and they were 6 of them, meaning a more meaningful
analysis than just comparing two projects. So we ended up contacting the DADIU director to get his approval for
our project, and we managed to convince three of the 6 teams to use open development.
We also had to change our initial thesis a lot, since for starter the DADIU teams are not aiming for a commercially
viable game. So we ended up picking 5 clearly deﬁned variables (stress, motivation, engagement, happiness and
workload) with the goal of ﬁnding evidences showing that open development was better for the team mental health
than waterfall (which had been hinted by developers using open development on various interviews).
C. Fewer Questionnaires Used than Intended
To monitor the effects of OD, we sent out questionnaires to the teams. We communicated with the teams through
either their PM or the QA via email, who then communicated to the rest of their respective team. The questionnaires
were shared through a URL to an online google form [26]. In the beginning, it was the plan to send out one
questionnaire to each of the teams every week, such that we would get as many data points in the time domain as
possible. However, as several teams either did not respond at all, or just very few people responded, we decided to
send out reminders. As described in section V-B, it took up to several days from the questionnaires and reminders
were sent out till we gathered enough data for a certain period. Since the data then ended up spanning two weeks
rather than one week as intended, there was only time to send out out three questionnaires throughout the six week
period production. As reminders were sent out, and as fewer people responded to the second questionnaire than
to the third, we also got the impression, that spamming the busy teams with questionnaires could discourage them
further from responding.

APPENDIX G
COMPARISON TO OTHER MEDIAS
While researching Open Development, it became obvious that this process took a lot from the advancement seen
in software development : it is heavily inspired by the agile methodology, iterating early and often. But there remains
an artistic component in the making of a video game, that cannot be found in software development. Video Games
can actually be compared to movies, with some examples even being said to be ”playable movies” [49]. One can
then wonder if there are ideas to steal from the movie industry.
After some research, and despite what one can initially assume, it turns out that the movie industry does not
have an established formula that guarantees commercial success, far from it. In fact, seven out of ten movies end
up losing money, two break even and only one makes a proﬁt [50]. As for the process used, it is still very waterfall,
and has nearly not changed since the beginning of movie making. It is a team effort, as in video games, but there
is very little feedback from the end user until nearly the end of the development process. This means that there is a
very high uncertainty at the release of a movie, and that even big budget block busters from established publisher
regularly end up losing money. It sometime happens that the feedback gathered from the these early feedbacks are
so bad that the movie ends up being reworked [51], but this is exceptional.
The Video Game industry then seems to be distancing itself from these waterfall process, and therefore from
the Movie industry. Indeed, while movies are often attributed to their directors, eclipsing the work done by entire
teams, in the indie sphere, we often hear about ”studios”, ”teams”, ”devs”, that is the entire team and not only one
person. But it seems that the inﬂuence is actually going in the other direction : Thomas Howalt, while talking about
the process of writing his next theater play, told us that he had been trying it in front of live audiences, reworking
what wasn’t working while keeping what got strong reactions, and going so far as to talk about ”Open Rehearsal”
[25]. Maybe then will the movie industry end up implementing these ideas as well.
Interestingly, it seems that this is starting to happen, as discussed in a recent article on NEW FOREST FILM
CO [52] defending ”Agile Film Making”. It is also interesting to note that this kind of discussion happened in the
video game industry around 5 years earlier, with for example an article on Gamasutra in 2014 [53] defending the
fact that open development was not design by committee, far from it.

The questions asked during the interviews of DADIU team members after the development period are listed at the
bottom of this section. The answers are recorded and can be found via the following URL:

Notice that after the ﬁrst interview, the structure became more organic, as it felt rather mechanic to begin with.
This led to more nuanced and in depth comments and insights during the rest of the interviews. Before writing
down the questions, a list of questions that we needed answered was formulated:
1) What are the different teams’ understanding of open development and how did they use it in their own
words?
2) To what degree did the teams use open development (using our deﬁnition of open development)?
3) Can we validate/invalidate our quantitative data with qualitative data?
4) Other quantitative data that might give us more insights.
The interview questions followed asked questions of the three OD pillars described in I (Access, Transparency,
and Feedback), and attempted additionally to get direct comments regarding the questionnaires.
1) In general, how would you describe your game development method and ﬂow?
2) (Question for OD Teams) In your words, how would you describe open development?
3) (Question for OD Teams) How did your team use open development?
4) (Transparency Questions) How were you present online?
• What social media platforms?
• What kinds of content or information did you share?
• How much and how often did you share content?
• How many people did you manage to get to follow you online?
• How engaged were your online followers?
• Were you present or did you communicate with users elsewhere than online?
• What effects did your presence have (pros, cons, worth it)?
5) (Access Questions) How were you present online?
• If so, how did you share or distribute it?
• How often did you distribute a new build?
• How many people did you reach out to?
• At what point did you start distributing the game and getting feedback?
• Did any of this change over time?
• What effects did sharing your game have (pros, cons, worth it)?

– Bugs
– Game design
– User experience
– Other?
