2. Abstract 
 
Soil microbes are living in highly complex structures in nature where the interactions in the 
community can facilitate antimicrobial resistance, plant growth promotion and even microbial 
biocontrol. In order to utilise the beneficial traits of microbial communities it is important to 
understand the relationships between the community members. For this reason, in the past 
decades researchers have applied simplified model systems to investigate and characterise 
these interactions.  
In this study a semi-synthetic microbial community was constructed consisting of Pedobacter 
sp., Rhodococcus sp., Stenotrophomonas sp., Chryseobacterium sp. and Pseudomonas 
protegens DTU 9.1. This project aimed at investigating the role of specific secondary 
metabolites from P. protegens in the model system by applying different secondary metabolite 
deficient mutants for the experiments. The mutations were targeting the production of the 
pyoluteorin, 2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol and an unknown type I polyketide. 
The synthetic community was constructed in a solid phytagel-alginate polymer bead system. It 
was demonstrated that pyoluteorin had an antimicrobial activity towards all the synthetic 
community members in a classic inhibition assay screen. However, investigating the 
constructed model system, the presence of pyoluteorin did not have a significant influence on 
the composition of the synthetic community.  
In addition, it was elucidated that in the constructed system a cyclic lipopeptide, orfamide A, 
produced by P. protegens DTU 9.1 is degraded by the synthetic community. In order to shed 
light on this mechanism, an orfamide A knock-out mutant was introduced to the SynCom as 
well.  Throughout the project it was shown that Rhodococcus sp. has possible degrading 
abilities by linearizing the cyclic lipopeptides via hydrolysis. However, the complete 
degradation of orfamide A by peptidase activity was not discovered in this study.  
It can be concluded that microbial communities are highly complex structures that has to be 
extensively studied in order to have a glance of the interactions and the dynamics in the 
system. The construction of semi-synthetic microbial model systems can lead to an improved 
understanding of the interactions occurring in microbial communities. However, this field of 
science still has its limitations since several factors have to be taken into account, such as 
environmental conditions, presence of pathogens and the host organisms. For future studies it 
is extremely important to further investigate microbial interactions in communities, since this 
knowledge can lead to the utilization of these systems in practice by plant growth promoting 
or even as biocontrol agents in the fields.

3. Introduction  
 
3.1 Microbial communities 
 
Microbial communities are complex systems that consist of thousands of different 
microorganisms. The term microbial consortia defines not only the microorganisms that are 
living together in a specific environment, but also the labour division between them and how 
they can work together to fulfil their main role in the community [1]. This means that in a 
microbial consortium, microbes live together and can interact with each other in several ways, 
such as mutualism, commensalism, predation, competition, amensalism and neutralism [2].  
 
 
Figure 1.: Motifs of microbial interactions. The interactions between the microbiome can be diverse. The 
relationships between the community members can be beneficial (+), disadvantageous (-) and even neutral (0) [2].  
 
The most important questions when analysing a microbial community are: ‘Who is there?’ and 
‘What is their role in the system?’. It is crucial for a scientist investigating microbial 
communities to try to elucidate the interactions between the community members for a better 
understanding of the role of the community [1]. Examining the composition and the 
relationships between the members of the microbial consortia can lead to a better understanding 
of natural communities and utilise this knowledge in future research [3]. 
Microbes living in microbial communities can be found almost in every segment of the world 
and have diverse roles in nature [4]. When examining a microbial community there are several 
factors that have to be taken into account, such as the relationships between the different 
members, environmental conditions and the dynamics of the community. Furthermore, each 
member has particular roles to accomplish in the community, which results in a stable natural 
system that has a well-defined environment [5]. It is vital to our understanding of microbial 
communities to investigate these relationships and the roles of the microbiome in order to 
determine their properties and even their limitations. Ecological studies showed that microbial 
communities in the soil can provide beneficial traits to its own environment. There are several 
microbes that are able to promote plant growth and even serve as a barriers against plant 
pathogens [6]–[8]. The plant pathogenic microbes are genuinely hard to control, even with the 
current usage of pesticides. Furthermore, the excessive usage of these chemicals can cause 
great environmental problems and can be even harmful for health [9], [10].  
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
 By characterizing the microbial communities, it could be possible to apply the beneficial traits 
of the microbes in the fields. Thus, describing microbial interactions can lead to supplement or 
even substitute chemical pesticides used to avoid polution and increase the yield of agriculture 
[8]. 
Utilising plant beneficial microbes might be able to overcome the problems with using 
chemical pesticides in the future. As it was mentioned before, some mutualistic 
microorganisms might serve as biocontrol agents themselves by producing antimicrobial 
compounds to fight against pathogens. Good examples for bacteria with biocontrol activity are 
various members of Streptomyces, Bacillus and Pseudomonas spp. [10].

3.2 Synthetic microbial communities 
 
In nature, microorganisms can be found in highly complex communities, thus influencing their 
environment by the co-occurring interaction between them. Analysing the isolated 
microorganism in monoculture can give an indication about the potential characteristics of a 
specific strain, such as phenotypic traits and production of antimicrobial metabolites. However, 
these microbes must be studied in more complex systems as well. Investigating singe isolates 
can give an indication about the nature of the specie in question. However, it is possible, that 
when the same specie encounters a microbial consortium, its properties could be significantly 
altered due to a highly regulated communication system between the community members. The 
microbial behaviour exerted in nature is highly dependent on the environmental conditions as 
well as the interactions between the community members. All community members together 
are responsible to fulfil their ecological role, such as plant growth promotion (PGP) or acting 
as biocontrol stains and facilitating the nutrition cycle as mentioned above. To be able to 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
understand how these communities work, it is important to take into account not only the 
individuals of the community but also the whole niche. However, these studies can be 
challenging because of the several factors and conditions that have to be considered in order to 
get a glance of microbial interactions [11]. 
One way of studying the microbial consortia is applying more simple model structures. These 
model systems can facilitate the better understanding of the most prominent properties of 
microbial communities in a well-defined and controlled environment mimicking a similar 
ecosystem as it is in the nature [2] [5]. In the past decade due to the technological development 
in synthetic biology and DNA sequencing, it became possible to characterise composition of 
microbial communities as well [2], [12].  
There are several approaches of investigating synthetic microbial communities (SynCom). The 
top-bottom method focuses on the functional determination of a designed community [2], [13]. 
In this case the synthetic communities are model systems where the performance, dynamics 
and the stability can be observed. The importance of this method is that this way different 
communities with the same function can be compared and the stability of the system can be 
more easily measured. As an example of this strategy, Kang et al. were working on finding a 
microbial consortia that is able to degrade keratinous materials. They constructed a simplified 
synthetic community called KMCG6, where the members were isolated from river bank soil 
sample and selected according to their  keratin degrading abilities [14].  
Another method is called the bottom-up approach when the artificial consortia is used for 
studying the conditions that are essential for the interaction patterns between the community 
members, such as symbiosis or competition, for example, for food source [2], [13]. For 
instance, Kong et al. created a two-strain synthetic consortia (two genetically engineered L. 
lactis, CmA and CMB) where each constructed model represented different mode of 
interactions, such as commensalism, amensalism, neutralism, cooperation, competition and 
predation [15]. 
The simplest studies involve the investigation of two-member communities. These models can 
give an indication what could be the fundamental mechanisms between the species. Minty et 
al. constructed a microbial community where the biological functions were divided between a 
cellulase secreting fungi, Trichoderma reesei, and an engineered Escherichia coli strain that 
metabolizes soluble saccharides for the conversion of plant biomass into biofuels [16]. 
However, if these microbial interactions have to be studied as a part of the ecosystem, they are 
over simplified models, they should be more extensively investigated in a more complex 
system. [11].  
In more complex synthetic communities, there are several factors that need to be taken into 
account. In this case the main aim is to examine and try to elucidate what could be the 
ecological role of each of the members to get a clearer picture from the communities in nature.  
Niu et al. assembled a greatly simplified, yet representative microbial community to study their 
effect on maize seedlings. In this study they selected 7 specific soil bacteria, that were isolated 
from maize roots and investigated the role of each of the members [6], [7] . They were using a 
method called “tailoring” to find out the role of each of the members in the synthetic 
community. This means that they were adding, removing, or replacing members of the 
community in order to see the change in the composition of the system. What they saw was 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
that in maize roots the removal of Enterobacter cloacae led to the complete loss of the 
community, suggesting that this bacterium is an important driver for community stability. 
In another study by Butaite et al. [17] they showed a cheating resistance pattern in terms of 
pyoverdine production of Pseudomonas strains. Pyoverdine is an NRPS derived secondary 
metabolite which is a well-known iron scavenging siderophore. They demonstrated the 
interactions between the pyoverdine producer and the non-producer strains. They concluded 
that the producers and non-producers can co-occur in the microbial communities where the 
non-producers are able to exploit the siderophores, thus gaining fitness advantage in the system. 
However, they found some pyoverdines produced by the community, that rather inhibited the 
growth of the non-producers. This shows the complexity and the diversity of microbial 
regulatory systems and how microbes can communicate with each other in order to establish a 
dynamically stable community.  
In an additional example de Boer et al. [18] found that in a 4 species community of 
Brevundimonas sp., Luteibacter sp., Pedobacter sp. and Pseudomonas sp. the mix of the 
different species led to a stronger growth reduction of Fusarium culmorum, Trichoderma 
harzianum and Rhizoctonia solani, three plant pathogenic fungi. They concluded that the 
community members all together triggered antibiotic production due to interspecific 
interactions, however, individually did not show inhibition towards the pathogens.  
Another example is the acquisition of antimicrobial resistance in a microbial community, while 
single members would die to exposure towards the same antibiotic. This phenomenon is called 
cooperative resistance [19]. Frost et al. showed in their study that in the presence of 
carbenicillin cooperative resistance was observed between the resistant and the susceptible 
colonies of Pseudomonas aeruginosa. In contrary, applying different conditions in terms of 
growth and the application of other antibiotics, only the resistant strains were protected and 
gained fitness advantage [20].  
It can be seen from the examples mentioned above that investigating the interactions between 
microbes leads to findings unobtainable from monoculture studies that greatly enhances our 
understanding of natural microbiomes. A comprehensive understanding of the microbial 
communities can facilitate the utilization of microbes in the fields as growth promoting and 
biocontrol agents [3]. While this field of study is promising and have a lot of potential to 
explore natural microbial interactions, it still has its limitations. In many cases the model 
system might not be able to represent all the conditions in nature. In addition, it is challenging 
to make exact conclusions about the role of the community members since the composition 
and the function of the natural microbial communities can be highly dependent on different 
environmental conditions [2]. 
 
3.3 Construction of a semi-synthetic microbial community 
 
In this study we were focusing on constructing a semi-synthetic microbial consortium 
consisting of 5 members: Pesobacter sp., Rhodococcus sp., Stenotrophomonas sp., 
Chryseobacterium sp. and Pseudomonas protegens DTU 9.1. The purpose of the study was to 
investigate the role of specific secondary metabolites produced by P. protegens DTU 9.1 in an 
artificial microbial consortium.

3.3.1 Pedobacter sp.  
 
The genus Pedobacter of Bacteroides phylum currently consists of 79 Gram-negative species 
[21], [22]. The genus was first introduced in 1998 by Steyn et al. and described as bacteria 
using heparin as carbon and nitrogen source, Pedobacter heparinus [23]. The members of the 
genus are motile or non-motile, rod-shaped and usually have a yellow or pink colour [22].  
Pedobacter species can be isolated from various environments: principally from soil but also 
from water, cooled food, fish, compost and sludge [21]. In addition, psychrophilic and 
psychrotolerant Pedobacter species can be found in extreme environmental conditions, such as 
glaciers from the Arctic and Antarctic areas.  
There have been numerous studies investigating the different properties of several members of 
Pedobacter genus. Garbeva et al. studied the expression of antimicrobial compounds of P. 
fluorescence Pf0-1 in the presence of a member of Bacillus sp., Brevundimonas sp. and 
Pedobacter sp. V48. According to their findings P. fluorescence was more effectively 
producing antimicrobial compounds against plant pathogenic fungi in a co-culture with the 
Brevundimonas sp. and the Pedobacter sp. but not with Bacillus sp. This study has revealed 
that microbial interactions between the different strains can give the microbial consortia 
ecological advantage to fight against plant pathogens for instance [24].  
Furthermore, in another study phenotypic changes were observed in the co-culture of 
Pedobacter V48 and Pseudomonas fluorescens Pf0-1 according to McCully et al. They showed 
that under certain conditions (using low nutrient agar plates) the co-culture of Pedobacter V48 
and P. fluorescens was able to acquire a swarming phenotype on hard agar surfaces, which is 
usually not conducive to swarming motility due to the low water content [11] [25]. 
 
3.3.2 Rhodococcus sp.  
 
Rhodococci belong to the phylum Actinomycetales that comprises of nonmotile aerobic Gram-
positive bacteria that have rod-coccus growth cycle. The members of the genus are able to live 
in several different environmental habitats, such as soil and waste water [26], [27]. The 
biotechnological importance of Rhodococci arise from their distinctive ability to degrade broad 
spectrum of organic compounds. Rhodococci are used in the industry for steroid production, 
biofuel desulfurization and the production of acrylamide and acrylic acid [26].  
Banerjee et al. reported that some Rhodococcus species (Rhodococcus N-774 and R. rhodcrous 
J1) produce nitrile-converting enzymes that can be applied in the synthesis of pharmaceuticals 
or chemicals, as well as in bioremediation. They are using nitriles as sole source of carbon and 
nitrogen [28].  
Recently, Chen et al. isolated a Rhodococcus strain from the Antarctic ice which was able to 
degrade diesel oil, which might be utilised in the future for bioremediation purposes [29].  
Similarly, Chaudhary et al. isolated a novel specie called Rhodoccus olei from oil contaminated 
soil, that was able to degrade different kinds of petroleum oils [30].

Despite of all the beneficial applications and the undoubted importance of Rhodococci, the 
members of the genus are still not well characterised yet [26].  
 
3.3.3 Stenotrophomonas sp. 
 
The genus of Stenotrophomonas was first defined by the characterization of Stenotrophomonas 
maltophilia (formerly Pseudomonas maltophilia and Xanthomonas maltophilia). The genus is 
consisting of Gram-negative rod-shaped bacteria and appear in a broad range of ecosystems as 
well, however, they can predominantly be found in soil and in plants as endophytes [31]–[33]. 
Stenotrophomonas maltophilia is often a dominant member of microbial communities in the 
fields. The representatives of the genus have a significant role in bioremediation and plant 
growth promotion. Even, some species are able to produce antimicrobial compounds, hence 
have plant protecting abilities against pathogens [32]. According to the latest research, there 
are 17 species identified within the genus Stenotrophomonas [31].  
According to the study of Alexander et al., Stenotrophomonas maltophilia is facilitating the 
growth and increase the antioxidant levels of peanut crops by nitrogen fixation under stress 
conditions. This was the interactions between plants and PGPB can enhance plant health and 
soil fertility [34]. It is important to note, that Stenotrophomonas maltophilia is not only a 
PGPB, but also an opportunistic multidrug resistant human pathogen [31].

3.3.4 Chryseobacterium sp.  
 
The genus Chryseobacterium belonging to Bacteroidetes phylum was first characterized by 
Vandamme et al. in 1994  [36].  The genus is composed of at least 117 species with published 
names [37]. The members of the genus are Gram-negative, rod-shaped, non-motile aerobic 
bacteria that are usually pigmented as well, ranging from yellow to orange. The representatives 
of the genus live in a wide range of natural habitats, such as soil, roots, fresh and waste water, 
sludge and dairy products [35] [37].  According to Dahal et al. some members of the 
Chryseobacterium genus are showing prebiotics, antioxidant, and protease production. 
Furthermore, they were able to isolate a strain (RP-3-3T), that was showing antimicrobial activity 
against pathogens, such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa KACC 10185. However, there are some 
members which are identified as human pathogens (such as C. indologenes and C. gleum)  [37], 
[38].   
 
3.3.5 Pseudomonas sp.  
 
The genus Pseudomonas comprises typically rod-shaped, aerobic Gram-negative bacteria, that 
exists in diverse ecological environments, such as soil, plant, water and animal tissues. The 
moving abilities are connected to the multiple polar flagella. The members of the genus are 
able to adapt to limitations of all kinds due to their complex regulatory systems. There are 
several members of the genus that has medical and agricultural importance due to the wide 
range of secondary metabolites produced [10], [39]. 
Several soilborne Pseudomonas species, such as Pseudomonas protegens, Pseudomonas putida 
and Pseudomonas fluorescence, have symbiotic relationship with plants, thus they are 
described as plant growth promoting bacteria. Some strains have an extensive repertoire of 
growth-promoting capabilities, such as nitrogen fixation, phosphate solubilization, innate immune 
response activation. In addition, they can even have biocontrol abilities against plant pathogens, 
thus facilitating disease suppression. In return the plants are assisting the nutrient acquisition 
of their environment  [10], [40].  
There are several well-known secondary metabolites produced by soil Pseudomonas that had 
been extensively studied because of their unquestionable importance in agriculture. Relevant 
antibiotic compounds produced by Pseudomonas are, for instance, phenazines, 
phloroglucinols, pyoluteorin, pyrrolnitrin, cyclic lipopeptides, phenazines and hydrogen 
cyanide. The biocontrol compounds had been identified to inhibit primarily fungal and bacterial 
pathogens in vitro, however, in certain cases they were bioactive against higher organisms as 
well [10], [41].  
 
 
 
==PAGE:13==

Figure 4.: Examples of bioactive secondary metabolites produced by Pseudomonas sp. [10], [39].  
 
There are numerous studies that were investigating the antimicrobial activity of Pseudomonas.  
Cesa-Luna et al. were demonstrating that P. protegens EMM-1 is a possible inhibitor of 
multidrug resistant (MDR) bacteria, such as Klebsiella sp. and Streptococcus sp., and some 
pathogenic fungi that has agricultural importance, such as Botrytis sp. and Aspergillus sp.[42].  
In another study by Cabanás et al. the natural biocontrol agents were investigated against 
Verticillium wilt, a fungal disease in olive plants. Characterizing the rhizosphere of the plant, 
they found that the representative of Pseudomonas sp, (strain PICF141) has significant 
biocontrol activity against Verticillium dahliae due to the production of pyoverdine and HCN 
[43].  
In addition, Tewari and Arora described in their research that fluorescent Pseudomonas sp. 
(PF17) showed antagonistic activity against a native phytopathogen, Macrophomina 
phaseolina. They showed that the secretion of lytic enzymes, such as chitinase and β-1,3 
glucanase were by Pseudomonas PF17 was responsible to degrade the fungal walls of M.  
phaseolina [44].  
Pseudomonas protegens (formerly Pseudomonas fluorescence) is a well characterised member 
of the genus. P. protegens is a rhizosphere bacterium that has an important role in ecological 
studies. It had been previously described by several researches that P. protegens has a 
significant biocontrol activity by producing many different secondary metabolites [41], [42], 
[45]. Secondary metabolites are defined as small organic molecules that are not essential 
compounds for its own producer in terms of growth, reproduction and development [46].  
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
In this research, we are focusing on polyketide, such as pyoluteorin, 2,4-diacetilphloroglucinol 
(2,4-DAPG), and a cyclic lipopeptide, orfamide A, secondary metabolite production of 
Pseudomonas protegens DTU 9.1.  
The molecules belonging to the group of polyketides are structurally diverse antibiotic 
compounds. The biosynthesis of polyketides is catalysed by different types of polyketide 
synthase (PKS) enzymes [47]. There are three types of PKSs that can be distinguished: Type 
I, where the molecules are large modular proteins, Type II, that has monofunctional PKSs and 
Type III where the enzymes have only one active site cysteine and there is no acyl carrier 
protein in contrast to the previous subgroups [39].  
Pyoluteorin (plt) is a secondary metabolite that has a strong antimicrobial activity against plant 
pathogen fungi and bacteria as well. It is secreted by several Pseudomonas strains, among 
them, P. protegens as well [48], [49]. Pyoluteorin belongs to the group of polyketide 
synthetases which consists of a resorcinol ring with a pyrrole group and two chlorine atoms 
[45], [50]. The biosynthesis of the compound is facilitated by 10 enzymes encoded by 10 genes 
of the gene cluster called plt. PltABCDEFGLM are structural genes, where pltB and pltC are 
encoding Type I polyketide synthetase and pltA, pltD and pltH are encoding halogenases. 
Furthermore, pltG is responsible for the expression of thioestherase and the gene cluster is 
regulated pltR gene [39], [48]. 
In addition to pyoluteorin, 2,4-diacetyl-phloroglucynol (2,4-DAPG) is another biocontrol 
compound produced by several Pseudomonas strains. 2,4-DAPG is associated with suppressive 
effects against numerous plant pathogens, such as bacteria, fungi, oomycetes, and nematodes.  
The biosynthetic pathway of 2,4-DAPG starts with phloroglucinol production and two step 
acetylation. As previous studies have shown, there is a highly conserved gene cluster, phl, 
which is responsible for 2,4-DAPG production. PhlACBD are the biosynthetic genes of the 
cluster. Furthermore, phlE is responsible for the efflux, phlG for the degradation and phlH and 
phlF are responsible for regulation [39] [46]. 
Some Pseudomonas strains are able to produce different types of cyclic lipopeptides (CLPs). 
CLPs are amphiphilic compounds consisting of cyclic oligopeptide lactone ring and a fatty acid 
tail [51]. It has been elucidated by previous research that these compounds can have broad 
spectra of ecological roles. They can have a mode of action as biocontrol agents, as in some 
cases they are able to insert into membranes of fungi or other bacteria, thus showing 
antimicrobial activity by cell lysis. Furthermore, cyclic lipopeptides can have a role as toxins 
in phytopathogenic pseudomonas as well as surfactant agents [10].  
The orfamide type cyclic lipopeptides produced by Pseudomonas are typically biosurfactant 
compounds that are playing a role in the lysis of different plant pathogens, such as Rhizoctonia 
solani that causes root rot disease on bean plants [51]. These molecules are synthetised by non-
ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs) and encoded by an gene cluster containing three 
adjacent genes: ofaABC [39], [51].  
According to Sobrero et al. the main CLP produced by P. protegens is called orfamide A [52] 
[53].  Orfamide A is revealed to be a compound that is responsible for the decrease of surface 
tension of water and is able to facilitate the swarming motility of its own producer [51], [52], 
[54]. In addition, Jang et al. showed in their study that orfamide A even have an antimicrobial 
activity against aphids, such as Myzus persicae [54].

to investigate the role of specific antimicrobial secondary metabolites of P. protegens DTU 
9.1. The researchers in the Center of Microbial Secondary Metabolites (CeMiSt) have 
previously developed a stable community of natural bacterial isolates, namely with Pedobacter 
sp., Rhodococcus sp., Stenotrophomonas sp. and Chryserobacterium sp. As a part of this study, 
we wanted to investigate P. protegens DTU 9.1 secondary metabolite production by 
introducing it to this semi-synthetic community. For this purpose, mutant variants of P. 
protegens DTU 9.1 were applied where entire biosynthetic gene clusters of polyketide type 
metabolites were knocked out previously.

Throughout the project, the composition of the synthetic community was investigated in the

We hypothesized that the differences between the systems should give indications about the 
role of the specific secondary metabolites in the community. Thus, this simplified community 
may serve as an insightful system where the principles behind the community assembly and 
possible beneficial or disadvantageous effects can be investigated. 
In agriculture the production and sustainability are currently the most significant concerns. 
Investigating the relationships between the members of a simplified model system of microbial 
communities can give an indication about their role in nature. For this reason, it is extremely 
important to have a better understanding of the interactions between the members of the 
synthetic consortia that resembles natural communities. Thus, this knowledge can be utilised 
in the future by applying microbes in agriculture as biocontrol and plant growth promoting 
agents in order to reduce the usage chemical pesticides that has adverse effect of the 
environment [9].

4.1 Bacterial strains 
 
The bacterial strains of the synthetic community consisting of Pedobacter sp., Rhodococcus 
sp., Stenotrophomonas sp. and Chryseobacterium sp. were provided by the researchers the 
CeMiSt Center. The species were isolated from the soil site P5 in Dyrehaven, a Danish natural 
reserve, and a stable semi-synthetic community was constructed for the experiments.   
P. protegens DTU 9.1 was isolated from the same soil site (P5) and different knock-out 
mutations were introduced by allelic replacement previously of this project. The mutations 
targeted the secondary metabolite production of P. protegens DTU 9.1: pyoluteorin, 2,4-DAPG 
and an unknown Type I. polyketide (see Table 1.).  
 
4.2 Inhibition assay with the overlay technique 
 
Overnight (O/N) cultures were made from the bacterial strains which secondary metabolite 
production and bioactivity was be inspected in Luria-Bertani (LB) media and incubated 
overnight at 30°C. The OD600 of the O/N cultures was measured and set to 1. 10 μl of these 
cultures were spotted on a 10% TSA plates. The cultures were incubated for 2 days on room 
temperature (22 °C). Afterwards, the samples went through chloroform treatment to kill the 
bacterial cultures on the plates. This way only the secondary metabolites that were produced 
throughout the incubation were able to interact. A 4X4 paper towel was placed on a glass petri-
dish that soaked with 400 μl chloroform. The samples with the bacterial spots were placed on 
the top of the glass petri-dishes with the paper towel soaked in chloroform and incubated for 
30 mins. The plates were placed under the fume hood in order to let the chloroform evaporate 
for additional 30 minutes. 
O/N cultures of the target bacteria were prepared in LB-media as well and incubated at 30 °C 
overnight. The next day the OD600 was set to 2 and 500 μl was measured in 6 ml 42 °C soft 
agar (10% TSA containing 0,3% agar). The soft agar with the target bacteria was vortexed and 
poured on the top of the plates which went through the chloroform treatment previously. The 
plates were incubated for 2 days at 22°C and the inhibition radius was inspected and 
documented afterwards.

4.9 Creating orfamide A mutant with two-step allelic exchange 
 
 
The mutant allels that are consisting of upstream and downstream homolgy regions flanking 
by the target gene (this case ofaA) was synthetised and cloned into allelic exchange plasmid, 
namely PNJ1. The PNJ1 plamid, which contains a tetracyclin resistant gene (TCR) and a gene 
that encodes the expression of levanosucrase (sacB), was introduced to the recipient cell by 
conjugation. The single-crossover mutants which were site-specifically integrated to the 
chromosome were selected by antibiotic resistance towards tetracyclin. A second counter 
selection was applied by the expression of levanosucrase facilitated by the sacB gene. The 
presence of levanosucrase in media containing sucrose catalyses the synthesis of levan 
polysaccharides, which are highly toxic for bacterial cells. Thus, only the doulbe-crossover 
mutants were viable in the selective environment. Therefore, the two selection step allelic 
replacement results either returning the WT or integrating the mutant allele [55].

4.10 MALDI imaging 
 
4.10.1 Sample preparation 
 
Bacterial strains were cultured on thin 10% TSA plates (1-1.5 mm) at 22 oC. After 3 days of 
incubation, the microbial colony and surrounding agar was sectioned and transferred mounted 
on an IntelliSlides conductive tin oxide glass slide (Bruker Daltonik GmbH).  The sample was 
covered with matrix by spraying 1.75 mL of a matrix solution in a nitrogen atmosphere. The 
matrix solution was 2,5-dihydrobenzoic acid (DHB) of 20 mg/mL concentration in 
ACN/MeOH/H2O (70:25:5, v/v/v).  
 
3.10.2 MALDI Imaging Mass Spectrometry (IMS) 
 
Sample was dried in a desiccator overnight prior to IMS measurement. The sample was then 
subjected to timsTOF flex (Bruker Daltonik GmbH) mass spectrometer for MALDI IMS 
acquisition. Calibration was done using red phosphorus. The sample was run in positive MS 
scan mode with 100 µm raster width and a mass range of 100-2000 Da. Briefly, a photograph 
of the colonies was loaded onto Fleximaging software, three teach points were selected to align 
the background image with the sample slide, measurement regions were defined, and the 
automatic run mode was then employed. The settings in the timsControl were as follow: Laser: 
imaging 100 µm, Power Boost 3.0%, scan range 26 µm in the XY interval, and laser power 
90%; Tune: Funnel 1 RF 300 Vpp, Funnel 2 RF 300 Vpp, Multipole RF 300 Vpp, isCID 0 eV, 
Deflection Delta 70 V, MALDI plate offset 100 V, quadrupole ion energy 5 eV, quadrupole 
los mass 100 m/z, collosion energy 10 eV,  focus pre TOF transfer time 75 µs, prepulse storage 
8 µs. After data acquisition, the data was analyzed using SCiLS software.  
 
4.11 Molecular Networking  
 
Molecular networks were created using the Global Natural Products Social (GNPS) online 
platform (gnps.ucsd.edu). Data were first converted to .mgf format with MassHunter software 
and uploaded to the GNPS analysis workflow, with activated MSCluster. The precursor ion 
mass tolerance was set to 0.05 Da and to a fragment ion tolerance of 0.05 Da. Molecular 
networks were generated using 6 minimum matched fragment ions, 6 minimum cluster size, 
and a cosine score of 0.7. Data were open and visualized using Cytoscape 3.4.0 software.

In this research a semi-synthetic community was constructed using 5 different bacterial species.

Formerly, the researchers of the CeMiSt Center were constructed a stable synthetic community

to optimize the system to maintain the stability. This way the specific secondary metabolite 
production (pyoluteorin, 2,4-DAPG and unknown Type I polyketide) could be characterized in 
the synthetic microbial consortium. The stability of the community was inevitable for the 
investigation of the secondary metabolites produced by P. protegens DTU 9.1.

After inspecting the individual interactions between P. protegens DTU 9.1 (WT and mutants) 
and the representatives of the synthetic community, the aim was to investigate the interactions 
of a community where all the members are present in a system at the same time. In this study 
we chose to implement a soil mimicking solid system, that consists of phytagel-alginate 
polymer beads. This system places the community in an environment where the microbes have 
spatial freedom of moving towards or even further away from each other. 
The advantage of this bead system, that it resembles the environmental conditions of nature 
and how the microbes might be able to encounter with each other. In this designed model, 
which is based on the study of Ma et al. [58] the microbes have the ability to escape from each 
other and even have a spatial distribution in the system.  
The constructed synthetic community was incubated throughout 7 days and samples were taken 
on day 1, 4 and 7. The samples from the bead system were plated out and the colonies were 
counted to see the distribution of each of the representatives of the model with the passing time.  
Firstly, P. protegens DTU 9.1 WT and mutants were introduced to the bead system 
individually, on order to see if they were able to establish in the system. It was concluded, that 
all the variants of P. protegens DTU 9.1 were able to grow on the beads in monocultures 
throughout the experiment. Most interestingly when introducing P. protegens DTU 9.1 WT 
and mutants to the SynCom, a stable community was established. On the first day sampling all 
the community members were present in the model. Even on day 7, Stenotrophomonas sp. and 
Chryseobacterium sp. were still present in relatively high numbers, despite P. protegens was 
tending to eradicate the system.

In addition, chemistry analysis was conducted on the samples of the liquid culture as well. The 
assay gave notably different results compared to the bead system. For instance, there was no 
2,4-DAPG production detected in the liquid culture. The difference between the results

enough to detect small molecules such as 2,4-DAPG.

probable that the degradation pattern that was seen the bead system is dependent on the 
environment where the synthetic community can establish.  
It is possible that the liquid system gave different results in terms of microbial interactions, as 
all species can meet each other at the same time, having no chance for avoiding each other or 
having a spatial distribution. In the bead system the members can have a so-called escaping 
ability, thus the different species might not even meet and still can live together.   
Considering all the results we decided to continue the experiments based on the findings of the

6. Discussion  
 
Synthetic microbial communities are serving as important models for researchers to study 
microbial interactions. The importance of these studies is to be able to have a glance what could 
be the role of the community in nature, since single isolates cannot give the whole picture of 
microbial behaviour. Soil Pseudomonas strains were extensively studied due to their well-
known plant growth promoting and plant protecting abilities. For instance, Zhuang et al. were 
studying a microbial community of 6 Pseudomonas strains isolated from garlic rhizosphere. 
As They were investigating the plant growth promotion in the semi-synthetic microbial 
consortia, they concluded, that the SynCom that they constructed was indeed able to induce 
plant growth [59]. As another example, Pseudomonas fluorescens CHA0 was studied by 
Natsch et al. in terms of biocontrol activity in a synthetic and natural microbial community of 
cucumber roots. They showed that the introduction of P. protegens CHA0 led to an increased 
metabolic activity in terms of 2,4-DAPG and pyoluteorin production in the constructed 
community on glucose dimers and polymers [60].  
In this study we aimed to construct a stable semi-synthetic microbial community for the 
purpose of investigating the role of P. protegens DTU 9.1 produced secondary metabolites. For 
the initial experiments previously crated secondary metabolite deficient mutants of P. 
protegens DTU 9.1 were used to be able to examine the impact of these metabolites. The 
mutations effected the pyoluteorin, 2,4-DAPG and an unknown Type I. polyketide production. 
These agents are known for having antimicrobial activity against plant pathogens. 
The 4 member synthetic community used in this research was provided by the researchers of 
the CeMiSt Center, which consisted of Pedobacter sp., Rhodococcus sp., Stenotrophomonas 
sp. and Chryseobacterium sp. They previously succeeded to construct and optimize a stable 
microbial consortium using these specific soil bacteria. In this study we further wanted to 
analyse this model by introducing P. protegens DTU 9.1 (WT and mutants) to the system.  
Before constructing the microbial community, the interactions between the community 
members and the different P. protegens DTU 9.1 variants (WT, ∆pltA, ∆phlACB and 
∆CLA_06265) were studied. Investigating one-to-one interactions was necessary to find 
assumptions what could be the main drive that leads to the dynamics of the community.  
To shed light on the individual interactions between the community members and P. protegens 
DTU 9.1 an inhibition assay was conducted using the overlay technique. This way the bacteria 
in the overlay was directly exposed to the secondary metabolites produced previously by the 
strains in the plates without interfering by other microbial interactions such as in live cultures.  
By looking at the results, it was concluded, that pyoluteorin had the most prominent 
antimicrobial effect against all the 4 community members. As several researchers reported 
previously, pyoluteorin is one of the major determinants of biocontrol activity in Pseudomonas 
[39]. It is an effective plant protecting agent by inhibiting the growth of several plant pathogens, 
such as Rhizoctonia solani [61].  
Evaluating these observations, the initial hypothesis at the beginning of the project was that it 
might be challenging to construct a stable community with P. protegens because of the 
presence of this bioactive compound. It was assumed, that only the pyoluteorin deficient mutant 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
will be able to establish a stable community because of the absence of the antimicrobial 
compound. Since the community members did not show inhibitory effects towards P. 
protegens according to the overlay assay, it was hypothesized that the presence and the 
concentration pyoluteorin will be the main drive of the establishment of the synthetic 
community.  
After the analysis of the biocontrol activity of each species towards each other, the main aim 
was to demonstrate that P. protegens DTU 9.1 was able to co-exist with the other members and 
form a stable synthetic community for a prolonged period of time. All the community members 
were co-cultured in a soil mimicking polymer bead system.  
In order to be able to establish a stable community, the polymer beads were inoculated with 
the SynCom members P. protegens DTU 9.1 (WT and mutants, separately). The co-existence 
was investigated by colony counting according to the different morphology of each strain. In 
the optimized and stable system, the secondary metabolite production, and the dynamics of P. 
protegens DTU 9.1 were inspected in the semi-synthetic community.  
Looking at the results, surprisingly there were no outstanding differences between the different 
models in terms of the community composition. This means that there were no prominent 
differences between the systems where the wild-type P. protegens DTU 9.1 was introduced 
and the ones where the mutant strains were present. The PCoA analysis showed that the main 
drive of the community structure is the sampling time. In addition, it was demonstrated that the 
type of P. protegens DTU 9.1 applied did not influence the community composition.  
It can be concluded from the results of the bead experimet that all systems constructed a stable 
community. In addition, it was shown that in all cases P. protegens DTU 9.1 was eradicating 
the system by day 7.  However, this phenomenon can be explained by the calculated growth 
rates (see Appendix I.), that shows that P. protegens is a fast-growing strain compared to the 
other community members.   
This means that the hypothesis that was made after the inhibition assay was inaccurate. 
According to that, only the pyoluteorin mutant should have been able to establish a stable 
community since it was the only case when the highly toxic compound, pyoluteorin, was not 
present in the system. However, this was not the case, all systems were similar in terms of the 
composition. Thus, the next step of the project was to elucidate what could be the reason for 
the fact, that the presence or the absence of pyoluteorin does not have a drastic influence on 
the community structure. 
At this point of the project, it was inevitable to examine and quantify the secondary metabolite 
production in the system. The controversial results of the initial inhibition assay and the bead 
system indicates that the pyoluteorin production was altered in the constructed synthetic 
community. This might be the reason why there was inhibitory effect of pyoluteorin towards 
the SynCom. It was assumed, that there was a regulatory system between the cells, that stops 
pyoluteorin production of P. protegens, acting as a protective mechanism against the 
antimicrobial compound.  
For this reason, LC-MS analysis was run on the samples to have a glance of the secondary 
metabolites produced by the newly designed synthetic community. When evaluating the 
pyoluteorin production, it was concluded that the compound was present in the bead system, 
except in case of the deficient mutant being introduced. In addition, the levels of pyoluteorin 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
did not decrease when P. protegens DTU 9.1 was together with the SynCom members in the 
system compared when it was alone in the beads. This means, that the expression of pyoluteorin 
was not inhibited.  
These results can be explained by many other theories as well. For instance, it is possible, that 
the concentration of pyoluteorin in the system was simply not eligible to reach the minimal 
inhibitory concentration (MIC). In order to test this hypothesis, samples should be taken from 
the bead system from which the supernatant should be extracted. This way another inhibition 
assay could be performed, but this time with concentration of pyoluteorin that resembles to the 
bead system. For this reason, agar plug assays could be implemented where the synthetic 
community members would be cultivated on agar plates. Afterwards agar plugs should be cut 
in the middle of the plates where the extracted supernatant should be measured. If the 
supernatant has antimicrobial activity against the bacteria cultivated on the plates, inhibition 
radius should appear on the plates, similarly to the overlay assay [62].  
 
 
Figure 35.: Demonstration of the agar plug assay to measure the antimicrobial activity of pyoluteorin 
expressed in the bead system.  
 
Furthermore, the reason why we see these results might be because of the “communication” 
between the bacterial cells. Cooperative resistance is a phenomenon when multiple species can 
cooperate to create a more beneficial environment for surviving. It is a common behaviour in 
nature when resistant bacteria are able for the deactivation of the target antibiotics, thus protect 
more susceptible bacteria from the bioactive agents. Even, it is also possible, that two sensitive 
bacteria are able to cooperate in order to inhibit the antibiotic agent that they are susceptible 
of. The study of Yurtsev et al. showed the cooperative mechanism of two Escherichia coli 
strains in the presence of ampicillin and chloramphenicol. They demonstrated that the co-
culture of the two strains was resistant to these antibiotics, however, the growth of both of the 
single cultures were inhibited by the same agents [63]. This theory could be investigated by 
repeating the initial overlay inhibition assay with mixed cultures of the synthetic community 
members. However, this assay requires previous optimization steps in order to construct a 
stable double and triple member co-culture from the SynCom members. Therefore, due to time 
concerns this assay could not be included in this research.  
According to a third theory, it might be possible that the bead system facilitates the spatial 
distribution of the cells. Thus, the different bacteria might not even meet each other in the 
system avoiding the interactions. Nunan et al. suggested in their study that microbial 
communities are not distributed in the soil by chance, there is a microbial spatial pattern that 
determines the interactions between the species. They were investigating the microbial 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
distribution by a method called thin-section technique, which is using fixing, staining and 
dehydrating solutions for sample preparation from which thin layers are cut out from the 
impregnated and cured samples. This way the thin (~35 μm) layers can be inspected under the 
microscope [64], [65].  
It is also important to mention, that every time before sampling the Falcon tubes with 20 ml 
inoculated beads were shaken, thus the composition and the spatial distribution was disrupted. 
This could mean that every time after sampling, the system might not be representative to 
examine the spatial distribution. Shaking the tubes before sampling could give an indication 
which community members are still present in the system and the relative abundance of them. 
However, it does not show whether the members will encounter each other during the 
incubation. Thus, another set-up of the bead system should have been tried out when the beads 
are forming only one layer in a petri-dish. This technique might be able to represent a layer in 
the soil, and by varying the sampling sites, it could be easier to investigate the microbial 
distribution in the system.  
In addition, not only the pyoluteorin production and resistance was unexpected when looking 
at the chemical profile of the samples from the bead system. There was an unknown peak on 
the chromatograms which only appeared when P. protegens DTU 9.1 was present in the 
synthetic community. Later, with the help of the researchers of the CeMiSt center, it was 
confirmed that the unknown compound was a degradation product of a cyclic lipopeptide 
(CLP), orfamide A. Orfamide A is a CLP produced by P. protegens. This molecule facilitates 
the moving motility of its own producer and acting as a surfactant agent. By molecular 
networking analysis several other degradation products were found which derived from 
orfamide A as well. This means that in the synthetic community orfamide A was fully 
degraded, starting with the hydrolyzation of orfamide A, that resulted in a linearized 
lipopeptide. Afterwards it was further degraded by peptidases first targeting the peptide bonds. 
Thus, the full degradation was facilitated by the cleavage of single amino acids from the chain 
of orfamide A.   
In order to elucidate the mechanism behind the degradation pattern it might be a promising 
approach to see if this behaviour is also present in case of other CLPs. Geudens and Martins 
were studying the different cyclic lipopeptides produced by Pseudomonas sp. where they were 
investigating the role and the structure of these molecules. Looking at the amino acid structures 
listed in this study, it might be advisable to conduct experiments with compounds that has a 
similar molecular profile to orfamide A [66]. For instance, xantholysin is a cyclic lipopeptide 
produced by P. putida that has a role in biofilm formation, surface colonization by swarming 
and antibiosis against fungal pathogens and Gram-positive bacteria as well. [67]. By the 
implementation of this compound, for instance, in the experiments, it can be investigated 
whether the degradation of orfamide A is compound specific, or similar cyclic lipopeptides can 
be degraded as well.

the environment dependency of the microbial interactions seen in the bead system. Looking at 
the results, it was concluded, that the microbial composition of the two system were highly 
similar. Inspecting the synthetic community alone in the liquid system, Stenotrophomonas sp. 
and Chryseobacterium sp. were dominating. However, Rhodococcus sp. was present in lower 
numbers and Pedobacter sp. was below the detection limit even on the first day of incubation.

experiment as well. In contrary, Rhodococcus sp. could be counted only on the first day of 
sampling and Pedobacter sp. was below the detection limit in this case as well. This means that 
Pedobacter sp. and Rhodococcus sp. are more susceptible for the direct interactions with the 
other community members.

detected in the liquid system. The explanation for this difference might be because of the 
sample volume of the liquid cultures (1 ml) was not eligible for the detection of small 
molecules, such as 2,4-DAPG. Moreover, it is possible that the degradation of orfamide A was 
dependent on the environmental conditions, thus it can only be inspected in the bead system.

As a continuation of the project, there were two main questions that we wanted to find the 
answer for. The first one was to elucidate which community members were responsible for the 
degradation of orfamide A. This way the microbial behaviour can be studied, and further 
assumptions can be made about the interactions in the nature. The second question was related 
to the ecological role of the degradation of orfamide A. Acquiring this knowledge could help 
to better understand what the role of the different microbes in natural microbial communities 
is by using these model systems.  
In order to shed light on which community member or members are responsible for the 
degradation pattern, several assays were applied.  
As the presence of orfamide A shows typical phenotypic features of P. protegens, the changes 
in the phenotypes could be observed in case of the absence of the compound due to the 
degradation.  
Orfamide A is a cyclic lipopeptide that enables its own producer, this case P. protegens DTU 
9.1, to swarm across solid surfaces [51]. This means, that in case of orfamide A is degraded, it 
might lead to the loss of motility. For this reason, the upcoming experiments were aiming to 
examine the changes in swarming in co-cultures of P. protegens and the SynCom members. 
As it had been described in the results section, the co-cultures of P. protegens DTU 9.1 and the 
synthetic community members did not show significant differences in terms of swarming. It 
could be explained by the fact that P. protegens is the fastest growing bacteria, and after 4 days, 
when the pictures were taken, it simply overgrew in the co-culture.  
In order to overcome the fact that P. protegens is dominating in the co-culture, filter paper was 
used, where one of the community members was grown first to produce and secrete secondary 
metabolites and peptides. After 2 days of incubation the filters were removed from the plates 
and P. protegens DTU 9.1 WT was spotted on the same spot where the colony of the other 
bacteria was. This way it can be avoided that P. protegens eradicating in the co-culture and the 
secondary metabolites can interact with P. protegens. The results from the filter paper assay 
showed, that the secondary metabolites produced by Pedobacter sp. had an effect on the 
swarming abilities of P. protegens. The experiment was repeated on LB swarming plates as 
well (see Appendix VII.) which had a similar outcome as it can be seen in case of 10% TSB. 
This indicates, that Pedobacter sp. was communicating with P. protegens in a way, that inhibits 
its motility.  
As Rütschlin and Böttcher demonstrated in their review, there is an easy approach to 
demonstrate of the inhibition of swarming motility. They showed that spotting the bacteria, in 
their case V. alginolyticus, which has swarming ability and a microbe that has inhibitory effects 
(here S. algae) next to each other can show a difference in spreading on the plate [69].

For the sake of to be able to determine the secondary metabolite production and the origin of 
the orfamide A degradation, MALDI-IMS analysis was applied. This way the production of 
specific molecules can be determined and captured. The assay was conducted with the help of 
the researchers of the CeMiSt Center which elucidated that Rhodococcus sp. was able to 
hydrolyse orfamide A into a linearized lipopeptide (m/z 1313,85). In addition, according to the 
research of Hermenau et al., Gram-positive bacteria, namely Mycetocola tolaasinivorans and 
Mycetocola lacteus were able to linearize a Pseudomonas derived CLP, tolaasin. Since tolaasin 
is a toxic compound which is the main virulence factor of blotch disease, the degradation of 
the molecule shows antivirulence strategy [70]. Rhodococcus sp. belongs to Gram-positive 
bacteria as well. This might confer the suspicion which indicates that Rhodococcus sp. was the 
community member that is responsible for the linearization of orfamide A.  
The other community members did not show degradation activity towards orfamideA 
according to the MALDI-IMS. In addition, the degradation products which derived due to 
peptidase activity did not show up on the MALDI images. This indicates that there might be 
several community members working together for the full degradation of orfamide A. 
Rhodococcus sp. might be a community member which is responsible for breaking up the cyclic 
chain of the lipopeptide by the hydrolyse activity and another, maybe several other SynCom 
members that are able to further disassemble orfamide A by secreting peptidases.  
To further analyse the microbial interactions, a new set-up was prepared for MALDI imaging, 
that would show whether the combination of two community members were able to degrade 
orfamide A as a cooperative mechanism. However, due to technical issues, the results could 
not be reported yet.  
Thinking further, an adequate approach to elucidate which enzymes are responsible for the 
degradation would be to inspect mRNA expression in the SynCom members. The reverse 
transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) is a method that could 
enlighten the gene expression, for example in case of microbial interaction. For instance, 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
Quecine et al. showed that the presence of a mycotoxin fusaric acid (FA) altered the 
transcription of biosynthetic genes of 2,4-DAPG and pyoluteorin [71]. They enlightened the 
nature of this phenomenon by using RT-qPCR. The analysis showed the overexpression of FA 
by F. verticillioides in the presence of P. protegens Pf-5 resulting in decreased 2,4-DAPG and 
increased pyoluteorin production.  
Turning to the second main question, which was aiming to answer the ecological role of the 
disassembly of the lipopeptide, an orfamide A deficient mutant was created by allelic 
replacement. Knocking out a gene called ofaA led to the assembly of a P. protegens strain that 
was no longer able to produce orfamide A [53]. The rationale behind this experiment was, that 
this way the previous results can be compared to new experiments, where orfamide A was not 
present anymore. The differences and similarities between the outcome of the analysis might 
be able to give an explanation behind the ecological role of orfamide A degradation.  
The presence of the mutation was verified by several assays. First of all, the sequencing results 
showed, that the gene ofaA was successfully knocked out from the genome of P. protegens 
DTU 9.1. In order to evaluate the success of the mutation in terms of the cease of the orfamide 
A production, phenotypic assays were applied.  
As it was demonstrated by several researches previously, the absence of orfamide A causes the 
inhibition of swarming abilities [51], [52], [72].  In this project the swarming motility assay 
was repeated with the newly created mutant as well. As it was already described in the result 
section, the orfamide mutant strain did not show motile activity which indicates the success of 
the mutation.  
In addition to the swarming motility, a droplet collapse assay was performed on the ∆ofaA 
mutant as well. This method analyses the presence of surfactant compounds as they are well-
known for decreasing the surface tension of water [52]. As orfamide A is a surfactant agent as 
well, in the presence of the molecule, the droplet will collapse on hydrophobic surfaces. For 
this experiment 3 biological replicates of P. protegens DTU 9.1 WT and ∆ofaA mutants were 
cultivated in King’s B (KB) media for 24 hours [52], [73]. The results clearly showed that the 
knock-out mutant has a similar phenotype as the control water droplet, which does not collapse 
on oil coated surface. In summary, this method verified the absence of orfamide A in the 
designed mutant as well. However, it is important to mention, that the experiment was 
conducted using LB and 10% TSB as the initial media for the cultivation as well (see Appendix 
VIII.). In those cases, there was no difference between the WT and the ∆ofaA mutant in terms 
of the surface tension. The droplets did not collapse in the WT samples, not even at the highest 
concentrations used. This means that orfamide A might be over expressed in KB media, that 
opens up new questions about the continuation of the project. It can be seen that the secondary 
metabolite production was media dependent, thus it would be a proper approach to continue 
the project by exploring the effects of using different media as well. Jens C. Frisvad 
summarized by the example of filamentous fungi, that the growth media and the incubation 
conditions have a strong influence of secondary metabolite production [74]. His research 
demonstrated that choosing the right media for the adequate secondary metabolite expression 
can be highly challenging because of the several different factors have to be taken into account, 
such as the ingredients of the medium, pH, temperature, stress and even light can be important 
factors.  
==PAGE:61==
60 
 
As the next step of the project, the ∆ofaA mutant was introduced to the synthetic community 
in bead system as well. By investigating the composition of the community members, it can be 
concluded, that there was no significant difference between the distribution of the synthetic 
microbial community compared to the system where P. protegens DTU 9.1 WT and secondary 
metabolite deficient mutants were introduced. The bead system served as an assay to explore 
the microbial interactions between the species in a semi-synthetic community. However, to 
investigate the role of the interactions that were found, the bead system might not be the proper 
tool that is able to demonstrate the ecological role of the interactions.  
Even though applying the ∆ofaA mutant in the synthetic community did not reveal the role of 
the degradation pattern, there were some assumptions made about the significance of the 
orfamide disassembly in the nature.  
According to previous studies, such as Jang et al., orfamide A shows antibiotic activity against 
plant pathogen fungi and oomycetes [54]. It might be possible that orfamide A acts as an 
antimicrobial agent against the community members. Thus, orfamide A was degraded by the 
SynCom due to a protective mechanism. To elucidate the potency of this hypothesis, the initial 
inhibition assay was accomplished on the ∆ofaA mutant as well. If the inhibition radius is 
significantly decreasing in case of the absence of orfamide A, it might indicate the verification 
of the hypothesis. However, looking at the results, the inhibition radius did not differ 
significantly from the antimicrobial activity of P. protegens DTU 9.1 WT. This observation 
belied the hypothesis which said orfamide A has a extensive antimicrobial activity against the 
synthetic community members.  
In accordance with a second hypothesis, orfamide A might serve as a food source for the 
synthetic community members. Throughout the experiments 10% TSB media was used which 
has limited nutritional sources. This might indicate that in the competition for food sources, 
some microbes acquired the ability to degrade and utilise secondary metabolites, such as 
orfamide A. It had been reported previously by McLeod et al. and Chen et al. that Rhodococcus 
sp. is able to degrade various organic compounds in order to gain food source even in extreme 
environmental conditions, such as oil contaminated soil [26], [29].  
This assumption could be tested for example using pure orfamide A as a sole carbon source in 
the media. The synthetic community members should be tested individually and in 
combinations of co-cultures whether they are viable in this new environment. This might 
elucidate which ones of the community members are able to degrade orfamide A and might be 
able to utilise the lipopeptide as a carbon source.  
Lastly, it has to be taken into consideration that orfamide A is a compound that facilitates the 
swarming motility of its own producer. Thus, another explanation could be that the community 
members are fighting against P. protegens eradicating the synthetic community by trying to 
inhibit its motility. For instance, Sardar et al. showed in their study, that a M. litoralis derived 
lipopolysaccharide was able to almost fully inhibit the swarming ability of P. aeruginosa PA01, 
hence stops its eradication in biofilms [75]. The experiments of the bead system shows that the 
composition of the community members did not change radically in case of introducing the 
orfamide mutant compared to the WT. However, as it was mentioned already, the bead system 
might not be representative enough to investigate the ecological role of this phenomenon. The 
synthetic community should be examined from a broader perspective, maybe in in vitro studies, 
to be able to understand what the rationale behind the microbial interactions is.

7. Conclusion 
 
The design of multispecies model communities is a part of the emerging field of synthetic 
ecology where synthetic communities are constructed to investigate basic microbial 
interactions. The detailed description of these model systems are prerequisite to understand 
natural systems.    
In order to elucidate the role of the microbes in a natural environment it is not enough to 
describe them as single isolates. The microbial interactions can highly influence the microbial 
behaviour and expression of different secondary metabolites. Thus, the characterization of 
synthetic microbial communities are inevitable for the application of their benefitial traits, such 
as plant growth promotion and biocontrol activity. 
In this study, a semi-synthetic microbial community was built up of 5 bacterial species: 
Pedobacter sp., Rhodococcus sp., Stenotrophomonas sp., Chryseobacterium sp. and 
Pseudomonas protegens DTU 9.1 (WT, ∆pltA, ∆phlACB and ∆CLA_06265). The 
implementation of P. protegens DTU 9.1 was successfully conducted despite of the presence 
of an antimicrobial compound: pyoluteorin. This indicates that there might be a way of 
communication between the SynCom members that leads to the cooperative resistance against 
pyoluteorin. Furthermore, it is also possible, that due to the spatial distribution of the 
microbiome the community members may never encounter the antibiotic compound. In order 
to have a better picture of the microbial interactions these questions should be elucidated with 
the methods listed in the discussion section of this project.  
In addition, a so far unknown behaviour was discovered in the project as well: the full 
degradation of orfamide A produced by P. protegens. It was demonstrated in this project, that 
multiple community members of the community are might able to cooperatively degrade 
orfamide A by hydrolase and peptidase activity. The role of this degradation is still unknown. 
However, one possible answer could be that in limited nutrition sources the community 
members are using orfamide A as a food source. According to another hypothesis, the 
community members might try to inhibit the surface spreading of P. protegens, thus stop the 
eradication of the community.  
Looking at the results of this ecological study, it can be concluded that the characterization of 
microbial communities is highly challenging due to the complex relationships between the 
community members. Even in a simplified model system, the microbes are communicating 
through complicated regulatory systems, that have to be well-characterized in order to conclude 
about their role in nature. However, acquiring this knowledge can facilitate the future 
application of microbial communities in agriculture. As it was described previously in this 
project, soil microbial communities can have the ability for plant growth promotion and 
producing antimicrobial compounds against plant pathogens. This is the reason why these 
studies are essential, since by understanding the function of microbial communities, these traits 
could be utilised as natural agents substituting chemical pesticides which cause huge 
environmental problems.
