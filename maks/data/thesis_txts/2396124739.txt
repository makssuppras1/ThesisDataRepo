Abstract 
Three Membrane-Aerated Biofilm Reactors (MABR) were run under steady 
state at the same operation and influent conditions for two months, aiming at developing 
an autotrophic nitrogen removal process. Synthetic wastewater containing ammonium 
as the only energy source was fed, and the nitrogen removal rate, biofilm inert fraction 
and possible substrate limitations were studied. The analysis of bulk liquid nitrogenous 
compounds suggested a diverse microbial community throughout the biofilm, composed 
of ammonium oxidizers (AOB and AMX), nitrite oxidizers (NOB) and heterotrophic 
bacteria. The calculated activity ratios of these species were AOB/NOB > 0.6 and 
AMX/NOB >0.1, suggesting nitrifying activity with low nitrogen removal. In-situ 
oxygen microprofiles were performed to determine the biofilm thickness and the oxic 
and anoxic biofilm fractions. The inert content of the biofilm was analysed by the ratio 
of volatile to total suspended solids, and showed a high inert fraction (VSS/TSS = 0.1). 
The potential AMX activity was quantified with a batch test, and indicated a nitrite-
limited process. The higher NOB to AMX activity acted as a sink for nitrite in the 
bottom oxic biofilm zone, thus limiting nitrite supply to the top anoxic layer of the 
biofilm where AMX were probably located. 
To improve ammonium removal rates in MABRs by AOB and AMX, and to 
suppress NOB two strategies were considered: a) Free ammonia and not ammonium as 
the true substrate for AOB growth, and b) higher nitrite availability will increase the 
AMX population/activity in the biofilm. Free ammonia was increased via higher 
ammonium loadings and higher pH, based on the equilibrium of both species. AOB 
activity increased from 1.5 up to 2.7 g N-NH4+/m2day and even though ammonium 
removal increased, the efficiency of the process did not. As a result, NOB activity was 
suppressed compared to that of AOB due to the lack of oxygen available. From this 
study it was also concluded that higher free ammonia enhances the oxygen transfer rate 
in MABRs. 
In the second strategy aiming at improving AMX activity nitrite was added 
together with ammonium in the influent. The AMX activity increased from 0.4 up to 3.9 
g N-NH4
+/m2day while outcompeting AOB and NOB for ammonium and nitrite supply.  
==PAGE:4==

Both strategies affected AOB activity, and as a consequence nitrous oxide 
production. Nitrous oxide was measured in the bulk and gas phases together with in-situ 
microprofiles in the three MABRs.  
Nitrous oxide emissions, calculated as the fraction of ammonium removed, 
ranged from 0.4% and 0.2% in the liquid and gas phases during baseline, up to 1.4% 
and 0.7% after changing influent conditions. Addition of nitrite spikes correlated with 
higher nitrous oxide emissions in both liquid and gas phase. However, nitrous oxide 
production in MABRs during continuous operation did not correlate to the nitrite 
loading, suggesting that bulk measurements are not representative of nutrient 
concentrations inside the biofilm. To predict nitrous oxide production in complex 
biofilms in-situ measurements of nitrogen species should be performed.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Nitrogen in the atmosphere 
Nitrogen is an element extremely abundant in Earth, mainly as unreactive di-
nitrogen (N2). Reactive nitrogen forms (e.g. ammonia, nitrate), provided by natural 
sources as biomass burning or biological nitrogen fixation, are the nitrogen forms used 
by plants and animals.  
In the last years, humans have released reactive nitrogen into the atmosphere 
from the production of fertilizers or the combustion of fossil fuels. These leakages have 
produced different environmental effects as drinking water pollution, eutrophication of 
water and soils and loss of biodiversity among others. (Erisman et al., 2011; Sutton et 
al., 2011). As a result nitrogen cycle has been modified being currently a problem of 
global interest (Figure 1). 
 
Figure 1. Nitrogen cycle (Erisman et al, 2011). 
Legislation in Europe regarding water quality 
Since mid-1970s ensuring the quality of water resources has been one of the 
priorities for the European Union. Regarding legislation in the area of protecting the 
environment EU has in place three main pillars: 
 
The Water Framework Directive: Sets up an innovative approach for 
water management based on the natural geographical and hydrological

units and river basins, and establishes specific deadlines for Member 
States to reach ambitious environmental goals for aquatic ecosystems. 
 The Nitrates Directive: is aimed at blocking nitrates from agricultural 
sources in order to avoid effects on ground and surface waters. 
 Directives on urban waste water treatment (Bloch, 2005; Wise, 2008). 
1.2. Nitrogen removal technologies 
Nitrogen removal from wastewater has attracted increasing attentions due to 
rigorous environmental rules on nitrogen discharge. Most of the reactive nitrogen in 
wastewater comes in form of ammonium.  Biological nitrogen removal is one of the 
most economical processes favoured over physicochemical processes due to the 
possibility of removing fixed nitrogenous compounds to harmless dinitrogen gas (N2) in 
a more effective way (Ahn, 2006; Kim et al., 2008). 
This process consists of two successive reactions: Nitrification and 
denitrification.

(AOA), but the last one is less common in wastewater treatment (Könneke et al., 2005).

Nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB) is the second functionally defined group of 
nitrifying microbes.  Some examples of NOB are the species Nitrobacter and 
Nitrospira. They are more physiologically diverse than AOB, not all being 
chemolithoautotrophs (Madigan et al., 2010). NOB employs nitrite as an energy source, 
being the neutral/protonated form HNO2 the true substrate and CO2 as a carbon source 
via the Calvin cycle (Ward, 2013). Many strains are considered mixotrophic or 
facultative autotrophs. Their metabolic capabilities for uptake and degradation of 
organic molecules are limited, but they can supplement their growth with organic 
carbon and, in several cases, grow gradually in the presence of organic substrates 
without nitrite. Conditions optimal for their reproduction are temperatures between 25 
and 35ºC and pH from 7.2 to 7.6 (Anthonisen et al., 1976; Hill, 2011; Pellicer-Nàcher, 
2013; Ward, 2013)

NOB have been shown to have a lower affinity for oxygen than AOB. Oxygen 
saturation coefficient of Monod kinetics for AOB and NOB are 0.2–0.4 mg/l and 1.2–

nitrite accumulation (Peng and Zhu, 2006). 
 
1.2.2. Denitrification 
Heterotrophic denitrification refers to the transformation of nitrate produced 
during nitrification into dinitrogen gas. It consists of four serial reductions, which 
produce nitrite, nitric oxide (NO) and nitrous oxide (N2O) as obligatory intermediates. 
Each reduction step is catalysed by one or more specific reductase enzymes (nitrate 
reductases (NAR), nitrite reductases (NIR), nitric oxide reductases (NOR) and nitrous 
oxide reductases (NOS)) and occurs at anoxic or microaerophilic conditions.

Even though some nitrifying microorganisms are capable of performing some 
steps of the denitrification pathway, the term denitrifying bacteria is usually applied to 
heterotrophic bacteria that can degrade biodegradable carbon (electron donor) to CO2 
with nitrite or nitrate as final electron acceptor and presents a net generation of 
alkalinity. 
The total removal of nitrogen from wastewaters that contain high nitrogen 
concentrations needs a quantity of organic carbon as electron source that, depending on 
the wastewater characteristics is not available (low C/N). As a result, denitrification 
normally is completed by supplying an external carbon source such as methanol or 
ethanol (Lu et al., 2014).

1.2.3. Nitrification technologies  
Traditional methods to remove reactive nitrogen from water, which are 
Anoxic/Oxic and Anaerobic/Anoxic/Oxic activated sludge systems (Figure 3), split the 
nitrification and denitrification processes in different bioreactors, providing different 
environments for the two types of bacteria, making the treatment system more complex 
(Lin et al., 2015a; Wang et al., 2008) . 
 
Figure 3. Schematic diagram of the anaerobic/anoxic/oxic (A2O) process (Sun et al., 2010). 
 
A2O configuration is easier to control as it needs more than one reactor. Apart 
from the multiple reactors economic disadvantage, the A2O configuration normally 
requires from the aerobic zone a high mixed liquid return ratio (2-4·Qin) to the anoxic 
zone to induce more NO3- back for denitrification. A DO concentration increase and 
COD dilution in the anoxic zone can result from high return ratios, which inevitably 
deteriorate the denitrification efficiency. Besides, more energy consumption and 
increased operating costs are results of the high return ratios (Sun et al., 2010). 
Moreover, denitrification is an anoxic process firmly dependent on the 
availability of organic carbon that serves as electron donor of the process. Normally a 
value higher than 10 in COD/N ratio is needed for a good nitrogen removal. However, 
nowadays wastewater in many places presents low COD/N, sometimes lower than 5, 
which seriously interferes with the performance of denitrification and leads on poor 
total nitrogen removal (below 50%) (Lin, Zhang, Li, et al., 2016). 
In response, new technologies such as short-cut nitrification denitrification 
(SND) and anaerobic ammonium oxidation (AMX technologies) have been proposed in 
recent years. Investigation of the shortened nitrogen removal pathway via nitrite 
revealed some promising numbers as: 40% reduction of COD demand during

denitrification, 63% higher rate of denitrification, 300% lower biomass yield during 
anaerobic growth and no nitrite toxicity consequences for the microorganisms in the 
reactor. If SND is supplemented by the inhibition of nitrite oxidized to nitrate, also a 
saving of organic energy of up to 40% could be realized (Wang et al., 2008). 
1.2.4. Anammox (AMX) 
Anammox bacteria utilize ammonium as electron donor and nitrite as electron 
acceptor to convert it anaerobically into mainly dinitrogen gas and nitrate (Strous et al., 
1998a).

AMX consume protons so is necessary to control pH to ensure bacterial growth. 
These microorganisms are mainly chemolithoautotophs. Their growth is 
extremely slow, with generation times on the order of 2 weeks (Terada et al., 2011), 
though recently much faster growth has been reported (2-3 days) (Zhang et al., 2017). 
Optimal conditions for these bacteria are temperatures between 37-40ºC and pH around 
8 (Egli et al., 2001). 
Anammox bacteria are strictly anaerobic and are inhibited by dissolved oxygen. 
Nevertheless, inhibition caused by low concentration of oxygen was demonstrated to be 
reversible by some authors. This reversibility makes partial nitritation and AMX 
possible in one reactor (Strous et al., 1997; Van Hulle et al., 2010). 
1.2.5. Anammox-driven technologies 
Some advantages of the AMX process compared with the conventional 
biological nitrogen removal, are savings on external organic carbon source, less oxygen 
demand, small excess biomass production, and reduced carbon dioxide (CO2), nitrous 
oxide (N2O), and nitric oxide (NO) emissions (Feng et al., 2007; Liu et al., 2008). 
In recent years AMX has attracted a lot of attention in the wastewater 
engineering field. However, in order to make use of the AMX bacteria, partial nitritation 
is required (Sun et al., 2010).

Two strategies are used in order to supply AMX with nitrite (the electron 
acceptor for the oxidation of ammonium). In the first one nitrite is produced in a 
separate aerated reactor and then is fed into an anoxic anammox reactor (SHARON-
AMX), while in the other option nitrite is produced in an oxygen limited single stage 
system (CANON)(Sliekers et al., 2003; Third et al., 2001; U et al., 2001). 
The practical application of the AMX technologies presents the major 
disadvantage of the low growth rate and low cell yield of AMX organisms needing as a 
result long start-up periods. By applying high sludge retention time the performance of 
these reactors can be enhanced developing biofilms in carrier materials or by self-
aggregation in granules (Van Hulle et al., 2010). 
The success of biofilm technologies is based on their ability to disengage two 
very important process parameters in wastewater treatment: solids retention time and 
hydraulic retention time.  In biofilms, chemical gradients can be created with suitable 
control of the oxygen flux. This stratification of redox conditions lets AOB to grow in 
the aerobic biofilm regions, while AMX colonize the remaining anoxic portion and 
enhances the reliability of the reactor facing toxic shock loads and changes in 
wastewater composition.   
Moving bed biofilm reactors (MBBR) or sequencing batch reactors (SBR) are 
some examples used for the one stage processes to create the conditions where AOB 
and AMX can coexist (Egli et al., 2001; Joss et al., 2009; L.W. and Oleszkiewicz, 2011; 
Sliekers et al., 2003). 
Single-stage processes, since the additional nitritation reactor volume is not 
necessary, imply lower capital costs and generally they have higher volumetric nitrogen 
removal rate. However, Nielsen et al. and Hao et al. reported difficulties regulating 
dissolved oxygen and showed incomplete nitrogen removal in those cases with high 
loaded wastewaters (Van Hulle et al., 2010).  
 
While the application of AMX related technologies in the side-stream is at present 
state of the art, the workability of this energy-efficient process at main-stream 
conditions is still under development and is still a challenge in the wastewater treatment 
field due to some aspects such as: lower ammonium concentrations and operation 
temperatures (Lotti, 2013). 
Overcapacity of AMX activity makes nitritation process the main bottleneck in 
some other author’s experimentation (Lotti et al., 2015).In contrast of conventional 
nitrogen removal systems, higher ammonium conversion rates were observed. Kinetics

1.2.6. Membrane-aerated biofilm reactors (MABR)  
Growing biofilms directly on aeration membranes is more advantageous to attain 
redox-stratified environments. The reason why this happens is because it is possible to 
control oxygen delivery independently from the ammonium load or changes in the bulk 
phase (Pellicer-Nàcher, 2013). 
The Membrane-aerated biofilm reactor (MABR) technology presents all the 
advantages of a conventional biofilm and also benefits as a very efficient and flexible 
gas delivery. In MABRs the membrane presents two roles: the material that allows the 
supplement of oxygen and be the support for biofilm growth. In an MABR, nutrients 
and oxygen are provided from two opposite sides of the biofilm (counter-diffusion). The 
air flows from membrane lumen and biofilm attached on membrane surface utilizes the 
oxygen, at the same time the pollutants penetrate biofilm from wastewater side. In 
comparison with conventional (co-diffusion) biofilm, the opposing substrate diffusion 
brings on a larger active thickness in biofilm and concentration gradients, which 
provides the environment for complete nitrification/denitrification process. A scheme of 
the system is showed in Figure 4 (Lin et al., 2015b; Sun et al., 2010). 
 
Figure 4. Schematic diagram of the simultaneous nitrification/denitrification in a MABR. 
In a conventional biofilm, due to oxygen competition between AOB and NOB, it 
is difficult to achieve SND of organic carbon-containing wastewater, yielding lower 
nitrogen removal efficiencies. However the different nutrient delivery regions into the 
==PAGE:19==

11 
 
MABRs allows SND due to the presence of different biofilm zones with the suitable 
conditions for each species to grow (Terada et al., 2006). 
Contrary to conventional biofilm systems, the adjustment of the oxygen load 
does not change the hydrodynamic conditions in the bulk liquid, and can be completely 
dissociated from the load of pollutants. Furthermore, stripping of non-desired gases and 
foaming are avoided due to bubbleless aeration. 
At the biofilm base, nutrient limitations can be avoided due to the efficient 
control of oxygen load that stimulates a better degree of microbial activity. This is 
another advantage from conventional biofilms where nutrient limitations unchain 
biofilm detachment events (Martin & Nerenberg, 2012; Pellicer-Nàcher, 2013; 
Semmens, 2005). 
 
1.3. Nitrous oxide (N2O) production in wastewater operations  
Nitrous oxide (N2O) is a greenhouse gas and stratospheric ozone depleter with a 
severe impact on the environment. Its global warming potential is about 300 times 
higher than that of carbon dioxide (CO2) (Tallec et al., 2006). 
The interest for N2O emissions has increased considerably since N2O was 
recently recognized as the most important threat to the ozone layer of the 21st century. 
In the anthropogenic water cycle N2O emissions can dispense up to 26% of the GHG 
footprint (Desloover et al., 2012). 
N2O is biologically produced during wastewater treatment by AOB and HB. 
AOB are able to produce N2O as by-product of hydroxylamine oxidation (NH2OH) or 
by nitrite (NO2-) reduction. During nitrate (NO3-) reduction, N2O can also be produced 
by HB if complete denitrification does not occur. The three pathways are regularly 
recognized as nitrifier nitrification (NN), nitrifier denitrification (ND) and heterotrophic 
denitrification (HD) (Domingo-Félez et al., 2017).

Based on these reactions many authors have correlated N2O emissions with 
dynamic variables as ammonium oxidation rate (AOR) and nitrite accumulation. 
Regarding ammonium oxidation rate different relationships were reported. Some 
authors suggested positive exponential correlations (Law et al., 2012) whereas others 
were not capable of stablish any clear relation between N2O production and AOR 
(Wang et al., 2016).  
N2O emissions were also positively correlated with NO2- concentrations and its 
accumulation was considered to be the most influential factor inducing NO and N2O 
production (Lotito et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2016) . 
Previous experiments had shown that dynamic conditions as changes in oxygen 
lead on nitrous oxide production (Kampschreur et al., 2008). Others related the 
generation of nitrous oxide to the imposition of anoxia or to recovery back to aerobic 
conditions after the anoxic shock in intermittent aeration. N2O was only produced 
during returning to aerobic conditions after a period of anoxia and correlated positively 
during anoxia with ammonia accumulation (Yu et al., 2010).  
True substrates of AOB and NOB are considered to be NH3 and HNO2 which 
are in pH-dependent equilibrium with NH4+ and NO2-. Consequently changes in pH 
towards acidification will lead to more N2O emissions (Frame et al, 2017).

nitrification rates as chemical oxygen demand or solids retention time (Figure 5).

In biofilms the spatial allocation of microbial communities and mass transfer

1.4. Characterization of biofilm reactor performance by use of 
microsensors 
Over the last years a large number of experimental techniques have been 
developed and improved to study the performance of an MABR based on bulk 
measurements and also microbial ecology and composition of biofilms. 
1.4.1. Microprofiling  
Even when microorganisms can be cultivated, physiological properties 
determined in the laboratory may not necessarily reflect the activity and physiology in 
the environments, where resource competition, environmental heterogeneity, and other 
inter- actions occur. Therefore, appropriate techniques and tools with sufficiently high 
spatial and temporal resolution are required to link microbial community structure with 
in situ function (activity) (Okabe et al., 2011b). 
Microsensors are sensors with a tip diameter of only a few microns (5-25 µm) 
and with a spatial precision of twice the tip size. With such small tip diameters (Figure 
6), they can be used to perform measurements in sediments and biofilms and determine 
in situ bacterial activity having only a small influence on structures and processes. They 
allow the determination of specific rates and regions of nitrification and denitrification 
in the biofilm (Beyenal and Jerome Babauta, 2013; Satoh et al., 2004). Electrochemical 
microsensors used in biofilms can be categorized by operating principle into two 
groups: amperometric and potentiometric (Figure 6).

1.4.1.1. 
Amperometric microsensors 
Amperometric microsensors measure a current induced by the electrochemical 
reduction or oxidation of the analyte in the tip, with a rate proportional to its 
concentration. Useful sensors based on this principle have been established for oxygen, 
nitrous oxide, sulfhydric acid and chlorine (Gieseke and de Beer, 2004). 
The measured current between the microelectrode and the reference electrode is 
proportional to the reaction rate inside the microelectrode, which depends on the 
reactant concentration and the mass transfer rate in the vicinity of the microelectrode 
tip. Some requirements for these sensors are being selective for the target analyte and be 
insensitive to stirring activity (Atci, 2016). 
Oxygen microsensor: It is the oldest one and its function has been optimized 
over the last decade. Electrons are consumed in the cathode for oxygen reduction. 
Electrons were released from an Ag anode (Figure 8). Oxygen concentration is 
proportional to this flow of electrons, which is measured by a picoammeter.  
Oxygen profiles (Figure 9) allows us to: correlate the microprofiling data 
obtained with the distributions of organisms and their spatial organization, calculate the 
aerobic and anoxic zone, the depth of the total biofilm and also the boundary layer 
thickness. Also combining microsensor measurements and FISH analysis it is possible 
to gain information about the substrate concentrations to which the different layers of a 
biofilm/aggregate are exposed (Downing and Nerenberg, 2008; Vázquez-Padín et al., 
2010).

1.4.1.2. 
Potentiometric microsensors 
These sensors detect the concentration of an analyte by measuring a potential 
difference between the microelectrode tip and a reference electrode. The most common 
example of this type is the pH microelectrode. Nernst equation can be used to describe 
its response. 
Usually the electrode is formed by an ion-selective membrane that can be full 
glass, metal oxide or liquid ion-exchange membrane (LIX). Comparison is unfavourable 
for LIX but for some species as nitrite or nitrate there are no alternatives. These hand-
made sensors can be used only for a few days.

2. Overall objective and aim of the thesis. 
The main objective of the thesis is the management of microbial communities to 
improve ammonium removal and also try to answer if it is possible to enhance AOB 
and AMX activity while maintaining low NOB under continuous aeration. NOB 
suppression is still one of the main concerns in wastewater treatment under this kind of 
technologies. While aiming this goals N2O emissions will also be studied. N2O 
emissions have a large impact on Carbon footprint so it might have a negative impact on 
the overall performance of the technology. The main objectives of the thesis are 
described below (Study 1, Study 2 and Study 3). 
Other secondary objectives are: 
 Operation and control of three nitrifying membrane-aerated biofilm 
reactors. 
 Physical characterization of a counter-diffusion biofilm. 
 Determination of parameters driving N2O emissions.

2.2. Study 2: Nitrite availability increases AMX activity 
If AOB activity is limited by the oxygen supply, AMX activity will also be 
limited by NO2- supply by AOB. In this study we aim at improving total nitrogen 
removal and reduce NOB activity. The hypothesis is that increasing nitrite availability 
will increase AMX activity. Because nitrite is directly fed from the bulk into the top 
anoxic biofilm layer, NOB cannot compete with AMX for the additional nitrite fed, as 
NOB also require oxygen, supplied by the bottom part of the biofilm. 
We expect to create a desired microbial community with excess AMX activity 
that will be capable of outcompete NOB for AOB-produced nitrite once external nitrite 
supply is stopped. In this study nitrite was fed in Reactor nitrite in the same bottle as the 
nitrogen source. 
Ammonium and sodium bicarbonate loading was kept as baseline and 15, 30 and 
60 mg N/L were added as sodium nitrite. To ensure the presence of nitrite in the influent 
and effluent and support the decision to continue increasing the loading of nitrite some 
samples were analysed using nitrite kits each week during the experiments.  
2.3. Study 3: Higher N2O production is expected due to higher NO2- 
availability 
The aim of this study is to demonstrate how the higher AOB and AMX activity 
will affect the nitrous oxide production either in liquid or gas phase, and their individual 
contribution to the total N2O emission of the MABR. 
As ammonium influent concentration and nitrite accumulation are directly 
related with nitrous oxide production pathway this study will be performed 
simultaneously with Study 1 and Study 2. 
In order to get the data necessary to correlate both species with nitrous oxide 
production we will measure N2O in gas from the aeration line with a gas filter 
correlation N2O analyser (Teledyne AOI, San Diego, CA, USA). To calibrate it we will 
use a saturated gas solution with 200 ppm of nitrous oxide and pure nitrogen as zero 
gas. In the case of the bulk we will use directly measurements in the effluent line and 
also microprofiling (3 replicates) with a commercial N2O sensor (N2O-25 Unisense, 
Denmark).  
All data points were obtained after at least one week of previous performance 
ensuring a steady state behaviour.

Oxygen balances 
Oxygen in the liquid influent diffuses through the biofilm and is consumed right 
away at the top of the biofilm. Based on stoichiometric coefficients consumption of 
oxygen by AOB and NOB was 111 mg O2/day. Just 0.05% of the oxygen provided by 
aeration is consumed. The efficiency of oxygen that is uptaken by the biofilm could be 
improved by reducing the flow supplied but even reducing the aeration flow from 0.5 
L/min until 0.05 L/min aeration efficiency will be 0.5% (Figure 19). 
As there is almost no oxygen in the effluent (below detection limit of the sensor) 
in these MABRs oxygen transfer rate (OTR) is considered the same that the Oxygen 
uptake rate (OUR). 
4.1.2. Oxygen profiles 
The MABRs present a diverse microbial community (AOB, NOB, AMX and 
HB). In order to elucidate the characteristics of an oxic and anoxic zone where all 
species can grow and also to determine biofilm depth and oxic zone depth we performed 
different oxygen microprofiles in parallel to the biofilm growth direction and 
perpendicularly to the liquid flow. 
The sensor was calibrated following the protocol explained in Materials and 
methods. Signals for the zero and saturation concentrations were corrected with 
temperature (according to reactor temperature) and then a two-point regression was 
performed.

4.1.4. Volatile and suspended solids content of the biofilm 
During the microprofile measurements the tip of some microsensors broke into 
pieces (visually checked under a light microscope). While removing the hard part of the 
biofilm to perform future nitrous oxide profiles pieces of biofilm were sampled by 
detaching it from the membrane. These samples were hard and brittle.  
The ratios VSS/TSS for two samples of the biofilm were 0.08 and 0.12, lower 
than 0.24 the value measured in the same MABRs months before (work by PhD student, 
Yunjie Ma). This ratio of VSS/TSS suggested that the inert content of the biofilm 
increased with time. 
The ratio is also lower for other types of biofilm reactors, granular reactors, and 
similar technologies (Table 10). Such difference between ratios proved that there is a 
precipitate around the biofilm. Apart from the hardness and brittleness of the biofilm the 
porosity of this precipitate from one sample taken during the cleaning of the biofilm 
surface was observable (Figure 23).

The amount of oxygen consumed increased as the ammonium load increased

that when MABR biofilms were operated at higher ASL their oxic volumes consumed a

AOB and NOB are oxygen limited in the top parts of the biofilm but because 
nitrite oxidizing bacteria have been shown to have lower affinity for oxygen than 
ammonia oxidizing bacteria (Oxygen saturation coefficient of Monod kinetics for AOB 
and NOB are 0.2–0.4 mg/l and 1.2–1.5 mg/l, respectively, (Picioreanu et al., 1997)) the 
increase in oxygen uptake is going to be higher for AOB (from 109 until 197 mg 
O2/Lday) than NOB (from 31 until 45 mg O2/L·day).

an alternative to other more difficult methods used by some authors to increase OTR: 
costly changes in air pressure and the use of pumps, or the periodic venting of lumen 
gases in a closed-end MABR (Perez-Calleja et al., 2017). 
 
 
Free ammonia vs. Ammonium as substrates for AOB growth 
As some authors reflected that the true substrate of AOB is free ammonia and 
not ammonium (Anthonisen et al, 1976), Study 1 was also subdivided in 4 pseudo-
studies to analyse the influence of ammonia and ammonium in AOB activity (Figure 
28). With this comparison we can demonstrate our hypothesis, AOB real substrate is 
free ammonia, due to improvement in AOB activity is observed when ammonium 
loading is the same but we increase pH (green and yellow points in figure 28). And also 
because the shape of the Monod curve for microorganism growth is followed in a more 
similar way by free ammonia substrate.  
Regarding R_FA.2 and R_continuous.1, both reactors followed a similar 
performance although in the case of R_continuous.1 AOB activity was higher at a 
similar FA concentration.  
It is important to state that FA concentrations (Figure 28) corresponded to bulk 
measurements, and in biofilms such as MABRs step biochemical gradients have been 
reported (Ma et al., 2017a). Ammonium is consumed in the bottom oxic layer of the

lowering as a result the available FA. Hence, the true local FA value at which AOB 
grown was lower than the bulk measured because of lower pH and lower ammonium

4.5.3. Higher free ammonia availability increases nitrous oxide production.

nitrous oxide production was more sensitive to other microorganisms or processes.

5. Conclusions 
 The high fraction of inerts (≈ 90%) in the biofilm suggests that this 
technology could not be suitable for long term operations in the 
presented MABR.  
 The ammonium load removed by AOB increased from 1.5 in baseline to 
3 gN/m2·day with a higher free ammonia loading. The oxygen transfer 
through the membrane was also indirectly enhanced.  
 The AMX activity was enhanced by addition of nitrite in the influent but 
the activity was not further maintained when nitrite supply was stopped.  
 Both nitrite addition and higher free ammonia levels increased nitrous 
oxide emissions, but not proportionally. 
 Bulk phase emissions contribute more to the total nitrous oxide footprint.  
5.1. Future work/suggestions 
 High inert content in the biofilm: This problem is something that should 
be furtherly studied in order to guarantee the viability of these systems in 
industrial and long term applications. 
 Other analytical techniques such as stable isotope tools (nitrogen and 
oxygen) to identify nitrous oxide pathway contributions. 
 In order to analyse nitrogen species (nitrite, nitrate and ammonium) 
through the biofilm in-situ study via microprofiling should be performed. 
Using in-house built LIX microsensors. In the initial planning of the 
project it was considered, but technical problems with the equipment 
prevented us from pursuing it. 
 Lower free ammonia values decreases AOB activity: Under the same 
theoretical principle than Study 1, a decrease in free ammonia 
concentration would decrease AOB activity. This change was not 
performed as we always aimed to improve the total ammonium removal.
