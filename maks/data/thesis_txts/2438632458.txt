Abstract
The art of additive manufacturing (AM) has become important for the industry, partly due
to the possibility of making better quality and stronger additive made specimens in the
resent years.
In this project an approach of reinforcing additive made specimens with continues carbon
ﬁbres is done, it is within the project to additive make and reinforce any arbitrary geometry
including geometries with concave surfaces.
A study of the state of art is carried out to ﬁnd similar project and to study continues ﬁbre
reinforcement machines like pullwinding and automated ﬁbre placement machines. The
study showed that the ﬁeld is yet to be fully explored and there is a lot of room for new
ideas.
To fulﬁll the project scope a 4. axis cylindrical coordinate big area additive manufacturing
machine is designed and realized. The designed machine is used for additive manufactur-
ing and afterwards extended for pullwinding to reinforce an additive manufactured speci-
men.
The work include the full design and realization of the machine, the electrical setup,
ﬁrmware and the development of a toolpath generator. The machine is enabled to ad-
ditive manufacture and eight different toolheads are designed for extended pullwinding,
these are tested on specimens with concave surfaces.
The additive manufacturing results came out with high quality, and the scope of reinforc-
ing any arbitrary geometries is promising.

Introduction
The automated production of products and prototypes has many advantages, it required a
reduced operator training, a higher duplication quality and in many cases reduced produc-
tion cost [20].
A key player in the automated production is additive manufacturing (AM), additive man-
ufacturing gives a freedom for production of complex structures compared to traditional
production methods [25]. Additive manufacturing is using a technique, where a given
3D geometry is sliced in layers with a computer software, and for the sliced geometry a
toolpath is generated. This approached gives the possibility of producing nearly any 3D
drawn geometry with layer build up and in this manner not limited a products geometry
for production [25]. By not being limiting to geometry a lot of beneﬁts for a product are
available, it is possible to produce light weight structures, among other from topology op-
timized structure results [11], furthermore it is possible to make molds with inner cavities
and without draft angles. The production of these parts are possible in many different
materials such as polymers, metal, paper and ceramics [36], to use the different materials
several technologies are possibly [12]. A widely used AM technique is material extrusion
process [13], in this additive manufacturing technique mostly thermoplastic polymers, in
form of ﬁlament, is feed into a heated nozzle, were it is melted and then extruded onto a
build plate[26], see ﬁgure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: The principle of material extrusion process, ﬁgure from [24]
The melted material is precisely deposit along the toolpath and in this manner building
the geometry layer by layer. A common material used for material extrusion process is
Polylactic acid (PLA), PLA allows to run the additive manufacturing process in normal
environments [15], and without special safety precautions, this advantage of PLA has

made it widely spread in additive manufacturing.
A research topic in additive manufacturing is to control the mechanical properties of ad-
ditive made specimens [38], the mechanical properties are dependent on the printing di-
rection, and thereby the toolpath layout, but it is also possible of reinforcing the additive
made specimen by using two or more materials to change the mechanical properties of
the printed specimen. One possibility to reinforce printed specimens is to use ﬁbres. Sev-
eral commercial companies have made additive machines that can do this and there is also
done research in this area [23]. The research, to reinforcement of additive made speci-
mens, can generally be put in two categories; continues ﬁbre reinforcement [16] and short
ﬁbre reinforcement [21],[6]. In a short ﬁbre reinforced additive manufacturing process,
short chopped ﬁbres are added to the matrix material, this is often done with a SLA ad-
ditive manufacturing technique [41]. A challenge by using short ﬁbre reinforcement, is
to control the ﬁbre direction in the additive made specimen, this means that the mechan-
ical properties also are difﬁcult to control [41]. Furthermore due to the separation of the
chopped carbon ﬁbres the strength is also reduced of the ﬁbre [16], even though the me-
chanical properties still have a very acceptable strength. A big achievement, for the US
army, was to have additive manufacture a full scale submarine with short ﬁbre reinforce-
ment called Oakridge. A possibility to improve the strength of additive made specimens
even further, is to use continues ﬁbres for reinforcing additive made specimens, researches
[16], [21], [35] and commercial companies like Makeforged and 3D HUBS have been
dealing with these techniques. The work done in this aspect is primarily dealing with con-
tinuous ﬁbre using in-nozzle impregnation or preimpregnated ﬁlaments based on FDM,
(see ﬁgure 1.2a) [14], [16], [21]. With this approach a preimpregnated carbon ﬁbre or
a directed in-nozzle impregnated ﬁbre, is extruded onto the build plate through a heated
nozzle. A limitation in this technique is that the ﬁbre angle is limited to the layer height of
the print, thereby it is difﬁcult to reinforce an additive made specimen in the longitudinal
direction and control the ﬁbre volume in the specimen [39].
A more traditional ﬁbre reinforcement process is pullwinding also known as ﬁlament
winding [1], [19], in this process a continues ﬁbre thread is pulled around the outer sur-
face of a specimen, and the pitch angle and winding numbers can easily be controlled,
see ﬁgure 1.2b. Thereby can the mechanical properties be controlled, see section 2.1 and
equation 2. The common technique in pullwinding is to rotate the specimen and move a
toolhead that guides the ﬁbre thread at a distance to the surface from the geometry. This
technique is well developed and has been used since the 1970s where it was developed
by the Danish inventor Frede Hilmar Drostholm [2]. The technique is used on cylindrical
convex and axis symmetric specimens, but the technique has also been developed for non
axis symmetric specimens [22]. A limitation of pullwinding; is to pullwind a ﬁbre layer on
a concave surfaces, the challenge is to get the ﬁbre thread to stick to the concave surface.

behind ﬁbre reinforcement, and areas of solid mechanics such as beam theory.
2.1
Overview of ﬁbre reinforcement
Fibre reinforcement is done by combining ﬁbres and a matrix material, such as epoxy.
This is generally done to optimize a specimens mechanical properties. By combining
two materials a mechanical properties such as the young’s modulus will be a combination
of this two materials. For a stress in the direction of the ﬁbres, is the modulus given in
equation 1, [42].
E1 = Efνf + Emνm
(1)
In equation 1 is Ef and Em the young’s modulus for the ﬁbre and the matrix respectively,
νf = Af/A and νm = Am/A were A is the cross section area of the specimen and Af and
Am the area fraction of respectively the ﬁbre and the matrix.
By optimizing the ﬁbre direction for a given load situation a structures mechanical prop-
erties for the load situation can be optimized. A common composite material is made by
a layer build-up and thereby can the ﬁbre direction be different in every layer. This can be
seen in e.g. a thin-walled cylinder with a pullwinded reinforced layer that is subjected to a
torque in the axial direction, see ﬁgure 2.1.

3
Machine construction
In this chapter, the process of the building the cylindrical coordinate Additive Manufac-
turing machine (in this report referred to as the machine) will be examined, and in short
words the used method is described. First the physical construction process of the ma-
chine will be elaborated, second the electrical hardware and ﬁrmware and third and ﬁnal
the programmed toolpath generator algorithm.
3.1
Construction of a cylindrical coordinate Additive Manufacturing
machine
To construct the machine an idea iterative process was carried out. The purpose was to
investigate different solutions for the physical construction of the machine, because the
ﬁrst idea not always gives the best solution.
General demands
To be able to ﬁnd the good solutions for the purpose of the machine, some general demands
were setup. These demands are made to elaborate the main purpose of the machine and in
this manner to make a more detailed overview of the requirements for the ﬁnal machine.
1. The basics demand for the design of a cylindrical coordinate Additive Manufactur-
ing machine, is that it should be able to ﬁbre reinforce any arbitrary geometric shape
by pullwinding (including shapes with concave surfaces), limited however to, solid
geometries without holes.
2. The machine should also have the ability to move in two linear directions and rotate
in an third, see ﬁgure 3.1. This ability will fulﬁll that every point in a polar coor-
dinate system can be reached. This means that, the same point on a geometry can
be reached several times, unlike other additive manufacturing machines, where it is
difﬁcult to reach a point that is already printed. If the same point cannot be reached
more than once, the printed geometry cannot be reinforced by pullwinding.

be calculated from the applied strain; δL = Lϵ, (δL is the elongation, ϵ is the strain and

limit of the gantry height. To ﬁnd the strain Hook’s generalized law can be used ϵ = E

Physical resolution of the printer
As showed would the tooth belt elongate (see equation 5), but the elongation is very low

used, the CS SeriesXY Gantry from NEWMARK has a resolution of 3.6 μm [32], and the

3.3
Arduino setup and wiring
In this chapter it is described how the machine control was designed and realized. To be
able to run a job with the machine it was necessary to give such a job as an input code that
described the path that the toolhead should follow, therefore a controller was needed.
For this an Arduino Mega with a RAMPS board 1.4 was used for controlling the machine.
An Arduino is an open-source electronics platform that consisting of hardware and soft-
ware, the Arduino micro controller has several output pins, that it uses to send electrical
pulses to e.g. steppermotors by assigning the pin high or low (0V or 5V) in this manner
it can control e.g. the direction and speed of the motors, [3], the position of the actua-
tors can be found as well with an endstop signal back to the Arduino at the beginning of
the job. The Arduino Mega is developed speciﬁcally with respect for controlling additive
manufacturing machines, and can control ﬁve stepper motors signals, ﬁve enstop signals,
temperature sensing/heating, and cooling fans.

3.4
Marlin ﬁrmware
To be able to send control commands for the motion of the stepper motors on the machine,
an input coordinate code format was needed. A very common format for AM and CNC
machines is G-code. The G-code format have several different movement commands and
control commands e.g. heating and temperate sensing. The movement commands are de-
scribed with a G1 command in front. To read a G-code a ﬁrmware for the Arduino Mega
was needed, it was decided to use an already made ﬁrmware that could be downloaded
called Marlin.
The Marlin ﬁrmware is a standalone open source ﬁrmware released August 12, 2011 on
github.com, [29], the Marlin ﬁrmware has become very popular as it can run on an in-
expensive 8-bit Atmel AVR micro-controllers which are the center of the popular open
source Arduino/Genuino platform. The ﬁrmware is developed with respect to Fused De-
position Modeling (FDM) and coordinates the heaters, steppers, sensors and LCD display,
28
==PAGE:37==
which makes it very use full in this project. To control the hardware, the input commands
are added to a movement queue in the ﬁrmware and a “stepper interrupt” processes in the
queue is converting linear movements into precisely-timed electronic pulses to the stepper
motors.
Several different versions of the Marlin ﬁrmware are written, and two of them are investi-
gated in this section.
3.4.1
Marlin ﬁrmaware R360
For the ﬁrst attempt on a ﬁrmware for the machine the open source R360 Marlin ﬁrmware
was downloaded from github.com. The Marlin R360 ﬁrmware is made for the polar coor-
dinate R360 3D printer, which is accessible with 3D ﬁles and BOM on reprap.com, [18].

see section 3.2. After some smaller modiﬁcations in the code, like steps per unit signal

length. In the Matlab toolpath generator a extrusion length was therefore calculated and

was found. After some testing with a layer height of 0.15 mm. a factor of 0.03 was found

Speed of printing
The used speed for printing, was based on a printing test and set to a reasonable default
velocity and acceleration. These settings were based on the rotating build platform, where

acceleration was too high the inertia of the buildplate could damage the Rotary stage and
decrease the resolution of the print.
The default rotational speed was set to 10 mm/s and the steps per mm to 320 step/mm , at
these settings a layer build of the geometry showed in ﬁgure 4.5 took 38s. With a time of
revolution of 38s, it took 48h to print a 150 mm high geometry showed in ﬁgure 4.5 , with

to the attached steppermotor. A last extruder output port was still free on the Arduino, and
could be used for this purpose. To get the angle of the winding direction a tangent function
was used in the pullwinding Matlab toolpath generator to calculate the angle between the
coordinate points in the toolpath, see appendix 10.5. The calculated angle between each
point in the toolpath was set in as a extrusion value in the output G-code, E’angle’. In the
Marlin ﬁrmware a second extruder was deﬁned, and the step/unit was set to ﬁt that extruder
length input of 360 resulted in one revolution. It was a possibility to implement another
cable wiring from the second extruder output port to the eighth toolhead steppermotor. In
this manner both the extruder output port for the ﬁlament printing and for the pullwinding
connected at the same time. This solution would not give any advantage because the
extruder port for the ﬁlament printing was not used. Instead it was chosen that the same
wire was used. The connection was then simply moved from the ﬁlament extruder port
to the second extruder port, then changing from additive printing to pullwinding. To be
able to use this solution with the Marlin ﬁrmware, the ﬁrmware was set up to be a single
nozzle printer with two extruders. At this time the heat element in the nozzle was not used
for pullwinding, therefore the input code had the G-code command; M302 P1, in the top
to disable the temperature sensor and ”allow” for cold extrusion. Furthermore the G-code

Even though the toolhead did not allow the pullwinding on a concave surface, a Matlab

done in parallel with the machine realization and would be of interest to test, even though

The challenges were that the single winding toolpath generator could only generate a tool-
path based on the CURA spiralize function, that allowed for a single path up the specimen.
Therefore it would only be possible to make a tight winding by making a very low wind-
ing pitch. A low winding pitch would give strong enforcement in the radial direction, but
low in the axial direction. Therefore for being able to control the mechanical properties it
should be possible to control the pitch, see equation 2 in section 2.1. But to retain a closed
winding with an arbitrary pitch, would it be necessary to pullwind several times, up and
down the specimen. The number of runs up and down is dependent on the specimen size
and the chosen winding pitch. A high pitch and a large specimen would require several
cross wingdings up and down the specimen.

had to rotate at a certain angle at the top and bottom of the specimen. This would give
better control of making a tight winding on an arbitrary geometry. If e.g. one winding and
four cross windings were required to make a close layer, the specimen had to rotate 45

Results and discussion
Along the chapters the designs and results have been discussed, in this chapter the most
important ﬁnding concerning the toolheads, in the aspect of the toolhead design for rein-
forcing arbitrary shapes, are the general considerations elaborated.
The challenges was to attaches the carbon thread after the deepest point of the concave
surface is reached, see ﬁgure 5.25. In the section below some more general solutions to
the challenges are discussed, that were not included in any toolhead iteration (due to time
limitations).
7.1
Toolhead considerations
There are several approaches that could lead to a solution of attaching ﬁbre thread to a
concave surface. Below, considerations in this aspect are listed, (forces referring to ﬁgure
5.25);
1. The reaction force F2 could be minimized.
2. An additional force could be added to the adhesion force F1.
3. The normal force F4 and the deﬂection of the toolhead mount, could be enlarged to
obtain better surface traction.
4. The adhesion force F1 could be enlarged.
5. A redesign of the toolhead, where e.g. the reaction force F5 did not inﬂuenced the
result in the same manner.
The ﬁrst point of minimizing the reaction force F2 would result in that the thread would
not be torn of as easily. It was seen that the reaction force F2 and F5 were dependent,
like nearly the rest of the forces. Therefore if the reaction force F5 could be minimized, a
smaller reaction force F2 would be obtained. The reaction force F5 is a result of the fric-
tion through the small guide hole in front of the toolhead tip (iteration eighth), the friction
through the epoxy bath and (if not avoided) the inertia in the thread roll. If a roll would be
placed before or after the small guide hole (or even replacing the hole) the reaction forces
would be minimized. This roll could be rotated by a stepper motor, in such manner that
the placed carbon thread length would be controlled by the rotation of the roll. The setup
could then control the forced used to drag the thread, it would demand a implementation
in the toolpath generator, which calculated the needed amount of thread.
In the second point it was considered to add a second force to the adhesion force F1. This
82
==PAGE:91==
could be done with a small roll, that were acting on the carbon thread after it was pressed
on the specimen. The roll could be placed right after the toolhead tip and press against
the placed thread. A difﬁculty in this idea, would be that the small roll probably had to be
placed on a second x-axis, to avoid to hit the specimen. This concern is due to the changes
orientation of the specimen when rotated, but could maybe be ignored if the roll and tool-
head tip were closer to each other. A drawback would be that the control of the setup
became more difﬁcult, and limited how sharp or deep corners could be winded around,
due to the enlargement of the toolhead.
If there was a possibility that the adhesion force could be enlarged if the pressure from the
toolhead was bigger, the third consideration point could be a solution. Furthermore if the
deﬂection, of the toolhead, in the x direction was bigger then the traction of the surface
could become better. By using this solution it should be investigated, if the adhesion force
becomes bigger with a bigger pressure. A difﬁculty would be, that by a bigger deﬂection
the toolhead trajectory would be more difﬁcult to control. A bigger pressure from the tool-
head would also mean a bigger deﬂection of the specimen.
A last consideration, is to somehow enlarge the adhesion force, to hold the thread onto
the concave surface, and this could be done with heat for hardening the epoxy faster. A
possibility would be to use the heat element from the printing nozzle, to heat the epoxy
impregnated carbon thread, after it was pressed on the test specimen. This would however,
again demand a second x-axis so the heat element could be pressed on the carbon thread.
Instead of this a reﬂow station could be used, with the reﬂow station using hot air to hard-
ening the epoxy faster. This could be done just after it was pressed on the specimen. In
this manner the adhesion force F2 would be enlarged. Such a solution is used in some
AFP machines, [20] and could perhaps be used with success. A drawback of the solution
would be that the next carbon thread layer could not be placed onto a wet layer.

8
Conclusion
The results in respect to the research questions are examined in this chapter.
How should a big area polar coordinate machine look like, and how should it be
build?
A polar coordinate machine was constructed and realized, with the overall dimensions of
2x2x1 m with an printing volume 900x900x1500 mm. The design and build of the cylin-
drical coordinate machine resulted in a full scale model with four movable axis. To fullﬁll
the main goals, several parts of the machine were designed and tested as well, including
an epoxy bath, eight toolheads, mounts and adapters.
In what manner should the machine be controlled?
A control strategy and electrical setup for the machine was also designed. This included
an Arduino Mega micro controller with a Ramps board 1.4 mounted on it. To deliver
enough power to the steppermotors (with a 1:20 gear ratio), four external motor drivers
were implemented, two power supplies were used; 12V (for the Arduino) and 36V (for the
external stepper drivers). All this electrical components were successfully wire connected
with a good practice setup. Signal and power wires were separated to minimized distur-
bance. This was done on a plywood plate with an engraved wire diagram.
Two ﬁrmwares for controlling the machine, were tested and the latest version of the
ﬁrmware (which is developed for additive manufacturing machines), Marlin (version 1.1.8),
was used to control the machine.
The modiﬁcations of the ﬁrmware were including
changes in the conﬁguration ﬁle, e.g. an default federate was set, that resulted in a build-
plate revolution time of 38 s with a average specimen (a bean shape with a 300x100 mm
cross section). This revolution time gave a build time of 33h for a 800 mm high specimen
with a layer height of 0.6 mm. With the ﬁrmware, was it possible to read an input G-code,
which resulted in the intended movement of the machine axes.
How should a toolpath input code be made?
The version (1.1.8) of Marlin that was written in Cartesian coordinates, which gave chal-
lenges for following the intended toolpath, the challenge was dealt with in a toolpath gen-
erator developed in MATLAB. The MATLAB toolpath generator algorithm was written in
a manner that it could read a G-code from the slicer software CURA and transform it into
polar coordinates. To be able to make cross winding with the machine, a second toolpath
generator was written. It was possible with help of a rotation matrix, to generate an input
G-code that resulted in a cross winding toolpath for the machine. The toolpath generator
could generate a 500 mm high specimens toolpath in 5 minutes for additive manufactur-
ing.
Is it possible to use such a machine for both additive manufacturing and pullwind-
ing?
84
==PAGE:93==
A setup with a hotend and extruder was successfully mounted on the machine. This en-
abled the machine for additive manufacturing, and geometries were additive made, includ-
ing specimens with concave surfaces. With a layer height up to 0.6 mm and an nozzle
diameter of 1.1 mm was the surface quality still good. Furthermore the machine design
allowed for pullwinding as well.
How can the machine attache ﬁbres on a specimen?
It was showed that the machine could be extended in a manner that it could attach ﬁbre
thread on arbitrary geometries, with some problems on concave surfaces. The toolhead,
for attaching a 10 mm wide ﬁbre thread, were iterative designed and resulted in eighth dif-
ferent toolheads, that were all tested and evaluated. The ﬁnal toolhead which was chosen,
consisted of a Teﬂon tooltip that could rotate around a fourth controlled axis, and place a
ﬁbre thread in the winding direction. The control of the fourth axis was implemented in
the toolpath algorithm and could move the toolhead with a speed of 1
5 rev./s
Is it possible to reinforce any arbitrary specimens, including specimens with concave
surfaces?
Eight toolhead iterations were carried out on a cylindrical specimen with a diameter of
100 mm, and after the selecting of the eighth toolhead, pullwinding on arbitrary speci-
mens were also carried out. At last a cross winding toolpath input code was executed,
which resulted in a cross winding that consisted of four single wingdings with a double
winding path, on a 200 mm high part of a specimen.
A challenge that still remains in the project, is to be able to fully attach a ﬁbre thread on
a concave surface of a specimen. The challenge is, that after reaching the deepest point
of the concave surface the ﬁbre thread is torn of the concave surface, before the deepest
point, even if the thread was attached to this part of the surface. A number of different
strategies has been discussed in this respect, and a promising solution could be to use heat
to ﬁrmly attach the ﬁbre thread while winding. In this manner the ﬁbre thread could not
be torn of after the deepest point on the concave surface.
In the approach of design iterations of toolheads for pullwinding, simple designs were
made to see if this was possible. If the design could be kept simple the toolhead would be
more robust, however after the challenging of attaching the carbon thread to the concave
surface, a consideration is that it could be necessary to make a more complex toolhead
with several actuators to control different properties. Properties such as the amount of
ﬁbre thread needed, and a ﬁfth axis control to make the toolhead normal to the surface
while winding, would be a good consideration.
Another challenge is the robustness of the ﬁrmware (Marlin ver. 1.1.8), the magnitude of
the printing coordinates in the input code had to be reset (and the extrusion had to be rela-
tive) to allow higher geometries to be made, which resulted in a slower printing speed. The
reason for the problem is not fully understood, but an approach could be to use another
85
==PAGE:94==
ﬁrmware such as the Marlin ﬁrmware for the polar coordinate 3D printer R360 which is
available on github.com. This could simply be done to try if a ﬁrmware that is specially
produced for a polar coordinate printer could solve the challenge, even if it was considered
less robust at the ﬁrst attempt.
In an overall view of the project, all the research question have been investigated and solu-
tions have been designed and tested, for all of them. A very good build platform for further
work in this interesting research area is now available and shows promising results.

Further Work
As described in the report there are still several challenges to deal with, like the winding
on concave surfaces. But beside that there are possibilities of improving the machine that
were not part of the research questions. In this chapter some considerations throughout the
project are elaborated.
9.1
Off axis printing
At the time does the specimens which is printed need to have a center axis that is deﬁned
and inside the whole geometry, and this has to be aligned with the center of the build plate.
This means that it is not possible, at the time, to 3D print or winde a specimen that has a
geometry were part of the geometry is off center, like the tip of a turbine blade sometimes
has. This challenges is dealt with in the R360 printer [18], and could also be implemented
in the current machine.
9.2
Machine veriﬁcation
A veriﬁcation of an additive made specimen would be of interest to elaborate on the pre-
cision of the machine. This could be done by 3D scanning and comparing with a CAD
model. Also a test and veriﬁcation, with respect to the precision of the layout of ﬁbre
threads would be of interest.
9.3
Build plate weight
The dimensions of the build plate leads to a relatively heavy weight, that sets a limit for the
maximum speed and specially the acceleration, see section 4.5. If another lighter material
than aluminum or a downsizing of the build plate were done the acceleration could be
increased and thereby the job time decreased.
9.4
Code optimization
The MATLAB polar toolpath generator code can be optimized in speed by changing and
optimizing some of the ’for’ loops. Apart from that, there could be implemented some
changes that could optimize the print quality when printing, like that the z-axis will of
course not be perfectly aligned from the bottom to the top. These results in which the
toolhead is not exactly at the center then printing at a higher position than calibrated at the
bottom, this could also be solved in the input code.

CS GANTRY SERIES
The CS XY Gantry Series is offered in travel lengths of 500mm, 1000mm, and 1500mm. 
Custom travel lengths are available, please consult factory. With speeds up to 1 meter/
sec the CS XY Gantry Series is prefect for applications requiring high speed positioning 
of light payloads.
The CS XY Gantry Series comes standard with high torque size 23 stepper motors. The 
motors are also available with 4000 count per revolution optical rotary encoders mounted 
to the back of the motors. Mechanical end of travel limit switches come standard on the 
gantry system.
The drive system utilizes a 9.5 mm wide timing belt to move the carriage. The carriage 
rides on four preloaded ball bearing wheels. The wheels are profiled to match the contour 
of the aluminum extrusion base to provide a larger contact area. 
All CS XY Gantry systems are machined from 6061 aluminum alloy and black anodized. 
The base is an aluminum extrusion with a clear anodized finish.
Typical applications for a gantry system includes pick & place, inspection, and 
dispensing.
