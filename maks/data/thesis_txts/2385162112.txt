Abstract
This thesis investigates the relationship between the lidar measurements in the wake and the
velocity deficits.
To analyze the same, a lidar was modeled, and was subjected to several
simulated wake fields following different ambient conditions. The lidar provides full-width at
half-maximum of the Doppler spectrum and the line of sight velocity as the output. Through the
exercise, a relationship was found between the velocity deficit, ambient wind speed, turbulence
intensity and the pitch angle of the wake source. Again a relationship was found with the
former variables and FWHM. As a process definition, when ambient wind speed, turbulence
intensity and the pitch angle is known, then the velocity deficit can be found from the Deficit
estimation table. Else when there is no knowledge about the ambient conditions FWHM and
the pitch angle can be used to extimate the deficit. The estimation of the velocity deficit has
been found to be very close to the actual deficit. To provide a quantitative example, the model
estimates the deficit as 2.599m/s, when the actual deficit is 2.5045.

1. Introduction
This chapter discusses the background, motivation and introduces the reader the project defi-
nition and overview of the approach.
Wind Energy has been the one of the front-runners of the global progress towards environmen-
tally friendly energy harvesting, and has been playing a significant role in the substitution of
fossil fuels alongside the mitigation of the increasing energy demand. For the past eighteen
years, the Wind Industry has exhibited average cumulative growth rates of about 26% GWEC
[2014]. It is projected that the wind power could enjoy a dominant share of 16-18% of the
global electricity demand by 2030. It will in return save 3 billion tons of CO2 emissions annu-
ally, which is an intended step forward towards the European Union’s (EU) targeted reduction
of greenhouse gas emission b 20% by 2020 and 80 to 95% by 2050 Energy2020 [2011]. In this
clear commitment towards decarbonisation of electricity sector, wind power is expected to cater
for about one-fifth of the total EU electricity demand by 2020 EWEA2020 [2014] and about
one-fourth by 2030 EWEA2030 [2014]. Categorically, the estimated offshore and onshore in-
stallations would be 23.5 GW and 169 GW by 2020, respectively and shoots up to 44.6 GW
and 206.3 GW by 2030. This effectively calls for cost reduction of wind turbines, applicable
to potential projects, and active research and development in fields like maximizing the energy
harvest from the wind for both the existing and potential projects. Development towards in-
creasing the size of the wind turbines and advancement in designs, may be a possible solution.
However, power harvested from a larger sized wind turbine would be an obvious compromise
with the manufacturing cost, so it demands an accurate assessment to ascertain profitability.
Another challenge is to optimally utilize the sites available for wind turbine layout. It is evident
that while selecting the location of wind farms alone, critical assessments of various environ-
mental, socio-economical and other mandatory aspects are essential. These obligations and
considerations may result in wind farms being located in the vicinity of each other, demanding
optimization in wind farm layouts and advanced control strategies. When the turbines is placed
in wind farms there can be an influenced by the wake from other turbines and this can lead to
larger fatigues. Advanced control strategies and sensors may be a possible avenue to address
the challenge. A possible solution to the sensor problem is a LIDAR, which can measure the
wind speed at a certain distance upstream.
1.1. Problem definition
This project endeavours to venture into the usage of one such assessment using Lidar. This
study is a part of a larger collaboration between DTU and Windar Photonics A/S. The total
project elaborates into the wake detection systems and turbine control algorithms, and to
enhance the utility of Lidars in wake situations. The current problem which this report will
focus is the analysis of wake detection using the lidars. To define the problem, it can be taken

which case the turbine is definitely in partial wake. In case all the lidar beams is subjected to

needs to be determined. Due to limited number of points and lidar measurement limitations,
it is complicated to characterize the wake. For the same, the wake field needs to be simulated.

∙Preparing a lidar model that can read 3-dimensional wind field and respond with the line

∙Finding a relation between the lidar measurements and velocity deficit covering different
ambient conditions

2. LIDAR System and Numerical
Model
The potential of LIDAR technology in meteorology and atmospheric researches was quickly
observed, following the development of pulsed laser.
With its first usage in meteorological
researches in lower atmosphere in 1963, the laser powered radar or lidar, emerged as an impor-
tant component in anemometry [Collis [1970]]. Since 1963 and assisted with the development
in the telecommunication industry, lidar has seen improvements with respect to cost, reliability
and robustness, promising viable deployment in large scale wind turbines. Besides the obvious
advantage of prior detection of the wind, which is clearly beneficial in wind turbine control
towards enhancing turbine performance and reducing potential fatigue damage, it is also a
potential substitute to the flexibility of choosing met-mast location, especially in the offshore
environment. The term ’lidar’, originally coined as an analogy to ’radar’ and developed as a
linguistic blend of ’light’ and ’radar’1, is often used as an acronym for LIght Detection And
Ranging.
Lidars, in general, are classified in many different ways including
∙Position of application - nacelle mounted or ground mounted for wind energy applications
[ZephIR-DTU [2014]], or, terrestrial, or air-borne in mapping applications [ESRI-ArcGIS
[2016]]. etc.
∙Emitted waveform - pulsed or continuous wave
∙Mode of detection - coherent or incoherent
∙Geometry of transmission and receiving - mono-static or bi-static depending upon whether
the telecommunication system is uni- or bi-directional [Pitter et al. [2015]].
In this report, the coherent mono-static continuous-wave Doppler lidar is discussed, and men-
tioned herein as Coherent Doppler Lidar (CDL), Coherent Laser Radar (CLR) or lidar2.
Initiating the report, this chapter encompasses the overview of the lidar system, leading to
the model which simulates the Lidar, given a numerical 3-dimensional windfield, followed by
related tests. The visual examples related to the descriptions are often referred to the simulation
results, for better representation.

2.1. Coherent Mono-static Continuous Wave Doppler
Lidar
In simple words, the radiation from the CDL system is focused on the moving target, in this
case the aerosols suspended in the atmosphere. A part of the incident beam reflects from the
aerosols and back-scatters onto the detector. A change in the frequency of the signal (Doppler
shift) is observed, proportional to the velocity of the aerosols: 𝑓= 𝑓0(1 + 𝑣/𝑐) , where 𝑓0 is
the frequency of the transmitted laser beam, 𝑣is the velocity of the aerosols and 𝑐denotes the
speed of light. Now, to observe the above, the return signal is mixed with a portion of the
transmitted signal, better exhibited as Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1.: General schematic of CDL system Pitter et al. [2015]
However, the figure illustrates a bi-static system, contrary to the mono-static system which uses
one optics arrangement for both transmitted and received signals, as also mentioned above. On
mixing the return signal and the transmitted signal, the resulting beat (minor difference) signal
is observed by the detector3. The beat is the summation of the Doppler shift and the frequency
offset, and yields the former as the latter is known, which is essentially the definition of Optical
Heterodyne (more than one signal) Detection, see Figure A.1 for a typical schematic example.
In Figure 2.2, the frequency distribution of the return signal is observed. The clear spike is the
depiction of the movement of the aerosols, which is due to the wind velocity. The remaining
portion of the spectrum with lower intensity denotes the random fluctuations of the molecules.
The CDL depends on the back scatter signal from the aerosols and assumes that the wind
flow is directly represented by the movement of the atmospheric particles. Thus it is equally
affected by suspended precipitation or dust in the atmosphere. The beat signal, being a minor
frequency deviation from the transmitted beam, necessitates that the spikes occurring at any
other wavelengths are to be neglected.
This is one of the major advantages of having the
reference beam [M.Harris et al. [2006]], along with its higher sensitivity and inherent calibration
capability.

is observed as a response. Due to the volume averaging to find the 𝑉LOS, it can be understood
as a moving average over all the different velocities instantaneously encountered by the beam
volume. This variations in wind speeds can be seen from the width of the instantaneous Doppler
spectrum. However, the centroid will always miss the extreme wind velocities encountered by
the beam. So the time series of 𝑉LOS looks like a low-pass filtered output of the true wind
speed time series, also see Figure 2.8. Furthermore, the ensemble average of all the Doppler
spectrum over the wind speed time series (< 𝑆𝑣𝑟>), can be represented as the probability
density function of the radial wind speeds 𝑣𝑟, considering that the 𝑣𝑟does not depend upon the
radial distance (𝑠) [Mann et al. [2010]].
However, it can be understood that in order to measure the complete wind velocity vector
consisting of the velocity in [𝑥, 𝑦, 𝑧] direction, 3 beams pointing at the same point is necessary.
For the setup that this report will utilize, only one beam focusses at one point. Thus it can be
understood that the 𝑉LOS detected by the lidar will correspond to the projection of the wind
velocity vector onto the beam. However, if the lidar is pointing straight into the incoming wind
flow, then the 𝑉LOS will essentially follow the u-component of the beam (considering 𝑢, 𝑣, 𝑤as
velocity components in [𝑥, 𝑦, 𝑧] directions). For the purpose of this report, the wake analysis
uses both straight beam and the angular beam of the lidar. Further on this topic will be dealt
in the following chapters.

3.1. Literature review
Wind flows in the wake of a wind turbine features a reduction in mean wind speed (known as,
and herein mentioned as, wake deficit or velocity deficit) and an increase in turbulence (Larsen
[2009]). This in general, changes the wind flow environment faced by the downstream wind
turbines, and impacts it with a loss of production and uneven loading. Wind turbine wakes in
general can be classified into two regimes, near wakes and the far wakes (Vermeer et al. [2008]).
Near wakes range approximately until one rotor diameter downstream and in this regime, the
influence of separate rotor blades can be distinguished. The far wake is the regime beyond
one rotor diameter, and in this region the interaction and the effect of the wake towards the
other wind turbines downstream becomes of the essence, rather than the influence of individual
blades [Vermeer et al. [2008]]. This report focuses on the analysis of the wake at a distance of
three rotor diameters, which can be seen as a far wake scenario.
Several wake models have been studied and reviewed throughout the literature. Vermeer et al.
[2008] categorizes far-wake models into Kinematic Models (as discussed below), Field models,
where the magnitudes of the flow field is calculated at every point; and Boundary layer model,
where the properties of the flow is modelled based on the atmospheric stability.
Kinematic models describes the wake from after its initial expansion. It models the velocity
deficits behind a turbine based on the momentum equations. These models are coupled together
with the turbulence model, in order to retrieve the entire wind flow. One such model is the
Jensen model, which assumes that the wake expands linearly downstream from the rotor. Due
to its computational ease, this model finds its use in many wind farm planning softwares, like
WAsP. However, the wake model considered in this report is the Dynamic Wake Meandering
Model (DWM model) implemented within the aeroelastic code HAWC2. Within the purview
of the report, the relevant features of DWM model is discussed and further reading is available
through Larsen [2009] and Larsen et al. [2008].

3.2.1. Dynamic Wake Meandering Model
Wind turbine wakes can be viewed from two reference frames. One is the meandering frame of
reference and the other is the rotor frame of reference. From the meandering frame of reference
the small scale turbulence can be observed. The small scale turbulence through its diffusion and
mixing tend to 𝑟𝑒𝑐𝑜𝑣𝑒𝑟the wind velocity and thus expand or widen the wake, viewed from the
meandering frame of reference. However, this small scale turbulence 𝑏𝑜𝑥exhibits movements
in very large turbulent pattern, which can be viewed 𝑖𝑛𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑡𝑎𝑛𝑒𝑜𝑢𝑠𝑙𝑦from the rotor frame
of reference. These large turbulence tends to cause advection of the wake as a whole. Thus,
from the rotor frame of reference the wind flow is seen to 𝑚𝑒𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑒𝑟its way downstream both
laterally and vertically, and thus the name. The description of the DWM model draws heavy
reference from Larsen et al. [2008], Larsen [2009] and Larsen [1988]
The DWM model has been described as a three block model [Larsen et al. [2008]]. The first
block constitutes those small scale turbulence and the related velocity deficit as discussed
above, depicted as the 𝑞𝑢𝑎𝑠𝑖−𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑎𝑑𝑦𝑤𝑎𝑘𝑒𝑑𝑒𝑓𝑖𝑐𝑖𝑡. The quasi-steady wake deficit is viewed
from the meandering frame of reference, and it primarily accounts for the wake expansion
as the wake travels downstream. The second block is the 𝑚𝑒𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑒𝑟𝑖𝑛𝑔process, which is the
transportation of large scale turbulent structures. This transport medium is inherently a large
scale stochastic turbulence field, which acts to move the deficit along with it. The third is the
wake 𝑖𝑛𝑑𝑢𝑐𝑒𝑑turbulence. These turbulence are contributed by the wake itself, as the wake
travels downstream and are not dependant on the ambient turbulence. Thus, the turbulence in
the wake consists of three parts, the ambient turbulence, the meandering turbulence and the
wake induced turbulence. The wake induced turbulence is modeled empirically by scaling an
isotropic and homogeneous turbulence field [Aagaard Madsen et al. [2010]]. The scaling factor
is given by,

3.2.3. Other factors affecting velocity deficits
At high wind speeds the induction factor is low (please see Figure 3.3 and related discussions
above), resulting the low thrusts and the velocity deficits. Furthermore, at high wind speed
the wind turbine operates near the 𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑙𝑙zone. Stall being characterized by a flow separation at
the low pressure side of the airfoil, caused by sudden generation of irregular turbulence, may
further increase turbulent mixing causing faster wake recovery [Laratro et al. [2014]]. All of
these may collectively impact the the velocity deficits rapidly at higher ambient wind speeds.
However, it can be understood and seen from the 𝐶𝑡curves that the negative pitch angles goes
to 𝑠𝑡𝑎𝑙𝑙at lower wind speeds as compared to positive pitch angles. Thus the cases considered in
this report are chosen accordingly. The increase of turbulence also plays a major role in wake
recovery. High wind speeds added with high turbulence intensities provide further assistance
in wake recovery. All of the above may contribute to the observation that at high wind velocity
and high turbulence scenarios, the 𝑚𝑒𝑎𝑛3 velocity deficit at all pitch angles are similar to each
other. However, high ambient turbulence has an impact on the 𝑠𝑐𝑎𝑡𝑡𝑒𝑟of the deficit values
across the wake field. Thus, the errors (discussed in the next chapter) may be observed to
increase as the ambient turbulence goes higher.

4.5.1. General Procedural Steps

∙With the ambient wind conditions known - That means at least one lidar beam is incident

4.5.3. Discussion
To briefly summarize the results, a nacelle based lidar pointing into the wake can face two
conditions, a half-wake scenario where at least one of the lidar beam is into the free-stream, or
a typical partial wake condition where the turbine faces partial wake but all the lidar beams
are in the wake. The aim of this study was to device a methodology and relationship through
which the velocity deficits in the wake can be estimated, under both the previous scenarios.
From the above examples, it can be understood that with the knowledge of the ambient wind
speed, turbulence intensity and the operating pitch angle of the upstream wind turbine, the
velocity deficit can be estimated. Without the knowledge of the ambient conditions, the FWHM
from the lidar can be used to approximate the velocity deficit. However, it may be difficult to
achieve a FWHM accurate enough to pin point the 𝑐𝑜𝑟𝑟𝑒𝑐𝑡deficit estimate. This is because the
measured FWHM is often seen to be very close to more than one value in the table. However,
it is also observed that the FWHM 𝑙𝑜𝑜𝑘−𝑢𝑝table [please see Figure 4.12] does not have any
overlaps when the operating pitch angle of the upstream wind turbine is known. For example,
in case of a positive pitch angle and a particular turbulence intensity, the FWHM values never
repeat. Thus it might be an option to reduce the error, if the ambient turbulence is known. For
application in a wind farm, the averaged prediction of the turbulence intensity may be attained.
It is also seen from the FWHM Table 4.8 and the Deficit Table 4.7 that for wind speeds close
to rated wind speed, the rate by which the deficit change for an unit change in wind speed
is more than that for an unit change in the turbulence intensity. Thus, with an approximate
knowledge of the turbulence intensity will help to search the measured FWHM within those
relevant 𝑐𝑜𝑙𝑢𝑚𝑛𝑠rather than the entire table. In addition to the above, this Deficit and FWHM
table can be effectively used by many other studies which require estimation of the velocity
deficit, for example yaw correction, etc. The deviations that had been observed in the previous
examples might be within the tolerance limit of the cumulative outcome.
It can be observed from the results that linear interpolation is a good approximation between
the turbulence levels and the wind speeds. In examples 1 and 3, velocities and turbulence
intensities are considered within the interpolated region. The actual deficit observed matches
closely with the estimate. However, for better estimation, similar process for all the intermediate
velocities and turbulence intensities is recommended for future work. Furthermore, as this study
considers no meandering, the future work also may include it. The study also endeavoured to
observe whether this model can estimate a velocity deficit with respect to a FWHM observed
at 5D distance, contrary to the 3D distance that this model is based upon. In one example the
FWHM read as 0.4736 with the upstream turbine pitch angle as 5∘. Corresponding to the table
deviced in this study, the estimated velocity deficit was 2.194 m/s. The actual deficit observed
was 2.4512 m/s. It may be because of the fact that in absence of the meandering process, the
major contributor to the wake turbulence is the ambient and wake induced turbulence. The
wake induced turbulence (with reference to Equation (3.1)) may be dependent on the deficit at
a far distance. However, this can also be a part of the future work in addition to this study.

5. Summary and Conclusion
The motivation of this work was to analyze the wind turbine wakes by the means of a continuous
wave, nacelle-based lidar.
The wake analysis was more focused on estimating the velocity
deficits in the wake from the lidar measurements. For approaching the work, a lidar model
was developed (MATLAB based) which was used to analyze different wake fields, generated
based on the Dynamic Wake Meandering model. However, some of the important practical
assumptions and focuses were considered in order to simplify the study. For example, it was
assumed that the wake does not meander, and that the lidar measurements were noise-free.
The study only focused at a downstream distance on 3𝐷(378 metres) and focused on using
the lidar to incident straight beam into the wake.
The study was primarily divided into three categories, the first to model the Lidar, the second
to formulate the relationship table between the ambient wind conditions and the wake charac-
teristics, and finally to formulate the procedure to use the results effectively. In the process,
the lidar was modeled which can respond to a 3-dimensional wind field and provide the line of
sight velocities 𝑉LOS, and the full-width half-maximum of the Doppler spectrum 𝐹𝑊𝐻𝑀as
output. The lidar was used on several 3-dimensional wake fields, which simulated the combi-
nations of wind speeds of 8m/s, 12 m/s, and 16 m/s; ambient turbulence intensities of 4%, 8%,
12%, and 16%; and collective pitch angles of 5∘, 2.5∘, 0∘and -2.5∘, and all the combinations
thereafter. For each of these cases, the velocity deficit across the velocity field was calculated.
From the each data set, one such 𝑟𝑒𝑝𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑣𝑒value was taken that would minimize the
Root-Mean-Square error 𝑅𝑀𝑆𝐸. These values exhibited the relationship between the velocity
deficit and the ambient and operating conditions. The data points thus received formulated an
estimation 𝑡𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒. As the study considered distinct ambient wind speeds, turbulence intensities,
the table was broadened by linearly interpolating between the ambient conditions. Through
the estimation 𝑡𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒it was thus shown that it is possible to estimate the velocity deficit values.
The only constant input that the 𝑡𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒takes is the operating pitch angle of the upstream wind
turbine.
While analyzing the wake deficits with the lidar, two procedures were considered,
∙If the ambient wind conditions are known, i.e. at least one of the lidar beam is in the
free-stream, or in other words it is a half-wake condition. It is possible to estimate the
velocity deficit based on the ambient wind speed, ambient turbulence intensity and the
operating pitch angle of the upstream wind turbine.
∙If the ambient condition is not know, i.e. a partial wake condition where the lidar beams
are falling inside the wake. In that case, the FWHM, which is an output of the lidar, can
be used along with the pitch angle, to reasonably estimate the wake.
The first procedure has been observed to estimate the velocity deficit very closely. The second
procedure process incurs some deviations. However, the velocity deficit estimation from lidar
measurement of the wake turbulence, may act as a tool for many other studies. As future
