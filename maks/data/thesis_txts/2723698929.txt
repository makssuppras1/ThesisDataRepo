Summary 
5 
The increasing melt of the Greenland Ice Sheet introduces a significant volume of freshwater in the ocean 
6 
and changes the coastal environments around Greenland. The increased melt causes glacier retreat, 
7 
reduces salinity, increases stratification, and delivers inorganic particle nutrients and organic matter to 
8 
Greenland fjords. Although there are several studies regarding specific fjords around Greenland, the 
9 
literature lacks a direct comparison between fjords in different areas during the peak discharge season. 
10 
This study compares on physical parameters, chlorophyll-a concentration, and copepod community along 
11 
three fjords in the northeast (Young Sound), southwest (Nuup Kangerlua), and west (Disko fjord) 
12 
Greenland, in August 2022. The study showed environmental gradients along fjords, influenced by the 
13 
type of discharge (i.e., tidewater and land-terminating glaciers) and adjusted water masses. Chlorophyll-
14 
a concentration was lower than previously reported values and the phytoplankton community was 
15 
dominated by large and small cells, in northeast and southwest, respectively. The Copepod community 
16 
was dominated by large Calanus species in Young Sound and Nuup Kangerlua, whilst, in Disko 
17 
Fjord, Pseudocalanus spp. was also found in high numbers. Moreover, small copepod distribution was 
18 
associated with increased temperature, salinity, and stratification of the water column. Based on this 
19 
study and those previously reported in the literature, there is amble evidence that the structure and 
20 
function of the pelagic ecosystem in Greenland is impacted by run-off. The continued increased melting 
21 
of the Greenland Ice Sheet is thus likely to cascade to higher trophic levels with likely socio-economic 
22 
ramification.

Introduction 
26 
Warming in the Arctic melts the cryosphere, and much attention has been given to the effect of changes 
27 
in sea ice on the marine ecosystem. A significant outcome of climate change is the melting of the 
28 
Greenlandic Ice Sheet (GIS), which can become unstable in the future (Boers & Rypdal, 2021). On a global 
29 
scale, the complete melting of the GIS will increase sea level by up to 7 meters in the next millennium 
30 
(Aschwanden et al., 2019), and it will slow down the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) 
31 
before the latter collapses and alters global climate (Boers & Rypdal, 2021). Locally, the increasing runoff 
32 
modifies the physical and chemical properties of the fjords and coastal areas around Greenland; thus, the 
33 
biological communities’ structure and function in them (Middelbo et al., 2018; Mankoff et al., 2020).  
34 
Description of coasts/fjords of Greenland 
35 
Fjords are pathways connecting the Greenland Ice Sheet and the coastal areas around Greenland. More 
36 
specifically, fjords play an important role in the general circulation and distribution of water masses 
37 
around Greenland and participate in the regulation of heat exchange between outer glaciers and the 
38 
ocean (Mortensen et al., 2013). Fjords in Greenland display a variety of physical and chemical 
39 
characteristics, like temperature and salinity, due to different boundary conditions of the systems, i.e., 
40 
the type of adjustment water masses, local atmospheric conditions, ice melt discharge, and the existence 
41 
of icebergs and sea ice (Sejr et al., 2017; Jorgensen et al., 2020). On the West Greenland Shelf, fjords are 
42 
affected by three distinct water masses (Southwest Greenland Coast Water, Arctic Water, and Subpolar 
43 
Mode Water), which differentiate the water properties between the North and South, and continental 
44 
slope and fjord entrances. More specifically, there is a northward coastal current of coastal water, 
45 
originating from east Greenland, and Atlantic water that go up to around 67oN, where it is blocked by the 
46 
Arctic water coming from Baffin Bay. The Arctic water mass, which has a lower temperature, nutrient 
47 
loading, and salinity than the subpolar mode water, creates a northward coastal current that extends 
48 
along the northwest Greenland coast (Rysgaard et al., 2020).  In the East Greenland Shelf, the main water 
49 
masses are Polar and Atlantic water. In contrast to the west, the coastal current is southward with the 
50 
upper column (~250m) consisted of Arctic water, whilst there is a northward Atlantic water current below 
51 
which approaches the Greenland shelf at around 84oN (Gjelstrup et al., 2022). The surface currents affect 
52 
the formation of sea ice. Sea ice is not found along most of the southwest Greenland. On the west, 
53 
northwest, southeast, and east of Greenland, there is sea ice formation, which melts during the summer 
54 
seasons, similarly to the northern American and Siberian coasts. Last, the northern coasts are covered by 
55 
multiyear ice. The discharge of meltwater from the Ice Sheet also shows regional differences. The 
56 
southeast and southwest basins in Greenland discharged three times more water, with a maximum of up 
57 
to 1.4e8 kg m-2, than the northern and central west basins during the period 2017-2021. Furthermore, 
58 
while variations in runoff are observed in the northern basins, the impact of these variations on the 
59 
southern basins has been found to be minimal. Interestingly, recent studies suggest that the runoff from 
60 
the northern regions is increasing at a higher pace, which could have important implications for the coastal 
61 
ecosystems in the region (Solgaard et al., 2022; Noel et al., 2019).  
62 
In Greenland, the ice sheet runoff is highly correlated with summer temperatures in the coastal areas and 
63 
annual snowmelt rates (Hanna et al., 2007 – see also Appendix, box 1). In addition to the seasonal export 
64 
of glacial meltwater, there is a significant input of nutrients, including Phosphorus, Silica, Nitrogen, and 
65 
Iron (Hendry et al., 2019), which influence the distribution and magnitude of biological production. As a 
66 
result, fjords can be both extremely productive and unproductive (e.g., Meire et al., 2017; Holding et al., 
67 
2019). For example, West Greenland fjords sustain a rich ecosystem with an abundance of fish, sea birds 
68 
==PAGE:6==
3 
 
and marine mammals and they generate high socio-economic value (Crosby & Arndt, 2017). On the other 
69 
hand, fjords in the northeast are considered low-production systems. Nevertheless, the productivity and 
70 
dynamics of the system are also linked to the type of glacier terminus and the adjusted water masses. 
71 
More specifically, land-terminating glaciers only provide freshwater at the surface of the water column, 
72 
while marine-terminating glaciers provide freshwater runoff both at the surface and in depth (figure 1.a 
73 
& b) (Meire et al., 2017). In land-terminating fjords, the surface inflow creates a strong stratification of 
74 
the water column (Stuart Lee et al., 2023). On the other hand, in the case of marine-terminating glaciers, 
75 
freshwater enters the system also at depth, resulting in upwelling and the input of nutrients and dissolved 
76 
inorganic carbon in the euphotic zone (Kanna et al., 2019; Mortensen et al., 2013). The latter, along with 
77 
less or nonexistent ice cover in southern regions, gives rise to late summer blooms (Juul-Pedersen et al., 
78 
2015). Additionally, these fjords have higher inflow rates of meltwater as glacial ice moves faster, and 
79 
freshwater is introduced in the systems both as water and solid ice, which also influences temperature. 
80 
In addition, marine-terminating glaciers have complex hydrodynamics, which are not fully described yet. 
81 
Nevertheless, some of the processes that affect them are the morphology, position, and depth of the 
82 
terminus and the formed plumes (Joughin et al., 2008; Mankoff et al., 2016; Cowton et al., 2019), tides 
83 
(Voytenko et al., 2015), mélange buttressing (Moon et al., 2015), and the rate of ocean warming (Straneo 
84 
et al., 2011, Cowton et al., 2015).

Biological community composition 
98 
The different environmental conditions influence the biological composition of the  systems. In land-
99 
terminating glacier fjords, maximum concentration of primary producers (DCM) can be located near the 
100 
nitracline (like in an oligotrophic system), or it can be in shallow waters, depending on the light and 
101 
nutrient conditions, for example when daytime is shorter, or turbidity is higher (similar to a typical 
102 
estuary). During summer, DCM is in greater depths than in winter. In northern Greenland, chlorophyll-a 
103 
concentrations peak up in July (Holding et al., 2019), with smaller cells dominating the area near the 
104 
runoff. Near tidewater glaciers, phytoplankton blooms occur in the boundary of fresh surface and turbid 
105 
submerging water (Kanna et al., 2018). Moreover, in tidewater glaciers in southeast Greenland, 
106 
chlorophyll-a concentration peaks from May (when there can be both a surface and sub-surface 
107 
maximum) to August (just in surface waters). During this period, the phytoplankton community is 
108 
dominated by diatoms and chrysophytes (Topp-Jørgensen et al., 2017). 
109 
Mesozooplankton communities in Arctic regions (over 65oN) are dominated by copepods. These 
110 
organisms have evolved numerous strategies in response to the annual changes in sea ice and irradiance 
111 
(Darnis & Fortier, 2014). Copepod community is not consistent along fjords (Middelbo et al., 2018). In 
112 
terms of biomass, the most abundant species belong to the genus Calanus. Their numbers are increasing 
113 
significantly during the productive season; thus, they are considered ‘the most im ortant gra ers’ in the 
114 
region (Middelbo et al., 2018). During winter, when primary production reduces dramatically, Calanus 
115 
species migrate in deeper waters, and they are minimizing their metabolism until the surface conditions 
116 
become ideal again (as feeding and reproducing grounds) (Darnis & Fortier, 2014). On the other hand, 
117 
smaller mesozooplankton species are active during the whole year (Zamora-Terrol et al., 2014), thus they 
118 
dominate the surface waters while the biggest species diapause (Madsen et al., 2008). Feeding habits of 
119 
small mesozooplankton vary, with smaller egg-carrying copepods (e.g., Oithona similis) mainly preferring 
120 
motile protozooplankton and phytoplankton cells (Zamora-Terrol et al., 2014). Other species like Triconia 
121 
borealis are exhibiting omnivorous behaviour (Martynova & Gordeeva, 2010), and Pseudocalanus species 
122 
are suspension feeders (Eiane & Ohman, 2004). Larger Calanus spp. copepods exhibit both herbivorous 
123 
and omnivorous behaviour (Hansen et al., 1994).

126

127

128

131

132

133

134 
availability in surface layers is reduced (Capotondi et al., 2012). Increased runoff can affect circulation 
135

Sea surface temperature and salinity decreased in locations between 50o-60oN during the last decades 
142

143

145

146

147 
During 1998-2009, primary production (PP) increased in eastern Greenland but decreased on the western 
148 
coasts and Barren Sea (Arrigo & van Dijken, 2011; Straneo et al., 2011). Studies were expecting that higher 
149 
PP would positively affect the abundance of all trophic levels in these environments (Slagstad et al., 2011). 
150 
However, these predictions were made with numerical models since remote estimates are impractical 
151 
and in situ data is sparse (Middelbo et al., 2018). Increased stratification and limited nutrient availability 
152 
favour smaller heterotrophic and mixotrophic phytoplankton cells (Li et al., 2009; Maselli et al., 2023). 
153 
Secondary producers are also affected due to changes in their available prey, and they exhibit reduced 
154 
growth in areas with small primary producers. The last has further implications in energy transfer 
155 
(Wassmann et al., 2011; Middelbo et al., 2017). 
156 
Scientific question 
157 
The spatiotemporal dynamics of ice melting make it challenging to compare different fjords during the 
158 
same period. Although there have been studies that focus on specific fjords and processes, there is limited 
159 
information on the similarities and differences among multiple fjords during the same period. Therefore, 
160 
it is crucial to compare the impact of meltwater on the physical and biological structures in various 
161 
Greenland fjords during the same time. 
162 
Aim of study 
163 
This study focuses on three different fjords in south-west (63oN, 50oW), central west (70oN 50oW), and 
164 
north-east (78oN, 20oW) Greenland during the peak of the melting season (early-mid August). The study's 
165 
purpose was to assess and compare the physical properties, chlorophyll-a and mesozooplankton 
166 
community of the fjords and investigate possible correlation to the physical and chemical environment 
167 
affect the systems' biological characteristics. Finally, the sensitivity of each fjord is evaluated based on 
168 
their physical attributes and biological communities.

246 
Light attenuation for the first five- and 50-meters was calculated by using the Weibull two-face function 
247 
(Murray et al., 2015). Photic depth was defined based on the location where the light intensity was equal 
248 
to 1% of the surface irradiance. Areal chlorophyll was determined by integrating chl-a values for the first 
249 
50 meters. Mixed layer depth was defined as the depth, where the biggest density difference per meter 
250 
was observed below five meters (Holding et al., 2019) and the stratification index (SI) was calculated by 
251 
subtracting the density at five meters from the density value at 50 meters (Murray et al., 2015). The wind 
252 
energy required to mix the first 50 meters of the water column was estimated based on the Simpson index 
253 
of the mixed layer (Simpson, 1981). Mixed layer temperature and salinity was estimated by taking the 
254 
average of all measurements down to mixed layer depth. The nitracline was identified after visual 
255 
inspection of the nitrate diagrams. Fresh water content of the first 50 meters was estimated by using the 
256 
formula of Stuart-Lee et al. (2021). The reference salinity value was set equal to the max measured in each 
257 
site. All environmental indices were tested in pairs for correlation (Pearson correlation, p<=0.05). 
258 
Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) and canonical correspondence analysis (CCA) were 
259 
performed with the aid of the vegan package (Oksanen et al., 2022). All data were transformed before the 
260 
examination to improve their normality and stabilize their variance. Cluster analysis was used to group 
261 
the stations based on their copepod abundance (Bray & Curtis 1957), and Similarity Percentages analysis 
262 
(SIMPER) was used to unveil the species responsible for the heterogeneity between groups. The seed was 
263 
set in 151091. Only copepods recognized at the species-level were included in the multivariate analyses.

biggest and smallest, respectively. However, there were trends in the environmental gradients along 
510 
fjords, that corresponded to glacier type and freshwater discharge. Temperature showed two patterns 
511 
along fjords, reflecting the type of inflow. In the land-terminating glaciers (YS, DF), the water formed a 
512 
warmer layer on top of the water column, like in other land-terminating fjords in Greenland (e.g., Stuart 
513 
Lee et al., 2021). On the other hand, in the marine-ending glacier in southwest Greenland (NF), the cold 
514 
water from the submerged glacier formed a cupule that extended from 5- to 45-meter depth and over 
515 
100km long, similar to the report of Stuart Lee et al. (2023). The surface temperature can be affected by 
516 
additional inflows in the system, like in the case of the Zackenberg river in YS (station YS. 3.14), which 
517 
reduced local surface temperature. Previous studies have found a wider mixed freshwater lens in YS 
518 
during this time of the year (Sejr et al., 2017). The difference could be explained by the lower 
519 
temperatures, the late ice break up this summer, and less freshwater inflow during the sampling period, 
520 
but it needs further investigation. In addition, sea surface temperature in DF, was lower than reported 
521 
values in July 2019 (Maselli et al., 2023). The salinity pattern was similar among sites, even though the 
522 
surface values differed from previous reports (e.g., Maselli et al., 2023). However, the freshwater lens was 
523 
wider in the NF, possibly caused by the larger amount of freshwater input in the system, and there was a 
524 
shallow salinity gradient in the surface water of YS and NF, like in other studies (e.g., Stuart Lee et al., 
525 
2023; Holding et al., 2019).  he turbidit ’s  attern also reflected the corres onding glacier t  e   ith land-

Additionally, in YS and NF, the turbidity was increased for a longer distance than in DF indicating larger

536 
The oceanic indices, apart from mixed layer temperature and chlorophyll-a concentrations showed 
537 
analogous trends in all sites. Mixed layer temperature was less in NF and the outer part of YS due to lower 
538 
temperature water coming both from land and the ocean. The T-S diagram clearly distinguished the 
539 
surface water of all areas, reflecting the different weather conditions in all areas. However, the deep T-S 
540

541 
Furthermore, it clearly distinguished the bottom water between east and west, which agreed with 
542 
previous reported findings (Rysgaard et al., 2020; Gjelstrup et al., 2022). In YS, the sill in the outer fjord is 
543 
blocking the dense, warmer Atlantic water from entering the fjord, resulting in a less saline environment 
544 
in depth compared to the other two systems. Moreover, the presence of the arctic water lens results in a 
545 
dominance of water with lower temperature inside the fjord compared to the other two systems, 
546 
reflecting further the difference from warmer water masses on the west Greenland shelf. This, along with 
547 
the different temperature and runoff among systems, indicates the impact by different boundary 
548 
conditions on both land and the ocean. (Sejr et al., 2017). Last, in YS, the low surface nitrate concentration 
549

indicates a strong stratification that blocks the upwelling of nutrients (Rysgaard et al., 1997). 
550 
Concentration of NH3 was higher in DF, compared to the other sites. However, further investigation is 
551 
needed to examine whether there is a surplus production from higher level organisms and the rates of 
552 
remineralization from the microbial community (Arendt et al., 2016). 
553 
Even though the oceanic indices were having similar trends and - in certain cases - values, the NMDS 
554 
analysis further revealed similarities and differences among sites and based on the relative position of the 
555 
stations. More specifically, stations in Disko fjord that were closer to the main freshwater inflow, had 
556 
higher mixed layer depth, temperature, salinity, and chlorophyll maximum. In Nuuk, the stations could be 
557 
divided into two categories. First, the stations closer to the glacier (NF_14, NF_13, NF_10, NF_9, NF_8, 
558 
and NF_6), which had increased light attenuation at 50 meters, higher chlorophyll-a concentration, and a 
559 
more stratified water column, and second, the rest of the stations (NF_5, NF_4 and NF_3), which had 
560 
increased mixed layer and euphotic zone depth.  Young Sound was the most diverse system, as its stations 
561 
were at three-quarters on the NMDS diagram. Stations close to freshwater inflow had greater 
562 
stratification and increased light attenuation at 5 meters, and stations at a greater distance had increased 
563 
euphotic zone and mixed layer depth. Moreover, the analysis provided indications that additional input 
564 
in YS further stratifies the water column and increases light attenuation at surface. However, the NMDS 
565 
analysis failed to properly place the DF stations on the graph regarding areal chlorophyll-a concentrations, 
566 
indicating the limitations of the analysis, when there are multiple relationships among variables and a 
567 
limited dataset. Nevertheless, the first stations in all sites were outliers, as they differed in environmental 
568 
conditions and biological community composition compared to the rest of the stations in the respective 
569 
systems. Distinct differences also existed due to local atmospheric conditions and geological and 
570 
hydrological characteristics (Maselli et al., 2023). An example of the geological influence is the high values 
571 
of Silica in DF. However, more information is needed to evaluate the exact forces that drive this result. 
572 
Silica can act as conservative and non-conservative nutrient, as estuarine mixing releases Si, and it can be 
573 
utilized by unicellular organisms, like diatoms (Hopwood et al., 2020).  
574 
Description and comparison of Biota  
575 
In general, the concentration of chlorophyll-a was lower compared to previous years (Arendt et al., 2013; 
576 
Stuart Lee., 2023). The higher and lower values of chl-a were observed at NF and YS, respectively (Holding 
577 
et al., 2019). The differences are owed, among others, to increased turbidity and decreased nitrate values 
578 
in surface waters in land-terminating systems and higher light availability and upwelling of nutrients in 
579 
tidewater glaciers (Juul-Pedersen et al., 2015). Moreover, the chl-a maximum was located below the 
580 
mixed layer when sufficient light penetrated the water column (ratio of euphotic to mixed layer depth > 
581 
2.5) (Holding et al., 2019). The chlorophyll-a concentration was increased in the inner DF and NF, but not 
582 
in YS, probably due to higher values of nutrients in these stations (Stuart Lee., 2023). On the other hand, 
583 
the concentration pattern was similar from the middle to the outer fjord in all sites, with higher values in 
584 
the southwest and west. However, the depth of chlorophyll-a maximum differed between the eastern 
585 
and western fjords. In the west, DCM was found in low depth along the most of the transects, whereas in 
586 
the east, DCM was in greater depth inside the fjord, due to nutrient-poor upper layer (Juul-Pedersen et 
587 
al., 2015, Murray et al., 2015). Moreover, in the outer part of NF, chlorophyll maximum was in shallower 
588 
waters compared to the other systems, reflecting the differences in the systems, caused by the different 
589 
water masses and meltwater input (Stuart Lee et al., 2023).  
590 
The phytoplankton cell size distribution varied among sites, with large cells contributing more to the total 
591 
mass in Young Sound than in Nuuk. More specifically, in YS, large cells increased with distance inside the 
592 
==PAGE:26==
23 
 
fjord and decreased in the outer station, which is congruent with the work of Sejr et al. (2022), although 
593 
in their work they reported dominance of smaller cells in the first stations, which is not verified by the 
594 
present study. On the other hand, the increased small phytoplankton cells in NF, as well as the higher 
595 
relative abundance in the outer fjord, comes in line with the report of Stuart Lee et al. (2023), in which 
596 
they showed an increase of smaller cells with distance during summer. Nevertheless, dominance of small 
597 
phytoplankton cells indicates a retreating glacier, and it is correlated with higher temperatures and runoff 
598 
volumes (Stuart Lee et al., 2023). This, along with the increased values of chlorophyll-a in the inner fjord 
599 
compared to the same study, reveal the importance of large cells in the total phytoplankton mass. 
600 
Nevertheless, the proportion of large phytoplankton cells in the total community was higher in Young 
601 
Sound than in Nuuk. The phytoplankton community composition can be assessed based on its grazers. For 
602 
example, Oithona spp. use ambush tactics to catch its prey, with a preference for mobile prey, especially 
603 
ciliates and dinoflagellates (Jonasdottir et al., 2013). This indicates high levels of ciliates and 
604 
dinoflagellates in all systems (Henriksen et al., 2007). The measured Calanus nauplii consume small-sized 
605 
food, i.e., flagellates, which indicates higher numbers in DF and lower in the other two systems. It's likely 
606 
that the calanoids also feed on other larger auto- and heterotrophic protists. 
607 
Regardless of the concentration of Chl-a being low in YS, copepod density did not follow a similar trend. 
608 
Instead, the larger phytoplankton cells seemed to affect the number of large copepods; thus, the overall 
609 
mass of the system. In the middle part of YS, copepod density was higher compared to Nuuk, and 
610 
compared to similar published studies (e.g., Middelbo et al., 2017). Additionally, the biomass in the middle 
611 
part of the fjord was higher compared to areas close to the estuary and to the open sea. The 
612 
mesozooplankton mass in YS was dominated by large Calanus species. More specifically, C. glacialis, C. 
613 
finmarchicus, and C. hyperboreus. This result matches the report of Middelbo et al. (2018), in which they 
614 
found dominance of Calanus species in the system.  In NF, Calanus species also dominated the niche, but 
615 
only C. finmarchicus and C. hyperboreus were found in the inner fjord and small numbers of C. hyperboreus 
616 
were found in the outer fjord. In DF, the large Calanus species showed a similar distribution to NF. Thus, 
617 
there were indicators that C. hyperboreus is mainly encountered on the eastern Greenland shelf, and in 
618 
the west, they can be found in the open sea rather than near the coast (Visser et al., 2017). Another 
619 
profound difference is the little contribution in the total mass of small copepod species in the large 
620 
systems, compared to DF. Moreover, the small copepod community composition is different in all sites. 
621 
T. borealis was found only in YS and the inner part of NF, indicating a preference for the organisms in large 
622 
systems and less saline (brackish) and colder waters. On the contrary, M. norvegica was present only in 
623 
western systems, indicating that sea ice and lower temperatures impede their distribution to the 
624 
northeast (Koski et al., 2021). Additionally, M. norvegica, like Oncaea spp. rely more on marine snow for 
625 
food rather than suspended phytoplankton and higher turbidity can increase their available food, which 
626 
explains the higher numbers in the inner station of DF (Koski., 2021, Stuart-Lee., Submitted). Also, their 
627 
smaller stages can be targeted by larger copepods, and reduced visibility provides protection against 
628 
predators (Middelbo et al., 2018). In NF, M. norvegica mass contribution to the total copepod mass 
629 
matched the reported values of Stuart lee et al. (Submitted), but they differed from other studies (e.g., 
630 
Arendt et al., 2013). However, in their work, Arendt et al. (2013) use a length-weight relationship that 
631 
overestimates the mass of the organisms compared to other published formulas (Appendix). Additionally, 
632 
Stuart Lee et al. (submitted) reported higher total mass of copepods compared to the present study. 
633 
Unidentified Pseudocalanus species were found in larger quantities in DF, which can be explained by the 
634 
shallower and warmer waters, as temperature enhances their metabolism and reproduction rates (Lischka 
635 
& Hagen, 2005; McLaren et al., 1989). The positive effect of temperature on copepod reproduction is 
636 
==PAGE:27==
24 
 
further supported by the increased numbers of eggs and nauplii in DF, in comparison to the other two 
637 
sites. P. acuspes was found mainly in the outer part of YS and NF, indicating their preference for colder, 
638 
less stratified waters. In NF, the total copepod mass was decreased compared to similar studies (e.g., 
639 
Stuart Lee et al., submitted) and the composition is different with more Calanus species and fewer small 
640 
species being present during this study.  
641 
The study's estimates of copepod abundances may have been affected by limited sampling depth. 
642 
Specifically, in DF, where the fjord is shallow, most of the water column was sampled, resulting in a low 
643 
error in total abundance. However, large Calanus species, which are not overwintering inside the fjord, 
644 
could have been underestimated, as they are advected from Disko Bay where they are more abundant 
645 
(Madsen et al., 2001). In Young Sound, copepods are predominantly found in the upper 100 meters of the 
646 
water column, except for C. finmarchicus and Microcalanus spp., which may have been underestimated 
647 
in this study (Arendt et al., 2016). Finally, in NF, sampling only in the upper 100 m could lead to a slight 
648 
underestimation of zooplankton abundance due to their vertical migration to deeper waters during the 
649 
day (Arendt et al., 2012). 
650 
The NMDS analysis revealed a clear distinction between DF and YS and NF. Based on the analysis, the 
651 
colder systems favor P. acuspes, T. borealis, P. minutus, P. glacialis, C. glacialis, M. pusillus, and C. 
652 
hyperboreus. However, the first three seem to prefer niches close to the estuary, whilst the rest prefer 
653 
ecosystems further away. Additionally, only M. norvegica was significantly correlated with the west 
654 
coastal sites. The CCA analysis provided similar results, with a clear distinction of the stations close to 
655 
estuaries, compared to the others of their corresponding sites. B. armatus, C. hamatus, M. norvegica, and 
656 
A. longiremis were associated with brackish, warmer, and less stratified ecosystems, whereas P. acuspes, 
657 
P. minutus, and T. borealis were associated with more stratified, colder, and less saline waters. Last, M. 
658 
pusilus, C. hyperboreus, and P. glacialis were associated with less stratified, colder, and less saline waters.  
659 
Due to the expected increase in Greenland sheet runoff in the next years, we could expect smaller 
660 
copepods like Microsetella spp. to gain the advantage over larger species like C. finmarchicus and 
661 
Pseudocalanus spp. (Arendt et al., 2011). With the anticipated rise in ocean stratification, the influence of 
662 
smaller species is expected to become even more significant. However, the effects of climate change on 
663 
M. norvegica and Oncaea spp. are expected to differ due to their distinct population dynamics, 
664 
environmental tolerances, and food preferences. Based on predictions of increased meltwater flow, 
665 
temperature, and suspended solids in glacial fjords, M. norvegica may be favoured over Oncaea spp. 
666 
(Koski et al., 2021) and Oithona spp. The last will affect the higher trophic levels as Oithona spp. is an 
667 
important carbon link (Jonasdottir et al., 2013).  
668 
Similarities and differences between Greenland and other Arctic fjords  
669 
Greenland's fjords share both similarities and differences with other arctic fjords. The coastal areas of 
670 
Greenland experience a higher freshwater inflow than other Arctic fjords, reflecting the important 
671 
influence of meltwater and runoff in these systems. In contrast to Greenland, Siberian rivers discharge 
672 
larger loads of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) originating from the Siberian steppes (Paulsen et al., 2017). 
673 
Small copepod dynamics in some Canadian fjords, are decoupled from the spring bloom, indicating that 
674 
fast-growing, omnivorous opportunistic feeders, and their copepodites may be better adapted to lower 
675 
phytoplankton concentrations (Tommasi et al., 2012), which further supports the decreasing mean size of 
676 
copepods due to climate change. Furthermore, high-latitude fjords in Norway exhibit higher chlorophyll-
677 
a concentrations than in the present study (e.g., Hop et al., 2002). DF and Svalbard's fjords share a similar 
678 
==PAGE:28==
25 
 
mesozooplankton composition, as they contain increased numbers of Bivalvia veliger and L. helicina 
679 
juveniles (Hop et al., 2002). Calanus spp. and Oithona spp. are considered essential in Norwegian fjords, 
680 
and they are abundant in all systems studied (Arendt et al., 2012; Walkusz et al., 2003). It is anticipated 
681 
that C. finmarchicus will dominate niches with Atlantic water, while C. glacialis and C. hyperboreus will 
682 
dominate ecosystems with Arctic waters (Hop et al., 2002). 
683 
Conclusions 
684 
The environmental conditions were strongly impacted by run-off in each of the studied fjords. In 
685 
Greenland, glaciers are the main source of meltwater discharge, and they are retreating at an accelerating 
686 
pace (Jonasdottir et al., 2013; Citterio et al., 2017). The last will eventually result in the conversion of 
687 
marine- to land-terminating glaciers, which in turn will change the coastal physical conditions, such as 
688 
stratification, turbidity, salinity, and nutrient availability (Jorgensen et al., 2020). Solid ice discharge is also 
689 
increasing; therefore, a less saline environment in such systems is eminent (Mankoff et al., 2019). 
690 
However, distinct differences also existed. The temporal differences in the biological community indicate 
691 
that fjord systems are sensitive to changes, and the higher abundance of copepods in DF compared to YS 
692 
and NF, indicates that turbid fjords can be hot-spots for secondary production and carbon turn-over. 
693 
Different microbial community structures will be reflected in higher trophic levels due to changes in their 
694 
available food (Wassmann et al., 2011; Middelbo et al., 2017). To what extend the pelagic system is 
695 
sustained by organic matter from land and assimilated through the microbial food web into higher trophic 
696 
levels is a relevant research element to explore (Balmonte et al., 2020). Any attempt to forecast how the 
697 
coastal marine ecosystem around Greenland will change in the future must include the increasing 
698 
discharge of meltwater from the Greenland Ice Sheet.  But what this study shows is that the local impact 
699 
of meltwater in a specific fjord is modified by a range of other drivers. Our current capacity to quantify 
700 
the cumulative impact of multiple drivers, which show high spatiotemporal variability, is very limited, but 
701 
it appears safe to conclude that the coastal ecosystem around Greenland is influenced by a complex and 
702 
unique combination of drivers which means that the ecosystem could develop along trajectories entirely 
703 
different from the rest of the Arctic.
