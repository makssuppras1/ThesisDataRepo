Abstract 
During this thesis enzyme kinetic was investigated via experimental data recording and kinetic modelling of 
the recorded data. Two systems were investigated during the time of the project, the first being the simple 
EAA system and the second being the more complex HEST system. Both systems have the common feature 
of being esters which are converted via a class of enzymes named CES (carboxylesterase) into the final 
products of carboxylic acids and alcohols. To record the data, the experimental technique of dDNP in 
tandem with NMR was applied to gain real time recording of kinetic measurements. This technique offers a 
wide pallet of opportunities as it outweighs the use of special designed chemical/bio-chemical assay by 
being a general approach. The technique of utilizing NMR and dDNP in tandem makes it possible to detect a 
projection of the true chemical reaction occurring in real time, seeing as the changes of concentrations are 
not directly observed but rather signal detected.  
However, not only is the data complicated on its own, also the recording of data is not without problems, 
and as of such complicates the kinetic modelling of the recorded data even more. The problem of recoding 
the data of both systems, or rather every system recorded via this technique, is that the that signal is not 
finite as relaxation and pulsing contribute to the signal decay. As of such each experiment can only be 
recorded within a limited time window as eventually the background noise from an NMR spectrum would 
overwhelm the signal. As of such one of the goals was to improve the SNR (signal to noise ratio) since this 
would provide a longer detection of signal over time. This goal was met for the chemical system of EAA 
investigated in this project, by simulating a kinetic ODE model varying the flip angle and repetition time. 
The results showed that the optimal conditions differed significantly from the standard, non-optimized 
conditions, stating that the average signal generated from the optimal conditions were higher than the 
non-optimized conditions. 
The problem of kinetic modelling occurs due to the experimental reproducibility, seeing as replicas of an 
experiment shows very different results when being modeled via an ordinary differential equation system 
(ODE). This should in theory not be happening as the kinetic rates are tightly controlled factors within an 
enzyme which should be the same when being subjugated to the same conditions. To solve this problem a 
new kinetic model never attempted before within the dDNP/NMR setting was created along with adding a 
heat exchanger to the experimental set up to bring down the overall variability of the kinetic rates due to 
temperature. It turned out that the ODE formulation did not account for everything within the reaction as it 
was observed that the residuals were far from uncorrelated when using the ODE formulation. So, to solve 
this problem an SDE (stochastic differential equation) formulation was implemented. It was shown for both 
systems, that a constant diffusion model greatly benefitted the likelihood and AIC/BIC of the model while 
also satisfying the conditions of the diagnostics to a higher extend. The model was further expanded into a 
splitting of the dDNP/NMR term from the chemical part of the model showing a large improvement from 
the starting SDE model. Finally, a model considering the underlying enzymatic mechanics of both systems 
were considered, this worked splendid for the EAA system, but for the HEST system there is still some 
progress to be made. The best model for each system was then in the end fitted via a multiple likelihood 
model, fitting every identical dataset to the model. As of such it was discovered that when using a heat 
exchanger, it was possible to generate a stable model which had identical kinetic rates when applying an 
SDE model which had the chemical states separated from the dDNP/NMR terms.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
This thesis revolves around enzyme kinetic modelling from data acquired through experimental 
investigation. The particular class of enzymes investigated during the project was that of the 
carboxylesterases (CES). These types of enzymes have the ability to hydrolyze esters turning theses into 
carboxylic acids and alcohols. Two types of these enzymes are of interest in this project. The first is the 
CES1 enzyme capable of hydrolyzing esters with small alcohols parts, and the second is the CES2 enzyme 
that has the ability to hydrolyze esters with bulkier alcohols. The experiments were carried out with two 
reaction systems, with the first system utilizing a small monoester named EAA (ethyl-acetoacetate) and a 
second, more complicated system, utilizing a larger ester nicknamed HEST, which is a molecule with two 
ester groups attached to it. Both systems are introduced in detail in the System introduction EAA section 
and in the System introduction HEST section. The goal of the kinetic modelling would in turn be to produce 
a model which would be capable of quantifying the kinetic rates of both enzyme from both systems with 
the use of multiple datasets. To achieve the goal, datasets with different conditions were produced with 
multiple replicas for each system. This was done to identify if conditions such as temperature stability and 
optimal data recording conditions influenced the dataset to dataset variation. 
When doing kinetic data modelling, many techniques may be utilized the classic choices may be a simple 
colorimetric assay or a fluorescence measurement[1]. These would in principle work with the kinetic 
measurements in this project as well, but they do have some shortcomings. The largest of these 
shortcomings is that they cannot measure enzyme activity in whole cells or tissue in real time as one cannot 
extract the sample without damaging the cells or tissue of interest. This would be of interest when doing 
cancer diagnostics, as seeing how the kinetic rates are behaving when in living cancer cells would generate 
a more exact picture of the current metabolic state of the cells via the kinetic rates. Therefore, another 
technique was applied in this project. This technique is called dissolution dynamic nuclear polarization 
(referred to as dDNP).  
The dDNP technique works in tandem with NMR (nuclear magnetic resonance) and makes it possible to 
follow a reaction via signal evolution in real time by recording multiple NMR spectra. The technique is very 
advantageous as it can be utilized for a wide array of chemical and biochemical kinetic analysis without 
having to design a clever assay on a case to case basis. The technique has, as mentioned earlier, also an 
advantage of being non-invasive, making measurements particularly interesting when quantifying in vivo 
biochemical reactions, but can also be utilized for enzyme kinetic modelling or modelling of other types of 
catalysts [2]. 
Getting a little more in depth with dDNP, it is a non-lasting, artificially high NMR signal is created by a 
process called hyperpolarization. In principle, all NMR active nuclei can be hyperpolarized, but this thesis 
deals only with 13C and the use of molecules enriched in this nucleus. The hyperpolarization is needed to 
gain a higher signal, as without the hyperpolarization, the pure (so called thermal) NMR would be 
considered a very low sensitive technique since very little signal would be measurable in each spectra (see 
Hyperpolarized experiments section for more detail).  
To get a spectrum, a pulse must be utilized hitting the sample producing an FID which is transformed into a 
signal which can be read of from an NMR spectrum. Within each spectrum peaks resides which corresponds

to a specific atom within the labeled substrate, the integral of this peak would in turn be converted into a

A new spectrum is then recorded as time progresses, creating a “stack” which would produce a kinetic 
curve as the integral of each peak from each unique spectrum. The challenges getting spectra’s when 
applying the dDNP technique is that each time a spectrum is recorded, the pulse is consuming some of the 
signal, so one cannot have endless measurements points. Also, another decay (called T1) is present which is 
the effect of the polarized substance retuning to its natural (thermal) state (unique for each nucleus at a 
certain magnetic field and temperature) which also consumes signal spectra (see the Hyperpolarized 
experiments section for more detail).

The hyperpolarized substrate is gradually converted via a chemical reaction into one or more products,

is not lasting forever as previously explained[3]. A more in-depth explanation of these experiments can be 
read in the Hyperpolarized experiments section. 
When carrying out hyperpolarized experiments the time limit is only one of the problems. Another problem 
is that of noise within each experiment, which poses some challenges, and even more troubling is the data 
to data variation when the same experiment is repeated. This would imply that these types of experiments 
may have many uncontrolled factors influencing the experiment from time to time[2] . As such, this 
provides challenges to get trustworthy estimates of different parameters. These parameters, specifically 
the reaction rate constants/kinetic constants, would often be linked to metabolic process within some 
biochemical system of importance. An example of such relevance could be that of cancer diagnostic via 
DNP where precision of estimation is essential, since different rates could e.g. be coupled to different 
stages of cancer. A less radical example could be that of quantifying new biochemical pathways where the 
highest pression is also wanted for the best results. As of such, this thesis tests the impact of grey box 
modelling[4] of experimental enzyme kinetic data, as well as optimal experimental conditions when 
recording the data.

Each system is unique and different conditions should be applied to gain the optimal experimental output, 
which means that the signal gained through time should be as high as possible. The optimal conditions of 
each system when recording data, focuses on identifying the optimal pulse angle (𝛽𝑜𝑝𝑡)  and the optimal 
repetition time (𝑇𝑅𝑜𝑝𝑡) of each system. The optimal conditions serve the purpose of lowering the overall 
noise in the data, resulting in an overall higher signal to noise ratio (SNR). Thus, from identifying the 
𝛽𝑜𝑝𝑡and 𝑇𝑅𝑜𝑝𝑡, the data would be, in theory, recorded with minimal noise [5]. That is the signal should 
exceed the random noise in the spectrum for a longer period. 
However, this is rarely enough to gain sufficiently reduced data noise, since during these types of 
experiments not only random noise is present, also multiple uncontrolled factors are in play [4] which may 
affect the data recording from time to time and traditional ODE (ordinary differential equation) modelling 
(white box modelling) of these experiments may be challenged. This would often be seen as the ODE 
modelling would lead to a correlation of residuals during model validation[4] .This is where the grey 
box/SDE (stochastic differential equation) modelling comes into play, as a “random effect” term, the so-
called diffusion term, is introduced within the system which makes up for the noise within the data such as 
mixing imperfections, temperature changes, pH changes or even just random unknown fluctuations[6].  
Contrary to pure statistical modelling (black box modelling), the grey box modelling retrains the physical 
modelling properties of the white box model, making it a combination of data stochasticity and mechanistic 
properties of the system[4]. These types of models have the advantage of being easy to diagnose as a 
correct model formulation would lead to uncorrelated residuals while a model mismatch would lead to a 
very large diffusion term. It is such that this type of model is chosen along with optimal experimental 
conditions for further investigation, as this may solve the problem of inadequate parameter estimation 
reproducibility of the pure white-box model.   
How the SDE models are formulated is explained in Stochastic differential equations and Parameter 
estimation of SDE’s sections. Additionally to provide  an understanding of the deterministic part of the 
models the DNP-technique is explained in the Hyperpolarized experiments section.  
Following the Theory section which clarifies both the DNP/NMR and SDE/modelling Theory, each chemical 
system is defined and explained. Optimal conditions of each system are then identified and compared with 
standard conditions, following modelling of each system with model diagnostics and a final discussion of 
the end results of each system. 
Note that throughout the project theory revolving around DNP/NMR and statistical modelling will be 
explained both as justification of certain points, but also as a clarification should the reader not be familiar 
with some of the technique/methods applied.

Hyperpolarized experiments 
This section goes over the properties of NMR and dissolution-DNP. As mentioned in the introduction these 
work in tandem to generate the data during the experiments carried out in this project. The experiments 
carried out is consisting of two phases, the first phase being the solid-state phase where the actual 
hyperpolarization occurs (the DNP part) and the liquid phase where the NMR measurements happens[7] . 
Before explaining the experiments, themselves some NMR theory is presented showing just how the signal 
occurs and is measured. The NMR signal is occurring due to a difference in spin population in different 
atoms. The difference between the spin populations varies depending on which atom is in play, but in this 
report the focus is solely on C13 atoms. The C13 atom has a quantum number of m=1/2 meaning that two 
configurations exist, a spin up configuration and a spin down configuration[2] . These states occur 
whenever the spins are presented to a strong magnetic field causing either an alignment or a counter 
alignment to the field. The difference of spin population is the foundation of an NMR signal, the higher the 
difference, the higher the signal.

Now each peak recorded within spectrum at a specific ppm has an area beneath it. Taking the integral 
under this peak would produce a signal data point for the given atom at a given peak. To couple the NMR 
spectrum to real time application, multiple spectra are recorded over time which generates a stack 
producing a 3d image of a signal decaying as time is progressing, an example of this can be viewed in the 
System introduction EAA section. 
This is all good and would be excellent on its own in theory as this could identify substances over time since 
it would be expected that the integral of each peak would get smaller and smaller in a kinetic reaction, that 
is the substrate signal would get smaller and the product signal would increase over time. However, there is 
a huge problem with using Traditional NMR which relies on the thermal (often room temperature) 
Boltzmann distribution to produce a signal. Here the population difference (polarization) in spin stages is 
very small (approximately 8ppm at 9.4T for C13) [10] and the natural abundance of the C13 isotope with a 
quantum number differing from zero is very low as 99 % of all carbon is C12 which is invisible to NMR. The 
second problem can be relatively easily solved as it is possible to make molecules consisting entirely of say 
pure C13 atoms, but the first problem of low spin population difference is harder.  Thus, to solve the 
population problem, hyperpolarization and DNP techniques were invented[10].  
The method revolves around “cheating” the Boltzmann distribution. At low temperature and high field, an 
electron is almost 100 % polarized. DNP works by transferring the polarization from electrons in a radical

When starting the process of the Kalman filtering one must provide an initial guess of the starting values, 
this guess is then used as a previous state which forms the base of generating a theoretical prediction of 
predicted stated. Sometimes if the initial guess of the parameters is too farfetched the process breaks 
down, usually the starting guess would be close to the first observation, but in the case of a hidden state 
some trial and error might be needed. The next step is then to calculate the Kalman gain, this calculated by 
figuring out how much the new predictions should be based upon the measured observation or the 
predictions from earlier when being applied to the updating equations for both the process covariance and 
the predictions of the next state. From this updating an output is made which can then be utilized by the 
ML optimization for parameter updates. The now previous state is then used for a theoretical prediction of 
the next time increment, which is then, together with the new measurement input put used to generate a 
Kalman gain once more. This new gain is utilized to evaluate the next prediction of the state matrix and 
covariance matrix which produces a new output and a new previous state for the same routine to run all 
over again until there are no more observations. The idea of this process is to limit the influence of 
fluctuation in the data so that predictions may not get too vulnerable to an odd measurement as time is 
progressing. Naturally the longer the process is running, the better predictions would be made, and single 
observations would have less and less effect.

Statistical testing and tools 
This section gives a short overview of the tools utilized for model testing, while diagnostics are explained in 
the next section, this section only deals with likelihood related tests. 
To test the models seeing if the modelling is improving some of the tools utilized would be either AIC, BIC 
or likelihood ratio tests[18]. 
These are defined in the following way 
Equation 55 
𝐴𝐼𝐶= −2 ∙𝑙𝑙+ 2 ∙𝑝 
𝐵𝐼𝐶= −2 ∙𝑙𝑙+ log(𝑁) ∙𝑝 
𝐿𝑅𝑇= −2 𝑙𝑙𝑠𝑚𝑎𝑙𝑙 𝑚𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑙
𝑙𝑙𝑙𝑎𝑟𝑔𝑒 𝑚𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑙 
 
The AIC and BIC are producing a single number, which on its own is not very useful, however when 
comparing model, the model with the lower number would produce the better fitting. So, each criterion is 
providing a penalty for using too many parameters and/or too many observations to generate a better 
likelihood and protects from overfitting[21]. 
The last test of likelihood ratio test (LRT) is tested vs a 𝜒2 distribution where the degrees of freedom is 
found from the parameter difference between the smaller and larger model[18]. Should a significant 
difference be found, the larger model would be contributing to the fitting while a non-significant difference 
would indicate the larger model does not benefit the model and the smaller model should be kept.  Note 
that the LRT is only valid when the models are nested so if data would be transformed the LRT can no 
longer be used directly nor can the AIC and BIC and a transformation theorem should be used. 
These tests are used though this project to assist the model diagnostic in identifying the optimal model.

Each criterion for the standardized residuals which is tested in appendix 1 are summed up by the following 
points 
1) Normal distribution 
This is tested via a QQ-plot plotting the standard normal distribution vs the actual residuals and a 
confidence interval found from the car-package in R[22] . Should all point fall within the confidence region, 
a normal distribution of the residuals has been achieved. As a supplement one could use a variety of 
normality tests, should these show non-significant results the normality would have been further 
confirmed. 
2) Constant variance and expected mean 
Plotting the residual vs fitted would in the perfect case show a constant variance of 1 and a mean value 
around 0. Furthermore, no pattern in the residuals would be observed and they would be randomly 
scattered around 0. This would mean the model would grasp all the data variance. Another approach which 
is utilized here is based on the same thing but now since the data is time series data the residuals is plotted 
vs time to see where in the process the constant variance assumption may would be violated[6]. 
3) Independence 
Since data is as previously mentioned time dependent, the testing of independence makes sense as each 
lag would be correlated in some way with time. The ideal system would exhibit minimum autocorrelation 
(ACF) and partial autocorrelation (PACF)[4] these are each defined as:

exponential decay, a seasonal significance or some kind of wave pattern[23].

Experimental set up (method and materials) EAA 
The experimental procedure is set up according to [26]. The BDPA radical concentration was set at a 
constant target concentration for each experimental replica at 25mM (as optimum found according to[26]), 
with each sample contained a volume of 75% unlabeled EAA substrate and 25% 1.3-C13 labeled EAA 
material. No additional glassing agent was added to the sample, as each EAA is a self-glassing agent [26] . 
Each sample was sonicated, spun down and vortexed to make sure the BDPA radical was properly dissolved 
within the EAA sample. A small amount of gadolinium was also added to the sample (1ul) to lower the 
overall polarization build up time.  
Each sample was subjugated to polarization using a polarizer until high polarization was achieved (70-80%) 
at an average polarization time of 50min. The polarization of each sample occurred at 1.5K and a field 
strength of 3.3 Tesla applying optimal conditions as implied in the Hyperpolarized experiments section.  
While waiting for the polarization, a sample of CES1 enzyme was prepared using 1ul enzyme mixed with 
1ml PBS buffer within a 10ml NMR tube. After preparation the 10 ml tube was place into the hypersense at 
a constant temperature of 37°C.   
After complete/high polarization of each sample had been achieved, the polarized EAA sample was 
subjugated to dissolution utilizing 5ml heated PBS buffer. Rapidly following the dissolution, 3ml of 
EAA/buffer solution was transferred to the 10ml NMR tube containing the enzyme within the NMR 
spectrometer via a tube with a dead volume of 1.1ml. After transfer, recording of NMR spectra was 
immediately started using either a combination of a pulse angle of 8 and TR of 1s or 20 degrees and TR of 
2s. When extracting the integral for each spectrum, manual phasing was conducted following a baseline 
correction before peak integral extraction in Mnova according to the principles described in[27] . The 
integrals of the peak were then collected in a table and exported into a CSV format later to be analyzed and 
modeled within R. 
The same experiment was replicated 3 times per sample, additionally 3 more samples were made for each 
with identical pule angle and TR combination now utilizing a heat exchanger, set to 37 degrees, coupled to 
the transfer tube.

revealed that the two variances were not significantly differing. In turn the effect of the heat exchanger 
would be revealed in the second part of this section when determining the parameters of each fit showing 
whether the heat exchanger adds to the estimation stability.  
Another point which may be relevant is the fact that the simulation of the optimal conditions is based on 
starting ODE formulation, may not be as valid as originally thought. This may be since it was identified that 
the hidden split model approach of the final model was much better at modelling the data suggesting that 
the deterministic part of the final model should be utilized to identify the optimal experimental conditions 
for this system instead of the original ODE model. Doing so, might provide more significant difference 
between standard conditions and optimal conditions and would in turn make data quality a lot better 
should this hold. More experiments utilizing the hidden state model as the base of the simulations would 
have to be carried out in the future to test if the new model would produce different conditions for the 
pulse angle and repetition time which would generate more highly significant results when compared to 
the standard conditions.

Some interesting results from the table 9-18 revealed that the diffusion is seemingly smallest in the 8-
degree datasets with the heat exchanger, while being higher in the datasets without the heat exchanger.

compared to the datasets from the experiments with a heat exchanger. This may indicate than whenever 
the data is recorded without a heat exchanger, the model should have some form of heating term included 
in the model. Identifying this term was tried out, but no useful results were identified. The 20 degree 
datasets seems to have a much higher diffusion term than the 8 degree datasets this may be due to as

recorded early in the experimental phase with the author having less experience when compared to the 8 
degree datasets. Note should the reader not be familiar, the columns of dPen/dpar and dF/dPar indicates 
how close one is to a boundary and how close one is to a global optimum. In the perfect case they should 
both be 0 but in practice this is impossible so they should not get too large. As seen from the data, only one 
dataset has a parameter close to boundary conditions while the rest of the datasets seem to behave nicely. 
Now the question needs to be answered, can the kinetic parameters be considered identical when fitting 
individual datasets? To figure this out, the kinetic parameters (k1, kb1 and k2) of all datasets are compared 
within their identical replicas. Seeing as the kinetic parameters are tightly controlled metabolic parameter 
which may be applied to analyze a condition within a cell, they should be identical (in this case pure 
enzyme is applied not cells). These parameters can be evaluated via their confidence intervals as they 
should be overlapping as the catalytic conditions should be the same for each identical sample.

Conclusion EAA 
Summarizing the EAA system the following conclusion can be drawn. Firstly, it was possible to identify 
optimal experimental conditions for the reaction. However, the small significant difference between 
optimal and standard conditions may be due to the system being simulated from a less adequate base ODE 
model.  This would have to be investigated in further. It was also discovered that the signal was higher in 
the case where the heat exchanger had been applied, though the difference were significant, it was not 
very significant. More datasets would have to made in the future where the quality of these may improve 
as the experiment experience has improved since the beginning of the project – this might change the 
outcome and a higher difference might be discovered. 
Doing the modelling of data, it was identified via the BIC and AIC that the SDE model formulation with 
constant diffusion were superior to that of the ODE base model. Furthermore, the SDE model was split into 
a chemical state and the solution of the NMR/DNP dependent part were inserted directly into the 
observation equation which also improved the likelihood as shown throughout the modelling section. This 
split provided better estimation of the T1 parameters and more stable variance as shown in the model 
diagnostics. Finally, the model was expanded from a 2-state model into a 4-state model with two hidden 
states describing an underlying enzymatic reaction coupled to the substrate deterioration and product 
build up while the NMR/DNP parts of the model were still split form the states. Seemingly the final model 
with the hidden state could produce multiple fits, giving reasonable estimates, standard errors while living 
up to the model assumptions as revealed by the diagnostics. The model, however only worked optimally for 
when the heat exchanger was applied, as when the other dataset was utilized, the model did not work as 
well. This might be due to the quality of the data or the model lacking a term making up for the 
temperature difference. Overall a model describing the underneath hidden enzyme reaction while being 
split form the NMR/DNP terms could produce good fits when a heat exchanger was applied during 
recording. This might be applied within the next system of this project, now knowing that a heat exchanger 
should be applied, and a hidden state model might be a solid choice to try out.

Experimental set up (method and materials) Hest  
The experimental set up is quite like the last in the EAA system, but instead of pure enzyme dissolved in a 
buffer, red blood cells were used. The red blood cells (RBC) were needed to catalyze the breakdown of the 
HEST molecule. This was due to the enzyme which these blood cells produced which was different than the 
CES1 enzyme and was presumably the CES2 iso-form of the enzyme which has an increased affinity for 
more bulky molecules such as HEST. 
The experiment had once again two parts, the solid-state DNP part and the NMR measuring part. The Hest 
samples were made with the same radical concentration as previously (25mM). No additional glassing 
agent was added to the sample, as HEST is self-glassing [26] . Each sample was subjugated to polarization 
using a polarizer until high polarization was achieved (20-30%) at an average polarization time of 50min. 
The polarization of each sample occurred at 1.5 K and a field strength of 3.3 Tesla applying optimal 
conditions as implied in the Hyperpolarized experiments section.  
While the polarization was building up, red blood cells (RBC) were prepared in an NMR tube and placed 
within the NMR magnet. The red blood cell preparation procedure was kindly provided and carried out by 
Professor Phillip Kuchel and may be explained/cited as the following:  
“The RBC’s were prepared from 50 mL of venous blood drawn from the cubital fossa, from a consenting 
volunteer, under Human Ethics Clearance. 
The blood was anticoagulated with 60 uL of stock heparin solution (Sigma-Aldrich; sodium salt from porcine 
intestinal mucosa, dissolved in water to form a solution of 5000 IU mL-1). 
The blood was centrifuged in a 50 mL screw-capped Falcon tube, at 3000 x g for 5 min at 21C. The 
supernatant plasma, and the buffy coat at the interface between the plasma and RBC pellet, were removed 
by vacuum aspiration via a Pasteur pipette. The RBC pellet of ~20 mL was resuspended to a final volume of 
50 mL in 154 mM NaCl, 15 mM glucose and then centrifuged as before. After removal of the supernatant, 
the RBCs were again resuspended in the saline-glucose medium. The sample was shaken between opening 
the  cap and closing it, five times, to exchange-in atmospheric oxygen and thus turn the RBCs to a bright red 
colour, indicating the formation of oxyhaemoglobin in the cells. This form of haemoglobin has the heme-
FeII in a low-spin diamagnetic state that yields high signal to noise in the NMR spectra of these samples. 
After the third centrifugation step the supernatant was again removed but sufficient was left to ensure a 
residual haematocrit (volume fraction of the sample that is cells)  that was ~70%. The RBCs were stored at 
4C until used, within 24 h”. 
 
After complete/high polarization of each sample had been achieved, each sample solid state sample was 
subjugated to dissolution utilizing 5ml heated PBS buffer. 3ml of dissolved HEST was transferred into the 
NMR spectrometer via a tube going through a heat exchanger, kept at 37 °C, having a dead volume of 
1.1ml. Contrary to the previous EAA experiments, an injection device was introduced during these 
experiments, minimizing the convection caused by mixing. Inside the spectrometer the sample RBC was 
placed within a 10ml NMR tube at a temperature of enzyme reaction optimum of 37° C. After injection of 
the hyperpolarized substrate, recording of spectra was immediately started with a pulse angle of 10 
degrees and TR of 1s. The recording started 2 seconds before injection, leaving the first few spectra as 
noise.  The same experiment was replicated 2 times. After recording the FID was imported into the Mnova 
program, phasing the spectras manually and afterwards applying a polynomial baseline correction

Overall Conclusion  
From the first system it was identified that the heat exchanger did provide data with more signal and it 
would seem the modelling of this data did turn out to more successful than the other data, as it was 
possible to fit the data to a multiple likelihood function, whereas the data recorded without a heat 
exchanger could not be fitted with the same model. This would most likely be due to the model lacking a 
heating term affecting the reaction rate when the experiments did not apply a heat exchanger. Also, it was 
seen that the optimal conditions, with respect to pulse angle and repletion time, provided a better base for 
fitting the system, this may be improved upon in the future should the better hidden enzymatic model be 
applied as the base of simulation. However, the difference between optimal and standard conditions may 
also be due to operator skill gap between the time of data recording. Unfortunately, the optimal conditions 
were not applicable to the HEST system as this system is not as well defined as the EAA system. But may be 
carried out in the future. 
From the modelling carried out in this project it may be concluded that the SDE model formulation does 
seem to be superior to the ODE formulation of both systems of HEST and EAA. It may also be concluded 
that when using the SDE modelling of multiple datasets via the joint likelihood function it is possible to get 
stable estimates of the kinetic parameters across all datasets.  
Specifically, when moving further with the SDE modelling it was shown in both systems that the splitting of 
the chemical part of the model from the DNP/hyperpolarized part was a big step in the right direction for 
generating an optimal model. In the case of the EAA system the underlying enzymatic mechanism seems to 
have been identified correctly and as of such greatly benefitted the modelling of data and as for the HEST 
system, the underlying enzymatic mechanism is yet to be identified but it is believed that when found this 
would greatly benefit the modelling of this system as well.  
As for the future of this project the ideal scenarios would be to get more datasets which would eventually 
form the base of training datasets using the identified models, then further proceed with utilizing new 
datasets as testing data to see how well these would be fitted into the model. Also, one might in the future 
identify an optimal diffusion structure for the T1 parameters which could provide a model capable of fitting 
identical T1’s as well across multiple datasets. One way to do this would be to create an even more ideal 
model, where a diffusion structure of the DNP/NMR part could be derived.

dataset with identical conditions into one model
