Summary 
Produced water contains dispersed oil that should be removed before being discharged to the 
environment or reinjected in production wells. Coalescence of suspended oil droplets is crucial for 
the separation efficiency of hydrocyclone during wastewater treatment and is achieved when the 
surfactant- oil- water (SOW) system is unstable. The hydrophilic- lipophilic deviation concept 
(HLD) introduced by Salager was applied to model and predict the stability of a known SOW 
which consists of an aliphatic hydrocarbon, an anionic surfactant and a salt. The reference system 
is Hexane- 2 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % KCl in aqueous solution and the tested SOW 
systems are: Heptane- 2 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % KCl in aqueous solution and 
Heptane- 0.1 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % KCl in aqueous solution.  Salinity scans with 
bottle tests and droplet- based microfluidics were performed for the tested SOW systems and the 
phase behavior of the SOW systems was characterized. The optimum salinity, where HLD = 0, 
describes the best separation efficiency. The phase behavior of bottle tests was correlated with 
microfluidics experiments. For the system Heptane- 0.1 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % 
KCl in aqueous solution droplet- based microfluidics verified bottle tests and HLD theory. The 
affinity of surfactant towards oil and water phase was assessed with partitioning method besides 
HLD theory for that system. Capillary electrophoresis (CE) experiments were conducted to detect 
the signal of the surfactant in the water phase of bottle tests and effluent of the microfluidics 
experiments so as to measure indirectly its concentration in water phase. Even though the results 
of partition coefficient do not complement HLD theory the results of all experimental techniques 
follow the same pattern which breaks at 3 w/v % KCl (Salinity 3 %) verifying the salinity range 
of HLD= 0.  
The novelty of the project is in the attempt to find the optimum salinity based on quantitative data 
produced by droplet- based microfluidics.  
Key words: HLD theory, SOW system, n- Heptane, SDBS, salinity, bottle tests, droplet- based 
microfluidics, partition coefficient, CE.

1. Introduction 
Produced water is the largest by- product in the oil and gas industry and strict regulations regarding 
the concentration of dispersed oil in water phase (≤ 30 mg/ L [1]) should be met prior to discharge 
in the sea. Production chemicals act as surface active components and stabilize the dispersed oil 
phase in water hindering the oil- water separation [2]. Typical concentration of production 
chemicals is 20-1000 ppm [3]. 
In the oil and gas industry bottle testing is the standard method for characterization of emulsions 
stability. Equal amounts of oil and water are mixed, and the system is left to equilibrate [4]. The 
method is simple but time and sample consuming. Additionally, the static nature of bottle test does 
not emulate the dynamic flow of a gravity separator during produced water treatment process [5]. 
To address this challenge droplet- based microfluidics will be investigated. Oil droplets are 
generated within the water phase in a microfluidic channel. The main destabilization mechanism 
of an emulsion is coalescence. The stability of droplets in water phase is assessed via the 
coalescence frequency.   
Two microfluidic devices (chips) will be used in this project. One commercial and one custom- 
designed for DOTC. The geometry of the custom- designed device aims to emulate the dynamic 
conditions of a separator and the bottle testing method. Flow sensors will be used for modification 
of the flow rates of oil and water phases. This will allow the control of the size of the generated 
droplets and valid quantitative data will be produced.  
To verify the bottle tests with droplet- based microfluidics a reference surfactant-oil-water (SOW) 
system was selected from the literature, and salinity scans were performed with both methods. The 
coalescence frequency of oil droplets in the microfluidic chip was calculated. The hydrophobic- 
lipophilic deviation (HLD) theory was used to access the SOW system’s stability and to find the 
optimum salinity (HLD= 0) where the SOW is completely unstable.  
Main objective of the project is the verification of bottle tests and HLD theory through droplet- 
based microfluidics. The novelty of this project is in the attempt to calculate HLD= 0 for a known 
SOW system. This will allow the prediction of the optimum formulation level of an unknown SOW 
system based on mixing rules which could be used for dosage control of production chemicals 
during oil and gas production.

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1. The HLD theory 
The Hydrophilic-Lipophilic-Deviation (HLD) theory was introduced by Salager to obtain the 
optimum formulation of emulsion treatment [6]. The HLD concept is a semi-empirical energy 
balance equation that considers the effect of at least eight (ten in presence of a co- surfactant) 
variables and integrates them in a simple linear relationship representative of the overall balance 
of surfactant-oil-water (SOW) system’s affinities. These variables are a) surfactant’s head group 
hydrophilicity and its tail lipophilicity, b) the salinity of the aqueous phase, c) the nature of the oil 
(ACN), d) temperature and pressure and e) two compositions variables (in the content of three 
different substances) [7]. The main HLD equation is presented below: 
HLD = F (S) – k EACN – f (A) – α (Τ − 25) + Cc 
where S is the salinity of the aqueous phase (gr /100 mL), F(S) is the function of salinity, EACN is 
the Equivalent Alkane Carbon Number representing oil’s hydrophobicity, f(A) is the function of 
alcohol, which usually acts as co-surfactant or co-solvent in the system and Cc is the indicator of 
hydrophilicity of the surfactant in the system[6]. k and α are constant parameters, depending on 
the nature of the system’s materials and T is the temperature deviation from 25 °C [8]. The HLD 
value is related to the Winsor’s types of emulsion behavior as observed in a SOW system as can 
be seen in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: Types of emulsion according to Winsor[7]. 
For HLD > 0 the Winsor Type II (water-in-oil emulsion) is formed indicating the 
solubilization of surfactant in water phase, for HLD < 0 the Winsor Type I (oil-in-water emulsion) 
is formed indicating the solubilization of surfactant in oil phase and for  HLD = 0 the Winsor Type 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
III (bi-continuous- middle phase, microemulsion) is formed indicating the equal affinity of 
surfactant towards oil and water phase. Winsor Type III corresponds to the optimum formulation 
where the SOW system obtains the minimum interfacial tension (IFT). Winsor Type IV represents 
the case when enough surfactant is present in the SOW to co-solubilize both oil and water phase 
and produce a single phase [7]. 
2.2. The SOW system and the screening variable 
The tested SOW system in this project consists of an aliphatic hydrocarbon (oil phase), an anionic 
surfactant (dissolved either in oil and water phase) and a salt (KCl) in aqueous solution. The 
screening variable to find the system’s optimum formulation is salinity. 
2.2.1. Oil phase 
n- Hexane and n- Heptane were selected for the oil phase. Both are aliphatic hydrocarbons and 
belong to alkanes which are colorless and odorless non- poral compounds. Their “straight-chain” 
structure of single bonded carbon and hydrogen atoms decreases the polarity of alkane molecules 
and favors the formation of the weak London dispersion forces resulting in low water solubility 
and low vapor pressure[9]. Both hydrocarbons have similar physical properties but n- Heptane is 
less toxic and less volatile (due to its lower vapor pressure and higher molecular weight) compared 
to n- Hexane making it easier and safer to work with. Figure 2 shows the chemical structure of n- 
Hexane and in Table 1 are presented the physicochemical properties of the hydrocarbons.

Two of the most important parameters that influence the solubility of an anionic surfactant in a 
solution are the critical micelle concentration (CMC) and the Krafft point (or Krafft temperature) 
as shown in Figure 5.  
 
Figure 5: General diagram for illustration of the contribution of CMC and Krafft point in surfactant’s solubility[8]. 
CMC is the concentration at which micelles are formed. When a surfactant is added in a solution, 
for example, an aqueous solution the surfactant molecules adsorb in the water- air surface and 
lower the surface tension. Micelles are aggregates of surfactant’s molecules and are formed to 
minimize the energy of the system when the water- air surface is completely “saturated”[14].  The 
addition of surfactant above CMC value will not lower further the surface tension or the interfacial 
tension between two liquids in case of a liquid- liquid interaction. This parameter is very important 
for many applications such as cleaning and in enhanced oil recovery (EOR). Studies have shown 
that CMC value of ionic surfactants decreases in presence of electrolyte in the solution[15]. 
Krafft point is the temperature at which micelles are formed in case of ionic surfactants. Below 
this point micelle’s formation is not observed, thus Krafft temperature is as important as CMC 
itself[14]. Studies have shown that Krafft point increases in presence of electrolyte [16][17] 
The influence of salt concentration in CMC value and Krafft temperature of the anionic surfactants 
is out of the scope of this project. However, for salinity scans with droplet- based microfluidics is 
very important for the oil and water phase to be homogeneous. The solubility of anionic surfactants 
in aqueous solutions was reduced due to the presence of salt and these two parameters were 
considered in our effort to create uniform solutions for microfluidic experiments.

For HLD calculations the surfactant’s characteristic value- Cc for these anionic surfactants is -0.9

2.2.3. Water phase 
Potassium Chloride (KCl) was selected as the salt and was dissolved in the water phase. According 
to the Hofmeister series the effectiveness of a salt to precipitate out of an aqueous solution proteins 
and macromolecules depends on its ion specificity [18]. This means that the capability of salts to 
stabilize proteins varies with regards to their ions. Shown below in Figure 6 are given the series of 
anions and cations according to their capability for salting- out effect. 
 
Figure 6: Hofmeister series[19].  
The salting out effect describes the precipitation of macromolecules out of the aqueous solution in 
presence of salt. For example, in an aqueous solution, when the salt concentration increases the 
water molecules are attracted by the ions and interact less with the charged part of the 
macromolecules (in our case the negative charged head of the anionic surfactant), thus the 
macromolecules precipitate out of the solution [20]. The strength of interactions of ions with water 
is different for each salt and different behaviors are observed. Hofmeister sequences are not unique, 
they depend on the system and are the result of pair interaction [21]. The effectiveness of different 
electrolytes in precipitation of anionic surfactants is out of the scope of this project. However, the 
Hofmeister series was advised for interpretation of the strong SDS and SDBS precipitation in 
presence of KCl. According to the series KCl favors the salting- out effect compared to most 
widely used NaCl.

2.2.4. Salinity scan 
The screening variable in this project is salinity. During salinity scans phase inversion takes place 
at the optimum salinity Figure 7. At low salinity the surfactant is expected to be soluble in water 
phase and micelles are formed that solubilize oil droplets in water phase (Winsor I). The increase 
in salinity decreases the solubility of the surfactant in the water phase (salting- out effect) and for 
anionic surfactants decreases the repulsive forces between the negative charged head- groups of 
the anionic surfactant [13]. Thus, the surfactant adsorbs in the oil/ water interface. At the optimum 
salinity the oil/ water interface is fully saturated, and surfactant has equal affinity towards oil and 
water phase and the minimum interfacial tension is observed (Winsor III). As the salinity level 
increases the solubility of the surfactant in oil phase increases and reverse micelles that solubilize 
water droplets in oil phase are formed (Winsor II).  
            
 
Figure 7: Interfacial tension versus salt concentration. The minimum IFT is observed at the salinity level where 
surfactant has equal affinity towards oil and water phase [13], [22]. 
The HLD theory is applied to find the optimum salinity for each of the tested SOW systems. Co- 
surfactants were not included in the system, and it is assumed that the experiments were conducted 
in atmospheric conditions. The HLD equation for the calculations in this project is given below: 
HLD = F (S) – k EACN + Cc 
Since co- surfactants are not present in the SOW system the term f(A) is not included and the 
temperature deviation from 25°C degrees is removed since it assumed that the experiments were 
==PAGE:16==

conducted in atmospheric conditions (T=25°C and P= 1atm). The k value is typically 0.16 for the

2.3. Bottle testing vs Droplet- based microfluidics 
Bottle testing is a traditional experimental method used in the oil and gas industry to access oil 
emulsion stability. The method is widely used for estimation of the optimal demulsifier 
concentration for breaking water-in oil emulsions. In bottle testing the demulsifier is usually mixed 
with crude oil and then equal amounts of oil and water are mixed, rigorously shocked and left to 
equilibrate within a bath at different temperatures. Protocols were developed by oil and gas 
companies regarding the bottle testing procedure so as reliable results to be produced. Despite the 
advances, the method is much time and sample consuming. Hundreds or thousands of bottles tests 
were conducted prior to field applications and the time for the emulsion system to equilibrate is 
usually more than a month. Additionally, bottle testing is a static method that does not consider the 
conditions of a “live” production scheme, for example, the stream pressure or the geometry and 
flow conditions in a gravity separator [4]. Droplet- based microfluidics aim to overcome these 
challenges and emulate the dynamic conditions of a separator. Microfluidics is less time- 
dependent (results are produced within 2 hours) and less sample- consuming method. Microfluidic 
devices were used for emulsion characterization [24] and coalescence of crude oil in produced 
water [25]. Small oil droplets are generated in water phase within microchannels due to shear 
forces and observed in the 2- D. The addition of surfactant changes the interfacial tension between 
the generated oil droplets (dispersed phase) and water phase (continuous phase). The microfluidic 
set- up mimics better the flow of water droplets in a crude oil inside the separator since it involves 
the shear flow effects on the emulsion. New experimental set- ups are investigated [5] for 
simulation of 3- D shear flow and observation of droplets interaction within the emulsion. In the 
present study salinity scans were performed with droplet- based microfluidics. The concentration 
of surfactant in the SOW system was constant and oil droplets were generated in water phase in a 
commercial and a custom- designed microfluidic device. The geometry of the custom- designed 
device aims to emulate the field separation conditions and bottle testing procedure. The 
observation of droplet interactions within the emulsion is in 2-D and the coalescence frequency of 
generated oil droplets in water phase is measured and quantitative results were produced.

3.2. Methods 
3.2.1. Bottle testing 
Bottle testing is the main experimental procedure for the determination of the behavior of a 
surfactant-oil-water (SOW) system. Equal amounts of oil (5 mL) and water phase (5 mL) were 
mixed in 12 mL glass tubes sealed with PTFE screw cap and Parafilm® laboratory tape, rigorously 
shaken and left to equilibrate in atmospheric conditions. Surfactants were dissolved either in oil or 
water phase while salts were dissolved in water. As the system equilibrates the tendency of 
surfactant towards oil or water phase is observed and different types of emulsions are formed. The 
evaluation of the emulsions formed was done qualitatively by visual observation of vials.  
3.2.2. Droplet- based microfluidics 
As an alternative to bottle testing droplet- based microfluidics can be used for determining the 
stability of a SOW system. This method exhibits higher complexity compared to bottle testing, but 
it enables data generation and reduces experimental time and sample consumption. A brief 
explanation of the method is provided in the next paragraphs. 
Experimental set- up. In droplet- based microfluidics oil- droplets (dispersed phase) are generated 
in water solution (continuous phase). The experimental set- up for droplet generation is shown in 
Figure 8. A pressure N2 gas (in- house) of 2-3 bars was applied to a pressure controller (OB1 
MK3+, Elveflow, France). The pressure controller was connected to a computer and the pressure 
was reduced to ~ 100mbar via Elveflow software. Two reservoirs containing oil phase and water 
phase are attached to the pressure controller via Tygon tubing with 2.0mm internal diameter and 
to the inlet channels of the microfluidic chip via ethylene propylene (FEP) tubing with 250μm 
internal diameter. Pressure-driven fluid was injected from the reservoirs to the microchannels 
where the oil and water phases eventually merged at the T-junction to generate droplets. Oil-in-
water droplets travelled from the T-junction towards the outlet through a straight microchannel. 
The two liquids exit the chip and are collected in a bottle that was discharged to waste in the end. 
For droplet observation and image generation the microfluidic chip was placed under an upright 
light microscope (Leica DM 2700 P) with an attached high- speed camera (M-PRI 1000, AOS 
Technologies AG, Switzerland) with a 1.6x adapter mounted. Prior to microfluidic experiments 
the microchannels were cleaned as follows: 1) 15 minutes flush with 1 M KOH, 2) 15 minutes 
flush with ultrapure milliQ water to remove KOH and 3) 15 minutes flush with air ~ 200- 300 bars

to dry the chip. The oil phase is filtered with a PTFE filter of 0.45 μm pore size and the water phase 
is filtered with Nylon filter of 0.2 μm pore size prior to experiments to avoid debris blocking the 
channels.

Microfluidic devices. Droplet generation was performed in two different types of microfluidic 
devices: a) a commercial microfluidic chip, Chip A (2 Reagent Droplet Chip, Dolomite, UK) and 
b) a custom- designed for DOTC microfluidic chip, Chip B (FEMTOprint AG, Switzerland). Both 
devices are made of glass and their main difference is in the geometry of their microchannels as 
presented in Figures 9 and 10. The commercial microfluidic Chip A consists of 4 inlet 
microchannels, 1 outlet microchannel and the T- junction. The depth of all microchannels is 50 μm 
while the width decreases in the T- junction from 250 to 55 μm. The two outermost channels are 
connected to the water phase and the two innermost channels are connected to the oil phase. The 
oil and water phases meet almost perpendicular at the narrowed width T- junction where the water 
phase cuts the oil phase (shear forces) in small droplets that remain stable within the water phase 
due to IFT.

3.2.3. Capillary Electrophoresis (CE) 
Experiments were conducted using a capillary electrophoresis (CE) instrument (Capel-205, 
Lumex, Germany). The inidrect UV-detection 230 nm for anions was used for the detection of the 
negative charged part of the surfactant. CE separation was performed in flexible fused silica 
capillary tube with a buffer solution obtained from SLB, Schlumberger-Doll Research, Cambridge, 
MA, USA. The protocol was designed to detect cations/anions and production chemicals in water. 
A small volume of sample was introduced hydrodynamically into the capillary to which an 
electrical potential (–30 kV) was applied. Charged species of the sample exhibit different effective 
electrophoretic mobilities (field strength reduced velocities) and are thereby separated. UV- 
detection was done near the outlet of the capillary while the water phase was injected into the 
system. The thermostatic system in all experiments was set to 20°C. The raw data was acquired by 
Elforun software developed by Lumex and the software was used to analyze all electropherograms.

4. Results and Discussion 
To verify bottle tests and HLD theory with droplet- based microfluidics the known from the 
literature SOW system: Hexane- 2 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % KCl in aqueous solution 
was selected. As a first step bottle tests were performed to reproduce the system. The 
reproducibility of the system was difficult due to experimental challenges and the oil phase was 
substituted with the Heptane. Microfluidics experiments were performed for the new SOW system 
to verify HLD theory and bottle test experiments. The concentration of SDBS was reduced to 0.1 
w/v % to overcome experimental challenges associated with surfactant’s concentration which was 
above its CMC value. For the new system bottle tests and microfluidic experiments were 
performed and showed similar results. A 2nd run of microfluidics experiments was conducted to 
assess the reproducibility of droplet based microfluidic results. The new SOW verifies partially 
the HLD theory since Winsor II and Winsor III were observed but not Winsor I. Besides the HLD 
theory, the method of octanol- water partition coefficient was applied for the last SOW system to 
assess the affinity of the surfactant towards oil or water phase with increase in salinity.

Bottle tests in Figure 15 show that the SOW system changes from water -in- oil emulsion (Winsor

unstable (Winsor III) in higher salinities. Microfluidics in Figure 16 and Figure 17 show that the 
system is stable at low salinities (Winsor I or II) except at salinity 2 %, unstable in salinities 5 %

reference system from the literature Figure 11. The two SOW systems are comparable since

4.3.5. Comparison of CE results with bottle testing and microfluidics  
By comparing the bottle tests of SOW system in Figure 22, the microfluidics experiments Figures 
28 to 37 and HLD theory with the measured signal of SDBS in Figure 39 the following 
observations were made. For Salinity 1 % a water-in-oil emulsion was observed the partitioning 
coefficient of SDBS at Salinity % verifies the affinity of surfactant toward oil phase. For Salinity 
2 % according to visual observation of bottle tests seems that a bi- continuous microemulsion was 
formed and according to microfluidic results coalescence frequency increased in this salinity. The 
signal of UV- absorbance of SBDS in Salinity 2 % is the same as in Salinity 1 %, thus the affinity 
of surfactant towards oil phase is expected to be the same. An inconsistency is revealed between 
the experimental results when the system is in an intermediate situation from stable to unstable. 
This inconsistency reveals the limitation of CE measurement to provide more accurate results (for 
example the signal of SDBS at Salinity 2 % was expected to be little higher compared to Salinity 
1 %). The pattern of all experimental methods (bottle testing, microfluidics, CE measurements) 
breaks at Salinity 3 %. This observation is more evident for the CE measurements. In bottle testing 
the emulsion breaks, in microfluidics experiments the coalescence frequency increases and in CE 
measurement a steep increase is the signal is observed. Thus, the stability of the system at Salinity 
3 % changes drastically. For higher salinities all experimental methods follow the same pattern: 
unstable systems for bottle testing, high coalescence frequency for microfluidics, high signal 
measurements for CE. The above observations verify that the salinity range 3 to 5 should be 
investigated for HLD= 0.  
The increase of SDBS signal in CE measurement with increase in salinity was not expected. 
Assuming that the measurements are valid, the increase in surfactant’s concentration in water 
phase should lead to the formation of oil-in-water emulsion in higher salinities which was not 
observed in the tested salinities in bottle tests. Bottle testing experiments at higher salinities are 
suggested to be performed to see whether an oil-in-water emulsion will be formed when the 
partitioning of SDBS in water phase increases.

5. Conclusion 
Taking into consideration the results and the observations of the previous chapter the following 
conclusions were made regarding experimental challenges, the selection of a known SOW system, 
the HLD theory and the microfluidic experiments. 
Experimental challenges. The preparation of uniform solutions in which the surfactant is 
completely dissolved in oil or water phase in presence of salt is crucial for valid microfluidics 
experiments. Precipitation of SDBS in presence of KCl was addressed by heating the water 
solution at temperature around 39 oC for 7 minutes while stirring. The water phase was constantly 
stirred during microfluidics experiments. Ternary SOW diagrams alongside the CMC value and 
the Kraft temperature (in case of ionic surfactants) should be consulted to address solubility issues.  
Reference system and bottle tests. According to the literature the reference SOW system Hexane - 
2 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % KCl in aqueous solution undergoes a phase inversion at 
Salinity 5 % and Winsor I- Winsor III- Wisor II microemulsions are formed with increase in 
salinity. The bottle test results of the reference SOW system could not be reproduced at DOTC 
Figure 11. The Winsor II microemulsion was formed at low salinities instead of Winsor I and the 
optimum salinity is Salinity 3 % where the SOW system changed to Winsor III. The Winsor I 
microemulsion was not visually observed Figure 12. This inconsistency between the results might 
be due to impurities in the materials used for the experiments. In a bottle testing experiment that 
was conducted for the same SOW system for Salinity 0 % a water-in-oil (Winsor II) emulsion was 
formed. The formation of Winsor II at zero salinity indicates that the surfactant-oil interactions 
(Aco) are stronger than the surfactant-water interactions (Acw) which can be explained by the 
chemical structure of hydrocarbon and surfactant Figure 2 and Figure 3. The alkyl chains of SDBS 
interact strong with the aliphatic n- Hexane. It is known [13] that the presence of salt in a solution 
were changed particles (for example ionic surfactant) are present will weaken the repulsive forces 
between the negative charged head groups and the surfactant molecules will adsorb to the interface. 
The salting out effect expects to drive system from Winsor I to Winsor III with increase in salinity. 
This was not observed for high salinities Figure 12. An explanation to this might be that the 
presence of the electrolyte disturbs the equilibrium of the SOW system (affinity towards oil phase) 
by increasing the interactions between the charged head groups of SDBS with the ions in water 
phase (surfactant-water interactions Acw).  
==PAGE:60==
52 
 
The bottle tests of Heptane- 2 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 w/v % KCl in aqueous solution in 
Figure 15 showed similar results with SOW system with n- Hexane. That was expected since both 
hydrocarbons are aliphatic and the EACN value of these two oils is almost the same.  
HLD theory. Due to limitation of time to find a new SOW system that undergoes phase inversion 
Winsor I-II-II and since the results of bottle tests that were performed at DOTC were reproduced 
Figure 12 and 15. It was decide that the SOW system Heptane- 2 w/v % SDBS- 1,2,3,5,6,7 and 8 
w/v % KCl in aqueous solution is a reliable system to work with. Microfluidic experiments were 
conducted with the commercial microfluidic chip and flow sensors were not used. To understand 
the stability of the system with increase in salinity the observed droplet events, the initial droplet 
size, the coalescence frequency at the entrance of the chip, the coalescency frequency at the outlet 
of the chip and the increase in coalescence frequency from the entrance to outlet should be taken 
into consideration Table 6. The microfluidic experiments Figures 16 and 17 seem to verify the 
HLD theory except at Salinity 2 % where an unexpected increase in coalescence frequency is 
observed. The system at Salinity 5 % and especially at Salinity 6% is very unstable which indicates 
that within this range optimum salinity (HLD= 0) can be found. The microfluidic experiments 
verified HLD but did not verify bottle tests.  
The concentration of SDBS in the SOW system was reduced to 0.1 w/v % to address the 
experimental challenges of SDBS precipitation in presence of KCl. Microfluidic experiments were 
conducted with the custom- designed microfluidic device for DOTC and flow sensors were used. 
To understand the stability of the system with increase in salinity the observed droplet events, the 
initial droplet size, the coalescence frequency at the entrance of the chip and the coalescence 
frequency at the square should be taken into consideration Table 6. The system at Salinity 1% is 
very stable. The increase in salinity increases the instability of the system Figures 23 to 25. At 
Figure 26 a steep increase in coalescence frequency is observed between Salinity 3 and 5 % which 
indicates that the optimum salinity (HLD= 0) is within this salinity range. The microfluidic 
experiments were reproduced and verified the salinity range where HLD= 0. The microfluidic 
experiments verified bottle tests and partially the HLD theory inversion from Winsor I to III. 
Complementary to the HLD theory the method of partitioning was decided to be explored for this 
SOW system in order to access the affinity of SDBS towards oil and water phase respectively. The 
signal of UV- absorbance of SBDS in Salinity 3 % drastically increased. The pattern of all 
==PAGE:61==
53 
 
experimental methods (bottle testing, microfluidics, CE measurements) breaks at Salinity 3 %.  By 
reducing surfactant’s concentration, the complexity of the SOW system was reduced and the 
changes in the system could be better observed (bottle tests), measured (microfluidics) or detected 
(CE).  But still limitations in all methods exist as was described in the previous chapter.  
Microfluidic devices and flow sensors. The geometry of the custom- designed microfluidic device 
emulates the bottle testing procedure. The space of the microchannel close to T- junction, where 
droplets are generated, is representative of the rigorously shaken part of the bottle testing procedure 
and the square is representative of the equilibrated conditions of the bottle tests. The square gives 
more time and space to oil droplets to interact. The initial generated droplets for a stable system 
will remain stable in the square in opposition to an unstable system where droplets coalescence 
and bigger droplets are formed.  Valid results were also obtained with the commercial microfluidic 
device, but the “equilibrated conditions” are not visually evident. The use of flow sensors allows 
the control of the number of generated droplets and the initial droplet size. The effect of salinity in 
the stability of the SOW system can be easily measured and quantified and the comparison between 
different salinities is facilitated. This makes easier the analysis and the interpretation of the results 
Table 8 compared to Table 6 where flow sensors were not used. The reproducibility of microfluidic 
results is possible but is challenging since it can be easily influenced by contamination in the 
microfluidic channels or debris in the tubing system. Creation of uniform solutions is a very 
important factor to avoid contamination in the channels (for example SDBS precipitation). The 
MATLAB script and Excel file provided by Dudek were used for calculation of coalescence 
frequency. The script shows limitations in the surface area of droplets that can filter in a very 
unstable system where coalescence of droplets is increased.  
Partitioning and Capillary Electrophoresis (CE). The detection of the anionic surfactant SDBS in 
presence of an electrolyte KCl is possible with capillary electrophoresis (CE) technique. It was 
assumed that the detected UV- absorbance is proportional to surfactant’s concentrations in the 
water phase and the partitioning of SDBS in oil and water phase was calculated. However, CE has 
some limitations since Cl- peaks are successfully detected but the accuracy of the measurement 
decreased with increase in salinity Table 15 and the detected signal of a specific SDBS 
concentration increased with increase in salinity Table 14.

6. Future Research 
The objectives of this research project to a) to verify bottle tests through droplet-based 
microfluidics and b) to verify HLD theory through droplet-based microfluidics were partially 
fulfilled. Further research is needed to define a SOW system that verifies the HLD theory and can 
be easily reproduced with droplet- based microfluidics.  More experimental runs are suggested for 
the tested SOW systems with the custom- designed microfluidic chip and the use of flow sensors 
to support the accuracy and the reproducibility of the results.  
The mixing region of the custom- designed microfluidic chip can be used for the verified SOW 
system for estimation of the salt concentration at which HLD= 0.  The known SOW system could 
be then used to find HLD= 0 for an unknown system based on mixing rules.   
Bottle testing is a static method while droplet- based microfluidics is a dynamic method. To 
understand better the SOW system’s kinetics CE measurements of the water phase of the effluent 
of microfluidic experiments are suggested to be performed immediately after the experiment and 
then compared with the CE measurements of the water phase of equilibrated bottle tests.  
Capillary Electrophoresis (CE) technique might be developed to overcome the observed 
limitations.
