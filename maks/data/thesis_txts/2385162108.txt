ABSTRACT 
In the past few years, the World’s population of white rhinoceroses (Ceratotherium simum) has 
decreased dramatically due to poaching. To save this animal species from potential extinction, dif-
ferent initiatives have been implemented in conservation programs, one of which is the relocation of 
rhinoceroses. The relocation involves a quarantine period in holding facilities before the animal is 
transported to a new destination. During the quarantine, 20-25% of the animals develop unspecific 
clinical symptoms, believed to be caused by a chronic stress response. If the animals at risk of de-
veloping disease can be recognized before clinical signs appear, these individuals can be released 
before the quarantine time is up, or perhaps even be identified before transportation to a holding 
facility. Haptoglobin is a common marker of inflammation in domestic animal species, and several 
studies have demonstrated that this protein can potentially be an indicator of stress, why it is con-
sidered relevant to evaluate, if this acute phase protein can be used for detection of inflammation 
and/or stress in rhinoceroses. We hypothesised that the serum haptoglobin concentration is signifi-
cantly different in an animal, expected to have an acute phase response, compared to a healthy ani-
mal, and that no significant difference in the serum haptoglobin concentration of captive and free-
ranging animals exists. An ELISA was set up and optimized for detection of haptoglobin in serum 
from white rhinoceroses. A total of 86 serum samples from 27 captive and free-ranging white rhi-
noceroses were included in the study. The objectives of this study were: (1) determine if haptoglo-
bin can be measured in white rhinoceros serum; (2) evaluate the assay regarding analytical perfor-
mance and overlap performance; (3) consider if haptoglobin can be used as a diagnostic marker, and 
(4) consider if haptoglobin can be used as an indicator of stress. We found that haptoglobin can be 
detected in white rhinoceros serum. The ELISA assay was evaluated, and was found suitable for 
measuring the haptoglobin concentration in serum samples, determined in arbitrary units. However, 
we found that haemolysis has a significant impact on the measurable haptoglobin concentration 
potentially causing determination of a falsely low concentration. The haptoglobin concentration was 
found to be significantly higher in free-ranging rhinoceroses, expected to have an acute phase re-
sponse, compared to clinically healthy free-ranging rhinoceroses, even though an overlap between 
the groups was seen. The haptoglobin concentration appeared to increase in cohesion to the severity 
of the cause of the acute phase response, and it seemed that the haptoglobin level in clinically 
healthy animals reflected if the animal was stressed. Our findings imply that haptoglobin could be a 
potential diagnostic marker, and additionally indicate that haptoglobin may be an indicator of stress 
in white rhinoceroses.

RESUMÉ 
I løbet af de seneste år er antallet af hvide næsehorn (Ceratotherium simum) faldet markant på 
grund af krybskytteri. Man har taget konsekvensen heraf og forsøger blandt andet at beskytte dyre-
ne ved at flytte dem til et nyt område, men inden da skal de først være i karantæne. I løbet af karan-
tæneperioden udvikler 20-25% af næsehornene uspecifikke kliniske symptomer, hvilket menes, at 
kunne skyldes et kronisk stressrespons. Det vil være ideelt at kunne detektere dyr i risiko for at bli-
ve syge inden, sygdommen indtræder, eller allerede inden dyret sættes i karantæne. Det forekommer 
derfor relevant at undersøge, om man kan finde en måde til at detektere dyr med risiko for at udvik-
le sygdom, hvilket kunne muliggøres ved hjælp af et akutfaseprotein. Haptoglobin er en almindelig 
kendt inflammationsmarkør i domesticerede dyr, og har tilmed vist potentiale som stress-indikator, 
hvorfor det anses for at være et potentielt egnet akutfaseprotein til brug i hvide næsehorn. I dette 
studie blev der udviklet et specielt ELISA assay til at måle koncentrationen af haptoglobin i serum-
prøver fra hvide næsehorn, bestemt i ”arbitrary units”. Dog viste det sig, at hæmolyse har en mar-
kant indvirkning på haptoglobinniveauet i en serumprøve, og det kan forårsage måling af en falsk 
lav koncentration. Vi opstillede en hypotese om, at der kan måles en højere haptoglobinkoncentrati-
on i syge næsehorn med forventet akutfaserespons i forhold til klinisk raske næsehorn. En anden 
hypotese var, at der ikke er nogen forskel på haptoglobinniveauet i ZOO-næsehorn ved sammenlig-
ning med vilde næsehorn. Formålene med studiet var, (1) at undersøge om haptoglobin kan måles i 
serum fra hvide næsehorn; (2) evaluere ”analytical performance” og ”overlap performance” for det 
udviklede assay; (3) overveje, om haptoglobin kan bruges som en diagnostisk markør; og (4) over-
veje, om haptoglobin kan bruges som stress-indikator. Vi anvendte assay’et til at bestemme hap-
toglobinkoncentrationen i 86 serumprøver fra vildtlevende hvide næsehorn og hvide næsehorn i 
fangenskab, i alt 27 individer. Det blev bekræftet, at dyr med et forventet akutfaserespons har en 
signifikant højere koncentration af haptoglobin i blodet ved sammenligning med klinisk raske næ-
sehorn. Det blev desuden vist, at næsehorn i fangenskab har et signifikant højere haptoglobinniveau 
end vilde næsehorn. Vi fandt desuden, at haptoglobinkoncentrationen så ud til at stige i takt med, 
hvor alvorlig årsagen til et akutfaserespons var, og det så ud til, at haptoglobinniveauet i et raskt dyr 
kan afspejle, om dyret er stresset. Baseret på de fund, der er gjort i dette studie, er der altså anled-
ning til at mene, at haptoglobin potentielt kan anvendes som en diagnostisk markør i hvide næse-
horn, og dette protein ser desuden ud til at kunne finde anvendelse som stress-indikator i hvide næ-
sehorn.

INTRODUCTION  
Approximately 30% of the World’s white rhinoceroses (Ceratotherium simum), are located in Kru-
ger National Park (Barnes, 2016) with the highest density in the southern part (Govender et al., 
2011). In one year more than a 100 animals are immobilized for a variety of reasons (Mathebula et 
al., 2012). One important reason is translocation, since poaching poses a continuous threat to the 
survival of the African rhinoceros species (Barnes, 2016). Relocation to protected areas is an im-
portant part of the conservation programs (Miller et al., 2011), but for some individuals the time 
spent in quarantine in a holding facility (boma), cannot be completed due to problems of adjusting. 
Out of all quarantined rhinoceroses, 20-25% become unthrifty with non-specific clinical signs such 
as anorexia, weight loss, and diarrhoea caused by Salmonellosis, etc. It is suspected that the prob-
lems of adjusting are associated with a chronic stress response (Henrik Hagbard Petersen, pers. 
comm. 23rd of March 2017), as capture, transportation, and adjustment to a boma must be consid-
ered stressful, for an otherwise free-ranging animal (Kock et al., 1999).  
Hematologic (Miller et al., 2015) and biochemical (Mathebula et al., 2012) reference values have 
been established for white rhinoceroses in Kruger National Park. These established baseline values 
are valuable in clinical evaluation of a rhinoceros, captured for relocation, as it enables detection of 
“at risk” animals (Mathebula et al., 2012), and regarding free-ranging rhinoceroses receiving treat-
ment in case of disease or trauma (Miller et al., 2015). In addition to the hematologic and biochemi-
cal parameters, it has been suggested that an acute phase protein, such as haptoglobin, could be im-
plemented as a screening indicator to identify “at risk” animals before translocation and additional-
ly, this marker could be used as a tool for measuring quarantine stress to help identify animals not 
adjusting to a boma (Henrik Hagbard Petersen, pers. comm. 23rd of March 2017). To date, only lit-
tle information on acute phase proteins in rhinoceroses exists. The acute phase protein serum amy-
loid A has been evaluated in black rhinoceroses as a biomarker of inflammation (Schook et al., 
2015), but to our knowledge, no studies have been published regarding acute phase proteins as di-
agnostic markers in white rhinoceroses.   
Acute phase proteins are increasingly applied in veterinary medicine for detection and monitoring 
of inflammation in domestic animals (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). In zoological medi-
cine, only a few studies have investigated acute phase proteins in nondomesticated animals, but it 
has been demonstrated that assays developed for domestic animals could potentially be used in re-
lated exotic animals (Bertelsen et al., 2009). Regarding the white rhinoceros, the horse is considered 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
Side 9 af 87 
 
the phylogenetically closest domestic relative (Vaughan, 2011a). In addition to using acute phase 
proteins as diagnostic markers of inflammation, the proteins have shown potential for prognostica-
tion (Westerman et al., 2015), selection of treatment strategy (Pihl et al., 2016) and monitoring of 
response to therapy (Hultén et al., 2003). Furthermore, some acute phase proteins, including hapto-
globin, have demonstrated potential as markers of stress in cattle (Lomborg et al., 2008), and horses 
(Casella et al., 2012). Animals in captivity typically mask their clinical symptoms, making health 
monitoring difficult, and therefore, it is considered relevant to gain knowledge of acute phase pro-
teins that can be used as inflammatory markers to detect disease (Bertelsen et al., 2009).  
A DTU VET enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), which was evaluated for use in pigs 
(Sus scrofa) in 2001 (Petersen et al., 2001), demonstrated promising results for determination of 
haptoglobin level in serum samples from white rhinoceros. In context to these findings, one of the 
main purposes of the study in this master’s thesis was established: To set up, optimize, and evaluate 
a DTU VET haptoglobin ELISA for application in white rhinoceros. The evaluation of the ELISA is 
carried out in accordance with international standards for validation of a diagnostic test. However, 
only analytical performance (phase I) and overlap performance (phase II) are conducted in this mas-
ter’s thesis. In addition, the variation of haptoglobin levels in individuals during a defined period of 
time is evaluated. Phase III (clinical performance) is not evaluated in our study due to a limited 
number of available white rhinoceros serum samples. In addition, phase IV (outcome and useful-
ness) is generally difficult to conduct in veterinary medicine due to small populations and a low 
number of cases (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011).   
When no contradicting information is given, the word “rhinoceros” refers to the white rhinoceros 
throughout the report. 
The objectives of this study are: 
- 
Determine if haptoglobin can be measured in serum from white rhinoceroses. 
- 
Evaluate a rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA regarding analytical performance and overlap per-
formance.    
- 
Consider the use of haptoglobin as a diagnostic marker for clinical implication in zoological 
medicine regarding white rhinoceroses. 
- 
Consider if haptoglobin has potential as an indicator of stress in white rhinoceroses.

THEORY   
1    White rhinoceros (Ceratotherium simum) 
There are five extant rhinoceros species of which two, the white rhinoceros (Ceratotherium simum) 
and the black rhinoceros (Diceros bicornis) reside in South Africa (Macdonald, 2006). The popula-
tion of white rhinoceroses in the park is estimated to 6000-8000 individuals, mainly living in the 
central and southern part of the habitat (Barnes, 2016), making up the largest free-ranging popula-
tion of white rhinoceroses.  
Poaching poses a great threat to the survival of all rhinoceros species. In Kruger National Park 
alone, one rhinoceros is killed every 11 hours by poachers (Vaughan, 2011a), hunting the rhinocer-
oses for their valuable horns. After being removed, the horns are distributed to Asia, where pow-
dered rhinoceros horn is sold as remedy for hangovers and as alternative medicine, even though 
medical science has repeatedly refuted any medical properties of the horns (Barnes, 2016). Reloca-
tion of the rhinoceroses within Kruger National Park is part of an anti-poaching strategy.  
A group of rhinoceroses is typically constituted of two to six adolescents of the same or opposite 
sex, or adolescents associating with an adult female without calf. A female will not tolerate any 
companions around the time of giving birth, meaning that an upcoming parturition will cause a 
group with an adult female to dissolve. Adolescents are often engaged in temporary associations of 
which the majority lasts for less than a month, but occasionally, the companionship between two 
adolescents, or an adolescent and an adult female last longer. Mature males live in solitary, but can 
accept another subordinate mature male within its territory. A rhinoceros calf is typically driven 
away by its mother at the age of 2-3 years, though white rhinoceroses do not reach socio-sexual 
maturity until a female is 7 years and a male 10-12 years (Shrader & Owen-Smith, 2002). As for the 
female, a male will be sexually mature at the age of seven to eight years, but will not be strong 
enough to establish and defend its own territory at that point (Macdonald, 2006). Based on the age 
of maturity, the following age classification used for black rhinoceroses (Schook et al., 2015) can 
also be applied for white rhinoceroses: adult > 7 years, adolescent 3-7 years and juvenile rhinocer-
oses < 3 years.  
Considering phylogenetic and morphologic aspects, the horse is the domesticated species most 
similar to the rhinoceros. Both species belong to the group of Perissodactyls (hoofed mammals, 
odd-toed), which include three extant families: Rhinocerotidae: rhinoceroses; Tapiridae: tapirs; and 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
Side 12 af 87 
 
Equidae: horses, donkeys and zebras (Price & Bininda-Emonds, 2009). It is generally accepted that 
Perissodactyls can be considered a monophyletic group, defined as a “phylogenetic lineage that 
arose from a single ancestor and includes only the ancestor and all of its descendants” (Vaughan, 
2011b). One of the shared characteristics of the members of the perrisodactyls is the composition of 
the perrisodactyl foot. The symmetry axis of the foot passes through the large central digit meaning 
that the perissodactyls bear most of their body weight on the third digit (Vaughan, 2011a). Other 
common traits are; an elongated rostrum with numerous molariform cheek teeth, a simple stomach, 
and hindgut fermentation. The white rhinoceros in particular, is classified as a grazing species, pri-
marily dieting on leaves and stems of grass (Steuer et al., 2010), a trait that the species have in 
common with the horse. Though the gastrointestinal tracts of the two species are similar (Vaughan, 
2011a), there are a few differences. In the horse, caecum is the main fermentation chamber of the 
gastrointestinal tract, whereas it appears to be the colon in the white rhinoceros (Endo et al., 1999). 
When the intestinal tract of a white rhinoceros was dissected, the caecum was found to be a relative-
ly small chamber, and the colon was found to be both long and large. This supports the theory of a 
well-developed colon, replacing caecum as the main fermentation chamber in the white rhinoceros 
(Endo et al., 1999).  
 
2   Acute Phase Proteins 
Acute inflammation is described by Baumann and Gauldie as the process that takes place in the case 
of an injury to the organism. The main functions of this process are to prevent ongoing tissue dam-
age, destroy potential infective organisms, and initiate repair processes necessary for the organism 
to re-establish its normal function (Baumann & Gauldie, 1994). During the early phase of inflam-
mation, both a local reaction at the site of injury and a systemic reaction occur. These reactions rep-
resent the acute phase response that is a part of acute inflammation. Locally, platelets aggregate and 
clot formation takes place, blood vessels dilate, and leakage of granulocytes occur. Mononuclear 
cells accumulate and are activated, resulting in release of the acute phase cytokines (Heinrich et al., 
1990). One of the systemic reactions is an increase in concentration of certain plasma proteins, the 
acute phase proteins (Heinrich et al., 1990).  
Acute phase proteins have been defined as plasma proteins whose plasma concentration increase or 
decrease by 25% or more following inflammatory stimulus. If the concentration increases, the pro-
tein is characterized as a positive acute phase protein, and if the concentration decreases the protein 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
Side 13 af 87 
 
is referred to as a negative acute phase protein (Mackiewicz & Kushner, 1993). In veterinary medi-
cine, the positive acute phase proteins are divided into three categories: 1) minor: concentration 
increases 50-100% , 2) moderate: concentration increases 5-10-fold, and 3) major: concentration 
increases 100-1000-fold (Eckersall & Bell, 2010).   
Various causes of eliciting an acute phase response (APR)  have been collectively listed in a review 
by Kushner, including but not limited to inflammation, infections, trauma (including surgical trau-
ma), tissue infarction, pregnancy, and neoplastic states (Kushner, 1982). 
2.1   Induction and regulation of acute phase protein release 
In the following section, a brief overview of induction, regulation, and termination of the acute 
phase response is given, as a detailed description is beyond the scope of this paper.  
The organ primarily responsible for the synthesis and release of the acute phase proteins (APPs), is 
the liver (Castell et al., 1989), but several studies on different animal species have demonstrated that 
acute phase proteins can be synthesized and released locally (Christoffersen et al., 2010; Eurell et 
al., 1993) and peripherally (Barrachina et al., 2016; Jacobsen et al., 2006, 2005), as well.  
Certain cell types at the inflammatory site are secreting protein-hormones, cytokines (Petersen et 
al., 2004), such as interleukins (ILs) and Tumor Necrosis Factor (TNF), which stimulate hepato-
cytes to induce the synthesis of APPs (Castell et al., 1989). The cells primarily responsible for the 
release of cytokines are monocytes (Petersen et al., 2004), or tissue macrophages (Baumann & 
Gauldie, 1994), but other cell types such as fibroblasts (May et al., 1988) and endothelial cells 
(Colotta et al., 1992) are also capable of releasing cytokines. In a review by Baumann and Gauldie 
(1994) inflammatory mediators are divided into four groups: 1) IL-6 type cytokines including IL-6 
and others, 2) IL-1 type cytokines, e.g. IL-1α, IL-1β, TNF-α, and TNF-β, 3) glucocorticoids, and 4) 
growth factors, including insulin. The cytokines, regardless of type, are responsible for the stimula-
tion of APP-gene expression, while the glucocorticoids and growth factors function as modulators 
of cytokine action. APPs can be divided into two groups, corresponding to the classification of the 
cytokines stimulating the induction and release of the proteins: type-1 APPs (IL-1 type APPs) in-
cluding serum amyloid A (SAA) and C-reactive protein (CRP), and type-2 APPs (IL-6 type APPs) 
which in most species studied include, e.g. fibrinogen and haptoglobin (Baumann & Gauldie, 
1994). It has been demonstrated in hepatoma cells from rats that the type-1 APPs require a combi-
nation of IL-6 and IL-1 for maximal in vitro expression, whereas type-2 APPs only need stimulation 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
Side 14 af 87 
 
from IL-6 for maximal expression. The IL-1 type cytokines such as IL-1 and TNF-α are released as 
part of a primary signal and appear on the site of injury, whereas IL-6 type cytokines which affect 
the liver are released during a secondary phase (Mackiewicz, 1997). IL-6 has been demonstrated to 
be the major regulator of APP synthesis in human hepatocytes by proving that the interleukin could 
induce release of the same APPs from the hepatocytes in vitro as seen during an acute inflammatory 
state in man (Castell et al., 1989). Additionally, IL-1β and TNFα have been demonstrated to induce 
a weak response regarding positive APPs, while having the same effect on the negative APPs as IL-
6, markedly decreasing the synthesis of albumin (Castell et al., 1989). 
Apart from the mutual effects of cytokines, a variety of mediators have an influence on the cytokine 
release and therefore APP synthesis. It has been shown that the combination of IL-6, IL-1 and dex-
amethasone is needed for an optimal synthesis and release of APPs from human hepatoma, HepG2, 
cells (Baumann et al., 1987). On the other hand, glucocorticoids also have an inhibitory effect on 
cytokines and are involved in a negative feedback loop. Figure 1 illustrates the relationship between 
glucocorticoids, cytokines and the synthesis of APPs. The figure is based on the findings of a study, 
in which IL-1 and IL-6 were demonstrated to be potent stimulators of adrenocorticotrophic hor-
mone (ACTH) release. In the same study, dexamethasone was found to inhibit the production of IL-
6, indicating a negative feedback loop (Woloski et al., 1985). 
 
Figure 1. A schematic overview of the relationship between interleukin-1 and -6, adrenocorticotrophic hor-
mone (ACTH), glucocorticoids, and the synthesis and release of acute phase proteins, interacting in a nega-
tive feedback inhibitory loop. The figure was constructed based on the findings of a study, indicating this 
relationship  (Woloski et al., 1985). 
Apart from glucocorticoids, insulin and IL-10 are other examples of mediators with an inhibitory 
effect on the release of acute phase proteins. In a study examining hepatic cells from humans and 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
Side 15 af 87 
 
rats, insulin has been demonstrated to be a rapid, non-specific and dose-dependent inhibitor of both 
cytokines and glucocorticoids, stimulating APP synthesis (Campos & Baumann, 1992). IL-10 
strongly inhibits production of other cytokines such as IL-1α and IL-1β, IL-6 and TNFα. However, 
the inhibitory effect of IL-10 does not set in immediately, as IL-10 does not reach maximum level 
until 24-48 hours after inflammatory stimuli, whereas the other cytokines reach maximum level 
within 4-8 hours (Malefyt et al., 1992). 
The effects of an acute phase response have collectively been described to be primarily beneficial to 
an injured organism, since the events taking place during this state of inflammation, have the pur-
pose to re-establish the disturbed homeostasis. Bleeding is controlled, necrotic tissue is demarcated 
and resorbed, proteases and exogenous substances are bound and removed, and reparative processes 
and wound healing are facilitated (Koj & Gordon, 1985).  
2.2   Acute phase proteins commonly applied in veterinary medicine 
Each animal species has a spectrum of acute phase proteins (APPs). The number and types of APPs, 
and their kinetics during an acute phase response (APR), varies between species (Mackiewicz, 
1997). Limited knowledge of APR in nondomesticated animals exists. However, it was demonstrat-
ed in a study including impalas, musk oxen, sitatungas, Asian elephants, reindeers, golden lion tam-
arins, Bennet’s wallabies, and chimpanzees that assays developed for humans and domesticated 
animal species can potentially be applied in wild animals. As expected, assays developed for cattle 
demonstrated the best results in ruminant species and correspondingly, a human assay detected C-
reactive protein (CRP) in chimpanzees (Bertelsen et al., 2009).  
CRP, SAA, and haptoglobin are examples of strongly reacting APPs in most species (Petersen et 
al., 2004). In addition, fibrinogen has been used for many years (Crisman et al., 2008). The listed 
acute phase proteins are all examples of positive acute phase proteins.  
The major APPs are considered ideal for use as diagnostic markers, as this group of proteins can be 
measured in blood a few hours after inflammatory stimuli, these proteins have a rapid decrease in 
plasma concentration when inflammation is no longer present due to a short half-life, and a low 
baseline level in the healthy animal (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). The concentration of 
major acute phase proteins usually peak after 24-48 hours, whereas moderate acute phase proteins 
typically peak after 2-3 days (Eckersall & Bell, 2010) and slowly decrease over the course of 
weeks, when inflammatory stimulus has ceased (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). The kinetics

2.3   Haptoglobin   
As far as the authors of this master’s thesis are aware, the classification of haptoglobin in rhinocer-
oses remains to be determined. However, haptoglobin has been classified as a minor/moderate acute 
phase protein in the horse (Jacobsen & Andersen, 2007), and a major in several other species in-
cluding cattle (Eckersall, 2000; Godeau et al., 2006). The equine haptoglobin was examined in a 
Japanese study (Taira et al., 1992) in which the glycoprotein, belonging to the group of α2-
globulins, was found to be a double protein with a molecular weight (MW) of 108,000 and 105,000 
Dalton with each fraction composed of 2 types of glycosylated subunits with MW of 47,500 and 
50,000 Dalton.  
One of the best-known functions of haptoglobin is the ability to bind haemoglobin. Electrophoretic 
studies on human serum have demonstrated that haptoglobin has the ability to bind at least two 
haemoglobin molecules at physiologic pH (Laurell & Nyman, 1957) and form a haemoglobin-
haptoglobin complex (Hb-Hp complex). The formation of a Hb-Hp complex has several properties 
regarding the protection of the organism. It is postulated to provide a natural bacteriostatic effect, 
since the binding to haptoglobin causes the iron of haemoglobin to become unavailable for incorpo-
ration into bacterial iron-binding proteins (Eaton et al., 1982). 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
Side 17 af 87 
 
In some conditions, decreased level of haptoglobin is seen, despite an inflammatory state in which 
an increased concentration would usually be expected. This is especially important, when dealing 
with medical conditions in which free haemoglobin is to be expected. A study has demonstrated that 
an increase in concentration of haemoglobin in serum results in a decrease in haptoglobin serum 
level (Eckersall et al., 1999). This corresponds to the results seen in two studies, in which haemoly-
sis were present in horses and cattle, respectively (Bremner, 1964; Dondi et al., 2015). A low level 
of haptoglobin in plasma was observed in calves suffering from haemolytic anaemia caused by 
babesiosis. Low levels of haptoglobin was also demonstrated in plasma from calves 14 hours after 
they were injected with haemoglobin (Bremner, 1964). In a study on horses with colic, a decreased 
haptoglobin concentration was found, compared to reference interval, in the horses having condi-
tions associated with haemolysis or muscle fibre lysis, or horses with an ischaemic or strangulating 
colic consistent with a greater degree of haemolysis compared to other forms of colic (Dondi et al., 
2015). This indicates that haemolytic conditions are associated with a decreased haptoglobin con-
centration. 
2.4 Stress  
It may be relevant to consider stress factors, when evaluating the concentration of acute phase pro-
teins in an individual. However, it is difficult to study stress, since the state exists in most incidenc-
es of an inflammatory reaction (Petersen et al., 2004). It is difficult to determine whether the in-
creased concentration of APPs is caused by inflammation or stress, but some studies have demon-
strated an increased concentration of APPs in animals exposed to stressful situations. One study 
compared the concentration of haptoglobin and SAA for a group of calves housed on a slippery 
floor to a group on non-slippery floor. SAA was significantly elevated in the calves housed on the 
slippery floor, and haptoglobin level remained unchanged (Alsemgeest et al., 1995), whereas a 
study on adult cattle, demonstrated an increase in both SAA and haptoglobin, after exposure to dif-
ferent stressors: transportation, isolation, and being tied on a slippery floor (Lomborg et al., 2008). 
An increase in haptoglobin was also demonstrated after transport-induced stress, when calves were 
transported 1400 km in two days (Murata & Miyamoto, 1993). In an equine study evaluating both 
haptoglobin and SAA, the stress inducer was transportation for 320 km in four hours. Both APPs 
increased, but haptoglobin increased more rapidly than SAA (Casella et al., 2012).

2.5 Veterinary clinical application of acute phase proteins 
Different clinical applications of acute phase proteins (APPs) have been investigated in veterinary 
medicine. APPs have primarily been found useful as a diagnostic aid, since the proteins have been 
demonstrated to perform as inflammatory markers in several domestic animal species. Serum hap-
toglobin has been evaluated as a marker of clinical disease in pigs, and an APR was demonstrated in 
cases with lameness, ear and tail bite (Petersen et al., 2002). APPs, especially SAA, have also 
proved useful in equine medicine regarding colic (Pihl et al., 2016), differential diagnostic workup 
on foals suspected to have an infectious disease (Hultén & Demmers, 2010), potential for identifica-
tion of bacterial pneumonia (Hobo et al., 2007), and haptoglobin has shown potential as a diagnostic 
aid in inflammatory airway disease (Bullone et al., 2015). Haptoglobin is also a potential disease 
marker for grass sickness (equine dysautonomia). The disease is not considered to be associated 
with inflammation, but  an increase in plasma cortisol is suspected to be involved in the stimulation 
of haptoglobin synthesis (Milne et al., 1991). In cattle, SAA has shown potential as a diagnostic 
marker of ongoing mastitis (Jacobsen et al., 2005), and SAA and haptoglobin, alone and as a ratio, 
have been helpful in distinguishing healthy animals from animals with an inflammatory condition 
on a more general level (Alsemgeest et al., 1994). These proteins may also be used in cattle to dis-
tinguish between acute and chronic inflammation (Horadagoda et al., 1999).  
Other than being useful diagnostic markers, APPs have been evaluated regarding monitoring and as 
guidelines for therapy. It has been investigated how APPs can be useful in measuring response to 
therapy and to evaluate disease progress. Furthermore, APPs have proven useful in monitoring of 
postoperative complications such as infections after surgery, e.g. castration (Jacobsen et al., 2010). 
Other than being used as diagnostic markers in colic, APPs have demonstrated potential as an aid in 
differentiating horses with acute inflammatory colic from horses with colic requiring surgery (Pihl 
et al., 2016). In pigs haptoglobin level has been demonstrated to be related to the health status of 
herds, meaning that APPs have potential to be used in monitoring of health status in larger groups 
(Petersen et al., 2002). Furthermore, APPs have proved useful in monitoring therapeutic effects of 
antimicrobial drugs in pigs (Hultén et al., 2003). Correspondingly, APPs can be applied in monitor-
ing health status on individual basis, and it has been suggested that APPs can be used to predict or 
describe the health status of an animal with a combination of the proteins in an “acute phase index” 
(Toussaint et al., 1995).  
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
Side 19 af 87 
 
Acute phase proteins have also been studied in relation to their application in veterinary medicine as 
prognostic markers in different medical conditions in several domestic species (Eckersall & Bell, 
2010; Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011; Murata et al., 2004). In equine medicine, SAA in par-
ticular has been evaluated for use as a prognostic marker, but haptoglobin has been evaluated, as 
well. In one study a SAA concentration ≥50 µg/mL at the time of admission of a horse with colic to 
the veterinary hospital, was found to be indicative of inflammation. It was applied as an important 
component of the medical condition, indicating a poorer prognosis of survival compared to horses 
with noninflammatory conditions (Vandenplas et al., 2005). The same could not be demonstrated in 
another study investigating SAA and haptoglobin as prognostic indicators in horses with inflamma-
tory diseases (Westerman et al., 2015). SAA and haptoglobin concentration at time of admission 
were not found to be significantly associated with chances of survival, but the results pointed to-
wards serial analysis of SAA during treatment as a better prognostic tool.  
3   Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay 
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is an immunoassay technique that was introduced in 
1971 by two independent working groups (Engvall E, 1971; Van Weemen & Schuurs, 1971).  
Prior to the ELISA method, the only available immunoassay was radioimmunoassay, a technique 
similar to ELISA, using radioactively labelled antigens or antibodies. In this technique, the radioac-
tivity provided the signal, indicating whether the sample contained a specific antigen or antibody 
(Voller et al., 1978; Yalow & Berson, 1960). Instead of radioactivity, the change in colour that oc-
curs when enzymes, for example horseradish peroxidase, and substrates, such as 3,3’,5,5’-
tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), react with one another, was used as a signal. For the signal to be asso-
ciated with the presence of antibody or antigen, the enzyme was linked to an appropriate antibody 
(Nakane & Pierce, 1967). The concept of preparing an immunosorbent, enabling adherence of anti-
bodies or antigens to the surface of a container (Wide & Porath, 1966), was needed, in order to re-
move unbound antibodies or antigens by washing. The three separate techniques mentioned above; 
immunoassay, enzyme conjugation and immunosorbent preparation, was combined, consequently 
resulting in the creation of the ELISA method (Engvall E, 1971; Van Weemen & Schuurs, 1971). 
3.1   Basic ELISA Procedure 
ELISA is designed for detection and quantification of a broad variety of substances including pep-
tides, proteins, antibodies and hormones (Yolken, 1980). The main principle of ELISA is an anti-
gen-antibody reaction. The antigen is immobilized on a solid surface, either directly or by a specific 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
Side 20 af 87 
 
antibody (i.e. capture antibody), and subsequently another antibody is added thus creating an anti-
gen-antibody complex (Shah & Maghsoudlou, 2016) Depending on the type of ELISA, the second 
antibody is either linked directly to an enzyme, or attached to a secondary antibody (i.e. secondary 
detection antibody). The addition of a substrate produces a colour signal indicating the presence of 
the antigen in the sample. The measurement of the optical density (OD) is proportionate to the 
amount of antigen concentration in the sample, as the plate is washed with a buffer solution be-
tween each step, whereby any unbound analyte in the sample will be removed. The specificity of 
the antibody-antigen interaction is a crucial element of the detection, as the complex must withstand 
the washing process. (Voller et al., 1978)  
3.2   Types of ELISA  
In some cases, the ELISA provides sufficient information in its simplest formats, but more complex 
versions can be useful if an enhanced signal or more precise results are required, or if certain rea-
gents are not available (Shah & Maghsoudlou, 2016). There are four different types of ELISA typi-
cally used; direct ELISA, indirect ELISA, sandwich ELISA, and competitive ELISA. The sandwich 
ELISA is briefly described below, as this is technique used in this study.  
3.3 Sandwich ELISA 
The sandwich ELISA is used to analyse more complex solutions. The name of the assay arises from 
the set-up. The antigen is “sandwiched” between two antibodies (He, 2013; Shah & Maghsoudlou, 
2016). The method usually requires the use of matched antibody pairs, of which each is specific to a 
different, non-overlapping part of the antigen molecule (i.e. epitope). Polyclonal antibodies can be 
used in a sandwich ELISA provided that variability is present in the polyclonal antibody allowing 
both capturing and detection of the antigen through different epitopes (Liddell, 2013). Initially, the 
coating buffer solution, containing a capture antibody, is added to a plate and incubated for a suffi-
cient amount of time for the antibody to adhere. Following incubation and washing, the blocking 
buffer is added to block the remaining binding sites in the well. In the next step, the samples are 
added to the wells and incubated for a certain amount of time. A detection antibody is then added to 
each well. If the detection antibody is conjugated to an enzyme, the assay is called 
a direct sandwich ELISA. If the detection antibody is unlabelled, a second detection antibody will 
be needed, resulting in an indirect sandwich ELISA (Self et al., 2013). The secondary antibody 
must be specific to the primary detection antibody only (and not the capture antibody), or the assay 
will lack specificity for the antigen (He, 2013). Finally, a substrate solution is added and the reac-
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
Side 21 af 87 
 
tion takes place in a dark environment. Once the reaction is stopped, a microplate reader is used to 
measure the result. Each step of the technique is visualized in figure 2. Sandwich ELISAs have a 
high specificity and sensitivity. Since two antibodies are used, the antigen/analyte is specifically 
captured and detected. They are suitable for complex samples, since the containing antigen does not 
require purification prior to measurement. Sandwich ELISAs provide high flexibility, since both 
direct and indirect detection methods can be used. (Shah & Maghsoudlou, 2016; Voller et al., 
1978).

3.4   Optimization of ELISA 
ELISAs can be optimized by altering parameters of the assay to increase the sensitivity or specifici-
ty, increase the optical density (OD), and obtain the results faster. The optimization should aim for 
achieving a high signal-to-noise ratio (He, 2013; Shah & Maghsoudlou, 2016). Optimization en-
sures that no component or variable of the ELISA is present in a limiting concentration, and in-
creases the sensitivity and specificity of the assay. Any variable of the assay can be optimized, e.g. 
antibody and antigen concentration, coating- and dilution buffer concentration and volume, temper-
ature, pH, incubation time (Yolken, 1980). There are many ways to carry out the optimizations pro-
cess. Other than the common trial-and-error technique, various methods can be implemented to in-
crease the efficiency of the optimization process, e.g. checkerboard titration (He, 2013). The check-
erboard titration technique is a way to quickly find the optimal concentration of one or two parame-
ters as well as the optimal ratio between these two parameters. The principle of this technique is to 
make serial dilutions, starting in a known concentration, of a reactant, e.g. antigen or blocking 
agent. Based on the results (OD, noise etc.), the optimal concentration can be determined (He, 
2013). A confirmatory test, for instance Western blot, is often used to validate an ELISA optimiza-
tion (Voller et al., 1978).   
4   Additional laboratory techniques  
Western blot and dot-blot 
Western blotting is commonly used technique to confirm positive results of ELISA tests. Western 
blotting is less likely to give false positive results than the ELISA, as it distinguishes between pro-
teins by both antibody specificity and size. However, Western blotting is typically used only as a 
confirmatory test, as it is difficult to perform correctly and requires a high skill level. Gel electro-
phoresis is used to separate the proteins in the sample, either by their three-dimensional structure or 
by the size of the polypeptide-chain of denatured proteins. Subsequently, the proteins are trans-
ferred to a membrane (typically polyvinylidene fluoride), and are stained using antibodies specific 
to the target protein (Mahmood & Yang, 2012).  
The dot-blot technique is a simplified alternative to other blotting methods, e.g. Western blot 
(Hawkes et al., 1982). The procedure is time-saving compared to other blots, as gel electrophoresis 
and the blotting procedures for the gel are not required. The technique allows efficient large-scale 
absence/presence screening of protein markers (Guillemin et al., 2009), and can be used successful-
ly with a broad variety of antigens (Bjerrum & Heegaard, 1988). However, it offers no information 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
Side 23 af 87 
 
on the size of the protein, and if more than one protein is detected, the result will still appear as a 
single dot. Therefore, a dot-blot can only confirm the presence or absence of proteins that are de-
tected by the antibody (Hawkes et al., 1982).  
Silver staining 
Silver staining is a technique used for visualization of proteins in a sample. After separation of the 
proteins by gel electrophoresis, the proteins are made visible by performing a protein-specific, col-
our-producing chemical reaction (Chevallet et al., 2006). Proteins bind silver ions, consequently 
decreasing the ion activity. Under appropriate conditions, the fixated silver ions, are reduced so that 
a visible image made of finely divided metallic silver is formed (Chevallet et al., 2006).The process 
usually consists of the following phases: protein fixation, sensitization, silver impregnation, image 
development, and preservation. Between each phase, diverse washing techniques are applied to im-
prove the sensitivity of the stain. Silver staining is a relatively difficult technique, as many variables 
affect the result, e.g. imprecise timing or insufficient purification of the sample (Chevallet et al., 
2006). 
5   Clinical validation 
In the following section the four phases of clinical validation are briefly described.  
In phase I, the analytical performance of a diagnostic test is evaluated. The evaluation comprises 
four basic steps: practicability, imprecision, inaccuracy, and determination of a detection limit of 
the test. Practicability is important to consider early in the evaluation of the test, since a high practi-
cability will help ensure that a test will be put to use. It should be questioned how expensive the test 
is, which type of results it can provide (quantitative or qualitative), and what is required regarding 
equipment and technical education of the laboratory staff (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). 
Imprecision is a measure for closeness of a series of measurements on the same material performed 
by the test. In this step, intra- and inter-assay performance are evaluated, and the coefficient of vari-
ation (CV), expressed in percent, is determined for either one (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). 
“Inaccuracy is generally defined as the agreement between the mean value of a series of measure-
ments on the same material and the true value” (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). The true value 
can be difficult to access, as it is supposed to be a species-specific specimen with a universally ap-
proved value. Instead, inaccuracy can be evaluated without the use of a true value e.g. by perform-
ing a study of linearity under dilution. It can be performed by measuring the analyte concentration 
in duplicate in a sample from a selected individual in different dilutions, and examine the relation-
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
Side 24 af 87 
 
ship between the measured concentration and the expected analyte concentration by regression 
analysis. Ideally, the recovery should be 100% (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). A similar way 
of evaluating inaccuracy is by dilutional recovery. The samples are diluted, the concentrations are 
determined from the assay, and the dilution curves for the samples are plotted with dilution on the 
x-axis and the measured concentration on the y-axis, ideally resulting in a straight line (Sheehan et 
al., 2013). The last step in phase I is determination of a detection limit for the diagnostic test. It can 
be defined as the smallest concentration that the laboratory test can distinguish from a blank sample. 
The detection limit can be estimated as mean value + 2 or 3 standard deviation determined from a 
set-up in which a sample with very low or no analyte content is measured 20-30 times (Jensen & 
Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). 
In addition, a fifth step may be relevant in the evaluation of analytical performance, if any sensitivi-
ty towards blood components such as haemoglobin, lipids, bilirubin, and glucose is to be expected 
from the assay (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). The DTU VET Haptoglobin ELISA is measur-
ing the content of haptoglobin in the samples. As mentioned earlier, haptoglobin binds haemoglobin 
in vivo, and it must be considered relevant to evaluate the effect of free haemoglobin on the perfor-
mance of the assay. This step can be performed by comparison of the concentration of an analyte 
sample with and without addition of differing amounts of the relevant substance to the sample to 
determine if, and at what concentration the substance influences the assay (Jensen & Kjelgaard-
Hansen, 2010). 
In phase II, overlap performance is evaluated, whereby it is investigated whether or not the test is 
able to discriminate between healthy and diseased animals. If the assay is good at differentiating, 
the overlap between the healthy group and diseased group will be very little or non-existent and the 
test is considered potentially good (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). The overlap performance 
can be evaluated by comparison of two groups each consisting of 20-30 animals, with one group 
comprised of diseased animals and the other group of healthy animals. It is common to select only 
obviously diseased animals, ensuring an easier discrimination between the groups and therefore a 
minimum overlap, if the test performs well. The data can be analysed visually and statistically. Vis-
ually, the data can be plotted in, for example, a dot plot diagram. For statistical analysis, an un-
paired t-test or a Mann-Whitney U-test can be used to assess if the test results for the two groups 
differ significantly from one another (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010).   
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
Side 25 af 87 
 
In phase III the clinical performance of the diagnostic test is investigated. In this phase, the main 
focus is the importance of minimizing bias of any kind, and provide transparency in the report on 
diagnostic accuracy of the assay (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). A working group formulat-
ed the Standards for Reporting of Diagnostic Accuracy Initiative (STARD initiative) in 2003 which 
covers a list of 25 checkpoints and a flow diagram designed to help, when evaluating the diagnostic 
capacity of a test. This assures that the above-mentioned focus areas are fulfilled, when reporting on 
studies of diagnostic accuracy (Bossuyt et al., 2003). From phase III, the diagnostic sensitivity and 
specificity of the assay should be established, and in addition, the negative predictive value (NPV) 
and the positive predictive value could be determined. When evaluating the clinical performance 
and usefulness of a diagnostic test, it is supposed to be performed in a relevant clinical target popu-
lation, and the total number of animals included should at least be 40. Some specific inclusion crite-
ria are to be established and should be applied when including animals, to make sure that the condi-
tion in question is present. As part of phase III, a cut-off value which indicate, if the test results are 
positive or negative depending on the value, is established (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010).  
In phase IV, it is evaluated what advantages the diagnostic test will provide an individual or a 
group, in the matter of disease management. However, it is rarely possible to evaluate this phase in 
veterinary medicine, due to a general lack of large commercial companies investing in large studies, 
small population sizes, and therefore also a low amount of cases. When this phase is performed in 
veterinary medicine, it is often limited to a relatively low evidence-level, typically case reports or 
case series, whereas high-evidence level meta-analyses are often performed in human medicine 
(Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011).

The DTU VET haptoglobin ELISA, a non-competitive sandwich ELISA, was tested to establish 
whether the assay could be directly applied to white rhinoceros serum. The initial results demon-
strated multiple problems, including a disproportion between serial dilutions and the optical density 
values (OD values), and a high level of noise in blank wells. It was observed that the blank wells 
had almost the same OD values as the wells containing the analyte. This clearly indicated a need for 
an optimization of the assay, to make it possible to determine haptoglobin concentration in serum 
from white rhinoceroses. 
The optimization process took place from 8th of February to 27th of February 2017, and the aim was 
to achieve a high signal-to-noise ratio, as well as maximizing the signal as much as possible (He, 
2013). The optimization was carried out with thorough guidance from professor Peter M. H. Hee-
gaard. An overview of the optimization is presented below. If incubation time, temperature or other 
aspects are not mentioned, they are constant during the optimization and are applied to the final 
assay without modifications. 
The first parameter taken into consideration, was the dilution buffer, PBS-T-BSA1% (phosphate 
buffered saline-Tween20-bovin serum albumin 1%). Several other buffers were tested, but it proved 
not to be solely enough to minimize the noise in the assay. Thus, the blocking buffer, also PBS-T-
BSA1%, was replaced as well. The blocking buffers, tested in the optimization process, is shown in

plate was covered and incubated for 30 minutes at room temperature on a Gerhard® laboratory

30 minutes was tested for the 5% skimmed milk blocking buffer, and was found to have no influ-

the remainder of the optimization process. When testing skimmed milk as dilution buffer, the tem-
perature was raised to 37°C during the incubation of the analyte. Skimmed milk 5% diluted in PBS-

is easier to handle and has a longer shelf-life than the skimmed milk buffer, casein 2.5% was even-

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
1   Collection of serum samples 
Serum samples were available from two groups of white rhinoceroses: one group consisting of Dan-
ish Zoo animals and one group consisting of free-ranging animals from Kruger National Park, 
South Africa.  
The information on the serum samples from South African rhinoceroses has been provided by veter-
inarian Dr Peter Buss, Senior Manager in the Veterinary Unit in Kruger National Park. The South 
African rhinoceroses are divided into three groups, depending on the destiny of the rhinoceros in 
question. 1) Nine paired serum samples from free-ranging rhinoceroses that were immobilized for 
relocation to holding facilities (bomas) within Kruger National Park. All animals in this group ap-
peared healthy upon capture, based on their body condition score and a physical examination. 2) Six 
severely injured animals, with no chance of survival, were immobilized and euthanized. A patho-
logical examination was performed on only two of these animals. 3) 16 injured animals with a 
chance of survival. Relocated to bomas for treatment.  
The relocation of animals was conducted according to Standard Operating Procedure for the Cap-
ture, Transportation and Maintenance in Holding Facilities of Wildlife. This protocol was approved 
by South African National Parks Animal Use and Care Committee. Healthy animals were located 
and darted from a helicopter. Drugs were delivered remotely using 3.0 mL plastic darts with a 60 
mm uncollared needle propelled by a compressed air rifle (DAN-INJECT®, International S.A., 
Skukuza, 1350 South Africa).  
The drugs used for immobilization, were a mixture of etorphine (M99, Novartis, Kempton Park, 
1619 South Africa), azaperone (Stressnil, Janssen Pharmaceutical Ltd., Halfway House, 1685 South 
Africa), and Hyaluronidase (Hyalase, Kyron Laboratories, Benros, 2011 South Africa).  
Once animals were immobilized, blood samples were collected before the rhinoceroses were loaded 
into transport crates, or euthanized. Blood, from the auricular vein, was collected in serum separator 
vacutainers (Fischer Scientific, Suawanee, Georgia, 30024 USA) within 15 minutes of immobiliza-
tion. Blood samples were kept in a cooler on ice blocks, until processing could be performed at the 
laboratory.

2   Measurement of haptoglobin concentration in white rhinoceros serum using 
a non-competitive sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay 
A microtitre plate (96 wells, Maxisorp; Nunc® AB 4-42404) was coated with 100 µl of 2.5 µg/mL 
rabbit anti-human haptoglobin polyclonal antibody (DAKO A030, DAKO A/S, Glostrup, Denmark) 
in 0.05 Na-Carbonate buffer (pH = 9.6) overnight at 4°C. The plate was then emptied and washed 
four times with phosphate buffered saline (PBS), produced from Milli-Q water, containing 0.05% 
Tween20 (PBS-T) using a manual ELISA plate washer (NUNC, NUNC-Immuno™ Wash 12). 200 
µl of PBS-Casein 2.5% was added to each well and the plate was incubated, covered, for 30 
minutes at room temperature on a laboratory shaker (Gerhard® laboratory shaker). The plate was 
emptied and washed four times with PBS-T. The standard and the samples were then applied in the 
wells. 100 µl of a dilution of white rhinoceros serum (the dilution level differed between 1:100 and 
1:320,000 depending on the estimated level of haptoglobin in the specific samples) in PBS-T-
Casein 0.1% was added to each well, and the covered plate was incubated at room temperature on a 
laboratory shaker for 1 hour. The plate was emptied and washed four times with PBS-T, and 100 µl 
biotinylated rabbit anti-human haptoglobin antibodies diluted 1:500 in PBS-T-Casein 0.1% was 
added. The covered plate was incubated on a laboratory shaker for 1 hour at room temperature. The 
plate was emptied, washed four times with PBS-T, and 100 µl streptavidin-conjugated horse radish 
peroxidase (streptavidin, DAKO P0397) diluted 1:2000 in PBS-T-Casein 0.1% was added to each 
well before 1 hour incubation at room temperature, covered, and placed on a laboratory shaker. The 
plate was emptied and washed four times with PBS-T before 100 µl 3,3’,5,5’-Tetramethylbenzidine 
(TMB PLUS 2, ECO-TEK®, KEM EN TEC Diagnostics) reagent was added to each well. For an 
optimal colour development, the plate was covered in thick aluminium foil, yielding a dark envi-
ronment, and was incubated for 5 minutes at room temperature. 100 µl 0.5 M H2SO4 was added to 
each well to stop colour development. The plate was not washed before addition of H2SO4.  
The optical density (absorbance) in the wells was determined using an ELISA-reader (Thermo Mul-
tiskan Ex) with a setting of 450 nm/650 nm. In this master’s thesis, the 450 nm readings are used in 
the data processing. All samples were tested in duplicate by the authors, who did not have experi-
ence in conducting ELISAs previous ahead of this study. If the optical density values differed by 
more than 10% between the duplicates, it was considered inaccurate and was repeated.

3   Additional laboratory techniques 
3.1   Biotinylation and dot-blotting 
200 µL of rabbit anti-human haptoglobin antibody (DAKO A0030) was mixed with 800 µL 0.2 M 
NaHCO3 (pH = 8.2). Furthermore, 1.6 mg biotin-N-hydroxysuccinimide (BNHS) was dissolved in 
dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) to respectively 1 mg/mL and 10 mg/mL. 100 µL BNHS-solution was 
mixed with the solution of A0030, and incubated for 2 hours at room temperature. Following incu-
bation, the solution was transferred to a dialysis tubing (SERVA SERVAPOR® dialysis tubing, 
MWCO 12,000-14,000, RC, diameter 6 mm) for dialysis against phosphate buffered saline (PBS), 
continuously stirred, at 4°C overnight. The PBS buffer was changed three times in total, with 4-24 
hours intervals, before harvesting the biotinylated A0030. 
A piece of nitrocellulose membrane was placed in a small Petri dish, and two drops of 2 µl of both 
the biotinylated A0030 and the pure A0030 was applied directly onto the membrane. In addition, a 
1:10 dilution in PBS of each of the solutions, was applied to the membrane. The samples were left 
to dry, before blocking for 5 minutes using TBS-T (a mixture of tris-buffered saline and Tween20) 
blocking buffer. After blocking, the membrane was washed with PBS on a shaking table for 3 times 
5 minutes, followed by 1 hour of incubation on a shaking table with alkaline phosphatase conjugat-
ed streptavidine, diluted 1:2000 in PBS. The membrane was washed once more on a shaking table 3 
times 5 minutes with PBS. For colour development, 1 BCIP/ NBT (SIGMA FAST™ BCIP/NBT = 
5-Bromo-4-Chloro-3-indolyl phosphate/Nitro blue tetrazolium) tablet was dissolved in 10 mL Mil-
li-Q water, and the solution was added to the Petri dish, until the membrane was covered. When 
colour development had stopped, the membrane was rinsed with Milli-Q water and left on a piece 
of filter paper to dry before the results were read. 
3.2   Silver staining and Western blotting 
An experienced laboratory technician at DTU VET conducted a silver staining and a western blot-

the protocols used at DTU. The examined haptoglobin sample was purified at DTU in 2011, and 
had been stored at -20°C.   
Two SDS-PAGE gels (12% Bis-Tris gel) were prepared. One was used for silver staining, and the 
other for western blotting. The samples were diluted 1:100 with phosphate buffered saline, and pre-

with 37% formaldehyde to complete the adsorption step with AgNO3. The adsorption was allowed 
to take place for 20 minutes before washing with Milli-Q water, 3x20 seconds. Subsequently, silver 
ions were reduced to metallic silver by addition of 60,0 g/L Na2CO3 mixed with 4 mg/L Na2S2O3,5 
H2O. 37% formaldehyde was added to the solution before use, and the gel was stored at 4°C until 
bands were visible. The reduction of silver ions was stopped by lowering pH, using a mixture of 
50% ethanol and 12% acetic acid, until no more bubbles appeared. To remove surplus of silver ions, 
the gel was washed for 20 minutes in 30% ethanol. Lastly, the gel was preserved with 3% glycerol, 
and was incubated for at least 1 hour, before the results were read.

3.3   Protocol for haemolysis of a blood sample 
Since haptoglobin binds free haemoglobin, it was considered relevant to test the effect of in vitro 
haemolysis on the haptoglobin enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Blood was collected 
from one white rhinoceros (pregnant adult female) from Copenhagen Zoo in an EDTA-tube (BD 
Vacutainer® EDTA-tube). The stabilized blood was centrifuged at 4400 G for 5 minutes, and the 
supernatant was removed from the tube followed by addition of phosphate buffered saline, at least 
two times the volume of the cell pellet. The cells were washed gently by carefully turning the tube. 
The sample was then centrifuged at 1000 G for 2 minutes. The washing and centrifugation was re-
peated twice more. After the last centrifugation, the supernatant was removed, erythrocytes were 
lysed by addition of Milli-Q water, two times the original volume and subsequent vigorous shaking. 
The sample was centrifuged at 4400 G for 5 minutes, and the supernatant, containing free rhinocer-
os haemoglobin, was harvested. The sample was divided into two cryogenic vials (Cryovial®), and 
stored at -20°C. When the haemoglobin was to be used in the assay, it was noticed that it had a matt 
appearance, and it was therefore centrifuged at approximately 12,000 G for 10 minutes which re-
vealed a cell pellet and a bright red supernatant. The supernatant was harvested once again and 
transferred to an Eppendorf Tube®. The following day, the sample was centrifuged once more at 
approximately 14,000 G for 10 minutes, but no sediment was seen. To ensure that no cell residues 
were left in the haemoglobin sample, half of the haemoglobin was filtered at 0.45 µm, as well, but 
no obvious difference between the filtered and non-filtered haemoglobin was observed in the results 
of the ELISA. 
To test the effect of haemolysis on the assay, a twofold serial dilution of haemoglobin, added to 
serum, was prepared. The serial dilution was made in three steps: 1) haemoglobin diluted in a two-
fold serial dilution of up to 20 steps with the dilution buffer, PBS-T-Casein 0.1%, and a pure sam-
ple used as start concentration 2) 10 µL of each haemoglobin dilution was mixed with 10 µL of one 
rhinoceros serum sample without any signs of red discolouring 3) serum-haemoglobin dilutions 
were further diluted with PBS-T-Casein0.1% corresponding to the known appropriate dilution of 
the selected rhinoceros serum. The twofold serial dilution was added to the plate along with a two-
fold serial dilution of the rhinoceros standard serum and the selected rhinoceros serum.

Measurement of haemoglobin absorbance  
The haemoglobin absorbance was measured spectrophotometrically at 540 nm using a Nanodrop 
2000®. The absorbance was measured in the serum-haemoglobin dilutions. A small drop of each 
sample (2.0μL) was pipetted directly onto the optical measurement surface of the Nanodrop. In be-
tween the measurements, the surface was wiped off with a lint-free lab wipe. The absorbance level 
of some low-dilution haemolysed samples exceeded the capacity of the reader, and was therefore 
diluted until they reached a readable level. The concentration in the samples was then found by 
backward calculation. The haemoglobin absorbance levels of the serial dilutions were compared to 
the corresponding ELISA results, to determine the maximum haemoglobin level, not affecting the 
amount of detectable haptoglobin in the sample. Furthermore, each sample was subjectively evalu-
ated, and scored on a scale from 0-4, based on how pronounced the red discolouration was., as it is 
seen in figure 4.

5   Determination of haptoglobin concentration in white rhinoceros samples 
The rhinoceros standard (Curt 01/06/11) with a haptoglobin concentration set to 1000 arbitrary units 
(AU) was consistently applied to the ELISA plates in a twofold serial dilution. The natural loga-
rithm to the haptoglobin concentration of each dilution of the standard was plotted along with the 
corresponding the optical density (OD) values, thereby creating a standard curve. A regression line  
(OD = a×ln(concentration[AU]) + b) was fitted to the linear part of the slope. The concentrations in 
the rhinoceros samples were then estimated from the respective OD values.

7   Pre-analytical variation 
7.1   Repeated freezing and thawing 
The potential variation in haptoglobin concentration due to repeated freezing and thawing was as-
sessed by duplicate testing of two serum samples collected the 10th of May 2017 from two white 
rhinoceroses (one adult female and one juvenile male). Both samples were tested, when they were 
thawed for the first time. Therefore, the concentration of haptoglobin in either sample was unknown 
prior to this evaluation. Both rhinoceroses were reported to be clinically healthy by the rhinoceros 
zookeepers. It was noticed that the serum sample from the juvenile male had a red discolouration. 
The samples were repeatedly frozen and thawed eight times in total. Each sample was divided into 
three separate containers to repeat freezing and thawing more than once per day, enabling the com-
pletion of measurements over the course of three days. Samples were stored at -20°C and thawed at 
room temperature immediately before testing in duplicates. Two plates containing identical samples 
were tested, aiming to secure that if the duplicate optical density values (OD values) differed by 
more than 10%, the corresponding duplicate on the other plate was included in the results instead. 
Both samples were added to the plate in two different dilutions, 1:10,000 and 1:20,000, with the 
intention of achieving OD values within the reading frame of the standard curve on the plates. 
7.2   The effect on haptoglobin concentration from long term storage of serum samples 
Samples from clinically healthy Danish rhinoceroses taken between 2006 and 2012 (n = 16) were 
compared to samples taken from clinically healthy Danish rhinoceroses in 2017 (n = 18), to deter-
mine if long term storage affects the haptoglobin concentration in serum samples.  
8   Evaluation of the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA 
8.1   Analytical performance  
Imprecision  
Imprecision was assessed by evaluating the intra-assay variation (CVintra) and inter-assay variation 
(CVinter) of the assay. Two serum samples from the same captive rhinoceros (Karen, female, 2003, 
blood samples from 19/08/10 and 16/05/11), known to have different levels of haptoglobin (high 
and low), were selected. Each sample was diluted in PBS-T-Casein 0.1% at two different levels 
(19/8/10: 1:64,000 and 1:128,000, 16/05/11: 1:16,000 and 1:32,000), to ensure OD values within 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
Side 39 af 87 
 
the reading frame. The samples were added to a microtiter plate in 10 duplicates, equivalent to a 
total of 20 wells. This was repeated for three consecutive days. CVintra was determined from the 
variation of 10 duplicate determinations of two serum samples within one plate. CVinter was as-
sessed from the variation of 30 duplicate determinations of two serum samples, with 10 duplicate 
determinations per plate per day (three plates in total). 
Inaccuracy 
Inaccuracy was indirectly determined by evaluation of linearity under dilution. Five serum samples 
from three captive rhinoceroses with a known haptoglobin level were selected: two samples with a 
low level of haptoglobin (Curt, male, 1996, 01/06/12; Minna, female 1996, 26/08/08), and three 
samples with a high level of haptoglobin (Karen, female, 2003, 08/06/10; Minna, female, 1996, 
15/12/08; Minna, female, 1996, 16/05/11). Samples containing different levels of haptoglobin, were 
selected to test whether the assay would yield consistent results, regardless of the level of haptoglo-
bin present in a sample. All samples were diluted in a twofold serial dilution starting with a dilution 
of 1:4000. The results were plotted with dilutions on the x-axis and concentration (AU) on the y-
axis, to graphically assess linearity.  
Detection limit 
Detection limit can be defined as the least detectable concentration (LDC). LDC is the lowest con-
centration of haptoglobin distinguishable from a sample with no haptoglobin (Petersen et al., 2001). 
24 negative control samples were added (100 µl PBS-T-Casein 0.1% with no rhinoceros serum) to 
the same microtiter plate, to evaluate the detection limit of the assay. The detection limit was esti-
mated as the mean OD value plus three standard deviations. The LDC of haptoglobin was deter-
mined from the lowest detectable absorbance by extrapolation of the standard curve, since LDC and 
lowest detectable absorbance are equivalent values (Petersen et al., 2001). Inspired by the method 
used in another study, evaluating a haptoglobin ELISA, the mean OD values and standard devia-
tions (SD) of the upper and lower end of the linear part of six standard curves, were used for statis-
tical evaluation of the performance of the assay. The six titrations of the rhinoceros standard serum, 
were conducted in six independent runs.

10   Statistical methods  
The statistical analysis was carried out using Prism Graphpad® 7.0 (Graphpad Software Inc., LA 
Jolla, USA) and Microsoft Office Excel.  
A Shapiro Wilk normality test (99% CI) was applied to all raw data, apart from the data used for 
determination of analytical imprecision. In this case, D’Agostino-Pearson normality test (99% CI) 
was applied. Only normally distributed data were included in parametric statistical analyses. In all 
aspects, a p-value <0.05 was considered significant.   
The coefficient of variation (CV) values were calculated from the general formula: CV = 
(SD×mean)/100, using routine descriptive statistical procedures (Box et al., 1978). Intra-assay and 
inter-assay variation were evaluated by determination of intra-assay CV (CVI) and inter-assay CV 
(CVG). A paired t-test was applied to determine whether a significant difference between each 
measurement of the duplicate testing was present. 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
 
Side 42 af 87 
 
Comparison of slopes was carried out using a two-tailed unpaired t-test, to determine whether the 
slopes were significantly different. 
Linearity under dilution, the variation in an individual animal over time, and the variation caused by 
repeated freezing and thawing were evaluated using linear regression and Runs test.  
The performance of the assay was statistically assessed by evaluation of the linear part of six titra-
tion curves of the rhinoceros standard. A B-value was calculated using the formula B = (mt – mb) – 
3(st + sb)^(mt – mb), in which mt and st are the mean and standard deviation of the upper OD-values, 
respectively. Correspondingly, the linear parts of the titration curves, mb and sb are the mean and 
standard deviation of the bottom OD-values, respectively. This enabled calculation of the signal 
window (SW) and Z’, using the formulas SW = B/st and Z’ = B/(mt – mb) (Iversen et al., 2006). 
As the dataset of the clinically heathy South African rhinoceroses was not normally distributed, 
overlap performance was assessed by performing a Mann-Whitney U-test. In a similar manner, clin-
ically healthy free-ranging South African rhinoceroses were compared to clinically healthy captive 
Danish rhinoceroses (samples taken 2006-2012). The same samples from healthy Danish rhinocer-
oses, were compared to samples from clinically healthy Danish rhinoceroses taken in 2017. The 
groups of each comparison were plotted in a dot-plot, and the mean of each of the groups presented

DISCUSSION 
A DTU VET haptoglobin ELISA was evaluated for measurement of haptoglobin in white rhinocer-
oses with the intention of determining if the acute phase protein haptoglobin can be applied as a 
diagnostic marker, and as an indicator of stress in zoological medicine regarding white rhinoceros-
es. Haptoglobin could become an additional valuable diagnostic tool in combination with haemato-
logical and biochemical parameters to detect “at risk” animals, when relocating rhinoceroses as part 
of an anti-poaching strategy.  
Pros and cons of each type of APP were considered when selecting haptoglobin. Since the closest 
domestic relative of the rhinoceros is the horse (Vaughan, 2011a), we assumed the APPs in the two 
species to have similar patterns of response. In the horse, haptoglobin is considered a minor or 
moderate APP, whereas SAA is a major APP  (Jacobsen & Andersen, 2007). Often, the major APPs 
are desired for use in clinical settings as this group possesses several advantageous properties: Rap-
id and marked increase in concentration in case of inflammation, low baseline level, and a short 
half-life causing a rapid decrease in plasma concentration, when inflammatory stimulus is no longer 
present (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). In comparison, minor and moderate APPs generally 
have a higher baseline level, the increase in concentration is less marked and slightly delayed, and 
the concentration remains elevated for days or weeks after the inflammatory stimulus ceases 
(Eckersall & Bell, 2010; Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). Therefore, haptoglobin was consid-
ered relevant, since we believe that a moderate APP could be advantageous regarding detection of 
an acute phase response in a free-ranging animal. The response pattern of moderate APPs provides 
a longer time frame in which an increased concentration of the protein can be measured. This is 
considered an advantage regarding detection in free-ranging animals as these animals typically 
mask clinical symptoms and might not be examined until several days after the onset of clinical 
signs.  
A non-competitive sandwich ELISA was set up and optimized for quantification of haptoglobin in 
rhinoceros serum. Subsequently, an evaluation of the ELISA regarding analytical performance and 
overlap performance was conducted, and it was determined if haptoglobin could be measured in 
serum from white rhinoceroses. A total of 86 serum samples from white rhinoceroses were availa-
ble in this study. Of these, 36 serum samples had been taken from South African rhinoceroses, free-
ranging or in bomas, between 2008 and 2013, and 50 serum samples were taken from captive Dan-
ish rhinoceroses in the period 2006-2012 (n = 28) and the spring 2017 (n = 22). 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
Side 57 af 87 
 
Despite some limitations, we found that the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA was suitable for measur-
ing haptoglobin concentration in rhinoceros serum samples, and therefore, the remaining objectives 
regarding the use of haptoglobin as a diagnostic marker and a potential stress indicator in white rhi-
noceroses could be evaluated. 
Before evaluation of the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA, the slopes of an equine and a bovine hap-
toglobin standard sample with known concentrations of haptoglobin were compared to the slopes of 
a rhinoceros sample. Both were found to be obviously different from the rhinoceros, despite the 
expectation of similarity between the equine standard and rhinoceros serum sample, considering the 
close phylogenetic relationship (Vaughan, 2011a), as shown in other studies regarding rhinoceroses. 
For instance, commercial assays applied in equine medicine have been validated for black rhinocer-
os serum (Schook et al., 2015), and an antibody to equine ferritin has been found to bind ferritin 
from rhinoceroses, despite the fact that antibodies to ferritin are usually species-specific (Smith et 
al., 1995). As neither of the commercially available standards could be applied, implementation of a 
purified rhinoceros haptoglobin sample and determination of the haptoglobin concentration of this 
sample was considered ideal. The results of silver staining and western blotting used to analyse an 
already purified and reduced sample were compared to the molecular weight (MW) of equine hap-
toglobin subunits, due to the phylogenetic relationship, to determine if the sample contained only 
haptoglobin. On a SDS-PAGE gel, reduced equine haptoglobin migrates as protein double bands 
demonstrating two types of subunits with MWs of 50 kDa and 47.5 kDa, respectively (Taira et al., 
1992). On both the silver stain gel and the western blot, a band with a MW of approximately 40 
kDa could be seen. Furthermore, a band with an MW of approximately 50 kDa was seen in the gel 
of the silver staining. This indicated the presence of subunits of reduced rhinoceros haptoglobin. 
However, several other bands were apparent in both the silver stain gel and the western blot indicat-
ing the presence of other proteins and/or protein residue, and the possibility that haptoglobin was 
fragmented, thus preventing further use of this rhinoceros haptoglobin sample. Collectively, this led 
to the implementation of a rhinoceros serum sample with a haptoglobin concentration set to 1000 
AU as a standard. 
Practicability of the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA was considered as part of the evaluation of the 
diagnostic test. The assay evaluated in this master’s thesis is a non-competitive sandwich ELISA, 
which is a commonly used laboratory method. Generally, the ELISA techniques require a minimum 
of equipment, and no further education of laboratory technicians is needed. Implementation of the 
rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA is fairly easy, as only basic ELISA equipment is needed. Since no 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
Side 58 af 87 
 
rhinoceros haptoglobin serum standard is available, the obtained results are semi-quantitative, and 
determined in arbitrary units (AU). Since the costs of performing a diagnostic test are also assessed 
during the practicability evaluation, the haptoglobin ELISA was chosen over commercial SAA-
assays, as it is less expensive.  
In evaluation of imprecision, the intra-assay coefficient of variation (CVintra) was <5% indicating 
only a small imprecision between intra-assay measurements. Typically, between 30-100 samples 
are tested in duplicate and then divided into three groups based on concentration (low, medium, 
high), or alternatively at least three samples with low, medium, and high concentration are tested in 
duplicate 10-30 times in the same analytical run, when evaluating intra-assay variation (Jensen & 
Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). However, only two samples, tested in multiple duplicates, were included 
in this study. In spite of the alternative approach, this set up still evaluates the ability of the test to 
repeatedly determine the same concentration in all duplicates on the plate. The total inter-assay co-
efficient of variation (CVinter) was found to be 21.5%. However, the determination of CVinter was 
based on just two samples, which is less than the recommended number of samples for determining 
CVinter. This could potentially cause a falsely high CVinter value. The recommended set up for test-
ing inter-assay variation, is to include a minimum of three samples, representing low, medium and 
high concentration of the analyte (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). This set up was used in a 
study evaluating a porcine haptoglobin ELISA, in which 50 samples with concentrations differing 
across the spectrum were included. In that study, the CVinter values varied from 0.01% in the sample 
with the highest concentration of haptoglobin to 30.4% in the sample with the lowest concentration 
(Petersen et al., 2001). In our study, the CVinter value was similarly found to be the highest in the 
sample with the lowest concentration of haptoglobin (CVinter = 27.44%) compared to the sample 
with the highest concentration (CVinter = 15.56%). This implies that inclusion of more samples cov-
ering the entire spectrum could potentially minimize the total CVinter of the assay. The assay should 
not be discarded due to the CVinter determined in this study, as the set up for determination of this 
value was not optimal.  
Inaccuracy was indirectly assessed by testing linearity under dilution. With one exception, none of 
the slopes deviated significantly from zero (p ≥ 0.1301) indicating that the assay may be able to 
measure the haptoglobin concentration in serum samples regardless of dilution. The slope which 
deviated significantly from zero (p = 0.0215) was derived from a sample (Curt 11/06/12) with a low 
haptoglobin concentration. However, two other samples with a similar concentration were linear 
under dilution. The samples were diluted in a twofold serial dilution, which could be the source of a 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
Side 59 af 87 
 
systematic error causing one of the slopes to deviate from zero. The test should be repeated to rule 
out this possibility by making the dilutions to each level separately (Sheehan et al., 2013). Another 
possible explanation is that the test does not always perform optimally, when the haptoglobin con-
centration in a sample is in the lower end of the spectrum. Correspondingly, the titration curves of 
some samples with a low haptoglobin concentration were found not to resemble the typical rhinoc-
eros titration curve. Based on these findings, it is suspected that a matrix effect (Smeraglia et al., 
2002) may overpower the signal produced by haptoglobin, when the concentration is below a cer-
tain point. Because of this, it is considered ideal to compare the slopes of the titration curves of all 
samples to the slope of the titration curve of the rhinoceros standard to ensure that matrix effect has 
no influence on the measurement of haptoglobin concentration in a given sample.  
The lower limit of detection was found to be markedly lower than the mean OD value in the lower 
end of the linear part of six standard curves, meaning that the actual lowest concentration detectable 
in a sample is markedly higher than the estimated least detectable concentration (LDC). Further-
more, the performance of the assay was evaluated by calculation of the signal window (SW) and Z’. 
It is recommended that the SW is >2 and Z’ is excellent when >0.5 (Iversen et al., 2006). The SW 
was found to be 6.55 and Z’ was found to be 0.81, both indicating a good performance of the assay. 
Already after evaluation of the initial test runs, we came to suspect that haemolysis had an influence 
on the amount of measurable haptoglobin as it appeared that samples with red discolouration gener-
ally had a low level of haptoglobin and titrated differently from the typical rhinoceros sample. In an 
equine study investigating colic, a low haptoglobin level was found in several of the horses ex-
pected to have an elevated level. This was considered likely to be caused by the presence of free 
haemoglobin, as in vivo haemolysis can be associated with colic (Dondi et al., 2015). In another 
study evaluating a porcine haptoglobin ELISA, the effect of free haemoglobin on the measurable 
level of haptoglobin was investigated in vitro by comparison of paired serum samples, one of which 
was haemolysed. It was found that almost all of the haemolysed samples had a significantly lower 
concentration of haptoglobin (Petersen et al., 2001). These findings correspond to the fact that hap-
toglobin binds free haemoglobin (Laurell & Nyman, 1957), thus causing a decrease in the level of 
detectable haptoglobin (Batchelor et al., 1989). The effect of free haemoglobin on the measurable 
level of haptoglobin in the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA was evaluated by an in vitro approach. 
From this, we concluded that haemolysis has a marked influence on the measurable level of hapto-
globin. By comparison of spectrophotometric absorbance measurements and the implemented hae-
molysis scale, we found that it is difficult to subjectively evaluate, if the level of haemolysis is too 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
Side 60 af 87 
 
high for trustworthy measurement of the haptoglobin concentration. Therefore, it is considered ideal 
to measure the level of haemoglobin spectrophotometrically if a haemolysed sample is to be evalu-
ated with the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA. Based on our findings, the haptoglobin level may be 
falsely low, if the absorbance level is above 0.037 absorbance units at 540 nm.  
Repeated freezing and thawing did not appear to have any influence on the level of haptoglobin in 
the serum samples, as the results do not deviate from linearity. However, our findings are based on 
two separate runs with a small sample size, since comparison of the results was prevented due to 
obvious differences between the two plates. Nevertheless, we do not believe that repeated freezing 
and thawing affects the level of haptoglobin, and corresponding findings were demonstrated in an-
other study on haptoglobin (Petersen et al., 2001). Furthermore, it was evaluated if long term stor-
age affects the level of haptoglobin in a biological sample. Samples from 2017 and samples from 
2006-2013 were compared, as we found no reason to suspect a decrease in haptoglobin level during 
storage at -20°C for several years as no significant difference (p = 0.3131) was found. This is sup-
ported by the assumption that equine haptoglobin is stable for at least 34 months at -20°C (Pihl et 
al., 2013).  
Even though the evaluation of the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA was mainly successful, we have 
observed that the slope of the standard curve sporadically diverged from the typical pattern, thus 
causing an inter-assay difference in the determined serum haptoglobin concentration. Such variation 
appeared, for instance, in the test on repeated freezing and thawing. This constitutes that the slope 
of a standard curve should be compared to other standard curves, when a difference in determined 
serum haptoglobin concentration is found between two or more runs. However, the difference was 
not causing the concentration to change in a way that would affect the health status of the animal 
meaning that this limitation is not considered critical when determining the concentration in a single 
screening.  
It was investigated if captive (Danish) and free-ranging (South African) animals could be viewed as 
one group to maximize the sample size for inclusion in the evaluation of overlap performance. A 
significantly higher haptoglobin level was found in captive rhinoceroses compared to free-ranging. 
The hypothesis that the haptoglobin levels in free-ranging rhinoceroses do not differ from the hap-
toglobin levels in captive rhinoceroses, was thereby rejected, and the captive animals had to be ex-
cluded from the evaluation of overlap performance. This resulted in a smaller sample size than the 
typical number of 20-30 individuals included in each group (Kjelgaard-Hansen & Jacobsen, 2011). 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
Side 61 af 87 
 
Despite the smaller sample sizes of clinically healthy rhinoceroses (n = 11) and rhinoceroses ex-
pected to have an acute phase response (n = 17), we decided to assess overlap performance. How-
ever, we considered the number of samples inadequate for evaluation of clinical performance, as 
this requires at least 40 individuals, classified as either healthy or diseased, to establish diagnostic 
sensitivity and specificity of a diagnostic test (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). Due to the small 
sample size, all animals expected to have an acute phase response, regardless of severity of the 
cause, was included, despite the recommendation that the group of diseased animals should only 
include animals in an advanced stage of the disease, to obtain an unrealistically high prevalence of 
disease, and thereby minimize overlap between the groups (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). 
Even though the conditions could potentially cause a major overlap, the two groups were found to 
be significantly different. The values of the group of healthy animals were found to be clustered 
(median = 154.8 AU) with an exception of three samples found to have haptoglobin concentrations 
more than three times higher than the rest of the samples. These three samples were taken from clin-
ically healthy animals in bomas, whereas the other samples were taken at the time of capture. The 
values of the animals expected to have an APR were disseminated with values ranging from below 
110 AU to just under 2000 AU with a median of 1128 AU. Despite an overlap between the groups, 
the hypothesis that rhinoceroses expected to have an APR have a significantly higher haptoglobin 
level than clinically healthy rhinoceroses was confirmed. 
Although overlap performance was evaluated despite access to a small sample size, we considered 
the number of samples inadequate for evaluation of clinical performance as this requires at least 40 
individuals, classified as either healthy or diseased, to establish diagnostic sensitivity and specificity 
of a diagnostic test (Jensen & Kjelgaard-Hansen, 2010). Therefore, we believe that no compromise, 
regarding the number of animals included in this evaluation, should be made.

tion into consideration, a graphical comparison of three groups (healthy, moderate APR, major 
APR) was made to evaluate if any cohesion between severity of cause of APR and the haptoglobin

thin. Therefore, it could not be determined what group this rhinoceros belonged to. It was demon-
strated that the haptoglobin concentration in serum generally increases with severity of tissue dam-
age, indicating a possibility of differentiating healthy animals from animals with an acute phase 
response. Another similar graphical comparison was made with the intention of maximizing the

differences between the three groups. One animal which was initially included in the group of “ma-
jor APR” was excluded, as it had an unexplainable low haptoglobin concentration (<110 AU). An 
explanation could have been haemolysis of the sample, but no obvious signs were present (score 1 
on haemolysis scale). It was estimated that the rhinoceros was injured seven days or more before 
the time of blood sampling, which may be a possible explanation for the low haptoglobin level. 
However, similar old injuries were seen in other case animals having a high haptoglobin concentra-
tion causing this explanation to be uncertain, as well. In addition, three samples derived from clini-
cally healthy animals at the end of the quarantine time in bomas were excluded from the healthy 
group. Inclusion of samples from these animals could cause a potential bias, since the conditions in 
bomas differ from the natural habitat of rhinoceroses. The comparison further clarified the possibil-
ity of evaluating the severity of a condition based on a measured haptoglobin concentration in a 
serum sample of a rhinoceros. However, some overlap between the groups exist, meaning that it 
may not always be possible to compare the character of the APR based on haptoglobin concentra-
tion with the severity of the disease or injury. Furthermore, it must be taken into consideration that 
this comparison was based on small sample sizes, and precautions should be taken regarding the 
calculated means suggesting a baseline level in the three groups. 
The findings of this study implied that haptoglobin could potentially be applied as a marker of in-
flammatory disease in white rhinoceroses. In captive rhinoceroses, haptoglobin could be applied in 
health monitoring, including detection of disease not clinically apparent, as they often conceal any 
illnesses (Bertelsen et al., 2009). A similar application in free-ranging rhinoceroses kept in quaran-
tine in bomas may be possible, to ensure detection of any disease developed during the quarantine. 
In a similar way, it is considered possible that haptoglobin could be implemented in the detection of 
“at risk” animals. If a high haptoglobin concentration is found in an apparently healthy rhinoceros 
captured for relocation, the animal is potentially in an increased risk of developing disease if ex-
posed to stressors. Similar to the proposal of implementing biochemical values (Mathebula et al., 
2012), haptoglobin may be used as a screening indicator to determine, if an animal should be re-
leased to avoid these complications. However, further research with larger sample sizes is needed as 
the clinical performance of the assay was not evaluated in this study.  
The haptoglobin levels in captive Danish rhinoceroses were found to be significantly higher than in 
free-ranging South African rhinoceroses (p = 0.0004), although both groups were clinically healthy. 
In captive black rhinoceroses, a variety of unusual diseases associated with an increased morbidity 
and mortality is seen (Schook et al., 2015). In a study on black rhinoceroses, conducted to evaluate 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
 
Side 63 af 87 
 
the effect of keeping this animal species in a zoo-environment, captive individuals were similarly 
found to have a significantly higher level of SAA and other inflammatory markers investigated 
compared to free-ranging black rhinoceroses (Schook et al., 2015). In that study, it was discussed if 
a zoo-based diet contributes to inflammation in black rhinoceroses in a similar way to what is de-
scribed in horses, in which an excess nutrient intake and low fibre diet have been demonstrated to 
increase inflammation. However, this calls for further investigation (Schook et al., 2015). White 
rhinoceroses have no apparent problems adjusting to a zoo-based diet, as it resembles the natural 
diet of this grazing species in contrast to the natural diet of the browsing black rhinoceros. In addi-
tion, white rhinoceroses do not seem to suffer from any unusual conditions that could explain higher 
concentrations of serum haptoglobin. This suggests that further investigations are needed to clarify 
whether the high haptoglobin level in captive individuals compared to free-ranging animals is actu-
ally caused by inflammation. 
We believe that stress may be another reason for the haptoglobin concentration to be higher in cap-
tive rhinoceroses compared to free-ranging rhinoceroses, considering that haptoglobin can be a po-
tential indicator of stress. Several studies in bovine and equine medicine have demonstrated an in-
crease in serum haptoglobin concentration after exposure to stressors such as transportation (Casella 
et al., 2012; Lomborg et al., 2008; Murata & Miyamoto, 1993), isolation, and slippery floors 
(Lomborg et al., 2008). Captive rhinoceroses are subjected to circumstances different from the natu-
ral habitat of the animal species especially regarding the social structure. In the wild, subadult rhi-
noceroses tend to have temporary social partners, adult females typically have one subadult social 
partner at certain times, but do not accept other rhinoceroses near the end of a pregnancy, and adult 
males lives in solitary (Shrader & Owen-Smith, 2002). A group dynamic as seen in nature is rarely 
attainable in a captive setting, which may lead to a constant source of stress to the rhinoceroses as 
indicated in a study investigating the structure of the social groups in captive white rhinoceroses. 
Social stress was supposed to be the cause of unusual behaviour observed in subordinate animals 
(Metrione et al., 2007). According to one of the rhinoceros zookeepers in Copenhagen Zoo, the four 
animals monitored for a five-week period may have some difficulties living in proximity to one 
another. The pregnant females do not get along and seem stressed, when being in the stable at the 
same time as the male, even though bars separate the individuals (Cate Liv Jensen, pers. comm., 
23rd of June 2017). Correspondingly, it appears that the haptoglobin level increases in rhinoceroses 
kept in bomas. We found that three rhinoceroses all had a haptoglobin level below 180 AU at the 
time of immobilization, and by the end of the quarantine, the haptoglobin level in the same three 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
Side 64 af 87 
 
individuals had increased to above 600 AU. A possible explanation could be exposure to stressors, 
such as being close to humans, in proximity to other rhinoceroses, and being kept in a confined 
space (Kock et al., 1999). However, no definite conclusion can be made as no statistic evaluation 
has been performed due to a small sample size, and it cannot be ruled out that this change in hapto-
globin level is caused by variation within the individuals. However, it is noteworthy that the hapto-
globin level increased in all three animals as the only apparent difference was the fact that the ani-
mals had been kept in a holding facility. Some rhinoceroses are released before the quarantine-time 
is up, as these individuals cannot adapt, and some individuals even develop unspecific clinical 
signs. Two rhinoceroses in this group of animals were found to have elevated haptoglobin levels 
before release compared to the haptoglobin level at the time of capture. It is suspected that animals 
not adapting to boma conditions may become unthrifty as stress related to capturing potentially in-
duce opportunistic infections or recrudescence of latent infections (Govender et al., 2011).  
We found noticeable variations, when examining the haptoglobin level in four animals for five 
weeks, potentially impeding the proposed applications of haptoglobin. In one rhinoceros, a concen-
tration measured in one week was five times higher than the lowest measured concentration in the 
same individual. Furthermore, the baseline concentration of haptoglobin was found to be higher 
than zero (markedly higher in captive animals), and the level of haptoglobin was consistently higher 
in the juvenile rhinoceros than in the other individuals. Although no conclusions can be made based 
on observations in one juvenile male, the higher level of haptoglobin could be associated with age. 
An equine study found the haptoglobin levels to be high in foals compared to adult horses (Taira et 
al., 1992), and in a study evaluating serum amyloid A (SAA) in black rhinoceroses, captive juvenile 
rhinoceroses were found to have higher levels than subadult and adult individuals (Schook et al., 
2015). The large variation in haptoglobin concentration found in individual animals would hamper 
differentiation of elevated levels of haptoglobin due to other causes than natural fluctuation. If hap-
toglobin is to be implemented as a diagnostic marker of disease or as a stress indicator in rhinocer-
oses, reference values should be established.

CONCLUSION  
In this study, a haptoglobin enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) was set up and opti-
mized for use in white rhinoceroses from a DTU VET haptoglobin ELISA. The analytical perfor-
mance (phase I) including practicability, analytical imprecision, inaccuracy, and establishment of a 
detection limit, and the overlap performance (phase II) of the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA were 
primarily evaluated in accordance with the international standard guidelines on diagnostic test vali-
dation. However, the evaluation departed from the recommendations in some instances. Some chal-
lenges that must be taken into consideration were noticed during the evaluation of the haptoglobin 
ELISA. It appeared that a matrix effect potentially affects the measurement of haptoglobin concen-
tration, if the concentration in the serum sample is low. Furthermore, the slope of the standard curve 
in some ELISA runs was different from the typical standard curve. Haemolysis was found to have a 
profound effect on the performance of the assay, meaning that a haemolysed sample cannot be ana-
lysed in the assay without certain precautions. The results of the rhinoceros haptoglobin ELISA are 
semi-quantitative, since no purified rhinoceros haptoglobin standard with a known concentration 
exists. Despite these limitations, the assay was considered suitable for measuring the concentration 
of haptoglobin in non-haemolysed serum samples from white rhinoceroses. A significant difference 
in the haptoglobin concentration in healthy and animals expected to have an APR was found, 
though an overlap between the two groups was seen. In addition, it seems that the haptoglobin se-
rum concentration increases with severity of inflammation. Healthy captive rhinoceroses were 
found not to be comparable with healthy free-ranging rhinoceroses due to significantly higher hap-
toglobin serum concentration. Measurements of the haptoglobin concentration in captive clinically 
healthy rhinoceroses, determined in arbitrary units, implied that the baseline level is not close to 
zero. Furthermore, variation in the level of serum haptoglobin was seen when measuring the con-
centration for five consecutive weeks. Although the cause of the high level of haptoglobin in cap-
tive rhinoceroses is uncertain, stress could be an explanation to this difference. In conclusion, hap-
toglobin can potentially be applied as a diagnostic marker of disease, for detection of “at risk” ani-
mals among free-ranging animals captured for relocation and in health monitoring of rhinoceroses 
in zoological collections for detection of disease before clinical signs appear. Moreover, haptoglo-
bin may be a potential indicator of stress.

PERSPECTIVES 
This study contemplates further investigation regarding the application of haptoglobin in white rhi-
noceroses. If the sample sizes are increased, more well-defined groups can be obtained, thus ena-
bling an optimal set up for overlap performance and further evaluation of the rhinoceros haptoglo-
bin ELISA regarding clinical performance. This will clarify, if haptoglobin can be considered a reli-
able diagnostic marker for differentiating rhinoceroses with an acute phase response from healthy 
individuals as indicated from this study. Moreover, reference values of haptoglobin concentrations 
in healthy rhinoceroses should be established, if haptoglobin proves to be useful as a diagnostic 
marker in this species. The difference in haptoglobin levels in captive rhinoceroses compared to 
free-ranging rhinoceroses should be further investigated to determine the cause of the increased 
level in captive rhinoceroses. In addition, the application of haptoglobin as an indicator of stress in 
rhinoceroses should be further investigated, since no conclusion could be made on this subject. The 
variation in haptoglobin concentration observed in individual animals will potentially make it diffi-
cult to determine whether an increased concentration is simply caused by normal variation. There-
fore, it may be relevant to investigate if other APPs (e.g. SAA) in the white rhinoceros demonstrate 
a more stable level of concentration in clinically healthy animals.
