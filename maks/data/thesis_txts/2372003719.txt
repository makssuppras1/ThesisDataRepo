Abstract 
Nanomaterials have gained growing attention in recent years. This is due to its innovative 
properties that can be incorporated into a wide array of products and an increase in production is 
therefore expected in the years to come. This also raises concerns regarding the environmental 
release and exposure of engineered nanoparticles (NPs) on aquatic organisms. In nature, NPs 
interacts with macromolecules derived from plants and animals (exudates), which may influence 
the fate and effect of NPs. For instance, a so called “eco-corona” is formed by macromolecules 
adsorbing onto the surface of NPs.  
The main focus of this project was to investigate if the toxicity of silver NPs (Ag NPs) was 
influenced by algal exudates. The chosen test organism was the microalgae Pseudokirchneriella 
subcapitata. Ag NP was chosen due to its relevancy as the most frequent used NP and its potent 
toxicity towards algae. This was done by performing two types of toxicity tests: 48 hours growth 
rate inhibition and 2 hours carbon assimilation inhibition. These two tests were chosen to assess 
the influence of the exposure duration on the test response of algae. Toxicity tests were also 
performed with silver nitrate (AgNO3) in order to assess any differences/similarities in toxicity 
between dissolved silver and Ag NPs when exposed to exudates. The stock suspensions/solutions 
used in tests was either aged for 24 hours or prepared fresh before each experiment. This was 
done to assess if the toxicity of Ag NPs and AgNO3 was influenced by interacting with the exudates 
over a longer period of time.  
For the 48 hours tests with Ag NPs, the EC50 values with aged stock suspensions was 210 µg Ag/L 
[180 ; 240]95% and 520 µg Ag/L [480 ; 570]95% in standard and exudate medium, respectively. Tests 
with fresh stock suspension had a low reproducibility and showed no difference in toxicity, 
indicating unstable exposure conditions. 2 hour tests with Ag NP showed similar toxicity compared 
with the 48 hour tests, with EC50 values of 180 µg Ag/L [150 ; 220]95% and 470 µg Ag/L [420 ; 
520]95% in standard and exudate medium, respectively.  The results indicated that Ag NPs partly 
dissolved during tests and thereby released Ag+ ions, which increased the toxicity in standard 
medium. However, the toxicity in exudate medium was significantly reduced by the influence of 
exudates. This was either due to the formation of silver complexes consisting of Ag+ ions and 
exudates, the adsorption of exudates onto the surface of Ag NPs (inhibiting the release of Ag+) or a 
combination of the two.  
In 48 hour tests with AgNO3 in fresh stock solutions, EC50 values of 11 µg Ag/L [9.9 ; 12]95% and 23 
µg Ag/L [23 [20 ; 27]95% in standard and exudate medium was obtained, respectively. For the aged 
stock solutions the toxicity was similar in both media types, with EC50 values of 19 µg Ag/L [27 
;22]95% and 21 µg Ag/L [18 ; 25]95% in standard and exudate medium, respectively. The 2 hour tests 
showed no  significant difference in toxicity when using fresh stock solution, with EC50 values of 6.6 
µg Ag/L [5.3 ; 8.2]95% and 8.1 µg Ag/L [6.5 ; 9.9]95% in standard and exudate medium, respectively. 
A reduction in toxicity was only observed in 48 hours tests, indicating that the dynamic changes in 
toxicity occurred faster in the presence of exudates.    
==PAGE:6==
IV 
 
Overall the results indicated that the toxicity of Ag NPs and AgNO3 was both reduced by the 
influence of algal exudates by a factor of 2.5 and 2, respectively. This indicates that the 
mechanisms of toxicity are similar and that the Ag+ concentration mainly governs the toxicity for 
both test materials. However, the dynamic changes in toxicity were reduced faster for AgNO3 
compared to Ag NPs. This suggested that Ag NPs released Ag+ ions due to dissolution during the 48 
hours toxicity test period. Additionally, a reduction in toxicity was first observed after aging the Ag 
NP stock suspension for 24 hours, indicating that a certain incubation period was necessary for the 
exudates to influence the toxicity. Further research regarding the interaction between Ag NPs, 
exudates and algae should be investigated in order to provide further information about the 
mechanisms of toxicity for Ag NPs.

egenskaber der kan anvendes i en lang række af produkter og en stigende i produktion er derfor 
forventet i de kommende år. Dette giver også anledning til bekymringer vedrørende miljøudslip og 
eksponering af syntetiske nanopartikler (NP) for akvatiske organismer. I naturen interagerer NP 
med makromolekyler udskildt fra planter og dyr (ekssudater), hvilket kan påvirke NP’s skæbne og 
effekt. For eksempel dannes en såkaldt ”eco-corona”, hvis makromolekuler adsorberes på 
overfladen af NP.

algeeksudater. Den valgte testorganisme var mikroalgen Pseudokirchneriella subcapitata. Ag NP

alger. Dette blev undersøgt ved at anvende to typer toksicitetstests: 48 timers vækstratehæmning

disse tests, blev enten forberedt 24 timer (ældet) eller én time før (frisk) hvert forsøg. Dette blev 
gjort for at vurdere om toksiciteten af Ag NP og AgNO3 var påvirket af interaktionen med 
ekssudaterne i forskellige tidsperioder.

Ag NP (hvilket hæmmer frigivelsen af Ag+) eller en kombination af disse to scenarier. 
I testene med AgNO3 i friske stamopløsninger var EC50 værdierne 11 µg Ag/L [9.9 ; 12]95% og 23 µg

toksiciteten ens i begge medietyper, med EC50 værdier på 19 μg Ag / L *27; 22+95% og 21 µg Ag/L 
[18; 25]95% i henholdsvis standard- og ekssudatmedie. Derudover, viste 2 timers testene med friske

1. Background and aim 
Nanomaterials are increasingly being incorporated into a wide array of products that most likely 
will lead to a proliferation of engineered nanoparticles (NPs) in the environment (Navarro et al., 
2008a). This is especially the case for the test material used in this project; silver nanoparticles (Ag 
NPs), as it is regarded as the most frequent used NP in consumer products (Vance et al., 2015; 
Woodrow Wilson Database, 2009). An increased amount of NPs are expected to be produced and 
released into the environment, which raises concern regarding the consequences of 
environmental exposure (Dowling et al., 2004 ; Maynard, 2006). Even though a substantial amount 
of research have been conducted regarding the benefits and applicability of NPs, there is still 
insufficient evidence on the environmental and biological impacts (Morris et al., 2011 ; Canesi & 
Corsi, 2016). It is therefore of great importance to understand the interaction between NPs and 
the environment to evaluate the associated risks (Rahman et al., 2013).  
NPs may interact with the environments by adsorbing macromolecules onto its surface. This 
phenomenon also occurs inside living organisms and is originally known from human toxicology as 
a “corona” formation (Lynch et al., 2014 ; Lundqvist et al., 2011). In the environment, this 
phenomenon is known as an “eco-corona”, which occurs when NPs are exposed to natural organic 
matter (NOM), which partly consists of secreted macromolecules from plants/animals (exudates) 
(Nasser & Lynch, 2016 ; Cupi et al., 2014 ; Lynch et al., 2014 ; Römer et al., 2016). This kind of 
transformation may alter the properties of NPs in different ways, such as transport (Lowry et al., 
2012), uptake (Nasser & Lynch, 2016), toxicity (Nasser & Lynch, 2016) and environmental fate 
(Lowry et al., 2012). However, not enough research has been conducted to completely understand 
the interaction between macromolecules and NPs (Lowry et al., 2012).  
Ecotoxicity tests can be useful tool to assess the toxicity of NPs, although challenges during tests 
may arise due to the presence of exudates, such as NP aggregation or dissolution (Hartmann et al., 
2012 ; Hartmann et al., 2010 ; Gao et al., 2012 ; Crane et al., 2008). In this relation, a rapidly 
growing algal species, Pseudokirchneriella subcapitata, is cheap to maintain and sensitive to 
stressors, which makes it suitable for ecotoxicity tests (Arensberg et al., 1995). Previous studies 
have performed toxicity tests with Ag NPs and NOM to other aquatic test organisms, which 
resulted in a reduction in toxicity (Cupi et al., 2014 ; Gao et al., 2012). 
 
Aim 
The overall aim of this project was to assess the influence of algal exudates on the toxicity of Ag 
NPs towards the microalga species, P. subcapitata. In addition to this, the investigation of the 
following bullet points is also included in this project:  
 
The formation of a corona and influencing parameters from a theoretical point of view.  
 
Quantification of the protein and carbohydrate content in algal exudates by colorimetric 
methods.  
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
 
Characterization of Ag NPs during tests determined by dynamic light scattering (DLS), 
transmission electron microscopy (TEM), inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry 
(ICP-MS) and single particle ICP-MS (spICP-MS). 
Two different toxicity methods were applied to investigate this subject: ISO 8692 48 hours growth 
rate inhibition and 2 hours carbon assimilation inhibition tests. These two test methods were 
performed to assess the influence of different exposure durations on the test response of algae. 
Additionally, the stock suspension used in these tests were either freshly made or aged for 24 
hours. This was done to assess if the toxicity was influenced by increasing the incubation time and 
to evaluate the reactivity between Ag NPs and exudates.  
Toxicity tests were also performed with silver nitrate (AgNO3) to investigate differences and/or 
similarities in toxicity between dissolved silver and Ag NPs when exposed to algal exudates. 
Additionally, tests with AgNO3 could provide information regarding the interaction between 
dissolved silver and other complexes in the medium.

2. Introduction 
The introduction will contain information regarding the interaction between macromolecules and 
NPs, since it is expected that some of the processes described in the upcoming sections, may occur 
in the experiments performed in this project. In this context, the formation of a corona and 
influencing parameters are explained from a theoretical point of view.  
 
2.1 
Nanoparticles and Macromolecules Interactions 
According to the European Commission (2011) a nanomaterial are defined as particles where at 
least 50 % have minimum one external dimension between 1 – 100 nm. This size range is also 
supported by several other studies (Dowling et al., 2004 ; Hansen et al., 2007). The properties of 
NPs and their interaction with living organisms are very different compared to their bulk 
counterparts (Auffan et al., 2009 ; Walczyk et al., 2010). This is mainly due to their small size 
(Dowling et al., 2004), large surface area to volume ratio (Eastman et al., 2001) and high free 
surface energy (Lesniak et al., 2012). The latter may cause an interfacial adsorption of 
macromolecules in order to lower the surface energy, which leads to the formation of coronas 
(Nel et al., 2009). 
Figure 2-1 shows the number of articles annually matching the keywords “nanoparticle” and 
“corona” in Web of science, which demonstrates that this topic has received increasing attention 
in recent years. 
 
Figure 2-1: The number of published articles annually containing  
the words ”nanoparticle” & ”corona” in the keyword description 
(data obtained from “Web of Science”, search date: 18/05-2017). 
In analogy to the corona, an eco-corona can form naturally in the environment by interacting with 
macromolecules, which adsorbs onto its surface (Lynch et al., 2014). In nature, the interaction 
between NPs and macromolecules is unavoidable, as the latter exists in concentrations orders of 
magnitude higher compared with the modeled concentration of NPs in the environment (Louie et 
al., 2014 ; Lynch et al., 2014 ; Gottschalk et al., 2013). The eco-corona may ascribe a biological 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
identity to NPs that conveys the interaction with organisms (Walczyk et al., 2010). Even the natural 
properties of NPs might be altered, such as toxicity and the formation of aggregates, which have 
been suggested by previous studies (Gao et al., 2012 ; Nasser & Lynch, 2016).  
As of yet, the corona has been the main focus of published research papers, in the context of 
different biological fluids, mainly biological serum or plasma (Lynch et al., 2014 ; Salvati et al., 
2012), whereas limited research has been conducted on the eco-corona (Nasser & Lynch, 2016). 
The main focus of this report will be on the eco-corona, however, due to inadequate research on 
the subject, relevant information about the corona will be described in details.   
 
2.2 
Corona Formation 
A corona initially forms by adsorbing the most abundant macromolecules onto the surface of NPs, 
as they interact at a higher rate compared to less abundant ones. Macromolecules with a higher 
binding affinity will later substitute the initially adsorbed macromolecules, as they come into 
contact with NPs. Macromolecules that have a low affinity can also attach onto the already 
adsorbed molecules on the surface of the NP (Monopoli et al., 2011).  
The formation of a corona and eco-corona is analogous in certain aspects. However, the 
composition and concentration of macromolecules are different in these two settings, which may 
influence the behavior and alterations of NPs in different ways, such as aggregation, accumulation, 
bioavailability and toxicity (Lynch et al., 2014 ; Rahman et al., 2013). A corona usually consists of 
various proteins, lipids and sugars in physiological environments, such as blood, cell cytoplasm and 
interstitial fluid (Rahman et al., 2013). However, in the environment an eco-corona typically 
consists of NOM, which amongst others include humic substances and exudates (Lynch et al., 
2014).  
The corona structure is typically composed of an inner and outer layer, which is most commonly 
known as the hard and soft corona as illustrated in figure 2-2 (Casals et al., 2010; Walczyk et al., 
2010). The hard corona is a layer that is tightly bound to the surface of NPs due to its very slow 
exchange rate with other macromolecules (Walczyk et al., 2010). On the contrary, the soft corona 
is loosely connected and has a rapid exchange with free macromolecules (Casals et al., 2010).  
Previous studies have reported that that only 50-120 proteins were identified in the hard corona, 
when performing experiments in human plasma (Monopoli et al., 2011 ; Tenzer et al., 2011 ; 
Martel et al., 2011), which contains over 3700 different types of protein (Pieper et al., 2003). At 
the same time, it was discovered that most of these proteins are not the most abundant ones in 
the plasma nor have the highest affinity to bind on NPs surface. This suggests that only certain 
types of protein are able to constitute the hard corona.

2.3 
Parameters that Influence the Corona Formation 
A variety of different parameters influence the corona formation (Rahman et al., 2013 ; Gao et 
al., 2012). For instance, Walkey and Chan (2012) compiled a list of 26 studies regarding the 
composition of plasma protein coronas for 63 different nanomaterials. They learned that the 
corona composition is complex and unique to each type of nanomaterial, even though the 
particles were incubated in the same biological medium. Table 2-1  provides an overview of 
parameters regarding the interaction between NPs, medium and macromolecules, which may 
influence the corona formation.

Parameters related to NPs 
Surface charge 
Determines the force of repulsion/attraction between NPs and 
macromolecules. A high attraction force results in a strong 
interaction between the two (Lynch et al., 2014).  
Hydrophilicity/Hydrophobicity 
Hydrophobic surfaces tend to increase the adsorption of proteins in 
comparison with hydrophilic surfaces (Cedervall et al., 2007; 
Lindman et al., 2007). For example, albumin adsorb onto 
hydrophobic NPs at a higher degree, even if the affinity for both 
types of particles are the same (Lindman et al., 2007). 
NP element type 
A study by Deng et al. (2009) indicated that NPs of different 
elements resulted in different corona compositions, despite having 
a similar surface charge. 
Size 
Studies have suggested that adsorption is greatest on small NPs due 
to their large surface area (Dobrovolskaia et al., 2009 ; Kennedy et 
al., 2012). 
Curvature 
If the surface is highly curved, adsorbed proteins tend to have fewer 
structural changes and interaction between proteins is reduced 
(Rahman et al., 2013). 
Surface coating 
NPs can be modified with functional groups or coating to prevent 
adsorption of macromolecules and to regulate the corona 
composition (Foroozandeh & Aziz, 2015). For example, polyethylene 
glycol can be used as a capping agent to decrease the adsorption of 
proteins (Gref et al., 2000). 
Parameters related to Macromolecules 
Size and flexibility 
Adsorption of larger and flexible proteins is stronger compared with 
smaller and rigid ones (Lynch et al., 2014).  
Concentration 
An increase in concentration promotes adsorption up until a certain 
point, untill the NP surface gets saturated (Yang et al., 2009).   
Parameters related to medium 
pH 
In case of proteins, the adsorption is strongest at its isoeletric point, 
as it has no electrical charge (Lynch et al., 2014).  
Ionic strength 
A strong ionic strength tends to boost hydrophobic interactions, 
which may increase the adsorption of proteins (Lynch et al., 2014).

2.4 
Silver Nanoparticles in Aquatic Environments 
The chosen test material in this project is Ag NPs due to its widespread use and potent toxicity 
towards aquatic organisms, including algae. A study material, AgNO3, was used as a basis of 
comparison between the toxicity of dissolved silver and Ag NPs under the influence of exudates. In 
this relation, information about the applicability, toxicity and fate of Ag NPs and dissolved silver 
are described in the upcoming sections.  
 
2.4.1    Applicability and Properties of Silver Nanoparticles 
 
Ag NPs are associated with a wide variety of consumer products, such as cloths, cosmetics, food 
containers, food supplements and home appliances due to its bactericidal property (Navarro et al., 
2008a ; Vance et al., 2015). According to various sources (Vance et al., 2015; Woodrow Wilson 
Database, 2009), Ag NPs are the most frequently used nanomaterial in consumer products but not 
in quantity. The production cost of these particles is also relatively low (Capek, 2004), which 
increases their popularity and market value (Fabrega et al., 2011). 
Ag NPs has many unique physiochemical properties, such as electrical and thermal conductivity, 
non-linear optical behavior, stability and catalytic activity that make them relevant within different 
industries (Capek, 2004). Additionally, Ag NPs have a significant bactericidal potential, which 
makes them attractive in a wide variety of consumer materials, as previously mentioned (Capek, 
2004). However, a study by Xiu et al. (2012) states that the bactericidal effects is not associated 
with the particle itself, but the release of Ag+ ions under oxidative conditions that contributes to 
the bactericidal effect.  
 
2.4.2   Release of Silver Nanoparticles in Aquatic Environments 
 
The annual European production of Ag NPs is estimated at maximum 10 tons/year (Piccino et al., 
2012), which is expected to increase in the years to come (Gomes et al., 2015). The particles may 
reach the environment through various exposure routes: during manufacturing, product usage, 
recycling and disposal of products containing Ag NPs (Köhler et al., 2008). The majority of released 
Ag NPs may enter a sewage treatment plant (Blaser et al., 2008) where they are incorporated into 
sludge that later is used as fertilizers in agricultural fields (del Real et al., 2016). This underlines the 
importance of identifying exposure routes to determine the release of Ag NPs into the 
environment.  
 
Estimation of Ag NP concentrations in environmental compartments has been attempted through 
modeling (Gottschalk et al., 2013), whereas little experimental data has been obtained (Fabrega et 
al., 2011). A study by Gottschalk et al. (2013) compared measured and modeled data from various 
studies, which suggested a concentration in the range of 7 ∙ 10-3 – 3 ∙ 10-3 µg/L and 10-5 – 4 ∙ 10-1 
µg/L in surface waters, respectively. Additionally, the release of Ag NPs may increase substantially 
due to its wide use in consumer products, such as socks (Benn & Westerhoff, 2008), toothbrushes

(Mackevica et al., 2015) and electronics circuits (Gottschalk et al., 2013. 
 
2.4.3   Aquatic Fate and Toxicity of Silver Nanoparticles 
 
Ag NPs usually consists of a core of silver (Ag0) that is covered by an organic surface coating (e.g. 
citrate), that is used to assure stability and avoid the formation of aggregates (Levard et al., 2012). 
Surface coating may also be used to control the shape and size of particles or reduce the release of 
Ag+ ions caused by oxidation, which may occur at room temperature (Fabrega et al., 2011 ; Levard 
et al., 2012 ; Li et al., 2010).  
 
Previous studies have investigated the toxicity of Ag NPs, which is presented in table 2-2. As seen, 
the effective concentration (EC50) of Ag NPs has a wide range which causes difficulties in 
comparison. The difference in observed toxicity  is most likely explained by differences in the 
experimental setup or particle properties. McLaughlin & Bonzongo (2012) underlined the 
importance of water chemistry when it comes to the biological effects of Ag NPs. For instance, 
media containing NOM may reduce the toxicity of the particles due to a potential coating. There is 
also a broad agreement that the size, coating, solubility and aggregation of Ag NPs have a 
significant influence on the toxicity (Ivask et al., 2014 ; Wang et al., 2012 ; Angel et al., 2013). It is 
also considered that the dissolution (release of Ag+ ions) of Ag NPs largely governs the toxicity 
(Wang et al., 2012 ; Ivask et al., 2014). 
According to Fabrega et al. (2011) two possible uptake processes of Ag NPs are: (1) endocytosis 
and (2) ion release: 
1. The principle of endocytosis is that uptake of Ag NPs occurs through the formations of 
vesicles that encloses the particles and is adsorbed by the cell.  
2. The release of free silver ions into a membrane can potentially occur if there is a

2.4.4   Toxicity of Ionic Silver to Aquatic Species 
 
Although silver is fairly harmless to humans, dissolved silver are considered highly toxic to 
organisms living in aquatic environments (Fabrega et al., 2011). Dissolved silver present itself as 
free ions that can bio concentrate within cells as uptake occurs through membrane ion transport 
(Luoma, 2008). The ionic form of silver is one of the most toxic metals towards aquatic organisms 
in controlled tests (Eisler, 1996). Formation of silver complexes may occur under the right 
conditions, which can affect the toxicity and bioavailability (Levard et al., 2012).  
Several studies have investigated the toxicity of dissolved silver on algae as presented in table 2-3. 
As seen, the effective Ag concentration varies between 1.3 – 21 µg/L, which makes it one of the 
three most toxic metals towards algae and invertebrates (Bryan, 1984).  Additionally, research 
made by Kennedy et al. (2010) backs up these claims and indicate that Daphnia are considerably 
more sensitive towards Ag+ compared to fish and algae.

Materials and Methods 
3.1 
Chemicals and Nanoparticles 
 
3,5-dichlorophenol (97 % purity) was purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Steinheim, Germany) 
and used as reference substance. 
 
Analytical grade AgNO3 was purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Steinheim, Germany) with 
purity above 99 %.   
 
Spherical citrate coated Ag NPs was purchased from Cline Scientific (Lot: 10173, Göteborg, 
Sweden) with a nominal size of 30 nm ± 2 nm in diameter. The particles were suspended 
in Milli-Q water at a concentration of 20 mg Ag/L and stored at 4 degrees in darkness.  
 
3.2 
Preparation of Stock Solutions/Suspensions 
In order to assure complete dissolution of stock solutions with 3,5-dichlorophenol and AgNO3, 
magnetic stirrer and sonication by a Bransonic© 3510 ultrasonic cleaner (Vlierberg, Netherlands) 
were applied. Only a magnetic stirrer was used for the stock containing Ag NPs to assure a 
homogenous dispersion. The procedure for each stock solution/suspension is described in the 
following bullet point: 
 
3,5-dichlorophenol: Stock solutions were prepared by dissolving 3,5-dichlorophenol 
directly in algal medium approximately one hour before initializing the test.  
 
Silver nitrate: Stock solution was prepared by initially dissolving AgNO3 in Milli-Q water, 
since it would precipitate if it was directly added in algae medium. A second stock solution 
was prepared by mixing a small volume of the Milli-Q stock solution with algal medium to 
obtain a suitable concentration and avoid precipitation.  
 
Silver nanoparticles: Stock suspensions were prepared by mixing the Ag NP suspension 
with algal medium.  
 
Fresh and Aged Stock Solutions 
Toxicity tests with AgNO3 and Ag NP were performed with either a fresh or aged stock solutions/ 
suspensions. This was done to assess the influence of a longer incubation period on the toxicity of 
both test materials. Aged stock solutions/suspensions were made 24 hours prior testing, whereas 
fresh ones were made 1 hour prior testing. An overview of the tests containing fresh or aged stock 
solutions/suspensions is presented in section 3.10, table 3-2. 
 
 
3.3 
Test Material Characterization 
This section describes the test material characterization methods used in this project. For more 
information regarding the principle behind each method, please refer to Appendix 3.

Dynamic Light Scattering 
Particle size and zeta potential was measured in a Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) device, Zetasizer 
Nano SZ, from Malvern instruments Ltd. (Worcestershire, UK). Three samples with an Ag NP 
concentration of 125 µg Ag/L in both standard and exudate medium was prepared. 1 mL of sample 
was transferred directly into a standard disposable cuvette or folded capillary cell for size and zeta 
potential measurements, respectively. Algae were not added to these samples as they would 
disturb the measurements. 
 
Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry 
Dissolved silver concentrations in all test suspensions/solutions for both Ag NPs and AgNO3 was 
measured through ICP-MS. Samples were prepared at the start and end of a toxicity test by mixing 
8.5 mL test suspension/solution with 1 mL concentrated nitric acid in plastic test tubes to dissolve 
the added silver. The samples were analyzed on an Optical Emission Spectrometer AvioTM 200 
(Perkin Elmer, Massachusetts, U.S.).  
 
Single Particle ICP-MS  
The particle size distribution and mass concentration of Ag NPs was analyzed by spICP-MS. Seven 
test concentrations with identical concentrations as used in the toxicity tests was prepared from a 
24 hour aged stock suspension. Subsequently, two toxicity tests were prepared – one with algae 
and one without – to assess if any measureable change in particle size or concentration occurs in 
the absence of algae. These samples were incubated under the same cultivation conditions as 
described in section 3.5. Additionally, the aged and a freshly made stock suspension were also 
analyzed to determine if any changes has occurred during the aging process. The samples 
containing algae was filtered and all test suspensions was diluted to 0.5 µg Ag/L before being 
analyzed by an ICP Mass Spectrometer NexIONTM 350D (Perkin Elmer, Massachusetts, U.S.). The 
samples were analyzed again after 48 hours by following the same procedure. 
 
Transmission Electron Microscopy  
Images of Ag NPs were obtained by TEM. A sample of 25 µL was directly transferred onto a small 
copper grid at the end of each Ag NP toxicity test. The grid was left out overnight to dry and 
covered with a petri dish to avoid dust particles to settle on its surface. The cobber grids were 
analyzed on a Tecnai T20G2 (FEI Company, Oregon, U.S.) with an operating voltage of 200 kV.  
 
Speciation Determination 
Speciation calculations were performed using an equilibrium speciation program called Visual 
MINTEQ (version 3.1). This was done to assess the speciation distribution and concentration of 
free silver in the toxicity test solution. Two simulations were made in both standard and exudate 
medium. All components from the algal medium were used as input together with the highest and 
lowest AgNO3 concentrations used in the tests. Calculations concerning Ag NPs were also made by 
adding a finite solid phase of silver corresponding to the highest and lowest Ag NP concentrations 
used in tests. The input is shown in Appendix 4. Dissolved organic carbon (DOC) was added in

3.5 
Cultivation of Algae 
Specific conditions that support algal growth were provided. Algal suspensions were placed on an 
orbital shake table (IKA® Schüttler MTS 4) set to 300 RPM with a room temperature of 20 °C and 
illuminated with white fluorescence light at 100 ± 10 µE/m2/S. The light intensity was measured 
with a LI-189 Quantum/Radiometer/Photometer (LI-COR, Nebraska, U.S.). 
The algae were cultivated in 20 mL scintillation vials (borosilicate glass). In order to acquire growth 
rates around 1.3 day-1, a pre-culture was made 2 or 3 days in advance of each test with a start cell 
density at 6 ∙ 104 cells/mL or 1.7 ∙ 104 cells/mL, respectively.  
The cell density was measured on a Beckman MultisizerTM 3 Coulter Counter (Indianapolis, U.S) by 
using Coulter® Isoton® II Diluent as diluent. A dilution factor of 90 or 99 was typically used, 
depending on the cell density. An average cell density was calculated based on three measured 
samples to minimize deviations.  
 
 
3.6 
Preparation of Exudate Medium 
The exudate medium was prepared by inoculating algae at a cell density of 1 ∙ 105 cells/mL in 
approximately 900 mL of standard algal medium. This solution was kept under similar conditions 
as described in section 3.5. The light intensity was set at 50 ± 10 µE/m2/S, a magnetic stirrer was 
used for mixing and aeration was achieved with an Eheim air pump 200 (Eheim, Germany). After 
two or three days, depending on the desired exudate concentration, the cell density was 
measured and the algae was removed by filtering the used medium through a glass fiber filter with 
a pore size of 1.2 µm (Advantec®, California, U.S.). In this process algae are discarded but 
produced exudates are maintained in the filtered medium. Subsequently, the nutrient solutions 
listed in table 3-1 were added to the filtered medium and pH was measured (required 8 ± 0.2) 
after 10 – 20 minutes of magnetic stirring. The exudate medium was stored frozen at -20 degrees.  
 
 
3.7 
Chemical Characterization of P. subcapitata Exudates 
The characterization of algal exudates was attempted by using colorimetric methods to determine 
the protein and carbohydrate content. Additionally, the non-volatile organic carbon (NVOC) was 
also determined. The methods are described in the upcoming sections.   
   
 
Non-Volatile Organic Carbon 
For the determination of NVOC a volume of 20 mL was collected of each batch of exudate medium 
and stored at 4 degrees in darkness. The NVOC content was measured on a Total Organic Carbon 
Analyzer TOC-V (Holm & Halby, Brøndby, Denmark) by adding phosphoric acid to the sample to 
obtain a pH at approximately 2-3. Nitrogen sparging is then applied on the sample to remove 
inorganic carbon in the form of carbon dioxide (CO2). Subsequently, sodium persulfate is added as 
==PAGE:26==
16 
 
an oxidation reagent. An infrared sensor measures CO2 that has been converted from remaining 
carbon in the sample, which is equivalent to the NVOC content (SHIMADZU, 2003). 
 
Protein Quantification 
The solubilized protein concentration in the exudate medium was quantified with a Bradford 
protein assay. Initially, a standard curve was created by preparing five test concentrations with 
bovine serum albumin (BSA) protein. In this case, the test concentrations used were 1.9, 3.8, 5.7, 
7.6 and 9.5 mg BSA/L. Subsequently, 0.8 mL of each test concentration was transferred to a test 
tube with 0.2 mL dye reagent. The test tubes were then thoroughly mixed by vortex and incubated 
for at least 5 minutes at room temperature. The exudate medium samples were prepared in the 
same manner. However, these samples were pre-concentrated with a factor of approximately 5. 
The pre-concentration was achieved by freeze-drying the samples in a vacuum to avoid protein 
degradation and bacterial growth. A Helios Epsilon 9423UVE1000E spectrophotometer (Thermo 
Spectronic, Massachusetts, U.S.) measured the absorbance at 595 nm. Based on these 
measurements a standard curve was generated and proteins in the exudate medium were 
quantified.  
The protein quantification development process is described in Appendix 1.  
 
Carbohydrate Quantification 
The carbohydrate content was estimated based on a method used by Dubois et al. (1956) and 
Stewart et al. (2013). A standard curve was created by preparing 8 test concentrations containing 
glucose: 20, 40, 60, 100, 140, 160, 200 and 400 µg glucose/mL. Subsequently, 0.5 mL of each test 
concentration was mixed with 0.5 mL phenol and 2.5 mL sulfuric acid by vortex. The acid and 
phenol reacts with sugars in the samples, giving an orange color to the solution. Subsequently, the 
samples had to cool for approximately 30 min before measuring the absorbance at 490 nm on a 
Helios Epsilon 9423UVE1000E spectrophotometer (Thermo Spectronic, Massachusetts, U.S.). 
Similarly to the protein quantification method, the standard curve was produced based on the 
absorbance measurements.  
The exudate medium was heated at 105 degrees in order to pre-concentrate the samples through 
evaporation. Two of the remaining batches (4 and 5, cf. table 4-4) were mixed together to obtain a 
sufficiently large volume. A pre-concentration factor of 91 was achieved. A quality control 
containing 0.5 mg glucose/L was also pre-concentrated in the same manner, in order to evaluate 
the recovery percentage of this method. Samples were mixed with phenol and acid, and 
absorbance was measured at 490 nm.  
The carbohydrate quantification development process is described in Appendix 2.

3.8 
48 Hours Growth Rate Inhibition Test 
This test protocol is performed according to ISO 8692 (2012), with some minor modifications. The 
standard test duration is 72 hours according to the ISO 8692, but in this project the duration is 
shortened to 48 hours. This was suggested by Arensberg et al. (1995) to avoid significant pH 
increase and lack of nutrients over time. Additionally, the start algal density has been increased 
from 104 to 105 cells/mL.  
An illustration is provided for the test procedure in figure 3-1. Typically, 6 to 7 test concentrations 
were prepared by mixing stock suspension, algae and medium together. A factor of 1.5 – 2 was 
applied between each test concentration and the concentration range for Ag NPs and AgNO3 was 
30 – 900 µg/L and 0.5 – 32 µg/L, respectively. The test concentrations and a control were 
distributed into 3 and 5 replicas, respectively, by transferring 4 mL suspension/solutions into 20 
mL open scintillation vials. The algae were cultivated for 48 hours under the same conditions as 
described in section 3.5.  
0.4 mL samples was taken from each replica at 0, 24 and 48 hours and mixed with 1.6 mL acetone 
for chlorophyll extraction. These samples were stored in complete darkness for at least 24 hours 
before measured on a Hitchi Fluorescence Spectrophotometer F-700 (Hitachi High-Technologies, 
Hitachinaka, Japan). Machine settings were set to 430 and 670 nm for excitation and emission 
wavelengths, respectively. A blank sample containing medium and acetone was also collected for 
background correction. Based on these measurements, the growth rate inhibition was estimated 
and used as endpoint.

Validity Criteria 
Two validity criteria had to be fulfilled for a test to be considered reliable (Kusk, 2012): 
1. The growth rate in the control replicas has to exceed 0.9 day-1, which resemble an 
increase by a factor of 6 in cell density after 48 hours. Typical growth rates are between 
1.5 and 1.9 d-1. 
2. The pH may not deviate more than 1.5 units from the initial pH in the control during tests.  
 
 
3.9 
2 Hour Carbon Assimilation Inhibition Test 
This test method was initially developed by Kusk & Nyholm (1992) and further modified by 
Sørensen & Baun et al. (2015).   
Preparation of test concentrations was performed in the same manner as described in the 48 
hours growth rate tests – identical concentration range, number of test solutions/suspensions and 
start algal density. In this case, 5 mL test solution and 50 µL 14C-bicarbonate isotope with a 
radioactivity of 20µCi/mL (DHI, Hørsholm, Denmark) was transferred to each replica in 20 mL 
scintillation vials. Immediately after adding the isotope, the samples were closed with an airtight 
lid and cultivated for 2 hours under the same conditions as described in section 3-5. After this 
period, the test were terminated by adding 200 µL HCL (10 %) pr. vial and left open overnight to 
assure complete evaporation of excess 14C that had not been incorporated in the algae. 
Subsequently, 10 mL Optiphase “Hisafe” 3 (Perkin Elmer, Massachusetts, U.S.) was added to each 
vial, which was mixed thoroughly and stored in the dark for a minimum of 8 hours. The isotope 
incorporated in the algae was analyzed by a Hidex 300S liquid scintillation counter (Hidex, Turku, 
Finland). Based on the liquid scintillation counting, the inhibition in carbon uptake was determined 
and used as endpoint.   
Six control replicas containing algae and medium, and three replicas only containing medium was 
also provided. The replicas only containing medium was used as background correction and 
validation that all added isotope was evaporated as transformed 14CO2.

4.3 
Reference toxicant test and validation criteria 
The purpose of a reference test is to determine the level of sensitivity of a specific test organism 
and evaluate the comparability with other studies. Identical test conditions and protocols have to 
be applied for any basis of comparison. If significant deviations are observed in obtained test 
results or if test validation criteria are unfulfilled, potential variabilities within test conditions has 
to be examined and corrected. 
==PAGE:36==
26 
 
The test was performed according to ISO 8692 (2012) with some minor modifications as 
mentioned in section 3.8. The chosen reference compound was 3,5-Dichlorophenol and five test 
concentrations with a range between 0.47 and 18 mg/L was included in the test. An EC50 value of 
3.8 mg/L [2.9 ; 4.8]95% was obtained with the resulting concentration-response curve shown in 
figure 4-2. ISO 8692 (2012) reported a mean EC50 value of 3.4 mg/L with a standard deviation of 
1.3 mg/L for the same compound, which is in agreement with the obtained value.   
Validation criteria were fulfilled with a mean growth rate above 0.9 d-1 and pH variations below 1.5 
units during the entire test period. This was the case for all toxicity experiments carried out and 
will therefore not be further mentioned in this report.

Discussion 
5.1 
Particle Characterization  
Particle size 
The Ag NP distribution determined by DLS suggested an average particle size of 30-35 nm, which 
remained stable in both medium types. This indicated that no substantial aggregation occurred 
during the toxicity tests. Since DLS measures the hydrodynamic diameter of the particles, it was 
expected to observe a slightly larger particle size than stated by the manufacture. Sørensen & 
Baun (2015) also reported a size of 38 nm by DLS for the same Ag NP (Cline Scientific, nominal 
particle size of 30 nm) suspended in ISO 8692 algal medium, which is consistent with the results 
obtained in this project. It should be noted, that DLS comes with its own limitations; poor 
detection at high or low particle concentrations, unable to detect specific materials and incapable 
of distinguishing between mixtures. For instance, the size estimation of a particle distribution with 
a primarily small size might get skewed towards a larger particle size by small quantities of larger 
aggregates. This might explain the slight increase in particle size over time in exudate medium, as 
the formation of a relatively small number of aggregates may have projected a larger particle size 
for both size peaks. Additionally, this method is incapable of measuring samples containing algae, 
which might had affected the particle size in other ways (e.g. aggregates or dissolution).  
The most frequent particle size measured by spICP-MS at t = 0 days was 37-40 nm, which is 
consistent with the DLS analysis. However, after 2 days the most frequent particle size had 
decreased substantially to around 27 – 34 nm. The reduction in size indicates that the particles 
had been partly dissolved during the test period, which also have been reported in previous 
studies (Lee et al., 2012 ; Liu et al., 2010). Only a slight difference (3 nm) was observed in particle 
size between samples with/without algae. There was no indication of the formation of aggregates, 
which is consistent with the DLS analysis. The advantages by using ICP-MS are its ability to detect a 
specific type of element/particle, as other irrelevant particles are ignored (Poda et al., 2011), such 
as macromolecules, dust particles or algae remains. However, a perfect spherical particle is 
assumed for all particles, which is not the case according to the TEM images.  
The TEM images showed a number of particles that may have been Ag NPs as their diameter were 
around 30 nm. The majority of these particles were around 30 nm, whereas others were 
substantially smaller and bigger. Additionally, what appeared to be an algal with Ag NPs around its 
surface was observed on one of the images. However, it is not known whether if these particles 
adsorbed onto the algal during the test or on the grid after the sample had evaporated. In order to 
confirm that particles in the images are Ag NPs other methods have to be applied, such as energy-
dispersive X-ray spectrometry to determine the elemental composition (Hodoroaba et al., 2016). 
Unfortunately, a sample of the batch suspension provided by Cline Scientific was not prepared, 
which would have provided for a good basis of comparison with the TEM images. According to 
Klein et al. (2011) the most reliable method to determine size and size distribution is TEM, as many 
other techniques (e.g. DLS) have limitations in their ability to estimate particle size. However, TEM 
==PAGE:45==
35 
 
comes with its own set of limitations. For instance, a very small proportion of the total particles 
are being observed, which may not represent the size distribution of the entire sample. 
 
Zeta potential 
According to Malvern Instruments (2004), suspensions that have a zeta potential below -30 or 
above +30 mV are considered stable. A similar zeta potential of -26 mV (SD = 4.5 mV) and -24 mV 
(SD = 3.4 mV) was determined in the freshly made suspensions with standard and exudate 
medium, respectively. After two days the mean zeta potential had slightly increased to -19 mV (SD 
= 1.4) and 22 mV (SD = 5.0) in the two media types, respectively. However, the suspension may be 
considered stable during the test period when taking the SD into consideration. This is potentially 
due to the stabilizing effect of the citrate coating (Tolaymat et al., 2010) or adsorption of exudates, 
which may prolong the stability of the suspended particles. A study by Sørensen & Baun (2015) 
reported a similar zeta potential for the same type of Ag NP (Cline Scientific, nominal particle size 
of 30 nm) in ISO 8692 algal medium. 
 
 
5.2 
Chemical Analysis of Silver Concentrations 
Similar to this project, previous studies have reported challenges regarding the quantification of 
dissolved silver and Ag NPs in relation to algal toxicity tests. A study by Sekine et al. (2015) 
reported that large quantities of dissolved silver adsorbed onto borosilicate glass container 
surfaces. Additionally, tests exposed to light, reduced the dissolved silver concentration to a 
greater extent, which is most like due to photo-reducing process on the surface of the container 
(Sekine et al., 2015). 
In this project, the proportion of adsorbed silver clearly increased in relation with longer 
incubation periods and the toxicity was mitigated over time. Additionally, borosilicate glass 
containers were used for cultivation of algae during toxicity tests. This suggests that adsorption 
onto the glass containers occurred as suggested by Sekine et al. (2015). This is consistent with the 
ICP-MS analysis in this project, as the recovery of dissolved silver in the test solution is reduced 
over time. However, this does not explain the low recovery of dissolved silver obtained at t = 0 
days, especially in the highest test concentrations.  
Basic test parameters such as the geometric design of containers and the speed of the orbital 
shaker may also increase the adsorption of dissolved silver or Ag NPs, since the rotation speed 
determines the surface area that comes into contact with the test solution. The adsorption of 
dissolved silver or Ag NPs onto glass surfaces will reduce the concentration in suspension/solution 
during tests. This will most likely cause an underestimation in toxicity towards algae as suggested 
by Sekine et al. (2015).  
Sekine et al. (2015) also reported a significant adsorption of Ag NPs onto container surfaces, which 
partly depended on the surface coating of the Ag NPs. In this project, the spICP-MS analysis 
suggested that all Ag NP concentrations had been significantly underestimated in all tests 
==PAGE:46==
36 
 
performed. This was due to the batch suspension provided by Cline Scientific not being treated 
with sonication, as 90 % and 8 % of the nominal concentration was recovered with and without 
sonication, respectively. Thus, the actual concentration of Ag NPs in all toxicity tests was most 
likely around 7-15 % of the nominal concentration, as all stock suspensions were prepared in the 
same way (without sonication). This was also suggested by the algal toxicity test that the spICP-MS 
measurements had been performed on, since a similar toxicity (EC50 = 230 [210 ; 260]95%) 
compared with other aged tests during the project was observed. Thus, these findings indicate 
that the concentration of Ag NPs was similar in all performed toxicity tests. Unfortunately the 
spICP-MS analysis was performed at the end of the project. It was therefore not possible to 
remake the toxicity tests by applying sonication to the batch container. Thus, the only option was 
to use the nominal concentration as an indicator of toxicity.   
 
5.3      Speciation Determination 
The Visual MINTEQ model predicted that mainly two silver complexions (Ag+ and AgCl) would form 
in both types of media. Interestingly, only a very small proportion (≈0.4 %) of the total Ag+ 
concentration was estimated to form complexes with DOC in the exudate medium.  This could be 
linked with the incorporated stability constants in MINTEQ. In this case, DOC would mainly form 
chemical complexes with other elements, if the stability constant was significantly lower for 
complexes containing Ag+ and DOC. This might be the case, since the total concentration of 
complexes consisting of DOC and NH4
+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe3+ and Mn2+ was substantially higher 
compared with Ag+. Combined with the fact, that most of these components have a significantly 
higher concentration in the solution compared to Ag+, a small proportion of complexes consisting 
of Ag+ and DOC were expected.  
The results regarding silver precipitation and speciation distribution might not be representable 
for the performed tests, since Visual MINTEQ assumes that the system is in equilibrium, which is 
unknown in our case. Additionally, obvious difference between a bulk silver (as estimated by the 
program) and Ag NPs exists. For instance increased surface area, coating, physical and chemical 
properties has to be taken into consideration when analyzing the results. This is also the case for 
the predefined DOC by MINTEQ, which most likely is incomparable to the chemical composition of 
algal exudates. The calculations suggested a similar silver species distribution with and without 
exudates, but the toxicity results clearly indicate that the bioavailability of dissolved Ag+ and/or Ag 
NPs are significantly reduced in the presence of exudates. For instance, it is previously suggested 
that algae reduce the bioavailability of toxicants by producing exudates that forms harmless 
complexes with toxic substances (Marsálek et al., 1996 ; Mio et al., 2009). Thus, Visual MINTEQ 
has several limitations within the program that causes unreliability regarding the speciation 
determinations. This is due to the theoretical approach of assessing the complexity of each 
chemical component and approximation of important parameters (e.g. DOC composition and 
stability constants).   
Silver species calculations were also calculated by Fabianne et al. (2015) in a different algal media 
with Visual MINTEQ. Similarly to this project, the authors found that chloride is the most dominant 
==PAGE:47==
37 
 
ligand at low dissolved silver concentrations. Additionally, the unknown composition of algal 
exudates made it challenging to predict its interaction with dissolved silver.  
 
5.4      Methods for Chemical Characterization of Algal Exudates 
For the characterization of exudates a number of indirect methods were tested in this project. The 
NVOC concentration was clearly proportional to the cell density, except in one case (batch 5) 
which had a significantly lower concentration than expected. Similar results were obtained by 
Koukal et al. (2007) who also showed a correlation between the exudate concentration and algal 
density. Koukal et al. (2007) reported that P. subcapitata exudates mainly consisted of 
carbohydrates (30 % of DOC), whereas the protein content was substantially lower (5 % of DOC). 
Koukal et al. suggested that three main factors influence the production of algal exudates; cell 
density, media composition and algal species. Additionally, Marsálek et al. (1996) proposed that 
stress caused by toxicants significantly increased the production of exudates in some cases. This 
indicates an increase in exudate production during toxicity tests.  
The protein concentration could not be determined for any of the batches, which indicated a 
concentration below detection limit for the applied method. The detection range of the protein 
assay is between 1.2 to 10 mg/L, which lies above the NVOC concentration. Based on this, it is 
assessed that the protein concentration is substantially lower than 1.2 mg/L, as a pre-
concentration factor of 5 was insufficient to detect any proteins in the samples. Koukal et al. 
(2007) detected a protein concentration of 0.12 mg/L by pre-concentrating the samples 7.5 times. 
However, a different colorimetric protein characterization method was applied. It must also be 
considered that proteins degrade at relative low temperatures. Thus, repeated freezing and 
thawing of the exudates may have affected the protein concentration in this project. 
The carbohydrate concentration was measured after pre-concentrating the sample by a factor of 
89. According to Dubois et al. (1956) the applied method worked with samples containing 
approximately 10 – 80 micrograms of carbohydrates. The absorbance measurements in this 
project indicated a high carbohydrate concentration of 1 mg/L in the exudate medium sample. 
This was higher than the NVOC content in the specific sample (0.90 mg NVOC/L), which is 
unrealistically high. Additionally, an overestimation by a factor of 3 was observed for the glucose 
concentration in the quality control. Assuming a similar fraction (30 %) of carbohydrates compared 
to NVOC as reported by Koukal et al. (2007), a concentration of 0.3 mg/L would have been 
expected. A similar value (0.33 mg/L) is actually acquired if taking the overestimation of the quality 
control into account. However, it must be assessed that the method was insufficient to determine 
the carbohydrate content of the exudates.

5.5      Influence of Exudates on Silver Compounds Toxicity 
 
Influence of Exposure Duration on the Toxicity of Ag NP and AgNO3 
The tests used in this project had different exposure durations: 2 and 48 hours. The shortened 
exposure (2 hours) can be useful to provide more stable exposure conditions during the tests with 
fresh stock solutions, since there is less time for aggregation, dissolution and sedimentation as 
suggested by Sørensen & Baun (2015). However, 2 hours test with Ag NPs were only performed 
with aged stock solutions to assure stable exposure conditions as recommended by Sørensen & 
Baun (2015). In this regard, processes such as dissolution and aggregation might have already 
occurred during the aging period. However, if the dissolution process continued after the aging 
period, the toxicity would have been greater in the 48 hours tests as more ions would have been 
released from Ag NPs in this time interval. The similarities in toxicity between the 2 and 48 hours 
tests indicated the dissolution of Ag NPs reached equilibrium during the aging process.  
The exudate concentration during tests in standard medium would remain very low in the 2 hour 
test, whereas a significantly higher concentration is expected to occur in the 48 hours test, as 
more exudates are produced over time by the algae. Thus, the 2 hour tests provided controlled 
conditions regarding the exudate concentration in toxicity tests with standard medium. 
Interestingly, the toxicity was similar in both exposure durations, which suggested that the 
exudates produced during tests did not influence the toxicity of Ag NPs significantly. 
For the 2 hour tests with AgNO3 only fresh stock solution was used. In this case, there would be 
less time for Ag+ to interact with exudates or medium compositions to form complexes. This is also 
reflected in the results, as the toxicity is similar to the 48 hours test in standard medium (fresh 
stock). This indicates that the toxicity of AgNO3 is not immediately influenced by algal exudates, 
which could not have been assessed by only performing a 48 hours test. On the contrary, without 
the 48 hours tests it would not have been possible to assess that a reduction in toxicity of AgNO3 
was induced by algal exudates. These findings suggest that the exposure duration is an important 
parameter to consider in ecotoxicity tests, as information regarding the dynamic changes in 
toxicity is obtained by adjusting this duration.  
 
 
Influence of Varying Exudate Concentrations on the Toxicity of Ag NP and Ag NO3 
The toxicity of Ag NPs and AgNO3 will be discussed in relation to the influence of varying exudate 
concentration (0.16 – 1.1 mg NVOC/L) in this section.  
In case of Ag NPs, the results do not indicate that the toxicity was significantly influenced with 
varying exudate concentration. This was the case of both types of test performed with Ag NPs 
(aged stock suspension and aged individual test suspensions). This indicates that aging Ag NPs in a 
suspension with 0.16 mg NVOC/L or 1.1 mg NVOC/L induces a similar toxicity. 
Assuming exudates coat the surface of Ag NPs, a full reduction in toxicity would imply that the 
entire surface area of the Ag NPs had been covered during tests, as suggested by Kennedy et al. 
==PAGE:49==
39 
 
(2012). Thus, the lowest exudate concentration (0.16 mg NVOC/L) may have been sufficient to 
coat the entire surface area of the Ag NPs in this project. However, the exudate concentration 
range is substantially lower compared with the concentration ranges used in previous studies 
(Kennedy et al., 2012 ; Cupi et al., 2014 ; Gao et al., 2012). For instance, Kennedy et al. (2012) 
suggested a DOC concentration of 4 mg/L was inadequate to coat the entire surface area of Ag 
NPs (4.5 mg Ag/L).  Furthermore, these previous studies showed that the toxicity of Ag NPs 
towards different test organisms decreased with increasing DOC/NOM concentration. Thus, it is 
possible that the toxicity was not significantly influenced in this project due to the considerably 
low concentration range of exudates (0.16 – 1.1 mg NVOC/L). To further investigate the influence 
of varying exudate concentrations on the toxicity of Ag NPs a wider exudate range is 
recommended.  
The results of AgNO3
 showed no statistical difference in toxicity between all test solutions. This is 
potentially due to the large discrepancies observed in relative inhibition within each test solution. 
However, it also indicates that the medium components alone are sufficiently reducing the toxicity 
by forming silver complexes in the aging process. This was also reaffirmed by the 48 hours tests 
with AgNO3 in aged stock solutions. If the test was performed with a fresh stock solution, the 
toxicity would potentially decrease proportionally with increasing exudate concentration, as the 
toxicity of AgNO3 is mitigated faster in the presence of exudates. 
       
Influence of Fresh Stocks with Exudates on the Toxicity of Ag NP and AgNO3 
For the fresh stock solutions with AgNO3, a similar level in toxicity was observed after 2 and 48 
hours in standard medium. The 2 hour AgNO3 test showed that the algal toxicity of dissolved silver 
occurred rapidly, as the EC50 values was within the same range compared to 48 hours test in 
standard medium. The difference in toxicity observed between 2 hour and 48 hour tests in fresh 
stock solution with exudate medium, clearly indicated that algal exudates reduced the toxicity of 
AgNO3 over time.  
Rapid toxicity of dissolved silver has previously been proven in algal tests with similar results as 
obtained in this project after 2 and 48 hours of exposure (Navarro et al., 2008a ; Sørensen & Baun 
2015). Slight deviations in the EC50 values are probably caused by minor differences in the test 
method, such as media composition and cultivation condition.  Kennedy et al. (2012) also reported 
a reduction in AgNO3 toxicity towards Ceriodaphnia dubia (water flea) caused by DOC after 48 
hours. This suggests that the bioavailability of Ag+ ions is reduced by the presence of algal 
exudates during toxicity tests, which mitigates the toxicity.   
Dissolved Ag+ is considered the dominating species that causes toxicity towards algae (Kennedy et 
al., 2010 ;  Wang et al., 2012 ; Ivask et al., 2014) and the uptake is highly dependent on the media 
composition (Lee et al., 2005). The reduction in toxicity observed for AgNO3 in the 48 hours test 
(fresh stock) indicate that algal exudates serves as ligands to dissolved silver and thereby 
decreases the concentration of Ag+ ions. Similar results have been observed by Koukal et al. 
(2005), who reported that P. subcapitata exudates clearly reduced the toxicity of other types 
dissolved metals (Cu, Pb, Cd and Zn). This was due to the formation of complexes containing 
==PAGE:50==
40 
 
dissolved metals and exudates, which reduces the bioavailability of toxic free ion metals. The 
exudates may also inhibit the uptake of dissolved metals by adsorbing onto the exterior of the 
algae (Koukal et al., 2005). This suggests that similar processes occurred during the toxicity tests in 
this project. 
In case of the 48 hours test with Ag NPs, the toxicity was not influenced significantly between the 
two media types. In general it was challenging to achieve results with good reproducibility in tests 
with freshly prepared Ag NPs suspensions. This was due to large variations in relative inhibition 
within each test suspension. This indicated that the exposure conditions in the suspension were 
unstable as suggested by Sørensen & Baun (2015). The spICP-MS analysis indicated that 
dissolution of Ag NPs occurred during tests, which potentially caused unstable conditions in the 
fresh stock suspensions. Sørensen & Baun (2015) reported an 48 hour EC50 value (freshly prepared 
stock) of 75 µg Ag/L [63 ; 90]95% towards P. subcapitata, with the same Ag NPs (Cline Scientific, 30 
nm) and test protocol. The significant difference in toxicity is probably due to the age of the Ag NP 
batch or storage conditions as suggested by Kittler et al. (2010).  
 
Influence of aged stocks with exudates on the toxicity of Ag NP and AgNO3 
For AgNO3, the results of the 48 hours tests suggest that the toxicity is influenced by both the 
aging process and algal exudates. In this case, tests with aged stocks in both standard and exudate 
medium reduced the toxicity of AgNO3 by a factor of 2 compared to tests with fresh stock solution 
in standard medium. Interestingly, the tests with aged stock solutions in exudate and standard 
medium showed no statistical significant difference in toxicity after 48 hours. This indicates that 
equilibrium in both media types was reached, as Ag+ forms complexes with molecules from the 
medium composition and exudates. Additionally, the results indicate that the mitigation AgNO3 
toxicity occurs faster in the presence of exudates, as seen in the 48 hours test with fresh stocks. 
This suggests that the concentration of accessible silver ligands is highest in exudate medium. A 
study by Cupi et al. (2015) also reported a reduction in the toxicity of AgNO3 by aging the stock 
solution.  
For Ag NPs, a reduction in toxicity by a factor of 2.5 was observed in exudate medium compared 
with standard medium. Additionally, an increase in toxicity was observed in standard medium 
compared in tests with fresh stocks. This indicates that Ag NPs released substantially more free 
silver ions due to dissolution in the aging process. Additionally, the reproducibility of the 48 hours 
tests were improved by aging the stock suspensions, which suggests that the exposure conditions 
had been stabilized. This was also suggested by the toxicity data, since no statistical difference in 
toxicity was observed between the 2 and 48 hours tests for each media type – even though the 
tests have different endpoints and exposure durations. This is consistent with Lee et al. (2012), 
who postulated that Ag+ released from Ag NPs should reach equilibrium inside 48 hours. A similar 
pattern was also reported by Sørensen & Baun, who observed an increase in Ag NP toxicity upon 
24 hours aging in standard algal medium. In this case, it was also suggested that the change in 
toxicity was caused by dissolution of Ag NPs. No visual sedimentation or detected aggregation 
occurred during tests, which also is in agreement with previous studies (Sørensen & Baun, 2015 ; 
Navarro et al., 2008a).  
==PAGE:51==
41 
 
Three possible scenarios may explain why exudates mitigate the toxicity of Ag NPs: 
1. Exudates coat the surface of Ag NPs, inhibiting dissolution and/or the release of free silver 
ions as suggested by Lee et al. (2012). If dissolution occurs, adsorbed exudates may 
quickly bind to released Ag+ ions. Bioavailability of both Ag NPs and Ag+ is reduced. 
2. Exudates do not coat the surface of Ag NPs. The concentration of released ions is reduced 
significantly faster in exudate medium due to the formation of complexes consisting of 
Ag+ and exudates as suggested by Cupi et al. (2014). Bioavailability of Ag+ is reduced.  
3. Or a combination of scenario 1 and 2. Exudates partially coat the surface of Ag NPs, which 
inhibits the ion release to a lesser extent than if they were fully coated.  
In regards to the first scenario, several studies have postulated that extracellular proteins (Yue et 
al., 2017) or NOM (Gao et al., 2012 ; MchLaughlin & Bonzongo, 2012) can bind to the surface of  
Ag NPs, inhibiting the release of Ag+ to its surroundings. Ag NPs have also been postulated to 
adsorb onto the exterior of algae (Yue et al., 2017 ; Lee al., 2005 ; Navarro et al., 2008b), which 
suggests that a similar interaction may have occurred in this project. In this context, Ag NPs might 
transfer Ag+ ions into the surface layer of algae cells, as suggested by Fabrega et al. (2011), but this 
process might be inhibited due to the formation of an eco-corona. Considering the dissolution of 
Ag NPs and the reduced toxicity of AgNO3 induced by algal exudates in this project, one of these 
three scenarios are most like true. According to dos Santos et al. (2005), humic substances are 
more likely to form complexes with metal ions instead of interacting with metals, which suggests 
that the second or third scenario are most likely to have happened. However, direct evidence has 
to be provided in order to establish if eco-coronas are formed.  
The composition of an eco-corona might vary over time which can be explained by the Vroman 
effect. This effect describes change of the corona composition by taking incubation time, 
concentration and affinity for proteins into consideration. It describes that the amount of 
adsorbed protein may stay constant, whereas variation in the protein composition will change 
over time. The Vroman effect also explicates how proteins with low affinity and high abundance 
initially will adsorb to the NPs, where they later are replaced by proteins with a higher affinity 
(Vroman & Adams, 1986; Vroman et al., 1980; Foroozandeh & Aziz, 2015). Taking the Vroman 
effect into consideration, it is evident that several parameters influence the interacting between 
exudates and NPs. This is also reflected in the toxicity tests, since a change in toxicity was 
observed between the fresh and aged stocks with Ag NPs. In this project, it was evident that 
exudates decreased the toxicity of both Ag NPs and AgNO3. However, the toxicity is maybe altered 
in other ways at different time durations or exudates concentration as suggested by the Vroman 
effect. Additionally, Nasser & Lynch (2016) observed an increase in toxicity of polystyrene NPs due 
to adsorption of D. magna exudates, whereas the toxicity for Ag NPs in this project was reduced. 
This suggests that even the elemental composition of NPs or exudates have to be considered in 
regards to the toxicity of eco-coronas. Thus, a general conclusion regarding the influence of 
exudates on NP toxicity is not recommended.

6. Conclusion 
Overall, the results indicated that algal exudates influenced the toxicity of both AgNO3 and Ag NPs. 
For AgNO3, the results suggested that the toxicity was reduced faster by the presence of algal 
exudates, as more accessible silver ligands were present. In this case, the EC50 was reduced by a 
factor of 2 in 48 hour tests with exudate medium compared with tests in standard medium (fresh 
stocks). No difference in toxicity was observed in the 2 hours tests, which indicated that exudates 
require more time to form complexes with Ag+ ions. Additionally, tests with aged stocks suggested 
that equilibrium between Ag+, medium components and exudates was reached during this time 
period. 
The toxicity of Ag NPs was significantly reduced by a factor of 2.5 in both 2 and 48 hours tests by 
algal exudates (aged stocks). This indicated that the dissolution of Ag NPs had reached equilibrium 
with the medium during the aging process, as a similar toxicity was observed after both exposure 
durations. Test with Ag NPs in fresh stock suspensions did not show any change in toxicity due to 
unstable exposure condition. Thus, the toxicity of AgNO3 and Ag NPs was influenced similarly by 
exudates as the toxicity was reduced to almost the same extent. This indicates that mechanisms of 
toxicity are similar for Ag NPs and AgNO3 and it must therefore be assessed that Ag+ ions mainly 
governs the toxicity for both Ag NPs and AgNO3. It should also be noted that the toxicity of AgNO3 
was mitigated faster compared with Ag NPs. This is potentially due to the release of small 
quantities of Ag+ ions from Ag NPs during tests. In relation to these findings, it is important to 
underline the importance of having two test methods with different exposure durations, as the 
majority of these findings would not have been discovered if one of these methods had not been 
applied. Additionally, the aging of stock solutions clearly influenced the results, as dissolved silver 
and Ag NPs had more time to interact with medium components and exudates during this time 
period.  
The size distribution of Ag NPs varied based on the different characterization methods. None of 
the applied methods indicated that aggregates occurred during tests. According to DLS analysis 
the size of Ag NPs remained between 31-38 nm over 2 days and did not vary considerably in the 
two media types. Similarly, the spICP-MS analysis determined a most frequent particle size of 37 
nm at t = 0 days. However, this size decreased to approximately 30-32 nm after 2 days, which 
indicated the particles partly dissolved in the medium. Particles in the same size range were also 
observed in TEM images. Additionally, the zeta potential remained stable in the exudate (≈ -21 
mV) and standard medium (≈ -25 mV) over 48 hours.  
The determined mass concentration of Ag NPs in the toxicity tests was only 7 – 15 % of the 
nominal value. This was caused by a lack of sonication on the batch containers. Additionally, a low 
recovery of dissolved silver was also determined, which partly was due to adsorption onto glass 
surfaces. Thus, the actual exposure concentration of both dissolved silver and Ag NP was 
substantially lower than the nominal value during all performed toxicity tests. In this relation, the 
toxicity of both Ag NPs and AgNO3 was most likely underestimated, as the toxicity was based on 
the nominal value. 
==PAGE:53==
43 
 
The attempt to estimate protein content failed due to the detection limit of the applied methods. 
The estimated carbohydrate content was higher than the measured NVOC concentration for the 
sample, indicating a substantial overestimation, which is consistent with the estimated 
concentration in the quality control. Based on the results, the content of protein and 
carbohydrates were substantially lower than 1.1 mg/L, as this was the highest measured NVOC 
concentration. 
The speciation calculations suggested a similar species distribution in the standard and exudate 
medium, with Ag+ and AgCl as the most dominant species. Only a small fraction (0.4 %) of Ag+ was 
estimated to form complexes with exudates according to the Visuel MINTEQ calculations. 
However, the toxicity tests clearly indicated that a substantially higher fraction of Ag+ formed 
complexes with exudates.   
Overall, the results underline the importance of assessing the toxicity of NPs in relation to natural 
occurring macromolecules by using methods with different exposure durations. In this regard, the 
significance of using methods with different exposure durations and prepared stock suspensions 
are valuable to understand the processes NPs and exudates undergo during controlled tests. 
Further research concerning the interaction between Ag NPs, exudates and algae are still 
recommended, as it would provide information regarding the mechanisms of toxicity of Ag NPs.  
Additionally, many challenges arose during this project and a proposal for future research is 
therefore provided in the next section.

7. Future Perspectives 
The following areas for future research emerged from this project: 
- 
Characterization of Algal exudates: The methods used to determine the carbohydrate and 
protein content of the exudates was insufficient. The methods could be reapplied on 
substantially higher exudate concentrations by increasing the nutrient components in the 
medium, algae cultivation period or pre-concentrating factor. Another possible method is 
Lowrys protein assay, which have a protein detection range between 2 – 100 µg (Goldring, 
2012). Additionally, methods such as mass spectrometry  and high performance liquid 
chromatography could possibly be applied to determine the carbohydrate (Mutenda & 
Matthiesen, 2007 ; Bailey et al., 2013)  or protein quantification (Sandin et al., 2014 ; 
Nasser & Lynch, 2016 ; Grotefend et al., 2012) of algal exudates.  
 
- 
Interaction between Ag NPs, algae and exudates: Based on TEM it was not possible to 
determine whether or not if exudates adsorbed onto the surface of Ag NPs. In this case, 
adsorption studies could be performed similarly to Gao et al. (2012) or Nasser & Lynch 
(2016) to assess the adsorption and composition of an eco-corona. The potential 
interaction between Ag NPs and algae could be investigated in future studies to 
understand the toxicity mechanisms of the particles. A possible method is scanning 
electron microscopy, which has previously been used to show the adsorption of TiO2 NPs 
on P. subcapitata cells (Huan et al., 2005).  
 
- 
 Ag NP mass concentrations: The low recovery percentage of Ag NPs in the spICP-MS 
suggested that the actual concentration was substantially lower compared to the nominal 
concentration. A lack of sonication on stock containers was determined to be the cause of 
this problem, as approximately 90-100 % of the nominal concentration was recovered 
after applying sonication. Thus, sonication should be included in the preparation of 
toxicity tests and samples for spICP-MS to determine the actual concentration and toxicity 
of Ag NPs during tests. Potential differences in dissolution rates of Ag NPs in standard and 
exudate medium during the aging process should also be determined through spICP-MS.    
 
 
- 
Silver speciation: A silver ion selective electrode could be used to determine the dissolved 
Ag+ concentration for AgNO3 and Ag NPs during algal tests. In this context, the interaction 
between Ag+ and exudates could be assessed in algal tests with exudate medium.  
 
- 
Test materials: The ICP-MS analysis indicated that a substantial amount of dissolved silver 
adsorbed onto laboratory equipment over time, such as algae glass containers or 
measuring flasks. Materials with a low affinity for adsorbing dissolved silver and Ag NPs 
should be used to ensure more stable exposure concentrations during toxicity tests. For 
instance, Sekine et al. (2015) recommended polycarbonate as a suitable material for 
dissolved silver.

Appendix 3 – Test Material Characterization Methods 
 
DLS method – Particle size and Zeta Potential 
The particle size and zeta potential was measured by using a Dynamic Light Scattering device 
(Zetasizer Nano SZ) from Malvern Instruments. The theoretical principles behind the 
measurements will be described in this section.  
The Zetasizer Nano SZ determines the size of particles through the Brownian motion by using 
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS). Brownian motion is defined as the random movement of particles 
in a fluid resulting from the collision with atoms or molecules that surrounds them. The movement 
speed of suspended particles is used to determine their size. According to Malvern Instruments 
(2004) the particle size is defined as “the diameter of the sphere that diffuses at the same speed as 
the particle being measured”.  It is known that the movement speed of a particle in a liquid is 
dependent on its size; small particles moves faster compared to large particles. Thus, by taking 
two “snapshots” within a 100 µs interval it is possible to assess the positional change of a particle 
and determine the size based on that information. If the positional change is minimal the particle 
is large, whereas it is small if there has been significant movement. The “snapshots” are taken by 
illuminating the samples with laser. The light scattering caused by the particles is then analyzed by 
comparing the change in short time intervals. If the correlation between two signals persists for a 
significant time period, the particle is considered large, whereas if the correlation persists for a 
==PAGE:68==
58 
 
short time period, the particles are small. A simplistic illustration of this principle is shown in figure 
6. 
(Malvern Instruments, 2004) 
 
   
 
 
The zeta potential is a measure that determines if particles are likely to flocculate in a liquid 
suspension and is measured with a folded capillary cell with electrodes of opposite charge in both 
ends. Thus, particles will move to the electrode of opposite charge and the velocity is measured 
with a technique called Laser Doppler Velocimetry (LDV). LDV is used to determine the 
Electrophoretic Mobility, which is done by assessing the viscosity of the particles and applying an 
electrophoresis test on the sample. The zeta potential is directly calculated based on the 
Electrophoretic Mobility and the Henry equation. 
(Malvern Instruments, 2004) 
The zeta potential is a measure of electrostatic repulsion/attraction between particles and is key 
parameter to assess the particle stability. As shown in figure 7, the zeta potential is a measure of a 
boundary called the diffuse layer, where loosely attached ions follow the particle movement. If 
particles in a suspension have a zeta potential that is highly negatively or positively charged (+ 
30mV or - 30mV) they tend to repel each other and are therefore considered stable as they are 
unlikely to flocculate. However, particles with a zeta potential between - 30 to + 30 are considered 
unstable as the electrostatic repulsion is too weak avoid flocculation within this range.   
(Malvern Instruments, 2004)

ICP-MS 
This method is capable of detecting small concentrations in the ng/L range of dissolved metals 
(Pace et al., 2011). The measuring of dissolved elements is performed by transferring a colloid of 
fine liquid droplets (aerosols) to a plasma chamber, where they are ionized and desolvated. These 
ions are transmitted to a quadrupole where the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) is determined 
(Stephan & Neubaur, 2014). Based on this ratio an intensity reading is assigned, which correlates 
the concentration of dissolved metal (Pace et al., 2011). Continuous m/z measurement are 
performed in the quadrupole, where each measurement takes a certain amount of time, called 
“dwell time”. Between each m/z measurement the electronics has to stabilize, which are called 
“settling time” and a substantial amount of signals are therefore not measured (Stephan & 
Neubaur, 2014). Thus, an average of numerous intensity readings is used to estimate the 
concentration in traditional ICP-MS. Samples containing dissolved elements are generally 
homogenous and the measurement will therefore roughly estimate the same concentration of 
metal in each dwell time (Pace et al., 2011).  
 
Single Particle ICP-MS 
This method is capable of detecting single particles as small droplets are transferred through a 
plasma chamber. In this case, the particles are not necessarily dispersed homogenously in the 
sample. Thus, the flow of ions running through the instrument is not constant similarly to ICP-MS 
(Pace et al., 2011). The droplets get desolvated in the plasma in which every individual particle is 
ionized. It is assumed that each burst of ions intercepted by the instrument represents a single 
particle. Ions enter a quadrupole where the mass-to-charge ratio is determined for each burst 
(Stephan & Neubaur, 2014). Special settings regarding low particle concentration, short dwell time 
==PAGE:70==
60 
 
and constant flow rate has to be considered in order to assure that each ion burst is derived from 
a single particle. The measured pulses from bursts of ions are related to particle size and particle 
number concentrations through various equations (Pace et al., 2011).  
 
TEM 
TEM is a microscopy technique that works by transmitting beam of electrons through thin 
samples. The electrons may penetrate, scatter or absorb to the sample, depending on the 
thickness and composition in a specific sample area. An imaged is projected based on the collected 
electrons that penetrates the sample and magnified by an objective lens. This is done by 
comparing the energy from the electrons before and after interaction with the sample. Contrasts 
in an image will appear when the electrons are absorbed or scattered (energy loss). This process 
has to be performed under a vacuum, as electrons are easily disturbed by molecules in the air. 
(Tatlock, 2011)
