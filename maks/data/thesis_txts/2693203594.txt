Abstract
Particle image velocimetry (PIV) is used to investigate the
wave boundary layer and its flow structures. PIV enables
the measurement of the velocity very close to the bed and
these are used to calculate the bed shear stress. For the
laminar case this works well and for the turbulent case it
works well until the resolutions is too small for the data
points to be in the viscous sub­layer. The measurements
are held against the work of B. Lykke Jensen 1989 and
validates the PIV method and the background analysis for
Re < 590.000 The method is also used for visualization of
the flow structures and vortex tubes are directly observed
from vector fields. Turbulent spots are observed from bed
shear stress time series.

1
Introduction
The aim of this study is to investigate the measurement technique PIV (particle image
velocimetry) and use it to describe the different flow features of the wave boundary layer.
The PIV measurement technique will allow a deeper understanding of the different flow
structures as they are, with other techniques, either recorded or visualized. With PIV it
is possible to do both of these at the same time and it permits further investigation of
the regimes and ranges in which each phenomenons occur.
The phenomenons are vortex tubes, turbulent spots and transitional phenomenons
around flow reversal. These phenomenons are directly visible from the PIV images
and in time quantifiable by their frequency of occurrence, size, shape and impact to the
bed.
The PIV method will also be validated against already proven methods for the wave
boundary layer. This means that the results from Carstensen 2006 and B. Lykke Jensen
1989 are critical as a foundation for this investigation.
The PIV method could further improve the understanding of the wave boundary layers
and boundary layers in general. However before it is a valid method for calculations it
needs to be documented well on its advantages as well as its limitations.
The work is not done with this report as the work load is very big. The amount of data
from PIV is so large that robust and efficient methods to analyse it needs to be made.
This report is a step in the direction of exploring the methods.
The thesis is written as an experimental report. First it covers the necessary background
to evaluate the results. Then it has an extensive methodology section needed to be
able to reproduce the data. The results are presented by themselves and discussed
separately.

2.1.2
The free stream region

to as the maximum value of the free stream velocity, hence the speed. In relation to
waves this is important, as U0m is a function of the phase ωt, see Figure 2.2. The flow
undergoes two stages. An acceleration stage where the flow gains move speed and a
deceleration phase where the flow slows down until eventually it reverse.

2.2
Laminar to turbulent
The wave boundary layer remains laminar until at critical Reynolds number is met at
around Recr = 150.000. When turbulence starts to occur it is first seen as very small
patches emerging from the bottom. This occur at the earliest at ωt = 30◦. They grow in
number, size and intensity as the flow develops, until they merge and eventually form a
fully developed boundary layer.

Vortex tubes
Another structure is also present in the flow. They are called vortex tubes and as the
name suggest a tube. They exist transverse to the flow direction and are seen as a
two­dimensional vortex extending across the flow. They are caused by an inflectional­
point shear­layer instability and can thus be triggered to appear. The vortex tubes are a
phenomenon of the transitional regime and emerges at the earliest at Re = 70.000 and
are not observed after 300.0005. The presence of these structures are a series of small,
almost indistinguishable from the noise, spikes in the bed shear stress. However, since
this flow structure is present as a 2D­structure, the PIV method can identify them.

The experimental setup operates in a wave flume, see Figure 3.4. The water is put
in motion by a piston and on the center of the flume is the test section, from where

A laser illuminates particles in the flow and a camera captures still images with short

3.2
PIV
Particle image velocimetry is a non­intrusive test method to quantify the velocity of the
flow field. The method uses a form of seeding submerged in the water column, see sub­
section 3.2.1 for more details, and measures the displacement of the individual particle
over a known time to calculate the velocity. Hence given is
Velocity [m/s] = Displacement [m]
Time [s]
(3.1)
Since the method builds on this simple relationship, a lot of effort needs to be put into
making sure the displacement and the time is correct. The time measurement is done
by the Synchronizer which in short is a device that synchronize the laser and the camera
to certain fix points where measurements are recorded. The displacement is directly de­
pendent on the seeding and its interaction with the fluid it is submerged in. The reason
for choosing the exact seeding is outlined in subsection 3.2.1. To briefly introduce the
major key points, the seeding must follow along the flow and being fairly evenly repre­
sented on the images. The first point means the density must match the density of water
and the second point means an appropriate size is chosen accordingly to image reso­
lution, light­source intensity and light scattering. The second points means the seeding
shouldn’t vary in size and shape across the flow and in time.
To be able to quantify the velocity better the PIV algorithm uses a so­called interrogation
window, in which it tries to identify the same pattern of particle between each image to
determine the bulk displacement of that section. Since the particles are all alike this is
necessary. The seeding level determines the limit of the interrogation window size. A
rule of thumb is at least 10 independent particles per interrogation window. With a high
seeding­density (amount of particles pr. ares) an interrogation window of 32x32 pixels is
sufficient for good results. This small size is important since the velocities within y+ < 5
and are need to determine the bed shear stress. It should be noted that since y+ is a
proportional to the bed shear stress and hence the velocities at the bed, the higher the
Reynolds number, the smaller y+ > 5 becomes pixel wise.
3.2.1
Seeding
The seeding used is S­HGS Silver Coated Hollow Glass Spheres with a diameter of
dp = 10µm. The seeding itself has a smaller diameter than a single pixel on the camera
with the chosen test setup. However, the light scattering of the Glass spheres is very
good, especially when the light diameter of the seeding is larger than the wavelength of
the laser, see Dynamics 2005, ch. 3, p. 5.
dp > λ
(3.2)
This results in the chosen seeding being around 2­3 pixels wide and tall with a distinct
perimeter.
The flow is seeded through a tube on the lid in the test section directly into the flow.
Before measurements a waiting period of 5­15 minutes depending on the flow velocity

is necessary for an even distribution of the seeding. The seeding is over time dissipated
by the wave throughout the whole water body and after ~ 1 hour of non stop wave action
the flow needs to be reseeded.
3.2.2
Interrogation area
The Interrogation area is the area in which the PIV algorithm evaluates the displacement
of the particles. Down through the water column different sizes are needed. The free
stream velocity is almost constant for water levels in the free stream region. However
in the logarithmic layer and closer to the bottom a much closer spacing is needed to
quantify the velocity profile and hence the stresses. Therefore the Dantec Danymics
build­in­function ”Adaptive PIV” is used. This does allow a different size for each In­
terrogation area and instead imposes a threshold requirement related to the good PIV
parameters. The Interrogation area side lengths are varied between 32 pixel and 196
pixel and does not necessarily needs to be rectangular.
To make sure that the interrogation area satisfy this, two limits are applied to the algo­
rithm. They make sure that the shape is regular and hence the gradients are not too
large. It is done by firstly limiting the absolute magnitude of each of the four gradients.
Secondly by limiting the combined effect of all four gradients.
|UX|, |V X|, |UY |, |V Y | ≤0.1

Good PIV parameters
When fine tuning the PIV some rules of thumb were kept in mind. The image density
should be at least 10 particles pr Interrogation area and the in­plane motion should not
exceed 1/4 of the Interrogation area width. Furthermore to reduce the stress on the PIV
algorithm the out­of­plane motion should not exceed 1/4 of the laser sheet thickness.
3.3
Experimental Setup
The experimental setup is made of several different devices. The section aims to de­
scribe the overall process of using the setup. Later, a section for each device further
elaborates and describes the individual equipment.
The PIV algorithm requires pictures of evenly distributed dots on a contrast background
to be able to identify the distance move across each time step. The water is seeded
with particles and eliminated with a laser. The camera is fixed at a distance to the flow
to capture an area of interest. The area pictures is 6.2 cm wide and 4.6 cm tall. This
enables the capture of the bottom and larger structures such and vortex tubes. This
compromise is necessary to measure the bed shear stress and see the structures on
12
Quantitative description of coherent turbulent structures in wave boundary layers
==PAGE:23==
the same time. A configuration with multiple cameras would improve the measurement
on both aspects, as it allows a camera closer access to the bottom and a broader view
to capture the structures in the same time step, not just phase.
The wavelength of the laser and a filter on the camera makes the particles stand out
to the impurities in the water making a uniform distribution of the seeding. The camera
and laser are synchronized to take pictures and make very short laser burst to freeze
the motion of the flow at the same time. This is independent of the shutter speed since
only the duration of the laser pulse is captured by the picture while camera only senses
black for the remainder of the shutter duration. At each step two separate pictures are
taken, with the laser beam being shot at the end of the first frame and at the start of
the second frame to make the time between the pictures short enough to have a large
correlation between the two pictures.
The processing of the images then involves a clean­up of the raw pictures and then the
PIV analysis with picture correlation between each double frame. The analysis uses
the time between each frame to calculate the velocity from the distance moved between
each frame. The PIV algorithm uses a mapping of the correlation areas to generate
areas with correlation. The area used is very small which gives a very good precision
from the technique, but ultimately creates a grid of 106x144 measurements. This is 144
measurements across the width at each specific time.
3.3.1
Laser
The laser used for the setup is Litron Laser, model LPY704­100 PIV. It provides short
laser pulses for 12 ns, which effectively freeze the particle motion. Its a dual cavity flash
lamp, which enables the use of two flashes in very close succession. This is needed
for especially low velocity measurements, however also utilized for higher speeds. The
laser output up to 800 mJ at wavelengths 1064 nm and 532 nm. Since the former isn’t
visible to the naked eye, the later is used out of caution. The maximum output is also
not utilised but set to 3/10 on a rotary switch. This part of the setup is a compromise
between illuminating the particles and excluding other forms of natural inherent seeding,
such as minerals and air bobbles on the images and being able to see the bottom of the
flume. The reason for excluding natural inherent seeding, is for the PIV analysis to have
an evenly distributed seeding with same particle size. Furthermore the identification of
the particles close to the bottom is essential in the calculation of bottom shear stress.
The laser is set to synchronous, and is synced with the camera by the Synchronizer box.
For specific synchronisation see subsubsection 3.3.4.2. The important thing is that the
laser can emit pulses up to 100Hz for each cavity enabling it to sync up with the camera.
More info on the laser at Ltd n.d. p. 8.

3.5
Vortex identification
The vortex identification process consist of two steps. (1) Visual inspection. The data
set is combed through for suitable pictures for vortex identification. The limitation of
the view field by the camera, since it is placed close to the flume for other reasons,
see section 3.8, makes, that each period does not necessarily capture a good vortex
at flow reversal. It is also a possibility that they are not present at each flow reversal.
However the inspection yields a series of suitable candidates for the vortex identification
algorithm.
(2) An algorithm for detecting vortex centers and edges. The vortex identification algo­
rithm is based on the work of Laurent Graftieaux and Grosjean 2001. The procedure
identifies the vortex by looking at turbulent fluctuations and the swirling motion. By
applying proper orthogonal decomposition, their contribution to the total energy is sep­
arated. Combined with a set of vortex identification functions, these functions identify
the locations of the centre and boundary of the vortex on the basis of the velocity field.
18
Quantitative description of coherent turbulent structures in wave boundary layers
==PAGE:29==
On top of this a threshold procedure is used to distinguish vortices from each other and
identify actually vortex tubes. Near the bed, long elongated vortex streets are present
at flow reversal, but since they are very different in height from their width, they are not
considered vortex tubes. The threshold criteria requires the width and the height to be
within 75% of each other, meaning the identified vortices have a round or at worst an
oval shape.
3.6
Analytic functions
In order to get meaningful results from the PIV measurement data analysis is needed.
The process is briefly described.
First a despiking method is applied to the data. It calculates a running median and
a running standard deviation at each height and replaces the value with the moving
median if the value is beyond two standard deviations away from the median. It is
important to keep in mind that the median is more suitable than the mean when working
with turbulence since a couple of faulty values otherwise could skew the data too much
in their direction.
The vector field from PIV is then trimmed to their correct size. It is originally 144x106
data points pr. time step, but about a third of the height is removed, leaving 144x70
data points left. This is a result of the bottom of flume always being in the frame of view.
These data points are heavily correlated with its neighboring data points but this just
means they can not be used as individual measurements. At the edges of the width the
flow can be a little distorted or out of focus, which makes the middle of the vector field
most suitable for further analysis. Instead to reduce the noise on the signal, a center
vertical vector field with the size 11x70 is chosen and a median filter is applied to reduce
it further to 1x70. The size 11x70 is a little arbitrary, however it is chosen with the size of
the individual flow structures in mind. The filtering should not remove the flow features
but just reduce the noise on the signal.
After this initial data cleaning the data is ready for further analysis. The rest of the data
analysis is done as the methods are described in the theoretical description.

3.8
Experimental discussion
The final experimental setup is a compromise between being far away and at the same
time close enough to the flow. It is strictly needed to be very close and preferably even
closer than this setup does to be able to calculate the bed shear stress directly from
the velocities as in section 3.7. This will become clearer as the results are discussed
and some of the features are not quite captured accurately due to low resolution of the
viscous sub­layer at the higher Reynolds number.
At low Reynolds numbers the need for close up of the bottom is not needed to the same
degree and a lot of the flow features requires much larger field of view. As an example
the vortex tubes can grow up towards 5 cm in diameter.
In the end the final setup tries to satisfy both needs from the laminar case and also
the turbulent case. What could have been instead, was to equip the setup with another
camera so the near bed resolution could be captured simultaneous with the zoomed out
view to inspect flow features. The advantage of this is that the flow features and the bed
shear stress could be directly coupled as they would be from the exact same data and
not just from the same range of Reynolds numbers. This would give a better description
of the bed shear stress peaks in a turbulent spot of the impact of a vortex tube.

Discussion
5.1
Verification of PIV
To verify the results from PIV the results are compared with existing data from on bed
shear stress from B. Lykke Jensen 1989 and velocity profiles from B. Mutlu Sumer
2020. The bed shear stress is seen on Figure 4.1 which shows the full period. The
free steam velocity is plotted on the lower plot as an indicator of the phase value and
matches the one on the x­axis. On the same plot, the near bed velocity is matched with
the theoretical expression for the velocity in the boundary layer, Equation 2.5. The two
profiles seems to be in total agreement throughout the whole period. This indicates that
the technique is solid.
When shifting the attention to the bed shear stress a more solid validation is given. The
bed shear stress is calculated from the bottom data points, as indicated in section 3.7.
This means that the measurement point should be within the laminar boundary layer
given as Equation 2.6. With ω given by the flume the boundary layer thickness is 1.8 mm.
With the first data point being at the height of 0.4 mm this is more than sufficient to get
a good result when calculating it based on the velocity. This is also seen on the values
for the bed shear stress on Figure 4.1. The measurement follow both approaches to a
theoretical bed shear stress very well and the zero crossing is spot on for both upwards
and downwards crossing. However the gradient is a little different on the up­crossing
which results in a lower peak value for the period.
Overall the method is very valid for determining the bed shear stress in a laminar bound­
ary layer.
5.2
Laminar velocity profile
The same picture is painted by Figure 4.3 and Figure 4.4. Here is the time development
of the velocity profile at every 15◦. The measurement points, again the dotted points,
are almost indistinguishable from the theoretical starred points for all phases. This tells
that the method for obtaining good velocities is very precise in the laminar boundary
layer.
By further inspection on especially 75◦­135◦, the high velocities in the bottom of the
boundary seems to be captured slightly off compared to the theoretical trend. The mea­
sured velocities are trending lower than their counterpart. It is clearly not an issue with
measuring high or very low velocities since the free stream is aligned. The same ob­
servation is also true for the profile at flow reversal at 0◦and 180◦. However this might
suggest that the method is a little conservative near the bottom.

5.3
Vortex tube identification
The vortex tube identification algorithm can find vortices and it is up to the user to set a
good threshold for when it is a vortex in the flow or an actual vortex tube. The problem
with vortex tubes is, that the method is a 2D method for identifying an 3D structure. The
vortex tubes are in an array of multiple overlapping and mixing structures, see B. Mutlu
Sumer 2020, p. 253. The 2D methodology is a premise for the PIV technique with
one camera and is in practice also hard with multiple cameras since the depth of the
flow shadows itself. To identify vortex tubes on an automated basis it gives rise to the
problem of distinguishing them from other vortices. The vortex tube will emerge from
the bottom and grow in size and rise up through the boundary layer until it is washed
away after flow reversal. By identifying these characteristics it should be possible.
This method focuses on the interaction between the visual inspection and the lock­on
method of capturing and following the vortex until it dissolves. The method extends its
range further back than the first visual inspection to search for the point of emerging. On
Figure 2.6 the earliest vortex tubes emerges later and disappears similarly at a earlier
time.
The vortex detection needs further attention if the vortex tube theory needs to be ex­
panded. The PIV method has promising results for individual vortex detection and they
are clearly visible from the vector fields. Yet the end goal is to statistically describe them,
their frequency of occurrence, their lifespan, size and shape. This is not satisfied yet.

6
Conclusion
The PIV measurement technique proved very good at quantifying the velocity in the
laminar case. In the turbulent case the results was also promising, however common
for both cases was, that very high or very small velocities are difficult to capture well.
Another issue with the method is the signal processing which is not set in stone yet,
and still causes some problems. These problems relates to flow features getting filtered
away by the very necessary noise filtering. A PIV measurement will always have some
noise in the signal, but to tell turbulence from noise is the difficult part. The specific
approach by this report does accomplish to identify both flow features and statistical
trends, however the quality of both could be even better. The suggestion of using a
zoom camera and a far field camera seems like the next logical step to improve the
method since the PIV algorithm and the experimental setup is calibrated to a certain
type of flow. By being able to capture both at the same time step at a higher resolution
enables the analysis to go even further.
The method showed that it is possible to identify large flow structures such as vortex
tubes and turbulent spots. The algorithm used to identify these are based on the bed
shear stress to align with the work of B. Lykke Jensen 1989. Is it not necessary to cal­
culate bed shear stress to identify larger flow structures, as they will also be detectable
directly from the velocity. Otherwise the beforehand mentioned improvement would also
improve this field as a very good near bed resolution of the viscous sub­layer and its cor­
responding visualization of the flow structure could give very good coherence between
the structure and the time series of the bed shear stress.
The PIV method validation is overall very satisfying as most of the measurements from
B. Lykke Jensen 1989 with a Hot film or a LDA can be reproduced by PIV. The PIV
method have some problems with large amounts of data and a troublesome digging
through to find the good results. Improvements in an automated process could reduce
the analysis time substantially.
