Abstract
The subject of this thesis is to study forecast models of CO2 emission intensity in
the electricity production. The forecasts is then used in a simulated model setup
to optimize the control of a heat pump, thus minimizing CO2 emissions while
keeping the building adequately heated.
For the forecasting process, a large set of data supplied by the company Tomor-
row, that includes weather forecasts and European electricity grid states and
forecasts were used. There have been made several different forecasting mod-
els, where one turned out to outperform the rest marginally. This is a linear
regression forecast made from a long list of variables, including splines variations
and exponential variations of the original variables. The forecast resulted in an
RMSE value of 39.05 kgCO2-eq
MWh
for six hour ahead emissions performed on a
period running from March 2017 to March 2018. By ensembling the models, the
value could be slightly improved to a RMSE value of 38.86 kgCO2-eq
MWh
.
Furthermore, three different types of buildings were investigated; a family house,
an office building and a commercial building. The various buildings were dimen-
sioned on the basis of standards and building regulations. A model operating
on model predictive control was made in Rstudio, this was where the optimiza-
tion of control of a heat pump, while minimizing CO2 emissions and keeping the
building adequately heated was controlled and simulated.
The main results of this thesis are expressed in a percentage reduction of CO2
emission. For a family house a reduction of 11.95% can be reached, for an of-
fice building 13.27% and for a commercial building 13.19%. Effectively, there is
more CO2 reduction to gain with larger buildings. Also the impact of a flexible
consumer was investigated, where the CO2 emission reduction at two different
maximum indoor temperatures in a family house for both floor heating and ra-
diator heating is concluded. Floor heating is the best option with a 33% CO2
emission reduction at both temperature limits. Where the radiator heating op-
erates best when the temperature is allowed to go up to 25 degrees, it reaches a
reduction of 19%.

Abstrakt
Emnet for denne afhandling er at studere prognosemodeller af CO2 intensiteten i
elproduktionen. Disse anvendes derefter til, i et simuleret modelsetup at optimere
styring af en varmepumpe, således at CO2 udledningen minimeres samtidigt med,
at bygningen opvarmes tilstrækkeligt.
For udviklingen af CO2 emission intensitetsprognosen er der blevet stillet et stort
datasæt til rådighed, der er leveret af virksomheden Tomorrow, herunder ve-
jrudsigter, data for elnettet og prognoser på det eurpæiske elekricitetsnetværk.
Der er lavet flere forskellige prognosemodeller, dog var der én enkelt, der skilte
sig ud. Denne var en lineær regressionmodel lavet fra en lang række variable,
herunder splines variationer og eksponentielle variationer af de oprindelige vari-
able. Denne prognose resulterede i en RMSE værdi på 39.05 kgCO2-eq
MWh
, ved at
kombinere modellerne kunne denne værdi forbedres en smule til en RMSE værdi
på 38.86 kgCO2-eq
MWh
.
Endvidere blev der undersøgt tre forskellige typer bygninger; et familiehus, en
kontorbygning og en kommerciel bygning. De forskellige bygninger blev dimen-
sioneret udfra standarder og bygningsreglementet fra 2018. En model er lavet i
Rstudio, der arbejder med modelforudsigende kontrol. Det er i denne model opti-
meringen af styringen af varmepumpen, samt minimeringen af CO2 emissionerne,
og bygningen bliver tilstrækkeligt opvarmet, styret og simuleret.
De overordnede resultater af denne afhandling er udtrykt i en procentuel reduk-
tion af CO2 emissioner. For et familiehus kan der opnås en CO2 reduktion på
11,95% for en kontorbygning 13,27% og for en kommerciel bygning 13,19%. Ef-
fektivt opnås der højere CO2 reduktion med større bygninger.
Dertil er det
også blevet undersøgt virkningen af en fleksibel forbruger, hvor CO2 emission-
sreduktion ved to forskellige maksimale temperaturer (22 og 25 grader celcius) i
et familiehus med både gulvvarme og radiatorvarme konkluderes. Gulvvarme er
den bedste løsning med en imponerende 33% CO 2 emissionsreduktion ved begge
temperaturgrænser. Hvor radiatorvarmen virker bedst, når temperaturen får lov
at gå op til 25 grader, så når radiatorvarmen en reduktion på 19%.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Buildings are responsible for roughly 40% of the total energy use in Europe [Ene]. There-
fore, it is a necessity for the future energy system to have control of the energy use in
buildings. The energy is primarily used for heating, lighting and electrical appliances. A
future main source for heating these buildings will be heat pumps combined with floor
heating [TEK18]. Heat pumps are very attractive because of their energy efficiency - with
a coefficient of performance of 3 or larger. Because heat pumps are driven by electricity
and can be connected to floors with large thermal capacity, they have great potential to
shift the electricity consumption and adapt to electricity production from wind turbines
[Hal+12]. A smart control of the heat pump can also allow the heat pump to heat when
CO2 emissions are low.
Energy policies in the Nordic countries designate that 50% of the energy consumed by 2025
should come from renewable energy sources [Ana08], by 2050 the aim is to be independent
of fossil fuels. A transformation of the energy system is necessary to reduce CO2 emis-
sions. To be able to reach this goal an introduction of a significant amount of renewable
energy and an efficient utilization of energy in buildings, industries, and transportation,
is necessary for phasing out the fossil fuel consumption. In the Nordic countries hydro
power and wind turbines will play a crucial part of the renewable energy. Heat pumps will
be used for heating on the consumption side, and in residential and commercial buildings.
Therefore, electricity will act as the main energy carrier in such an energy system inde-
pendent of fossil fuels. Conditional of the rate of implementation of electrified vehicles
40-70% of the energy consumption will originate from electricity in 2050 [Hal+12], com-
pared to the current 20%. Since electricity is more difficult to store than fossil fuels an
intelligent power system that balances the power consumption and power production is
required. This balancing requires control of the power consumption from electrical vehi-
cles and heat pumps so that excess wind energy is used as it is produced. Heat tanks in
residential homes as well as district heating plants must be established so that electricity
can be stored as heat when electricity prices are low.
In this paper, first a CO2 emission intensity forecast is produced. Model predictive control
based on these CO2 emission signals are used to control a heat pump such that certain
indoor temperature limits in a building with a floor heating system are respected.

Cooperative Parties
This study is a cooperation between the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and
Tomorrow. Tomorrow is the supplier of the data used in this study. They have different
products.
For example an average CO2 emission intensity, hourly basis, forecast for
the different bidding zones in ENTSOE, the electricity transmission system opereator in
Europe. This forecast has been improved for one of the bidding zones in Denmark, DK2,

2.2.3
Model Selection for the ARIMA Model
To find a suitable ARIMA model, the auto correlation function (ACF) and the partial
auto correlation function (PACF) can be used. ACF simply calculates the Pearson corre-
lation between itself and lagged versions of itself. Lag is here understood as a shift in the
data, so a lag by one time step. Formally, that is ACF = corr(xt, xt+h) where h is the
lag. In time series, there typically will be some correlation between what happens now
and what will happen soon. This correlation will decay over time unless some periodic
pattern is present, which in this case would increase the correlation for lags of that period.
PACF calculates the correlation, that do not exist in earlier lags. So, for h = 1 it is the
same as ACF. It is a conditional correlation [Uni18];
PACF = corr(xt, xt+h|xt+1, xt+2..xt+h−1).
(2.7)
This is useful information for selection of an AR model. If for instance, the first lag is
highly correlated and later lags are not, then it implies dealing with an AR model with
a lag equal to one. Higher order lags will simply not be significant, as all information is
explained by the first. Lots of information can be used from these functions in order to
select the model orders (p, d, q) for an ARIMA model. Several rules have been defined by
various sources to help determine the parameters. A few of them are listed here:
• Rule 1: ”If the series has positive autocorrelations out to a high number of lags (say,
10 or more), then it probably needs a higher order of differencing.” [Rob18]
• Rule 2: ”If the partial autocorrelation function (PACF) of the differenced series
displays a sharp cutoff and/or the lag-1 autocorrelation is positive–i.e., if the series
appears slightly ”underdifferenced”–then consider adding one or more AR terms to
the model. The lag beyond which the PACF cuts off is the indicated number of AR
terms.” [Rob18]
• Rule 3: ”If the autocorrelation function (ACF) of the differenced series displays a
sharp cutoff and/or the lag-1 autocorrelation is negative–i.e., if the series appears
slightly ”overdifferenced”–then consider adding an MA term to the model. The lag
beyond which the ACF cuts off is the indicated number of MA terms.” [Rob18]
There are many more in the given reference, which can be useful in model selection of
an ARIMA model. This is the manual method for fitting an ARIMA model. Like most
things these days, there is a way to do it automatically. There is an R implementation for
this called auto.arima() from the package forecast. This one does not work with PACF
and ACF, but simply just considers the AIC scores for several models; ARIMA(1,0,0),
ARIMA(2,0,0) and so on, starting by approximating the AIC of the models to save time,
and then it re-fits the best models and selects the model with the best AIC.
For seasonality to be considered as well, the model needs to be extended to an
ARIMA(p, d, q)(P, D, Q)m model, where m is the number of observations in the seasonal
period. In case of daily seasonality, m = 24 will be chosen. The seasonal terms (P, D, Q)
are similar to the original terms, but explain the seasonal period through backshifting of
that period. These components can also be optimized using auto.arima.

2.2.6.3
Forward Selection
Forward feature selection is a simple algorithm, but it gives reasonable results if done
carefully and with a proper cross validation strategy, as the one proposed in Section 2.1.2.
First, one needs to choose a forecasting method, for example a simple linear regression
model. Then you calculate all the available features’ performance individually, and choose
an evaluation method (BIC, AIC or RMSE to name a few). You pick the best performing
feature, and start from there. Then you add features, one by one, if they happen to
improve the forecast, you keep them, otherwise, you leave them. At the end of the day,
you will have a set of variables that all contribute well to the forecast.

validation set you have chosen. The LASSO regression will try and do this automatically,
but LASSO is fixed for only working with linear regression.
2.2.7
Cubic Splines
Splines are a way to approximate functions given a set of data points. The simplest repre-
sentation is to represent straight lines between each point. This is a first degree spline. A
second degree spline enables one to draw quadratic lines between the points, and finally
the third degree enables one to draw cubic lines, hence the name Cubic Splines. With big
data sets, with possibly 10,000 points, it will be very time consuming and not to mention;
overfitting, to do this between each and every point, so therefore knots are introduced.
So, the splines will only be calculated between these knots. Usually, the knots are by
default the quantiles, but this is a tune able parameter.
The Cubic splines have a few requirements.
First, they need to be function approxi-
mations, that are continuous at merging points. Second, these function approximations
need also to be continuous in their first and second derivatives at merging points. This
is illustrated in Figure 2.5.

These are also known as basis splines. Another type of splines that is used is the natural
spline. This one just adds one more requirement; At the end points, also referred to as
extreme or boundary knots, the second derivatives must be equal to zero. What this
means, is that the concave at the merging point must be zero. In other words, an S-curve

Model Ensembling [KAU17]
Several methods for modelling good forecasts are suggested in this study, and some are
better than others. To make use of all the advantages from different models, it is encour-
aged to find a way to combine them. This is called ensembling and can be done in a few
different ways;
• Averaging: Simply take the average of all the predictions you have for one spe-
cific point in time. This is usually not the best idea, if the models differ a lot in
performance, as this will give equal weight to all models.
• Weighted averaging: Same as averaging, but with assigned weights.
• Majority voting: Mostly for classification. Go with the prediction that most models
voted for.
So, the task is to weigh the models properly. This, on the other hand, can be done in
several ways. Three different approaches are covered in this study. First things first, what
are the advantages of ensembling your models?
• 1) It is a proven method, in most cases, to improve the accuracy of the best per-
forming forecasting model.

country, with parameters like CO2-intensity, which is the parameter that is to be fore-
casted, and fifty other real time parameters (not forecasts) along with an additional twenty
parameters which are forecasts. These include weather forecasts, consumption, produc-
tion, import and export. All the forecasts given in these data sets are the latest provided
forecasts - 6 hours intervals.
3.1.3
Wind and Solar Power Production Forecast
ENTSOE, electricity transmission system operator in Europe for the pan-European Mar-
ket, is the source for Tomorrow’s data. At their website transparency.entsoe.eu, they also
have forecasts for the power production from both wind and solar for each bidding zone in
the market. These forecasts are released at 6 pm Brussels time every day and cover every
hour of the next day. This means, that the data cannot be used for running updates on
forecast for the MPC-model, at least only for specific hours a day and specific horizons.
At 6 pm Brussels time, it can for instance be used to predict values for the whole following
day. From 6 hour horizons to 30 hour horizons. So any prediction of CO2 emissions after
6 pm Brussels time will be given 24 hours or longer before, which means the accuracy is
fairly low.

4.3.1
Variations of the Linear Regression
Not all the features are best described, as in their original scaled form. Sometimes an
exponential form exp(x) is better, and sometimes perhaps the splines, as described in
Section 2.2.7, will be beneficial. Splines exist in many forms, the base spline and the
natural spline to name a few, which will be the ones dealt with here. The splines can
be adjusted with parameters, like the knots and the degrees of freedom, introduced in
Section 2.2.7, where the knots are fairly difficult to tune, especially when dealing with lots

of variables, and therefore the default will be used, which are the quantiles. The degrees
of freedom can easily be tuned with either AIC and BIC.
The implementation of exponential functions and splines is done by preparing the data
set prior to implementation, thus all columns have a corresponding column with splines,
and another one with degrees of freedom tuned splines and yet another one for the expo-
nential term. The exponential variations are easy to implement, thus Xi = exp(Xi). To
implement splines in a linear regression model, Equation 2.9 describing a linear regression
model, is extended to

doing a LASSO regression on such a model, it will be allowed to pick different pieces of
a spline and take that as a variable. Therefore, Equation 4.3 will be applied to every
explanatory variable on beforehand instead, and the residuals from those models will be
the new explanatory variables. As mentioned, then the degrees of freedom will be tuned
in all the models with AIC and BIC. There are two options of splines to be considered,
either the base splines, bs(), or the natural splines, ns(), which are described in Section
2.2.7. So for each explanatory variable, there will be three bs() versions, one tuned with
AIC, one with BIC and one that is not tuned, hence the default value of 3 degrees of
freedom. The same goes for ns(). Then there is the exponential version and the original
explanatory variable. That leaves eight times as many variables as originally, and hence
the computation time follows that. This however, makes it possible to do a LASSO re-
gression on the data, which is a very efficient algorithm compared to the forward feature
selection.

4.6
Support Vector Machines (SVM)
Support Vector Machines can be very expensive, computationally. But, they do not have
to be. By using linear Kernels it becomes much less complex. In fact, the computation
time is almost as it is in the linear regression.
Instead of scaling exponentially with
the number of features, Support vector Machines scale exponentially with the number of
observations. So, the complexity does not increase with the number of features. Therefore,
it is actually faster to do the forward selection algorithm with Support Vectors, when using
linear Kernels. However, most R packages for SVM use a nonlinear solver, even when
the Kernel is set to be linear. Therefore it is important to pick the right package for this
process. A good option for nonlinear purposes is the parallelSVM package, which has
integrated the concept of parallel computation (splitting work to the cores of the CPU),
allowing the computer to spend more computer power at once. Here, a linear Kernel is
also an option, but the computation time does not differ. Instead, the package LibLineaR
is used. It works with support vectors, and depending of the type-argument, it is either for
support vector regression or classification. With this implementation, and all tune-able
parameters as default, the forward selection is performed on all the 702 non correlated

4.8.1
Arbitrated Dynamic Ensemble
The first step of the ADE model, is to train meta models to combine the base models
with dynamic weights. That is, for each base model created, a corresponding meta model
should be created, that will try to predict the error from the given base model.
For
creating the meta models, only inexpensive algorithms, like SVM, Linear Regression and
Random Forests are considered. This is because, to make the proper prediction of the
error, you will need all features, that made the prediction in the first place. And since
both the GLM and SVM models a lot of features, it will become unfeasible to use Neural
Networks for instance. The data to be used here can only be the metatraining data. One
issue here is the number of observations available, as there only are 545 in each part.

5.1
Heat Pump
A heat pump is a device that transfers heat from a low temperature zone to a higher
temperature zone. A heat pump can supply heating and cooling, but in cooler climates,
like Denmark, heating is more occurring. Reversible heat pumps are heat pumps that can
both provide heating and cooling. Heat pumps draw energy from the air or the ground
(geothermal) and uses a vapor compression refrigeration cycle.
This cycle consists of
the four basic components as sketched in Figure 5.1. The transfer of energy takes place
since refrigerant alternately evaporates and condenses. The four basic components are a
compressor, an expansion valve, a condenser converting the working fluid from its gaseous
state to its liquid state, and the opposite an evaporator converting the working fluid from
its liquid state to its gaseous state.
Figure 5.1: Heat pump vapor compression refrigeration cycle [Hal+12]
Figure 5.1 shows a heat pump vapor compression refrigeration cycle. The temperatures
==PAGE:74==
56

are denoted T with subscript c or e for condenser or evaporator, respectively, while i or
o denotes input or output.
The heat energy that the heat pump draws from air or the ground is delivered to the
refrigerant in the evaporator, thus evaporation of the refrigerant. This means that the
refrigerant changes phase from liquid to gas. When the gas is compressed in the com-
pressor, the pressure is then raised and the temperature rises as an effect of this. The
refrigerant then releases the energy in the condenser where the refrigerant flows through
the expansion valve, cools and returns into the liquid phase and back to the evaporator,
where the process starts over again.
The amount of heat transferred from the condenser to the water, Qc, is related to the
work of the compressor, Wc, using the coefficient of performance:
Qc = COP · Wc
(5.1)
The coefficient of performance COP for heat pumps varies with type of heat pump, out-
door ground temperature, climate, and the condenser temperature. A thumb rule is that
all heat pump have a COP of at least 3, this means that the heat pump produces three
times at much heat as it uses in electricity.
When dimensioning the heat pump, over-sizing is always better than under-sizing the
heat pump. The heat pump has to be able to handle ambient temperatures of -10 °C.

the state variables.

needed. In this thesis only simple models are used, and since these models are stochastic,
that is, SDEs (stochastic differential equations), the random variation that affects the

state space model in continuous time [Not18]:

5.3
Model Predictive Control
Model Predictive Control (MPC) is a control method that computes an optimal control
action based on a model of a dynamical system and its predicted future progression. In
other words MPC is a feedback control algorithm that uses a model to make predictions
about future outputs of a process, and it solves an optimization problem to select the
==PAGE:76==
58
5 Theory Behind MPC
best control action. For any type of controller to be acceptable, it must be very reliable.
The objective and the mathematical model is formulated as a real time optimization
problem. The real time optimization problem repeatedly computes the control inputs.
The objective function can be programmed to maximizing profit, minimizing costs, or
forcing the system to follow a pre-computed set point trajectory. Just the computed inputs
related with the present time step is activated on the physical system. Another current
model state is evaluated routinely when new estimations are accessible and the real-time
optimization procedure is repeated [Mac02; CB07]. This principle is illustrated in Figure
5.2 and is also often referred to as Receding Horizon Control. As illustrated in Figure 5.2,
the estimation part uses historical data to estimate the current state. A Kalman filter
does precisely this for linear models subject to process noise and measurement noise, see
Section 5.3.1.
Figure 5.2: The basic idea of MPC. Left: Estimation and regulation problem.
Right: Moving horizon implementation [Jør18]
The regulation part computes a trajectory of the manipulated variables, u, so that the
predicted output follows the target as good as possible. The procedure is computational
intensive as large-scale optimal control problems must be solved numerically in real-time.
The idea of moving horizon estimation is to estimate unknown variables or parameters us-
ing a series of measurements observed over time, containing noise and other disturbances.
Moving horizon estimation is an optimization approach that requires an iterative approach
that depends on linear or nonlinear programming solvers to find the solution. Moving
horizon estimation becomes the Kalman filter under certain simplifying conditions (see
5.3.1 for Kalman filter). An evaluation of the extended Kalman filter and MHE found
better performance of MHE with the cost of increased computational expense. MHE is
generally applied to systems with greater computational resources because of this larger
expense.
For over 30 years, MPC has been utilized in the oil refining industry just as in the pro-
cess businesses. The explanation behind the early adoption of MPC by these businesses
is the moderate time consistent of the procedures included and the accessibility of PC
control [CB07]. The early executions of MPC depended on linear convolution models,
==PAGE:77==
5.3 Model Predictive Control
59
for example, impulse and step response models. Even today, most of MPC applications
depend on linear models. Discrete time convolution models, input-output models (ARX,
ARMAX), and state space models are utilized for the separating and forecasts in MPC.
The computations in these implementations are based on a state space representation of
the system. For MPC based on linear prediction models the final optimization problem is
a linear program or a quadratic program. Optimization algorithms for taking care of these
issues have been developed. One advantage of MPC is to solve most problems with much
lower computational effort like with dynamic programming. Thereto, MPC can handle
bigger problems. Furthermore, MPC can handle system limitations by adding them as
constraints in the optimization problem. Constraints are very important, as neglecting
them may lead to undesired consequences. Another great advantages of MPC is that
it can handle multi-input multi-output (MIMO) systems that might have interactions
between their inputs and outputs. These interactions between inputs and outputs are
often challenging to design in MIMO systems when using traditional controllers such as
PID, because this leads to tuning to many controllers. The advantage of MPC is that
it can simultaneously control all the outputs while considering input-output interactions
through a multi-variable-controller. This ability can lead to greater profits and hence
better performance.
MPC also has preview capabilities, if set point changes are known in advance, the con-
troller can react to those changes and improve the performance. Choosing proper values
for the design parameters in the MPC is very important as they affect both the controller
performance and computational complexity of the MPC algorithm that solves an online
optimization problem at each time step. To reduce the complexity of MPC calculations,
model order reduction techniques can be useful, such as: To use shorter prediction and
control horizons, reduce the number of constraints, and use lower-precision data represen-
tations and operations.
To sum up Model Predictive Control typically is used in the process control layer, and has
the ability to optimally control MIMO systems, it accounts for inputs and state constraints
and suggests for integrating economic optimization with MPC. The main advantage of
MPC controllers lie in their ability to handle constraints.

The Kalman filter is an iterative mathematical process that uses a set of equations and
consecutive data inputs to estimate the real value, position, velocity etc. of the object
being measured, when the measured values contain unpredicted or random error, uncer-
tainty or variation. The Kalman filter does not wait for a hole bunch of inputs, it very
quickly starts to narrow in to the true value by taking a few of those inputs and by
understanding the variation or uncertainty of those data inputs [Mat17].

Robust Model Predictive Control
Robust MPC (RMPC) is an improved form of the nominal MPC that is robust even
with uncertainty.
The main objective of robust model predictive control is to design
an optimization based control method that accounts for the complex interactions of the
uncertainty with the system, constraints, and performance criteria. In MPC the many
design procedures can achieve robust stability in two different ways. First indirectly by
specifying the performance objective and uncertainty description in a way that robust
stability is reached by the optimal control computations. Second directly by enforcing a
robust reduction constraint which guarantees that the state will become smaller for all
plants in the uncertainty set. Stability in the presence of uncertainty [BM]. Robust MPC
is used if a violation of the constraints has fatal consequences. Since in this case if the
temperature in the different buildings rises above the limits or falls under the limits, it
does not have fatal consequences, robust MPC is therefor not relevant in this case.

grey-box model amount to some stochastic differential equations joined with some dis-
crete time observation equations.
The grey-box models can include both system and
measurement noise.

instance and this will result in a change of thermal resistance, R. All this is done in a
script so it can be ”automated” so finally graphs showing saved CO2 as a function of
thermal resistance and heat capacities are concluded.
A short definition of the two main parameters is given below, namely thermal resistance
and heat capacity. Thermal resistance is property of a layer, and is calculated as the
thickness of the material divided by the thermal conductivity of this material. Thermal
conductivity is a measure of a material’s ability to transfer heat with a unit of W/mK
this leaves us with a unit of m2K/W for thermal resistance. Secondly, heat capacity is
the ratio of the heat added to (or removed from) an object to the resulting temperature
change. The unit of heat capacity is joule per Kelvin J/K.
An estimation is already obtained from [[DTU]] so the model is fitted on data and the
parameters that are estimated. These result are held up against a physical calculation
made by a simplified model where arguments are made for the parameter values used,

with certain physical properties given by the model and the values of the parameters.

6.3.4
Handling Uncertainty
Uncertainty plays a crucial role in economic MPC models because real models are imper-
fect and the economic operation of a plant typically operates the system around one of
its constraints that has received little attention yet. One way to handle uncertainties are
with a robust model predictive control to make the the controller robust to model uncer-
tainties. Another way to handle uncertainties could be to make a modeling framework
for online estimation of states and unknown parameters.
==PAGE:99==
6.3 The Model

Nominal model predictive control is chosen for cases with a low level of model uncertainty
(0–30%) and robust model predictive control is the preferred choice for models with higher
uncertainty (30–67%) [Maa+14]. As mentioned earlier, robust MPC is used if a violation
of the constraints has fatal consequences. As this particular model is in the low end of
uncertainties the nominal MPC is seen as sufficient in this case.

7.0.1
Error Criteria (EC)
The error criteria that is to be optimized is the relative reduction of total CO2 emissions
compared to a controller, that uses no forecast. For this reason, the no forecast case needs
to be evaluated for every scenario that is investigated in this analysis. Of course, the goal
in the end is to emit as little CO2 as possible, but to reach that goal, it is encouraged to
find the buildings that will benefit the most from an MPC implementation. For instance,
it may be very profitable in terms of CO2 emission reduction, to implement it in buildings
with bad insulation, because the gain might be larger than in a well insulated building.
The well insulated house will emit less no matter what.
7.1
Cases to be Investigated
The investigated parameters are as follows:
• Horizon of forecasts: This parameter is important, to get an idea about how long
horizons actually are needed to get a well performing MPC.
• Varying temperature requirements: Before going deeper into the parameters,
it is worth investigating the impact of having varying temperature requirements. If
more loose requirements during the night can help the MPC to perform better.
• Size of heat pumps: This parameter is essential to compare the buildings and to
know whether the potential is reached. In an economic sense, it is also important, as
the price increases a lot with larger heat pumps. It will in the end be a compromise
between price and CO2 emission.
• Insulation in the walls and roof (Ra): This parameter can be very interesting to
look into. Perhaps there is a local optimum value for this in terms of CO2 emission
reduction. A value too small will require the heat pump to run all the time, which
==PAGE:102==
84

will reduce the impact of an MPC. A value too large will ultimately result in no use,
which naturally also reduces the impact of an MPC.
• Heat capacity (Cm) in the floor and the thermal resistance (Rm) between
the floor-indoor: These parameters are also interesting. The heat capacity in the
floor can be viewed as an accumulation tank to store the heat. The larger it is, the
greater the flexibility. However, it needs a high conductivity too, which means a low
insulation, in order to pass on the heat to the room. So, in this model, the thermal
resistance between the floor-indoor, Rm, can be viewed as the floors property to
share its heat with the room, hence a too high Rm is a bad thing.
• Heating type: This is for comparing how the chosen floor heating system performs
in contrast to a simple radiator system. It is expected that the floor heating system
will benefit the most from implementation of an MPC, as it better can store heat
in the floor.

7.7
Flexible Consumer
Knowledge about the functioning of the Nordic electricity market is required. Basic infor-
mation can be found in the FlexPower project report of EA Energy Analyses [BFA12].
This is an important aspect and is one of the main reasons for doing this kind of re-
search. One thing is to minimize the average CO2 emissions from the electricity origin
for a given hour. This is easy to relate to, however, in reality the production happens
on a much smaller resolution than hourly. Perhaps, the consumption for a given hour is
higher than expected, and the power producers need to start up their gas turbines to meet
the demand. Even though the average CO2 emissions are low for this hour, by starting
the heat pump, it (among other consumers) encourages the gas turbines to start. So, all
of a sudden, the electricity consumed, by the heat pump, is not due to cheap renewable
energy but due to a case, where up regulation has been activated. So, even though the
CO2 emission intensity a given hour, is low and the MPC signals to start the heat pump,
this action should be reconsidered, as this may not be the case, if up regulation has been
announced. Likewise, if the MPC signals to wait, it is worth reconsidering that thought
too, even though the CO2 emission intensity a given hour, is high. If down regulation has
been announced, it may be better to use that electricity, as this will represent power that
has been produced by uncontrolled sources (wind and solar) or a lack of demand, and is
therefore likely more green than any future suggested production by the MPC. On top
of that, the down regulation prices are lower than spot prices, so there is an economic
incentive too. The economic potential from this is beyond the scope of this paper.
In addition to having a very decent MPC to minimize the CO2 emissions based on hourly
average CO2 emissions intensities, it should be considered to add in a bidding strategy
too. If the electricity provider has enough customers with an MPC controlled heat pump
(minimum 10 MW installed capacity), this provider can offer that capacity in the regu-
lating power market. There are, roughly, two ways to have regulating resources on the
demand side.

needs to start. This will rarely happen, but the extreme case needs to be considered.

Discussion
In this section a comparison of the buildings, flexible consumer, a critic of the emission
calculation method as well as further investigation of the model is discussed.
8.1
Comparison of the Building Types
The implementation of MPC can make considerable CO2 emission improvements for all
the three building types with floor heating. Recall the results from Table 7.2, the office
building show the greatest improvements with 13.35% CO2 emission reduction, during
stationary temperature settings. This is not a setting, that would be set for an office
building, since nobody is going to sleep there anyway, so it may even be set even lower
than 18 °C and start earlier than 6pm and end later than 6am. The reason why the office
building is a better option than the commercial building, is the thicker insulation in the
walls and floor. Generally, bigger buildings are better, due to the higher floor area to
wall area ratio, which means a smaller area the heat can escape through compared to the
volume that stores the heat. The family house is the weakest option of the three with
11.95% CO2 emission reduction, which is still a great improvement. The family house is
the case that has been investigated the most, even though it leads to the least reduction
in CO2. The reason for this choice, is that it is easier to relate to and most likely the most
correct model. It is a very simple model and the results are simulated, so it will of course
be necessary in the long term to demonstrate the potential of real buildings. However,
a stochastic simulation is accounted for and noise is taken into account, still it cannot
cover the unforeseen events in a real building with users. For example, office buildings
usually consist of several storeys, and this research only applies to one storey buildings.
Secondly, a commercial building can be many things, with many different purposes, and
is therefore not easy to relate to either.
All the buildings modelled and estimated are buildings of the future, all very well insu-
lated and above average in the different categories. The family house is designed after
the BR18 (building regulation requirements for 2018), this means the houses are very well
insulated in relation to an average house in Denmark in 2018.
Housing with CPR registered persons in 2018 were 2,679,112 buildings [Sta18b]. Where
28,100 homes were in 2018 construction completed [Sta18a]. This concludes that only 1
% of the building stock is replaced annually, this means that even in 50 years the results
would not be particular representative. And still some houses are never renewed. To
make the results more representative the family house should have been designed after
perhaps the building regulations from 1985. If time and resources were limitless, test
houses should have been used to make the model and results more representative.

Well insulated houses have greater heat capacity and you can turn down the heat for a
long time. So the results for typical older family houses would be different. The flexibility
would be less since they have a greater heat loss and the heating of the house can not be
postponed as long as in a BR18 house.
The purpose was of this thesis was not to simulate the Danish building condition, but to
use buildings in calculations and illustrate differences between different types of buildings:
family houses, office buildings and commercial buildings.
8.2
Flexible Consumer
Flexibility has a certain value. How is the value of flexibility determined? And how can
it be used? The value of flexibility is determined by the amount of energy that can be
shifted in time, prices within markets and by the scope of how the mentioned can be align
with each other. Flexibility is very valuable when there is uncertainty about the future.
As mentioned, floor heating system is clearly the best option for the family house. In
the radiator case, a CO2 emission reduction of 19% is reached in the case where the
temperature is allowed to go up to 25 degrees. If this is limited to 22 degrees, only 3%
CO2 emissions can be reduced. For the floor heating case, a CO2 emission reduction of
33% is reached for both cases of maximum temperature settings. The reason why the
result does not vary for the floor heating case, is that the floor needs a lot of heat in order
to raise its temperature to 25 °C,
It is no news, that flexibility is very valuable. On a large scale, only electric boilers in
the demand sector, are used for this purpose. This can be done because there are always
other sources of heat, like gas boilers. So the electric boiler only becomes active, when the
electricity prices are low enough to compete with the gas price, or when down regulation
is announced [Ene18a]. To the knowledge of the authors of this study, the concept has
not been applied to heat pumps on a larger scale. With the implementation of MPC, it is
possible to do without compromising the temperature in the house, and in a combination
with a CO2 emission intensity forecast, the overall emissions become very low.
As mentioned, a solution could be storage that can store more heat than the floor can do.
For example is this MPC only made for floor or radiator heating, but it can be extended
to hot water consumption too. This is however more difficult to predict, but there is a
potential for the MPC here too. For many heat pump installations, an accumulation tank
is also installed, which could be a solution.
8.3
Criticism of the Emission Calculation Methods
The CO2 emissions are first calculated using the average CO2 emission intensities during a
given hour. For cases, bidding into the regulation power market on down regulation where
the real wind power production is higher than the forecast, it is assumed to be completely
green energy. There are arguments for not using either of those methods. The average
==PAGE:137==
8.3 Criticism of the Emission Calculation Methods
119
CO2 emission intensities do not reflect the CO2 emissions related to a change in demand.
The change in demand will be described by the CO2 emissions coming one generator,
the so called marginal generator. This is most likely the most expensive generator, as
all the cheaper ones will run on full load already. This measure is called the marginal
CO2 emission intensity. Second, the assumption that the electricity offered during down
regulation should be completely green is justified by the quote
”Aktiveringen af elpatronerne sker oftest i situationer, hvor afvigelser i vindproduktionen i
forhold til vindprognosen forårsager nedregulering i elsystemet.” [Ene10]. Translated into
english, it means that the electric boilers on the demand side in the regulation power
market are most often activated when there is too much wind energy compared to the
forecasted production of wind energy. Whether that will be the case or not, is not certain.
There are many regulations that make it less transparent. For example, there are times
where the wind farms are paid to shut down the production will Energinet pay wind
farms to shut down their production to keep the price for electricity up and attractive
[Ene17a]. Wind farms do not have big interests in selling their electricity for free. If this
statement is assumed to be true, it can only be true for a certain amount of buildings.
There is only a certain amount of excess electricity available, so if scaled up, there will be
a so called marginal building, in which the electricity cannot be assumed to be green any
longer. Since there already is installed 500 MW (in 2016) of electric boilers to consume
the excess production of electricity [Ras16]. However, the heat pumps can be argued to
be prioritized higher than the electric boilers, since the COP factor is 3-5 times higher
depending on the temperature. For electric boilers the COP factor can never exceed 1,
whereas it in heat pumps can vary a lot depending on quality, evaporating and condensing
temperature, but it is often seen between 3 and 5. Therefore the heat pumps should be
used rather than the electric boilers.
So, what is the actual CO2 emission from starting up a heat pump? The most reflective
value of this might be the Marginal CO2 emissions, which reflects the CO2 emission
related to the marginal generator. That is the one generator, that will be influenced by
the start up of that heat pump. The company, Tomorrow, has developed a methodology
on this matter, showing that about 80% of the marginal CO2 emissions in DK2 actually are
related to import from Sweden, which mainly consists of nuclear power [Cor18]. Therefore,
the marginal CO2 emission in DK2 is actually much lower than the average CO2 emission
intensity on average.
The marginal CO2 emissions can be expected to be the actual CO2 emissions for starting
up a heat pump at a given point. The question is, whether that is the right measure
in order to affect the market.
If the consumers shift their consumption to low CO2
emission periods, it may not cause any CO2 emission reduction. However, if the electricity
producers start to expect the consumers to consume their electricity in low CO2 emission
periods, there is a chance that they will reduce the production of electricity in the high
CO2 emission periods. There is also the question of market power, i.e. if the moved
demand is so great that it has a strong influence on CO2 emissions. This is probably
partly covered by using marginal CO2 emissions, but not entirely, because if sufficiently
moved, it will also affects the margin.
The results from this study are given under the conditions present from March 2017 to
March 2018. When more wind occurs, the flexibility will be even more important and it
will be possible to save relatively more CO2 emissions as a flexible consumer. Therefore,
flexibility will become even more important in the future!

8.4
Further Investigation
As of today, the model is adequate for the purpose of this study.
There is naturally
always improvements that could be implemented. As previously mentioned the buildings
could be more specific. The family house could be divided up in multiple rooms, the office
building could be in several storeys and have walls of glass and the commercial building
could be defined with a purpose and then be dimensioned for this purpose.
Another point of interest in further investigations could be to simulate data in scenarios
with a higher proportion of wind energy production. The expected result of this would
probably be that the savings by being flexible enough would increase.
The following subsections present some other actions that could be taken into account in
a further investigation.
8.4.1
Inputs for the Model
As discussed previously the model can reach more and more realistic results by moving
towards a more realistic simulation setup. A series of different setup options for the model
was presented in Section 6.3.2. The model is fed with climate forecasts in the form of
both solar radiation and ambient temperature forecasts. The model is modelled with a
setup option of ”none” for users. Finally, the penalty in this case CO2 is setup to different
forecasts. The impact of the different settings and comparing the output of the different
CO2 forecasts gave an estimation of the effect of using perfect forecast in comparison to
using other forecasts.
To make the model even more realistic, it could take residents into account. Thereto, ac-
count for when opening a window and have fluctuating temperature in the building/room.
If there is enough ground for it, it will be even more attractive to implement an MPC
in one or more test houses with representative families to acquire real world data, with
sensors monitoring climate and penalty (CO2).
Another important thing to consider is the heat pump efficiency.
In the model used
for this study, the efficiency only varies with the ambient temperature. In reality, the
compressor in the heat pump will have a nominal power, at which the efficiency is highest.
This is usually at around 70% of the maximum power. Beyond and before this point, the
efficiency will be decay. Therefore, this aspect could be interesting for the MPC to take
into account. It is obviously a bad idea to be running at low speeds, like the controller
without an MPC often will do in the simulations in this study. It might on the other
hand, also be a bad idea to run at high speeds like it is often done in the cases with either
perfect or realistic forecasts.
8.4.2
Reversible Heat Pumps
In family houses, air condition is rarely something people prioritize in a country like Den-
mark, since natural ventilation, opening doors and windows, is often good enough and
gives reasonable comfort. Air conditioning is therefore often not a necessity for family
houses. In offices and commercial buildings there are other standards, which makes the
==PAGE:139==

reversible heat pump an attractive topic for further investigation. According to Danish
Working Environment Authority the general temperature should be kept at 20-22°C un-
der normal climate and working conditions during sedentary work and must not exceed
25°C [Dan08]. To make the model more sensible an air condition aspect should have been
included, this could be done with a reversible heat pump.
Using heat pumps for cooling in the summer time will increase the overall energy con-
sumption in Denmark. Therefore it is not encouraged by the authorities. Sparenergi.dk,
is the homepage of the Danish Energy Agency for advising consumers on energy savings,
on this web page cooling is not mentioned. The receiver of this web page are consumers.
For office buildings it is often another matter, because the well insulated buildings with
glass facades often require cooling.
For commercial buildings the need for cooling will depend on the use of the building-
some industrial processes will require cooling but most often cooling will not be required.
8.4.3
Implementation of Barry The Future Electricity Provider
The price of electricity fluctuates over the hours, most pay a fixed average price, composed
in different packages and with fees written in small print. Among others Barry, which is
a new electricity provider, offers power to the purchase price, thereto the ability to follow
the electricity price and amount of CO2 emissions in the electricity for a given hour, hour
by hour on your smartphone.
Perhaps, it is possible to combine for instance Barry and the live update of the spot
prices and the CO2 intensity of the electricity with the model. This could lead to a CO2
reduction and possibly a reduction of the electricity bill. Thereto, a pad on the shoulder
after choosing to push your laundry cycle to a cheaper CO2 emission intensity hour, this
is possible because of a conscience choice based on knowledge on how green the electricity
is at the given hour.

that, the use of the marginal CO2 emission intensity should be investigated and a forecast
related to that value should be implemented to gather more accurate measures of the CO2
emission reductions.
