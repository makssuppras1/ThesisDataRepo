Abstract
The aim of this master thesis is to identify and develop approaches to utilize alarm data
from an alarm management system. The main objective is to improve throughput, safety,
quality and to enable continuous improvements in the ROCKWOOL Group.
Prior to the digital revolution and modern controls (e.g., the SCADA system), the addition
of new alarms to an alarm management system was limited due to space availability and
costs. Consequently, each alarm was carefully evaluated and selected. However, this matter
was drastically changed after the digital revolution in the 1980s, where both the limitation
and cost of additional alarms were almost non-existing, and the number of alarms has in-
creased ever since. The ROCKWOOL production plant in Cigacice in Poland possesses a
considerable number of sensors that create alarm data to monitor the production processes.
These available alarm data have been identified to hold great potential when utilized to get
production insights for systematic problem solving and maintenance tasks.
The combination of cheap sensors, no associated cost of additional alarms, and no guide-
line for when alarms should be configured resulted in over-configurations of alarms in alarm
management systems. This caused an overflood of alarm occurrences, which operators did
not have the ability and time to react upon, which has been identified as the case for ROCK-
WOOL in production line 12 in Cigacice. This drastically decreases the eﬀiciency of the
alarm management system, as it gets more diﬀicult to react during an abnormal situation.
A concern is the safety issues both employees and the plant may experience, leading to
stress and accidents. Furthermore, the alarm overflood can lead to unplanned downtime,
equipment overpressure, and failure, affecting both the throughput and quality.
This study found that in order to have an ideal utilization of the alarm data, initiatives to
reduce noise and non-critical alarms must be completed first. The present study revealed
that the clean-up could be initiated by alarm re-configurations, supported by visualizations
of alarm data in a Power BI report, that is developed incorporation with the envisioned users.
Once the alarm data is cleaned from noise and only critical alarms are apparent, utilization
of the data is possible with systematic problem-solving sessions or continuous improvement
initiatives. The visualizations will enable industrial practitioners, with and without a data
scientist background, to identify patterns and trends in the alarm data. The acquired and
extended knowledge from the visualizations will help to identify the root cause of a problem,
plant correlations, alarm correlations, and the effect of an initiated action. Thus, improving
the daily operations of production line 12 and the associated employees working conditions.
According to literature reviews and experts in the plant in Cigacice, the implementation of
the findings of the present study could lead to a 5-15% reduction in maintenance costs, as
well as an increase of 3-5% throughput. Thus, an eﬀiciently working alarm management
system could result in a safer work environment for the operators, improved throughput and
quality, and savings for the plant.

Report Structure
This master thesis report is structured and divided into 17 sections, as shown in figure 1.
The structure of the report is ordered logically, where each of the sections is linked. The
design and structure will guide the reader through the report and enable the reader to main-
tain the understanding of the project’s process as flow is established with a coherent text.
As depicted in figure 1, the report initiates with a description of the origination of the
project and an explanation of the benefits related to an eﬀicient alarm management system.
This section links down to the second chapter, which describes the purpose of this master
thesis with the corresponding project objective and the problem statements. This chapter
is followed by an introduction to the limitations of this master thesis. Afterward, in the
fourth section, a description of the history behind the ROCKWOOL Group (RG) is given
with the economic key numbers, the business areas in the RG, the purpose and strategy
of the RG, and lastly, the ROCKWOOL Operational Excellence (ROPEX) team where
this project originates from. This chapter is followed by a description of the production
processes, which is split into a hot and cold end. After a comprehensive introduction and
description of the project, the RG, and its production processes, the literature review and
theory are presented. The literature review covers the methodology for how relevant and
suitable literature has been discovered. The theory uses this acquired literature and gives
a comprehensive description of it. As depicted in the figure, the theory covers the topics
of alarm management systems, human factors, visual management, LEAN, and stakeholder
management. The methodology for answering the project objective, problem statements,
and the application of the current literature and theories are discussed in the eighth chapter,
methods.
This chapter is followed by the analysis, which investigates the current state
situation in the factory in Cigacice (CIG) and their alarm management system. Moreover,
this chapter also investigates and identifies the key stakeholders. Based on the results from
the analysis, the 10. chapter is presented. During this chapter, a comprehensive description
is given of how alarm data can be utilized in the form of visualizations. Based on these
visualizations, scenarios are made in chapter 11, which aims to describe the intended future
use of them. Afterward, in the 12. chapter, the costs, benefits, and risks related to the above
recommendations are identified and discussed. All the gathered knowledge from the previous
chapters will lead down to the 13. section, which presents a framework for how alarms can
be utilized in all RG’s manufacturing facilities. Later in the next chapter, an implementation
plan for Cigacice production plant 12 (CIG12) will be presented along with the perspective of
change management. This chapter is followed by the chapter discussion where the problem
statements are answered and discussed. The second last chapter concludes the master thesis
by answering the project objective. Finally, in the last chapter (future work), suggestions
are given to the RG for how they can extend the work of this master thesis.

rooms. The operators are presented with alarms from an alarm management system on
WinCC screens that indicates active alarms in different plants.
1.1
Back to the Future
The ongoing project “Back to the Future” from the RG uses three production sites as
pilot plants where two of them are located in Germany in Gladbeck (GLA) and Neuburg
(NEU) and one in Poland, Cigacice (CIG). The projects will, as a start, be performed
and implemented on the three production sites, afterwards more complete solutions will be
aimed towards all the production sites in the RG. First run of the project was, amongst
others, to investigate the alarm management system on the production lines where the
problem of too many alarms was identified. Too many alarms on the WinCC screen that
are red can be overwhelming to the operators and thus reduce the reaction time. A method
was introduced to re-categorize the alarms into warnings and messages, which will allow
for a more significant process understanding in the control rooms to ensure better time
management. Furthermore, user-friendly functions were created in the alarm management
system, which created solutions for the operator to give direct feedback regarding the event
data in the system, which were a direct alternative to that time’s current post-it management.
The project objective to reduce some events from alarms to messages and warnings will
continue on an ongoing basis.

1.2
From Back to the Future to this Master Thesis Project
After the first “clean up” of alarms in June 2020, the ”Back to the Future” project group
did not see a tendency of performance improvements in the main KPIs for the production,
as initially expected. Furthermore, reflections upon the lack of alarm data in the daily con-
tinuous improvements were brought to light. Questions were then raised on how a alarm

could be utilized better and create greater transparency for the data, allowing for a more
significant process understanding on shop floor that could lead to continuous improvements.
These questions were the start of this master thesis project on how to combine an alarm
management system with RG’s wish for daily continuous improvements. This master thesis
project will, from this starting point, use the site in CIG, Poland, production line 12 as a
case study.
1.3
Potential Gains of an Eﬀicient Alarm Management System
The number of configured alarms per employee has increased significantly over the years due
to a missing guideline and standard for configuration and the easy and inexpensive process
of configuring an alarm. The results of the inconsistency of alarm configurations and missing
guidelines leads to floods of alarms that indicate the status rather than abnormal situations.
Hence, more alarms are created than one can understand and react upon. This reduces the
eﬀiciency of the alarm system as it gets more diﬀicult to react to abnormal situations due
to a high number of alarm occurrences.
Numerous industries have paid a high cost for the above-mentioned ineﬀiciencies, as it has
lead to significant accidents and incidents. The Abnormal Situation Management Consor-
tium has estimated that over 20 billion dollars per year are lost due to abnormal situations
(for example, accidents, unplanned downtime, etc.) in the US alone. The ineﬀiciency of
the alarm system plays a significant part in this loss as the abnormal situations could be
mitigated or avoided with a well-functioning and eﬀicient alarm management system. It is,
however, much more complicated to identify the savings after an alarm management system
improvement, as there are numerous contributing factors related to the plant performance.
An eﬀicient and well-functioning alarm management system focuses more on preventing and
reducing the effect of abnormal situations rather than improving production throughput.
Nevertheless, an eﬀicient alarm management system will increase the operator’s ability to
react to abnormal situations and thus reduce and prevent their consequences. As the equip-
ment encounters less overpressure and stress, the number of breakdowns decreases. This
will consequently save money as plant upsets, incidents, and shut-downs are prevented, or
their intensity or duration are diminished (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).
The savings are among other things from reduced maintenance, costs, lower insurance rates,
increased quality, improved operations, etc. It has been proven from general alarm improve-
ment results that plants see between 5%-15% reduction in unplanned maintenance costs
and that European manufacturing companies see 20%-30% reduction in their risk operation
insurance as a result of the implementation of the Engineering Equipment Materials Users’
Association (EEMUA) 191 alarm improvement guideline. This guideline describes, among
other things, how to accomplish an eﬀicient alarm management system. Moreover, it has
been documented that 3%-5% of throughput is lost due to plant upsets and other incidents.
Investigations of the incidents have shown that a considerable part of the abnormal situations
and thus the cost of them are unnecessary and avoidable. Hence, it is savable if continuous
improvements are made to the alarm management system and the policies surrounding it
(Rothenberg, 2018).

4.1
The ROCKWOOL Group A/S
The foundation of the RG was laid in 1909 as H.J. Henriksen & V. Kähler I/S by Henrik
Johan Henriksen and Valdemar Kähler. The company’s operations included gravel, marl,
and lignite activities, which were reformed in 1937 by Gustav Kähler, who initiated the
production of aerated concrete and rock wool, from where the company got its current name
from. The production originated in its first factory in 1937 in Hedehusene, Denmark, where
the headquarter is located today(ROCKWOOL, 2021b), (Berlingske, 2012).
From World War II and up until today, the RG keeps finding new innovative ways of
producing, developing, and expanding its business areas which have resulted in a strong
market position. As the market position grew, it was vital that the company could keep
up with the demand. Consequently, the business was expanded geographically in its first
non-Scandinavian subsidiary in Germany in 1954, and the geographical expansion has ever
since continued. In 1962, Kähler and Henriksen agreed to split the company. The Kähler
family continued with the rock wool activities in RW, whereas most of the other activities
were continued by the Henriksen family in Henriksen and Henriksen A/S. After the division
in the company, RW has continued to utilize the properties of rock wool, which have ex-
panded their product portfolio and business areas. In 1976 the RW International A/S (the
RG) was founded as a holding company for the entire Group and its different business areas.
In the late 1980s, the RG became a global player in the insulation business as the Group
also expanded to Russia and Asia. In 1996, the RG went from a privately held company
to a publicly-traded company as RG got listed on the Copenhagen Stock Exchange, (Geday
and Dietrich, 2014), (ROCKWOOL, 2021b), (Berlingske, 2012).
Today, the RG is a leading manufacturer of stone wool solutions within buildings insulation,
insulation for process industries, marine and offshore, customized solutions for industrial
applications, wall and facade systems, acoustic ceilings, horticultural substrate solutions,
engineered fibers solutions, noise and vibration control.
The RG addresses modern soci-
ety’s challenges by enriching modern living. This is accomplished by its five business areas
(brands): RW, Rockfon, Rockpanel, Lapinus, and Grodan. A description of these businesses
will be given in a later section.
The RG employs more than 11,700 people worldwide in 39 countries and currently has

• Thermal properties – The stone wool is engineered to reduce airflow and heat flow
which provide optimal indoor climate and ensures that the buildings remain warm
in cold areas.
Moreover, the thermal properties of stone also protect people and
properties from mold and other fungi. The thermal properties reduce heating, cooling,
and ventilation costs and hereby also the building’s carbon footprint (ROCKWOOL,
2021f), (ROCKWISE, 2021a).
• Acoustic properties – The stone wool and, hereby, the insulation has a high density,
which makes it resistant to airflow and exceptional at noise reduction and sound absorp-
tion. Thus, the products have a positive impact on the physiology, learning capacity,
and social behavior of people (ROCKWOOL, 2021f), (ROCKWISE, 2021a).
• Water properties – The stone wool can be engineered to repel or absorb water, depend-
ing on its application of it.
• Fire resilience – Stone wool is fire resistant and can resist temperatures up to 1,000 de-
grees Celsius. Consequently, it helps to contain the fire and prevents it from spreading
the fire and toxic smoke (ROCKWOOL, 2021f), (ROCKWISE, 2021a).

ROPEX
ROCKWOOL Operational Excellence (ROPEX) is a department based on Lean principles
and is located in Hedehusene, Denmark, and currently consists of 16 team members, whereas

capability to learn from the different employees, projects, and factories. In order to ensure

sharing is on the local Lean/ROPEX manager). In this manner, the employees are empow-

company (Intranet, 2021).
The RG plants and oﬀices are classified and part of different groups called an operating com-

plant or oﬀice. There is a total of 18 OPCO’s, and for example, the plant in CIG that this
master thesis will be focussing on is with 11 other factories and oﬀices part of the OPCO
in central-east Europe. Each OPCO division has ROPEX coaches who set the direction for
the Lean/ROPEX manager at the specific plant/oﬀice. It is, however, the ROPEX team
that pushes material out to the OPCO ROPEX coach (Casparij and Raben, 2021).

are applied to the factory. If a work package or best practice does not exist on the exact
problem/potential, a package will then be created. Afterward, a framework is made which
can be applied by the different equipment’s or plants (Casparij and Raben, 2021).

5 Manufacturing Processes
The on line production can be split into two sections: the hot end (red) and the cold end
(blue), which is illustrated in figure 5.1. The hot end handles the processes from the charg-
ing to the curing oven whereas, the cold end handles the processes from the cooling to the
packaging. Thus, the hot end turns the raw materials into cured wool, whereas the cold end
turns the cured wool into either finished products or semi-finished products. All the on line
production processes differ and are adjusted according to the individual customer order and
what the product is intended to be used for. A specific customer order could, for example,
differ in density and size.
Consequently, it results in flexible production lines which are
capable of producing various products according to specific customer requirements (ROCK-
WOOL, 2021i), (ROCKWOOL, 2021h). The two subsections below will give a description
of the processes in the hot end and the cold end, respectively.
5.1
Hot End
This sub-section will give a description of the manufacturing processes in the hot end, which
are the charging, melting, spinning, layering, compression, and curing processes.
5.1.1
Charging
The charging process is also called material handling and is the first process. During this
process, raw materials (volcanic stones) and materials such as slag (recycled material from
the steel industry) and up-cycled secondary raw materials are delivered to the site and fed
to the indoor silos by enclosed conveyors. Hence, creating a feeding system to the following
process (melting) (ROCKWOOL, 2021h), (Discovery and Science, 2012).
5.1.2
Melting
During the melting process, the raw material and the other mentioned materials are mixed
with coal, natural gas, and combustion air in a cupola furnace. Coal and natural gas are
necessary in order to melt the mix of materials, which happens at a temperature around
1,500 degrees Celsius. Thus, recreating a volcanic process. Figure 5.2 illustrates the melted

Control Room
Both the hot and cold end has a dedicated control room which is located next to the processes.
The control room consists of one to two control operators (dependent on the production
line and site). The control room operators monitor the processes in the specific end via
a SCADA (Supervisory Control And Data Acquisition) system.
A SCADA system is a
collection of software and hardware components, which allows for observation and control
of production processes and their components. The SCADA system collects the real-time
data from the plant floor devices (for example, a pump or a valve) and transfers it to the
PLC’s (Programmable Logic Controller) (REALPARS, 2021). The PLC is designed for the
automation and control of a process, for example, the opening and closing of a valve or the
control of temperature or pressure. Thus, performing tasks at pre-defined setpoints or times
(defined by the program). The first step in the PLC is the input scan, which detects the
state of all input devices that are connected to the PLC. The second step is the program
scan, which scans the pre-defined program. The last step is the output scan, which, based
on the input and the pre-defined program, activates the programmed action. Activation of
an action could, for example, be the opening of a valve or the increase in temperature in a
process.
The PLC also communicates and interacts with a system of networked devices, such as an
HMI (human-machine interface). An example of an HMI is a screen or tablet. The HMI
software interprets and displays the data, allowing the control room operators to monitor,
control, and react to abnormal events. Thus, the HMI works as the link between the PLC
and the user. The main difference between the SCADA system and the HMI is that the
remote SCADA system is used to collect and communicate data, whereas the local HMI does
the same, but in addition, it connects the control room operator to the plant processes. This
is done by the visualization of data, enabling a better understanding and control. Additional
software is usually needed to program the HMI, such as the WinCC software, which the RG
uses (LOGIMATIC, 2020), (DOSupply, 2019). Figure 5.5 illustrates a WinCC interface in
a RG production.

7.1.1
Terms and Definitions
Within the area of alarm management systems follows a wide series of essential terms to
understand the different events in the alarm management system and the associated condi-
tions. Some of these will be presented and described below. The words presented are the
ones that were widely used in the articles found during the literature review and used in
this master thesis.
• Alarm flood: A condition where the alarm rate is greater than what the operator can
manage effectively. According to DS/EN 62682:2015, an alarm flood occurs when there
is more than 10 alarm per 10 minutes per operator.
• Nuisance alarm: A nuisance alarm (also known as bad actor alarm) is an alarm that is
categorized as meaningless, as it does not require a specific action or response from an
operator. Hence, nuisance alarms do not affect the process even when they are ignored
and not acknowledged by operators (EEMUA, 2013), (Rothenberg, 2018), (Hollifield
and Habibi, 2006). The alarm flood of nuisance alarms creates a “cry wolf” effect,
which can lead to a lack of confidence in the alarm systems among the operators.
Consequently, a critical alarm may be overlooked (Wang et al., 2016).
• Chattering alarm: A chattering alarm (also known as a cycling alarm) is a nuisance
alarm that continuously (at least three times in one minute) transitions between the
alarm state and the normal operating state (Rothenberg, 2018), (Wang et al., 2016).
These alarms are not cleared or reacted upon by the operators and are generated
due to random noise and disturbance on process variables that are configured for the
alarms (EEMUA, 2013). Chattering alarms are the ones that contribute to the overall
alarm flood. An analysis was compiled by Bill Hollifield and Eddie Habibi, where 75
alarm systems were analyzed. Table 7.1 shows the results, (DS/EN/62682:2015, 2015),
(Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).

• Fleeting alarm: A fleeting alarm is an alarm that transitions between an active state

7.1.2
Basics of an Alarm Management System
According to the Danish Standard DS/EN 62682:2015, an alarm (correct alarm) is defined
as “audible and/or visible means of indicating to the operator an equipment malfunction,
process deviation, or abnormal condition requiring a timely response”. Hence, alarms control
the production steps and indicate whenever there is an abnormal situation that can either
damage the safety of the workers, the production equipment, or the product quality (Melhem
et al., 2017).
Alarms can be managed by an alarm management system, which is “the
collection of hardware and software that detects an alarm state, communicates the indication
of that state to the operators, and records changes in the alarm state”(ANSI-ISA, 2016).
When an alarm is detected by the alarm management system, it is presented to the operator
in an audible or visual form. After the occurrence of the alarm, the operator can respond
in two ways:
– The operator takes corrective actions to bring the state of the alarm back to normal.
An example of an action could be the adjustment of control parameters (Hu et al.,
2018a).
– The operator acknowledges the alarm as a nuisance alarm and does not take any
corrective actions (Hu et al., 2018a).
All the data regarding the alarms and operator response/acknowledgment can be stored in
an Alarm & Event database. Every alarm in the database is accompanied by a set of at-
tributes to identify, describe, and locate the alarm. Figure 7.1 illustrates a set of frequently
used attributes that are part of the alarm management system and the Alarm & Event
database (Hu et al., 2018a), (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).

7.1.3
The Importance of an Eﬀicient Alarm Management System
An alarm system is a vital part of a safe and eﬀicient manufacturing plant (Bristol, 2001).
Some of these reasons are listed below:
– The alarm management system can detect errors and near misses so an operator can
take corrective actions in order to get the state of the alarm back to normal. Subse-
quently, the system can increase safety and prevents accidents (Wang et al., 2016).
– The alarm management system can maintain the eﬀiciency of the manufacturing plant,
as a need for maintenance of a process or machine is detected. Thus, an operator is
informed so that corrective actions can be taken in order to prevent the process from
shutting down (Wang et al., 2016).
– The alarm management system can monitor the right conditions for the manufacturing
equipment and processes to ensure the desired product quality output (Wang et al.,
2016).
However, the advantages and the goal of an alarm management system can be diﬀicult
to exploit if alarms are raised too frequently as it will reduce the ability of operators in
the control room to manage the abnormalities (Melhem et al., 2017).
The alarm flood
is, unfortunately, a well-known problem in industrial alarm management systems, which
decreases the performance and the functionality of the system, as the operators receive
more alarms than they can handle (Wang et al., 2016), (Melhem et al., 2017). This was
also evident in the analysis (compiled by Bill Hollifield and Eddie Habibi) mentioned in
the previous section. As a result of the alarm flood, the operators can miss critical alarms,
which can lead to catastrophic damage to the workplace and hereby affect the workers
and equipment.
As stated by Gupta, Giridhar, Venkatasubramanian, and Reklaitis, an
eﬀicient alarm management system must detect and inform the operators immediately about
abnormalities and not mislead, distract or flood the operators with alarms (Gupta et al.,
2013). In order to help achieve this, a variety of industry standards and guidelines (such
as DS/EN 62682:2015, ANSI/ISA-18.2:2009, and EEMUA-191:2013) have been developed.
These standards guide the design, implementation, and maintenance of alarm management
systems. Moreover, they contain methods and tools to improve the alarm monitoring (Hu
et al., 2018a). Hollifield and Habibi have listed seven steps to achieve a highly effective alarm
management system, which has been highly recognized. These seven steps will be presented
and described below.
==PAGE:47==
7.1 Alarm Management Systems

1. Step 1 – Develop, Adopt, and Maintain an Alarm Philosophy
Before designing a new system or adjusting an already existing system, the first step
is to develop an alarm philosophy. An alarm philosophy is a document that contains
the objective and requirements of the alarm management system and serves as a guide-
line for the development, implementation, and maintenance of an alarm management
system that the company wants/has. Such a guideline could, for example, be when an
alarm can be added to the system and a detailed overview of steps that an operator
must take when responding to an alarm. Thus, helping an operator to take the right
action at the right time (DS/EN/62682:2015, 2015), (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).
2. Step 2 – Benchmark alarm management system
In order to improve the alarm management system, it is necessary to analyze the
system. The company needs to understand its starting point. Hence, a baseline re-
port is needed to enable improvements towards industry standards and best practices
(Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).
3. Step 3 – Perform Bad Actor Alarm Resolution
In this step, the bad actor alarms (nuisance alarms) are identified and configurated to
improve the performance of the system (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006). As mentioned
in the previous section, the bad actor alarms account for more than 70% of the total
alarm occurrences due to a misconfiguration. By ranking the frequency of the alarms,
the bad alarms will quickly be identified, and a correction of them will hereby reduce
the total alarm occurrences drastically (Hollifield, 2016).
4. Step 4 – Perform Alarm Documentation and Rationalization
In this step, a comprehensive review of the configuration and purpose of every ex-
isting alarm in the alarm management system is made to ensure that they compile
with the principles in the alarm philosophy. This is called Alarm Documentation and
Rationalization (D&R) and is also known as an Alarm Objective Analysis. For an al-
ready existing system, the D&R involves a re-examination of the alarms in the system,
whereas, for a completely new system, the D&R is used as a method to determine the
proper alarm configuration. The result of a D&R are alarms that are either added,
kept, modified or deleted and consistency between the alarms and the alarm philosophy
(Hollifield, 2016), (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).
5. Step 5 – Implement Alarm Audit and Enforcement Technology
After improving and rationalizing the alarm management system, it is important that
its configuration does not change over time without authorization. As the alarm set-
tings are easy to change and can be changed by various sources (operators, technicians,
engineers, etc.), it will quickly lose the benefits from improvements and rationalization.
Thus, authorization is essential to avoid uncontrolled change (Hollifield, 2016), (Holli-
field and Habibi, 2006).
6. Step 6 – Implement Advanced Alarm Management
In this step, advanced alarm capabilities are implemented to achieve a particular per-
formance and manage specific issues. An example of such alarm capability is alarm
flood suppression. An alarm flood suppression temporarily eliminates the distracting
nuisance alarms, which then leaves only the relevant alarms for the operator. Thus,
ensuring eﬀicient handling of abnormal situations (Hollifield, 2016), (Hollifield and
Habibi, 2006).
7. Step 7 – Control and Maintain Your Improved System
The improved and rationalized alarm system needs to be controlled and maintained
in order to operate eﬀiciently. Hence, an ongoing analysis of the system is needed,
which for example, can include KPIs that include alarm data and systematic problem
handling (Hollifield, 2016), (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006).

7.2
Lean Philosophy
The Lean business philosophy has become an essential and important part of modern man-
ufacturing systems to ensure value and eﬀicient, driven production. In today’s industries,
many have become more dependent on manufacturing.
The associated performance and
quality play a significant part in the company’s ability to compete in the related market and
ensure profit, allowing the companies to invest in development. The Lean philosophy in the
manufacturing industry is with a combination of behavior and tools striving to eliminate
non-value adding processes, general waste types, bottlenecks, and to improve capability’s,
flow time, and communication (Hossein et al., 2015).
7.2.1
Lean Principals
The Lean thinking can be defined within the five following principles: value, value stream,
flow, pull, and perfection, according to James P. Womack and Daniel T. Jones. The princi-
ples will allow for the opportunity to eliminate waste and create eﬀiciency.
==PAGE:49==
7.2 Lean Philosophy
25
7.2.1.1
Value
The first and often considered most crucial principle is specifying the actual value. The
value can only be defined by the end customer, who pays the producer to deliver the end
product or service. When only value-adding processes and actions are strived for in the Lean
approach, it must be clear what is expected of the end product as stated in the book Lean
thinking “Providing the wrong good or service the right way is Muda (waste)” (Womack
and Jones, 2003).
7.2.1.2
Value Stream
The second principle is about identifying all the actions and tasks required to deliver a
specific product to the customer from the design phase, production, and delivery to the
customer. When such analysis has been accomplished, it will most likely show three types
of processes that either are direct value-adding for the product, non-direct value-adding
but necessary, for example, to uphold quality standards through inspections, and non-direct
value-adding with no essential purpose which should be avoided.
When making all the
process steps transparent, it allows for a better understanding and knowledge, which is an
essential key for taken the right actions towards a more Lean process (Womack and Jones,
2003).
7.2.1.3
Flow
When the first and second principles have been identified and accomplished, one should
focus on creating flow. Flow is thwarted by the concept of departmentalized thinking, where
products are handled in batches and groups by the different functions or departments. This
thinking creates waste in terms of waiting time for the different units and queuing that can
result in critical bottlenecks in the process flow. Flow can then be created by rethinking
product flow through all the value-adding steps.
It should be ensured that the path is
optimal for a balanced process flow minimizing queues and waiting time and eliminate
intermediate inventories through products teams.
Flow can be generated within all the
different functions and departments but would optimally be coordinated through the value
stream of value-adding processes (Womack and Jones, 2003).
7.2.1.4
Pull
The fourth principle is related to creating a pull flow, which means that the customer is
able to pull the product from the supplier instead of the supplier pushing the products out
with no guarantee of actual demand. The push strategy can result in great waste levels
in inventory, which can be highly expensive for the producer.
The pull strategy leaves
out the uncertainties from forecasting but can create concerns regarding the customer lead
time. These concerns can be met with the three first principles that will minimize the lead
time in all the processes from development to sales, production, and delivery with the right
approach. This will allow for a more agile and robust business towards demand variation
and can handle the pull strategy and increase customers satisfaction (Womack and Jones,
2003).
7.2.1.5
Perfection
The fifth and final principle is about striving for perfection. Through the accomplishment
of the above four principles, a number of improvements areas will come to light. These can
be seen as continuous opportunities for improvements within effort, time, space, cost, and
mistakes, but another perspective is always keeping the business processes up to date. The
perfect business today will not necessarily be a perfect business tomorrow. This is affected
==PAGE:50==
26
7 Theory
by a world that is constantly evolving and developing new demands (Womack and Jones,
2003).
7.2.2
Waste
The above described Lean principles are all striving for the elimination of the defined Lean
waste categories, also known as Muda. The waste types are all opponents to the idea of Lean
manufacturing. Eight waste groups have been identified and are described below (Hossein
et al., 2015).
– Transport - Is the transportation of products or components. The transportation can
both be seen within production facilities and between different facilities. Transporta-
tion is a non-value-adding activity and cannot be eliminated completely but should
instead be minimized as much as possible.
Transportation between plants is often
associated with high transportation costs and the situation of being more exposed for
damages in the process. Internal transportation is typically managed through a mate-
rial handling system, whose eﬀiciency is affected by transportation methods and plant
layout (Hossein et al., 2015).
– Inventory - Inventory as waste is the storage of both materials, semi-finished products,
and finished products. Inventories can occur if the production system is based on a
push strategy instead of pulling, as described in the Lean principles section above.
Suppose the demand is not equal to the produced products. In that case, inventory will
occur, which will take up expensive space areas and expose the products for situations
where they could either expire or be damaged in the waiting time. Inventory will also
occur if there is no balance in the production flow, and inventory is needed to hold
semi-finished products during the waiting time (Hossein et al., 2015).
– Motion - Motion is the waste of unnecessary motion from the involved employees
and is often also concerned with ergonomics. Unnecessary motion can be generated
by non-strategic tool placements and position by, for example, having the tools placed
far away from the process where it is needed. This will then require the employees
to move and use the extra time on non-value-adding activities and possibly create a
longer cycle time. Unhealthy motion can be just as critical if the employee is obliged
to perform unhealthy lifts, motions, and uncomfortable repetitive movements outside
healthy ergonomics advice. This can result in employee sick leaves, which can be very
expensive and increase cycle time due to missing competencies (Hossein et al., 2015).
– Waiting - Waste in terms of waiting is generated through several situations. Waiting
can be seen as a situation where no value is being generated for the manufacturing
processes. The perspective to the waiting situation can both be seen in relation to
the humans, equipment, and material/products. Each actor can either be in a waiting
position or cause situations that create waiting scenarios. For example, the equipment
can wait for materials and standstill with the result of wasting expensive equipment
resources. On the other side, the equipment can break down and create severe produc-
tion downtime, which can create waiting time for materials and humans. Waiting is
often an overlooked waste type that often comes in small insignificant cases – but when
waiting times are summed over longer periods of time, it will add up to a significant
waste type where expensive resources are wasted. To eliminate this waste type, an
eﬀicient workflow is required, which should be driven by a strong management team
(Hossein et al., 2015).

– Overproduction - Overproduction occurs when too much is produced compared to
what the customer needs. Overproduction can be caused due to large batch sizes in
the production or poor communication through the supply chain. The consequences
can, amongst others, be seen as storage costs and a decrease in productivity. If there is
no demand for the finished products, a worst-case scenario would be that the supplier
is forced to scrap the finished products. The scrap of finished products is a direct
cost waste for the materials and value-adding actions from production resources. To
eliminate overproduction, a typical approach is towards the principles of Just-In-Time
(JIT) (Hossein et al., 2015).
– Over Processing - Over processing is the waste type that describes the processes
that are performed but are not needed or required from the customers. This means
that these activities are performed even though the customers are not willing to pay
for them. Over-processing can be seen when adding to tight tolerances that are not
needed regarding the wanted quality, too advanced production methods that create
high costs, or the processing of materials or areas that cannot be seen. Too extreme
inspections processes are also often seen, which is exceeded both regarding regulations
and quality. Over-processing is the waste of the suppliers’ resources that could be used
for value-adding processes instead. Communication is the essential tool for eliminating
overprocessing and should ensure a communication loop between customer, designer,
and manufacturer (Hossein et al., 2015).
– Defects - Defects is the waste of materials that are either being scrapped or reworked
due to deviations towards the specifications. Defects can occur and be detected in sev-
eral places in the supply chain with the highest consequences as closer to the customer
the products come. If value-adding processes are kept getting added to a defective
product, more significant resource waste is created when the product is scrapped in
the end. Another severe consequence is if the customer catches the defect, this could
result in loss of customer that will not purchase the product again. The quality of
the production processes and products will help the elimination of the defects. Stan-
dardized processes can support the quality, production processes that are designed
to identify abnormalities, or Poka-Yoke solutions in the production and inspections
(Hossein et al., 2015).
– Skills - Skills is the waste type for un-utilized resources. In a production system, one
must understand the importance of employee experiences and capabilities for eﬀicient
and successful production. Skills can both be seen as competencies that the employ-
ees already possess and the opportunities for developing talents and skills that can be
achieved through training. The waste type can be eliminated by discovering competen-
cies and potential talents achieved through communication between the management
and the employee’s (Hossein et al., 2015).
When examining the 8th waste type of un-utilized human skills from the production system,
it can be considered and reflected upon which other available resources/information are not
utilized to its fullest. In today’s production industry, IT systems have become a significant
and more critical part of daily operations. The systems control, communicate and monitor
processes from material handling, alarm sensors, production processes, warehouse informa-
tion, etc. Within these systems, tons of data are stored, describing events, trends, and states
of the production system. Knowledge is power, and every data and information should be
considered an opportunity to improve the knowledge base (Hossein et al., 2015), (Womack
and Jones, 2003).

This subsection will elaborate on the matter of systematic problem solving (SPS) for the
aspects of root cause analysis and the systematic approach for standardized problem analysis.
7.2.3.1
Root Cause Analysis
As previously stated, the Lean philosophy also focuses on problem-solving. A problem can
be handled and analyzed in several ways and levels with different success outcomes.
A
problem can be solved by handling the symptoms of the problem or by identifying the
actual cause of the occurred problem and thus find a solution that will prevent the problem
from reoccurring. The root cause analysis is addressed as one of the fundamental blocks
within the Lean philosophy (Holweg and Bicheno, 2008). The root cause analysis can be
performed by applying certain techniques that this section will address. When a problem
is investigated, it is of great importance to identify the problem owner as the root cause
can be found in different places across the value chain of the problem, e.g., from customer
to delivery company to the supplier. Therefore, the solution to the root cause can not be
evaluated before the problem owner is identified.
The solution must then be considered
according to effectiveness and cost. Finlow Bates has expressed six steps for the root cause
analysis (Holweg and Bicheno, 2008):
1. Specify subject and deviation
2. Identify the direct physical cause
3. Map the events of the causes to the problem
4. Find problem owner at each stage
5. Find the point of cause to intervene for a long-term solution
6. Recognize the most cost-effective solution to the problems root cause
The 5xWhy technique will initiate the analysis for the root causes by simply asking why
a problem has appeared. It is of high importance not to direct the why’s with a negative
perspective and aimed towards human error. The robust root cause analysis aims towards
the system and why the system allows for errors. The system perspective can also be aimed
towards the solution. The solution will have the most substantial effect by achieving a Poka-
Yoke solution. Poka-Yoke translates to set up conditions that will not allow for errors in
the future and means directly mistake-proofing. By addressing the condition, the solution
will then focus on the system and environment instead of human errors. The Poka-Yoke
solution will not always be possible but should be aspired to address systems instead of
people (Holweg and Bicheno, 2008).
7.2.3.2
Standardized Problem Solving
To achieve the systematic approach for problem-solving, the tool “A3 problem solving” is
introduced. This tool allows for a standardized problem analysis incorporating the PDCA
cycle approach for: Plan, Do, Check, Act. The A3 format demands the actor to be brief
and precise, which benefits the process for addressing the essential aspect of the problem.
The documentation of the actions can also be seen as beneficial in the A3 format for sharing
learnings and for future inspection where the essential findings in the analysis are easily found.
The A3 addresses: Problem Statement, Problem Analysis, and Root Cause Countermeasures,
all according to the root cause analysis and the approach for the PDCA cycle. The systematic
approach first examines the current state of the problem and the surrounding conditions of
the problem and is afterward focusing on the future state for potential countermeasures

that will avoid reoccurrences of the problem. The PDCA cycle is then also incorporating
the “check” perspective, where potential solutions are tested and evaluated before being
accepted as a successful implementation (Holweg and Bicheno, 2008).
7.3
Visual Management
This section will describe what visual management is and how it can contribute to employee
engagement and continuous improvement. Moreover, the success factors and barriers of the
implementation and maintenance of visual management will be discussed.
Visual management is a communication tool that visually communicates information about
performance, standards, warnings, etc., in a way that requires non or a minimal amount of
training to interpret (McIntosh, 2017). These visualizations can take form as signs, markers,
labels, flowcharts (on a communication board/visual management board), check and control
sheets, light signals, etc. (Jaca et al., 2014). Thus, visual management allows for an instant
understanding of the performance of the different functions in an organization and allows
for an easy way to navigate around the organization. It is a tool within the philosophy of
Lean that connects the data/information and people in an organization and has in recent
years evolved from being just a principle for workplace design to a control and leadership
tool (Kurpjuweit et al., 2018).
It is one of the main factors contributing to employee engagement and continuous improve-
ments, as it creates ownership and drives shop floor teams to be involved in the developments
and improvements (if implemented and maintained correctly) (Bateman et al., 2016). Ad-
ditionally, it creates transparency and removes the gap between the management and the
various functions, as strategic information is visible for the whole organization. As a result,
it creates an organization with a united goal (Bititci et al., 2016), (Clarity, 2021).

Human Factors
This section will give an introduction to the human factors in control room environments
and how this affects the performance of an operator and the processes. Moreover, some
recommendations for the good cognitive design of the interfaces in the control room will be
given.
7.5.1
Human Factors and Performance in Control Rooms
Human factors (also known as ergonomics, which translates to the science of work) signifies
a wide area of things such as the physical activities and demands of a job and how the hu-
man mind understands instructions and interfaces (Berlin and Adams, 2017). With human
factors in mind, one can provide and design objects and other things for people to use with
their capabilities and limitations in mind (Rothenberg, 2018). Control room operators have
the job of monitoring processes in a plant to ensure eﬀicient and safe operations. However,
to fulfill this job, it is essential that the operator performs various psychological and physical
tasks in series and simultaneously over a period with a minimum amount of error. These
psychological tasks (also referred to as cognitive tasks) varies in complexity and frequency,
which affects the amount of stress and workload that the operator experiences (Knisely et al.,
2020).
According to the guideline EEMUA-191 and DS-EN:62682-2015, less than one alarm per
10 minutes per panel operator is acceptable, one alarm per 5 minutes per panel operator is
manageable, one alarm per 2 minutes per panel operator is over-demanding, and more than
one alarm per minute per panel operator is unacceptable. The other tasks that an operator
usually needs to perform, along with the monitoring of the alarm management system, are
also considered in these numbers with the span of control of a single operator. Thus, if an
operator may not always be in the control room, he/she is not capable of handling the same
amount of alarm load as a dedicated control room operator.
The cognitive tasks that an operator in a control room typically is performing are the de-
tection of the alarm, silencing and/or acknowledging the alarm, the visual scanning of data
and information, analyzing the process situation and root cause, decision making upon the
analysis, implementation of the decision, monitoring the process to confirm the right action
was taken to correct the situation/process, whereas the physical tasks are movements (Schu-
macher et al., 2010), (Hollifield and Habibi, 2006). Whenever an alarm occurs, and there is
an abnormal situation, the operator accumulates the knowledge from previous training and
previous experienced abnormal situations to make a decision (Nimmo, 2002).
Cognitive workload refers to the required mental effort and resources that a person needs
in order to complete a task. The cognitive workload is typically applied as an indicator to
measure the complexity of a task as it affects the ability to process information, react, and
decision making (Haapalainen et al., 2010), (Reliability, 2021). When the cognitive workload
increases excessively, so does the human error rate and accidents, which in the end can cause
fatal consequences (Young et al., 2015). Moreover, human error can also affect throughput
and the quality of the products. Although, human error can not only be caused by workload
but also by poor operator interface design, operator experience, and lack of communication.
The cognitive workload can, for example, be measured by comparing the accuracy and
timing of task completion and self-reporting (surveys, questionnaires, etc.) (McKendrick
et al., 2021). The physical workload can, however, be measured by, for example, the heart
rate variability, body temperature, and pupillometry (Shakouri et al., 2018), (Wright et al.,
2002), (Marquart and de Winter, 2015).

With the application of these psychological and physical measurements, it is possible to de-

Historically, human error has been a vital part of catastrophic incidents in, for example,

A study at 15 different

authors, therefore, requested that the management of a company with an alarm management

convenient to automate the alarm management system. Although this is not the case, and
according to Meshkati, the explanation behind this is that the designers of the automated

9.1
Data Attributes
This section will aim to give a description of the alarm attributes that are stored in the
Alarm & Event database as these attributes are the ones to be exploited. The alarm data
that this master thesis is utilizing and analyzing are categorized within descriptive alarm and
event attributes and three alarm states. These are described in the alarm data output, which
describes and tracks information about abnormalities in the production line. The incoming
alarms and events are activated when upper or lower tolerance values are exceeded. When
the alarm/event is activated, it is shown as state 1 in the Alarm & Event database. Figure
9.1 illustrates the steps during an alarm/event activation.
Figure 9.1. States of an abnormal situation (alarm, warning, message)
As illustrated, when the alarm is activated (state 1), then the alarm is either acknowledge
or unacknowledged by the operator. From here, the alarm state (state 1) can change either
==PAGE:66==
42
9 Analysis
to state 2 or state 3. State 2 is generated when the alarm turns inactive without the alarm
being acknowledged. State 2 is either caused by fleeting alarms or slow operator response,
whereas a fleeting alarm is defined in DS/EN 62682:2015 as “…short-duration alarms that
do not immediately repeat”. After the alarm turns inactive, the state must be acknowledged
by the operator before it can move back to the starting point. From state 2, alarm chatter
can appear, which is the scenario where an alarm becomes active again (state 1) before
being acknowledged by the operator as inactive multiple times in a row. DS/EN 62682:2015
defines a chattering alarm as an “alarm that repeatedly transitions between the alarm state
and the normal state in a short period of time”. State 3 describes the time stamp in the
alarm data output where an operator has acknowledged the active alarm and initiated an
action to get the conditions back to the accepted values.
When the alarms/events and hereby the states are activated, they are described in the Alarm
& Event database by data attributes. These alarm attributes can be seen in the section below
(Sartzetakis, 2021).
• Message number (Msg.Nr.): Defines the unique ID of the alarm.
• Type: Defines whether the incoming abnormal situation is an alarm, warning, or
message.
When an alarm, warning, or message is activated (state 1), it becomes
visible on the WinCC by either a red, yellow, or blue block, respectively.
• DateTime: Defines the specific timestamp of the occurrence of the abnormal situation.
• DeltaT (i.e., TimeDiff): Time difference between one state to another state.
• Area: Describes which production area the incoming alarm/warning/message comes
from.
• Plant: Describes the specific production process that the incoming alarm/warning/mes-
sage comes from. Every plant has a unique set of alarm IDs.
• Text: Defines what the problem is and is dependent on the Msg. Nr.
• Category (Cat): Defines each data entry where the alarm/event is activated and in
state 1. The activated alarm/event can be described with the following categories.
o Fleeting: If the next state is 2 (inactive) and DeltaT is between 120 seconds and
86400 seconds (24 hours expressed in seconds), then the alarm/event instance is
categorized as fleeting. An alarm/event is also categorized as fleeting if it goes
back to its normal state, without an acknowledgment, within two minutes.
o Discarded: If the next state is 3 (acknowledged) and DeltaT is less than 5 seconds,
then the alarm/event instance is categorized as discarded.
o Slow: If the next state is 3 (acknowledged) and DeltaT is greater than 3600 sec-
onds (1 hour expressed in seconds), then this alarm/event instance is categorized
as slow.
o Normal: If the next state is 3 (acknowledged) and DeltaT is between 5 seconds and
3600 seconds (1 hour expressed in seconds), then this alarm instance is categorized
as normal.
o Stale: If the next state is 2 (inactive) and DeltaT is greater than 86400 seconds
(24 hours expressed in seconds), then this alarm/event is categorized as stale.
• Response: Describes if the alarm/event is “Acknowledged” or “Unacknowledged or
Fleeting”.
This master thesis will solely focus on state 1, as it is the activation of the abnormal situations
that are interesting to consider. It would be misleading to include the remaining two states,
as it would have been seen as the abnormal situation had appeared multiple times. Moreover,
only alarms are considered and not the two other types (warning and message), as the alarms
are the disturbing critical category.

Escalation System in CIG
The site in CIG has developed its way of escalating through a post-it system. If the operator
experiences problems on the production line that they cannot find the root cause for, if there
is a quality issue, or if there are safety problems, then the issue will be written down on
a post-it and placed on the associated machine board. The post-its are color-coordinated,
where a yellow post-it represents technical problems and a red represents safety issues. The
coordinators, foreman, quality controller, and maintenance coordinator of the production
line will at the machine board then evaluate which post-its that need to be brought to
the managers’ Oobeya meeting. The initiating of escalating issues is highly valued in the
management team, as identifying issues gives an opportunity for improvements.
When a post-it reaches the Oobeya meetings, then the problem is placed on a planning board
where all the active post-its from all departments are located. The post-its are tracked on
the board based on the job’s status, next action, and the responsible and will not be removed

9.3.4
Systematic Problem Solving
To enable alarm data analysis in future SPS sessions, the current situation and setup for
the factory’s general SPS will be examined. These findings will help to identify the proper
forums and approaches to incorporate the new alarm data perspective. Furthermore, it will
be assessed how mature the organization is concerning working with data as the main driver
for understanding problems and making the right decisions.
The factory’s Lean/ROPEX manager is facilitating a weekly two-hour SPS session each Fri-
day. The session participants depend on the issues that are being identified and investigated
from the performance visualizations board at the factory. The initiating could be based on
a specific KPI which is trending negatively and where no actions have been able to stop
the trend. The SPS session is initiated to analyze and identify the actual root cause of a
problem. This is especially necessary when a problem at the site does not have an obvious
solution or if a specific problem occurs multiple times and an implemented solution is not
corrective.
The SPS session initiates by defining the actual deviation from the expected and ideal
situation. Then the team is using several tools as a funnel to isolate the actual problem
with problem-solving methods. These methods are the cause and effect approach (fishbone)
and the problem identification approach, 5xWhy. The SPS sessions include the department
manager, maintenance, quality manager, operators, and coordinators. The Lean/ROPEX
manager makes sure to invite people from outside the central problem area to give an
objective perspective to the problem-solving process. This approach creates good reflections,
especially in the 5xWhy process, as the fresh view has non-prior assumptions of the problem’s
origin. This also gives a great indication of the maturity level of Lean methodologies in the
organization.
The weekly SPS session is stored and tracked in Microsoft Planner and includes an overview
of the needed actions and the person responsible for them. These will be placed in one of the
following four categories as the SPS project is evolving: To-Dos, No action, Completed tasks,
and Tests. The storage of the SPS solution in Microsoft Teams indicates that the employees
in CIG can retrieve previous solutions and their associated processes quickly. SPS in CIG
is greatly dependent on data from the production; however, this is not the case for alarm
data. In a dialogue with the Lean/ROPEX manager in CIG, it was revealed that only 5%

9.4.1
Pareto Analysis
According to Bill Hollifield and Eddie Habibi, it is the ten most frequent message numbers
that are causing a significant fraction of the load on the alarm management system. In order
to find this fraction, a Pareto analysis has been performed. Alarm data from January 2021
to March 2021 has been analyzed to find the top contributors of alarm occurrences. During
this period, 2,796 unique alarm messages occurred, and they accounted for a total of 150,426
alarms. Figure 9.5 shows a Pareto analysis with the ranking of the most frequent to least
frequent message numbers during the assessment period. The calculations and the complete
overview of the message numbers and the cumulative percentage can be seen in the Excel
sheets ”Alarm data for Pareto” and ”Pareto analysis”.
Figure 9.5. Pareto with the most frequent to least frequent message numbers (from January 2021 -
March 2021)
Figure 9.5 illustrates that only a small amount of the message numbers accounts for a
significant load of the alarm management system.
For example, the ten most frequent
message numbers account for 20% of the entire system load. The Italian statistician Pareto
formulated the 80-20 rule, where 80% of the alarm occurrences are caused by 20% of the most
frequent message numbers (Jensen, 2012). When considering this rule, it can be determined
that 20% counts for 560 unique message numbers. The cumulative percentage of the first
560 message numbers (with the ranking most to least) is 93%, thus indicating that only 20%
of the message numbers are causing the most significant load on the system. This is also
illustrated by the Pareto analysis in figure 9.6.
It is often the case that these alarms and message numbers were not intentionally configu-
rated to occur this often. Consequently, this analysis identifies the areas where improvements
will have the most significant impact. These improvements should be made by initiating
message number re-configurations based on the investigated alarm data.
The above current state investigation for the alarm management system reveals that the
current condition is affected by a non-ideal state, according to the standards and guidelines.
It has been assessed that the initial objectives, to utilize the alarm data for improvements
in the organization as SPS, and planning and review of maintenance will only be ideal if
the alarm data is cleaned up according to the standard and the associated noise is removed.
From this point, the master thesis will aim to reach the ideal situation for the system by
utilizing the data insight, for example, the Pareto and future data interpretations. As the
alarm management system is improved continuously, it is still the goal to find approaches
to utilize alarm data for SPS and maintenance.

9.4.3
Current State of Alarm Re-configurations
This part of the analysis will examine how the re-configurations of alarms are performed
currently. Since the start of the project ”Back to the Future,” the project team has continu-
ously encouraged the operators to continuously propose alarm re-configurations, which are
then evaluated monthly by a programmer. The programmer has good production process
knowledge and can ensure that standards, safety regulations, and CE markings comply. The
proposed re-configurations are based on the operator’s general knowledge about the produc-
tion system and the knowledge associated with the criticality of the message numbers.
The re-configurations on a continuous basis did not give the aspired effect timely. As the
need for cleaned alarm data is essential in order to increase the eﬀiciency of the system and
to utilize the data (as seen in the current state analysis of the system), a one-week workshop
in CIG12 in week 19 2021 was prioritized by the members of the project ”Back to the Future.”
The aim of this visit was primarily the clean-up of message numbers, i.e., re-configuring the
alarm to either messages or warnings. Throughout the one-week workshop, operators from
every shift were involved in re-configuration sessions. During this visit, 456 message numbers
were re-configurated into warnings and messages, in which approximately 23% of the 456
message numbers were changed to messages, and approximately 77% of them were changed
to warnings. The proposed re-configurations from the operators were afterward sent to the
programmer who had to review and evaluate them.
The process of these re-configurations was as described in the previous subsection based
on gathered knowledge and experience and not data. Consequently, the risk of changing a
critical alarm is critical as the re-configurations are based on subjective views, which vary
from operator to operator. A finding related to this matter is that new employees are not able
to participate in the re-configuration competition as a significant amount of work experience
and knowledge are needed. The workshop based the re-configurations on alarms with an
occurrence of zero (from January to June 2021). As there is a total of 9,000 unique alarms,
it is implausible that an operator can remember the criticality of proposed changes to an
alarm that has not appeared this year. This can lead to the fact that a lot of time and effort
is placed towards alarms that are not disturbing the current alarm situation. Findings from
the Pareto show that it is of high relevance to focus on the top contributors, creating the
most significant effect. These considerations are also important due to the bottleneck at the
programmer, who should then focus on the evaluations with the most significant potential.
Therefore, a gap has been identified in terms of what the requests are based on, and especially
in the future when more frequent alarms need to be assessed. When these should be assessed,
it is evident that the evaluation should be supported by alarm data based on categories and
appearance that can provide the necessary information to evaluate if the message number
should be changed.

10.5
Spiral Graph
The spiral graph depicted in figure 10.12, illustrates the alarm floods during the month of
March. As previously stated, according to DS/EN 62682:2015, an alarm flood occurs when
there is more than ten alarms per 10 minutes per operator. The alarm data output is from
two control centers with two console operators in each. Therefore, this threshold has been
set to 40 alarms per 10 minutes for four operators.
Figure 10.12. Spiral graph for the month of March, showing the burst of alarm floods
As illustrated, the spiral grows clockwise and continuous in circles, where each circle repre-
sents a day, and each point in the circle represents a time instant (10 minutes in this case).
Since the alarm data is for March, 31 circles (days) are depicted in the figure. The time
series starts at 00:00:00 at the innermost circle and goes clockwise until 23:59:59 at the out-
ermost circle. The color of the points represents the number of alarms within a 10-minute
interval. If the number of alarms per 10-minute interval is equal to or greater than 40, then
the point will turn red; if not, the point will either turn green or yellow (depending on the
value). Thus, patterns can easily be identified. However, if there is a time interval with no
alarms, the point will turn white (Hu et al., 2018a), (Hu et al., 2018b).
The spiral graph has been developed in Python (see Appendix 2 for the script) based on
the following input. As the graph illustrates N days, then the time instant t most be scaled
to range [1, N . . . 86, 400] (one day expressed in seconds). Hence, the angle for each time

10.5.1
Advantages of the Spiral Graph
The spiral graph has a significant advantage when it comes to the identification of patterns
and relationships when at the same time utilizing the screen space. It supports the visual-
ization of large data sets and provides a significant amount of detail where the users can
identify time stamps and days that receive a higher amount of alarms. Additionally, it al-
lows for an exact identification of timestamps where an alarm flood starts and ends (red or
reddish areas). Comparisons between the other circles and the neighbor timestamps allow
for the detection of periodic behavior. In comparison to a heat map, the continuity of the
data (with respect to time) is not broken, which allows users to perceive the deviations from
the end of one day to the beginning of the next day. The spiral graph also provides an
insight into the condition of the alarm management system. It is evident that the current
view is affected by noise and alarm floods, which disturbs the opportunity to identify normal
alarm patterns and correlations. Therefore, the full potential of the spiral graph can only
be achieved after the aforementioned clean-up.

Concluding Remarks
It is essential to state that each of the presented visualizations has its own advantages.
However, combinations of the visualizations during either re-configurations or SPS sessions
significantly increase their benefits and effect.
The general feedback from the workshops and meetings with the stakeholders was positive
as the participants were able to interact with the user-friendly report quickly, made sugges-
tions to justifications, and also suggested other ways in which the visualizations could enable
improvements. For example, it was stated by a control room operator that the visualiza-
tion would enable them to communicate with alarm data with the maintenance team rather
than just assumptions. Another positive feedback was that the visualizations would help to
catch a problem before it escalated and would help to see if an action on an equipment was
corrective or not. The workshop participants were also very eager to start using the report
and asked for a weekly report.
All of the visualizations (besides the spiral graph) have been developed in a way that enables
easy filtration so that the users easily and quickly can distinguish between different plants,
areas, time, and the hot and cold end.
From the alarm data, it could be relevant to investigate correlations between the alarms
and the KPIs at the production site.
This could, for example, be to find a correlation
between downtime and the number of occurred alarms. However, data scientists have already
==PAGE:99==
10.6 Concluding Remarks
75
investigated this, and it was found that this was not possible to achieve. The developed model
could not identify a significant correlation due to data noise in the alarm management system.
The constant periods of alarm floods and fleeting alarms prevent a relevant correlation
analysis. Therefore, the alarm data needs to be cleaned before it can be utilized to make
this kind of correlation analysis, which this master thesis is aiming towards.
Another challenge when investigating downtime for a RW production is considering the
case of line production.
For the analysis, data of full production stops were considered.
However, due to the case of line production, the entire production line will not experience
a complete unplanned breakdown on all the equipment’s at the same time. If there is a
breakdown for one of the equipment, the previous processes at the line will lower the speed
instead of stopping and exploit the buffer stations.
A proper correlation analysis would
instead focus on downtime on each equipment that is not available at this time. Benefits
and correlations should be aimed towards production speed as this is the consequence of
equipment downtime. For the case of quality and line speed, the data registration is not
comprehensive. To investigate the correlation between the alarm data and KPIs, a setup
of OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness) data for each equipment would be beneficial.
The OEE contains quality, performance, and availability, which covers speed, scrap, and
downtime.
Setting up the OEE for each piece of equipment, the data regarding quality,
performance, and availability could then be used for the correlation analysis between the
alarm data.

11.1
Alarm Data Visualizations within Re-configurations
In the section for the gap analysis between the industry standards and the current state,
it was proven that the current alarm management system and the operators are highly
overflooded with alarms and that the operator’s response capability is highly exceeded. In
order to achieve an eﬀicient alarm management system, the first thing to do is re-configuring
the alarms into either a warning or message. The current way of doing this is by intuition
and experience. Hence, the reasoning of the re-configuration is not based on data which
can lead to critical alarms being changed to a warning or message. Consequently, this can
affect the production and the safety of the operations and operators.
Moreover, it was
found that time spent on re-configurations of the alarms did not significantly impact the
current situation for the non-ideal alarm management systems condition, which is critical
for the identified bottleneck at the evaluation process (programmer). To prevent this from
happening, the reasoning behind the re-configurations must be based on data rather than
only intuition. The visualizations can help with this by the visibility of bad actor alarms.
These visualizations are envisioned to be developed once a week and collected in a single
Power BI report.
It is recommended that the Power BI report will be available on the
computer in the control rooms and the oﬀices of the other users. When making the report
available once a week, the next step is to initiate the re-configurations. The flow of this
matter has been illustrated in the flowchart depicted in figure 11.1.
Figure 11.1. Flowchart of re-configurations

As depicted, in figure 11.1 is sectioned into two coherent flowcharts. The first is from the

criticality, and the CE markings. From the current state analysis, it became evident that
the programmer is also doing this per feeling and years of experience. Thus, indicating an
uncertain process as all of the knowledge regarding the acceptance or cancellation of the
proposals are centralized. Consequently, if the programmer becomes ill or resigns from the
RG, then there is a significant problem as these re-configurations and other tasks cannot be
completed timely. It is therefore suggested that these tasks and processes need to become
standardized. Therefore, standard operating procedures need to be developed to assure that
the needed investigations become explicit so that a potential new programmer can perform
the tasks without years of experience. Moreover, the programmer is a bottleneck as he is
the only one that makes these reviews and evaluations once a month. Therefore, the stan-
dard could also be utilized in this matter, as other employees can learn to carry out the
tasks. The last step of the flowchart from the programmer’s perspective is evaluating the
proposals, which are either accepted or canceled based on the knowledge acquired from the
visualizations in the report.
As new alarms are added frequently, the effect of the re-configurations could be dimin-
ished. In order to prevent this from happening, it is suggested that the new alarms are
inspected a month after they have been added to the alarm management system. This in-
spection should also be based on the report and the visualizations to see the distribution of
bad actor alarms. Thus, indicating if the newly added alarms create noise and should be
removed/re-configurated or if it signals about abnormal situations and therefore should not
be removed/re-configurated.

PS1: What is the current condition of the alarm management system in Cigacice
production line 12 and is the data qualified to be incorporated in improvement
processes?
The first examined problem statement concerned the initial condition of the alarm manage-
ment system and the associated data in CIG12. This was an essential foundation to establish
and understand, as the alarm data is to be applied during continuous improvement initia-
tives such as SPS and maintenance planning. A gap analysis was conducted based on the
difference between the ideal state of an alarm management system (according to industry
standards and guidelines, DS-EN 52582:2015, EEMUA-191, ISA-18.2) and the current state
of the alarm management system in CIG12. The analysis revealed a significant gap from
the ideal situation regarding too many alarms per operator and a flood of alarms. During
the examined period (01/01/2021-31/03/2021), it was revealed that the alarm occurrences
per 10 minutes per operator were more than double the maximum manageable stated in the
standards and guidelines. This also indicated to investigate alarms flood occurrences, which
revealed that there were alarm floods during 11% of the total production time. This state
was worsened by the poor prioritization distribution of the events, which revealed a gap of
59% (Alarms), 1% (Warnings), and 58% (Messages). This poor event prioritization over-
floods the system and the operators with alarms that cannot be prioritized or distinguished
according to their criticality and importance. Further investigations showed that the alarm
categories often presented themselves as fleeting alarms, contributing to the high amount of
alarm floods and thus general noise in the alarm data. These findings were evaluated and
discussed with a data scientist, who earlier had tried to deduce correlations from the alarm
data and KPIs, concluding that the alarm data contained too much noise to use directly
for data analysis in, for example, SPS. This noise can, for example, be seen as the fleeting
alarms that are triggered with no purpose and the missing alarm handling due to the alarm
floods.
These findings lead to providing CIG with tools that can be utilized to clean the alarm
management system from noise. Thus, the alarm data can first be incorporated in data
analysis and improvement investigations after a clean-up has been initiated.

CHAPTER16
Conclusion
The possible ways to utilize alarm data from a RW production line were investigated in this
study. The primary purpose was to improve the daily throughput, safety, and quality and
enable continuous improvements by using alarm data. Based on a close collaboration with
the RW manufacturing plant in CIG (production line 12) in Poland, literature reviews, and
analysis of the alarm data, it was possible to investigate the factors mentioned above.
It is concluded that alarm data can be utilized in the form of visualizations. These have
been constructed as spiral graphs, heat maps, tree maps, ribbon charts, bar charts, and high-
density charts. The visualizations are developed to ease the understanding, interpretation,
as well as analysis of the alarm data by employees across different functions in CIG. This
also applies to the employees without a data science background, such as operators. The lit-
erature review revealed a lack of studies on alarm data utilization by industrial practitioners
without a data science background. However, this gap has been filled by this study, as the
visualizations also apply to them.
The developed visualizations (the tree map, the stacked bar chart, and the high-density
chart) will enable the operators to propose suggestions on re-configurations. Thus, an alarm
is changed to either a warning or message instead. According to standards and guidelines
(EEMUA-191, DS/EN 62682:201 2015, ANSI/ISA 18.2 2016), this can bring the alarm man-
agement system to its ideal state, whereas the event distribution is 5% alarms, 15% warnings,
and 80% messages.
First and foremost, the re-configurations will increase the eﬀiciency of the alarm management
system, as the alarms will be revealing abnormal situations. Secondly, the re-configurations
will reduce the overflood of alarms, thereby the stress that operators endure. The operators’
abilities to react to abnormal situations will increase and their ability to reduce and prevent
such situations. Consequently, the production line and the equipment will encounter less
overpressure, and the number of unplanned downtimes can decrease.
In the chapter for
scenarios the alarm report has been utilized and tested for the use of the identification of
proposals of re-configurations based on alarm information regarding fleeting alarm distribu-
tions. Thus, the throughput and quality of the production line and the products can increase
if the suggestions are accepted by the programmer.
The spiral graph, heat map, and ribbon chart for the point of view of the throughput can
be interpreted and analyzed to identify trends and patterns of the alarm data. This can
enable better planning of the maintenance and catch a problem before it escalates and causes
potential downtime. Thus, shutdowns, their intensity, and duration can be diminished or
completely prevented. In the chapter for scenarios the alarm report has been utilized and
tested for the use of the identification of patterns leading up to an equipment stop, which
were identified. As a result of utilizing the alarm data, the throughput and the quality of
the production line and products can be increased. In addition, savings can be achieved
from reduced maintenance costs and improved operations.

16 Conclusion
For the alarm data and the developed visualizations to enable continuous improvements and
systematic problem-solving sessions, the data must be cleaned from the bad actor alarms
(e.g., noise). Noise created by the bad actor alarms makes the interpretation and analysis
of the visualizations diﬀicult. Thus, the data needs to reflect on abnormal situations and
actual production conditions with only critical alarms. This clean-up can be initiated by
message number re-configurations, which are based on the visualizations. Continuous and
afterward, the visualizations can be utilized, during systematic problem-solving sessions, to
acquire comprehensive production knowledge from the alarm data. This comprehensive pro-
duction knowledge will help identify equipment correlations, the effect of initiated actions,
and trends and patterns in the production. Thus, it can help identify the root cause, result-
ing in improved daily operations.
If the above suggestions are implemented and maintained in CIG12, then it will, according
to literature reviews and experts from the RG, lead to a 5%-15% reduction in unplanned
maintenance costs and an increase of 3%-5% throughput.
In conclusion, this study provided a solution for the issues associated with CIG’s alarm
management system and how the alarm data can be utilized with visualizations, which
benefits both the employees and the RG as a whole. Future work should focus on cleaning
the alarm data to enable continuous improvements.

CHAPTER17
Future Work
The purpose of this section is to put the achieved results into perspective and provide
suggestions for future work that the RG can execute. These suggestions will help strengthen
the quality and results of this master thesis as a more comprehensive understanding will be
uncovered.
These recommendations will be provided in the form of bullet points, as seen below.
• It is recommended that the weekly updated report will be available on a daily basis.
This can be achieved automatically by the functionalities of the Power BI Desktop
and by a Power BI expert or data scientist.
The daily report will enable the key
stakeholders to interact and react to the alarm data daily, which will create a greater
foundation of continuous improvements.
• It is further recommended that the RG investigate within alarm segmentation towards
the specific production equipment’s. This will allow for a more excellent and more
detailed production knowledge and enable a more thorough systematic problem-solving
session as the specific message numbers will be linked to the specific equipment. This
is not the case at this moment, as the specific message numbers are only linked to the
specific plants. This makes the root cause analysis more diﬀicult as a plant has several
numbers of different equipment.
• After the data has been cleaned, and a greater detail level has been reached, it is
recommended that the alarm data will function as performance KPIs where specific
targets and trigger points are defined and trends are monitored. This will allow for
continuous awareness of alarm trends and enable the right decisions as actions are
supported on alarm data as well. Furthermore, the awareness of the alarm trend will
help remediate an evolving problem before it escalates. These KPIs should be placed
on the machine boards and the Oobeya meetings at the appropriate detail level.
• It is also recommended that the RG will examine the impact of alarms on the produc-
tion. This could, for example, be how the number of alarms impacts the speed of the
line, the throughput, or quality. When the effect of the alarms is identified, then it is
evident where to prioritize improvements initiatives and thus focus on the alarms that
are the most critical.
• During interviews and workshops, it was revealed that the RG has a strategy towards
more digitalization, especially in the production and in the control rooms. This would
require either mobile devices or tablets on the shop floor. The digitalization would
enable the key stakeholders to communicate and utilize the report more eﬀiciently
and on the go, as they will be closer to the actions (e.g., production). Therefore it is
highly recommended that the RG will prioritize this matter as it comes with significant
advantages for not only the alarm data utilization but also other purposes.
• Lastly, it is recommended to develop a standard that describes how to categorize new
message numbers into either an alarm, warning, or message. It was discovered that 500
new alarms were added to the alarm management system since September 2020. These
new alarms have not been evaluated towards the three different categories. Thus, the
