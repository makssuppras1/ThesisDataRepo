III 
Abstract 
pH gradient plays a critical role in many important biological processes including carcinoma 
development. Reconstructing cells extracellular pH gradient in vitro would advance the 
understanding of more intrinsic mechanism in cancer cell growth. So far, many efforts have 
been paid on forming extracellular pH gradient in microfluidic systems. However, the existing 
microfluidic systems either had a narrow range for cell research or had the problem of exposing 
cells to undesired mechanical stress. Moreover, convectional technologies for fabrication of 
these microfluidics were complicated and expensive due to the involvement of clean room and 
photolithographic resources. To address these challenges, in this project, we aimed to develop 
a more affordable microfluidic assay device, which could generate specific pH gradient in a 
no-flow slit and thus facilitate array of cells imaging.  
 
In this project, three-dimensional (3D) printing technology was applied to replace conventional 
fabrication methods and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), the most popular material for cell 
culture in microfluidics, was used. It is acknowledged that PDMS are gas permeable, 
biocompatible, and transparent. With a commercial stereolithography (SLA) 3D printer, we 
demonstrated a simple protocol which facilitated the generation of PDMS-based microfluidic 
devices by using 3D printed Clear resin mold. The protocol allowed the direct transfer of mold 
pattern into PDMS and the delivered resolution was up to 20μm. In the end, we fabricated a 
microfluidic device, which composed of two conjunction channels, and two microchannels at 
either ends of a rectangular slit. Conjunction channels were to balance the pressure difference 
between two microchannels. The slit was 600μm wide, 2mm long and around 20μm high. By 
introducing continuously stable flows using a commercial air pumping system, we 
demonstrated the generation of linear fluorescein gradient in the middle of the slit with a 
distance of 1.2mm. The establishment time was less than 1h and the gradient was kept stable. 
We believed that this system could also have strong implications for establishing pH gradient, 
as fluorescein gradient generation was complicated than pH gradient.  
 
Techniques used in this work include: 1) 3D printing; 2) soft lithography technique to fabricate 
PDMS-based microfluidic device; 3) gradient generation by diffusion properties in laminar 
flows; 4) surface characterization using scanning electron microscope (SEM); 5) gradient 
characterization with fluorescein

1 Introduction 
1.1  Motivation 
        Cells, as the basic units of all living organisms, are consistently exposed to a complex in 
vivo microenvironment where physicochemical and mechanical factors would change spatially 
and temporally [1]. The dynamic changes and the resulting spatial gradients of these factors 
like pH play essential roles in many biological processes including embryonic and cancer cell 
development [2]. However, until now, there still are many unsolved mysteries about how static 
or dynamic factor gradients exactly mediate the behaviors of normal and pathological cells, 
both intracellularly and extracellularly. In principle, intracellular signaling responses can be a 
result of chemical signals stimulation existing in physiological microenvironment of cells. 
Therefore, for better exploring their intrinsic precise mechanism, a powerful strategy is to 
mimic or reconstruct cells extracellular environments with high fidelity in vitro, so that 
researchers have the opportunities to introduce and track cellular responses in real-time either 
qualitatively or quantitatively.  
 
Nano- and micro-technology has attracted much attention due to their capability to generate 
different biological stimulation responses or other multimodal  systems in 2-dimension (2D) 
or 3-dimension (3D) [3][4]. Among these technologies, microfluidic systems are especially 
popular for their ability of precisely spatial and temporal control. As a result, they have been 
widely studied to simulate extracellular environments like chemical gradients[5][6]. Many 
microdevices have been successfully constructed to model oxygen (O2), carbon dioxide (CO2), 
chemoattractant and pH gradients[6][7][8][9]. All of them helped deepen our knowledge in 
carcinoma development.   
 
Specifically, in this project, pH gradient will be the major topic considering its broad 
applications in both analytical chemistry and pharmacy biology. Right now, several methods 
have been established to created pH gradients, however, they all have their own limitations. 
For example, pH gradient can be established in capillaries using traditional method, in which 
ampholytes and electric current are given, but it can result in the unstable gradient and bring 
difficulties in measurement.  Besides, the direct contact between electrodes and fluids would 
bring some cross-contamination. Right now, much more preferred strategy is to utilize 
diffusion-based mixing within continuous laminar flows. They offer the possibility to generate 
==PAGE:11==
 2 
a continuous spatial gradient while remaining stable at the same time. People have tried to 
generate immobilized pH gradient in a chamber between two microchannels with different 
concentration of solutions. Polyacrylamide membrane was inserted by photopolymerize the 
monomer at one end of the channel. But the use of photopolymerization technology 
complicates the fabrication and can be time-consuming. Establishing pH gradient among two 
or multiple adjacent laminar flows was also studied. However, these devices needed strict and 
precise control of flow rate and the range of pH gradients was usually narrow. Moreover, if 
used for cell culture, exposed to flow can bring the undesired mechanical stress on cells and 
some important factors produced by cells may be flushed away[10][11].  
 
It is noteworthy that, the fabrication of most microdevices mentioned were costly and 
complicated since the involvement of photolithography. To use this technology, cleanroom 
infrastructure equipped with essential expensive machines is required and people need to 
master specialized skills. Efforts to simplify device fabrication promotes the emergence of 
several promising alternatives such as mask-less lithography, low-cost nanoimprint 
lithography and more affordable microscope projection lithography [12][13][14]. 3D printing 
is another potential technique. It enables the avoidance of cleanroom resources and meanwhile, 
facilitates the fabrication without special knowledge and skills. This brings great benefits for 
microfluidic technologies.   
 
The work in this thesis is part of a larger project, aiming to establish a more affordable 3D-
printing-based PDMS microfluidic assay for imaging cell behaviors in chemical gradients. To 
create a diffusion-gradient, two microchannels are set at either end of a cell-culture slit that has 
a large area (cm2) so that it can accommodate an array of single cells. It is hoped that by 
continuously providing laminar flows with different chemical concentrations in each channel, 
diffusion would occur through the slit, establishing a stable chemical gradient in this cell 
culture slit. With this assumption, multiple gradients generation were expected to be realized 
in this device, which may not be possible with flow-based devices. In addition, a diffusion 
gradient can facilitate the cell research by removing shear stress on cells. Moreover, such 
device would enable the time-modulation of the gradient by replacing the chemical reservoirs, 
not just a time-constant gradient as normal. However, this microdevice has a high requirement 
for the flow rate. The flow should be zero in the gradient, to achieve this, hydraulic resistance 
should be higher enough to suppress the flow rate. Theoretically, hydraulic resistance is 
decided by the size of microchannels and slit. But in this project. considering the use of

Stereolithography (SLA) 3D printer which usually has a lower resolution than traditional 
photolithography, we desired to explore the size limitation for printing at the same time.

1.2 Background 
1.2.1 pH gradients for cancer cell development 
It has been well studied that in healthy cells or tissues, the average extracellular pH (pHe) is at 
around 7.4, more basic compared to that in cancer cells (~6.8-7.0). While, on the contrary, the 
intracellular pH (pHi) increased in cancer cells (~7.2 versus ~7.3-7.6)[15][16][17]. Such 
reversed pH gradient indicates the early start of cancer development and further influences the 
cancer cell behaviors in proliferation, migration and metabolic adaptation since they are very 
sensitive to any changes in pH[17]. So far, people have found that the initiation of these 
processes are controlled by specific pH-sensitive proteins – pH sensors. Ion transport proteins, 
such as NHE 1, Na+- driven bicarbonate transporters and H+/K+-ATPase proton pump, have 
been proved their role in generating pHi-dependent proliferation even in vivo. These sensors 
may dysregulate the cell cycle activity by promoting specific phase (G2/M) entry and transition, 
or they can block normal apoptotic signaling process thus enhancing cancer cell growth. It is 
also believed that cancer cell proliferation takes the advantage of metabolic reprogramming 
during which glucose uptake and production of lactic acid increased, enhancing the cell 
resistance to hypoxia and enabling the fast transversion of nutrients into biomass. Thereby, the 
increased glycolysis has been referred as a common feature of cancers. pH sensitive glycolytic 
enzymes - lactate dehydrogenase and phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1), phosphofructokinase-2 
(PFK-2) and lactate dehydrogenase A chain (LDHA), were speculated to be involved into such 
process. However, for all the above description, more investigations are expected to Figure out 
their precise mechanism. For the role of pH gradients in cell migration and metastasis, research 
has shown that increased pHi or decreased pHe can induce the increased cancer cell migration 
and invasion, by activating acid-activated matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) which will 
contribute to the degradation of matrix.  
 
Not only can significantly affect diverse cancer cell behaviors, dysregulated pH dynamics can 
also influence the growth of stromal cells such as endothelial cells and fibroblasts, although 
still with unprecise mechanism[18]. So, with the consensus that there’s a push to expand our 
knowledge of gradient changes in cell migration, cell signaling or cell differentiation, many 
technologies have been developed, by either creating specific sensors to measure the difference 
at single cell level, or fabricating microdevices that can mimic the physiological 
microenvironment. The better understanding can also provide more clear clues for cancer 
therapy.

1.2.2 pH gradients generation in microfluidic devices 
Microfluidics have shown great potential in many scientific fields, such as in optics, informatic 
science, chemical synthesis and bioanalysis[5]. The ability to precisely control fluids in very 
small volume (10-3 to 10-12 microliters) offers microfluidic systems several advantages: 
enabling the minimum consumption of samples and reagents; capable of manipulating in situ 
or real-time analyzing in a high-throughput manner by coupling with different detection 
techniques including UV absorbance and fluorescence; high sensitivity; saving time and 
materials; easy to dispose and so on[5]. Eukaryotic cells, with the volume ranging from 10-3µl 
to 10-8µl, are well compatible to the current microfluidic devices, enabling the detection in high 
resolution (single cell). 
 
Forming pH gradient in microfluidic systems has also received great interest considering its 
importance in bioanalysis and cellular studies as mentioned before. At present, several methods 
have been developed and they can be summarized in two categories: electrochemical and non-
electrochemical (Table 1.1). In electrochemical methods which are generally to realize 
isoelectric focusing (IEF), carrier ampholytes (CAs) are commonly used.   pH gradient can be 
established by adding CAs into flowing buffer while in the presence of electric power[19] [20]. 
But they have poor temporal stability. Moreover, CAs are expensive and are easy to interact 
with proteins, this will cause cross-contamination and disturb further analysis if in a multi-
dimensional separation and analysis system[21][22][23]. Such phenomenon prompts much 
research on generating stable pH gradients without CAs. At this point, non-electrochemical 
methods utilizing diffusive mixing in laminar flows come into practice and can be further 
divided into two subsets: flow-free and flow-based. Laminar flows provide some features 
useful to generate a stable and measurable gradient, they can regulate molecules or ions to 
diffuse through concentration gradient in a confined environment.  
 
In flow-based microfluidics, two or more laminar flow streams with different concentrations 
were designed to converge within a channel without disturbance,  the diffusion would happen 
at the interface of every two adjacent fluids, getting an almost linear gradient [24][25]. Such 
strategy can precise the pH gradients by controlling the concentration of chemicals. And the 
gradient would not degrade because of the constant flow of solutions. However, the devices 
can be quite sensitive to several factors including pressure value and flow rate, so, careful 
manipulation is required. In addition, the channels are narrow and not idea for assay technology, 
==PAGE:15==
 6 
otherwise, it would complicate the design. Also noticed that, the designed flow rate may 
produce enough shear stress on cells, making devices not suitable for cell studies where soluble 
factors are important. Flow-free based device thus was thought to be an alternative. 
 
For flow-free devices, they have no flows in the middle slit, but may have flows in opposite 
two channels, with one channel serving as source supplier while the other as a sink[2]. Such 
system needs to precisely match the fluid levels, ensuring that pressure difference at both side 
of the slit is equal (ΔP=0), otherwise, fluid flow can be observed. To suppress the convective 
flow caused by pressure imbalance, usually, a porous membrane is used, since membrane can 
significantly increase the system fluidic resistance (flow rate is proportional to pressure 
difference and inversely proportional to fluidic resistance, Q=ΔP/Rhyd)[11]. This, meanwhile, 
would not affect the diffusive transport of small molecules.  In 2008, Sommer et al. managed 
to produce a precise linear immobilized pH gradient (IPG) (4 - 6.8 pH) in a 100um-wide, 6mm-
long microslit[26]. In the paper, high and low pH monomers were filled in inlet reservoirs at 
two ends of the long microchannel and diffusion occurred under continuous flow condition. A 
gel membrane was fixed by introducing and photopolymerizing the monomer close to low-pH 
end of the channel. Once obtaining the gradient after overnight, the whole device was 
photopolymerized, eventually getting the IPG. For such device, the problems were that they 
had to be stored at very low temperature (-20℃), and their gel surface can be sensitive to 
external factors like water [27]. Also, it took a long time to get a stable gradient and the steady 
state can be pseudo for the low concentration of chemicals gradually accumulating in the 
channel. Abhyankar et al. tried to avoid the accumulation of chemical species by setting a large 
volume sink reservoir at one end of the channel[11]. However, in their device, flowing fluid 
streams were given up, resulting in a time-constant gradient. Moreover, the existence of 
polymer or membrane might alter the actual gradient. Table 1.1 summarizes the commonly 
used pH gradient generation microdevice and their own advantages and disadvantages.

1.2.3 Conventional rapid prototyping for PDMS-based microfluidic devices 
Although glass and Si/SiO2 are traditionally used in microfluidic device fabrication process, 
they are not preferred especially in life science research. The drawbacks are mainly on that 
silicon is non-optical transparency and the use of glass and silicon materials is of high 
complexity and cost[28]. Instead, polymer-based technologies have attracted more and more 
attentions, because unlike glass and silicon, polymers are less fragile, making the fabrication 
process faster, cheaper and more accessible.[29] They are also able to be integrated with other 
simple components such as tubing. Thereinto, polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS)-based 
microfluidic systems has been mostly investigated owing to their promising applications in 
cytobiology, drug discovery, diagnostics and biomedical researches. PDMS is a relatively cost-
effective biocompatible polymer, it enables the easy and rapid prototyping of a mold in 
microscale. Specifically, PDMS is optically transparent with a refractive index similar to glass 
(1.4), favoring the observation of entities in microfluidic channels visually or through a 
microscope [30]. It also possesses the gas permeability and liquid impermeability, indicating 
its application prospect in cell culture as these properties enable the transport of molecules like 
O2 and CO2 which are important for cell growth [31]. Many commercial microfluidics actually 
made by PDMS casting have been successfully into market, for example, Fluidigm single-cell 
analysis systems (CyTOF® Systems) used for immune profiling and cancer research.  
 
In 1998, Duffy et al. published their work on rapid prototyping of PDMS microfluidic devices 
(within 24 hours) with the combining of high-resolution printing and photolithography [29]. 
==PAGE:17==
 8 
To fabricate a microfluidic PDMS device, a master is typically shaped on silicon wafer at the 
beginning through compliated lithography technology in cleanroom. Subsequently, PDMS 
mixed with curing agents is poured over the master placed with posts, after degassing and 
curing at recommended temperature, the PDMS replica can be peeled out and finally be bonded 
to another smooth plastic or glass platform with oxygen plasma treatment (Figure 1.1) [29]. 
Obtaining a master is a quite limiting factor for the whole soft lithography process. 
Photolithography (also called UV lithography) is a common method to create a master by 
transferring the pattern from an optical mask to a photosensitive photoresist that have been 
coated on a substrate. Until now, it offers the capacity to fabricate channels having the width 
larger than 20µm (enough for most microfluidic applications) [32][33]. Moreover, the process 
parameters can be controlled precisely, resulting in the low rate of sample defection. However, 
because of the usage of cleanroom resources where essential expensive machines are equipped, 
this method is quite costly. In addition, usually specialized skills from highly trained people 
are required. All these narrow the access for many users in different fields. Some alternative 
methods have been produced to overcome these limits. For example, microscope projection 
techniques can facilitate the fabrication process by simplifying the demand of 
photolithographic masks while remaining the high resolution printing (0.6µm), cleanroom is 
still necessary for other procedures like resist coating [34]. Thanks to the advent of 3D printing 
technology, scientists are now able to develop much simplier, faster and cheaper prototypes by 
reducing the requirements for expensive infrastructure resources and specialized skills. This 
provides more choices for microfluidic devices research also because 3D printing enables the 
fewer steps in mold fabrication and allow more freedom in 3D design.  But there still exist 
some limitations for 3D-printed microfluidics: a) limited resolution. So far the smallest 
acquired 3D-printed channel was 200um wide; b) less flat surface profiles due to limited 
resolution; c) the biocompatibility of most 3D printing materials are still unkown; d) it is still 
expensive for buying a 3D printer with high resolution [35].

1.2.4 3D printing technology in microfluidics fabrication 
Nowadays, 3D printing has been widely explored to print more affordable masters for PDMS 
casting[14][36]. Comina et al. successfully fabricated PDMS-based micromixers using a 
commercial micro stereolithography (MS) 3D printer[14]. The templates were produced at a 
high resolution of 50µm and their surface was smooth enough to be sealed to glass properly. 
However, due to the fact that PDMS could not cure on the templates while contacting directly, 
==PAGE:19==
 10 
ink was employed to coat the surface before templates completely drying out. Such step can 
complicate the fabrication and influence the further use of PDMS microdevices if ink comes 
into the polymer. In Kamei et al. study, more simplified fabrication process was tried by using 
a different machine - ink-jet 3D printer[36]. PDMS was cured directly on UV-curable resin 
mold at 65℃ for 48h in an oven. Based on this, microfluidic channels with minimum height 
of 50µm was proved to be producible and eventually a hydrogel-based 3D microfluidic cell 
culture slit was created to generate chemical concentration gradient. 3D printers are also 
feasible to create complex 3D structures while traditional photolithography technique only 
enables the planar structures fabrication[37]. Saggiomo et al presented an easy method to 
achieve multilayer PDMS-based 3D microchannels[30]. In this paper, intricate acrylonitrile 
butadiene styrene (ABS) scaffold was printed from fused deposition modeling (FDM) 3D 
printer, the scaffold was then dissolved in PDMS-resistant solvent to finally get empty channels 
in cured PDMS. What is more attractive is that PDMS microfluidics can even be directly 
printed, like using CO2 selective laser baking (SLB) technique[31]. Considering the strong 
application prospects of 3D printers in microfluidics, we would like to combine the 3D printing 
technology in our project, thereby stereolithography (SLA) 3D printer with a higher resolution 
(25µm) was employed.  
 
SLA 3D printing has become more and more popular since its first appear in 1970s.  SLA 3D 
printers with two different setups were built: bottom-up desktop and top-down industrial SLA 
printer. Bottom-up printer is easier to operate and more available for relatively lower price, and 
therefore is widely used for academic research. The typical components of a SLA 3D printer 
are shown in Figure 1.2A. Typically, the technology adopts the principle of irreversible vat 
photopolymerization (called this because the use of a vat of liquid photopolymers), in which, 
polymer resin is solidified layer-by-layer by exposing to an ultraviolet (UV) laser beam (Figure 
1.2B). The working process is as follows: 
1. Firstly, the build platform is fixed and the elevator would move the platform down to 
the photopolymer tank, dipping the surface one layer high (25µm to 100µm) into the 
polymer liquid.  
2. The layer is cured and solidified after UV laser beam scans the whole cross section. A 
set of mirrors, named Galvos, are used to determine the focusing path of laser beam. 
3. When finishing each layer, the platform moves up, waiting for the sweeper blade re-
coating its surface, then the curing process repeats again. 
==PAGE:20==
 11 
4. To complete the printing, for some resin, post processing procedures are needed, like 
post-curing under UV light for fully solidification. 
 
As the most cost-effective 3D printer by now, SLA can offer several advantages: a) speed. 
Intricate parts are able to be printed and the producing time is from hours to one day, depending 
on the complexity of their structure; b) accuracy and precision. The height for each printed 
layer ranges between 25µm and 100µm, ensuring the high dimensional accuracy of molds (±0.5% 
for desktop and ±0.15% for industrial). What’s more, since UV lights are applied instead of 
heat, the printing will be taken at room temperature, thus avoiding the possible thermal 
expansion contraction artifacts; c) smooth surface feature. SLA has been viewed to be great 
enough for getting a smooth surface finish comparable to traditional fabricating methods 
including injection molding. The surface is still smooth enough for proper sealing to glass 
substrates (also because of the high resolution of 25µm); d) isotropy. SLA assures the printing 
of highly isotropic molds profiting from layer to layer chemical photo-polymerization; e) 
material versatility. Various SLA resins are provided, until now, commercial ones are Standard, 
Clear, Castable, Tough or Durable, High Temperature and Dental Resin. Each has their own 
merits and weaknesses. For example, clear resin, which will be used in this project, is great for 
printing fluidics or any parts requiring internal features displaying. However, post-processing 
is necessary to get a clear final product, which will be described in detail in Chapter 3.  
 
Importantly, PDMS replica cannot be casted directly on SLA printed mold as PDMS will not 
cure. This phenomenon might be caused by the chemicals from the resin that will diffuse into 
PDMS mixture and prevent PDMS completely curing. So, modification of mold surface was 
needed to avoid the direct contact between resin mold and PDMS mixture. This step can bring 
another problem, which is that chemical modifications may stick to the PDMS replica, 
changing its biocompatibility. However, as mentioned before, Kamei et al managed to get a

1.3.3 Scanning electron microscope  
Scanning electron microscope (SEM) was used to help us analyze and learn more about the 
topography of the SLA 3D printed molds and the PDMS replica. It was to test the fidelity from 
designed ones to actual entities. SEM has been viewed as a powerful analytical instrument to 
investigate the ultrastructure of a solid specimen surface. To produce images, a focused 
electron beam is usually implemented and scans the sample in a raster scanning manner. 
Theoretically, when a high-energy electron beam hits the atoms at various depths within sample, 
different signals would be released including low-energy secondary electrons (SEs), high 
energy backscattered electrons (BSEs), characteristic X-ray, cathodoluminescence (CL), 
transmitted electrons and so on, each reports different facets about morphology of the sample 
surface (Figure 1.3A). Among these signals, SE and BSE are most commonly used for SEM 
imaging: Low-energy electrons can have quite limited mean free path (1-20nm) (the distance 
of an electron traveling between collisions) [41]. Since SEs have very low energy (less than 
50 eV), they will be emitted only few nanometers from the top of surface and thus are valuable 
for collecting topographical information on samples at a very high resolution (minimum 1nm). 
BSE are part of the incident electrons that reflected back from the nucleus of solid samples. 
BSE signals indicate the difference in atomic number (the higher, the brighter) and therefore 
are used to display the distribution of different elements for rapid phase discrimination.  
 
For a SEM instrument, essential components include electron beam emitted from an electron 
gun; electron lenses for electron focusing; deflection coil for raster scanning and magnification 
control; vacuum system for avoiding the absorbance or offset of electrons in their path; 
specimen stage for sample holding; detectors for collecting all interested signals and output 
display devices (Figure 1.3B.). In this project, tabletop electron microscope TM3030Plus 
(Hitachi, Krefeld, Germany) was utilized to characterize the samples. This machine can support 
the SE imaging, BSE imaging and mixed mode observing without metal coating which is 
usually needed in traditional SEM instrument to increase sample conductivity.

1.3.4 Fluorescence microscope 
Fluorescein will be used in this project to track the gradient generation. As an organic dye, it 
has been commonly used in many applications including in cellular biology, working as a 
fluorescent tracer. To image fluorescein, fluorescence microscopes would play an essential role. 
They are fabricated based on the phenomenon of fluorescence which was first described by 
George Stokes in 1852[42]. Fluorescence increases the contrast because the excitation and 
emission can be separated so the background is low. Its indeed fundamental is illustrated in 
Figure 2.2A. When hit by light with sufficient energy, the electrons in the stable ground state 
will be excited to higher energy singlet states. These very unstable excited states then return to 
lower energy state by giving off photons as light. However, during this process, some of the 
energy would be lost internally releasing as heat, consequently, the energy of emitted photons 
is lower than that of excited ones. According to the Planck’s equation, photons energy is 
reversely proportional to light wavelength. So, the emitted light has a longer wavelength than 
excitation light, and actually displays as a different color. For example, fluorescein, used in 
this project, has excitation peak at 494nm (blue light) and emission peak at 512nm (releasing 
as green light).   
 
==PAGE:26==
 17 
Fluorescence microscopes are used to generate images by detecting the much weaker 
fluorescence emitted from specimen after illuminating them with higher energy light. The basic 
working process is shown in Figure 1.4B. Light of a specific wavelength is selected by passing 
through an excitation filter and then is reflected from a dichroic mirror to illuminate the 
specimen. This special mirror enables the reflection of shorter wavelengths, but is transparent 
to certain longer wavelengths. Thus the emission light can pass back through the dichroic 
mirror, and before reaching a detector, it is filtered by an emission filter which is for blocking 
the unwanted wavelengths that causing the residual excitation light. Because the fluorescence 
light may be several orders of magnitude weaker than the excitation light and the excitation 
filters are not good enough to filter these lights.

2 Materials and methods 
2.1 Workflow 
     To get the microfluidic devices, the fabrication process was mostly similar to the what 
depicted in Figure 1.1, except that the template was 3D printed instead of using 
photolithography technology. The template needed was first designed in a 3D CAD software, 
which was then imported into a 3D printer to print.  After several post-processing procedures, 
the template was obtained. The pattern in the template was, afterwards, transferred into a 
PDMS replica by covering it directly with uncured PDMS mixture. Under specific condition, 
the PDMS mixture would be completely cured. After peeling off the PDMS replica from the 
3D printed template, it was bonded to a silicon glass slide by oxygen plasma treatment. In the 
end, a microfluidic device was fabricated and ready for the microfluidic experiment. Before 
this, SEM might be required to characterize the micro-level structure in the template, PDMS 
replica or even in the device after bonding (cross-sectional structure). Microfluidic experiment 
would be done on two pressure-driven systems, one where the flow was maintained by an air 
pressure system and one where the flow was obtained by hydrostatic pressure. Dye solutions 
(fruit or fluorescein dye) and PBS buffer were then pumped into two microchannels at the same 
time under same pressure. The generation of gradient in the slit between two microchannels 
was characterized by using given microscope (Lecia Microscope or Inverted Nikon 
Microscope). The detailed methods are described in the following sections.  
2.2  Microfabrication of microfluidic devices 
2.2.1  Microfabrication of 3D printed mold for PDMS layer in a microfluidic device 
As mentioned earlier, 3D printing is an accessible way to create microstructures for users 
without a training in microfabrication because of the simplicity of the workflow. In this thesis, 
a SLA 3D printer (Form 3B) was employed to print clear resin 3D molds (Clear 
V4(FLGPCL04)). Once designed in a standard CAD software (Solidworks), the file was saved 
as STL format and imported to PreForm software. In PreForm, the model was laid out on a 
nearly 45˚ tilt before auto-generating tree-like supporters. The touchpoint size of supporters 
was set as 0.5mm at a density of 1.00. Then the program was transferred into 3D printer by 
setting each printing layer thickness at the highest resolution (25µm). Since the molds were 
printed layer by layer, the whole time was dependent on the number of layers.  When finishing, 
the part coming out was covered with uncured resin. It was subsequently immersed into an 
==PAGE:29==
 20 
ultrasonic bath for 20 minutes to get a clean finish. The part was then washed by 90% isopropyl 
ethanol (IPA) for further clean and put into an UV cure box for complete curing. Post-curing 
was carried out at 60℃ for 1h. Last step was to carefully remove the tree-like supporters using 
flush cutters. If this was done before UV curing, the thin substrate of mold might be distorted 
a bit due to the application of external force to the unhardened part.  
2.2.2 Soft lithography for PDMS replica molding 
At the time when my thesis began, it was thought that PDMS replica could not be completely 
cured by directly pouring PDMS mixture on 3D printed mold. The reason can be that some 
chemicals in the resin mold would diffuse into the PDMS disturbing the cross-linking reaction, 
or some chemical reactions occur at the surface between mold and PDMS, thus impeding the 
fully curing of PDMS. Consequently, things like ink would always be coated onto the mold to 
prevent PDMS from sticking. However, inspired by study from Kamei et al., we also found the 
conditions under which PDMS could be cured directly on the mold.  
 
PDMS monomers was mixed with its activator (lguracure D2959) at the weight ratio of 10:1, 
and the mixture was stirred until small bubbles filled fully in the plastic plate. The bubbles 
were removed by putting the mixture into an vacuum container for around 1h. Cover PDMS a 
bit higher than the mold and put the whole system back to the vacuum container again. This 
was to remove the air beneath the mold and it took around 40 minutes. Next, the rest of the 
PDMS mixture was poured slowly into the plate and the procedure for pumping air out was 
repeated for another 40minutes. Subsequently, the whole system was moved into an oven, 
curing at 60℃ for 40h. The whole process took around two days. The mold can be resused for 
molding new PDMS replica. 
2.2.3 Microfluidic device sealing 
Oxygen plasma treatment has been extensively used to produce strong, covalent bonds between 
PDMS and silicon glass slide. Plasma is a gas that has been excited to higher energy states and 
been partially ionized by giving further energy. The activated gas is a mixture of atoms, ions, 
free radicals, electrons and UV light, endowing the plasma with high amount of internal energy 
which is strong enough to break most organic bonds (Wikipedia). PDMS polymer is believed 
to be consist of repeated units of -O-Si(CH3)2-, so, when exposing to oxygen plasma, silanol 
groups (Si-OH) would be introduced by giving off methyl groups (-CH3).[43][44] The 
increased concentration of hydroxyl groups (-OH) not only renders the surface more 
==PAGE:30==
 21 
hydrophilic, but also stimulates the intermolecular bonds formation when brought into contact 
with another oxidized glass surface. Si-O-Si bond was finally formed between PDMS and glass, 
at the expense of a water (H2O). Such water-tight covalent bond results in a tight and 
irreversible sealing, which is idea for further microfluidic device research. The contaminants 
produced will be pumped out by a vacuum system.  
 
Surface cleanliness and roughness would affect the bonding efficiency. As mentioned in 
Chapter 1, 3D printing technology results in less flat surface profiles due to limited resolution, 
therefore, the requirements like time used for treating oxygen plasma may be different from 
typically used (usually less than 1 minutes) and need us to find new suitable conditions to get 
a good bonding.   After many attempts, the sealing process was concluded as follows. Once 
the PDMS replica was obtained, its rough edges were cut clean. Then both the PDMS replica 
and glass slide were first rinsed with Deionized (DI) water, followed with 70% ethanol, and 
dried in air at room temperature. After drying, adhesive tape was used for further removing the 
dirt on the components. To enable PDMS replica permanently adhere to the glass slide, the 
samples were placed in a plasma instrument (Diener) facing the sides ready to be bonded 
upwards.  The samples would be treated with oxygen plasma up to 6 minutes. This is longer 
than that typically required as a result of less smooth PDMS replica molding from 3D printed 
template. Moreover, the quality of the plasma is also depending on the ratio of oxygen and it 
is actually difficult to adjust on this instrument. Once finished, the PDMS mold and the slide 
substrate were taken out immediately and pressed together carefully for tight bonding. In this 
step, clamps would facilitate the bonding by keeping the device under pressure overnight.  
2.3 Topography characterization with SEM 
To characterize the topography of samples using SEM, we did the preparations as follows: 3D 
printed molds or PDMS replicas with no surface modification were stuck on a holder rigidly 
using a conductive adhesive. The holders with different angles (0°, 45°, 90°) were chosen

2.4.1 Constant flow rate set-up 
Pumping strategy has been acknowledged as a widely used method to generate a stable,

3 Microfluidic devices containing wells and pillars 
3.1 Microfluidic devices containing wells and pillars: Design 1 
3.1.1 Design and microfabrication of microdevices  
As mentioned earlier, initially, we aimed to develop a new flow-free based microfluidic device 
in which linear pH gradients can be generated in a shorter time, and real-time high-throughput 
cellular study can be conducted. The design concept was to put a bit larger (cm2) slit between 
two microchannels, so that the diffusion would happen and gradually reach steady state in this 
slit while two laminar flows continuously passing through its two ends. We assumed that the 
slit should be as large as possible for an array of cells to be covered by the gradient, but 
meanwhile enabling the tracking under specific confocal microscope. Our original idea was to 
isolate cells in wells in order to make an array. Therefore, in the first device design, several 
small wells would be fitted at the surface of slit for single cell culture.  
 
It is noteworthy that for slit design, there may exist some pitfalls when it comes to bonding to 
glass. As depicted in Ruiz et al. study, while investigating microcontact printing (µCP) 
technique, there would be some requrements on the aspect ratios (height/width, h/w) of the 
micro-structures since the stamp is deformable. The improper aspect ratio can mainly cause 
three types of pitfalls during microcontact printing, called lateral collapse, collapse and sagging 
seperately[45]. Such phenomenon can also apply to our project since the slit is desired to keep 
high fidelity (Figure 3.1A). If the width (w) of the slit is smaller than its height (h), the structure 
has a high aspect and serious distortion of walls can occur after  mechanically pressing for 
PDMS-glass bonding(Figure 3.1B). In addition, if w/h ratio is much too small, then the walls 
of this feature would have a high risk of coming into contact with each other and keeping 
adherent, as a result(Figure 3.1C). On the other hand, when the width (w) is much larger than 
the height (h), the roof of the slit collapses downward, ending up with the connection between 
PDMS replica and the silican glass (Figure 3.1D).

3.1.3 Microfluidic experiments with constant flow rate set-up 
Microfluidic experiments were performed on constant flow rate set-up described in Chapter 2. 
The first step was to wet the microfluidic device, meaning washing the device with solutions 
without introducing the air. However, it was discovered that air bubbles were easy to be 
produced and stuck at the intersection of microchannels and slits. Bubbles were also found to 
be firmly trapped into the slits especially for the device whose supporters could touch the glass. 
==PAGE:42==
 33 
The reasons can be that: 1) due to the pillars connecting the top and bottom of the slit. The 
height of the slit was small, therefore, the flow resistance was higher so that one needed to 
apply a higher pressure to remove the bubbles; 2) the pillars created areas where the bubbles 
‘liked’ to stay due to higher area of PDMS. It is important to extrude the airs out of the device 
in order to establish stable flows. The air bubbles in microchannels can be either submerged 
into liquid or attached to the wall of the microchannel. When the bubbles are immersed in the 
liquid, the differential pressure produced by the surface tension counteract with each other, as 
a result, no additional pressure is generated which can affect the flow of liquids. However, 
since the bubbles also flow with the liquid, the volume of the bubbles will change as the 
pressure changes. This will change the flow rate of the liquid and the change is related to the 
cross-sectional position. When the bubbles are attached to the wall of the channel, they will 
keep still due to the surface tension, but this would reduce the sectional area of the channel and 
increase the hydraulic resistance. Meanwhile, these bubbles would move along the channel or 
burst according to the liquid status, resulting in unstable flow. The problems mentioned above 
can happen at the same time. All these result in the changes in flow characteristics, leading to 
flow instability. 
 
After a series of test, we got the most efficiency method to avoid the introduction of air. 10% 
ethanol prepared in PBS solution was first pumped into one microchannel at very low pressure 
(1mbar), this was to ensure the solution was slowly spread over the whole slit. Then pump the 
solution into the other channel at the same pressure value. The following was second-washing 
the device by PBS solution. Although air would be introduced again into the microchannels on 
account of the untight connection between tubes and inlets, it could be excluded by applying 
much higher pressure (over 50mbar) to three of the four holes at the same time, pushing the air 
out of the device from the rest holes.  
 
After wetting the device and ensuring the exclusion of air bubbles, the solutions in reservoirs 
were replaced with degassed fruit dye and PBS buffer separately. During this step, we need to 
be careful that the whole tubes should always be full of solutions especially when taking out 
the tubes from reservoirs. When doing such microfluidic experiment on constant flow rate 
system, no gradient was generated. We assumed it was because fluctuation of pressure value 
in the set-up caused pressure difference that large enough to suppress the diffusion in the slit.  
Then, the set-up was laid 1cm higher or lower than the reservoir to produce stable hydrostatic 
==PAGE:43==
 34 
pressure (upper diagrams in Figure 3.8A and 3.8B). We calculated the pressure-driven flow 
through the microchannels using designed hydraulic resistance value (1.61x1011 Pa.s/m3), 
obtaining the flow rate and velocity in each channel as following:

∆P = 1x103kg/m3 x 9.8N/kg x 1cm = 98N/m2 = 98Pa = 0.98mbar

Under this condition, we still observed that for both devices, the fluids would always be 
observed in the slit unequally distributed and diffusion did not occur, as exhibited in Figure 
3.8. Actually, at the interface of two liquids, we could view the fluctuation, indicating the 
existence of flows in the slit. Ideally, the pressure in each microchannel would decrease linearly, 
thus the pressure difference between two microchannels should always be the same (∆P=0). 
This means the flow rate at two ends of the slit is zero and only diffusion occurs, helping us to 
obtain a linear gradient in the slit. In practice, however, there always exhibited flows (Figure 
3.8). It may because that the dimensions of the two channels are not exactly the same. The 
change of the shape could influence the hydraulic resistance of the channel. If in some place, 
the resistance is higher than the same position in the other channel, the pressure increased as 
well, resulting in a bit higher pressure difference than theoretical value. The higher pressure 
would push the flow towards the microchannel with lower one, causing a non-linear 
distribution. What’s more, the pressure difference is easy to create flows in the slit if its 
hydraulic resistance is not large enough. As the pressure-driven flow is added to the diffusion, 
the gradient is actually a result of the combination of both diffusion and the transport by the 
flow. If such pressure-difference-driven flow rate is too high, it would lead to a larger Pe 
number (Pe >>1), at this time, convective transport dominates in a flow instead of the diffusion. 
Assuming that the pressures at the ends of the slit is 100%, 10% or 1% error from what they 
should be when the pressure at the microchannel inlet is 1 mbar, its corresponding flow rate 
and flow velocity are shown in Table 3.4. As the percentage of error decreasing, the flow rate 
also reducing, close to zero. The influence of the pressure difference was significantly reduced 
as the hydraulic resistance increased by an order of magnitude. However, we could see that 
even for 1% error, they could lead to large Pe number. So, it is important to decrease the 
influence of pressure difference at two ends of the slit. One way is to increase the hydraulic 
resistance in the slit, which is mainly influenced by its height (equation 1.4).

3.3 Conclusion 
In this chapter, PDMS microfluidic devices based on rough designs was investigated to figure 
out its suitable fabricating conditions by only using a SLA 3D printer. Molds containing inlets, 
outlets, wells, microchannels, slit and support pillars were successfully printed and replicated 
into a PDMS substrate directly. The design rules changed between design and practical values 
were gained, especially for these hundred micron-level structures. For example, the height of 
actual pillars decreased by almost 60µm compared to design value. On contrary, the real depth 
==PAGE:49==
 40 
of printed slit increased when the designed values were above 100µm, although the growth 
percentage became smaller as the design value also got smaller. When the designed height of 
slit was smaller than 100µm, it showed that the actual height became smaller than the design 
one (for example, 75µm in design, 60µm in practice). Such rules would help design the 
parameters for next devices. So far, we have confirmed the successful fabrication of wells with 
the height of 120µm and the diameter of 200µm; support pillars with the diameter of 150µm 
and the height of 92µm or 110µm; even much smaller pillars with the diameter of 130µm and 
the height far lower than 60µm. This, to our knowledge, has never been done before by just 
using SLA 3D printer. We also fabricated the slits that was 200µm, 107µm and 60µm high 
separately, to increase the hydraulic resistance of the slit in order. However, for all these 
devices, it turned out to be difficult to establish a diffusion gradient in the slit. We instead 
observed that there always existed convective flows in the slit separated by a circular curved 
line. This was because the pressure difference at two ends of the slit would introduce a pressure-
driven flow that suppress the diffusion in the slit. The pressure difference can be caused by the 
structure difference between the two microchannels. The other reason was due to the 
fluctuation of pressure in the constant flow rate system. But any way, all these demonstrated 
that pressure at two microchannels should be strictly controlled as the same to avoid the flows 
in slit, which was quite unfeasible in these devices. The other strategy was to suppress the 
effect of pressure difference by increasing the hydraulic resistance of the slit. Therefore, in the 
next chapter, we would further explore the minimum height that slit could have, to ensure the 
hydraulic resistance of slit reach its max and thus desensitize the device.

4 Study of linear gradient generation in microfluidics 
4.1 Investigation of the aspect ratio of the slit: Design 3 
Previoulsy, we have investigated the device with 75µm-high slit. Since in our device 
architecture, we want to suppress the pressure driven flow in the slit in order to establish a 
diffusion gradient, in this chapter, we would like to further explore the smallest height that the 
slits could have using 3D printer.  To my knowledge, no one had tried this. This was to ensure 
the hydraulic resistance of slit as large as possible. The SLA 3D printer that we have access to 
is able to produce the mode layer by layer on a minimum setting at 25 microns. As the smallest 
values can be printed theoretically, 25µm and 50µm were thus chosen as the heights of slits. 
Meanwhile, as indicated in device on Design 2, slit with smaller apsect ratio did not contact to 
the glass substrate even with no obvious support pillars. It was desired to avoid the existence 
of support pillars considering they would: a) introduce the air bubbles and thus unstabilize the 
flow condition; b) influence the accuracy of iamging result; c) unconvenice the further cell 
culture process; d) complicate the design and fabrication. Therefore, slits were designed 
without pillars. These slits had constant height (25µm or 50µm) but different width, so that we 
can establish an architecture in which slits with various aspect ratios can be fabricated while 
remained the fidelity (no collapsing).

4.1.1.2 Detection of device bonding status  
The PDMS replicas were bonded to glass slides as described earlier. However, we observed 
something new:  for both devices, at the beginning of bonding, all the enclosing slits were not 
collapsed, by displaying as several less opaquer rectangles visible to naked eyes (Figure 4.2, 
==PAGE:52==

left). However, 6 hours later, these positions where slits once stood became more and more 
transparent, indicating slits contacted to the glass, leaving no space between the glass and 
PDMS replica. Eventually, the slits collapsed. The reason can be that, for very shallow slits, 
even small pressure difference would create a suction clogging the space.  The heavy roof of 
slits was forced to collapse downward, extruding the air into or out of gas permeable PDMS 
polymer, and thus further vacuuming the space due to the pressure difference in and out of 
these confined slits. Since slits in Figure 4.2A had much smaller height, they are more prone 
to sag with the whole plane. But for device in Figure 4.2B, there was more gas in the higher 
enclosed slits, when collapsing occured, air that cannot be released through gas permeable 
PDMS on time, would be squeezed to a small unattached part as its surrounding area was glued 
to the glass gradually. Interestingly, two slits with the smallest width (1000µm) still remained 
opened, without any collapsing. This may because the narrow width ensured the firm structure 
of slit, making them much harder to be distorted. In this case, we also found that the sagging 
may have few relationships with the aspect ratio of slits, when the h/w is small. For the slit 
25µm high, when it had the largest aspect ratio (1/40), its shape did not change (Figure 4.2A, 
right, the fifth slit). However, for 50µm high slit with the same aspect ratio (Figure 4.2B, right, 
the third slit), it collapsed. The value of width might play a more important role for avoiding 
the sagging.

4.1.2 Microfluidic architecture containing microchannels 
4.1.2.1 Design and fabrication 
To confirm our assumption that pressure difference would push the silt towards glass slide, a 
new mold based on formal design was created only by adding microchannels to either side of 
the slits, so that there was air access (Figure 4.3). Our hypothesis was that with air access, 
pressure inside and outside the slits would not change and collapse therefore would not happen 
after bonding. Posts on the mold defining inlets and outlets in the PDMS were also added. This 
actually ensured the components of device consistent as formal ones, making it more suitable 
for microfluidic testing. The cross section of microchannels were still square, but their size was 
reduced from 350 µm to 150µm, not only expecting to increase the hydraulic resistance 
dramatically and thus slow the flow rate, but also to lessen the degree of distortion during 
fabrication. Therefore, the two microchannels would be more identical with respect to 
hydraulic resistance. Once finishing the design, two devices with slits either 25µm or 50µm 
high were prepared using the same methods described above (Figure 4.6A and B). This time, 
for both devices, none of the slits collapsed which would result in more transparent images. 
This confirmed our hypothesis that the collapsing of slits in the previous tested devices was an 
air pressure issue.

4.1.2.3 Characterization of device bonding condition with fruit dye 
Next, the device was tested by introducing red fruit dye into. Concentrated red solution was 
first 100 times diluted, then it was filtered to remove the larger grains, getting some clean, 
homogeneous solution. A micropipette (1000µL) was used to pump the liquid in. At first, food 
dye was slightly pumped into one of the microchannel through pipette tip, until liquid spread 
over all the slits with no empty space. During this process, it was observed that the spreading 
liquid would stop at the intersection between slits and the microchannel having no flow. Next, 
pump the fruit dye into the other microchannel slowly. By this method, air bubbles could be 
easily avoided, especially in the slits. If there exists small empty space in the slits, they could 
be squeezed out into one channel by scrapping along the edge using tweezer. Once into the 
microchannel, they became quite small bubbles, which can be removed by pressure pumping. 
In conclusion, compared to the devices with larger size (Chapter 3), it was found that airs were 
much easier to be avoided or extruded out of devices in smaller size. Both devices were filled 
with fruit dye using the same methods (Figure 4.6A and B). The experiment was repeated again 
with new devices.  
 
Diluted red dye was successfully introduced into the devices, filling in the channels and slits. 
In Figure 4.6A and B, food dye spread over all the slits, indicating that there was no contacting 
between the glass and slit roof. The colors in 50µm-high slits were deeper than that in 25µm-
high slits, which was reasonable because much more dye would be contained in these parts and 
the thickness of dye solution imaged was larger. So far, we have already proven that silts with 
very low aspect ratio (1/120 minimum) could still be fabricated and bonded to a glass slide

4.3 Microfluidic device to balance pressure difference: Design 5 
4.3.1 Fundamentals in Design 5 
In previous studies, to establish linear gradients in slit, we have designed slits with series of 
dimensions to increase their hydraulic resistance. However, we found out that our system was 
very sensitive to liquid pressure in microchannels, and the pressure difference from 
microchannels had big effects on establishment of linear pH gradients in the slit. Since we had 
successfully generated linear gradients in experiment in Chapter 4.2, we assumed that the 
hydraulic resistance of slit with 20µm-high is high enough to resist the flow. The major 
challenge was to keep the pressure of two microchannels the same. To achieve this goal, we 
proposed a new design (Figure 4.12, upper), in which two flows would conjunct into a low-
path channel (Zoom-in images in Figure 4.12), aiming to balance the pressure difference 
between two microchannels, so that when they came out, the two pressure-driven flows could 
be the same. But it is important to carefully decide the dimensions of these part.

spread diffusively along the flow until fully mixing happens. The width of channel required for 
full mixing is related to Pe, a dimensionless number indicating the domination of convection 
or diffusion in flows (equation 1.7). And the distance flows need to reach fully mixing can be 
calculated with following equation[40]:

So, the distance for total mixing was 0.26m, and was much bigger than the dimension usually 
applied in a microfluidic device

4.4 Conclusion 
So far, we have investigated the aspect ratios of the slit, managing to fabricate the slits with 
height of 20µm which is lower than the resolution of the SLA 3D printer. We also explored the 
relation between width and length of the slits to find out the dimensions that can be used for 
gradient generation. In this architecture device, we got the linear gradient in shorter slits (1mm 
and 2mm-long) very fast (2 minutes) without flows. However, gradient still could not be 
generated with constant flows. As we have tried to increase the hydraulic resistance of slits as 
high as possible, the other strategy was to ensure the pressure difference was zero. In the end, 
we fabricated a new device with a slit 25µm-high, 600µm-wide and 2mm-long for fast 
diffusion. In this new device, to reduce the pressure difference between two microchannels, 
two conjunction channels (200µm and 1mm long) was intergraded into this new device, aiming 
==PAGE:77==
 68 
to balance the pressure value, so that when two laminar flows coming out, they could have the 
same pressure value. We found that the diffusion occurred in both hydrostatic pressure-driven 
system and constant flow rate set-up. However, in hydrostatic pressure-driven system, a linear 
gradient was gradually established in the whole slit (2mm), while in pumping pressure-driven 
system, linear gradient can only be observed in the middle of the slit (1.2mm).  The flow rate 
in two microchannels might play critical role, as in formal system, the initial given pressure 
was around 0.6mar and flow rate became smaller and smaller. But in constant flow rate set-up, 
the pressure value was kept at 1mbar during the whole process, as a result, there were always 
some convective flows at two ends of the slit, preventing the occurrence of diffusion. All in all, 
we successfully fabricated a microfluidic device that stable linear chemical gradient could be 
generated by providing two continuous flows. The gradient was established in the area that was 
600µm wide and 1.2mm long. And the generation time was less than 1h.

5 Conclusion and future prospect 
The ultimate goal of this project was to fabricate a microfluidic assay device, which can be 
used for imaging large array of cells under controlled pH gradient. To achieve this, so far, we 
have done the following work: 
 
Firstly, to make such device more affordable, we decided to use 3D printer for producing a 
microfluidic mold instead of traditional photolithography technology, as 3D printing 
technology was easy to be grasped even for a novice. Moreover, the expensive clean room 
resources can therefore be avoided. In our project, Stereolithography (SLA) 3D printer with a 
resolution of 25µm was employed, and the pattern in the SLA 3D printed mold could be 
replicated into a PDMS substrate by directly casting the PDMS mixture on mold. The molds 
covered with PDMS mixture were put in the oven at 60°C for 40h and PDMS polymer was 
completely cured. This, to our knowledge, has never been discovered before. Such new 
discovered technique enabled the fabrication of our microfluidic devices on much simplified 
process.  
 
Secondly, to image cells under controlled pH gradient, the initial design concept was to set two 
microchannels at either end of a slit (Figure 3.2), and the slit would contain an array of wells 
for isolating cells. Considering the low aspect ratio of slit, pillars were thought needed to 
support the slit and prevent sagging. The conclusion of my initial experiments was that we 
should abandon the use of the wells and that the pillars were not needed in the slit if a set of 
design rules established during this project was followed.  Even though, as we have known, we 
were the first ones who managed to find out the design rules of SLA 3D printed pillars and 
holes between designed value and practical value (the pillars and holes in mold turn out to be 
wells and support pillars in PDMS replica). For example, the changes in diameters of pillar 
were contrary to that in holes. The actual holes diameter was almost two times smaller than the 
designed value, while the pillars would become larger than design ones.  
 
Thirdly, in this device, it was hoped that diffusion would only occur through the slit by 
continuously providing laminar flows with different chemical concentrations. To avoid flows 
in the slit, at the beginning, we aimed to ensure the pressure provided to two microchannels 
was the same (ΔP = 0). However, it turned out to be quite difficult. We can only observe linear 
gradient generation with no flows (Figure 4.7 and Figure 4.10). One reason was that the 
==PAGE:79==
 70 
dimensions of two microchannels was always different after bonding. The other reason was 
that for both type of experiments, one where the flow was maintained by an air pressure system 
and one where the flow was obtained by hydrostatic pressure, there can exist some pressure 
differences between two ends of the slit that cannot be adjusted to the same manually. We then 
tried to suppress the flow in the slit by increasing its hydraulic resistance. Hydraulic resistance 
of a slit is mainly influenced by its height (equation 1.5). This parameter was further limited 
by the use of SLA 3D printer, which usually has a lower resolution than traditional 
photolithography.  Still, after a series of experiments, I managed to fabricated serval devices 
having 20µm-high slits, a height lower than the printing resolution. And in spite of the absence 
of pillars to support the slit, collapsing did not happen. 20µm was the smallest height I could 
have, and I believe, no one has managed to use a 3D printer to increase the resolution of device 
up to 20µm.  
 
Although the hydraulic resistance of slit was increased as high as possible, the results showed 
that linear gradient cannot be generated when there were continuous flows in the microchannels. 
These devices were still sensitive to even small pressure difference. At the end, to reduce the 
pressure difference, conjunction channels were integrated into the previously mentioned design. 
The two flows would first join at these junctions, so that the pressure in the two microchannels 
is equal at least at those junction points. So, there is a better chance that the pressures at the 
ends of the slit are balanced. Once coming out, the pressure in two flows would be the same. 
With this new concept, we successfully fabricated a device with the components as follows: 
two 150µm (w) × 150µm (h) × 71mm (l) microchannels, a 600µm × 25µm × 2mm slit, a 
200µm-long conjunction channel and a 1mm-long conjunction channel. The results showed 
that a linear gradient was generated in the middle of the slit by providing two continuously 
pressure-driven flows (1mbar) and the diffusion time was less than 1h (Figure 4.15). 
 
So far, we have fabricated microfluidic devices to generate linear fluorescein gradient. This 
system was more complex compared to generating a pH gradient. Indeed, the diffusion 
coefficient of pH solution is 8000µm2/s, around 10 times smaller than the diffusion constant of 
fluorescein. It means, the device will be less sensitive to pressure difference (equation 1.7 and 
2.0) when used to establish a pH gradient that when used for creating a fluorescein gradient. 
However, there still many challenges ready to be solved. For example, it is better to establish 
the gradient along the whole slit by removing the convective part (Figure 4.15). The slit used 
now has a relatively small area, which might not ideal for array of cells imaging. For these 
==PAGE:80==
 71 
purposes, we may: 1) set several parallel slits between the two microchannels, so the total area 
would be increased; 2)  add more parallel microchannels (four channels), so that small slits 
between every two microchannels can layer together, integrated as a relatively big and longer 
slit. If do so, four streams of liquids with four different concentration could be introduced at 
the same time, the range of the gradient also increase; 3) the shape of the slit can be changed 
to the one like a ‘lemon’, whose two ends are very narrow and middle part is wider. Thereinto, 
two narrow ends are aimed to remove the convective flow part always existed in the slits 
(Chapter 4.3); 4) at two ends of the slit, many micropillars can be orderly arranged in order to 
separate the intersection between microchannel and slit. These micropillars may help resist the 
leakage of solution into slit, expecting to resist the occurrence of convective flow. 
 
In this thesis, the PDMS device was permanently sealed with glass, however, this may not be 
good if we introduce the cell culture. A better bonding method is needed in the future.

PBS (1.07µL) buffer were introduced into each channel at 2mbar pressure with no mixing in

the device flatly in a black box. In this circumstance, microchannels served as reservoirs

supplying the sample solutions. After overnight, a fluorescein gradient can be seen in the slit 
between two microchannels (Supplement Figure 1B). However, there are a number of 
unwanted effects. The transparent pillars in the slit appeared dark when in a totally black 
environment.  We also observed that both the microchannels displayed different fluorescent 
intensity which was not expected since we would like one of the channels (filled with PBS 
before) to be empty of fluorescein. Air appeared at bottom left due to the gradually drying of 
fluorescein solution at room temperature. As a source of fluorescein, left microchannel released 
the highest light intensity (85 gray value) and the brightness was kept consistent along the 
horizontal direction of whole channel (290µm wide) (Supplement Figure 1C). Then diffusion 
occurred once entering the slit, and the intensity of fluorescence decreased linearly until 
reaching steady state (Supplement Figure 1D). It was noteworthy that at the very front of the 
slit, the brightness was still high, but dropped dramatically from 75 to 45 gray value within 
around 100 micrometers. It may because the border just connecting to the microchannel was a 
bit deeper than the rest of the slit (Supplement Figure 1B), causing the amount of fluorescein 
solution imaged to be higher at that part. The other reason can be that since microchannel was 
much higher than the slit and had the highest intensity, its light might scatter around, increasing 
the bright value of its surrounding part. Ideally, the fluorescein concentration and hence the 
fluorescence intensity should be decreasing linearly along the slit if a diffusion gradient is 
established. The reality was that there were always downward fluctuations (smaller value than 
their surroundings) along the descent line. This was reasonable considering the existence of 
well-arranged black dots with lowest gray value. At the very end of the slit, the intensity 
increased sharply from around 4 to 30, then was kept at almost the same level among the other 
channel (270µm wide). During the diffusion process, fluorescein would diffuse from high 
concentration to lower concentration, and eventually into channel with PBS buffer. As more 
and more fluorescein accumulated into PBS channel, the intensity in this channel would rise. 
But the fluorescein in this channel was more like a further diluted solution, of less concentration 
than the one in fluorescein resource channel. Thus, we got a microchannel that had gray value 
three times lower than the other one (Supplement Figure 1E). The reasons for significantly 
rising intensity at the end of slit can be the same as explained before: increased depth of a very 
small area; close to the microchannel with high light brightness (scattering effect).
