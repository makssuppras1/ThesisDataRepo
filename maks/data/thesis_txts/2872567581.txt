Abstract 
This study examines the feasibility of treating harvested rainwater to drinking‐water standards for 
industrial use at Novo Nordisk’s Hillerød facility, aiming to reduce groundwater dependency.  
Rainwater samples exhibited an alkaline pH (7.6), high turbidity (9.5 NTU) and conductivity (3.62 
mS/cm), and elevated organic TSS (24 mg/L), COD (57 mg O₂/L), PFAS (1.2 µg/L) and ionic loads, 
all reflecting surface runoff and industrial point-source contamination. Four treatment trains were 
compared for optimal performance: coagulation-flocculation with nano‐bubbling (CF), ultrafiltration 
(UF), CF→UF, and UF→reverse osmosis (RO). The UF→RO sequence proved superior, being the 
only process to meet drinking water turbidity and conductivity standards (each reduced by over 93%) 
and delivering almost complete COD removal. It also removed > 93 % of major ions (NO₃⁻, NO₂⁻, 
K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Si, P) and ≈75 % of NH₄⁺ and Mn, bringing all analytes into drinking water 
compliance, except Mn (detection limit uncertainty) and pH (6.65 vs. 7–8.5), which can be corrected 
via remineralization or closed-system operation. Economic modeling shows, that UF→RO treatment 
costs (~5.2 DKK/m³) are lower than the 11-16 DKK/m³ groundwater tariff and could save up to 
300,000 m³ of groundwater annually. Overall, UF→RO represents a promising, cost-efficient 
approach for purifying rainwater to drinking-water quality for pharmaceutical use at Novo Nordisk, 
Hillerød.

1. Introduction 
With climate change intensifying, its consequences are becoming increasingly severe, and one of the 
most pressing global challenges is water scarcity. Rising temperatures, more frequent droughts, 
heatwaves, and unpredictable weather patterns are reducing freshwater availability while demand 
remains high (United Nations, n.d.). In Denmark, industry accounts for 23% of the country’s total 
water consumption, and the growing pharmaceutical sector is putting a significant pressure on 
regional groundwater reserves (Louise Wistrup Gerau, 2023). Municipalities like Hillerød are 
particularly affected, as high water demands from Novo Nordisk and other major pharmaceutical 
companies is accelerating groundwater depletion. The municipality projects an annual shortfall of 
one million m3 of water from 2024 and onward, highlighting the need for alternative water sources 
(Rothenborg, 2023).  
Paradoxically, while some Danish regions experience groundwater scarcity, precipitation is rising 
due to climate change. Since the late 1800s the average wet deposition has increased with 100 mm, 
suggesting that rainwater could serve as a viable alternative to groundwater for industrial applications 
(Danish Meterologican Institute (DMI), 2021). However, despite its abundance, rainwater remains 
largely unutilized in Danish water management systems (Goodley Dannisoe, n.d.). One key challenge 
is the limited understanding of rainwater composition, both in terms of natural impurities and human-
made contaminants, such as per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS). These pollutants raise 
concerns about water safety and require advanced detection and treatment technologies (Cousins et 
al., 2022). However, with climate change accelerating, freshwater demand remaining high, and 
rainwater becoming increasingly abundant, exploring its potential as an industrial water resource 
seems both relevant and necessary. While rainwater is often considered relatively clean, it becomes 
stormwater once it hits impervious surfaces (i.e. roofs, roads, etc.), picking up pollutants as it flows.  
This report investigates the feasibility of utilizing rainwater as a sustainable water source for 
pharmaceutical processes in Denmark, thereby reducing industrial reliance on the limited 
groundwater reserves. It explores the development of treatment technologies capable of purifying 
rainwater to drinking water standards, ensuring its suitability for industrial applications. Additionally, 
the study assesses the economic viability of implementing rainwater treatment solutions at Novo 
Nordisk's Hillerød facility. By doing so, the research aims to contribute to innovative water 
management strategies that promote sustainability and resource efficiency in the pharmaceutical 
industry.

1.1 Literature Review on Rainwater Pollutants in Denmark  
Danish rainwater quality has been extensively monitored in the last few decades. Indeed, national 
programs such as NOVANA or the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) have catalogued 
an impressive number of pollutants, including heavy metals, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons 
(PAHs), pesticides, pharmaceuticals, alkylphenols, and PFAS.  
Recent large-scale studies, including the decade-long assessment by Mutzner (Mutzner et al., 2022) 
and the national Miljøstyrelsen (MST) Rapport (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2022), 
provide robust datasets on pollutant concentrations and their spatial and temporal variability. We aim 
to present a comprehensive and concise assessment of the occurrence and environmental risks of 
pollutants in Danish rainwater, thereby allowing direct comparisons with both international studies 
and the legal requirements for water quality in Denmark. A figurative illustration of critical pollutants 
found in rainwater is presented in Figure 1-1. 
Mutzner et al. classified the pollutants found in stormwater outlets (SWO) by comparing their 
occurrences and risk quotient (RQ), allowing researchers to prioritize the most dangerous 
micropollutants (Mutzner et al., 2022). The data, sourced from Europe, Australia and the US, 
identifies the following top ten micropollutants: benzo[a]pyrene, fluoranthene, pyrene, copper, zinc, 
mercury, benzo[b]fluoranthene, chrysene, benzo[ghi]perylene, dibenz[ah]antracene. These findings 
indicate that the highest risk quotients are associated with metals and PAHs, a conclusion that 
resonates strongly with the Danish monitoring results.

 Heavy Metals  
Heavy metals are persistent concerns in Danish rainwater, particularly in urban areas where their 
accumulation is caused by road runoff, building materials, and atmospheric deposition. Studies by 
Birch et al. (2011) and the MST Report (2022) indicate that metals, like copper, zinc, lead, and 
cadmium are frequently detected in stormwater, supporting Mutzner’s findings.  
Danish studies report type numbers indicating that copper concentrations average around 9 µg/L in 
rainwater discharges, values that can be up to 13 times higher than the Danish legal standards for 
surface waters (Birch et al., 2011). Similarly, zinc is observed at approximately 130 µg/L, far 
exceeding the prescribed quality criteria of 7.8 µg/L (Danish Ministry BEK nr 796, 2023). Lead and 
cadmium are also consistently detected, albeit at lower concentrations; lead is typically detected in 
the range of 3–5 µg/L, and cadmium at sub-µg/L levels, nonetheless both raising concerns about 
bioaccumulation and sediment contamination.

Metals have been identified as the dominant contributor to ecotoxicity in stormwater discharges, 
reaching a staggering 90% contribution, reinforcing the need for continued monitoring and mitigation 
strategies (Brudler et al., 2019).  
 
Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) 
PAHs are of significant concern due to their persistence and carcinogenic properties. Studies have 
identified benzo[a]pyrene, fluoranthene, and pyrene as highest‐risk PAHs in urban wet-weather 
flows, based on their occurrence and hazardous nature (Mutzner et al., 2022). The Danish EPA reports 
type numbers for these PAHs as approximately 0.05 µg/L for benzo[a]pyrene, 0.013 µg/L for 
fluoranthene, and 0.015 µg/L for pyrene (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2022), greatly 
exceeding the Danish legal thresholds. For instance, MAC (the Maximum Allowed Concentration) 
for benzo[a]pyrene in surface water is 0.027 µg/L, which means that the observed type number of 
0.05 µg/L exceeds the permissible limit by nearly a factor of two. (Bekendtgørelse om fastlæggelse 
af miljømål for vandløb, søer, overgangsvande, kystvande og grundvand, 2023). 
Data from Birch et al. on separate stormwater discharges reveals substantial variability in PAH 
concentrations across different Danish sites. For instance, the type number for benzo[a]pyrene in 
rainwater discharges ranged from <0.01 µg/L to 0.31 µg/L. Moreover, it was found that the total PAH 
concentration in some stormwater samples exceeded the EQS (Environmental Quality Standard) by 
up to 500 (Birch et al., 2011). 
Pesticides  
Pesticides, while typically present at lower concentrations than heavy metals or PAHs, remain a cause 
for concern because of their persistent occurrence in rainwater, and biological activity even at trace 
concentrations. The Danish Environmental Protection Agency identified glyphosate in nearly all 
sampled wet deposition events, with a type concentration of 0.40 µg/L, and its primary degradation 
product, AMPA, at about 0.11 µg/L (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2022). These values 
often exceed the very low legal limits established for drinking water in Denmark (0.1 µg/L individual 
pesticides; 0.5 µg/L sum of all pesticides (Miljøministeriet, 2025), highlighting chronic exposure 
risks posed by pesticide residues in urban runoff. Other pesticides identified in stormwater include 
CSPA (0.17 µg/L), mechlorprop (0.014 µg/L), diflufenican (0.023 µg/L), diuron (0.011 µg/L) and 
MCPA (0.013 µg/L).

Substances that are no longer authorized for use in Denmark have also been detected in precipitation 
and runoff samples. Asman et al. reported maximum concentrations of 15 ng/L and 71 ng/L for 
atrazine and isoproturon in Danish rainwater. The continuing detection of these banned compounds 
suggests long-range atmospheric transport or residual contamination from historical applications 
(Asman et al., 2005). 
Beyond conventional agricultural pesticides, urban stormwater frequently contains biocides 
originating from building materials, antifouling coatings, and personal care products. Bollmann et al. 
analyzed 191 stormwater samples for 11 different biocidal substances in Denmark. They found 
carbendazim and terbutryn in all samples, with median concentrations of 45 ng/L and 52 ng/L, 
respectively. Other compounds such as isoproturon, propiconazole, mecoprop, diuron, and iodocarb 
were also frequently detected at lower concentrations (2–7 ng/L), indicating diverse sources and 
persistent urban use (Bollmann et al., 2014).  
 
Pharmaceuticals and Personal Care Products (PPCPs)   
Pharmaceuticals and personal care products (PPCPs) are emerging micropollutants increasingly 
detected in Danish rainwater and stormwater. While typically present at trace levels (<1 µg/L), 
compounds like caffeine, triclosan, acetaminophen, nicotine, and metformin raise concerns due to 
their persistence and potential endocrine-disrupting effects (Spahr et al., 2020). Danish studies 
confirm PPCPs in both combined sewer overflows and separate stormwater discharges, with sources 
including domestic wastewater, road runoff, and atmospheric deposition. Notably, benzothiazoles 
and benzotriazoles, used in antifreezes and corrosion inhibitors, are common even in separate 
stormwater systems (Zhang et al., 2024). 
 
Other Organic Compounds (Alkylphenols, Plasticizer, etc) 
Alkylphenols, especially nonylphenol (NP), are legacy pollutants in Danish rainwater, typically 
below 1 µg/L but still of concern due to their endocrine-disrupting effects (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, 2022). Type numbers from the MST report show concentrations of ~0.040 µg/L 
for NP, ~0.080 µg/L for BPA, and ~0.20 µg/L for phenol, sometimes exceeding environmental 
quality standards. Recent analyses show Bisphenol A (BPA), NP, and octylphenol (OP) are frequently 
detected in highway runoff, primarily from traffic-related materials and infrastructure chemicals 
(Beryani et al., 2023). 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Plasticizers, mainly phthalates like DEHP and diisononyl phthalate, are also common in stormwater, 
with DEHP measured up to 130 µg/L and BPA up to 12.7 µg/L in international studies, far above 
drinking water guidelines. 
Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), though banned since the 1970s, persist in runoff due to leaching 
from old building materials. Danish and European studies report PCBs, notably PCB-138, in 
stormwater at lower concentrations (max ~0.1 µg/L), but still widely detected (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, 2022 ; Zhang et al., 2024). 
 
Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances (PFAS) 
PFAS have recently emerged as contaminants of significant concern, due to their extreme persistence 
and bioaccumulative nature. Denmark follows an approach that focuses on four key perfluoroalkyl 
acids (PFAAs) in drinking water: 
• 
PFOA (Perfluorooctanoic Acid) 
• 
PFOS (Perfluorooctane Sulfonic Acid) 
• 
PFHxS (Perfluorohexane Sulfonic Acid) 
• 
PFNA (Perfluorononanoic Acid) 
These four PFAS are prioritized due to their high toxicity and persistence in human blood, which can 
lead to adverse health effects. They are specifically targeted for restriction or phase-out under 
international regulations like the EU Drinking Water Directive and the Stockholm Convention. The 
four compounds are among the most widely detected PFAS in surface water and groundwater, making 
them suitable markers for regulatory compliance. Denmark enforces a particularly stringent drinking 
water limit of 2 ng/L for the sum of these four PFAS (Σ4 PFAS), reflecting the precautionary stance 
the country has taken toward PFAS contamination (Cousins et al., 2022).  
Despite the relevance of PFAS monitoring in rainwater, monitoring in Danish rainwater is scarce. 
However, a Swedish study detected PFAS in 100% of 22 rain events, with mean concentrations of 
PFOA, PFNA, PFOS, PFHxS reaching respectively 650 pg/L, 178 pg/L, 81 pg/L, and 16 pg/L. In this 
case, the sum of these four remained below the requirement of 2 ng/L, however other PFAS, such as 
PFHxA reached concentrations as high as 1.7 ng/L in some events (Filipovic et al., 2015). The EU 
set a directive on the quality of water intended for human consumption, targeting 20 PFAS with a 
quality limit set at 100 ng/L for the sum of their individual concentrations. (Berthou et al., 2024).

Nutrients and Ions 
Inorganic ions in rainwater can originate from both natural processes and industrial activities. These 
ions include dissolved minerals, salts, and organic compounds, with the majority being inorganic 
cations (e.g., H⁺, Ca²⁺, Na⁺, Mg²⁺) and anions (e.g., HCO₃⁻, Cl⁻, NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻). Although these ions 
are naturally present in rainwater and generally require high concentrations to pose a toxicity risk, 
extremely polluted rainwater, such as surface runoff from industrial areas, can contain levels that far 
exceed drinking water standards (Morales-Figueroa et al., 2023). 
Rainwater also carries low levels of nutrients, including NH₄⁺, NO₂⁻, NH₃, and PO₄³⁻, which primarily 
derive from human activities like combustion, agriculture, and vehicular emissions. These nutrients 
fuel plant growth which is a problem in aquatic environments, where algal overgrowth leads to 
oxygen depletion and ecosystem disruption. Furthermore, because these nutrients are toxic to humans 
even at low levels, they are strictly regulated, with drinking water limits all below 150 µg/L 
(Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen, 2024). 
 
Microplastics  
Microplastics (MPs) are increasingly present in water, notably water intended for human 
consumption. They are formed from plastic debris weathering in nature until reaching less than 5mm 
in size. The most frequently detected polymers are high‐density polyethylene (HDPE), low‐density 
polyethylene (LDPE), polypropylene (PP), polyvinyl chloride (PVC), polyethylene terephthalate 
(PET), polystyrene (PS) and polyamide (PA). (Wei et al., 2023). 
 Major sources of MPs in stormwater include tire and road wear particles (TRWPs), littering, and 
road dust. (Shafi et al., 2024) Wet deposition plays a key role in transferring airborne microplastics 
from the atmosphere to terrestrial and aquatic environments in both urban and remote areas. 
Additionally, their large surface-area-to-volume ratio allows them to adsorb other contaminants such 
as heavy metals, organic micropollutants and pathogens, turning them into mobile carriers that 
amplify ecological and human-health risks (Wei et al., 2023). 
 
Bacterial Pollution in Rainwater 
While rainwater is often perceived as a cleaner alternative to surface water, it can still carry significant 
bacterial contamination from atmospheric deposition or collection surfaces (i.e. rooftops). Airborne 
bacteria and bioaerosols can be transported by wind and incorporated into raindrops during 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
precipitation events. The microbial composition of rainwater is thus influenced by local atmospheric 
conditions, including wind speed and direction. (Evans et al., 2007). Moreover, rooftop harvesting 
systems can also introduce bacteria from organic debris, dust, and animal droppings (Evans et al., 
2007). Rainwater stored in rooftop tanks often exhibits significant variations in microbial diversity 
depending on catchment material, maintenance practices, and environmental exposure (Meera & 
Ahammed, 2006). Finally, as rainwater flows over roads, highways, and other impervious surfaces, 
it can accumulate additional microbial contaminants, including coliform bacteria, with runoff studies 
detecting fecal bacteria at levels that depend strongly on local conditions. (Ahmed et al., 2019) 
Studies show that bacteria are frequently detected in harvested rainwater, with microbial 
contamination being a common issue. In a study of seven Danish rainwater harvesting systems, 
bacteria were found in nearly all samples, and 12 out of 27 samples (44%) contained at least one 
potentially pathogenic organism, such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Legionella non-pneumophila, 
Campylobacter jejuni, and Mycobacterium avium  (Albrechtsen, 2002). In contrast, reference 
drinking water systems did not contain these pathogens, confirming that harvested rainwater 
consistently introduces microbial risks.

1.2 Traditional Approaches to Rainwater Treatment 
The treatment of rainwater relies on a combination of disinfection and filtration processes, designed 
to address the diverse range of contaminants in the water (Raimondi et al., 2023). Disinfection 
methods are essential to ensure the microbiological safety of rainwater. Chlorination remains the most 
widely used conventional approach, valued for its low cost and efficiency against most waterborne 
pathogens. However, the formation of disinfection by-products (DBPs), such as trihalomethanes and 
haloacetic acids, poses notable health concerns, and requires careful operational management (Latif 
et al., 2023). Ultraviolet (UV) disinfection has emerged as an alternative method, achieving >99.99% 
inactivation of bacteria, viruses, and protozoa without introducing chemical residues. However, its 
lack of residual effect makes UV systems vulnerable to post-treatment recontamination if storage and 
distribution systems are compromised (Raimondi et al., 2023; Zdeb & Papciak, 2023). Other 
approaches, such as solar disinfection (SODIS) and thermal treatments, are better suited to household-
scale systems, given their dependence on environmental conditions. They also do not provide any 
residual disinfection (Amin et al., 2014). 
Filtration methods are essential for removing suspended solids, turbidity, and certain chemical 
components. Slow sand filtration (SSF) is a commonly used technique because of its simple design 
and low cost.  It uses sand on top of gravel beds to remove suspended solids, organics, and pathogens, 
with reported turbidity removal rates exceeding 95% (Raimondi et al., 2023). However, the large 
spatial footprint of SSF limits its industrial scalability. Granular activated carbon (GAC) filtration 
enhances dissolved organic carbon removal through adsorption, though it lacks microbial removal 
capacity and requires periodic replacement (Gibert et al., 2013). Dual-media filters combine sand and 
GAC, offering improved performance, particularly for organic contaminants and taste/odor 
compounds. Ceramic filters effectively removes bacteria and particulates, but their application is 
constrained by low rainwater input (Raimondi et al., 2023). While these conventional processes 
remain cost-effective and widely adopted, they face limitations in addressing emerging contaminants 
such as pharmaceuticals, microplastics, and persistent organic pollutants. Moreover, the dependency 
on chemical reagents such as coagulants and chlorine raises sustainability and operational concerns.

1.3 Advanced Rainwater Treatment: Membrane Technology 
Membrane-based approaches have emerged as leading alternatives to conventional rainwater 
treatment, offering high removal efficiency for a broad spectrum of contaminants, at lower costs 
(Devaisy et al., 2023). These technologies rely on size exclusion, charge interactions, and diffusion-
based separation, with performance varying by membrane pore size and material, and operating 
conditions. Common membrane technologies include microfiltration (MF), ultrafiltration (UF), 
nanofiltration (NF), and reverse osmosis (RO), each distinguished by its pore characteristics and 
rejection mechanisms. Figure 1-3 illustrates these four membrane types alongside the contaminant 
classes they target. 
MF membranes, with pore sizes of 0.1–5 μm, effectively remove particulates and bacteria (>99% 
bacterial removal). However, MF offers low removal rates of large organics and pathogens, therefore 
limiting its use in rainwater applications unless combined with other water treatment processes (X. 
Liu et al., 2021a). 
UF is characterized by tighter pore sizes (5-100 nm) and superior removal of suspended solids, 
colloids, bacteria, and macromolecules, achieving over 99% pathogen removal and making it highly 
suitable for rainwater treatment. Compared to MF, UF provides higher filtration precision and can 
produce demineralized water without the high energy demands of RO, resulting in lower operational 
costs and cleaning frequency (X. Liu et al., 2021b). Additional advantages include UF’s tolerance to 
acidic, alkaline, and high-temperature conditions. However, challenges remain in handling grease, 
removing heavy metals, and mitigating membrane fouling, thus limiting its broader application 
(Raimondi et al., 2023). 
NF bridges the gap between UF and RO, offering partial desalination by rejecting divalent ions, 
sulfates, and natural organic matter. NF membranes typically achieve >90% removal of organics and 
>99% removal of sulfates, making them highly suitable for rainwater treatment (X. Liu et al., 2021a). 
Compared to RO, NF operates at lower pressures and offers higher water flux, making it a more 
energy-efficient option (X. Liu et al., 2021). However, NF systems still require careful pretreatment 
to prevent membrane scaling and fouling, and further research is needed to address the removal of 
emerging contaminants (X. Liu et al., 2021; Raimondi et al., 2023). 
Reverse osmosis (RO) represents the highest standard for high-purity water production, using dense 
membranes to reject virtually all dissolved salts, heavy metals, micropollutants, and pathogens, with 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
removal rates exceeding 99.9% (Fu & Wang, 2011). However, its deployment in rainwater treatment 
systems is often constrained by high energy consumption, frequent maintenance demands, and 
susceptibility to membrane fouling, which can lead to increased operating pressures, reduced flux, 
and shortened membrane lifespan (X. Liu et al., 2021). 
While membrane technologies individually offer substantial advantages over conventional rainwater 
treatment methods, each comes with inherent limitations, particularly regarding membrane fouling, 
energy consumption, or incomplete contaminant removal. Notably, UF provides excellent retention 
of suspended solids, colloids, and microorganisms, but struggles with dissolved salts and 
micropollutants. In contrast, reverse osmosis RO offers near-total rejection of dissolved and low-
molecular-weight contaminants, yet its operation is energy-intensive and highly susceptible to 
membrane fouling. Consequently, pretreatment, such as sedimentation or coagulation–flocculation, 
is often required to protect membranes and ensure efficient operation in rainwater harvesting systems.

1.4 Project scope 
Context and Problem Statement 
Novo Nordisks production facility in Hillerød faces acute water scarcity due to rising industrial 
demand and tightening groundwater reserves. Yet, paradoxically, the region experiences abundant 
annual precipitation that remains largely untapped. This presents a compelling opportunity: could 
harvested and treated rainwater be used as an alternative water source, specifically for technical 
applications, thereby reducing reliance on groundwater? 
Scope 
The scope of this thesis encompasses four main parts:  
- 
Pollutant Characerization: Conduct a detailed characterization of the pollutants present in 
harvested rainwater to quantify critical parameters for treatment. 
- 
Comparative Treatment Configurations: Compare different configurations of three treatment 
processes: (a) Coagulation, Flocculation, Nano-Bubbling, (b) Ultrafiltration, and (c) Reverse 
Osmosis to select the optimal treatment train for meeting drinking-water standards. 
- 
Performance Validation: Assess and evaluate the efficiency and performance of the chosen 
treatment system, and verify its ability to achieve drinking-water quality. 
- 
Feasibility Assessment: An in-depth evaluation on the practical applicability of this 
technology at the Novo Nordisk Hillerød site, examining whether harvested and treated 
rainwater could feasibly replace groundwater for technical uses.

2 Theory 
2.1 Membrane Technology 
Membrane technology refers to the scientific and engineering principles involved in the separation or 
transport of substances through semi-permeable membranes. It plays a vital role in various industrial 
sectors, including food processing, pharmaceutical production, and biotechnology, owing to its 
multidisciplinary nature and its ability to perform highly selective separations. 
Membrane technology uses semi-permeable membranes to separate components of a mixture. These 
membranes are selective barriers, meaning that they selectively allow certain molecules or ions to 
pass through, while others are retained based on size, charge, or chemical properties. The mixture 
entering the system is called the feed flow. After separation, the fraction passing through the 
membrane is called the permeate, while the fraction that is retained on the feed side is referred to as 
the retentate or concentrate (Saleh & Gupta, 2016). This mechanism is illustrated in Figure 2-1. 
 
Figure 2-1: Typical transport terminology for membrane modules. Figure taken from Pal, 2017, 
p.176. 
There are different types of material used for membrane processes. Most commonly are polymeric 
and ceramic membranes. Ceramic membranes are strong and stable, and they can resist high 
temperatures, flows, and strong chemicals. Additionally, these membranes are less prone to fouling 
because they can be aggressively cleaned, which makes them ideal for industrial wastewater. 
Polymeric membranes are much more sensitive, but they offer more flexibility in design, and they 
are much cheaper. Due to the price, these membranes are more commonly used in industrial 
applications, specifically in pharma and biotech processes.

2.2 Membrane Transport Mechanism  
Membrane transport refers to the movement of a solute through a membrane. In membrane 
technology, this transport is driven by a gradient, typically a pressure or concentration gradient. 
However, the mechanisms by which solutes are transported vary depending on the membrane type. 
This section outlines two primary transport models: pore flow, which describes solute movement 
through UF membranes, and the solution-diffusion model, which explains transport through RO 
membranes. 
2.2.1 Pore Flow Model 
Pore flow refers to the movement of a mixture through a microporous membrane. In these 
membranes, transport is dominated by convective flow through pores, which act as physical barriers

water is 1.00 kg/m·s, but at 5°C the value is 1.52. This means that using colder water can significantly

2.2.2  Solution-Diffusion Model  
One of the most important characteristics of membranes is their ability to control the rate and 
selectivity of species permeation. For dense, non-porous membranes such as those used in RO, the 
prevailing theoretical framework describing this transport is the solution–diffusion model (SDM). 
This model assumes that permeants are first absorbed (or “dissolved”) into the membrane material at 
the high-pressure side, then diffuse through the membrane driven by a chemical potential gradient 
and finally desorb into the low-pressure side. Permeates are therefore separated based on their 
individual solubilities in the membrane material and diffusion rates.

Solution–Diffusion Model Assumptions 
Several simplifying assumptions are used to describe membrane systems: 
1. Thermodynamic equilibrium at the membrane interfaces: The fluids contact with the 
membrane is assumed to be in local thermodynamic equilibrium with the membrane material. 
This implies that the chemical potential is continuous across the interface, and sorption-
desorption rates are much faster than diffusion through the membrane. Thus, the rate-limiting 
step in permeation is the diffusion through the membrane, and not the interfacial mass transfer. 
2. Uniform pressure within the membrane: The pressure throughout the membrane is constant 
and equal to the higher-pressure side. This is a key distinction from convective models. The 
assumption is valid because dense polymer membranes lack pores through which pressure 
gradients can develop. Consequently, the chemical potential gradient within the membrane 
reduces solely to a concentration gradient. This assumption allows flux to be described in 
terms of solute diffusivity and concentration difference, greatly simplifying the analysis. 
Conversely, models such as the pore-flow model account for convective transport driven by 
pressure gradients, which are absent in the SDM.

2.4 Concentration Polarization and Membrane Fouling 
Concentration Polarization 
Membrane performance can be affected by concentration polarization (CP), where rejected solutes 
accumulate at the membrane surface: As water permeates the membrane, solutes are largely retained, 
forming a concentrated boundary layer, where concentrations at the membrane surface can reach up 
to 50× those in the bulk solution. In RO, this solute accumulation raises the local osmotic pressure, 
diminishes the net driving force and thus reduces water flux. In UF, CP simply increases flow 
resistance, even though TMP remains unchanged.  
In addition to a steep concentration gradient, the boundary layer also exhibits a turbulence gradient: 
due to friction, flow transitions from turbulent in the bulk to laminar near the membrane surface, 
significantly lowering local velocity: flow is fastest in the channel center and essentially zero right 
next to the membrane. Within this concentration boundary layer, molecular diffusion becomes the 
dominant mode of solute transport away from the membrane, rather than convective mixing. As the 
permeation of water continues, solutes become increasingly concentrated in this boundary layer 
unless efficiently removed or dispersed. This mechanism is presented in Figure 2-2. 
Fouling Mechanisms 
Concentration Polarization often leads to fouling, which further reduces membrane flux. Two primary 
fouling mechanisms are surface fouling and internal membrane fouling, illustrated Figure 2-3.  
• Surface Fouling: Occurs when particles larger than the membrane pores accumulate on the 
membrane surface. Over time, these retained particles form a cake layer, reducing flux and 
preventing smaller particles from passing through. Fortunately, surface fouling is reversible, 
which means that proper cleaning can restore the membrane’s original performance.

2.5 Ultrafiltration  
UF is a widely used membrane filtration technology developed by Bechhold in the early 1900s. It’s 
a pressure-driven separation process, meaning that a pressure gradient forces liquid through a semi-
permeable membrane, enabling separation between the permeable and impermeable solutes. The 
membrane consists of a finely porous surface layer on a microporous sublayer. The pore size of the 
surface layer ranges between 0.01–0.1 µm, allowing it to effectively retain macromolecules, colloids, 
suspended solids and bacteria. UF operates at industrial pressures of about 3.4–8.3 bar, significantly 
lower compared to RO’s 14–500 bar (Baker, 2012; Khan et al., 2022).  
Membrane characterization 
Ultrafiltration membranes are anisotropic, with properties, that vary with thickness. A finely porous 
skin layer carries out the separation, while a coarser microporous support beneath provides 
mechanical strength. UF membranes are characterized by a Molecular Weight Cut-Off (MWCO), 
which is a size-exclusion mechanism. MWCO refers to the lowest weight of a solute for which 90% 
are retained by the membrane. In general, this means that 90% of molecules this size will be retained, 
while 10% will pass through the membrane. The MWCO is related to the pore size of the membrane, 
where a lower MWCO means smaller pore sizes, so smaller molecules are retained. However, this 
also leads to a lower flux and increased risk of fouling. A higher MWCO allows larger molecules to 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
pass through, improving flux but reducing retention efficiency. For UF systems, MWCO is typically 
between 1-500 kDa (Baker, 2012; Imbrogno et al., 2025). 
Ultrafiltration in Rainwater Treatment 
In rainwater harvesting and treatment, UF technology is seen as a promising membrane treatment, 
because it effectively removes macromolecules, particularly colloids, suspended solids, and 
microorganisms, resulting in excellent turbidity reduction. Studies report 100 % removal of Total 
Suspended Solids (TSS) and 66 % Bacterial Oxygen Demand (BOD) reduction. The treatment is 
often compared with microfiltration because of their similar characteristics. However, UF is generally 
considered more promising for rainwater treatment since it retains the macromolecules commonly 
found in rainwater, whereas MF only captures a portion of them (X. Liu et al., 2021; Wartalska et al., 
2024). 
However, UF is less effective at removing dissolved salts, heavy metals, certain PAH’s, pesticides, 
pharmaceuticals, and other low molecular weight contaminants. It also struggles with grease removal 
and is prone to membrane fouling. Consequently, UF technology still requires improvements to 
become a more attractive option for rainwater treatment. In the mean time, pre‐treatment steps like 
sedimentation or coagulation/flocculation are necessary to minimize fouling and maintain 
ultrafiltration performance (Raimondi et al., 2023). 
2.6 Reverse Osmosis (RO) 
Reverse Osmosis Principle 
Reverse Osmosis is a pressure-driven membrane process that produces highly purified water by 
forcing it through a semi-permeable membrane that rejects nearly all dissolved and suspended 
contaminants. The technology can effectively remove mono- and multivalent ions, therefore rejecting 
impurities such as heavy metals, salts, low molecular weight (LMW) organics (pesticides, PAHs, 
PPCPs), and small microorganisms (small viruses and pathogens). The primary purpose of RO in 
rainwater treatment is to produce ultra-pure water that meets stringent drinking water standards, 
making it especially useful when prior treatment steps leave behind dissolved contaminants or when 
water quality is highly variable. (Raimondi et al., 2023) 
Osmosis is a natural phenomenon, where a solution of lower solute concentration will naturally 
migrate to a solution of higher solute concentration when separated by a semi-permeable membrane. 
When two solutions with different concentrations are separated by a semi-permeable membrane, 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
water molecules naturally flow from the less concentrated solution toward the more concentrated 
solution until equilibrium is reached. The driving force for this flow is the chemical potential gradient, 
which, under ideal conditions, can be equated to an osmotic pressure difference (Δπ). 
RO inverts this natural flow by applying an external pressure (ΔP) greater than Δπ, forcing water to 
move from the high-solute concentration side (feed) to the low-solute concentration side (permeate). 
In RO systems, a high-pressure pump applies pressure to the feed of the system, forcing water across 
the semi-permeable membrane while leaving behind 95% to 99% of dissolved contaminants in the 
reject stream. The pressure required for this process directly correlates with the solute concentration 
of the feed water, as higher concentrations necessitate greater pressure to overcome the osmotic 
resistance (Baker, 2012; S. Jiang et al., 2017). Osmosis and RO principles are presented in Figure 2-
4. 
 
 
 
 
 
The performance and efficiency of RO systems are intrinsically linked to the membrane materials 
and their characteristics. These membranes are typically made of thin-film composite (TFC) 
polyamide materials, featuring a dense selective layer (~0.2 µm) atop a porous support (~50 µm). 
This asymmetric structure offers both high rejection efficiency and mechanical robustness. The 
membrane permits the passage of water molecules while rejecting dissolved salts, heavy metals, 
microorganisms, and organic pollutants including pesticides and PFAS (Raimondi et al., 2023). 
Reverse osmosis is remarkably effective, with pollutant removal rates consistently reaching 99%. It 
allows for the efficient removal of both particulate and dissolved pollutants from feedwater, including 
colloids, microorganisms, salts, heavy metals, organic micropollutants, and PFAS. A main strength 
of RO membranes is their ability to reject microorganisms and colloidal matter with near-complete 
efficiency. Studies report removal rates exceeding 99.9% for bacteria, protozoa, and viruses, as these 
larger biological molecules are unable to permeate the nanostructured polyamide layer (S. Jiang et 
al., 2017, L. Jiang et al., 2018). RO membranes also exhibit high rejection rates for inorganic ions, 
including monovalent and divalent salts: Sodium (Na⁺) and chloride (Cl⁻) ions are typically removed

2.6 Coagulation, Flocculation & Nano-Bubbling  
Given the variability in rainwater quality, pre-treatment is often necessary to enhance the overall 
performance of treatment systems. Pre-treatment is a preliminary process applied before the main 
treatment step to protect downstream systems and optimize efficiency. In rainwater treatment this 
process plays a critical role in removing particulate matter, suspended solids or other pollutants that 
contribute to fouling and performance decline. This section focuses on three pre-treatment processes: 
coagulation, flocculation and nano-bubbling.  
Chemical Coagulation 
One of the most used pre-treatment methods in rainwater treatment is chemical coagulation. 
Coagulation involves the addition of a coagulant to neuralize negatively charged particles that remain 
suspended due to repulsing forces. When the coagulant, such as ferric sulfate (Fe2(SO4)3) or 
aluminum sulfate (Al₂(SO₄)₃), is added to a solution, Fe3+ and Al3+ destabilizes the negatively charged 
particles and promote the formation of microflocs, which is the aggregation of tiny particles. The 
microflocs are slightly positively charged so they continue to attract negatively charged particles, 
gradually forming larger and more visible aggregates.  
The coagulant dosage depends on several factors, including pH, zeta potential, pollutants in the water 
and their concentration. The reaction is promoted by alkaline environment, meaning that if the water 
is too acidic, alkalinity-adjusting agents may be needed to ensure proper reaction conditions. 
Additionally, coagulation requires rapid mixing of the aqueous medium for fast dispersion of the 
coagulant (Pal, 2017). 
Flocculation  
Flocculation typically follows coagulation to further enhance particle removal. It involves the 
addition of a flocculant, which helps the microflocs to aggregate into larger flocs that can be more 
easily removed by sedimentation or filtration. Flocculants are usually long polymer chains, such as 
polyacrylamide or chitosan, and may be cationic, anionic or nonionic depending on the water 
chemistry.  
Just like coagulation, the flocculant dosage also depends on the purity of the water. During 
flocculation, gentle mixing promotes collisions between microflocs and flocculant molecules. Acting 
as a kind of molecular glue, the long polymer chains can bridge multiple particles, which enhances 
floc formation without breaking them apart. 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
In the context of rainwater treatment, the combination of coagulation-flocculating has been shown to 
effectively remove small colloids, turbidity and color. Additionally, studies have demonstrated that 
the treatment combination can reduce particle-bound heavy metals and organic compounds, TSS, E. 
coli and total coliforms (Bergamasco et al., 2011). Additionally, it helps to reduce membrane fouling 
in subsequent ultrafiltration or reverse osmosis systems, extending membrane life and improving 
operational efficiency (X. Liu et al., 2021). The treatment combination is beneficial when the 
rainwater has been collected from areas or surfaces with high pollution levels. 
Nano-Bubbling 
Nano bubbles (NB) are very small bubbles, with a diameter of 1-200 nm that can be used to enhance 
the efficiency of particulate removal. Due to their small size, they remain suspended in a liquid 
medium for a very long time. They have several properties that are beneficial for contaminant 
removal: a hydrophobic and charged surface, that makes them repel water and adhere to hydrophobic 
or charged particles, including fats, oils and grease (FOG), suspended solids, and colloids. They have 
a strong surface that makes them able to bump into contaminants and sweep them away. Lastly, due 
to a high gas-transfer, they improve aerobic processes and accelerate microbial biodegradation 
(MOLEAER, n.d.). 
Due to relatively recent development, nano bubble technology is not as widely adopted in water 
treatment applications compared to more established methods. However, studies on NB-assisted 
wastewater treatment have demonstrated its ability to reduce levels of nanoparticles, organics, 
suspended solids, pathogens, heavy metals, and odors. It has also been shown to mitigate membrane 
fouling in subsequent steps. However, the efficiency of NB treatment is highly influenced by factors 
such as operational design and water properties, including pH, temperature, pollutant type and 
concentration (Jia et al., 2023).

This treatment setup combined coagulation, flocculation and nano bubbling. The following section

3.4.1 Materials and system components

• Nano bubble generator. 
• Tubing and fittings connecting nano bubble generator to jars. 
• Coagulant: Poly Aluminum Chloride 15% (PAX). 
• Flocculant: Polyacrylamide-based polymer (Aquaflok). 
• pH adjuster: Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH), used to raise pH to 10. 
A treatment chain diagram of the system setup is presented in Figure 3-2.  
3.4.2 Procedure 
1. 1.5 L Rainwater was added to each jar. 
2. 3 mL of NaOH was added, and the jars were stirred for 1 minute to ensure even distribution. 
3. 562.5 µL of PAX was added to each jar and mixed at 200 rpm for 3 min. 
4. 562.5 µL of Aquaflok was added to each jar and mixed at 50 rpm for 3 min. 
5. The mixture was allowed to rest for X minutes to promote floc formation. 
6. Nano bubbles were introduced until the liquid volume reached 2 L. 
7. The system was allowed to stand for X minutes to let nano bubbles rise. 
8. Finally, treated water was collected from the bottom of each jar via the sample tap.

Rainwater quality 
The contamination profile of the collected rainwater ultimately dictates the most fitting treatment 
train, as different treatments target different pollutants. This section provides an overview of the 
initial rainwater quality, and highlights the atypical parameters that stand out in order to discuss their 
sources. To support the results, the section references external data coming from a separate sample 
collected during the same rain event. This will be noted as “Niras findings”.  
Physio-chemical properties 
Physio-chemical parameters are excellent indicators of the overall quality of rainwater. The physio-
chemical results are presented in Table 4-1.  
The rainwater’s slight alkaline pH of 7.6 contrasts with its acidic nature of around 5.6. Northern 
European rain is typically even more acidic due to elevated anthropogenic emissions of NOx and SO2. 
This suggests that the rainwater has been in contact with surfaces, where alkaline compounds have 
leached into the water, increasing the pH.  
The high conductivity of 3.62 ms/cm also suggests surface contact or polluted areas, as recordings of 
rainwater collected directly from the sky are typically below 0.2 ms/cm. In urban or industrial areas, 
with high pollution levels, conductivity can reach up to 2 mS/cm (Beysens et al., 2010; Zdeb et al., 
2018), indicating a high pollution level at the collection site. The finding highlights a substantial 
presence of ions in the rainwater, likely leaching from polluted surfaces.  
TSS measurements (24.2 mg/L at 105 °C, 0 mg/L at 500 °C) show that nearly all suspended solids 
are organic rather than mineral. A high organic content is also supported by the high turbidity of the 
rainwater at 9.47. This value exceeds typical ‘clean’ rainwater recordings, that often lie below 5 NTU.

Coagulation, Flocculation, Nano Bubbling (FC) 
A treatment step of Flocculation and Coagulation with nano bubbling was tested to investigate its 
necessity to fulfill drinking water standards. Conductivity saw only minor decrease, with a removal 
efficiency below 20% and a post-treatment value over drinking water limits. Also, turbidity remained 
above the 1 NTU limit, but with a higher reduction of roughly 50%. The most notable improvement 
was a 55% COD reduction, although it remained relatively high at 20 mg/L. The treatment also 
effectively reduced the water’s yellow tint, which implies a substantial reduction of greasy matter.  
These findings align well with the expectations: FC target colloids, suspended solids, and greasy 
substances that drive turbidity and COD, and nano bubbling enhances their removal. The small 
conductivity reduction likely reflects the removal of particle-bound ions. This treatment alone does 
not satisfy turbidity and conductivity requirements, which is expected, as it serves as an initial step 
before subsequent filtration. In rainwater applications, FC has proven effective at reducing fouling in 
downstream membrane processes. Though, it may be unnecessary, when the rainwater is not very 
polluted. Also, NaOH was added during the FC treatment to raise pH, as no visible floc formation 
occurred at the initial pH. Since the treatment already relies on flocculants and coagulants, 
introducing more chemicals further complicates the process and increases handling precautions. 
 
Ultrafiltration (UF) 
UF alone achieved a strong turbidity reduction of over 90%, bringing levels to 0.66 NTU, below the 
drinking water standard. For COD, UF achieved the poorest results, showing a 44% reduction, which 
means that still many oxidizable organic compounds are present in the treated water. The worst UF 
performance was seen for the conductivity, where it only managed to decrease the conductivity by 
2%, having results still higher than the drinking water standard. Color also showed minimal reduction 
and appeared almost the same as the raw rainwater, indicating poor removal of greasy matter. 
These results match expectations: UF effectively removes macromolecules, such as colloids and 
suspended solids that contribute to turbidity and COD. However, UF is known to be ineffective at 
removing ions and dissolved substances like salts, nutrients, and heavy metals. This was demonstrated 
by the low 2% drop in conductivity, indicating that certain ions (e.g., NH₄⁺) would still exceed 
drinking water limits after treatment. Additionally, low molecular weight organics (e.g., PFAS, 
pesticides and phenols,) are too small to be retained by UF pores. Given the high levels of PFAS, it 
is unlikely that UF alone could bring these concentrations within drinking water standards. Moreover, 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
the yellow color after treatment suggests the treatment would likely fail the color and NVOC drinking 
water criteria due to a high residual organic carbon load.  
Coagulation, Flocculation & Nano Bubbling + Ultrafiltration (FC+UF) 
FC + UF achieved the second highest removal efficiencies of resp. 93% and 61% for turbidity and 
COD and bringing turbidity below drinking water standards. The lowest removal was seen for 
conductivity, with an efficiency below 20% and a value still exceeding the drinking water 
requirements (2.97 NTU vs 1.0 NTU).  
The results show the complementary between FC and UF: FC effectively reduces COD and color, 
while UF lowers turbidity. The main advantage of this approach is that it is cheaper and less energy-
intensive than e.g., RO. However, the treatment struggles to remove free ions and small dissolved 
molecules, such as heavy metals, as these do not form flocs and are too small for UF membranes to 
retain. Given the high concentrations of compounds like PFAS and NH4+ in the rainwater, this 
treatment would likely struggle to meet drinking water standards. Still, it could be interesting to 
explore its potential if rainwater was collected directly from the sky in a clean, non-industrial area. 
Ultrafiltration + Reverse Osmosis (UF+RO) 
UF+RO was the only treatment train to comply with the reuired drinking water standards. Across all 
tests, the treament consistently achieved removal efficiencies above 98.5% and met drinking water 
standards for turbidity and conductivity. Additionally, a COD of 0 mg/L indicates that almost all 
oxidizable organic compounds have been removed from the water. A more thorough investigation of 
UF+RO performance is presented in Section 4.3.  
Choice of Final Treatment Train 
The Ultrafiltation + Reverse Osmosis treatment train was selected as the optimal configuration for 
bringing rainwater up to drinking water standards, outperforming both UF alone and FC + UF. The 
primary reason is the extremely high ionic content of the rainwater samples, which necessitates RO 
to meet drinking water limits for conductivity and specific ions with elevated rainwater levels. 
Considering FC as a pre-treatment for UF + RO could likely help reduce fouling, particularly for UF, 
because it lowers turbidity and COD by removing particles that might clog the UF membrane. 
However, because FC only achieved results when NaOH was added to raise the pH to 10, and 
considering the extra chemicals and complexity this additional step would introduce to the system, it 
was deemed unnecessary for this specific scope.

4.4 
Water Quality Assessment Following UF-RO Treatment 
This section provides a comprehensive assessment of water quality improvements from raw rainwater 
to post-treatment. It compares the treated water’s values against drinking water standards to evaluate 
compliance in detail. Additionally, it delves into removal efficiencies to attribute reductions to UF or 
RO and discusses potential outliers. 
pH 
The pH of 6.65 was the only parameter not meeting drinking water standards (7–8.5). This low value 
might be a result of CO₂ absorption from the air: Ultra-pure water has very low ionic strength, making 
it highly sensitive to only small ionic changes. Since the final conductivity showed a very low ionic 
strength, the pH decrease might be a result of this (Thermo Fischer Scientific, 2016). To minimize

Uncharged Low Molecular Weight Organics 
Reverse Osmosis does not effectively remove all compounds, particularly uncharged low molecular 
weight organics like small polar compounds (e.g., alcohols, PPCPs) and amid organics (e.g., specific 
secondary pesticides), some which can show rejection rates even below 15%. Small uncharged polar 
organics pass through mainly by diffusion due to their size and minimal electrostatic interactions with 
the membrane (Kang et al., 2023). Some amide pesticides also engage in dipolar interactions and 
hydrogen bonding with the membrane, increasing their concentration at the membrane surface and 
allowing more to pass through than size exclusion alone would predict (Fujioka et al., 2020).

Although no pesticides, PPCPs were analyzed in the rainwater or treated water, due to unavailable 
equipment, they are known to occur in rainwater and must be monitored to ensure compliance with 
strict drinking water standards. Given that Novo Nordisk is a pharmaceutical company, particular 
attention should be paid to the presence of pharmaceuticals. Similarly, small polar organics, 
especially volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that can evaporate into the air from industrial 
activities, require careful monitoring due to specific drinking water limits. E.g., acetonitrile 
measurements in Niras’ rainwater samlpe exceeded drinking water requirements of 0.1 µg/L by a 
factor 10. Additionally, RO shows very low rejection rate (6-14%) (Pegg et al., 2021), making it 
highly unlikely that the compound will comply with the standards. 
Bacteria  
No tests specifically measured bacterial levels in the water. Given their ubiquity, including in 
rainwater, and potential pathogenic risk, they must be monitored. In Danish drinking water, the 
maximum allowable bacterial count is 200 CFU/mL at 22 °C. The measured BOD of 9.21 mg/L in 
rainwater also indicates the presence of biodegradable organic matter that bacteria could consume.  
Ultrafiltration can achieve up to a 3-log reduction of bacteria and viruses, while RO can exceed a 5-
log reduction, making the UF–RO combination highly effective against these microbes (Taligrot et 
al., 2024). However, because no measurements were taken, it is not possible to confirm their removal.

As raw rainwater flows across the membrane, colloidal particles build up in a thin layer at the 
membrane surface. This elevated local colloid concentration increases hydraulic resistance 𝜅,

Simultaneously, once particles accumulate, they begin forming a more consolidated “cake.” This

may also adsorb into any surface pores (contributing an irreversible internal fouling resistance 𝜅#).

An economically meaningful view of our rainwater‐treatment plant emerges only when we look 
beyond membranes and energy alone to include pumps, installation, labor and maintenance. Figure 
4-11 presents the in-depth CAPEX/OPEX analysis of the UF-RO treatment system. Membrane 
modules account for roughly 28% of the total life‐cycle cost (i.e., CAPEX + OPEX). In contrast, 
pumps represent 29% of RO CAPEX, while energy for RO pumping (38 % of OPEX) and labor (40 
% of OPEX) together outweigh membrane‐replacement costs. In other words, shaving a few 
membrane elements by pushing pressure or flux has only a marginal effect on overall cost, because 
the large pump infrastructure and the recurring labor and energy bills drive most of the expense. A 
truly optimized design must therefore focus first on pump efficiency (i.e., right‐sizing, VFDs, high‐
efficiency motors), realistic fouling and cleaning frequencies (so as not to shorten membrane life).  
 
A total water production cost of 5.2 DKK/m3 is obtained, of which 4.01 DKK/m3 derives from RO, 
and 1.22 DKK/m3 from UF. Modern large RO plants achieve low unit costs thanks to economies of 
scale : producing drinking water from seawater costs about €0.5–1.0 per m³ (~4 to 8 DKK/ m³), and 
from brackish water about €0.3–0.5 per m³ (~2 to 4 DKK/ m³) (Smart Water Magazine, 2021). Small 
industrial RO installations typically have higher unit costs than large municipal plants, due to 
diseconomies of scale and proportionally higher labor and overhead. Another study found a range of 
$0.11 to $1.10/m3 (DKK 0.72 to DKK 7.2 /m³) for brackish water RO plants (WRA, 2012). It’s worth 
noting that rainwater feed is very low in salinity compared to brackish groundwater, which reduces 
RO energy consumption. The calculation assumed only ~10 bar RO pressure, yielding a low specific 
energy use, giving an efficiency advantage over typical brackish or seawater RO and thus a lower 
cost.

This analysis gains further relevance when compared against the local water supply costs in Hillerød, 
Denmark. The site currently receives groundwater from Hillerød Forsyning, the municipal utility, 
where potable water costs approximately 11 to 16 DKK per cubic meter excluding VAT (Skævinge 
Vandværk, 2024). In comparison, the UF/RO system's unit cost of 5.2 DKK/m³ is notably lower, 
suggesting a potential for significant savings. This difference arises largely from the fact that the 
municipal tariff includes not only operational costs but also infrastructure depreciation, taxes, and 
other regulatory charges. In contrast, the cost structure of the proposed system is centered purely on 
the direct expenses associated with water treatment, including amortized capital investments in pumps 
and membranes, labor, energy, and routine maintenance. From an operational economics standpoint, 
this makes the in-house treatment of rainwater a financially attractive alternative, especially for 
industrial users such as the Novo Nordisk Hillerød site. It must however be noted that it’s likely that 
some costs have been underestimated or excluded, so a more conservative estimate might exceed 
5.2 DKK/m³, aligning better with typical small-RO plant costs in practice. 
Hillerød receives just under 400,000 m³ of rainwater annually, of which an estimated 300,000 m³ 
could feasibly be treated and reused by the UF/RO system. This would represent approximately one-
third of the total water demand at the Hillerød industrial site, providing substantial resource 
substitution potential and supply independence. 
4.8 
Rationale for Integrating UF and RO in Rainwater Treatment 
 
The choice to combine ultrafiltration and reverse osmosis in our rainwater treatment system is 
motivated by both technical necessity to reach the desired water quality, and operational stability. UF 
is used as a pretreatment step to reduce the fouling potential of the RO membrane, thus improving 
the long-term reliability and performance of the system. Indeed, this treatment chain is particularly 
well suited to treating rainwater which contains low levels of dissolved salts but carries suspended 
solids, organic matter, and other microorganisms. UF membranes offer effective removal of colloids, 
bacteria, and suspended matter, while operating under low transmembrane pressure. Their use helps 
stabilize the feedwater entering the RO unit by lowering turbidity and reducing the concentration of 
foulants such as organic macromolecules. As noted in Sun et al. (2015), UF pretreatment significantly 
reduces both reversible and irreversible membrane fouling in RO systems. In the absence of such 
pretreatment, particulate and biological fouling are likely to occur, especially when the feedwater 
exhibits biological activity or high COD. The surface-based separation mechanism of UF offers a 
clear advantage in protecting the denser and more sensitive RO membranes downstream. In addition 
==PAGE:65==
64 
 
to reducing fouling, UF provides clear benefits in terms of energy efficiency and scalability. It 
operates at low pressure, requires no chemical additives, and has a relatively simple modular design, 
making it suitable for industrual application.  
 
The RO stage is essential to meet desired water quality targets, particularly for dissolved particles, 
trace metals, and organic micropollutants. However, our system is operated at relatively low pressure 
compared to typical industrial RO units. This choice is based on experimental evidence that lower 
transmembrane pressures not only reduce energy consumption but also improve rejection of certain 
small organic compounds, such as phenols. It was observed that phenol rejection decreased with 
increasing pressure, likely due to increased convective transport at higher pressures, which reduces 
diffusive retention of smaller organics. Operating at lower pressures also reduces the formation of 
dense fouling layers. As noted in the same study by Sun et al., cake layer resistance was the dominant 
component of total membrane resistance, especially at higher TMP (X. Sun et al., 2015). This finding 
supports the use of moderate pressure in RO systems treating low-salinity rainwater.  
At the Novo Nordisk Hillerød site, there is a clear mismatch between water availability and water use 
as groundwater supplies are increasingly constrained while average annual precipitation remains 
high. A rainwater treatment system based on UF and RO provides a high-efficiency solution that 
could substitute groundwater in non-potable technical applications. By combining a pretreatment 
stage with low-pressure RO, the system minimizes the risk of operational disruptions due to 
membrane fouling while meeting water quality targets in a cost-effective and energy-efficient 
manner. UF reduces the cleaning frequency and extends the service life of RO membranes, while RO 
ensures that the treated water meets the necessary purity standards. 
 
4.9 
 Limitations and Future Work 
Unmeasured Parameters 
Several planned quality tests were not conducted due to resource and time constraints. A major issue 
was the lack of rainfall during the first two months of the study, which prevented us from collecting 
sufficient sample volume. By the time rain finally arrived in early April, we were already halfway 
through the project timeline, and the testing instruments were fully booked, leading to significant 
delays or the inability to perform the. These tests include: 
==PAGE:66==
65 
 
• PFAS, treated water (SPE-LC-MS/MS): This test required a large sample volume to achieve 
very low detection limits and precise results, meaning all remaining treated water would be 
used. As a result, it was scheduled only after all other analyses. Furthermore, a lengthy queue 
for the SPE–LC–MS/MS instrument meant this test could not be completed before project 
submission. 
• 
PAHs, Pharmaceuticals, PPCPs, Pesticides (HPLC): A combination of method development 
for some compounds and a long instrument wait time made HPLC testing impossible to 
perform within the research period. 
• 
Heavy metals (ICP-MS): ICP–MS was out of service due to mechanical issues, so heavy metal 
analysis could only be performed after the hand-in deadline. 
• 
SO42- and HCO3- (Gallery): No established method was available in time, so testing could not 
be carried out. 
Additionally, untested parameters with drinking water criteria that were not addressed in this research 
include NVOC, Bacterial Count, Disinfection By-products and Oxidants, radiological parameters and 
low molecular weight organics (a complete list of untested parameters is provided in Appendix 1). 
To fully determine whether the treated rainwater meets all drinking water requirements, all these 
compounds must be analyzed in future studies. 
 
 
Seasonal and Geographical Variability 
Rainwater pollution experiences seasonal variations due to shifts in biogenic, industrial, and 
agricultural activities: In spring and summer, increased biogenic activity leads to higher loads of 
organic debris. In winter, higher heating demands lead to increased combustion byproducts in 
rainwater. Likewise, agricultural fertilization periods raise nutrient levels in rainwater runoff. These 
variations also depend strongly on geography and land use: areas with heavy industry or dense traffic 
tend to have more contaminated rainwater, while rural  regions see much cleaner precipitation. 
Originally, the plan was to sample rainwater in March, April, and May at the Novo Nordisk Hillerød 
site to capture these seasonal and local influences. However, limited precipitation allowed only one 
collection, and lack of access to the Hillerød location meant samples were taken at Måløv instead. 
Future work should implement year-round sampling at the Hillerød site to validate UF–RO 
performance under seasonal and site-specific pollution variations in rainwater composition. 
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
Long-Term Pilot Runs 
Although fouling was not a primary focus of this study, the observed >20% flux decline in both UF 
and RO trials clearly indicates the onset of membrane fouling or concentration polarization. In 
industrial practice, fouling is the leading challenge for membrane systems, where uncontrolled 
buildup of particles and organics accelerate cleaning cycles, shortens membrane life, increases energy 
use, and drives up operating costs. Additionally, fouling behavior varies greatly between lab and full-
scale operations:  Our tests were run in batch mode using low volumes, small-scale membrane 
modules and had relatively short runs, whereas industrial plants often run continuously with big 
volumes, and larger modules; conditions that often amplify both reversible (boundary‐layer) and 
irreversible (cake/pore) fouling.  
To better understand real‐world fouling dynamics, a long‐term pilot is needed. A six‐month 
continuous UF→RO trial on harvested rainwater at Hillerød, using appropriately scaled membrane 
modules, will quantify true fouling rates and mechanisms. These insights will guide the optimization 
of cleaning procedures and intervals, ultimately maximizing system performance and extending 
membrane lifespan in industrial applications. 
5. Conclusion  
The research set out to determine if harvested rainwater, an abundant but underutilized resource in 
Denmark, can be purified to drinking-water quality for use in industrial processes at Novo Nordisk’s 
Hillerød site, thereby easing pressure on dwindling groundwater reserves. Key findings include: 
Raw Rainwater Quality: The rainwater samples exhibited pollutant loads well above typical “off-
sky” precipitation, likely boosted by an extended dry spell. The elevated turbidity (9.5 NTU), organic 
TSS (24 mg/L) and COD (57 mg O₂/L), and the relativy low BOD (9 mg O₂/L), indicates a heavy 
load of refractory particles and organic debris. A high ionic load, particularly the alkaline Ca²⁺ (46 
mg/L) and Na⁺ (>100 mg/L), drive the elevated conductivity (3.62 mS/cm) and alkaline pH (7.6), 
indicating significant leaching from concrete and roofing materials. Concerning PFAS concentrations 
exceeding 1.2 µg/L, points to a nearby industrial source, while trace metals were found in low 
concentrations. 
Treatment-Train Comparison: Four treatment configurations were evaluated: coagulation–
flocculation with nano-bubbling (CF), ultrafiltration (UF), CF followed by UF (CF+UF), and UF 
followed by reverse osmosis (UF+RO). Only UF+RO met both turbidity and conductivity standards, 
==PAGE:68==
67 
 
achieving over 93% removal of each and nearly 100% COD reduction. While all other trains removed 
55–61% of COD and both UF and CF+UF met the turbidity criterion (>93% removal), they all fell 
short on conductivity, with just 2–18% ion removal. Consequently, only the UF+RO sequence 
delivered the comprehensive performance required. 
Water Quality and Treatment Performance: The UF+RO train delivered outstanding results, 
removing over 93% of major ions and elements (NO₃⁻, NO₂⁻, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Si, P) and roughly 75% 
of NH₄⁺ and Mn. All measured analytes in the treated water met Danish drinking-water limits, except 
Mn, whose compliance is uncertain due to a high detection limit, and pH (6.65 vs. the 7–8.5 
requirement), which can be adjusted via simple remineralization or closed-system operation. 
Economic and Upscaling Outlook: The economic analysis demonstrates that a UF-RO rainwater 
treatment system in Hillerød can deliver water at a competitive cost of 5.2 DKK/m³, lower than the 
local municipal groundwater rate of 11–16 DKK/m³. The main cost drivers are energy and labor, not 
membranes, highlighting the importance of optimizing pump efficiency and operational parameters 
over simply reducing membrane count. Operating at 10 bar achieves the best balance between capital 
and energy costs. With potential to treat up to 300,000 m³ of rainwater annually (about a third of the 
site's demand) the system offers both cost savings and improved supply resilience. While some costs 
may be underestimated, the system remains financially viable and well-suited for industrial reuse as 
modular UF–RO systems offer scalable implementation. 
Next Steps: Future research should measure key untested parameters, including PFAS in permeate, 
PAHs, pharmaceuticals/PPCPs, pesticides, heavy metals, bacteria, and microplastics, to fully confirm 
regulatory compliance. Additionally, conducting year-round sampling at Novo Nordisk’s Hillerød 
site is vital to capture seasonal and spatial variations in rainwater quality and validate system 
performance under actual site conditions. Following this, a pilot-scale UF→RO trial on harvested 
rainwater treatment will quantify real-world fouling rates, cleaning intervals, and membrane lifespan.  
Overall, the UF→RO train shows strong technical promise, economic viability, and environmental 
benefits for converting harvested rainwater into technical water for pharmaceutical processes. It could 
provide Novo Nordisk with a clear pathway to bolster water security and reduce reliance on scarce 
groundwater resources in Hillerød.
