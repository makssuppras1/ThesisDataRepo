Abstract 
 
Molecular dynamics simulations are an indispensable tool for computational discovery in 
fields like energy storage and catalysis. Heterogeneous catalysis is today often conducted by 
use of nanoparticles on a support. It is therefore of interest to create models capable of per-
forming simulations on such systems. In this project the feasibility of modelling a nanoparti-
cle-support system with significant van der Waals (vdW) interactions, graphite supported 
рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2, using state-of-the art equivariant graph neural networks is investigated. This involves 
creating and developing a dataset of density functional theory (DFT) calculations on relevant 
structures and optimizing the performance of the model with regards to both accuracy and 
computation time. Throughout development an ensemble approach was used both as a way 
to gauge simulation error and as part of an active learning process to identify new structures 
to add to the dataset. The model reached accuracies comparable to typical DFT uncertainties 
and proved capable of running molecular dynamics on systems of thousands of atoms. In 
simulations it also proved capable of correctly handling the presence of the vdW interaction. 
 
The model was applied to examine the vibrational behaviour of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2. A collective behav-
iour was observed with increasing vibrational amplitude towards the edge of the nanoparti-
cles. Using transmission electron microscopy simulations on molecular dynamics data, a 
comparison to experimental results was made possible. The simulations and experiments 
showed good qualitative and to a lesser extent quantitative agreement on the vibrational be-
haviour. 
 
Finally, the model was extended to also be able to simulate hydrogen by constructing and 
adding additional systems including hydrogen to the training data. Some preliminary simu-
lations examining the dynamics and reactions with hydrogen were performed. In a proof-of-
concept system it was shown that the model could recreate the reaction to form рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ and in 
general could handle the addition of an additional chemical element. However, more work 
on the training data is likely needed to get the adsorption of hydrogen right.

1 - Introduction 
 
1.1 - Context & motivation 
 
Heterogeneous catalysis (catalysis where the reactant and products are in a different phase 
than the catalyst) is a vital part of many chemical processes and one of the preferred meth-
ods in industry1. A heterogeneous catalyst is often a nanoparticle2, and a key factor influenc-
ing its reactivity is the surface geometry of the nanoparticles. This can be affected by the 
choice of support, hence why running simulations of nanoparticles with various supports is 
of interest. It is not practical to do this with density functional theory (DFT) due to the large 
number of atoms and computation power required. However, it has been shown that DFT 
can be approximated by much faster calculations using a machine learning potential trained 
on relevant smaller DFT calculations. In particular the E(3)- equivariant machine-learning 
inter-atomic potentials of NequIP shows promising results for being able to accurately repli-
cate DFT results and simulate molecular dynamics3, which are an indispensable tool for 
computational discovery in fields like energy storage4, catalysis5 and biological processes 6. A 
NequIP application of note for this project are simulations of рқ‘ғрқ‘Ў nanoparticles on oxide sup-
ports done in conjunction with the MasterвҖҷs thesis of Mathias Stokkeby Nissen7. 
 
1.2 - Aims & objectives 
 
In this project the aim is to expand on some of the results of the above-mentioned thesis in 
two main ways. Firstly, by examining рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 catalysts supported by multi-layer graphite in-
stead of platinum on an oxide, the van der Waals forces also becomes a very important fac-
tor for the structure of the system.  It is then of interest to see if NequIP can handle van der 
Waals bonds equally as well as the covalent bonds. рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 as a catalyst is used for removing 
sulphur from fossil fuels and is a very promising catalyst for the hydrogen evolution reac-
tion8 occurring at the cathode in electrolysis cells.  
Secondly the project will thus also be applying the model to examining dynamics of systems 
with added hydrogen.

The overarching goal of the project is, to be able to generalize DFT computations performed 
on smaller system to larger systems using machine learning potentials and then apply these 
on some specific cases like the one mentioned above. A more detailed breakdown of objec-
tives to achieve this goal is as follows: 
 
вҖў A relevant set of structures for the training data must be created. NequIP typically 
does not require large amount of training data and as such a database of relevant 
рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2-graphene systems can reasonably be generated early in the project.  
 
вҖў Density functional theory calculations must be executed. NequIP requires data on 
forces and energies of the atoms in the systems. This information will be retrieved by 
use of DFT calculations. The calculations should be sufficiently accurate without be-
ing too computationally demanding, so some parameter convergences must be car-
ried out. Lastly a way to implement vdW interactions in DFT must be decided. 
 
вҖў Creation and evaluation of an initial model based on the first set of structures. The 
training of the NequIP neural network model must be executed to create the model. 
To quantitatively compare the model to the DFT results and later models, a specific 
test dataset must be created and a script written to calculate the test-error. 
 
вҖў Data set improvements through active learning. To fill out holes in the initial dataset 
new structures on which the initial model has issues handling must be added to the 
training data. The aim is to do this with an iterative active learning process, where an 
ensemble of models is used for running a molecular dynamics simulation and for de-
ciding on what structures to add to the dataset based on their disagreement. 
 
вҖў A screening of hyperparameters. Models with several configurations of hyperparam-
eters must be trained to try and optimize both the accuracy and the computation time. 
 
вҖў Application of the finished model for select nanoparticle simulations. The finished 
model should be used to perform molecular dynamics on larger systems. One appli-
cation is to examine the vibrational behaviour of atoms in рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 and comparing to 
transmission electron microscopy experimental data. Another is to be able to examine 
dynamics of a system with added hydrogen.

2.1.7 - Structure relaxation 
 
Structure relaxation will be a common use of DFT calculations in this project. A structure re-
laxation is when the individual atoms are gradually moved along with the net-force acting 
on them until the forces have decreased below a specified threshold. In practice this happens 
by repeatedly calculating a potential energy surface for a system, calculating the gradient of 
that to find the forces and then move against that gradient for a short time-step. This creates 
a new structure for which the process repeats. 
 
The specific algorithm used for relaxation in this project is the QuasiNewton algorithm build 
into ASE. To identify where to move the atoms in each step of the optimization it uses the 
forces as well as an approximation of the Hessian matrix of the total energy of the structure. 
For each step a line-search is then performed to determine the step size and the Broyden-
Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (BFGS) update formula is used for adjusting the Hessian approxi-
mation along the way. The algorithm is ideal for systems that are already close to their mini-
mum such that the local potential energy surface is quadratic18.

2.1.3 - Hyperparameters 
 
Hyperparameters are parameters that once set do not change during the training of the 
model, unlike the weight and bias parameters. They impact many things from the size and

go into the model to how the model learns does during training.

2.1.4 - Interatomic machine learning potentials  
 
DFT enables the computation of material properties at an atomic scale. But while it circum-
vents the exponential wall of trying to solve the regular SchrГ¶edinger equation where each 
new particle adds an additional 3 variables, the computational cost of DFT still scales heavily 
with the number of atoms in the calculation. A 2020 review of the status of DFT describes it 
as вҖңa quantum nightmare to get all of the properties right at a reasonable computational costвҖқ.20  
 
Interatomic machine learning potentials (mlp) are a possible solution, where machine learn-
ing is used to bridge the gap between accurate, but computationally heavier DFT and less 
accurate but computationally lighter approximated potentials. The newest developments in 
the field have led to mlpвҖҷs capable of executing calculations up to 20 million times faster 
than comparable DFT calculations in the form of Matlantis21. 
 
A typical mlp maps the atomic structure to the potential energy surface by taking atomic co-
ordinates and types as inputs and outputting their potential energies by use of a NN for each 
type of element. For each atom itвҖҷs interactions with other atoms are taken into account by 
considering neighbouring atoms within a set cut-off radius рқ‘ҹрқ‘җ.  
Some mlp apply atom-centered symmetry functions to convey information about transla-
tional, rotational and permutational invariances. Other mlp do not use pre-determined sym-
metry functions and instead develop their own descriptors during the learning process22. 
These later ones are graph neural networks.

2.1.5 - Equivariant graph neural networks 
 
A graph neural network model treats molecules as three-dimensional graphs, in which the 
atoms are the nodes of the graph, and connecting edges are made between atoms, if they are 
within a distance рқ‘ҹрқ‘җ (cut-off radius) of each other.  
 
 
 
Figure 3 вҖ“ Sketch of 6 atoms, where рқ‘ҹрқ‘җ is chosen to match the distance to the nearest neighbour-
ing atom. In this case each atom / node has two connected atoms through nodes, effectively 
recreating the bond-structure of a benzene like molecule. 
The graph neural network uses feature vectors describing the state of the atoms (for example 
position and forces) and then iteratively updates the feature vectors as information about 
neighbouring atoms is also gained through a series of message functions and convolutions. 
What features the NN uses determines what kind of type it is. In the case of an equivariant 
NN it uses features that are equivariant with respect to the symmetry operations belonging 
to the Euclidean Group E(3). That is all transformations that preserve the Euclidean distance 
between two points i.e. translations, rotations, reflections as well as any combination of 
these. Being equivariant to the operations can be understood as the inputs and outputs 
transforming in the same way. For example, an input can be a set of atoms and the output 
the calculated interaction forces. If the entire system is rotated by an angle рқңғ, then the calcu-
lated forces will also all end up being rotated by an angle рқңғ and the forces feature will have

entirely unchanged and would instead have been invariant to the rotation of the system.

2.4 -Transmission electron microscopy 
 
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is an experimental technique used for imaging thin 
slices of materials, through which electrons can pass, down to the atomic level. The main 
parts of the microscope are an electron beam source, electromagnetic lenses, and an electron 
counter. The electron beam is produced, accelerated, and focused through the sample by the 
lenses. Depending on the structure of the sample, the electron beam is modified when pass-
ing through. The modified signal is then magnified by other lenses and detected by the elec-
tron detector, typically consisting of a fluorescent screen and an image recording system.  
 
In TEM the wave-particle duality of electrons is exploited, and the electron beam is treated 
as an electron wave. The goal of imaging methods like TEM is then to modify the electron 
wave in such a way that the exiting wave provides information about the sample. Atomic 
scale resolution images require a very thin sample and high energy electrons. However, for 
thin enough samples the sample can be considered an object that no longer absorbs electrons 
and thus no longer changes the amplitude of the electron wave. Instead, electron scattering 
off atoms inside the sample corresponds to a phase change and phase effects dominate the 
image27. 
 
2.4.1 - Image reconstruction 
 
The importance of phase effects means the detected image is also sensitive to several internal 
factors in the microscope. Things like the size of the aperture in the detector, various phase 
aberrations from things like microscope misalignment and defocus as well as temporal and 
angular spread of the electron beam affect the final image. In mathematical terms this corre-
sponds to taking the convolution between the electron wave and a contrast transfer function, 
with the effect being some frequencies being blocked out and some loss of resolution. 
 
These things should ideally be compensated for to align the detected image signal with the 
signal exiting the sample. This is possible by taking a series of measurements where the vari-
ous components of the microscope responsible for introducing aberrations are gradually 
modified. Then reverse engineering from the imaged signal using the knowledge of the de-
pendence of the imaged wave on the component settings28.

3.6 - Activate learning with PALS 
 
The basis model performs very well in tests because all of the test- and training data is the 
same вҖңtypeвҖқ in terms of how the structures are generated. To improve the models handling 
of unseen structures activate learning is used to expand the dataset. Essentially the model is 
purposefully exposed to its weakness in order to build up resilience against that weakness. 
 
The training data is partitioned into 5 different вҖңsplitsвҖқ of equal size. 5 versions of the model 
are then each trained on 4 of these splits, each version omitting 1 specific split. During train-
ing a NN optimizes a cost-function in a many-dimensional parameter space of fitting param-
eters through a gradient descent algorithm. Gradient descent ideally finds the global mini-
mum but can get stuck in a local minimum too. The cost function depends on what data is in 
the training file, which means each training can end up in a different local minimum and 
hence produce a different model with somewhat different weights and biases. Thus, training 
the model with the same hyperparameter settings but on different splits of the data is a fast 
way to get several similar versions of a model, each making a slightly different prediction. 
 
The parallel active learning suite (PALS) was then used to run a Langevin molecular dynam-
ics simulation with an ensemble of the 5 versions of the model. The atoms were assigned a 
==PAGE:39==
~ 38 ~ 
 
temperature according to a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution centered on either 300 K, 400 K 
or 500 K (randomly chosen, but probabilities weighted towards the lower temperatures) and 
400 steps, each corresponding to 1 fs, were run. For each time step all the models make their 
predictions for the forces on each atom in the system and system is then updated based on 
the average of these predictions. At the same time the predictions of each model are stored, 
meaning a standard deviation of the force predictions can be calculated for each atom. The 
average of this standard deviation across all atoms was then used as an indicator of disa-
greement between the models, meaning at least one model them was likely exposed to 
something it couldnвҖҷt accurately handle. 
 
A script was written to read the average standard deviation (asd) of forces across all the 
steps of the MD simulation. For each MD simulation, it sequentially identifies the 3 highest 
peaks (values) in asd. However, it was generally found that structures with comparatively 
large force amplitudes (relative to other steps in the simulation) increased the test error, so 
some additional filters were added. 
 
вҖў If a threshold of 100 
eV

value (a typical value is less below 1

3.7 - Data set generations 
 
The base model / generation 0 training data only contained relaxed structures. Subsequent 
generations of the training data would contain those same relaxed structures and addition-
ally structures generated and selected with MD and PALS. The generation number here re-
fers to the latest data to be added to the dataset, as each generation also contains the data of 
all the generations with a lower number than it. 
 
The idea with the generations of the dataset was that the model should become gradually 
better at handling unseen structures. In each generation the model was actively exposed to 
its weak points with PALS, but ideally these weak points become smaller each generation. 
The process can be summarized as: 
 
вҖў Model A is made from generation N training data. 
 
вҖў Test error X by model A on generation N test data is calculated. 
 
вҖў Ensemble of models made from generation N training data run PALS on new struc-
tures. Structures from this simulation are then picked out and added to generation N 
to form generation N+1. 
 
вҖў Model B is made from generation N+1 training data. 
 
вҖў Test error Y by model B on generation N+1 test data is calculated. 
 
Etcetera. Ideally when comparing the test errors on the newest part of the dataset рқ‘Ӣ> рқ‘Ң, as 
the weakness generated by model A (and hence trained on by model B) should be smaller 
than whatever weakness the precursor to model A had. If рқ‘Ӣвү…рқ‘Ң or рқ‘Ӣ< рқ‘Ң, then the iterative 
process stops as the model is no longer getting better at handling unseen structures.

3.8 - Hyperparameters 
 
Having found the active learning to be sated at generation 5, several different versions of the 
model were trained on that data with different hyper-parameter settings. There are two cate-

vant for how the model is trained. The second category are hyperparameters relevant for 
how the model is build / structured. This includes the atom cut-off radius рқ‘ҹрқ‘җ, the number of 
layers / interaction blocks рқ‘ӣ and the maximum rotation order / maximum allowed tensor 
rank of a feature рқ‘ҷрқ‘ҡрқ‘Һрқ‘Ҙ, both of which were explained in more detail in the section on NequIP. 
 
For the first category the batch size and the learning rate and associated features like the 
learning rate patience and learning rate reduction factor were tested. The batch size is the 
number of data points from the data used in each epoch (iteration) of the training.

~ 47 ~ 
 
The learning rate is related to how large the updates to the parameters in the model in each 
epoch of the training are. The learning rate patience is a number of epochs after which to re-

those epochs. The reduction factor is simply by which factor to reduce the learning rate by if 
that stagnation in learning occurs. Since the learning rate is mostly about being able to 
properly find the minimum during training, the validation and training errors (which are 
generated during training - see figure 18), were used to judge the choice of these hyperpa-
rameters, rather than the model test-error calculated with the external script. The errors were 
generally very close, but the default NequIP parameter values of a 0.005 learning rate, a 100-

3.9 - Lessons learned: The doвҖҷs and donвҖҷtвҖҷs  
 
Not everything in the development process has been documented, as some things did not 
contribute towards the final model. Nevertheless, there can still be some value in summariz-
ing some of the problems that were encountered and solved to avoid repeating mistakes. 
 
вҖў Experimenting with the validation set size. As mentioned earlier the training data is 
split into data on which the model is trained and on data in which that trained model 
is concurrently being validated against. NequIP automatically produces a breakdown 
of various measures of model-error during training using this validation data. How-
ever, since we wrote our own script for calculating a test-error, we tried minimizing 
the size of the validation all the way down to 1 to maximize the amount of training 
data. This had the effect of the model performance not following a trend of improving 
with never generations of the training data and generally increasing the test-error. See 
figure 19 for a comparison between the test-errors when using a validation test data 
size of 1 and a test data size of 1/5 the training dataset size.  
 
==PAGE:51==
~ 50 ~ 
 
Upon investigation two things were found: First that the learning rate of the model 
was constant whereas it should ideally decrease as the minimum error in parameter 
space is approached so as to not overshoot it.  Second that with the minimized valida-
tion data size the validation model error instead of decreasing towards 0 with number 
of training epochs (as in figure 18) would instead grow several orders of magnitude 
within a few epochs. This growth and training of the model would seemingly have 
continued indefinitely if not for the built-in limit on number of epochs during training 
(in this case 500). Both these things point towards the network getting bad feedback 
about in which directions to change its parameters.

3.10 - Summary & conclusions 
 
A dataset containing рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 pieces supported by graphene sheets was constructed. The forces 
and energies were calculated with density functional theory and the PBE DFT-D3 functional 
to allow for handling of vdW-interaction. Machine learning potential models were trained 
using NequIP. These potentials were gradually refined and made more robust against unfa-
miliar structures by expanding the dataset across several generations, with each generation 
adding additional structures that were specifically picked to maximize the amount of learn-
ing. For generating these structures molecular dynamics simulations were carried out with a 
PALS-ensemble of models and the simulation steps with the most disagreement amongst 
these models were picked, subject to some filters. An important insight during the expansion 
of the dataset was to remove too large forces (> вүҲ100

4.1 - Graphite and van der Waals interactions 
 
The layered structures with consistent layer separation distance of graphite are caused due 
to the vdW-interaction between the individual graphene layers. Thus, one way of testing if 
the NequIP mlp can reproduce the vdW interaction is by testing if it is able to reproduce 
such a structure. A system with 3 sheets of stacked circular graphene and a system with 3 
sheets of stacked square-shaped graphene were constructed. Every atom was then perturbed 
in the рқ‘Ҙ-, рқ‘Ұ- and рқ‘§-directions using a value drawn from a gaussian distribution with a mean 
of 0 and standard deviation of 0.6 Г….

of the unit cell. Left: Initial condition. Right: Relaxed condition. 
 
 
Figure 22 вҖ“ MLP relaxation of 3 square-shaped perturbed graphene sheets. The square sheets fill 
out the entire unit cell and thus effectively extend infinitely in the рқ‘Ҙ- and рқ‘Ұ- directions due to the 
periodic boundary conditions. Left: Initial condition. Right: Relaxed condition.

From figures 21 and 22 the mlp is able to recreate a clearer layered structure with roughly 
constant spacing вҖ“ around 3.4 Г… and 3.7 Г… for the tested pairs in the square and circular 
sheets respectively. The layers are not flat and instead buckle a bit, however this is also 
something observed in practice. The important part for demonstrating presence of vdW-in-
teractions is that the layer separation is roughly constant despite the local elevation chang-
ing. The typical flatness of the layers is due to the bonds between carbon atoms inside that 
layer. The ability to buckle is related to the restorative force created when bending the рқҗ¶-рқҗ¶ 
bond being quadratic with distance unlike the restorative force created when elongating, 
which is linear according to HookeвҖҷs law. This means for small perturbations the restorative 
force is very small and buckling can occur38.  
 
The vdW interaction should not just create a consistent offset between two planes, but also 
the same offset every time. To test this, two more circular systems are made: One where the 
initial offset is reduced by a factor 0.8 and one where it is increased by a factor 1.25. Due to 
the irregular layer separation the periodic boundary conditions in the рқ‘§-direction were also 
removed for the purpose of these tests. The perturbation is also decreased to 0.1 standard de-
viation for these systems to more clearly be able to see the layer movement.

4.2.2 - Initial simulations and repairs 
 
The molecular dynamics simulations used as part of active learning where on smaller sys-
tems where DFT calculations were still feasible (on the order of 100вҖҷs of atoms). The systems 
on which the finished model was applied where much larger (up to 10000 atoms). At this 
size the complexity of the model was also important, in part because of computation times, 
but also because the most accurate model рқ‘ҹ6рқ‘ӣ4рқ‘ҷ2 was not able to perform the MD simulation 
on the available hardware. This further validated the earlier choice of also considering the 
computation time when selecting hyperparameters. All the reported simulation results from 
here on out were done with the рқ‘ҹ5рқ‘ӣ3рқ‘ҷ1 model or extensions of it (cut-outs and hydrogen). 
 
Early simulations had several issues that needed to be fixed. There were two system-wide 
issues. Firstly, the nanoparticle as a whole had a tendency to rotate and translate in space. 
Secondly the graphene layer had a tendency to rise up outside of the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 forming an al-
most wave-like shape. The translation was solved by adding the Stationary function to the 
collection of atoms, which calculates the velocity of the center of mass and subtracts that 
from all the atom velocities. However, the function does not prevent an angular momentum 
around and hence rotation around the center of mass. To address the two remaining issues a 
combination of more layers of graphene and constraining the lowest graphene layer to not 
==PAGE:60==
~ 59 ~ 
 
move during the simulation was used. This turned out to prevent rotation and provided the 
required stability for the graphene layers to remain in their expected flat shape.  
 
There were also local oddities such as individual рқ‘Җрқ‘ң atoms detaching from the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 parti-
cle, migrating down to and embedding themselves into the graphene, which is not some-
thing observed in practice.

4.2.3.2 - Tunnelling electron microscopy simulations 
 
The next two sections cover an application of the machine learning potential that can be 
compared to transmission electron microscopy (TEM) experiments. The experiments in 
question were performed by a group including project supervisor Stig Helveg. The data they 
achieved can also be used to inform about atomic vibrations inside the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 particle, how-
ever the data is cast in a different form. To create more comparable data TEM simulations 
based on results from MD simulations are thus carried out with the Python abTEM package. 
 
The simulations are carried out according to the steps laid out in the earlier section on ab-
TEM. The main difference is that instead of using the build-in frozen phonons module of ab-
TEM, a 1000 data points sampled evenly from a 10000 step long MD simulations are used. 
This means that the frozen phonons object now also contains information on the collective 
vibrational effects that occur with the increasing vibrational amplitude towards the edge of 
the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 particle. The build in frozen-phonons module would have used uncorrelated ran-
dom displacements of the atoms to simulate the effect of thermal vibrations instead. Thus, it 
would not have been able to catch any possible collective effects, which we now know exist.

4.4 - Summary & conclusions 
 
A set of much larger systems than before including a large circular рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 nanoparticle was 
constructed. These included nanoparticles with radiuses of 20 Г… and 30 Г… and for the latter 
also particles with an рқ‘Ҷ-dominated edge and with an рқ‘Җрқ‘ң-dominated edge. 
Using the model with optimum balance of accuracy and computation speed from the section 
on вҖңData set construction & model developmentвҖқ, a series of simulations were executed: 
 
вҖў Perturbed graphite structures were relaxed with the mlp. The structure was able to be 
properly reconstructed according to what presence of vdW interactions would dic-
tate. This was a good indication NequIP is able to properly learn the vdW interaction. 
 
вҖў It was shown that problematic issues during MD simulations could be вҖңcuredвҖқ by 
adding cut-outs of the problematic part of the system to the model training data. 
 
вҖў MD simulations of 10000 steps were done and data gathered on the atomic positions. 
A standard deviation of the atomic positions was used as a proxy for vibrational am-
plitude and a trend of increasing vibrational amplitude towards the edge of the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 
nanoparticle was discovered. 
 
вҖў abTEM simulations based on information from MD simulations were carried out. Us-
ing the imaginary value of the electron wave exiting the sample, data was gathered 
on atomic vibrational amplitudes in a way that allowed direct comparison to experi-
mental TEM results. The simulated and experimental results were consistent in both 
showing increased vibrational amplitude at the particle edge with reasonable expla-
nations existing to account for the differences in the details. 
 
вҖў The dataset was extended to also include structures with hydrogen present, but this 
time only one generation of active learning was done as well as some systems only 
containing рқҗ» and рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 and some only containing рқҗ» and рқҗ¶ being added to the dataset. 
Using a model trained on this extended dataset structural relaxations and MD simula-
tions were carried out to examine the ability to predict chemical reactions in the form 
of рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ formation. Some limited рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ formation in вҖңproof-of-conceptвҖқ systems was ob-

model had room for improvement when it came to learning the hydrogen interac-

5 - Future work suggestions 
 
5.1 - Refining and applying the hydrogen model 
 
Unlike the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2-рқҗ¶ systems were 6 generations worth of structures were added, only a single 
(albeit larger) set of structures generation with PALS were added to the dataset when ex-
panding to also cover hydrogen. This means there is likely still some room to optimize the 
model with regards to handling hydrogen structures.  
 
Particularly it might be useful to add some smaller structures to the dataset, which are more 
specifically targeted towards allowing the network to learn the bonds between individual 
atoms. For example, some systems consisting of an S atom and a рқҗ»2 molecule with different 
angles and spacing between the рқ‘Ҷ and рқҗ»2. The MD simulations done with hydrogen showed 
there to be some dependence on the vertical displacement off of the side of the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 on 
whether the hydrogen was вҖңejectedвҖқ, bound to an S-atom to form рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ or neither. 
Hence some smaller systems focused on individual atom bonds would probably allow the 
model to be better at predicting when hydrogen would adsorb to the surface of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2. 
 
If the model was perfected in this regard, it could have great potential in designing the sur-
faces of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 nano-catalysts, as the model would be able to inform on what kind of surface 
defects (if any) lead to the greatest adsorption rates of hydrogen. This would be of use in 
electrolysis cells, where the hydrogen evolution reaction at the cathode is limited by the ad-
sorption rate of рқҗ»+ to the catalyst surface.   
 
5.2 - Chemical elements 
 
The main body of this work has shown that NequIP can be used to create a great machine 
learning potential for systems with 3 different chemical elements. It has also shown that this 
could be extended to systems with 4 different chemical elements by adding hydrogen, alt-
hough these later systems have not been as thoroughly tested.  
A possible future extension of this work could thus be to examine the capability of NequIP 
to handle an increasing number of chemical elements in a single system.

6 - Overall conclusions 
 
In this project the feasibility of modelling systems of a catalytic рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 nanoparticle and 
graphite support, a system with significant van der Waals (vdW) interactions, using the 
equivariant graph neural network NequIP has been examined. A series of models have been 
created and improved by first constructing a data set of atomic structures and DFT-calcu-
lated forces and energies on which NequIP can train, and then extending the data across sev-
eral generations of additional structures.  
 
The structures are generated with the atomic simulation environment and with several ran-
domizable parameters to create variation in the dataset. For extending the dataset active 
learning has been carried out with an ensemble-approach. A molecular dynamics (MD) sim-
ulation was with 5 models in conjunction, with the ensemble models each being trained on a 
number set of training data splits. This allowed for a measure of simulation uncertainty 
through the standard deviation of the modelsвҖҷ predictions on the forces. The structures with 
largest disagreement, which also passed a series of filters on the forces, were picked out 
from the molecular dynamics simulation, and added to the existing training- and test-data. 
This was repeated until the test error calculated on the test data no longer decreased, which 
happened after 5 repetitions / generations of the data.

to be the most accurate, but with a вүҲ3рқ‘Ҙ decrease in computation time achievable at the cost

The model was applied for a series of MD simulations on much larger systems than it was

indication of NequIP being capable of properly handling vdW forces too. Secondly it was 
shown that an MD simulation could be вҖңrepairedвҖқ by feeding the problematic area of the 
structure to the training data on the model.

An examination of the vibrational behaviour of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 was carried out using data from MD 
simulations, by sampling the atomic positions of the last 5000 of 10000 frames and calculat-
ing the positional standard deviation as a proxy for vibrational amplitude. A collective be-
haviour in the vibrations was observed, with the vibrational amplitude increasing towards 
the edge of the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 nanoparticle, particularly when the edge was dominated by рқ‘Ҷ-atoms. 
 
This data was also reproduced in a different form through transmission electron microscopy 
simulations with abTEM on MD data. Here information on the atoms and size of their vibra-
tion is contained in the imaginary value of the electron wave exiting the sample. Identifying 
and sorting the peaks into рқ‘Ҷ and рқ‘Җрқ‘ң, a trend of decreasing imaginary value, corresponding 
to increasing vibrational amplitude, towards the edge of the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 was observed. This form 
of the data allowed for direct comparison to experimental TEM measurements of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2. Ex-
perimental and simulated data agreed qualitatively on the trend and to a lesser extend quan-
titatively on from where and how much the vibrations decreased, with several practical rea-
sons given that could have reasonably caused the discrepancies. This also showed the im-
portance of using MD data to account for thermal vibrations in the sample during measure-
ments, as the default abTEM frozen phonons module uncorrelated, random atomic displace-
ments, which would not have caught the collective behaviour in the vibrations. 
 
 
The training data was extended with systems containing hydrogen. Systems with рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2, gra-
phene and рқҗ» and systems with рқҗ», but missing one of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 and graphene, were added. This 
allowed the model to do some preliminary simulations on the interaction of hydrogen with 
рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2, a common catalyst for the hydrogen evolution reaction 2рқҗ»+ + 2рқ‘’вҲ’вҶ’рқҗ»2. The focus 
was on a simple reaction in the form of рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ formation, for which several proof-of-concept 
systems with an in practice excessive amount of hydrogen were constructed. The model was 
capable of bonding рқҗ»2 to рқ‘Ҷ on the рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2 edge, but there were signs that the reaction was only 
вҖңlearnedвҖқ for a small range of separation distances. In MD simulations рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ formation was 
observed on a limited scale and without it breaking completely free of the vdW forces from 
and permanently leaving the bulk of рқ‘Җрқ‘ңрқ‘Ҷ2. The limiting factor on рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ formation was detach-
ing the рқ‘Ҷ atom without also detaching the hydrogen atoms or having the hydrogen diffuse 
far away first. These processes are temperature dependent and as such MD simulations were 
done for several temperatures, but without clear differences in the amount of formed рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ 
from 300 K to 700 K. Simulations at 1000 K and 1200 K clearly showcased an upper tempera-

showed that a freed рқҗ»2рқ‘Ҷ can also be created when an рқ‘Ҷ atom вҖңcatchesвҖқ a fast moving рқҗ» atom 
into an orbit around the рқ‘Ҷ atoms, with the kinetic energy transferred from the рқҗ» atom con-

Introduction and motivation 
 
Heterogeneous catalysis (catalysis where the reactant and products are in a different phase 
than the catalyst) is a vital part of many chemical processes and one of the preferred meth-
ods in industry51. A heterogeneous catalyst is often a nanoparticle, and a key factor influenc-
ing its reactivity is the surface geometry of the nanoparticles. This can be affected by the 
choice of support, hence why running simulations of nanoparticles with various supports is 
of interest. It is not practical to do this with density functional theory (DFT) due to the large 
number of atoms and computation power required. However, it has been shown that DFT 
can be approximated by calculations using a machine learning potential trained on relevant 
smaller DFT calculations. In particular the E(3)- equivariant machine-learning inter-atomic 
potentials of NequIP are promising, with simulations of Pt nanoparticles on an oxide sup-
port having been done.52 
 
Here we aim to expand on some of the conclusions of the above mentioned work by also ex-
amining if the modelling method can also handle nanoparticles bound to the support 
through weak van-der-Waals forces. To do this MoS2 nanoparticles supported by multi-
layer graphite will be examined. MoS2 as a catalyst is used for removing sulphur from fossil 
fuels and for the hydrogen evolution reaction occurring at the cathode in electrolysis cells. 
As such part of the project will concern the binding of hydrogen to the surface and various 
defects in the nanoparticle. The overarching aim of the project is, like in ref [2], to be able to 
generalize DFT computations performed on smaller system to larger systems using machine 
learning potentials and then apply these on some specific cases like the one mentioned 
above.

Main objectives 
 
Generating a relevant dataset: Creating the systems 
 
NequIP typically does not require large amount of training data and as such a database of 
relevant MoS2-graphene system can reasonably be generated early in the project. This in-
volved manually constructing sample systems with some free parameters like MoS2-gra-
phene distance, number of graphene layers, amount and type of defects etc. and then creat-
ing permutations of these features in the atomic simulation environment [ASE].  
 
Generating a relevant dataset: Calculations on the systems 
 
DFT calculations on the resulting structures will then be performed to obtain the energies 
and forces necessary to construct the machine learning potential [MLP]. The calculations will 
mainly be performed on the NIFLHEIM super-computer, the use of which will have to be 
learned. 
 
The calculations should also be made as computationally efficient as possible for example by 
finding minimum required calculator-parameter values for the calculations to converge and 
an efficient way to implement van-der-Waals forces into the calculations. 
 
Creating and optimizing models: Initial model(s) and hyperparameters 
 
An initial model will be trained with the default Nequip hyperparameters and a relaxation 
of a MoS2-graphene system will be attempted and qualitatively assessed. To quantitatively 
compare the model to the DFT results and models with other hyperparameters, a specific 
test dataset will be created and a script written to calculate the test-error. 
 
Different values of hyperparameters and resulting test errors will be examined. 
 
Creating and optimizing models: Active learning to improve system database 
 
An ensemble of models will be tested on a new set of systems to identify potential deficien-
cies in the training data and the results will be used to generate an improved set of training 
data.

Creating and optimizing models: Active learning to improve system database 
 
It has been observed with transmission electron microscopy53 that larger vibrations exist at 
the edge of MoS2 nanoparticles, potentially caused by the electron beam. This is likely to af-
fect the catalytic activity. We will therefore model the vibrational amplitudes of larger nano-
particles at different temperatures and compare with experimental data. 
 
Applying the model(s): Adsorption of hydrogen to MoS2 
 
As part of the hydrogen evolution reaction, hydrogen molecules adsorb to the surface of the 
catalyst to increase reactivity. We will attempt to model this binding between hydrogen and 
MoS2, possibly by using the nudged elastic band method, available through ASE.
