Abstract
As inverter-based resources (IBRs) such as wind and solar systems have become in-
creasingly common, the need for dependable detection of Loss of Mains (LoM) events
has grown, particularly to ensure grid safety and protect personnel. Although passive
detection approaches, including Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF), Vector Shift
(VS), and RMS voltage monitoring, are widely used, they often fail to detect events
reliably in weak grid or under balanced load-generation conditions.
This thesis evaluates the effectiveness of standard LoM detection methods under vary-
ing grid strengths and load conditions. It introduces a lightweight active enhancement:
a small reactive power step (Q-step) introduced after breaker operation to speed up
frequency deviation and improve detection reliability. The reliability of this method
is a key factor that will make power system professionals and grid safety regulators
feel secure about its performance.
The study uses a simulation model of a wind-connected power system built in MAT-
LAB/Simulink, incorporating variable loads and adjustable grid strengths. Four de-
tection approaches: RoCoF, VS, RMS voltage flag, and a combined LoM flag were
tested across all scenarios, including weak and strong grids as well as ±5–10% load
mismatches.
Results show that passive methods face significant detection delays and Non Detection
Zone (NDZ) issues under matched-load conditions in weak grids. The proposed active
or Q-step method substantially shortens detection time; for example, reducing delay
from 111 ms to 44 ms, while avoiding false positives. The method also aligns with
compliance requirements from IEEE 1547.
The results demonstrate that even modest active techniques can effectively enhance
LoM detection performance, especially in environments dominated by inverter-based
generation and weak grid conditions.
Keywords: Loss of Mains (LoM), Inverter-Based Resources (IBRs), RoCoF, Vector
Shift, RMS Voltage, Reactive Power Step, Weak Grids, Non-Detection Zone (NDZ),
IEEE 1547, Danish Grid Code, Wind Power, Simulink.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The transition towards renewable energy has changed how modern power systems
operate. In conventional grids, large synchronous generators provided inertia that
naturally stabilized frequency during disturbances. As more inverter-based resources
(IBRs) are connected, this stabilizing effect is reduced because IBRs do not contribute
inertia in the same way. The result is a grid that becomes more sensitive to small
fluctuations, particularly under weak grid conditions where the system strength is
low [1], [2].
One of the key reliability concerns in this context is the occurrence of Loss of Mains
(LoM), also known as unintentional islanding.
LoM happens when a distributed
generator becomes electrically separated from the main grid but continues to supply
power to local loads [3].
If this condition is not detected quickly, it can expose
utility workers to unsafe operating conditions, risk damaging equipment, and cause
violations of grid codes and safety regulations [4].
Detecting LoM accurately is therefore a mandatory requirement in most intercon-
nection standards, including IEEE 1547, and has been widely studied in both pho-
tovoltaic and wind-based distributed generation systems [2], [5]. However, reliable
detection remains a challenge under conditions where generation and load are closely
balanced. These operating points, often described as the Non-Detection Zone (NDZ),
are especially problematic for conventional detection methods.
1.2
Motivation
As renewable generation expands, protection systems are required to handle condi-
tions they were not originally designed for. One of the most important of these is
the detection of Loss of Mains (LoM). If an islanding event is not recognized quickly,
it can create unsafe working conditions, interfere with grid protection coordination,
and cause damage to connected equipment [3], [4].
Detecting islanding is most diﬀicult in weak grids dominated by inverters. Here, the
local load can nearly balance the generator output, leaving only minimal variations
in voltage, frequency, or phase, often insuﬀicient to trigger passive schemes. This

operating range, often referred to as the non-detection zone (NDZ), is where passive
methods are least effective [1], [5].
Addressing this gap requires methods that can shorten the detection time while still
being practical for use in inverter controllers. The purpose of this work is to evaluate
the performance of existing passive techniques under realistic weak-grid conditions
and to demonstrate how a small active disturbance can improve reliability without
adding unnecessary complexity.
1.3
Problem Statement
Conventional Loss of Mains (LoM) detection methods, such as RoCoF, vector shift,
and RMS voltage monitoring, are widely used because they are simple and already
integrated into inverter controllers [2], [5]. However, these methods face significant
limitations in weak grids, especially when the generator output and local demand
are closely matched. In such cases, frequency and voltage remain nearly constant,
resulting in delayed or even missed trips.
This shortcoming defines the non-detection zone (NDZ), where passive methods can-
not provide the reliability required by modern grid codes [1], [4]. In wind and solar
plants connected to weak grids with low short-circuit capacity, missed detection be-
comes especially serious. It raises both safety risks for personnel and the possibility
of violating interconnection requirements.
The central problem addressed in this thesis is therefore the need for a LoM detection
strategy that reduces NDZ sensitivity under weak-grid conditions while remaining
practical for real-time implementation.
The objective is to compare conventional
passive methods with a simple active approach and demonstrate how detection speed
and reliability can be improved.
1.4
Objectives
This thesis investigates how conventional passive Loss of Mains (LoM) detection
methods perform in wind power systems and where they fall short under practical
conditions. To address these limitations, there is a need for a method that improves
the sensitivity and response time of LoM detection, especially when load and gen-
eration are closely matched. This project looks at how LoM behaves in weak grid
conditions where wind generation makes up a large part of the system. A basic active
method is added to improve how consistently islanding is detected. The detection
times from all test cases are checked against the limits defined in IEEE 1547 [4].

The specific objectives of the study are:
1. Evaluate conventional LoM detection methods: Analyse the behavior of
Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF), Vector Shift (VS), and RMS voltage
monitoring in weak grid conditions with wind energy systems.
2. Assess the influence of grid strength and load conditions: Investigate
matched and mismatched load cases to identify issues such as NDZs and false
trips.
3. Propose and evaluate an enhancement: Introduce a method that improves
detection speed and sensitivity under weak grid and NDZ scenarios.
4. Compare active and passive methods: Measure detection times for both
approaches and verify compliance with IEEE 1547 detection windows.
1.5
Method Overview
The thesis work is performed using a simulation-based approach in MATLAB/Simulink
to analyse LoM detection in wind power systems. Conventional passive detection
methods: Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF), Vector Shift (VS), and RMS voltage
monitoring are modelled and tested under weak grid conditions with high renewable
penetration. Both matched and mismatched load scenarios are considered to capture
NDZs and possible false trips.
The proposed enhancement applies a small reactive power step following a breaker
disconnection to trigger measurable system changes. Detection speed, sensitiv-
ity, and compliance with IEEE 1547 are compared between passive and active
methods. Simulations are conducted under controlled variations in grid strength
and operating conditions to represent a realistic range of scenarios.
1.6
Scope and Assumptions
This study covers:
• System type: Inverter-based wind energy systems connected to the grid.
• Detection methods: Passive techniques already in use, along with a basic
active method introduced for better response.
• Operating conditions: Both weak and strong grids, using load cases that are
matched or mismatched to test for non-detection and false trip risks.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
2.1
Overview of Islanding and LoM
Islanding occurs when a distributed generator remains connected to local loads after
being unintentionally separated from the main grid. In this condition, the generator
continues supplying power within a limited electrical area, forming what is commonly
called an “island.” The event is referred to as Loss of Mains (LoM) or unintentional
islanding [3].
If an islanding condition continues, it can create hazards for personnel, interfere
with the correct operation of protection devices, and expose connected equipment to
unstable voltages or frequencies. In addition, grid operators require all distributed
resources to disconnect quickly after LoM to maintain system stability and comply
with safety standards [4].
For these reasons, LoM detection is a mandatory feature in IBR units, including solar
PV and wind power systems. A reliable detection scheme ensures that the generator
ceases to energize the network once disconnection occurs, preventing unsafe island
operation and maintaining compliance with interconnection requirements [2], [5].
2.2
Passive Detection Methods
Passive LoM techniques operate by observing locally measured parameters at the
point of common coupling (PCC). Rather than injecting any external disturbance,
they depend on observing shifts in voltage, frequency, or phase that occur after the
grid is disconnected. Their main strengths are that they are straightforward to imple-
ment and do not degrade system power quality. However, they are prone to delayed
or missed detection when the local load closely matches the generator output, which
defines the non-detection zone (NDZ) [2], [5].
2.2.1
Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF)
RoCoF detection is based on monitoring the derivative of the measured frequency.
A sudden separation from the grid can cause the frequency to drift, and its rate of

normal disturbances.
2.2.2
Vector Shift (VS)
The vector shift detection observes the instantaneous phase angle of the PCC volt-
age. When the breaker opens, the phase relationship between the generator and grid
changes abruptly. A displacement beyond a set threshold is interpreted as an island-
ing event [2]. Although relatively easy to implement, VS can be insensitive when
phase variations are small, which again occurs near NDZ conditions.
2.2.3
RMS Voltage
Voltage-based detection monitors the RMS value of the PCC voltage. After discon-
nection, mismatches between generation and load typically result in an under- or
over-voltage condition. A threshold is defined so that if RMS voltage falls outside the
acceptable range, a trip is issued [4]. The drawback is that in matched-load cases,
the RMS value may remain within limits for an extended time, delaying detection.
Overall, passive methods are widely used because they are simple, fast in non-matched
cases, and require no additional control action.
Their limitation is clear in NDZ
scenarios, where frequency, angle, and voltage remain stable enough to prevent a

2.3
Active Detection Methods
Active approaches enhance detection by deliberately applying a small disturbance to
the system. Unlike passive methods, they do not wait for natural deviations; instead,
they provoke a measurable change that helps distinguish between normal operation
and an islanding event [6], [7].
2.3.1
Reactive Power Injection
One of the simplest active methods is to inject a step in reactive power. When the
grid is connected, the system can absorb or balance this change. After disconnection,
the same step creates a noticeable variation in frequency or voltage, which makes
detection easier. The size of the disturbance must be chosen carefully: too small may
go unnoticed, while too large can harm power quality.
2.3.2
Slip Mode, Impedance Injection, and Other Techniques
The SMS method works by slightly altering the phase angle of the inverter output. If
the grid is present, this shift is resisted. In islanded operation, the phase continues to
diverge, forcing the system away from synchronism until detection is triggered. SMS
is effective but can introduce distortion if applied too strongly [8].
Other active methods rely on modifying system impedance or injecting small har-
monics into the current. These perturbations are minimal during grid connection
but lead to detectable voltage and frequency changes once the system is islanded [9].
Their advantage is higher detection certainty, though they come at the cost of added
control complexity and possible power quality concerns.
In general, active methods shrink the NDZ compared to purely passive schemes. Their
drawback is that the deliberate disturbances, if not tuned properly, can increase har-
monic distortion or reduce eﬀiciency. For this reason, active methods are often studied
in combination with passive detection to balance reliability and system performance.

2.4
Hybrid Detection Methods
Hybrid detection combines features of passive and active schemes to achieve better
reliability than either method on its own. The passive stage monitors local signals
such as frequency, phase angle, or voltage and only activates the active stage if is-
landing is suspected. This way, the system avoids unnecessary disturbances during
normal operation while still reducing the NDZ when needed [8], [10].
One common approach is to pair RoCoF monitoring with a small phase or power
disturbance.
If the passive threshold is crossed, the disturbance is introduced to
confirm the event. This strategy minimizes false trips caused by natural fluctuations
while keeping the response time short.
Another example is the use of impedance measurement triggered by voltage or fre-
quency anomalies. The impedance check, carried out only when required, improves
detection certainty without continuously stressing the system [11].
Hybrid methods are especially attractive for weak grids where passive indicators alone
may remain flat under near-matched load conditions, but a continuous active distur-
bance would negatively impact power quality. By combining both techniques, hybrid
schemes provide a compromise between sensitivity and system performance.

2.5
Non-Detection Zone (NDZ) Problem
The non-detection zone (NDZ) is the range of operating conditions in which islanding
takes place but the protection system does not activate quickly enough to detect it.
It is most common when generation from the inverter closely matches the connected
load, leaving almost no noticeable shift in voltage, frequency, or phase angle after
disconnection [2], [5].
For passive methods, the NDZ is the range where signal deviations stay below their
respective thresholds.
RoCoF may remain too small, vector shift angles may be
negligible, and RMS voltage may continue within acceptable limits, preventing a trip.
This makes NDZ the most critical weakness of purely passive approaches [11].
Active methods reduce the NDZ by forcing a measurable disturbance, but even then
the zone is not entirely eliminated. The challenge lies in selecting a disturbance mag-
nitude that is large enough for reliable detection but small enough to avoid degrading
power quality. Hybrid schemes attempt to further narrow the NDZ by combining
passive monitoring with conditional active steps [8], [10].
In practice, the NDZ determines the effectiveness of any LoM strategy. A smaller
NDZ means faster, more reliable protection, while a larger NDZ leaves the system
exposed to delayed trips and unsafe island operation. Evaluating and reducing this
zone is therefore central to assessing detection methods.
2.6
LoM Challenges in Weak Grids
A weak grid is identified by having a low short-circuit ratio (SCR), meaning its fault
current capacity is limited. In such conditions, even small disturbances can cause
noticeable changes in voltage and frequency, and the interaction between inverter-
based resources and the network becomes more pronounced [1]. Detecting LoM is
therefore more diﬀicult than in a strong grid.
When an inverter operates in a weak grid, small changes in active or reactive power
can cause significant deviations in frequency or phase. At the same time, the system’s
limited inertia allows fluctuations to persist longer. Under matched or near-matched
load conditions, these effects can mask islanding events and expand the non-detection
zone (NDZ) [2].
Conventional passive methods such as RoCoF and vector shift often face diﬀiculties
in this environment. Thresholds that are high enough to avoid false trips in strong
grids may miss islanding in weak grids, while lower thresholds may lead to nuisance
tripping during normal variations [5]. Active or hybrid methods are therefore seen
==PAGE:20==
10
Literature Review
as more promising in weak-grid contexts, since they can enforce detectable changes
without depending only on natural disturbances.
2.7
Grid Code Requirements (IEEE 1547)
Modern grid codes require distributed energy resources (DERs) to detect LoM rapidly
and disconnect within a defined time window. The main objective is to prevent un-
intentional islanding while ensuring that DERs remain connected during acceptable
disturbances. According to IEEE 1547, distributed generators are required to discon-
nect and stop supplying power within a maximum of two seconds after an islanding
condition occurs [4].
Compliance with such standards is mandatory for grid connection. For both equip-
ment manufacturers and plant operators, LoM protection must satisfy two conditions:
it has to function correctly in practice and it must meet the timing and threshold
limits stated in the standards [12]. Non-compliance can result in grid disconnection
and safety concerns.
2.8
Research Gaps and Project Aim
Despite extensive work on LoM detection, several limitations remain. Passive meth-
ods such as RoCoF, vector shift, and RMS voltage are simple to implement but un-
reliable within the NDZ, particularly under weak-grid and matched-load conditions
[2], [5]. Active approaches can shorten detection delays, but using them continuously
risks lowering power quality or making the control more complicated [7], [9]. Hybrid
methods try to find a balance, yet they usually need extra tuning and coordination
[8], [10].
Current literature is also dominated by PV-based studies, while wind systems in weak
grids are less explored. Moreover, most results come from idealized simulations, with
only a few cases using hardware-in-the-loop or real grid testing [13]. This leaves a
noticeable gap between theory and practice.
The key research need is for LoM detection strategies that remain effective in weak
grids, minimize the NDZ, and meet grid code timing requirements without introducing
unnecessary complexity. Addressing this gap is the focus of the present work.

3.1.4.3
Load Modeling Decision (Series RLC Justification)
The load was modeled using a three-phase series RLC branch that accepts active
and reactive power setpoints. This structure allows direct specification of P and Q
without requiring manual tuning of resistance, inductance, or capacitance values [11].
The load block in Simulink takes the assigned active and reactive power values as
inputs and internally adjusts its impedance to match those values at the system
voltage. This allows the same block to be used for all test cases without requiring
manual changes to R, L, or C.
Since the power stays fixed during each run, the load behavior remains stable both
before and after disconnection. This setup avoids dynamic effects and makes it easier
to observe how LoM detection methods respond to different load conditions [1], [5].

4.3.1
LoM Detection Delay Calculation Method
Detection delay was calculated by measuring the time between the breaker opening
(at 0.5 seconds) and when the LoMflag signal was triggered. This comparison was
done using the Simulation Data Inspector by overlaying both signals. The delay was
recorded in milliseconds and used to evaluate how quickly each method, passive or
active, responded under different load and grid conditions.
4.3.2
NDZ Observation and Measurement Technique
NDZ behavior was observed by checking for delayed or missing detection during mis-
match cases. When the load closely matched the generator output, passive methods
either did not trip or responded with a delay. This confirmed that an NDZ exists
in these conditions, although its exact boundary was not measured. The mismatch
range showed the most sensitivity. Applying a reactive power disturbance as part of
the active method helped reduce the delay and ensured the LoMflag was triggered
more consistently.
4.3.3
False Trip Incidence and Reliability
False trips were avoided by design. RoCoF detection used filtering to block initial
transients, and vector shift was only active after the breaker opened. This ensured
the system didn’t respond to startup conditions or minor fluctuations. No false trips
occurred in any of the simulations, confirming that the detection worked reliably
across both weak grid and mismatch cases.

5.1.1
NDZ Limits and Classification Criteria
The NDZ refers to conditions under which passive LoM detection methods fail to
operate reliably or within acceptable time limits. In this work, the NDZ classification
is based on the detection delay measured after the disconnection event at 0.5 s.
• Outside NDZ: Detection delay of 50 ms or less. These cases are considered
promptly and reliably detected.
• Borderline NDZ: Detection delay between 50 and 100 ms. These cases show
inconsistent or marginal behavior near the NDZ boundary.
• Inside NDZ: Detection delay of 100 ms or more. These cases are assumed to

This classification is applied throughout the result analysis to distinguish which mis-
match cases were reliably detected, which were marginal, and which failed to trigger
timely LoM detection.

5.6
Case Comparisons
5.6.1
Matched vs Mismatched Loads
The test cases confirm that matched and mismatched load conditions have a signif-
icant impact on passive LoM detection reliability. Under matched load conditions,
the inverter output closely balances the local load, resulting in negligible power ex-
change at the PCC. In such cases, detection delays are consistently high 111.4 ms
in the strong grid, both classified as inside the NDZ. In contrast, even a moderate
mismatch in load improves detection time. For example, mismatches of –14% and
+15% result in delays of 48.9 ms, respectively, both outside the NDZ.
With the active method, detection delays remain fast across all load conditions, in-
cluding the matched case. The reactive power step forces a frequency disturbance
that eliminates NDZ behavior. Detection delays for both matched and mismatched
cases range from 14.4 ms, confirming reliable performance regardless of load balance.
5.6.2
Passive vs Active
The passive method relies entirely on natural grid transients after disconnection, mak-
ing its performance dependent on the operating condition. As a result, detection de-
lays are highly variable, from as low as 20.9 ms in low load conditions to over 111 ms
in matched load cases. Several cases fall inside or at the edge of the NDZ, raising
reliability concerns.
In contrast, the active method introduces a deliberate reactive power disturbance
immediately after the breaker opens. This ensures that frequency or phase changes
become more pronounced, even under marginal grid disturbances. The result is a con-
sistent detection delay across all test cases, always below 17 ms, and full elimination
of the NDZ.

NDZ Boundary Shift
The NDZ behavior observed in the passive method disappears completely with the

the NDZ is effectively eliminated. The active method provides consistent performance
across the full operating range, including cases that are typically problematic for
passive schemes.
5.7
Standards Compliance and Practical Implications
This work was evaluated with respect to IEEE 1547-2018, which requires distributed
energy systems to stop energizing the grid within 2 s after a disconnection. In practice,

faster response is expected, typically below 100 ms, to ensure safe coordination with
grid protection systems.
The passive method met this requirement only in cases with clear power imbalance. In
matched or near-matched conditions, detection delays exceeded 100 ms. These cases
fall outside the preferred response window. The active method improved detection
across all conditions, with delays consistently under 17 ms. This performance meets
the practical expectations of international grid codes.
5.7.1
Limitations and Assumptions
All results in this study are based on simulation. No real-time or hardware-in-the-
loop testing was performed. The grid is modeled as a simple Thevenin source with
fixed impedance. Load conditions are kept balanced and ideal.
Inverter control is implemented without switching effects. Reactive power injection
for the active method is applied as a fixed step at a known time. The control logic
responds instantly once triggered.
There are no delays from communication, and
controller limits like saturation are not included. These choices made it easier to
study the LoM detection itself, but they also mean the results don’t capture all real-
world effects.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion and Future
Work
6.1
Key Findings Summary
The results showed that passive LoM detection was not reliable under all conditions.
When the load closely matched the inverter output, especially with a weak grid,
detection delays went above 100 ms. These cases would not meet the fast response
expected by most grid standards.
The active method, based on injecting a small reactive power step, resolved this issue.
It gave consistent detection across all test cases, with delays staying below 17 ms as
seen in figure 5.41. This included the matched load cases where the passive method
failed.
Cases covered both load variation and load mismatch scenarios under weak and strong
grids.
The active method removed the NDZ and made detection stable, without
needing complex logic or additional hardware.
6.2
Practical Contributions to LoM Design
This method improves LoM detection by addressing a key limitation of passive tech-
niques. In matched load conditions, especially under weak grids, passive methods
often fail or react too late. The reactive power step introduced here creates a mea-
surable frequency disturbance that helps the system detect islanding reliably under
those conditions.
The approach does not rely on extra sensors or advanced logic. It can be added to
existing inverter control systems with minimal changes. This makes it a practical
solution for reducing NDZ-related issues in real-world systems without increasing
system complexity or cost.

74
Conclusion and Future Work
6.3
Future Extensions
This study was limited to simulation, but several extensions can make the work more
complete and practical. Machine learning methods could be tested to classify different
grid events and further reduce false detections. Hardware-in-the-loop experiments
would provide realistic validation of timing, switching effects, and controller dynamics.
Finally, the method could be integrated into commercial inverter firmware to confirm
that it can run on standard hardware without requiring new components.
6.3.1
ML-based Detection?
Machine learning could be applied to improve LoM detection by separating true
islanding events from normal grid disturbances. This would require collecting large
amounts of data and careful training, which is outside the scope of this work.
6.3.2
Hardware-in-the-loop Testing?
All results in this study were obtained through offline simulation. Hardware-in-the-
loop testing would give more realistic results by including switching effects, delays,
and controller limits. This step would be important before considering field use.
6.3.3
Integration into Commercial Inverters?
The active method used here is simple and does not need extra hardware. It could be
tested in commercial inverter firmware to check if it works reliably in practice. This
would confirm whether the method can be adopted in real systems.
