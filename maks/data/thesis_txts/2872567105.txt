Abstract
The construction sector contributes significantly to global CO2 emissions, underscoring
the urgent need for sustainable, low-carbon building materials. This thesis investigates
the feasibility of using unfired clay as a structural material in Nordic building design. It
addresses the material’s performance, environmental impact, and regulatory challenges.
Through a case study of a tiny house in Denmark, this research employs a multi-structured
methodology. Parametric design variations were developed to assess the impact of window
sizing and roof overhangs on daylighting, and wall thickness on thermal performance.
The structural integrity of various unfired clay wall assemblies, including cavity walls
and single-leaf walls with protective cladding, was evaluated against Eurocode and DIN
standards. Furthermore, building performance was analyzed using dynamic thermal (EN
ISO 13786) and hygrothermal (EN ISO 13788) calculations to determine heat buffering
capabilities and moisture risk. Finally, a comparative Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) using
Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) quantified the Global Warming Potential
(GWP) of the clay systems in comparison to conventional construction methods.
The results demonstrate that unfired clay is a structurally viable material for low-rise
construction in a Nordic context, provided that moisture is meticulously managed through
protective architectural detailing such as sufficient overhangs and external cladding. Thermally,
clay’s high mass provides excellent heat buffering, with the potential to reduce energy use
and enhance thermal comfort. The LCA results are particularly compelling, revealing that
unfired clay wall assemblies can achieve a net-negative Global Warming Potential (GWP),
acting as carbon sinks, in contrast to the significant carbon footprint of traditional concrete
and masonry walls.
This study concludes that unfired clay offers a viable route for decarbonizing construction
in the Nordic region. However, its practical implementation depends on an integrated
design approach that emphasizes moisture protection while addressing existing regulatory
gaps.
Keywords: Case Study, Earth Constructions, Unfired Clay Bricks, 3D Printing, Struc-
tural Design, Sustainable Construction, Hygrothermal Performance, Thermal Mass, Life
Cycle Assessment (LCA), Nordic climate

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Over the past decade, the search for sustainable building materials has grown, driven
by rising concerns over CO2 emissions and climate change.
During the 2015 COP 21
summit in Paris, nations agreed to pursue a low-carbon future, aiming to limit global
warming. The agreement seeks to limit the global temperature increase to 1.5°C while
enhancing countries’ ability to manage the impacts of climate change. Additionally, it
emphasizes aligning financial flows with a low-Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions pathway
and a climate-resilient future [2]. In Europe, the European Union (EU) has since aimed

2.1
Clay as a Construction Material
Clay is a complex and versatile material with a wide range of properties relevant to its
use in construction.
While it offers clear environmental and architectural benefits, it
also presents technical challenges—particularly with regard to strength, durability, and
moisture sensitivity. However, many of these challenges can be addressed through natural
optimization strategies such as drying, compaction, and grain-size adjustment.
The motivation for using clay lies primarily in its ecological advantages.
As a locally
available, low-energy, and recyclable material, it aligns well with the principles of sustain-
able building design. In the following sections, the fundamental characteristics of clay will
be introduced, followed by a presentation of its mechanical and hygrothermal behavior,
strategies for enhancing its performance, and its environmental impact.
2.1.1
What is Clay?
Clay is a naturally occurring fine-grained material formed through the weathering of
silicate-bearing rocks. It is primarily composed of phyllosilicate minerals such as kaol-
inite, illite, and montmorillonite (a type of smectite), whose layered structures govern
their plasticity, water retention, and swelling behavior [12, 13]. When dried, clay becomes
stiff and coherent, losing its plasticity and forming a hard, solid mass [12]. These mineralo-
gical characteristics strongly influence construction-relevant properties such as cohesion,
dimensional stability, and moisture sensitivity [14].
Based on grain-size classification, clay refers to particles smaller than 2 µm, as defined by
ISO and the Unified Soil Classification System. However, this size-based classification does
not necessarily correspond to mineralogical composition, since other fine non-clay minerals
may be present in this fraction [13]. Anglade et al. [14] emphasize that particle size alone
does not determine clay behavior, highlighting that different clay minerals exhibit distinct
moisture-related properties.

2.1.2
Physical Properties of Clay in Earth Construction
As a construction material, clay exhibits key performance-defining properties such as plas-
ticity, shrinkage, cohesion, water retention, and thermal response, all of which are governed
by its mineralogy and particle structure [13, 15]. Its high plasticity allows for moldabil-
ity and compaction, but also leads to dimensional changes and cracking as the material
dries [13].
The mechanical performance of clay-based materials is highly sensitive to both min-
eral type and moisture content. Anglade et al. [14] emphasize that expansive clays like
smectites exhibit greater water uptake, shrinkage, and cracking, while kaolinite offers im-
proved dimensional stability.
Furthermore, higher water content was found to reduce
compressive strength, tensile strength, and Young’s modulus — an effect attributed to the
loss of capillary forces and reduced cohesion at elevated moisture levels. This underscores
the need for climate-adapted design, moisture management, and suitable mix design in
structural applications.
In addition to mechanical characteristics, clay enhances the indoor environmental quality
through its hygrothermal properties. Its ability to regulate humidity [16] and store thermal
energy due to relatively high specific heat capacity and density [17] supports a stable and
energy-efficient indoor climate, particularly in passive building strategies.
2.1.3
Optimizing the Structural and Thermal Performance of Clay
For civil engineering applications, clay must be dried to reduce plasticity and develop
mechanical strength. This transformation, from a moldable mass to a stiff, cohesive solid,
is essential for structural stability [13].
Although reduced moisture content improves
strength, Anglade et al. [14] note that it may also decrease thermal inertia due to the
lower heat storage capacity of drier materials.
Mechanical performance can also be enhanced by adjusting the grain-size composition.
Anglade et al. [14] demonstrated that increasing sand content in clay–sand mixes reduces
shrinkage, increases Young’s modulus, and improves compressive strength. However, ex-
cessive sand content was found to reduce tensile strength, highlighting the need for a
well-balanced mix. The same study showed that sand addition had negativ effects on
thermal conductivity, but increased thermal inertia.
Compaction represents another effective strategy. Dormohamadi and Rahimnia [18] found
that higher compaction levels significantly increase compressive, flexural, and tensile strength
in earthen materials. Compaction also improves thermal inertia, as it raises material dens-
ity, a key factor in heat storage capacity. These enhancements are attributed to denser
particle packing and stronger interparticle bonding, resulting in more cohesive and load-
resistant structures.

to produce compared to conventional materials such as concrete and fired bricks. This
translates to a substantially lower embodied energy and carbon footprint [7]. Additionally,

Traditional and Modern Earth Building Techniques
Earth-based structures and buildings made from locally dug soil (e.g., clay, salt, etc.) are
among humanity’s oldest building traditions and methods. The oldest known construction
dates back to 10,000 BC and was found in Mesopotamia. Moreover, the method of rammed
earth dates back as early as 5000 BC. In addition to the various mud-brick temples of
ancient Egypt and the adobe villages found in Morocco, these types of structures and
architecture have been present for ages. [20]
As of today, about 30% of the world’s population still lives in houses made out of soil,
illustrated in Figure 2.1. This includes both developing and industrialized countries. In
recent years, these types of buildings have renewed interest due to their low-embodied-
carbon footprint and high thermal capacity. Although concerns exist about using this
material as a structural element due to its low strength and sensitivity to erosion, these
issues can be mitigated through effective architectural design, including the use of large
overhangs, elevated flooring, and efficient drainage solutions. [20]

Wattle and Daub
Wattle and Daub is one of the oldest methods for making weatherproof walls. It involves
creating a framework of vertical wooden stakes woven with horizontal twigs and branches,
which is then coated with daub—a plaster-like substance made from clay or mud. This
technique was used in various structures, such as Iron Age dwellings in England, where
stakes were driven into the ground, and as infill for half-timbered buildings in medieval
Europe. [21]

Cob is a building technique in which plastic, clayey soil mixtures are formed into balls
and then stacked on top of each other to create walls, as illustrated in Figure 2.2. The
soil mixture usually consists of soil and water, often supplemented with straw and sand.
This method is widely used in regions such as Africa, India, and Saudi Arabia.

Rammed Earth
Rammed earth is a construction technique that has been used for thousands of years,
dating back to the Neolithic period [23].
The method involves using a damp mixture
of soil, which is poured into a temporary, rigid framework and compacted layer by layer
using either mechanical or manual ramming. This method produces walls with a distinctive
horizontal striped pattern due to the stratification of the compacted layers [20], seen in
Figure 2.3. Typical traits of the rammed earth wall is the unique stripes of different soils.
The method can be used for both load-bearing and non-load-bearing structures [9].
Figure 2.3: Rammed Earth Wall [24].
Earth Blocks
Earth blocks, as a building material, are an ancient method. There are three main types
of earth blocks, categorized into adobe blocks, cut blocks, and compressed earth blocks
(CEB), each defined by their manufacturing process and performance. [20]
1. Adobe Blocks: Adobe blocks are essentially sun-dried bricks. They are made by
molding a muddy soil mixture, often containing chopped straw, into a block shape
by filling a formwork. After being shaped, these blocks are left to dry in the sun.
[20]
2. Cut Blocks: In regions where the soil has sufficient natural cohesion, blocks can
be cut directly from the ground using a technique known as ”cut blocks”. Laterite,
a soil rich in iron and aluminum, forms in tropical climates and is ideal for this
purpose.
When exposed to air, these blocks harden due to the dehydration and
rearrangement of their components. This method is commonly used in India. [20]
3. Compressed Earth Blocks (CEBs): The technique for producing Compressed
Earth Blocks (CEBs) using a manual press became popular in Europe in the 18th
century.
It allows for lower water content in soil mixtures compared to adobe,
enabling immediate stacking after production. Additives such as cement or lime
often stabilize the mixture, improving its strength. [20]
While industrial presses are available, manual presses are still widely used. The block
shape is defined by the mold, allowing for various designs, including interlocking
shapes. The mixture (soil, cement, and water), the degree of compaction, and the
correct curing conditions are important for strength. [20]

2.3
Innovative Construction Methods of Earth
Traditional earthen construction has long been a cost-effective method with minimal en-
vironmental impact, due to the use of locally available materials. However, it also tends
to be slow and labor-intensive, relying heavily on in-situ forming, long drying times, and
weather-dependent work schedules that can extend the construction period over many
months.
In response to these construction challenges, three innovative methods have
emerged to enhance the efficiency of earthen construction.
2.3.1
Unfired Clay Bricks
Unfired clay brick construction, with roots tracing back to Mesopotamia around the third
millennium BC, has a long history as a fundamental building method. Its use has de-
clined since the end of the nineteenth century with the development of new construction
materials. However, a renewed interest has emerged due to its significant potential for
sustainable construction, particularly its ability to reduce CO2 emissions associated with
conventional fired clay bricks drastically. Unlike traditional clay bricks, unfired versions
are not subjected to high-temperature kilning; instead, the bricks are typically air-dried
after manufacture, a process that reduces shrinkage, enhances strength, and, crucially,
eliminates the use of fossil fuels in their production. [25]
The production of unfired clay bricks offers substantial environmental benefits, primarily
due to their very low embodied energy, as the energy-intensive firing process is omitted.
They are generally manufactured by extracting suitable clays, which are then molded or,
more commonly in mass production, extruded into brick shapes, similar to the process
used in the production of fired bricks, as seen in Figure 2.4. Some bricks are also formed
by pressing or compaction, which uses a lower initial moisture content (around 10-13%)
for the raw material. Once formed, they are dried in controlled conditions. This results in
a cost-effective construction material with a very low environmental impact, characterized
by low carbon emissions, minimal waste, and high recyclability, as the bricks can often
be returned to the earth or remade at the end of their life. Furthermore, they have no
adverse health effects and can positively contribute to indoor environments. [26]
Unfired clay bricks possess several advantageous characteristics. They are relatively ro-
bust, fire-resistant materials that offer significant thermal mass, beneficial in the passive
regulation of internal building temperatures. [26]
The compressive strength of unfired clay masonry is comparable to that of aircrete block-
work, typically ranging from 2 to 3 MPa, depending on the clay content and manufacturing
methods. This strength is generally sufficient for normal-duty fixings. Construction can
be undertaken by bricklayers with little or no retraining, and the material is typically
workable on site, similar to aircrete. [26]
In terms of application, unfired clay masonry can be utilized for both load-bearing and
non-load-bearing walls. While traditional unfired clay walls were often thick to ensure
stability through self-weight, modern usage, especially with improved mortars, allows for
much thinner wall constructions.
These thin walls are often best suited for non-load-
bearing internal partition walls within a weatherproof building envelope.
If protected
from direct wetting, they can be used in internal ”wet areas” like bathrooms and kitchens,
where their additional vapor permeability is an advantage. [26, 25]

2.3.2
Applications in 3D printed Clay Construction
Recent advancements in technology, especially additive manufacturing, also known as 3D
printing (3DP), have created transformative opportunities in construction industry. This
technology, which builds structures layer by layer based on a digital model, is being used
more frequently for earthworks, providing a pathway toward more sustainable construction
practices [28, 29]. This method simplifies the development of load-bearing structures using
natural, locally sourced materials, often available directly from the construction site, and
significantly contributes to low-impact construction projects [28, 30].
Some of the key advantages of this method include the use of materials with low embodied
energy, high recyclability, limited waste generation, and potentially favorable thermal
mass properties.
Furthermore, the 3DP offers great design freedom, automation, and
customization. [28]
Among traditional earthworks, cob construction—a wet-based method using clayey soil,
water, and straw without a framework— has the most similar properties to the 3DP
extrusion process [28, 30]. 3DP can be seen as an automated and optimized version of cob
construction, leveraging digital control for precision and efficiency.
An example demonstrating the practical application of this technology comes from Italy,
led by WASP (World’s Advanced Saving Project). Their GAIA (2018) and TECLA (2021)
projects serve as essential prototypes (see Figure 2.5) [31]. These prototypes are built using
a mixture consisting of site soil, clay, straw, rice husk, and hydraulic lime [30]. The 3D
printing process itself relies on precise control of parameters such as material flowability,
curing time, water content, and extrusion energy to ensure proper layer adhesion and
structural integrity [28]. The printer commonly utilized for these large-scale projects is
WASP’s “Big Delta” printer, a remarkable 12-meter-tall machine that operates using solar
energy [30]. For the simplest version of printing (GAIA), the module requires just 100
hours of printing, and the company WASP is promoting zero-kilometer home construction
[29].

2.4.1
Moisture-Induced Deterioration Mechanisms
Moisture enters earth structures through capillary rise at the base and wind-driven rain
(WDR) on exposed facades. Water content plays a critical role in structural integrity: A
study found that as the moisture level increases, the hydrophilic nature of clay particles
leads to reduced internal cohesion and compressive strength [35]. This was illustrated by
the compressive strength of an unstabilised rammed earth containing 9% clay was found
to decrease from 1.7 MPa at 2% moisture content to 0.3 MPa at 12%.
Repeated wetting and drying leads to swelling and shrinkage of clay particles, creating
cracks that increase water ingress during subsequent moisture events [35]. This sets up a
feedback loop of damage.
Surface erosion is another key vulnerability in unprotected earthen walls, particularly
under WDR exposure. As internal cohesion decreases with rising water content, erosion
accelerates rapidly and can lead to progressive material loss [35].
In cold climates, earth materials are susceptible to freeze-thaw cycling. Expansion during
freezing disrupts the soil matrix, reducing cohesion and strength over time [36, 37]. Liu
and Wang [38] showed that freeze-thaw cycles cause progressive microcracking and pore
expansion in adobe, which increases moisture uptake. This accelerates damage in sub-
sequent cycles, confirming a self-reinforcing deterioration mechanism that leads to erosion
and structural weakening. While some unfired clay bricks have shown improved mechan-
ical properties after limited freeze-thaw exposure [39], the consensus remains that frost
resistance depends critically on preventing near-saturation.

2.4.2
Surface Protection Strategies Against Rain-Induced Damage
To ensure long-term durability in exposed conditions, surface treatments are often ap-
plied to improve water resistance without compromising the wall’s vapor permeability or
ecological compatibility. This section presents a range of surface protection strategies,
from traditional lime-based plasters to engineered hydrophobic coatings, with a focus on
performance, sustainability, and applicability in natural construction.
Impermeable surface coatings, such as paints, are generally unsuitable for earthen walls.
They tend to crack under volumetric changes and trap moisture beneath the surface, which
can accelerate erosion [41]. For earthen construction, coatings should be vapor-permeable
to allow the structure to dry out between wetting events.
Natural and Traditional Plaster Systems
Lime is commonly used to improve the durability and water resistance of raw earthen
plasters.
A study by Germinario et al. [42] evaluated different clay types mixed with
sand and stabilised with up to 5% lime for use in bio-based construction. The results
showed that lime addition increased water resistance, but also led to increased cracking
due to higher water demand. Adhesion strength generally decreased, especially in highly
plastic clays, as lime reduced the clay’s bonding capacity. Among the tested soils, illitic
earth performed best, offering good water durability with minimal cracking, making it
a promising candidate for natural plaster systems where moderate water exposure and
thermal insulation are priorities.
Tadelakt is a traditional Moroccan lime plaster system known for its hydrophobic yet
vapour-permeable surface. The water resistance results from the reaction between lime
and soap, forming calcium stearate which seals surface pores without blocking diffusion.
In a study conducted by D´ıaz Mac´ıas [43], a modified tadelakt made from locally available
materials was applied over adobe samples.
Permeability tests using the Karsten tube
method showed water absorption values in the range of 0.10–0.20 cm3/(10 min·cm2),
classifying the surface as waterproof while preserving the hygrothermal buffering function
of the earth substrate.
Another surface protection method is a tri-layer coating system, inspired by traditional
waterproofing practices in West Africa. This system combines local soil with hydrolysable
tannins extracted from the bark of Bridelia trees. The coating consists of three distinct
layers: (1) an adhesion layer (soil + Bridelia sediment + tannin), (2) a structural coat-
ing body (soil + 7–12% Bridelia powder), and (3) a protective surface layer made from
concentrated tannin solution. In field tests, compressive strength dramatically improved
from 1.01–1.96 MPa (untreated) to 3.17–4.98 MPa in dry conditions, and notably, it re-
tained 0.2–0.67 MPa under saturation, whereas untreated samples nearly disintegrated.
Furthermore, after four years of natural weather exposure, tri-layered walls remained in-
tact, while untreated soil coatings had eroded completely [44]. While traditional systems
offer compatibility and natural aesthetics, engineered coatings aim to extend durability
and performance under more demanding exposure conditions.

High-Performance and Engineered Coatings
Superhydrophobic coatings, characterized by water contact angles above 150°, have demon-
strated excellent effectiveness in reducing water absorption, erosion, and freeze–thaw dam-
age in earthen sites. These coatings typically achieve their performance by combining low-
surface-energy materials with nano- or micro-structured surface roughness. For instance,
waterborne polyurethane coatings modified with nano-silica have exhibited hydrophobi-
city and deep substrate penetration while effectively avoiding toxic solvents. While the
performance of superhydrophobic coatings is promising, challenges persist regarding their
long-term durability, reversibility, and overall ecological impact, particularly concerning
fluorinated compounds commonly employed in commercial formulations [45].
Beyond superhydrophobic systems, Liu et al.
also discuss the advantages and disad-
vantages of other weatherproof materials for earthen sites. Inorganic solutions, such as
potassium silicate, offer low cost and good weather resistance but are limited by low
initial strength and poor water resistance. Organic resins and silicones provide strong
water repellency, yet they are often expensive, toxic, or prone to aging. Similarly, natural
solutions like sticky rice paste or fiber-reinforced mud layers, while compatible with soil,
demonstrate limited durability. Consequently, no single material comprehensively fulfills
the demanding criteria for long-term, reversible, breathable, and eco-friendly protection,
underscoring the continuous need for material innovation [45].
In response to these environmental and conservation-related limitations of traditional coat-
ings, several more sustainable alternatives have emerged. Liu et al. highlight fluorine-free
alkylsilane-based treatments, such as methyltrimethoxysilane (MTMS), which achieve high
water repellency while maintaining vapour permeability and visual compatibility. Further
approaches include coatings based on bio-based waterborne polyurethanes and porous
silica networks, which primarily rely on structural water exclusion rather than chem-
ical repellency. These innovations represent a promising direction for combining effective
moisture protection with compatibility for heritage conservation and broader low-impact
construction practices [45].
2.4.3
Protective Function and Design of Roof Overhangs
Roof overhangs are a widely used passive strategy to reduce wind-driven rain (WDR)
exposure on building facades. Multiple studies confirm that rain impact is not evenly
distributed over the wall surface, but is strongly concentrated at the upper third. A long-
term field study by Concordia University and BC Housing [46] found that approximately
70% of WDR impacted the top 30% of the facade, and that a 1.2 m overhang reduced this
exposure by up to 99% within the top 0.6 m. Even a 0.3 m overhang provided measurable
benefit, though protection increased non-linearly with depth.
Wind speed significantly affects the performance of roof overhangs against WDR. Con-
cordia University and BC Housing showed that increasing wind speed from 1.5 m/s to
4.7 m/s reduced the WDR reduction coefficient by nearly 50% for overhangs of 0.6–1.2 m,
while the effect was negligible (under 10%) for a 0.3 m overhang. This suggests that while
larger overhangs provide greater protection under moderate conditions, they are also more
sensitive to wind-induced turbulence at higher velocities.
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations by Foroushani et al. [47] support this,
showing reduced shielding performance under high or oblique winds.
Field-based ISO

assessments on mid-rise buildings by Souri [48] further support these findings, emphas-

wind direction, and turbulence intensity — factors that must be taken into account in

At the same time, undersized overhangs may offer little to no protection - especially in

moisture-sensitive wall systems such as earth construction.
Design guidance also supports the protective role of overhangs. The Danish BYG-ERFA

While deeper overhangs provide increased protection against wind-driven rain, they may
also introduce structural limitations. For instance, the International Residential Code spe-
cifies that unsupported roof overhangs should not exceed 610 mm without dedicated uplift
protection [53]. This highlights the importance of considering structural detailing require-
ments when designing for moisture protection, especially in wind-exposed or lightweight
construction scenarios.
These findings collectively demonstrate that overhang effectiveness is influenced by a com-
bination of overhang depth, wall height, exposure severity, and construction type. Multiple
studies and guidelines highlight the importance of accounting for these parameters to re-
duce moisture loads on building envelopes, especially in climates with frequent driving
rain.
2.4.4
Additional Moisture Protection Measures
To prevent rain-induced damage from capillary moisture uptake at the base of earthen
walls, several foundation detailing strategies are recommended. These include elevating
the wall on a plinth of masonry, concrete, or stabilised earth, and incorporating damp-
proof barriers between the footing and the base of the wall [35]. Gravel-filled trenches are
another effective measure, facilitating evaporation, reducing the capillary rise of water from
the soil and mitigating splashback of water hitting the ground [35]. Lastly, the Danish
Building Regulations 2018(BR18) §335 [54] requires that all wall constructions—including
those made of earth—are designed to prevent the occurrence of interstitial condensation.

3.3
Regulatory Framework
European structural design is primarily governed by the Eurocodes, which provide detailed
descriptions of material properties, loads, and safety factors. These standards allow for
national annexes to address local conditions, traditions, and regulations. Nordic countries,
including Denmark, have adopted Eurocodes in their national building regulations, with
an emphasis on durability, safety, and energy efficiency. In Denmark, Eurocodes have been
the sole legal basis for structural design since 2009, as per the building regulations. [66]
The current Danish Building Regulations (BR18) (effective 2018, updating BR15 [53]),
implement the requirements of the Building Act concerning fire safety, general safety,
health, and energy efficiency [67].
The Eurocodes address conventional materials, such as concrete, steel, and timber, but
lack guidance for earth-based materials.
Historically overlooked, earth construction is
gaining recognition due to a rising interest in sustainable materials. By 2012, 18 countries,
including Germany, New Zealand, India, and the U.S., had established 33 standards for
structural earth materials, detailing construction methods, material properties, and load-
bearing requirements. [20]
There is no universally accepted standard for earthen construction, as terminology varies
across different codes and there are no recognized laboratory tests to assess the strength or
durability of earthen structures [20]. The absence of guidelines complicates the integration
of earthen materials, such as unfired clay bricks, into construction projects, necessitating
extensive custom documentation and approvals.
However, Danish regulations allow deviations from conventional building standards if jus-
tified by scientific evidence and comparable safety levels. Historical norms, such as those
established by the Danish Society of Engineers, support the use of alternative materials
when validated by engineering principles. [68]
BR18 §356 [54] permit exceptions to certain requirements, stating that deviations are

allowed if they are documented as reasonable and maintain the safety levels outlined in
Section §344(2)(1). As of now, certified professionals are required to verify the safety of all
structures; this requirement previously applied only to high-consequence structures prior
to 2020. [54]
To design earth-based structures suitable for Nordic climates, this project applies the
German national standard DIN 18945 [69] to define material characteristics and classi-

The standard defines performance requirements and testing procedures for units, classi-

structural design calculations for masonry walls, DIN 18945 ensures that the design pro-

relevant to this project include:
• Compressive Strength: A minimum of 2.0 N/mm2 is required for structural ap-

• Bulk Density: Classes range from 0.5 g/cm³ to 2.2 g/cm³, influencing mechanical
strength and the wall’s thermal mass, which is critical for thermal efficiency in Nordic

• Modulus of Elasticity: a minimum of 750 N/mm² modulus for load-bearing ele-
ments.
Essential for calculating deformation and ensuring stiffness, particularly
important for earth-based structures where deformations must be minimal.
• Durability: The standard includes requirements for water absorption, moisture
behavior, and freeze-thaw resistance. Its exposure classes (AK Ia, AK Ib, AK II,
AK III) are used to determine if protective measures are necessary to maintain
material integrity in the Nordic climate.
While DIN 18945 sets material performance limits, EN 1996-1-1 is employed for structural
design calculations (e.g., shear capacity, buckling stability). DIN 18945 ensures that the
material quality assumptions underlying the EN 1996-1-1 calculations are valid. In this
project, the standard outlines the minimum material requirements for earth-based struc-
tures, ensuring they possess sufficient compressive strength, density, modulus of elasticity,
and moisture resistance. By integrating DIN 18945 for material specification with EN
1996-1-1 for structural design, the aim is to produce reliable and compliant earth-based
structures for Nordic conditions.

3.4
Common Design Principles Applied to All Cases
The design principles outlined in this chapter are rooted in established and well-recognized
construction methods for high-performance walls in Nordic climates. These standard prac-
tices have been adapted to accommodate the specific properties of clay-based materials.
All case-specific wall assemblies share a common set of design principles to ensure robust-
ness and durability in Nordic climates. These encompass the foundation-to-wall interface,
the wall system, and the roof-to-wall connection, with a primary focus on mitigating
moisture-induced degradation.
Wall system components: Each wall design includes four common elements. Internal
clay plaster is applied to the inner surface to create an even surface. Wood fiber insulation,
chosen for its sustainability and vapor permeability, is attached directly to the earth wall
using insulation screws and wooden discs, eliminating the need for timber framing. A
ventilated cavity is included outside the insulation to allow drying of moisture from infilt-
ration or condensation. Finally, wood fiber wind barrier boards, pre-laminated with a wind
barrier and featuring taped joints, are placed on the insulation’s exterior to ensure con-
tinuity and wind-tightness. This vapor-open and stiff design eliminates on-site mechanical
fastening, safeguarding both the insulation’s thermal performance and the earth masonry’s
structural integrity from penetrations. Their specific design and functional viability are
assumed for this project.
Foundation design: The foundation layout (see Figure 3.5) includes several features
tailored for earth construction. An elevated base raises the wall to protect against splash
back water, snow, and damp ground.
A damp-proof course (DPC) between wall and
foundation prevents capillary moisture rise. On the interior side, a raised plinth or high
foundation block protects the wall base from cleaning impacts, accidental water exposure,
and mechanical wear.
Roof and Wall-Roof Connection: While the roof structure itself falls outside this
study’s primary design scope, its junction with the walls is considered in detail. For load
calculations, a compact prefabricated roof is assumed, with focus solely on its connection
with the wall. Crucially, this roof is equipped with stiffening elements designed to effect-
ively transfer horizontal loads to the building’s main resisting elements: the earth walls.
A roof overhang is incorporated as a passive rain protection strategy, tailored for the spe-
cific facade, as supported by Section 2.4.3. A wooden top plate (48×98 mm) is placed on
top of the wall to reduce load eccentricity. Although not designed, this timber element
is assumed to be protected from clay-induced moisture through a surface treatment or a
vapor-tight membrane. To support the exclusion of a wall vapor barrier, the roof vapor
barrier terminates at the top of the wall and is sealed beneath the top plate. Vertical
loading is assumed sufficient to maintain air-tightness at this junction. It is also assumed
that a continuous anchorage system connects the roof through the walls and down to the
foundation to handle uplift forces, but this system has not been designed.
Openings (windows and doors): Windows are located exclusively on the long facades,
except in Case I Solution I, where an additional window is included on one of the short
walls.
To accommodate the resulting discontinuities, the adjacent wall segments are
thickened to carry the increased structural loads. Given the low flexural strength of un-
fired clay, all window openings are assumed to be spanned by structural lintels-potentially
made of timber, steel, or fired clay-to ensure reliable load transfer across the openings.
Alternatively, an arched window head constructed from unfired clay bricks could be used

4.4.2
Design of Vertical Load-Bearing Capacity
Material model for unfired clay bricks
For structural calculations, the elastic modulus of unfired clay was defined using a simpli-
fied material model based on the methodology presented in Calculation of Load-Bearing
Capacity of Unfired Clay Walls [68], and supported by compression tests conducted at
DTU on clay samples from Vinge and Landerslev [91]. The stress–strain response exhib-
ited a linear-elastic region followed by non-linear softening, similar to concrete.
The secant modulus was calculated from the initial linear portion using:
Es = kE · fc
(4.1)
where fc is the compressive strength and kE = 100, in accordance with [68]. This approach is
consistent with Eurocode 6 provisions for masonry stiffness.

4.5.2
Hygrothermal Analysis (Glaser Method, EN ISO 13788)
Moisture performance was assessed for the two unfired clay brick wall assemblies (Case I, Solu-

regulation BR18 (§334–§336) [54]. The method provides a conservative moisture safety screening
based on steady-state vapor diffusion and monthly-averaged conditions. Despite neglecting buffer-
ing effects, hysteresis, and transient behavior, it is widely accepted in Danish practice, including
SBi guidance.
The analysis was implemented using a layer-based model with monthly average temperature and
vapor pressure profiles. Condensation is assumed where partial vapor pressure exceeds saturation
pressure. Each wall assembly was discretized into planar layers with constant vapor diffusion res-
istance (µ). Results were evaluated independently per month, with annual accumulation assessed
as a post-processing step.

For the wood facade wall, the outer climate boundary was set at the
exterior insulation face (ventilated cavity excluded). For the cavity wall, the outer boundary was
the exterior clay leaf. Internal and external surface resistances were assigned per EN ISO 6946.
Internal and external vapor pressures were calculated using monthly average temperatures and
relative humidities with the ideal gas law.

0.5 l/(s · m2).

diffusion, isothermal layers, no moisture storage, and constant material properties. Liquid trans-
port and buffering were excluded.

Wall Section Life-Cycle Impacts using EPDs
This thesis also presents a comparative life cycle assessment (LCA) of various wall sections to
quantify their environmental impacts, with a focus on global warming potential (GWP). Using data
from Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs), the analysis highlights key life cycle stages that
influence the initial carbon footprint and end-of-life implications. The selected life cycle modules
[96], as defined by EN 15804 [97] , include:
Modules A1-A3 (Product Phase): Covers the “cradle-to-gate” impacts, including raw material
extraction (A1), transport (A2), and product manufacturing (A3), representing the initial carbon
footprint.
Module C1 (Deconstruction/Demolition): Considers environmental impacts during the dis-
mantling or demolition of the wall section at the end of its life.
Module C3 (Waste Management): Assesses the impacts of waste management for recycling
or energy recovery of demolished materials.
Module D (Benefits and pressures beyond the system boundary): Quantifies potential
environmental benefits from the reuse or recycling of materials, indicating the potential for a
circular economy.
This combined analysis provides a thorough understanding of each wall section’s environmental
profile, including its production load, end-of-life processes, and contribution to a circular economy,
which helps in selecting options with a lower overall environmental footprint [96].

A significant challenge for unstabilized earth walls is the natural variability of clay and the limited
understanding of how regional differences impact performance. Philipp Wiehle highlighted this
fundamental knowledge gap:

you take it out of the ground.”
—Phillip Wiehle
To analyze the material’s composition, Wiehle uses grain size distribution and X-ray diffraction.
He noted that in Germany, for instance, variations in the raw material affect its properties mainly
through grain size distribution rather than sheer clay content. This leads to a critical trade-off:

Adapting earthwork standards for colder climates is challenging due to the risks of moisture and

“Using earth masonry in external walls requires insulation on the outer side, bringing

—Philipp Wiehle

Philipp Wiehle highlighted that unfired clay brick masonry is ideally suited for low-rise build-

Dr.-Ing. Sigurdur Gunnarsson, a structural engineer at Asplan Viak, discussed the potential of

Key Insigths and Main Challenges
Gunnarsson emphasized that the primary barriers to utilizing abundant clay reserves in Norway
and Denmark are not technical limitations, but rather systemic issues in how the material is valued
and managed. He explained that vast quantities of usable clay are simply treated as waste:
“The biggest challenges aren’t technical; they’re human and systemic. Clay itself isn’t
particularly demanding technically, especially here in Norway.
We have plenty of

once clay is deemed unusable for concrete (too fine, requiring too much cement), it’s
considered waste, often buried or dumped.”
—Sigurdur Gunnarsson
Millions of cubic meters of clay from tunnel excavations remain unused [107], and Denmark gener-
ates approximately 15 million tons of excess soil annually [11]. Gunnarsson suggests this ”waste”
could be managed through sorting and processing.
Cost-Effectiveness and Sustainability
Surplus clay has significant potential. Gunnarsson cited the Fornebu Line project, which removed
2.6 million cubic meters of clay, as an example where material typically discarded could be processed
locally. He explained: “For example, Fornebu removed 2.6 million cubic meters. They have top-
notch data and excellent logistics—staff, machinery, everything in place.”
Sorted fractions could serve various uses: coarser fractions for road/rail applications, finer ones for
insulation or acoustic panels. Existing sorting equipment and geotechnical expertise are sufficient,
and companies like ConClay Gmbh could adapt to processing clay.
Surplus clay is often free, since it is a byproduct, reducing material expenses and eliminating
disposal costs, making its use economically viable and environmentally beneficial.

Tiny houses are exceptionally well-suited for both prefabrication and DIY construction. Their

Discussion & Future Research
This study aimed to explore the potential of using clay as a structural material in Nordic building
design, identify key opportunities and limitations of its integration, and establish how it can
be optimally used to improve sustainability and functionality.
The research includes insights
from expert consultations, parametric studies, performance analyses, and life cycle assessments.
It provides clear answers to these questions, emphasizing both the benefits and challenges of
incorporating clay into modern construction practices.
10.1
Expert Insights & Consultation: Challenges and Op-
portunities
The expert consultations and roundtable discussions offered essential real-world insights for this
study, linking theoretical analysis to the practical realities of the Nordic construction sector. In-
terveiws with Dr.-Ing. Sigurdur Gunnarsson from Asplan Viak and Dr.-Ing. Phillip Wiehle from
Levita Lehm, as well as a roundtable discussion focusing on 3D printing, highlighted a shared
understanding of the key challenges and opportunities associated with using clay and soil as struc-
tural materials. A central, recurring theme is the critical challenge of moisture management, which
has a significant impact on durability, structural integrity, and thermal performance in the Nordic
climate.
The Importance of Considering Moisture Control and Protection
Both Dr. Gun-
narsson and Dr. Wiehle clearly stated that managing moisture is the top priority when designing
with clay. Their insights redirect technical analysis from theoretical computations toward creating
practical solutions for this significant issue. Dr. Gunnarsson emphasized a fundamental design
principle, asserting, ”If you use clay on the warm side of the building, you have only advantages:
moisture uptake/regulation, acoustics, and fire resistance”. This idea of an internally oriented ap-
plication, protected from direct exposure to the elements, was emphasized by Dr. Wiehle, who
highlighted the need for a multi-layered protection strategy. He argued that for earthen walls to
be feasible in the Nordics, they require ”insulation on the outer side, bringing the external walls
closer to an internal wall”. This protective barrier is essential for long-lasting durability. Dr.
Wiehle stressed the importance of strong architectural defenses, insisting on a roof overhang ”as
large as possible” and foundations with a minimum height of ”at least 50 centimeters” for partially
unprotected exterior walls. Beyond architectural design, he emphasized that earthen walls typic-
ally require external protection through cladding or specialized renders. He highlighted a wood
facade as a ”perfect” solution, as ”driving rain puts up the moisture content very quickly”. This
agreement sets forth a clear design directive: earthen construction in the Nordics should adopt a
”systemic” approach, wherein the building envelope is conceived as an integrated system designed
to regulate moisture transfer and protect against weather-related effects.

Bridging the Gap: Transforming ”Waste” Clay into Value Resource
Experts
highlight a notable difference in perceptions of surplus clay. Dr.Gunnarsson highlighted the paradox
in Norway and Denmark, where large quantities of high-quality clay are extracted for infrastructure
projects and then treated as surplus waste. He highlighted the Fornebu Line project, which removed
2.6 million cubic meters of clay, as a prime example of a missed opportunity. This vast amount of
material, rather than being wasted, could have been processed and used locally. The presence of
”top-notch data and excellent logistics—staff, machinery, everything in place” at these locations
offers a straightforward way to convert a costly waste stream into a valuable, sustainable resource.
This indicates that the most significant barriers to adoption are not technical challenges, but rather
logistical, regulatory and organizational issues that hinder the integration of this resource into the
construction chain.
Scalability: Prefabrication vs. 3D Printing
Navigating the path to broader adoption
for clay construction involves overcoming economic and logistical challenges that experts describe
as ”human and systemic” rather than purely technical. Dr. Sigurdur Gunnarsson pointed out the
irony of treating surplus excavated clay as waste when it could be a valuable, low-cost resource
that significantly reduces material expenses and eliminates disposal costs. However, this economic
advantage is countered by labor intensity, which varies considerably depending on the construction
method. In-situ methods, such as rammed earth, are far more labor-intensive than conventional
concrete, which drives up costs.
To overcome these challenges, the discussion on scalability highlighted a strong preference for
prefabrication. Both experts regard prefabricated, unfired clay bricks or blocks as an efficient and
scalable solution, particularly for the typical two- to three-story residential buildings that comprise
the majority of new construction. Dr. Wiehle highlighted the practicality of earth masonry using
”basically dried blocks,” explaining that because the material is available on-site, packed, and
wrapped, it presents no issues.
Stating that ”They’re packed.
They are wrapped. It’s not an
issue”.This method avoids the lengthy and risky on-site drying times associated with monolithic
approaches, such as rammed earth, which can take ”3-4 months, maybe a year” in a humid climate.
As a result, prefabrication stands out as the most effective and scalable approach to economically
leverage the advantages of clay.
In contrast, 3D printing remains a more complex and debated area. Dr. Gunnarsson expressed
considerable enthusiasm for the technology, calling it “super exciting” and predicting that if it can
be “fast enough”, it will undoubtedly become relevant. Dr. Wiehle’s skepticism tempered this
optimistic view. He questioned market readiness, citing the slow adoption of 3D-printed concrete
as a cautionary note, and emphasized the significant challenge of long drying times for current
3D-printable clay mixes. The 3DPA roundtable presented a compelling counter-narrative, show-
casing the innovative work of companies like WASP. Projects such as the TECLA and GAIA
houses demonstrate the technological feasibility of 3D printing large-scale, load-bearing earthen
structures, as well as potentially cost-effective earthen structures. This contrast of perspectives
positions 3D printing as an innovative technology that, despite its significant potential for sus-
tainable construction, encounters considerable technical and economic challenges before it can be
widely and practically implemented.

Daylight Quality Over Quantity
This analysis is undertaken to establish a design ap-
proach that prioritizes the quality of daylight and occupant comfort over simply maximizing
the quantity of light.
The results indicate that achieving optimal daylighting involves a com-
plex trade-off between the quantity and quality of light, rather than simply maximizing window
size. The analysis showed that while the Average Daylight Factor (ADF)—a measure of daylight
quantity—increases in a predictable linear trend with a larger Window-to-Building Area Ratio
(WBAR)(Figure 6.1b), the performance of Useful Daylight Illuminance (UDI) is far more complex
non-linear trend(Figure 6.1a). The UDI, which measures the percentage of occupied hours within
a comfortable illuminance range (300-3000 lux), was found to peak at a WBAR of approximately
20%.
Beyond this point, additional increases in glazing led to diminishing returns, as a higher number
of hours surpassed the 3000 lux threshold, resulting in potential glare and visual discomfort. This
nonlinear trend highlights the limitations of designing for daylight alone. It indicates that UDI
serves as a more important performance metric for occupant-centric design since it takes into
account the detrimental impacts of excessive brightness. These findings support the logic behind
established building standards, such as Denmark’s BR18, which recommends moderate glazing
ratios to balance daylighting with visual and thermal comfort(WBAR ≥10% WBAR).
The Dual Role of Overhangs in Simulating Daylight
The parametric study also
examines the impact of roof overhang as a weather protection element, which is essential for
clay’s durability. As expected, the analysis confirmed that increasing the depth of an overhang
consistently reduces the total amount of daylight entering the space, as shown by a strong negative
correlation with ADF across all WBARs tested (Figure 6.3). This is a direct consequence of the
overhang reducing the visible sky from the interior, which is the primary determinant of ADF
under overcast sky conditions. The results also indicate that scenarios with higher WBARs show
a steeper ADF reduction with increasing overhang length, indicating a greater sensitivity of ADF
to shading elements when window areas are large. This is a direct result of the overhang, which
minimizes direct solar penetration and glare, thereby increasing hours within the “useful” lighting
range.
However, the impact on the quality of available light (UDI) is complex and relies heavily on the
window size. For instance, larger windows result in taller windows that are more prone to being
shaded by any overhang. This means that there are diminishing returns on larger windows after a
certain size in combination with overhang length.
For the facade with large window sizes (e.g., 30-40% WBAR), a modest overhang of around 500
mm proved to be beneficial, measurably increasing the UDI (Figure 6.2). This occurs because
the overhang is most effective at blocking the high-angle sun that causes excessive brightness and
glare, thereby shifting hours that were previously ”too bright” back into the ”useful” illuminance
range.
In contrast, for designs with smaller windows (e.g., 10% WBAR) that already struggle to achieve
sufficient illumination, the introduction of nearly any overhang negatively impacts the UDI. In
these cases, the overhang’s light-blocking effect outweighs any potential benefits of glare reduction,
further reducing the number of hours that receive sufficient lighting.

highlights the need for a truly integrated facade design approach when using clay as a building

incorporating substantial overhangs (at least 500 mm) is essential for protecting the structure
from moisture and ensuring the long-term durability of clay-based structures, as supported by
both research and interview data.
At the same time, meeting daylight standards, such as the recommended ADF range of 2–5%,
is a delicate balance between selecting a sufficient WBAR and an appropriate overhang length.

Structural Loading Assumptions and Application
The applied loading strategy fol-
lows Eurocode principles and Danish National Annex provisions, using clearly separated action
types and appropriate ultimate limit state (ULS) combinations per EN 1990 and EN 1991. No
serviceability limit state (SLS) checks were performed. While the current approach is sufficient
for early-stage safety assessment, serviceability limit state (SLS) checks—such as those addressing
cracking, creep, or shrinkage—may become relevant in more advanced design phases. All applied
simplifications may require further evaluation to ensure alignment with Eurocode requirements
and national provisions.
Loads were applied as uniformly distributed, with the pressure on the roof overhang averaged
into the total roof wind load. While this simplification facilitates analysis and is suitable at early
design stages, it may underestimate peak effects near openings, eaves, and corners, where local
suction or compression forces can be significantly higher. These simplifications should be revisited
in detailed design using localized loading or finite element methods to ensure sufficient robustness
in anchorage, wall–roof interaction, and local detailing.
Vertical loads consisted of the wall’s self-weight, the estimated roof load, and the loft structure.
While roof and loft dead loads may vary in real-world applications, their overall influence is limited,
as the self-weight of the earthen wall is the dominant vertical force. Accurate specification of earth
material density is therefore critical for reliable structural assessment.
The dead load of the mortar was not included in the density calculation, which means the wall’s
vertical load was based solely on the brick density. Since mortar typically has a lower density
than clay brick, including it would reduce the overall wall weight. In practical terms, this would
make the current Load Combination 1 slightly conservative and Load Combination 2 somewhat
underestimated.
Snow load was modeled as a uniformly distributed surface load. This approach may underestimate
localized effects from snow drift accumulation, but it was considered a reasonable simplification
for the wall geometries and configurations analyzed.
Material Model and Vertical Load Design
The structural material model for unfired
clay strikes a balance between realism and conservatism. A secant modulus based on the linear
portion of the stress–strain curve is used to approximate stiffness, consistent with EN 1996-1-1
assumptions for brittle, low-tensile materials. The kE · fc formulation reflects established Danish
practice and provides a simplified yet cautious representation of deformation behavior. Moisture-
dependent scaling factors were also applied, though their accuracy may vary depending on actual in-
service humidity conditions. Further calibration against measured moisture profiles could improve
84
Investigation of Clay as a Structural Material in Nordic Building Design
==PAGE:101==
reliability.
Vertical load capacity was evaluated using Ritter’s method, aligned with EN 1996-1-1 section
7.5.1.3 for effective height and section 5.8.1 for eccentric loading effects. A key variable is the
assumed buckling length, which strongly influences the critical load. Although Eurocode-based
assumptions provide a safe basis, the proper degree of lateral restraint, e.g., from adjacent walls, was
not explicitly verified. Finite element modeling or parametric studies could improve the accuracy
of this parameter and reduce conservatism where appropriate. The method is also sensitive to
eccentricity, particularly near the utilization limit, where small geometric or loading changes may
cause significant variation in resistance.
For 3D-printed walls, Ritter’s method may not fully capture the structural behavior due to differ-
ences in geometry, bonding, and internal voids. Some literature proposes alternative approaches
to printed materials. Additionally, standardization of strength values and partial safety factors
remains lacking, making cross-study comparisons and optimized design difficult. Treating the 3D-
printed cross-section as non-solid is a justified correction, but further work is needed to establish
more representative models.
All wall types were assessed using consistent design assumptions, but their structural requirements
differ. The single-leaf wall is optimized for vertical loads and can remain relatively slender. The cav-
ity wall must resist both vertical and lateral forces, requiring increased thickness. The 3D-printed
wall relies on increased moment of inertia, achieved through ribbed geometry, to compensate for its
low material strength and to prevent buckling. Direct comparison between wall types utilization
is therefore not meaningful, but all configurations remain within acceptable safety margins.
Out-of-plane bending was modeled as one-way spanning; however, in practice, some two-way action
may occur due to transverse wall stiffness, potentially reducing bending moments and eccentricity.
The two leafs of the cavity wall was modeled as structurally independent, a conservative simpli-
fication that avoids overestimating stiffness and capacity. In practice, some interaction is likely
due to mechanical ties or material bonding, which would enable partial load sharing under ec-
centric vertical loading. Incorporating this composite behavior, particularly in moment of inertia
calculations, could improve accuracy and enable more efficient designs.
Future applications of
Ritter’s method should therefore explore dual-leaf modeling better to represent realistic structural
performance in cavity wall systems.
Overall, the methodology reflects a cautious, Eurocode-consistent approach adapted to the specific
challenges of designing with unfired and 3D-printed clay. While simplifications were necessary,
they are generally conservative and appropriate for the early-stage design level of this study.
Out-of-Plane Bending Resistance
The bending resistance assessment follows Eurocode
principles, using linear tensile stress distribution and a rectangular stress block for compression.
These assumptions is consistent with EN 1996-1-1 and require that plane sections remain plane
and that tensile stiffness is present. However, since Eurocode does not directly cover unfired clay,
these assumptions represent an approximation of the material’s stress–strain behavior. In reality,
unfired clay cracks early under tension, and stress redistribution in compression may be less uniform
than the rectangular block suggests. As a result, the calculated bending capacity may be slightly
overestimated.

Shear Capacity and Stability Verification
Shear resistance was calculated based on
the compressed portion of the cross-section, assuming a linear stress distribution enhanced by
vertical pre-compression.
While consistent with Eurocode-based methodology, this assumption
may overestimate capacity if cracking or inaccuracies in eccentricity reduce compression. This is
particularly relevant for the single-leaf wall, where shear governs the required wall thickness in
critical cases.
The load-sharing ratio between wall segments was determined from relative shear stiffness and
area, without considering edge conditions or rotational constraints. This simplification may distort
internal force distribution, especially near openings. In cavity walls, perfect interaction between
leaves was assumed, which increases shear capacity but requires careful detailing to ensure reliable
force transfer in practice.
The coefficient of friction (µ) was adopted from values for fired masonry. Still, the interface behavior
between unfired clay and clay mortar, particularly under wet conditions, remains uncertain and
is likely to be lower. Since shear capacity depends on both µ and cohesion (fvd), more accurate
data under relevant service conditions is needed to ensure reliable and Eurocode-compliant shear
design. A moisture-dependent reduction factor, similar to the compression strength modifier (M)
in DIN 18940, should also be developed for shear capacity.
Sliding resistance checks confirm stability across all wall types under the applied wind actions. The
most critical case occurred in the cavity wall’s short segment with a large window opening, where
dry friction without adhesion resulted in a maximum utilization of 82%. Wet friction without
adhesion was not considered due to its conservatism. Still, a more detailed understanding of fric-
tion–adhesion behavior between clay and adjacent materials, particularly under varying moisture
conditions, would improve reliability as utilization approaches critical thresholds.
Overturning was also verified across all configurations, with utilization ratios (URTilt) below 31%.
The stability is primarily due to the high self-weight of the earthen material and sufficient wall
dimensions. The single-leaf wall, despite lower weight, remained stable due to reduced wind loading.
Overturning force transfer in wall segments adjacent to windows was assumed to be proportional to
their shear stiffness. However, if the load is not evenly distributed in practice, this assumption may
underestimate the force experienced by specific segments. Slender panels near openings remain a
potential weak point. While mechanical anchoring can enhance performance, it is often unsuitable
for earthen materials due to their limited tensile and pull-out capacities. Ensuring sufficient wall
width next to openings is a more robust and material-compatible strategy.

Adapting EN 1996-1-1 for Unfired Clay Masonry
The methodology in EN 1996-1-1,
calibrated for standardized, high-strength fired masonry with relatively brittle and linear-elastic
behavior, relies on assumptions that may not accurately reflect the moisture-sensitive, non-linear,
and often more ductile properties of unfired clay. While Ritter’s method offers a mechanics-based
approach that more transparently accounts for slenderness, eccentricity, and material stiffness,
direct application of EN 1996-1-1 necessitates significant adaptations across several key structural
design aspects.
Beyond the initial adjustments to material factors, stiffness values, and reduction factors, a com-
prehensive adaptation of EN 1996-1-1 for unfired clay masonry necessitates numerous further con-
siderations. These encompass, but are not limited to, the following key areas identified in this
work:
Recalibrating empirical formulas and partial factors (γM) (EN 1996-1-1 Table 4.1) for material
strength and variability (e.g., fk, fb, fxk1, fxk2, fvk0, µf from Sections 5.7.1, 5.7.4, 5.7.2) based
on extensive experimental data, particularly addressing moisture content influence and statistically
deriving characteristic values. Accurately representing deformation behavior (EN 1996-1-1 Sec-
tion 5.8, Table 5.10 (NDP)), which for unfired clay involves non-linear stress-strain response, lower
stiffness (E, G), and higher creep (ϕ∞) and shrinkage, requiring specific experimental determina-
tion under varying moisture conditions. Re-evaluating buckling resistance (factors Φ, NRd) and
slenderness limits (EN 1996-1-1 Section 8.2.2 and 7.5.2) to align with unfired clay’s unique stiff-
ness and ductile properties. Incorporating more stringent considerations for moisture-dependent
durability (beyond EN 1996-1-1 Section 6) through robust detailing due to unfired clay’s inherent
sensitivity in Nordic climates. Finally, thoroughly characterizing joint behavior and mortar
properties (EN 1996-1-1 Section 5.2), including clay mortar properties, bond strength, moisture
movement, and overall composite action when applicable, as Eurocode’s standard rules may not
directly apply.
In summary, adapting EN 1996-1-1 for unfired clay masonry is not a simple matter of minor para-
meter tweaks but necessitates a fundamental re-evaluation of material properties through extensive
experimental testing and a critical assessment of the code’s underlying behavioral assumptions to
accurately reflect the unique characteristics of unfired earth construction. This often leads to more
conservative design values or the need for a performance-based approach, as highlighted by Ritter’s
method.

Building Performance in a Nordic Climate: Thermal
and Hygrothermal Evaluation
The assessment of building performance offers a detailed understanding of the possibilities and
limitations of clay in a Nordic context, helping to inform how the material can be optimally
utilized.
While clay can provide exceptional thermal benefits, these are highly dependent on
design-specific factors. The hygrothermal results reveal a clear paradox: the potential to eliminate
vapor barriers introduces hygrothermal risks that require careful design strategies and ongoing
moisture management.
Thermal Evaluation
Clay’s high thermal mass presents a key opportunity for low-energy
design, as demonstrated in Section 6.2 and evaluated through the dynamic thermal properties
defined in EN ISO 13786. Chapter 8 confirms that thermal performance is not solely material-
dependent, but closely tied to wall design and configuration.

==PAGE:104==
For instance, the double-leaf cavity wall (Figure 3.4), which combines two unfired clay masonry
leaves with 200 mm of wood fiber insulation, exhibited favorable behavior for Nordic conditions. It
achieved a time lag of 17.08 hours and a low decrement factor of 0.061, indicating strong thermal
inertia and effective buffering of temperature fluctuations. This significant delay means that the
peak external temperature does not impact the interior until the next morning, which significantly
contributes to thermal stability and comfort. This reflects the interplay of mass and insulation: the
outer leaf dampens exterior variations, the insulation slows heat flow, and the inner leaf stabilizes
interior conditions.
In contrast, the other wall configurations exhibited lower thermal inertia.
The single-leaf wall
showed a time lag of 8.02 hours and a decrement factor of 0.343. Among the three solutions,
this combination represents the shortest delay in thermal response and the highest proportion of
external temperature variation reaching the interior. The 3D-printed wall, modeled as a solid clay
section, showed a longer time lag of 10.29 hours, which may be attributed to its greater total
thermal mass. For this analysis, the printed wall was idealized as a solid section with the same
thermal properties as unfired clay bricks, given the current lack of specific material data. However,
further analysis would be required to fully assess the influence of material distribution and thermal
coupling in each configuration.
These results are consistent with theoretical expectations. This alignment with established prin-
ciples of thermal dynamics is critical; the reduction in the decrement factor quantifies the wall’s
ability to attenuate external temperature variations, while the time lag quantifies its delay. Longer
time lags are generally advantageous, as they shift peak heat gain to periods when it is less crit-
ical. The results confirm that time lag increases roughly linearly with wall thickness, while the
decrement factor improves more gradually.
Although not explicitly shown in the results, it is essential to note that thermal buffering primarily
depends on the thermal mass located on the internal side of the insulation. Comparable thermal
inertia could therefore be achieved across different wall types, provided sufficient interior mass is
present to interact with indoor heat gains and losses.
This ability to moderate thermal swings enhances comfort and reduces energy demand. In summer,
with optimal design peak heat arrives indoors during cooler night hours; in winter, daytime solar
gains are released gradually overnight. Such regulation makes clay a strong candidate for resilient,
energy-efficient buildings.
However, optimal design requires holistic evaluation. High thermal mass is generally beneficial in
heating-dominated climates; however, its effectiveness depends on the specific climate, orientation,
and heating strategy. Although the cavity wall performed well thermally, its suitability must also
be assessed against other factors, such as durability, architectural intent, and structural behavior.
Thermal performance, while important, is only one part of an integrated design process.
Hygrothermal Evaluation
The hygrothermal analysis was conducted using the Glaser method,
as specified in EN ISO 13788, and aligned with Danish practice for early-stage moisture safety eval-
uation (BR18 §334–§336). The technique assumes steady-state, one-dimensional vapor diffusion
and excludes liquid transport, hysteresis, and moisture buffering, limitations acknowledged in both
the standard and SBi guidelines. While suitable for conservative screening, these assumptions limit
accuracy for hygroscopic materials such as unfired clay and wood fiber insulation. The use of con-
stant µ-values and exclusion of surface coatings further reduce precision, especially near surface
interfaces.
Boundary conditions were idealized according to EN ISO 13788, excluding ventilated cavities and
using indoor climate data from SBi 224. While these standard assumptions support consistent
analysis, they reduce the accuracy of results for buildings with more complex shapes or ventilation
conditions. The 3D-printed wall was excluded due to insufficient data on its moisture transport
behavior, highlighting the need for further characterization before reliable modeling can be per-
formed.
The results confirm acceptable hygrothermal performance under the tested conditions. The single-
88

==PAGE:105==
leaf wall with exterior wood fiber insulation remained free of interstitial condensation throughout
the year, even at the minimum ventilation rate. This outcome is attributed to the wall’s external
insulation, which maintains warm internal temperatures and prevents dew point conditions. Ad-
ditionally, the diffusion-open character of all materials supports steady vapor transport and avoids
pressure buildup, effects that are directly captured by the Glaser method.
In contrast, the cavity wall exhibited localized condensation during winter at a ventilation rate of
0.3 l/(s · m2), with moisture accumulation occurring at the interface between the clay outer leaf
and the wood fiber insulation. This location corresponds to a sudden increase in vapor resistance
and a local temperature drop, due to the absence of exterior insulation. These conditions together
form a vapor trap under cold, humid conditions. Despite this, moisture levels remained low and
were fully dissipated from May through October, indicating adequate drying capacity. However,
whether this constitutes safe or recommended practice requires further investigation using more
advanced methods and simulation tools. When the ventilation rate was increased to 0.5 l/(s · m2),
condensation was completely avoided, highlighting the sensitivity of vapor control to cavity air
exchange rates.
Although moisture buffering was not captured in the simulation, the hygroscopic nature of unfired
clay and wood fiber may offer additional safety in practice by absorbing and re-releasing mois-
ture under changing conditions. Furthermore, the Glaser method does not account for capillary
liquid transport or transient climate variations, both of which may enhance drying potential and
reduce condensation risk. Conversely, the technique also overlooks critical effects such as moisture,
rainwater ingress, and long-term material degradation, which could compromise moisture perform-
ance in real-world conditions. Therefore, while the results offer valuable insights, they should be
interpreted with caution and supplemented by more advanced hygrothermal simulations where
necessary.
The findings are consistent with BR18 moisture control principles and demonstrate the viability
of diffusion-open wall systems when paired with appropriate detailing and ventilation strategies.
However, they also underscore the need for more advanced modeling and further research to estab-
lish robust design approaches across varying wall types, material systems, and climatic conditions.
Overall, the results from the hygrothermal analysis support the use of vapor-open, unfired clay
masonry without vapor barriers in Nordic climates, provided that:
• Exterior rain protection is ensured through appropriate facade detailing (e.g., roof overhangs
or cladding),
• Cavity ventilation is adequately designed to prevent moisture accumulation,
• Construction avoids materials or layers that create abrupt vapor resistance jumps without
sufficient drying potential,
• Surface finishes remain vapor-open to maintain drying potential,
• Interior humidity is managed with proper ventilation,
• Materials used at vulnerable interfaces (e.g., base, openings) are moisture-tolerant, and
construction moisture is minimized and allowed to dry out before closure or occupancy.

Life Cycle Impact: The Path to Carbon-Negative Con-
struction
The Life Cycle impact, as conducted using Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs), provided
compelling evidence of clay’s enhanced environmental performance, as seen in Chapter 9. The find-
ings that unfired clay wall assemblies can achieve a net negative global warming potential (GWP),
as illustrated in Figure 9.1 and Figure 9.2, highlight their potential as a key strategy for decar-
bonizing the construction industry. Although the choice of primary structural material primarily
drives these differences, the wall’s total GWP reflects the contribution of every component. In
the clay-based design, bio-based elements — such as wood-fiber insulation and timber cladding —
offer minimal embodied carbon and, in some cases, negative net values. For instance, the wood
fiber insulation used across several assemblies consistently contributed a negative GWP during its
production phase (Modules A1-A3), actively reducing the overall environmental burden. There-
fore, a comprehensive evaluation of all components is crucial: Combining a low-carbon core with
low-carbon add-ons can make the system more environmentally beneficial. Consequently, pairing
a low-carbon core with high-carbon ancillary products can undermine the system’s environmental
advantage.
These net-negative results, which indicate that the wall functions as a net carbon sink, are mainly
achieved because the significant carbon benefits from the material components during the produc-
tion phase (Modules A1-A3) and end-of-life reuse (Module D) outweigh the emissions from other
life cycle stages. This is also illustrated in the life cycle contribution graph (Figure 9.3), where
the system based on unfired clay brick achieves this performance through a combination of low
production emissions and the benefits of reuse and recycling at the end of life.
This result highlights the sensitivity of the Life Cycle Impact outcomes to the assumptions and the
available EPDs. When comparing two versions of a similar ”Cavity Wall - Unfired Clay Bricks,” the
model using the Clay Tec EPD yields a total GWP of -53.59 kg CO2 eq./m2, while the model using
the DTU EPD gives a result of -1.93 kg CO2 eq./m2. This difference is due to the assumptions
made for the end-of-life scenario (Module D) in the source of EPDs, as detailed in the Appendix.
Even so, it is clear that the environmental benefits of unfired clay brick solutions surpass those of
traditional methods, even the best-performing traditional masonry wall assembly, which carries a
burden of + 5.70 kg CO2 eq./m2 (Figure 9.2).
While these findings highlight the environmental potential of clays, it’s essential to interpret them
within the study’s methodology. A fundamental assumption is the Reference Service Life (RSL) of
materials. For instance, Clay Tec bricks are assigned a theoretical RSL of 100 years based on their
EPD, which significantly influences their calculated annualized impact. However, this material-
level durability does not guarantee a 100-year lifespan in practice.
Architectural renovations,
changes in building standards, or damage often result in components being replaced long before
their material RSL is reached. The environmental advantages of a long-lasting material, whether
in the main structure or its additional components, can only be fully realized if it remains in use.
Similarly, minor components were excluded from the analysis for the sake of simplification. For
example, steel wall ties were not considered. This omission may influence the results by giving
the clay cavity wall assemblies a more favorable performance than would be expected in a fully
detailed real-world scenario.
More significantly, the comparison was based on a uniform wall
thickness for all wall assemblies(clay walls and traditional walls). This approach enables a direct
environmental comparison but does not reflect an optimized design where thickness would be
tailored to each material’s specific properties. Therefore, to fully understand the true benefit of
clay, future research should consider fully optimized, functionally equivalent wall systems, which
provide a more definitive picture of performance in realistic construction scenarios.

Weather Protection Strategies
Ensuring effective moisture protection is critical when designing unfired clay masonry in Nordic
climates. Without adequate protection, prolonged exposure to wind-driven rain and freeze–thaw
cycles can lead to surface erosion and structural degradation over time. In addition, a lack of
protection compromises the wall’s hygrothermal performance, as a wet facade, regardless of the
facade solution, will hinder both the drying of internal moisture and its outward diffusion. Passive
and active strategies should therefore be combined to achieve robust and durable performance.
Among the surface treatments evaluated, lime-stabilized plasters and bio-compatible hydrophobic
coatings currently appear most feasible. Lime-based systems are vapor-permeable and relatively
low-impact, but their freeze–thaw durability remains sensitive to the type of clay, formulation, and
substrate compatibility. Although some unfired clay bricks have demonstrated improved mechan-
ical properties after limited freeze–thaw cycling, the broader consensus suggests that frost resist-
ance remains highly dependent on avoiding near-saturation conditions. Natural treatments such
as tadelakt, and polymer-enhanced coatings like MTMS, have demonstrated promising water re-
pellency while maintaining breathability. Yet, their performance under cold and humid conditions,
such as in Copenhagen, requires further field validation. High-performance synthetic coatings of-
fer reliable water protection but raise concerns regarding aging, reversibility, and environmental
impact. As no single material fully satisfies all performance, durability, and sustainability criteria,
the selection of surface treatments must be considered in relation to the overall design strategy.
In this context, architectural detailing becomes a key complementary strategy. Roof overhangs, for
instance, have been shown to substantially reduce wind-driven rain exposure, particularly in the
upper zones of the facade. Based on findings in the literature, a 1.0 m overhang above the exposed
clay facade and a 0.6 m overhang above the wood-clad variant was selected as protective measures
in the case design presented in this thesis. These values are expected to significantly reduce rain
loads; however, they remain estimates and should be verified through site-specific analysis or field
testing. While these measures cannot replace surface treatments, they effectively reduce moisture
stress and can support the use of more vapor-open or environmentally friendly coatings.
Importantly, these strategies involve trade-offs. Deeper overhangs and raised foundations, while
improving moisture protection, increase material use and embodied carbon. From a sustainability
perspective, it is therefore not sufficient to evaluate coatings or design features in isolation. A
balanced approach must consider the total environmental impact and functional performance of
the wall system as a whole. For instance, a more durable and hydrophobic coating might justify
simpler detailing, whereas minimal or reversible coatings may require more substantial protective
architecture to compensate.
In practice, the implementation of such strategies must also consider constructability, detailing
complexity, and maintenance over time, factors that may influence the choice between traditional
plasters, engineered coatings, or architectural overhangs.
Ultimately, the design of weather protection systems for earthen buildings in Nordic contexts must
be guided by an integrated assessment of material behavior, environmental exposure, and life-cycle
impact. Further research, particularly building on the findings and design explorations presen-
ted in this thesis, is needed to validate material performance under real climatic conditions, refine
simulation models, and develop context-specific design guidelines for weather-resilient earthen con-
struction in Nordic settings.

Future Research
This thesis highlights significant knowledge gaps that limit the broader use of clay as a structural
material in Nordic architecture. The next section presents specific research opportunities aimed
at improving clay’s performance, durability, and practical application in cold, wet climates.
1. Hygrothermal Performance & Long-Term Durability
The preliminary moisture risk assessment in this study is insufficient for the complex dynam-
ics of clay. Future work should aim to establish a more robust understanding of long-term
moisture behavior and its effects.
• Experimental Monitoring: Construct a full-scale test wall in a Nordic climate,
equipped with sensors to monitor temperature, humidity, and moisture content over
several years. This will provide invaluable real-world data on seasonal wetting/drying
cycles and the efficacy of protection strategies.
• Advanced Hygrothermal Modeling: Use data from experimental monitoring to
validate and refine advanced, non-steady-state hygrothermal models, moving beyond
conservative design calculations.
• Freeze-Thaw Durability: Conduct comprehensive freeze-thaw cycle testing on loc-
ally sourced Danish clays, incorporating various fibers (e.g., straw, hemp) and additives
to enhance resilience in Nordic conditions.
2. Material Science, Structural Validation, and Standardization
The performance of earthen materials is highly dependent on local properties and construc-
tion methods. Research is needed to standardize materials, validate structural performance,
and optimize mixes for Nordic climates.
• Material and Mix Optimization: Focus on optimizing clay mixes (grain-size, min-
eral composition, compaction) and developing breathable plasters or hydrophobic coat-
ings to enhance workability, crack resistance, and durability.
• Structural Performance Validation: Conduct full-scale structural tests (buck-
ling, flexure, shear) under Nordic climate conditions to validate Eurocode-based design
methodologies for unfired clay structures.
• Component Behavior: Investigate clay mortar cohesion and friction under wet/dry
cycles to better understand shear/sliding resistance.
Also, assess tie interaction in
composite cavity walls to optimize stiffness and robustness.
• Engineering Standardization: Define characteristic material properties and partial
safety factors specifically for 3D-printed and molded unfired clay, calibrated for Nordic
climatic conditions.
3. Scalability, Economic Analysis, and Circular Economy
Broader adoption requires improved economic viability, streamlined construction, and integ-
ration into a circular economy.
• Prefabrication and Scalability: Explore prefabrication workflows for unfired brick
and 3D-printed systems to increase scale, improve quality control, and reduce costs
compared to on-site construction.
• Economic & Environmental Benchmarking: Conduct a comparative life-cycle
cost analysis against conventional materials, factoring in thermal performance, main-
tenance, and embodied energy. Develop third-party verified Danish EPDs for products
made from local surplus soils.
• Supply Chain Innovation: Investigate policy and business models to transition
surplus clay from a ”waste” stream to a certified building material.
This includes
developing standards for salvaged materials and logistics for a circular economy model.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the potential of unfired clay as a structural and functional material
in Nordic tiny house construction.
Through a theoretical case study, it explored three wall
configurations—single-leaf brick, 3D-printed solid clay, and a clay cavity wall—and evaluated their
structural feasibility, thermal and hygrothermal performance, and environmental impact under
relevant climatic and regulatory constraints.
The research questions defined in the introduction are answered as follows:
1. How viable is unfired clay as a structural and functional material for sustainable tiny house
construction in Nordic climates, considering its material performance, environmental impact, and
cost-efficiency?
The results confirm that both unfired clay masonry — constructed entirely with earth bricks and
clay mortar — and 3D-printed earth structures can be viable structural solutions in Nordic cli-
mates, provided that design adaptations account for moisture sensitivity, slenderness, and material
variability. For the 3D-printed and cavity wall configurations, wall thickness was primarily gov-
erned by buckling resistance.
In contrast, the single-leaf wall was governed by shear capacity,
primarily due to the low cohesion and limited friction at the clay mortar–concrete foundation
interface.
In terms of building physics, dynamic thermal calculations confirmed that thick, unfired clay walls
can offer significant thermal mass, showing the potential to improve indoor comfort and energy
usage through reduced temperature fluctuations. Hygrothermal calculations further demonstrated
that vapor-open walls have the potential to perform safely in Nordic climates, provided that ventil-
ation and weather protection strategies, such as sufficient overhangs and diffusion-open facades, are
implemented. However, sensitivity to moisture accumulation in cavity designs remains a limitation
that must be addressed through detailed design and effective air exchange strategies.
From an environmental perspective, a life cycle analysis has shown that unfired clay walls, partic-
ularly those constructed with air-dried bricks or 3D-printed using local soil, can achieve a negative
net global warming potential when biogenic carbon storage and end-of-life benefits are considered.
Compared to traditional masonry or aerated concrete systems, these alternatives offer substantial
carbon savings.
The cost-effectiveness of unfired clay construction is highly context-dependent. Using site-local
or surplus soil can significantly reduce material and disposal costs. However, this benefit may be
offset by the labor-intensive nature of certain construction methods. In contrast, unfired clay brick
masonry tends to be more economical, as it aligns with existing skills and workflows. Ultimately,
cost efficiency depends on the chosen technique, availability of local resources, and the skill level
of the workforce.
To provide scientifically grounded and risk-aware design guidance, it is recommended to use an
external cladding system when employing unfired clay or earth as a load-bearing wall material. Al-
94
Investigation of Clay as a Structural Material in Nordic Building Design
==PAGE:111==
though protective strategies such as roof overhangs and water-repellent treatments show potential,
the long-term durability of exposed earth surfaces under Nordic climatic conditions remains insuf-
ficiently documented. Until further empirical data become available, enclosing the structural clay
elements within a protective and ventilated facade offers a robust and code-compatible solution for
both vertical and horizontal load-bearing applications.
2. What are the main design strategies, challenges, and regulatory aspects of incorporating unfired
clay in modern Nordic building practices? How can its use be optimized to achieve a balance of
sustainability, functionality, and compliance?
The study identified several key design strategies to balance functionality and durability. These
include using exterior insulation for hygrothermal safety, utilizing thermal mass as a contributor
to the internal environment, architectural detailing and designing for mechanical robustness. The
interaction between roof overhangs and daylight revealed a trade-off: while overhangs enhance dur-
ability, they must be balanced with window size to ensure adequate interior illumination. Regulat-
ory challenges remain, as unfired clay is not directly addressed in the Danish Building Regulations
(BR18) or the Eurocodes. However, by adopting conservative assumptions and methods based
on EN 1996-1-1 and supported by expert interviews, this thesis demonstrated a feasible pathway
toward code-aligned design.
Based on the findings of this thesis, unfired clay bricks are currently recommended over emer-
ging technologies such as 3D printing. Their compatibility with established construction prac-
tices—requiring minimal retraining or specialized equipment—and the more reliable base of ma-
terial data make them a more practical choice for near-term implementation. Until further em-
pirical validation and standardized design guidance become available, enclosing structural clay
elements within a protective, ventilated facade remains the most robust and code-compatible ap-
proach for balancing sustainability with long-term durability. With further research and technical
standardization, 3D printing may offer a viable path toward increased geometric flexibility, process
automation, and optimized material use in earth-based construction.
In conclusion, unfired clay shows significant potential as a low-carbon, thermally beneficial, and
structurally viable material in Nordic environments, provided it is supported by careful engineering,
thoughtful architectural detailing, and suitable regulatory adjustments. More research and field
validation are required to confirm long-term performance and facilitate wider adoption.
