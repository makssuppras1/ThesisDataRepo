Abstract
This study investigated the optimal conditions in pretreatment of landfill leachate with poly
aluminium chloride (PAC) and calcium hydroxide as coagulants. Different dosages of the
coagulant and various operation conditions were investigated and the results show that
PAC1 (Alumichem) has the highest removal rate on Total organic carbon (TOC), UV254,
and Turbidity as common pollutants in landfill leachate.
A lab­scale foam fractionation setup was built for optimizing the co­surfactant type, dosage
on PFAS removal. As a result, all six PFAS species detected reached maximum removal
rate with cationic co­surfactant addition after 15 minutes of foaming with 1mL/min co­
surfactant dosage. PFPeA experienced the lowest removal with sodium dodecyl sulfate
(SDS) as co­surfactant, the removal rate is 24.46% after 15 minutes of foaming.
Ion exchange resin as another typical separation technology for PFAS removal, was also
assessed with its capacity of PFAS removal. All the PFAS species were removed with >
300 mg (10 mg/mL) ion exchange resin dosage. This study optimized the pretreatment
process for landfill leachate and enhanced my understanding on PFAS removal technolo­
gies.

1
Introduction
1.1
Landfill leachate
Landfill leachate is a liquid with significant toxicity and irritant properties that is generated
as a result of the combined influence of natural precipitation and microbiological processes
[1]. Landfill leachate poses a significant environmental concern since it contributes to the
contamination of both groundwater and surface water resources. Hence, it is impera­
tive to appropriately treat the leachate. Landfill leachate contains significant quantities
of both suspended/colloidal and dissolved substances and large amounts of colloids and
suspended particles disturb the efficiency of pollutant removal in post­treatment [2].
1.2
Coagulation/flocculation
Coagulation has found extensive application in the treatment of wastewater across various
industries, including but not limited to oil treatment, tailings management, textile process­
ing, aquaculture, and municipal wastewater treatment [3]. Coagulation and flocculation,
are extensively employed in the United Kingdom, Germany, and several European na­
tions for the purpose of pre­treatment or comprehensive treatment of landfill leachate.
The aforementioned techniques facilitate the extraction of non­oxidizing humic and ful­
vic acids from the leachates, along with other targeted persistent pollutants [4, 5] Lippi
et al. (2020) stated that the main advantage of this treatment is its high effectiveness in
removing organic matter, suspended solids [6].
1.3
Per­ and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) and its
presence in landfill leachate
Per­ and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) in the environment have garnered significant
attention due to their potential adverse toxicological effects and high resistance to treat­
ment systems [7, 8, 9, 10]. PFAS are synthetic compounds that have been extensively
utilized in various industrial and consumer applications across the globe, including the
production of paper, leather, textile coatings, and firefighting foams [11]. The exposure
of PFAS in various ecosystems, including water, air, sediment, and organisms, has been
consistently widespread due to historical deficiencies in treatment methods [12]. Cur­
rently, PFAS has been detected in several environmental compartments such as water,

sediment, animals, and crops. Moreover, urban water sources have been found to be
contaminated by PFAS to varying degrees. [13]. The primary sources of PFAS pollutants
have been shown as originating from the discharge of firefighting and industrial wastew­
ater, as well as leaching from landfills [14, 15].
According to Solo­Gabriele et al. (2020), the levels of PFAS found in landfill leachate tend
to be significantly elevated compared to other sources of pollution [5]. This is caused by
the accumulation of PFAS in landfill and leachate’s complexity. Various species of PFAS
were found in landfill leachate including short­chain and long­chain PFAS [16].
1.4
Foam fractionation
There are two broad categories of technologies treating PFAS in water: separation and
destruction. Foam fractionation is a representative separation technology and it is well­
established for the removal of hydrophobic molecules from a liquid solution using rising
columns of foam [17]. The foam will be collected and form a low volume wastewater with
high contaminant concentration. it has been reported in both lab­scale and pilot­scale
studies that Foam fractionation is efficient in PFAS removal [18, 19, 20]. Many PFAS
species are anionic, thus cationic co­surfactants can enhance the adsorption of PFAS
to air­water surfaces in foam fractionation [21]. Anionic co­surfactants although are also
anions, can increase the foams formed in the process thus increasing the efficiency of
foam fractionation. In this study, both co­surfactants were analyzed.
1.5
Ion exchange
Ion exchange (IX) is also a separation technology for PFAS removal. In recent discourse,
IX has garnered attention as am advanced adsorbent for the purpose of PFAS removal.
This is primarily attributed to the regenerative properties of IX, its enhanced efficacy in
eliminating short­chain PFAS, and its overall superior adsorption capacity [22].
1.6
Scope
Scope of this study is to find an optimized pretreatment process for PFAS removal in
leachate as well as to investigate the mechanics and efficiency of foam fractionation
and ion exchange processes as two post­treatment processes to remove PFAS in landfill
leachate
In this study, Coagulation is employed as the pre­treatment process. 3 types of coagulants
under different dosages and pH were tested for the removal of common pollutants in
==PAGE:11==
leachate. Then the PFAS removal efficiency is investigated with foam fractionation and
ion exchange technics.

2.3
Experimental set­up and Methodologies
In this study, three types of experiments/tests are conducted, including coagulation (Jar
test), Foam fractionation (Batch experiment), and Ion exchange.
2.3.1
Coagulation
Jar tests were carried out to reduce the concentration of suspended solids, organic matter,
and certain dissolved contaminants for further treatment processes. The experimental
setup of jar tests is shown in Figure 2.1. It is assembled with six propellers and a 220
V variable speed controller (Shin Myung Servo CO., Ltd, South Korea). Six 1 L beakers
were placed under the propellers and 6 L of raw leachate is treated during each jar test.
The prescribed dose of coagulant was added to each jar by using a pipette after adjusting
pH. Then the speed controller was turned on for a 30­second rapid mixing with 1000 rpm.
After that, flocculant (polymer) was added to each jar if needed and the mixing speed was
decreased to 200 rpm for 5 minutes. 1 hour of sedimentation time was needed for all the
large flocs to settle down at the bottom of the jars. Samples for TOC, UV254, and turbidity
measurements were collected using 5 mL needle syringes. Samples for post­treatment
processes were collected by repeating the jar test with the optimized operation condition
12 times. 800 mL of settled water was separated into a plastic bucket from each jar and
9.6 L pre­treated leachate was collected in total.
4 sets of jar tests were carried out to assess the impact of pH, coagulant dosage, and
performance of both PACs. the dosage ranged from 0.1 mL/L to 0.6 mL/L with a 0.1 mL/L
interval, pH 8 (raw pH) and pH 7 (optimized pH condition) were tested.
In preliminary experiments, the amount of flocs produced and undissolved Ca(OH)2 was
observed with a pH range from 7­14 and Ca(OH)2 dosage from 0.05 g/L to 1 g/L. Few flocs
were produced when pH < 8 while a lot of undissolved Ca(OH)2 was present with dosage
==PAGE:14==
> 0.6 g/L. A lot of flocs started to form at pH=12. Thus, Ca(OH)2 dosage is determined
from 0.05 g/L to 0.6 g/L, and pH=8 (raw leachate pH) and pH=12 were tested.

2.3.2
Foam fractionation
The setup for Foam fractionation batch experiments is modified from the same equipment
used in the thesis of Mónika Büki [23]. It features a column made of polymethyl methacry­
late, which was adjusted by attaching an additional tube to allow the foam produced to
bypass. The column’s dimensions are 46.9 cm in height with a 5.5 cm internal diameter.
500 mL of landfill leachate is under treatment during the experiments. The column was
affixed to a laboratory stand, facilitating the introduction of gas and co­surfactants from
the bottom. Both exits were sealed with a check valve to prevent water from flowing back.
A ball valve is connected to the column to release treated water during and after each
experiment, preventing mixing with the foam above. Co­surfactants were introduced con­
tinuously using a peristaltic pump (BT1002J, Longer pump, flow range of 0.0002 mL/min
to 380 mL/min). The pump’s calibration and details are presented in Table A.1 in the Ap­
pendix. Gas flow was quantified by a Brooks Instrument flowmeter (flow range of 1 to 5
L/min), connected to the lab air tap within the fume hood. A stainless­steel bubble stone
was utilized for the even dispersion of bubbles, generating bubbles with an approximate
diameter of 1 mm. A sample port at the bottom of the column was used to extract 10 mL
of solution at predetermined intervals of time. A schematic of the experimental setup is
illustrated in Figure 2.2.
==PAGE:15==
Figure 2.2: Foam fractionation setup
Before each experiment, the column, and the connecting pipes were washed thoroughly
with DI water for 4 times. As the foam produced by the raw leachate itself is not enough
to reach the collection point of the setup, selected co­surfactants need to be added during
the experiments. The flow rate of co­surfactant was controlled by the peristaltic pump and
three different dosages were tested, at 0.5 mL/min, 0.75 mL/min, and 1 mL/min. Airflow
is constant at 4 L/min and monitored by a flowmeter. Duration of each experiment is 15
minutes and 10 mL samples were extracted from the sampling port at 1 min, 5 min, 10
min, and 15 min.

Ion exchange

50mg, 100 mg, 300 mg, 500 mg, 1000 mg, 2000 mg, 2500 mg. Mixing time of 24 hours
with 100 rpm rotation. This rotation speed was determined by observing that the resin
was mixed well with pre­treated leachate.

than SDS and long­chain PFAS can be effectively removed by foam fractionation with
co­surfactant addition. PFPeA has the lowest removal among all species.

Ion exchange
The PFAS removal with ion exchange resin is significant. All the compounds reached
maximum removal rate with 300 mg/higher resin dosage. Thus, the data of removal rate
with dosage more than 300 mg was not presented in Figure A.1
Figure 3.7: PFAS removal data with different ion exchange resin dosage

influence factor on PFAS removal is the adsorption capacity of ion exchange resin. All
PFAS species showed a maximum removal with 10 mg/mL resin dosage.

4
Conclusion
In the present study, the efficiency of coagulation on common pollutants removal was
assessed. A series of foam fractionation experiments were carried out to determine the
performance of two different co­surfactants in PFAS removal. The PFAS removal by ion
exchange resin was investigated. The results can be concluded that:
• PAC1 has a better removal of the pollutants in landfill leachate than PAC2. only
36.07% of TOC was removed with PAC2 at pH7 while 60.23% of TOC was removed
with PAC1 addition at pH7.
• Calcium hydroxide can only remove turbidity at a low dosage level (0.05 g/L to 0.02
g/L). The removal efficiency of other pollutants are insignificant
• In foam fractionation, the removal rate of all PFAS species reached maximum with
1 mL/L cationic co­surfactant dosage, while only long­chain PFAS (PFOA, PFOS)
were fully removed with anionic co­surfactant addition (SDS).
• The removal rate of PFBS, PFHxAm PFPeA ith 1 mL/min SDS addition after 15
minutes of foaming were 63.81%, 86.7%, and 24.46%, respectively, which are all
short­chain PFAS, indicating that cationic co­surfactants have a better performance
on removal of short­chain PFAS compared to anionic co­surfactants.
• All PFAS were removed and reached maximum removal rate with ion exchange resin
addition > 300 mg (10 mg/mL). The removal rate is high even with a relatively low
ion exchange resin dosage. All PFAS species experienced >80% removal with only
20 mg (0.67 mg/mL) except for PFPeA. A relatively low removal of this specie can
be also observed from the results of foam fractionation experiments. Only 24.46%
of PFPeA was removed with 1 mL/min SDS dosage after 15 minutes of foaming.
