Abstract
This thesis investigates how high voltage offshore cable manufacturers can facilitate a
transparent End-of-Life decision making for decommissioning high voltage offshore ca-
bles. The findings can contribute to tackling resource scarcity and reducing waste by
promoting End-of-Life strategies.
Using Design Research Methodology and the ready2LOOP platform, the thesis aims to
develop and evaluate a tailored support for the case company, NKT, which is a high
voltage offshore cable manufacturer. A systematic literature review and insights from
the ready2LOOP process identified key challenges and enabling factors influencing the
adoption of End-of-Life strategies for high voltage offshore cables. Additionally, findings
throughout the thesis indicate that the complete decommissioning of certain wind farms,
including HV offshore cables, has already been technically carried out, showing that it is
possible. However, the literature lacks a connection between technical feasibility, prof-
itability, and environmental considerations for HV offshore cable decommissioning.
As a result, the process led to a support development. The developed support is a config-
urator, designed to help high voltage offshore cable manufacturers facilitate a transparent
End-of-Life decision making for their clients. The configurator brings together economic
and environmental aspects in a clear and structured interface that is designed to sup-
port consistent and transparent choices. This is done by a profit assessment, a CO2e
screening, and a material flow overview.
Additional research could focus on how specific data points can influence the financial
and environmental outcomes and thereby the decision on an End-of-Life strategy. For
the theoretical research, it is recommended to increase the research on environmental
impacts such as the effect on biodiversity which could be included in the support.
Looking ahead, the broader adoption of supports like this configurator is crucial for ad-
vancing circular economy approaches in the HV offshore cable and offshore energy in-
dustries. This will help actively address the growing problem of resource scarcity.

Introduction
Our society is increasingly confronted with the problem of resource scarcity. Critical raw
materials that are essential in everyday life, have been exploited in recent years making
their extraction progressively more complex. We must take action to ensure a more re-
sponsible material use to secure that the demand for these materials can be met in the
future (European Commission, 2023). Circular economy (CE) is considered a vital model
for tackling resource scarcity and reducing waste by promoting strategies that redefine
production and consumption systems (Kravchenko et al., 2020). To pursue a more sus-
tainable future, industries across the globe need to rethink their business model on how
to transition from a linear economy to a CE.
One of them is the offshore energy industry, specifically the high voltage (HV) offshore
cables industry, which relies heavily on scarce materials like copper. Despite the growing
focus on sustainability, strategies for managing these assets at the end of their opera-
tional life remain underdeveloped. In the coming years, more and more offshore energy
infrastructure will reach its End-of-Life (EOL)1 (Topham & McMillan, 2017). The common
practice in the industry is to leave the cable after the end of operational life on the seabed,
left in-situ2 without recovery or further use (Topham & McMillan, 2017).
The offshore energy landscape is complex, not only in terms of infrastructure handling, but
also in terms of ownership. HV offshore cables are not typically owned by manufacturing
companies, but by offshore wind farm owners, such as transmission system operators or
private companies. This adds more complexity to the EOL decisions, as responsibilities
and incentives can differ across the value chain.
This thesis seeks to address these challenges by developing and evaluating a support that
creates transparency on the topic of decommissioning HV offshore cables by facilitating
EOL decision making.

5.2.2
Circular Economy
This section explores the available literature on the concept of CE and the closed loop
economy system. It should provide a precise and consistent definition of CE.
Definition
The concept of CE has been formed since the late 1970s with an increase in popularity in
recent years (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). The focus shifted to how natural resources are
intertwined with the economy, serving as inputs for production and consumption, as well
as outputs in the form of waste. When viewing the earth as a circular and closed system
with restricted capacity, the importance strikes that the environment and the economy
should coexist in balance (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017).
The traditional and widely present linear economy, follows the extract-produce-use-dump
principle, representing an open-ended resource flow (Korhonen et al., 2018). In contrast,
the CE can be defined as “an industrial economy that is restorative or regenerative by
intention and design” (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019). Resources should be used
through numerous phases while always striving to keep their highest value and utility. This
aims for a closed loop flow of resources in the economic system, limiting input, emissions,
and waste (Korhonen et al., 2018).
System
Different approaches for a closed loop economy system have been developed, among
others by the Ellen McArthur Foundation who visualizes the continuous flow of resources
in the so-called butterfly diagram, see Figure 5.2.

==PAGE:28==
Figure 5.2: Butterfly diagram (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2021)
The butterfly diagram can be split into a technical cycle on the right-hand side and a bi-
ological cycle on the left-hand side of the diagram. The technical cycle targets products
that are used rather than consumed, while the biological cycle encompasses the loop of
biodegradable materials like food. It should be noted that the cycles can intertwine as
certain biodegradable materials like wood can switch from the technical to the biologi-
cal cycle at EOL (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2021). This thesis is positioned on the
technical cycle, as it focuses on cable solutions.
Four different loops can be observed on the technical cycle of the diagram. Sharing
presents the innermost circle of the technical cycle. It targets an increase in the intensity
of product utilization through combined usage. Examples of sharing models are libraries
or car sharing systems. The next loop is maintaining, which focuses on extending the
usable life of products to maximize value. This can be done among others through repair
services. Moving to the loop reusing and redistributing. Similarly to the other two mea-
sures, these measures keep the products in their original condition and for their intended
use while shifting the ownership or the intended market. Examples can be reusable pack-
aging or the second-hand clothing market. The refurbishing loop restores the value of the
product through replacement, updates, improvements, or repairs. If the product needs
more intensive work to remain circular, re-manufacturing is done through re-engineering
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
15
==PAGE:29==
which aims for an as-new condition. Recycling presents the outermost loop. It is applied
as the final option and transforms the products materials in the cycle to new materials,
avoiding waste (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022a). In the literature, additional CE ap-
proaches are discussed, which are not present in the butterfly diagram. One approach is
repurposing, which alternates the use of a product (Blomsma et al., 2019).
The smaller the loop the more value can be preserved as a greater share of the original
value of the product can be retained by keeping it complete. As a result, the inner loops
of the diagram such as sharing or maintaining should be prioritized. The outermost loop,
recycling, should come last in a CE as the product is broken down into its base materials
reducing its value and the time and energy invested in the past is lost. These loops
often reduce cost savings for both customers and companies, as they utilize products
and materials that are already in circulation instead of creating new ones (Ellen MacArthur
Foundation, 2022a).
The best circularity measure for a product depends on the context but should be con-
sidered in the design of products. With 80% of environmental impacts determined in the
design stage, the product’s design becomes critical in enabling product and material cir-
cularity (Tan et al., 2024). For example, shared and reused items should be designed
for durability as they are exposed to intensive usage. Products can be designed for easy
repair or for modularity to replace and easily remanufacture components. Design for re-
cyclability aims for effortless separation of materials. It should be noted that a product
can also be designed for multiple loops (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022a).
The concept of CE can be connected to different levels of the economic system (Kirchherr
et al., 2017):
• Micro level - Consumers and businesses
• Meso level - Industries and regions
• Macro level - Nations and global context
Thereby, the overarching goal for all levels is to strive for sustainable development to
achieve economic prosperity, social equity, and environmental quality for present and
future generations (Kirchherr et al., 2017). This thesis focuses on the micro level by
assessing the CE efforts and possibilities of a company in the HV offshore cable industry.

5.2.3
Circular Economy Regulations
This section explores the literature on current CE legislation for the industry. To guaran-
tee coherence between the environment, the climate, and the industry when adopting CE
principle, different regulatory bodies worldwide have developed CE regulations (Mhatre
et al., 2021). This research focuses on the measures taken by the EU, as Europe was
determined as the geographical focus for this thesis. To note the EU is generally acknowl-
edged as a global leader in the development of CE policy (Ellen MacArthur Foundation,
2022b).
In 2020 a new EU Circular Economy Action Plan (CEAP) was adopted by the European
Commission, replacing its predecessor from 2015. It is part of the European Green Deal,
which aims to create sustainable growth. The transition towards a CE should limit the
loss of biodiversity, minimize the impact on natural resources, and is a necessary step to
achieve the EU climate neutrality target in 2050 (European Commission, 2025b).
The focus of the new CEAP is on the complete life cycle of products. This includes the
design of products, promotion of CE processes, calls for sustainable consumption and
waste prevention as well as longevity of resources in the EU economy. To achieve that,
35 different initiatives were listed and a Circular Economy Monitoring Framework, being
revised in 2023, is in place to monitor the progress (European Commission, 2025b). Due
to extensive resource usage and high potential for circularity there will be a focus on
the following sectors: Electronics & information communication technology, packaging,
plastics, batteries & vehicles, construction & buildings, textiles, food, water, and nutrients.
NKT’s HV cables are not part of the scope, but can potentially be part of it in the future
(European Commission, 2025b).
One of the CEAP measures is a Sustainable Product Policy Framework to support com-
panies in designing sustainable products. Part of that is the Ecodesign for Sustainable
Products Regulation, which entered into force in July 2024. It sets Ecodesign require-
ments for all physical goods sold in the EU with exception only for feed, medicine, and
food. The EU Commission adopted a first work plan in April 2025 for the products that
will be prioritized in the next years, working plans for others will follow after. HV cables
are not included in the prioritized products.

Another initiative of the Sustainable Product Policy Framework is a digital product pass-

It was further announced in February 2025 that a circular economy act will be established

5.2.4
Circular Economy in the Cable Industry
This section explores the literature on CE, key challenges, and opportunities to achieve
circularity in the cable industry. The cable industry, including companies such as NKT,
plays a crucial role in the economy and the stability of power infrastructure. It is directly
involved in ensuring the livelihoods of people and developing the economy, since it covers
the basic guarantee for the normal operation in today’s society (Zhao et al., 2024).
Beyond power infrastructure, cables are essential in households, powering a wide range
of electrical and electronic devices such as vacuum cleaners, washing machines, refrig-
erators, mobile phones, computers, and televisions. With over 20 kg of electrical and
electronic products introduced to the European market per person annually, the grow-
ing demand for cables underscores the need for sustainable solutions that align with CE
principles (EEA, 2020).
A key focus of CE literature on cables is waste management, which aims for a system
where waste is eliminated. When products cannot be reused, recycling becomes the
most viable solution. Therefore, most of the research on cables concentrates on the last
loop, recycling, in the butterfly diagram, see Figure 5.2.
Cable recycling technologies vary, but they all share a common goal. They separate the
metal core, such as copper, from the plastic sheath, which together form the cable com-
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
18
==PAGE:32==
posite (Blinová & Godovčin, 2021). The two main types of cable recycling are mechanical
and chemical recycling (Wedrychowicz et al., 2023).
Mechanical recycling has been the dominant method for cable recycling (Wedrychowicz
et al., 2023). Research highlights that the technology is simple, cost-effective, has lower
material losses, and offers significant adaptability (Wedrychowicz et al., 2023). The pro-
cess primarily involves cable stripping and crushing. Copper, being theoretically a 100%
recyclable, retains its quality and functionality through repeated recycling cycles (Blinová
& Godovčin, 2021). One specific mechanical recycling method is Cryogenic Comminu-
tion, which can be applied to power cables and flow lines similar in composition to HV
cables. This method enables cost-effective material recovery by cooling, fracturing, and
separating the product into constituent materials. However, this method is not yet on an
industrial scale (Oluwoye & Mathew, 2023).
However, mechanical recycling also has its limitations. It is energy intensive and gener-
ates significant dust and noise pollution (Wedrychowicz et al., 2023). Additionally, larger
cable diameters complicate the separation process as more collision power is necessary
to crush the sheathing, which is particularly relevant for HV cables compared to low volt-
age cables (Lu et al., 2023).
There is limited availability in the literature on the chemical recycling method. The litera-
ture indicates that chemical technology requires the use of a large amount of solvents or
leaching solutions, which need to be disposed of after the recycling process (Wedrychow-
icz et al., 2023). Chemical recycling is therefore not the preferred value recovery solution.
Despite advancement in recycling technologies, several challenges hinder the transition
towards a CE for HV cables. The literature shows that recycling facilities have limitations
on an industrial scale, as some material recycling technologies remain at the laboratory
stage, requiring further research and development (R&D) (Lu et al., 2023).
One of the main barriers to investing in recycling facilities is economic viability. In the case
of solar photovoltaic cables, calculations estimate that global photovoltaic waste will reach

Reuse and Repurpose of subsea cables
This section explores key findings on the enablers and challenges associated with the
reuse and repurposing of power cables. This area is relatively unexplored, as the SLR
identified only two papers proposing solutions. Therefore, the discussion is limited to the
findings of these papers. Given the increasing number of wind farms that are approach-
ing the end of their operational life, repurposing existing infrastructure offers a potential
strategy to reduce decommissioning costs (Magdy & Mahmoud, 2021).
Magdy and Mahmoud (2021) suggest that power cables used by the oil and gas indus-
try could be modified for use in offshore wind farms. This approach enables a delayed
decommissioning of the cables and reduces the capital costs associated with wind farm
development. The size of power cables for oil and gas is typically 16 mm2 and 32 mm2,
whereas current HV cables for wind farms are 50 mm2. The current HV cables therefore
have a higher capacity than the power cables for oil and gas. The authors mention that
existing oil and gas cables could be modified to meet the required capacity to bridge this
gap, however, it is not addressed how the modification can be done. This highlights the
need for further research to explore viable modification techniques and ensure compati-
bility with current and upcoming wind farm requirements.
To investigate if oil and gas power cables could be considered to be repurposed for wind
farms a process is suggested. Firstly, existing cables should be assessed to determine
whether the cables can be usefully repurposed, by assessing the performance of the
cable. Magdy and Mahmoud (2021) propose an early qualification method to evaluate
existing cables. This method assesses their performance and feasibility for repurposing
by comparing their characteristics with those of new cables. According to their findings, if
an existing cable retains less than 50% of the performance of a new cable, it is not a practi-
cal alternative. However, if it can maintain 75–90% or more of the expected performance,
then it is a good alternative to a new cable. The paper does not disclose a recommenda-
tion for a cable performance between 50–75%. The performance is assessed based on
several parameters such as physical capacity, age, cost efficiency, and length (Magdy &
Mahmoud, 2021). This methodology provides an indication that reusing is possible under
certain conditions.

Decommissioning of subsea cables
When the repurposing of subsea cables is not feasible, decommissioning becomes a
necessary step to allow for further treatment or disposal. This section outlines the insights
from the literature, highlighting both the main challenges and an identified enabler related
to the decommissioning of HV offshore cables.
Small et al. (2015) discuss the feasibility of decommissioning subsea installations such
as pipelines and cables. They address the physical load, which is the physical forces
acting on the installation, as one of the challenges that need to be considered for decom-
missioning. The load to recover the installations increases due to different environmental
factors such as marine growth or changes in the soil of the seabed. Also, geotechnical
effects such as the development of a negative pore pressure cause the soil to contract
and grip the installation more tightly. These effects increase the technical requirements
on the construction vessel when removing the cables. On top of that, Small et al. (2015)
argue that the clay surrounding the installation, can harden up due to the weight of the
installation. Thereby, the decommissioning is impeded even further.
Looking from an environmental perspective, Sheehan et al. (2020) are also discussing
challenges with respect to decommissioning offshore cables. It is argued that decommis-
sioning is leading to the destruction of the developed habitat on the cable installation. In
that case, they are suggesting partial decommissioning, as the best possible strategy.
Thereby, the newly built up habitat can be kept intact. At the same time they are stressing

full scale. Nevertheless, the device has not been used by the offshore industry and needs
to be further evaluated to be suitable for a market implementation (Martinelli et al., 2023).

Other enablers of recycling described by Wedrychowicz et al. (2023) are the upgrading of
recycling processes by utilizing AI and automation. To overcome obstacles proposed by
the workforce, the process can be improved through the introduction of robots which would
increase efficiency and profitability. As well as optimize recovery and minimizes hazards
for workers and reduce labour time and cost. There are robots that enable precise material
grouping with a success of approximately 96%. The implementation of AI and automation
tools are relatively new in the industry. Another upcoming recycling method is the use of
microorganisms that feed on the plastic, leading to mineralization (Wedrychowicz et al.,
2023).
The papers by Wedrychowicz et al. (2023) and Oluwoye and Mathew (2023) indicate that
recycling methods for HV offshore cables are available. The new mechanical recycling
Cryogenic Comminution is not scaled up yet, but offers promising potential to decrease the
operational expenditures (OPEX) of subsea cable recycling. Further AI and automation
technologies could potentially increase efficiency and decrease cost in the future, making
recycling even more attractive. To achieve a closed recycling loop, the full recycle ability
of plastics needs to be considered, by utilizing chemical recycling or microorganisms as
presented prior.
Conclusion
The SLR highlighted both enablers and challenges related to EOL for HV offshore cables.
In general, a key challenge identified is the relative immaturity of CE approaches for the
EOL of HV offshore cables. Approaches targeting reuse, repurpose, decommissioning,
and recycling are explored but often with limited industrial scale solutions in place. Out
of the mentioned strategies recycling is the most common and scaled up one. Moving
forward literature on recycling could be explored further to receive a more comprehensive
insight into the area.
Additional enablers of EOL are the following:
• Old offshore cables can be integrated into new wind farm developments, which could
lower the LCOE and CAPEX needed (Magdy & Mahmoud, 2021).
• Seabed environment can be protected by with specific installation techniques (Shee-
han et al., 2020).

• Development of new technology that can enable the decommissioning of HV off-
shore cables going forward (Martinelli et al., 2023).
• Mechanical recycling is available on an industrial scale (Wedrychowicz et al., 2023).
• Cryogenic comminution is a new offshore cable recycling method that has a poten-
tially low OPEX (Oluwoye & Mathew, 2023).
• Chemical methods and microorganisms offer possibilities for the complete recycling
of plastics (Wedrychowicz et al., 2023).
• AI and automation developments in recycling processes can increase efficiency and
profitability (Wedrychowicz et al., 2023).
An area not covered in the reviewed papers is the reuse and repurposing of decommis-
sioned cables that have been retrieved from the seabed. The potential of using HV off-
shore cables in urban infrastructure planning could be explored. For instance, to support
the growing demand for electric vehicle charging networks.
Through the identified papers the following challenges can be summarized:
• Assessment methods for reused offshore cables remain in an early and immature
stage (Magdy & Mahmoud, 2021).
• Risk of destroying the epibenthic species habitats created in the cable installation
by decommissioning (Sheehan et al., 2020).
• The environment the cables are installed in sets high technological requirements on
decommissioning (Small et al., 2015).
• No industry standards and regulation for EOL for HV offshore cables are given. This
may be one of the reasons that CE approaches for EOL are not on an industrial scale
(Small et al., 2015).
• New recycling methods could face challenges in being scaled up and profitable (Olu-
woye & Mathew, 2023; Wedrychowicz et al., 2023).

The first decision point of an HV offshore cable at EOL is the current performance of the
cable. Depending on the performance, the cable can either be kept in the seabed to aim
for reuse and repurpose, or decommissioning can be considered. If the performance of
the cable is high enough and a new wind farm with the same specifications is installed in
the area, the cable can be reused or if the specifications of the new wind farm are different
a modification of the cable can be considered (Magdy & Mahmoud, 2021). If no new
wind farms are planned to be installed in the same area or the cable performance is low,
the decommissioning of the cable can be considered. Depending on the environmental
impacts and technical opportunities, the cable can be partly or fully decommissioned and
then recycled. If decommissioning is not possible, the cable will be left in-situ (Sheehan
et al., 2020; Small et al., 2015). In the case of an installation of a cable through rock
armouring, left in-situ, it can serve as an environmental protection measure for human
interventions (Sheehan et al., 2020). If full or partial decommissioning is possible it should
be recycled afterwards (Oluwoye & Mathew, 2023; Wedrychowicz et al., 2023). However,
none of these decisions are covered by official legislation (Small et al., 2015).

In 2016 the first offshore wind energy project, Yttre Stengrund located in Sweden, was
successfully decommissioned. The wind farm had a rated power of 10 MW, with five wind
turbines. Rated power is the highest electrical output the wind farm can produce under
optimal conditions. The farm was in operation for 14 years which is less than the expected
life time for wind farms. Refurbishment was considered, but due to high financial costs
and technical limits neglected (Topham & McMillan, 2017).
Another example of a completely decommissioned wind farm is Vindeby in Denmark with
a rated power of 4,95 MW and consisted of 11 wind turbines. It is the first offshore wind
farm that was ever installed and it reached 25 operating years. The wind farm was de-
commissioned in 2017, with the owners deciding against refurbishment as there was no
business case to replace the wind turbine gears. Pulling the cables out of the seabed
posed no challenges and the process went smoothly (Power Technology, 2017).
The most recent known successful decommissioning project in Europe is the Blyth Off-
shore wind farm and was completed in 2019. It included two wind turbines with a total
rated power of 4 MW. The service lifetime was 19 years from 2000 to 2019. It is the first
offshore wind farm that was commissioned and decommissioned in the United Kingdom.
In total 1,4 km of cables were removed as part of the project. The cable decommissioning
started with the disconnection, termination and discharge of any residual current. After-
wards a vessel dragged the cable on board. Depending on the topography through which
the cable ran, different decommissioning methods were applied that led to a complete
recovery (Fugro, 2025).
It can be concluded that decommissioning wind farms is an immature field, as not many
farms have reached their EOL life yet. There are still three examples in Europe as in
Yttre Stengrund, Vindeby, and the Blyth offshore wind farm which can serve as pioneers
for future decommissioning programs. In the three mentioned examples above the cable
decommissioning was part of the project, even though the life time is significantly longer
with 40 instead of 20-25 years. As wind farm sites differ, the decommissioning process will
be different based on conditions such as available equipment, weather, and soil conditions
(Topham & McMillan, 2017).

Profitability Breakeven
In order to assess how much cable needs to be decommissioned to generate profit, a
breakeven assessment is made. The objective of this analysis is to identify the point at
which total costs are equal to total revenue as a function of cable length. This intersection
indicates the breakeven point.
To carry out the assessment, both fixed and variable costs were identified. The fixed
cost for the breakeven includes the cost for the feasibility study, the permit for decom-
missioning, the vessel rate, and the project management hours. The variable cost for the
breakeven includes all of the other cost components, as well as the entire contingency
budget.
The methodology used to construct the breakeven chart is detailed in Appendix A.6. This
provides a quick understanding of the minimum cable length required for the project to be
economically viable.
Limitations
The profitability assessment considers the identified revenue streams, cost components,
and a breakeven assessment. However, methodological limitations should be considered
when assessing the results.
General limitations:
• Case specific: The logic applied in the assessment of both the stages transporta-
tion to waste treatment and the waste treatment itself is based on NKT’s current
waste management practices. It has not been verified whether this setup is repre-
sentative for the industry. As such, the model assumes NKT’s approach as a case
example, which may limit the objectivity of the findings.
Cost assessment limitations:
• Vessel use: The number of vessel days used is based on a static input number
rather than being dynamically linked to the length of cable to be decommissioned. In
practice, the required vessel time will likely scale with the volume of cable removed.
• Project management hours: Similarly, the estimated project management hours
are static and not dependent on the amount of cable being decommissioned. This
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
61
==PAGE:75==
may affect the accuracy of cost estimates for larger or smaller decommissioning
scopes, which the contingency budget could potentially account for.
Revenue assessment limitations:
• LME value steel: The steel price is not based on the LME price. It already includes
a deduction on the material quality decrease through recycling. This means for
steel a double deduction is made, which results in a lower revenue than the actual
revenue would be.
• Avoided costs: The current assessment does not account for potential avoided
costs. However, there may be potential revenue gains from decommissioning an HV
offshore cable. These could be from avoided cost for the preparation of being left
in-situ which would occur to secure a cable from the environment. Another avoided
cost could potentially be the Polluter Pays Principle1 in which the cable manufacturer
would need to pay for leaving the cable in-situ. However, this topic needs to be
researched further to understand the applicability for HV offshore cable in the future.
• Potential rewards: Other revenues could come from potential governmental sub-
sidies for decommissioning in the future. This revenue also needs further research
if it is applicable to the case of HV offshore decommissioning.
Breakeven limitations:
• Contingency budget: In the breakeven assessment, the contingency budget is
treated as a variable cost. However, in practice, the contingency applies to both fixed
and variable costs. This means that if fixed costs increase, the total contingency
should also increase. A limitation of the current approach is that the contingency
budget is not split between fixed and variable costs, which may affect the accuracy
of the breakeven calculation.

7.5.3
Environmental Assessment
Besides the economic assessment, the environmental impact of the full decommissioning
EOL scenario is assessed. It aligns with the must-have requirement, see Section 7.1.
The environmental assessment is split into two parts. First, it considers the material flow,
followed by a carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) assessment. The reasons for choosing
those environmental factors are described in the following.
As this thesis focuses on CE measures, it is imperative to consider the material flow in
the environmental assessment. The flow should provide transparency on how much of
the material could be circulated when it is decommissioned. It compares the total amount
of material input and the total amount of material recycled.
In addition, the CO2e footprint of decommissioning is calculated, since the cables are built
from a large number of materials and consist of scarce metals. It is stated that 60% of
global greenhouse gas emissions can be assigned to material resource extraction and
processing activities (International Resource Panel, 2024). The materials that have the
highest CO2e footprint are metals with 7,8% of the total global emissions (Intergovernmen-
tal Panel on Climate Change, 2023). Through circular strategies, the virgin raw material
demand can be lowered and support for emission reduction is given. By recycling, the
carbon-intensive mining of new material or production of products can be limited (Wang
et al., 2022). As an example, the recycling of aluminium reduces the energy footprint of
virgin manufactured aluminium by 95% (Material Economics, 2018). As much valuable
material would be neglected when left in-situ, an opportunity to avoid emissions would
be missed. The environmental assessment is therefore including the CO2e footprint of
decommissioning of HV offshore cables.
It was considered to further include biodiversity factors in the assessment, due to the
decommissioning impact on the seabed and species (Sheehan et al., 2020). However,
it is disregarded for this assessment due to a lack of information and data on the topic.
The following section will outline the logic used to assess the material flow, as well as the
CO2e footprint.

investigation of how to allocate the emissions could be considered.

• Vessel use without cable on board: The distance that the vessel travels before

and applying predefined constraints to produce a specific output (Hvam et al., 2008).
The configurator developed in this thesis functions similarly to that. It has a user inter-
face to collect input, a database with embedded calculations that represent the system’s
constraints, and an output displayed on a dashboard.

logic of the assessments on profitability and environmental impact. It was created through

Concept Use
Two primary roles have been identified for operating the configurator. These are a support
user who will solely work with the user interface and a support owner who will additionally
to the user interface also have access to the background databases and calculations.
The user of the configurator will be a Tender or Project Manager. The role of the user
is to communicate the results within the tender process or in the early stage of a project
with the client. This user group and its role was identified throughout discussions with
NKT. Due to the fact that a cable manufacturer like NKT does not have ownership of the
HV offshore cables after the sale, they also do not have the decision power on the EOL
strategy. Therefore, the point of influence for NKT is during the tender process or in the
early stage of a project where decisions on the EOL by the clients are made. During
this phase, the clients apply for cable laying permits where they need to disclose what
happens at EOL. NKT can provide the results of the configurator to show the economic
and environmental impacts and thereby possibly promote decommissioning at EOL.
In contrast, the support owner is represented by one designated person. The support
owner should have a comprehensive understanding of CE and the overall company pro-
cesses. This can be for example a Sustainability Specialist on the group level. The sup-
port owner’s task is to maintain the support with relevant updates and support the use of
the support.

7.7
Concept Limitation
The developed configurator has the purpose of facilitating decision making for EOL of
HV offshore cables. Through the development and validation process, certain limitations
remain. Specific limitations for both the economic and environmental assessment are
displayed in Sections 7.5.2 and 7.5.3. Additional limitations for the configurator are the
following:
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
80
==PAGE:94==
Support application
• Decision making: The configurator does not provide a recommendation if decom-
missioning is favoured. The support serves as a source of knowledge to facilitate
future decisions.
• EOL scenario: The configurator focuses on full decommissioning. The user has
the option to assess partial decommissioning only through specific data entry in the
cable input panel. No explanation is given in the support guides on how to assess
partial decommissioning in the configurator.
• Industry: The configurator is developed in collaboration with NKT. For other compa-
nies in the industry, it may have to be adapted depending on the cable and company
specifications.
Content
• Cable specifications: The configurator is developed for newer cables, such as
XLPE cables. Materials found in older cables installed in the past, which contain for
example paper and oil are not included in the assessment.
• Breakeven: No breakeven diagram is presented for the CO2e assessment. No
breakeven point will ever be achieved since every emission created and potentially
reduced is directly related to cable and decommissioning specifications which are
variable.
• Feasibility: Technical feasibility, sea conditions, and burial depth are considered in
the feasibility study of the cost assessment. However, the impacts could be further
specified and explored in the support.
• Transportation to waste facility: The transport from the port to the waste treatment
facility was assumed to be made by truck, with a capacity to carry 16 tonnes of load.
No further specification on the vehicle or fuel was considered. Specific fuel types or
other characteristics could have an impact on the emissions and profit.
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
81
==PAGE:95==
Ecoinvent limitations
• Ecoinvent regions: In the selection of the Ecoinvent process the geographic lo-
cation was considered. If possible, processes for Europe (RER) or Europe without
Switzerland were selected in alignment with the assessment scope. European pro-
cesses provided a higher accuracy, as they align with the geographical scope of the
thesis. If not available, a Global (GLO) or Rest-of-World (RoW) process was cho-
sen. The GLO processes indicate the average global production of emissions for
the specific activity. The RoW processes differ from the GLO ones, as they indicate
the additional availability of non-global datasets. (Ecoinvent Support, 2025).
• Ecoinvent process types: For the assessment the most accurate Ecoinvent pro-
cesses were selected, there might still be limitations due to the limited number of
processes available. When selecting Ecoinvent processes, it can be distinguished
between unit and market processes. Market processes are chosen when specific
supplier data is not known. Those market processes represent average decom-
missioning services or materials based on regions. If specifications are available,
unit processes are selected (SimaPro, 2025). For all the processes in the assess-
ment, except waste treatment processes, market processes were chosen from the
Ecoinvent database (ecoQuery, 2025). This was done because for waste treatment
processes, specifications are available. Changing the process would impact the
EFs, decreasing or increasing the total numbers.
• Transportation during waste treatment: There are inconsistencies in the Ecoin-
vent processes for waste treatment regarding the inclusion of additional transporta-
tion during waste treatment. Potentially, not all transport emissions are accounted
for.
Future developments should aim to tackle the listed limitations. To identify further limi-
tations and capture the value of the developed configurator, an evaluation was planned
with the user, owner, and key stakeholders.

Currently, this phase often involves a wide range of stakeholders and is complex. This

calculations only to the dashboard. The business expert had access to the calculation

The user and owner would be more confident in the results when they could input accurate

the future, the configurator could also be extended to include HV onshore cables, which
may be simpler to remove, particularly where they can be pulled through existing pipe

done on a fly-by basis to significantly reduce the cost of the vessel. For example, if a
vessel is transiting between two locations and has a 10-day availability window, a decom-
missioning task on the way could be scheduled during that period.
Another suggestion from the business expert is to apply a reverse assessment approach.
In this approach, the total costs for transport and waste treatment, along with expected
revenue, are calculated. Based on this the remaining budget available for cable uptake is
determined. If the uptake cannot be carried out within this budget, then the project should
be reconsidered.

General
The general process of this thesis was efficient and well organized. This is mainly due to

This thesis followed the process of the DRM which provided a well structured path. The

Key Findings
The following section discusses the overall findings of the DRM stages. Specific reflec-

One of the key findings is that the perception of NKT’s employees on circularity and the
company’s CE readiness differ. When conducting the ready2LOOP readiness assess-
ment, the score changed significantly with the incorporation of two compared to three
NKT employee assessments. An internal alignment on circularity is important to find fo-

and especially interactions with stakeholders, it was observed that the same debate is
now present in the wind farm industry with no common agreement yet. This underscores
the need for future investigations on this topic to facilitate a transparent decision making
on EOL.
In Descriptive Study I, the feasibility of decommissioning has been explored in the wind
farm industry. From a technical perspective, it has been shown that decommissioning of
HV offshore cables is possible as three wind farms in Europe, including their cables, have
been successfully decommissioned (Fugro, 2025; Power Technology, 2017; Topham &
McMillan, 2017). In the following years, when many wind farms reach their EOL, it will
be decisive whether decommissioning will be the standard practice (Topham & McMillan,
2017). Environmental and financial factors, as well as site-specific conditions, could be
crucial for project specific decisions. For example, there is a risk of destroying habitats
for species when decommissioning, which would lead to a biodiversity loss (Sheehan
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
101
==PAGE:115==
et al., 2020). However, as discussed in the SLR, new decommissioning techniques are
explored, which could have less impact on the environment and are less cost intensive
(Martinelli et al., 2023). If technical developments like this are applicable in the industry,
they might influence the EOL decision towards decommissioning.
A key finding of the Descriptive Study I is that recycling HV offshore cables is the most
common practice described in the literature for CE approaches on EOL. After the cable
is decommissioned, the majority of the cable material could be recycled (Wedrychowicz
et al., 2023). Recycling practices for almost all materials are already in place on an in-
dustry scale today based on HV offshore cable manufacture insights. In recent years,
certain types of plastics that were not commercially viable for recycling as mentioned
in the SLR, have become possible through chemical methods (NKT Cables Group A/S,
2025b; Wedrychowicz et al., 2023). Recycling promises great potential. Exploring the
possibilities of setting up logistic processes and volume possibilities with waste treatment
partnerships is key to moving forward.
Other CE approaches for EOL explored in the literature review are reuse and repurpose.
The reuse of HV offshore cables is in an early and immature stage and has not been
explored in practice yet. Similarly, repurposing should be explored further as it could offer
financial benefits, which would make it attractive for the industry (Magdy & Mahmoud,
2021; Sheehan et al., 2020). In the next step, the feasibility and potential implementation
of reuse and repurposing cables should be observed.
For all CE approaches for EOL explored in the literature, recycling, repurposing, and
reusing, it is important to notice that NKT and other cable manufacturers do not have
ownership of the cables, but their clients. Therefore, NKT has only limited influence on
the final decision making. They can only provide transparency to clients, in which the
developed configurator plays a key role. It is crucial to understand what is needed to
influence the client’s decision making to increase the control in this step.

9.3
Key Contributions
The following section discusses how the thesis contributes both to scientific research and
to the HV offshore cable manufacturing industry. For the industry-specific contribution,
scenario testing has been conducted to identify potential recommendations and practical

9.3.1
Scientific
One of the main scientific contributions is addressing research gaps in the existing litera-
ture. The literature showed that decommissioning is feasible from a technical standpoint
(Topham & McMillan, 2017) but is currently not examining environmental and financial
factors combined to show the full picture of a decommissioning project. This thesis pro-

general need to take action on sustainability to tackle resource scarcity and reduce waste
(Kravchenko et al., 2020). Altogether, the developed support offers the possibility to ex-
amine the feasibility of CE approaches in the HV offshore cable manufacturing industry.
Furthermore, the thesis identifies and integrates key economic and environmental fac-
tors relevant to EOL assessments in the HV offshore cable industry. These insights can
support future academic work aimed at developing policy guidance and identifying critical
factors in decommissioning processes.
9.3.2
Industry
In addition to scientific contributions, the configurator also has an impact on the industry.
The main contribution that the configurator offers the industry is a support for decision
making. As many windfarms reach their EOL in the next years, the decision in favour of
or against decommissioning becomes increasingly urgent. The developed support can
provide insights and transparency to the industry on both environmental and economic
factors of decommissioning an HV offshore cable. It can serve as a starting point for con-
versations in the windfarm industry towards cable EOL. By considering EOL, HV offshore
cable manufacturers are placing themselves as a proactive stakeholder in the discussion.
Further, the support can be used to initiate a pilot project on decommissioning with a client.

EOL can also be influenced by technical factors which can have an effect on the feasibility
of lifting the cable. These technical factors could include seabed conditions, depth of the
seabed, and decommissioning equipment. This could be integrated into the configurator
going forward. On top of that, biodiversity factors could also be included in the future.
As highlighted in the SLR, decommissioning can impact local species due to habitat de-
struction during cable removal (Sheehan et al., 2020). Finally, a regulatory perspective
on decommissioning legislation could be integrated into the support. It might be relevant
in the future to display an overview of the legislation. It can be included and helps to
understand what impact legislation might have.
9.5
Thesis Limitations
Several limitations should be considered when discussing the results of this thesis. This
section discusses the most significant ones that should be taken into account when re-
flecting on and utilizing the findings.
First, while the SLR provided a solid foundation for understanding the current possibilities
of EOL in the industry, the limited amount of research in this field reflects uncertainties.

108
==PAGE:122==
Many CE approaches for EOL are immature and not on an industry scale today, which
limits the availability of validated procedures.
Second, the thesis did not incorporate specific data from the value chain. For example,
involving waste treatment partners could have increased the support’s accuracy by using
specific data on recycling rates. Company data from NKT has not been used either due
to confidentiality. Instead, several assumptions were made, potentially decreasing the
accuracy of the output. Therefore, internal and external validation is recommended before
using the support in practice.
Third, while the thesis was developed in close collaboration with NKT and thereby pro-
vided many company specific insights, the developed support may not be directly trans-
ferable to the other cable manufacturers. An example of this could be differences in waste
treatment methods or variations in cable composition. When adapting the findings and
the support to other companies, the accuracy should be validated.
Fourth, the support was developed by external researchers and not by internal stakehold-
ers at NKT. This may affect the level of engagement and adoption of the configurator, as
it can lead to a reduced sense of responsibility among internal stakeholders. In contrast,
internally developed support could involve closer collaboration within the company, which
often fosters stronger buy-in and smoother implementation. To mitigate this risk, the re-
searchers were regularly present in the company and had ongoing dialogues with internal
stakeholders. The incorporation of ready2LOOP has tried to establish internal buy-in by
including stakeholders throughout the entire process and keeping them informed.
Finally, the geographical focus of this thesis is Europe. Potentially, practices can differ
in other areas of the world and might limit the applicability of the thesis outcome for the
whole industry.

empirical, strategic developments for NKT, and theoretical research possibilities.
Potential empirical research could be to explore NKT’s and other cable manufacturers’ po-

Conclusion
This study investigated how HV offshore cable manufacturers can facilitate a transpar-
ent EOL decision making. To strengthen the case company’s circularity efforts, the EOL
strategy decommissioning of HV offshore cables remains a crucial step. Through this,
highly valuable materials can be recovered, generating revenue and potentially avoiding
emissions from raw materials sourcing.
The Research Clarification revealed that limited literature on the HV offshore cable indus-
try towards circularity is available. However, an increase in legislation promoting circu-
larity in general and in the EEE sector can be observed. It was further identified that the
company’s CE readiness is in alignment with other component manufacturers.
To increase the readiness, CE dimensions were prioritized which led to the Descriptive
Study I exploring key challenges and enabling factors influencing the adoption of CE ap-
proaches for the EOL of HV offshore cables. One of the biggest key challenges identified
is the relative immaturity of CE approaches for the EOL of HV offshore cables. Most
approaches such as reuse or repurposing are superficially explored in literature but with
limited industrial scale solutions in place. Still, it was revealed that recycling methods of
HV cables are available on an industry scale and decommissioning is technically feasible
being the dominant enabling factor influencing the adoption of CE approaches for EOL.
The results of Descriptive Study I resulted in scoping the thesis to focus on decommission-
ing and recycling at EOL. It led to the development of a support which was conducted in
the Prescriptive Study. The aim of the support is to enhance transparency and circularity
efforts for high voltage offshore cable manufacturers based on profitability and environ-
mental impact. The support is a configurator that uses manual input data and gives an
output from an economic and environmental point of view on the EOL strategy decommis-
sioning for HV offshore cable. Specifically, it models scenarios where recycling is used
as the treatment method once the cables are recovered from the seabed.
In the Descriptive Study II evaluations of this configurator were conducted with internal
stakeholders at NKT. The evaluation demonstrated that the configurator offers a solid
Facilitating transparent End-of-Life decision making for high voltage offshore cables
112
==PAGE:126==
foundation for enhancing the usability and trustworthiness of EOL decision making pro-
cesses. It can support more informed, transparent, and collaborative discussions between
manufacturers and clients, ultimately contributing to more sustainable and economically
sound outcomes.
The results show that the configurator has the potential to significantly impact NKT and
the HV offshore cable industry in general by facilitating transparent decision making for
the EOL. The configurator helps address the complexity of decommissioning by creating
a transparent assessment of how the decommissioning of an HV offshore cable impacts
the economy as well as the environment. This not only helps to provide clarity of the
implications but also to understand what is possible going forward.
Looking ahead for the HV offshore cable industry and the offshore energy industry, the
broader adoption of supports like this configurator is crucial for advancing CE approaches
and actively working toward addressing the problem of resource scarcity.

A.14
Appendix - Aluminium Conversion
The following appendix presents the conversion from copper to aluminium to enable a
comparison of the economic and environmental impacts in the scenario testing. The data
used is from public avalible sources. To calculate the amount of aluminium required to
achieve the same electrical conductivity as a cable containing 35,5 kg of copper per meter,
the following equation is used:
WeightAl[kg/m]
= WeightCu[kg/m] · (DensityAl[kg/m3]/DensityCu[kg/m3]) · (1/Conductivityfactor)
(A.1)
Where:
• WeightAl, is the weight of aluminium per meter required to achieve the same elec-
trical conductivity as a cable containing 35,5 kg of copper per meter.
• WeightCu, is 35,5 kg of copper per meter.
• DensityAl, is the density of aluminium. It is assumed to be 2,710 kg/m3 (Thyssenkrupp
Materials, 2023).
• DensityCu, is the density of copper. It is assumed to be 8,960 kg/m3 (Boyi Technol-
ogy, 2024).
• Conductivity factor, is 0,61, which means that aluminium has 61 percent of the
conductivity of copper (Anixter, 2025).
Equation A.2 is used to calculate the amount of aluminium required in a cable to achieve
the same electrical conductivity as a cable containing 35,5 kg of copper per meter:
35, 5[kg/m] · (2, 710[kg/m3]/8, 960[kg/m3]) · (1/0, 61) = 17, 6[kg/m]
(A.2)
Thus, to replace 35.5 kg/m of copper, 17.6 kg/m of aluminium is required to maintain the
same electrical conductivity in the cable.
