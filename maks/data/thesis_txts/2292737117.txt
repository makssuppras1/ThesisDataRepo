Preface
This master’s thesis has been prepared at the Department of Applied Mathematics
and Computer Science, Technical University of Denmark (DTU Compute) and marks
the end of my pursuit of the Master of Science degree within Mathematical Modeling
and Computing. The thesis represents a workload of 35 ECTS, carried out during
the fall of 2014 and winter of 2015.
The main task of the thesis was to apply mathematical analyses to neurophysiolog-
ical data sets to draw inference about neurodynamics. It was, therefore, necessary
to properly understand the data and apply suitable mathematical techniques. Con-
sequently, the project work was supervised by both Associate Professor Line Katrine
Harder Clemmensen (DTU Compute) and Professor Per Roland (Department of Neu-
roscience and Pharmacology, University of Copenhagen).

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Scientists have shown how the eye translates light photons into electrical impulses
which travel through the optical nerve to the visual cortex. The physical organization
of the primary visual cortex in ferrets have been shown by diﬀerent techniques. It
has been shown how neurons in diﬀerent visual areas are connected as well as how
neurons in these areas connect to diﬀerent cortical layers.
These physical connections imply that activity of these neurons aﬀect each other
to some degree.
By measuring neuron activity through the cortical layers on the
borders between the ferret’s visual areas 17/18 and 19/21, the goal of this thesis is
to investigate how the dynamics of these neurons aﬀect each other.
In order to do that we need to be able to characterize the neurodynamics, thus allowing
us to determine whether or not a neuron, or an ensemble of neurons, is reacting to a
stimulus or simply expressing spontaneous activity. A key task to accomplish this is
to be able to analyze dynamics of single trials with high temporal resolution.
We therefore need to develop a set of methods to characterize neuronal activity, before
diving into investigating correlations between neurons in diﬀerent areas and layers.
We will go through some of the tools classically used in the community but also
introduce lesser used frameworks, which have shown to have convincing abilities.
Initially, however, a basic description of the physiological and mathematical back-
ground, and experimental setup will be carried out.
1.1
Structure of the Thesis
The thesis is structured in the following order:
Chapter 2 This chapter is an introduction to the physiology needed to fully understand
the data and goal of the analysis in the following chapters. It will describe the
basic functionality of neurons and the visual cortex.
Chapter 3 The chapter introduces the most important mathematical concepts used during
the analyses.

2
1 Introduction
Chapter 4 Describes the experimental setup and terminology. Here, diﬀerent variations of
the data will be plotted and give the reader a feel of the nature of the data, as
well as the challenges with analyzing it.
Chapter 5 In this chapter, the post-stimulus time histograms will be analyzed, and models
will be built based on cross-correlations between areas of interest.
Chapter 6 This chapter proposes a method which, contrary to the ones in the previous
chapter, allows analysis of single trials by analyzing peak spike rates.
Chapter 7 This chapter will discuss diﬀerent ways to apply matrix decomposition methods
to the data. Pros and cons of diﬀerent approaches will be discussed, and the
neurodynamics will be analyzed qualitatively.
Chapter 8 Based on the results from the previous chapter, principal component analysis
is applied to neurons within same cortical layers. This framework will be used
to investigate the neurodynamics both qualitatively and quantitatively.
Chapter 9 The previous chapters all have either comprised temporal resolution or failed to
analyze single trials. In this chapter, a method for analyzing individual action
potentials in single trials is proposed, based on the insights on neurodynamics
from previous analyses.
Chapter 10 This chapter will summarize the results from the previous chapters and propose
tasks and analyses for future work on the topic.

CHAPTER 2
Physiological
Background
This chapter will brieﬂy describe the physiological background of the visual system.
Some of the following will be general and some will be speciﬁc to the ferret, since
the data used during the thesis come from ferrets. We will see how light photons are
translated to electrical impulses and how these impulses travel through the axons of
the neurons as well as between neurons. The functionality of the visual will then be
described before a short discussion of the current theories on how neurons process
information.
2.1
Neurons and Electricity
In the following, the basic composition of neurons will be described as well as the
ability of neurons to carry electrical signals and transfer them to other neurons.
2.1.1
Basic Physiology of the Neuron
Figure 2.1 shows an illustration of the basic structure of a neuron.
Basically the
neuron consists of a cell body and an axon. A number of dendrites emanate from the
cell body and branch out and become thinner and thinner.
Also emanating from the cell body is the axon. The neuron only has a single axon,
unlike its several dendrites. The axon can extend long distances and thus connect
areas which lay far from each other. Sodium (Na+) and potassium (K+) channels are
placed along the axon. These channels allow sodium and potassium ions to ﬂow in
and out of the cell. The channels are voltage gated such that ions cannot ﬂow freely,
sustaining a voltage potential between the inside and outside of the membrane. As
illustrated in Figure 2.3, the axon is covered by myelin sheaths only exposing the cell
membrane to the outer environment at short distances at a time. As a result, the
ﬂow of sodium and potassium between the axon and the environment is limited to
these exposed areas, denoted nodes of Ranvier.
Telodendria branch out at the end of the axon. At the telondendria, synaptic ter-

2.1.2
Action Potentials
As mentioned in Section 2.1.1, there is an electrical potential between the inside and
the outside of the neuron, which is called the membrane potential. When the neuron
is at rest, the membrane potential is kept at approximately -70mV by a sodium-
potassium pump which keeps sodium outside the cell and potassium inside the cell.
The sodium channels along the axon allows sodium ions, Na+, to ﬂow if the membrane
potential reaches a certain level. When the membrane potential reaches this threshold
level, sodium channels open and allow an inﬂow of sodium ions causing a depolariza-
tion and consequently a positive membrane potential. At some point the potassium
channels open and allow an outﬂow of potassium ions, K+, and the sodium channels
close. This repolarizes the cell and decreases the membrane potential. Before the
potassium channels close, a hyperpolarization occurs due to a too large potassium

This cycle of ion-ﬂow is called an action potential. During the period of the action

Because of the fact that the membrane potential increases drastically during an action
potential, and the action potential duration is relatively short, it is often referred to
as a spike.
2.1.3
Electrical Flow Within and Between Neurons
From the previous sections we have covered the main factors involved in how neurons
are able to receive signals from neurons in one area, carry them over long distances,
and pass them on to other neurons.
When a neuron receives neurotransmitters in the dendrites it changes the chemical
composition. Excitatory neurotransmitters increase the probability of an action po-
tential, whereas inhibitory neurotransmitters decrease the probability of an action
potential. Some neurons release excitatory neurotransmitters and other release in-

2.3
Theories on Neural Coding
We now have a basic understanding the physical properties of the visual system and
interaction between neurons. However, we have no detailed knowledge on the link
between stimuli and spiking patterns, or neural coding.
In general there are two
main theories of how neurons process information.
The general thoughts behind
these theories will be described in the following sections.
2.3.1
Rate Coding
The idea behind rate coding is that information about the stimulus is reﬂected by
the spiking activity of the neuron and that the times at which the neuron is spiking
is not important. In 1926, Adrian and Zotterman [Adr15] showed that there was a
relationship between the intensity of the stimulus and the spiking frequency. Since
then, researchers have used spiking frequency to investigate the nature of cortical
sensory neurons, such as those found in the visual cortex.
A typical measure related to the rate coding theory, is to average the number of
spikes over a relatively large time period, since the timings are not important. As a
consequence, reaction times would be slow, since the information is coded by averaging
over long time periods. From behavioral studies it is possible to measure reaction
times, e.g. from humans reacting to an image. Such studies suggest that the typical
time period used for averaging is too long and would result in slower reaction times
than are measured in behavioral studies.
In order to obtain a ﬁner temporal resolution one can average the spiking events over
trials, thus giving an average spiking rate over trials in ﬁne temporal resolution. In
this thesis we will use the term Post-Stimulus Histogram (PSTH) for such averages.
The PSTH does in fact show changing spike rates in 1ms resolution when applied to
our data, see Figure 4.7. It is clear that the brain does not work by averaging over
trials and therefore estimating the spike rate in single trials must be done. It turns
out that relying on spike rate to carry information poses several challanges which will
be discussed in Section 4.2.5.
2.3.2
Temporal Coding
In temporal coding, the amount of spiking is not important as such.
Rather the
patterns at which the spiking appear. Many attempts on temporal coding has been
done. It has been showed that many mathematical calculations would be possible by
designing circuits of diﬀerent kinds of neurons, since the existence of excitatory and
inhibitory neurons makes it possible to create logical gates, similar to those modern
electronics are composed of.
Such circuits are fully deterministic and need to be
governed by a global clock.

In statistical modeling it is important to be able to validate one’s results. This is
done by building models on subsets of the data and testing their performance on

other portions of one’s entire data set. This process is known as cross validation and
is done to ensure that the one does not build models that overﬁt and/or to test the
predictive powers of one’s models. Overﬁtting is a term for having built a model that
is so complex that it ﬁts noise particular to the data set used to build it. Therefore,
cross validation is important either if the aim is to draw inference based on estimated
parameters or to be able to do predictions based on future observations.
Depending on the amount of data available, there are diﬀerent methods to do this
[Sha11]. When the data is supervised and the model should be used for classiﬁcation
or prediction, a commonly recommended approach is to randomly split the data into
three subsets:
Training set is used for data exploration and modeling. This is used to understand the data
and propose diﬀerent models to capture the structure. This should comprise of
app. 50% of the entire data set.
Validation set is used to validate the proposed models.
The best performing model when
applied to the data set is selected. This should be app. 25% of the original data
set.
Test set is used to test the strength of the model. This is used to be able to estimate
how accurate the selected model can be expected to perform. This should be
app. 25% of the entire data set.
During some of the analyses in this thesis our data will be supervised even though the

3.3.4
T-Tests
T-tests are used to test diﬀerences in means of normally distributed data. Several
versions exists and we will brieﬂy mention them here. Commonly for all is that they
assume data to be normally distributed and samples to be independent. [EC12]
One-sample t-test is used to test if a sample of normally distributed data are sampled from
a distribution with mean diﬀerent from zero.
Two-sample t-tests are used to test if to samples of normally distributed data have are
samples from distributions with diﬀerent means. This can be done both with
and without assumptions of shared variance and with equal or unequal sample
sizes.
Paired t-test is used when the two samples are dependent. E.g. when measuring im-
provement of athletes after following a diet: Before starting the diet each ath-
lete’s performance is measured. Then after 10 weeks of diet their performances
are measured again. Because of the fact that the performances measured before
and after the diet were of the same athletes, the observations in the two samples
(before and after) are dependent.
3.3.5
Multiple Testing and Bonferroni Correction
When doing a statistical test, a null hypothesis is tested at some signiﬁcance level, α.
The null hypothesis, H0, is then either accepted or rejected based on how likely it is
to be true given the observed data. If the probability of observing the given data if
the null hypothesis is correct is less than α, the hypothesis is rejected. This means
that there is a probability of falsely rejecting H0, namely α. This means that the
more tests one performs the greater the probability for falsely rejecting H0 becomes.
Falsely rejecting the null hypothesis is known as type I error.
A simple way to correct for this error is to simply adjust the signiﬁcance level according
to the number of tests performed, such that the probability of making one type I is α.
This method is known as Bonferroni correction. There exists other alternatives and
Bonferroni is known to be very conservative. [BA95] [BH95]

not for an ensemble of neurons. Data which have not been spike sorted is known as
multi-unit data, while spike sorted data is known as single-unit data.
An important aspect in analyzing the neurodynamics is to be able to distinguish
excitatory and inhibitory neurons.
The action potential durations are longer for
excitatory neurons than for inhibitory neurons. Spike sorting would therefore not
only allow us to analyze data on single neuron level but additionally be able to
classify excitatory and inhibitory neurons.
For this thesis, spike sorting was attempted using the MATLAB toolbox WaveClus
by [QNBS04]. However, this was not done satisfactory and therefore we will only
consider multi-unit data. The fact that we analyze multi-unit data may hide high
resolution temporal coding, as discussed in Section 2.3.

4.2.4
Post-Stimulus Time Histograms
Another widely used method to analyze spike data is to average the spike rasters
over trials. If the number of trials is suﬃciently large, one gets an estimate of the
instantaneous spike rate. These averages are called post-stimulus time histograms,
PSTH, and are seen in Figure 4.7. By averaging the spikes over trials, PSTHs gives a
clearer idea of the neuron activity at high temporal resolution than the spike rasters.
However, unlike when looking at spike rasters, the inter-trial variance is completely
lost when analyzing the PSTH.
Due to the inter-trial variance, the temporal resolution of PSTH is lower than of the
rasters, meaning that one should be aware of interpreting PSTH. For instance, the
widths of the peaks in the PSTH should be wider than what the true change in spike
rate of single trials would be due to inter-trial oﬀ-sets.
From the plot of the PSTHs we draw the same conclusions about the increase and
decrease of spiking activity after stimulus on-set and oﬀ-set. However, an oscillating
behavior becomes more apparent.
Particularly for condition 2 at 100-300ms and
400-600ms.

want to be able to estimate the spike rates in single trials. This can be done by
applying moving average ﬁltering to single trial spike trains. Choosing an appropriate

a study leading up this thesis, during the Spring 2014, the Mean Integrated Square

indicate that using σ = 2 is too narrow and σ = 20 is too wide. σ = 6 and σ = 10
may be equally suitable depending on how much inﬂuence single action potentials are
allowed to have, and how high temporal resolution is wanted. In the following we will
use the narrower kernel width (σ = 6).

Cross-Correlations of
Post-Stimulus Time

In this chapter, cross-correlation between the post-stimulus time histograms (PSTHs)
of diﬀerent cortical areas and layers are investigated. As discussed in Section 4.2.4,
analyzing PSTHs preserves temporal resolution and does give information about the
on average spiking activity. However, single trial analysis becomes impossible due to
the low number of spikes in single trials.
As shown in Section 4.3.2, we have multiple leads recording from each cortical layer.
The recordings of these leads will be averaged per layer. However, the number of
leads are still too few to obtain proper density of the PSTH.
We should keep in mind that cross-correlations between two layers may not be able
to detect both feedforward and feedback.
Analysis in this chapter uses data that has not been split into training-, validation-,
and test sets, since the results are unsupervised and we do not have parameters to
validate.

CHAPTER 6
Peak Spike Rates
In the previous chapter, we analyzed the cross correlation of the entire time of the
stimulus. This assumed that the propagation between the layers are similar through-
out the time period. That is, the relative dynamics between the areas and layers
is similar for both reaction to stimulus on-set and oﬀ-set, as well as for the inter-
mediate time periods. Here, we propose another strategy that does not require this
assumption.
From the PSTHs, it is clear that the spike rate increases readily after stimulus on-set
and oﬀ-set. By comparing the time-points for the maximum spike-rate after stimulus
on-set and oﬀ-set, respectively, we may be able to ﬁnd communication patterns related
to this initial behavior - which is the most apparent from the exploratory plots.
Just as in Chapter 5, we will ﬁrst investigate the eﬀect across penetrations before
looking within penetrations. Contrary to before, we estimate the spike rate by ap-
plying a Gaussian moving average ﬁlter with σ = 6. Also, contrary to previously, we
therefore do not need to do bootstrapping to increase the sample size for the PSTHs,
since we apply the ﬁlter and thus average activity over a longer period than 1ms.
The cost of this ability to analyze single trials, is that we lose some of the temporal
resolution.
Before doing the analysis, a short description of the peak detection and usage of
training and test sets will be carried out.

The analysis methods used until now have simply averaged over multiple leads in each
layer, and used crude methods to ﬁnd propagation between layers. In this chapter
the topic is how to use matrix decomposition methods to reduce dimensionality and
obtain knowledge about the neurodynamics. This chapter will be very qualitative
and form the basis for quantitative analysis in Chapter 8.
For now, the analysis of propagation between layers have relied on an equal mean
between leads in each layer and treated each layer independently from each other. In
this chapter all leads will be analyzed simultaneously and the entire system will be
evaluated. However, the data is 32-dimensional (we sample from 32 leads simulta-
neously). Since 32-dimensional data is diﬃcult to visualize, the goal here is to use
matrix decomposition methods, often used in multivariate statistics, for dimension-
ality reduction in the hopes that the high-dimensional system can be reduced in a
reasonable way.
Since the matrix decomposition methods do not work well on binary data, we analyze
spike rates, r, estimated with Gaussian kernel with σ = 6.0.

to apply multi-way decomposition methods [SBG05]. The full data is 6-way with the

7.2.1
Unfolding Data
Applying PCA means that we can only reduce one dimension - the columns. Therefore,
when unfolding, we have to think about which dimension we believe is best suited for
dimensionality reduction. The answer to this question depends on what inference we
want to be able to make. In this case, where we are interested in understanding the
single trial neurodynamics, we want to either reduce the temporal dimension or the
neurons.
In the following, we will discuss these two unfolding schemes and apply the latter to
the data and draw some inference about the system dynamics.

Spike Rates
Unfolding such that columns becomes the time dimension, such that Xij returns the
spike rate at time j, suggests that the spike rates over time are similar for all animals,
cortical areas and layers, conditions, and trials. By deﬁnition we do not assume that
the spiking activity over time is similar for diﬀerent conditions. However, we might be
inclined to believe that the activity is similar for the other variables - except, perhaps,
the cortical layers.

Z ∈RN×T , where N = Nanimals · Npenetrations · Nleads · Ntrials is the total number of

If we assume that the spike rate is unaﬀected by animal, penetration, lead, and trial,
we would be able to reduce the full matrices into a linear combination of a few vectors;

of neurons related to hand movement.

deterministic and that the neurons behave similarly to stimulus every time the same

conditions during the 100-200ms time interval, while no clear diﬀerence straight after
on- or oﬀ-set.
We have seen that the spontaneous activity seems to be characterized by a limited
spike rate and highly non-synchronized paths, making it a high-entropy system. After
stimulus the system moves very fast and becomes very synchronized, giving it low
entropy. We notice a third state where the system tends to become synchronized and
fast-moving, but still not as evident as immediately after stimulus on- and oﬀ-set.

Conclusion
We showed that using PCA to correlate instantaneous spiking activity of neurons it is
possible to reduce the system dynamics to two dimensions. Plotting the trajectories
indicate that most of the dynamics related to stimulus can be described in a two-
dimensional state-space.
From analyzing the 2D dynamics we see that the single trial activity changes from
being very diﬀerent under spontaneous activity to being very similar when reaction
to stimulus. The dimensionality reduction seem to capture relevant dynamics and
give insight that are diﬃcult to gain from the classical methods to represent data,
discussed in Section 4.2.
Plotting the angles of the 2D state-space velocities over time, Figure 7.6, indicates
diﬀerences between the 1-square stimulus (condition 2) and 3-square stimulus (condi-
tion 3). There seem to be much clearer collective dynamics after the initial reaction
to stimulus for condition 2. Looking at the trajectory speeds, Figure 7.7, does not
seem to express as clear a diﬀerence.
Because of the dynamics revealed by analyzing velocities (speeds and directions) a
classiﬁer was built such that it is possible to classify non-spontaneous activity from
spontaneous activity from the 2D state-space velocities.

Conclusion
Applying PCA on each layer individually allows us visualize dynamics between two
layers in an easy to interpret manner. This is because the relevant dynamics of each
layer can be reduced into a single component which thus can show dynamics between
two layers in 2D plots.
It was shown that diﬀerent inference can be made from plotting single trial trajectories
and multiple trajectories together. Single trials allow us to see detailed communica-
tion between layers in high temporal resolution. Multiple trajectories reveal dynamics
that indicate that the system is highly deterministic when not expressing spontaneous
activity.
Lastly, we showed that modeling the transition times, classiﬁed from trajectory ve-
locities, with linear mixed models succeeded with providing a framework that could
explain details about the total experimental data. The fact that one model can be
used to explain the entire data set, displays its strength compared to the methods
analyzed previously in this thesis.

As mentioned in Chapter 1, an important goal of the work done during this project is
to be able to analyze and characterize neurodynamics on single trials, while keeping
high temporal resolution.
The analysis until now has suggested that, for the stationary stimuli, the system can
either be in a resting state or in an evoked state. Throughout the thesis we have not
regarded neuronal activity as discrete even though data suggests it; either an action
potential is triggered or not.
This gives rise to applying statistical process control [Oak08], which is used to de-
termine if a stochastic process has shifted state, e.g. to determine if the number of
surgical mistakes is normal or something should be corrected [Spl10]. If we assume
that the timings of a neuron’s action potentials are independent, we can regard the
spiking activity as a stochastic process and apply statistical process control to the
raw spike data. The high variance of spike timings, in both rest and evoked states,
suggests that the independence assumption is reasonable. However, there is a long
discussion of whether the system is stochastic or deterministic (chaotic) in the com-
munity, which will not be carried out here.
In the following, the Exponentially Weighted Moving Average, EWMA, Control Chart
model from [Spl10] will be applied to our data and the results will be analyzed using
a linear mixed eﬀects model as in the previous chapter.
Analyzing Inter-Spike Intervals has been done before, e.g. in [SKS+09].

We will build the control chart such that we can detect activity that diﬀers from
spontaneous activity. In order to build the control chart, we need determine three

smoothing parameter, λ, as large as possible such that we keep as high temporal

Conclusion
Viewing the a neruon’s action potentials, when expressing spontaneous activity, as
independent events allows us to very the spike train as a stochastic process and
apply build a control chart to determine whether an action potential unlikely to be
expressing spontaneous activity.
The control charts, seen in Figure 9.1, does not look promising and show that only
few action potentials are classiﬁed as non-spontaneous. This does not fall in line with
what exploratory plotting suggests.
Looking at the proportion of trials evoked in each layer, in Figure 9.3, shows that in
fact many of the trials become evoked as response to stimulus on-set.

chronous, indicating some highly deterministic dynamics captured by the control
chart.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion and Future
Work
This chapter will summarize the ﬁndings of the previous analysis and suggest analyzes
and experimental setups for further investigating the neurodynamics.
10.1
Conclusion
During the thesis we gained experience with some of the diﬃculties with analyzing
electrophysiological data.
The problem being to deal with the sparsity of action
potentials and the large within and between trial variance.
Analyzing correlations by averaging spiking activity over trials and calculating the
cross-correlations between areas, it was found that in order to detect any signs of
propagation it is necessary to stay within penetrations.
The across penetrations
variance is simply too great.
Because of the sparsity of the data, it is therefore
necessary to do bootstrapping.
By doing that it was possible to ﬁnd some signs
of propagation from 17/18 to 19/21. However, this does not facilitate single trial
analysis.
Using the times of peak spike rates of single trials allowed to analyze single trials
and thus increases the chance that we analyze the neurodynamics in a way similar
to how the brain functions. Testing the time of peak rates did not show many signs
of propagation and it is therefore not believed to be a relevant feature for describing
the dynamics in the cortical areas.
A diﬀerent approach to analyze single trials was to correlate neurons and thus creating
a two-dimensional state-space that allowed for easy visualization of the dynamics.
This turned out to reveal some aspects of the dynamics system that is not easily
found in classical data representations. It allowed us to analyze the dynamics both
qualitatively and quantitatively. Incorporating the results in a linear mixed eﬀects
model revealed some signs of propagation that had not been found in the previous
methods.
Lastly, the control chart methodology was applied with the purpose of truly analyzing

the dynamics of the action potentials. Although, not looking too promising on the
visual investigations, the control chart methodology did ﬁnd signiﬁcant estimates of

In conclusion we found that both the PCA trajectories and EWMA control chart
allowed us to analyze neurodynamics on single trial level, while keeping high temporal

Applications of
Multi-Way Models in
Neurodynamics
Lars Holtse Bonde, Technical University of Denmark
September 25, 2014
U
sing multi-way state space models to
analyze data from in vivo extracellu-
lar electrophysiology experiments gives
high temporal resolution of single neuron dy-
namics, allowing analyses revealing dynamics
hidden by classical analysis methods, such as
peristimulus time histograms (PSTHs) and
estimated ﬁring rates.
1 Introduction
In vivo extracellular electrophysiology allows measur-
ing the activity of single neurons in live animals while
subjected to diﬀerent stimuli. Due to the high vari-
ance of neuron activity, one often most compromise
temporal resolution or average over several trials.
Both of which hides details about neuron activity. In
the following, the reader will brieﬂy be introduced
to the data and typical analysis methods.
1.1 Extracellular Electrophysiology
It is known that neurons communicate by absorbing
and releasing neurotransmitter molecules. The den-
drites, which absorbs neurotransmitters, are placed
close to the cell body. The synapses, which releases
neurotransmitters, are placed far from the cell body,
connected via the axon. When a neuron receives
a certain amount of certain neurotransmitters, an
electrical signal ﬂows from the cell body, through
the axon, to the synapses where molecules are re-
leased. When the electrical impulse passes certain
points along the axon, an action potential occurs.
During an action potential a large ﬂow of sodium
and potassium ions passes the cell wall, changing
the membrane potential from app. -70mV to app.
30mV. By inserting an electrode into the brain, it is
possible to detect these action potentials, and thus
measure the neuron activity.
1.2 Description of Data
Even when a neuron is at rest, and not reacting to
a stimulus, it does ﬁre action potentials. Analyses
suggest that the ﬁring of a neuron at rest is chaotic.
It has been shown that the ﬁring rate of a neuron
reacting to a stimulus increases, related to the in-

not neurons code information simply by ﬁring rate

former sacriﬁces temporal resolution and it has been
shown, by behavioural studies, that the brain cannot
process information this way, as subjects were able
to react faster than information could be processed.
The latter is therefore often done as it preserves high
temporal resolution. ﬁg. 2 shows a peristimulus time

across trials and keeps the 1ms temporal resolution.
However, analyses have shown that this approach
hides temporal details, as there is in fact a temporal
variance across trials.
Recently it has been shown that by projecting
single trials onto a state space spanned by the ﬁrst
3 principal components, it is possible maintain high
temporal resolution. However, the data has multi-
way structure and therefore needs to be unfolded
in order to do principal component analysis, PCA.
Thereby, information about inﬂuence of diﬀerent
animals, penetrations, cortical areas and cortical
depths is lost. It is therefore necessary to develop a
multi-way framework to do similar projections.

The data used here comes from in vivo extracellular
electrophysiology experiments from the ferret visual
cortex, from eight diﬀerent ferrets. Two electrodes
are inserted into the ferret cortex on the borders
between area 17/18 and 19/21 respectively. Each
electrode having 16 leads spaced with 100 µm from
each other.
Thus giving simultaneous recordings
throughout the cortical layers in both areas. During
the recordings, the animal is placed in front of a
large screen.
Between trials, the screen is black.
During a trial, one of three images can appear; either
nothing changes, a single white square appears after
200ms and disappears after additional 250ms before
leaving the screen black for 550ms, or three white
squares appear after 200ms and disappears after
additional 250ms before leaving the screen black for
550ms, giving three stimulus conditions. Each of
these stimuli are given 100 times, giving 100 trials
lasting 1000ms. After a session, one or both of the
electrodes are moved along the border of the areas it
is placed. Each of these penetrations are numbered
a b, with a describing penetration number of the

Due to computation times and not having to deal
with unbalanced experimental designs and newly dis-
covered (and not yet interpreted) neurodynimics, the
material used here consists of data from all animals,
ﬁrst two penetration sets, leads 7-10 (corresponding
to cortical layer IV) from the ﬁrst electrode, all 3

penetration eﬀect, since the electrode has not been
moved.
The discrete data has been ﬁltered with a gaussian
moving average ﬁlter with σ = 6.0ms and width
20ms.

penetration × lead × condition × trial ×time. How-
ever, especially for the data used here, some of the
modes may not be inﬂuential in describing data. For

tial since it is just repetitions. It is, though, unknown
if there are any signiﬁcant diﬀerences between ani-
mals. In the following, diﬀerent 2-way and multi-way
approaches to analyzing the data will be taken with
the purpose of assessing their strengths and weak-

In the following we will look at three diﬀerent 2-

neurons on single trial level. The results from the
strengths of these models, appropriate multi-way
models will be applied.
In this case, the tucker
models seemed most appropriate, as we will see in
section 3.

The loadings span an orthogonal state-space of the
variables. The loadings are chosen in order to de-
scribe as much as the variance of the data as possible.
The scores are projections of each of the observations
onto this space. For our data, the variables are time-
points and the observations are the diﬀerent trials
(for diﬀerent animals, penetrations, leads, and con-
ditions). ﬁg. 3 shows three observations projected
onto the 3-dimensional space, spanned by the ﬁrst
three principal components.
Earlier work has shown that using the speed of
the trajectory to determine system state gives nice
results in accordance to PSTHs, ﬁg. 2, on single trial
level. The system state is deﬁned with determining
a speed threshold from the blank trials, where the
system by deﬁnition is at rest.
The threshold is
chosen such that 1% of the blank trials are classiﬁed
as being evoked at any one timepoint. The results
of these classiﬁcations are seen in section 3.
2.2.2 3 Class PLS-DA Trajectories
Since the classes are known supervised learning is
possible and we might use this to our advantage.
Partial Least Squares Discriminant Analysis, PLS-
DA, is very similar to PCA described in section 2.2.1.
However, we introduce a response variable, Y , which
describes which class an observation belongs to. In
this case Y is binary, and since we have three classes:
Y ∈Rn×3, X ∈Rn×p.
Where PCA chooses components to describe as
much variance in X as possible, PLS-DA chooses
components in order to discriminate between classes
as correct as possible. The spike trains and PSTHs

2.2.3 1 Class PCA Trajectories
Looking at the PSTH, ﬁg. 2, the rest dynamics seems
to be constant. Since PCA tries to describe varia-
tion, a single component will not be attributed to
such a constant. Therefore, doing separate PCAs
on each condition may reveal more information and
better discrimination between classes. It is another,
more crude, way to force components to describe the
dynamics of each condition, like PLS-DA.
2.2.4 Tucker
Due to mathematical diﬃculties, linear algebra can-
not easily be expanded to tensors. The tucker models
are multi-way models which are similar to the PCA
model used on 2-way data. Where PCA gives a set
of scores and loadings, an N-way tucker gives a set
of loadings for each mode as well as a core array
which mixes the loadings. Because if this mixing
tensor, it is possible to have a diﬀerent number of
loadings in each mode, unlike PCA. Here we will
build tucker models on the original 6-way data as
well as on partially unfolded (3-way) data.

did not seem to be relevant for describing the data.

over-ﬁtting, it is therefore desirable to unfold these
modes. Therefore the penetration, lead, and trial
modes are unfolded, giving a 3-way structure of
animal × penetration, lead, trial × time. The mod-
els are again ﬁtted for each condition, this time with
8 loadings in each of the three modes.

the tucker3 model in general describes more of the
variance with the same amount of loadings.

4 Discussion
4.1 Conclusion
It has been shown that building state-space models
using 2-way matrix factorization methods enables us
to get an insight in neurodynamics on single trial
level, which is diﬃcult by traditional analysis tech-
niques. Building a good system state classiﬁer can
give hints of when system dynamics change and thus
the raw data can be analyzed with that in mind. It
may also be possible to characterize system dynamics
from the state-space models directly.
It was shown that the state classiﬁcation abili-
ties are sensitive to which state-space model is used.
From the data used throughout this article, it is
clear that a separate model should be build for each
stimulus condition. We have, however, not compared
the condition classiﬁcation abilities of such a model.

4.2 Future Work
This has merely been a motivation to take a deeper
look at multi-way state-space models for analyzing
neurodynamics and there are several loose ends.
Firstly, one could apply SIMCA and compare its
stimulus condition classiﬁcation abilities to PLS-DA.
Secondly, other multi-way models should be ex-
plored, such as PARAFAC and PARAFAC2.
Of
course one should do so with physiology in mind,
which may give PARAFAC and PARAFAC2 little
hope, unless one believes in high structural neural
coding.
The work done here should be redone with a larger
dataset, including recordings from diﬀerent layers
and areas, since structural diﬀerences between such
may be revealed by the multi-way models. Also, one
could do spike sorting and thus work with single unit
recordings. Multi-way models may be able to detect
diﬀerent types of neurons based on their spiking
dynamics.
When a proper model has been built, state-space
trajectories should be calculated and studied.
It
would be interesting to see how much, and in which
way, these trajectories diﬀer from the 2-way state-
space trajectories.
Finally, validation should deﬁnitely be done since it
has not been done for now. This has been skipped in
order to get a hands on experience with the diﬀerent
models instead.
