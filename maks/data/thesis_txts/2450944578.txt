Abstract
X-ray computed tomography and image analysis can be used to investigate the relationship
between microscopic ﬁber structures and macroscopic material properties (material thermal
insulation, stiﬀness, and more) in materials consisting primarily of ﬁbers.
The aim of
this thesis is two-fold; To model the underlying ﬁber orientation distribution of a ﬁber
material, which can be used in modelling material properties, and to provide a metric
of the dissimilarity between two ﬁber orientation distributions, with the main purpose of
comparing production methods.
A pipeline is presented where structure tensors are used to determine ﬁber orienta-
tions, and a mixture model of Watson distributions (WMM) is used for quantifying and
modelling the ﬁber orientation distributions.
Furthermore, the implemented WMM is
extended with regularization of the concentrations. Two modelled ﬁber orientation dis-
tributions are compared by ﬁrst registering the distributions to account for diﬀerence in
material orientation during scanning. Subsequently, the earth mover’s distance (EMD) is
used for quantifying the dissimilarity between them.
The pipeline is used on a set of two carbon ﬁber composites and a set of two cotton
samples.
In both cases, a good correspondence between the known and the modelled
attributes is presented.
The introduced dissimilarity measure EMD is discussed, and it seems to be mostly
suited for quantifying relative changes between multiple samples. The suggested regular-
ization of the WMM improve the model for all the tested images.
The WMM shows good results in modeling ﬁber orientation distributions, but more
work is required in exploring the potential and investigating similar models, e.g. a mixture
model of the more complex Bingham distribution.

Introduction
The orientation distributions of this project are studied in the context of the underlying
orientations of ﬁber in materials consisting of ﬁber. The study of these ﬁber structures
is generally concerned with the relationship between the microscopic ﬁber structure and
the macroscopic properties of a material consisting of ﬁbers (Redenbach, Rack, Schladitz,
Wirjadi, & Godehardt, 2012). In this speciﬁc case the study is focused on quantifying the
ﬁber orientation structures, and not with modelling macroscopic properties. By scanning
ﬁber materials in high-resolution X-ray computed tomography (CT) scanners, analysis of
the ﬁber orientation distributions can be performed on the resulting images. Variations of
such analysis have been conducted numerous times e.g. by
· Saxena et al. (2019) where ﬁber orientation distributions are used to investigate
whether using diﬀerent silicone cores during production results in diﬀerent ﬁber ori-
entation distributions in the produced paper bottles.
· Chapelle et al. (2018) where material stiﬀness in stone wool and glass wool is modelled
from ﬁber orientation distributions.
Some ﬁber analysis techniques are based on ﬁber segmentation and subsequent ﬁber skele-
tonization, which can be computationally heavy (Tan, Elliott, & Clyne, 2006; Couprie,
Meulenyzer, Salem, Talbot, & Pourcel, 2014). Another approach is to use structure tensor
(ST) to extract an orientation for each ﬁber voxel, which can be done eﬃciently and in par-
allel. Skeletonization is the process of ﬁnding medial axes in the ﬁbers of an image, i.e. all
points in the center of ﬁbers. Skeletonization can give some additional insights of the ﬁber
network, e.g. crossing ﬁbers. Skeletonization is very susceptible to noise and the choice of
threshold parameter for removing background (Couprie et al., 2014). An ST approach is
used here and in the aforementioned Saxena et al. (2019) and Chapelle et al. (2018). The
ST is good when working with distributions, as any noise is mostly insigniﬁcant in the
aggregate.
This project is an attempt to visualize and extract usable statistics of the ﬁber orientation
distribution based on CT images of a ﬁber material, and give a metric of the dissimilar-
ity between the ﬁber orientation distributions estimated from images of diﬀerent samples.
The steps of the approach is to ﬁrst extract ﬁber orientations using ST, then ﬁt a mixture
model of Watson distributions with an appropriate number of kernels to estimate means
and concentrations of clusters in the ﬁber orientation distribution. To compare orientation
distributions from diﬀerent images, some registration between the two distributions needs
to be determined. The registration is conducted by ﬁrst using principal component analysis
(PCA) of the orientations to ﬁnd a transformation which roughly registers the distribu-
tions. Afterwards, a rotation based on two modelled ﬁber orientation distributions are
used to minimize dissimilarity. After registration, a distance between the distributions are
calculated using a χ2 based dissimilarity measure and the Earth Movers Distance (EMD).

sphere. The ﬁber orientation distribution is used as the model presented here, describing
the underlying distribution using a mixture of Watson distributions.

1.1
A note on 2D visualization of the sphere
When making 2D visualizations of the surface of a sphere, which is essential when work-
ing with orientations, some compromise is necessary in regards to which information is
most important, as a transformation between a sphere and a plane will not preserve all
properties of the sphere. A transformation mapping the surface of a sphere to a plane is
called a map projection and is an extensive topic with a lot of mathematical, cultural and
historical contexts, worthy of a lengthy discussion, but will only be brieﬂy touched upon
here. (Hodgkinson, 1991; Wu, 2007)
Any map projection is a compromise to preserve some properties of the map on the sphere,
e.g. preserving local angles and thereby shapes (conformality) and preserving area mea-
sure (equal area). The properties of a map projections is often visualized using Tissot’s
indicatrix, which shows identical circles in diﬀerent places on the map to visualize how the
projection distorts shape and area. (Goldberg & J. Richard Gott III, 2007)
(a) Mercator projection truncated
at ±85° with Tissot’s indicatrices of
diﬀerent sizes in the form of circles,
indicating that shape is preserved,
while area is distorted.
(b) Mollweide projection with Tissot’s indicatrices of constant
size, but varying shape, indicating that area is preserved, while
shape is distorted.
Figure 1.2: Comparison of the Mercator and Mollweide map projections overlaid
with Tissot’s indicatrices, which shows how the size and shape of a circle distort in
diﬀerent places on the map.
For visualizing the World the choice has historically been the conformal Mercator projec-
tion, which is seen in Figure 1.2a. The problem with the Mercator projection, for the
purpose of looking at histograms and distributions, is that it exaggerates areas far from
equator, i.e. it is not an equal area projection. This is clear from the Tissot’s indicatrices
and it is very notable when looking at some of the land masses, e.g. the fact that Green-
land and Africa appears to be of the same size, even though the area of Africa is roughly
14 times larger than that of Greenland. This area distortion increases towards the poles,
where the area is inﬁnite. The Mercator projection notably has parallel lines of longitude
and circles of latitude (lines oriented north-south and east-west respectively). (Wu, 2007)
For this project the sphere is visualized using the Mollweide projection, seen in Figure 1.2b.
The Mollweide projection has parallel circles of latitude, but curved lines of longitude
creating an elliptical projection without area distortions, but notable distortions of shapes.
Since there is no area distortions, it is very usable for visualizing distributions, and is
also why it is the projection usually associated with visualizations of cosmic microwave
background radiation. (Wu, 2007; NASA, n.d.)

When working with the orientation of ﬁbers it is notable that the orientation of a ﬁber can
be described identically by a vector v and its opposite −v, i.e. a ﬁber has no “forward” and
“backward”. In this thesis “orientation” is the term used for this notion of the unsigned
vector, while “direction” is the term used for the signed vector. Due to this, when looking at
the maps in this thesis, it is important to have a sense of where antipodes are situated in the
Mollweide projection. An antipode to a point is the diametrically opposite point, i.e. the
point furthest away on the surface of the sphere. In Figure 1.3 the Mollweide projection is
visualized with 10 antipodal pairs. Note how the antipodes to the points along the central
line of longitude appear on both edges, where the projection wraps around.
Figure 1.4: Orthographic projection.
Another consequence of the fact that v and its opposite −v describes an orientation iden-
tical is that all information of an orientation distribution can be on a single hemisphere, so
an orthographic projection will also be used for visualizations, since it removes the need to
keep track of antipodes, but it has increasing shape and area distortions towards the edges.
An orthographic projection is made by having a tangent plane to a sphere and projects
each point onto the closest point on the tangent plane. It is equivalent to the perspective
of a sphere from an inﬁnite distance. In Figure 1.4, the orthographic projection of the
World can be seen, where the tangent plane has been placed at the point where the prime
meridian and equator cross. (Weisstein, n.d.)

Theory
In this chapter, the theoretical background for the tools used in the pipeline speciﬁed in
Chapter 3 is introduced. This includes the motivation and interpretation of the structure
tensor and two methods for tessellating the sphere. Furthermore, the Watson distribution
is introduced along with a mixture model of Watson distributions and the earth mover’s
distance and a χ2 based metric for measuring the dissimilarity between distributions.
2.1
Structure Tensor
For an image, I, of ﬁbers, the ﬁber orientation for each ﬁber voxel can be modelled using
an ST approach. It is based on an assumption that the orientation with the least change
in intensity is the ﬁber orientation. This can be formulated as an optimization problem,
where for each ﬁber voxel, represented by the position vector, x, a ﬁber orientation vector,
u(x), is found by
u(x) = argmin

|v| = 1

i.e. letting u(x) be equal to the unit vector describing the direction with the smallest
intensity change. A good approximation to Eq. (2.1) is the ST approach, which consists
of a few simple steps (Krause, Hausherr, Burgeth, Herrmann, & Krenkel, 2010)
1. The image is ﬁrst smoothed with the Gaussian kernel h(σ2) after which the gradient
is determined as the vector of derivatives in the x, y and z directions
∇Iσ(x) = ∇(h(σ2) ∗I(x))
(2.2)
The image is smoothed as the gradient is susceptible to noise.
2. The outer product of the gradient with itself gives a tensor, which is then regular-
ized using element-wise convolution with a Gaussian kernel, w(ρ2), resulting in the
structure tensor, Jρ(x), describing the general image intensity change in the area,
Jρ(x) = w(ρ2) ∗(∇Iσ∇I⊤

(2.3)
The regularization gives locally homogeneous orientations, such that tensors inside
ﬁbers tend to be oriented the same way, while tensors outside of ﬁbers tend to be
more isotropic.

2.2
Sphere Tessellation
In order to work with orientation histograms and distributions, some bins need to be
deﬁned on the sphere surface. This process is called sphere tessellation and is deﬁned by
Górski, Hivon, Banday, Wandelt, and Hansen (2005) as
“An exhaustive partition of the sphere into ﬁnite area elements.”
Furthermore, they sum up the complexity of the problem as
“There is no known point set that achieves the analog of uniform sampling in
Euclidean space. All existing practical schemes proposed for the treatment of
such discretized functions on the sphere introduce some systematic errors de-
pendent on the global properties of the point set. The goal is to minimize these
errors and faithfully represent deterministic functions as well as realizations of
random variates while maintaining computational eﬃciency.”
This implies that any sphere tessellation will have some trade oﬀ. Some of the inherent
problems of the sphere tessellation are very similar to the problem of map projections, as
the problems are closely related.
In the context of sphere tessellation for the purpose of making a histogram on the sphere,
the words face, pixel and bin are used mostly interchangeably for the area elements of the
tessellation.

One way to tessellate the unit sphere is to start with an icosahedron and split each face into
smaller faces based on a subdivision frequency, ν. This approach is visualized in Figure 2.2
and further exploration and an implementation can be seen in Appendix A.2. In essence,
it has turned out not to be viable as the implementation was slow, and the area of the
faces is not equal with the described approach.
Another approach to create a tessellation with equal area based on the icosahedron can
be seen in Tegmark (1996), but it has not been explored further, as no readily available
implementation exists and making such an implementation is out of scope.

resulting tessellation consists of equal area elements. The tessellation is based on 12 faces,
each of which is split based on the resolution parameter Nside to get the desired resolution.
The splitting of each face is done in a similar manner to the presented icosahedron tessel-
lation, i.e. each edge is split into Nside smaller edges, creating a total of 12Nside2 faces (See
Figure 2.3). The HEALPix scheme is very eﬃcient, both by design and implementation. It
is hierarchically structured allowing for fast lookups, analytical expressions exist for pixel
centers and boundaries and it is implemented in C++ with Python API through the healpy
package. (Górski et al., 2005) (http://healpix.sf.net)
Because of the eﬃciency and availability of the HEALPix implementation, it is used in
this project in favor of the implementation of icosahedron based tessellation.
2.3
Watson Distribution
Since the observations are not directions, but orientations where observed vectors x and
−x are equivalent, it is appropriate to use an antipodally symmetric distribution, i.e. a
distribution where the probability density function satisﬁes
f(x) = f(−x)
(2.5)
A simple antipodally symmetric distribution is the Watson distribution.
The Watson
distribution has circular contours on the sphere around the mean orientation, i.e. it is
rotationally symmetric around the mean orientation.
It is parameterised by the mean
orientation µ and the concentration κ. The probability density function for the Watson
distribution is
W(x|µ, κ) = c(κ)eκ(µ⊤x)2
(2.6)
where c(κ) is a normalization constant. (Mardia & Jupp, 2000, p. 181)
The concentration parameter κ determines the dispersion of the distribution. For positive
κ, the concentration is roughly equivalent to the inverse of the variance of the well-known
Gaussian distributions, as the probability density function concentrates around the mean
orientation for increasing κ. For negative κ the probability density function concentrates
around the great circle orthogonal to µ for decreasing κ. For κ = 0 the distribution is
simply uniform on the sphere. (Sra & Karp, 2013)

2.4
Distribution Distance
To measure the distance between two ﬁber orientation distributions, p and q, two metrics
are used here, a χ2 based dissimilarity measure and the earth mover’s distance. The χ2
distance is well known and simple to compute, while EMD has some nice properties, which
makes it worth exploring.
Something to keep in mind for distances on a sphere is that when two objects on a sphere
move away from each other, they will eventually start getting closer to each other again,
implying that a distance measure should have a maximum.
For both metrics, the distributions p and q are initially binned, i.e. a histogram of the
distributions is determined as two vectors of weights, wp and wq, each normalized to unit
sum. The metrics are used for quantifying the dissimilarity between these vectors.

Method
In this chapter the implementation details and computational considerations of the pipeline
are provided. The pipeline, as seen sketched in Figure 1.1, consists of computing ﬁber ori-
entations and from these model the ﬁber orientation distribution. Multiple orientation
distributions are registered and ﬁnally the distances between the distributions are com-
puted.
3.1
Fiber Orientations
The ﬁber orientations are extracted by ﬁtting an ST to each ﬁber voxel and solving for the
eigenvector with lowest eigenvalue. The ﬁber voxels are found by creating a simple mask
by thresholding the intensities of the image. Implementation of the method for ﬁtting the
ST as described in Section 2.1 is simple and quick. The part which can take a long time
is solving the resulting eigensystem for each voxel. Looking at Eq. (2.3) it is seen that the
ST is symmetric and real valued, which means that the eigenvalues can be found eﬃciently
with the simple algorithm given in Smith (1961). In Appendix A.2 Section 1, this method
is compared to a standard eigensystem solver and a 95% speed up in computations time
is seen.
3.2
Orientation Histogram, Distribution and Statistics
When the ﬁber orientations have been extracted, an orientation histogram is created using
HEALPix. Inspection of the histogram or prior material knowledge is used for determining
the number of kernels for ﬁtting the WMM presented in Section 2.3. The WMM is used
to obtain statistics about the ﬁber orientation distribution. The method has only been
implemented and tested for the case of κ > 0, as it is the only relevant case for the test
data used, but there should be no hindrance in extending the implementation to κ ∈R
following the algorithm given in Sra and Karp (2013). An implementation with κ < 0
can be used to model data with ﬁber orientations distributed around a great circle on the
sphere.

3.3
Distribution Comparison
Finding a measure of the distance between distributions ﬁtted to diﬀerent samples requires
ﬁrst registering the distributions to a common coordinate system, before computing the
distance using the χ2 distance metric and EMD. As there is no expectation of a ﬁxed coor-
dinate system between samples during the scanning process, there is no expectation that
the orientation of mean vectors of distributions from otherwise similar samples will be the
same. Because of this, distributions need to be aligned before the distance between them
can be measured. The initial registration is using principal component analysis (PCA) of
the set of extracted ﬁber orientations. PCA is used, as it can give 3 directions in the dataset
independently of the used basis. Both of the modelled ﬁber orientation distributions are
transformed to be in the principal component basis to roughly align the distribution. The
determined mean orientations are then used to ﬁnd another transformation between the
distributions to tweak the registration.
3.3.1
Principal Axis Transform
The principal components are found by ﬁrst deﬁning the 2N × 3 data matrix X of N
ﬁber orientations, xi, and their N opposites, −xi. Usually, the mean would be subtracted
from each row of the X matrix to ﬁnd a centered data matrix, but the way X is deﬁned
including the opposites, the mean of each row is already 0. The principal components are
then simply found as the unit eigenvectors of the covariance matrix of the data matrix,
C = X⊤X. (Bishop, 2006, p. 561–563)
The ﬁrst principal component, u1, will then represent the orientation of highest variance
in the dataset, the second principal component, u2 is the orientation with highest variance
when projecting the data onto the plane orthogonal to u1 and ﬁnally u3 is the cross product
u1 × u2. Note that the sign of eigenvectors can be chosen arbitrarily, so using the cross
product to ﬁnd the last component ensures that the orthonormal basis spanned by the three
unit vectors u1, u2 and u3 is right-handed. For the sake of determining the transformation
it does not matter that it is a right-handed basis, the important thing is that the basis is
the same handedness for the principal components of both distributions.

The matrix A grows quickly with the number of pixels, but it is very sparse — just Npixel2
of the 2Npixel3 entries are non-zero. The linear program has been implemented in the open-
source Python package Scipy and the Python API for the commercial optimization solver
Gurobi. In Table 3.1 the Scipy and Gurobi implementations can be seen compared using
HEALPix sphere tessellation with various Nside. It is seen that the Scipy implementation
runs out of memory for Nside > 4, while the Gurobi manages the problem until Nside > 16.
Both implementation becomes increasingly slow with increasing Nside, with the Gurobi
implementation having a runtime of 31⁄2 minutes for the largest problems it can handle at
Nside = 16. Even though the Gurobi solver clearly performs better, both implementation
can be useful, as the Gurobi implementation requires a commercial license. However, the
EMD is computed using Gurobi in this thesis.
The implementation of the χ2 metric is trivial as seen from its formulation in Eq. (2.17)
and as such it will not be discussed.

3.3.4
Distance Metric Choice
The initial implemented method for registering the orientation distributions relied on mul-
tiple computations of the distance between the orientation distributions to ﬁnd the best
registration. Some details of the initial implementation is expanded upon in Section 5.1,
but the implementation was abandoned in favor of the one which has been presented here,
as the results where simply not as good. Using this implementation, the distance metric is
used solely to give a metric for the dissimilarity between the ﬁber orientation distributions
after they have been registered to a common space, and as such only one computation of
the distance needs to be performed when comparing two modelled orientation distributions.
This removes some of the relevance of the computational considerations that have been
presented here, both in the choice between using the Scipy and Gurobi implementation of
EMD, but also as to whether to use the EMD or χ2. However, in Table 3.1 a comparison
between the time required to compute EMD and χ2 is seen for various Nside, where it is
seen that χ2 is much faster to compute than EMD and the runtime changes very little with
increasing Nside.
As discussed in Section 2.4.1 and 2.4.2, the χ2 values will be in the range (0; 2) and EMD
will be in the range (0; π
2 ). Knowing this makes it easy to normalize the metrics to the
range (0; 1) going forward, to have easily comparable metrics.
To get an idea of the behavior of the metrics, Figure 3.6 on page 22 illustrates how χ2 and
EMD measures distances between distributions with diﬀerent mean and concentration. It
can be seen that EMD gives close to the same values for Nside 2, 4 and 8, with the values
for 2 being a bit less smooth. This seems to indicate that there is not a lot to be gained
from using Nside higher than 4 when calculating EMD. This also shows that there is not
a lot to be gained from using Gurobi in favor of the open-source Scipy, as Scipy can be
used to compute EMD up to Nside = 4, where it seems to be stable. As the χ2 does not
depend on the distance between bins, it quickly rises to values near its maximum and then
ﬂattens, when the angle is varied, as seen in Figure 3.6a. For the EMD it is seen that it
rises almost linear with angle between distributions.
EMD is used as the dissimilarity measure going forward.
This choice is made on the
background that computation time is not really an issue, that EMD is very simple to
interpret and that EMD changes almost linearly with angular displacement.
Pipeline
The ﬁnal pipeline consists of determining ﬁber orientation in all ﬁber voxels using ST,
and ﬁtting a WMM initialized based on the orientation histogram to model the underlying
ﬁber orientation distribution. Registration between ﬁber orientation distributions modelled
from diﬀerent images are conducted by making a rotation based on the principal axes
of the ﬁber orientations and then a small rotation based on the modelled distributions.
Finally, the dissimilarity is determined between ﬁber orientation distributions by using
EMD implemented in Gurobi or Scipy depending on availability of the Gurobi license.
In the next chapter results based on this pipeline will be presented.

Discussion
When making computations on these images with voxel count up to 109 (e.g. as seen in
Table 4.4), the ﬁrst concern is to have all the data in memory. Both the image, but also all
the calculations to obtain the results. The computations of the results seen in Section 4.3
and 4.4 required approximately 100 GB memory to obtain. The results has been obtained
using a computer with 500 GB RAM and Intel® Xeon® E5-2643 v4 CPU. Most of the
pipeline has not been optimized for parallel processing, so some time could deﬁnitively be
cut of the one hour needed to obtain some of the results.
The results for testing the pipeline seen in Section 4.2 matches almost perfect, which is a
prerequisite for the pipeline to work. Besides noting that, it is not of much interest to go
further into those results.
The two composite images seen in Section 4.3 are a bit diﬀerent in nature, which is evident
from the histograms (Figure 4.9), where the three minor orientations are very feint in
image B compared to image A. Another notable diﬀerence, which is not immediately
obvious from the images or orientation histograms, is that each of the images seem to
have a cluster with a much lower concentration than the other three, which for image A
is ﬁtted with kernel number 1 and for image B is kernel number 2. This suggests that
there is some diﬀerence between ﬁber orientation distributions of the two images, as this
low-concentration orientation cluster is shifted approx. 90° from the major direction in
image A and approx. 45° from the major direction in image B, which can be seen from
Table 4.5. However, this could be an artifact of the background noise, where it has been
seen that one kernel tends to ﬁt to the noise.
The two cotton images seen in Section 4.4 are mostly similar, but as mentioned, the
distribution for the untreated sample converges to a slightly smaller angle between the
orientations at 79° vs. 87° for the enzyme treated sample. This diﬀerence is most likely
due to the fact that concentrations are very low, which will give a mean estimate with
higher variance — just like for N Gaussian distributed random variable with variance σ2,
the variance of the sample mean is σ2
N . A similar relationship is bound to apply here as
well, so a more exact orientation can be estimated by simply having a larger sample.
The speciﬁc metric for the dissimilarity between orientation distributions are a bit hard to
interpret without further context. It might be more valuable to use relative to other images,
i.e. to ﬁnd which images have the most similar ﬁber orientation distributions in a network
of images. That said, the relation between the three EMD metrics computed, 4.64 · 10−6
for the test images, 0.0656 for the composite material images and 0.0200 for the cotton
images are in correspondence with what has been observed, i.e. the score is very close to 0
for the test images and the highest score is observed for the composite images, where most
diﬀerence is seen upon inspection of the images and the ﬁber orientation histograms.

CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
Anders Nebbelunde
In Chapter 4 the main output from the pipeline is represented as a classiﬁed image, where
the classes are corresponding to each other between the two images. A similar result could
be obtained more directly without having to ﬁt a WMM by following the pipeline up to the
point where the mean orientations are estimated using the histogram peaks described in
Section 3.2.3. The distance between each orientation and the estimated mean orientation
can be used to classify the ﬁber orientations and the classes can be matched between
images by a registrations as described in Section 3.3. In that context, I think it is worth
noting that the main results are not just those images, but the statistics parameterizing
the ﬁber orientation distributions and the distance estimated between ﬁber orientation
distributions.
5.1
Alternative orientation registration
As mentioned in Section 3.3.4, an implementation has been made which did not use PCA
for registering the orientation distributions. It works by pairing the kernels in two mod-
elled orientation distributions by an exhaustive search over all combinations. For each
combination, a rigid transformation is used as described in Section 3.3.2 and the dissim-
ilarity between the resulting orientation distributions is measured, which is why multiple
distances had to be calculated. The main problems with that method is that it is diﬃcult
to determine what vector representation to use for each orientation distribution, as the
vector representation has to be the same between two distributions in order to do the
registration.
What turned out to work best is the presented solution, were two orientation distributions
are initially registered using PCA based on all orientation vectors. Afterwards the ﬁtted
mean orientation vectors are assumed to be close to each other for similar images, which
is why they are used to optimize the registration.
5.2
Further Work
Besides some work in decreasing the computation time, e.g. by solving the independent
eigenvector systems in parallel, some other areas have been identiﬁed, where further work
or research could beneﬁt the results and the ease at which they are obtained.
Input parameters
The pipeline has some input parameters, some of which can poten-
tially be estimated unsupervised. The ﬁbers in the images have been segmented by simply
selecting a threshold by inspection of the bimodal histogram of voxel intensities. This
threshold could readily be selected using Otsu’s method (Otsu, 1979).
The number of kernels for the WMM are chosen by inspection of the ﬁber orientation
histogram, where numerous ways of automatically determining the number of clusters
exist, e.g. by ﬁnding the number of model parameters, k, which minimizes the Akaike
information criterion (AIC) or Bayesian information criterion (BIC) (Schwarz, 1978)

Structure tensor
For preprocessing, alternatives to the Gaussian ﬁlter have been sug-
gested, as linear ﬁlters tend to blur the ﬁber edges, which can lead to an overestimation of
the object size. An alternative for removing noise before ﬁtting the ST is to use a median
ﬁlter (Chapelle et al., 2018), which has not been explored here. Furthermore the kernel for
estimating the gradients for the ST used here is a 3D extension of the simple Sobel kernel
(Engel, Hadwiger, Kniss, Rezk-Salama, & Weiskopf, 2006, p. 112). Alternatives have not
been explored, but an interesting one to test would be the Scharrr kernel (Scharr, Black,
& Haussecker, 2003).
Fiber orientation distribution model
The EM algorithm employed to ﬁt the WMM
converges to a local minima, i.e. not necessarily the global minimum. For Gaussian mix-
ture models (GMM), this is often1 solved by ﬁtting multiple models using diﬀerent random
initializations and using the ﬁt with the largest likelihood. The same approach can readily
be applied here as an alternative to the initialization methods presented in Section 3.2.3.
Using such an initialization method combined with one of the aforementioned kernel num-
ber estimations would naturally result in the need to ﬁt many models. It is notable that
the model ﬁtting is generally the slowest part of the pipeline in the current implementa-
tion. An alternative to the EM algorithm used to ﬁt the WMM exists by using Bayesian
estimation of WMM with variational inference. The variational inference approach pre-
sented by Taghia and Leijon (2016) speciﬁcally claims the approach is better in practice
that the implemented EM approach by Sra and Karp (2013), as the number of kernels
self-regulates. Besides self-regulation of the number of kernels, the results from the vari-
ational inference approach seems to be comparable to the used EM approach. Another
unexplored alternative is the Bingham distribution (Mardia & Jupp, 2000, p. 181). The
Bingham distribution is very similar to the Watson distribution, but it can have elliptical
contours on the sphere rather than the circular contours of the Watson distribution. The
theoretical justiﬁcation for using mixture models of Bingham distributions are however
lacking, e.g. in Glover, Bradski, and Rusu (2012) a mixture of Bingham distributions is
ﬁtted by iteratively ﬁtting a Bingham distribution, B∗, to a dataset, and removing the
points with a high posterior with respect to B∗, before trying to ﬁt another Bingham
distribution. It is unknown whether using a mixture of Bingham distribution would be a
better model, but when comparing the ﬁber orientation histograms and ﬁtted orientation
distributions for the multi-cube images in Section 4.3, it is noted that the shapes are not
exactly the same, which indicates that elliptical contours could be a better ﬁt.

Conclusion
The main objectives for this study were to provide statistics for characterizing the ﬁber
orientation distribution of a CT image of a ﬁber material and to give a metric of the distance
between two ﬁber orientation distributions estimated from diﬀerent samples. Both of these
have been accomplished. Statistics for characterizing a ﬁber orientation distribution are
represented by the mean, µi, concentration, κi and prior, φi, which are found by ﬁtting a
WMM using an EM-algorithm to a set of orientations extracted using ST. The distance
between two such ﬁber orientation distributions are estimated using EMD found by solving
a linear program. I think the main use case for this would be to investigate relative change,
e.g. in the case of the cotton samples, multiple enzyme washing processes could be tested
and for each a distance from a control can be estimated using EMD to give a metric for
which process inﬂuences the ﬁber orientations the most.
Some areas to do further work have been identiﬁed, notably the time can be decreased by
looking into optimizing the WMM implementation and some more work should probably
be done to make an eﬃcient implementation of ST in Python, as the current one does
not utilize any parallel processing. Besides computational considerations, WMM needs to
be further explored in modelling ﬁber orientation distributions along with other models
for the ﬁber orientation distribution, e.g. the mentioned Bingham distribution, with ellip-
tical contours on the sphere, rather than the circular contours modelled by the Watson
distribution.

The purpose of this report is to show an example of how to extract ﬁber directions from a
3D image using structure tensors and visualize the ﬁber direction distribution using the faces of
a tilled icosahedron as bins.
The project has been implemented in Python and consist primarily of the modules for
structure tensor and the icosahedron based orientation histogram. The structure tensor im-

Structure tensor
The structure tensor can be used to ﬁnd ﬁber directions when the image has a good contrast
between the ﬁbers and the background. The implemented approach works for just one ﬁber
scale.

For computing the structure tensor, Sw, the image gradient is required. For this purpose

gradient is very susceptible to noise, and this σ should be chosen based on the noise level of the
image. The gradient, ∇I(x), is computed using convolution of the smoothed image with a 3D
Sobel kernel.
The eigenvectors of Sw(x; ρ) can be interpreted as the distribution of gradient directions in
a ρ-scaled vicinity of x. Within a ﬁber the largest intensity change is expected to be orthogonal
to the ﬁber direction, and as such, the eigenvector, e1, corresponding to the smallest eigenvalue,
λ1, is the primary direction to the ﬁber.

Notes on implementation
Looking at Eq. (1) and (2), it is seen that the structure tensor is
3 × 3, symmetric and real valued. In this case the eigenvalues can be computed eﬃciently using
a simple algorithm. (Smith, 1961) Also because of the symmetric structure, only 6 values needs
to be stored rather than 9.
Furthermore, the only eigenvector of interest is the one corresponding to the smallest eigen-
value, which can also be utilized in a custom implementation of the solution to the eigenvalue
problem.
This approach was tested against a generic eigensystem solver, numpy.linalg.eigh, which
utilizes Hermitian symmetry, i.e. a matrix which is equal to its conjugate transpose, A = ¯
AT —
a weaker symmetry than the the matrix symmetry utilized, A = AT . On an image of size (167,

described approach solves the eigensystem in 1.9 seconds on the same hardware, which translates

Orientation histogram
A simple way to create as histogram of direction would be to create rectangular bins based
on the azimuthal angle, θ, and polar angle, φ, which can be derived from each vector. Such a
histogram would suﬀer from the fact, that bins near the polar regions would represent a much
smaller area of the sphere, than bins near the equatorial region.
Instead, the orientation histogram is based on the regular icosahedron, Figure 1a.
The
icosahedron is used as it consists of equally sized equilateral triangles, each of which can be
easily split into more equilateral triangles for a better resolution. The icosahedron does not
have poles, and as such does not have a strong dependency on the axis of the image.

Binning orientation vectors
A non-zero vector w is binned in two steps, ﬁrst ﬁnding the
face the vector points towards in the original icosahedron (Figure 1a) and then ﬁnding the
subface resulting from the tilling.
The face is identiﬁed by ﬁnding the face with the three vertices for which the dot product
between w and the vertex is largest. This is because the dot product is largest when the angle
between the two vectors is smallest. When arranging all the 12 vertices in a 12 × 3 matrix V
and the N vectors to be binned in a N × 3 matrix W all the dot products can be calculated as
V W T .

be found, when the barycentric coordinates, α, β, γ, of a point is known within the face. This

Final thoughts and future work
Visualization
Going forward with this project, I would look into improving several aspects
of the work. The visualization of the orientation distribution histogram is crude. Notably the
color on each face can be interpolated between vertices to give a smooth and more coherent
look. This could probably be achieved by using the Mayavi library, rather than the current
implementation, that relies on the Matplotlib library, which is very lacking in 3D plotting
abilities. However I had several issues with the Mayavi library, which seems to be hardware
related. Furthermore the histogram needs a good visualization of axis directions.
Structure tensor
It would be interesting to compare the structure tensor implementation
with other Python implementations, but none seem readily available, e.g. the structure tensor
implementation in the scikit-image library is only available for the 2D case1.
The struc-
ture tensor could be extended by looking into GPU based computation and only compute the
structure tensor for voxels inside a mask.

Varying face sizes
The sphere tilling splits the edges in ν pieces of equal lengths, but when

could no longer be computed using Eq. (3) and (4).

an expression for the face sizes as function of ν or looking into the distribution of the face sizes
for various ν.
