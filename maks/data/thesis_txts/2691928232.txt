Abstract
This thesis discusses the smoke production of a fire, with focus on the importance of
the soot yield, and the geometric- and technical design. A thorough study has been
conducted, in order to evaluate the minimum soot yield value of 0.06 g/g, published
in the guidelines for BK3 in the Danish Building Regulations in 2018, while two types
of FDS models are created, in order to study the geometric- and technical design.
Firstly, The building regulations in Denmark has been studied, in order to achieve
basic knowledge of fire safety engineering in Denmark. Here, the importance of the
heat of combustion was established, which, according to the guidelines in BR18, are
set to a minimum value of 20 MJ/kg.
Next, with a focus on the soot yield, building regulations in other relevant countries
were studied, in order to compare the course of action, concerning the soot yield and
heat of combustion. The study showed a minimum soot yield of 0.06 g/g are likewise
used in Sweden, while a soot yield of 0.07 g/g is to be used in both New Zealand and
Australia. Building regulations in countries like Great Britain, Norway, and Hong
Kong does not apply a specific minimum soot yield value, hence these countries have
more free rain, concerning the production of species.
The study lead to an investigation of the composition of combustible materials for
different occupancies. It was clearly shown, that wood and plastic are the two most
dominant combustible materials and with a larger percentage of plastic, the soot yield
will increase and more smoke will be produced.
It is concluded, that a soot yield of 0.06 g/g is conservative for most occupancies,
hence a recommendation is that for an effective heat of combustion of 20 MJ/kg,
oﬀices, schools, and hospitals could use a soot yield value of 0.05 g/g, while hotels
and motels should use a soot yield value of 0.07 g/g.
Concerning the geometric- and technical design, respectively, a simple and advanced
model, in relation to the geometry, were created in the CFD program FDS. Both
models were tested with soot yield values 0.02 g/g, 0.04 g/g, and 0.06 g/g, and the
different soot yields were tested with both natural and mechanical smoke ventilation,
hence 12 simulations were run.
The simple models showed that over time, an upper and lower smoke layer will be
created, yet an increase in the soot yield will not change the height of this layer. The
technical design did not have much influence in the simple models.

Abstract
Finally, the results of the advanced model showed, that a spill plume in an open
atrium, will significantly increase the smoke production, hence an increase in the soot
yield will have much more influence on the production of soot compared to a simple
geometry. The technical design is also seen to have great influence, thus mechanical
smoke ventilation can be used to control the movement of the smoke in order to create
better conditions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
While designing a building, it is important to account for the safety of the people
inside the building. One important aspect is the case of fire, where it is essential to
make sure that people can safely escape the building, before any critical conditions
occurs.
Temperature and radiation are two factors which, when too high, will become critical
for humans, while also the smoke exposure from the fire, or more specifically the
combustion products, can become critical. Carbonmonoxide (CO) and Carbondioxide
(CO2) are both harmful species produced during a fire, and thus a high concentration
of either of these two gases will be critical.
Other harmful gasses might also be
produced, yet it depends on the type of fuel feeding the fire.
Another product normally produced during a fire is soot/ carbon (C). The carbon
element is in itself not harmful, but the reason for the black color of the smoke, hence
having a large impact on the visibility. For an eﬀicient escape, it is essential to be
able to locate the exits, and the soot yield is therefore of great importance.
Today, fire safety engineers in Denmark have to follow the Danish Building Regula-
tions from 2018 (BR18), which include guidelines setting a few acceptance criteria.
These criteria cover the just mentioned critical conditions, namely the temperature,
radiation, and smoke exposure. With a focus on the smoke exposure, and more specif-
ically the soot yield, the guidelines for BR18 generally demands a soot production
of minimum 0.06 g/g in Fire Class 3 (BK3) (0.10 g/g for sprinkler controlled fires
in BK3). Such a value has not previously been specified in Denmark, thus creating
challenges that in some situations can be diﬀicult to overcome, as the value is higher
than what mostly has been seen in the industry in Denmark.
In reference to the previous paragraph, the minimum soot yield value of 0.06 g/g is
therefore questioned in this study, and possible alterations for the criteria are inves-
tigated. The study includes looking at specific soot potentials for different materials,
and the composition of these, while also looking at how the soot is handled in other
countries.
Another aspect of the study concerns to what extend the geometric- and technical
design interferes with the dispersion and production of soot, and thereby its influence
on the visibility.
The first hypothesis is that the geometric design will have much influence on the

features used in this study. It will explain the meaning of combustion products, smoke
potential, optical density, and visibility, while introduce the key words, namely soot
yield and effective heat of combustion.
2.1
General about Fire Safety Engineering
In order to build and understanding of the soot yield of a fire, it is important to
define the properties of fire. Generally, three things must be present for a fire to
occur, namely oxygen, heat, and fuel. Oxygen is one of the main gases in the Earth’s
atmosphere, thus it is always present in the initial state of a building fire. Fuel can
consist of many different materials of either solid, liquid, or gaseous forms, hence
there are many different fuel sources in a building, such as chairs, computers, books,
detergents, etc. When heat is applied to one of these fuels, in a room full of air, a fire
might ignite. The fire will then grow larger as long as there are still a suﬀicient amount
of fuel and oxygen present, and due to oxidation, different combustion products will
be produced.
The development of a fire can generally be described as shown in figure 2.1. First
the fire ignites and the temperature starts to slowly increase, while the fire grows.
The larger the fire becomes, the faster the temperature increases until, in most cases,
flashover is reached. This is the state of the fire that occurs just before a steady
state, and the temperature in the enclosed fire compartment has normally arrived at
500-600oC. When steady state is reached, the fire is said to be fully developed, since
the energy released in the enclosure has reached it maximum. The temperature in the
room will now stay somewhat constant and have a value between 700-1200oC. After
a while the fire will burn out and the temperature will start to decay [26].
Generally, building fire regulations concerns three things, namely the life safety of the
occupants, the structural stability of the building, and safety of the rescue service.

CHAPTER 3
Soot Yield Study
This chapter will explain how the soot yield, the effective heat of combustion and
the visibility is to be handled according to the guidelines in the Danish Building
Regulations (BR18). Likewise Building regulations in other countries will be studied,
in order to figure out how they are handling these matters. Last, a study of the
composition of materials inside a building will also be conducted.
3.1
The Danish Building Regulations
Buildings in Denmark must comply with the Danish Building Regulations (BR18).
BR18 Chapter 5, §82 - §158 concerns the different fire safety requirements, thus to
satisfy these requirements, the building must be classified in one of four Fire Classes
which are displayed in BR18 Chapter 27. BR18 chapter 29, 30, 32 and 34 describes
how the documentations should be developed, and which certifications are necessary
to act as a certified fire safety consultant [36]. The regulations are also supplemented
with appendixes and guidelines, to help fire proof different types of buildings. Besides
complying with the rules set in BR18, also the Danish standards (DS) must be obeyed.
The guidelines for BR18 includes a description on how to implement CFD modelling,
which will be addressed in chapter 5
3.1.1
Fire Classes
The four Fire Classes which are used in Denmark are simply classified as Fire Class
1-4 (BK1-BK4), and they are specified by using the Categories of use and Risk Classes
mentioned in BR18 Chapter 5. A building is to be divided into one or more building
sections, where each section will be categorized and classified. A building section can
include one or more Categories of use, and one or more Risk Classes.
The Category of use is the first to be decided, where one is to look at whether the
building section is decorated with sleeping space, if the occupants are aware of the
escape possibilities, if the occupants can bring themselves to safety and how many
people the single rooms are designed for. Different combinations of these four aspects
creates six Categories (Category of use 1-6). For a better understanding, the Category
of use are displayed in appendix A.

3.4
Composition of Materials
Chapter 2 shows that when looking at the soot yield of a building, the composition of
the combustible materials inside the room has a large influence. As seen in equation
2.7, the yield is defined by the mass of the species produced and the mass of the fuels,
hence it is the weight of the combustible materials, which will have a large influence
on the soot produced.
Literature provides a few different survey methodologies concerning the composition
of combustible materials in a room. However, the surveys focus on the importance
of the fire load, and even though it does mention its importance when looking at the
smoke production, it is not specifically considered in relation to the soot. Despite the
lack of interest, the composition studies can however be used when the smoke is in
focus.
3.4.1
Survey Methodologies
Before going into depth with displayed material compositions in literature, it is im-
portant to look at the possible different survey methodologies. It is the weights of the
combustible materials which are of importance, and to find these, two essential meth-
ods are used, namely the inventory and the weighing method, however a combination
of the two is also be beneficial. Both of the two methods are conducted using survey
methodologies, where the inventory method is performed using the volume and the
density of the materials, and the weighing method is performed by simply weighing
the materials. According to the National Fire Protection Association (NFPA) [31], a
fire load survey must be conducted using one of these two methods, or a combination
of the two, hence when investigating smoke production, it would be reasonable to
likewise use these inventory and/ or weighing method.
When looking at the composition of materials, it is also important to distinguish
between the fixed and the content combustible materials. Fixed materials are defined
as the structural elements, the interior finish and the concealed installations, hence
all of the building materials and the installations. The content materials are defined
as all the movable contents and furnishings materials, hence everything which can be
moved without changing the building. A sum of the fixed- and the content materials
will provide the total combustible material inside the building or room [39].
In 2013, Ehab Zalok and James Eduful published a paper concerning the assessment
of fuel load survey methodologies and its impact on fire load data [39]. Other survey
methodologies than the inventory and weighing method, were also discussed, nonethe-
less they establish that the inventory and weighing methods are the most accurate.
The assessment were based on a survey conducted in five different oﬀice buildings
with a total of 103 rooms.
Despite the fact, that the inventory and weighing method are the best survey method-
ologies, Zalok and Eduful point out some uncertainties concerning the precision of the
==PAGE:48==
28
3 Soot Yield Study
measurements. The inventory method is not very precise for irregular shaped objects,
and it might not be easy to find the exact density of a specific material. The weighing
method has a diﬀiculty in accounting for fixed combustible materials, since it might
be impractical, while it is also likely to disrupt business activities or invade privacy
while conducting the survey in buildings which are in use. Both methods lacks in
precision concerning the weights of objects containing multiple materials, when in
some cases materials might not be combustible. Here, a good example is steel, which
is used for many structural elements and furniture.
Product descriptions of both
fixed and content materials are of great value, however they would have to be very
specific, and they might not be easy to find, especially for older buildings and/ or for
buildings already in use.
It can be derived that the accuracy of the two survey methodologies will not reach
perfection, and it is therefore important for the sample sizes of the surveys to be large
and to be critical when analyzing the results. For this reason, most literature studies,
concerning the composition of materials, are comparing the mean values of the fire
load densities (some times fire loads) with the 95th percentile while also looking at
the standard deviation, in order to assess the reliability of the survey.
Zalok and Eduful completed their study in regards to the inventory method, the
weighing method, and a combination method. It was clearly seen that the inventory
method resulted in the highest fire load densities, while the weighing method resulted
in the lowest fire load densities. The combination method showed results in between,
and is stated to be the most appropriate method.
Regarding the fixed, content, and total fire load values, Zalok and Eduful also looked
at the mean values, concerning which of the categories had the largest contribution
to the fire. It was clear that the content materials contributed significantly more
to the fire load and fire load densities than the fixed materials. It has to be noted,
however, that the survey does not mention what type of structural material was used
in the oﬀice buildings. Steel and concrete are not combustible, and thus these types
of structures will result in a smaller contribution of the fixed materials than a wood
structure.
When evaluating the composition of the materials, it must be decided which materials
are of most relevance. This might vary with different building types, however the most
frequently appearing materials are textiles, plastics, wood and paper. In some cases
rubber, leather and foods are also common, but it depends on the building type.
Furthermore, it can be relevant to regard the matter of different types of plastics.
Plastic can be produced of different substances, e.g. polyethylene, polypropylene and
polystyrene. Plastics are also seen in the shape of foams, where polyurethane is the
most common in both flexible and rigid forms. These substances, shown in table 2.1,
are seen to have very different soot yields while also the heat of combustion varies.

5.1
Introduction to FDS and SMV
In the fire safety engineering industry, Computational Fluid Dynamics is a useful tool,
when looking at the fire driven fluid flows. If working with a larger area or with an
advanced geometry, computer simulations can be very helpful, since hand calculations
including, two-zone models, can be diﬀicult to perform, and even if it is possible, the
results will most likely be unacceptably far away from reality. The numerical course
of action is thereby very constructive, and a great tool for the fire safety engineers.
One of the most used CFD programs is the so called Fire Dynamics Simulator de-
veloped by NIST. The program has been verified and validated thoroughly, where
simulation models have been compared to real life experiments. It has been devel-
oped in order to solve practical fire problems in fire safety engineering, while also to
provide a tool to study fundamental fire dynamics and combustion [29]. The pro-
gram is thereby stated to be a good fit for the investigation in this project, hence
FDS version 6.7.5 and its visualizing program Smokeview version 6.7.15 will be the
software used in this project.
The program will help investigate factors such as the temperature, velocity, volume
flow and most importantly the visibility, for two different building types with two
different smoke ventilation systems. To make sure that the accuracy of the models is
suﬀicient, they have been developed in accordance with the guidelines for BR18 [4].
5.2
CFD Modeling of a Fire According to the
guidelines in BR18
In Denmark, CFD modeling can only be used for documentation in BK3 and BK4
(see table 3.1), and the guidelines are therefore described in Chapter 5, Chapter 8:
Verification [4].
The verification specifies that the purpose of the model must be
stated, while the reasoning for the choice of materials, gird size, running time etc.
must be described.
The geometry of the model must be presented to get a clear understating of the
dimensions and volumes, due to its large influence on especially smoke spread. This
==PAGE:64==

includes both outer and inner geometry such as walls, stairs, decks, columns etc. Also
the geometry of openings and the technical design are of great importance.
The properties of the building materials also have to be carefully considered, especially
factors such as density and thermal conductivity. These values can specially have a
great impact on the smoke-gas-temperature and the radiation in the building
The mesh of the CFD model is essential for the accuracy of the results. For larger
models, it can be beneficial for the grid to be coarse far away from a fire while finer
close to the fire or in other vulnerable areas. However, in FDS there must be a smooth
transition between the two grids, meaning a coarse grid of size 0.6cm must align with
a finer grid of for example size 0.3cm, while a grid size of size 0.4cm will create a
grid which will not align with anything, and the program will therefore not be set up
correctly.

The air flow in and out of buildings are also of great importance, because if the air
flow is too large, it might be uncomfortable for humans to pass these openings of to
walk through them. The guidelines do not indicate a maximum air flow for natural
ventilation, however for mechanical ventilation the air flow must have a maximum
mean value of 5 m/s over each opening, where people will either escape from or pass
by.
Finally, for the openings to work effectively, they must be placed with a certain
distances from the edge of the mesh. This distance varies depending of the grid size
however the guidelines express a minimum value of 9 gridcells.
5.3
Best Practices
Besides following the guidelines for BR18 when creating a CFD model, it can be
beneficial to look further into other studies. In Denmark a CFD Best Practice was
conducted in order to help engineers set up their CFD models, and it was created by
a larger work group [13]. Likewise, a work-group in Sweden developed a Best Practice
guideline for the proper use of CFD-models when performing a ASET analysis [32].
The Swedish work-group had no representative from the authorities and they discuss
the many choices which must be made while conducting a CFD model.
The two Best Practice studies support what is written in Chapter 5, Chapter 8:
Verification in BR18 concerning CFD modelling, hence the selected measures are well
studied and is therefore a reasonable guide. On another account, the Swedish Best
Practice states that even though they apply recommendations, one should always use
engineering judgement when conducting and evaluating a CFD model.
5.4
Uncertainties with CFD and FDS
When simulating a CFD model, many approximations are made both in the set-up
of the software and from the user. For this reason, the user must be critical when
analyzing the results of the model.
With a focus on the smoke concentration, a concern could be that the visibility is
over-reported, hence the model indicates much more smoke than what would actually
be produced. At a workshop concerning FDS (and PyroSim) from February 2021,
a bias for the smoke concentration in FDS is measured to be 2.59 [28]. This is an
extremely high bias, meaning that the program does in fact over-report the smoke
concentration, and with a pretty large amount. The workshop also comments, that
the FDS model does not take all of the smoke deposited on walls and ceilings into
account, while referring to the validation guide where it is written that the smoke
concentration predicted by FDS were up to five times greater than measured smoke
concentrations compared to experiments.

or mechanical smoke ventilation are activated after 60 seconds. The time delay is due
to the time it takes for the windows to fully open, and for the MSV to register and
detect the fire, while also so the MSV does not to make any noise while the alarm
informs the people of the fire. Further, the mechanical smoke ventilation is set to
reach it’s maximum volume flow after further 30s. These values are all set by the
recommendations from DBI, where it is stated that it must be fully functional after
90s [11].

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Initially, it can be concluded that it is not only the soot yield value which is of
great importance, when looking into the smoke production of a fire in regards to the
visibility. The corresponding heat of combustion is seen to be equally as important,
thus the relation between these two values must carefully be considered.
Furthermore, it is concluded that the composition of the combustible materials also
have much impact on the smoke production of a fire. The two most frequent com-
bustible materials are wood and plastic (of different types), and it is clearly seen that
plastic will produce much more soot than wood, hence it is important to take both
materials into account. For some occupancies, other materials might also be relevant
to take into account, yet a specific composition of materials for different occupancies
is not easy to establish. It can, however, be stated that of the occupancies considered
in this study, it is generally most commercial premises and hotels which will have the
most critical composition of combustible materials.
The guidelines for the Danish Building Regulations applies a specific soot yield value
of 0.06 g/g and a heat of combustion of 20 MJ/kg for a well-ventilated fire. This is
based on the Swedish Building Regulations, which assumes a 50/50 composition of
wood and plastic. In New Zealand, a soot yield value of 0.07 g/g and a belonging
heat of combustion of 20 MJ/kg is used, and this is based on a burning arm chair
in a dwelling experiment compared with CFD simulations. It is therefore assumed
that the combustible materials includes a lot of plastic. Other countries around the
world seems to have a more flexible course of action towards the smoke characteristics,
meaning they do not have a specific soot yield or heat of combustion value that must
be used. Some countries do however point out that fires in smaller rooms will in most
cases become under-ventilated, and then the restrictions specified in BR18 will not
be usable, and another course of action should be considered.
Deriving the production of soot in relation to time for different occupancies, the
guidelines for BR18 and other recommendations, it is seen that most occupancies
will produce less soot per second than what can be derived from the guidelines for
BR18.
It is only very specific commercial premises such as shoe storage, storage
areas in general, and computer stores which can challenge the soot production per
second derived from the guidelines, together with motels and hotels. It can thereby
be concluded that a soot yield value of 0.06 g/g and a belonging heat of combustion
value of 20 MJ/kg, might be slightly conservative for most occupancies, yet it might
==PAGE:114==
94
7 Conclusion
still be reasonable for some relevant occupancies. Generally, for all occupancies in
this study, except motels and hotels, a soot yield value of 0.05 g/g would be suﬀicient
with an effective heat of combustion of 20 MJ/kg.
The FDS studies with focus on the geometric design proved that for a simple geometry,
which is rectangular and without any disturbances, an increase in the soot yield will
in fact have impact on when critical conditions will occur. Over time, an upper and
lower smoke-layer will be created, yet the height where the two layers separate is the
same in spite of different soot yield values. The upper layer is seen to become more
dense over time, and only when it has reached a minimum visibility, the smoke will
fall and spread in the lower part of the building.
When considering a more advanced geometric design, where a large atrium is connect-
ing four floors by an open center, an increase in the soot yield is seen to have much
impact on when critical conditions will occur. With a greater soot yield, the open
center will be filled with smoke much faster for the larger soot yields, making the
spill plume create more soot. Thus, the soot on the 1st-3rd floor becomes much more
dense with an increase in the soot yield, while the smoke will also spread quicker.
The FDS studies with focus on the technical design proved that for the simple geom-
etry, the MSV will create more turbulence in the lower part of the building, meeting
critical conditions earlier than the NSV. However, over time when the upper smoke-
layer becomes very dense, it is the MUA openings which has the greatest influence on
the turbulence. It must be noted that the volume flow added to the MSV can have
much influence and might affect the outcome of the simulations.
Finally, when considering the technical design of the advanced model, the MSV is
seen to create better conditions compared to the NSV. The suction of the MSV will
make more smoke stay in the open center of the atrium, and critical conditions will
therefore be met later than if using NSV.
7.1
Future Work
Although the soot yield study has been thorough, it may be possible that literature
on national regulations or studies relevant for this topic has been overlooked. Such
literature might further improve and support this study.
The composition of other occupancies, such as dwellings or parking basements, might
be interesting to study, while along with more specific situations such as for example
specific exhibitions and industry buildings/ warehouses.
According to the FDS models studying the geometric design, it might have been
beneficial to study buildings where the geometries are more similar to each other.
The simple and advanced geometry studied here are very distinct, hence a simple
addition of for example down stands in the simple geometry might be interesting to
study, to see if this will likewise cause an upper and lower smoke-layer.
