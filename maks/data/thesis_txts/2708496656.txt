Abstract

Abstract
In the spring time Arctic rivers are subject to large variations in water level as the
thawing of the surrounding area take place. This event is known as the spring ﬂood.
This study uses data from the ICESat-2 satellite to monitor when the spring ﬂood oc-
curs. The use of laser altimetry performed by the ICESat-2 satellite facilitates previously
unattainable resolution when monitoring inland water surface heights.
The study covers the basics of altimetry, introduces the data used and covers the process-
ing steps needed for constructing a time series of changing water levels for an Arctic river.
The study investigates the correlation between the length of the used river segments and
the resulting temporal resolution achieved using ICESat-2 data. It was found that for a
100 kilometres segment an average temporal resolution of 20 days between each data point
was achieved.
The study focuses on the Mackenzie River in Canada and analyses segments on the main
stem of the river along with tributary river segments. Furthermore the study analyses
river segments on the Yukon River in Alaska, the Ob River in Russia, the Lena River in
Russia and the Yenisei River in Russia. A comparison of the timing of the spring ﬂood on
the ﬁve major Arctic rivers is presented.

Introduction
River systems are a vital part of earths climate. In newer times the monitoring of rivers
in Arctic regions has been of particular interest, as these can shed light on the implica-
tions of the earths climate changing. Furthermore the discharge into the arctic ocean has
large ramiﬁcations on the area covered by sea ice as the warmer freshwater aﬀect the heat
budget of the Arctic ocean[1].
Traditionally the surveillance of the water level has been performed by measuring stations
located along the river path. This naturally involves building and maintaining the sta-
tions which are costly. Because of this there has been a steady decline in the number of
measuring stations since the 1980’s[2].
Since the 1970’s radar altimetry has proven a strong tool in monitoring the characteristics
of oceans and ice sheets. The further development of radar altimetry hardware/processing
has enabled researchers to estimate the water level of inland water bodies. The ability to
determine the water level of inland water bodies using radar altimetry depends on many
conditions. The echoed waveform is highly aﬀected by the surrounding topography and
therefore it becomes diﬃcult to adequately track the water surface of inland water bodies.
Furthermore the return echo for a radar highly changes when encountering ice, making it
increasingly diﬃcult to determine the water surface level in arctic rivers.
With the ICESat-2 satellite, NASA has taken another approach to measuring distances.
The satellite uses a highly powerful laser emitting in the visual spectrum in order to mea-
sure distances. As the footprint is much smaller than that of a similar radar altimetry
mission, this eliminates the eﬀect of nearby topography.[3] This study will use the ICESat-
2 data to investigate the changing water surface level in arctic rivers.
Arctic rivers diﬀers from other rivers in the fact that they are situated in areas where ice is
prevalent. This in turn means that when the ice/snow in the surrounding area thaws the
rivers undergo a large variation in water level in the spring. This is known as the spring
ﬂood. Tracking when the spring ﬂood occurs is of high interest, as it can give information
about the changing arctic climate. Tracking when the spring ﬂood occurs require a high
temporal resolution. As the repeat period of ICESat-2 is 91 days, and this is not enough
to track when the spring ﬂood occurs, it is necessary to relate measurements on diﬀerent
locations along the river in question to one another.
The study will seek to answer the following questions.
How does the width of the river aﬀect the ability to measure the water surface level?
How does the length of the river segment aﬀect the temporal resolution of the con-
structed time series?
How accurate can the spring ﬂood be determined using data from ICESat-2?
Lastly the study will investigate the spatial correlation in water level variations of
Arctic rivers i.e.
investigating if the spring ﬂood occurs at similar times at diﬀerent
locations along the river, in tributary rivers and in neighbouring rivers.

3.1
Altimetry
Altimetry is the science of measuring distances using remote sensing systems. The method
originated in the 1970’s and was originally intended for use in oceanography. The principal
idea is quite simple and in many ways resemble that of sonar.
But where sonar uses
sound to ping objects of interest, altimetry operates using electromagnetic radiation. The
remote sensing system sends out a ping and waits for the return. The ping hits the object
in question and bounces back. The remote sensing system is then able to time how long
it has taken for the ping to go back and forth, thereby enabling the system to calculate
the distance to the object. Thus the formula for calculating the distance to the object
becomes.
d = δt · c
2
(1)
where δt is the two way travel time and c is the speed of light. Naturally c depends on
a lot of atmospheric conditions, and these need to be accounted for in order to properly
determine the distance. This especially hold true for satellite systems as the distances are
so large, and small changes to c amounts to large discrepancies in distance estimations.
Furthermore one has to take into account various geophysical corrections. When perform-
ing measurements over the ocean, one has to correct for the tides as well as atmospheric-
oceanic interactions such as the inverted barometer eﬀect and wind ﬁeld eﬀects. When
performing measurements over the land the main corrections one has to perform are that
of solid earth tides and the eﬀect from oceanic tide loading.
Figure 1: Illustration of how a satellite altimetry system could function. [5]
While this technique in principle sounds rather simple, building a remote sensing sys-
tem capable of doing this down to high precision surely is not.

3.2
ICESat-2
The ICESat-2, (Ice, Cloud and land Elevation Satellite-2) spacecraft was launched on the
15th of September 2018. Its main purpose consist of being able to measure the elevation of
ice sheets with a precision of a few centimetres. It has an orbit of 500 km altitude, ﬂying
at a 92-degree inclination with a 91 day repeat cycle.
The main instrument of the ICESat-2 spacecraft is the ATLAS(Advanced Topographic
Laser Altimeter System). This is the height measuring instrument responsible for trans-
mitting, receiving and recording the travel time of the photons.
Accompanying the ATLAS instrument on the ICESat-2 satellite are systems providing the
necessary power, propulsion, navigation, thermal control, data storage/handling, ground
communication etc. Since the primary function of the spacecraft is to measure heights
with a high precision it is necessary to know the exact position of the satellite in space.
The on board GPS allows the position to be known down to a precision of approximately
5 meters. This, along with regular ground calibration studies, enables the system to very
precisely determine where measurements are conducted. [3]
3.2.1
ATLAS
ATLAS measures surface height by emitting a laser pulse and then timing the photon travel
time. The system has a primary and a backup laser emitting a bright green laser with a
wavelength of 532 nanometers. ATLAS is equipped with a ﬁlter that only lets photons
with this particular wavelength through, thereby minimising the noise induced by sunlight
and other disturbances. ATLAS emits 10,000 laser pulses every second enabling it to take
measurements every 70 centimetres along the ground path of the satellite. The high data
resolution, generates more than 580 gigabytes of data every day.
Prior to being emitted to the ground the laser pulses go through a series of mirrors and
lenses ensuring that the wavelength and timing is as desired. Furthermore the lasers are
split into six beams, three primary and three secondary. [6]
Figure 2: Depiction of the beam constellation of ICESat-2. [7]
As Figure 2 illustrate the two lasers are split up into six beams. The beams are split

such that there are three beam pairs consisting of one primary and one secondary beam.
The primary and secondary beams are split by a distance of 90 meters and each beam

While the high data resolution and precise measurements are far superior to previous
satellite systems, using radar, there are some trade-oﬀs in using laser. Given that the
emitted light is in the visible spectrum the emitted photons can not penetrate clouds,
whereas the systems emitting in the microwave frequency can.
In summary: The spatial resolution of the measurements performed by ICESat-2 is far
superior compared to similar radar-based missions. The emitted light, being in the visible
light spectrum, however cannot penetrate clouds. This means that if it is cloudy, no valid
water surface data is recorded.

Data
The National Snow & Ice Data Center, NSIDC, publishes the ICESat-2 data in the
HDF5 format. The NSIDC publishes numerous data products, ranging from raw photon
cloud data(ATL03) to a mean sea surface height over larger areas.
4.1
ATL03
The ATL03 product contains the global geolocated photon data obtained by the ATLAS
instrument. The data product consists of latitude, longitude and elevation measurements
for each received photon. The data is split according to each of the six separate beams,
and along with the height measurements are ancillary data/statistics used for estimating
these values. ATL03 segments have a typical size of 3-4 GB of data and hold as much as
20 million measurements. [8]
4.1.1
Conﬁdence level
The ICESat-2 instruments measure a plethora of both signal photon events and back-
ground photon events caused by the Earths reﬂection of the Sun.
The signal ﬁnding
algorithm of the ICESat-2 assumes that the background photon events follow a Poisson
distribution.
Outliers from the Poisson distribution is therefore assumed to be signal
photon events. In order to detect this the algorithm constructs histograms wherein the
background photon events are randomly distributed and the signal photon events are
clustered together in few bins. This in turn is used to generate photon ellipsoidal heights,
relative to the WGS-84 ellipsoid.
Ensuring that the photon events end up in the right bins is a strenuous task when sig-
niﬁcant surface slopes are present. Thus the algorithm generates a ﬂag for each event
depending on whether the algorithm considers the event a background measurement or an
actual photon event. [7]

4.2
ATL13
The along-track surface water product for inland water bodies, or ATL13, provides water
surface height, standard deviations, wind speed etc. for lakes, rivers, reservoirs, bays and
for a near-shore buﬀer. As with the ATL03 product, the ATL13 product is published in
the HDF5 format. The ATL13 product enables the user to only download the data that
is pertinent for researching inland water bodies. The ATL13 data segments span as much
as 4 orbits of the earth and has a typical size of 20-70 MB, orders of magnitude smaller
than the ATL03 product.
The ATL13 product is derived from the ATL03 product. The inland water heights are
calculated for segments containing a minimum of 100 signal photon events. This ensures
that the water surface height is adequately tracked. Naturally this means that the spatial
resolution becomes lower. Whereas the ATL03 has a spatial resolution of 0.7 meters, the
ATL13 has a spatial resolution between 30 and 100 meters, depending on signal quality,
water conditions and atmospheric conditions.
The ATL13 algorithm takes the ATL03 product and applies masks ensuring that only
data pertaining inland water body information is saved. The masks only keep data from
crossings over inland water and adds a 100 meter buﬀer in order to be able to observe the
water/land interface.
In order to properly calculate the mean surface water level for each segment, the algo-
rithm ﬁrst estimates the volume scattering parameters of a longer segment(typically 1 to
3km). Furthermore the algorithm removes background photon events. All of this enables
the algorithm to calculate the surface water height, a coarse resolution water depth, wave
heights and the along track surface slope. The error associated with each ATL13 mea-
surement is estimated to be 6.1 cm.
The surface water height is provided both as height above the WGS 84 ellipsoid and as
height above the Earth Gravitational Model 2008. In this project I will be focusing on
the EGM2008.[10]

Processing/method
As mentioned in section 3.2 the ICESat-2 satellite has a repeat period of 91 days. Only
using data from the same river crossing would thus result in four measurements a year.
This naturally would not be able to estimate when the spring ﬂood occurs. Therefore I
not only have to make sure that the data used in my analysis is taken over the river, but
it is also necessary to relate measurements performed at diﬀerent locations on the river to
one another. To adequately do this I have to account for the diﬀerence in topography.
5.1
Method diagram

Processing
Page 11
To arrive at the ﬁnal product, a time series of changing water surface level, a number of
processing steps have to be applied. As the diagram in Figure 8 illustrates, the ﬁrst step
the algorithm takes is applying a shapeﬁle. The shapeﬁle is a simple polygon constructed
from a centerline segment that has a buﬀer added to it. The river centerline data comes
from the Global River Widths from Landsat (GRWL) database.[16] As the shapeﬁle for
the river segment is so simple, this will not eliminate the fact that some measurements
comes from the river banks and the surrounding area.
Hence the algorithm applies a
watermask to ensure that only measurements taken above the river surface is kept.
Now that the previous steps have ensured that only pertinent data are left, the algorithm
projects the data onto the river centerline. The centerline is split into points spaced 10
meters apart, and the data location is compared to these points. Each observation is then
allocated to the corresponding centerline point. The centerline used for this is the same
as the one used for creating the shapeﬁle.
An inverse problem is constructed to approximate the slope of the river. The slope can
then be used to move every data point along the centerline of the river, thereby correcting
for the discrepancies associated to the surface water level measurements being performed
at diﬀerent locations of the river. Finally a median for every day with pertinent data is
taken, and the time series of the surface water level can be constructed.
The steps taken in Figure 8 will be further explained in the following sections.

5.1.2
Slope correction
Now that the ATL13 data product has been conﬁrmed as being able to track the water
surface level, progress can be made in attaining a potential time series for the changes in
the water surface level of the river.
ICESat-2 has a repeat period of 91 days. Only getting data once every three months, in
combination with the fact that clouds obscure the laser from the ATLAS instrument, is
not enough to produce a time series capable of estimating when the spring ﬂood occurs.
Therefore it is necessary to compare water surface level measurements performed at diﬀer-
ent locations of the river. To do this it is imperative to ﬁrst estimate the slope of the river
segment in question. This is achieved by projecting every data point onto a centerline of
the river.

is expected to produce a spike in the water level. This means that the changes in water

to perform the slope estimation solely using months where the water level is somewhat
constant i.e. only using data from the winter months.

5.1.3
Time series
Now that the slope of the river has been estimated it is possible to correlate measure-
ments performed at diﬀerent locations along the river to one another. This is simply done
by applying the model found in section 5.1.2. Each observation had the slope element
subtracted from its height measurement and a median for each date was calculated. Fur-
thermore dates with less than 5 data points were removed, as it was deemed too uncertain
in estimating the water surface level suﬃciently.
Lastly error bars were added. The error was calculated by dividing the standard devia-
tion, for each date with data, by the square root of the number of data points for said
date. This is the error when assuming no correlation between data points. As the data
are highly correlated this is an under estimation of the error and it should be assumed
that the error is higher than the plotted error bars.

Future work
The processing method has proved a viable tool in assessing the changing water levels
in Arctic rivers. Further spatial analysis on the major Arctic rivers could lead to insight
into how the timing of the spring ﬂood occurs as one moves up-/downstream. Analysis on
other Arctic rivers would also be intriguing, for example Kolyma River.
Figure 52: Map of the Arctic watershed.[21]
Where the topography allows for adequate measurements by Sentinel-3 and other radar
missions it would be advantageous to combine the data from ICESat-2 with that of the
radar missions, thereby gaining a better temporal resolution for the time series of the
changing water level.

Conclusion
The Advanced Topographic Laser Altimeter System on board the ICESat-2 satellite
allows for levels of data acquisition unmatched by any other system.
It was found that the ATL13 inland water product, produced from the ATL03 photon
data, adequately estimated the water surface level. It was found that some data was lo-
cated along the river banks. A water occurrence mask was used to make sure that only
pertinent data was used in the water level assessment. The slope of the river was as-
sessed using a damped least squares method. Removing the topographic element from the
measurements allowed for comparison of water level measurements, performed at diﬀerent
locations along the river, thereby producing the desired time series of the changing water
level.
It was found that for a 100 kilometre segment one could expect a temporal resolution
of an average of 20 days between each data point. Increasing the length of the segment
naturally improved the temporal resolution i.e. at a length of 200 kilometre the aver-
age number of days between each data point would be 10 days. While a longer segment
naturally is desired this is not always feasible, as the characteristic of a river can change
dramatically over a longer distance. Nevertheless it is recommended having a segment
length of more than 300 kilometres, in order to get at least one data point per week.
An analysis on ﬁve continuous segments of the Mackenzie River was performed, and
it was found that the spring ﬂood occurred earlier as one moved upstream the river. This
was attributed to an increase in temperature as one moved south. Moreover it was found
that the signal of the spring ﬂood was attenuated when looking at the delta area of the
river.
It was found that for narrower river segments the algorithm can still properly estimate
the water level changes but is limited in the temporal resolution. This proves to be an
issue as smaller rivers have much faster responses to an increase in precipitation, leading
to much more variability in the water surface level. As such the algorithm can only track
peaks in the water surface level if the ICESat-2 satellite happen to perform a passing at
the time of the peak.
Lastly an analysis on the ﬁve major Arctic rivers was performed. The Analysis looked
at river segments from the Mackenzie River in Canada, the Yukon River in Alaska, the
Ob River in Russia, The Yenisei River in Russia and the Lena River also in Russia. It was
found that for the Mackenzie River, the Yukon River and the Lena River, the spring ﬂood
occurs at the same time. The timing and amplitude of the spring ﬂood for the Yenisei
River was found to vary signiﬁcantly over the three year time period used in this study.
For the Ob River segment it was found that the spring ﬂood event lasted more than two
months. This was because the river segment used for this analysis was located in the Ob
River delta, and as mentioned earlier this attenuates the signal and makes it last longer.
