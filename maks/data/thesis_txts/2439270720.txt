Definitions 
Occupational accident: A suddenly and unexpected event that causes personal injury which occurs in relation to    
performance at work.  
Near miss: An  accident that was close to happening, but so did no physical harm to any persons or material objects 
of personal value. 
Risk: “Risk refers to uncertainty about and severity of the events and consequences (or outcomes) of an activity with 
respect to something that humans value.” * 
Safety: A condition that is free from any harm of danger.  
Practice: A solid routine of doing work which is the normatively correct way of doing the work for those who 
participates and observes the work from the outside. ** 
Community: A group of people who share common concern or passion for a topic they are depended on their 
expertise and knowledge as they are shared on an ongoing basis. *** 
Induction: Any consciously planned events formed to seducing the newcomer towards the community and 
familiarizing the newcomers to the communities practice. ** 
Introduction course: Any deliberately planned event designed to make the newcomer familiar with the organization, 
general conditions at work, work practice and adopted standards regarding safety. ** 
Canonized safety practices:  A safety practices that are formalized for educational and training purpose which can be 
characterized through formal descriptions of tasks, safety instructions and safe execution of the work practice. ** 
Non-canonized safety practices:  Is a safety practice transmitted through participation in practice. It is taught and 
handed over typically through tacit practices. ** 
Safety representative: An employee who ensure the safety at the work place. Also know as working environment 
representative or health and safety representative. 
Staff representative: An employee who ensure the wellbeing of colleagues. Also known as representatives of the 
trust or union representative. 
Canonized safety practices: A safety practices that are formalized for educational and training purpose which can be 
characterized through formal descriptions of tasks, safety instructions and safe execution of the work practice. ** 
Non-canonized safety practices: A safety practice that handed over through participation in practices. It is taught 
through implicit, unpredictable and unreflective practice. ** 
This list of definitions has been compiled to support the 
reader by explaining the meaning of the terms and 
placing the terms in a context of practice-based studies.

Abstract 
The thesis examines how to raise safety for new employees so that they can avoid occupational accidents at 
work. New employees are a relatively overlooked group with many facets and challenges especially in the 
metal industry, as they usually work under high-risk conditions. By providing new employees with tools and 
knowledge that can focus their attention on their own personal safety and help them reflect on their work 
practice, I hope that the number of occupational accidents will be reduced for new employees.  
The CEO and top management are responsible for safety in the workplace and the new employees are 
responsible for participating in the safety culture. However, if the safety culture is not up to the necessary 
standards, and the management are not taking their role seriously, the new employee is placed in a position 
with few choices, as they are lacking seniority and possible experience of their work practice. In this position, 
new employees are forced to seek inspiration from their colleagues and use other methods that are available 
to them to learn the safety procedures and gain knowledge about the work practice. In this regard, several 
actors are introduced, who have a role to play in the safety culture and the new employee’s induction 
processes.  
For a new employee to become a skilled worker or aligned with the company’s practice, they often participate 
in an introduction course which may use canonized and non-canonized practices to train them in the tacit 
and embodied skills of the practice. In this process, the new employee develops an identity or so called 
performed self, which is in consonance with the identity of the community.  
There is a great diversity among new employees when it comes to their challenges and obstacles, which can 
be discovered in relation to their qualifications, work tasks, work organizing and workplace charisma. 
Knowledge about these challenges and obstacles is crucial in the prevention of occupational accidents for 
new employees and providing them with optimal learning conditions.

1. Introduction  
With a background as Design and Innovation student from the Danish Technical University (DTU , 2015) I 
have worked with product development and entrepreneurial knowledge along with its understanding of the 
synergy between users, companies and technologies. By focusing on the users and relevant stakeholders, we 
can gather a unique insight into various systems, products and how people interact with the established 
systems around us. This way of thinking often requires a process that is interdisciplinary and at the same time 
explores some of the social and cultural aspects in depth. The real or underlying problems in this context are 
rarely found and it is these which need to be discovered before a solution can be found. This approach to 
problem solving often inspires new innovative ideas which can make a difference for users in their everyday 
lives.  In other words, my work frequently draws on multiple disciplines, such as social entrepreneurship, user 
experience, system thinking, holistic thinking, management of change, co-creation and so on. 
I have previously been unfortunate enough to experience a work injury, which has made me aware of how 
fast things can evolve and go wrong when it comes to safety. If the safety elements are not optimal and there 
is an inattentive work process, accidents can often occur. Due to this work injury, I have received great 
motivation and interest in the role of safety and its usefulness in relation to product development. I wish to 
create a safer world for new employees as I myself was unaware as a trainee and did not know how to react 
or organize a safe working practice for myself. Today I still have loss of amenity after my occupational 
accident and I will probably always be highly at risk when working with epoxy chemicals due to allergic 
reaction.    
So, based on this I have understood the importance of being aware of working practice as the smallest 
mistake can lead to major changes in life both in the short term but also for the rest of one’s life.

1.3 Problem observation 
In spite of the Danish work force typically uses their bodies increasingly less for hard physical work than in 
the past, there are still more than 42,000 people (Arbejdstilsynet, 2017 (h)) who experience an occupational 
accident every year. Approximately 30 % of all these accidents occur amongst new employees (across all 
industries). (Arbejdstilsynet, 2017 (i)) In this thesis, there will be investigated the possibility of reducing 
occupational injuries for new employees in the metal industry by taking a holistic view of the Danish Working 
Environment (WEA), employers organizations’ and labor unions’ roles concerning safety. An occupational 
injury is a general term for occupational accident and occupational disease exemplified by an accident 
involving personal injury, wear and tear injury or mental injury suffered over a long period of time. 
New employees are a particularly vulnerable group for which beneficial changes, might be considered as they 
are often hired during busy periods or because of their lower wages to reduce the production costs for the 
company. Even further, several new employees also have variable working hours or night shifts, which may 
lead to further exposure to risks. Often this type of changes in the work schedule and challenging situations 
will prevent employees and new employees from fully participating in a good introductory course in the 
workplace, which of course will increase the risk of occupational injuries for the new employees even further. 
(Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) 
By looking at the new employee's work processes in tasks which are particularly vulnerable, such as working 
with machinery, metal, wood, plastic and many more, an understanding of the individual's needs in these 
pressured situations can be gained. Here the safety culture, the working environment and rewarding high 
standards in the workplace could be important elements regarding new employee’s safety and ability to 
question their own safety. 
At the same time, most new employees have difficulties defining, when a task is finished, how quickly it 
should be performed and to what extent it is possible to ask for help, without appearing less capable or 
unsuitable for the job. (Brachefælleskabet for arbejdsmiljø og velfærd og offentlig adminstration, 2016) 
Even further there is a common hiring and firing standard among companies, called “last in first out”, which 
creates a great deal of performance anxiety and stress for new employees, this can easily lead to unnecessary 
errors and occupational accidents, due to lack of consideration regarding work processes. (Ballisager, 2015) 
In 2016, the expenses for accidents at work regarding sickness benefits, healthcare and lost tax income 
increased to the amount of DKK 790 million for the Danish government. Due to the immense lack of 
registration of occupational accidents, the estimated losses for all accidents is adjusted to be between DKK 
1.3 and 1.6 billion for the government. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2017 (a)) The employer’s total cost of occupational 
accidents is estimated to be DKK 5.1 billion in 2016, and due to lack of registration it is estimated to be 
between DKK 5.5-5.8 billion. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2017 (d))

1.6 Delimitation and limitation 
This thesis is delimitated in relation to the Danish industry regarding physical occupational accidents that 
occur for new employees. Working with occupational injuries would be a to broad topic of investigation since 
occupational injuries involve accidents, wear and tear injuries and mental injuries. However, there are only 
a few documented mental injuries at today’s workplaces because these cases, among other things, often lose 
in the courts. Wear and tear injuries are common among employees and they are also represented among 
new employees, but this is unusual because the injury must occur within the first year of employment for 
them to be recognized as new employees according to this thesis. 
In addition, a delimitation has been made with respect to the choice of industry and thus it falls upon the 
metal industry and employees who handle machines, such as tool engineers, smiths and industrial 
technicians, among many others. Because this choice was a large representation of occupational accidents 
in the metal industry and it is one of the most expensive sectors for the government in relation to sickness 
benefits, healthcare and loss of tax income. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2017 (d)) Further, there is an increased risk and 
higher degree of damage when it comes to working with machines compared to working in an office.  
In the metal industry, the technological development has changed significantly in recent decades, and this 
will probably continue. The work has gone from being more human-based to being fully automatic and 
machine-driven in both large and small production facilities. Many productions have gone over to 3D Print, 
where the work is done on computers, and afterwards printed out and assembled. (Knudsen, 2018) There 
has also been an increase in workforce when it come service technicians who probably will be in greater 
demand in the future. These changes in the metal industry would of course change the output of 
occupational accidents and the type of accidents. However, it would be too huge an amount of work to follow 
these changes in this thesis.  
There are often big differences from company to company for example simple things as size or type of work 
make a huge difference when talking about safety. Therefore, it is difficult to define and demarcate a 
company that is suitable for these studies. Although these studies only focus on companies in the metal 
industry, there is still huge diversity as in any other industry, for example it may be a production company, 
service company, development company and so on. However, this variation among companies leads to 
various occupational accidents, which again are seen with reference to the internal departments such as 
human resources, production, administration and finance.  
To complicate things even further, the departments may not even be located in the same place, for example, 
the administration may be in North Zealand and production in South Jutland. This would of course mean that

there are different cultural opinions and practices that are necessary to include in the work on safety for a 
specific workplace.  
A new employee in this thesis would be seen as an employee who has started in a company within the last 
year. The person may have worked with exactly the same profession before but may also have changed from 
one industry to another. The employee can have any background although they must have changed from 
one company to another to be categorized as a new employee.  
In this thesis there have been several different limitations which have changed the outcome of the decisions

the thesis have not been further elaborated, such as the analysis, empirical studies, appendix and so on. 
Regarding the empirical studies, a great effort was made to get interviews and permission to observe with

for the working environment. Establishing contact with new employees could have been done in many other 
ways, but due to the transition over to workshops and developing a solution the time was better spent 
otherwise. Nevertheless, the timing was bad in the testing phase due to the summer vacation.

2. Literature review  
This chapter revolves around the effects or narratives and interactive storytelling as expressed through 
gaming and its possibilities in relation to safety. This leads on to the trade-offs of the gamification process 
and its motivational patterns of social gaming. When it comes to safety, it is important that narratives and 
learning materials are placed in a context which employees can see themselves in and that have similarities 
to their own work practice.  
2.1 Narratives  
’Narratives’ is in general used as a broad term regarding all forms of creativity such as literature, theatre, 
songs, speeches, television productions, journalism, videogames etc. They affect more than the process of 
creativity since the understanding of our own collected information and everyday behavior is expressed by 
stories that are saved in our memories. (Ricketts, 2007) Memory works by the reminding process, so each 
time people hear a story it reminds them of a story that they have already heard before. But in a 
conversational situation they tell that story as it seems fitting to the audience and thereby adapt it as the 
conversation proceeds.  (Schank R. C., 1995)  
 
Sometimes it can be difficult to comprehend what other people are saying or have written down in another 
context even though the words are understandable, but the words are not understandable for the mind of 
the receiver because it is not related to the other person’s experience. So instead the mind tries to 
understand what it is capable of understanding, by relating to its own previous knowledge or what is 
important to the individual what they are prepared to hear. (Schank R. C., 1995) If the person has heard the 
same story before it would be easy to understand what is being told again. In addition, it is also easier to 
learn new information when the information is conveyed in the form of a story rather than abstract rules, 
since stories enable us to save information in an intuitive way. (Schank R. C., 1999) “To understand how this 
relates to safety and health messages, consider how hard it can be to communicate the true significance of 
injury-prevention recommendations.” (Ricketts, 2007) 
According to Schank (1999) People often have a hard time remembering an abstract rule they have heard 
from other people i.e. when it comes to safety regulations or using safety equipment. Because without a 
context, the validity of regulations or an order cannot be stored adequately in the memory likewise people 
are more responsive to take advice if what is being said occurs in a convincing story. (Schank R. C., 1999) 
“The understanding problem is simply that humans are not really set up to hear logic. People tell 
stories because they know that others like to hear stories. The reason that people like to hear 
stories, however, is not transparent to them. People need a context to help them relate what they 
have heard to what they already know. We understand events in terms of other events we have 
already understood.” (Schank R. C., 1995) 
As shown in the cited text above, Schank (1995) frequently uses the term ’people’ in his presentation of 
narratives. This makes it challenging for the reader to understand who is being spoken about. The author 
here generalizes the term ’people’ to mean every human being and their knowledge constructed from only 
previous stories in their lifetime. Schank's (1995) use of the word ’people’ here makes me feel that the text 
is not specific to me even though it involves my previous stories and experiences. This is rather odd in relation 
to narratives which revolve around presenting information in an easy and responsive way.   
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
A story is useful due to the many indexes it comes with, such as locations, beliefs, attitudes, dilemmas, 
decisions, conclusions and so on, which can easily reside in our memory. (Schank R. C., 1995) Stories remind 
people of past experience and provide critical detail that they would not have thought of on their own. Other 
narrative tools such as personal anecdotes, analogies, case histories, stories and testimony are also 
exceptionally effective in promoting strong memories by evoking images and arousing strong emotions for 
the audience. (Ricketts, 2007) (Brock, 2000) Stories remind people of past experience and provide critical 
detail that they would not have thought of on their own. Other narrative tools such as personal anecdotes, 
analogies, case histories, stories and testimony are also exceptionally effective in promoting strong memories 
by evoking images and arousing strong emotions for the audience. (Vaajakallio, 2012)  
 
The composition of a narrative story is often set up with a purpose, chain of events, identifiable structure 
(like a beginning, middle and end), time span, agency (actors or entities), narrator and a reader. (Osborne & 
Avraamidou, 2009) Further, narratives are usually written in a clear and direct manner that is simple to 
understand and have a lively approach which contain many causal connections that are easy to remember. 
This is of course beneficial to story-based safety messages along with the potential to produce: 
1. “Advances in understanding (e.g., productive meaning making as the result of narrative 
encounters). 
2. Increased intepretive competence (e.g., competence in finding multiple interpreations of a 
particular phenomenon or event).  
3. Richer practice repertoires (e.g., narrative repertoires that become part of one’s personal 
practical knowledge).  
4. Changes in life (e.g., autobiographical narratives cause changes in personal lives).  
5. Visions gained (e.g., moral modeling agendas).”  (Conle, 2003) 
 
Stories affect our judgments and make the improbable events seem self-evident, for example if an accident 
happens in a story, the memory stores the story as a likely event and if the brain perceives something as 
likely, then it follows that the event may conceivably happen again, even though the chance of it is absurd, 
such as an airplane crash. (Ricketts, 2007) This kind of reasoning is crucial when it comes to story-based safety 
messages as the memory that is stored is likely to be used again in other contexts, such as preventative 
measures against accidents. However, it can be questioned how well this works or on what level of 
understanding since almost everybody who flies by airplane knows that there is a possibility of crashing, yet 
thousands of people still fly every day.  
In addition, when old beliefs or stories are repeated, it strengthens a person’s habitual behaviors, and 
correspondingly when a person encounters new stories which conflict with the old beliefs and stories, this 
creates a change in the behavior. (Ricketts, 2007) Stories that result in a distrust of  a certain event seem to 
lead to a perception that all of these events are likely to happen, i.e. a news story about dangerous chemicals 
in specific toys from China, transforms into a perception that all toys from China are dangerous. Also, here it 
is possible to question how this information affects our choices, since so many people still buy toys from 
China every day. Another critical element could be: what do these studies show about the relations to other 
products from China or similar countries in that region?

Interactive storytelling 
Narratives usually work as a non-interactive tool were the audience experiences another universe and is 
absorbed into it. However, by combining narratives with other tools like a game, it generates a set of new 
possibilities for the audience which creates space for the audience to make different choices and thereby 
receive a more customized experience. (Sundar, 2007) This is also called interactive storytelling which is 
difficult to define due to the many different facets and approaches that change the gameplay, such as 
immersive storytelling, emergent storytelling, interactive authoring, plot-based systems, character-based 
systems and so on. (Bostan & Marsh, 2012) These approaches are often a trade-off between each other, for 
example how should the player experience the game with choices (storytelling) versus entirely creating the 
game by the players own actions. Further, how should “the trade-off between character and plot, narrative 
causality, the problem of narrative control, and the relations between story generation and presentation.” 
(Charles et al, 2003) Every single decision relating to these conditions changes the gameplay for its audience 
and the possibility inherent in the game. But these decisions are also to be taken when it comes to AI 
techniques, planning formalisms, story representations and so on which are related to the transmitted 
messages of previously exchanged information, attributes or the level of player control. (Bostan & Marsh, 
2012) In this context “transmitted messages” should be understood as the player’s interactions in the game 
which change the gameplay for the person themselves or other players. This transmitted message does not 
need to be known or acted upon but is necessary for the game maker to understand.  
“interactivity…is not located in the features of the medium or user perceptions but in the relatedness of 
transmitted messages with previous exchanges of information where sender and receiver roles become 
interchangeable. In this sense, messages transmitted by a computer game are related with the previous 
exchanges of information or the former actions of the player, although the complexity of this relatedness 
varies from simply keeping track of game scores to interactive storytelling systems that analyze player actions 
throughout the game for a customized experience.” (Rafaeli, 1988)  
 
These previously transmitted messages which Rafeli (1988) refers to are usually input by the player’s 
interaction with a mouse, keyboard or controller which are imposed on a character, object or location in the 
game. However, new media such as tablets, iPhones, voice detectors, interactive TV’s and virtual reality (VR) 
are creating new possibilities regarding the way people use media and interact with them. However, virtual 
reality has not had a big breakthrough, even though the first mind-controlled VR has already been created, 
which again offers a whole new set of interactions to games. (Leary, 2017)   
 
Another kind of usage of transmitted messages are design games that work as a boundary object in co-
creation with participants, usually at a workshop. Here the dialog is supported by the purpose of the 
workshop. The facilitator chooses various tools, rules and cultural understandings, etc. which meditate the 
transmitted messages into the next dialog, step or topic of the workshop. (Vaajakallio, 2012) 
 
With reference to the previous text about interactivity, it is possible to see the complexity of creating a 
definition of this topic. However, a broad definition of interactivity could be separated into three levels: non-
interactive, interactive/reactive and responsive/fully interactive. Non-interactive games do not provide any 
acknowledgment of the player’s input. If the game provides direct responses to players’ input, it can be 
considered as a reactive interaction. Lastly, if a game reacts to the latest and previous inputs, it can be 
considered a responsive interaction. (Bostan & Marsh, 2012) Nevertheless, interactive storytelling is a gaming 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
experience that changes the content of the game in real time to preferences of the player’s choice to provide 
sense of control in a virtual world. 
 
2.2 Gamification 
The term gamification was first used in 2003 by Nick Pelling for making hardware more fun to use and learn. 
(Dale, 2014) But does gamifying something like safety issues mean that it will be fun? Those who are familiar 
with developing games and playing at a high level understand that there is a problem with this assumption 
since good mechanisms are hard to create. All games are not fun and compelling but at its core, gamification 
is about engaging people and motivating them to achieve their goals. One way to motivate the audience is 
to present them with fascinating and personalized challenges that encourage them to evolve and learn 
through the levels of the game. (Dale, 2014)  
“However, it is also clear that games and gamification can serve as supportive tool for the 
challenges that training activities are facing. The motivational challenges where games and 
gamification can have the most influence are the awaking of interest towards the training activities 
that the employees might have and to serve as a motivating learning environment.” (Höglund, 
2014)   
This awaking interest towards gamification might be the use of motivational patterns that can be used 
regarding safety. However, motivation can be a lot of things and is rarely the same for everybody. Amy Jo 
Kim (2012) tries to capture some of these motivational patterns for social gaming through her model called 
the “Social Engagement verbs”. These motivational patterns unfold around Content and Players (x-axis) and 
Acting and Interaction (y-axis) as expressed in figure 2. On the axes, the four categories are shown: express, 
compete, explore and collaborate. They are followed up by a set of explaining verbs for each of these 
categories.  
 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
Figure 2. Social Engagement verbs, shows four motivational patterns that express: compete, explore and collaborate which is 
placed between acting or interacting & content or players. Adapted from (Kim, 2012) 
 
Express: The possibility to show oneself and express who I am or what I have created to others is a meaningful 
driver and a huge motivation for many people playing games.  
Compete: The eagerness to improve own skills, metrics or being better than the last time are some of the 
main drivers for players today and is taking the competition of eSports to a whole new level. However, other 
studies show that safety might be negatively affected in relation to individual learning platforms if the game 
is designed to be too competitive. (Höglund, 2014) 
Explore: Exploring a content or other kind of attributes can be an extremely rewarding activity for many 
people who like to be drawn into another universe and discover its possibilities.  
Collaborate: Socializing and playing together is a powerful motivator as well as the loyalty factor in gaming. 
Despite playing together, being in a larger group or guild can also establish the feeling of doing something 
that is larger than oneselves or impossible to do by yourself. 
Whether or not motivational patterns are restricted to these four motivational patterns alone is open to 
discussion. Furthermore, one can question whether they have the possibility to work alone or can they only 
be seen in a seamless web of structure. Further, what would it mean to a game if only some of these 
motivational patterns were accessible? Would it then change some people’s view of the other motivational 
aspects?  Many questions could be raised in this regard, but maybe this should be seen as a generic model.   
According to Ricketts (2007), it is clear that the motivational patterns of safety narratives have changed in 
the way are they pursued from text-based laws and regulations along with accident statistics to other non-
interactive narratives that involve stories about injuries or close to accidents which are made for an 
educational purpose and have shown to increase the effectiveness of safety messages. (Ricketts, 2007) 
Examples of such narratives could be anecdote-based publications, brief occupational accident reports that 
involve hazard illustrations and video training materials. (Ricketts, 2007) Although these tools are easily 
understandable and accessible, they often lack the deeper professional knowledge and complexity which is 
equally important for having a safe work practice.  
“Games and gamified activities can increase the motivation and interest creation towards the 
training activities as they are a familiar and safe environment for learning, which also affect one of 
the cornerstones of engagement, the feeling of safety.” (Höglund, 2014)  
 
Gamification is about shifting the complexity off the users on to something else i.e. software which makes 
the topic playable for humans to further develop their cognitive, social and affective skills. By removing the 
complexity of the task, the user is no longer required to have specialized knowledge or skills to begin their 
journey but will acquire it as they play the game. (Dubbels, 2013) Therefore, gamification should emphasize 
user-centered design by creating interface, feedback and interaction systems in the game which can clarify 
the process, purpose and interpretation of the game. In addition, gamification often uses social dependence 
with an aesthetic environment and reward-action contingencies (RACs) to motivate people into playing the 
game. (Dubbels, 2013) When these elements between the player and the game are balanced in the right 
composition, the players experience “flow”. (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) “Flow is an experience “so gratifying 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
that people are willing to do it for its own sake, with little concern for what they will get out of it, even when 
it is difficult or dangerous. (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990)  
Further, Csikszentmihalyi (1990) states that the flow experiences consist of following eight elements.  
1) a task that can be completed  
2) ability to concentrate on the task  
3) concentration is possible because the task has clear goals  
4) concentration is possible because the task provides immediate feedback  
5) ability to exercise a sense of control over actions  
6) deep but effortless involvement that removes awareness of worries and frustrations  
of everyday life  
7) concern for self disappears but sense of self emerges stronger afterwards  
8) sense of duration of time is altered” (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990)

3. Theory 
This chapter outlines the practical theoretical learning perspective in relation to understanding a new 
employee’s safety at work as well as explaining definitions that are used throughout this thesis. Furthermore, 
the idea of safety is examined in terms of a social practice in the community which is investigated through a 
notion of the safety culture. In addition, the established knowledge in communities is seen as a social activity 
that changes the identity of the community itself and its members. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) Joining a 
community requires an induction process where new members learn the practice. This is both explicit and 
tacit and later on can lead to a position of expertise in the community. 
3.1 What is safety? 
Establishing what is a safe or dangerous is determined by man (consciously and unconsciously) since it is a 
process of thought and perception made by the individual consciousness together with the surrounding 
dynamics of a social context. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) This social context can be a nation, industry, 
professional community and so on which develops its own set of rules and definitions about what is 
dangerous and safe. Following this perception then, we can say that that safety and danger elements are 
defined by communities and that practice, through the attitudes and behaviors of its members, protects the 
environment, health and welfare of its affected surroundings. Safety in this context is therefore an ability 
that is unfolded and realized in practice that can be both tacit and explicit which are stored and transmitted 
within the community of practice. (Gherardi, Nicolini, & Odella, 1998) A community of practice in theory 
should be understood as a group of people who share common concern or passion for a topic. They are 
dependent on their expertise and knowledge as they are shared on an ongoing basis. (Lave & Wenger, 1991) 
However, Gherardi qualifies this further and uses the term community of practitioners for technical reasons 
as follows. “that a pratice and the traditional of a pratice do not respect organizational boundaries but instead 
traverse several organizations.” (Gherardi S. , 2006, p. 108) 
 
On the other hand, when it comes to a definition of practice, there are multiple different versions of how a 
practice could be defined, solely within the two domains of a practice-based standpoint or a work-based 
learning point. This is far beyond the reach of this thesis. (Gherardi, Corradi, & Verzelloni, 2010) So, in this 
thesis a practice is meant as a solid routine of doing work which is the normatively correct way of doing the 
work for those who participate and observe the work from the outside. (Nielsen et al., 2017) However, 
another promising definition within the field could be the citation below but be aware of the difference 
between a theory of social practices (Praxis) and a practice (Praktik). 
“A ‘practice’ (Praktik) is a routinized type of behavior which consists of several elements, interconnected to 
one another: forms of bodily activities, forms of mental activities, ‘things’ and their use, a background 
knowledge in the form of understanding, know-how, states of emotions and motivational knowledge.” (Buch, 
Andersen, & Klemsdal, 2015)

3.2 Practical theoretical learning perspective 
The choice of theory for this thesis is the practical theoretical learning (Wenger, 2003) perspective that 
focuses on social practice, which are practices constituted and demonstrated by concrete actions. The field 
of research is related to various forms of induction and training practices which new employees participate 
in at their workplace. This choice has been taken since I am particularly interested in knowing how new 
employees learn safety in practice and thereby avoid occupational accidents.  
Learning through practical theoretical studies is often used in conjunction with posthuman studies (Sorgner, 
Hughes, & Shin, 2017) that try to decentralize human relationships by concentrating on what is created 
socially and in the environment. There are many genres of posthuman studies but by having a focus on social 
practice and its relational thinking, this thesis leans towards social constructionism. Social constructionism 
attributes "meanings" through language and social life such as rules, traditions, behavior and so on. The 
destination is then found in certain social contexts that create the framework for actions. In this context the 
political and ethical discussions inside the community would always interfere and describe the reality of its 
members. Further, the relational thinking as mentioned before emphasizes the social practice 
interdependence between subject and object, person and world and network and society. (Nielsen et al., 
2017) 
Based on these cultural competences, a social constructionist approach would therefore be relevant to the 
notion of a safety culture. A notion leading to the overall functioning of a system like a community that is 
characterized by a set of beliefs, attitudes, norms and practices which can targeted at both social and 
technical dimensions with the purpose of reducing the vulnerability of an actor(s) situation under dangerous 
conditions. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) 
 
Regarding the notion of safety culture, there are typical three mistakes according to the understanding of its 
conditions. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) Firstly, a safety culture is not something that can be possessed by 
anyone or a group of people, such as an organization rather it is a set of actions that take place in an 
organization. 
 
Secondly, it should be condemned that the idea of an existent safety culture is belonging to only a single but 
shared organization are misleading since it conflicts with the argument of various culture and different 
organizations around the globe. But also due to the great plurality and conflict going along in organizations 
comparatively to the consensus of sharing values, ideas and projects. 
Thirdly, the pervasive idea that the safety culture is a product of the individual’s intellectual and mental 
thought process, or in other words, the way which members of an organization talk and are convinced about 
safety. Instead consideration should be given to how the cultural and symbolic point of view emphasizes the 
organizations material, social and constructive dimensions of safety. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) An 
understanding of these elements will generate a learning opportunity of the safety culture and the 
knowledge within.  
“Safety culture is a process, not a content or an object, and as such, it must be constantly sustained by 
appropriate organizational practices.” (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007, p. 3)  
So, in this context safety culture should not be seen as something that can be explained but rather as 
something that evolves and becomes manifested in the interaction of various aspects of safety in practice.

3.3 Knowledge through practical learning 
In the perspective of social practice learning, where new members learn to be part of a work community and 
through that training become considered as experts, learning is to be understood as a process of participation 
in social practice learning. This also applies when it comes to learning about safety and its bodily activity. 
In this context, knowledge is not just something intellectual in the head or written on paper but a social 
practice learning that new members participate in through the community. (Gherardi S. , 2015) This raises 
several questions, such as do humans have direct access to gain knowledge and observe the world, or should 
a person look behind the observed world to find out about its existence and coherence to get a more accurate 
explanation. In this regard, Peavy (2000) states that from the point of view of holistically-orientated social 
constructionism, it is the way that people are talking about things that constructs the reality  they live in. 
(Peavy, 2000) So, together in the community we create the reality, but it also creates us. In other words, 
everything that we create generates relations with our surroundings.  
“The main concern of a practice-based approach to working practices is to understand the logic of 
the situation and the performance of action as practical knowledge, which connects working with 
organizing and knowing with practicing. It assumes the term ‘practice’ as epistemology, following 
the shift from knowledge to knowing – and therefore from an epistemology of possession of 
knowledge to one of practice”  
(Gherardi S. , 2015, p. 1) 
 
Conventionally, there is a perception in today’s society that knowledge is something, that is possible to own 
or possess, but according to Gherardi & Nicolini (20007) this is not possible; knowledge is not just a social 
practice but a movement from ”knowledge to knowing” where knowing should be understood as “knowing-
in-practice” (Gherard, 2018).  
Organizational knowledge can then be described as a social and collective phenomenon created on the idea 
of a community of practice and the notion of practice, which is a network constructed by various actors. 
There is a causal unity between community members despite the shared ways of understanding events and 
performing their work.  
In communities of practice, relations are established around activities that take shape in social relations 
where experiences are gathered by those who perform them. Thus, when knowledge and skills are learned 
in social groups, the experience is developed into an individual identity in each person, which establishes a 
consonance with the identity of the community. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) At the same time, this 
transmitted knowledge goes the other way around on a higher level for example, such as a nation which is 
constantly developing its identity through the interaction of its communities. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) 
Similarities are shown in figure 4. which shows four levels of observations: the individual, collective, 
organizational and societal in a field of safety practice which are connected in a single seamless web of 
knowledge. (Gherardi S. , 2012) 
At the bottom of figure 4., becoming a practitioner in the community is understood as a staircase where a 
novice or new member learns the practice of the community, but also at the same time develops an identity, 
“a performed self”. (Gherardi S. , 2012)  This performed self is interconnected with the social learning as 
there take places in the collective identity of the community. The arrows at the figure are signs of transmitted

knowledge which are represented as the learning potential on the right side, and the knowledge that is

The levels of learning are connected to the practice of the community due to the knowledge that is mingled 
and distributed in the community. In addition, learning requires involvement and contribution to the

“Learning safety means knowing how to behave as a competent member in a culture of safety

In addition, the holistically-orientated social constructionism states that people are connected through 
systems, structures (such as language) and relations which influence the way people act, think, choose, gain 
experience and the future possibilities. (Galbin, 2014) in this connection the individual is seen as a multi-
individual who changes their identity in relation to the environment which it is located in, due to the many

choices that to be taken. Therefore, self-identity is a reflective and lifelong project, consisting of a coherent 
self-narrative about oneself in relation to the possibilities that person has. (Prakash & Girishwar, 2012) 
Learning causes changes in identity for all members of the community, but it is especially prominent when 
new members arrive and start participating in the practices of the community. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) 
The members of the community learn the safety culture and practice by participating in the past and present 
of the community, which applies as their current education and learning process. The relationship between 
new members and society is therefore mutual but also asymmetrical since novice and new members are not 
just passive spectators but have their own innovative contribution to the safety. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007)  
However, the communities often tend to continue in their own practice, even though the practices are 
outdated and irrational or no longer effective. This can often be a safety issue and especially when it comes 
to novice and new members who usually adapt to the current practice of the community to avoid exclusion. 
Focusing cultural factors which prevent practical safety measures does not make sense in terms of rationality 
and common sense, but it makes sense for members of the community and is the way forward to behave 
appropriately within the established rules. Nevertheless, the renewal of communities happens though new 
members that are introduced to the practice of the community and have gained knowledge, expertise and 
seniority which they leave as a legacy with modifications to the next successors. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007)  
“The pleasure of practicing and sharing that pleasure, passion as an attachment to the object of 
the practice, and mediation with the tools of the practice are further elements that sustain 
reproduction of the practice and that make it possible to answer the questions as to why a practice 
continues to be practiced and how it changes by being practiced.” (Gherardi S. , 2015, p. 9)  
3.4 A knowing in practice 
Practice is not only a motive and set of activities that attempts to achieve a specific goal, it consists of several 
layers and dimensions which are involved with each other. These layers and dimensions are often expressed 
when a person is able to do something, but unable to describe how they did it in a scientific or analytical way, 
for example riding a bike. Another way to express it is that a practice is more than the sum of activities; it 
involves the ways of doing things and how they are socially supported. (Gherardi S. , 2015)  
 
“A practice is a constantly disputed terrain and is supported both by forms of sharing and harmony 
and by forms of dissent and conflict. In fact, being an ‘expert’ practitioner gives entitlement 
(legitimate authority) not only to express value and aesthetic judgments but also to express an 
ethical and aesthetic judgment that supports the practice socially.” (Gherardi S. , 2015, p. 3) 
 
Whatever is discussed, negotiated and contested socially within a given practice and its participants, is 
neither the effectiveness or the efficiency, but the way the world is collectively seen by the participants. 
These participants have an emotional, aesthetic and ethical bond to the practice as they are supporting and 
reproducing.  (Gherardi S. , 2015)  
 
The participants sense of what to do and what should be done next in an appropriate matter of right and 
wrong, effective or ineffective, aesthetic or rational and so on is part of the practice of doing. Practical 
knowledge is created on a sensory and aesthetic level in the body, which is not controlled by the intellectual 
properties of the mind, but how the body is feeling and sensing the practice of doing. (Gherardi S. , 2015) 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
“Knowledge is not what resides in a person’s head or in books or in data banks. To know is to be 
capable of participating with the requisite competence in the complex web of relationships among 
people, material artifacts, and activities.” (Gherardi S. , 2015, p. 4) 
 
When safety is considered as a “knowing-in-practice” (Gherard, 2018) It is looked upon as a kind of knowledge 
that refers to reliability in a context of practice rather than risk. In its essence, any activity should be reliable 
according to what it accumulates and how it effects the social dynamics. But since the world is not perfect, 
Gherardi also uses the term “competence-to-act” (Gherard, 2018) which is an underlying desire of the 
individual and collective identity in the community of practice to repair a system with obstacles. After a 
possible repair there will be an eagerness by the individual or the community to explain what they have 
learned and try to transfer these learned competences outside of its origin, into new areas. 
 
3.5 Introduction to the community 
There is a body of practical knowledge to master when becoming an expert in a discipline, which requires an 
understanding of tacit knowledge that is collectively managed by the community. However, the community 
can do a lot to compensate for new members in this process by introducing reliable personal assistance 
together with fixed routines or for example by using methods such as narratives, story-telling and other kinds 
of rituals which facilitate the induction of new members into the safety actions of a socialization practice. 
Most of the learning that happens through the introduction course is by observation and imitation of others, 
which creates an idea of how new actions are performed on later occasions. The purpose of the introduction 
is to discover what to do in different situations and when to do it. Further it should be established as a 
reasonable presentation of how new members’ practice is performed according to routines and usage of 
artifacts among other members of the community. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2002)  
 
Being in a community of practice does not mean that learning possibilities and social relations are equal, for 
example like items on a list that can be checked off. (Grytnes, 2018) The relationship to teachers and other 
members is important since they notice how the task is done and which possibilities there are in the new 
member of the community. Teachers can in this relation be seen as gatekeepers because they decide which 
tasks the new member is ready to solve and how the person acts in a social practice. Thus, his or her way of 
participating in the social context can ultimately end up in blocking doors or giving access to special skills and 
expertise which can be used later on. (Grytnes, 2018) In this context the teachers learn how to handle risk, 
but also more importantly how they are positioning themselves in more complicated work tasks where they 
are giving away their experience and understanding of how dangers are linked to certain tasks.(Grytnes, 
2018)  
 
So, in this way it could be said that learning does not exist without conflict, any changes of knowledge in a 
community is perceived as a way of adjusting the established power relation in a social context which involves 
actors of the community. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2002) When a working practice in a community is related to a 
trainee’s final exploration process, they are often empowered with the possibility of questioning the habitual 
way of working and acting in the community. (Gherardi S. , 2015)  
However, when it comes to the induction process, there is a mindset where induction is viewed as any 
consciously planned activity whose purpose is to facilitate the socialization process of new members in the 
community of practice. (Nielsen et al., 2017) This process could involve cases, peer-to-peer training and other 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
kinds of training that may be successful, unsuccessful or even a combination which will change the 
relationships between the new employee and the community. 
 
“any arrangement made to familiarise the new employee with the organization, safety rules, 
general conditions of employment, and the work of the section or department in which they are 
employed. This is a minimal definition, in that “any arrangement” may vary from mere information 
lasting as long as an interview to a training course of even very long duration.” (Gherardi & 
Perrotta, 2010, p. 85)  
 
By adopting this view induction could also be seen as a set of activities which are deliberately planned by the 
community for making the new employee familiar with safety rules at the workplace. So, when a new 
employee of the community is involved in its practice and role models, he or she learns the professional 
expertise and empowers the knowledge through the body of practical knowledge. This requires an

Nevertheless, this will be elaborated further in the analysis with other characteristic elements of the

However, when it comes to the critical view of practice theoretical approaches, there is a heated discussion 
about the degrading part of dualism and its framework of human activity in a seamless continuity of doings 
and sayings which should be the object of critical analysis. (Buch, Andersen, & Klemsdal, 2015)

Even further, practice theoretical approaches cover a broad aspect of social sciences which often have in

4. Methodology 
The work process and mindset of this thesis has been oriented towards the Double Diamond (Design Council, 
2005) that is used to solve complicated and undefined problems. By this it should be understood that the 
work process is often done where neither the problem nor the solution is given in advance, so also there is 
no immediate solution or technical route to clarification despite the general lines that have been formulated 
in advance for this thesis. Therefore, time must be spent on problem-solving activities that can define and 
shape the problem into a workable state. Subsequently, the requirements can be clarified for a given solution 
and thus a prototype can be created. 
The Double Diamond is generally used in conjunction with development projects or other projects where 
there is a demand for innovation and creativity as the problem is not given in advance. Further, the Double 
Diamond is based on an understanding of the actor’s needs that usually require anthropological studies or 
other forms of communication that can express these actor’s needs. The method is divided into four phases: 
discover, define, develop, deliver as seen in figure 5. 
 
Figure 5. Double Diamond: The four phases discover, define, develop and deliver with the represented divergent and convergent 
thinking.  Own diagram. 
Discover 
The first phase of the Double Diamond “discover”, is about exploring and understanding the complexity of a 
problem. Here the designers try to look at the world with an open mind and consider everything about the 
actors without constraining themselves., This practice is also called divergent thinking where an effort is 
made to understand actors and their needs. Methods involving, i.e. market research, user experience related 
research and best practice are commonly used in this phase for getting an awareness of where the obstacles 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
are. Often empathy is used to investigate behaviors and interaction between the humans and the product or 
a field of research. 
 
Define 
In this phase, the knowledge collected from earlier is used to make sense of all the possibilities that have 
been identified to collect and achieve focus on the project direction. In other words to construct specific and 
concrete problem(s) which will be the framework of the project. In addition, the previous assessments are 
revised, such as chosen points of view and delimitations, to have a more defined approach to the project. 
This is also called convergent thinking. 
 
Develop 
At this point half-way through the Double Diamond, divergent thinking is used again but this time by 
generating innovative and creative ideas which are developed into mockups and prototypes that can be 
tested. Here trial and error help the designer to improve and refine the idea into a workable solution.  
 
Deliver 
During this phase, the convergent thinking is used to close untied business or unknown fields of research to 
produce a final solution. The final tests are also conducted to reduce the number of childhood diseases, 
improve the solution and minimize the chance of setbacks or other critical issues related to the launching of 
a new product or service. Subsequently, a new iterative process starts again or maybe another spinoff project 
from the established solution is carried out.  
 
In figure 5. the white arrows are a symbol of an iterative process between two phases since a designer’s 
process is rarely linear, as a result of new discoveries or changes that are valuable and significant to integrate 
into a solution. Often when new discoveries are made, they are not explicit or expressed in a comprehensible 
way. This requires contemplation and an iterative process for further understanding and implementations.

4.3 Personas 
After the interviews were completed and gone through, it became clear that there was a basis for creating 
several personas that could be an interesting point of departure in the analysis to explain how actors establish 
or maintain safety actions and safety culture in a company. A persona (Cooper, 2004) is a fictional character 
who is created based on research to distinguish between different actors or archetypes to understand users’ 
needs, behaviors and goals. Six personas have been made, each representing an ideal performance regarding 
safety approaches and actions inside a company. However, this description is not meant to be perfect or used 
in every company but as an example of a way to practice safety. It is important to emphasize here that using 
personas would probably not be the best method to use in every company due to various types of 
occupational injuries and accidents.  
Personas were created by transcribing existing findings from previous interview recordings which were then 
merged into specific information on individual actors and their work in safety. In this context, citations were 
used to describe good safety practices and the desire for a specific action or attitude towards the safety 
practices of various actors. In other contexts, special challenges were addressed by the informants’ own work 
or knowledge of other actors’ practices which then were made into the subject field for analysis. Based on 
this type of information, it was possible to sample some of the goals and frustrations from each actor. 
Regarding the new employee, it did not make sense to describe his/her positive impact on safety so instead 
the focus was placed on some of the challenges, obstacles and wishes for the new employee at work.  
The personas are made up of pictures, such as cartoon figures for creating universality, although they are still 
wearing objects which make them recognizable as a job position or for distinguishing between the cartoons.  
(Cable, 2017) In addition, the personas are heavily loaded with information that makes it less intuitive but 
removes the focus from the biography section which is the essential feature of these personas. In the 
personas there is also a short description of some of the frustrations and goals for each actor along with 
quotes, motivations and social activity which make them appear a bit more human or personalized and 
thereby create a better relation to the reader.

5. Background research 
This chapter revolves around safety in Danish society in relation to occupational accidents at a macro level. 
In this context I will present the Danish model and other organizations which are involved in safety in daily 
practice. I will also investigate the registration of accidents or near miss accidents, which is a warning sign or 
opportunity to work with safety. Finally, I will briefly consider the economic issues related to occupational 
accidents for employers and the government.  
5.1 The Danish model 
The Danish work force is unique in its construction compared to many other countries around the world. This 
is largely due to the Danish model, which unfolded in 1899 under an agreement between the employer 
organizations, labor unions and the government, as seen in figure 10. (Arbejdsgiverne, n.d.)1 The Danish 
model is characterized by a high agreement coverage because a high proportion of the workforce has 
traditionally been organized in trade unions and many employers have been organized in employer’s 
organizations. These collective agreements between trade unions and employers’ organizations have formed 
the general framework for salary and working conditions in the Danish labor market. 
 
Figure 10. The Danish model. Three parties: The Government, Employer and Labor unions and their members reach voluntarily 
negotiated agreement in the 3-party negotiations. Adapted from (EMU Danmarks læringsportal, u.d.) 
 
Another distinctive feature of the Danish model is the flexicurity which is a special combination of flexibility 
and security. (Fagligt fælles forbund, 2018) So, compared to most other countries, it is easy for an employer 
to hire and fire employees in Denmark, on the other hand there is a safety net in the form of an 
unemployment insurance fund and an active employment policy. Furthermore, it is possible to get free 
education and reeducation if the same job no longer exists. 
                                                            
1 The sources in this section are not intended as a biased description of the Danish model.  
  
==PAGE:39==

These conditions provide flexibility in the labor market, so that the employees do not get stuck in their jobs, 
but rather seek new opportunities and challenges, which makes room for newly qualified individuals, or 
others, to enter the labor market. Likewise, the employer can ensure that the workforce matches inconstancy 
in the company's production and the growing need for new competencies. (Fagligt fælles forbund, 2018) 
Despite the good things that result from a changing work force, it also generates a lot of new employees 
which means there is a higher risk of occupational accidents. 
Summary 
In this section a short description of the Danish model has been introduced along with the principle of 
flexicurity which is essential for understanding how the Danish safety net works on a macro scale in contrast 
to many other countries. The relatively high replacement culture helps create more new employees and 
occupational accidents, but on the other hand the safety net prevents people from being cast out of the 
system and it gives them opportunities to get back onto the labor market.      
Understanding the Danish model helped the process of divergent thinking in the discovery phase of the 
Double Diamond due to the actors that are involved and their way of looking at safety practice. Further 
findings were revealed through phone calls and interviews with the Work Environment Authority (WEA), the 
Danish Industry (DI) and unions which each represent a part of the Danish model.   
5.2 Reporting of occupational accidents 
According to the Danish legislation, companies are obliged to register occupational accidents in EASY, which 
is an recording system for the labor market occupational safety and labor Inspection System, which can be 
found on virk.dk. After the registration in EASY, the employer must send a copy of the notification to the 
injured employee and the internal safety representative group which analyzes the accident and draws up an 
action plan for avoiding future accidents. Registration of an occupational accident must be reported within 
9 days of the accident if the employee had more than a one-day absence from work in addition to the injury 
date. However, near miss accidents are not notifiable accidents but can be registered continuously by the 
employer, but an action plan must also be prepared to avoid future accidents.  (Arbejdstilsynet, n.d.) 
 
In addition to the employer, doctors, lawyers, unions, insurance companies and the injured person 
themselves can also report occupational accidents in EASY. When an accident has been reported in EASY, the 
notification will automatically be forwarded to the appropriate actors. This could for example be the 
employer’s insurance, the labor market occupational safety authority (AES) or the Working Environment 
Authority (WEA). 
When the employer is responsible for an injury or illness, it is possible to claim additional compensation to 
the traditional cover by the labor market occupational safety authority (AES), which is claimed through the 
employer’s insurance. These additional compensations are: 
1. 
Sore and pain relief 
2. 
Loss of income 
3. 
Loss of earning capacity 
4. 
Permanent injury 
5. 
Subsidies for some of the attorney's costs

The insurance company deals with the case and follows-up on cases where fraud is suspected.  If the case is 
approved, compensation will be paid out to the company and/or the individual. As a private person it is

insurance or other insurance which might cover personal damage and injury. 
According to the WEA there is a rule of thumb when it comes to occupational accidents which states that 
only about half of all the notifiable accidents are reported by companies. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2017 (j))

Despite registration of accidents, near misses can be an event which an employer or employee does not even 
recognize or accept as an accident. Nevertheless, a near miss accident can be a terrible event that leaves 
both physical and mental reminders. An individual suffering a near miss accident might not have the 
possibility to share the event and its psychological effects due to fear of exposing other colleagues.  
But since a near miss accident does not cause minor injuries or important material damage, they are often 
forgotten and made insignificant even though they could easily be used as a learning potential or heads up 
for something not okay. This does not mean that every minor detail or near miss accident should be 
controlled or focused on, but sometimes accidents come from the same cause. They can be due to a technical 
issue, a human condition, an organizational structure, a cultural problem and so on which is not foreseeable.  
However, according to Henrich’s Domino Theory, all incidents are directly related to a previous unsafe 
condition that starts in the social environment which might come from an undesirable personal characteristic 
- stubbornness, recklessness etc. (Disaster Management Institute, 2018) Then at some point, a chain of 
events occurs, like dominos falling, when a person who makes a mistake has an inborn or acquired character 
flaw which leads to an unsafe act or condition as seen in figure 12.  
In connection with unsafe acts or conditions there are four causes that lead to an accident, an “improper 
attitude, lack of knowledge or skill, physical unsuitability and improper mechanical or physical environment”.  
(Disaster Management Institute, 2018) An accident is simply defined by Henrich as a culmination of events 
or circumstances that occur in a logical order that may end with an injury.

Near miss accidents are a warning signs that next time it may go wrong and when it does go wrong, expensive 
resources will have to be used to repair the damage from the accident. However, if an employee gets sick or 
injured or other colleagues need to work harder to recover what is lost, it will again create a negative cycle, 
which is not in anyone’s interests.  
Therefore, near miss accidents are advisable warnings there should be taken seriously and dealt with, for 
example by using the method “From UPS to OBS" (Industriens Branchearbejdsmiljøråd, 2016) 
The method from "UPS to OBS" is supported by two schemes: 
• 
"UPS – This was really close to happening" is used by employees to report a near miss accident 
• 
"UPS became OBS" The working environment group or safety representative has a conversation with 
the employee who has reported the near miss accident with a view to preventing future accidents.  
 
Everything that is about an "UPS" - could be incident just about to happen and therefore it could easily be 
gathered at a central location in the company such as "UPS station" or "UPS board". When an employee 
becomes aware of a near miss accident, filled out with name, date and a small note about the situation, it 
reveals a situation that requires awareness and a preventive solution. The working environment group will 
then contact the employee within a short time and fill out the form on the UPS station, which in this context 
turns into a OBS. The OBS process makes it clear for employees to see what happened, what should be done 
and who will take care of it and when.  
==PAGE:42==
42 
 
Next, if a solution is found to a specific problem, the station should have a section with the information on 
how to proceed with that specific problem and what the employees should be aware of in the meantime. 
Final solutions that seem to work can also be conveniently linked to the company's workplace evaluation 
(APV).  
There are many other methods for detecting near miss accidents. A common strategy for this is: 
1. Training to spot risks: If you can 'see' the accident before it happens, you can also prevent it. 
2. Making it easy to find the risk in every situation and act upon it. 
3. Agreeing who gathers information and how action and feedback are ensured in the system.  
 
Among other things, the Danish Work Environment Authority (WEA) experience that some of the small 
companies do not know the value of workplace evaluation (APV) and that many companies have a difficulty 
implementing an APV. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2010) If APV is implemented fully, it can be an important tool when 
it comes to safety. This is because there is a thorough description of how the work is to be carried out and 
by what means, such as safety equipment and so on. The APV also states what the employees have to do 
when accidents occur, along with other valuable information. Finally, the APV can be used by new employees 
or other co-workers in the company who need information if an employee needs to be replaced. 
 
According to the WEA, a quarter of the Danish companies interviewed do not prepare or have a tolerable 
work place evaluation (APV). In these studies, there is many small businesses that often lack action plans for 
solving safety problems or occupational accidents. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2010)  Other businesses that have 
temporary workstations or shifting workplaces also have challenges when it comes to work place evaluation, 
since the work conditions are constantly changing. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2010) 
 
Summary  
Since its compulsory to report occupational accidents in Denmark and there is still only a vague tradition for 
reporting it among companies, the problem can not only be narrowed or solved by laws and regulations. 
Terms like culture, norms, social responsibility, awareness of the problem and so on need to be considered 
to get a full picture of the obstacles that lead to accidents. In this context, the Domino Theory states that 
accidents occur as a chain of sequential events that fall like dominos. By following the circumstances that 
occur in a logical order, it is possible to use near misses and accidents as a learning potential or heads up for 
changes that are needed before they occur. The UPS to OBS method can be used for making the near miss 
accidents visible and accessible to other employees so they can be discussed at meetings and used to 
establish/improve a safety culture.  
Defining where the responsibility lies in relation to companies and individuals makes it easier to focus on 
where the problem is and helps to clear up ambiguities or gray areas. In relation to the Double Diamond, this 
would be the convergent thinking, which seeks answers to specific questions and seeks to understand who 
has the responsibility for these actions. In this section, I have also presented a method which is open to 
changes and modifications but is also specific in its approach to handling safety and near miss accidents.

5.2 Organizational actors 
 When it comes to safety, there are many different actors in the field who work within distinct areas and 
sometimes they overlap as seen in figure 13. These actors establish laws and regulations, ensure compliance 
with the rules, offer information and advice and act when huge damage has happened in a company. The 
actors also enter into agreements, research in safety, act as advisor to the Ministry of Employment, protect 
the work environment at work and provide financial support for projects around the country.  
Figure 13. Organizational actors show the different organizational actors and their roles when it comes to safety matters. Almost 
all of the actors are public institutions with a few exceptions, such as the safety consultant. All of the actors are equal in 
importance although the layout can be interpreted differently. Adapted from(Branche Fællesskab Arbejdsmiljø, 2018) 
All this focus provides safety development from top to bottom and bottom to top which brings the risk of 
occupational accidents down each year. But according to Seim et al, (2012) there is rarely a direct route from 
the central work environment actors, who are trying to reduce occupational accidents, to a given company 
where the necessary changes are required (Seim et al, 2012) The effort will take place in a flow where it 
passes from one actor to the next and beyond to additional actors. When it arrives at the companies, there 
is also a flow from one actor to the next. A new policy rule will for example, go through the WEA and move 
from a central level of supervisors, to the Industry of the Working Environment Community (BFA) that works 
out guidelines for how the policy rules can be met along with giving advice to companies. All these actors 
have their own understanding of how the rule should be interpreted and what significance it should have in 
practice. (Seim et al, 2012) In the company, the rule and its interpretation may be received by a safety 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
representative and safety coordinator, who will communicate it to the work environment organization (AMO) 
and then go on to the managers and employees, where the changes will hopefully influence their practices. 
This means that efforts gradually take on their own lives and are more or less transformed by the participants’ 
own understanding of how it makes sense. (Seim et al, 2012) These actors as presented inside a company 
will be elaborated further in the analysis on page 52.  
Companies with more than 10 employees are obliged to have a work environment organization (AMO) 
(BrancheFællesskabet for Arbejdsmiljø, 2017) which handles the safety culture and follows up on 
occupational accidents. Nevertheless, it can be difficult to create a functional work environment organization 
since it requires commitment from the employees, which is not necessarily there, and often its status is not 
recognized by colleagues. Lack of commitment and status means that there is less motivation and drive to 
train to be a safety representative. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2010)  
People who have important key functions in relation to the company's work environment plans, such as the 
employer, an appointed workforce and elected occupational safety representative often comprise the 
members of a work environment organization. If an employer has more than 35 employees, the work 
environment organization often consists of a safety committee and several work environment groups. 
However, if the employer has less than 35 employees, the safety committee and work environment group 
are combined into one. (Arbejdsmiljø, 2018)  
 
The safety committee is responsible for the strategic planning, managing and coordinating of the company's 
cooperation between different departments and work environment groups with regard to safety and health. 
The safety committee can also take initiatives for greater cross-cutting workplace efforts and deal with 
problems when local work environment groups cannot solve issues themselves. The safety committee also 
participates in the preparation of the company's work place evaluation (APV) and sets up principles for 
training and instruction. Furthermore, the safety committee must aid in the planning of changes regarding 
production or service if it has something to do with the working environment. This applies in situations such 
as: 
• 
If the company is to be expanded or rebuilt 
• 
If there is a need for changes to machinery and technical aids 
• 
If new technology is introduced 
• 
If substances and materials are purchased which have not been used by the company before 
• 
If new or changed workflows, processes and methods are introduced. (Arbejstilsynet, 2016) 
 
The local working environment groups are the bedrock of the safety work and are responsible for the daily 
operational tasks. It consists of leaders and the safety representative who are chosen by the employees at 
the workplace. (Arbejdsmiljø, 2018) The working environment group is the contact point between the 
employees and the safety committee and is responsible for helping to ensure that the work processes, 
workplace, technical aids and substances and materials comply with the laws and guidelines as stated by the 
Danish working environment authority. The working environment group must work consistently to prevent 
accidents at work and carry out a thorough analysis of the causes and learning potential for an accident when 
it has occurred. The working environment group must also continuously monitor safety and health among 
employees to prevent the occurrence of occupational health problems.

Summary 
In this section I have referred to different organizational actors that are involved in safety matters as well as 
given an example of how different actors can interpret a policy rule which is then passed on to other actors. 
Subsequently, the safety committee and work environment group were presented as the bedrock of safety-
related matters. Here the leaders and safety representatives have a huge role to play regarding the safety 
culture and its internal safety practice especially when new technology, machines, materials, and so on are 
introduced. 
In relation to the Double Diamond this section ranged back and forth between the divergent and convergent

individuals and practices that can help new employees to be safer at the workplace. 
5.3 Finances regarding occupational accidents 
There do not exist any standards for how to work out a safety economic analysis regarding occupational 
accidents, but a frequent approach is to calculate the cost of an accident at work. Are done by using the sum 
of salary due to absence and multiply the sum of other costs caused by the accidents. (Industriens 
Branchearbejdsmiljøråd, 2016) Such costs could include: 
❖ Minor and major injuries and, worst case scenario, death. 
o Fines and forced closure due to unsafe working environment. 
o Costs for the injured victim and their relatives. 
o Negative reputation due to safety issues. 
• 
Employment issues. 
• 
Unstable working conditions and fear among employees. 
❖ Cost for time spent by managers, safety representatives and other coworkers on stop and follow up 
on the accident. 
❖ Cost of repairing material damage. 
❖ Production losses. 
❖ Costs due to higher rates for temporary workers and possibly overtime work. 
❖ Costs due to lower productivity. As a rule of thumb, the productivity costs are 1-2 times greater than 
the total cost of salary in the absence period.

6. Analysis  
This analysis presents different levels of safety understanding and various actors that are involved in the 
safety of new employees. Firstly, several of the actors appear as personas, who point out possible pitfalls and 
how they can make a positive impact on safety internally.  Secondly, I will consider potential risk factors for 
new employees in relation to work qualification, work tasks, organizing work tasks and workplace charisma. 
Finally, induction and introduction courses are designed in connection with safety and other preventive 
procedures. 
 
6.1 Working with and across levels of safety  
Safety is a strange entity that can be found on many different levels and is practiced in many ways that are 
both explicitly and tacitly expressed. Furthermore, the knowledge is embedded in social relations and the 
participants’ bodily skills which are transmitted and stored in the community. (Gherardi S. , 2006) In this way, 
learning is established through involvement and participation in a practice, where safety becomes a 
“knowing-in-practice”. (Gherard, 2018) This is further elaborated in the theory section on page 23.  
For creating a visual presentation and showing how to interact with the different levels of safety practices I 
have crate figure 14., which show five levels of safety understanding that express different perspectives of 
working with safety. Nevertheless, these perspectives are mingled together in a seamless net of practice 
where one cannot be without the other if there is a sincere wish to avoid occupational accidents.  
 Figure 14. Levels of safety understanding. The diagram shows 5 different levels of development of safety prevention from the 
technological perspective to the refraction perspective. Own Diagram. 
 
The first level of understanding is the technical perspective, which revolves around the development of safety 
machines and protective personal equipment. This is, for example, seen in cases with emergency stop 
buttons on machines, protection of sharp blades, protection of loose objects, blocking of holes and cracks 
that cause jamming and so on. 
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
At the second level, it is recognized that safety is not only about preventing access or stopping further 
damage in a critical situation since it requires more involvement regarding the humans’ actions and their 
complexities. An example of this could be protective personal equipment that is too complicated or hard to 
use, for example due to fitting, itching or sweating issues.  Further, machines without warning signs, sound 
alerts, instructions, intuitive setup and so on are also problematic in relation to occupational accidents.  
By following this perspective, safety can also be seen as a socio-technical system where the end result is a 
construction of unity collective process of doing that is concerned with people, symbolic forms and 
technologies assembled in a system of material existence. This system is made up of heterogeneous 
components like a hybrid network where there is no distinction between action and constraints, cultural and 
natural aspects and human and non-human elements. (Latour, 1993) Instead all these elements are 
interlinked with each other in a continual renewal process to bring forth what appears to be actors, objects 
and phenomena of everyday life. (Fenwick, 2015) So, in this way a safe workplace or community is the 
product of heterogeneous elements, such as competencies, relations, communications, materials and people 
that are integrated in various work practices. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) 
 
The third level in figure 14. presents the organizational perspective that involves the topics of safety culture, 
leadership and procedures. This will be further elaborated through the personas later in this analysis. 
However, generally when reference is made to the organizational perspective and a strong safety culture, 
there is often focus on the management and the extent to which they achieve unity within an organization 
even though this undertaking might be misplaced due to work from all the other employees in the 
organization. Safety observed from an organizational perspective is often about responsibility and managing 
risk through organizing humans, resources, technology and artifacts into reliable systems that can deal with 
safety as a daily practice. This has also been called the “technical route to safety” which is further elaborated 
in Appendix 4. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007)  
The forth level of the social perspective considers safety to be a collective competence that is generated in 
organizational and interorganizational work practices that protect both the environment and individual’s 
welfare. Safety is therefore an ability that is unfolded and realized in practice, that is socially constructed as 
well as transmitted between employees and new employees in the company. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) 
Furthermore, this practice is transmitted out in the community of practice where it is embedded in social 
institutions, standards and norms as well as knowledge. This level is explained further in the theory chapter 
and later on in the analysis regarding induction and introduction courses.  
The refraction perspective, the fifth level, is about engaging safety with others to find alternative solutions 
to safety issues. This happens through establishing networks that analyze and inspect near misses or 
accidents and integrate their perspectives into the community. On the other hand, refraction is also about 
changing the established norms and culture which are not working in favor of the safety practice. Finally, the 
mindset of individuals in the community is likewise to be changed into a more reflective, questioning habit 
of why the practice is done as it is. For the rest of this analysis, the focus will be on the 3 last perspectives: 
the organizational, social and refraction perspectives.

6.2.1 Management at strategic level involvement in safety 
Insufficient management can become an expensive affair for the company, both financially and in terms of 
human suffering and possibly death as the result of accidents. Traditionally, the leaders at the strategic level 
fundamentally ensure the company's existence regarding the market, economy and technology. But 
managing exclusively with only these parameters constitutes the easiest form of management, according to 
WEA. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2000 (b)) A professional leader understands that it is the human being that is the key 
to value creation. (Pigenur & Osterwalder, 2010) 
When looking at legislation, management is responsible for occupational accidents at the 
company, but at the same time it can be said that management is responsible for involving 
employees and employees are responsible for their involvement in safety. Regine Grytnes from 
Arbejdsmedicin universitetsklinik – own translation. 
As described in the quotation above, the CEO and strategic level have the overall responsibility for safety at 
the workplace. Their choices and focus on how the company should be run, as well as handling economic 
measures such as KPI, have great importance for the safety culture and safety at work. In this context a 
company can assume an infinite number of views when it comes to safety but generally these views may fall 
under three categories according to the WEA;  
• 
Passive attitude - Little focus on safety. There is little to do even after an accident, even if an accident 
is reported, little improvement is made to the conditions. “The accident was just an accident”. 
 
• 
Reactive attitude - management does not do anything in anticipation. Nevertheless, the 
management and person(s) responsible for safety respond to the issues after an accident has 
occurred. This response can possibly prevent a new accident of the same nature, but the actual cause 
of the accident will not be addressed, which may lead to other occupational accidents. 
 
• 
Proactive position - the level of safety is monitored continuously. Safety issues are anticipated before 
they occur, and prevention is implemented before the accident happens. Contingency plans for 
minor and major accidents are planned in advance and the person responsible for each safety

are maintained at a high level and stay up to date with new safety regulations.

6.2.3 A foreman’s positive influence on safety 
A foreman is responsible for ensuring good inspection and maintenance routines at work, which is an 
important part of preventive safety management. The planned inspections are a central tool in the work of 
identifying problems before accidents or injuries happen. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2000 (b)) In addition, inspections 
can also be an indicator of how efficiently the work is done in relation to safety planning, internal 
communication and purchasing control.  
However, these kinds of inspections are described as keeping track and maintaining order at the workplace 
in the description of persona 3., at the next page where specific demands are made on the employees. The 
employees are expected to wear personal protective equipment, use lifting equipment, watch out for each 
other and clean up after themselves when they are in the production facility.  
According to WEA, it is also important to make what they call safety inspections which include equipment, 
materials, chemicals and observation of workmanship. This will provide the foreman and management with 
feedback on how good their maintenance is on machinery and equipment. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2000 (b)) 
Furthermore, the safety inspections will also establish a picture of how well the safety procedures, rules and 
work permits are being upheld. In this regard, planned inspections should likewise cover situations where 
there are no established routines, such as R&D, pilot projects, ground research etc., or other technical 
modifications and changes in the production. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2000 (b))

6.2.4 A safety representative’s positive influence on safety 
A safety representative is elected for two years among the employees in the department of the company, 
where the work environment group is to perform its duties. The safety representative does not need to be a 
member of any union or organization. (Arbejstilsynet, 2016) In addition, the same person can take on the 
role of both a staff representative and safety representative. The safety representative is protected from 
==PAGE:59==

being dismissed and suffering any other down grading of his or her working conditions, in the same way as 
the staff representative, in relation to their professional work. (Arbejstilsynet, 2016) Furthermore, the safety 
representative cannot be placed at a disadvantage due to the activities associated with the work. 
The elected employee announces the result of the election to the employer and after that the protection 
occurs from the time the employer or his organization becomes acquainted with the election to the role, 
regardless of whether the function occurs sooner or later. (Arbejstilsynet, 2016) 
There is a great deal of variety in the job descriptions of safety representatives. Nevertheless, the focus is 
always on securing the safety and health of everyone in relation to the company and its practice. On a daily 
basis, the safety representative either sits in a working environment group or is in close contact with the 
working group. They also work with the workplace leader, who is able to draw on information from the safety 
representative in connection with planning, purchasing and so on.

As presented in persona 4., coworkers should be able to talk with a safety representative and get advice 
about a case or issues that are related to safety and health. But first of all, the safety representative is 
expected to make it clear that everyone has responsibility for both their own safety and collectively for the 
other members of staff.  
It's not me who are going home and telling your wife that you accidently fell down from a ladder 
and broke your arm, and then got fired as result of it because you did not want to comply with the

equipment that you need to do the job, it's me you must come to, then I arrange it. Henrik safety

In the quotation above, the safety representative is making a clear statement that the employees should 
think of safety because of him but take care of themselves and think about how an occupational accident 
could affect them. The collective responsibility is also emphasized by asking the employees to come to him 
for assistance. However, a safety representative can for example also assist the preparation of workplace 
evaluations and discuss how to prevent and treat any occupational accidents. This also counts when it comes 
to assessment for new tasks, internal conflicts, bullying and so on.  
Nevertheless, according to WEA the written contingency is to be taken a step further than presented by the 
persona by developing a contingency plan for all likely emergency situations. These contingency plans made 
by the safety representative and others should be sufficient to support and handle any emergencies that may 
come along and reduce the effects of them. (Arbejdstilsynet, 2000 (b)) 
The relationship seen in figure 15. between the safety representative and staff representative is often related

6.2.5 A staff representative’s influence on safety 
The staff representative is the employee's intermediary, who helps both employer and trade union matters. 
A staff representative is typically chosen for a professional group. So, if there are more professional groups 
at a workplace, there may be several staff representatives, who typically work together. But there are also 
many companies without a staff representative. 
Usually a staff representative would stand up and negotiate on behalf of the employees’ terms regarding 
senior policy, competence development, personnel policy, payment, vacation, retirement, etc. Furthermore, 
a staff representative can also negotiate advanced and local agreements in accordance with a trade union. 
(HK, 2014) Despite the staff representative’s important role in relation to the employees by negotiating terms 
and establishing a good work culture, they do acutely not have anything to do with the new employees that

6.2.7 Coworker’s influence on safety 
Safety at work is formally the CEO’s and strategic management’s responsibility but in practice it is important 
that the management and employees in collaboration address safety issues through dialogue, meetings, 
instruction and analysis of near misses and occupational accidents. 
It is essential to understand that safety is a group effort, since the employees at work form the safety culture, 
where the applicable norms are recognized as acceptable. For example, is it acceptable to work with or 
without a helmet, and when is it okay to do without, for example when digging a hole. A safety culture is of 
course not a static element and it can be changed over time, but it is often difficult to change established 
norms. Nevertheless, coworkers can help each other by reminding their colleagues to: 
• 
change position when working and use personal aids. 
• 
distribute the tasks so that no one is charged unilaterally for too long, too much, or has too many 
bad shifts. 
• 
remember breaks and time for exercise, preferably with other colleagues. 
• 
encourage colleagues experiencing pain to keep on working, but in other roles, where the pain can 
be managed daily. 
• 
take care of each other and have an open policy about insufficient safety measures and procedures.  
(bfa-i, 2015) 
Employees in a company interact with each other in various ways; something that seems like nothing or small 
talk can be developed into a new business idea, a huge event, an internal revolution or even a crisis. So, 
therefore it is important to understand the cultural interweaving and its effects on social practice as 
described in the theory chapter. But this is beyond the scope of this thesis.

6.2.8 Human resources’ influence on safety 
The human resources department (HR) and the surrounding work environment is increasingly integrated in 
the efforts to construct healthy, secure and more evolving jobs by focusing on common goals and tasks.  Thus, 
the company can be utilized to its full potential. The HR department handles the recruitment of new 
employees and tries to get the right people for the tasks that need to be solved internally in the company. 
An incorrect assessment can lead to misappropriation and unwanted costs as well as intrigue in the 
workplace. A misappropriation can lead to the decision to fire an employee, which is never easy and can 
create extra work for the HR department, since they sometimes need to go in and, for instance, give 
psychological support to the manager due to the event. 
In relation to new employees, the HR department is often the first point of contact in the company when 
they are come for a job interview. Afterwards HR documents the new employee in the internal system with 
everything that follows, such as giving them access keys, a phone, an email account, necessary information 
regarding their job and so on.  
The center of well-being is often located in the HR department as they can offer their help in many different 
situations and deal with the determination of salaries, bonuses, employee benefits and employee 
development. This assistance in providing an overview of relevant data, which the safety representative, staff 
representative and manager can use in their work related to safety. This data is collected through project 
coverage regarding staffing of posts, proficiency profiles, sick leave, travel days, flexibility and so on. 
However, the understanding of handling instructions for machinery, does not need to be a perfectly written 
description in multiple languages but can be a set of instructions produced in various forms, for example a 
guide which only uses pictures to describe the risks and how to handle the machine.  
Over the course of time, the HR department learns how new knowledge and information is disseminated 
within the organization, which of course is valuable information for the safety representative and managers 
when it comes to safety and spreading the message.

6.3.1 Work qualifications 
There are special rules that apply to Danes below the age of 18 regarding employment. This includes 
supervision by an adult, which of course is done for a good reason, since a higher accident rate among this 
population has been recorded, compared to other groups. However, it is unusual for people under 18 years 
of age to be working in the metal industry since it requires a qualification as a craftsman, which takes several 
years after completing lower secondary education. Furthermore, young employees rarely have the 
opportunity to collect a huge amount of life experience, which they can use to predict accidents and know 
how to react when they happen. 
Young people do not always have the life experience and tools that are necessary, for example to 
mentally deal with patients, citizens or customers who may be violent. Often it is also a bit of a 
combination because in some jobs they are asked to perform slightly more dangerous tasks or hard 
demanding tasks, which places greater stress on the body. Pete Kines from NFA – own translation. 
Life experience is not something that comes suddenly. It steadily develops over time and yet the human being 
is so cleverly adapted that each new experience strengthens the ability to overcome new situations, 
challenges and issues. In addition, consciously or unconsciously, experience creates a basic sense of security 
and courage to face the obstacles of tomorrow. Unfortunately, the opposite can also happen and when more 
bad experiences are gathered, the more uncomfortable and skeptical a person becomes and less daring to 
try something new. Competencies that can be achieved through life experience and used regardless of the 
situation that occurs in life are: 
• 
Intuition 
•

• 
Self-management, management and conflict management 
• 
Collaboration 
• 
Communication skills 
• 
Creativity (Kirketerp-Møller, 2017)     
Another important part of the work qualification is the work moral, which again is a broad composition of 
components that can vary from company to company. However, if an employee is not ready to work and 
take orders and would be on his/her phone or playing games, it is clear they are not going to be in the job 
for long. So, skills like turning up to work fresh and be prepared for work and being socially active and able 
to self-manage are important competencies, among many other things, for it to be said that one has a high 
work morale.

worker. Apprentices that are becoming practitioners in a community are beginning to identify themselves

they start to learn the collective knowledge embodied within the community. 
Even though the apprentice does not see themselves as a risk-taker, they are placing the risk within the work 
conditions of their practice as they are doing. This is a huge difference from the skilled workers, who know 
how to practice safety but tend to be over familiar with their practice. 
Temps are a common group when it comes to new employees and most of the time they are skilled, trained 
and educated in a similar field in relation to the metal industry. However, temps do not necessarily have prior 
knowledge of the workplace, other employees or more specific information which is usually required for a 
safe work practice. 
Nevertheless, the categories of skilled worker and apprentice will often see their relationship to safety and

they are not afraid to share, and does not reflect on their standard of work. Furthermore, norms and safety 
culture for these categories of workers are developed though training and work practice, which are learned 
at the particular work place.

6.3.2 Work conditions 
Industrial sectors that require hard physical work is evident in all statistics when it comes to occupational 
accidents. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) In addition, other tasks that require working with machinery, chemicals 
and biological production are also vulnerable and are at high risk regarding occupational accidents. 
The fact that apprentices and new employees are at greater risk of occupational injuries is not so 
much about taking chances because that is the common perception, but that they are required to 
do the most dangerous work. Jan Toft Dansk Metal. 
Regarding physical activity and heavy lifting work, it appears that young people aged between 18-28 years 
generally report higher physical work demands than people over 30 years old, especially when it comes to 
• 
More pushing and pulling during working hours. 
• 
More work with back angled or bent. 
• 
Less time with sitting down at work. 
Likewise, young people lift too many things over 15 kilos during a work day, compared with people over 30 
years old, which of course increases the likelihood of joint injury, but that is beyond the scope of this thesis.  
(Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) However, since this approach is common among young men, it has been called the 
Tarzan syndrome. Young men often tend to present themselves as someone that can deliver good results 
both quickly and efficiently, which is a good thing. The problem with the Tarzan syndrome is that there are 
often little or none careful thoughts given to how the practice will be conducted and the stressed conditions 
there are pressed to almost the limits of its capability. This can of course easily lead to unnecessary errors 
and an increased number of accidents due to lack of consideration for the work process. 
Because the young new employees had not caught that you could actually get recognition for doing 
the job correctly and slowly but surely, they were focused on how quickly they could finish the work 
and complete it in such a way that they would get recognition for the work. It surprised me how 
attentive the young new employees were in doing the work in a manner that meant that they would 
get recognition by their colleagues. Johnny Dyreborg from NFA – own translation. 
In relation to the Tarzan syndrome, it may be wrong to only blame the new young employees. Perhaps the 
responsibility should be placed in a social context at the workplace, where several guidelines for the new 
employees are outlined, which are followed up by the colleagues in a constructive way.  
 
Seniority 
Building seniority in the workplace appears to have a greater value than first thought according to IDA. 
(Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) Especially for new employees when it comes to occupational accidents, since it is 
one of the reasons why new employees are more often affected by accidents. Lack of awareness about how 
to find and use tools and where to get help with using the tools in a safe and secure way is an obvious way 
that mistakes occur. A long-term seniority will reduce these elements and make it easier to turn away from 
risky and unsafe jobs.

"It is clear that the incidence of people with short seniority is highest, i.e. they are at a significantly 
greater risk of being notified of an occupational accident."  (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014, pp. 15-16) 
 
Furthermore, the statistics show that new employees with seniority who also change from one industry to 
another, are more likely to experience occupational accidents. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) In addition, people 
who have been unemployed for a longer period are also at higher risk for accidents.  
Despite this, the interviews conducted with the safety representative showed on two occasions that new 
employees who had established some seniority and been in the company for a couple of years were actually 
coming up with new ideas that changed the way of communication internally, and which subsequently 
improved safety for all employees due to using new online media solutions. 
Instead of having datasheets, we have created an app where you can find it. Because some new 
employees came in and requested this, since it was difficult to understand, read and find the 
datasheets. So, now all our security sheets are on the app, so you can easily see and compare them 
with other datasheets. Henrik safety representative – own translation. 
6.3.3 Work organizing 
New employees are a particularly vulnerable group to negative economic trends as they often are hired into 
busy periods or because of their lower wages, so the company can reduce their costs. However, this is 
unusual for the metal industry, since the newly qualified workers have been through education and training 
courses that train them to be skilled workers. 
Then there are also some companies that have this shelter for the new ones, where there is a whole 
structure around the new employee, so that they do not even need to be trained. Because the safety 
is built into the physical structure which makes the training visible. Sadly, there are just not that 
many companies who do that. Regine Grytnes from Arbejdsmedicin universitetsklinik – own 
translation. 
As mentioned in the quotation, some companies organize their work around the new employee to avoid 
occupational accidents and give them a learning opportunity by taking extra care of them by placing them in 
some physical structures that ensure safe practice. Sometimes it requires making physical changes at a 
workplace to adapting a safer procedure, as described in figure 14. In the technical and socio-technical 
perspective. 
If you need to change something in regard to safety, it must be based on the structure that is in the 
workplace. It may be that they say that these machines should be set up in another way or they 
need some extra equipment attached to them, so that you do not always have to remember what 
you have to do to remain safe. Johnny Dyreborg from NFA – own translation. 
However, the physical changes need to be adjusted according to the assignments and the safety culture at 
the workplace otherwise, no changes would occur in the behavior of the employees.  
One of the most predictable things in a workplace is human behavior, but of course there is a variety 
in the work. But sometimes it may also require a certain variety in the work to make the product. 
But that's how it's part of the human nature, but people's behavioral patterns are extremely 
predictable. Johnny Dyreborg from NFA – own translation.

Nevertheless, the changes to the system also need to be flexible, since a company rarely does only one 
thing in relation to their production. Adjustments are made on a daily basis for creating a product, which 
needs to be considered before the changes of the physical structures are made.   
However, in some companies there is a common hiring and firing policy among companies called “last in first 
out”, which creates a great deal of performance anxiety and stress for new employees, since they feel that 
they must over perform to keep their jobs in the company. 
 
6.3.4 Work obstacles  
Even though the metal industry is big and involves many different companies, the most common 
occupational accidents are still ordinary and consist of the same accidents as other sectors, for example: 
• 
Risk of getting hit by falling objects, especially when it comes to hoisting, stacking goods and loose 
assembly pieces. 
• 
Risk of being knocked down by internal traffic. 
• 
Risk of falling and stumbling when moving around at the workplaces. 
• 
Risk of falling off ladders, machines and structures.  
• 
Risk of injury while using machines (I-bar.dk, 2016) 
However, there are various types of jobs where these accidents are possible and they all have different 
approaches or practice in such cases. This means that there is no real standard for how the work should be 
performed, but here are a few highlighted obstacles in relation to safety for new employees.  
Changes in the work site at workplaces, such as in the construction industry, where the staff arrive at a new 
building site that has been ravaged by the weather or built on since last time they were there. This leads to 
an uncertainty about where things are and what dangers the staff are likely to encounter in the area they are 
working. However, In the metal industry, this is usually seen in job positions such as service technicians, who 
investigate and fix customer production facilities, machines, pipes and so on at different locations.  
Several new employees also have variable working hours or night shifts, which may lead to further exposure 
to risks. In many cases, these disrupted safety routines and challenging situations can interfere with a good 
introduction course, which of course further increases the risk of occupational injuries for the new 
employees. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) An increased or excessive production speed can likewise cause 
problems for the introduction of new employees, since there often will be no time for questions and 
important safety issues may be forgotten. The high pressure from intensive production may also pose a 
greater risk of errors or lead to employees going down with stress.  
“It’s all a chain, so that working quicker means that there is less time to keep the site tidy. If the site 
is tidy, accidents are less likely to happen.” (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007, p. 8) 
IT systems are used in almost every company today both for planning and information management, but 
other IT systems can also be used to control the rhythm and pace of production speed, which again affects 
employee safety. 
IT systems can create more optimization and accessibility for a lot of situations. Unfortunately, the IT systems 
can also generate challenges and changes in unintended ways such as: 
==PAGE:71==
71 
 
• 
Changes in the content and priorities of the work. 
• 
Changes in the content of the profession and the mission statement in the work. 
• 
Changes in learning and development opportunities. 
• 
Changes in communication and the current framework of communication. (Hellerup, 2018) 
Another disadvantage is the inconvenience of the systems in relation to inflexibility such as unsubscribe, 
medical visits, accidents, etc.  
Because IT systems have unintentional features, it is important to know and have a sense of who has the 
overall responsibility for changes in the IT system and the chain reaction that comes with it. In terms of safety, 
it is also important to know who to go to when things do not work properly. 
In the meantime, many questions may arise in the gray zone, for example, what is legitimate and not in the 
form of: 
• 
Independence versus control requirements of the company. 
• 
Private life versus working life. 
• 
Safety versus job execution. 
• 
Freedom and time to live versus responsibility. 
 
This balance between these elements differs from company to company and is often included in the 
workplace's culture and norms and standards. 
 
6.3.5 Workplace charisma 
By looking at the new employee's work processes it is possible to determine what makes them particularly 
vulnerable, as previous described. Here the safety culture, the working environment and rewarding 
standards at the workplace come into play, as they contribute to creating a safety net for new employees.  
New employees have a greater tendency to forget about what they have learned regarding safety before and 
uncritically start working under the company’s culture and current workplace practice, which is not 
necessarily good or safe.  
In the metal industry, we have seen the example of the repair pit where employees jump over the 
repair pit even though they know perfectly well it's wrong. It is seen when a young new employee 
comes in and is carefully instructed by his safety representative that he should always go around 
the repair pit. But then about 2 hours later, the new employee does exactly the same as all their 
colleagues and skips over the repair pit. This is a good example of the fact that the work practices 
which the young person starts out with are more crucial to the young person than the safety 
instructions that he learned at school or just learned from the safety representative. Johnny 
Dyreborg from NFA – own translation. 
In the example above, we see how impactful the social dynamics at a workplace may be on the new 
employees’ understanding of safety practice. Furthermore, the new employees’ ability to question their own 
safety is usually also forgotten due to the cognitive demands of taking on board on so many new things and 
the wish to blend in. However, an understanding of the individual's needs in these pressured situations can 
be created, it might be possible to avoid occupational accidents.  
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
The new employees do not have much to reflect on and they do not consider themselves as special. 
It is very rare that the new employees speak up and say that they have a particular need; they do 
not want to stick out from their colleagues. Regine Grytnes from Arbejdsmedicin universitetsklinik 
– own translation. 
At the same time, most new employees have a difficult time deciding when a task is completed, how quickly 
it should be performed and to what extent it is possible to ask for help without appearing less able or 
unsuitable for the job. Here again the Tarzan syndrome and the opportunity to show off comes into play; 
there is a desire to create a picture of an eager and hardworking new employee. 
The biggest challenges with new employees are the conflicting requirements and sometimes even 
their own resources. They have been told that the working environment might be important but 
that they should focus on quality, service benefits and production goals. So, we see the dilemma 
raised by the working environment being very important, and they themselves seeing that they can 
be injured but they do not think about it at that age. Pete Kines from NFA – own translation. 
Colleagues and social activities are therefore of huge importance to the working environment and to the 
retention processes of the new employee. This will be described further in the induction of new employees 
in the next section. But working with colleagues can also create an increased risk of occupational accidents 
e.g. miscommunication, translation difficulties with foreign colleagues and communication with multiple 
actors working on the same object at the same time.  
“we were starting to mount and change a lock, which was full of dust, with a German employee 
who could not speak English. So we communicated by hand signals and I had to then push the 
machine in place with my other hand, so it fitted with the bolts. At the same time as this was 
happening, I was telling him to put the machine down, but he pulled on the wrong handle, which I

student at Roskilde, working as contractor and smith. Victim of several occupational accidents – 
own translation. 
As just mentioned in the quotation above, the miscommunication and poor translation resulted in a work 
accident that could have been avoided. Furthermore, sometimes lack of communication may also cause 
damage as in the example below: 
Communication is very important both if there is something that is harmful, but also if you have 
tested and found out that it is good enough. We need to get the information out to people, so it 
demystifies everything about it. The same goes for our wells. We thought they were asbestos-free 
from the late 1980’s but it turned out that they had not been well cleaned, so there was still 
asbestos residue in some of them. Apparently one of our co-workers could not sleep at night and 
thought he would die because of it. But with information and testing they did later, we could see 
that according to the threshold values we were exposed to, it was not harmful. Kim safety 
representative – own translation.

6.4 Induction of new employees 
The first few months of the hiring of new employees will create an attachment to the workplace, where it is 
especially important to get support from the manager and colleagues. So, the new employee feels accepted 
and recognized as part of the workplace community and thereby helps to create a good work environment.  
However, particular attention should be paid to fulfilling the expectations created in the recruitment process, 
since more than 50 % of new employees experience that the job does not live up to what was intended or 
promised. (OnboardingGroup, 2016) The consequence of this may be that the employee cannot relate to the 
new workplace and therefore finds another job. Here statics show that 22 % of all new employees resign 
within the first 45 days of their employment. (OnboardingGroup, 2016) 
It is not the young new employees that are the problem, but it is the culture and the way that the 
workplaces introduce young people and new employees, which is the problem. If the workplaces 
become better at introducing new employees, it will also be better for the young people. We believe 
there should be more focus on the workplace as a whole. Regine Grytnes from Arbejdsmedicin 
universitetsklinik – own translation. 
As mentioned in the quotation above, the new employees are not to blame although they do also have a 
responsibility to be flexible and ready to adjust to the norms at the new workplace. But on the other hand, 
the new employee must also be critically curious and hold on to his own knowledge and professionalism as 
well as asking questions about how the practice is usually conducted at the workplace.  
Nevertheless, the management is responsible for establishing the overall framework for the introduction and 
identifying members of staff who are supposed to ease the process for the new employee in cooperation 
with the working environment group and other relevant forums. (BrancheFællesskabet for Arbejdsmiljø for 
Velfærd og Offentlig administration, 2018) In addition, the colleagues play a crucial role when it comes to a 
good introduction process by introducing aspects of culture, formal and informal norms and values at the 
workplace. At the same time, colleagues can contribute to the introduction process by acting as contacts or 
mentors for new employees. A good induction may result in;   
• 
Better well-being for new employees 
• 
Greater quality regarding the assignment 
• 
Better retention of employees 
• 
Fewer accidents and injuries 
• 
Fewer sick days 
• 
The new employees can relieve their colleagues from various types of work tasks. 
• 
The new employees become more productive more quickly, thus shortening the time before they 
deliver a full potential. 
Establishment of a stronger community at work. (BrancheFællesskabet for Arbejdsmiljø for Velfærd og 
Offentlig administration, 2018) 
 Despite the importance of a good induction, it is the safety culture that matters, which is express through 
the colleagues taking care of each other and composing a safe practice. Practicing a safety culture is not 
something that comes easily and is a continuous process that must be actively maintained. The Kap score in 
this context is a model that can be used to clarify the level of a safe practice or safety culture by asking

questions in accordance with its measuring system that goes from ‘unaware’ to ‘behavior. (Rapid Asia, 2018) 
In the beginning of learning a new practice, the new employee is unaware of the safety aspects as seen in

Many of the young people reflect a lot about what they do and so do the older colleagues, but many

6.5 Introduction courses for new employees 
When it comes to the posthuman studies, it is a matter of seeing the whole individual and thereby 
understanding what is essential for the new employees. In this context the company is supposed to 
investigate the new employee’s background and sense which kind of skills and experience the new employee 
has in relation to the work practice as well as how they function socially in the work place. At the same time, 
it is important to know the new employees present circumstances and future wishes to understand how they 
position themselves in relation to the practice, taking risks and their time perspective for being in the 
company, which is further elaborated in section 6.3.1 Work qualification. 
However, introduction courses are often taught by various job trainers who instruct through peer-to-peer 
training, where the quality of the training depends on the teaching skills of the individual. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 
2014) The degree of planning that goes into the introduction courses vary according to the company's 
resources and focus on the work environment. Consequently, there can be a large variation in the scope and 
quality of the safety instructions from one company to another company.  
The size of the company often has an impact on the quality of an introduction course. Since larger companies 
in general are more organized and have fixed schedules regarding introduction courses compared to smaller 
companies. Smaller companies normally deliver ad-hoc introductions which means that no general plan is 
followed and therefore important safety principles may be forgotten or overlooked due to other incidental 
events there is taken the focus instead. In this context, the Work Environment Council recommends that the 
company develop a set of minimum requirements for ensuring proper instruction for new employees which 
==PAGE:76==
76 
 
of course contains safety and health instructions with description of how the practice could be done by the 
job trainers. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) In this context it is important that the teaching material corresponds 
to the safety culture inside the company, otherwise it will create doubts and misunderstandings among the 
new employees, which will lead to lack of credibility. 
A good introduction for new employees is training along with other employees, so they get training 
with others and see how others do it and do it the same way. Sometimes they get a training that is 
contrary to what actually happens in the company, for example by watching a film or something 
similar. One must ensure that training reflects what is happening in the company. No matter what 
training and tools you give them, if they see that the culture is different, that's what they do. Pete 
Kines from NFA – own translation. 
The way the workplace regards new employees is of utmost importance for the introduction at the 
workplace. For example, if the workplace regards the new employee as part of workforce who should be 
included in the company's operations as quickly as possible, or whether they see them as a person who, for 
a period of time, must be protected and have a period which is primarily about learning, where the output 
of operation is less important. (Nielsen et al., 2017). However, when it comes to introduction courses, they 
can be separated into five different categories of doing;  
1. None/limited introduction: There is typically not planned any introduction regarding the work and 
safety practice for the new employee. So, it is self-study with help from colleagues. 
2. Ad-hoc introduction: The workplace has not prepared a detailed introduction for the new employee, 
so it is unclear for the job trainer and the new employee what exactly they are going to do and learn 
at the workplace. However, the new employee is continuously introduced with challenges that arise 
ad hoc. The Ad-hoc training might be done by various colleagues who are less aware of their role and 
importance for the new employee. 
3. Fast track introduction: A short version of the originally planned introduction course to safety 
practice at the workplace. Sometimes the fast track introduction is planned beforehand but other 
times elements are just skipped from the original plan.  
4. Prepared and planned introduction: The workplace has beforehand prepared an introduction course 
for the new employee which is to be performed by the job trainer. The introduction does not 
necessarily need to be written but there are some prepared guidelines and safety practices which 
the new employees is introduced to. Despite the given instruction, the material is typically not made 
for reflection and will focus on the new employee’s adaption to work process and tasks, instead of 
learning how to ensure a safe work practice in any situation and questioning the established practice.  
5. Reflexive introduction: Both the workplace and the new employee know exactly which tasks are to 
be performed and what is expected to be learned from the practice. Beforehand it has been agreed 
who introduces and has the responsibility for training the new employee in the different safety 
procedures and tasks. This does not necessarily need to be the same person. There is ongoing 
reflection on the practice and how it is possible for the new employees to adapt to a new situation 
and thereby prevent occupational accidents. Furthermore, the importance of new employees asking 
their colleagues if they are any doubt about the practice is emphasized (Nielsen et al., 2017) 
 
Despite these 5 categories of introduction, the reality is sometimes more complex when it comes to working 
with humans as mentioned in the quotation below. New employees do not have the same needs or start on 
==PAGE:77==
77 
 
the same level of understanding when they arrive at work. Therefore, it is a job for the job trainer to clarify 
and discover when they are training the new employees.    
 
We sometimes hear from companies that’ this guy has been in many other places, we do not need 
to instruct him’. In any case, we have seen with other studies, that even if you have experience from 
another job, machines are always slightly different, expectations a bit different, and things are a 
little different. It is clear for a completely new apprentice, you do not have the same need as if you 
already have some qualifications, but you still need the recognition that says here take some extra 
time to make sure it's done in a safe way, that it's actually something we approve of on from the 
management point of view. Johnny Dyreborg from NFA – own translation. 
For this training there are various teaching strategies with new employees and it is not possible to say which 
one is better or worse because of different practices and individual preferences. Nevertheless, some of the 
different strategies, among many others, could be; 
• 
Ad-hoc instruction 
• 
Fast track instruction. 
• 
Self-study 
• 
Online learning 
• 
Class teaching 
• 
Trained from small to large tasks prepared and planned beforehand 
• 
Trained by multiple employees 
• 
Peer-to-peer training 
• 
One-to-one - canonized introduction 
• 
Intensive training modules 
 
Despite the best intentions to protect the new employee from accidents, the worst thing a company or 
job trainer can do is to let them stand and watch without being able to take part in the practice. 
The worst thing you can experience as an apprentice is just to look and do nothing. Then the

that you have accomplished something. Regine Grytnes from Arbejdsmedicin universitetsklinik – 
own translation. 
The job trainer often works as a mentor or role model for the new employees that introduces the safety 
practice through canonized and non-canonized practices. Those practices there are defined as canonized 
performance can be passed on to new employees through the traditional education and training principles, 
while all the non-canonized practices are safety instructions handed over through participation but taught 
through implicit and unreflective practices. (Nielsen et al., 2017)  
By relating safety as a practice where learning is connected to professional expertise, the process that is 
changing within the individual develops an identity over time, which is intertwined with the community’s 
practice. However, first the new employee needs to be comfortable with the practice.

a new task that is unfamiliar. It is like driving a car when you have just received your driving license. 
Johnny Dyreborg from NFA – Own translation. 
While the practice is in progress, an awareness and focus on safety is needed, which raises the importance

“This ability was evaluated partly in relation to whether the apprentice got the work he was

the importance of participating in the activity that is happening as a way of learning the cultural competences 
in the community.

6.6 Partial conclusion 
The understanding that new employees can be equated with being a young employee has been refuted even 
though there are many similarities and an overrepresentation of young people among the category of new 
employees. A category based solely on age is an over-simplified approach that cannot prevent occupational 
accidents among new employees. The incidence of reported occupational accidents is particularly high for 
new employees and especially those with a shorter seniority of service, who at the same time are switching 
from one industry to another industry at the beginning of their employment. 
However, it is recommended that the prevention of occupational accidents among new employees be 
nuanced so that different considerations come into play, such as the topics analyzed in relation to work 
qualification, work tasks, organization of work and workplace charisma. If these nuances are not addressed, 
there is a risk that too much attention will be paid to the individual new employees' cognitive abilities and 
experience in recognizing the workplace’s structure, practice and cultural preferences. This will be difficult 
for a new employee even though they are experienced in switching to a new job.  
Exercising one's practical skills and experience is the primary source of feeling safe on the job. Therefore, in 
an industry with constant changes in tasks and assignments, it appears to be more attractive for new 
employees to carry out current tasks in a competent manner at the beginning of their employment, rather 
than to plan and work with the forward-looking work environment and safety culture in the company. This 
happens through the induction which is crucial for maintaining the safety culture and the first step towards 
retention of employees.  
In the induction process, connections are established to other colleagues, who are responsible for the new 
employee’s safety at work and teaching the collective and organizational knowledge within the company. As 
presented in the personas, safety is not only one thing, but a practice done by various actors each in their 
own way. Even though the personas tell a story of their positive impact on safety, so too do the challenges 
presented due to the many facets of safety. After all, what might work in one company can be a wrong 
decision in another regarding safety.  
The investigation also shows that the new employees are responsible for participating in the practice 
although it is the management that is responsible for setting up the overall framework. The colleagues are 
supposed to ease the process for the new employee in cooperation with the working environment group. 
The new employees will then bring knowledge, experience and new ideas to the workplace which can be 
adopted to the existing safety culture. 
The new employees gain trust and knowledge about safety by concentrating actually handling the assignment 
using previously learned knowledge to identify potential sources regarding dangerous situations. In this 
understanding, doing and knowing are intertwined were knowledge is not a simple object that can be 
transmitted from one person to another, but rather a practical activity in itself that is a consistent source of 
doing. However, the knowledge is not only expressed in an explicit way but is also tacit and latent, which 
emphasizes the importance of participating in the activity as a way of learning the cultural competences.   
By using the reflexive introduction courses, the new employees become familiar with the safety procedures

7.2. Solution 
By doing a quick market research on existing games on the internet, I found various types of educational 
games that challenge and develop players’ skills regarding logic, vision, language, mind and bodily skills.  A 
few of those games also set up global dilemmas which make the player reflect over their choices. In any case, 
these games do not usually confronting safety issues at the work place or make the players reflect over their 
own dilemmas and practices as a new employee. The only game found their confront safety issues were the 
E-learning software called Convergence Training. (Convergence Training, 2018) However, the solution that 
has been selected is called the work environment game as seen in figure 18.  and it has been further 
developed since last presented in the selection matrix. 
Figure 18. The Work Environment Game. Own picture. 
 
The solution revolves around an online learning platform with an interactive game that uses narrative stories 
and dilemmas that are supposed to articulate the tacit knowledge and get the new employee to question the 
canonized practice due to the cases presented in the game and the job trainers’ introductory course, which 
has a possibility to be coupled up with the learning platform.   
The purpose is to give new employee’s additional tools and the possibility to reflect over their own safety 
practice. On the other hand, playing the game should help new employees to increase engagement and self-
motivation towards safety knowledge as well as giving them an independence sense of personal 
responsibility and better problem-solving skills.  
The game also offers the opportunity to see the situations from the outside and thereby gain an 
understanding of possible issues, conflicts and misunderstandings that can be dealt with at work without 
getting harmed. However, the game is not a replacement for the actual training that takes place at the 
workplace but should only be seen as a supplement with additional help to the new employee and the job 
trainer. 
==PAGE:88==
88 
 
With an online platform there is an opportunity to customize the game in relation to difficulty, education and 
job position, which makes it possible for the game to be aligned with the new employees’ practices and 
training programs as chosen by the job trainer. The job trainer has access to the platform and shares a link 
with the new employee which contains the selected safety instructions that are important to learn at the 
workplace. 
The safety instructions are delivered in the form of a game where the job trainer gets to know the results of 
the new employee’s answers. The new employee can have several attempts to get a good score. However, if 
the score is too low, the new employee should not be allowed to follow the practice at work before having a 
talk or being supervised by the job trainer. The score is counted as number of sick days for wrongly answered 
questions that leads to an accident. There is also a social status regarding dilemmas, which is indicated by 
smileys from sad to beloved as seen in the game. 
The job trainer is informed, and it is emphasized that not to criticize the result of the new employee but to 
use it for discussion. The job trainer is also supposed to expand on the answers with additional information 
and show where the safety knowledge can be learned and how to conduct practice safely in relation to the 
elements that are presented in the game. The conversation could lead on to other questions which are 
important for the new employee and thereby start a debate about attitudes to the safety culture internally 
at the workplace. There might be behaviors or practices that could be reconsidered regarding safety.  
After a couple of weeks, the job trainer would get a reminder to pick additional safety instructions games 
that are related to the new employees upcoming work process, but this time on a more difficult level than 
before. 
Depending on the topic of the game, which could be truck driving, personal protection equipment, chemicals 
and epoxy learning, etc.  there will be several different cases and dilemmas that are supposed to be solved 
or interacted with. The interaction possibilities make it possible to take different routes in the game, so the 
player has the freedom to choose and experience new material, if the scene or game are played again. 
7.2.1 Development choices 
During the development of the game, there have been several considerations to make even though it is a 
prototype that was created in PowerPoint, for example, should the game be created in two dimensions or 
three dimensions. 3D provides a more realistic and engaging effect than 2D, but it also complicates things 
when playing the game since it is usually necessary to learn and control more buttons than in a 2D game. 
However, the interaction and possibility to explore the universe is greater in a 3D game.  
 
Other considerations were also made or thought of in relation to the tradeoffs as presented in the literature 
review, such as player vs. computer control, building an avatar vs. choosing a character, one storyline vs. 
multiple storylines, singular time vs. open timeline, one narrator vs. multiple narrators and so on.  
 
Choices about how heavily movie-based the game should be were also important as a lot of text and non-
interactive elements are boring but nevertheless necessary to have to establish an understanding of what to 
do or what the goal is. In this context, there was a dilemma when deciding whether to use the term “new 
employee” in the game or shape it so everybody could play without misunderstanding their character and 
involvement in the game.  
 
==PAGE:89==
89 
 
The transition from creating an online game to something that involves face-to-face training at the work 
place was not that simple, even though it is only a conversation and a platform combination with the 
introduction course. It is considered to have an extensive effect on the new employees’ understanding of the 
work practice and safety culture as well as the ability to remember the game because of the many senses 
and past experiences that come into play. 
    
The decision about designing a simplified training course, improving certain skills or doing something else 
was likewise a tough decision. But it is believed that interacting and getting feedback from various job 
trainers, new employees and safety representative is more relevant for the development of the game than 
taking the decision on an uninformed basis.  
 
7.3 Testing the prototype 
In the deliver phase of the Double Diamond, a solution was chosen that was made in PowerPoint and tested 
at the company Alfa Laval A/S. The feedback that was given after the test was excellent and showed the 
possibility to go on with the game and make a business out of it as long as a management platform was 
created around it as an online cloud solution. This platform redefined the solution space and made room for 
a whole new iterative design process.   
All the participants at the test enjoyed playing the game with that in mind that the game was a prototype 
and only four out of eight cases where finished. The cases were created with inspiration from the most 
common occupational accidents in the metal industry, and focused on: 
Case 1. Social dilemmas along with understanding how well a task can be solved.  
Case 2. Risk of falling from heights by playing a mini interactive game. 
Case 3. Finding five safety issues in three different pictures which had multiple accident types. 
Case 4. Avoiding dangers of getting hit by falling objects through learning crane signals.  
Since the game was only the first prototype, it had several different errors and issues that were pointed out, 
for example the measuring system was problematic when clicking too many times on the picture in case 4. 
One of the cases was also presented too fast, which made it hard to read and get through without failing.  
The case about social challenges for new employees and the problem of completion or the quality of their 
work could be argued as ethically “correct” if their answers were wrong. This feedback gave rise to reflection 
on whether it would be better to use a different tactic to assess answering dilemmas. 
During the test several participants responded incorrectly in a case or two, which came as a surprise since 
these were employees who had experience and seniority with working in the metal industry. This led to a 
conclusion that the game had reached a good level of difficulty for employees and new employees bearing 
in mind that it was intended to start a discussion of reflection regarding safety practices.  
After the test, an engaging discussion started about the reception of goods at Alfa Laval A/S involving several 
different safety issues and preventive actions. It was inspiring to see how the game could become a focal 
point for a discussion about the safety culture and opened up for employees to share their points of view on 
the obstacles.   
==PAGE:90==
90 
 
Alfa Laval A/S also had cases of psychological stress symptoms in their department which they did not know 
how to deal with or prevent from happening. But it was believed that playing the game and learning about 
when to act on stress would ease the process and make it easier for managers to take action, based on the 
feedback from the game.   
Redefining the solution space as mentioned before was concerned with building a platform around the game 
to help managers and job trainers in their work, but also to focus on other employees, not just the new ones 
to deal with psychological obstacles. Furthermore, it was recommended that the game be used as a refresher 
and an anchor of established knowledge that could document the results, so the manager had tools and 
guidance to understand the progression of knowledge about safety in his department. If the progression 
deteriorated, employees might need extra courses, or someone should be invited to help them with their 
problems, however this is not within the scope of the solution space.

8. Discussion  
New employees as a group, have been statistically overrepresented regarding occupational accidents for 
decades, which may be due to the subjects identified in the analysis. But for some reason this negative 
statistic has not been improved by using the tools that are available today. however, it is considered by 
evolving the social and refraction perspectives into preventing occupational accidents, there might be a 
change in the statistics. This is further supported by the interviews made with leading researchers, safety 
representatives and representatives from public institutions that all emphasize the importance of the social 
aspect when it comes to safety. In general, they are told about the importance of working with near miss 
accidents and being self-critical about one’s work processes along with gathering a team to address the safety 
issues that may exist. This is likewise considered to be the refraction perspective which seems to be 
understood among researchers and public institutions who work with safety, but is relatively unclear for 
companies and safety representatives.  
Furthermore, it is pointed out that the company has the responsibility for the safety at work and the 
colleagues are supposed to follow their decisions and try to support the safety culture from their perspective. 
This correlation is understandable and there should be no doubt or ambiguity about who is responsible, but 
more initiatives, involvement and requirements for the staff are lacking (bottom up method). Of course it is 
only possible to address this if there is support from the top management. It seems as if various actors’ 
support to new employees has been overlooked or put to one side, even though they are the ones forming 
the induction process and introduction courses for the new employees.   
Regarding the empirical studies it has clearly been a disadvantage that this thesis failed to get any observation 
with new employees in relation to their practice and introduction at work. Empirical studies of this kind would 
have strengthened the thesis and its understanding of embodied knowledge, which has been overlooked so 
far.  
By examining the field of research from multiple approaches, such as observing new employees, it can be 
analyzed and worked with from different perspectives which establish a more holistic understanding of the 
situation. Multiple approaches also avoid uniformity and show various interactions that occur within the field 
of research. In this connection it is suggested than rather searching for reasons of organizational and social 
behavior and its discrete actions, motivations and individual rational decision in the community. There should 
be investigation into why these things are happening the way they do to uncover safety issues, gray areas 
and change misunderstood habits in the company.  
Likewise, it has not been possible to get interviews with new employees, which is critical since it is critical to 
understand how new employees relate to the safety culture. Furthermore, it is crucial to recognize what new 
employees need and how they value safety compared to other needs at the work place. Safety might not be 
the most important need that the new employee from their point of view. It is considered that understanding 
new employees needs and obstacles at the workplace would make a more suited and usable solution for 
them. This could be done by taking another iterative process in relation to the Double Diamond. 
In the Double Diamond some activities can be categorized as divergent or convergent depending on how 
they are framed, for example, the use of Haddon's Matrix in this thesis was intended to develop ideas 
regarding safety-related topics by indicating them in the matrix (divergent thinking). However, Haddon’s 
Matrix could likewise have been used to find a company's safety-related costs (convergent thinking). This 
==PAGE:92==

process of framing is not suitable for all activities, such as brainstorming, but it does raise the question of 
how the activities are used and in which context it leads to new discoveries. 
The practice-based studies cover a broad aspect of social sciences, which often have in common that they 
are shift focus from analysis of the individual actors or isolated subjects to social practices. This shift can be 
confusing and misleading as it is hard to identify when and where it happens. Sometimes it is on purpose and 
other times not, as has been the case in this thesis. Black boxing can also be discovered in words like practice, 
knowledge and community of practice which may be black boxed.  
Another critical element regarding communities of practice is, for example, when members from the inside

9. Conclusion 
Approximately 30 % of all occupational accident occur amongst new employees every year even though the 
CEO and top management are responsible for avoiding accidents by establishing safe and secure frames for 
the employees. However, colleagues are also responsible for maintaining and evolving the safety culture as 
well as establishing a good induction for the new employee. The social relationships among employees are 
crucial for the retention process of new employees and their involvement in the community’s practices. 
Furthermore, it is recommended that companies introduce requirements for planned introductory courses 
and create teaching material that corresponds to the safety culture in the company, otherwise it will lead to 
doubts and misunderstandings among the new employees’ practice, which will lead to a lack of credibility 
and a slower retention process. 
There are several different strategies for training new employees to become skilled workers but most 
importantly it requires participation in the work practice, as it is a question of learning a body of practical 
knowledge. The job trainer often appears to be a mentor or role model for the new employees, who 
introduces the safety practice through canonized and non-canonized practices. Non-canonized safety 
practices are passed on in implicit, unreflective practices which involve sharing tacit knowledge through 
participation.  
The new employees are evaluated in their ability to use their earlier experience, embodied knowledge, 
theoretical knowledge and sense of knowing what to do next, which provides them with sources of resilience 
against occupational accidents. However, this rarely protects the new employees from a lacking or weak 
safety culture, since the new employees usually adapt quickly without questioning or reflecting on the 
established conditions. Sometimes new employees know how to engage in safe practice but they avoid doing 
it for fear of being excluded at the work place. However, to become an expert within a field of practice, it 
requires an understanding of the collective, organizational and societal knowledge as they are within the 
community of practice.  
As established in the induction and introduction courses, it is not enough to focus on the technological, socio-
technological and organizational perspectives regarding safety of new employees. It requires the social and 
refraction perspective as well as new tools that are more involving, intuitive, motivational and inspiring in 
their safety instruction for new employees, which make it easy to reflect on one's own practice. 
In this thesis such a tool has been created in PowerPoint as a game with safety components that can articulate 
tacit knowledge and get the new employee to question their canonized and non-canonized practice due to 
the cases presented in the game. Although, the game is only in the first stages of a prototype that was tested 
at Alfa Laval A/S. This showed the possibility to go on and make business out of it if the game were to be 
developed further and a platform around the current game were created.  
The game gives an opportunity to see situations from the outside and thereby become aware of possible 
issues, conflicts and misunderstandings that can be dealt with at work without being harmed. Because the 
game is online, there is a possibility to customize the game in relation to difficulty, education and job position, 
which means that the game can be aligned with the new employees’ practices and training programs. Several 
considerations have been decided involving the gamification process which is a tradeoff between narrative 
elements and interactivity within the game.  
==PAGE:94==
94 
 
In this thesis I have presented various actors and their relations to safety at macro and micro level. Some of 
these micro actors have also been constructed as personas with a view to describing how they can make a 
positive impact on safety in relation to new employees. Some of these actors also emphasize the importance 
of using near miss accidents, since they are considered as warning signals for situations that next time may 
cause an accident. Near miss accidents do not cause any injuries or significant physical damage, but they can 
have psychological effects and create a negative cycle, which is in no one’s interests. Near misses can be used 
as a learning potential or heads up for changes that are needed.

Appendix 4. Technical route to safety 
The traditional approaches of thinking safety, in relation to industrial disasters and workplace accidents have 
been consider as technical systems that is objectified as “safe” through technologies and artifacts. This 
perspective has been called the “technical route to safety” and work as the common sense of safety today. 
(Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) For instance, the introduction of risk analysis and technical-engineering approach 
are increasingly used by companies due to the evaluative tools there are directly connected to measurement, 
quantification and risk reduction advances. (Project Management Institute, 2008)  
Gathering this kind of quantitative data applying a source of information about uncertainties, variabilities 
and treats there can have catastrophic consequences. The evaluative tools rates sources of danger together 
with advising the management of which safety productive activities there could prevent future accidents 
which are supposed to create a proactive safety approach. However, there is a need of creating balance 
between cost and risk for avoiding accidents. (Ortwin Renn, 2008) 
“Managing risk means identifying and taking opportunities to improve performance as well as 
taking action to avoid or reduce the chances of something going wrong.” (Standards Australia 
International, 2007) 
Managing risk involves following a pre-develop plan and identify potential variations that can allocate 
resources in the most effective way based on gathered information. This also counts when it comes to 
reduction in loss and cost of risk, including insurance premiums. (Standards Australia International, 2007)  
It is recommended to develop the plan after the guidelines of risk management that follows the framework 
as seen at figure 19 on the next page. 
 
==PAGE:110==
110 
 
 
Figure 19. ISO 31000:2009 Process Overiew present a process of risk management were communication and monitoring is a 
constant iterative element of building safety. Adapted from (Staigerwald, 2018) 
 
The technical approaches also emphasize the importance of training employees as well as elaborated sets of 
rules and regulations there prescribe, how to act safe for individuals and collective behavior execution. So, 
as described the technical approach to safety focus on the dual concept of rationalization and training.  
 
Nevertheless, the route to safety has been criticized for its tendency to consider risk as serial sets of neutral 
accidents and events, there often are detached from each other. (Nicolini & Gherardi, 2007) 
The goal is to support management decisions about the risk of the social impact, the degree of safety level 
and cost benefits also in relation to new machines and technological installments, which might require 
additional prevention equipment and training.

Appendix 5. Technical route to safety 
Sabbath year workers are young people working often full time while having a break for studies and earning 
money to support themselves. Sometimes they take on a longer journey to find themselves and clarify what 
they want to be educated in. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) In addition, sabbath year workers are often employed 
in the workplace for an extended period of time about 1-2 years and they may have been part of a part time 
job earlier in their life, where they have several years of experience at work. Due to a relatively short time 
horizon at work they can still have great responsibility and carry out independent work in their year(s) as 
sabbath workers. (Dyreborg et al., 2013) 
Students worker is most of the time young people working at a part-time job, while they are in an education 
that is not connected to the current job. The work is rarely study related and do not further qualify the 
person. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) These students in work are often working outside normal working hours, 
weekends and holidays so they can accommodate their time with education. This means that they are often 
unskilled at the routines of work and are working alone or together with other students or temporary workers 
there are young at the workplace, which of course have a greater likelihood of occupational accidents. 
(Dyreborg et al., 2013) 
Educational dropouts are mostly young people working at full time in unskilled jobs which may go on for 
several years. Educational dropouts have typically discontinued educational paths or more than one courses 
in different directions. (Arbejdsmiljørådet, 2014) Further, the young people in this group may potentially 
work in unskilled jobs for many years (i.e. in the retail industry) if they manage to keep their jobs.  
A Freelancer can both be self-employed and employed as an employee. In this thesis which focus about new 
employees, only the employed freelancer is relevant. Freelancers who are employed may be physically 
present in the company as they hired to for an extended period, but they may also be individuals working 
outside the company and only in contact by phone and mail. General these persons have a specialist role or 
opportunity to solve smaller tasks for the company. Often these smaller tasks are beyond the company's own 
competences or in another area than they are used to work in like IT or software.
