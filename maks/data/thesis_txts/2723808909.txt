Abstract
The purpose of this project was to investigate the characteristics of a active gas foil bearing
based on experimental work.
The characteristics covers determination of the bearing properties as stiffness and damping
under static condition, hydrostatic lubrication and hybrid lubrication.
Static properties of
the bearing was determined with force and displacement measurements. Experimental modal
analysis was used to determine the damping of the bearing under hydrostatic- and hybrid
lubrication.
The bearing was tested in an existing test rig consisting of shaft- bearing system, where a disc
is mounted on the shaft. A small test set up has was made to make the static measurements
and measure the clearance.
All adjustments done to make the bearing work as intended, and results of the adjustments
are documented and discussed. So is construction and changes on the test rig to make the
construction work as intended.

Introduction
Bearings are unavoidable components in all kinds of rotating machinery such as pumps, engines
and wheel hubs. The essential function is that it allows one component to rotate freely from
an other. When it comes to selection of a bearing load capacity, stability and lifetime is the
most important factors. The most known bearing is the ball bearing, which is used in most
constructions, since it is cheap and easy to implement. The problem with these bearings is that
these gets worn, causes friction and requires a lubrication which often is desired to keep away
from the surrounding environment.
An alternative to the ball bearing is the fluid bearing, which is used for more specific purposes
where their properties are requested. One of many variations is a gas bearing, where gas is used
to create a fluid film between hub and shaft. Gas bearings has some advantages since there is
no contact between rotor and hub, which reduces friction and wear. Atmospheric air can be
used in gas bearings, since it is cheap and clean to operating environment. Gas bearings also
has its challenges limitations, especially when it comes to load capacity, stability and mounting.
These challenges can be overcomed by creating an active gas foil bearing.
Experimental modal analysis (EMA) is used to investigate the bearing, the same method has
been used previous on the test rig. EMA is a analysis of the vibration response to excitation
on the structure. The excitation can be impacts or oscillations depending on equipment. The
purpose of EMA is to determine natural frequencies and damping factor of the bearing, which
are important parameters of the structure. The natural frequencies are important to determine,
since this is where the structure becomes unstable. The frequencies where the structure is
instable should be avoided, since this will result in significant vibrations.
With experimental modal analysis the natural frequencies of a structure can be identified based
on its response in the frequency domain. This method is used in all industries that has to with
rotating machinery. The purpose of the analysis is to identify natural frequencies within the
operational range. Most products are designed to do a specific job, which has nothing to do with
vibrations. It will be very demanding to design a product based on avoiding vibrations,therefore
it is easiest to implement damping to the system.

1.
Theory
1.1.
Bearings in rotating machinery
Bearings are build to absorb the forces, which is transferred from a shaft.
A ball bearing
consists of a rotating shaft and a stationary hub, Where balls are placed between shaft and
hub. The purpose of the ball bearing is to decrease friction and support the shaft.
In a gas bearing forces are transferred between shaft and hub through a gas film. Because of the
small gap between shaft and hub the pressure increases and the gas rotates around the shaft,
this is called aerodynamic lubrication or hydrodynamic lubrication. The pressure is highest in
the bottom of the bearing, because of the gap is smallest, the pressure should be high enough
to carry the shaft. The shaft will also move horizontally, where the direction depends on the
rotation because of the pressure profile. Since the rotation creates the gas film, the functionality
of the bearing depends on the rotational speed of the shaft. Natural frequencies can occur at
low speeds, and therefore the rotational speed can be an issue. This also means that the shaft
is laying on the hub from start, until the rotational speed is high enough to create a sufficiently
strong film. The advantage of the bearing is that the construction is very simple and the friction
is very low. The problem is that start and stop period causes friction between bearing hub and
shaft, which results in wear.
Figure 1: Concept of gas bearing 1: Bearing hub, 2: Shaft, 3: Gas film.
Gas can be injected directly into the hub, this is called an active gas bearing (AGB). The
increased pressure allows the bearing to operate at low speeds.
This lubrication is called
hydrostatic lubrication and this is the work principle of AGB. The concept will be combined
with the hydrodynamic lubrication and result in hybrid lubrication. This solution solves the
issue at low speeds.

Foils can be inserted inside the bearing hub, this is called a gas foil bearing (GFB). The
bumpfoils contributes with more damping to the system by acting like a mechanical spring.
The foils reduces wear since the shaft can rest on them, and the topfoil can be coated with low
friction materials. The topfoils can afterwards be replaced, when these are worn. At standstill
the shaft rests on the foil, during the start up the pressure increases, which result in the foils
are pushed back. In a GFB the gas film will be enough to carry the shaft when the foil is
pushed back. In case of instabilities the foil and gas film will interact with each other and
create damping, and at large displacement the shaft will touch the foil directly.
Figure 3: Concept of gas foil bearing 1: Bearing hub, 2: Shaft, 3: Foils.
All three concepts can be combined into an active gas foil bearing (AGFB), which creates a
much more robust solution. The bearing relies on hybrid lubrication and stiffness of the foils.

1.2.
Bearing Properties
The shaft and bearing system can be seen as a damped mass spring system. To obtain the
properties of the bearing its seen as a single degree of freedom (SDOF) system. The strength
of the gas film can be denoted as the stiffness kair, the film stiffness will increase with the
air pressure. The strength of the foil can be noted as the mechanical stiffness kfoil, and this
stiffness is constants under the assumption that the foil behave linear elastic. These two stiffness
constitute the total stiffness of the bearing k. The total damping of the system consists of the
damping of the gas foil damping dair and the damping of the foil dfoil. The gas film damping
will decrease with the air pressure. The damping and stiffness of the system has a large effect
on the natural frequencies. Mass and stiffness are the main factors to the natural frequencies,

1.3
Experimental Modal Analysis
The properties of the gas bearing is difficult to predict theoretically, the stiffness and damping
can be found experimentally from the natural frequencies.
The parameters is found with
Experimental modal analysis (EMA). This is done by excite the system by impacting the shaft
and measure the force, beside of that the displacement is measured in horizontal and vertical
direction. The shaft and disc are considered as one mass, and the EMA experiments is done for
one direction at the time. That’s reason the system is considered as a single degree of freedom
(SDOF), where the shaft and disc mass is supported from the mass and stiffness form the gas
bearing and the ball bearing.
Figure 6: 1: Disc 2: Gas bearing 3: Shaft 4: Ball bearing
1.3.
Experimental Modal Analysis
In experimental modal analysis a input and output is measured. By dividing the output with the
input, a transfer function is made, with this transfer function the frequency response function
(FRF) is obtained.
The analysis can be done with different kinds of inputs and outputs. In this case the input
is a impulse force, and the output is primarily measured as displacement.
The input and
output are used to calculate the Autocorrelation and Crosscorrelation. The Autocorrelation
and Crosscorrelation are Fourier transformed in to the frequency domain. This leads to the
power Spectral density (PSD) and the Cross Power Sprectal Density (CPSD). The two transfer
functions H1(ω) and H2(ω) are obtained by dividing PSD and CPSD with each other. These
two transfer functions H1(ω) and H2(ω) are used to obtain the FRF.

1.3.5.
Modeshape
Mode shape is the way the shaft vibrates at natural frequencies. Each natural frequencies has a
mode shape, where the simplest most shape corresponds to the lowest natural frequencies. In a
shaft bearing system as in the project, the first modes shape could be a rigid body modeshape
or a bending modeshape. A rigid body modeshape is when the shaft is stiff and moves in a
straight line. A bending modeshape is when the shaft moves in a arc formation. Since the
shaft can move in x and y direction, horizontal and vertical mode shapes exists. If the shaft
system and its constrains are rotation symmetric, the horizontal and vertical mode shapes are
the same. When the shaft rotates the horizontal and vertical mode shape will be combined in
to a circular mode shapes. Where one of the mode shapes will rotate in the same direction as
the shaft and the other mode shape will rotate in the opposite direction.

1.3.7.
Filtering
When measurements are collected, different kinds of noise in the signal can affect the data. The
noise can be caused in many ways, the source is often high frequency electronic components.
Filters are used to remove the noise, which is not possible to remove from the directly from the
source. In this case a low pass filter is used, when the data is collected. In the data processing
a Butterworh filter is used.
1.3.8.
Segmentation, Averaging and Windowing
In the experiment the system is exited by a short impact, which results in a impulse in force
and displacement. One data set of one impact is enough to obtain the FRF, to improve the
reliability of the measurements, multiple impacts is done. Each impact is segmented in to a
block, this is done for the force and displacement signals. The PSD and CPSD is then calculated
for each segment and are averaged afterwards. The advantages of this method is that signals
which are not receptive are removed from the signal.

Bearing Characteristics
Before taking the AGFB in to use, it was desired to examine the characteristics of the bear-
ing. Characteristics covers determination of the clearance and the stiffness of the foils. The
properties is found by plotting a force-displacement graph. The clearance is the displacement
part until the top foils are engaged with the bump foils. This can bee seen from the force-
displacement plot where the slope of the graph increases, this is where the bump foils acts like
a spring and starts to deform. The clearance is defined in two ways regression clearance and
no regression clearance. No regression clearance is defined as the exact point where the graph
becomes linear. Regression clearance is defined as the point, where the linear regression line
crosses the x-axis. The linear regression line is used to determine the stiffness of the foils, which
is slope of the linear part of the force-displacement graph. The clearance is very important for
air foil bearings, since the it affects the pressure around the shaft. An initial sketch of a force-
displacement curve is shown in fig.(18). CR indicates the regression clearance for the linear
regression line. CNR indicates the no regression clearance.

4.2.
Adjustments
The following adjustments was done in relation to change the clearance.
4.2.1.
Leading Edge
In the beginning of the project the bearing was handed over with unspecified leading edge. It
was discovered that the teflon on the leading edge was worn. Since the clearance is much lower
than in previous reports [2], it was decided to reduce the slope height by pressing foils further
into the bearing brass. The purpose was to reduce the leading edge, to avoid further wear
and maybe adjust the clearance. After the intervention the top foils was tilted and became
very stiff. The stiffness was high enough to affect the measurements. The adjustments was
afterwards done by elevate the foils, and then insert a piece of plastic, with a known thickness
and press the foils down.
4.2.2.
Pressurisation of Foils
Trough a conversation with a delegation from the company GEA NIO, it was proposed to
pressurise the bearing to make the foils settle correct, when the leading edge is changed. The
pressurisation is done by mounting the bearing in the test rig and apply 7 bar from all injectors.
After two attempts the foils had deformed properly.
4.2.3.
Adjustment of Rotor
A way to directly increase the clearance is to grind the rotor. This intervention has to be done
in the workshop, and is very time consuming. Since the process can not be undone, one has
to be very sure, about how much material that should be removed from the rotor. The first
measurements showed that 30 µm, should be removed, since the desired result was not obtained
30 µm was removed two times. Then 100 µm was removed two times. Since there is a limit of
how little material, that can be removed while grinding the shaft for each process, it is difficult

5.
Air Injecting
The whole construction consists of multiple parts and air passes over several connections. For
the functionality of the bearing it is important, that the connections between each part is tight,
to secure that the pressure in the bearing is the same as intended. Leaks has been a large
challenge during the project.
When the test rig was assembled, it seemed like the amount of air from different injectors was
different. Orientation of the injection seals was examined, to secure all holes was aligned. It
was noticed, that the injection was very sensitive to the movements of the pins inside the seals.
The assembly drawings was examined to understand the exact work principle of the pins. It
was noticed that the gap between seal and pin was very small, and a small misalignment could
block the air. The distance between end of the pin and the hole in the bottom of the seal was
very small and could also easy be blocked by misalignment. The purpose of the pins is that
the can block or partially block the air injection, when these are pushed down by the piezo
actuators. To push the pin up three belleville springs is mounted under the pin. To secure that
enough and equally amount of air was injected, two more belleville springs was added. This
was done increase the space between pin and the injection hole in the sealing. The intervention
worked as expected but with the results that the piezo actuators can not be used. The injection
seal an pin with 5 belleville springs are shown in fig.(27.b)

6.
Test Procedure
Experimental Modal Analysis is performed on the shaft bearing system, determination of mode-
shapes and calibration of the force transducer.
The general and individual procedures are
described in this section.
6.1.
EMA
EMA was performed by exciting shaft with the impact hammer on the same spot on the brass
bushing, as shown in fig.(30) in vertical or horizontal direction. Each test series consists of 30
impacts, with 2 seconds between every impact. The impacts is done by hand with the impact
hammer. The raw data is shifted, so the mean value is zero. The data is afterwards filtered
with a low pass Butterworth filter to remove high frequent signal noise. Segmentation is used
to isolate all 30 input and output signals, the hamming window is applied to each segment. The
data is then used for calculation of the Autocorrelation and Crosscorrelation for each windowed
segment. These were then Fourier transformed to calculate the Power Spectral Density (PSD)
and the Cross Power Spectral Density (CPSD) of the force and displacement data. H1 and H2
are then calculated and plotted in the frequency domain. The processing was done with a built
in program in matlab using the Welch method. The processing script is based a script provided
by Ilmar Ferreira Santos.
Figure 30: The brass bushing on the shaft is used as impact spot.
The shaft bearing system is first tested with hydrostatic lubrication. The tests is done for four
injection configurations and injection pressure ranged from 3 to 7 bar. First one configuration
is tested for each pressure. Then the next configuration is tested, and each test is done for
horizontal and vertical direction. The purpose of the experiment is to investigate the influence
of the configuration, and then see the influence of the pressure.

Modeshape of Shaft
To find the bending modeshape the shaft was hung in to lines. This was done to let the shaft

be stopped by hand, before the next impact.
The FRF is plotted, where the magnitude is used to determine the position on the y-axis. The
phase of the FRF was used to determine if the point oscillation positive or negative direction.
The whole plot shows the bending modeshape of the shaft.

6.3.
Force Transducer Calibration
Calibration of force transducer was done two times first with the B&K type 8200. The force
transducer has been used a lot in previous experiments, and suddenly forces above 1000N posi-
tive and negative occurred, without any logical reason during the experiments. The reliability of
the measurements was not good enough. To identify the problem the transducer was grounded,
and another charge amplifier was used, and another computer with dspace module was tried.
It was decided to replace the force transducer with a B&K type 8230.
The calibration is performed by using a steel block with a mass of 12.1 kg hung in strings as
shown in fig.(32). An accelerometer is mounted on one side, the other side is impacted with the
force transducer hammer. EMA is performed by averaging force and acceleration of 10 impacts.
The inverse mass is found at he linear part of the peak of the FRF with the accelerance function
as seen in fig.(7). The ratio between the calculated mass and known mass is implemented in
dspace as the calibration factor.

7.4.1.
Summary of Hybrid Lubrication Test Series
The vertical response is affected by multiple of natural frequencies, specially one between 10-
20 Hz dominates the plot. The frequency clearly increases with the rotational speed, therefore
it is assumed that the frequency is caused by unbalance in the shaft. The natural frequencies
known from the hydrostatic tests around 20 Hz and 50 Hz are also visible in all the plots. The
50 Hz natural frequency is not affected by rotational speed and barely affected by he pressure
and injection configuration. It is shown that the damping ratio increases with the pressure, the
rotational speed and injection configuration does not have significant impact.
The horizontal response is much more clean, the natural frequency known from the hydrostatic
and foil response around 20 Hz dominates the FRF plot. The frequency increases with the
pressure and when side injection is used as seen in the hydostatic tests. The hybrid lubrication
tests shows that the natural frequency is not affected by the rotational speed. The tables shows
that the damping ratio is only affected by the pressure. Against the expectation the damping
ratio is not affected by the injection configuration.

vibrations of this natural frequency could be heard and seen, it was desired to see the exact
frequency and its magnitude. This was done by rotating the shaft from 400 to 2000 rpm and
measure the displacements. The measurements was done with the bottom injector open from
3 to 7 Bar pressure.

Discussion
9.1.
Characteristics Measurements
There is a large deviation between the clearance measured in 2019 [2] and the new measure-
ments.
It is assumed that these differences has occurred, when the foils was removed and
Page 86 of 108
==PAGE:93==
9.2
Air Injection
mounted again in a previous project [1]. Regarding the adjustments the measurements shows
that removal of the 100 µm of the diameter of the rotor result in approximately 10 µm clear-
ance. It was expected that the increase of clearance would correspond to the removal. The
reason is difficult to tell, the shaft was grinded three times and the measurements shows the
same relation between removal and clearance. The Clearance is determined in to ways CNR
and CR, both can be difficult to determine. Since it can be difficult to see, exactly where the
linear part of the force displacement plot starts. There is some deviations in clearance between
different angles. The reason is probably, that there are small differences in the mounting of
the foils. The injection pipes can also cause friction. It is also expected, that the stiffness is
higher where the foils are mounted into the grooves in the bearing brass. The test setup has
some uncertainties, since the characteristics are not measured directly. The ball bearing is a
pivot point of the shaft, so the shaft is tilted inside the AGFB, which result in a uneven contact
between rotor and bearing. The force measurements are based on moment equilibrium for a
specific point in the middle of the bearing. The displacement is also measured at one point,
and projected to find the displacement in the middle of the bearing. The middle of the bearing
was considered to be the best place to measure, it would be more optimal, to find a way to
measure the system more direct.
9.2.
Air Injection
By observing flow and pressure and listening to the test rig, it is supposed that the shaft holds
very tight on the injection hole in the bottom of the bearing. The tightness should only be an
advantage when the bottom injector is open. It seems like the tightness is a problem, when all
injectors are open through start up. Since the shaft almost blocks the bottom injector hole, it
is easier for the air to stream through the other holes. This results in a very low pressure in the
bottom, which is most important and a higher pressure from top and sides, which also pushes
the shaft down to the injection hole.
9.3.
Response of Shaft under Hydrostatic Lubrication
The Vertical responses to vertical impacts are very clean with i high coherence up to 200 Hz.
One natural frequency around 50 Hz is very dominant. The horizontal mode of the shaft affects
the response in some measurements, as the natural frequency around 20 Hz can be seen. By
impacting without pressure it is assumed that the peak around 60 Hz is the natural frequency
of the foils.
The horizontal responses to horizontal impacts was affected a bit more by other natural fre-
quencies. The vertical mode can be seen in some of the plots as the peak around 50 Hz. The
dominant peak in horizontal direction around 20 Hz, occurs very clearly as the frequency of the
foils. The magnitudes of the of the peaks decreases a lot in test with injection configurations
with side injectors open.

9.4.
Response of Shaft under Hybrid Lubrication
It was difficult to test the bearing in higher rotational speeds than 1000 rpm because of a
natural frequency around 1200 rpm. The stacked plots shows that the frequency of the peak
does not change with rotational speed. This is probably because speeds are not high enough
to have an impact. The hybrid lubrication has an impact since the frequencies and magnitudes
has changed from the hydrostatic tests.
The same frequencies occurs in the vertical and horizontal responses as for the hydrostatic
responses. The horizontal mode is much more visible in the vertical response, especially in
the experiments without air injection from the sides. A new peak has occurred in the vertical
response Between 10 Hz and 20 Hz. The peak is very pointy, and its frequency and magnitude
increases significant with the rotational speed. Because of these observations it is expected that
this natural frequency is caused by unbalance in the shaft.
For both vertical and horizontal direction the damping factors are not affected significantly by
the rotational speed, and in fact the damping ratios are lower than the hydrostatic damping
ratios. The reason could be that the rotational speeds are to low to affect the damping.

Conclusion
Through this project an active gas foil bearing has successfully mounted in the test rig. The
construction has been studied trough previous reports, and different investigations has been
done to be the make the test rig run.
The clearance of the bearing and stiffness of the foils have been measured on a simple test setup.
Based on these measurements the shaft-bearing system has been adjusted, and the impact of
the adjustments has been documented. In general the results of the measurements are difficult
to verify, since the results are difficult to relate. The relation between adjustment and impact
on measurement has been investigated. The desired clearance has been obtained and it is shown
that the clearance can be adjusted with shims. The stiffness of the foils has been determined.
Experimental Modal Analysis has been used to determine properties of the bearing and insta-
bilities of the construction. The plots of the hydrostatic tests are in general very clean and
have i high coherence. The hybrid lubrication plots in vertical direction are affected by a lot of
noise with multiple peaks. This is probably caused by unbalance in the shaft or other sources
in the test rig. The hybrid lubrication test for horizontal direction looks very similar to the
hydrodynamic tests.
In vertical direction a natural frequency around 55 Hz is the most significant. In the hydrosttic
tests the frequency is not affected significantly by the pressure and injection configuration. By
testing the system without air injection a significant natural frequency appears at 63 Hz, this
is assumed to be the same as the 55 Hz. Therefore it is concluded that the injection pressure
makes the natural frequency decrease. By making experiments with hybrid lubrication it is
shown that this natural frequency is not affected by the rotational speed.
In the horizontal FRF plot a natural frequency is the most significant. The hydrostatic tests
shows that the natural frequency is affected a lot by the pressure since it increases with the
pressure. The test without injection shows the same frequency as without sideways injection,
therefore it is assumed, that the frequency between 20 Hz-30 Hz is the frequency of the foils.
The hybrid lubrication experiments shows that the frequency is not affected by the rotational
speed.
The reason that the hybrid test does not show any remarkable results, regarding the natural
frequencies is probably that rotational speeds are not high enough to have significant impact.
The properties of the AGFB are compared with the properties of the previous AGB. It is shown
that the damping ratio is significant higher for the most test series. As expected the AGFB
was easier to mount sience no alignment was needed. However the friction in the AGFB is a
lot higher since it is measurable, where the AGB was almost friction less, when the test rig was
aligned correctly.
