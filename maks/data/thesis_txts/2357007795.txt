Abstract
The interest in cooperative-systems have increased rapidly in the last decade. The promise of collaborating
autonomous systems allows for these costly and time consuming mission to be performed cheaply and
rapidly. In this endevor, it is crucial for the long term operation of autonomous vehicles that faults can be
found and mitigated.
Fault-diagnosis for cooperative-systems have seen an increase in research, yet only speciﬁc solutions often
tailored for speciﬁc systems have been developed. This thesis tries to take some of the ﬁrst steps towards the
development of formal theoretical approaches to fault-diagnosis for cooperative-systems. The ﬁrst steps
include the deﬁnition of a fault-diagnosis model for cooperative systems, fault-diagnosis scopes and the
extension of commonly known diagnosis metrics to cooperative systems. Two propositions for detectability
and isolability for cooperative systems are derived. Further two proposition for the connection between
fault-diagnosis scopes and communication conﬁgurations are derived.
The availability of exteroceptive sensors and feature extraction algorithms, for vehicles in cooperative
systems is also investigated. It is investigated how comparison of features extracted at different agents, in
a cooperative system, can be used to enhance fault-diagnosis. This is done by proposing a uniﬁed model
for two classes of exteroceptive sensors. Then proposing a structural formulation of feature extraction
algorithms. Through a study case it is shown how the proposed models can be used for fault-diagnosis
analysis. The analysis on the study case shows comparison of extracted features enhance fault-diagnosis,
which is veriﬁed by simulation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The use of autonomous vehicles for performing operations in hostile or expensive to reach areas have
increased in recent time. This leads to a need for reliable performance, through fault-tolerance. For larger
tasks, a single vehicle is not always sufﬁcient and cooperative systems are then employed. The REMORA
project, which this thesis is a part of, focuses on the development of autonomous underwater platforms to
carry out missions in hostile environments, such as oil platforms. REMORA deals with the application and
control of cooperative-systems, along with fault-tolerance of cooperative system among other things. The
REMORA project has multiple underwater vehicles of the type BlueROV available, these will be used as
study case for this thesis.
In the last couple of decades a lot of research has been carried out on cooperative control. Along with
the successful development of cooperative control the research into cooperative fault-diagnosis and fault-
tolerant cooperative control (FTCC) have seen a surge. Research on FTCC is scattered among different
methodologies, ranging from distributed Kalman ﬁlter approaches, to adaptive, fuzzy and system speciﬁc
approaches.
Among some of the results using a distributed Kalman ﬁlter are Daigle et al. (2007) Azizi and Khorasani
(2009) Azizi and Khorasani (2011). More recently adaptive schemes for FTCC have been developed see Zuo
et al. (2014) Wang and Yang (2015) Yang and Deng (2016). Methodologies such as H∞based observers has
been explored in Léchevin and Rabbath (2007) Meskin and Khorasani (2009b) and FTCC through robustness
of controllers in Semsar-Kazerooni and Khorasani (2009) Semsar-Kazerooni and Khorasani (2010) a.R.
Mehrabian et al. (2012). Fuzzy logic based methodologies has been investigated in Shen et al. (2014) Chen
and Lin (2014) along with less conventional approaches which has been explored in Meskin et al. (2009)
Yang et al. (2011) Chen and Song (2014).
Common for the research in FTCC is a focus on speciﬁc methods applied on a speciﬁc systems. This
means that a lot speciﬁc residual generators and decision methodologies for cooperative system have been
developed. However only a few results have been emerging of general theoretic nature, with Meskin and
Khorasani (2009a) extending the results of Gertler (1998) on fault signature selection based on full row
canonical decompositions to multi-agents systems (MAS). In Azizi and Khorasani (2009) a framework for
discrete event time systems for reduced order observer based detection was developed.
1.1
Problem Description
Due to the few analytical frameworks proposed in the literature, this project investigates formal theoretical
approaches for fault-diagnosis of cooperative systems. The development of theoretical frameworks allows the
developers of cooperative systems to focus on derivation and implementation of fault-diagnosis procedures,

Beside the investigation into theoretical approaches for fault-diagnosis, a more speciﬁc approach to fault-
diagnosis based on exteroceptive sensors is taken. This approach utilizes the extraction of features from the
environment, along with the availability of communication to enhance fault-diagnosis. This allows for an
increase in reliability of cooperative systems, which can utilize this methodology.
1.2
Thesis outline
Chapter 2 - 4 introduce the necessary theory for the work performed in this project. Chapter 5 - 6 presents
the original work done in this project and chapter 7 contains the conclusion. A brief summary of the chapters
are as follows:
• Chapter 2: A Brief description of the dynamical model for the vehicle which is used as study case.
• Chapter 3: Introduces graph theoretical tools and the design of cooperative control laws based on the
Ricatti equation.
• Chapter 4: Summarizes the results on fault-diagnosis that is necessary to investigate diagnosis in a
cooperative system.
• Chapter 5: Deﬁnitions of fault-diagnosis for cooperative systems is introduced and metrics for fault-
diagnosis is extended to cooperative systems. Theoretical results on fault-diagnosis for cooperative
systems is developed.
• Chapter 6: A uniﬁed modelling approach for two classes of exteroceptive sensors is proposed. A
structural model for feature extraction based on exteroceptive sensors is proposed. The proposed
models are used in fault-diagnosis to demonstrate an enhancement of diagnosis, through comparison
of features extracted at different agents.
• Chapter 7: Summarizes the results of the project and lists some of the directions in which the work
of this thesis can be extended.
1.3
Contributions
The contributions of this thesis are developed in chapter 5 and 6 and are as follows:
• A deﬁnition of a cooperative system for fault-diagnosis.
• Classiﬁcation of fault-diagnosis scopes in cooperative systems.
• Extension of fault-diagnosis metrics for cooperative systems.
• Two propositions on detectability and isolability of linear cooperative systems.
• Two propositions on the connection between fault-diagnosis scopes and communication topologies.
• An approach to modelling exteroceptive sensors and feature extraction for structural analysis.
• A study case which shows that comparing features extracted at different agents in a cooperative system,
can enhance isolability at the individual agents.

CHAPTER 2
BlueROV Dynamical Model
Despite the fact that the primary contributions of this project are theoretical in nature, it is still desired
to demonstrate the results on as system of practical relevance. The chosen system is an Autonomous
Underwater Vehicle (AUV) of the type BlueROV by BlueRobotics. A model of the BlueROV was developed
and system identiﬁcation was carried as part of the thesis Hansen (2016), which is the work this chapter is
based on.
The model developed in Hansen (2016) was based on the robotics model for marine vessels developed by
Fossen (1991). Which has been used extensively in the literature and since extended.
The model of a marine vessel consists of a kinematic modelling, relating the geometrical movement of the
marine vessel to a ﬁxed reference frame, as well as a kinetic model describing how forces and moments act
on the vehicle. The kinematic equation is given in (2.1), and describes the transformation of the velocities of
the vehicle in body frame ν into the velocities of the vehicle in a ﬁxed reference frame ˙η using the absolute
orientation between the vehicle body frame and the ﬁxed reference frame Θnb. The kinetic equation is
given in eq. (2.2). This equation relates the forces and moment produced by propulsion τ to the velocities
and accelerations ν, ˙ν through an inertial component M, a centripetal and Coriolis component C(ν), a
hydrodynamic damping component D(ν) and a gravitational and buoyancy component g(η).

Cooperative control
Cooperative control denotes a control law implemented in each agent of a mulit-agent system, such that

agents synchronize their direction of motion. The ﬁgure illustrates three stages of achieving consensus; the

communication is introduced the development of an optimal cooperative control law for a homogeneous
multi-agent system can be introduced. This is followed by the description of a cooperative Leunberger

3.1
Graph Theory and Communication
The communication among agents is modelled using graph theory. The review of the necessary theory is
based on Lewis et al. (2013) and Godsil and Royle (2013) and the notation is adopted from Lewis et al.
(2013). When modelling the communication between agents in a multi-agent system, the structure of the
resulting graph is sometimes called the communication topology. In this work the words communication
graph and communication topology are used interchangeably.
3.1.1
Graph theory
The aim is to model the communication between the agents as a graph, and through this modelling be able
to represent the graph in terms of linear algebra. The treatment of graph theory in terms of linear algebra
is called algebraic graph theory (Godsil and Royle (2013)). In ﬁgure 3.4a four agents are illustrated with
their respective communication links. The same is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.4b where each agent is represented
in a more abstract way by a dot. The representation in ﬁgure 3.4b is more general, where each agent is
represented by a vertex, denoted by v and a number. These vertices are sometimes called nodes, and the
term vertex and node are used interchangeably.
In graph theory, the set of all vertices of a graph is denoted by V = {v1, v2, . . . , vN } where N is the number
of vertices in the graph. In ﬁgure 3.4 the set of vertices would be V = {v1, v2, v3, v4}, and thus N also
denotes the number of agents. The communication links are called edges. The set of all edges of a given graph
is given by E = {(vi, vj), (vk, vz), . . . } where the pair (vi, vj) denote an edge with base at vertex i and head
at vertex j. This is represented by arrows in illustrations of the graph. Again, turning to the graphs in ﬁgure
3.4, this leads to the set of all edges becoming E = {(v1, v2), (v1, v3), (v2, v3), (v2, v4), (v3, v1), (v4, v3)}.
In the example it is noteworthy, that the bidirectional communication between vertex 1 and vertex 3, has
two indices in the set of edges, since an edge denote a directed path. A pair, consisting of a set of vertices
and edges constitute a graph and is expressed as G = (V, E). Along with a direction an edge can also be
associated with a weight. In a communication context, this weight could represent the communication delay,
such that sending a message from agent one to agent three takes longer, than sending a message from agent
one to agent two in ﬁgure 3.4a. A weight associated with the edge (vi, vj) is denoted eji. Note that the order
of the subscripts in the weight is reversed. This reversal is such that a matrix of the weights will have each

A third concept in graph theory is the directed tree. Figure 3.5c shows a directed tree, which is a graph in
which all vertices except the root vertex have only one edge going into it. The root vertex is the top vertex
for the tree. The number of edges going into a vertex vi is called the in-degree of a vertex vi. Thus a directed
tree can be deﬁned as a graph such that all vertices have in-degree equal to one, except the root vertex which
has in-degree equal to zero. The directed tree is an interesting construct for communication graphs, because
if a directed graph such as the one in ﬁgure 3.4 can be made into a directed tree containing all the vertices,
by removing some of the edges, the directed graph is said to contain a spanning tree. It is possible for a
single graph to contain multiple spanning trees. If a directed graph contains a spanning tree, it is guaranteed
that at least one vertex has a path to all other vertices. For a multi-agent system, the condition that the graph
contains a spanning tree, means that at least one agent is able to get a message across to all other agents in
the system.
The last important concept in graph theory is the strongly connected graph. A strongly connected graph is
deﬁned as a graph in which all vertices have a directed path to any other vertex in the graph. The graph in
ﬁgure 3.4 are strongly connected, because any vertex can reach any other vertex by traversing the arrows in
the ﬁgure.

constructed by the weights of all the edges. Thus the adjacency matrix can be deﬁned as E = [eij] where
eij > 0 if (vj, vi) ∈E otherwise eij = 0.

Fault Diagnosis
Fault diagnosis of dynamical systems is the process of distinguishing the normal behaviour of a system, from
faulty behaviour. A lot of research has been carried out in the ﬁeld of fault diagnosis, hence only the speciﬁc
tools relevant to this thesis are reviewed. The content of this chapter is based on Blanke et al. (2016)[Chap.
5.].

4.1
Structural Analysis
Structural analysis is a systematic approach to ﬁnding residual generators and determine which faults can be
detected and which can be isolated. This is done by making a structural representation of the system, which
is based on the mathematical model. This structural representation is then modelled as a graph, which is
used to derive residual generators through ﬁnding redundancies in the mathematical descriptions.
4.1.1
Structural Description
The structural representation of a system S = (C, Z) is the pair of constraints and variables, where C denote
the set of system constraints and Z denotes the set of system variables. The set of constraints includes all
equations, differential and algebraic, of the system. The set of variables includes all signals in the system.
The set of variables Z can further be divided into known variables K and unknown variables X. This leads
to the grouping of constraints into CK, which is all constraints containing only known variables, and CX
which is all constraints containing at least one unknown variable.

4.1.3
Matching
Matching in bipartite graphs is the tool used to ﬁnd analytical redundancy relations (ARR) in the structural
model, which are then made into residual generators. Matching in bipartite graphs is the process of ﬁnding a
set of edges, such that no edge in the set have any common vertices either C or Z. Figure 4.4 illustrate two
matchings of the mass-spring-damper system.
Matching of a bipartite graph of a dynamical system can be interpreted as assigning an orientation to the
graph. This orientation represent which constraints are used to calculate which variables. The orientation is
assigned using the following rules
• Matched constraints: The edge matched with the constraints is considered the output, and is depicted
by an arrow going from the constraint to the variable. Edges not matched are considered inputs, and
depicted as arrows going from the variables to the constraint.
– Since all non matched variables are considered inputs, known variables are always depicted as
being inputs to all constraints, whether matched or not.
• Non-Matched constraints: all variables are considered inputs, and the output is directed to the
ﬁctitious variable denoted *.
Applying these two rules to the matchings of ﬁgure 4.4 leads to the directed bipartite graphs of ﬁgure 4.5
where the rule lead to the matching of c1 to u to be ignored, as u is a known variable and as such is always an
input to the constraints. In the top matching of ﬁgure 4.5 an algebraic loop is introduced, since the output of
c1 depend on D2 and D2 depend on ¨x and ¨x depends on c1. This loop mean that even though the matching
can be made, the subsequent calculation of the variables cannot be solved.
The matchings made in the graph can also be made directly in the incidence matrix associated with the
graph. A matching in the incidence matrix is indicated by 1 , and to ensure no edge connect the same two
vertices, each row and each column may only contain one matching 1 . The two matchings of ﬁgure 4.5 is

Fault-Diagnosis Metrics
It was shown in the example how, using structural analysis, residual generators could be generated. When
assessing fault-diagnosis the two metrics, detectability and isolability, is used to quantify the information
about the set of faults that is available through the set of residual generators. These two metrics are deﬁned
in Blanke et al. (2016)[sec. 5.6.4] using the signature matrix:
M : c →r
(4.19)
Which is a matrix mapping the constraints c ∈C onto the residuals r. This means that M shows which
residuals depend on which constraints. Each column of the matrix M represent a constraint and each row
represent a residual. The metric for detectability can then be stated as:
Lemma 4.1. A violation of a constraint c is structurally detectable if and only if it has a non-zero Boolean
signature in some residual r
c ∈Cdetectable ⇔∃r : c ̸= 0 ⇒r ̸= 0
(4.20)
Using the signature matrix the metric for isolability is:
Lemma 4.2. A violation of a constraint ci is structurally isolable if and only if it has a unique signature in
the residual vector, i.e. column mi of M is independent of all other columns in M
ci ∈Cisolable ⇔∀j ̸= i : mi ̸= mj
(4.21)

CHAPTER 5
Fault-Diagnosis of Cooperative Systems
The application of fault-diagnosis on cooperative systems is based on the deﬁnition of how cooperative
systems are modelled, how the inclusion of communication is made and how the result of the analysis
is interpreted. This chapter presents results on all these matters, along with general conclusions on fault-
diagnosis for linear cooperative systems. This chapter is a central part of the work performed during this
project. This chapter will present the theoretical results produced along with alternative approaches which
were not successful. The content of this project has been developed as part of this chapter unless stated
otherwise.
In the literature no attempts have been made to make a general deﬁnition for how to deﬁne a cooperative
system in terms of fault-diagnosis and fault-tolerance. Also no consistent metric for the resulting analysis has
been deﬁned. Due to the lack of a general consensus on the methodology in these two parts of fault-diagnosis,
it is necessary to ﬁrst introduce the modelling approach and the metric for the resulting analysis. The
modelling of a cooperative system is deﬁned ﬁrst then, based on this deﬁnition a distinction between analysis
of individual agents and the cooperative system will be deﬁned. Following these deﬁnitions the analysis can
be carried out.
5.1
Models in Cooperative Diagnosis
In cooperative systems there is an important distinction between the individual agents, and the combined
cooperative system. The dynamical model of the individual agents will be called the local models, and the
model of the combined cooperative system will be called the combined model. It is important to clearly
deﬁne what constitutes a local model, the combined model and a subset of the combined model.
5.1.1
Local Model
The local model refers to the model of an individual agent and is denoted by S. In the following the terms
individual agent and local model will be used interchangeably. Multiple approaches might be taken in the
bounding of an individual agent, but the approach adopted here is a boundary of physical connection. That
is; a local model is a model of a system that is physically decoupled from all other models in the system.
As such, any connection between individual agents are by deﬁnition communication. For linear systems,
a single local model is given by a general LTI model and the cooperative system might consist of several
differing LTI models of the form:

strongly connected. Figure 5.1 shows the partitioning of three different graphs into the strongly connected

Based on the previous chapter, it is concluded that interconnection of multiple agents by communication and
cooperative control does not enhance the local or global diagnosis, based on the deﬁnitions in the previous

Most autonomous vehicles have some way of sensing their surrounding world, either for Simultaneous
Localization And Mapping (SLAM), obstacle avoidance or for some other purpose. The ability to sense
the world is often realized through identiﬁcation of objects in the world, such as a ball, a wall segment or a
table. If all these identiﬁed object are grouped in the common term feature, the comparison of identiﬁed
features might enable additional isolability of faults in cooperative systems. The idea is illustrated in ﬁgure
6.1, where two agents both have the ball within the sensing ﬁeld of their respective exteroceptive sensors. If
one of the agents is subject to a fault, the comparison of the position of the ball identiﬁed by the different
agents might enable the isolation of the fault at the faulty agent.

marked in blue and red, respectively and the overlapping area is marked i green.

common classiﬁcation for sensors that allows for feature extraction can be found, based on the classiﬁcation

6.1
Sensor Classiﬁcation
A large assortment of sensors is generally available to autonomous systems, but not all are applicable in the
process of sensing the world or extracting features. In Siegwart et al. (2011) a comprehensive classiﬁcation
of sensors is made, which divides sensors into Proprioceptive (PC) and Exteroceptive (EC) sensors. PC
sensors are sensing internal properties of the vehicle that is, sensors like gyroscopes and accelerometers.
EC sensors are sensing external properties that is, sensors like camera and sonar. The sensors are further

6.1.1
Ranging and visual sensing
Ranging sensors span a wide range of different sensors, from the simple acoustic sonar sensor to Light
Detection And Ranging (LIDAR) sensors. They function by emitting a signal, and receiving it again, and
through analysis of the received signal, one or more points in the environment can be identiﬁed. Either
through the accumulation of points, or utilizing a sensor returning multiple points, a point cloud can be
created. This point cloud can then be analysed and features of the environment can be extracted. Vision
sensors function by capturing an image, which can be seen as a dataset of points associated to color
information. Through image analysis features of the environment can be extracted. The extraction of features
from images or point clouds constitute entire research ﬁelds on their own, and a comprehensive survey of
the available methodologies are outside the scope of this project. Therefore it is assumed that algorithms
already exist, for the two classes of sensors such that features can be extracted accurately and reliably.
6.1.2
Feature sensing

capable of extracting features. Physically ranging sensors and visual sensors might differ signiﬁcantly, specif-
ically since most ranging sensors return a relatively small amount of points with very clear interpretations,
whereas a visual sensors often return millions of data points with very vague interpretations.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis has been to investigate formal methods for performing fault-diagnosis for
cooperative systems. This has been investigated using two different approaches: Structural analysis and a
system based approach.
Any approach for fault-diagnosis of cooperative systems relies on a formal description of the system, along
with a set of metrics on which the results can be judged. This thesis deﬁnes a cooperative-system with
respect to fault-diagnosis along with metrics for the diagnosis results. The deﬁnition of a combined system
was given such that diagnosis on the combined system would yield the best case diagnosis result for a
cooperative system. As metrics for diagnosis of cooperative systems the detectability and isolability metric
of classical fault diagnosis was extended. The extension introduces the distinction between the diagnosis of
an individual agent, diagnosis of an ensemble of agents and the diagnosis of the combined system, along
with deﬁnitions for all three.
It was shown that structural analysis could be readily applied to the cooperative-system deﬁned. The result
of this analysis showed that the fault-diagnosis metric was the concatenation of the local metric, for the study
case. That is the connection of multiple agents based on a cooperative control law for the speciﬁc system,
did not increase the amount of fault that was detectable or isolable. The successful application of structural
analysis to the system came after an unsuccessful attempt, in which a faulty formulation of the control law
lead to a wrong conclusion.
A system based approach for fault-diagnosis developed by Nyberg and Frisk was introduced, based on
their results, two propositions were derived. Based on the deﬁnition of a cooperative system of this thesis,
the two propositions show that all linear cooperative systems will be the concatenation of the local metric.
That is, detectability and isolability of the individual agents is all that can be achieved for the cooperative
system. The derivation of the two propositions was the result of several different attempts, starting with
the Chow-Willsky method and following with several different manipulations of the system based on the
Krondecker product, the Tracy-Singh produckt and the Kathori-Rao product.
It was then investigated how the local metrics could be propagated using the communication graph. That is,
how the diagnosis of a fault at a single agent, could become diagnosed as other agents in the cooperative
system. Two propositions were derived, they showed that despite the results of diagnosis analysis, the local
diagnosis can become a global or ensemble diagnosis based on the chosen graph topology.
It was further investigated how the availability of communication and exteroceptive sensors, could be used to
enhance the local fault-diagnosis. The enhancement is achieved through the ability to extract features from
the surrounding environment, and comparing the resulting features among the agents in the system. Two

of the two sensors and algorithms for feature extraction was developed. It was then showed how, using

7.1
Future Work
There are numerous directions in which the work can be extended, many of the directions are large in
magnitude and complexity. Among the immediate directions is experimental veriﬁcation of the results. This
pertains to both the results on diagnostics and the results on comparison of extracted features. The two
propositions developed on fault-diagnosis of cooperative-systems are only valid for linear systems, hence an
extension to non-linear system could be investigated.
For the fault-diagnosis based on exteroceptive sensors a lot of open questions remain beside the experimental
veriﬁcation. Two assumptions were made, investigating how to handle the case where the two assumptions
are not true would be a point for future work. Beside that many different cases where multiple object are
extracted and how to handle an error in the extraction of these, could be investigated.
It could further be investigated if the structural analysis changes if each agent knows the relative distance to
all of its neighbours and how it effects the system used to derive proposition 5.1 - 5.2.
