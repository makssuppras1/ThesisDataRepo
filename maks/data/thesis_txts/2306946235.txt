Abstract
The purpose of this project is to develop an automatic detection of cortical
arousals in sleep. Arousals are transient events in sleep, that lead to a shift to-
wards wakefulness. The presence of arousals is a central element in diagnosing
sleep-wake dysfunction. Arousals are visible on the sleep electroencephalog-
raphy signal as abrupt changes in frequency.
Currently sleep technicians and physicians visually inspect hours of sleep data,
to manually annotate where arousals occurred. This task is not only expensive
and time-consuming, but it is also tiresome and human errors are likely to oc-
cur. This implies a very low agreement between human scorers. Since 1999
researchers have attempted developing algorithms for automatic detection of
arousals, but it has been proven a challenge due to the poorly deﬁned gold
standard.
The proposed algorithm applied advanced signal processing methods for fea-
ture extraction on PSG recordings, but also adapts recent advancements in ma-
chine learning by using Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks for
classiﬁcation. LSTM networks excel on sequential data and are becoming in-
creasingly popular in other engineering ﬁelds. Given the nature of arousals
being described by temporal change in frequency content, the LSTM networks
are expected to improve detection performance signiﬁcantly, compared to cur-
rently published methods.
The algorithm was trained and tested on 190 nocturnal PSG recordings in the
Wisconsin Sleep Cohort, that have been annotated by two independent human
scorers. The trained algorithm was subsequently validated on an excerpt of
the inter-scorer reliability test (ISRT) carried out by the American Association
of Sleep Medicine, in which the same set of data was annotated by more than
5,000 independent human scorers.
On data from Wisconsin Sleep Cohort, the algorithm reached an overall recall
(TP/(TP+FN)) of 0.82 and precision (TP/(TP+FP)) of 0.76, and is the ﬁrst study
to detect arousals based on such large-scale data.
The conclusion was that the implementation of the arousal detection algorithm
was very successful, and may even lead to the development of new biomarkers
for describing sleep-wake dysfunction.

Resumé
Formålet med dette projekt er at udvikle en automatisk detektion af kortikale
arousals i søvn. Arousals er kortvarende begivenheder i søvn, der fører til
et skift i retning af vågenhed. Tilstedeværelsen af arousals er et centralt el-
ement i diagnosticeringen af søvn-vågen-dysfunktion. Arousals er synlige på
søvn elektroencefalograﬁ, hvori de ses ved pludselige ændringer i frekvensind-
holdet.
Nuværende metode til detektion af arousals er visuel inspektion af søvnlæger.
Dette er ikke blot en dyr og tidskrævende proces, men det er også en udmat-
tende opgave og menneskelige fejl kan ikke undgås. Dette medfører en meget
lav overensstemmelse søvnlægerne imellem. Siden 1999 har forskere forsøgt
at udvikle algoritmer til automatisk detektion af arousals, men det er blevet en
udfordring på grund af den dårligt deﬁneret gold standard.
Den foreslåede algoritme anvender avanceret signalbehandlingsmetoder til fea-
ture extraction på PSG-optagelser, og tilpasser sig ydermere de seneste frem-
skridt i machine learning ved at bruge Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neu-
rale netværk til klassiﬁcering. LSTM-netværk udmærker sig på sekventielle
data, og er under stigende interesse iblandt andre tekniske anvendelser. I og
med arousals er deﬁneret ved den tidsmæssige ændring i frekvensindhold, for-
ventes LSTM-netværk at forbedre ydeevnen for arousal detektion markant.
Algoritmen er blevet trænet og testet på 190 PSG-optagelser i Wisconsin Sleep
Cohort, som er blevet annoteret af to uafhængige søvnlæger. Den trænede al-
goritme blev efterfølgende valideret på et uddrag af det inter-scorer reliability
test (ISRT) udført af American Association of Sleep Medicine, hvor det samme
sæt af data blev annoteret af mere end 5.000 uafhængige søvnlæger.
På data fra Wisconsin Sleep Cohort, nåede algoritmen en samlet recall (TP /
(TP + FN)) på 0,82 og præcision (TP / (TP + FP)) på 0,76, og er den første un-
dersøgelse til at undersøge arousals baseret på så stor en mængde data.
Konklusionen blev at udviklingen af algoritmen til detektion af arousals var
meget vellykket, og endda kan føre til udvikling af nye biomarkører til beskriv-
else af søvn-vågen-dysfunktion.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Cortical arousals are transient events in sleep visible on the measured EEG.
The developed system for detection of cortical arousals receive the EEG and
EMG signals as input, and within seconds returns the exact locations in time
and characteristics of the arousals. This is a task that takes a sleep technician
or physician several hours to complete.
1.1
Project Statement
The purpose of this project is to develop an algorithm for the automatic detec-
tion of cortical arousals in sleep. This objective is to be completed through the
following sub objectives:
• Developing an understanding of signal processing on sleep data, along
with the problems occurring in real-world polysomnography recordings.
• Gaining insight and knowledge on state of the art of arousal detection
through an extensive literature study.
• Implementing the following modules for the algorithm:
– Signal pre-processing through ﬁltering and removal of artifacts.
– Feature extraction through advanced signal processing.
– Classiﬁcation through state of the art machine learning.
• Conveying the research results through composing draft for a journal pa-
per submission.
1.2
Thesis Outline
The primary product of this thesis is the journal paper draft. From the draft, it
is possible to recreate the proposed detection system in its entirety.
Chapter 2 provides the clinical background for reading and understanding the
content of the journal paper, primarily through an introduction to the ﬁeld of
sleep medicine and the polysomnography testing. Cortical arousals are intro-
duced and why they are important.
Chapter 3 presents the journal paper draft itself. This includes a literature
review, pre-processing, feature extraction, classiﬁcation and post-processing.
1
==PAGE:24==
Furthermore, the results of the developed method are analyzed and a discus-
sion on the applicative perspective is carried out.
Chapter 4 presents the detail of the advanced signal processing tools that have
been used in the proposed system. This includes FIR and RLS adaptive ﬁlter-
ing for the removal of artifacts, as well as Wavelet Packet decomposition for
spectral analysis. Furthermore, the tools used are evaluated and other possible
options to be used are presented.
Chapter 5 presents the detail of the machine learning methods used. This in-
cludes an introduction to the Long Short-Term Neural networks used in the
project, accompanied by a description of the conﬁgurations used and an anal-
ysis of performance.
Chapter 6 concludes the thesis and summarizes all the essentials to draw from
the work that has been done. The various discussions are summarized, a con-
clusion is made and proposals for future work outlined.

CHAPTER2
Preliminaries
Objective: The objective of this chapter is to introduce the reader to the basic processes
that alter sleep-wake patterns, while giving a more detailed introduction to the clini-
cal aspect of sleep medicine - the polysomnography test, leading to the description of
cortical arousals causing sleep-wake dysfunction.
2.1
Clinical Background
Sleep is a central element of our daily lives, that takes up on average a third of
our time. Getting a good night of sleep is important not only for mental aspects
like cognitive awareness, happiness and overall comfort, but also physiological
health aspects, as sleep deprivation can lead to cardiovascular diseases, among
others [1]. However, in our modern society many people forget to practice get-
ting enough sleep, let alone getting good sleep. Technological advances in the
recent century have allowed researchers to study sleep scientiﬁcally.
Sleep is naturally regulated by both the circadian rhythm and sleep-wake home-
ostasis [2]. Genetic differences can affect these factors, implying that some peo-
ple require less sleep than others. External factors such as stress, food diges-
tion, caffeine and alcohol obviously also factor in.
Figure 2.1: Two-process model of sleep regulation. Homeostatic drive for sleep and circadian drive for arousal
are the two most driving mechanisms in sleep-wake patterns.
Source: http://www.howsleepworks.com/
Figure 2.1 shows the sleep-wake pattern of a healthy person modulated by
homeostatic and circadian drives. However, often times the sleep pattern is not
as stable, and people may have trouble falling asleep, or even ﬁnd themselves
waking up repetitively during a night. This is called sleep-wake dysfunction,
and can lead to excessive daytime sleepiness or cognitive impairment if not
mitigated. Sleep-wake dysfunction is believed to be highly correlated with an
3
==PAGE:26==
event called an arousal, which is a brief awakening during sleep not necessar-
ily leading to consciousness, but potentially causing irreversible fragmentation
of sleep progression [3]. It is a central element in diagnosis of multiple sleep
disorders. Detection of arousals is currently done through visual inspection by
human scorers. This approach not only time consuming, but also expensive
and there is a very low agreement between human scorers upon what exactly
constitutes an arousal [4].
This project will detail the development of a computer based algorithm for the
automatic detection of arousals, and furthermore discuss how the ﬁeld of sleep
medicine may to move forward in gaining knowledge on the nature of arousals
by the use of biomedical signal processing and machine learning. Before intro-
ducing development of the proposed method, the ﬁeld of sleep medicine is
introduced.
2.2
Polysomnography
People suspect of suffering from a sleep disorder are examined using a polysomnog-
raphy (PSG) test. PSG is an extensive study performed at a sleep clinic, in
which the subjects have several electrodes attached all over their bodies while
they sleep. The electrodes are recording various signals concurrently in the
search for biomarkers that may eventually lead to a diagnosis. Measured sig-
nals include brain activity, eye and leg movements, breathing patterns, heart
rate, blood oxygen level, and more. Before the computer age, signals were im-
mediately printed out on consecutive sheets of paper, with each paper ﬁtting
30 seconds of data. This duration is named a 30-second epoch, and is still used
as the basis for analysis today. Figure 2.2 illustrates a PSG setup, and Figure
2.3 is an example of a 30-second epoch from data readout of a PSG recording.
Manual Scoring
After ended session a technician reviews and annotates the data. Rechtschaffen
and Kales (R&K) manual for scoring human sleep was published in 1968 and
was the ﬁrst widely accepted guideline to be followed [5]. In 1992, the Ameri-

EEG is the measurement of brain activity. There are several ways of measur-

cerebral cortex would provide the most accurate result, as it is closer to the

2.2.2
Sleep Staging
Sleep staging, or sleep stage classiﬁcation (SSC) is an attempt to describe the on-
going processes in a sleeping subject. As of the current scoring guidelines,
sleep is a cyclic pattern that consists of the following stages [11, 10]:
Non-REM sleep. Non-REM is the initial stage. During non-REM the heart rate
is stable, blood ﬂows to muscles and limbs, and generally many physiologi-
cally restorative processes are believed to occur. Non-REM is further classiﬁed
into three sub-stages enumerated 1 through 3, or N1 through N3. N1 is more a
transition period than an actual stage, as it is the period of time a person is in the
process of falling asleep and gradually lose consciousness. After N1 one enters
actual sleep and progress from N2 to N3, during which brain waves gradually
become slower and more synchronized. In N3, also referred to as deep sleep or
slow wave sleep, the EEG signals are dominated by very distinct slow waves,
as shown in Figure 2.5.
REM sleep. REM (rapid eye movement) sleep is the opposite of non-REM sleep.
During REM sleep dreaming occurs which implies that the brain waves of a
person in REM sleep look almost like the brain waves of a wake person. How-
ever, muscles and limbs are completely paralyzed, a phenomenon which is
9
==PAGE:32==
believed to be a neurological barrier not to act out the dreaming. During REM
heart rate destabilizes, blood pressure rises and under the eye lids, the eyes
are moving around rapidly. A person in REM is thought to undergo cognitive
restorative processes.
AASM has identiﬁed several biomarkers for SSC based on the signals from
a PSG. These biomarkers are outlined in Table 2.2. Their scoring guidelines
cover multiple modalities, but studies have found it possible to approximate
sleep staging based on EEG only, with few mistakes. Clinically, however, it is
still a complete assessment. SSC is the ﬁrst step of analysis, and to provide
an overview the stage for each 30-second epoch is plotted on a time series,
creating what is called a hypnogram. Sleep cycles vary in duration but typically
the ﬁrst cycle lasts 70-100 minutes and later cycles 90-120 minutes. Progression
is typically from N1 through N2 and N3, brieﬂy back to N2 before entering
REM, and repeat (Figure 2.10). Most people do not tend to wake up after REM,
but one is more likely to wake up after REM than any other stage.
Figure 2.10: Example of a hypnogram. Showing the cyclic patterns of sleep stages accompanied with prolonged
REM sequences as sleep progresses, as well as shortened durations of deep sleep as sleep progresses.
Source: http://www.howsleepworks.com/types_cycles.html
Hypnograms reveal patterns of sleep. If a subject has a very regular sleep pat-
tern and process all sleep stages as expected, everything appears to be ﬁne.
However, if a subject wakes up repetitively, or has trouble falling asleep, this
will appear on the hypnogram. For automated analysis, metrics have been in-
troduced to quantify some characteristics of a hypnogram, which include sleep
onset latency (time from lights out to sleep onset), REM latency (time from
sleep onset to ﬁrst REM epoch) and wake after sleep onset (WASO, minutes
spent awake after ﬁrst sleep onset).

Automatic Detection of Cortical Arousals in Sleep
using LSTM Neural Network and Large-Scale Data
Abstract. Arousals are transient events in sleep that
are crucial for diagnosing sleep-wake dysfunction. Gold
standard for arousal detection is annotation by a human
scorer. This study proposes a computerized method to
automatically detect arousals in sleep based on feature
extraction from electroencephalogram, electromyogram
and the outputs from a sleep stage classiﬁer are used
as predictive features, as well. Long Short-Term Memory
neural networks are used for classiﬁcation on 190 subjects
from the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort. On a test set consisting
of 57 subjects, the performance reached an overall recall
of 0.82 and precision of 0.76, resulting in an F1 score of
0.79.
I. INTRODUCTION
Arousals are brief awakenings during sleep, that are per-
fectly normal in the right amount [1]. Too many arousals,
however, cause sleep fragmentation and can lead to cognitive
dysfunction expressed through excessive daytime sleepiness,
memory impairment and depression, among others. Thus,
the presence of arousals in nocturnal sleep recordings is an
important biomarker and a central element in the diagnosis of
various sleep disorders. Arousals can be caused by patholog-
ical events like periodic leg movements or sleep-disordered
breathing (SDB) provoking a natural arousal response, or
occur spontaneously for no apparent reason. It is natural to
have an arousal with or without apparent triggers once or twice
an hour, but too many spontaneous arousals may suggest an
underlying pathological condition.
State of the art for arousal detection is manual annotation.
Sleep technicians and physicians are trained in recognizing
patterns on sleep data, and annotating which periods of
time are associated with arousals. This is an extremely time
consuming and tiresome task, and errors are likely to occur.
Furthermore, the accepted scoring guideline of arousals made
by the American Association of Sleep Medicine (AASM) is
vague and up for subjective interpretation, leading to poor
inter- and intra-scorer agreement [2].
Initially research groups investigated arousal detection
based on, and validated against, a single technician as gold
standard. Using a rule-based paradigm, De Carli et al. (1999)
reported a true positive rate (TPR) of 0.88 by use of statistical
parameters on EEG and EMG. Sugi, Kawana, and Nakamura
(2009) also implemented a rule-based paradigm with the
addition of a respiratory trace, and obtained a TPR of 0.86
on SDB arousals [4]. In the machine learning paradigm, Cho
et al. (2006) obtained 0.75 TPR with sleep-wake annotations as
post-processing and an SVM classiﬁer for decision making [5].
Sorensen et al. (2012) adapted a neural network, and obtained
a TPR of 0.89 [6], while ﬁnally Shahrbabaki et al. (2015)
furthermore included respiratory tracing and leg movements,
to obtain a TPR of 0.79 [7]. All studies were tested on small
data sets of different origin, so the metrics are not directly
comparable.
More recent studies have investigated the effect of multiple
scorers contributing to gold standard, to try and model the
inter-scorer agreement. Shmiel et al. (2009) adapted a rule
based-algorithm validated against three different human scor-
ers, and obtained a TPR of 0.75 with a positive predictive
value (PPV) of 0.77 on 20 subjects [8]. Furthermore, Coppi-
eterst Wallant et al. (2016) compared against 6 human scorers
on 60 subjects and reached a TPR of 0.61 [9]. This decrease in
performance compared to previous studies does not necessarily
imply worse algorithms, but in fact it simply illustrates the low
inter-scorer agreement.
Here we aim to develop a highly accurate and consistent
algorithm for the automatic detection of arousals, regardless
of the sleep disorder. The proposed method is based on feature
extraction from EEG, EMG and sleep stages, while classiﬁ-
cation will adapt recent advances in machine learning with
the implementation of an Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
neural network on 190 subjects from the Wisconsin Sleep
Cohort (WSC). LSTM networks excel in processing sequential
data, and are thus an obvious choice for the detection of
arousals. The WSC is annotated by two independent human
scorers, and the data should therefore be able to express
both inter and intra scorer variability. For validation, prior
to any analysis data was split randomly to training set (133
subjects) and test set (57 subjects). Performance on the test
data provided a TPR of 0.82 and PPV of 0.76, leading to an
overall F1 score of 0.79. To our knowledge this is the largest
data set with independent raters upon which such an algorithm
has been developed and validated.
Finally, the the proposed method will be compared to an
excerpt of the AASM inter-scorer reliability test, in which 200
epochs of a single recording have been annotated by more than
5,000 scorers.

C. Preprocessing
Several types of artifacts arise from real-world PSG record-

equipment in the clinic [12], but unfortunately that is rarely the
case and digital signal processing is required. The proposed
algorithm assumes a clean signal, but removing all artifacts
would be a complete study in itself. However, the most
prevalent artifacts need removal to justify automated analysis.
1) Electrocardiography Interference: Electrocardiography

on the removal of electrooculographic artifacts from EEG, a
problem that is of a similar nature to that of ECG artifact
removal.

E. Normalization
Normalization of features is an important step in devel-

of data sets. First, a data transformation scheme to assert
that input to a classiﬁer across different features have similar
characteristics is implemented. Second, a data normalization
scheme in which it is assured that data from various subjects
have similar characteristics, is implemented as well. There are
numerous reasons for inter-patient and intra-patient variability
between recordings, both physiological and due to difference
in equipment used.
1) Transformation: The EEG and EMG features designed
express absolute measurements, and e.g. an increase in beta
power of 1µV does not necessarily imply the same change

relative, e.g. the absolute change from 1 to 2 is 1 and the
change from 10 to 20 is 10, whereas the change from log(1)
to log(2) is equal to the change from log(10) to log(20). Hence,
the relative changes are found. For this application, we desire
to attenuate the inﬂuence of recording speciﬁc differences,

point, i.e. instructing a patient to ﬂexing a muscle for a couple
of seconds, and use that period as reference. Submental muscle
tone is known to peak in wakefulness, base in REM and
undergo a gradual decrease throughout NREM [23]. For this
reason, the pseudo representatives of min and max are chosen
to be the lowest and highest signiﬁcant bins respectively in
the histogram. A signiﬁcant bin is in this context deﬁned to
be a bin with a value of at least 0.5% of total signal duration,
corresponding to 2.4 minutes in an 8-hour session. This is an
attempt to use physiologically meaningful references.
An overview of the entire transformation and normalization
scheme is shown in Fig. 4.

III. MACHINE LEARNING USING LSTM NETWORKS
Long short-term memory (LSTM) [25] recurrent neural net-
works (RNN) were introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber
in 1997 and have recently gained a lot of interest in ﬁelds like
natural language processing, speech recognition and computer
vision [26]. Most recently biomedical signal processing also
began adapting this type of networks [17, 27, 28]. RNNs are
sub types of artiﬁcial neural networks that are dealing with
sequential data where the output of a hidden unit is fed to the
hidden unit itself at the consequent time step. However, RNNs
have had issues with long-term dependencies, and in general
neural networks have had issues with vanishing gradients in
training on large scale data sets, both of which reasons that
inspired Hochreiter and Schmidhuber to develop LSTMs.
A. Architecture
In LSTM networks information ﬂow through the cell state,
and at each input xt the network can remove information from
the state using the forget gate or add information to the state,
using the input gate. Based on the previous cell output ht−1
and current input xt, the forget and input gates decide what
information to keep and what to discard. The cell state is
what allows LSTMs to adapt long-term dependencies. Using
the updated state, a non-linear activation combines to produce
cell output ht, which continues to xt+1. For more details on
LSTMs, refer to Understanding LSTM Networks by Olah [29]
or the original publication by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber
[25].

In the proposed algorithm, the input layer is connected
through linear activations with a hidden layer that contains N

Training a neural network on large scale data can not be
done all at once. Instead batches of a certain duration are
extracted from the data set, and the neural network performs
the optimization on a random batch for each iteration. This
approach is called the stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The
duration of each batch has to be chosen for the application,
and a sequence length of 4 minutes (240 time samples) was
chosen for the proposed method. The duration has to be long
enough for the neural network to learn the context, but not too
long for the computational complexity to inﬂate.

G. Regularization
To assure the trained network does not overﬁt to training
data dropout was implemented. Dropout is a simple way to
prevent neural networks from overﬁtting, where each hidden
unit has assigned a probability of being blocked out for an

regularization) converged faster and did not overﬁt, and is
therefore used.

A. Sleep Stages
First, it is important to investigate performance distribution
across sleep stages. Fig. 12 (b-c) reveals that AD in N1 stages
has a lower recall compared to AD in N2, N3 and REM. This
makes sense because of the sleep staging convention and the
fact that N1 periods are very brief: Epochs are only scored as
N1 if at least 50% of the epoch shows N1-like characteristics.
Thus, if an arousal occurs within the ﬁrst 15 seconds of an
epoch, with the prior epoch being scored as wake, the entire
sequence will be considered stable wake by the post processing
scheme (see Section II, E) and disregarded from analysis. This
potentially removes otherwise correctly scored arousals, and
the amount of false negatives increase with recall decreasing as
a result. This issue improves if an automatic SSC that omits the
use of sequential 30-second epoch convention is used instead
of manual annotations.
B. Arousal Type
Another interesting analysis is the distribution of perfor-
mance versus type of arousal. For this speciﬁc analysis only
recall is available, since the proposed method does not provide
arousal type as output, and we are left with types of the
annotated arousals. Fig. 12 (a) shows recall with respect to
arousal type. Recall for spontaneous arousals is signiﬁcantly
lower than recall for LM and SDB arousals. This indicates
that either LM and SDB arousals appear differently (e.g. more
severe) on feature space (which is limited to EEG, chin EMG
and sleep staging), or human scorers are less precise when
annotating spontaneous arousals. Regardless, it shows that the
respiratory and/or leg movement traces are not required for
detection of arousals caused by SDB or LM.

VI. DISCUSSION
A. Comparison with Previous Models
Comparing the proposed method with previously published
models is challenging due to the fact that different performance
metrics are reported, and that different data sets are used.
Table IV summarizes the previous models published in state
of the art. Generally, there has not been any steady increase
in performance, as one would expect with the availability
of increasingly sophisticated machine learning methods. This
emphasizes the need for large scale approaches with multiple
scorers.
The method proposed by Coppieterst Wallant et al. (2016)
and Shmiel et al. (2009) are the only methods to examine the
effect of inter-scorer variability. The performance reported in
the studies are deﬁnitely lower compared to any previously
published methods. That does not necessarily imply that the
algorithms would perform worse on comparable data sets.
It emphasizes, however, the fact that while the single-scorer
validated methods are somewhat comparable to each other
(showing the ability to model a single scorer on a small data
set), they are not comparable to the methods validated against
multiple scorers, on larger data sets. Coppieterst Wallant et al.
reported that their proposed method performed better overall
compared to any of the 6 scorers in the study. Keeping this
in mind, the results achieved in our proposed method are
extremely satisfactory. It is shown that the model has con-
verged to the average of scorers, considering the comparison

with ISRT, in which case only slight improvements to the
performance metrics are possible.
This, of course, is under the premise that we apply equal
weight to the opinion of each and every scorer, and accepts this
as the truth. In supervised learning it is extremely important
that the training data is representative of the problem at hand,
and that includes the labels. There is no guarantee that the
underlying truth is found by modeling the average of all
scorers. One might in fact be able to single out the few very
reliable and skilled scorers, and train a model based on them.
However, this requires a precise deﬁnition of arousals, and
one that is not designed to assure high predictability among
different scorers, but one that is correlated the best with a
clinical outcome.
B. Machine Learning for Medical Decision Making
Machine learning is becoming increasingly popular for
decision making in many ﬁelds, including medicine. In this
paper, we have successfully established the proposed system
as a valid predictor of arousals. While this is great, how
can we best utilize it? It has been validated for its purpose,
arousal detection, but what knowledge do we gain from this?
There are many possible applications of the proposed
method. Correlating the model to clinical outcome is now
possible due to the consistency in predictions, compared to
human scoring. The suggestion is that neuro-cognitive dys-
function is mostly related to arousal index, and thus measures
for cognitive impairment such as excessive daytime sleepiness
or memory dysfunction are possible end points. Evaluation of
sleep disorders is also possible: The presence of NA provides
a high-arousability phenotype, which could very well be used
as a biomarker for insomnia. WASO describes the macro-
structure of sleep-wake, and the currently practiced arousal
count is an attempt to describe the micro-structure of sleep
wake. The proposed method is essentially providing an even
ﬁner level of detail and describing the sleep-wake mechanism
on nano-structure.
With gold standard being human scoring, which is known
for having poor inter-scorer reliability, we can also start to
challenge the current deﬁnition. The deﬁnition of arousals
was initially deﬁned as a matter of convenience, and this
method allows us to more rigorously test the hypothesis: do
arousals have a clinical impact? Additionally, what should
truly constitute an arousal (duration, periodicity, sleep stage,
is it cause or effect, etc.)?
The proposed method is built upon annotation of arousal
onset only, and yet is still rather capable of modeling arousal
duration as well (as per Figure 11). However, it is not perfect,
and it will not become perfect until there is a consistent
gold standard. A way to propose a data-driven deﬁnition of
arousals could be by initially training an algorithm based on
manual annotations (as in the proposed system), and then use
the output of the ﬁrst model as targets for a new iteration of
the model, and repeating until the predictions converge. This
approach could potentially help deﬁne what truly constitutes
an arousal.
The challenge with machine learning is the fact that the
models are very difﬁcult to interpret. In neural networks
humans provide the input data, but it is the network itself that
adjusts the weights and thereby deﬁnes the rules of decision
making. This is different from the previous rule-based
paradigm, in which the human would pre-deﬁne a set of
rules, and verify them in a machine learning environment. In
such case, we would not be assured that it was the optimal
set of rules, but at least we would know the rules. For us
to gain any knowledge from a model like the proposed, we
need to be able to open the black box, and ﬁnd out what is
inside. Mathematically speaking, we do have access to the
parameters in the trained network, upon which the decisions
are made, so it is possible to interpret, the tools are simply
not fully developed yet. This is something machine learning
researchers are working on.
Clinicians, on the contrary, may not care what is going on
in the black box, as long as it can help diagnosing patients and
saving lives. Bonnet, Doghramji, and Roehrs concluded that in
the case computerized arousal prediction deviates from manual
scoring, the output must be conﬁrmed on a clinical outcome.
It can be argued that the prediction deviates quite a bit,
especially considering arousals with a duration less than three
seconds. Even looking at sub-threshold arousals as indicated
by the ISRT comparison might open new doors. Correlating
this model with CAP rates could also be of interest, since CAP
rates are shown to be correlated with some clinical end points.
Generally, arousals are suspected of being correlated with
sleepiness, and thus evaluation the proposed method against an
objective measure of sleepiness, e.g. the multiple sleep latency
test (MSLT), could be the ﬁrst step in revealing the actual
clinical signiﬁcance of arousals.
VII. CONCLUSION
An algorithm for automatic detection of arousals based
on LSTM networks was presented, with a high performance
in both precision (0.76) and recall (0.82). The model was
compared against multiple AASM certiﬁed technicians, trained
on 133 subjects and tested on 57 subjects from the Wisconsin
Sleep Cohort. Furthermore, the model was visually compared
to 140 epochs from the ISRT, indicating an overall agreement.
The future of the algorithm lies within pushing the deﬁnition of
arousals in a way that is more correlated in clinical outcomes
rather than convenience.

CHAPTER4
Biomedical Signal Processing
Objective: The objective of this chapter is to introduce the reader to the signal process-
ing tools used in development of the proposed method. The reader is expected to have
a basic understanding of signal acquisition, and an understanding of the terms time
domain and frequency domain.
4.1
Introduction
Feature extraction includes all steps from pre-processing of the signal (e.g. ar-
tifact removal) to obtaining the ﬁnal parameters for classiﬁcation. The focus of
this project is not pre-processing, but rather the actual feature extraction and
classiﬁcation. Therefore, this chapter brieﬂy introduces removal of the most
prevalent artifacts, and then further details the signal processing tools used.
4.1.1
Artifact Removal
Artifacts are deﬁned as transient or sustained components of a recorded signal
that are extraneous to the variable of interest [12]. Artifacts can be instrumental
(e.g. malfunction of an electrode), environmental (e.g. equipment near the
recording generating electrical potential) or physiological (e.g. contamination
of other physiological processes that are not of interest in the study). In PSG
recordings, common artifacts include (but are not limited to):
• Power line interference (electrical potential generated by power supply)
• Electrode popping (typically isolated to a single electrode, artifacts oc-
curring due to changes in electrode-to-patient impedance)
• Electrocardiographic artifacts (blood vessels facilitate conduction of elec-
trical potentials, and therefore electrodes located near blood vessels are
prone to contamination of ECG artifacts)
• Respiration artifacts (if a patient is lying on the side with an electrode
resting between the pillow and scalp, movements from breathing can
contaminate the signal)
• Sweat artifacts (sweating cause changes in electrode potential and con-
tamination in signal)
Automatically eliminating artifacts from a PSG, would be a study in itself. For
this project, focus is on developing an arousal detection algorithm, and there-
fore presence of some artifacts is accepted. However, the most prominent and
27

disturbing artifacts must be removed to justify automated analysis, and there-
fore it was decided to mitigate power line interference (PLI) and ECG artifacts.
PLI appears as a static 50 or 60 Hz component depending on the country of
origin. Luckily, all signals of interest for arousal detection contain content of
frequencies below 50 Hz, and therefore a simple ﬁnite impulse response (FIR)
ﬁlter can be used to remove this artifact. ECG artifacts are more difﬁcult to re-
move, since they are not well-deﬁned in frequency domain, but have a very dis-
tinct morphological structure in time domain (Figure 2.9). Adaptive recursive-
least-squares (RLS) ﬁlters are well-suited for removal of noise contamination
from a known source. ECG signals are acquired in the PSG studies, and hence
the application of RLS ﬁlters for removal of ECG artifacts is clear-cut.

4.1.2
Spectral Analysis
Spectral analysis in this project touches on the change in frequency distribu-
tion over time, i.e. time-frequency analysis. EEG signals are non-stationary
processes, and therefore simply applying a Fast Fourier Transform is not suf-
ﬁcient - instead, the Wavelet transform is used. The mathematical foundation
behind Wavelet theory is attached in Appendix A. This section will introduce
29
==PAGE:52==
Figure 4.2: Schematic representation of an RLS ﬁlter showing the recursive property.
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursive_least_squares_filter
how Wavelets are used in the context of signal processing.
The continuous Wavelet transform (CWT) decomposes a signal into shifted
and scaled versions of a mother Wavelet, where the discrete Wavelet transform
(DWT) essentially is the same, but with a ﬁnite set of scales and shifts. The
DWT is an attempt to decrease the computational complexity of CWT, while
obtaining a desired frequency and time resolution.
In terms of signal processing, a mother wavelet ψj,k can be interpreted as a
high pass ﬁlter, whereas the associated scaling function φj,k (or father wavelet)
can be interpreted as a low pass ﬁlter. The relationship is deﬁned as:
ψj,k
=
2j/2hj(l −2jk),
(4.9)
φj,k
=
2j/2gj(l −2jk),
(4.10)
where hj are the high pass ﬁlter coefﬁcients, gj are the low pass ﬁlter coef-
ﬁcients, j is the scaling parameter and k is the shift parameter. The spectral
coefﬁcients are then computed by applying the ﬁlters on the signal at various
levels of analysis: Initially the ﬁlter is applied on the sampled signal with scal-
ing j = 1. This decomposes the signal to two parts, with output from the high
pass ﬁlter called the detail (denoted Dj(k)) and output from the low pass ﬁlter
called the approximation (denoted Aj(k)). They are computed as
Dj(k)
=
x(t) ∗ψj,k,
(4.11)
Aj(k)
=
x(t) ∗φj,k,
(4.12)
with x(t) being the input signal. Each derived approximation signal is then fur-
ther ﬁltered into an additional level of approximation and detail signals, with
the scaling parameter incremented, i.e j = 2 at level 2, j = 3 at level 3, and so
on. For each level of decomposition the frequency resolution doubles, and the
time resolution halves. In DWT, at each level of decomposition the approxi-
mation coefﬁcients are used for further ﬁltering whereas the detail coefﬁcients
are used as a measure of energy representing the frequencies at that level. This
results in good time resolution at the higher frequencies, and good frequency
resolution at the lower frequencies. The concept of decomposition is shown in
Figure 4.3.

4.2.1
Power Line Interference
PLI is removed from both EEG and EMG signals. This was carried out using
an FIR low pass ﬁlter with cut-off frequency at 45 Hz and 100dB attenuation

frequency and phase responses, seen in Figures 4.6 and 4.7. The EMG channels
were allegedly pre ﬁltered not to contain signal below 10 Hz, but inspection
of the data revealed otherwise. Therefore the EMG channels are additionally

attenuation at 5 Hz.

4.2.4
Segmentation
All signals in the PSG recordings from WSC are sampled with 200 Hz, which
means that 200 samples are collected each and every second. This translates
to a time resolution of 0.005 seconds, which for arousal detection is way too
short. Therefore the signal is collected and described through features in time
bins that each represent 1 second of the signal. This means that time bin no. 1
describes samples 1 through 200, time bin no. 2 describes samples 201 through
400, etc.

CHAPTER5
Classiﬁcation using LSTM Neural Networks
Objective: This chapter aims to explain the concept of machine learning and neural
networks. The increasingly popular Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural net-
works are introduced, and the training of the network is analyzed. Furthermore, an
extended analysis comparing LSTM networks with regular neural networks is carried

5.1
Introduction
Machine learning can be described as the attempt of teaching a computer how
to predict an outcome, and is either supervised or unsupervised: Supervised
learning requires a data set with a given input associated with a desired output.
In unsupervised learning the target is unknown. The model will attempt to
collect data in clusters that have equal characteristics. Neural networks can
be used for both supervised as well as unsupervised learning. In this project,
neural networks are used for classiﬁcation (pattern recognition) in a supervised
environment.
5.1.1
Feed-Forward Neural Networks
Consider the example shown in Figure 5.1, and assume that the data in blue is
drawn from one distribution, whereas the data in red is drawn from a different
distribution. The objective at hand is to, given a new data point of unknown
origin, provide the best estimate which of the two distributions this derives
from. However, we shall deﬁne the rule before being presented the new data
point.
Looking at the data (Figure 5.1a), we could obtain a rather good classiﬁcation
performance by simply looking at the value on the y axis, and classifying ev-

decision boundary (Figure 5.1b). This would cause some problems around
x = ±1 and x = 0, and we can do even better. With the application of a non-
linear transformation on the data, we are able to separate the two classes per-
fectly by drawing a straight line in the transformed data (Figure 5.1c), which
corresponds to a non-linear decision boundary in original data space, as seen
in Figure 5.1d.

• Input gate (that can add information to the state)
• Forget gate (than can remove information from the state)
• Output gate (that determines which information from the state is relevant
at this particular iteration)

5.2.1
Batching
It is not computationally possible to optimize the model parameters with re-
spect to all data at once, and moreover, there are some periods of data that we
do not want the model to train on. For instance, a PSG recording may include
long wake periods before, during or after night, during which the electrodes
are recording. These periods are eventually disregarded from analysis, and
thus it does not make sense to include them as training data.
Therefore training batches are introduced. A batch is a sequence of data, on
which the model parameters are being updated iteratively. For training the
proposed model, a sequence length of 4 minutes was chosen (due to consider-
ations discussed shortly), which eventually leads to approximately 100 batches
per night recorded. This is visualized in Figure 5.9. Such scheme implies a total
of approximately 10,000 batches if the training set consists of 100 subjects. In
order to speed up convergence of network parameters, the order of batches is
shufﬂed using random permutation.

5.2.2
Optimization
The goal of optimization process is to update the network parameters in search
of a global minimum in weight-error space, as illustrated in Figure 5.10. This
is done by iteratively calculating the prediction error of the model on batches,
and updating the weights accordingly to minimize this particular deﬁned loss.
Each optimization step implies
1. Predict the model on a single batch of training data
2. Evaluate the averaged loss on the batch
3. Approximate the gradient in weight-error space
4. Update the weights in opposite direction of gradient
The method is known as stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which is a stochas-
tic approximation of the steepest descent method (also known as simply gra-
dient descent), and is deﬁned by the recursive update of weights by
w := w −η ∇Qi(w),
(5.12)
where Qi(w) is the loss function given weights w, η is a step size controlling
the learning rate, and ∇is the gradient operator. The i subscript on Qi denotes
that the gradient is calculated based on a single batch of data (batch number
i), and the fact that the batch is selected randomly makes the approximation
stochastic. SGD is typically used with an adaptive learning rate that initializes
at a certain value and decreases exponentially. This ensures that the learning
eventually converges, and does not end up oscillating around a minimum, but
that the actual minimum is found.
In the proposed system, the initial learning rate η0 was chosen at 0.1, decreasing
with a factor for 0.95 for every 10, 000 iterations. This is realized by multiplying
∇Qi with an exponential decaying term, through deﬁning

Regularization
Assuring that the network does not overﬁt to training data is another impor-
tant aspect. Different methods exist, including (but not limited to)
• Penalize high-valued weights in loss function
• Adjust the ratio of training data and network parameters (either more
data, or less parameters)
• Introduce random dropout on hidden units
Dropout [25] is a method in which the hidden units are randomly omitted from
training iterations. A keep probability α is decided (e.g. α = 0.5), determining
whether or not to omit any hidden unit. This is applied on all hidden units
on all iterations during training. The method can be compared to training sev-
eral weak classiﬁers, as opposed to one strong, and it is thus more robust and
prepared for new data.

5.4
Discussion
5.4.1
Evaluation of Model
Until this point the training and test errors are somewhat arbitrary and difﬁcult
to interpret. It is obvious that in Figure 5.15 there appears to be little difference
between the various models, and that there relative to this is quite a gap in
between the FFNN performance and LSTM performance in Figure 5.16. How-
ever, how does this translate to actual performance? How big a difference does
this make for the actual detection of arousals?
As described in Chapter 3, the output from the network can be interpreted as a
probability of each time bin containing an arousal, and obviously the end val-
idation compares whether or not arousal events are detected by the algorithm.
This is what constitutes the post processing of Chapter 3, and requires tuning
of a threshold parameter θ. Threshold θ indicates how certain we want the
network to be of an arousal occurring, before we indeed classiﬁes it as one.

5.4.2
Class Imbalance
Class imbalance is a problem when dealing with supervised learning when
there is an odd distribution between the possible output classes. In arousal
detection less than 1% of the data are positives (i.e. arousal), which means that
52
==PAGE:75==
the network can achieve a very low error by simply predicting negatives at all
times. Class imbalance is not uncommon, and strategies to overcome include,
but are not limited to:
• Under sampling of majority class, i.e. removal of some sequences not
containing arousals.
• Over sampling of minority class, i.e. duplicating sequences with arousals,
or artiﬁcially generate samples. [26]
• Modify loss function to penalize one type of error more severely than
others.
Initially under sampling the majority class did not perform well. For example,
the under sampling could remove periods of stable sleep with no arousals, but
then the network would not have seen such periods and fail to predict properly.
This introduced a question of how to under sample; what would be the optimal
ratio between positives and negatives? This question would also be raised if
modifying the cost function, and a bias is eventually introduced. Inspired by
Peng [27], an adaptive under sampling technique was implemented, in which
for each batch re-shufﬂing, all the batches that had a perfect accuracy in the
prior iteration, would be left out for the subsequent. This can be interpreted as
adaptively focusing on the samples near the decision boundary.

Conclusion
The proposed method makes use of LSTM networks, that is a state of the art
machine learning tool. LSTM networks were introduced, and the concepts be-
hind training such discussed. The network converged well, and has resulted
in a predictor with auPR at 0.84.

CHAPTER6
Discussion
This concludes report on automatic detection of arousals. Throughout the the-
sis, different discussions and sub conclusions have been presented:
• Chapter 3 introduced the journal paper, which analyzed the results of the
proposed method, and discussed future applications hereof.
• Chapter 4 introduced the signal processing tools used, and discussed
how well artifacts were mitigated and quality of spectral analysis.
• Chapter 5 introduced the machine learning for sequential data analysis,
and compared the performance to that of a regular neural network.
6.1
Conclusion
The goal was to develop an automatic detection of cortical arousals in sleep.
This was done by performing a pre-processing and feature extraction on EEG
and EMG signals, followed by an implementation of a state of the art LSTM
neural network for classiﬁcation.
For pre-processing an FIR ﬁlter was used to remove power line interference,
while a RLS adaptive ﬁlter removed ECG artifacts. Statistical parameters as
RMS and Hjorth parameters were used as features on EEG and EMG, while a
Wavelet Packet decomposition was applied on the EEG, as well, quantifying
the spectral content distribution.
The model was trained and validated on 190 nocturnal PSG recordings from
the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort. On the test set, the network performed with a
recall of 0.82 and precision of 0.76. This is among the best, if not the best,
method in comparison with state of the art.
6.2
Future Work
There are a couple of implementations that could a) improve the feasibility
and applicability of the system, while other improvements could b) improve
the performance of the system. Furthermore, there are some ideas that could
enhance medical research, not mentioned in the journal paper.

6.2.1
Medical Research
Ideas to enhance medical research include:
Multi-class detection. The proposed method is able to detect all three types of
cortical arousals, including SDB, LM and spontaneous arousals. Further-
more, it was shown that recall is indeed higher for SDB and LM arousals.
However, the algorithm itself only provides a binary label whether it is
an arousal or not, and does not investigate whether this was due to a leg
movement, SDB event or a spontaneous arousal. Knowing the trigger, or
lack thereof, of an arousal is very important and could be implemented
rather easily: Respiratory and leg movement traces are available in the
PSG recordings, and it is simply a matter of designing features that de-
scribe the events of interest, which is already done in literature [28], and
re-training the algorithm on the data set in a multi-class environment.
6.2.2
Applicability
Improvements to aid applicability of the proposed system include:
Preprocessing. Preprocessing of a signal is, as discussed in Chapter 4, an en-
tire study in itself. However, automated analysis of PSG recordings are
only justiﬁed if the recordings contain few artifacts. For a system like the
proposed to be applicable clinically, a preprocessing scheme is required.
Coppieters’t Wallant et al. (2016) implemented a module for automatic
data rejection based on noise and artifact detection, and published their
code online. Implementing their work prior to analysis is worth investi-
gating.
Automatic sleep staging. The objective of analysis was to create a fully auto-
matic detection of arousals. Automatic sleep staging algorithms are avail-
able, and most PSG setups have software implemented to do this. Thus,
the proposed model did not implement automatic sleep staging. How-
ever, for the model to work stand-alone an implementation of a sleep
staging classiﬁer is required. Feature space is built with sleep staging in
mind, and it is only a matter of training a network to do so. This is also
likely to improve performance.
6.2.3
Performance
Improvements to aid performance of the proposed system include:
Bidirectional LSTM. Machine learning is a ﬁeld in rapid evolution. Bidirec-
tional LSTM (BLSTM) networks are also available, in which two LSTMs
are trained on the same problem but while one network analyzes the data
from left to right, the other network analyzes the data from right to left.
They are then interconnected, to make decisions combined. This will al-
low the network to make decisions not only based on past data, but also
based on what happens in the future. Human scorers annotate arousals
based on the 30-second epochs, and thereby knowing what happens in
the future, as well.

Wavelet Theory
The following section will introduce Wavelet theory, and make use of some,
in some engineering terms, unfamiliar mathematical notation. L2(R) is the L2
space on real numbers, meaning a set of real-valued vectors on which the L2-
norm is well-deﬁned. In practice all sampled signals are on L2(R), it is just a
matter of mathematical stringency. Furthermore, Dj is deﬁned as an operator
that scales a function on L2(R) by a factor of j, deﬁned by:
Dj : L2(R) →L2(R), (Djf)(x) := 2j/2f(2jx).
(A.1)
Likewise, Tk is deﬁned as an operator that translates a function on L2(R) by k,
deﬁned by:
Tk : L2(R) →L2(R), (Tkf)(x) := f(x −k).
(A.2)
Now, ﬁx ψ ∈L2(R) and let the notation ψj,k denote the scaling and translating
of ψ, i.e.
ψj,k(x) := (DjTkψ)(x) = 2j/2ψ(2jx −k), j, k ∈Z.
(A.3)
Then ψ is called a wavelet if, and only if, the set {ψj,k}j,k∈Z forms an orthonor-
mal basis (ONB) on L2(R). Then, given that ψ is an ONB on L2(R), all members
of L2(R) can be generated through a linear combination of ψ. Let f ∈L2(R),
then

(A.4)
