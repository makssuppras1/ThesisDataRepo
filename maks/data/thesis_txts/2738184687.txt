ABSTRACT 
This thesis explores the establishment of a hierarchy of data sources for multiple physicochemical 
properties for life cycle toxicity characterization by assessing the predictive performance of 
various Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) tools relevant to the USEtox® model. 
The objective is to determine how to optimize predictions through the combination of different 
models.  
The methodology involved identifying key physicochemical properties and the QSAR tools capable 
of predicting them. Experimental data for these properties were extracted, and the predictive 
performance of individual QSAR tools was analyzed and compared. A consensus model was 
developed, combining the predictions of the top three performing QSAR tools to enhance 
predictive accuracy. This strategy was evaluated and applied to substances integrated into the 
USEtox® tool to improve the reliability of the assessments. 
The parameters examined included vapor pressure (VP), water solubility (WS), partitioning 
coefficient between organic carbon and water (KOC), Octanol–water partition coefficient (KOW), 
Atmospheric hydroxylation rate (AOH), Henry law coefficient (HL) and Acid and Base dissociation 
constant (pKa/pKb). Eight QSAR tools were identified for this purpose: EPI Suite™, OPERA, 
ACD/Labs, IFSQSAR, QSAR Toolbox, VEGA, TEST, and QSARins. 
Results demonstrated that OPERA emerged as the top performer across all studied parameters 
except KOC, with root mean square error (RMSE) values ranging from 0.24 to 1.04. When 
differentiating based on OPERA’s applicability domain (AD), OPERA remained the top performer 
across all parameters except KOC within AD, with RMSE values ranging from 0.09 to 0.70, whereas 
outside AD the consensus model outperforms OPERA, with RMSE values varying from 0.68 to 1.96. 
One finding of this study that should be highlighted is that the predictive performance of all 
individual QSAR tools for pKa, both acid and base, was very low, indicating that further research 
should be conducted. For the other parameters, the high correlation achieved suggested that the 
proposed strategy is valuable, as the consensus model demonstrated superior predictive 
performance outside OPERA’s AD. 
The application of this strategy to the USEtox® tool dataset has contributed to the update of the 
USEtox® model by substantially increasing the number of chemical characterization factors by a 
factor 2 for freshwater ecotoxicity, by a factor 5 for human toxicity and adding 366 new factors 
for terrestrial ecotoxicity. These enhancements improve the model’s reliability by reducing data 
uncertainty and filling data gaps, thereby providing more accurate support for decision-making 
regarding the environmental impact of products and services.

1.  INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background 
The latter half of the 20th century the chemical sector has grown significantly, driven substantial 
investments in research and development, alongside a surge in the variety of products. This era 
has been characterized by a consistent introduction of novel innovations and a vast array of 
chemical products. Chemical substances have become fundamental to numerous industrial 
activities and are found in a huge number of everyday products such building materials, clothing, 
electronic devices, cleaners, and cosmetics. It is well known that the industry plays an important 
role in contributing to economic progress and improvement of living standards. Nonetheless, 
there is increasing concern regarding the environmental and health ramifications of the 
widespread use of man-made chemicals. To date, the commercial market is saturated with 
millions of distinct chemical substances, with the United States listing approximately 100,000 of 
these chemicals commercially (Peter P. Egeghy, 2012).  
Evaluating the potential impact of chemicals on humans and the environment, along with 
quantifying exposure in different scenarios, depends significantly on the inherent properties and 
characteristics of these substances. In certain instances, these compounds are categorized based 
on their risk of exposure, independent of the quantities discharged into the environment. Such 
classifications take into account attributes like durability in the environment, the tendency to 
accumulate in biological organisms, and the capacity for widespread dispersal through the air. 
These attributes are key considerations for the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) when 
evaluating and approving new chemicals for market entry. Similarly, these considerations are vital 
for carrying out exposure assessments as required by the European Union's REACH (Registration, 
Evaluation, Authorization, and Restriction of Chemicals) regulations (Peter P. Egeghy, 2012). 
Therefore, it is crucial to utilize a standardized and uniform protocol for the evaluation of 
exposure risk and toxicological effects. Such assessment guidelines could provide a replicable and 
reliable assessment of effects that arise from chemical emissions and contact with chemicals in 
different product compositions, substances, or items throughout their entire life span. Focus is 
required on providing guidance for universally relevant indicators that are oriented towards the 
entire life cycle, and also identification of the most effective methods for the quantitative analysis 
of human health and environmental toxicity risks. To address this need, the Life Cycle Initiative, 
under the auspices of the UN Environment Program, has adopted the USEtox® model, which 
represents a consensus-based scientific tool.  
USEtox® has been designed to assess the impacts on human health and the environment due to 
chemicals, providing as its primary output a database, which constitutes a compilation of 
characterization factors that include considerations of fate, exposure, and effects (Fantke P. C., 
2021). USEtox assesses the environmental fate of chemicals by solving multi-media mass balances 
assuming steady-state conditions. It can describe the behavior of both organic and inorganic 
chemicals using various substance-specific information required to calculate characterization 
factors. Table 1 provides an overview over all USEtox input parameters and whether they are 
mandatory, as some of the non-required parameters could be derived internally using integrated 
calculations or assumptions (Fantke P. , 2015).

The development of the USEtox® databases aims to achieve data consistency, uphold a standard 
quality for the data included and cover a comprehensive range of chemicals for which 
characterization factors are computable. The tool's developers prioritize experimental data over 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
predicted data when analyzing the physicochemical properties of substances. As a result, there 
has been a focused effort to source and compile data from existing, critically evaluated databases. 
Numerous publicly accessible and proprietary databases are particularly valuable as they 
consolidate scientifically verified information, having carefully reviewed and selected data from a 
wide range of literature sources to ensure that the information included in the databases is 
accurate and reliable. However, challenges emerge due to the vast number of substances in 
USEtox® databases and the numerous mandatory input parameters, as experimental data are not 
available for all required information (Fantke P., 2018). 
This lack of experimentally measured physicochemical properties is a widespread issue in the field 
for a significant number of chemicals, mainly due to constraints related to time, financial 
resources, and ethical concerns regarding the use of animals. Furthermore, experimentally 
derived data are incomplete or missing for most chemicals used in commerce, a situation that is 
even more pronounced for premanufactured chemicals or chemicals in design. Regulatory bodies 
have therefore investigated alternative approaches to estimate anticipated values to bridge data 
deficiencies (data gap filling). Therefore, for an extensive number of chemicals, faster and cost-
effective in silico methods like Quantitative Structure-Activity/Property Relationships 
(QSAR/QSPR) modeling are utilized (Kamel Mansouri, 2018). 
1.2 QSAR modeling theoretical background 
The QSAR technique is directly related to the molecular structure of the chemicals and is grounded 
in the principle that structurally similar compounds share similar properties and biological 
activities. QSAR/QSPR approaches have extensively modeled crucial chemical properties for 
toxicological studies, risk assessment, and exposure studies (such as bioavailability, permeability, 
absorption, transport, persistence). These models are derived from existing experimental data. 
Key parameters include the octanol–water partition coefficient, water solubility, melting point, 
bio-concentration factor, and biodegradability (Kamel Mansouri, 2018). 
Computational techniques require a structured representation of a molecule's structure. Various 
methods characterize chemical compounds, including molecular formulas, structural depictions, 
and different naming conventions such as generic, systematic, common, and trade names. The 
most commonly used method is the chemical name, for which a uniform naming system has been 
introduced by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry, called the IUPAC system. 
However, a disadvantage of this method is that for a given compound, there often are several 
names that are acceptable by IUPAC rules (Pharmacopoeia, 2011).  
Another prevalent method commonly used for regulatory identification of chemicals is the CAS 
Registry Number (CAS RN). This unique numerical identifier, which can be as long as 10 digits, 
corresponds to a single substance, so that it facilitates unambiguous referencing (CAS, 2024). 
Although this is intended, several issues have been highlighted regarding CAS Registry Numbers. 
For example, there might be different indexing policies, change of CAS numbers or multiple 
numbers existing for one chemical, different stereoisomers of a substance may also have different 
CAS numbers while the same substance with unspecified stereochemistry has its own number 
(Ellegaard & Wallin, 2013). 
Structural identifiers are also utilized to uniquely identify chemicals. They are used to describe the 
molecular structure in a more detailed and complex manner than the previous methods. The

molecular structures as a two-dimensional representation, which involves detailing the 
connections between atoms, including the types of atoms and the bonds that link them. As a 
result, a single molecular structure might be depicted by various SMILES strings, particularly for 
molecules that are large or complex, with numerous atoms, bonds, branches, and aromatic rings. 
In computational settings, any correct SMILES string is sufficient for the purposes of interaction 
(EPA U. , 2012).  This includes any valid SMILES notation(s) associated with it, thereby connecting

Although these characterizations are crucial for systems that manage chemical data, they 
frequently require transformation into numerical formats to be compatible with computational 
analysis. These numerical descriptors can vary widely, and they can encapsulate the chemical 
properties that dictate a molecule's activity. Created through appropriate algorithms, these 
descriptors serve as input variables for constructing models that can predict molecular behavior 
(Guha, 2012). These descriptors can be of various types, including physicochemical (such as 
hydrophobicity, steric factors, or electronic properties), structural (reflecting the presence of 
certain substructures), topological, electronic (derived from molecular orbital theories), 
geometric (related to calculations of molecular surface areas), or they may be simple binary 
indicators. For the development of any Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) 
model, the selection of descriptors and their capacity to capture molecular structural attributes 
that are relevant to the predicted response are key to the model's accuracy. The process by which 
a molecular structure is converted into a predicted response within a QSAR model involves 
computing multiple numerical descriptors, a procedure that is graphically depicted in Figure 2.

The field of QSAR modeling evolves rapidly, with increasing methodological diversity. Datasets are 
becoming more structurally diverse, numerous, and complex, rendering preliminary 
physicochemical descriptors insufficient for correlating molecular structure with biological 
activity. QSAR models use various statistical methods, broadly categorized into regression-based 
and classification-based modeling. Regression-based models, employing continuous variables, 
include techniques like Multiple Linear Regression (MLR), Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression, 
Principal Component Regression Analysis (PCRA), and Ridge Regression. Classification-based 
models, using discrete data for binary outcomes, include Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), 
Logistic Regression, and Cluster Analysis. Additionally, machine learning algorithms, driven by 
artificial intelligence, have become prevalent in contemporary research, surpassing traditional 
methods (Roy K. C., 2023). 
Overall, caution is advisable, as not all QSAR models ensure transparency regarding data, 
algorithms, and computational processes, indicating that not every QSAR model is equally 
reliable. A common reliability issue arises from the lack of a determined confidence in predictions 
and a poorly defined applicability domain. The applicability domain outlines the model's 
boundaries and indicates if a model can be applied to a query compound. However, many models 
either have an ill-defined applicability domain or lack one entirely, which compromises their 
reliability (Gómez-Jiménez, et al., 2018). Other limitations associated with QSAR models include 
the low predictability of mechanistically complex endpoints, contradictory predictions from 
different QSAR models, and the varying performance of QSAR techniques depending on the 
chemical set under investigation (Shah & Jain, 2022).  
A method proposed in the scientific literature in order to mitigate the possibility of inaccurate 
predictions of a single QSAR model is by considering predictions from multiple models. This 
practice is grounded in the assumption that QSARs/QSPRs utilizing different algorithms may 
account for, or assign varying weights to, distinct molecular structures or descriptors, have 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
different applicability domains and trained on different datasets. Therefore, when multiple 
predictions from QSAR models are available for a single property, it is advisable to compute the 
mean (e.g., geometric, arithmetic, harmonic, or least-squares mean) or other central-tendency 
estimates, commonly referred to as the "consensus value." According to the findings of (Li Li, 
2022), it was observed that combining the two highest-performing models yielded superior 
results for predicting octanol water partition coefficient, compared to using single models or 
aggregating multiple QSARs. This example illustrates the advantageous use of consensus values 
in chemical assessments, particularly beneficial for high-throughput screening and prioritizing 
chemicals with limited data, even though the adoption of consensus values from in silico 
predictions remains constrained to date (Li Li, 2022). 
1.3 Scope of thesis 
The scope of this study is to evaluate the predictive ability and the performance of different QSAR 
models and investigate how to combine models to optimize predictions for physicochemical 
properties pertinent to USEtox® 2.0 tool. The specific objectives include: 
• 
Determine the key physicochemical properties that are integral to the USEtox® model. 
• 
Identify available QSAR tools and models capable of predicting each property. 
• 
Compile experimental datasets for the identified properties. 
• 
Analyze and compare the performance of different QSAR tools in predicting the 
properties individually. 
• 
Propose and evaluate a robust strategy for combining multiple QSAR tools to enhance 
predictive accuracy. 
• 
Apply this strategy to the substances integrated in USEtox® tool in order to update the 
repository and upscale the chemical coverage, to meet the required data for USEtox 3.0. 
This research will explore the intersection of various QSAR methodologies to refine the accuracy 
of physicochemical property predictions, thereby contributing to more reliable USEtox® 
assessments.

2. METHODOLOGY 
2.1 Selection of USEtox input parameters for modelling chemical fate  
For this thesis, the emphasis has been placed on the physicochemical properties datasets required 
by USEtox® 2.0, in particular the ones that are mandatory for the chemical fatec. After assessing 
the required physicochemical properties as inputs in USEtox® 2.0 demonstrated in Table 1, the 
publicly available experimental dataset, as well as the time constraint of a master’s thesis 
completion, the properties identified to be assessed include Vapor Pressure (VP), Water Solubility 
(WS), Partitioning coefficient between organic carbon and water (KOC), Octanol–water partition 
coefficient (KOW), Atmospheric hydroxylation rate (AOH), Henry law coefficient (HL) and Acid and 
Base dissociation constant (pKa/pKb). Information regarding each physicochemical property 
follows below.  
a) Vapor Pressure 
The vapor pressure of a substance refers to the pressure at a certain temperature where the vapor 
is in equilibrium with its liquid or solid state. It indicates how strongly a compound's molecules 
adhere to each other; compounds with strong intermolecular bonds exhibit low vapor pressure 
(indicating a lower tendency to transition to the vapor phase), whereas those with weak 
intermolecular bonds have high vapor pressure. Various models have been developed to predict 
vapor pressure (Voutsas, 2007).  
b) Water Solubility 
Water solubility plays a crucial role in determining a chemical's behavior and movement in the 
environment, as well as its potential toxicity. Among the various factors influencing soil/air 
partitioning, water solubility stands out as one of the most significant. It is defined as the 
maximum concentration of a chemical that can dissolve in pure water at a given temperature. 
When this concentration is exceeded, two phases will coexist if the organic chemical is solid or 
liquid at the system temperature: a saturated aqueous solution and a separate solid or liquid 
organic phase. Extensive literature exists on estimating water solubility, particularly through 
regression-derived equations and fragment constant approaches (Voutsas, 2007). 
c) Partitioning coefficient between organic carbon and water 
The partitioning coefficient between organic carbon and water or soil organic carbon–water 
partition coefficient (KOC) is used to describe how an organic chemical distributes itself between 
the soil and the aqueous phases. KOC is defined as the ratio of the chemical’s concentration in a 
state of sorption (i.e., adhered to soil particles) and the solution phase. Thus, for a given chemical, 
a smaller KOC value indicates a higher concentration in the solution. Chemicals with a low KOC value 
are more likely to leach into groundwater compared to those with a high KOC value. Sorption for a 
given chemical is greater in soils with a higher organic matter content, resulting in slower mobility 
in such soils compared to those with lower organic matter content. Since experimental KOC data 
are not available for many chemicals, many correlations with other properties, such as the 
octanol–water partition coefficient or water solubility, have been proposed (Voutsas, 2007).  
d) Octanol–water partition coefficient 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
The octanol-water partition coefficient (Kow) is a physical property used extensively to describe a 
chemical's lipophilic or hydrophobic properties, thus making it a very valuable parameter with 
numerous environmental applications. Kow, is defined as the ratio of a chemical's concentration 
in the octanol-phase to its concentration in the aqueous phase of a two-phase system at 
equilibrium. The temperature is an important variable and should be considered for the 
determination of Kow values, by default it is considered at 25°C. (Voutsas, 2007).  
e) Atmospheric hydroxylation rate 
Atmospheric hydroxylation rate (kOH) is the rate constant for the atmospheric, gas-phase reaction 
between photochemically produced hydroxyl radicals and organic chemicals. The rate constants 
are used to calculate atmospheric half-lives for organic compounds based upon average 
atmospheric concentrations of hydroxyl radicals (EPA U. , 2012). 
In USEtox® 2.0, degradation rates in air (kdegA in 1/s) are required for the calculations, which are 
derived from the multiplication of the kOH with the [OH] (the hydroxyl radical concentration in 
units of molecules (or radicals) per cm3). The default [OH] is set at 1.5 × 106 molecules 
(radicals)/cm3 per 12h of daylight. Ultimately, the half-life in the troposphere is calculated as: 
t½ = 0.693/kOH[OH] 
where kOH is the hydroxyl radical rate constant in units of cm3/molecule-sec and [OH] is the 
hydroxyl radical concentration in units of molecules (or radicals) per cm3 (Fantke P., 2018).   
f) Henry law constant 
Henry's law is a gas law that states that when a liquid and gas are in contact, the weight of 
dissolved gas in a liquid is directly proportional to its partial pressure above the liquid. The 
proportionality factor is called Henry's law constant (H) and is dependent on the solute–solvent 
pair, temperature and pressure. Henry’s law constant is a measure of a chemical’s volatility from 
a solvent - the larger a compound’s H value, the more volatile it is, and it is also easier to be 
transferred from the aqueous phase into air (Voutsas, 2007).

IFSQSAR is a publicly available tool that can be used to apply QSARs developed by Trevor N.

8. QSARins 
QSARINS-Chem standalone version is a multiplatform tool, freely downloadable, for the in silico 
profiling of multiple properties and activities of organic chemicals developed by the QSAR 
Research Unit in Environmental Chemistry and Ecotoxicology of University of Insubria (Gramatica, 
2014). The QSARINS-Chem standalone version is an informative and solid tool, which is useful to 
support the assessment of the potential hazard and risks related to organic chemicals and is 
dedicated to users which are interested in the application of QSARs to generate reliable 
predictions. 
An important consideration when using different QSAR tools is that they do not all uniformly 
accept or process various SMILES notations consistently. This inconsistency arises because 
multiple SMILES strings can represent the same molecular structure. Although in computational 
settings, any correct SMILES string should theoretically suffice for interaction purposes, this is not 
always the case with all QSAR tools. To ensure compatibility and accuracy, the standardization or 
canonicalization of SMILES notations is often necessary before their use. Canonicalization, for 
instance, involves converting different valid SMILES strings of the same molecule into a single, 
unique SMILES string, thereby ensuring a consistent representation across datasets and tools. 
Tools like RDKit and Open Babel can perform such tasks effectively. Without these preprocessing 
steps, several SMILES may be rejected, leading to the exclusion of many chemicals from analyses 
(O'Boyle, 2011). In this case study, no manual pre-processing or canonicalization of SMILES 
notations was performed, thus resulting to the omission of many substances due to lack of 
uniform SMILES notation acceptance across the tools.

2.4 Comparison of the predictive capabilities of different QSAR models

present study adopts a focused approach centered on the application of statistical modeling 
techniques. These techniques are generally divided into two primary groups: regression-based 
modeling and classification-based modeling. The type of data produced by the quantitative 
structure-activity relationships (QSARs) in this study, which is continuous by nature, makes

widespread application. LLS regression works by comparing actual and predicted data points to 
calculate the best-fitting regression lines, thus providing a solid foundation for the validation 
process of the models being examined.

2.5 Strategy  
The final objective of this thesis is to develop a robust and effective strategy for integrating 
multiple QSAR models to enhance the accuracy and reliability of predictive performance. The 
strategic approach outlined in this research is designed to identify a dependable and easily 
replicable methodology for estimating values by leveraging a consensus of various QSAR models 
and tools for each specific property. The key steps of this process are schematically presented in 
Figure 4. 
 
Figure 4. Decision tree for strategic combination of QSAR predictions. 
To optimize the predictive accuracy for a specific property using a QSAR tool, the initial step 
involves identifying all available models within the tool relevant to that property. If multiple 
models are identified, an average value is calculated from these models to establish a consensus 
value; if only one model is present, its prediction is used directly. The next step is to compare the 
predictive performance of each individual QSAR tool utilizing the specified equations and 
methodologies. When more than three QSAR tools are available for the property, the three 
highest-performing tools are selected and combined to form a consensus. The consensus is 
calculated as the arithmetic mean value on the logarithmic scale, also known as the geometric 
mean. Conversely, if fewer than three QSAR tools are available, all existing tools are combined to 
calculate the consensus.  
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
The scope of the thesis includes the evaluation of the suggested strategy. To facilitate this 
comparison, the consensus approach will be applied to the top two and top four performing 
models, where data allows. This calculation and subsequent presentation will enable a detailed 
analysis and aid in forming conclusive insights. This comparative analysis will not only test the 
efficacy of the consensus method but will also provide a clearer understanding of the predictive 
power and reliability of the selected QSAR models within the scope of the study.  
Part of the evaluation of the strategy will be the coverage percentages of the top one, two, three, 
and four performers. This approach is based on the assumption that combining the top three 
models will provide the most comprehensive coverage due to the inherent variability in the 
substances that different tools can accept and predict. Essentially, the "coverage percentage" 
measures how many of the SMILES notations from the original experimental dataset are 
represented by one or more of the QSAR tools under consideration. 
Lastly, the strategy proposed is applied to the dataset of the USEtox® tool, meaning the list of 
chemicals incorporated in the backend repository according to which the existing version of the 
model calculates the characterization factors for human toxicity and ecotoxicity, based on 
modelling of environmental fate, exposure, and effect parameters for the substances.

4. DISCUSSION 
The study's results reveal several key insights into the modelling performance of various QSAR 
tools used to predict selected physicochemical properties relevant to the USEtox® 2.0 model. In 
this section, a deeper examination of the predictive performance of these QSAR models and their 
consensus will be conducted. Strengths and weaknesses of these tools will be highlighted, and the 
advantages and disadvantages of combining different QSAR tools will be identified. Additionally, 
potential avenues for improving model performance will be discussed. The implications of these 
findings for future research and practical applications will also be considered. 
USEtox® increase in data availability 
This work has significantly enhanced data availability in the USEtox® model. Specifically, it has 
doubled the number of chemical characterization factors in the freshwater ecotoxicity module 
and increased them nearly fivefold for human toxicity. Additionally, it has enabled the calculation 
of 366 new factors for terrestrial ecotoxicity. Consequently, the USEtox® model can now be 
updated to USEtox® 3.0. This update improves the reliability of model outputs by reducing data 
uncertainty and filling data gaps, thereby allowing the tool to more accurately support decision-
making on products and services.

A crucial aspect of this study is the evaluation of the proposed strategy, which assumes that 
combining the top three performing tools, when available, will yield better predictive 
performance and coverage of the overall dataset. In the Results section the metrics for all 
available individual models and the consensus models of the top two, three, and four performers

for each property were presented. Generally, the consensus of the top two performers

dataset into PFAS and non-PFAS substances showed that for non-PFAS substances, the consensus

coefficient, a physicochemical property not examined in this study, the combined use of the three 
best performing models provides the top prediction performance.

universally valid.

Limitations and further research 
A notable challenge with coverage allegations stems from the limitations of SMILES notations. 
These notations often present multiple string representations for the same molecular structure, 
particularly for complex compounds. While it's assumed that any accurate SMILES string should 
serve the purpose of interaction adequately, practical application reveals otherwise. Different 
tools may fail to accept all potential twin SMILES strings or misinterpret them, leading to 
discrepancies in data coverage. This sheds light on why different tools may fail to cover certain 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
substances and underscores the necessity for a more standardized and uniform approach to 
indicating the molecular structure of compounds through 2D notation. A common strategy to 
address these challenges involves meticulous data curation and the standardization of chemical 
structures prior to advancing into any model development or evaluation (Mansouri, Grulke, 
Judson, & Williams, 2018). It is recommended that for further research, pre-processing and 
canonicalization of all SMILES notations should be conducted. This will ensure that more 
substances are accepted by all the QSAR tools, thereby minimizing the omission of SMILES 
notations. 
The most prominent limitation of this study is the potential overlap between the dataset used 
and the training sets of the evaluated models. This overlap is unknown, which may lead to a 
seemingly favorable performance for some models, depending on the extent of the overlap. As a 
future improvement, the dataset used to assess each model's performance should be explicitly 
identified as not being part of any of the models' training sets, a criterion that should apply to all 
models under consideration. However, a challenge in this regard is that not all QSAR tools provide 
an indication or documentation of their training sets, which was why this aspect was not further 
investigated in this case study. Nevertheless, OPERA and EPI SUITE™, the leading performers 
according to the findings, do provide information about their training and testing datasets, so 
these two models could be further investigated. 
One more important concern revolves around the reliability of experimental data sourced from 
databases, which are typically regarded as trustworthy but may not always be so. In this case 
study, the acid and base dissociation constant experimental data sourced from two different 
platforms, DataWarrior and DrugBank, serves as an illustration of how such data might be 
inaccurate, unreliable, or inadequately categorized, as the results do not demonstrate a linear 
behavior. This is a recurrent issue discussed in the literature, as it has been observed that certain 
data points labeled as experimental may, in fact, represent values derived from QSAR model 
predictions (Trophsa, 2023). 
Another challenge is the lack of applicability domain indicators for tools other than OPERA and 
VEGA. In the scope of this study, it would be of great importance to be able to assess predictions 
for substances that fall outside the scope of all the utilized tools in the consensus models. Such 
capability would provide a more comprehensive quantification of how the consensus model 
outperforms the individual tools. 
As part of further research, it is recommended to explore datasets of other chemical classes, 
specifically substances that are structurally dissimilar to the training sets and therefore more 
challenging to predict. In this case study, PFAS was studied, and a clear pattern emerged in the 
case of KOC. Such investigations would provide a better understanding of how well different QSAR 
tools can predict unknown substances, thereby improving our insight into their predictability.

5. CONCLUSION 
This study delves into the predictive performance of various QSAR tools for determining 
physicochemical properties relevant to the USEtox® 2.0 model, revealing key insights into their 
individual and combined effectiveness. Overall, the results and findings discussed above have 
broader implications for environmental risk assessment, regulatory toxicology, and the 
development of computational models. 
QSAR modeling continues to advance, particularly with AI and machine learning algorithms like 
OPERA, which excel when trained on extensive datasets. However, these models have limitations, 
especially with data outside their applicability domain (AD), highlighting the need for conventional 
methods and mechanistic models, as diverse modeling techniques are necessary to capture the 
full spectrum of chemical behavior and enhance predictive accuracy. The reliability of QSAR 
models depends on factors such as AD and confidence intervals, which many tools currently lack. 
Expanding AD features to all QSAR tools would enhance the ability to assess and trust predictions, 
especially for novel compounds. 
This study demonstrates the continued advancement of QSAR modeling in predicting key 
physicochemical properties, highlighting both its current strengths and potential areas for 
optimization. The growing attention to artificial intelligence, particularly machine learning 
algorithms, is well justified. In this case study, OPERA's consistent performance underscores the 
predictive performance of machine learning approaches trained on extensive datasets. However, 
it is crucial to recognize the limitations of these models, particularly concerning the training set. 
Data falling outside the applicability domain, and therefore outside the training set, cannot be 
accurately predicted. This limitation underscores the importance of conventional methods and 
mechanistic models, as diverse modeling techniques are necessary to capture the full spectrum 
of chemical behavior and enhance predictive accuracy. 
Consensus models often outperform individual machine learning models, particularly outside 
their AD, underscoring the potential of combining multiple QSAR tools to improve predictive 
accuracy and robustness. Trustworthy experimental data is critical for training and validating 
machine learning models. High-quality, well-curated datasets are essential to support the 
development of accurate and widely applicable QSAR models. Additionally, standardizing SMILES 
notation is necessary to address inconsistencies in chemical structure representations that 
undermine QSAR model reliability and applicability. Improving SMILES standardization and data 
curation practices will enhance the accuracy, reproducibility, and utility of predictive modeling. 
The implementation of QSAR models has extensive applications across various fields, including 
pharmaceuticals, regulatory agencies, and environmental sciences. They aid in designing safer 
drugs and assessing chemical hazards, streamlining the evaluation of chemicals for regulatory 
action. Consensus modeling, in particular, enhances the robustness of chemical risk assessments 
by mitigating uncertainties and biases associated with individual models. 
Future research should prioritize several critical areas for advancement. Establishing protocols for 
standardizing chemical structure representations is essential to enhance data consistency and 
reliability. Developing comprehensive AD indicators applicable across all QSAR tools will improve 
model reliability. Leveraging diverse and high-quality data sources is crucial for training more 
robust QSAR models capable of handling a wide range of chemical properties and scenarios. 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
Ensuring transparency in model development and validation against independent datasets will 
bolster confidence in QSAR predictions. Additionally, assessing new datasets, especially those 
with organic chemicals unrelated to the training set, will validate and generalize the proposed 
strategies. 
Addressing these key areas will improve the reliability, accuracy, and applicability of QSAR models 
in various applications, from environmental risk assessment to drug discovery. This study 
highlights the strengths and limitations of current QSAR tools and underscores the value of 
combining multiple models to enhance predictive performance. Addressing challenges related to 
data standardization and applicability domains is crucial for advancing QSAR modeling. These 
improvements will support better risk assessments and regulatory decisions for chemical 
substances, benefiting both the scientific community and regulatory efforts.
