Abstract
In recent years the use of glass in buildings has increased signiﬁcantly as a result of
the high demand for transparency in modern architecture. There exists a need for
expansion of knowledge within this ﬁeld in order to meet the demands.
This thesis addresses the topic of laminated glass, more speciﬁcally the strength of
a SentryGlas®-glass laminate. Laminated glass is of great importance when it comes
to safety as the interlayer both keeps the glass splinters in place and adds ductility
to the element in case of fracture. The properties and bond mechanisms of a lami-
nated element is of high importance for the post-breakage behaviour. This research
contains pure tension and shear experiments on a SentryGlas® laminated sample as
well as combined tension and shear experiments. Moreover, the average experiments
has been modelled using Abaqus FEA.
Values for the SentryGlas® stiﬀness have been found and compared to literature. The
stiﬀness given in the literature is of varying magnitude and thereby supports the ﬁnd-
ings of this research, that the material behaviour, and the stiﬀness in particular, is
highly dependent on time, temperature and strain rate. Furthermore, the laminated
bond is very dependent on the lamination to have been carried out properly. Small
areas of imperfect lamination can cause debonding of the entire laminated surface at
low stress. Finally it was found that the strength of the laminated bond is determined
by chemical properties of the bond when exposed to pure tension, whereas the glass
strength is the limiting factor in shear.

Preface
Light and transparency; two desired elements in today’s construction industry. Glass
plays a major role in the attempt to satisfy this demand, and is constantly the cause
of challenges for the engineer who has to ensure both strength, stability and safety.

experimental and a numerical study of the bond when exposed to tension and shear

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Many people react to the concept of glass beams ﬁrst with scepticism and then follows a
degree of fascination. Glass is presumed to be very fragile, which it is indeed, yet when
laminated with a ductile interlayer, composite action is obtained, and glass beams are
no longer unrealistic ideas but very realistic structural elements.
Over the last decades the use of glass in the construction industry has become more
widespread as a result of the constant increasing knowledge about the material. The
increasing knowledge inspires to more demanding uses which in return pushes for
the knowledge to be taken even further. A simple internet search for modern iconic
buildings shows how glass and irregular shapes are in high demand and has been
in recent years.
Consider e.g.

Headquarters in Abu Dhabi or The City of Arts and Sciences in Valencia, all build
within the last 20 years, Figure 1.1.

1.1
State of the art
In decades researchers have explored many aspects of glass. Though, the objectives
of the research has changed over the years concurrently with the use of glass. Within
today’s architectural society there exists a desire to create lighter and more transpar-
ent structures and for that reason the use of glass in the building industry has gone
from glazing via structural glazing to structural glass. The desire to use glass as a load
carrying material causes measures to be taken to increase the ductility and safety of
structural glass. Many research groups or institutions are established all over the world
in order to investigate within those overall topics. In The European Research Network
on Structural Glass (COST Action TU0905) many of the European researchers within
the ﬁeld are assembled, and technology is developed and shared. The network is di-
vided into Working Groups which addresses diﬀerent aspects of the ﬁeld.
The investigation in the network have resulted in knowledge about many aspects of
glass in construction, for instance, the eﬀects of glass type, reinforcement percentage
and beam size on the structural response of SentryGlas® (SG) laminated reinforced
glass beams (Louter et al., 2012). In the article by Louter et al. (2012) it was con-
cluded that the glass type has great inﬂuence on the initial failure strength as well
as the post-breakage strength and the ductility, due to the diﬀerent fracture patterns
of annealed, heat strengthen and fully tempered glass. With regards to the reinforce-
ment percentage it was found that an increase in the amount of reinforcement caused
the post-breakage strength and stiﬀness to increase. Thus, for laminated glass beams
adding reinforcement could be a good idea which does not compromises notably with
the transparency or aesthetic. Though, still many factors concerning laminated glass
beams and glass in structural applications in general exists and is investigated many
places outside of the COST Action. For instance, at the University of Cambridge in
the Glass & Façade Technology research group where the post fracture performance
of laminated glass is to be investigated by determination of the viscoelastic-plastic

1.2
The present thesis
The present thesis has the objective to take a step back and investigate the fracture
strength an mechanisms of the SG-glass bond in order to gain knowledge about the
grade of adhesion. The bond is to be investigated under pure tension and pure shear
load and also combined tension and shear. The goal is to establish information on the
fracture surface which in the long term could be used to determine the mechanics in
the delamination between glass and SG.
The approach for the research consists of an experimental study for which the results
afterwards are compared to similar results in the ﬁeld.
Then numeric calculations
of the experiments are done in order to further investigate the mechanisms of the
bond. The experimental procedure presented in this study has only been tried a few
times before, with indeterminate results, see Appendix B. Thus, in order to establish
knowledge about the bond strength and the dependency of load type, it is necessary
to expose the interface to loading which causes the fracture to be pure, that is no
combined fracture modes.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
Failure of a structural element is almost always undesirable and therefore it is impor-
tant for a structural engineer to understand the mechanics of various failure modes.
Though, even if the causes and mechanics of failure as well as the material behaviour
are known, it is impossible to completely prevent failure. Within the ﬁeld of structural
glass, safety glass is used to decrease the risk of failure and limit the danger in case of
failure. Safety glass can be made both by heat treatments of glass and by lamination
of annealed or heat strengthened glass.
In this chapter the reader will ﬁnd an introduction to glass lamination, SentryGlas® as
an interlayer material, fracture mechanics and a more detailed description of cohesive
elements (CEs) and cohesive interactions (CIs) which are used to model delamination
in Abaqus and are used in the chapter about numeric models, Chapter 5.
2.1
Introduction to Glass Lamination
The glass lamination process is of heigh importance in structural applications. Heat
treatment of glass increases the structural capacity and impact resistance, but the mate-
rial is still brittle and therefore not optimal for use as a structural element, (Haldimann
et al., 2008). When two or more glass panes are laminated together with a plastic in-
terlayer the post-breakage behaviour is increased as the glass fragments sticks to the
interlayer after breakage and keeps some amount of structural capacity. The safety of
laminated glass is therefore many times greater than that of a single layer of tempered
glass. Laminated glass elements can be composed of both ﬂoat glass and tempered
glass. With regard to the safety and post-breakage behaviour, laminated ﬂoat glass is
preferred due to the fracture pattern of ﬂoat glass compared to the fracture pattern
of tempered glass. As seen in Figure 2.1 ﬂoat glass breaks in large pieces whereas
fully tempered glass shatters in many small pieces. For the scope of this thesis only
laminated ﬂoat glass is considered.
The manufacturing process of laminated glass is more or less similar for all glass
laminates, but time, temperature and pressure varies dependent on the plastic foil or
resin used for the laminate. Descriptions of the lamination process can be found at
many manufacturer’s webpages, e.g. (PFG, 2014; EuroGlas, 2014), and if interested
the reader is also referred to (GANA, 2014) which contains a video presentation of
the process. First part of the manufacturing is the cleaning of the glass panes. It is
important that they are cleaned thoroughly as the surface must be free of dust and
dirt for the laminate to adhere properly to the glass. Next, the glass panes and the

2.2

2.3
Introduction to Fracture Mechanics
In structural engineering two types of fracture exist: ductile and brittle fracture. The
classiﬁcation is based on the materials ability to experience plastic deformation. For
both fracture types defects play a big role in the mechanisms of failure. The mechanism
can be said to consist of two steps - the crack formation and the crack propagation.
Cracks in brittle materials (such as glass) are said to be unstable as they will propa-
gate extremely rapidly and without no visible deformation (Callister, 2007). This is
highly undesirable and ductile fracture, which contain large plastic deformations in
the crack vicinity and therefore propagates slowly and with visible deformations, is
therefore preferable. In Figure 2.4 the diﬀerence between brittle and ductile fracture
is illustrated. The absence of plastic deformation in the brittle fracture is clear.
For structural glass the typical way of adding ductility to the post-failure behaviour
is by laminating thin glass sheets with a relatively soft interlayer. If the element is
exposed to a damaging hit the laminate is able to absorb part of the impact and dis-
tribute the stresses to the remaining glass sheets and hopefully limit the fracture to one
glass. Furthermore the laminate has the ability to keep the glass fragments together,
thus avoiding dangerous falling pieces.

Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics
The topic of fracture has been studied and described many times over the last 100
years, see e.g. (Callister, 2007; Roylance, 2012; Gdoutos, 2005; Recho, 2012), since C.
E. Inglis investigated stress concentrations at the end of an elliptical hole (De With,
2006) and A. A. Griﬃth realised that fracture in glass occurred at stress levels much
lower than theory suggest (Sun and Jin, 2012). According to the book by Sun and
Jin (2012) two approaches exists to establish a fracture criteria within the Linear
Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM): the crack tip stress ﬁeld (a local) approach and
the energy balance (a global) approach. In the book it is described how the stresses
near the crack tip vary with r−1/2 where r is the distance to the crack tip. From this
it is seen that the stresses at the crack tip goes to inﬁnity as r approaches zero. Thus,
a singular point exist at the crack tip which is inconsistent with the classical strength
of material failure criteria 4. In fracture mechanics the theoretical stress singularity at
the crack tip is accepted, but the problem is avoided as the stresses are kept out of
the calculations, (Sun and Jin, 2012).
Three basic failure modes exists and all failures can be described as a superposition of
these three. In Figure 2.5 the three modes are depicted. Figure 2.5(a) illustrates the

Cohesive Zone Model
In many ways it has been tried to remove the stress singularity at the crack tip, amongst
other methods (not mentioned in this report) Irwin’s introduction of a plastic zone.
A diﬀerent approach was taken by Barenblatt who’s model is the forerunner of the
Cohesive Zone Model (CZM) used today.

crack tip (Sun and Jin, 2012). Figure 2.6 illustrates the CZ together with the cohesive
separation and the cohesive traction which acts in the CZ and holds the surfaces
together.

Cohesive Modeling in Abaqus
When using Abaqus for modelling of cohesion two approaches can be taken. One is
the cohesive element (CE) which typically are used to model adhesive behaviour, de-
lamination of interfaces, gaskets and rock fracture (Abaqus, 2013). It is possible to
choose between three mechanical constitutive responses when using CE which each is
used for diﬀerent purposes. The documentation describing the use of Abaqus (Abaqus,
2013) gives the following connection between constitutive response and usage:
Continuum-based
Modelling of material (e.g. adhesive joint where the glue has
a ﬁnite thickness)
Traction-separation
Modelling of interfaces (e.g. debonding in composite mate-
rial where the glue can be considered to have zero thickness)
Uniaxial stress-based
Modelling of gaskets or single adhesive patches
The other method is CIs which is only able to describe a traction-separation response
for surfaces. Though, the documentation claims that this method typically is ”easier
to deﬁne and allows simulation of a wider range of cohesive interactions”. Also in
(Abaqus, 2013) it is stated that the cohesive interaction is primarily for situations
where the thickness of the cohesive layer is negligible.

Pressure During Lamination
In the lamination guide (DuPont, 2012) the lamination process is described using an
autoclave and the pressure is recommended to be kept between 1.2MPa−1.4MPa. For
the experiments the lamination was done in a regular oven and pressure was applied
using a compressed spring, see Figure 3.11(b). The diﬀerence between the two methods
is the direction of the applied pressure; in an autoclave the pressure is applied on all
sides of the specimen, and with the spring the pressure is only applied in one direction
allowing for the ionoplast to ﬂow out. At ﬁrst the thickness of the SG ring was found to
vary a great deal and taking a maximum value of 0.65mm. The width/thickness ratio
of the ring should be as small as possible but due to the pressure the ratio increases
beyond what is desired. A sample was then made without any pressure. This resulted
in visible air bobbles between the glass and SG and was therefore not a solution as air
bobbles reduces the strength of the bond.
It is theefore found necessary to apply pressure to ensure a proper bond.
As rec-
ommended a pressure of 1.2MPa was applied during heating. Thus, when applying
pressure even small misalignments in the set-up causes the disks to loose parallelism,
and therefore, 3 spacers with thickness of 1mm were placed between the glass disks
during heating, see Figure 3.11(c). When the lamination set-up was removed from
the oven the spacers were immediately removed to avoid introducing stresses in the
specimens. With this approach all specimens ended up with parallel glass disks and
with a SG thickness of 0.9mm.

At authorised laminated glass suppliers the lamination is done in sterilized rooms or
cleanrooms. Professional facilities were not available for this project and contamination
from dust in the air occurred. Measures were taken though, to minimize this. Before
cutting of the SG rings the equipment were cleaned with cleaner’s benzine. In spite
of the cleaning of equipment, the laminate was exposed to dirt from the surroundings.

to get stuck on the rings. To insure as good a bond as possible during lamination the
SG rings were cleaned with lint free wipes just prior to assembly. The glasses were
also cleaned prior to assembly. This was done with detergent and afterwards rinsed
with de-mineralized water. Both methods are recommended by DuPont (2012).
Direction
During the production of glass one side is exposed to molten tin and during this process
diﬀusion of tin atoms into the glass surface occurs, (Haldimann et al., 2008). As a
consequence here oﬀthe two faces are not chemically identical and this aﬀects both
the mechanical strength of glass and the bond between glass and adhesive. DuPont
(2012) describes the importance of laminating on the tin side of the glass as the bond
strength is greater than that of the air side. If using the air side for lamination purpose
(e.g. multi-ply sheet lamination) an adhesion promoter must be used (DuPont, 2012).
For the experiments within this thesis both specimens laminated Air side/Tin side -
SG - Tin side/Air side (ATTAs) and specimens laminated Tin side/Air side - SG - Air
side/Tin side (TAATs) have been tested. The diﬀerence between the two faces can be
seen in UV-C (short wave) light were the tin side ﬂuoresces. According to Haldimann
et al. (2008) the mechanical strength of the tin side has been found to be marginally

Set-up: First Thoughts
The idea behind round glass disks was to ease the lamination procedure and the instal-

Two methods were considered for the connection between

used in the end. Though, in short it can be said that the bolt and fork connection
might be better to correct for eccentricities, where the welded connection have fewer
points where the rotation can be lost.
Due to the irregular shape of the glass a new approach had to be taken. Many ideas
with similar principles as the original idea were considered, i.a. the method shown in

Set-up: Final Solution

a screwed connection could be made to an element which could be attached in the
machine. This set-up is shown in Figure 3.16. The downsides to this approach are

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The material properties of SentryGlas® and the mechanical properties of the laminated
SentryGlas®-glass bond have been investigated. The strain rate dependency on both
material and mechanical properties were sought to be studied. Experimental work on
small laminated samples was performed, in which the samples were loaded in pure
tension, pure shear and a combination hereof. All experiment types were conducted at
multiple strain rates. In order to establish a greater understanding of the mechanisms
in the laminate and the cohesive surface FE models were created in order to simulate
the average experiment.
Through the work on experiments, it showed to be highly diﬃcult to apply the load
without introducing large eccentricities. Furthermore it was found that only small
eccentricities would lead to sizeable errors in the assumed stress distribution. Even
though several measures were taken in order to reduce eccentricities and misalignments
in the set-up, results were still widely aﬀected by the imperfections.
Another challenge regarding the experimental set-up was the mounting of the speci-
mens in the machine. The highly sensitive nature of the specimens combined with the
misalignments caused multiple samples to break even before load was applied. And
yet other specimens broke at very low stresses.
The bond strength showed to have very varying strength, which was found to be a
result of the lamination procedure. During lamination, dust particles in the air were
not completely avoided and these are thought to carry part of the explanation for the
varying strength of the lamination. For the experiments exposed to pure tension, the
fracture was for all experiments found to be from debonding between the glass and the
laminate. For the shear experiments the fracture was found to be caused by fracture
of the glass surface.
Fracture stresses up to 7.10MPa in pure tension and 11.83MPa in pure shear were
calculated analytically. From the numeric calculations it was found that the actual
stresses for pure tension load were a bit lower than those calculated analytically due to
simpliﬁcations used in the analytic calculations. The numeric calculations resulted in
an average fracture stress of 4.34MPa and 6.23MPa for tension experiments carried out
at 0.1mm/min and 0.05mm/min respectively. Due to the few combined experiments
no conclusive is to be said about the fracture stress for combined loading. The results
indicates a somewhat linear connection, but in order to conclude anything further,
several experiments should be conducted and the eﬀects from the applied strain rates
should be taken into account.
==PAGE:84==
68
6 Conclusion
The stiﬀness of the SentryGlas® interlayer were considered as well. Again the sim-
pliﬁcations made in the analytic calculations led to large deviations in the results
calculated from the tension experiments. From the numeric calculations it was found
that the material stiﬀness decreases when the strain rate is similarly decreased. This
is in accordance with the theory. The magnitudes of the stiﬀness found in this re-
search corresponds fairly well to values provided by Kuraray in (DuPont, 2008). But
others have made completely diﬀerent ﬁndings, which is a result of the large time,
temperature and strain dependency.
6.1
Future work
The experimental procedure presented in this thesis has showed to cause many diﬃcul-
ties which in the end aﬀects the quality of the results. For future reference it might be
proﬁtably to consider alternative experimental procedures or at least be prepared for
a time-consuming process in which the specimens have to be glued inside the machine,
by which misalignments can be avoided. A diﬀerent experimental approach is recom-
mended by the author. This could for instance consist of double cantilever beam tests
as described in e.g. (Troczynski and Camire, 1995). The brittleness of the glass can
be partially remedied using thin glass plates with metal plates attached on the outside.
Regarding further research within the ﬁeld, it would be relevant to thoroughly ex-
amine and potentially establish a connection between the temperature and the strain
rate dependencies of the material properties in SentryGlas® and polymers in general.
Many investigations are made into either the temperature dependency or the strain
rate dependency, but not much information is available on the combinations of the
two.

I did several laminations with SG, bot with and without the vacuum bag.
Generally speaking, i observed that most of the case you might need many
attempt to find the best configuration.
This is because it depends on the specimens shape and size.
The main problem of laminating SG is to get rid of air in‐between SG and
other surfaces.
In principle, you can use a simple oven with some weight to guarantee
pressure between sg and glass.
however most probably you will not be able to apply 12‐14 bar as it is in
the "glass" autoclave.
if the size is limited it might work but you could still get some air
inclusion.
After this attempt I used a system of spring to apply higher constant
pressure during the lamination.
I built up a small steel frame around the specimens with springs in
between.
This worked better, but from my experience you can never be 100% sure to
avoid air inclusions.
Then I found an small autoclave in material science department, and so I
used the vacuum bag.
this worked much better because the vacuum bag considerably help to get
rid of the air.
you might check if the material science department at DTU has an
autoclave. you should look for laboratory doing research on composite
material.
If this is not the case, maybe an idea could be to introduce a vacuum pump
in the oven. Maybe using a very old oven, drilling a hole through it?
At the end, all these attempts I did worked quite well.
However I used them only to make exploratory specimens for preliminary
tests.
Then, all definitive specimens were made by a glass manufacture.

Regarding cutting the SG I see 3 option:
1. to punch out the shape you need using a sharp metal piece and a
pressing machine. the metal piece edges, machined accord to the shape you
need, should be very sharp, but at the sam time extremely rigid otherwise
you could surprisingly bend the steel.
2. to cut out the shape you need using a numerically controlled cutting
machine. This type of machine is not particularly special, is has a
