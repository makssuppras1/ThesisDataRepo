Abstract
The coming global warming undoubtedly aﬀects every person on Earth. To slow down and eliminate
the existing consequences of an increase in the concentration of green house gases in the atmosphere
of our planet, many countries of the world have decided to decarbonize the energy sectors and have
set speciﬁc goals for solving this problem. The main tool to reduce harmful emissions is to increase
the share of renewable energy sources and, in particular, solar energy in the total energy mix of
consumption and generation of electricity by the state, region, city, household.
There is no feasibility study on the potential of residential rooftop photovoltaic (PV) systems in
Norway, Denmark and Ukraine, which will help future PV investors, politicians and the public in
these three countries, but especially in Ukraine, to see the potential of PV systems and make an
informed decision when installing or drafting laws for a residential solar power plant understanding
all the pros, cons and risks. The main objective of this thesis is to analyze and compare the techno-
economical potential of residential rooftop photovoltaic (PV) systems in Norway, Denmark and
Ukraine to solve this problem.
The project will consist of a review of the PV market penetration, main marked drivers, solar
resource, and residential energy consumption pattern in Norway, Denmark and Ukraine.
Based
on hourly consumption data and simulated solar power generation proﬁles for households, percent
of self-consumption of generated PV electricity will be calculated for a typical average household
electricity consumption and various values for the installed capacity of a residential rooftop PV plant.
By analyzing monthly electricity consumption, seasonal variations in consumption will be identiﬁed.
Thereafter, scaling of a solar power plant in the locations of Denmark, Norway and Ukraine will be
modeled in order to study and compare feasibility indicators such as payback time (PBT), levelised
cost of energy (LCOE) based on the local solar resource, percent of self-consumption of generated
electricity, cost parameters and incentives.
This study has shown that as the size of residential rooftop PV system increases in Denmark and
Norway, the economic proﬁtability of the project decreases, while in Ukraine increases. The key to
getting maximum beneﬁt from the installation of a solar power plant in Denmark and Norway lies
in maximizing the percentage self-consumption of generated electricity. The options for increasing
the percentage of self-consumption are proposed, which are a plan for further research.

Motivation
The problem of Global Warming is perceived by the Slavic people with disdain caused by the lack of
information from the media. On the other hand, Scandinavians pay due attention to this problem
by studying it in schools, universities and regulating carbon dioxide emissions at the legislative level.
But both of them are faced with the problem of increasing the cost of energy and undoubtedly want
to reduce the bills of electricity, water, gas, etc. The main objective of this thesis is to analyze and
compare the techno-economical potential of residential rooftop photovoltaic (PV) systems in Nor-
way, Denmark and Ukraine, with the aim of increasing knowledge and awareness of the potential
beneﬁts and opportunities of solar PV amongst in these countries.
Introduction
According to the Bethan Davies [14] by using ice-core sample measurements we can determine CO2
concentrations well into the past. Atmospheric science is very complex and historical data can tell
how CO2 aﬀects the environment. Since 1000 AD the CO2 concentration has been relatively stable
(approximately 270 ppm) until about 1769 - The Industrial Revolution.
According to the Allen et al.[15] it is estimated that because of the human activities, 1.0 ± 0.2◦C
of global warming have caused above pre-industrial levels. It is also estimated that between 2030
and 2052, temperature will reach 1.5 ◦C if no action will be taken [15]. Human-induced warming
from the industrial revolution to the present will persist for many centuries and will cause long-term
changes in the climate system, such as sea level rise, with associated impacts on biodiversity and
ecosystems, including species loss and extinction. Nowadays we are at a CO2 concentration (410
ppm) that has never been seen before.
According to IPCC, current anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions are the highest in the his-
tory. There are two main factors that aﬀect greenhouse gas emissions in the energy sector (60%
of global greenhouse gas emissions are caused by the energy sector [16]). The ﬁrst factor is global
electriﬁcation with subsequent increase of demand for electricity, and the second factor is the decar-
bonization of the energy sector. Humanity should make every eﬀort to ensure that the superposition
of these factors leads to a decrease in greenhouse gas emissions into the atmosphere.
In order to fulﬁll the decarbonization requirements of our planet, in September 2015, 194 countries
adopted the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals [17], it is 17 goals with 169 targets.
One of the most relevant goals is Goal 7. [17] - ”Ensure access to aﬀordable, reliable, sustainable
and modern energy for all” with subgoal 7.2 ”By 2030, increase substantially the share of renewable
energy in the global energy mix” Consequently , all renewable energy technologies will continue to
increase their capacities thought the World to decarbonize the electricity sector.

5.1
Norwegian, Ukrainian and Danish Current Electricity System
According to Energy Facts Norway [18], Norway has the highest share of electricity produced from
renewable sources in Europe, and the lowest emissions from the power sector. At the end of 2020,
the total installed capacity of the Norwegian power supply system was 37 1680 MW, and normal
annual production was 153 TWh. Norway is the European leader in the production of electricity
from renewable sources. Renewable sources account for the largest share in the energy mix of the
Norwegian energy sector leading to record low anthropogenic emissions from the power sector. Total
installed capacity of the Norwegian electricity supply system was 37 1,680 MW, and the annual out-
put was 153 TWh for 2020.In 2020, Norway set a new electricity production record of 154,2 TWh.
At the moment, the main focus of investments in the renewable sector is wind energy accounts for
10% of the instilled capacity.
On the other hand, Norway is one of the largest oil and gas exporters in the world. Hydropower
storage capacity in Norway is 86 TWh, wich provides balancing power to neighbouring countries.
It corresponds to 70% of annual Norwegian electricity consumption. Hydropower is a great source
of baseload electricity, on the other hand, the volume of solar energy generation has a pronounced
proﬁle with a daily frequency, which makes solar energy and hydropower complementary.

5.2.1
Status of the solar PV market
Norway is a major renewable energy developer in Europe, mainly through its development of hy-
dropower. Research [20] has shown that solar energy also has potential in Norway, however, the
cumulative installed photovoltaic capacity is slightly more than 160 MW at the end of 2020 [4].
Growth of installed PV capacities in Norway is shown on the Figure 3.
Figure 3: Cumulative Installed PV Capacity in Norway (MW) [3], [4]
Annual installed capacity of solar PV has increased every year from 2012 to 2020. This fact is

Fundamental PV Theory

6.1
Modern Photovoltaic Technology
Two key factors that PV industry is trying to develop further and further are higher eﬃciency of
the cells and cheaper production price. There are three main technologies on the PV market:
• Mono-crystalline silicon solar cells (mono-Si). Production share: 40 %
• Mutli-crystalline silicon solar cells (multi-Si). Production share: 54.5 %
• Thin ﬁlms solar cells. Production share: 5.5 %
World production share between this three PV technologies at the end of 2019 is indicated at the
end of each item according to [35].
Figure 10: Schematic view of a typical single junction solar cell [8]
First generation solar cells consists of only one layer of light absorption material (single junction
shown on Figure 10). It is mono-Si and multi-Si solar cells. According to the Best Research-Cell
Eﬃciency Chart [36], eﬃciency records of 27.6% ± 0.5% for mono-Si and 23.3% ± 0.4% for multi-Si
are already achieved.
In order to make solar cells cheaper, second generation PV cells was developed. They are also single
junction cells, but less materials are used in the manufacturing process. They are manufactured
using the technology of layer-by-layer deposition of elements onto a substrate and they can be
sorted according to the materials from which they are made
• CdTe solar cells. Eﬃciency record: 22.1 %
• CIGS solar cells. Eﬃciency record: 23.4 %
• Amorphous silicon solar cells (a-Si). Eﬃciency record: 14.0 %
Eﬃciency record for each item is presented according to Best Research-Cell Eﬃciency Chart [36].
This materials are relatively good and even better absorbers than for the ﬁrst generation despite
the fact that fewer materials are used to create solar cells. They are also called thin ﬁlm solar
cells. The main disadvantage of 2-nd generation is fast and unpredictable degradation rate and poor
reproducibility. This makes these solar cells less common on a commercial scale.

Single junction solar cells are limited by Shockley–Queisser limit [37], it is a fundamental physi-
cal limit which shows that the maximum eﬃciency of a single junction solar cell can be not more
than 34%. Since it is not possible to surpass this limitation for one junction, multiple junction solar
cell made of materials with diﬀerent band gap energies was invented (third generation solar cell).
The word eﬃciency laboratory record (according to the BestResearch-Cell Eﬃciency Chart ) for
multiple junction solar cell is 47.1 % [36].
6.2
Photovoltaic Modules
When solar cells are connected in series, the voltages are summed up, but the current is the same in
all cells and is determined by the worst cell. When solar cells are connected in parallel, the voltages
are the same, and the currents are summed up. The solar panel is assembled depending on the
desired current-voltage characteristic. The same rules apply when connecting modules to a solar
power plant. Figure 11 shows the components of the solar module (serial connection of cells).
Figure 11: Glass-foil module. Adapted from [8]
All solar cells connected in series depend on the weakest cell (it can be either shaded or damaged).
If all cells have a constant load, and one cell has lover current because of shade or damage, then
fully functioning cells will produce high voltage and act as a reverse bias on the damaged cell. This
cell will start to heat up, wich can lead to signiﬁcant losses in production. Hot spots can be detected
using the Infrared Thermography diagnostic.
To minimize the above-described eﬀect, bypass diodes are installed in the module. These components
allow the module to continue to produce current at the level of normally functioning cells [38]. Figure
12 illustrate diode working principle.
Figure 12: Red arrows indicate the direction of the current with an active bypass diode (one cell is
shaded).

6.4
External Performance Factors
Performance of a solar power plant is strongly eﬀected by surrounding factors such as shading of
the PV surface, element temperature, solar spectrum, etc.
The Solar Irradiance and Spectrum
Before solar radiation hits an absorbing surface, it passes through the Earth’s atmosphere, which
absorbs, reﬂects and scatters solar radiation that hits the surface of the atmosphere (solar radiation
on top of the atmosphere is called TSI).
Equation 1 represent three main components of solar radiation on the Earth surface.
Gg = Gd + Gdiff + Gr
Gd - Direct component.
Gdiff - Diﬀused component.
Gr - Ground reﬂected component.
Gg - The sum of all three components.
Most solar power plants are designed to maximize direct radiation. If it is necessary to maximize
the scattered component of the radiation, then the modules should be deployed horizontally. Such a
situation can arise when the sky is covered with clouds and there is practically no direct component
of solar radiation.
Inﬂuence of Atmosphere
The longer the path of solar radiation passes through the atmosphere, the more it is scattered,
reﬂected, absorbed, and thereby weakened. A term that characterizes the length of a path through
the atmosphere compared to the shortest path perpendicular to the earth’s surface is called the air
mass coeﬃcient (AM), is shown on Figure 14.

Feasibility Studies of Energy Projects Theory
A feasibility study is the analysis and evaluation of a proposed project in regards to its attractiveness.
The aim of a feasibility study is to reveal the strengths and weaknesses of a project in several diﬀerent
areas. It provides the basis for an informed and rational decision making about if the project should
be carried out or not - and sometimes in what form the project should be carried out.
Feasibility study is one step in the business assessment and business development process [41]. It
helps identifying which business ideas are valuable and worthwhile to further explore. Feasibility
studies are undertaken rather early in the process, before the actual business development begins.
Figure 16: Feasibility study stages
Figure 16 describes the process of developing and treating business ideas. After having developed a
number of business ideas, the ﬁrst step of concept development is to make a preliminary high-level
assessment. The focus of this master thesis is Concept Development stage of the project.
7.1
Feasibility Variables
Discounted cash ﬂow (DCF) analysis is the standard framework for evaluating investment projects.
It is based on assessing the costs and beneﬁts of a project over its lifetime, and making them
comparable by discounting. These costs and beneﬁts are purely based on cash ﬂows related to the
project. Cash Flows (CF) are the movement of money into and out of a business, a project, a
ﬁnancial product, etc. over a speciﬁed, ﬁnite period of time.
The following key indicators are relevant for an economic feasibility analysis [42]:
• Net Present Value (NPV)
• Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
• Payback time (PBT)
• Levelised cost of energy (LCOE)

14.2
Hourly and Seasonal Power Consumption and PV Power Generation
Variations Discussion
It is not possible to compare the hourly variation of the consumption proﬁle and solar generation in
Ukraine due to the lack of an hourly proﬁle of electricity consumption for the whole year.
In Ukraine, the legislation is structured in such a way that the comparison of the amount of electricity
consumed by the residence and the amount of electricity generated by a home solar power plant takes
place once a month, and not every hour as in Denmark and Norway (monthly time horizon). As a
result, when the maximum allowable size of a solar power plant is set in the poorest month in the
sun in Ukraine (December), generation exceeds the average consumption for a household by at least
4 times. The monthly time horizon helps to maximize the percentage of self-consumption over the
hourly time horizon.
An analysis of the hourly proﬁle of consumption and solar generation in Denmark and Norway shows
that solar generation peaks at the minimum household electricity consumption in both countries.
This is explained by the fact that the sun is most active during the daytime, and residents are
mostly outside the house at the same time. Since the goal of homeowners in Denmark and Norway
is to maximize self-consumption to maximize savings on their electricity bills, there are two ways to
accomplish this. The ﬁrst way is to adjust the consumption proﬁle so that it is maximum during
the daytime.
This can be achieved by installing a smart home system and programming it, or
by changing your own habits in order to shift the maximum of household chores to the daytime
(washing, showering, watering ...) The second method is to accumulate the generated energy for
consumption in the evening, night and in the morning, when the instantly generated electricity is
not enough or not at all. This can be achieved by installing rechargeable batteries.

14.4
Feasibility Studies of Energy Projects Discussion
As mentioned in the introduction and objectives of the thesis, there is no feasibility study for the
residential rooftop PV sysstems in Denmark and Norway. Only general European reports are made
[55] for spectral distribution of LCOE of rooftop solar PV systems in the EU. According to this
report, LCOE of rooftop solar PV systems in Denmark ranges from 0,16 to 0,22 USD/kWh, which
is comparable with the results obtained in this work, which lie in the range from 0,15 to 0,18
USD/kWh. Data for Norway is not available in this report. Probably, the lack of such calculations
and articles is due to the fact that in Denmark and Norway the installation of a solar power plant
is not a business project, as, for example, in Ukraine. The payback period for solar power plants in
Denmark and Norway with installed capacities of more than 3 kW and more than 5 kW, respectively,
exceeds 20 years. After the FIT support schemes for solar energy were stopped and the payback
period of the newly installed power plants increased signiﬁcantly, the facts about the payback period
of solar power plants ceased to be published. Articles with the calculation of the payback period
can only be found with outdated data on support schemes in the form of FIT.
On the other hand, the installation of residential solar power plants in Ukraine is still supported by
the green FIT tariﬀand is a business case for both residential and industrial scale. On the Ukrainian
market of solar power plants, there are many proposals for the installation of turnkey solar power
plants under a green tariﬀ[56], [57]. According to these proposals, the estimated payback period
varies from 3 to 4 years, depending on the components used and the decency of the installer. NPV,
which is promised by companies selling solar power plants in Ukraine, is in the range from 40 [56] to
160 [57] thousand dollars when 30 kW system is installed, which is clearly a commercial trick or an
ill-advised economic calculation that does not take into account the degradation of solar panels, the
cost of money and the very duration of the green tariﬀ. There are a lot of such proposals and they
are designed for wealthy but technically or ecconomically illiterate investors. This work is intended
to clarify the real prospects for investment in a solar power plant in Ukraine, namely NPV in the
amount of 28 thousand dollars for 20 years, of which 23 thousand will be earned in the ﬁrst 10 years
of the project.
According to these commercial proposals [57], LCOE for residential rooftop PV systems in Ukraine
does not exceed 0.024 USD / kWh, which is also a commercial gimmick. In fact, LCOE for residential
rooftop PV systems in Ukraine is 0.047 USD / kWh, which is approximately equal to the retail
electricity price.

Conclusion
15.1
Opportunities of solar PV in Denmark and Norway
Calculations have shown that in Denmark and Norway, due to high tax rates on the sale of electricity
to the grid and the lack of strong government incentives to install solar power plants at home in
the form of green FIT tariﬀs or other signiﬁcant support schemes, the key to getting the maximum
beneﬁt from the installation of a solar power plant lies in maximizing the percentage self-consumption
of generated electricity.
The high tax rate on the sale of electricity generated at a solar power plant is due to the fact that
with a large volume of installed capacity at the peak of daily solar activity, an imbalance in the
frequency (its increase) occurs in the public grid, which requires additional actions and costs to
balance and maintain the quality of electricity in the grid. The own consumption of the generated
energy does not aﬀect the frequency in the public grid and for this reason is stimulated by the state.
Based on the analysis of the average daily proﬁle of electricity consumption for an average household
and the proﬁle of solar electricity generation for the maximum permitted size of a solar power plant
in the respective country, it is understood that the peak in solar electricity production falls on the
middle of the day and at the same time there is a drawdown in the consumption proﬁle, since often
during the day, residents of houses are at work or outside. Home electricity consumption peaks in
the morning when breakfast is being prepared and getting ready for work, school, etc. and in the
evening when the whole family gathers at home again, eats dinner, cleans, takes a shower and does
household chores.
Based on this, we can conclude that in order to increase the percentage of self-consumption, it is
necessary to shift the electricity consumption at the time of the peak of its production at the salt
power plant. This can be done either by changing your own rhythm of life or by installing a smart
home system and programming it so that the maximum of household chores is performed at the
time of maximum generation. On the other hand, to increase the percentage of self-consumption,
an attempt can be made to shift the generation proﬁle to morning and evening periods of maximum
power consumption. This can be achieved by installing a battery system that will work in com-
bination with salt panels and instead of selling ”extra” (at the moment) electricity to the grid at
low prices, it will store it with high eﬃciency in batteries. The economic aspects of these proposals
require further study and represent a great interest for the solar energy industry.
15.2
Opportunities of solar PV in Ukraine
The situation in Ukraine is diametrically opposite to the situation in Denmark and Norway. Due
to the over-stimulation of the population to install high-power home solar electric systems up to 30
kW (NPV of the project grows linearly with the increase in the installed capacity of the solar power
plant) through the green tariﬀFIT, by the end of 2019 the volume of installed photovoltaic capacity
in the country exceeded 3.5 MW which is 3 times more than in Denmark and 22 times more than
in Norway.
This, at ﬁrst glance, an outstanding achievement of Ukraine has a great potential danger for every
owner of a solar power plant. The cost of electricity in the Ukrainian power grid is more than 4.5
times less than the price paid by the state to the owners of private power plants. At the same time,
the colossal volume of installed capacity leads to the task of stabilizing the frequency of electricity
of the public network during the daytime due to the characteristic proﬁle of solar energy generation,
which leads to additional costs on the part of the state. Considering this, from a purely economic
point of view, Ukraine, like Denmark and Norway, is not interested in increasing the share of solar
electricity in the total energy mix of the grid. In order to avoid problems with balancing the frequency

in the grid, Ukraine should also begin to stimulate its own consumption of generated energy and
minimize the percentage of exports to the grid. At this stage, the opposite situation is observed.
The contract with the state for the export of solar electricity from a private household to the public
grid is concluded for 10 years, and based on this, a technical and economic calculation is made. In
Ukraine there is a high probability that the contract with the state may be terminated or the rate
of payment for the generated energy approved in the contract may decrease due to the unstable
political situation in the state. It is this aspect of the business case that is most diﬃcult to correctly
account for in the calculations. The trend is that for the owner of solar power plants with a capacity
of more than 30 kW, the rate approved in the contract has already been lowered by the decision of
the state in 2020, and the likelihood of a decrease for private households has increased. The payback
period for a power plant in Ukraine is at least three times less than in Denmark and Norway, which
are countries with stable economies. This reasoning casts doubt on the transparent and promising
economic calculation for the installation of a solar power plant in Ukraine, introducing the diﬃculties
of accounting for risks.
Drivers and Barriers of solar energy development in Ukraine
• Beneﬁts of geographic location and climate.
• An attractive green tariﬀrate for investors of 0.18 / kWh - for home stations, while at the
global level, countries are already moving away from the state policy of ﬁnancial incentives for
the introduction of renewable energy sources.
• Instability of the regulatory framework of state regulation of the market.
• The likelihood of a reduction or cancellation of the ”green” tariﬀfor already built power plants,
which may negatively aﬀect investor conﬁdence.
• Complex system of obtaining permits and approvals for SPP operators.
• SPP occupy large areas - each 1 MW of power requires at least 1.5 hectares of land.
15.3
Limitations
One of the most signiﬁcant limitations of this work is the fact that the analysis of the consumption
proﬁle was based on speciﬁc cases, one from each country. This is due to the fact that this type of
data is very rarely measured and it was a great success to ﬁnd at least one example of an hourly
consumption proﬁle for Denmark and Norway. I would like to thank Sergiu Spataru from DTU and
Gaute Stokkan from NTNU for providing this data. For a better analysis, it is worth considering
the consumption proﬁles in diﬀerent regions of each country, since the habits of families can vary
signiﬁcantly depending on a large number of factors.
