Abstract
Topology optimization and transient dynamics that includes geometrically nonlinear effects
are both established research fields. However, only little exploration has been conducted
on the unity of the two, although the combination could have great potential in increasing
the robustness of the optimized solutions. This thesis introduces a fully discrete transient
topology optimization scheme that can describe geometrically nonlinear effects such as
large deformations, rotations and rigid body motions.
The fundamental equations are discretized using the finite element method with an
immersed boundary formulation based on the ersatz material model, as is the norm within
the field of density-based topology optimization. The domain is stabilized using the fic-
titious domain formulation that introduces an interpolation resulting in linear, nonlinear
and intermediate element formulations.
The time integration relies on the Newmark-ОІ time integration scheme, and equilibrium
is reached within each time step using an energy error formulation.
Additionally, the
unstable geometrically nonlinear formulation is stabilized by introducing an adaptive time
stepping scheme that allows smaller time step sizes when required.
The topology optimization method is introduced with a least square objective function
that allows control in both time and space. Furthermore, three different penalization func-
tions are also introduced to alleviate intermediate element hinges, although the proposed
proved to be insufficient in some cases.
Finally, three experiments showcase the capabilities of a geometrically nonlinear tran-
sient topology optimization framework. Here it is showcased that the implementation does
seek toward the given goals, although the goal might be difficult to define. The framework
shows excellent potential for future usage and development and can be extended in many
directions.

Introduction
This thesis examines the effects of employing transient dynamics with large deformations
on density-based topology optimization (TO).
Throughout human civilization, striving for technological advancements has pushed our
understanding and utilization of the world far beyond our initial hunter-gather society. Our
continuous search for superior optimal solutions and the huge advancements throughout
the centuries has given rise to engineered methods that can help us obtain just that.
Within the field of engineering, most designs have historically arrived from a rather
ad hoc approach by guessing and then redesigning for loads. Although this method might
be simple, it does not ensure optimal or even close to optimal designs. However, this can
be done with optimization methods like gradient-based TO. This method uses a range
of inputs such as domain, boundary conditions, physics, constraints, etc. to iteratively
find gradients that lead to optimal designs, that minimize given objective functions by
distributing material within a domain.
In recent years, the method has been used to
optimize everything from aeroplane wings to antennas to many others as computing power
and recognition of the method grows.
In the field of mechanical and civil engineering, the study of dynamics is of high im-
portance as the introduction of inertia introduces resonance. Even if a design statically
performs well, it might perform poorly in an environment where it is loaded or excited
close to a resonant frequency, where even a small load can cause catastrophic failure. An
example of this, although not catastrophic, is the swing, cf. Figure 1.1a. Even for a small
input forcing, by displacing the centre of mass at the right timing, huge swinging/vibrations
occur. When a structure in equilibrium or steady-state undergoes a change that affects the
equilibrium, the following response is called the transient response and ends when equilib-
rium or a new steady-state is reached due to damping. This response is often neglected
although being where the largest deformations typically occur. By introducing transient
dynamics into the TO method, designs will be generated with both the transient and the
dynamic phenomena included and final designs will be designed on the basis of that.
Engineers tend to make assumptions to simplify calculations. Small deformation is a
classical assumption as it linearizes many problems. In solid mechanics, this assumption
stays true as long as the deformations are small enough that they barely are visible. This
assumption can, however, easily be unfulfilling. An example of this could be when mod-
elling a fishing rod cf. Figure 1.1b. As a load (typically a fish) is introduced at the end,
a moment is generated around the tip. When the fish is pulled in the rod deforms greatly
causing the moment to change which would not be describable by a linear assumption.
Other examples could be the design of flipping effects used a lot in the emerging field of
compliant mechanisms cf. Figure 1.1c, descriptions of rotating solids or again when dealing
with transient dynamics close to resonance. Large deformation modelling is often referred
to as geometrical nonlinearities since the geometry can not be described linearly when
deformed greatly.

where the full or at least a large amount of the transient response has died out due to
damping in design domains large enough that interesting details can be captured. This
has to the best of the authorвҖҷs knowledge not been investigated as only short time series
have been taken into account due to the immense computational cost.
1.1
Previous Works
With the early seminal work by BendsГёe and Kikuchi, 1988 an extended range of sub-fields
arose within TO. One of these is the field of transient TO, where the optimization process
always has been quite expensive compared to static and steady-state optimization, as the
complete temporal response has to be calculated at each design step.
Although this sub-field was facilitated at an early point with formulations of the linear
and nonlinear transient sensitivity analysis by Haug and Arora, 1978; Michaleris et al.,
1994. It first began to form at the beginning of this millennium with notable works like;
Chung et al., 2000 solving dielectric identification problems, Pedersen, 2004 solving designs
for crashworthiness using frame structures, Turteltaub, 2005 finding optimal transient dy-
namics of non-homogeneous composites and lastly, Dahl et al., 2008 where one-dimensional
wave propagation was optimized to increase band gaps from the time domain. With the
latter laying the foundation for 2- and 3-dimensional transient TO. The method was fur-
ther developed by J. S. Jensen et al., 2014, where the differentiate-then-discretize approach
was shown to give consistency errors, whereby the discretize-then-differentiate approach is
favourable. The general method is summed up by Kristiansen and Aage, 2022 where the
general method is presented as an introduction to a transient extension of the large-scale
framework introduced by Aage et al., 2015.
The linear transient TO method has been extended in many directions over the years.
Of the many are the following mentioned: J. S. Jensen, 2009 introduced transient optimiza-
tion with both space and time as design variables, generating structures that change over
time. J. Jensen and Sigmund, 2011 sum up an extended range of articles within the field
of transient optimization of nanophotonics. Zhao and Wang, 2016 worked with the two

further discussed and implemented in the following chapters. Another way of regularizing
the low-density elements is to use the method introduced by Bluhm et al., 2021. Here, a
penalty term was introduced in the strain energy density resulting in regularization of the
void and internal contact.
Lastly, it should be mentioned that some works have worked on the optimization of

in non-physical element stretching when displacements are large enough. This is seen in
Michaleris et al., 1994 which discusses the nonlinear transient sensitivities, Nakshatrala
and Tortorelli, 2016 deals with nonlinear elastodynamics subjected to impact loading,
Alberdi et al., 2018 where path-dependent sensitivity analysis is examined for a long-range
of nonlinearities, including large-strain dynamics and Kristiansen et al., 2021 discusses
transient frictional impacts with geometrical nonlinearity.

pursued within each time step until Werr < 10вҲ’8W, where W are the sum of the current

Adaptive time stepping
Since the NR problem changes at each n and that uncondi-
tional stability is not assured, the difficulty of finding a solution changes throughout the
time steps. At some time steps a solution is found within a single NR iteration and at other
times the solution might never be found given the time step size, вҲҶt. Consequently, for a
constant вҲҶt the time step size should be chosen small enough that solutions will be found
at all iterations. This quickly becomes rather expensive as construction and solution of
the entire EOM are required at each NR iteration. A rather simple adaptive time stepping
scheme is proposed instead consisting of two rules:
вҖў When the number of NR iterations surpasses 7 iterations, вҲҶt is divided by four.
вҖў When a NR solution was found in less than 3 iterations, вҲҶt is doubled.
In order of keeping track of time, a parameter t is introduced that is defined as the sum of
all previous time steps. This method is highly inspired by Bluhm et al., 2021, where this
is done after each design iteration.

вҖў Sizing optimization typically seeks to find the optimal thickness distribution given

position and shape of holes and their connectivity. It is hence a more flexible strategy
than the two previous and ideal for cases where designers have little or no knowl-
edge about the optimal material distribution in advance. As this thesis deals with
problems where just that is the case, this method is used in this thesis.

Filters
Filtering is a method used in topology optimization to avoid well-known issues related
to a pure implementation of topology optimization; Checkerboards and mesh-dependent
designs. Checkerboard regions are areas where the optimizer will place alternating solid and
void elements as shown to the left in Figure 2.5a. These appear as the optimizer exploits
the first-order Q4-elements formulation that assumes the displacement field varies linearly
between the element nodes. So by defining the region with the alternating elements, an
effective stiffness of E1/2 is obtained using effectively an average of ПҒ = 1/2 in the region,
although this is not possible at an element basis since the stiffness has been penalized using
RAMP cf. Diaz and Sigmund, 1995. Similarly, mesh-dependent structures arise due to
similar exploits of the low order element formulation as the classical non-physical hinge
seen to the left in Figure 2.5b. Here, an overly compliant hinge is created by abusing the
before-mentioned element assumption.

2.3
Implementation
The implementation of the above-described theory was written in the high-level program-
ming language Matlab. This was chosen due to its excellent debugging tools, its many
mathematical functions that run naively without the need of external libraries and ulti-
mately since the author is most experienced in this language. Matlab is written on top
of C and uses LAPACK to efficiently solve linear algebra, meaning that efficient usage of
functions can highly increase computational speeds has been shown between the top99.m
code and the top88.m codes cf. Andreassen et al., 2011; Sigmund, 2001. This code runs
exclusively in a sequential manner and no attempts have been made on parallelization.
This could potentially be done by extending the linear transient PETSc implementation
introduced by Kristiansen and Aage, 2022, though this would extend the problems into
the third dimension.
2.3.1
Improving Efficiency
Since the algorithm by itself requires a lot of steps with NR, time stepping, isoparametric
elements, etc. was some energy spent on decreasing wall-clock time per iteration. The
profiling/timing tool in Matlab was used a lot here, where flame-graphs4 was essential as
they give a clear overview of the distribution of total time spent on each function and
its corresponding sub-functions. It should be noted that all the following improvements
have been included in the sections above, but this is underlined here for clarity. The most
significant changes are listed below, although many small tweaks were introduced:
вҖў A rather low-hanging fruit was to decrease the number of times the stiffness matrix
and internal force are constructed. Since the NR iterations are run using the solu-
tion from the last iteration (cf. Equation (2.2)), could this be moved outside the
NR iterations. This means that only the internal force vector has to be constructed
once per NR iteration and that it then can be saved for the next time step, where
the tangent stiffness matrix is constructed once per time step. The Rayleigh damp-
ing formulation from Equation (2.3) is formulated to use the linear stiffness matrix
since the number of tangent stiffness matrix evaluations is reduced. In this way, the
damping matrix stays constant throughout each design iteration. This modification
is rather similar to the Modified NR algorithm described by Cook et al., 2002.
вҖў The full isoparametric formulation is significantly reduced since the implementation
relies on the top88.m code (Andreassen et al., 2011) and that the large displacement
nonlinearity implemented uses a total Lagrangian approach (meaning that each NR
refers to the undeformed state). By rejecting the unstructured element formulation
and all the linear isoparametric formulations the number of operations to obtain the
local stiffness matrix or internal force vector is halved. This is quite significant as
the function is run four times per element.
вҖў Next, to further increase the construction time some experimenting was done by
compiling parts of the construction scripts to Mex files, which is a functionality where
Matlab compiles functions to a given low-level language, in this thesis case C. In
Figure 2.7 the wall clock time is shown as a function of the number of elements
for a range of different construction methods.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to demonstrate topology optimization of designs where the full nonlinear
transient response was included in meaningful spacial resolutions.
Geometrically nonlinear finite element formulation was successfully implemented using
a Total Lagrangian formulation and further reformulated using a fictitious domain formu-
lation through energy interpolation between linear and nonlinear elements. This was then
unified with a transient finite element formulation which relied on Newmark integration
using an energy error expression in order to ensure equilibrium between the time steps.
The unified nonlinear transient finite element implementation was further stabilized using
adaptive stepping that decreased the time step size if solutions were difficult to reach in a
given time step.
The full topology optimization formulation was introduced with the least square func-
tion as the objective function, which allowed for control in both time and space. Further-
more, three penalization functions were introduced to the objective function, in order to
penalize nonphysical solutions. A sensitivity formulation was then introduced, completing
the total design algorithm.
A range of changes was introduced to the implementation layout to ensure that the
implementation was running efficiently. This consisted, among others, of a reformulation
of the elements, restructuring of the Newton-Raphson iterations and compiling parts of the
code prior to running. This showed significant improvement in the computational efficiency
and an example indicated an improvement of approximately 4000 to 8000%.
The transient and geometrical nonlinear implementations were both validated success-
fully in isolation from one another. This was done using theoretical results and commercial
software. The transient and geometrically nonlinear validation found a maximum relative
error below 4% and 10%, respectively.
Finally, three experiments were run using the design algorithm; 1) a reproduction of
a beam structure, 2) a structure with a rotating core and 3) a structure with an anti-
resonant point in the middle. 1) showed that a close approximation of the time response
was successful. However, the final reproduced beam lacked a range of finer details, which
ultimately was explained by the simple forcing step response that solely excited the first
natural frequency of the structure.
2) had a goal of a core rotation of 45в—Ұ.
This was
not obtained, instead, a maximum of 9в—Ұwas obtained.
This experiment truly showed
the strength of a nonlinear formulation.
3) was mostly unsuccessful, which showcased
the complexity of the problem formulation which require a well-contemplated problem
specification.
The goal of designing structures using long transient nonlinear responses was somewhat
successful in the first experiment, indicating the potentials of the framework. The third
experiment was mostly unsuccessful, leaving room for an improved problem formulation.

5.1
Future Work
The field of unified geometrically nonlinear transient topology optimization is relatively
untouched due to its large computational costs. This means that further exploration within
the field could be taken in a multitude of directions. Some of the most evident extensions
are presented below:
вҖў Full solutions. First and foremost, it would be interesting to pick up where this thesis
ends and compute solutions for the three cases in a higher resolution and with a more
exciting time response. This would not require any significant changes in the current
framework but solely require additional time.
вҖў Reworking the efficiency. An obvious problem with the implementation is the calcula-
tion speed, which rises quickly as the spatial and temporal discretization is increased.
Solutions to this could be to use model reduction methods, check-pointing and maybe
even rewriting the code to a compiled language such as C++ or Julia.
вҖў Extension into 3D. As a three-dimensional linear transient topology optimization
PETSc framework is available with the work of Kristiansen and Aage, 2022, would
it be relatively straightforward to extend this with geometrical nonlinear element
formulations using this thesis as background.
It could e.g.
be interesting to see
the rotation experiment in a three-dimensional version, where multiple compliant
features could be utilized to increase the rotation.
вҖў Introduce the internal contact void regulation scheme cf. Bluhm et al., 2021. With
this introduced, it could be possible to find structures that utilize internal impacts
to achieve even difficult objectives.
вҖў Introduce plates and acoustics. The linear formulations quickly fall short even for
small rotations in plate formulations. By introducing plate element formulations,
instead of the solid elements used in this thesis, many interesting aspects could be
explored, e.g. if coupled to an acoustic domain.

Two further validation steps has been taken to ensure that the implementation produce
results similar to those obtained in previous works.
C-beam deformation
The first of these is shown in Figure 2.3 on page 7. This is a clas-
