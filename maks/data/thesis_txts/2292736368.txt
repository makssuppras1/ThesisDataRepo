Abstract 
In 2009 and 2014 the Danish Science Factory conducted a campaign,  where the 
pupils and their teacher from 785 classes measured the CO2 concentration and 
temperature in their own classrooms. Both campaigns concluded that in more 
than half of the classes (56% in 2009 and 60% in 2014) the CO2 concentration 
was higher than 1000 ppm. Insufficient air quality reduces pupils’ learning ability 
and performance. 
 
Detailed knowledge about associations between building characteristics and sat-
isfactory indoor air quality is missing, making the municipalities’ school renova-
tions challenging. 
The challenge is increased by variation in the number of schools and amount of 
money allocted for school renovations in each municipality. Furthermore, not two 
schools are similar, making each renovation unique.  
 
The thesis has investigated the associations between the CO2 concentration and  
building characteristics using statistical analyses conducted on two datasets.  
Regression analyses conducted in Microsoft Excel are used to produce a mathe-
matical description of earlier discovered relations for the CO2 concentration.  
The project focuses on five different dependent variables, which describes the 
CO2 concentration in a classroom.  
The equations have a R2 value between 0.19 and 0.36. Similar statistical anal-
yses were performed on residential buildings, and showed relations between the 
air change rate and buildings chracteristics by a model with a R2 = 0.09. 
It was seen that naturally ventilated classrooms had a tendency to have higher 
levels of CO2 concentrations compared to classrooms, which were ventilated by a 
mechanical system. Naturally ventilated classrooms were associated with older 
schools commonly built before 1930, while mechanical ventilation with exhaust 
only was associated mostly with schools built between 1930-1970. Schools built 
after 1970 commonly have mechanical ventilation with both supply and exhaust.  
 
Based on the performed analyses it was possible to develop a tool, which makes 
a rough estimation of the expected air quality in a classroom. 
The tool is manageable, since it is based on simple inputs that describes the 
building characteristics. Thereby it is easy to identify schools with the most ur-
gent need for improvements of the air quality.

Resumé 
Danish Science Factory udførte i henholdvis 2009 og 2014 et masseeksperiment, 
hvor elever og deres lærer fra 785 klasser skulle foretage en øjebliksmåling af 
CO2 koncentrationen og temperaturen.  I begge landsdækkende undersøgelser 
blev det konkluderet, at over halvdelen af alle landets klasseværelser (56 % i 
2009 og 60 % i 2014) havde en CO2 koncentration over 1000 ppm. Elevers ind-
læringsevne og præstation reduceres når luftkvaliteten forringes.  
 
Kommunerne mangler detaljeret viden om sammenhængen mellem en tilfreds-
stillende luftkvalitet og bygningskarakteristika, hvilket gør renoveringen af sko-
lerne udfordrende.  
Ydermere varierer antallet af skoler og kommunernes økonomiske midler til re-
novering, ligesom to skoler ikke er ens. Dette gør hver skolerenovering unik og 
omkostningsfuld. 
 
Specialet har undersøgt sammenhængen mellem CO2 koncentration og byg-
ningskarakteriska baseret på statistiske analyser udført på to datasæt. 
Regressionsanalyser udført i Microsoft Excel er blevet brugt til opstille matema-
tisk modeler der beskriver en forventede CO2 koncentration i et klasseværelse. 
De fundne ligninger havde R2-værdier mellem 0,19 og 0,36. Tilsvarende statiske 
analyser er blevet udført for beboelsesbygninger, og viste forholdet mellem luft-
skifte og bygninskarakteristika ved en model med R2 = 0,09.  
Analyserne viste, at naturligt ventilerede klasseværelser havde en tendens til 
højere CO2 koncentrationer i forhold til klasseværelser med mekanisk ventilati-
onsanlæg. Ældre skoler bygget før 1930 var oftest naturlig ventileret, mens sko-
ler bygget mellem 1930-1970 oftest havde mekanisk ventilation med udsugning. 
Skoler bygget efter 1970 havde oftest mekanisk ventilation med både indblæs-
ning og udsugning. 
 
Baseret på de udførte analyser var det muligt at udvikle et værktøj, der kunne 
give en grov estimering af den forventede luftkvalitet i et klasseværelse. 
Da værktøjet er baseret på simple inputs som beskriver bygningskarakteriska, er 
det nemt og overskueligt at anvende. Derved bliver det nemmere at identificere 
skoler med det største behov for forbedring af luftkvaliteten.

1 Background 
Numerious projects and experiments have been conducted over the years, all 
addressing different aspects of the indoor environment in schools. A majority of 
the reports concluded that pupils’ learning and performance are affected by the 
indoor environment, and with poor indoor environment comes lower test results 
and reduced concentration.  
 
In Denmark the first 10 years of education is mandatory, and the young children 
therefore spend a large amount of their time indoors. An extended school day is 
seen with the implementation of a new school reform, which introduced addi-
tional hours of teaching per week.  Increased hours of teaching combined with 
insufficient indoor environment reduces the pupils’ learning ability. Therefore it is 
even more crucial to ensure a good learning environment in the classroom. 
Many parameters (social background, motivation, different intellect etc.) influ-
ence pupils’ learning ability, however the indoor environment should not be a 
factor that challenges the pupils and makes learning harder for them.  
 
Two large measuring campaigns with focus on the indoor environment in Danish 
schools have been carried out by DSF and DTU during 2009 and 2014. The cam-
paign from 2009 concluded that 56% of the schools, who participated in the 
measurements, had a CO2 concentration higher than 1000 ppm. The Danish 
Building Regulations 2010 requires a CO2 concentraion, which must not exceed 
1000 ppm for a longer period of time. 
Several activities and awareness campaigns including a prevention package from 
Danish Health and Medicines Authority were executed in order to put more focus 
on the importance of a good indoor environment. The results from the campaign 
in 2014 showed no improvements in the indoor environment since 60% of the 
participating schools having a CO2 concentration higher than the recommended 
level of  1000 ppm as seen in Figure 1 (Toftum & Clausen, 2014). 
 
 
 
Figure 1: Comparison of the two campaigns regarding the indoor environment in Danish 
schools (Toftum & Clausen, 2014).

Despite no improvement in the air quality from 2009 to 2014 the analysis of the 
data showed significant relations between the presence of a ventilation system 
and the CO2 concentration. Classrooms with mechanical ventilation had a ten-
dency to have a lower CO2 concentration while other relations are unknown 
(Toftum & Clausen, 2014). The two campaigns raised additional questions which 
should be answered in order to achieve an improvement in the indoor environ-
ment in the Danish schools.  
  
In Denmark schools have been built for the last 150 years, with only a few new 
schools being built today. The large variations in construction years lead to dif-
ferent characteristics of the buildings. A large percentage of the school buildings 
are in need of improvements of both the physical framework and the installations 
(Alectia, 2014). 
Some of the schools have been renovated during the years, however stricter 
energy requirements in the building sector have made it difficult to create 
schools where both a satisfactory indoor environment and a low energy use is 
obtained. There has been extended focus on reducing the energy consumption of 
buildings over the years in order to achieve the climate plan 2020 and 2050 from 
the Danish Energy Agency. This extended focus has resulted in insufficient focus 
on the indoor environment when reducting the energy consumption of the 
schools, and consequently the renovations have not always improved the air 
quality in the classrooms (Energistyrelsen, 2015). 
 
An literature review showed a lack of tools to conduct easy and affordable as-
sessment of the indoor environment at specific schools. Today a detailed as-
sessment of the indoor environment can not be made without doing time con-
suming measurements that are executed by professionals in order to produce 
reliable results.   
Knowledge about the association between building characteristics and air quality 
is missing today, leading to an uncertain and doubtful basis when the decisions 
regarding school renovations are made in the municipalities and government.     
 
There is a need for a more affordable and effective method to estimate the air 
quality, and thereby identify primary schools in which the worst indoor environ-
ment conditions are seen. If such a method could be created it is assumed that it 
would be easier to allocate funds directly to the individual schools, which needs 
an improved indoor environment. Furthermore, such a method would provide 
knowledge, so school renovations in the future could be executed in the right 
way to get the highest effect and yields, and secure a better learning environ-
ment for the future generations.

2 Objective 
The two measuring campaigns in 2009 and 2014 concluded that a mechanical 
ventilation system in classrooms reduces the CO2 concentration compared with 
naturally ventilated classroom where pupils and teachers have to manually open 
widnows. It is still unclear what other building characteristics are important to 
ensure good air quality in the classrooms.  
Information about the relationship between building characteristics and air quali-
ty will result in a better understanding of how to improve the air quality in Dan-
ish primary schools. 
 
The objective of this thesis was to propose a methodology to identify school 
buildings with poor and good air quality based on simple building characteristics. 
The results of this project  described the indoor environment in general school 
typologies and identified the factors with the largest impact on the air quality in 
classrooms.  
2.1 Approach 
A literature review presented essential knowledge regarding the air quality, in 
order to define reasonable hypotheses about potential associations between air 
quality and building characteristics.  
 
Existing data collected in a large campaign conducted in 2014 posed the basis of 
additional data collection. An online questionnaire sent to all participating schools 
from MX2014, alongside measurements and registration conducted at 11 prima-
ry schools formed two seperat sets of data. Statistical analyses were conducted 
on the collected data to study association between building characteristics and 
CO2 concentration.  
 
The statistical analyses were used to evaluate the hypotheses, and as the basis 
of a tool to predict the air quality in classrooms. The tool may be used to assess 
the air quality in classrooms based on simple and easily accessible input data.  
2.2 Scope 
The objective dealt only with primary schools in Denmark, since this was the 
target group. The thesis focused on the air quality and the thermal conditions in 
a classroom, and did not investigate light and sound aspects.

3 Literature Review 
General indoor environment parameters and the applicable requirements and 
recommendations primarily regarding the air quality were presented. Further-
more, state-of-the-art research within the indoor environment in school envi-
ronments were studied. 
3.1 Indoor Environment  
To assess the quality of a good indoor environment in a room four overall pa-
rameters are important to consider; air quality, thermal conditions, sound and 
light.  
 
 
 
Figure 2: Parameters affecting the indoor environment. 
 
However, the four parameters seen in Figure 2 can be influenced by the follow-
ing: 
 
 
Design of the building  
 
Installations (e.g. type of ventilation)  
 
User behaviour 
 
Construction types and interior materials 
 
Air quality is one of the essential parameters in the indoor environment. The 
parameter is well studied and there is much data to document the effect of good 
air quality on pupils’ learning.  
 
The occupants are affected by the indoor environment in a room, and good way 
to estimate whether good or poor air quality is present, is to measure the num-
ber of CO2 molecules in the air. 
==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis | Study of associations between building characteristics and the CO2 concentration 
 
 
 
Page 16 / 95 
The CO2 concentration depends, among others, on the number of people present 
in the room, the volume of the room and the air exchange rate.  
Occupants produce different amount of CO2 depending on their activity level and 
age. A sedentary (1.2 met) adult produces approximately 18.7 liter CO2 per 
hour, while sedentary (1.2 met) children in the lower classes will produce 10.4 
liters CO2 per hour (Petersen, 2005). 
Other sources of pollution like furniture, building materials and frequency and 
quality of cleaning could also affect the air quality, however these pollutions are 
often perceived as e.g. smell. 
 
As mentioned, the indoor environment and thereby the air quality is affected by 
the building design including the  type of ventilation system present in the room 
as well as the occupants beaviour.  For instance in a classroom with a high room 
depth and a low ceiling height the air quality can be expected to be poor if no 
ventilation system is present and acceptable if the correct system is present 
(Alectia, 2014). 
 
Ventilation is thereby an important means to reduce pollution levels. Determinat-
ing the capacity of the ventilation system is typically based on assumptions 
about the number of pollutants and the geometry of the room. 
The CO2 concentration can be measured and also estimated through a calcula-
tion. The calculation includes the volume of the room, the CO2 emission of the 
occupants and the air change in the room (Dansk Standard, 2011):

COଶ,୲୭୲ୟ୪ ୰ୣ୪ୣୟୱୣ
air change ∙volume ൅ COଶ,୭୳୲ୱ୧ୢୣ ୪ୣ୴ୣ୪ 
 
The concentration of CO2 is given in ppm, indicating the number of CO2 mole-
cules among one million molecules of air. The standard outdoor CO2 level is 
around 380-400 ppm, depending on which literature is used. 
 
This report identified possible associations between the CO2 concentration and 
different building characteristics.  
A brief introduction to parameters, which was thought to have an effect on the 
CO2 concentration will be given: 
 
 
The number of pupils, since the pupils are the primarily CO2 pollution 
source. 
 
The volume, increasing the volume would lead to more room in which the 
CO2 molecules could be distributed, and thereby leading to a lower CO2 
concentration. 
 
The temperature, it is assumed that increased temperatures are caused 
by increased number of pupils. The CO2 concentration will increased with 
increased number of pupils, since they are the main pollution source. A 
relation between temperature and CO2 concentration was therefore ex-

3.3.1 Air quality

3.4 Influence of poor indoor environment on pupil’ learning 
In several studies indoor environment parameters have been shown to have an 
effect on the pupils' learning. The affecting parameters are often air quality and 
thermal conditions, and their impact will be briefly described in the following sec-
tion.  
 
The design and use of the building are parameters, which influence air quality 
and thermal conditions in the rooms. Poor values for these two parameters lead 
to a poor indoor environment which can affect the pupils’ learning ability.  Air 
quality and thermal conditions are often challenged when the energy require-
ments for buildings are adjusted (Toftum, Wargocki, & Clausen, 2011) but they 
have not always been in focus when renovating school buildings. 
 
A wide range of symptoms associated with indoor climate are documented and 
known by the common name for indoor climate symptoms. Symptoms include; 
irritation of the eyes and nose and the more common symptoms such as sense 
of heaviness in the head, fatigue, difficulty concentrating. A good indoor envi-
ronment in primary schools is important as this is the beginning of the childrens 
school experience and affects  their learning abilities and their amount of ab-
sence due to sickness (Toftum et al., 2011). 
It is important to understand that the symptoms are rarely caused only by one 
specific factor. Other causes not relevanted to the indoor environment can lead 
to the same symptoms, however when several people in the same building ex-
hibits the same symptoms it is described as the phenomenon of 'sick building 
syndrome’ (Toftum et al., 2011). 
Poor indoor air quality may also be related to diseases such as allergic respirato-
ry diseases and respiratory infections. Especially in the classrooms, the concen-
tration of dust particles can  be high since pupils are often in close contact with 
pets at home. It is now seen that one in three pupils suffer from allergies or 
asthma which often gives absence causes in school. In insufficient ventilated 
rooms with many people, the risk of transmission of common airborne infection 
between people, e.g. colds and sore throat is increased (Toftum et al., 2011).  
 
A study by Dansk Center for Undervisningsmiljø (DCUM) in 2013 based on ques-
tionnaires, showed that schools with a good indoor environment improves a good 
teaching environment and gives the pupils the best prerequisites for appropriate 
learning processes.  The study does not include scientific measurements but un-
derlines the importance of a good indoor environment and that a good indoor 
environment provides a better teaching environment due to better sound levels,

3.4.1 Effect of poor air quality 
The air quality in classrooms is important for pupils’ learning and absence level. 
A clear correlation can be seen between ventilation rate and the performance of 
typical school assignments. An increase of the ventilation rate of 6 l/s per pupil 
(current Danish requirements) to 8 l/s per pupil (current Swedish requirement) 
lead to an expected increase in performance of 6%. Similarly, if the ventilation 
rate is increased to 10 l/s per pupil the performance will increase by 10%.  Be-
sides the reduction of the performance, the inadequate ventilation and poor air 
quality also influence pupils' absence level. If a CO2 concentration above 1000 
ppm is present, there is a tendency of higher absenteeism; 10% to 20% (Toftum 
et al., 2011). 
3.4.2 Effect of unacceptable temperatures 
Even small changes in the temperature in a classroom has a significant effect on 
pupils' learning and concentration as well as their performance. Every 1 oC tem-
perature is lowered below 25 oC increases the percentage of passed pupils on a 
concentration test with 2% to 4%. A temperature of 25 oC and above might only 
seem applicable in summer, but is also seen in winter and early spring when the 
sun is low in the sky, especially when sufficient sunscreens are missing (Toftum 
et al., 2011). Studies have shown that if the temperature is kept at 20-21 oC for 
seated work, it is possible to achieve up to 20 percent higher yields. Higher tem-
peratures can lead to pupils becoming less willing to learn and lose concentration 
(Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2012). 
Pupils' behavior changes with higher temperatures as it often takes a long time 
before they adapt their clothing to ambient temperature. At high temperatures, 
it is observed that girls are troubled but continue to work, while boys are unfo-
cused and undisciplined (Toftum et al., 2011). 
3.5 Ways of improving the indoor environment 
A ventilation system has a great influence on the air quality in a classroom. The 
thesis will focus on three main ventilation principle; natural ventilation, mechani-
cal ventilation with exhaust and mechanical ventilation with supply and exhaust 
briefly explained below.  
3.5.1 Natural ventilation 
Natural ventilation is one way to improve the air quality in a classroom and can 
be achieved in several ways. However the CO2 concentration can be difficult to 
control with natural ventilation, especially if manual opening are applied.  In this 
case the users should be aware of the importance of venting the room by open-
ing the windows and not overrule any pre-programmed openings of windows. In 
addition to this, natural ventilation might not be the optimal ventilation solution 
in the winter months, since it is difficult to keep satisfactory indoor temperatures 
when the window is opened and the temperature is low outside.  
 
It is noted that the Danish Building Regulations states required ventilation rates, 
and these exact requirements might be difficult to achieve with natural ventila-
tion since no automatic settings are available. However it is possible to deviate 
from these requirements, if other measures are made.

3.7 Status of the indoor environment in Danish Schools 
A national overview of the indoor environment in Danish primary schools was 
given by two national campaigns conducted in 2009 and 2014, called Masseek-
sperimentet 2009 and Masseeksperimentet 2014. Both surveys concluded that 
more than half of the participating schools had a CO2 concentration above 1000 
ppm, which is the required limit by BR2010. The results led to the conclusion 
that no improvements were seen from 2009 to 2014, even though poor envi-
ronment was concluded in the campaign in 2009, and several campaigns were 
seen, (Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2012) regarding indoor environment in schools. 
 
Furthermore, Masseeksperimentet 2014 concluded that the CO2 concentration 
could be decreased significantly if all pupils left the classroom, and the class 
vented during the breaks. Having a ventilation system with both supply and ex-
haust led to the lowest CO2 concentration, while having no ventilation led to the 
highest CO2 concentrations. It was thereby concluded that having a ventilation 
system would improve the air quality.  
87 % of participating schools had a temperature in the acceptable range of 20-
26 oC, only a few classes had either too high or too low temperatures. This leads 
to the conclusion that generally a good thermal environment is seen in most 
Danish primary schools.

3.8 Budget for renovation of building mass 
In 2014 a new school reform was introduced to all schools in Denmark leading to 
changes in the teachers and pupils normal school/workday. More classes were 
introduced resulting in longer school days for the pupils and teachers spending 
more time in the school buildings. In the time leading up to the introduction of 
the new school reform focus was put on the physical framework of the schools 
with requirements that the classrooms should be more flexible and allow inter-
disciplinary work (Undervisningsministeriet, 2015).  
 
A large number of older primary schools do not have acceptable physical frames 
for the new kind of learning environment. A lot of renovations are required in 
order for the Danish primary schools to be ready for the new school reform and 
to provide the pupils with the best possible learning environment. Most of the 
renovation measures included improvement of the physical framework and in-
ventory. It does not seem that improving the indoor environment is of much im-
portance.  
 
Information on specific improvements of the indoor environment was difficult to

4 Method 
This section will describe the methods and approaches used in the practical as-
pects of this work both when executing measurements and performing analyses 
of data.  
 
The statistical analyses conducted in this report are based on two datasets re-
ferred to as ‘77SCHOOLS’ and ‘11SCHOOLS’. The method section will be divided 
into three sections, the first two describing how data was collected for each da-
taset and a data processing section describing the statistical approach to the 
collected data.  
4.1  Collection of data for ’77SCHOOLS’ 
The dataset 77SCHOOLS include data from Masseeksperimentet 2014 and addi-
tional data collected from online questionnaires based on the same participating 
schools as in MX2014. 
Throughout the report the reference to this dataset is 77SCHOOLS, since this is 
the number of schools that replied to the online questionnaire. 
 
In 2014 pupils in 785 classrooms conducted several measurements of the indoor 
environment as part of large campaign called Masseeksperimentet 2014. The 
measurements resulted in a dataset representing approximately 10 % of the 
total number of Danish primary schools. The dataset provides an overview of and 
insight into the indoor environment in these 785 classrooms and includes among 
others measurements of the CO2 concentration and temperature. 
  
The pupils and teachers were the ones performing the measurements under 
guidance from professionals. Pupils measured CO2 concentration in their class-
room twice, one week apart. The measurements were made at the end of the 
lesson.  
The measurements of the CO2 concentration were performed by drawing air 
through a CO2 measuring tube. This simple method indicated the CO2 concentra-
tion at the specific time of the measurements. To minimize mistakes the pupils 
had been allowed to practice a few times before the final measurement was 
made. In addition the pupils had to measure the indoor temperature at the be-
ginning of the lesson.  
At measuring day 1, the pupils were asked to use the classroom as they normal-
ly do, but were asked to keep the windows closed, in order to simulate a heating 
season scenario. At measuring day 8 the pupils had been outside during the re-
cess prior to the measurement and the classroom was ventilated during the re-
cess by opening the windows. (Danish Science Factory, 2014) 
The teachers entered the measurements online and answered questionnaires 
regarding the school building’s characteristics and user behavior.

Data from MX2014 was used in the initial regression analyses, which showed 
limited significant relations between the CO2 concentration and the given param-

In order to develop a useful model that associated the CO2 concentration with 
different building characteristics, additional information was required about the 
building and classroom.  
 
A simple questionnaire containing 17 questions was designed and sent to the 
teachers who participated with one or multiple classes in MX2014.  
The questionnaire was designed online using the web service surveymonkey.dk. 
The online questionnaire was primarily chosen since the only contact information 
available on the teachers was their email addresses making the questionnaires 
easy to distribute directly to the teachers.  
 
The questionnaire was divided into two sections. The first section contained gen-
eral information about the school and class name as well as questions on school 
level e.g., which building typology the classroom was located in, on which level 
and the surroundings.  
The next section contained questions on classroom level, with questions regard-

4.2.1 Conducting the measurements 
In each classroom the measurements were conducted for 2-3 school days. All 
schools were completed over 4-5 weeks. Approximately 10 classrooms were 
measured in each of the 11 schools. 
 
The following indoor environmental parameters were measured: 
 
 
CO2  concentration [ppm] 
 
Air temperature [°C] 
 
Relative humidity [%] 
 
Sound pressure level [dB(A) and dB(C)] 
 
A checklist was made for each room, including measurements of the sound pres-
sure level outdoors, the room dimensions, window dimensions and placement 
and information regarding the ventilation system and surroundings. Some of the 
classrooms had additional windows beside the façade windows e.g. skylights or 
windows placed either high in the outer or inner façade. The area of these win-
dows is included in the calculation of the entire window area for each room. If 
these windows could be opened, either manually or automatically, then this 
would help reduce the CO2 concentration in the room. This was noted during the 
registration and is included as a parameter in the excel sheets for each room. 
 
An additional checklist was left for the teacher to fill out and referred to the user 
behavior of the pupils and teachers during the measuring period. Questions were 
asked regarding how often the classroom was ventilated and whose responsibil-
ity it was. Both checklists can be found in appendix 2.

The geometry of the rooms and windows were measured using a laser meter and

4.2.2.1 Measurement of CO2 concentration, temperature and relative humidity 
The CO2 monitors (VAISALA) contain a sensor that measures the CO2  concentra-
tion and then transmits a current signal to the data loggers (HOBO) for storage. 
The data loggers are able to measure temperatures from -20 °C to 70 °C, with an 
accuracy of +/- 0.35 °C in the interval between 0 °C and 50°C. (Onset Computer 
Corporation, 2010) Before being able to perform the measurements the CO2 
monitors (VAISALA) needed to be calibrated.  
 
The calibration was performed by attaching the sensor to a tank of pure nitrogen 
and a tank of CO2 0.5 %. The tank of nitrogen was used to establish a zero point 
while the tank with 0.5 % CO2 was used to determine the upper limit of 5000 
ppm.  
 
The calibration is done approximately once per year on each monitor to ensure 
that they have correct reference points. The calibration is performed according to 
the surrounding conditions at the applicable time and inputs of the temperature 
and pressure are required. Figure 16 shows the calibration process.

4.2.2.2 Measurement of sound pressure level 
The outdoor sound pressure level was measured during the registration by open-
ing the window and using the sound pressure meter or the smartphone app Dec-
ibel Ultra. The measurement was performed by holding the device outside the 
window until a stable reading could be made.  
The sound pressure level was set to measure A-weighted sound pressure level 
dB(A) since it is suitable for measuring the noise level perceived by the human 
ear (Maekawa, Rindel, & Lord, 2011). Performing sound pressure levels with an 
A-filter means that the sound pressure meter is less sensitive to very high and 
very low frequencies. 
 
Some specific scenarios required a sound pressure level measurement with a C-
filter dB(C), which is practically linear over several octaves and includes more of 
the low-frequency range of sounds than the A-filter. It is suitable for subjective 
measurements at very high sound pressure levels and is done to obtain noise 
levels that the human ear is less sensitive too. The sounds were seen to be 
caused by low frequency noise below approximately 100-150 Hz. The causes of 
low frequency noise are often industry related e.g. external sources like work 
with machinery close by or road traffic.

4.3.2 Statistical methods 
All the data has be analysed using following presented statistical tools and meth-
ods. Same statistical approach was used for the two datasets.  
 
The associations between the CO2 concentration and building characteristics 
were studied by a statistical approach. 
The method and approach are briefly described in the section below divided into 
chronological steps (Vejrup Hansen, 2012). 
 
Step 1: Descriptive analyses 
The first analyses performed on the two datasets were made using descriptive 
statistical tools such as histograms, division of data into intervals presented in 
tables and calculating percentages. The purpose of the initial simple analyses is 
to be familiar with the content of the datasets and to gain an overview of what 
data is included in the datasets.  
 
A general overview of the datasets from 77SCHOOLS and 11SCHOOLS was 
gained by dividing the data into simple categories like typologies, type of ventila-
tion system and building characteristics and compared against each other e.g. an 
investigation of the associations between defined typologies and room character-
istics.   
 
Step 2: Identification of dependent variables 
The data from 77SCHOOLS only contains two measurements of the CO2 concen-
tration for the two measuring days. The measurement is considered as a meas-
urement at a certain time of the day and does not necessarily represent the 
maximum value of CO2 concentration or the average value over the whole day. 
It is chosen to work further with CO2 (day 1) and CO2 (day 8), keeping in mind 
that the two CO2 concentrations represent different user behaviors.  
 
==PAGE:53==
Master Thesis | Study of associations between building characteristics and the CO2 concentration 
 
 
 
Page 52 / 95 
The dataset 11SCHOOLS contains measurements of the CO2 concentration every 
5 min for two whole schools days. A significant amount of data is seen in the 
dataset and several values can be calculated from these values e.g. the maxi-
mum value of CO2 concentration and the percentage of the usage time where the 
CO2 concentration is above the limit of 1000 ppm.  
 
The initial processing of the data showed a large variation in the CO2 concentra-
tion for the two days for the same classroom. One reason for this could be the 
user behavior (number of pupils, venting, the specific usage of the room etc.). 
Information about the user behavior is lacking in the dataset, and it is therefore 
difficult to evaluate the CO2 concentration based on user behavior.  
It was not always the case that each class complied with the official usage time 
and e.g. leaves the class around lunch.  To obtain the most representative CO2 
concentration for the classes in the dataset 11SCHOOLS several dependent vari-
ables were chosen; the maximum CO2 concentration, the median of the CO2 con-
centration each day in the defined usage time and the percentage of the usage 
time where the CO2 concentration was above 1000 ppm. Thereby three different 
parameters could be used to predict the CO2 concentration in a given classroom. 
 
Step 3: Univariate regression analyses 
A univariate regression analysis is a statistical analysis of relations between two 
variables, a dependent and an independent variable.  
Performing a regression analysis in Microsoft Excel results in a p-value, R2-value 
and a mathematical equation can be made expressing the relation between the 
dependent variable, Y and the independent variable X. A equals the interception 
with the y-axis and B is the calculated coefficient for the given independent vari-
able. 
Y = A + BX1 + Error 
 
P-value is the direct indication of the significance and the probability of the null 
hypothesis, i.e. the coefficient b is equal to zero. Typically, the p-value must be 
less than 0.05 - equivalent to a significance level of 95%. In some cases a higher 
p-value can be accepted. The lower the P value, the lower the probability that 
the coefficient is equal to zero, leading to strong significance. This report will 
evaluate the P-values based on 4 different levels of significance, to ensure that 
no significant parameters are left out from the multivariate regression analyses.

R2 value expresses the proportion of the variation in the dependent variable Y 
that is explained by the model or line. Value is between 0 and 1, and the greater 
the better. Making a regression based on two points R2 will be 1. More points will 
decrease the likelihood of a high R2. It is difficult to state an acceptable R2 value 
for the conducted regression analyses. Similar statistical analyses were per-
formed on residential buildings, and showed relations between the air change 
rate and buildings chracteristics by a model with a R2 = 0.09. If the regression 
analyses conducted in this thesis show a similar or higher R2 value, it is consid-
ered to be an accurate model, that can be used for rough estimations of the air 
quality in a classroom (Bekö, Toftum, & Clausen, 2011).

It shall be noted that the datasets include both qualitative and quantitative data. 
The qualitative data detects various characteristics or categories like e.g.  year of 
construction given in ranges, presence of smell, occurrence of opening of win-
dows, cleaning pattern and type of ventilation system etc. The quantitative data 
detects a quantity and are described by numeric variables e.g. CO2 concentra-
tion, air temperature, volume of classrooms and number of pupils in the rooms 
etc.  
 
The independent variable X1 can be both quantitative and qualitative variables. 
The qualitative variables do not contain a numeric value and are therefore trans-
lated by using dummy variables included as 0/1 as described above. 
When dealing with qualitative data and dummy variables a reference category 
must be used for each variable implemented in the regression. The reference 
category will apply when the other variables are 0. The coefficients for the inde-
pendent variables (X1, X2, ..) are thereby an expression of the effect on the de-
pendent variable (Y) in relation to the reference category.  
The qualitative data are divided into categories, and sometimes it is seen that 
not all categories of the given variable are significantly different from the refer-
ence category. Thereby the insignificant category equals the reference category. 
 
Univariate regression analyses are performed for the two datasets with the pa-
rameters one at a time. The number of regression analyses varies between the 
two datasets since different numbers of variables are present in the datasets. 
The regression analyses are performed according to the approach and criteria 
explained above.  
 
The investigated variables in the analysis were selected datasets based on the 
background engineering knowledge presented in the literature section. The im-
portance of choosing the right variables to be included in the analysis is great 
and the results of the analysis will depend on the variables used.  
 
The purpose of the regression analyses with single parameters is to determine 
which of the parameters alone influence the CO2 concentration. After determin-
ing which of these parameters have effects on the CO2 concentration the param-
eters are then combined in multivariate regression analyses. Interrelations be-
tween parameters are neglected when performing regression analyses with sin-
gle parameters, but this will be accounted for in the multivariate analyses. 
 
Step 4: Correlation analyses 
A correlation analysis has been made in order to estimate whether there is a 
linear association between two of the parameters. This type of analysis was 
made on all the parameters combined, since two parameters with a strong linear 
correlation should not be combined as independent parameters in a multivariate 
regression model.  
 
A correlation coefficient (Pearson Product Moment correlation coefficient) was 
found in Microsoft Excel using the ‘Data Analysis Tool’. The correlation coeffi-
cient, r, ranges between -1 and +1. If a correlation between two variables is 
positive, higher levels of one variable are associated with higher levels of the 
==PAGE:55==
Master Thesis | Study of associations between building characteristics and the CO2 concentration 
 
 
 
Page 54 / 95 
other, while a negative r indicate that higher levels of one variable are associat-
ed with lower levels of the other.  
 
The magnitude of the correlation coefficient indicates the strength of the associa-
tion. A correlation close to zero indicates no linear association between two con-
tinuous variables. With correlation coefficient above or equal 0.5 two variables 
are not to be combined in the multivariate regression analysis.  
 
Based on the correlation analyses different feasible combinations have been 
identified. 
In order to conduct a useful model in the end it was desired to combine as many 
parameters as possible in the multivariate regression analyses. However, the 
combinations should only consist of parameters that are “safe” to combine with-
out being interfered by linear correlations.  
 
Step 5: Multivariate regression 
Multivariate regression analyses are conducted for each possible combination of 
dependent variables identified in the correlation analyses. 
 
Multivariate regression analysis was performed and evaluated in the same way 
as described for the univariate regressions. However, the mathematical equa-
tion, which is found for the multivariate regression will contain more independent 
variables (X1, X2, ..) 
 
Y = A + BX1 + CX2 + ....+ Error 
 
The coefficient for each independent variable is expressed by B, C, .. and A indi-
cate the interception with the y-axis. 
 
Furthermore, the tendencies were evaluated based on engineering knowledge, in 
order to assess whether the mathematical equation makes sense from an engi-
neering perspective. Thereby it was possible to select 2-3 possible linear combi-
nations for each dependent variable, which were reasonable candidates for a 
final tool. 
 
Step 6: Residual analyses 
A residual expresses the deviation between the observed value and the predicted 
value of the dependent variable. 
 
Residuals were evaluated for each selected combination to check the quality of 
the fit, identify possible outliers and check the distribution of errors.  
 
The residuals analysis contained a histogram of the error distribution compared 
to the normal distribution and a normal probability plot.

5.6 Final evaluation of equations 
The results of the possible combinations conducted in the multivariate regression 
analyses were evaluated based on the R2 value and the plot of the residuals in 
relation to the normal distribution curve.

trations a linear trendline can be made to evaluate the selected models. R2 val-
ues different from the regression models in Table 29 - Table 33 are graphically 
illustrated in Figure 27-Figure 31. 
 
The models are also compared to the data that the equations are derived from in 
order to validate the models and see how well the models estimate the CO2 con-
centration. Using information about the building characteristics from different 
classes from the two datasets, the CO2 concentration is calculated using the de-
rived equations and compared to the measured CO2 concentration from the same 
class. The results are seen in Table 34 and Table 35 respectively for the two da-
tasets. The ideal situation would be to validate the models using ‘out of sample’ 
method where new data is used to validate the model. However, this was not 
possible to conduct within the time period of this thesis (Frost, 2013).

6 Developed Tool 
The developed tool consists of two Excel spreadsheets, one for each dataset, and 
it contains equations for estimating the CO2 concentrations for different scenari-
os. When using the developed tool, five different CO2 concentrations are calcu-
lated based on the equations found from the regression analysis with the differ-
ent dependent variables. Each scenario and calculated CO2 concentration pro-
vides a picture of the indoor air quality and it is recommended that all equations 
are taken into consideration when evaluating the overall status of the indoor 
environment.  
A description of the developed tool is found is this section and screen shots are 
included to demonstrate the steps required in estimating the CO2 concentrations.  
6.1 Step 1: Gather and enter simple parameters 
The user is asked to conduct simple measurements and observations which are 
necessary in order to estimate the CO2 concentration. The parameters are kept 
simple and the measurements are easy to make to ensure that the developed 
tool is not limited to professionals within indoor environment. 
The tool is developed in Excel making it useful and accessible for the users. The 
cells that need to be changed are marked by blue and important information 
with yellow and the final estimation of CO2 concentration with green.  
 
Figure 35 shows the input cells where the users are required to enter the meas-
ured values. Drop-down menus are used where possible to make the tool easier 
to complete. 
 
 
 
6.2 Step 2: Allocation of typology 
The typology is automatically allocated primarily based on inputs describing the 
year of construction and type of ventilation in the classroom.  In the cases where 
these two parameters are not sufficient to define the typology, additional param-
eters, room height and volume are included as seen in Figure 36. The typology is 
allocated based on the findings in the descriptive analysis and the regression 
analysis.

7 Discussion 
A useful tool to estimate the CO2 concentration was developed in the work of this 
thesis. The tool includes input variables that are easy for the user to estimate or 
measure and will provide a rough estimate of expected CO2 concentrations.  
7.1 Tool to predict the CO2 concentration 
The final tool contains two spreadsheets with equations that can be used to es-
timate the CO2 concentration in a classroom for different scenarios and based on 
different input parameters. The equations implemented in the tool show that it 
was possible with reasonable validity to estimate the CO2 concentration based on 
a few simple input factors described through the characteristics of the building 
and the classrooms.  
If a school comprises buildings from different years of construction it is recom-
mended for the users to calculate an estimate of the CO2 concentration in a 
classroom in each building. All equations and their corresponding CO2 concentra-
tion will briefly be described in the following. 
 
Equation 1 will provide the user with the most accurate estimation of the CO2 
concentration during a school day in a classroom. The model estimates the per-
centage of the usage time where the CO2 concentration is above 1000 ppm. 
By calculating how much of the actual usage time the CO2 concentration is above 
1000 ppm, a rough insigt of the overall air quality can be found.   
 
Equation 2 estimates the maximum value of the CO2 concentration in a class-
room during the school day. The maximum CO2 concentration appears in peaks 
and thereby only in a small periods of the entire usage time. Worst case scenario 
of the air quality in the classroom can be estimated with this model. However, it 
should be remembered that the maximum CO2 concentration can be very high 
even in classrooms with mechanical ventilation e.g. when the ventilation system 
is not dimensioned, maintained or used correctly.  
 
Equation 3 will provide the user with a more general estimate of the CO2 con-
centration during the occupied time. Model 3 neglects the influence of high and 
low peaks of CO2 concentration during the usage time. Unlike an average value 
of the CO2 concentration the median is not affected by extreme values, e.g. max-
imum values. The median CO2 concentration provides a more overall estimate of 
the CO2 concentration during the usage time, compared to model 2.  
 
With Equation 4 the user can estimate a CO2 concentration, which is built up 
during the winter season when the pupils and teachers have a tendency to re-
duce the amount of time they open the windows due to low outdoor tempera-
tures.  
 
With Equation 5 the user can estimate a CO2 concentration, which is built up 
during the summer season when the pupils and teachers have a tendency to 
increase the amount of time they open the windows due to high outdoor tem-
peratures increasing the air change. 
==PAGE:89==
Master Thesis | Study of associations between building characteristics and the CO2 concentration 
 
 
 
 
Page 88 / 95 
 
 
When the purpose of the investigation is which classrooms and schools that 
needs to be renovated, it is considered ideal to look at the maximum and median 
CO2 concentration alongside the percentage of usage time with a higher CO2 con-
centration than 1000 ppm. This is recommended in order to gain the most accu-
rate picture of the indoor air quality in the specific classroom throughout a whole 
school day.  
7.1.1 Parameters not included in the equations 
The equations are presented in appendix 5, and it is noted that the same build-
ing characteristics are present in the equations in different combinations e.g. 
typology, type of ventilation, room height and location of the classroom. This 
pattern in parameters describing the building characteristics is considered to be 
encouraging for the reliability and usability of the model.  
 
However, it is noted that the number of pupils present in the classrooms was not 
significant. Based on the background knowledge this parameter was expected to 
be significant since the pupils are the main source of CO2 in the classroom.  It is 
seen that equation 4, which describes the CO2 concentration during a winter sea-
son, includes the parameter volume per pupil, which means that the number of 
pupils indirectly influenced the CO2 concentration. Only minor deviation of the 
number of pupils in the classrooms are seen in the two datasets, which are as-
sumed to reduce the likelihood of having a significant relation between the num-
ber of pupils and the CO2 concentration.   
 
Type of ventilation is not present in any of the equations derived from the da-
taset 11SCHOOLS. This is explained by the results from the correlation analysis 
where the type of ventilation could not be combined with the parameters de-
scribing the typology nor the placement of the classroom. However, the typology 
and type of ventilation system was proven to have a close relation in section 5.1.  
It is believed that estimating the type of ventilation system is easier and more 
objective than allocating a specific typology to the school building in where the 
classrooms are located.  
7.2 Reliability of the tool 
Analysis of the residuals were executed and presented in the results and will act 
as a background for discussing the reliability. 
7.2.1 Residual analysis of the tool 
Section 5.1.1 presented that the two datasets represented 6% and 1% of the 
entire building mass of primary schools in Denmark, and the statistic uncertainty 
was calculated concluding that only the dataset 11SCHOOLS was representative 
for the entire building mass.  
 
Despite the limited number of schools in each dataset and lack of representative 
data compared to the entire building mass of primary schools in Denmark it is 
unlikely that a better and more representative dataset of measurements of the 
indoor air quality, building and room geometry, and user behaviour exists today.  
==PAGE:90==
Master Thesis | Study of associations between building characteristics and the CO2 concentration 
 
 
 
 
Page 89 / 95 
 
 
The equations in the developed tool result in residuals lying within the normal 
distribution curves. Only minor deviation between estimated and measured CO2 
concentration were seen indicating that the equations seem acceptable for gain-
ing a rough estimation of the CO2 concentrations. 
7.3 Possible errors affecting the tool 
The discussed deviations could be explained by small errors and limitations of 
the model as described below. 
7.3.1 Excel as a statistical tool 
The program Excel was chosen as the only statistical tool when conducting anal-
ysis for this thesis, since the knowledge and abilities of the authors within the 
field of statistics was limited when starting the thesis.  
 
Limitations of independent variables 
Excel is a sufficient statistical program, but requires many steps to be done 
manually compared to other statistical programs. Excel does have some limita-
tions and these have been factors that the authors have had to consider and 
include in the work. One limitation was that when performing the regression 
analysis only 16 dependent variables could be included in order to conduct an 
analysis. Working with datasets with several qualitative variables this number 
becomes a limitation. Each qualitative variable needs to be translated into dum-
my variables, and in several cases multiple dummy variables were needed. This 
means that the maximum number of variables is quickly reached. Other statisti-
cal programs, like R, do not have this limitation. 
 
Significance level 
The significance level used in the regression analyses can be discussed from a 
statistical point of view since high p-values were accepted when evaluating the 
results of the regression analysis. From a statistical point-of-view the standard 
significance level is between 5-10%. A significance level under 20% is accepted 
in this thesis since a model containing multiple variables is desired and the high 
significance levels makes space for all significant relations to be shown in the 
results.  
7.3.2 Allocation of typologies and the effect on other variables 
Typology could cause errors in the developed tool since some uncertainties lie 
both in the definition of the typology and in the tendencies found in the univari-
ate regressions.  
The definition of each typology was frequently made based on year of construc-
tion. However, the ranges of years are only indicative, and when allocating a 
typology other building characteriscis, such as: type of ventilation, room height 
and volume, must be taken into considerations. In some cases the schools de-
spite having a year of construction within a certain range will actually be another 
typology based on the characteristics of the typology.  
 
==PAGE:91==
Master Thesis | Study of associations between building characteristics and the CO2 concentration 
 
 
 
 
Page 90 / 95 
 
Since allocating a typology can be subjective the developed tool helps to allocate 
a specific typology based on a few input factors like room height, volume and 
type of ventilation system. The relations between these input factors and the 
typology are confirmed by the simple analysis performed using descriptive statis-
tics. The problem with typology being tricky and subjective is minimized in the 
developed tool.  
 
The other issue with typology is that schools often contain buildings within dif-
ferent typologies and therefore it can be difficult to allocate just one typology to 
the whole school. Despite this difficulty in allocating the correct typology, the 
parameter was still significant in all the univariate regression analyses for all 
dependent variables. However, the tendencies varied for each dependent varia-
ble. The typology is important to the objective and were therefore kept for the 
multivariate regressions and are present in the final equations.  
7.3.3 User behavior 
As seen in the data processing throughout the thesis some of the collected pa-
rameters were directly or indirectly influenced by the user behaviour of the pu-
pils and teachers. Such parameters include usage of the shared spaces and 
number of times the windows are opened. The remaining parameters describe 
information regarding the building and room characteristics. Some of these pa-
rameters were seen to be affected by the user behaviour in different degrees, 
despite a high percentage of possible openable window area, it is up to the users 
to open them all manually. The objective of this thesis was to investigate wheth-
er it is possible to estimate the air quality in the Danish primary school based on 
information from few building characteristics. Based on the objective, the user 
behaviour of the pupils and teachers has not been the main focus of the thesis 
and has therefore not been registered thoroughly.  
 
Another aspect of the user behaviour is that it can be difficult and elusive to reg-
ister, since people's behaviour can be unpredictable. A detailed registration of 
the user behaviour in classrooms could be executed by physically observing the 
class during all school hours, placing motion sensors on the windows or providing 
detailed guideline for the user behaviour alongside CO2 concentrations measured 
using the HOBOloggers.  
 
The behaviour of the pupils and teachers during the school day has a large influ-
ence on the indoor air quality and can in some cases overrule the influence from 
the variables related to the building characteristics. 
 
Since registration of the user behaviour has not been the main focus of the the-
sis some of these variables were not included in the regression analysis and 
therefore excluded from the developed tool.

8  Conclusion 
The possibility of estimating the CO2 concentration using parameters describing 
building characteristics was investigated. Analysis shows that significant relations 
were seen between certain building characteristics and the CO2 concentration. A 
manageable tool was developed where the CO2 concentration can be estimated 
based on simple parameters which are easy to gather.  
 
In naturally ventilated classrooms the CO2 concentration was higher than in 
classrooms with a mechanical ventilation system. Two types of mechanical venti-
lation systems were seen, where a system with both supply and exhaust was 
better than a system with exhaust only.  
 
The ventilation system is the most dominating factor affecting the CO2 concen-
tration in the classrooms. However, a clear relation was seen between the school 
typology and the ventilation of the classrooms. Naturally ventilated classrooms 
were associated with older schools commonly built before 1930. Mechanical ven-
tilation with both supply and exhaust was associated mostly with schools built 
after 1970.  
 
When identifying the schools, ith the most urgent need of an improved indoor 
environment, both the typology and the type of ventilation have to be taken into 
consideration. The type of ventilation overruled the typology in some cases, 
since a mechanical ventilation system could have been implemented in schools 
built before 1930.  
Based on statements regarding the typology and ventilation system, it is rec-
ommended to prioritize school buildings with natural ventilation in planning the 
future school renovations. 
 
Another finding was that the user behaviour in some cases can have a dominat-
ing influence on the CO2 concentration and can vary a lot from class to class. In 
some cases the user behaviour determined how well the room characteristics 
could predict CO2 concentration e.g. the amount of windows opened, and use 
and maintaince of the ventilation system. Similar investigations could be per-
formed to investigate the effect of user behaviour alongside building characteris-
tics. However, this would require a detailed registration and control of the user 
behaviour.  
 
The developed tool includes the possibility of estimating different CO2 concentra-
tions based on five scenarios:  
 
 
Maximum CO2 concentration  
 
Median CO 2 concentration  
 
Percentage of the usage time where the CO2 concentration is above the 
limit of 1000 ppm  
 
CO2 concentrations in during winter season.  
 
CO2 concentrations in during summer season.

For estimating the five CO2 concentrations parameters describing the room 
and/or building characteristics are required. The findings from the regression 
analysis of the two dataset are the underlying factor in the five estimations and 
a high R2 value is seen for all regressions (R2= 0.20-0.35), indicating significant 
relations between the parameters and the CO2 concentrations.  
The work in this thesis has given an indication of where the efforts of renovating 
the primary school in Denmark should be. The findings are acceptable compared 
to other similar investigations of the influence of building characteristics on the 
indoor environment (Bekö et al., 2011).  
 
The developed tool is considered to provide the users with an insight into the air 
quality of the classrooms in the primary schools in Denmark. The tool can be 
used in the initial phase of evaluating the indoor environment of specific class-
rooms. Additional measurements and investigations should be conducted follow-
ing the use of the developed tool.
