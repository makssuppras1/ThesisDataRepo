Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to contribute to the development of a new method for carry-
ing out high quality magnetic surveys, namely the Long range Unmanned Aerial Vehicle
for high-Quality Magnetic Surveying (UAV-QMS). This thesis focuses on the measuring
instrumentation of the project, specifically the magnetometers. Three magnetometers
have been characterised, tested, and investigated to determine their suitability for use
in UAV-based magnetic surveying. The three instruments are the GSM-19 Overhauser
and GSMP-35u Potassium magnetometers from GEMSystems, and the MFAM from
Geometrics. First, the stability, sensitivity and potential drift of each instrument was
tested, and it was discovered that the MFAM drifts in the first 30 minutes upon startup.
From this test it was determined that the GSMP-35u is the most stable magnetometer
for prolonged surveys. Secondly, the relatively large heading error of the MFAM was
investigated and attempted compensated for by testing different configurations of the
two sensors. It was possible to reduce a heading error from a reported +/- 30 nT to a
Peak-to-Peak value of 2.62 nT if put in the right configuration and pointed in the right
direction. Lastly, the effect with which vibrations may have on the magnetometers was
investigated and tested, however, nothing conclusive has come of such tests since the
designed test setup turned out to produce magnetic signals itself. In the end, recommen-
dations on what magnetometers to use in both a Single Sensor Bird and a Gradiometer
Array are given. For the Single Sensor Bird, the GSMP-35u is recommended if one pri-
oritizes data quality over flying time, however, if survey length is a priority, the MFAM
can be used if put in the proper configuration. For a Gradiometer Array, a horizontal
array of 4 MFAM’s, pointing in alternating directions, is proposed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
UAV-QMS - Drone-based Magnetic
Surveying
On the 18th of December 2016 Innovation Fund Denmark granted DTU Space DKK 11
million to develop a new method for carrying out high quality magnetic surveying. The
project is called Long range Unmanned Aerial Vehicle for high-Quality Magnetic Sur-
veying (UAV-QMS), and is scheduled to run from 2017-2021. Magnetic surveying serves
multiple purposes, however, the UAV-QMS project focuses mainly on natural resource
exploration and locating unexploded ordinances (UXOs). Such airborne magnetic sur-
veys are typically carried out using airplanes or helicopters. These require pilots which
drives up price and risk, but also reduces the flexibility of the surveys. These draw-
backs can be eliminated by using a drone. In the UAV-QMS project, the MUNINN VX1
SMART UAV developed by Sky-Watch in collaboration with DTU is used [1][2].
Figure 1.1: Illustration of the main idea behind UAV-QMS - a magnetometer bird hangs
below a drone, continuously measuring the magnetic field. A UXO, and its
contribution to the magnetic field, will be measured by the magnetometer
bird.
==PAGE:21==
2
1 Introduction
The idea is to attach a magnetometer bird to the UAV as it flies above the area of interest
while the magnetic field is being recorded, as illustrated in Figure 1.1. This can be in
a Single Sensor Bird (SSB) configuration providing the surveyors with a total field map,
or in a multi-magnetometer Gradiometer Array (GA) configuration which will produce
a gradiometer map from which a total field map can be derived.
1.2
Goal of the Project
In this thesis, three magnetometers designed for magnetic surveying will be investigated.
These are the GSM-19 Overhauser and the GSMP-35u Potassium magnetometers man-
ufactured by GemSystems, based in Canada. The third magnetometer is the MFAM
produced by Geometrics, based in the U.S.A. There are two distinct goals of the project,
the first is to test and characterise each magnetometer in order to recommend the best
magnetometer to use in the UAV-QMS project. This includes making recommendations
as to what magnetometer to use in a SSB or a GA configuration. The second goal of
the project is to determine whether vibrations have an impact on the measurements of
the magnetometers.
1.3
Outline
This thesis is outlined as follows:
• Chapter 2: Theory - The theory behind the geomagnetic field is presented, as well
as an explanation of how to measure the magnetization of buried ferromagnetic
bodies. A brief overview of how each magnetometer operates is given, followed by
the theory behind magnetic gradiometers.
• Chapter 3: Performance Considerations - The important characteristics of the
magnetometers are presented and compared. The idea that magnetometers express
sensitivity to vibrations or shaking is also presented.
• Chapter 4: Test Design - The process behind the design of each test is presented.
The final design and the data processing methods are also presented.
• Chapter 5: Results - The results of each test are presented.
• Chapter 6: Discussion - The results are discussed, and the magnetometers are sub-
sequently compared to one another. A recommendation for which magnetometer
to use in a SSB configuration is given, and a discussion of a possible configuration
for a multi-sensor GA is presented and discussed. Suggestions on how to move
forward in the project from here is then given.
• Chapter 7: Conclusion - The project is concluded.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter, the theory behind the magnetic field of the Earth is presented to give
an understanding of what is being measured in a high quality magnetic survey. The
temporal and spatial variations of the field are characterised and described, followed by
a brief presentation of how each magnetometer investigated in this project works. Lastly,
the theory behind magnetic gradiometers is presented, and the benefits, drawbacks, and
possible configurations of such a system are explored.
2.1
Earth’s Magnetic Field
The magnetic field of the Earth, also called the geomagnetic field, can approximately
be described as a dipole tilted roughly 11◦from Earths rotational axis. In reality, the
field measured at a certain point on the surface is a superposition of several magnetic
sources which are commonly categorized into those of internal or external origin, or
contributions from below and above the surface. 95% of the field strength measured
at the surface comes from the outer core, where it is believed that a self-sustaining
dynamo mechanism is operating, driven by liquid metal of high electrical conductivity
vigorously convecting. A second major contributor to the internal field is the lithosphere,
or the crust, of the Earth. Here, magnetized material create local variations of varying
strength. The external contributions are mainly generated by varying currents induced
by a constant interaction between charged particles and plasma, emitted by the Sun, and
Earths ionosphere and magnetosphere. Because these currents, to a large degree, are
correlated with solar activity, they are highly dynamic and can contribute with hundreds
and sometimes thousands of nanoteslas (nT) [3].
2.1.1
Describing the Earths Magnetic Field
The geomagnetic field is often denoted as B, and is a three-dimensional vector field,
meaning that it can be described by three independent numbers, or elements. An ex-
ample of a fully described field is that of X, Y , and Z. Here, X is pointing towards
geographic North, Y is pointing towards geographic East, and Z points directly down-
ward [4]. All seven geomagnetic elements are listed in Table 2.1.

2.1.2
Temporal Variations of the Geomagnetic Field
Because the geomagnetic field is produced by large geophysical processes that vary in
time, the strength of the geomagnetic field also varies in time. These variations span
from seconds to millions of years, and they can therefore have a huge impact on a
magnetic survey. The most important temporal variations to keep track of during a
survey are diurnal variations and magnetic storms.
The diurnal variation span periods ranging from a second to roughly a day. Figure 2.1
show the temporal variation of the magnetic field strength over the course of one day.
As can be seen, the field strength decreases as the sun starts to rise, and increases again
as the sun starts to set. The effect is created by the local heating of the atmosphere
by the Sun, and thus follows the Sun around the world. This variation is called the
solar-quiet variation, or Sq, since the magnetic signal is relatively quiet when the sun
is on the other side of the Earth. However, most magnetic surveys will be conducted
during the day, so this variation will have quite a large effect if not accounted for [5].
Over the course of a day, it might also be necessary to account for magnetic storms.
During events known as Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs) the earth is bombarded by
plasma and there is an increased activity in the ionospheric and magnetospheric currents.
This increased activity results in disturbances of the magnetic field when measured on
the surface. The level of disturbance can be determined by using the Kp-index, which is
calculated every three hours. It gives a value from 1-9 of the current geomagnetic activity.
According to the U.S National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), a Kp
value above 4 indicates high geomagnetic activity, and surveys during such events should
be avoided [6].

2.2.1
Fluxgate Magnetometer
The Fluxgate Magnetometer was invented in 1936 and was used extensively during World
War II in the detection of submarines using aeroplanes. The fluxgate is built by wrapping
two cores of magnetically susceptible material with two identical coils each; a primary
and a secondary coils. An alternating current is then passed through the primary coils,
generating an alternating magnetic field, which is equal but opposite for each core, and
the cores become saturated. The effect of the alternating magnetic field is picked up
by the secondary coils. When there is no external field, the cores saturate at the same
time, and the voltages in the two secondary coils cancel each other out. However, in
the presence of an external field, saturation occurs earlier in one of the cores, causing
a difference in voltage in the two secondary coils with a magnitude proportional to
the amplitude of the external field component. The cores measure the component of
the external field with which they are aligned, meaning that in a combination of three
systems, each aligned in the X,Y and Z direction of the field, the entire field can be
measured using one instrument [8].
2.2.2
Overhauser Magnetometer
The Overhauser magnetometer operates on roughly the same principle as other nuclear
precession (or proton precession) magnetometers. A container is filled with a fluid which
is rich in hydrogen nuclei, ie. protons. These nuclei act as small dipoles, aligned with the
ambient magnetic field Be, as illustrated in Figure 2.4a. These protons precess around
the magnetic fields, like a spinning top on a table. The container is wrapped with a
coil through which a current is passed. This current produces a magnetic field, called
the polarizing magnetic field, noted as Bp. As illustrated in Figure 2.4b, the protons
shift their spinning orientation and align themselves along Bp. In order to drown out
the influence of Be, the polarizing magnetic field needs to be several magnitudes higher

3.2
Resolution
The resolution of the magnetometer is another important characteristic. The resolution
is defined as the smallest change in the magnetic field which the magnetometer can
output. The ideal choice of magnetometer is one where the counter resolution is smaller
than that change. Choosing a magnetometer that does not satisfy this criteria can lead
to qualitatization errors, meaning that the shape and character of an anomaly may be
improperly defined. This would be problematic in the case of the UXO surveys, since
it is a goal to be able to distinguish mines or bombs from other non-explosive magnetic
material.

3.3
Noise and Sensitivity
Noise is defined in the literature as any variation in the measurements which is not
caused by what is being measured. It is sometimes also referred to as sensitivity, and
different manufactures have different ways of describing the same objective: ensuring
that the smallest size anomaly can be detected by a magnetometer. This is achieved
by maximizing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Increasing the SNR can be done in two
ways. The magnetometer can be moved closer to the source of the signal, or the noise
level can be reduced. Since the instruments investigated in this report, along with the
UAV’s carrying them, are quite expensive, there is a trade-off between risk and quality
that needs to be determined. One way to move the resulting compromise further towards
quality is to try and minimize the sensitivity or noise of the magnetometer systems.
There are a number of sources of noise or unwanted signals. In Chapter 2, the noise
that is caused by natural variations in the geomagnetic field are discussed. There is,
however, also noise coming from the instrumentation itself, electrical noise signals from
the power supply, and even noise from complications in the communication between the
sensor and the recorder. In the particular case of the UAV-QMS project, there will be
a variety of unwanted signals coming from the UAV - these are attempted removed by
placing the magnetometers in a bird hanging sufficiently below the UAV.

3.4
Absolute Error and Drift
The absolute error of a magnetometer is the difference between a measurement made
by the magnetometer and the true magnetic field. This is often also referred to as the
absolute accuracy. The drift of a magnetometer is defined as the change in absolute error
over time. It is quite clear that measurements should be as close to the true field as
possible, however, the importance of this depends heavily on what the magnetometer is
used for. If the magnetometer is used for observatory purposes, then the absolute error
and drift are primary factors when choosing the instrument. In the case of magnetic
surveying, the objective is to locate anomalies in the survey region. This means that the
importance of the absolute error decreases, as long as it is consistent. If the absolute
error changes, ie. there is a large drift in the instrument, this will create unwanted
artifacts in the survey data.

3.5
Heading Errors
In Chapter 2 the theory behind magnetometers were discussed, and specifically how
scalar magnetometers operate and measure the total field strength of the magnetic field.
These measurements are dependent on the orientation of the sensor in the field. This
dependence is called the heading error of the instrument, and it is highly desirable to
minimize the effect of such an error. It becomes increasingly important in the QMS-
UAV project, since the magnetometer bird will be measuring in two different directions
as a consequence of the flight patterns illustrated in Figure 1.1. In a) the UAV changes
direction on each survey line, while in b) the direction of the survey lines are grouped. It
is possible to compensated for heading errors in post-processing by destriping, however,
it is desirable to lower the effect as much as possible.
Heading errors in magnetometers are caused by a variety of factors. A major contributor
is the material of which the instrument is build. Since ferromagnetic particles within the
sensor can contribute with a permanent magnetisation, a rotation of the sensor will also
cause a rotation of this permanent magnetisation. This variation of the total field will
be picked up by the sensor. The magnetometer can also be built using paramagnetic

CHAPTER 4
Test Design
In this chapter, the process of testing and characterising the magnetometers is described
and documented. Each test was designed to explore one of the performance consider-
ations presented in Chapter 3, so the magnetometers could be fairly compared against
one another. Additional tests were conducted with the purpose of obtaining an under-
standing of each instruments behaviour.
All tests were conducted at the Brorfelde Geomagnetic Observatory in Denmark. The
tests cannot be properly executed on DTU Lyngby Campus, or any other place closer
to civilization, since the magnetic disturbances from human activities, such as buildings
or cars, are too extensive. This presents a logistical difficulty since each test becomes
an excursion, and the execution of tests becomes dependent on the possibility for trans-
portation. However, in the period between February 2nd and June 27th 2018, a total of
8 excursions were made to Brorfelde.
The presented tests in this chapter only include tests which had a useable outcome. In
the first few months it was discovered that the MFAM development box was magnetic,
and therefore it had to be separated from the actual sensor. This rendered a number
of tests unusable and slowed down the testing process drastically until a solution was
implemented two months later. A step-response test was also conducted, however, the
data processing did not produce anything meaningful, and it has therefore not been
reported in this thesis.
Three tests are presented in this chapter. All tests results are all presented in Chapter
5. The outline of this chapter is as follows: first, the design of the test is presented
along with the thought process and iterations leading up to the final design. Then a
step-by-step guide on how to recreate the test is presented, and lastly a description of
the data processing is provided.

The setup for this test is quite simple, and did not need many iterations. All three

4.2
Characterising Heading Effect
The purpose of this test is to characterise and determine the influence of the heading
errors of the magnetometers on measurements. Since the GSM-19 Overhauser has no
heading errors, it will not be subject to this test. As discussed earlier, this is an important
performance consideration, since the magnetometers without a doubt will be subject to
twists and turns during an airborne survey. The result of this test will be a heading
error, as a function of degrees, and it is therefore desirable to obtain as good a resolution
Figure 4.3: Illustration of the a) clover-leaf test and the b) rotation test.
==PAGE:46==
4.2 Characterising Heading Effect
27
as possible.
There are a multiple ways in which the heading effect can be characterised, however,
only two were considered for this project. These are the clover-leaf test, and what will
henceforth be referred to as the rotation test, and each have been illustrated in Figure
4.3. The clover-leaf test, as illustrated in Figure 4.3 a), test begins with choosing a
point on the ground at location (x, y). The magnetometer is then carried from South
to North, and the field is recorded as the magnetometer passes over the chosen point.
Constantly turning left after each pass, the magnetometer is then carried from West to
East, from North to South, from East to West and once again from South to North.
Every time the magnetometer passes the chosen point, the magnetic field is recorded.
A stationary magnetometer records the diurnal variation over the course of the test,
and this is subtracted from the magnetometer being tested. The five values are then
compared to determine how direction, ie. the heading error, influences the output of the
magnetometer [19].
There are a number of drawbacks to using this test for the purpose of this project.
Firstly, the resolution of the heading error as a function of degrees is too low, since it
is only characterised for four different degrees. The test is useful for calibration once
flying, which is also the context in which the test was developed. Secondly, the test
has to be done in a survey-like setting, which means that the magnetometer is moving.
For this to be possible, a datalogger has to be developed which can properly collect and
store positional, temporal, and magnetic data. The development of such a datalogger is
outside the scope of this project, and therefore the heading errors have to be characterised
using another test.
The rotation test, illustrated in Figure 4.3 b), is a good alternative to the clover-leaf test.
A magnetometer is placed in the middle of a circular plate, which can rotate about its
own center. Again, a reference magnetometer is placed nearby to record the background
field.
This magnetometer should be far enough away from the magnetometer being
tested that stray fields don’t interfere with the measurements made by the reference
magnetometer, but close enough that the diurnal field can be assumed similar. The test
magnetometer is then rotated and a heading error as a function of degrees can then be
obtained [14].
This method has some significant advantages over the clover-leaf method. The test is
stationary, eliminating the need for a datalogger, since the measurements can be collected
using a computer. The resolution is tuneable, meaning that it is up to the tester how
many degrees is in between each turn.

4.3
Testing for Vibration Sensitivity
The purpose of this test is to determine if vibrations of the instruments have an impact
on the measurements made by the magnetometers. As mentioned in Chapter 2, earlier
in the QMS-UAV project, abnormal oscillations were detected when the magnetometer
was shaken. These oscillations were first of all observed when characterising magnetic
fields induced by the electronics in the UAV, and a second time when attempting to
locate UXO’s in Køge Bay.
It was a challenge to design the proper test for investigating the effect of vibrations,
since nothing in the test setup can be magnetic. The first iterations involved making a
platform vibrate using sound, however, speakers operate on magnetism, so that option
was ruled out. A second idea was to make a platform vibrate using a pnuematic vibrator,
but these operate using metal balls in the center, which would create significant magnetic
distubances. After consulting Professor Ilmar Santos, it was clear that a more simple
approach to the problem existed.
4.3.1
Final Design
In Figure 5.28 the test setup is illustrated. The test stand is constructed using two
triangular stands, upon which an aluminium knife-edge is placed. An alumnium beam
is then placed unto the two knife-edges, and if the aluminum beam is struck, it will
begin to vibrate at a certain frequency

In the following, each configuration will be presented with a picture of each MFAM
configuration alongside its Lobe diagrams. The top Lobe diagram is of the two sensors
individually, while the bottom is of the mean between the two. Each configuration’s
performance will be presented with a table of statistics and a description of its behaviour.
It should be noted that a limit of 40 nT has been put on the Lobe diagrams for two main
reasons. First, theoretically the heading errors should not exceed +/- 30 nT as discussed
in Chapter 3. Since what is being plotted is not the actual heading error but the gradient
between the test magnetometer and a reference magnetometer, this variance needs to be
captured. When comparing the Lobe diagram it became clear that 40 nT was a fitting
limit. Second, in order to fairly judge each configuration against each other, they must
be plotted with the same scale.
Please note that the optimal shape of the Lobe diagrams is a perfect circle, since this
would indicate that there was no heading error in the magnetometer.
So what the
configurations are being judged upon is not as much the value of the gradient between
the test and the reference magnetometer, but more the consistency of this value with
respect to degrees, ie. how close to a circle the Lobe diagram is. This is also quantified
in the statistics tables, where the optimal peak-to-peak (PP) value is 0 nT, since
PP = max(Fmag) −min(Fmag),
(5.1)
where Fmag is the gradient between the test and reference magnetometer. The PP of
each half circle of the Lobe diagram is given as well, with north being the lower half,
from 90-270◦going clockwise, and south being the other half.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In the following, the performance of each magnetometer will be discussed, as well as
the heading error characterisations of the MFAM. From this discussion, a list of optimal
configurations of the MFAM in a potential gradiometer is discussed. This is followed
by a discussion of the vibration test and the effect of vibrations on the magnetometers.
Lastly, everything is summarized and the recommendations are presented and discussed,
followed by a presentation of what should be worked upon in the future following the
conclusion of this project.
6.1
Magnetometer Performance
6.1.1
Sensitivity and Stability
It is evident from the long duration tests that all three magnetometers have the sensi-
tivity required to be used in aeromagnetic surveying. The raw data from the MFAM is
much more noisy than that of the other magnetometers as the sampling rate is signifi-
cantly higher. But it was also shown that using the right filtering techniques, the MFAM
SNR could be lowered drastically to almost match those of the others. This provides the
MFAM with a huge advantage since it is the only magnetometer with which it is possible
to properly sample the everpresent 50 Hz from the powergrids. This is not possible with
the other magnetometers, and there is actually a risk that this noise is aliased into the
data of the other magnetometers measurements.
It is clear, that the GSMP-35u is the most stable magnetometer of the three. The GSM-
19 Overhauser showed jittering behaviour in both tests, and the MFAM has a tendency
to sometimes fall out, and misread the field greatly, causing multiple outliers in the data.
One of the MFAM sensors, the Mag2, also shows abnormal behavior in the start up, the
reason for which has not yet been investigated. Fortunately, the number of outliers in
the MFAM data is rather low and differ significantly from the true measurements, which
makes it fairly easy to distinguish and remove them from the dataset.
==PAGE:83==
64
6 Discussion
6.1.2
Drift
It is important that the magnetometers do not drift over a short period of time during
a survey. A significant drift over the course of two hours, as an example, will create
artifacts in the data potentially making it more difficult to properly measure anomalies
in the magnetic signal of the crust, but also properly describing artifacts such as UXOs.
As mentioned in the results, neither the GSM-19 Overhauser nor GSMP-35u Potassium
magnetometers show any signs of significant drifting over the course of an hour, while
the MFAM drifts significantly in the first 30 minutes of operation before stabilizing. On
one occasion, the two MFAM sensors even showed drifting between the sensors.
The reason for the drift observed in the MFAM can have many different origins, the most
likely being that of temperature. It can be the temperature of the electronics used to log
and transfer the data to the computer, or the temperature of the actual sensors. Before
an OPM can operate properly, the liquid inside the cell must be heated until it reaches
a gaseous state. The magnetometer then starts operating, however, the temperature
still rises for a bit before it stabilizes. According to Geometrics (see Appendix A), the
MFAM has four temperatures that can be logged:
1. Board temperature: The temperature of the printed circuit board (PCB)
2. Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) temperature: Monitors the FPGA - if
it gets too hot the MFAM shuts itself down.
3. Compass and Accelerometer/Gyroscope temperature: Two separate temperatures,
one for compass, and one for accelerometer/gyroscope. The purpose of these is to
allow for temperature compensation if desired.
Unfortunately, the temperature of the sensors are not reported, meaning that a simple
investigation of the relationship between sensor temperature and magnetic field reading
is not possible as of right now.
It is possible to simply investigate the relationship
between the temperature of the electronics and magnetic field reading by logging the
temperature of the compass or accelerometer/gyroscope, since these a located inside the
electronics box.
It is of course an option to simply turn on the MFAM and letting it run for 30-40
minutes before starting the survey in order for the magnetometer to stabilize. There are
no indications of drifting after stabilization has been achieved, but it does present some
other challenges. This will also solve the problem of the abnormal behaviour discussed
in the sensitivity section above, since this has only been observed at the start-up of the
magnetometer. Letting the magnetometer run before a survey will require power, either
from a battery or from another source (in the field, it will most likely be from a battery),
and 30-40 minutes are effectively cut from the possible survey times. If it is determined
==PAGE:84==

that the drifting is correlated with temperature, it should be possible to quickly change
batteries after the initial heating and thereby regaining the lost survey time.

As discussed above it is possible, using MFAM Configuration 2 to reduce the PP value

6.4
The Effect of Vibrations on the
Magnetometers
The test presented in the results of the effect of vibrations on the magnetometers showed
that a signal was being generated and picked up by the vibrating magnetometer. How-
ever, the same signal was also picked up by the magnetometer which was not moving.
If the signal was a consequence of the magnetometer being shaken it would not have
been picked up by the stationary magnetometer. Neither would it have been measured if
the signal was a consequence of the vibrating magnetometer moving in a gradient. The
signal has to come from the movement of the test setup, and indeed it did. The beam
was made of aluminum, and aluminum is a conducting metal, meaning that when it is
moved in Earths magnetic field, small electrical currents are induced in the aluminum
which in turn produce their own magnetic fields. It is these induced magnetic fields
from the aluminum that are being measured. This can be accounted for by replacing
the aluminum beam with a beam made of a non-magnetic and non-conducting material.
In fact, a test was performed using a ”beam” of wood as the vibrating platform. In this
test, there were no signs of a signal being generated from the vibrations, however, the
data from this test has been lost and it therefore not possible to document this result
as of yet. It has also not been possible to find any documentation of this effect in the
literature.
The oscillations which sparked the investigation into the effect of vibration on magne-
tometers were measured while the magnetometer was on a boat. Assuming that tests
conducted in the near future document that low frequency vibrations do not have an
effect on the output of a magnetometer, there are several reasons why such oscillations
might have been measured. It is highly likely that there was magnetic material on the
boat at the time of the survey, and a rocking motion of the boat could have caused the
magnetic field to change at the position of the magnetometer. If the boat was made
made of conductive material, then the same effect that was observed from the vibration
test stand would also be present here, ie. induced magnetic fields from eddy currents in
the material of the boat.
==PAGE:87==
68
6 Discussion
6.4.1
Swell Oscillations
It is also possible that the oscillations actually came from the movement of the water
upon which the boat was sailing. Such oscillations have been documented earlier, and are
called swell oscillations, or swell noise. The signals are generated from electric currents
induced in the seawater, which is conducting, by the movement of water particles in the
Earths magnetic field. This means that the oscillations could have come from the actual
waves in the water at the time of the survey. Nonetheless, this is an effect that needs to
be accounted for when airborne surveys are conducted above water [21] [22].
6.5
Summary - Recommendations
6.5.1
Single Sensor Bird
Using the results of the tests, as well as the performance considerations in Chapter 3,
the recommendations are given in the following. If a SSB is desired, one should refer to
Table 6.1 in order to make a decision about what to prioritize.
The GSMP-35u is the all-around most stable, robust and reliable magnetometer out of
the three. It will deliver the highest quality data, even though the MFAM has a higher
sampling rate. If, however, the duration of survey time is of higher priority than stability
and heading errors, one should go with the MFAM in a configuration corresponding to
that of Configuration 2. This will result in an increase of time spent post-survey data
processing, but it is possible.
6.5.2
Gradiometer Array
For the GA, the recommendation is to build it using MFAM magnetometers.
The
amount of time and energy that can be won by using the MFAM rather than the GSMP-
35u’s is immense, at the cost of very little heading errors, once compensated for by
proper configuration and post-processing.

6.6
Future Work
There are multiple exciting prospects of the QMS-UAV project with regards to the
magnetometers that need to be worked upon in the future:
• A datalogger has to be developed for the MFAM and the GSMP-35u. This will
open the door for a variety of interesting tests, one being actually conducting a
test flight with the MFAM and the GSMP-35u to compare the quality of the data.
• The optimal configuration of the MFAM suggested by the manufactures Geomet-
rics themselves has to be tested in the same way as all the other configurations
to see if truly is the optimal configuration. If so, the prospects of using such a
configuration in a GA needs to be investigated.
• A GA has to be developed and built if the gradient continues to be of interest to
the project, and the recommendations of this thesis can be used if one wishes to
continue using the MFAM.
• According to an engineer at Geometrics, Lynn Edwards, a new generation of the
MFAM is to be released in the near future (see Appendix A). It is claimed that the
heading error will be lowered substantially, at the expense of a small increase in
noise. It will be possible to switch between a low heading error modes and low noise
mode, depending on what is needed for a specific survey. This new generation of
MFAM should be investigated in the future, to see if they significantly outperform
the previous generation.
• Finally, the vibration test setup for needs to be finally developed to rid it of
moving conducting material. Once this has been achieved, proper testing of low
frequency vibrations influence on a magnetometers readings can be investigated. It
is also interesting to investigate whether high frequency vibrations have an impact,
however, some work is needed in order to find a non-magnetic method for such a
test.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The goals of the project were to investigate three different magnetometers designed for
magnetic surveying by characterising and testing each of them. The three magnetome-
ters were tested on stability, sensitivity and drift over three tests. It was shown that
the GSMP-35u was the most stable of the three, and that the MFAM showed a drifting
behaviour in the first 30 minutes upon startup. Heading error characterisations of differ-
ent configurations of the MFAM were made in order to find the optimal configuration,
ie. the configuration that showed the lowest variance in field strength as a function of
degrees. It was shown that is possible to reduce a potential error of +/- 30 nT to 2.62
nT if the MFAM is put into the right configuration. A test which would help determine
whether low frequency vibrations had an effect on magnetometer readings was designed
and executed, however, it was determined that the signal measured came from the actual
test setup rather than the magnetometer.
In the end, it was recommended to use the GSMP-35u in an SSB if data quality is
prioritized over flying time, however, if survey length is deemed more important, the
MFAM can be used if put in the proper configuration. For a GA, a horizontal array
of 4 MFAM’s, pointing in alternating directions, is recommended over one made of two
GSMP-35u.

There are four different temperatures measured inside the MFAM that can be displayed.
Here is a description of the four:
1) Board Temp: The sensor measures the temperature of the PCB close to the master

2) FPGA Temp: This temperature sensor is built into the FPGA die and is used to
monitor the FPGA core temperature. The FPGA is the hottest part in the MFAM. If
this temperature gets too hot the MFAM will shut itself down.
3) Compass Temp: This temperature sensor is built into the compass integrated circuit.
The purpose of this is to allow temperature compensation of the compass data, in case
anyone wants to do that.
4) Accelerometer/Gyro Temp: The Accelerometer and Gyro sensors are on the same
integrated circuit: BMI160. There is a temperature sensor built into this chip as well.
The accelerometer and gyro readings are already internally temperature compensated,
but the temperature is also output with the data for further compensation if desired.
5) The temperature of the sensors is held constant under normal operation and not
reported.
If you are recording binary data (as compared to ASCII data) then the Board and FGA
temperatures are present in the data. They’re multiplexed in with the Compass/Ac-
celerometer/Gyro fields and would have to be decoded. If you’re saving ASCII data
then these fields are not available. The SPI packet specification needed to decode the
binary data is attached.

Magnetometer Tests – 26/04/18 
1. Introduction 
This report outlines the tests that should be conducted on the Gem Systems GSMP-35U Potas-
sium magnetometer and the Geometrics MFAM magnetometer on Thursday 26th of April 2018. 
These will be the first tests conducted on the MFAM after the development board have been 
separated from the instrument. 
 
The tests must be completed in a relatively magnetically quiet location - therefore the Brorfelde 
Geomagnetic Observatory have been chosen as a suitable location. 
 
2. Investigation 
The overall intent is to determine whether the MFAM magnetometer can be used in UAV mag-
netic surveying. 
 
On testing day, three different investigations will be made; (1) An investigation of the precision 
of the MFAM magnetometer, (2) an investigation into the effect that vibrations have on the mag-
netometers, and (3) an investigation of the gradient measured by the MFAM. 
 
3. Experiments 
3.1 
Precision test 
The experiment will be conducted in the Absolute House at the Brorfelde Geomagnetic Obser-
vatory. 
 
3.1.1 Equipment

∙ Geometrics - MFAM Magnetometers 
∙ GEM Systems – GSMP-35U Magnetometer 
∙ 2 laptops with GEMLink and MagViewMFAM installed 
∙ Suitable platform to place magnetometers 
  
3.1.2 Method

Turn on the GSMP-35U Magnetometer and make sure that data is collected with 
GEMLink.

MagViewMFAM. 
3. 
Place the GSMP-35U on the platform and record the strength of the magnetic field. 
4. 
Remove and replace the GSMP-35U with the MFAM and record the strength of the 
magnetic field.

Compare the two values.
