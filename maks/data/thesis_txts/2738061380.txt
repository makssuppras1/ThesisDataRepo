Abstract
In this thesis we develop rigorous foundations for describing the behaviour of
a probabilistic functional programming language (pFL), posing a particular em-
phasis on the behaviour of recursive functions. We introduce the syntax and
the type system of the language, as well as its formal semantics, which maps
programs to their described probability distribution.
Recognising that the semantics of recursive functions are often not com-
putable, we present methods for bounding their described distributions. Specif-
ically, we present techniques to compute lower and upper bounds, along with
conditions under which iteratively computing these bounds generates a sequence
that converges to the precise semantics of the function. Additionally, we provide
methods for verifying whether a given distribution is an upper bound. All of
these methods have been proven sound and have been formalised in the Lean 4
theorem prover.
Finally, we study the behaviour of pFL when functions are limited to a
deﬁned subset of the language in which only recursive calls of a particular form
are allowed.
For such functions, we provide a simple closed form for their
semantics.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Uncertainty surrounds our everyday life. We often interact with systems that
do not behave in a deterministic manner, but rather, probabilistically. These
range from real-world systems that are inﬂuenced by randomness, like computer
networks (which are impacted by the randomness involved in the transportation
of packets), to programs that voluntarily use randomness in order to achieve
their purpose, like cryptographic protocols (which use randomness in order to
ensure that some security properties are satisﬁed) and Monte Carlo algorithms
(which use randomness in order to trade correctness for eﬃciency). It is therefore
important that we develop theories and tools that help us express, model and
reason about these systems, in order to understand how they behave.
Probabilistic Programming Languages [9] (PPLs) provide explicit support
for modelling systems with such random behaviour, for example, through state-
ments that enable the sampling of values from probability distributions. When
reasoning about programs written in these languages, it is not enough anymore
to only think about their output as a single value, but it is instead necessary
to reason about the distribution of their possible outputs. A multitude of tools
[2, 6, 7, 10, 11, 19, 20, 22, 23] have been developed to achieve this goal, and
broadly, they can be split into two categories: languages that use sampling in
order to give an approximation of the distribution of the output, and languages
that use logic in order to reason exactly about their behaviour. Although the
ﬁrst category of systems is easier to implement and describe, it is inherently
imprecise. In fact, even though these tools are able to quickly give an approx-
imation of the distribution of the outputs of a program, they will often yield
vastly diﬀerent distributions on every attempt [3].
A tool that uses the latter (exact) approach is dice [13], a PPL created by
Holtzen et al. in 2020, built with the goal of making a discrete probabilistic lan-
guage that can be eﬃciently reasoned about. dice, however, like many similar
PPLs, is quite limited in its expressiveness, as it does not allow its programs to
have any form of unbounded iteration. This thesis aims to improve the expres-
siveness of the dice language, by allowing the expression of additional categories
of programs that contain recursive functions, in order to allow reasoning about

1.1
Contributions and synopsis
In this thesis, we give theoretical foundations for a language that allows the
expression of recursive functions, and provide and prove correctness of methods
to reason about them. Speciﬁcally,
• we provide a formal description of the syntax, type system and semantics
of a probabilistic functional language, in particular, with respect to the
recursive aspects of the language;
• we describe and prove correctness of methods to reason about the bounds
of recursive functions through ﬁxpoint iteration and invariant reasoning;
• we ﬁnd a closed form for computing the precise semantics of recursive
function in a limited subset of the language; and
• we provide an executable Lean [18] formalisation1 of the semantics, along
with computer-checked proofs for the main results of Chapter 3 and Chap-
ter 4.2
Having outlined the main contributions, we now delve into a more detailed
discussion of what is treated on each chapter.
Chapter 2.
We provide an overview of the language that we will analyse in
the rest of this thesis (pFL) by means of several examples, presenting the main
constructs of a probabilistic programming language, and the challenges that
arise when reasoning about such a language.
Chapter 3.
We formalise pFL, providing its syntax and type system, and pre-
senting its formal semantics, which maps each program to their described sub-
probability distribution. Additionally, we oﬀer an informal explanation of the
semantics’ intuitive behaviour. To ensure that the semantics are well-deﬁned,
we prove statements about the domain of distributions (that distributions form
a pointed complete partial order) and about the semantics itself (that they are
Scott-continuous).
Chapter 4.
We provide alternative methods to reason about functions in the
form of bounds. In order to do so, we adapt the semantics to work with semi-
distributions, a generalisation of sub-distributions. We then give an intuition on
the domain theoretic properties of the semantics, and we show how lower and
upper bounds can be computed by iterating from the least and greatest element

1.2
Related work
This work is based on the dice language introduced by Holtzen et al. [13]. As
previously mentioned, many other languages that attempt to solve the problem
of expressing and reasoning about probabilistic programs have been described
and implemented [2, 6, 7, 10, 11, 19, 20, 22, 23].
Techniques for reasoning about probabilistic programs that support un-
bounded iteration have already been introduced: Benjamin Lucien Kaminski,
in his thesis [14], presents a calculus to reason about programs in pGCL [15,
17] (a simple imperative language that supports probabilistic statements). In
particular, he reasons about the weakest-preexpectation [17] of a program, that
is, the average value of a formula on the program’s output. In the rest of his
thesis, he also provides a calculus to reason about expected runtimes, as well as
rules for proving termination of programs.
A diﬀerentiating factor of this work lies in the focus on a functional language
(which gives the semantics a diﬀerent ﬂavour, as loops are replaced with func-
tions, and state is lost), as well as reasoning about distributions of outputs as
opposed to solely focusing on the expected value of a function on the program’s
outputs, as similarly done by Kozen in [15] where he introduced semantics for
reasoning about the distribution of pGCL-like programs.
For pGCL programs, Batz et al. [4] have worked on methods to synthesise
super-invariants of expectations given some constrained candidate upper bound
(which may not be inductive), using a CEGIS-style loop.
Hark et al.
[12]
introduce a proof rule to verify sub-invariants for a certain category of programs
that are ”positively” almost-surely terminating (that is, with a ﬁnite expected
runtime). Furthermore, Batz et al. [3] provide a formula for reasoning in a
precise manner about the execution of programs in a variant of pGCL similar to
that which we present in Chapter 5.

Chapter 2
Overview
In the rest of this thesis we will consider a simple probabilistic functional lan-
guage, which, for short, we will refer to as pFL. The presented language is an
extension of the probabilistic language dice [13] which, unlike pFL, does not
support semantics for recursive functions (or any kind of unbounded iteration).
In this section, we will take a look at a series of examples in order to understand
what kind of programs we can express in pFL, as well as what their intuitive
behaviour is.
We begin with an example that only makes use of simple constructs such as
tuple construction and destruction, and the probabilistic flip expression.
Example 1. Double Coin Flip
Consider the following question: what is the probability that, when two fair
coins are ﬂipped, one of them will ﬂip to heads, but not both? This question
can be encoded in pFL with the following program.

probabilistic expression that evaluates to true half of the time (with probability
0.5), and that evaluates to false the other half of the time (with probability
1 −0.5). The program then evaluates to the last expression: the conjunction
between fst coins || snd coins (one of the two coins ﬂipped to heads) and
not(fst coins && snd coins) (but not both of them).

to true (if the two coins ﬂip to diﬀerent values) and sometimes to false (if the
two coins ﬂip to the same value). As we know with what probability the two
ﬂips will evaluate to true or false, we can reason not only about what values the
program can evaluate to, but also with what probability. In this example, the

When studying the probability distribution of some system, we often do not
want to study its overall distribution, but rather its distribution only when some
event occurs. This is where the concept of conditioning [5] comes into place.
Consider a scenario in which we want to study the behaviour of a test for
some rare disease, and in which we want to ﬁnd out if it is a good test. We then
may check if the probability of the test giving a false negative is low enough. In
order to do this, we would not simply look at how likely it is for a person to get
a negative result (which would be almost all the time, as most people will not
be positive of the rare disease), but we will look at that probability given that
the tested person has the virus.
In pFL, this concept is encoded via observe expressions, which restricts the
probability space to only those executions that satisfy the observed expression.

Example 2. At least one head
Consider the following question: after ﬂipping two (fair) coins, we observe that
(at least) one of the two has ﬂipped to head; what is the probability that the
ﬁrst of the two coins ﬂipped to head? We can model this scenario in pFL with
the following program:
let x = flip
0.5 in
let y = flip
0.5 in

x

of the two coins has ﬂipped to head), and then evaluates to x (the value that
the ﬁrst coin ﬂipped to).

When ﬂipping the two coins, we have four possible execution paths. Each of
these scenarios is equally likely, having a probability of 1/4. However, one of
these four execution paths does not match our observation that at least one of
the two coins has ﬂipped to head (the one in which both coins ﬂipped to tails).
What we’re really asking is then the following: assuming that we’re in the space
of events in which only the ﬁrst three events are possible, how likely is it that
the ﬁrst coin ﬂipped to head? As the total probability mass of the ”possible”
outcomes is 3/4, and the ﬁrst coin ﬂipped to head with a total probability mass

In pFL, however, we choose not to rescale the distribution to have a total prob-
ability mass of 1. Instead, we leave the resulting distribution ”as is”, with a
probability of 2/4 of evaluating to true, and a probability of 1/4 of evaluating
to false. In pFL, in fact, programs do not represent regular distributions, but
sub-distributions, that is, distributions with a total probability mass of at most
1. Note that, nonetheless, one can normalise a sub-distribution by scaling it by
the inverse of its total probability mass.
To justify this choice, we can think of the missing probability mass as a measure
of ”executions that do not count”, allowing us to also study how likely it is for
the scenario that we have observed to occur. This same behaviour will show up
when dealing with non-terminating programs, allowing us to understand how
likely it is for the program to terminate.
In this thesis, we (mostly) focus on the behaviour of recursive functions,
which pose a number of challenges when reasoning about them. We take a look
at two examples of programs that make use of recursive functions.
Example 3. Keep Flipping
The following program takes a fair coin and ﬂips it. If it ﬂips to heads, it return
true; if it ﬂips to tails, it tries again from the beginning.
fun
keep_flipping (x: bool): bool {
if x then x
else
keep_flipping (flip
0.5)
}
keep_flipping (F)
Clearly, this program never returns false; but can we say that it always returns
true?
Interestingly, this program does not always terminate.
In fact, there
exists the remote possibility of the coin ﬂipping to tails an inﬁnite number of
times, not letting the program terminate. This, however, is extremely unlikely:
ﬂipping to tails n times will happen with probability 2−n. At the limit, we see
that ﬂipping to tails an inﬁnite number of times happens with probability 0.
This is an example of a program that does not always terminate, but terminates
with probability 1. In the literature, programs of this form are said to be almost-
surely terminating [25].
Next, we take a look at an example of a program that does not terminate
with some probability greater than 0.

We, lastly, take a look at a program in which a recursive function contains
an observe expression.
Example 5. Keep Observing
Consider the function from Example 3. We modify it by adding a conditioning
expression at the beginning of the body, checking if the received argument is
equal to false.
fun
keep_observing (x: bool): bool {

if x then x
else
keep_observing (flip
0.5)

Note that almost all executions will, eventually, not match the observed expres-
sion: the only run that can be considered valid is the one in which the function
keeps ﬂipping to tails on its recursive call, event which, as noted before, hap-
pens with probability 0. As the only valid execution is the one that does not
terminate, we can conclude that the program evaluates to both true and false
with probability 0. In this case, the missing probability mass describes how,
with probability 1, the conditioning expression (observe) will eventually fail
(as, to not fail, it would have to succeed inﬁnitely often).
This category of programs behaves especially badly when analysed with sampling-
based techniques. These techniques run the analysed program operationally,
evaluating each ﬂip expression to either true or false. After running the program
a large number of times, these techniques check how many times the program

has evaluated to true, and how many times it has evaluated to false, throwing
out those executions that do not (seem to) terminate, or in which an observation
has failed. When the program misses a large amount of probability mass (that
is, when the function often does not terminate, or when it is unlikely to satisfy
the observed conditions), many of the runs will have to be thrown out, making
the sample size a lot smaller within the allocated analysis time. Consequently,
these methods often yield widely varying distributions from run to run unless
substantial computational resources are used to increase the number of runs. In
the literature, techniques have been developed to estimate how long a program
should be sampled for in order to have a ”good” distribution [3].
Due to these challenges, we develop formal foundations to reason exactly
about programs containing recursive functions, even in cases involving condi-
tioning expressions.

The syntax of the language follows.
Deﬁnition 6. Syntax of pFL programs
Every program p in the language is constructed according to the following
grammar, where r ∈R∗

A program p is a sequence of functions terminated by an expression. A function

formalisation of the language assumes programs to be in administrative normal
form (ANF) [24], i.e. all complex expressions must be split up into a sequence
of let-expressions. It is important to note that it is immediate to translate any

Semantics prerequisites
A pFL program is nothing more than a description of some probability distribu-
tion. In this section we lay the foundations towards the deﬁnition of a function
󰌻·󰌼that maps a program to its represented distribution. Here we will deﬁne many
of the concepts that were informally introduced previously in Chapter 2. We be-
gin by describing the concept of probability along with the operations that can
be used to manipulate them, the concept of sub-distribution (the mathematical
entities that are described by an expression), and the concept of parameterised
sub-distributions (the entities that correspond to functions). One can refer to
[5] for a more in-depth treatment of probability theory.
3.2.1
Probabilities
At the core of our semantics, we have the concept of probability [5]. Probability
is a measure of the likelihood that a particular event will occur. Concretely, a
probability is a real number between 0 and 1, where a probability of 0 represents
an event that cannot occur, and a probability of 1 represents an event that is
certain to occur.
Deﬁnition 11. Probability
A probability is a real number p in the set of real numbers between 0 and 1
(inclusive) R∗
≤1.
In the case of pFL, the events that we examine are those of an expression eval-
uating to some particular value. We will often multiply the probability of two
(independent) events, in order to obtain the probability of both events happen-
ing at the same time, and add them, in order to obtain the probability that one
of the two (mutually exclusive) events will occur.

3.2.2
Distributions
While the concept of probability describes the likelihood of an event occurring,
what we’re really interested in is not the likelihood of any particular event, but
the distribution of all possible events, that is, the likelihood of an expression
evaluating to every particular value of its type.
While regular probability distributions have a total probability mass (the
total sum of probabilities in the distribution) of 1, i.e. one of the events must
happen, the distributions that we work on need not to satisfy this constraint.
In fact, in our semantics we allow not only for distributions, but also for sub-
distributions, a generalization of distributions that only need to have a proba-
bility mass of at most 1.

Conditioning.
The semantics of an observe statement are, essentially, split
into just two cases: either the value being observed is true, in which case
󰌻observe T󰌼= [T] · 〈T〉= 〈T〉,
or the value being observed is false, in which case
󰌻observe F󰌼= [F] · 〈T〉= 0.
Essentially, the expression retains all probability mass when the observed value
is true, and gives it all up when the observed value is false. Note that the value
being observed has to be an atomic expression of type bool, and cannot be a
more complex (possibly probabilistic) expression. Because of this, the semantics
may be surprising: we may expect the semantics of an observe expression to
be more complex, as they need to handle the ”removal” of possible executions;
however, because the programs we consider are in A-normal form, this behaviour
is, in a way, handled by the semantics of the let expression.
Moreover, note that when the value being observed is true, the expression
evaluates to true: this is arbitrary; indeed, the expression could also have eval-
uated to false, as we do not really care what it evaluates to, but only about the
loss of probability mass.

semantics to handle the case in which the guard is probabilistic.

is, X = 0. In general, what we really want is to always choose the ”smallest”
possible X, that is, the solution with the least probability mass. This element
is called the least ﬁxed point [1] of Φ.
We will now prove that such an X always exists, and we will formally de-
scribe the solution that we want to pick. We begin by formalising the concept
of ”smaller distribution”, by deﬁning an order pointwise on sub-distributions,
parameterised sub-distributions and tables.

only be moved around but cannot negatively inﬂuence the mass that was al-

that this holds also for conditioning expressions: although an observe may
remove probability mass, adding mass to the table can only result in some of
that added mass being thrown out, but cannot result in more of the ”already

least as large.

Finding bounds
Although we have found a description for the meaning of recursive functions,
this does not mean that we have found a concrete way to compute it. In fact,
computing sup{Φn(0) | n ∈N} involves ﬁnding the limit of the sequence
0, Φ(0), Φ2(0), . . . ,
task which, for many functions, is not computable in general. Although we may
not be able to ﬁnd the precise distribution that describes a function, this is not
always needed: when reasoning about a system, we may only want to know some
bounds on its distribution. Often, in fact, we only care about some good event
happening often enough, or some bad event happening rarely enough. In this
chapter we will explore how we can exploit our semantics in order to deduce how
to reason, in an easier way, about bounds on the described distributions, and
what it even means for a distribution to be bounded by another distribution.
4.1
Lower bounds
As our semantics work with distributions, we do not deﬁne our bounds on the
probability of some speciﬁc event happening, but on the (parameterised) sub-
distributions themselves. For us, a lower bound of a sub-distribution, is simply
one that has less probability mass pointwise (that is, it is smaller according to
the previously deﬁned ordering). Note that this will almost never be a proper
distribution as, unless the lower bound is exactly equal to the value we are
bounding (and unless that value has full probability mass), it will not have a
total probability mass of 1. This loss of probability mass makes it so that, when
reasoning about lower bounds, we can no longer interpret the missing mass of a
distribution as the probability of the function not terminating, but only as an
upper bound of this probability.

Chapter 5
Closed forms
While it is true that reasoning about the bounds of a function is often enough,
sometimes we do want to know the precise distribution that a function describes.
Although we do not have a way to compute this in general, this does not mean
that we cannot ﬁnd such a method for a class of programs that has enough
”structure”.
In this chapter we will consider a restricted subset of the language which
limits what kind of recursive calls are allowed. We will provide an intuitive
understanding of the subset and explain its signiﬁcance, before delving into its
formal description, and into how we can reason about functions of this form.
5.1
Overview
Reasoning about recursive functions, in general, is a tricky task. Recall that a
function, essentially, is an expression parameterised over a list of parameters.
When a function is not recursive, working out its semantics is relatively straight-
forward: when the function is called, it is enough to instantiate its body with
the provided arguments and evaluate its semantics; however, when functions are
recursive, simply instantiating the parameters is no longer suﬃcient as the ex-
pression may depend on itself. This issue is further complicated by the fact that
each instantiated expression may depend on a diﬀerently instantiated expres-
sion (as the function may call itself with diﬀerent arguments), creating complex
graphs of interdependence that make reasoning about the function diﬃcult.
To address this complexity, one must either consider each possible input sep-
arately (a task that becomes exponentially harder with an increasing number of
parameters) or must manually examine the speciﬁc function to identify patterns
in its behaviour. Alternatively, one can limit the ways in which a function can
depend on itself, greatly reducing this complexity and making it possible to ﬁnd
methods to reason about these limited functions in general.
We take a look at an example of such a limited function, and show how its
structure allows us to ﬁnd a clear way to reason about its semantics, before

Chapter 6
Conclusions
In this thesis we have provided a formal description of the syntax, type sys-
tem, and semantics of pFL, a probabilistic functional language that allows for
the expression of recursive functions. Methods to reason about the bounds of
recursive functions (and programs) have been described and proven correct; in
particular we have provided a method to compute lower and upper bounds, as
well as a method to check if an upper bound is correct. For a small subset of
the language, we have found and proved correctness of a closed form for the
semantics of functions.
6.1
Future work
This work can be expanded in various ways, which we present in the order that
similar topics have been addressed throughout this thesis.
A more expressive language.
It may be interesting to explore whether or
not extending the language to more complex types breaks our results. In par-
ticular, the language could be extended with bounded integers (which, however,
can already be encoded in terms of Booleans), unbounded integers (introducing
inﬁnite types), rationals, and reals (introducing uncountable types).
Super-invariant synthesis.
Although we have described a method to check
for correctness of a super-invariant, we have given no clear way on how to ﬁnd
such invariants (apart from ﬁxpoint iteration, which usually does not converge
to the semantics). Although one can reason manually about the program to
ﬁnd these invariants, it would be of value to ﬁnd methods that can synthesise
these invariants for, at least, a certain category of programs.
Sub-invariant reasoning.
Just as we have given a formula to check for up-
per bounds (in the form of super-invariants), we may want a rule to check
for correctness of lower bounds. It would be beneﬁcial to explore how similar
69
==PAGE:72==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS
70
approaches described in the literature for weakest-preexpectations [12] can be
adapted in the context of pFL.
A more general closed form.
The closed form that we have found in Chap-
ter 5 only works for a very limited family of programs. Future work could in-
volve dropping some of the imposed constraints. It seems to be possible to use
a similar method to reason about functions that are not parallely-constant, by
dividing the graph of calls into simpler cycles, but this has not been thoroughly
explored. It is, however, not clear to us, how the tail-recursiveness constraint
could be removed.
Runtime analysis.
It may be interesting to reason not only about the distri-
bution described by a pFL program, but also about its runtime, by mapping a
program to a distribution on its possible runtimes (that is, a distribution on the
natural numbers). Note that this is actually relatively complex: such a distri-
bution would not have a ﬁnite support, and the results that have been achieved
for our semantics may not hold in the case of inﬁnite (although discrete) distri-
butions. Furthermore, such a distribution would not be expressive enough: as
on the presence of a let expression let x = e1 in e2 one has to reason about
the distribution of e2 on each possible value that e1 may assume, it seems to be
necessary to do the same when reasoning about the program’s runtime, need-
ing to distinguish between the runtime of an expression on each of its possible
outputs. It would not be enough, therefore, to reason about the distribution of
runtimes, but it would be necessary to reason about a distribution on runtimes
parameterised over the possible outputs of the expression. This, however, has
not been thoroughly explored, and is left for future work.

Proof of monotonicity from Scott-continuity
Let f be a Scott-continuous function. Then, let a, b ∈D such that a ≤b. We
want to prove that
f(a) ≤f(b).
Note that, as f is scott-continuous, we have that
sup{f(a), f(b)} = f(sup{a, b}) = f(b).
Furthermore, as f(a) ∈{f(a), f(b)}, we have that
f(a) ≤sup{f(a), f(b)}.
It follows that
f(a) ≤f(b).
Thus, f is monotone.
□
