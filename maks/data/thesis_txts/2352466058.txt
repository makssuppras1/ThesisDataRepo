Marcin Sawczuk – Development of a nanomaterial for selective extraction of naphthenic acid 
II 
 
Abstract 
Magnetic core@shell Fe3O4@SiO2 nanoparticles with functionalized surface are possible 
effective means for removing naphthenic acid (NA) from the heavy crude oil. This work is 
focused on proving this concept by testing process of NA extraction from the model oil (hexane) 
using the functionalized nanoparticles, as well as naphthenic acid desorption from the 
nanomaterial in MeOH, MeOH + NH3(aq) and NH3(aq). Amine group, pyridine ring and 
tetrazole ring are used as ligands attached to the nanomaterial surface in order to increase its 
affinity to the NA. 
Magnetite nanoparticles of 6÷8 nm were synthesized by precipitation from the Fe2+ and Fe3+ 
solution in the basic pH range and under the ultrasonic treatment. Magnetite was then coated 
with silica by the means of TEOS hydrolysis in the ethanol-water-NH3(aq) mixture under the 
ultrasonic treatment to obtain core@shell structures. Thickness of the coating varied from 8 
nm for coating single cores up to 70 nm when the large magnetite agglomerates were coated, 
resulting with the of sizes of the nanoparticles ranging from 25 nm to more than 200 nm. 
Surface functionalization of obtained nanomaterial was done in the Dean-Stark trap by 
precipitation of silanes from toluene and their further modification to obtain functionalization 
with amine group, pyridine ring and tetrazole ring. Successful functionalization was confirmed 
by the means of the FT-IR spectroscopy. 
Dispersing around 200 mg of nanoparticles of each kind allowed for lowering NA concentration 
in hexane from 2% to 1.71÷1.83%, depending on the nanomaterial used. At the same time, 
NA of the smaller molecular weight turned out to be more mobile and adsorbed easier on the 
nanomaterial. Desorption allowed for recovery of the significant amount of the NA. However, 
the mass balance exceeded 100% in most of the cases, proving errors in quantification. 
Analysis was done using ESI-MS method. 
For the samples from adsorption experiment, around 2% of NA in hexane, mean measurement 
uncertainty was estimated to ±0.07% of NA. For the desorption in MeOH, at concentrations 
around 0.5%, uncertainty oscillated around ±0.20%. Desorption in MeOH + NH3(aq) resulted 
with concentrations around 0.05±0.04% (wt), therefore quantification was not possible. For the 
desorption in NH3(aq), NA concentrations were below the detection limit. 
Conclusion from the project is, that the concept was proven. Significant adsorption of NA 
occurred for all the nanomaterials. Moreover, nanomaterials seem recoverable as mass 
balance of the adsorbed and desorbed NA closes for most of the cases. NA quantification 
methods still have to be improved.

Chapter 1. 
Introduction 
This work is an attempt to find a novel solution to the problem of naphthenic acid removal from 
the crude oil and, as a consequence, facilitate production and processing of a heavy 
hydrocarbon resources. Naphthenic aid, predominantly present in the heavy crudes and

According to the studies, heavy oil has few properties that might contribute to its high viscosity. 
First, it has a high average molecular weight, with C20+ fraction present predominantly. 
Moreover, many of those heavy particles are not the straight-chain structures, but rather much 
more complex isomers. Those include branched, polycyclic and aromatic structures, 
contributing to the asphaltenes and resin fractions. Methodology for their separation is given 
in the Figure 1-3 [7]–[9].

One of the theories regarding heavy oil formation is that it is created in the conditions of poorly-
sealed, young reservoir [2]. Because of the formation age, there was no sufficient time for 
degradation of the complex hydrocarbon structures into simpler and lighter components. 
Moreover, poor sealing and resulting exposition to the environment allows for mass exchange 
and biodegradation of the reservoir. In such conditions, heterocyclic, organosulfuric and 
oxygenated compounds can be formed. As a consequence, heavy oil typically contains high 
percentage of sulphur, nitrogen, oxygen and metals (Figure 1-4). Mixture complexity, resulting 
primarily from the young formation age, is further increased by the presence of the heteroatoms 
migrating into the reservoir. This change of a chemical composition contributes to the physical 
properties, such as viscosity and density.

1.2 Naphthenic acid properties 
Naphthenic acid is an oil fraction corresponding to up to 3% of the total mass of the heavy 
crude. It is believed to contribute to the high viscosity of the heavy oil and cause several flow-
assurance problems. Hence, extensive research is conducted to determine its properties [11]. 
From the chemical point of view, naphthenic acid is a generic name for the mixture of 
cyclopenthyl and cyclohexyl organic acids of the molecular weight averaging between 120 u 
and 700 u [12]. Due to the presence of a carboxyl group, they are Lewis acids and therefore 
increase the TAN of the oil [10]. Usually, these compounds have a highly developed molecular 
structure. Molecules with heavily branched chains and polycyclic compounds are commonly 
present among the NA. General formula of the NA is expressed as CnH2n+ZO2 [12], [13]. Z 
parameter is equal to 0 for the fatty acids and decreases depending on the saturation of the 
compound and the number of ring structures within the molecule. This parameter is therefore 
convenient for classification of the molecules forming the NA (Figure 1-7).

NA is formed of the saturated compounds and only traces of the unsaturated and aromatic 
components are present in this group [12]. Moreover, it is considered as a part of the resin 
fraction of the oil [9]. Due to the resulting properties (high boiling point and molecular weight), 
it is predominantly present in the heavy diesel and fuel oil fractions of the refining products 
(Table 1-2). Oil-thickening action of NA is not yet fully understood. As mentioned before, 
intermolecular forces creating hydrogen bonds between the polar parts of the particles are 
suspected to be the key contributor to the phenomenon (Figure 1-8).

Naphthenic acid is amphiphilic, due to its hydrophilic carboxylic group and hydrophobic tail of 
the particle. Such compounds are soluble in both polar and non-polar solvents, with solubility 
in water usually much lower than the one in the non-polar solvents. This behaviour originates 
from the size disproportion between the carboxylic group and the rest of the molecule (usually 
of high molecular weight)[14]. In the two-phase liquid systems they can act as surfactant. As 
such, NA deposits on the oil-water phase interface, increasing stability of the droplets. 
Therefore, high content of the NA enhances formation of emulsions and causes foaming. 
Although, it is not exclusively NA property – fine solids, asphaltenes or other resin components 
might also contribute to the emulsion stability [9]. 
From the practical point of view, naphthenic acid causes some severe problems for the oil 
production, processing and HSE. First of all, extreme viscosity of the crudes with high content 
of NA requires implementation of special production methods, described in detail in the next 
section. Despite those techniques, recovery rates for some reservoirs can be as low as 5% of 
the initial oil in place [2]. Additionally, high viscosity also causes complications for the further 
stages of production. Transportation of such crude is costly due to the large pressure drop and 
requirement for heating-up the pipelines. Moreover, refining of heavy crude is troublesome. 
Due to the scarcity of the light fractions, desired as fuels, further processing by cracking is 
needed. Those are high-temperature processes requiring expensive catalysts, which tend to 
become inactivated in the process very quickly. All those factors contribute to the price of the 
final refining products.

Apart from the contribution to the oil viscosity, naphthenic acid causes several other flow 
assurance problems. Metal naphthenates are salts of the NA, which often deposit in the 
equipment used for the oil processing and storage. Insoluble naphthenates obstruct the flow, 
lower efficiency of the oil processing equipment and finally lead to the installation shutdown for 
cleaning (Figure 1-9). Deposition of the NA salts, so-called naphthenate scaling, increases 
operational expenditure by affecting maintenance schedule and increasing necessity for 
replacement of elements such as packed beds and catalysts. Removal of the deposits is 
particularly complicated, as they harden when exposed to the air. During one clean-up 
operations of the FPSO vessel producing oil from one of the Nigerian fields, more than 30 tons 
of the calcium naphthenate had to be sent onshore. Moreover, operation required shipping 
additional tools, due to the air-hardening of naphthenate deposits. Total cost of the six-week 
operation was estimated to 6 million USD [15]. 
 
Figure 1-9: Hydrocyclone filled with the naphthenate deposit [16] 
NA was reported to be the main contaminant released in the process of bitumen production 
from the Canadian tar sands, therefore making it serious concern from the HSE perspective 
[17]. Although no carcinogenicity was reported, NA can cause skin irritation, allergies and 
serious eye irritation. Oral LD50 for rats was reported to be 3 000 mg/kg, but it may be toxic to 
this species in chronic exposition from the dose of 60 mg/kg [18]. NA is classified as harmful 
to the aquatic life, with LC50 for fish over the exposure of 96 hours equal to 5.6 mg/l [12]. 
Research from Canada confirms that acute toxicity caused by the contamination from the 
bitumen production is unlikely, but long-term adverse health effects on the wildlife are highly 
possible. 
Finally, naphthenic acid causes so called NAC – naphthenic acid corrosion, leading to the 
damages of the oil-processing equipment [11]. NAC is considered a “sweet” corrosion, 
resulting from plain dissolution of steel in acidic environment, which leads to pitting in the steel 
surface. Several research projects investigating NAC were conducted, showing that it is a 
complex process, dependant on the crude composition, temperature, flow and type of steel.

1.3 Scope of the project 
The project addresses an issue of how to decrease effects of the NA presence, influencing the 
crude oil flow properties, equipment durability and the environment. This task should be 
accomplished preferably by the NA removal – some adverse effect of the NA presence can be 
inhibited, but only removal of those compounds allows for targeting all the negative effects in 
one operation. It is clear that the selective extraction of the NA would be beneficial for both the 
production and the mid-stream processing, as both stages are affected by the high viscosity, 
acidity of the crude and the deposition of the naphthenates. Moreover, environmental impact 
of the oil production could be lowered – by applying more sophisticated methods for the NA 
removal, less water and chemicals could be used. 
This project is a first attempt to deal with this problem, was not supported with any previous 
research, and as such it does not aim at providing a complete solution for the problem. It is a 
proof of the concept work, meant to show possibilities within the area and become a foundation 
for further research. 
1.4 Proposed solution 
Functionalized Fe3O4@SiO2 nanoparticles are considered an interesting option for an NA 
binding/extracting agent. Magnetite core allows for easy separation of the nanomaterial and 
crude. Moreover, due to the SiO2, the structures are stable in a vast range of conditions – silica 
shell makes them resistive to virtually entire range of pH, high temperatures and presence of 
oxidants and organic solvents. Finally, functionalization process allows for attaching organic 
molecules to the nanomaterial surface, and therefore modifying the surface properties 
according to the needs (Figure 1-10) [19]–[21]. Moreover, their synthesis method is well-
established, so they are available for commercial use [22].

An important advantage of the Fe3O4@SiO2 structures, in comparison with functionalized 
colloidal SiO2, is the ability for easy magnetic separation of the suspension after the extraction. 
Magnetite is well known for being the most magnetic among all the naturally-occurring 
materials [23]. Bulk masses of magnetite are ferrimagnetic, meaning that the material has a 
permanent magnetic moment resulting from the properties of its crystals (Table 1-3, Figure 
1-11). However, small magnetite fines represent superparamagnetic behaviour, i.e. their 
magnetism depends on the presence of an external magnetic field [24]. Without external 
magnetic field, their magnetic moments change randomly due to the temperature-induced 
vibrations of the crystalline network. Overall, they agglomerate only to a limited extent and their 
affinity to steel is very small. However, when the external magnetic field is present, magnetic 
moments of the magnetite fines become stable and they start to represent ferrimagnetic 
behaviour typical for larger magnetite crystals. It would be especially valuable in the industrial 
scale, as presence of the magnetic field can be a factor determining behaviour of the 
nanomaterial. Without external magnetic field, they do not disturb the flow; when the magnetic 
field is present, they can be easily separated and removed from the mixture for recycling. 
Superparamagnetism is only present when the grain size of magnetite is smaller than the size 
of a magnetic domain in the crystal. Magnetic domains are regions within the material in which 
magnetization is in a uniform direction, and their size and form depend not only on the 
properties of the crystalline network, but also largely on the size and form of the analysed 
crystal itself. For magnetite, they are reported to have from 50 nm (for the cubic magnetite 
crystals) to 600 nm or more for elongated shapes [25]. Sizes of the fines in a typically obtained 
nanofluid oscillate at around 10÷20 nm, therefore such material should be superparamagnetic.

Nanoparticles have to be protected from the destructive influence of the environment. 
Magnetite itself is not a very stable compound, especially when it has as high specific surface 
area as the NPs do. Exposed to the environment, it can be easily oxidized or damaged by the 
extreme pH. In order to prevent it, each of the fines is coated with a layer of glass. Those shells 
not only prevent the NPs from damages, but also allow for attaching functional groups, which 
modify surface properties of the obtained material [21]. 
Functionalization of the surface allows for increasing affinity of the nanomaterial to the NA. It 
is done by the hydrolysis of the silanes on the SiO2 surface of the nanoparticle shell. Depending 
on the functional groups attached, a certain types of bonds formed by the organic silicon are 
fairly stable, while other hydrolyse easily. Silanes used for functionalizing the SiO2 surface 
typically have three easily hydrolysing bonds (usually hydrolysis of esters to alcohol and silicic 
acid is used) and the forth one which is much more stable (Figure 1-12).

When such hydrolysis of the less stable bonds occurs on the negatively-charged surface of 
silica shell, silicon atom of the silane becomes incorporated into the structure of the silica, while 
maintaining the non-hydrolysed bonds intact [27]. By proper design of the ligands, organic 
compounds of the desired properties can be attached to the surface of silica. Three functional 
groups and non-functionalized silica are taken into account when considering functionalization 
of nanoparticles for the naphthenic acid removal (Figure 1-13): 
1. Amine group is a functional group containing a basic nitrogen atom with a free pair of 
electrons. Amine group is a derivative of ammonia in which one or more hydrogen 
atoms are replaced with organic carbon. It interacts with the NA by its free pair of 
electrons on the basic nitrogen. Presence of amines was proven effective contributor 
to lowering viscosity of the oil, as well as amine group is a popular binding agent for 
the NA [28]–[30]. 
2. Pyridine ring is structurally related to benzene ring, with one of the –CH– links 
replaced by the nitrogen atom. It has the properties typical for both aromatic 
compounds (due to the resonance structure of the ring), and tertiary amines due to the 
free pair of the electrons on the nitrogen atom. Pyridine in the water solutions is slightly 
basic. Similar to the amine, its interaction with the carboxylic acids is expected to occur 
by the means of the free electron pair at the nitrogen. No literature showing application 
of pyridine-derivatives for NA extraction or thinning of the heavy oil was found. 
3. Tetrazole ring is a synthetic, heterocyclic structure consisting of the four nitrogen 
atoms and one carbon atom. It is considered a bioisostere for the carboxylic group. 
Due to its pKa slightly lower than the ones reported for the NA, it is expected to become 
part of the three-dimensional structures formed by the NA and therefore trap it on the 
NPs surface. Such application of tetrazole was not found in the literature. 
4. Non-functionalized silica is used to assess efficiency of the functionalization as NA 
extracting agent. Non-treated Fe3O4@SiO2 nanoparticles are used as a blank sample. 
It is particularly useful to determine influence of the other factors than the functional 
groups presence on the NPs-NA interaction. Such factors could include large specific 
surface area of the nanomaterial, surface charges promoting NA adsorption or changes 
induced by the presence of the dispersant phase used for forming the nanofluid.

Chapter 2. 
State of the art 
This chapter covers the topic of current state of the art in the field of naphthenic acid handling. 
There is a special emphasis on analysing solutions similar to the one proposed in this work, 
but alternatives are described too. On top of that, one section is dedicated to the current 
methodology in the nanoparticle synthesis and surface modification. 
2.1 Similar solutions 
Literature research revealed only two works of the similar scope. Both are relatively new 
(master thesis from the June 2015 and journal paper from the November 2013) and come from 
the two top-ranked European universities. 
Researchers from the Imperial College of London tried to apply magnetic nanoparticles with 
polymer coating for selective extraction of the NA from the model oil [31]. Special emphasis 
has been put on the hydrophobic properties of the polymer, as well as on the good dissipation 
and colloidal stability of the nanoparticles in the solution to prevent sedimentation. Design 
incorporated a swelling polymer, allowing for absorbing the NA within the polymer structure, 
not only on the NP surface. Effectiveness of this solution was proven in the paper. For the 
recovery of the NPs, methanol was used as an agent causing desorption of the NA from the 
polymer. Recovery of 98% of the NA was reported, showing that interaction between the 
polymer and methanol is stronger than the one between the amine group and NA. Several 
cycles of adsorption-desorption were performed, showing some degradation of the NPs, 
resulting with lowering performance. 
Master thesis from the Norwegian University of Science and Technology covers the topic of 
Fe3O4@SiO2@amine application for the selective removal of the aromatic organic acid (4-
heptylbenzoic acid – Figure 2-1) from the model oil (octane) [32]. The work mentions some 
preliminary research done at the NTNU before, but no trace of that research was found in the 
publications. This thesis was focused on the amine-functionalized nanoparticles. Both 
commercial nanofluid and the one synthesized by the author were tested. In order to monitor 
the adsorption process, UV-vis spectrometry was applied. It is important to notice that the 
organic acid used for the experiments is a derivative of a benzoic acid (pKa 4.2), in which 
placement of the aromatic ring affects charge distribution within the carboxyl group making the 
molecule more acidic than the aliphatic analogue (pKa 4.9 for the cyclohexanecarboxylic acid) 
[33]. Use of such compound facilitates quantification by the means of UV-vis, but it should be 
kept in mind that only trace levels of such species are present among the real NA components 
and the results for the real NA might be different.

2.2 Current solutions to the NA problem in the industry 
From the industrial point of view, the main challenge present while producing the crudes 
containing high percentage of the NA is their high viscosity. In order to overcome that problem, 
several methods are used. Cold Heavy Oil Production with Sand (CHOPS) involves using the 
downhole pump for producing both bitumen, water and sand in the same time, with further 
separation on the surface. It allows for overcoming the pressure drop problem, as the entire 
formation is produced, but is mainly applicable for non-consolidated sandstone reservoirs, 
such the ones present in Canada [2]. 
 
Figure 2-2: Steam-assisted gravity drainage; one of the thermal EOR methods applied for 
heavy oil recovery [34] 
Several methods for EOR are used as well – steam injection, various methods of in situ 
combustion and solvent extraction (Figure 2-2). Those methods are energy-consuming and 
their effectiveness is poor. In some cases as little as 5% of the initial oil in place can be 
produced. Another option is to dilute heavy crude with the lighter fractions. This however, is 
not the most cost-effective solution, as highly valuable light fractions have to be used for the 
production of low-quality, heavy crude. Finally, for the extremely viscous bitumen sands, open 
pit mining is used (Figure 2-3). 75 m is a limiting depth for this method [2].

In order to solve the rest of the problems related with high TAN of the oil, NA has to be extracted 
from the crude. To remove the NA from the already produced crude oil, liquid-liquid extraction 
can be used. Use of basic solutions, such as aqueous solution of NaOH or KOH, allows for 
extracting NA from the organic phase to the water phase, as formed naphthenates are water-
soluble soaps [14]. It could allow for solving all the issues related to the NA presence in one 
operation. Problem with that approach is that soluble naphthenates are very good surfactants 
[36]. Therefore, the separation of such oil-water emulsion becomes complicated. That leads to 
lowering recoveries and production of a harmful waste, but so far this is the only complete 
solution for the NA removal on the oil production stage. 
2.3 Naphthenic acid handling in the laboratory 
As there are only a few methods for the NA handling used in the industry, numerous research 
projects are focused on solving that problem. In the laboratories, several solutions for the NA 
extraction or degradation have been already developed. Some of them are meant to be 
developed into the large-scale solutions for the industry, while others are methods for 
extracting the NA from the crudes for further analysis and properties estimation in the 
laboratory scale. 
2.3.1 Catalytic decarboxylation 
Use of the steel slag, by-product from the steel industry, for the catalytic decarboxylation of the 
NA was analysed [37]. Crude oil of high acidity (TAN = 4.79 mg/g and S=1.022%wt) was treated 
in the thermo-catalytic processes with powdered slag. Degradation in 300°C and 350°C was 
monitored over the period of 6 hours by the negative-mode ESI-MS characterization of the 
degradation products. 
==PAGE:32==
Marcin Sawczuk – Development of a nanomaterial for selective extraction of naphthenic acid 
17 
 
Slag was characterized by various analytical methods. It turned out that the catalyst was 
composed mainly of the calcium carbonate, silicon oxide and magnesium oxide. Out of those 
three, magnesium oxide was reported previously as a mean of promoting organic acid 
decarboxylation. Experiments gave promising results, as 43.50% of the NA was 
decarboxylated after 6 hours of treatments in the 350°C. 
Catalytic decarboxylation of NA was first reported by Zhang in the year 2006 and gave a rise 
to many research projects analysing this field [38]. This first work showed that pure magnesium 
oxide catalyst is an effective means to lower TAN of the crude oil with high NA content in the 
tubular flow reactor. More than 90% acid conversion was observed in the temperature of 
250°C. 
2.3.2 Catalytic esterification 
Esterification of the NA with alcohol, primarily methanol, was examined by Wang [39]. In his 
work, he synthesized tin dioxide catalyst, which was then used to facilitate reaction between 
the NA and methanol. Reported optimal concentration of the methanol in the crude was 5%wt 
and the concentration of SnO catalyst (mesh 100) was 4%wt. 
Samples of different compositions (proportions between the crude, MeOH and the catalyst) 
were kept in the autoclave for different periods and in varying temperatures in order to find the 
optimal conditions for handling the esterification process. Blank samples without the catalyst 
were analysed too. 
Good performance of the catalyst was reported. In the optimal temperature of 300°C 89.50% 
drop of the NA content was observed. That corresponded to the TAN decrease from 4.10 to 
0.43 mgKOH/g. Interestingly, increase of the methanol concentration or reaction temperature 
seemed to decrease the overall performance of the process. 
2.3.3 Amino acid ionic liquids 
Amino acid ionic liquids were used for the liquid-liquid extraction of the NA from the crude oil 
[29]. For some cases, the process allowed for removal of more than 40% of the NA. AAILs 
were analysed to reveal mechanism of the extraction and possibility of recycling the ionic liquid. 
Deprotonation of the carboxyl group by the amine leads to the formation of zwitterion (dipolar 
ion; molecule without overall electrical charge, but with the stable local charges within the 
particle). It turned out that the process is a reactive extraction and the mechanism is formation 
of the zwitterionic complex of amine and carboxylic acid. This process can be reversed by acid 
wash. Instead of the wash with mineral acid, typically used in the industry to recover the NA, 
wash with carbonic acid is used. It allows for separating the NA from the ionic liquid, while still 
letting for easy recovery of the AAIL for further use.

2.3.4 Ammonia solution in ethylene glycol 
Another means of LLE of the NA could be by the use of basic solutions of the organic solvents 
[30]. In that particular paper, authors investigate use of the ammonia solutions in the ethylene 
glycol for the LLE extraction and recovery of the leaching phase. High performance of the 
method (more than 85% of the NA extracted) was reported. Optimal conditions for the process 
were found out to be at 50-60°C and around 3-5% of NH3 content in the glycol phase. 
Moreover, recovery of the glycol phase was proven feasible. It is washed with ether to remove 
the ammonia naphthenate formed during the extraction. Ether can be then distilled and the 
soap decomposes in 150°C, releasing pure NH3. 
The main drawback is the need of large quantities of the leaching phase. 
2.4 Nanoparticles and functionalization process 
According to the IUPAC, nanoparticles are particles of a diameter within 1÷100 nm [40]. Such 
classification results from the change of material properties between the molecular scale 
(properties strongly influenced by the quantum effects) and macroscale (properties 
independent on the quantum effects), occurring normally around 100 nm to 500 nm, thus 
making nanoparticles a link between the molecular-scale properties of a chemical compound 
and a bulk volume material. Several synthesis methods are used for producing nanoscale 
materials. Pyrolysis of the substrate, condensation from the high-temperature gas using an 
inert gas stream and precipitation from the solution (either through the hydrothermal synthesis 
or the sol-gel method) are the most common and widely used in the laboratory and industrial 
practice [21]. 
Such-obtained nanoparticles usually require coating to prevent them from undesired 
interaction with environment and enhance stability of the suspension. Typical method of 
protection is forming a core@shell structure. Silica, carbon, precious metals and polymers are 
commonly used for that purpose. Such coating not only protects nanoparticles from 
degradation, but also enhances stability of the nanofluid. Shell formed of an inert material 
enhances dispersion of the NPs, which would otherwise form agglomerates. Moreover, shells 
are often formed of the materials which allow for surface functionalization, in order to modify 
properties of the NPs [26]. 
Surface functionalization is one of the surface modification processes used for material 
science. It is applied not only in the nanomaterials, but also in several other fields. In the 
electronics, it is used for passivation of the semiconductor surface, creating nanopatterns and 
modification of the interfacial electron transfer. Biomedical science uses surface 
functionalization for creating biocompatible material which could enhance tissue regeneration 
==PAGE:34==
Marcin Sawczuk – Development of a nanomaterial for selective extraction of naphthenic acid 
19 
 
process, but also for as sophisticated applications as mimicking the body tissue to avoid 
reaction from the immune system. For the everyday use items, surface functionalization is 
used extensively, for example to change surface properties of the lenses or to create 
hydrophobic coatings. 
Magnetite nanoparticles are usually created in the sol-gel method, in which they precipitate 
from the solution due to the pH change. They are particularly interesting because of their high 
density and magnetic properties. Due to their tendency to agglomerate and sensitivity to 
environmental conditions, they are often coated with a silica shell forming Fe3O4@SiO2 
structure. This is usually done by degradation of the silanes in the suspension of nanoparticles. 
Such nanomaterial is then functionalized to obtain desired properties. Several methodologies 
for the silica functionalization are reported in the literature. Most of them are based on the 
same concept – mixture of an organic solvent, water and silane is put in contact with the 
surface, which will be functionalized. That leads to building-in organic molecules into the 
structure of the silica shell, and therefore permanently modifies surface properties of the 
material [19], [20].

Chapter 3. 
Methodology 
This chapter describes the final methodology for the experiments, which resulted from the 
multiple corrections of the initial approach. They were necessary mainly due to the problems 
arising during the experiments. These problems, as well as solutions allowing for transition 
from the initial concepts to the final ideas and descriptions of the auxiliary experiments, are all 
described in details in the Appendix A. 
3.1 Synthesis and functionalization of the NPs 
In order to obtain functionalized NPs for the further experiments, three steps are needed. First 
one is to synthesize magnetite nanopowder. Crucial parameters here are small particle size 
and uniform size distribution. Second stage is magnetite coating with silica shell. During the 
coating, the most important feature is coating thickness – sufficient for providing chemical 
stability, but not increasing greatly size of the structure, which would lead to less favourable 
specific surface area of the final product. The last phase is nanoparticle functionalization. At 
that stage, it is important to deposit on the NPs surface as much organic material as possible, 
but avoiding precipitation of the free silanes if they are not chemically bound to the silica shell. 
3.1.1 Synthesis of the magnetite core 
Magnetite nanoparticles were synthesized using a modified Massart method [22]. Aqueous 
solution of FeCl2 and FeCl3 with addition of HCl(aq) is added dropwise into a vigorously-stirred 
solution of high pH (NH3(aq) is commonly used to increase the pH). As a result, small crystals 
of magnetite Fe3O4 precipitate in the solution. Due to the properties of magnetite, non-magnetic 
mixing has to be used on that stage – otherwise magnetite will agglomerate due to the external 
magnetic field. After the reaction, precipitate is being recovered either by the centrifugation or 
by the external magnetic field. Such precipitate is then washed several times in order to remove 
remaining of the reaction mixture and dried. General precipitation reaction is expressed by the 
Equation 2: 
 
2 𝐹𝑒3+ + 𝐹𝑒2+ + 8 𝑂𝐻−→ 𝐹𝑒3𝑂4 + 4 𝐻2𝑂 
[Eq. 2] 
For the purpose of this work, the smallest possible size of the NPs is desired in order to 
maximize specific surface area per gram of the obtained material. To achieve that, several 
modifications of the well-established sol/gel process can be applied. Proportion between the 
Fe2+ and Fe3+, as well as the concentrations of the ions, can be modified. According to the 
literature, optimal proportion between the Fe2+ and Fe3+ is stoichiometric 1:2 (mol), but excess 
of the Fe2+ is preferred over the excess of the Fe3+ [21]. Moreover, FeCl2 solutions should be 
prepared with 0.05 M HCl(aq) as a solvent. In his original work, Massart reported that increase 
==PAGE:36==
Marcin Sawczuk – Development of a nanomaterial for selective extraction of naphthenic acid 
21 
 
of the pH in the alkaline precipitation stage and proportions as close to stoichiometric as 
possible minimize the size of the obtained NPs. 
Ultrasonic treatment was proven to be better than the mechanical stirring as it leads to 
precipitation of the smaller magnetite crystals [41]. Ultrasound-induced cavitation turned out to 
be optimal for preventing the magnetite crystals from growing – magnetite precipitation is 
almost immediate, therefore frequencies of 20 kHz or higher are needed to interfere with the 
fast crystal formation. NPs as small as 6 nm can be obtained by the use of the ultrasound, 
when the typical values reported for the precipitation using mechanical stirring are around 
10÷20 nm [27], [42], [43]. 
Moreover, additives can be used both in the synthesis stage and for the washing. For the 
synthesis, antioxidants (such as hydrazine) are commonly added to prevent oxidation of Fe2+ 
ions and surface oxidation of the precipitated NPs [41]. For the same purpose, saturating 
reaction mixture with inert gas is used by various authors. These methods, however, are not 
used in this project. 
Additives for the washing stage include all kind of dispersing agents, such as surfactants or 
pH-adjusting compounds. In this project, 0.001 mol/l citric acid is used for the last stage of 
washing in order to induce negative charge on the surface of the NPs. Isoelectric point of the 
NPs was reported to be at 6.5-6.8 pH [44], therefore such concentrations of citric acid should 
be sufficient for surface charge accumulation. In the same time, they were reported to cause 
only minimal damage to the magnetite [45]. 
It is important to maintain low concentration of the citric acid in order to avoid dissolution of the 
NPs, as the smallest ones have the least favourable mass/surface ratio. Therefore, the most 
precious fraction of the nanomaterial is the one most prone to being lost due to the citric acid 
dissolution. Even though ferric citrate is only slightly soluble in water, presence of ammonia 
might lead to the formation of an ammonium ferric citrate, which is highly soluble. For that 
reason, use of the mineral acid might be considered in the future, as they are believed to be 
less effective leaching agents for the magnetite. Despite this, some of the authors suggest 
using much higher concentrations of the citric acid [42], [43]. However, their approach is 
different and they aim for the surface passivation of the NPs with citric acid rather than 
dispersing them due to the pH modification and thus do not follow with the ammonia-catalysed 
coating stage. 
3.1.2 Coating with silica 
SiO2 coating is done using a modified Stöber process, historically designed for synthesis of 
monodisperse particles of free silica (Figure 3-1) [46]. The process principle consists in a 
hydrolysis of TEOS in a mixture of water and alcohol, catalysed by the OH- ions from ammonia 
==PAGE:37==
Marcin Sawczuk – Development of a nanomaterial for selective extraction of naphthenic acid 
22 
 
dissociation. It was observed that the same process could serve for the SiO2 coating of the 
nanoparticles. Dispersing negatively charged Fe3O4 NPs in the reaction mixture and constant 
stirring causes precipitation of the SiO2 shell on the nanoparticle surface. 
If the ultrasonic treatment is applied, magnetite NPs work as scavengers for SiO2 nuclei 
created at the wall of the cavitation bubble [41]. Cavitation causes locally-occurring extreme 
conditions of high temperature and pressure, therefore enhances hydrolysis of TEOS. At the 
same time, magnetite particles tend to accumulate on the wall of a growing cavitation bubble 
as it occupies more and more space in the volume of the liquid. In such conditions deposition 
of the SiO2 on the NPs is very likely due to the local increase of the free silica concentration in 
the zone where it can be scavenged by the magnetite grains. 
 
Figure 3-1: Mechanism of the TEOS hydrolysis and agglomeration 
Right proportion between the alcohol and the water, the type of the alcohol used and optimal 
concentration of the catalyst (usually NH3(aq), but other catalysts can be used as well) are 
crucial to control the structure of the deposited shell. According to the publications, EtOH-
water-NH3(aq) (9:1:1 vol.) are suited for precipitation of solid silica slow enough to obtain the 
core-shell structure within reasonable time (6 hours under the ultrasonic treatment was 
reported), but preventing precipitation of the free SiO2 grains [47]. Use of a citric acid solution 
in which the NPs were dispersed in the previous step instead of water should not affect the 
process, as concentrated ammonia is used in quantities that allow for neglecting the influence 
of the citric acid on the pH.

Interestingly, optimal regions for the catalysing the TEOS were reported both in the acidic and 
basic range of the pH scale. However, pH affects form of the precipitated silica – 
monodispersed solids are typically obtained in the basic range, while silica gel is typically 
precipitated from the acidic solution. Despite this, methods for precipitating dense SiO2 from 
the acidic solutions were reported too [48], [49]. Therefore acid-catalysed coating might be 
considered in the future, as it allows for maintaining the NPs in the acidic solution, therefore 
keeping the cores dispersed due to the surface charge deposition [50], [51]. 
It is worth mentioning that charging the magnetite surface in a basic pH range was reported 
too [21]. According to that work, dispersing the magnetite by the negative surface charging 
could be an option for optimizing the process. 
Independently, it has to be taken into account that high content of the alcohol in the solutions 
might significantly affect dissociation and therefore pH of the solution. Moreover, pH meters 
are normally calibrated for water-based standards. Therefore it is important to clarify, what 
measurement method is used to determine pH of the solution with high alcohol content, and 
to keep that factor in mind when analysing the literature. 
Several methods for drying can be used. Oven-drying is the simplest method, but leads to 
significant nanoparticle agglomeration. Vacuum-drying reduces slightly that effect, but freeze-
drying was proven the most efficient and is used in this project. Freezing the sample 
immobilizes the solids and therefore prevents agglomeration of the nanoparticles in the 
solution during the drying process. Obtained NPs are in the form of a very fine powder, easy 
to disperse for further use. 
3.1.3 Functionalization 
At this stage, the reaction environment is considered highly important. Two mechanisms of 
silane deposition on the NPs surface can be observed – actual chemical binding of the silicon 
atom with the silica structure and adhesion of the partially-hydrolysed silane to the 
nanomaterial surface. Clearly, the first one is desired. This fact led to several problems in the 
project development, when functionalization in the reaction environment which was 
excessively humid for the easily-hydrolysing silanes resulted with poor attachment of the active 
molecules to the NPs. As a consequence, they were later detached from the NPs in the 
ultrasonic bath and no organic material deposited on the nanomaterial surface could be 
detected by the FT-IR [27], [52]. 
To avoid that, functionalization is done in the low-humid environment of azeotropic toluene 
distillation. The nanomaterial is initially dispersed in small amount of water, then it is mixed 
with toluene and dried in the Dean-Stark trap under the constant stirring for at least 3 hours 
(Figure 3-2). Initial dispersing in water is used, as NPs disperse poorly in the non-polar

functionalization step. The reaction is left under the same conditions for another day (Figure 
3-3). After that, reaction mixture is separated using a magnet and washed several times in the 
ultrasonic bath, primarily with EtOH and then with water in order to remove any residue of the

3.2 Performance estimation 
The second step after the nanomaterial synthesis is the estimation of its performance as a 
binding/extracting agent for the naphthenic acid. Several trials to find a reliable method for 
performance estimation were made. Those included estimating quantities of the NA based on 
the dry mass of the NPs in the multiple adsorption-desorption cycles or using UV-vis 
spectrometry to measure concentration of the NA in the solution. The final idea for estimating 
the nanomaterial effectiveness was adsorption of the NA from the model oil and desorption of 
the bond fraction using various solutions and ultrasonic bath. The process was monitored by 
the means of the ESI-MS spectrometry.

3.2.1 Adsorption and desorption 
Stock solution of the NA in hexane was prepared. Based on that solution, series of solutions 
for calibration were made, as well as solutions from which the NA was removed by the means 
of the nanomaterials. Calibration curve is done in the range from 0 to 2.5% (wt). Initial 2% 
solution was chosen for the adsorption stage, as it corresponds to the conditions of moderate-
high content of the NA in crude oil. 
In order to perform the extraction, each of the samples is dispersed in a small amount of 
hexane using the ultrasonic bath and 2.5% stock solution of NA is added, so that the final 
solution contains 2% of NA and dispersed nanomaterial. Samples from each of the adsorption 
experiments are collected over the period of 24 hours. After the adsorption, nanomaterial is 
centrifuged and washed with pure hexane to remove the residual NA contained in the volume 
of the solution filling the pore space. No sonication is used for the washing, to avoid desorption 
of the NA from the nanomaterial. Additionally to the tubes with NA solution, another tube was 
filled with pure hexane and samples in the same time intervals over the duration of the 
experiment. The purpose of that was to allow for analysis of a possible leaching of the 
plasticizers from the HDPE tube during the prolonged exposition to the organic solvent, as 
such contaminants might affect the results. 
Each of the three consecutive steps of desorption involve 30 minutes of the ultrasonic 
treatment in MeOH (often reported as an agent used for NA detaching from the amine groups), 
then in MeOH-NH3(aq) (10:1) and finally in NH3(aq). The reason for such combination is 
insufficient performance of the pure MeOH in the previous experiments (described in the 
chapter A.2). Samples are recovered and analysed the same way that the ones obtained in 
the adsorption stage. Calibration curves are prepared separately for all the solvents used for 
desorption, as influence of the solvent on the ESI-MS results depends on multiple factors and 
thus cannot be excluded a priori. 
For the quantitative analysis, ESI-MS is used. Typically, this method requires polar solvent for 
carrying the sample to allow for charging the solution in the electrospray chamber. In order to 
assure ionization of the NA, device is run in the negative mode and the used solution contained 
NH3(aq). It assures ionization of the carboxyl group and prevents from unpredicted ionization 
of the other parts of the molecule. Due to the low miscibility of hexane with polar solvents, 
mixture with high content of acetonitrile is used. Its miscibility with non-polar solvents makes it 
a useful tool for obtaining a one-phase system for ESI-MS analysis of the non-polar samples. 
Experiments determined H2O-acetonitrile (1:6 vol.) with 1% of NH3(aq) as an optimal solvent.

Results and discussion 
This chapter provides results of the experiments with comments. Results of both nanomaterial 
characterization and performance estimation are given here. 
5.1 Nanoparticle characterization 
Main problem with the nanoparticle preparation was fairly low efficiency of the magnetite 
synthesis stage (85% of the iron mass recovered), as well as synthesis of the large magnetite 
crystals (or crystal agglomerates, as will be shown later on in the text), which could not be 
dispersed using the ultrasonic bath. Both phenomena influence the synthesis performance and 
lower process efficiency. Reasons for such situation are currently unknown. Two factors are 
considered important as possible causes of those problems: 
1. Contamination of the FeCl2 with solid oxidation products. This phenomenon might 
have double effect on the synthesis process. It not only disturbs reagent proportion to 
the less favourable dominance of the Fe3+ ions, leading to possible formation of phases 
such as maghemite or hematite, but also provides large insoluble solids that might 
serve as crystallization nuclei, preventing correct precipitation of NPs in the solution. 
2. High concentration of iron in the reaction mixture. High quantity of the magnetite 
fines might lead to agglomeration of the forming NPs. The boundary concentration for 
the stability of the magnetite ferrofluid was reported to be very low for aqueous 
suspensions, around 12 mg/l [41]. Normally, magnetite agglomerates can be dispersed 
easily using the ultrasound, but if the concentration of Fe3O4 in the suspension is high, 
immediate re-agglomeration occurs, causing necessity to apply various dispersing 
agents when handling magnetite NPs. Usually, it is not a problem for the synthesis, as 
the crystals growth stage is relatively short. However, if the concentrations of the iron 
ions are too high, they might be affecting that process. Magnetite is claimed to 
precipitate in two consecutive stages – nucleation and growth. In case if agglomeration 
of the nuclei happens before the crystal growth stage terminates, agglomerates might 
be impossible to dissipate afterwards as precipitating magnetite forms links between 
the agglomerated grains. 
Either one or both of those factors might play a role in the in the formation of large crystals. 
Possible reasons for the 15% loss of the overall iron mass are still unknown. 
In order to determine properties of the obtained material and possibly answer the above-
mentioned questions, various characterization methods were used.

5.1.1 Crystalline phase determination 
XRD analysis of the Fe3O4 NPs revealed the majority of the peaks corresponding well with the 
reported XRD pattern for the magnetite. Two peaks characteristic for the magnetite were not 
detected – 18.4° was covered by instrument-induced noise and 37.2° was merged with more 
prominent 35.6°. Moreover, no additional peaks were observed. Therefore, no additional 
crystalline phases are present in the Fe3O4 NPs (Figure 5-1). 
XRD analysis for the Fe3O4@SiO2 reveals presence of the amorphous phase (first visible peak, 
very broad) originated from the SiO2 coating. Due to the specificity of the process, silica does 
not form crystalline network but instead is deposited on the NPs surface as glass. Thus, no 
additional crystalline phases are formed and the only signal corresponding to the SiO2 is a 
broad peak with maximum at around 21°.

which did not disperse in the ultrasonic bath (Figure 5-2). Potential presence of hematite Fe2O3 
could be a reason for formation of large particles. Fe2O3 (or other insoluble Fe(III) species) 
fines, formed due to the oxidation of FeCl2 present in the reagent, could act as crystallization 
nuclei on which magnetite was deposited. Analysis revealed that there is no hematite present 
in the sample – 24.2° and 49.5° peaks were not observed, and those are the main indicators 
allowing to distinguish between the magnetite and hematite. Therefore, Fe2O3 could not act as

5.1.2 Electron microscopy imaging 
TEM imaging of the magnetite NPs revealed that they were monodispersed, with the diameter 
ranging from 6 to 8 nm. This is a very good result, proving correctness of the sonication as a 
method for obtaining magnetite crystals smaller than the ones produced by stirring of the 
reaction mixture. Structures formed by the nanoparticles on the carbon mesh are 
predominantly thin layers, which indicate that they disperse easily in water and then sediment 
on the mesh (Figure 5-3). 
On top of that, combined SEM and TEM analysis of the large magnetite grains which could not 
be dispersed in the ultrasonic bath revealed that they are agglomerates of exactly the same 
magnetite particles which were easily dispersible. TEM images of the grains crushed in the 
mortar show that the agglomerate is formed of the crystallites of the same sizes that the size 
of the dispersed magnetite NPs (Figure 5-4, Figure 5-5). It indicates that precipitation of the

Nanoparticles were efficient means of adsorbing NA. The main problem was related to the

5.2.3 Concentration plots for adsorption and desorption 
For each series of samples corresponding to the adsorption experiment, curve showing 
decrease of NA concentration was prepared. Apart from this, curves showing abundance 
decrease for each ion and for ion groups were prepared (Figure 5-21 to Figure 5-25). 
==PAGE:66==

Adsorption experiment reveals that it is easier to remove lighter fraction of NA. It is explained 
easily by geometric factors – polar carboxyl group has larger contribution to the overall size in 
small particles than in large ones. Thus, particle geometry makes it more probable that smaller 
NA molecule gets in a favourable position for getting attached to the nanomaterial surface. 
Finally, one can see that the shape of the concentration vs. time curves corresponds to the 
Langmuir adsorption isotherm, which is normally related with the process driven by the 
chemical reaction rather than physical process [57]. It indicates that there is only a single layer 
of the NA deposited on the nanomaterial, as multiple layers cannot be deposited by reacting 
with the nanoparticle surface. The original description of the model made by Langmuir 
corresponds well with the physics of the system – amphiphilic particle with small polar carboxyl 
group attached to a large non-polar structure is attached to the silica surface by the polar group 
and therefore creates type of a non-polar coating, which prevents further NA deposition. In 
such model, functional groups attached to the nanomaterial surface are the equivalent sites to 
which NA can be attached. 
Analysis of the desorbed NA revealed that after two stages of desorption, almost no NA is 
further removed from the nanomaterial in the third stage (Figure 5-26 to Figure 5-28). That 
might be due to the lack of the NA attached to the nanomaterial surface, or due to NA 
deposition via mechanisms which are harder to reverse. This concept should be further verified 
by the means of FT-IR of the recovered nanoparticles. Moreover, lighter NA is predominant in 
the desorbed samples. It is a consequence of the previous observation, stating that this fraction 
is more prone to being adsorbed on the nanomaterial.

Chapter 6. 
Conclusions 
This project was a preliminary-stage research, verifying correctness of the idea. As such, it 
served well for its purpose, indicating that the surface-modified Fe3O4@SiO2 can be used for 
the selective NA extraction and therefore, that further researching the topic is well-founded. 
Despite this, research methodology can be improved in many areas. The following sections 
list conclusions from the project. Moreover, they indicate the problems which appeared during 
the project development and give possible solutions. 
6.1 Proof of the concept 
The concept is proven correct, but additional research has to be conducted. In all the 
experiments, NPs were adsorbing significant amount of NA – it was observed both as a 
concentration drop visible in the ESI-MS and as a mass growth of the particles (in the 
preliminary experiment described in the Section A.2.1). Apart from this, NA was detected in 
the solution in which NPs were regenerated. It serves as an ultimate proof for the correctness 
of the idea. 
6.2 Nanomaterial recycling 
There is no NA detected at the last stage of desorption. It potentially indicates that NPs are 
reusable. Alternatively, NA might be permanently bond with the surface. Additional trials have 
to be taken in order to verify it. Those might be TGA of FT-IR of the recovered NPs, which can 
be compared with the initial results of the FT-IR or TGA. Possible changes in the quantity of 
the organic fraction revealed by those tests will indicate that not all the NA was desorbed. 
Additional study is required to analyse the adsorption process itself. The interaction of NA with 
amine group is well understood, but interactions with pyridine and tetrazole are not properly 
described in the literature. Depending on the surface functionalization type and reaction 
environment (solvent polarity, pH and temperature), various mechanisms of adsorption can be 
present. Some of those can lead to formation of the relatively strong bonds, lowering capability 
of desorption. 
Another topic is presence of the solid residues (most likely naphthenates) in the dried NPs 
from the cyclic adsorption-desorption trials, as well as the observed performance loss in that 
test (Section A.2.1). It might indicate that desorption does not remove all the attached NA, 
even when the modified desorption procedure is applied. 
On top of that, recovery of the nanomaterial from the polar solutions has to be improved. The 
reason for that is their visible hydrophilic behaviour, resulting from the nature of the attached

6.3 NA adsorption on non-functionalized nanoparticles 
Significant adsorption of the NA on the non-functionalized NPs was observed. Reason for that 
behaviour is still not clear. It is even more interesting, as NA mass balance for the non-
functionalized NPs differs significantly from the ones obtained for the other materials. It might 
be that adsorption on the NPs occurs due to their large specific surface area and the NA are 
lost at the stage of hexane-washing the samples to remove residual NA contained in the void 
spaces between the NPs. The solvent used for the wash was not analysed for the presence of 
NAs and that should be changed in the future trials. 
6.4 Quantitative analysis of the NA 
ESI-MS is confirmed as a very useful method for the qualitative analysis of the NA. However, 
during the realization of the project need for the quantitative analysis arose. Four methods 
were taken into account: 
1. ESI-MS – highly unstable results, overall abundance tends to vary a lot even for the 
same sample. Consequently, the calibration is difficult and normalization of the 
readings has to be applied. Problems with the calibration curve for the solutions in 
hexane might lead to the necessity of verifying ESI-MS with another method (Section 
A.3.3).  
2. FT-IR – method is current state of the art solution for the quantitative analysis of the 
NA [58]. Confirming its applicability for the project failed due to the wrong sample

This method is particularly interesting, as it serves for analysing both liquid and solid 
samples. However, it is highly sensible to the water content (Section A.3.2).

6.5 NPs diameter and coating thickness 
Although procedure for the NPs synthesis was modified, the size of the obtained particles is 
still not satisfactory. Oxidation of one of the reagents was initially suspected to be the cause, 
leading to the formation of insoluble crystallization nuclei present in the solution. Despite this,

neither XRD (no other crystalline phases than magnetite were detected in the nanomaterials 
samples), nor TEM (large grains were shown to be composed of the identical NPs as the 
dispersed one) support this claim. 
Hypothesis confirmed by the TEM is magnetite agglomeration and coating of multiple nuclei. 
This can be avoided by improved dispersion in the citric acid. In order to control the 
nanoparticle dispersion, DLS can be used. This method allows for determination of the real 
efficiency of the citric acid treatment, depending on the treatment time and the acid

predominant in such measurements. 
An important factor might be presence of the large magnetite grains. They were impossible to 
disperse in the ultrasonic bath. Similar mechanism might occur for smaller magnetite fractions, 
therefore improving initial dispersion of the magnetite is crucial for better coating. Therefore, 
concentrations of the reagents during the magnetite synthesis stage have to be lowered. 
6.6 Characterization and repeatability of the functionalization 
For the estimation of the nanomaterial’s efficiency, method for quantification of the functional 
groups present on the NPs surface is necessary. Few options are available here. 
 
Titration with acid solution under the conductivity/pH measurement; this kind of 
analysis requires high purity of the medium and well-defined methodology for sample 
conditioning and purification. Amounts of compound attached to the silica shells is 
relatively small, therefore such measurement might be difficult. 
 
TGA can be used for determination of the organic fraction present in the NPs. It is very 
well-suited for application with Fe3O4@SiO2 NPs, as neither magnetite nor silica 
evaporate in low temperatures and silica shell prevents oxidation of the material. The 
main drawback of that method is destruction of the sample, required in relatively high 
quantities. 
 
XPS can be particularly useful for that task, as it determines principally the surface 
composition of the sample. In that case, ratios between detected carbon and silicon 
can be used for relative quantification of organic compounds present on the surface. 
Moreover, calibrated with TGA can be used for absolute quantification in a non-
destructive way.

6.7 Coating and functionalization environment 
Additional research of the SiO2 coating and NPs functionalization processes has to be done. 
According to the literature, both acidic and alkaline environment catalyse degradation of TEOS. 
At the same time, in order to assure better dispersion of the magnetite NPs, pH is lowered with 
citric acid. In such case finding optimal dispersion and applying TEOS in acidic conditions 
could lead to avoiding agglomeration and therefore to better results in the coating part. This 
was clearly seen on the TEM images, showing that the presence of multiple nuclei is actually 
a reason for a large size of the NPs. Moreover, various authors reported control of the TEOS 
degradation by the means of ultrasound. Adjusting the pH to the isoelectric point of the 
magnetite and precise control of the shell thickness are of a high interest. 
Apart from that, some authors report conducting the functionalization process in a manner that 
allows for modification of nanomaterial wettability. As an example, hydrothermal treatment by 
autoclaving of the silica was stated as an effective method to increase the number of the 
silicon-bond hydroxyl groups per unit area of the material, therefore possibly increasing 
hydrophilicity and consequently number of equivalent sites at which NA can be adsorbed [59]. 
Increasing of the surface fractality by modification of the coating process could give similar 
result [52]. Defining whether hydrophilic, hydrophobic or neutral NPs are the most effective for 
both NA adsorption and further desorption process, and combining it with more advanced 
knowledge of surface modification, could be of a high interest. Taking into account that non-
functionalized NPs had adsorption efficiency of 18%, comparing with 24÷33% or the 
functionalized ones, improvement of the silica properties might be the key to the better overall 
performance.

6.8 Structure of the ligands 
Particles used for functionalization are complex, with multiple free pairs (Figure 6-4). In such 
system, several factors can affect the performance. Particularly, not only the functional group 
positioned at the end of the particle can contribute to the overall adsorption. Each free pair 
present in the molecule structure can interact with the NA present in the solution. 
On top of that, structure of the substituted particle itself has a huge influence on the overall 
hydrophilic/lipophilic behaviour of the NPs. Nanoparticles tend to inherit properties from the 
molecules with which they are functionalized. This aspect is interesting mainly for the tetrazole. 
It has similar pKa to the carboxyl group, but is far more lipophilic. Therefore could serve as a 
good agent for adsorbing organic acids from the non-polar solutions. 
Despite this, the behaviour of the tetrazole-treated NPs in the suspensions was not very 
different from the hydrophilic amine-treated NPs. It might indicate that substitution with 
tetrazole occurs differently from the reaction shown in this work, which leads to the necessity 
for further investigation of this topic. In order to verify real behaviour of tetrazole, mechanism 
of the reaction has to be known. Depending on the place on the ring where the addition occurs, 
components of different properties can be obtained. The substrate used for the reaction was 
tetrazole amine, thus it might be that the amine group stays intact (in contrary to the reaction 
proposed in this work) and it has the key influence on the properties of the nanomaterial.

Appendix A 
Methodology improvements 
The project was one of the first attempts to apply Fe3O4@SiO2 nanoparticles made within the 
GIFTEX research group. Therefore, initial methodology was a subject to multiple modifications 
in the course of the project. Several experiments failed and obtained experience and 
knowledge was used in the proceeding ones. Some others were designed to solve problems 
which were not directly related with the main objective, but rather resulted from the 
complications encountered, mainly in the synthesis and functionalization of the NPs, but also 
in the quantitative analysis of the NA. 
A.1 Synthesis and functionalization of the NPs 
A.1.1 Synthesis of the Fe3O4 
Initial procedure included oven-drying of the magnetite followed by the dispersion in the 0.3 M 
citric acid – much more concentrated than the one finally used. Significant loss of the magnetite 
mass was observed after the first Fe3O4 synthesis according to this procedure, as well as a 
hydrodynamic diameter much larger than the expected size of the NPs. Even though further 
literature study revealed that hydrodynamic diameter is normally larger than the actual size of 
the suspended solids, prevention of the mass loss was considered important. As mentioned in 
the main document, the finest NPs contribute to the majority of the mass loss in such systems, 
due to their least-favourable mass-to-surface ratio.

caused by the 0.3 M citric acid. Investigation revealed that citric acid at such concentrations 
might be used as a leaching agent for the magnetite ore (Figure A-1). Even though the iron

At the same time, preparation of the modified silanes was attempt. Initially functionalization 
was planned to be done with the silanes synthesized before attaching to the silica surface. 
Several attempts to the synthesis were made, but all of them failed. At each time, multiple 
products of the reaction with silanes were observed (Figure A-9). Performance was usually 
analysed by the means of HPTLC as a first stage and then by the ESI-MS. 
Multiple reaction products most probably indicates that reaction conditions were not sufficiently 
dry to prevent degradation of the silanes. This leads to the formation of silane polymers of 
various molecular masses and varying hydration levels. 
This situation was occurring repeatedly in various attempts of silane synthesis. Finally, 
decision of abandoning the concept of synthetizing the free silanes was made. Procedure 
including surface functionalization with a commercial silane and customization of the already-
deposited molecules turned out to be much easier to perform. Even though it requires use of 
more reagents to ensure that the reaction on the surface proceeded with 100% efficiency 
(counting the molecules deposited on the NPs surface), no need to protect sensitive silanes 
during the reaction compensates for that.

It can be seen that both issues come from the same reason – instability of the silanes in the 
presence of water. Based on such experience, final procedure was developed to solve both 
problems at the same time. It was decided to use the environment of the azeotropic distillation 
of the toluene-water mixture, with previous dispersion of the NPs in a small amount of water 
[27]. Water content in such environment is high enough to allow silane hydrolysis on the 
nanomaterial surface, but low enough to prevent precipitation of the free silica in the bulk 
volume of the reaction mixture. For further modifications of the such-deposited silane, the same 
reaction mixture can be used after addition of the third reagent. 
A.2 Performance estimation 
A.2.1 Adsorption and desorption 
Initial idea for determining performance of the nanomaterials was cyclic adsorption and 
desorption of the NA. Adsorption was done from the 2% (wt) NA solution in toluene, while 
desorption was done in MeOH and enhanced by the ultrasonic treatment. In order to determine 
actual performance of the nanomaterial, small amount of the NPs was dispersed in the NA 
solution for 12 hours. After that NPs were centrifuged, washed with pure hexane to remove 
residual NA and then dried in the oven at 80°C for 4 hours. Mass of the deposited NA was 
determined and the NPs were dispersed for 30 minutes of the ultrasonic treatment in MeOH. 
After that, they were centrifuged again, dried for 4 hours in the MeOH and their mass was 
compared with the initial one (Table A-2). Such cycles were meant to be repeated several 
times to observe nanomaterial performance decrease. 
MeOH with the desorbed NA was moved to the pre-weighted vials and evaporated in the oven 
at 80°C until dry. Residue was weighted and left for further analysis using the ESI-MS.

A.3 Naphthenic acid quantification 
Crucial part of the project was analysis of the NA – both qualitative and quantitative. For the 
qualitative analysis, ESI-MS is a method of choice which served its purpose very well. For the 
quantitative analysis, various methods were used. From the multiple techniques reported in 
the papers, UV-vis, FT-IR and ESI-MS were tested. 
A.3.1 UV-vis 
Although generally NA are considered saturated, polycyclic organic acids – and as such should 
be invisible in the UV-vis spectrometry – they contain some contaminants which might be used 
for indirect quantification [13]. This however seemed to be the key limitation when considering 
UV-vis as a spectroscopy method which might be used in the project. 
Even though the calibration curve was ideally fitting the measured points (Figure A-12), results 
from the experiments were unclear. It turned out that NA spectrum was changing its shape 
during the adsorption. Moreover, most of the adsorption occurred immediately after the 
exposure of the NPs to the NA solution (Figure A-13). After 10 minutes spectra were almost 
not changing. It might indicate that aromatic compounds, which are the trace contaminant used 
for the UV-vis quantification, represent high affinity to the NPs and are quickly removed from 
the solution.

A.3.2 FT-IR 
FT-IR was reported as a method for NA quantification [58]. Moreover, it can be applied both in 
the liquid and solid samples. Main limitation of that technique is its sensibility to the water 
content. In order to quantify the NA, quantification of the carboxyl groups has to be done. On 
the FT-IR spectra, carboxyl groups show several absorption bands, with one of them present 
around 1690÷1720 cm-1. Quantification based on that feature resulted with a very accurate 
calibration curve (Figure A-14). 
Unfortunately, a problem with quantification of the NA in the real samples appeared. Due to 
the poor quality of the vials in which the samples were stored, solvent evaporation occurred. 
This problem was not visible in the previous test, as the samples were kept in the low 
temperature, thus preventing evaporation. Transporting the samples to the FT-IR lab caused 
significant (visible with a naked eye) evaporation of the solvent in all the samples apart from 
the first one (for t=0 min). It can be seen that the first sample is quantified correctly, but the 
concentration in the rest is significantly overestimated (above the initial level of the NA used in 
the experiment). The conclusion is, that FT-IR might serve as a good quantification method to 
be used in the future. On top of that, sample storing methods have to be improved. 
Another conclusion is, that FT-IR used together with the UV-vis might serve for verification of 
the hypothesis of higher affinity of the unsaturated fraction of the NA to the NPs. Both aromatic

reveal a relationship between the shape change of the UV-vis spectra and disappearance of

A.3.3 ESI-MS 
ESI-MS is a good tool for qualitative analysis of the naphthenic acid. Its main drawback is high 
instability of the results. Therefore it is difficult to do quantitative analysis using this tool. 
Method developed for quantitative analysis with ESI-MS included normalization of the signal 
for detected peaks with average signals for the same masses, detected for the 2% NA in 
hexane. This allowed for neglecting the factor of various intensities resulting from different 
peaks and for treating each of the peaks with the same importance. Additionally, it turned out 
that some of the ions are sources of the noise and have to be removed from the overall 
concentration estimation. 
Another aspect which might be examined is composition of the solvent. In case if the solvent 
without addition of the NH3(aq) is sufficient for ionizing the sample, avoiding addition of this 
volatile compound might positively influence stability of the results.
