Abstract
The objective of this master's thesis is to investigate the changes in physical and chemical
properties of native (unprocessed) rm and thin egg white during storage of 45 days at 5 ◦C.
The physical and chemical properties investigated are pH, content of total solids, weights
and rheological properties, additionally the technological/functional property foaming of
whole egg whites are investigated. The potential of predicting selected physical and chemical
properties by Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression models from Near Infrared spectra is
investigated.
Before initiation of the storage experiment development of the analytic laboratory techniques
were made. In this work the rm and thin egg white were found to have dierent properties
in terms of rheological characteristics. The rm egg white was characterised gel and thereby
investigated by oscillatory testing in the main experiment. The thin egg white was found to
be a liquid and therefore tested by rotational viscometry.
In the morning at day 0 of the storage period 380 newly laid eggs from hens by the strain LSL
Lohman (White) were collected from a conventional cage egg farm. After arrival at DTU all
eggs were numbered and weighed. At each day of experiment 28 eggs were randomly picked
and weighed again. Subsequently the eggs were broken, separated in yolk, thin and rm egg
white and the fractions weighted in this procedure. Four eggs were randomly assigned each
experiment except of the foaming experiment which was assigned eight eggs, as the volume
of two egg whites were needed for the whipping procedure. This way each of the experiments
was made in quadruplicate. The experiments were made at egg age 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 7, 10, 14,
17, 24, 31 and 45 days.
During the rst 7 to 10 days of storage an increase in pH of both thin and rm egg white
was found. The pH of rm egg white increased from 8.37 to 9.06 at day 10 the corresponding
increase in the thin egg white was from 8.14 to 9.07. After day 10 and to the end of the
storage period the pH levelled out around 9. Within the same storage period as pH increased
several changes in the other properties were found. The initial content of total solids in the
rm and thin egg white were found to be 12.40 % and 14.17 % respectively. A rapid reduction
of the initial dierences in the content of total solids was found. The content of total solids
in rm increased and the content in thin egg white decreased. At day 7 or 10 the content of

Chapter 1
Introduction
The objective of this master's thesis is to investigate and explore the physical and chemical
changes in the unprocessed (native) rm and thin egg white during storage. In the reviewed
literature not much work on the changes in physical and chemical properties of the separated
and native fractions of the thin and rm egg white during storage is found. In this master's
thesis the physical and chemical changes of native separated rm and thin egg white in terms
of rheology, content of total solids, weight, pH and technological/functional properties will
be characterized and coupled. This is interesting from a scientic point of view and will
contribute to the knowledge within this eld. Additionally this exploratory work could be
benecial for the stakeholders in the egg industry who process and prepare native egg white
in relation to production scheduling, dimensioning, design and recipe development. Near
Infrared (NIR) spectroscopy on the separated egg white fractions is recorded to investigate
the opportunity of a quick on-line measuring procedure for the industry to predict some of
the physical, chemical and functional properties.
In addition to this, a part of the master's thesis process is to gain more knowledge in the
eld of rheology and learn how to measure and analyse such results.
1.1
Background Knowledge and Hypotheses
Egg from the hen (Gallus domesticus) is a raw material which is used in a great variety in the
human diet and is simultaneous a rich source of protein and nutrients (Belitz et al., 2009).
Egg products on the market varies from shell eggs, pasteurized yolks and whites, prepared
scrambled egg mix, precooked and chopped eggs, long eggs to egg powder (Danæg, 2009, 2015;
Sanovo Egg Group, 2015). This broad range of products involves dierent stakeholders in
form of egg producers, egg product manufactures, the ingredient industry, food authorities,

caterers and end consumers. These stakeholders got dierent focus areas which they would
like to address primarily. However in relation to food technology it is important for the
stakeholders to be able to process the eggs quickly, easily and cheap into products of high
functional quality with low waste in a protable way.
The many dierent applications of egg in household and industrial cooking reects that egg,
as whole or divided into yolk and white, got a range of dierent functional/technological
properties. The most appreciated functional properties of egg are the ability to form foam
when whipped, coagulate into gel when heat treated and emulsify solutions of water and oil
(Belitz et al., 2009). To benet from the functional properties, obtain the desired products
and meanwhile satisfy the stakeholders involved it is important to know the physical and
chemical properties of the raw material and their relation to the functional properties.
Outer conditions such as forage, environment, hen age and hen strain/line are factors aecting
the quality of the egg before being laid (Shenstone, 1968). For instance Alamprese et al.
(2012) found that the mechanical and rheological properties of fresh pasta made by eggs
from hens during a lifecycle varied signicantly with hen age.
Another well known condition which changes the initial egg properties is storage (Shenstone,
1968).
Some of the specic changes found in the egg during storage are evaporation of
CO2 followed by an increase in pH, thinning of the rm egg white, changes in proteins and
migration and evaporation of moisture (Shenstone, 1968). Except from egg white thinning
these changes are found across the yolk and white (Stadelman, 1995; Li-Chan et al., 1995).
However the major changes are somehow related to alterations in the egg white. The changes
rises from the moment the egg is laid by the hen and alters the egg white properties during
storage (Belitz et al., 2009).
The hypotheses put forward for this master's thesis are therefore:
1. The physical, chemical and functional properties of rm and thin egg white changes
signicantly during storage of egg in the shell.
2. Native rm and thin egg white are to some extend initially dierent and the changes
in these during storage are therefore dierent.
3. The changes observed in the physical, chemical and functional properties during storage
are correlated to evaporation of CO2 and the following increase in pH.

2.1
The Native Egg
Egg white is a part of the whole egg and before going into details with the specic physical,
chemical and functional properties of egg white a short description of the structure of whole
native egg is provided to understand the natural context of the egg white.
The three main constituents of egg are the shell, white and yolk. Over the years authors
have reported the percentages of these constituents of the total egg weight, see Table 2.1.
Some of these reported values are found along with the total egg weight. These ndings
suggests that there is approximately twice as much egg white as egg yolk in an egg and that
the distribution between yolk, white and shell has not changed substantial over the years of
investigation.
Reasons for the variation reported in the literature can partly be described by natural varia-
tion between eggs and precision of analysis. The natural variation in the internal constitution
and size of the egg are highly dependent of the laying hens and age of the egg (Shenstone,
1968; Li-Chan et al., 1995; Silversides & Scott, 2001; Li-Chan & Kim, 2008).
Hen age,
strain/line and environmental conditions are variables found to be of importance in relation
to the natural variation between eggs (Shenstone, 1968; Hill & Hall, 1980; Silversides & Scott,

In the following the structure and constitution of the native egg is briey described with
main focus on the egg white since this is the component investigated in this project. The
references used to derive this paragraph are Shenstone (1968), Stadelman (1973), Li-Chan
et al. (1995), Li-Chan and Kim (2008), Belitz et al. (2009) and Mine (2013).
A cross section of an egg is seen in Figure 2.1 (adapted from Belitz et al. (2009)). The yolk
is found in the centre of the egg. The yolk consists of light and dark yellow layers. The
latebra goes from the centre of the yolk to the surface of the yolk where the germinal disk
(blastoderm) is located. A water permeable vitelline membrane made of proteins surrounds
the yolk and encapsulates the yolk from the egg white.
The egg white is made up of four layers: Two thin layers and two rm layers inner and outer,
respectively. Around the egg yolk an inner layer of rm egg white is found, this layer can
be called the chalaziferous layer as it branches into the brous chalazae in the smaller and
larger (top and bottom) end of the egg. The function of the chalazae is to suspend the yolk
centred in the egg by anchoring in the next layer of rm egg white. This second (outer)
layer of rm egg white is found as the third layer of egg white from the yolk. Within the
two layers of rm egg white the layer of inner thin egg white is found. The outer and fourth
layer of egg white is thin egg white and is positioned as the last aqueous layer before the
shell structure. The distribution of the four egg whites are dependent on hen strain/line,
hen age and egg age (Stadelman, 1995). Values for fresh eggs are reported by weight to be
2.3 %, 16.8 %, 57.3% and 23.3 % for the inner rm, inner thin, outer rm and outer rm egg
white, respectively (Burley & Vadehra, 1989).
An inner and an outer shell membrane surrounds the egg white.
Between the two shell
membranes an air cell is located in the bottom of the egg. The air cell is formed when the
egg cools down immediately after the egg is laid. In this cooling process water evaporates
through the membranes and the pores of the shell to the surroundings and the content of
the egg contracts.
The outer surface of the egg is the shell, this is made up by three layers, the inner mammillary

carbonate (H2CO3) in the egg white mainly determines the pH in the egg white (Shenstone,
1968; Li-Chan et al., 1995) However, the proteins contributes to the pH to some degree. The
equilibrium is called the carbonate-bicarbonate buering system and is seen in Equation 2.1.
The equilibrium are dependent of the partial pressure of CO2 in the surrounding atmosphere
(Li-Chan et al., 1995). The low atmospheric concentration of CO2 (approximately 0.034 %
of the atmosphere) results in release of CO2 from the egg white through the pores of the
egg shell to the surroundings (Li-Chan et al., 1995).

When air is introduced to the egg white by whipping, blending, bubbling or shaking protein

of introducing air to the egg white the proteins unfolds from their native conformation

is exposed to the air phase while the hydrophilic groups stays in the liquid phase (Alleoni,

During whipping air is trapped in bubbles/air-cells and air-liquid interfaces/surfaces are
established at which the proteins adsorb and promote bubble formation (Alleoni, 2006; Davis
& Foegeding, 2007). Each bubble established is surrounded by a thin continuous cohesive
lm of proteins and the bubbles are separated by a liquid lamella (Alleoni, 2006).
The
protein interactions at the air/water surface to produce the cohesive lms are a mix of
hydrogen bonds, van der Waals forces, electrostatic, hydrophobic and covalent disulphide
bonds depending on the proteins available (Hammershøj et al., 1999; Alleoni, 2006).
After foam formation the stability is very important. Important protein abilities reported to
stabilise the foam are among others decreasing surface tension and improving lm elasticity
(Hammershøj et al., 1999; Alleoni, 2006).
The foam instability can be detected by liq-
uid drainage, bubble coalescence and bubble disproportionation (Hammershøj et al., 1999).

cohesive viscoelastic lm via intermolecular interaction". It is the mixture of the egg white

2.3.1
Buer Capacity
The pH of the egg white is dependent on the equilibrium in the carbonate-bicarbonate buer
system as mentioned previously. Buers are by denition able to absorb relatively large
amounts of protons (H+) or hydroxide ions (OH−) resulting in minor pH changes. The
buer capacity of a solution is the amount of H+ or OH−the solution can absorb without
large changes in pH. Therefore it is hypothesised that buer capacity measured in egg white
during storage time reveal other information than the corresponding pH value.
Heath (1977) suggests a procedure to test the carbonate-bicarbonate buer system in egg
white. This approach encompasses sample preparation in terms of homogenisation, titration
from pH 8 to pH 5 with 0.375 M HCl and evaluation of data based on the volume HCl used
to reach pH 5.
Instead of using the volume added acid/base in a specic pH interval the buer capacity can
be calculated from the titration curve. In the titration curve the measured pH are plotted
against the concentration of acid/base (Cab) in the solution. The magnitude of the slope
indicates the buer capacity. A small slope indicates that the system absorbs the added
acid/base and act as a buer, a high slope indicates the opposite. The buer capacity is

Rheology of Egg White
Rheology is dened as the science of deformation and ow of matter. This includes the study
of how foods (and other materials) responds to applied stress (Stee, 1996). For solid, liquid

and semi-solid foods the rheological properties can change greatly according to mechanical
force, time and temperature applied during processing. Mechanical force applied is often
applied to foods during processing in form of e.g. establishing and maintains of ow rate or
pressure. In relation to food science/technology the rheological characteristics are important
for optimal production design and -operation and quality in terms of eating perception of
the nal products among others (Friis et al., 2014).
The physical structures in foods are often complex and a result of a mixture of interac-
tions between proteins, carbohydrates and lipids and the processing history. This nature
of foods often gives raise to a physical state which is a combination of liquid-like (viscous)
and solid-like (elastic) behaviour. The combination of viscous and elastic properties is called
viscoelastic behaviour. Foods are therefore very rarely classied as an ideal solid (Hookean)
or an ideal liquid (Newtonian) (Friis et al., 2014).
In rheology the terms strain, shear rate and stress are used to describe the properties/state
of materials. The extent of deformation is dened by the strain (γ) and the time derivative
(velocity) of the strain is the shear rate (˙γ). The resistance to deformation is dened as the
stress (τ or σ) (Stee, 1996).
In the following sections the prerequisites for rheological characterisation and interpretations
of liquid foods by rotational viscometry and viscoelastic foods oscillatory testing will be
elaborated. The literature used in the following are Stee (1996), Rielly (1997) and Friis et
al. (2014) and will not be specied further.

3.5
Whipping to Foam
Foaming of egg white can be done in dierent settings and with a variety of machinery. In
this preliminary work the whipping ability of three dierent mixers were tested. Ultra Turrax
(Yellow Line DI 25 basic) and OBH Nordica immersion blender were not able to whip the

was able to mix egg whites to foam in less than 40 seconds with the standard beater blade
mounted.
It was found that the volume of one whole egg white (thin and rm) is not enough to whip
egg white to foam. A volume of two whole egg whites (rm and thin) was tested and found
sucient to whip to foam in a 250 mL glass beaker. In the main experiment two whole egg
whites are merged in a glass beaker and whipped with the BaMix blender in 40 seconds.
An estimate of the foam volume after whipping is important to calculate the foaming ca-
pability (called overrun dened in Chapter 2). During the preliminary work the following
observations were made in relation to estimating foam volume: Some of the foam is trapped
in the blender head after whipping and the foam does not have a plane surface after whip-
ping. It was decided to transfer the foam trapped in the blender head back to the beaker by
gently beating the blender head against the hand. The transferred foam results however in a
more height variable foam surface. This makes the volume estimate dicult and eliminates
directly reading o the volume from the beaker. Measuring several heights of the foam in the
beaker followed by estimating the foam volume from the average height and beaker diameter
is suggested by Alamprese et al. (2012a). This approached was tested with four measuring
positions throughout the beaker. The variable foam surface caused measuring diculties
and a gentle smoothing of the foam surface was applied. The smoothed surface made the
measurements more reliable and therefore it was adapted to the main experiment along with
the four heights procedure.

4.8
Data Analysis
The median of the results obtained at each day of experiments is chosen to represent results
obtained in this study. This is done as the median is robust to extreme samples (outliers)
which the mean is not, especially when working with small sample sizes. The sample size of
the experiments is four, except for weight data where the sample size is 28.
Statistical analyses of data are done by paired t-test, one-way ANOVA followed by Turkey's
test (Multiple Comparison Test) and linear regression in MatLab 2014b (MathWorks). In
this relation the obtained results are assumed to be normally distributed and the samples to
be independent.
Partial Least Squares (PLS) regressions were used to predict the parameters storage time
and pH based on NIR measurements. Models were built for the thin and rm egg white
individually as well as a combined model on both data sets. The PLS regressions on the
recorded NIR spectra and pH values and storage time were made in Unscrambler 10.3 (Camo
Software).
The wavelength range was reduced to 873,3-1890,9 nm to avoid noise in the
spectra. Further variable selection was made with the build-in function Uncertainty-test
and applied in the regressions which performance benets from the procedure. All of the
NIR spectra were preprocessed with Multiplicative Scatter Correction (MSC) to eliminate
undesired scatter eects. The number of components in the regression models was chosen
based on the explained variance. The actual component was found as the last component
where an actual increase in explained variance is found. The validation procedure was done
by cross validation by leaving random sets of four and two samples out for the storage time
and pH regressions, respectively. The sample size of the PLS regressions made with storage
time as dependent variable is 48 for thin and 47 for rm egg white. Whereas the sample size
for the PLS regressions made with pH as dependent variable are 12 for the whole storage
period and 7 for the rst 10 days in which the pH increased approximately linearly. This
reduction in sample size is necessary as the pH values were not obtained from the same egg
white samples as the NIR spectra were recorded at. PLS regressions were made on both rm
and thin egg white data individually and combined.

the eggs were hatched on the same day, fed the same forage and kept under the same living
conditions in the same farm. This indicates that egg is a raw material with great natural
variation in weights.
The initial egg weight was found to be 46.79 g. Compared to other studies this is a low initial
weight, as the mean egg weight is reported to be approximately 58 g to 60 g (Shenstone,
1968; Li-Chan et al., 1995; Belitz et al., 2009). The eggs used in this study are from young
hens just started producing eggs. This can explain the lower weight recorded in this study
compared to the values reported in the literature as there is a positive correlation between
hen age and egg weight (Hill & Hall, 1980; Silversides & Scott, 2001). The farmer who own
the hens which supplied the egg for this study told that the weight of the hen is positively
correlated with initial egg weight. The relation between hen weight, hen age and initial egg
weight would be interesting in future work.

5.2.5
Weights and pH
The total weight of the eggs keeps falling linearly throughout the storage period. The pH
levels o at values of approximately 9 and does thereby not increase through the whole storage
period. This means that there is a constant evaporation from the eggs throughout the storage
period which is not reected in the pH value. Two dierent mechanisms explaining this are
hypothesized. The carbonate-bicarbonate equilibrium has reached a pH range where a buer
system in the egg white is present. In this case CO2 evaporates without the evaporation being
reected in the pH. The other hypothesis is that the amount of CO2 (g) which evaporates
the rst 10 days are negligible compared to the amount of water (g) evaporating from the
egg. A theoretical calculation of the amount of CO2 (g) evaporated could be done to verify
the second hypothesis which is found mos likely.
The weights of FEW and TEW are identied to be on a weight plateau until approximately
10 days of storage. Day 10 of storage is additionally identied to be the day from which the
recorded pH values levels exceeds 9 and levels of in the rest of the storage period for both
FEW and TEW. This indicates that the FEW is not converted into TEW before the pH
is elevated to a value of approximately pH 9. Further work on development of separation
technique for the whites and the following weighing are needed to conrm these results.

Further examination of the experimental data obtained when increasing the shear rates re-

whole storage period. The Power Law which is used widely in the literature can therefore
not be tted to the whole shear rate range (0.1 s−1 to 400 s−1) obtained in this experiment.
Therefore it was decided to divide the whole shear rate range into more appropriate ranges
trying to catch the movements in the experimental data. From approximately 10 s−1 to
the last shear rate (400 s−1 ) before decrease all the curves during the storage period in-
creases more steeply and linearly compared with the shear rates below this point. The lower
boundary of 10 s−1 can be discussed as the steep increase sets o earlier than this point
for the most of the experimental data obtained. By using 10 s−1 as lower boundary the
steep increase is found in all of the samples and by this approach the latter data treatment
is made on the same basis. The remaining part of the data from 0.1 s−1 to 10 s−1 is not
equally easy to interpret. In the beginning of this range (approximately 0.1 to 0.2 s−1) the
most of the data have a start up tail, where the shear stresses increases more rapidly with
increasing shear rates than in the rest of the range. This initiation behaviour can originate
from measurement diculties in the instrument. After this initiation the stresses are found
to level out and almost no increase in shear stresses is found until a shear rate between 1
s−1 and 10 s−1 approximately. In this range the stresses gradually increases until a certain
point where the steeper increase begins which for all samples is at last at shear rate 10 s−1.
By eliminating the start up diculties and reducing the range from 0.1 s−1 to 10 s−1 to the
range 0.2 s−1 to 10 s−1 a linear regression can be made on the data. However, the t of this
lower range is expected to be worse than the t in the upper range between 10 s−1 to 400
s−1 due to the dierent movements in the experimental data in the lower shear rate range.

The recorded NIR spectra were used to make PLS regressions to predict the quality parame-
ters storage time and pH. The results of this work are found in Table 5.2. The pH regressions

and 0.1991) are much higher than the RMSEs for the calibration (0.0200, 0.0492, 0.0732 and

dicts the low pH values higher than the actual values and predicts the high pH values lower
than the actual values. The low number of samples along with the use of mean values of the
pH is found to account for the poor performance as full cross validation did not improve the
performance signicant. The regression on the combined data set (rm + thin) performs
slightly better. However, the same systematic error is found for this regression. Since there
is found a slight increase in performance by increasing the sample number in the regression it
is found possible that paired data (NIR and pH measured on the same sample) and a higher
sample size will be able to predict the pH in egg whites.
The storage time was found to be predicted well with relatively low validation RMSE's for
the individual data (4.6966 and 4.2635) and an improvement in the RMSE of the validation is
found for the combined regression (3.2584). Rough variable selection by the build-in function
in Unscrambler was done for all three calibrations and improvements in the performances
were found.
The systematic error reported for the pH regressions were only found to a
very small degree in the individual regressions of storage time for rm and thin egg white.
The regression on the combined data did not show the systematic errors and performed
generally in a very satisfactory manner. This model are, however, not directly applicable
in the industry as a new strategy of assessment of egg freshness. The model is built on
one batch of highly similar eggs from the same strain/line and of the same age etc. and
is therefore not robust against the biological dierences. This issue should be faced as it
have been demonstrated in the literature that the Haugh Unit is inadequate to describe such
biological dierences in the eggs. A new method should not have the same shortcomings.
Doing several storage experiments on a broad range of egg types and doing careful data
pretreatment and subsequent variable selection would achieve a more complete regression
model. Additionally the regression should be updated frequently to be robust against general
drifts in the egg whites. However, the result obtained for the combined regression is very
promising and denitively a method with potential for quickly and easily determination of
egg age in the future.
This is supported by the ndings by Németh et al.

Closing Arguments
In the following the ndings in this study are summarised and coupled to pH. This is done as
pH is hypothesised to be the most important factor for the changes during storage. Within
the rst 7 to 10 days pH was found to increase to approximately pH 9. In this period there
was found a rapid reduction of the initial dierences in TS content between FEW and TEW,
a 3 to 4 days increase in the moduli and therefore in the overall resistance to deformation
(G*) was found in the FEW, and the foam instability increases rapidly. After day 10 the pH
was levelled out around pH 9 and from this day the ratio of FEW/TEW began to decrease,
a global increase in TS content was found and the consistency indexes for the TEW were
found to be overall lowered.
The rapid reduction of the initial dierences in TS between FEW and TEW while pH in-
creases was not reected in the weight data. The weight of FEW and TEW rst decreases
after pH have reached pH 9. The changes in TS are hypothesised to be by migration of
water which is described in the literature. This migration is supposed to change the weight
of the egg whites and since this was not recorded the migration may include some exchange
of TS content. The overall reduction of the initial TS content dierences is hypothesised to
be dependent of osmosis rather than the increase in pH.
In relation to the rheological properties it can be hypothesised that the pH between 8.36 and
9.06 in the FEW and between 8.14 and 9.07 in the TEW somehow enhances or stabilises the
internal structure. This was clearly pronounced in the FEW where a signicant rise in the
elastic and viscous moduli was found. After pH reached 9.07 in the TEW the consistency
indexes of the TEW were lowered which indicates that some structural degrading changes
may have happened. The causality of the relation between pH and the rheological trends
could be veried in future work by further study of the protein interactions and rheological
tests on egg white with controlled pH.
During the entire storage period the weight decreased linearly and the ow behaviour indexes
behaved randomly. These two variables are therefore hypothesized to be the only variables
which are not highly dependent on pH. However, a fraction of the weight lost is constituted
by CO2 otherwise pH was not able to increase. In future work a theoretical estimate of the
amount CO2 evaporated could be done.
The degree of causality between the dierent physical and chemical changes can be ap-
proached in future work by eliminating the pH changes in the egg. This can be done by
oiling the eggs or by storing eggs in modied atmosphere with increased amount of CO2
present immediately after being laid, cf. Chapter 2.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this study changes during 45 days of storage in the physical and chemical properties
in terms of pH, weight, content of total solids and rheological properties of rm and thin
egg white have been investigated and characterised. Additionally the functional property
foaming on whole egg white has been investigated and characterised along the other prop-
erties. During the development of the laboratory techniques the rm and thin egg white
were characterised as a gel and as a liquid, respectively. This resulted in development of
dierent rheological measuring procedures for the two fractions based on descriptions found
in the reviewed literature. Oscillatory testing techniques were developed for the rm egg
white and rotational viscometry technique was developed for the thin egg white. In general
most of the properties investigated in this study were found to change during storage. This
conrms hypothesis 1 presented in the introduction which is that the physical, chemical and
functional properties of egg whites changes signicantly during storage in shell.
The pH of both thin and rm egg white was found to increase from initial values of 8.14 and
8.37 to 9.07 and 9.06 at day 10, respectively. From day 10 to the end of the storage period
(day 45) the pH levelled out around 9. During the storage period the measured pH of thin
egg whites was found to be signicant (p<0.01) lower than the pH of rm egg white. By
weight the rm egg white constituted approximately twice as much as the thin egg white
from day 0 to 10 in storage period. At storage beyond day 10 this ratio decreased as the rm
egg white was thinned into thin egg white and evaporated to the surroundings. The content
of total solids (%) was found to decrease from 14.17 % to 13.21 % in the thin egg white during
the rst 4 days of storage. In the same storage period the content of total solids was found to
increase from 12.40 % to 13.17 % in the rm egg white. After 10 days of storage the overall
content of total solids in both rm and thin egg white began to increase. The rheological
behaviour of the rm egg white was characterised to be a gel with elevated elastic and viscous
moduli (G' and G) and therefore increased overall resistance to deformation (G*) at day 3
and 4 and partly at day 7. The remaining days (0, 1, 2, 10, 14, 17, 24, 31 and 45) in the
storage period showed no signicant dierences in the moduli. The rheology of the thin egg

white was characterised to be a liquid with shear thinning behaviour in the shear rate range
0.2 s−1 to 10 s−1 and shear thinning to Newtonian behaviour at shear rates in the range 10
s−1 to 400 s−1. No signicant changes in the consistency indexes or ow behaviour indexes in
the thin egg white were found during the storage period. However a not signicant tendency
of lowered consistency indexes was found after day 14. As the moduli of the rm egg white
were not changed at storage beyond 10 days it is proposed that the internal structure of the
rm egg white is not altered by thinning. No correlations between the moduli of the rm
egg white and ow behaviour indexes and consistency indexes of the thin egg white were
identied during storage. The experiment made on foaming capability was found dicult to
conduct and very diering results were obtained from which no conclusions are made. The
foam instability test was easier to perform and more consistent results were obtained in this
experiment. The foam instability reached highest levels for 30, 60 and 90 min. storage in
refrigerator at day 4 and levelled out from this point. The most stable foams were produced
at day 0.
The rheological characteristics, the content of total solids, weights and pH are found to be
dierent in the two egg white fractions initially and during storage. Hypothesis 2 presented
in the introduction saying that the native rm and thin egg white are initially dierent
and therefore changes dierently during storage is conrmed for these physical and chemical
properties.
In general the results can be divided into three groups, the changes which happened while
pH increases, the changes which happened after pH reached and stabilised at approximately
pH 9 and the changes which seemed independent of the changes in pH. The changes which
happened while pH increases are, rapid reduction of the initial dierences in total solid
content in rm and thin egg white, increase followed by similar decrease in the elastic and
viscous moduli of rm egg white and the foam instability increasing and levelling out rapidly.
The changes which happened after pH levels out is, decrease in the weight ratio of rm/thin
egg white, increase in content of total solids in rm and thin egg white and an overall lowering
of the consistency indexes for the thin egg white. The weight of the eggs decreased linearly
through the storage period and was therefore not found strongly aected by changes in pH.
The same was concluded for the ow behaviour indexes of the thin egg white which behaved
randomly during the storage period.
To determine the causality of the correlations and
relations between pH and the individual physical, chemical and functional properties during
storage more work is needed. The extend of hypothesis 3 presented in the introduction saying
that the changes in physical, chemical and functional properties during storage are correlated
to the increase in pH is therefore not fully elucidated yet.
The PLS regression models on the recorded NIR spectra with the storage time and pH
as dependent variables were found promising and clearly indicate that the NIR spectra
contain information related to storage time and pH. The regression models made on the
mean NIR spectra and pH values were found to perform poorly in the validation process in
this study. Whereas the regression models made on the NIR spectra and storage time were
found to perform very well both on individual rm and thin egg white and especially on the
combined data. Renement of the both types of regression models by increasing sample size,
including biological dierent eggs, using paired data and further calibration work is found

Chapter 7
Future Work and Perspectives
During this master's thesis suggestions for future work have been put forward. The most
emphasized suggestion for further work is analysis of the proteins in the rm and thin egg
white.
The protein analyses which are suggested to be done in future work are namely
quantication of total amount of protein and quantication of the dierent proteins along
with qualication.
Such results would reveal the dierences between the two egg white
fractions and improve understanding of the physical, chemical and functional properties of
the egg whites. Dierent analyses have to be tested and developed before the work during
a storage period can begin. For example ovomucin has a too large molecular weight to be
detected in 2-DE (2 dimensional electrophoresis) (Omana et al., 2010). Samples of rm and
thin egg white for protein analyses were made and put in the freezer during the experimental
work in this master's thesis. However, there was not found time to plan and conduct the
protein analyses of the thin and rm egg white in the time frame of this master's thesis.
Another experiment which could be done to improve understanding of the physical, chemical
and functional properties of the egg whites is an experiment with reduced or eliminated
evaporation of CO2 and thereby reduced or eliminate the increase in pH. The causality
between the properties tested and the pH could be rejected or conrmed. This could be done
by either oiling the eggs immediately after laying or by storing the eggs in an atmosphere
with elevated CO2 content. If the properties are highly pH dependent and the changes in
these can be reduced by elimination of evaporation of CO2, modied atmosphere packaging
or oiling could be considered in the future.
Experiments which could be added to the ones conducted in this master's thesis are testing
the buer capacity as described in Chapter 3, further rheological measurements and experi-
ments investigating the functional property gelling which is important in the use of eggs in
the industry and in household kitchens. Additionally separating the rm and thin egg white
in the outer and inner layers would be interesting. However, this is not found to be easy and
applicable so the relevance in relation to the food industry would be minor.

determine the potential of the NIR combined with PLS regressions as quick on-line measuring

All of the above mentioned experiments would be interesting to conduct throughout a laying
season of the hens. Hereby the eects of hen age and weight on the physical, chemical and

It has to be mentioned that there has been some considerations regarding the repeatabil-

uncertainty would be an advantage in future work along with increased samples sizes.
As nal remark it is expected that a full characterisation and understanding of the egg
whites during storage will be benecial for the stakeholders in the egg industry related to
food technology. By fully understanding the raw material dierent steps in the processing
chain can be optimised and yielding better and more stable end products. PLS regression
models on NIR spectra and either pH or storage time is a possible tool to use in the process.
