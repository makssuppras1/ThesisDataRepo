Preface
This thesis is the fruit of the last semester of stud-
ies for us, Villads Sørensen & Andreas Ranch, 
at the Design & Innovation programme at the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). We 
have been engaged with the thesis work since 
the end of January 2018. The written thesis is 
accompanied by a defence on the 11th of July 
2018. The thesis has been undertaken based on a 
proposed concept idea from the start-up com-
pany SOWA Speakers: to develop a concept for 
a wireless multi-speaker solution for the profes-
sional audio industry. We are both engaged with 
SOWA directly as co-founders.
The SOWA project stems from the course “Inno-
vation and Product Development” (IPD) of 2017 
in which students from various programmes 
are mustered to develop a spin-out company 
from an idea provided by a case company. Our 
case company was PointSource Acoustics out of 
Roskilde Denmark – a loudspeaker design and 
manufacturing consultancy. The inceptive idea 
was reframed from speakers for hotel guests to 
wireless loudspeakers for events, as the members of 
the team had a common background in sound 
engineering and music. The experience in the 
professional audio industry among the team 
members coupled with the notion of a wireless 
speaker sparked the question: why are wireless 
speakers so rare in this field? A short field study 
and a high level product and business concept 
was developed, and due to the feedback received 
on the project, the start-up was founded in the 
fall 2017. PointSource Acoustics are still part of 
the team. 
	
Our experiences with the professional au-
dio industry are polar: Andreas has seven years of 
experience with equipment, venues and working 
with other technicians, Villads has no prior ex-
perience in the professional audio industry. This 
difference has been helpful many times through 
the thesis and project work, by providing a 
balance between challenging assumptions, ask-
ing questions about the “obvious”, and breaking 
down the contents of problems.
Villads Sørensen & Andreas Ranch, Copenhagen,

IV
Abstract
This thesis takes on the observation that wireless 
speakers are rarely found in the professional 
audio industry, although other equipment cat-
egories have become wireless over the years: e.g. 
microphones and monitors. 
Using Actor-Network Theory combined with 
Effectuation, we set out to investigate entrepre-
neurial opportunities for the start-up SOWA 
Speakers.  Through field work gathered and 
synthesised inputs from relevant actors in the 
professional audio industry,  a new approach to 
mapping a “Development Arena” is employed. 
This approach introduces a three dimensional 
mapping of “actor-worlds” that reside within the 
arena in order to identify relevant use cases for 
conceptualisation, as well as to explore how the 
actors within the co-existing actor worlds (use 
cases) ascribe different values to the contents of 
the development arena. This is done in order to 
suggest a wide ranging concept, satisfying several 
use cases. The identified use cases are talks, play-
back and party.  
	
Through a functional decomposition, 
combined with brainstorming, a product devel-
opment specification was developed for SOWA 
along with 4 concepts for embodiment. One 
concept was selected over the others, as its simple 
embedded handle and shape allowed for great 
ergonomics and versatility in placement. The 
concept was presented as a future vision for 
SOWA to work towards, using functional proto-
types to engage their network in the professional 
audio industry.

VI
Motivation
Having followed the Design & Innovation pro-
gramme at The Technical University of Denmark 
through to the Master’s thesis, both authors are 
schooled in developing innovative problem-solv-
ing and user-centered solutions. However, the 
context in which we as design engineers work 
is not always clearly defined. In this thesis we 
wish to look into how an entrepreneurial context 
affects both the development process and the 
final concept. We wish to do so by a literature 
review  on entrepreneurial opportunities and also 
by concept development in the case of wireless 
speakers for professionals.  
	
Wireless and portable speakers are 
everywhere in the consumer market and comes 
in all sizes and shapes. It is out observation that 
the professional audio industry has yet to adopt 
the wireless technology in loudspeakers, though 
other products in the industry have been wire-
less for many years (e.g. microphones & in-ear 
monitors). The case company SOWA believes 
that the future in professional event-loudspeakers 
is wireless, and we seek to identify how SOWA 
should go about bringing a wireless loudspeaker 
into existence.

1.1  Introduction
The present thesis engages with two questions 
related to development of a wireless speaker 
concept in case of the startup company SOWA 
Speakers. On the product-development side 
it concerns re-opening the frame provided by 
SOWA, by opening up interactions with relevant 
actors and scrutinising translations. By this we 
set out to suggest a concept that not only show-
cases the idea of ‘wirelessness’ in a professional 
field, but also takes into account how the ascrip-
tions of relevant actors affect the more detailed 
embodiment of the concept. 
	
On the theoretical side, the thesis takes 
up the question: how should the design engi-
neers go about employing design methodology 
in a start-up, as contrasted to a corporation? This 
question arose from the observation that much 
of our own education at The Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark has revolved around a framing 
that the hypothetical design work conducted by 
students is carried out in a fictional setting of 
a large company. Some of the methods tought 
require a supportive organisation, departments 
of knowledge, quantitative data and so forth. 
The start-up framing, we find ourselves in with 
SOWA, as case company, has other priorities, 
budgets and last but not least possibilities. We 
therefore explore answers to how design in start-
ups can be conducted, as well as an attempt to 
implement our findings.

Audio used in a professional context can be 
anything from the speakers used at an airport or 
in public transport for providing information, to 
a restaurant with background music, to a board-
room for conference calls. During the thesis 
the pro audio industry will be referred to as the 
industry SOWA should target with a wireless 
multi-peaker product, but what is meant exactly 
by the “professional audio industry”? 
	
Professional-grade audio equipment is an 
entire category of products known as ‘pro au-
dio’. This category has probably come to exist in 
a need for equipment that can do more or other 
things than regular consumer audio equipment. 
This notion of more refers to the need of many 
sound sources and speakers running in parallel, 
and the need for the product to be sturdy as it 
can be moved around to events regularly. The 
professional-grade audio equipment also rely on 
other connectors and cable standards than the 
ones found on consumer equipment. 
In the context of SOWAs wireless multi-speaker 
concept, we will limit the scope of the profes-

speakers for events and productions. The purpose 
of this scope is to make a reasonable delimina-
tion for the thesis. One can also rationalise that

of the Double-Diamond model of design (Nor-
man 2002). The double diamond process is a 
model for ensuring human centered design, and 
that the resulting product is usable, accomplishes 
desired tasks and the use of it is enjoyable (Nor-
man 2002 p. 219). This is done by continuously 
iterating on observation, idea generation, proto-
typing, and testing (Norman 2002 p. 222).
As visualised in Figure 3, the pre-thesis SOWA 
project led to an idea for a business model along 
with two prototypes (see “SOWA Pre-thesis” on

diverging and converging diamond, we seek to 
discover (and verify) problems and define suita-
ble use cases. The purpose of the second diamond 
is to generate concepts and lastly select the most 
suitable concept for SOWA to develop in the 
future.

2.1  Entrepreneurial 
Management
Traditionally startups have been viewed as small 
versions of established companies, thus using 
similar tools for management (Blank 2006). 
Every company (startup or corporate giant) have 
their own product development, launch and 
life-cycle management, which include "detailed 
plans, checkpoints and goals for every step in getting 
a product out the door; sizing markets, estimating 
sales, developing marketing requirements documents, 
prioritizing product features" (Blank 2006, p. 9), 
which falls under the category of stage-gated 
models. Stage gate can be characterized as a 
“conceptual and an operational model for moving a 
new product from idea to launch” (Cooper 1990, 
p. 1). The model oftentimes contain defined 
phases with predetermined results required to 
continue to the next step. "Stage gate models are 
typically introduced in companies once they grow 
over a certain size, in order to make the innovation 
process more manageable, to facilitate coordination 
of information and tasks" (Ahmed-Kristensen & 
Daalhuizen 2015, p. 1). Facilitating the coordi-
nation of information and tasks is also described 
by Ulrich & Eppinger 2008 (p. 380), who refer 
to project management as the “activity of plan-
ning and coordinating resources and tasks to achieve 
these goals” which occurs during both project 
planning and project execution. Ulrich & Ep-
pinger 2008 present  set of tools to identify task 
dependencies and timing along with representing 
relationships among project tasks.
As a response to the stage-gated models, move-
ments in entrepreneurial management have 
emerged in an effort to reduce complexity and 
timely management. While some are based on re-
search (e.g. Effectuation, Agile and Scrum), oth-
ers are less scientifically grounded. An example of 
the latter is “The Lean Startup" movement, first 
presented by Eric Ries in the book of the same 
title. While just few of the ideas in the book were 
groundbreaking or new, Ries managed to convey 
the principles of testing, measuring, changing, 
and retesting several hypotheses before a product 
launch, using "minimum viable products" abbre-
viated MVP (Stagars 2014). This overall idea of 
The Lean Startup is shared between most entre-
preneurial management philosophies: spending 
as little resources as possible on market analyses 
while quickly gathering insight and customer 
feedback with the least amount of time and mon-
ey spent. Effectuation share some of the same 
core principles, which can shortly be summa-
rized: "start with a given set of means focusing on 
what can be done, risk no more than you can afford 
to lose, leverage contingencies, form alliances with 
committed stakeholders, and focus on the things you 
can control to shape the future when it is seen as 
unpredictable" (Agogué et al. 2015, p. 632).
Agile is a generic description of methods that 
"are low ceremony, people-centric, less formal, iter-
ative, and collaborative-based" (Gandomani et al. 
==PAGE:20==
10
2014, p. 1). One of them is Scrum. Scrum is a 
framework for developing and sustaining com-
plex products. Scrum builds on three pillars of 
empirical process control: (1) transparency, (2) 
inspection, and (3) adaptation. This means that 
(1) significant aspects of the process must be visible 
for relevant team members, who also share a common 
definition of what “done” means. (2) Scrum work 
is based on "sprints" and users must frequently be 
inspected to detect undesirable variances. Lastly (3) 
if one or more aspects of a process deviate outside 
acceptable limits, adjustment must be made as soon 
as possible to minimize further deviation (Schwaber 
& Sutherland 2016).
Being agile or effectual is not without compro-
mise though, as Gandomani et al. 2014 (p. 240) 
describes: "There are different challenges, obsta-
cles, and problems that companies encounter when 
adopting Agile methods". That is why "A new trend 
is being observed, where gated models are becoming 
more agile, and agile models more gated. This trend 
indicates a need to combine the models, recognizing 
the limitations of both" (Ahmed-Kristensen & 
Daalhuizen 2015, p. 1). The need for combin-
ing effectual and causal methods have also been 
identified in the effectuation literature: "In terms 
of entrepreneurial expertise, a combination of causal 
and effectual thinking is necessary to ensure exploita-
tion of already discovered or created opportunities 
and exploration of new ones" (Sharma & Salvato 
2011, p. 1).
2.2  Opportunity Exploration or 
Creation
An opportunity is in the Cambridge English Dic-
tionary defined as an "occasion or situation that 
makes it possible to do something you want to do, or 
have to do, or the possibility of doing something". In 
the context of entrepreneurship this definition 
seems lacking in the sense that it does not define 
the purpose of wanting to do something, neither 
does it cover where and how to discover oppor-
tunities. Further, this definition seems to imply a 
connotation of opportunities to entail or promise 
success – put differently, in the daily word oppor-
tunity, there is connotation of the opportunity 
to be directly seizable. A problem is defined as a 
“situation, person, or thing that needs attention and 
needs to be dealt with or solved”. Problem solving 
is thus, as opposed to the definition of oppor-
tunities, directly addressing a need or a purpose, 
without necessarily detailing success.
In his book Engineering Design Methods (Cross 
2008) Cross covers the subject of Identifying 
Opportunities (p. 59 forward). Cross avoids a clear 
definition of opportunities, and employs instead 
a practical approach to identifying opportunities. 
Cross points out that "most producer companies" 
release "many products ... that seem not to have 
been designed with their user in mind." (p. 59). 
He then proposes the method of user scenarios as 
==PAGE:21==
11
providing "a useful starting point and focus for the 
design process by taking the user's point of view, and 
identifying opportunities for creating new products 
that aim at satisfying users' needs." In our interpre-
tation Cross suggests searching for opportunities 
in a not clearly defined group of users. In an ef-
fort to understand the larger scope and environ-
ment around Cross’ design processes in relation 
to product planning and development (p. 204), 
his context is centered around established com-
panies with clear management, marketing, and 
design teams (p. 205). At the end of the book he 
suddenly employs another definition of oppor-

opportunities for new product development in

3.3  Effectuation
The study concludes that these entrepreneurs pre-
dominantly utilise a decision process that Saras-
vathy calls ‘effectual reasoning’ rather than causal, 
or predictive, reasoning (Sarasvathy 2001a). 
Effectuation originates in entrepreneurship, 
namely the creation of new markets and new 
businesses and is extremely stakeholder-depend-
ent and means-driven (Reymen 2009). It begins 
with a pre-determined goal and a given set of 
means, and seeks to identify the optimal (fast-
est, cheapest, most efficient, etc.) alternative to 
achieve the given goal (Sarasvathy 2001a).  
 
Effectuation works both as an entrepreneurial 
mindset but essentially the purpose is to use the 
logic of crontrol: “To the extent that we can con-
trol the future, we do not need to predict it” (Saras-
vathy 2001b, p. 252). Cusation is often referred 
==PAGE:31==
21
to as the opposite of effectuation, and rests on 
a logic of prediction instead: “To the extent that 
we can predict the future, we can control it” (ibid.). 
Sarasvathy continues to summarize effectuation 
into four core principles:
1. “Affordable loss rather than expected re-
turns”: Instead of focusing on the highest po-
tential returns, the entrepreneur should focus on 
how much loss is affordable given the (limited) 
means, while keeping as many opportunities 
as possible open (see how we define the term 
‘opportunity’ in “Opportunity Exploration or 
Creation” on page 10). The effectuator should 
prefer strategies that provide options in the 
future over one that maximize returns in the 
present.
2. “Strategic alliances rather than competitive 
analyses”: Effectuation emphasizes partnerships 
and commitments over competitive analyses. 
Partnerships and commitments are seen as a way 
to reduce uncertainty and also break potential 
barriers.
3. “Exploitation of contingencies rather than 
exploitation of preexisting knowledge”: The 
future is uncertain and surprises are not inherent-
ly something bad. When contingencies arise they 
should be exploited over time.
4. “Controlling an unpredictable future rather 
than predicting an uncertain one”: When the 
future is predictable and goals are well known 
and easily defined, causal logic can be useful. 
When creating opportunities (see litterature re-
view for elaboration of “2.2 Opportunity Explo-
ration or Creation” on page 10) the goals and 
tasks can be unclear, and in these cases the entre-
preneur should focus on what can be controlled.
The four principles of effectuation are utilised 
mainly in two ways: in our engagement as 
designers with actors during field work and, as 
a driver for decision during the conceptualis-
ation phase of our design project, along with the 
knowledge gathered from our field study: specif-
ically, it has been used to select the product most 
suitable for an entrepreneurial venture: which 
concept would be the most suitable to develop 
given the existing network SOWA is connected 
with, and which provide future options without 
substantial potential loss?  Lastly, the principles 
are also used to suggest further work for SOWA 
towards the end of the project.  
	
Employing effectuation is based on a 
choice of approach towards entrepreneurial man-
agement (see Litterature review, “Entrepreneurial 
Management” on page 9). 
Effectuation indicates  that many things are hard 
to predict, and that the entrepreneur should 
“start somewhere”, and pivot according to the 
feedback one gets. We see this as very close to the 
notion from ANT, that networks are created and 
defined by translations between actants. Effectua-
tion in this sense provides a practical, in-the-mo-
ment ANT analysis for the entrepreneur: act on 
the network around you, and respond according 
to the feedback actions (translations). In this 
sense, the ANT and effectuation-aware entrepre-
neur sees him- or herself as an actor network that 
is very willing to respond to translations from 
other actors.

4.2  Assumptions in the 
Professional Audio Field
One may say that assumptions are the norm in 
our every day activity and that life would be too 
complex were it not for assumptions (Brown 
2006, p. 3); indeed “We are assumption machines.” 
(Niquette 1996) and almost any statement we 
make may be considered a simplification of – or 
subjective viewpoint upon the world. It is there-
fore not surprising that many design methods 
seek to challenge the assumptions of both design-
ers and users. Brown 2006 lists a range of major 
reasons why assumptions are made, among them 
the “arrogance of experts”.
The professional speaker market is tied to wired 
speaker solutions based on assumptions. At the 
core of these assumptions we find, among the 
actors of our field studies and previous experience 
the assumptions, that:
1.	 The latency assumption  Wireless trans-
mission of audio induces too much latency 
for it to be used in professional speaker 
contexts.
2.	 The unreliability assumption  Wireless 
technology is inherently fragile and unrelia-
ble.
3.	 The stereo assumption  Speaker systems 
should usually consist of a stereo setup (two 
speakers).
The first two assumptions are often met among 
technicians when confronted with the idea of 
employing wireless speakers in their work. In 
the field studies we have conducted, the subjects 
were either approached with a request for them 
to imagine using wireless speakers in their work, 
or an introduction to the initial SOWA idea of 
employing wireless speakers in professional use, 
with a follow-up question of what their opinion 
towards that idea was. A common response to 
both of these two ways of initiating conversation 
were often met with a reply of “that it will not 
work because [assumption 1 or 2]”. It would often 
take some convincing, reminding the subjects 
that live professional audio indeed already uses 
wireless technology with microphones and 
control signals. Subsequently the subjects would 
mostly “open up” towards the idea.

In the following paragraphs we seek to explain 
why and how these three base assumptions linger.
Assumption About Latency
A statement often heard from sound technicians 
is something in the direction of: “Wireless trans-
mission of audio induces too much latency for it to 
be used in professional speaker contexts”
The assumption contains two fallacies:
1.	 All speaker use in professional contexts is 
latency sensitive.
2.	 A recorded sound cannot be transmitted 
wirelessly at a speed such that the latency is 
imperceptible by the human ear when heard 
in playback comparison (monitoring).
As explained in “Boundary Objects & Points of 
Tension” in appendix 44, not all profession-
al use cases are latency sensitive, meaning that 
some use cases would still be feasible with several 
milliseconds of latency. Case in point: a musician 
or DJ may be very sensitive to latency as they rely 
on the speaker sound for timing, contrary to a 
computer or tablet playing background music.
It is true that digital solutions will always in-
duce latency due to the way digital buffering of 
analog signals work. This latency is dependent 
on the size of the buffer and how many samples 
it draws of the analog signal. Thus, the smaller 
the buffer size, the lower the latency will be from 
recording of a sound until it can be played back 
by a speaker. Smaller buffer sizes demand more of 
processing power. It could thus be popularly said 
that latency in digital systems decrease according 
to the so called Moore’s law. 
	
Analogue signals, however, can be trans-
mitted with virtually zero latency, which is the 
technology used in many wireless microphones.

Assumption About Stereo 
Setups
Since the invention of stereo audio in the late 
19th century, up until its first applications in 
broadcasts to consumers homes in the mid 20th 
century, the stereo platform in sound has estab-
lished itself as the main and inescapable means of 
storing and playing audio.
However, sound does not need to be conveyed 
in stereo. One may go as far as to claim that all 
sounds by definition are monophonic, and that 
stereo speaker setups only make sense in situa-
tions where several sound sources are played back 
in the same recording, or when a sound source 
needs to be illusionally moved around in relation 
the sound-spatial perspective of the listener.
In many applications monophonic sound is ei-
ther sufficient or more suitable than stereo audio. 
Music played at low levels at a hotel or restaurant 
as a sonic backdrop may probably be satisfying 
enough when played in mono, since the “lis-
tener” is not listening closely or even noticing 
the music. In conveying speech, as in radio, or 
someone talking from a stage, the voice would 
always be played in mono because the objective 
is understanding for as many listeners as possible, 
not spatial awareness of the sound source.
The general assumption of stereo audio we find 
in interaction with relevant actors to wireless 
speaker development, and our background ex-
perience, and it entails a general default assump-
tion of speaker systems to be stereo. This default 
assumption again entails, that a speaker system 
consisting of more than two speakers is some 
kind of deviation from the norm, something 
different which needs to be defined, mainly: is it 
a speaker system of many speakers that convey 
a stereo signal to different listening locations, or 
is it a multi-speaker system conveying a mono 
signal anywhere covered by the speakers.

or over time as an event may change character

Selecting Use Cases Effectually
Effectuation urges entrepreneurs to use their 
network and competencies at hand to develop 
a first version of a product quickly, rather than 
the ‘best’ version of a product (see “Effectuation” 
on page 20 in the “Theoretical Framework” 
chapter). Effectuation also states that one should 
prefer strategies that provide options in the future 
over one that maximize returns in the present. 
As we see it, the product that provides the most 
options in the future, should be a product that 
targets as many use cases as possible. 
For SOWA to be able to develop their first 
product, which use cases should they target? By 
interviewing and observing actors in the defined 
use cases, we have learnt that each use case has it’s 
own demands for a product regarding size, vol-
ume, battery life, maximum latency, etc. Based 
on our holistic understanding of the professional 
audio industry, we investigated the possibility 
of targeting multiple use cases at a time. In an 
effort to quantify how suitable one use case is for 
SOWA to target compared to another, the table 
in Figure 16 was constructed. It is important to

our personal intuition, but as the theory of effec-

analyses.

4.6  Development Arena for 
mapping actants
We employ the Development Arena (DA) on 
the case of ‘Wireless Speakers in a Professional 
Context’ to understand the heterogeneous actor 
worlds (networks) in which this proposed prod-
uct concept exists, and to identify shared bound-
ary objects among these worlds. Specifically, the 
DA seeks to identify points of tension between 
the different actor worlds, due to differently 
ascribed values to boundary objects, thus provid-
ing a foundation from which the most suitable 
product may be developed. Boundary objects are, 
in this context, the actors which are present in 
multiple actor-worlds within the Development 
Arena of wireless speakers. The job that each of 
these actors has to perform in the respective actor 
worlds may be translated differently from one 
to the other, even though the core functionality 
is the same across actor-worlds. The purpose of 
making the Development Arena is to identify 
both challenges and possibilities from which the 
most suitable product may be developed.

objectives and functions is intended as a means

the tree, where each fruit represents a potential 
sub-solution to the overall objective that is ripe 
for picking - what we like to call ‘objectives tree 
with function fruits’ (the entire tree can be found 
in “Functional Decomposition” on page 30).

Prototyping 
Prototypes have been utilised at three stages in

non-technical users would express concerns

of The Rusty Jigger cocktailbar during Distortion

The functionality of a wireless speaker concept

the acoustic properties and wireless technology of 
a speaker, and these are parameters that enevita-
bly can make or break the product - especially in 
the professional audio industry where both sound 
quality and reliability are very important factors.  
	
How the product is to operate describes 
parameters such as ergonomics, placement of 
interface, compatibility with auxillary equipment 
etc. These are all design/embodiment factors that

Discussion of Theoretical 
Framework 
It may be worth posing the question, if it is at 
all necessary to couple the methodology from 
ANT with an entrepreneurial mindset such as 
effectuation? Entertaining this thought, one may 
argue that the methodological approach from 
ANT will entail creation of a network that takes 
into account the status of the start up which is 
suggesting a technological shift, thus clarifying 
the translational effect that this start-up status 
has on the shaping of technology. This is however 
not necessarily an argument against employing 
effectuation, since effectuation in this case just 
acts as support for ANT when encountering this 
sort of network.  
	
One could argue that the suggestions of 
effectuation are more suitable in projects of either 
low technological complexity and or low cost 
products. As of now, the entrepreneur who tries 
to open an indian restaurant (see “Pursuing the 
Opportunity” on page 13) can be compared 
to the arbitrary person Elon, who wants to start 
a private company offering space travel to Mars. 
Effectuation literature does in fact mention that 
causal processes can be beneficial in high-tech-
nology projects: ”When preexisting knowledge, 
such as expertise in a particular new technology, 
forms the source of competitive advantage, causation 
models might be preferable. Effectuation, however, 
would be better for exploiting contingencies that 
arose unexpectedly over time” (Sarasvathy 2001b, 
p. 252). One could argue that the success of the 
Mars-travel company relies more on investments 
and scientific projects, than the effectuation prin-
ciples of affordable loss and embracing contin-
gencies. This thesis, however, has not been based 
on ground-breaking technology, though SOWA 
as a company may also go in this direction in the 
future1.
How has the effectual mindset affected the pro-
cess of developing a new concept in this entrepre-
neurial context? Firstly it has encouraged and en-
sured that our own and SOWA’s existing network 
has been utilised and expanded. Secondly, it fed 
into the decision of building functional proto-
types to test two of the selected use cases at an 
early stage. Lastly, and very specifically, effectua-
tion let us decide on a concept without systemat-
ic scoring of concepts in a decision matrix (which 
is widely used and well described in engineering 
design literature, see “Pursuing the Opportunity” 
on page 13). One could argue that the deci-
sion matrices are based on subjective selection 
==PAGE:80==
70
already, thus being a systematic approach to justi-
fy selecting the concept one believes in the most. 
Effectuation has led us, as design engineers, to 
choose the concept which we believe in the most, 
based on our current understanding of needs 
and the feedback from SOWA (in the future, the 
concept will be subject to change as actors in the 
industry provide feedback).

The overall outputs from the field work and

needs or ascribe different values to these bound-
ary objects. While it is difficult to say anything 
conclusive about the quality of the analysis-out-
puts, as SOWA has yet to launch and sell any 
products targeted at these use cases, it is possible 
to discuss our confidence in the outputs.

gange some relevant people in the audio industry. 
Secondly, that the three selected use cases, for 
now, all seem relevant. 
It is worth noting that there may exist relevant 
use cases that have been completely overlooked 
during fieldwork and analysis. Having followed 
effectual guidelines, we have focused our energy 
on identifying opportunities in the immediate 
proximity of SOWA. As such, there has been no 
international outlook, and while our personal 
experience in the field tells us that the Danish 
professional audio industry has similarities to 
other large markets, we currently have no con-
crete evidence to back it up.
Discussion of the Proposed 
Concept
During the analysis we identified five boundary 
objects of importance for the embodiment of 
a SOWA concept, as they could cause points 
of tension across different use cases: volume & 
dimensions, design language, sound characteris-
tics, portability and compatibility (with auxillary 
equipment). We will briefly go over them one by 
one, discussing how these areas have affected the 
concept and which shortcomings there might be. 
	
Volume & dimensions: The size of the 
speaker has increased from housing a 6” to an 8” 
unit. While the ‘talks’ use case oftentimes can do 
with a smaller speaker size, we believe the com-
==PAGE:81==
71
promise is worthwhile, as it enables SOWA to 
target two use cases with just one product (talks 
& playback) and potentially a third use case (par-
ties) when expanding the product portfolio with 
a subwoofer in the future.  
	
Design Language: The main observation 
from our field work in terms of design language, 
is that professional products can have complex 
interfaces, that potentially can discourage less 
technical users/prosumers. While designing an 
interface optimised for both professionals and 
prosumers is a complex task, that could be a 
whole thesis in itself, we have made a suggestion 
for how to go about developing the interface. 
In order to remove complexity from the master 
unit, without compromising the functionality for 
the audio-professional, we have divided it into 
a physical and a digital interface. The physical 
interface only contains the minimum amount of 
controls needed to connect two microphones and 
an iPod. All other functionality can be moved to 
a digital platform, as we have observed that tablet 
apps are widely used in the industry already. The 
port selection of the master unit has also been 
placed so that the ports needed for microphones 
and iPod are placed on the front, ensuring that 
the less technical users have everything available 
on one side. The remaining ports, that will most-
ly be used by technicians, are placed on the back.  
	
Sound characteristics: It is of our best 
belief that the proposed concept is highly versa-
tile in it’s sound characteristics, as it should be 
since the three use cases have different demands 
for this. This is backed up by Morten Halvorsen, 
who is the acoustics engineer in SOWA. As a 
prosumer, you can quickly select between equal-
iser presets that are optimised for music and for 
speech. If you want to, you can also select ‘man-
ual’ and adjust the equaliser on a mixer (or in the 
SOWA app in the future). The 8” coaxial speaker 
unit is suitable for speech and when accompanied 
by the subwoofer, the whole setup should also 
handle parties well. 
	
Portability: In order for a wireless audio 
system to be truly portable, it must both be 
easy to transport and setup and also run reliably 
without any additional power when running the 
event. The first of the two criteria (easy transport 
and setup) has been a very important focus point 
during the conceptualisation phase. The concept 
has a handle that is practical both when placing 
the speaker on a stand, hanging it from a steel 
bar, or when picking it up from the ground or 
case. The handle is also placed by the edge of the 
speaker, which makes it less uncomfortable to 
walk with, as it may carve into ones legs if the 
handle is unthoughtfully placed.  
	
The second criteria (having sufficient 
and reliable power) is tied to more uncertainty. 
SOWA has yet to decide on a technology for the 
wireless transmission, which means it is cur-
rently impossible to measure how much power 
the transmission will require. Some wireless 
technologies are more power consuming than 
others, which only allows us to come up with a 
best guess for a battery size. The ‘Soundboks’ is 
a Danish developed portable speaker targeted at 
consumers, and it can play up to 40 hours on a 
single charge, on a 8 Ah battery that is approxi-
mately 15x10x6 cm. While the wireless transmis-
sion technology in a SOWA speaker may be more 
power consuming, it seems realistic to provide at 
==PAGE:82==
72
least 10 hours of battery, using a similarly sized 
battery.  
	
As these speakers are likely to be used 
with other equipment (mixers, microphone 
transmitters, DJ turntables etc.), the complete 
audio setup is only as portable as the equipment 
used around SOWA. Therefore, SOWA could 
look into developing a batterypack solution 
(preferably in rack dimensions, like the master 
unit), that will enable other equipment to go 
wireless with the SOWA system, resulting in an 
even more portable solution.  
	
Compatibility: The new concept is 
compatible with light- and speaker stands, super 
clamps and any other product that use a spigot 
for mounting. The concept can even be tilted if 
it is placed on the floor. In terms of connectivity, 
the master unit has both XLRs, jacks, phono, 
Dante and MADI ports, providing all the port 
requests we have encountered during our field 
work. It has shape of a rack unit, which allows 
technicians to fit it into a rack - something that is 
widely done in the industry. The charging cable 
is a standard IEC 320, which is the universal 
standard within the industry, which means cables 
can be swapped between devices. 
Overall, the concept does take all the selected 
boundary objects into consideration without 
compromising the functionality in any of the use 
cases significantly. Introducing a tablet app and a 
subwoofer alongside the existing master unit and 
8” speaker, does however add to the complexity 
in the long run. SOWA can, for now, launch just 
one speaker and a master unit, as the original 
intention was, and then expand the product line 
as the company (hopefully) grows.
Future Work for SOWA
The concept presented in this thesis is meant 
as a ‘new vision’ for SOWA - a vision they can 
continue to refine and use to engage actors in the 
audio industry. The proposed concept is only one 
of multiple potential solutions that fit the cur-
rent product development specification. SOWA 
should work in small steps towards the presented 
concept, without investing more than the com-
pany is ready to loose. As of now, SOWA should 
work on making a larger amount of functional 
prototypes that can play simultaneously. The pro-
totypes can easily be made of lasetcut plywood 
and with standard wireless transmission compo-
nents, just like the current functional prototypes. 
Wood is a great material to make speakers, and 
SOWA has already shown, with their first visual 
prototype, that they can produce speakers with a 
decent finish. Along with the prototypes, SOWA 
can build a flight case to transport and charge 
the prototypes in. It could make sense to partner 
with a company that already makes flight cases, 
in order to provide flight cases to customers in 
the future.
The prototypes could be used to run multiple 
events of larger scale than previously, within the 
SOWA network, that is now expanded due to the 
performed field work. By offering the service of 
a fully functional and truly wireless set of event 
speakers to the network, SOWA may be able to 
land some pre-orders or simply raise attention for 
the upcoming product. 
==PAGE:83==
73
During the thesis, we have mainly been explor-
ing the concept of a wireless speaker, but also 
conceptualised a master unit. There are some 
very specific requirements for the master unit, 
in the product development specification, such 
as dimensions, port selection, equalizer pre-
sets, compatibility with the rack standard etc. 
It would be very interesting for SOWA to test 
different embodiments of a master unit, in order 
to develop an interface that is intuitive for the 
prosumer to use, while still being powerful for 
the professional. As mentioned earlier, this devel-
opment could be a whole UX thesis in itself, but 
could also be the key to attract both professionals 
and prosumers in the industry.
The additional products that we have suggested 
for SOWA, are merely suggestions for expanding 
the product family in the long run. They should 
focus all their energy on developing the 8” coaxi-
al speaker with a master unit first, and preferably 
doing it in small steps that don’t require large up-
front production costs. The tablet app, subwoofer 
and batterypack are all suggestions for SOWA 
to expand their product family to make a more 
complete system of wireless audio equipment for 
professionals. For now, technicians can pair the 
SOWA speakers with a mixer in order to obtain 
a higher level of controls, and the prosumers can 
(hopefully) benefit from fewer buttons on the 
physical interface.
Further Research
On the theoretical part, we have two suggestions 
for future work. The development of a toolset 
for constructing a development arena, is rooted 
in our own need for guidelines in the process of 
outlining the arena for wireless speakers. In our 
methods paragraph (page 22), we suggest one 
way of constructing a development arena, includ-
ing a ‘three dimensional’ version, which includes 
the actor-worlds that are identified to be of rele-
vance. The method worked for us, but it may not 
be the best generic toolset for developing arenas 
of development for others. 
	
The second suggestion is to further devel-
op a notion of entrepreneurship in engineering 
design methods, which is closely related to the 
Jørgensen & Valderrama 2012 article, where 
they conclusively raise the question “How can 
we continue to structure a more robust notion of 
innovation and entrepreneurship to find a place in 
the core curriculum [in Engineering and Design Ed-
ucation]?” (p. 414). They suggest that the Design 
& Innovation education is already better ground-
ed in entrepreneurship than the technology-driv-
en and business selection educations, due to it’s 
technical, socio-technical and design capabilities 
in the core of the curriculum. While we agree 
that the combination of technical, socio-technical 
and design capabilities make a great foundation 
for identifying and creating opportunities, it 
could be beneficial to incorporate an understand-
ing of how the entrepreneurial context affects 
the process of product development, as opposed 
to the context of larger coorporations. We both 
enrolled in Design & Innovation in 2012 and 
much has likely changed since then, but based on 
our experience and this thesis literature review, it 
could be of interest to study how entrepreneur-
ship could become more apparent in the engi-
neering design methodology.

7.2  Conclusion
The aim of the thesis has been to investigate the 
entrepreneurial opportunities for SOWA, and 
propose a concept of wireless speakers to fit in 
the professional audio industry, while taking into 
account the case company’s status as a start-up, as 
opposed to being an established corporation.
With an outset in current scientific literature on 
entrepreneurial opportunities and market cre-
ation, we have built a theoretical framework to 
guide identification and creation of opportunities 
for SOWA. An entrepreneurial approach to con-
ceptualisation has been suggested based mainly 
on the theories of Effectuation an Actor Network 
Theory, while allowing effectuation to shape the 
more traditional engineering design methods 
employed during the design phase. 
	
Further, a methodical approach to em-
ploying the notion of a “Development Arena” has 
been suggested, introducing a three dimensional 
approach to mapping “actor-worlds” that reside 
within the arena. This has allowed establishment 
of a set of use cases, and an understanding of 
how networks of different actants within the 
Development Arena are translated across these 
actor-worlds as “boundary objects”. 
	
A large number of relevant actors have 
been engaged in the project, through both in-
terviews and observations, and as a result, three 
main use cases have been identified and selected 
for a SOWA product to exist in. We have also 
suggested and described a “new” specific actor, 
the “prosumer”, which exists in all three of these 
use cases, and is a very relevant actor to design 
the interface for, as long as the interface is not 
compromised for audio-professionals.  
	
A speaker concept has been envisioned, 
based on a product development specification 
that contains requirements for a future wireless 
SOWA speaker. The proposed speaker concept 
and product development specification are to-
gether our recommendations for SOWA as a new 
‘vision’ to work towards in small steps using min-
imum viable products to engage relevant actors, 
while creating the market for wireless speakers in 
the professional audio industry. This proposed 
speaker concept should be constantly challenged 
and subject to change as SOWA encounter new 
opportunities along the way.
