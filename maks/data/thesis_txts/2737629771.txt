Abstract
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has been described as a global ticking time bomb and to-
day it constitutes a major global health problem.
More than 33,000 deaths could in 2015
be contributed to infections with antibiotic-resistant bacteria. One of the most predominant
pathogens responsible for these infections is extended-spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL) producing
Escherichia coli (E. coli). ESBL E. coli has multiple reservoirs, including humans, animals and
the environment, which makes mapping the transmission dynamics complicated.
The objective of this project is therefore to develop a mathematical model to assess and quantify
the transmission dynamics of ESBL E. coli in Denmark. The model consists of two coupled
models, one for transmissions within a pig herd and one for the human population. The farm
model is based on a model developed by Tuominen et al. 2022 whereas the human model consists
of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The prevalence in the pig herd is used as input in
the human model. Coupling pig farming with transmission in humans is new when compared
to previously published models for ESBL E. coli transmission.
In the baseline scenario the model predicts a prevalence between 20-50 infected pigs per 1,000
across the different life stages in the model, whereas the prevalence in the general human
population is around 40 per 1,000. For the occupationally exposed groups in the human model,
the farmers and slaughterhouse workers, the prevalence is between 75 and 100 per 1,000 for
both groups. The most influential parameters for the pig prevalence are the disease duration in
the pigs and which the target prevalences for the parameter estimation. The human prevalence
is driven by human-to-human transmission and is therefore highly effected by travellers who
return infected. The model also predicts a prevalence limit, at around 1.5 % in the pig herd, and
below this the disease always dies out after 5 years. It is also observed that with a prevalence
of approximately 5 % the disease dies out half of the time.
Mitigating spread is difficult, but by adhering to strict management strategies on the farms and
decreasing the amount of travellers who return infected the prevalence in the general human
population can be reduced with up to 32 %.

Resume
Antimikrobiel resistens (AMR) er blevet beskrevet som en global tikkende bombe og det udgør
i dag et stort globalt sundhedsproblem.
I 2015 kunne mere end 33.000 dødsfald tilskrives
infektioner med antibiotikaresistente bakterier. En af de mest udbredte antibiotikaresistente
bakterier er extended-spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL) producerende Escherichia coli (E. coli).
ESBL E. coli har flere reservoir, hvilket gør det svært at kortlægge smittekæderne.
Formålet med dette projekt er derfor at udvikle en matematisk model, der kan bruges til at
vurdere og kvantificere ESBL E. coli smittekæderne i Danmark. Modellen består af to koblede
komponenter, en som beskriver smitte i en svinebesætning og en for smitte i mennersker. Mod-
ellen for svinebesætningen er baseret på en model udviklet af Tuominen et al. 2022, mens
modellen for mennesker består af ordinære differentialligninger. Prævalensen fra svinebesæt-
ningen bliver brugt som input til menneske modellen. At lave en model som kobler smitten i
en svinebesætning med mennesker er nyt sammenlignet med tidligere publicerede modeller for
ESBL E. coli smitte.
I basis scenariet forudser modellen at der en prævalens på mellem 20-50 inficerede grise pr
1.000 på tværs af de forskellige livsstadier og en prævalens i den generelle befolkning på cirka
40 per 1.000 personer. For de grupper, i menneske modellen, som bliver udsat i forbindelse
med deres erhverv, landmændene og slagterimedarbejderne, er prævalensen på mellem 75 og
100 per 1.000 for begge grupper. Den mest dominerende parameter for prævelensen i svin er
sygdommens varighed samt hvilke prævalenser som bliver brugt som mål i parameterestimerin-
gen. Prævalensen i mennesker bliver drevet af person-til-person smitte og derfor bliver den
kraftigt påvirket af rejsende, der er inficerede ved hjemkomst. Modellen viser også at hvis præ-
valensen er under en vis grænse så dør sygdommen ud af sig selv. Ved en prævakens på 1.5 %
i svinebesætningen dør den altid ud efter fem år og med en prævalens på 5 % vil den dø ud
halvdelen af tiden.
At begrænse sygdomsspredningen er vanskeligt, men ved at opretholde en streng administration
af gårdene og ved at reducere antal rejsende, der vender tilbage inficerede, kan prævalensen i
den generelle menneskelige befolkning blive reduceret med op til 32 %.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Amid the COVID-19 pandemic in 2021 the World Health Organization (WHO) released an
overview of 10 global health issues which should be tracked in the coming years.
Battling
COVID-19 was of course included, but so was reducing antimicrobial resistance (AMR) (World
Health Organization (WHO) 2020).
WHO began its focus on AMR back in the 1970s and
already in 2013 it was described as a global ticking time bomb and equated with the threat
from climate changes (Podolsky 2018). It has been estimated that infections with antibiotic-
resistant bacteria in the EU and European Economic Area (EEA) region alone accounted for
671,689 infections and 33,100 deaths in 2015 (Cassini et al. 2019).
Due to the increasing problem surveillance of AMR has become a focus area for both EU
and Denmark. The level of AMR in livestock, food and humans along with the development
in antimicrobial use (AMU) are monitored continuously and comprehensive annual reports
are published (EFSA and ECDC 2022). The program in Denmark is called DANMAP. The
report from 2023 showed that the majority of antibiotics used is penicillins whereas last resort
antibiotics, such as e.g. carbapenems, are some of the least used. The use of this type has
remained almost stable since 2013 (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023).
The antibiotic-resistant bacteria responsible for most of the more than 600,00 infections is third-
generation cephalosporin-resistant Escherichia coli (E. coli). This bacteria was accountable for
approximately 300,000 infections in 2015. E. coli can develop this resistance by obtaining differ-
ent traits, but the most common one leads to a production of extended-spectrum β-lactamase
(ESBL) (Cassini et al. 2019). This trait can make E. coli resistant towards multiple antibiotics
including penicillin and third- and fourth-generation cephalosporins (Picozzi et al. 2014). High
prevalences of ESBL E. coli have been reported in humans, livestock, food and companion
animals. This means that human infections can have many sources, but the epidemiology and
influence of each source are not yet fully understood (Mughini-Gras et al. 2019).
In Denmark the pig population size was 12.4 million in 2022. Even though this is a smaller
population compared to previous years, it is still a substantial amount (Landbrug & Fødevarer
2023). Pigs are a proven reservoir for ESBL E. coli (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023).
The objective of this project is to develop a model which can be used to assess which transmission
dynamics are the primary drivers of spread of ESBL E. coli in Denmark.
Due to the size of the pig production industry in Denmark it is relevant to include it in the
modelling framework. The model will therefore account for zoonotic transmission due to occu-
pational exposure from pigs in addition to describe transmission within an average pig herd on
a farrow-to-finish farm. The modelling framework will also take other factors into account such
as imported disease to the general population from travellers and how management strategies
on a farm impact the human population.
Very few models have been developed for ESBL E. coli transmission in Europe and those
which have are limited to either pigs or humans. The impact of occupational exposure from
pigs on the prevalence in the general human population has not yet been investigated with a
simulation model. In the model the impact is investigated by running multiple different scenarios
and then, by comparing the outputs, conclude which pathways are dominating. Finally some
recommendations for mitigating spread will be presented.

Theoretical background
2.1
Extended spectrum β-lactamase producing Escherichia coli
Escherichia coli (E. coli) is a gram-negative bacteria which is found in the intestinal tract of
humans and animals including livestock. The majority of E. coli strains are commensals, but
pathogenic strains also exist (Ramos et al. 2020). In humans E. coli is primarily known to cause
diarrhoea, urinary tract infections (UTIs) or bacteremia. Diarrheagenic E. coli (DEC) can be
transmitted from person to person, from animals to humans or through faecal contamination
of food or water (Statens Serum Institut 2019).
Antibiotic-resistant E. coli is an increasing problem. In Europe more than half of the reported
isolates in humans in 2020 were resistant to one or more antibiotics (WHO European Centre
for Disease Prevention and Control 2022). In food-producing animals the reported levels of
AMR in indicator E. coli in Europe were very dependent on the type of resistance. Resistance
to e.g. ampicillin was reported as being high in all animal categories whereas resistance to
third-generation cephalosporins was reported as rare to low (EFSA and ECDC 2022).
Multiple different traits can confer AMR in E. coli.
One of these makes E. coli extended
spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL) producing. ESBL E.coli produces an enzyme which makes the
pathogen resistant to β-lactams such as penicillins and first-, second-, and third-generation
cephalosporins (Ramos et al. 2020).
Another very similar trait is AmpC β-lactamase production. This trait exhibits almost the same
resistance as ESBL E. coli, but the two differ genetically (Jacoby 2009). One trait does not
exclude the other which means E. coli can be both phenotypic ESBL- and AmpC producing.
Due to the similarity, the two traits are often grouped in literature and surveillance programs.
An example is DANMAP in which surveillance is conducted for both traits, but they are often
reported as being one (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023).
ESBL E. coli can in humans both manifest itself as an asymptomatic intestinal carriage or
cause infections, such as bacteremia and UTIs, just like regular E. coli. Infections with ESBL
E. coli have an increased morbidity especially when it causes sepsis (Bezabih et al. 2021). Due
to the asymptotic carriage, the prevalence of ESBL E. coli in humans is difficult to determine
without targeted studies.
ESBL E. coli can be classified into three genotypic subtypes called TEM, SHV and CTX-M with
CTX-M being the dominant subtype today (Ramos et al. 2020). A high frequency the CTX-M
genes have been observed in both the open community, in clinical patients, in farm animals,
in meat and in the environment among others (Mughini-Gras et al. 2019). This indicates that
ESBL E. coli has many different reservoirs.
In Europe the occurrence of ESBL E. coli in food-producing animals is considered high, but
during the past years a decreasing trend has been observed. For example 27 out of 30 countries,
which report data on pigs, had a decreasing trend in overall resistance from 2009 to 2019 (EFSA
and ECDC 2022).
Decrease in resistance in livestock has also been observed in Denmark.
E.g.
a decrease in
broilers was observed in 2022 and a minor decrease was observed in pigs from 2019 to 2021.
The resistance in Danish broilers went from 3 % to 1 % in samples from 2020 to 2022 (A. Ribeiro

approximately 8 % to 4 % (Helle Bisgaard Korsgaard et al. 2020; Høg et al. 2022). For humans

2.2
Development of AMR
AMR can be acquired by bacteria in different ways. The pathogen can get mutations in their
pre-existing genes or they can obtain new genes from other bacteria by a mechanism called
horizontal gene transfer (HGT). Without any external influential factors AMR would develop
naturally, but excessive use of antimicrobials (AMs) can advance the process by increasing the
selective pressure and HGT.
Antibiotics are mostly used to treat infectious diseases, but they are sometimes also administered
prophylactically in humans and animals. Furthermore, they can be used as growth promoters
in livestock, but this has been banned in EU since 2006 (Bennani et al. 2020). Multiple studies
have shown that there is a strong correlation between the use of AMs and development of
AMR in food-producing animals (Bennani et al. 2020). A study conducted in seven European
countries (including Denmark) showed high correlations between specific AMs and resistance
towards these in commensal E. coli in poultry, cattle and pigs on a national level (Chantziaras
et al. 2014).
The relation between antimicrobial use (AMU) in animals and AMR in humans are not well
established. Resistant bacteria can spread from animals to humans either directly or through
the food chain, but the extent of the problem is unknown (Bennani et al. 2020). In the Danish
population the most predominant ESBL E. coli gene is CTX-M-15. This is very different from
the genes dominating the animal strains and it is, therefore, difficult to assess the importance
of zoonotic transmission (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023).
On the other hand some studies have also shown that human carriage of ESBL E. coli can be
linked to occupational exposure. A study from the Netherlands has found that slaughterhouse
workers have a slightly higher prevalence and shared proportion of gene type with pigs when
compared to the general population (Dohmen, Van Gompel, et al. 2017). The same conclusion
is made for farmers for which an association between contact with pigs carrying ESBL E. coli
and human carriage has been found. For this group the ESBL gene types were often shared
and this suggests presence of zoonotic transmission (Dohmen, Bonten, et al. 2015).
Another risk factor for human carriage is foreign travel. High prevalences of ESBL E. coli have
been reported for Asia (20 %) and India (60 %) which is in contrast to the reported prevalence
in Northern Europe of around 5 %. One Swedish study showed that travellers returning from
Asia have a post-travel colonization rate of 32 % while it is 88 % for India (Tängdén et al.
2010). Another larger study conducted in Germany, the Netherlands and Romania showed that
individuals are four times more likely to carry ESBL E. coli after travelling to Northern Africa,
Sub-Saharan Africa and Asia compared to individuals who have not travelled (Rodríguez-Molina
et al. 2022).
2.3
Pig Production in Denmark
In Denmark most pigs are reared on a conventional farm and, despite small variations, the life
cycle is very similar across farms. On a typical farm there are five types of units in which a

pig can reside and where it resides depends on its age, size and purpose. In the breeding unit
sows and gilts are inseminated and they are afterwards moved to the gestation unit where they
stay until right before the expected farrowing (Landbrug & Fødevarer 2020). The breeding

ESBL E. coli in pigs
Every second year surveillance of ESBL E. coli in Danish pigs and pork meat is performed as part
of the DANMAP program. The level of ESBL E. coli in pigs in Denmark has decreased slightly
since 2017 whereas the level in Danish pork meat remains almost unchanged. In 2021, 21 % of
the samples from pigs were resistant to third generation cephalosporins with approximately 4 %
due to ESBL production. In contrast the percentage of samples with ESBL E. coli in imported
pork meat has increased. This indicates that even though the trend in Denmark is decreasing
the problem is still present (Høg et al. 2022).
AMU in Danish pig production
AMU on a national level in Denmark has been monitored since 1995 and throughout the years
multiple legislation and initiatives have been implemented to decrease the use. This includes
a ban on the use of AMs as growth promoters in 1995. In 2022 approximately 83 % of all
AMs prescribed to animals were consumed by pigs, corresponding to approximately 71.3 tonnes
of active compound. This is primarily due to the large production of pigs in Denmark. In
general a slight decrease in treatment proportions have been observed since 2013, but in 2022
an increased uese in weaners was observed. The main reason for prescription to weaners is
post-weaning diarrhoea and the prescription rate has especially increased after zinc oxide was
banned as a treatment option in June 2022. Despite the high consumption no last resort third-
or fourth-generation cephalosporins were prescribed (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023).
2.4
Modelling advancements
Mathematical disease transmission models have two primary purposes. The aim is either to
predict the spread of a disease or broaden the understanding of the e.g. transmission dynamics
(Keeling and Rohani 2008). To be able to accurately predict spread an understanding of the
transmission dynamics are required. For ESBL E. coli the knowledge about the dynamics is
still limited even though some modelling advancements have been made within the field.

The models developed have been used to quantify and investigate risk factors for ESBL E. coli
and spread of other antimicrobial-resistant pathogens in livestock herds. Mughini-Gras et al.
2019 measured in their study the frequency of different ESBL genes in humans, animals (farm
and companion), the environment and different food.
By using a source attribution model
they quantified the attributable sources of human intestinal carriage of ESBL or AmpC E. coli.
They also developed a simple transmission model (based on ODEs) which describes ESBL E.
coli transmission within human households. The model also takes into account external sources
(non-human) by implementing a constant external acquisition rate. The study concludes that
human to human transmission within the open community is the most attributable source. On
the other hand they also highlight, by using their transmission model, that colonisation with
ESBL E.coli in humans is not self-sustaining and therefore external reservoirs remain interesting
(Mughini-Gras et al. 2019).
In another study Tuominen et al. 2022 developed an extensive model to describe the transmission
of livestock-associated methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (LA-MRSA) on a farrow-to-
finish farm. LA-MRSA is an antibiotic resistant bacteria which, like ESBL E. coli, can transmit
between humans and animals and thereby pose a threat to human health.
The model is a
stochastic compartment model which also incorporates an environmental factor. The pig herd
is divided into the relevant groups (sows, growers etc.) to represent the production cycle and
within each group two compartments, susceptible, S, and infected, I, is modelled. The rate at
which pigs move from the S to the I compartment is influenced by both direct transmission from
other animals and indirect transmission from the environment. A group of pigs is also assigned
different locations which represent pens. The model is implemented using the SimInf package
in R. This package can be utilized to implement discrete event-based disease models where the
transitions between the compartments, in the model, are simulated as a discrete time Markov
chain. This means that the current event does not depend on past ones. The Markov chain is
implemented using Gillespie’s algorithm. The study, by Tuominen et al. 2022 uses their model
to investigate the impact of different management strategies on the LA-MRSA prevalence in
a pig herd and concludes that especially mixing of pigs in the finisher unit has a large impact
(Tuominen et al. 2022).

Modelling ESBL E. coli transmission
In the following sections the framework for the model development is presented. Initially the
overall model structure is introduced followed by some more detailed descriptions of the model
components. Afterwards the methodology behind the parameter estimations, model validations
and sensitivity analysis is described. Lastly the scenarios, which are used to analyse the trans-
mission dynamics, are established. The models are developed using R (version 4.3.2) and all
scripts, results etc. can be found in the attached zip folder Model.zip.
3.1
Overall model structure
Two distinct, but coupled, models are developed to describe and assess the transmission dynam-
ics of ESBL E. coli in the human population and a pig herd. The transmission in the pig herd
is described using an event-driven model whereas the transmission between humans is described
using ODEs. An overview of the model can be seen in figure 1.
Figure 1: Flowchart showing the two models including the groups
within each model and how they are coupled.
As seen in the figure, it is assumed that pigs can infect occupationally exposed humans whereas
humans cannot infect pigs. This unidirectional transmission means that the two models can be
run sequentially. One realisation of the farm model is used to simulate the spread in the pig
herd and the prevalence of infected pigs in the different groups is calculated. The prevalence is
then used as input to the human model.
The coupled models are used to run multiple scenarios to asses the influence of different trans-
mission dynamics. The scenarios will differ by changing certain parameters in the model system.
3.2
Event-driven farm model
The spread of ESBL E. coli in a pig herd is implemented using the modelling framework
presented in the study by Tuominen et al. 2022. This means that a lot of the model structure
and assumptions are taken from or based on this study. Some are modified to represent Danish
norms, instead of Swedish, if information is available.
The model components presented in
the following sections should therefore be considered referenced to this study unless otherwise
specified.

The model can be perceived as consisting of two concurrent dynamics; The population dynamic
which includes farrowing, slaughter, movement of pigs between different housing unit etc. and
the disease dynamic which describes the transmission of ESBL E. coli between the pigs. A factor
which describes which describes transmission of disease due to environmental contamination is
also incorporated.
The two dynamics will be presented in the following sections and going
forward this model component will often be referred to as the farm model.
3.2.1
Model implementation
To implement the farm model the SimInf package in R (version 4.3.2) is used. This package is
efficient when making stochastic event-driven models. In the framework a network of nodes is
used to describe subpopulations, such as e.g. pens. Nodes can be linked to simulate some kind
of flow between them e.g. transmission of disease. Each node has a state and a local dynamic
whereas the interactions between the nodes are referred to as the global dynamic. Within a
node, in the farm model, the disease transmission is described with a compartment model.
Scheduled events are used at each time step to change the state of the individuals within a
node. There are four different types of events; enter, internal transfer, external transfer and
exit. Enter and exit events are e.g. used to describe births and death. Internal transfer is
movement of individuals within a node whereas external transfer is movement from one node to
another. Multiple events can be scheduled to happen at the same time (Widgren et al. 2016).
3.2.2
Population dynamic
As part of modelling the transmission of ESBL E.coli on a pig farm it is crucial to implement a
population dynamic. As previously described conventional Danish farrow-to-finish pig farms are
often organized very similar. This is utilized in the modelling framework to describe the spatial
structure of the farm and the life cycle of the pigs. The population dynamic is implemented
such that the population has a steady-state in which the population size fluctuates around a
constant value. All elements, described below which include entering, exit or movements, are
implemented as events in the SimInf framework. The complete list of numbered events can be
found in appendix A.
In the following sections the model components, rates and assumptions related to the events
will be presented. All these remain constant across the scenarios which are presented later.

consists of 6 different units; Breeding, gestation, farrowing, growing, finishing and gilt growing.
Furthermore, the pig herd will also exhibit dynamics such as natural death, births etc. An

Gilts, Growers and finishers
The piglets, which become gilts are moved to the gilt grower unit. They remain in this unit for
a maximum of 192 days. The number of gilts moved from growing to breeding depends on the
number of empty farrowing pens, the number of sows returning from farrowing and the number
of pigs in the sow and gilt breeding buffer. Animals from the breeding buffer are moved to
the breeding unit before the growing gilts. The gilts which have reached the maximum time
without being moved are slaughtered.
The piglets, which are not selected to be gilts, are moved to the growers unit. Besides natural
death and euthanasia no other events happen in this life stage. After 52 days in the growers
unit the majority of the growers will become finishers, but 10 % will move to the growing buffer
unit instead. This is implemented to represent the variety in growth rate which can be observed
among pigs. The pigs in the buffer unit will spend an additional 23 days in this unit before
moving to the finisher unit.
Finishers are slaughtered when they are 5-6 months old which corresponds to day 70-98 in the
finisher unit. It is assumed that one third of all finishers are slaughtered on day 70, half on day
84 and the rest on day 98. This also means there is a possibility that some finisher pens are
partially emptied. Meaning that some finishers leave at day 70 whereas others in the pens will
have to wait until day 98. At day 98 the pens will be emptied and the downtime period will be
set for the given pens. The model also has a parameter which can simulate mixing of finishers
between the pens.

3.3.2
Approximate Bayesian computation
The transmission rates are estimated using approximate Bayesian computation (ABC) which is
another feature of the SimInf package. ABC circumvents calculation of the likelihood which is
otherwise often used in estimations like this. Instead it uses simulations to compute posterior
distributions of parameters by combining tolerances and minimisation of a distance function
(Martin, Kumar, and Lao 2021). The rates in the model become the median of these posterior
distributions. As input ABC requires a prior distribution and for the estimation of the trans-
mission rates these are uniformly distributed. The uniform distribution used for the estimation
of the transmission rates is defined as U(0.0001, 0.3). The same prior distribution is used for
all the transmission rates. As part of the result analysis it is made sure that the posterior
distributions differ from the prior distributions, since it is an indicator of convergence.
Furthermore, to be able to estimate the transmission rates one random realisation is made by
running the model for 1825 days (5 years). Only the time span from from day 1200 to 1825
is used in the estimation to ensure that the transient does not affect the results.
Multiple

simulations, called generations, are made for different set of parameters which are drawn from
the prior distribution. For each set the simulated data are compared to the target value at
pre-specified time points using a distance function. The time points are based on model events
such that the life stages are sampled at
• Gilts and sows when they enter the farrowing unit
• Piglets 12 days after farrowing
• Growers 21 days after being weaned (moved to the growers unit)
• Finishers 65 days after moving to the finishing unit
The time point for the sows and gilts is selected since this is the easiest time to sample the groups
collectively. The three other time points are selected to be approximately halfway through the
total time a pig spends in the life stage.
For all time points the distance function calculates the sum of squared error between the target
value and the simulated ones.
In total 505 comparison points are used.

that this set of parameters gives prevalences within 5 % of the target values (Tuominen et al.

3.4
Validation of the farm model
The farm model is validated by comparing the model output of the two dynamics, population
and disease, with known data. The population dynamic is validated first to make sure it gives
an appropriate output. This is necessary since the prevalences, which are used to validate the
disease dynamic, are dependent on the population size etc.
3.4.1
Population dynamic
29.9 % of pig productions, in Denmark, have a pig population of 3000 - 7499 pigs. Furthermore,
46.1 % of farms have between 200 and 749 sows (and gilts) (Landbrug & Fødevarer 2023).
Danmarks Statistik also shows that the majority of farms have 1000-2999 finishers and 1000
- 4999 growers (Danmarks Statistik 2022). The criteria used to extract these numbers can be
found in appendix C.
For the validation 15 random realisations of the farm model is made by running the model
for 2500 days.
The population is initiated by adding 21 susceptibles gilts and one infected
gilt to the population every week for the first 21 weeks (147 days). The realisations are made
using the medium scenario fitted transmission rates since these do not influence the population
dynamic. To investigate the realisations the daily mean of the total herd population is visualised.
Afterwards the mean along with the 5 % and 95 % quantiles for different life stages are calculated
for each week and compared to the known statistics.

3.4.2
Disease dynamic
The same 15 random realisations as above are used to validate the disease dynamic. The output

3.5
Human transmission model
ESBL E. coli transmission from pigs to humans and transmission within the human community
are modelled using an SIS model which consists of two compartments susceptible (S) and
infected (I ) within each group. The transitioning between the compartments is governed by
ODEs. The I compartment contains individuals who are carriers as well as those who have a
clinical infection.
The population is divided into three groups; Farmers, slaughterhouse workers and the general
population. It is assumed that farmers and slaughterhouse workers have occupational exposure
to ESBL E. coli whereas the general population is infected through contact with other humans.
Travelling to non-Western territories, Asia and Africa, is also implemented in the model since
this is a proven risk factor for intestinal carriage of ESBL E. coli (Rodríguez-Molina et al. 2022).
Since the simulation time is multiple years, births and natural deaths are also implemented. It
is assumed that these events only influence the general population which also means that the
farmer and slaughterhouse worker populations are assumed constant.

studies since other data is not available. The death and birth rates are calculated using numbers
from Danmarks Statistik. In 2023 a total of 57,469 individuals were born and the average life
expectancy for male and female were 79.6 and 83.4 years respectively (Danmarks Statistik 2023b;
Danmarks Statistik 2023c). Assuming that the births are equally distributed throughout the
year and the male and female life expectancy weight equally the rates for births and mortality
are ν = 157.45 individuals per day and µ = 3.36 · 10−5 per day.
Since the birth and death rates are non-equal the total population size N needs to be calculated
at each time step. N is the total sum across the compartments and groups, even though the
farmer and slaughterhouse worker populations are constant in time.
3.5.1
The recovery rate γ
The duration of intestinal colonisation of ESBL E. coli has mostly been studied in individuals
returning from travelling or in nursing homes.
One study made in a Dutch nursing home
showed that the median duration of carriage is 13 and 11.2 months for two subtypes of ESBL
E. coli respectively (Weterings et al. 2022). Another study has shown that carriage duration
in travellers decreased over a 12 month period and that 32 % were positive after 3 months, 25

% after 6 months and 14 % and 11 % after 9 and 12 months respectively (Östholm Balkhed
et al. 2018). The decrease in carriers can therefore be described using an exponential decay
function. Since the diversity in this study is larger than the one from the nursing home the
recovery rate for the model is fitted using only this data. Doing so yields a recovery rate of
0.007 per day which corresponds to an average recovery time of approximately 143 days, which
is approximately 4.5 months.
3.5.2
The infected traveller rate λ(t)
Travelling is implemented in the model as a number of individuals per day who are moved from
the S to the I compartment in the general population. To estimate this, data from 2019 is
used instead of the newest from 2022 since some COVID-19 restriction were still active then.
In 2019, 5 % and 1 % of all travellers went to Asia and Africa respectively (Danmarks Statistik
2019b). Since these destinations are almost exclusively reached by plane the total number of
travels with this type of transport is used. A total of 4,217,596 trips with plane from Denmark,
which lasted more than four night, were made (Danmarks Statistik 2019a). This means that
253,055 Danes travelled to Asia and Africa in a year which yields an average of 693 travellers
per day. Filtering details used to retrieve data can be found in appendix C.
The seasonal variation is implemented using a step-wise function for which the travelling activity
is tripled during the summer break. The summer break is assumed to be between day 183 and
225 of the year which correspond to beginning of July until the middle of August. To adjust
for this increase the daily numbers are decreased during the rest of the year. This results in
2080 and 513 travellers per day to Asia or Africa during the summer break and all other times
a year respectively.
Only a proportion of the travellers returning are carriers of ESBL E. coli.
A large study
conducted in Germany and the Netherlands showed that somewhere between 16 % to 25 % of
travellers returning were infected. The proportions for Africa and Asia were similar (Rodríguez-
Molina et al. 2022). Another study conducted in Sweden in 2010 showed a post-travel infected
proportion in travellers from Asia of 32 % when India was excluded (the proportion for India was
88 %) (Tängdén et al. 2010). Lastly a third study from Norway showed that 18.2 % and 29.4 % of
travellers returning from Africa and Asia respectively carried extended spectrum cephalosporin
resistant E. coli which can be caused by ESBL production (Espenhain et al. 2018).
Using
these numbers a normal distribution is fitted and for each day a random percentage of infected
travellers is drawn. This assumes that the risk of getting infected in Asia and Africa is equal.
The percentage is used in combination with the daily number of travellers to calculate how
many individuals are moved from the S to the I compartment. λ(t) for one year is visualised

as the difference between the number of infected individuals given the target prevalence and the
weekly average from the model in the three subpopulations. The errors are, furthermore, only
based on data from year 2 and onward in the simulation to avoid influence from the transient.
Normalization is obtained by dividing the error with the subpopulation size.

Subpopulation prevalences
To make the optimisation feasible data regarding the prevalence of intestinal carriage in the

A large study in Sweden tested for ESBL E. coli in 2,134 faecal samples taken from randomly

of ESBL E. coli (Raffelsberger et al. 2023). No larger scale study similar to the ones above has
been conducted in Denmark which makes these two the most representative. An average of the
prevalences, reported in the two studies, is therefore used as target for the general population.
For the slaughterhouse workers the prevalence has shown to be dependent on what tasks the
worker has. This has been established by Dohmen, Van Gompel, et al. 2017 who conducted
a study at a Dutch slaughterhouse. They collected faecal samples from 334 employees and in
their analysis they stratified workers into high and low exposure groups. They showed that the
prevalence in the high exposure group was 10.5 % and 2.6 % in the low exposure group. The
overall prevalence in their study was 4.8 %, but this is could be influenced by two-thirds of the
study population being from the low exposure group. Since data regarding the total number
of workers in the high and low exposure group in Denmark is not available it is assumed that
the two groups are of equal size. This means a simple average of the prevalences in the two
groups is used as target value, even though it is higher than the overall prevalence reported in
Dohmen, Van Gompel, et al. 2017. The target value can be seen in table 7 below.

3.8
Sensitivity analysis
As part of the model evaluation and before running scenarios it is relevant to make a sensitivity
analysis. The purpose of a sensitivity analysis is to test how changing the input parameters
affects the model output. The input parameters also include the initial conditions.
The sensitivity analysis is performed by looking into possible linear correlations between input
parameters and output, which is visualised using scatter plots, and by calculating the Partial
Rank Correlation Coefficient (PRCC). The scatter plot matrix used to evaluate linear correla-
tions is made using the R-function pairs. The PRCC can be used to assess correlation between
a single parameter and the output and is calculated using the R-function epi.prcc. This func-
tion returns the coefficient along with the test statistics and a p-value. Only parameters with
a coefficient which is significant on a 5 % significance level are reported.
Both functions used in the analysis require a single output value from the model. Since the
model is made up of two distinct coupled models the sensitivity analysis is conducted separately
for the farm and the human model.
For the farm model 200 realisations are made using the same seed and for each realisation the
selected input parameters are varied. The parameters included are the four transmission rates
(βpiglets, βmature, βgrowing and βfinish), the disease duration in the pigs D and the decay rate
µ(t) of ESBL E. coli in the environment.
These are for each realisations sampled randomly from a uniform distribution which is defined
as ±20% of the parameter value used in the model validation. The transmission rates used are
the ones from the medium scenario. The model output is for this part of the analysis defined
as the total number of infected pigs across the life stages after 5 years (1825 days).
The human model is evaluated similarly but a few things differ. Most of the model parameters
are drawn from a uniform distribution defined as in the farm model. These are the transmission
rates βph, βc and βh, the recovery rate γ and the mean of the distribution from which the
travellers rate λ(t) is drawn. The increased farmers contact factor α is drawn from a uniform
distribution with limits of 1 and 1.5 since this parameter is defined as being larger than 1.
The initial proportion of susceptible in the general population PSGP (0) is drawn from a dis-
tribution with 1 as upper limit and lastly the initial proportion of infected in the farmers and
slaughterhouse workers is drawn from a uniform distribution with limits 0 and 0.05.
The pig prevalence used as input in the human model is kept constant throughout the runs
used for the analysis. In contrast to the analysis for the farm model the correlations of the
parameters in the human model are evaluated separately for each population group. This also
means that the output of the human model is defined as the prevalence per 1,000 individuals
in the given population group at year 5. In total 300 runs of the human model with different
parameter values are made.

3.9
Model scenarios
To be able to assess the dynamics of ESBL E. coli transmission multiple different scenarios
are made and analysed. Some of the scenarios are related to uncertainty in model components
whereas others represent strategic variations such as changing the management strategy of the
farm or implementing interventions in the human population.
Across the scenarios some parameters and initial conditions are kept constant whereas others
are varied to investigate a dynamic. Furthermore, all simulations are made using the same seeds
to ensure that the effects observed is not due to stochasticity in the system. Unless otherwise
specified the parameters and initial conditions used are the ones described previously and the
ones used in the baseline scenario, which is described in the following section. This e.g. means
that all scenarios are run for a 5 year period (1825 days). In the analysis the results for the pig
herd and human population are presented as infected pr. 1,000 individuals. For some scenarios
the impact on the pig herd and the human populations are presented. For other scenarios only
the impact on the human population is analysed.
3.9.1
Baseline scenario
The first scenario, which is referred to as the baseline, is made using the parameters from the
medium scenario of the farm model (table 9). In total 100 realisations of the farm model is
made and used as input to the human model.
The baseline is assumed to be the scenario which represents the current situation in Denmark
best, given the model assumptions The prevalences from this scenario are therefore used as
comparison points for all other scenarios.
3.9.2
Uncertainty scenarios
The next group of scenarios are related to the uncertainty of specific model components. The
data used for some of the model parameters and the parameter estimation in the farm model
is, as previously mentioned, very sparse. It is therefore relevant to run alternative scenarios to
investigate the influence of this uncertainty. The scenarios described below highlight different
aspects of this uncertainty. To quantify the impact on the human population the prevalences
at the beginning and the end of steady-state (day 731 and 1825) are found. These are then
compared to the corresponding values in the baseline scenario. The percentage difference is also
calculated.
High and low prevalence in the farm model
As described in section 3.3.1 three sets of target values for the parameter estimation in the farm
model are used since data is sparse. Different model transmission rates are obtained for each set
of target values. The medium values are used in the baseline scenario, whereas the other two
are used to run scenarios. These scenarios describe the situations where the prevalence is high
or low as a default premise for the disease dynamic. The scenarios are simulated by making
100 and 50 realisations of the farm model for the high and low values respectively and for each
realisation the human model is run subsequently.

3.9.4
Management and interventions
The next group of scenarios will evaluate the impact of different management strategies or
specific interventions.
The first scenario is made to highlight how the system is affected when the management strategy
on a farm does not work perfectly. In the baseline scenario it is assumed that finishers (and all
other life stage groups) follow an all-in-all-out system. This means that all pigs within a pen
are moved through the system together and that they are not mingled with other pens along
the way after becoming growers. This is a known management strategy within pig production.
In the alternative scenario it is implemented that finishers have a 10 % probability of being
mingled with other finishers instead of 0 % which is used in the baseline.
The mingling is
introduced in the finisher life stage since this is the stage in which the pigs spent the most time
and therefore the probability of intermixing is the largest.
It is also interesting to investigate the impact of interventions in the human population. Since
the model focuses on ESBL E. coli carriage it is most relevant to look at strategies which can
prevent this and not strategies for disease treatment etc. In the baseline the mean percentage
of travellers infected upon return is 22.7 %.
In this scenario the impact of decreasing this
percentage is highlighted. It is investigated what happens if it is be decreased by 5%, 20 %
or 50 % to 21.5 %, 18.2 % and 11.4 % respectively by increasing awareness about hygiene
importance and disease sources pre-travel to high risk areas. The standard deviation and the
distribution of travellers during summer is assumed to be the same as in the baseline. The three
scenarios are referred to as Travel05, Travel20 and Travel50 respectively.

5
Discussion
In the following sections different aspects of developing and working with the model are dis-
cussed. The section is concluded by looking at possible expansions of the model and other
future perspectives.
5.1
Definition of ESBL
One of the initial challenges when collecting data for ESBL E. coli is the varying definition
and grouping which is used in literature and surveillance programs. Multiple systems for clas-
sification of ESBL production exist and there is no consensus about when the different ones
are used (Husna et al. 2023). Furthermore, it often happens that some of the subgroups in
the classification systems are grouped when doing studies or surveillance. One example is the
grouping of ESBL and AmpC in the DANMAP surveillance program. In other cases the studies
are conducted only for a specific subtype such as TEM, SHV or CTX-M.
Another challenge is the difference between phenotypic and genotypic testing. Although the
phenotypic testing is standardised across Europe, the method used to determine the underlying
genes is not. Furthermore, all mechanisms leading to a phenotypic ESBL resistance is not yet
fully understood and therefore it could be that a sample can be genotypic negative and still
exhibit ESBL resistance phenotypically (Perestrelo et al. 2023). Lastly many studies are not
specific for E. coli, but instead looks at all Enterobacteriaceae which often include Klebsiella
pneumoniae in addition to E. coli. All these different setups have made it difficult to collect data
and it cannot be excluded that some data outside the scope has been included unknowingly.
Another challenge when collecting data, especially for humans, is the diverse ways of reporting
antibiotic resistance. Understandably antibiotic resistance is often reported as which antibiotics
the pathogens do not respond to. Since ESBL is a mechanism by which the pathogen becomes
resistant towards multiple antibiotics it can be difficult to deduct if this is due to ESBL pro-
duction or not. If human data was also tested for ESBL production in more studies it would
likely have increased the amount of data available to use in this project.
5.2
Data
During the model development data was needed to estimate the parameters. In the current
setup the parameters in the farm and human model are estimated using prevalences reported
in different studies and surveillance programs across Europe. Even though the European coun-
tries used are comparable to Denmark some national variations do exist. In relation to the
target values for the farm model Denmark has for example implemented a voluntary ban on
cephalosporins in pigs in 2010 (Agersø and Aarestrup 2013). In the Netherlands national ac-
tions have also been taken but a complete ban has not been implemented (Alliance to Save
Out Antibiotics 2016). Since a strong association is proven between AMU and AMR (Bennani
et al. 2020) it is likely that governmental recommendations like these have a large influence on
the prevalence of ESBL E. coli. This also means that the studies conducted in other countries
might not be as representative for Denmark as assumed.
For the human model a large uncertainty is related to the prevalence in the farmers and slaugh-
terhouse workers. In general very few studies have been conducted investigating this and none
of the used studies are from Denmark. A crude simplification is also made regarding the target
59
==PAGE:65==

prevalence for the slaughterhouse workers. In the estimation it is assumed that the exposure
risk for the slaughterhouse workers are equally distributed, but this is likely not realistic. In a
work setting more attention to protective measures is probably paid to the high risk situations
and this might make a difference.
Taken all of the above into account there is also a possible advantage of using this approach. In
biological systems prevalences are not constant and the uncertainty related to the used targets
could coincidentally capture some of this natural variation. Overall it would have been beneficial
to have larger data set for the prevalence in pigs and humans in Denmark.
5.3
The model structure
In the process of developing the model some assumptions and choices have been made. These
are necessary to obtain the simplicity needed for the model to work, but inevitably it also
means that the model differs from real life. For the model implemented in this project it is both
relevant to look at the model as a whole and to dive into the two model components separately.
When looking at the entire model structure one of the most crude simplifications is the assump-
tions that humans cannot transmit ESBL E. coli to the pigs. Even though the impact of this
is unknown it has been shown for other pathogens that humans can be the source of infection
(Epstein and Price 2009). Humans does not necessarily transmit the pathogen directly, but they
can facilitate transfer of the pathogen from one farm environment to another if the bio-security
measures taken are insufficient (Graham et al. 2008). Therefore it might have been beneficial
to include this in the model as well. The advantage of excluding this transmission pathway is
that the farm and human model can be run sequentially.
Another choice which is relevant to discuss is the fact that the model output is intestinal carriage
instead of cases of clinical infection. Since ESBL E. coli can be carried in the intestine without
causing clinical infection only modelling infections would likely underestimate the prevalence
of the bacteria. In addition the model investigates the impact of transmission in a pig herd
on slaughterhouse workers and the general population. These groups would, neither directly
or indirectly, be affected by pigs with clinical infection since these would have been removed
from the system. For the purpose of the model carriage is therefore more favourable. This does
not mean that the clinical infections are not important, especially when taking the increased
mortality in humans into account (Melzer and Petersen 2007), but developing a model for these
would probably require a different setup. Lastly, carriage within pig herds is more frequently
investigated in studies than clinical infection. This means that if infection is to be modelled the
already limited amount of data would likely have been even more sparse.
In the evaluation of the model results it is assumed that a high carriage prevalence is related
to a high risk of extra-intestinal infections. This relation might not be as straight-forward as
assumed.
A study conducted in Sweden showed that community carriage and bloodstream
infections are dominated by different strains.
Therefore it might be more important which
strains dominate in a community when actions are taken to minimise the risk of infections
with ESBL E. coli (Ny et al. 2016). On the other hand it can be argued that high carriage
increases the risk of HGT to non-resistant E. coli as well as other more pathogenic bacteria,
e.g. Salmonella (Oladeinde et al. 2019). High carriage is therefore, no matter the pathogenicity
of the specific strain a large problem.

The farm model
For the farm model there are some things which are also relevant to discuss. First it could be
argued that population dynamics in the farm model only represents a fraction of pig production
facilities. In Denmark, and in the European Union in general, pig production can be facilitated
and managed in multiple different ways. The model represents a conventional farrow-to-finish
farm, but in Denmark approximately half of farms only have finishers. Furthermore, many
farmers in Denmark only produce growers which are then exported. E.g. in 2022 13.8 million
growers were exported and this is the majority of all pigs raised (Landbrug & Fødevarer 2023).
In addition to this a small percentage of farms are organic (Landbrug & Fødevarer n.d.[b]). All
of these factors would likely have some influence on the transmission dynamics due to elements
such as transportation and less strict containment of the pigs. This does not make the model
less relevant, but it is important to consider that it represents a fraction of the entire production
system.
Furthermore, in the farm model it is assumed that no transmission occurs between the pens
within the sections. This is not likely, since pens are often only separated by fence and not
walls. This means that contaminated soil, faeces or other organic material can be transferred
between two adjoining pens and thereby spread the disease.
Even though there are elements in the farm model which make generalisation difficult there are
also a number of benefits. These are primarily related to the modelling framework on which
the farm model is built. In the case of the pig farm it would have been inaccurate to model the
entire herd as being homogeneous since the production cycle involves multiple steps even though
it would have been more simple. On the other hand there is also no need to keep track of every
single individual pig. Farmers are more likely to be interested in if a cohort of pigs is infected
rather than if a single pig is and modelling individuals would have increased the complexity
significantly. Therefore it is beneficial to make the farm model an event-driven model since
it offers a good trade-off between stochasticity, the ability to model individual groups and a
spatial dimension without having to keep account of every single individual.
The human model
In the human model, some choices and assumptions have also been made. The human model
is based on ODEs which entails both advantages and disadvantages. Overall this model type is
simple and works really well when modelling large populations. The model structure also has
a built-in assumption that the population modelled is homogeneous in relation to intermixing
and characteristics, e.g. age, race, gender etc. It can always be argued that no population
is homogeneous especially in relation to characteristics. On the other hand these assumptions
function well for the general population due to its size and the purpose of the model. For the two
other populations, the farmers and slaughterhouse workers, the number of individuals are more
limited. Therefore, an improvement to the human model would have been an implementation
which would take the heterogeneity within these groups into account. An example of common
heterogeneity is the difference in contacts when a population is divided into age groups. Children
have more direct contacts when compared to adults and this would highly influence transmission
(Mossong et al. 2008). In a modelling perspective this diversity is often implemented as a contact
matrix.

When looking at the individual parts of the human model some other simplifications have
been made. In the ODEs for the slaughterhouse workers disease transmission is affected by
the number of infected finishers in the pig herd per time step. In reality it is not all of the
finishers which come into contact with the slaughterhouse workers per time step, but only the
proportion which is sent to slaughter. The downside of this implementation is that the workers
in the model have contact with more infected pigs than in real life and thereby have a higher risk
of getting infected. On the other hand it can be argued that the used number could represent
that slaughterhouse workers have contact with both the live pigs and then the carcasses. This
means that they have repeated contact with the potential transmission source.
One of the factors which influence the prevalence in the general population is the travellers
rate. The rate is implemented as a normal distribution with seasonal variation. The seasonal
variation is based on an assumption about vacation patterns, but there is no actual data behind
the decision. Furthermore, the implementation also assumes that only travellers returning from
Asia or Africa can be infected with ESBL E. coli. This is a simplification since other areas with
high prevalence also exist. Graham et al. 2008 e.g. showed that travellers returning from the
Middle East also has an increased risk of carrying ESBL E. coli. Furthermore, the mean and
standard deviation of the used normal distribution is based on studies and among these the
percentage of individuals which returned infected varied a lot.
5.4
Model initiation
One of the decisions made during the process of developing the model is how to initiate it.
The population dynamics of the farm model is initiated by introducing susceptible gilts to the
pig herd. This life stage group is used, instead of any other, to ensure that the population is
established before pigs are slaughtered. It might be that a steady-state population could have
been obtained faster if the population had been initiated with individuals in other groups as
well. In relation to the disease initiation in the pig herd the gilts are also used. In this case
other life stage groups could also have been used, but by using gilts, the disease is introduced
early in the life cycle. Furthermore, it is likely that it is this life stage which is purchased from
outside the farm when a breeding program is initiated. When purchasing pigs from outside the
farm there is a risk of introducing disease. The impact of using other groups have not been
investigated, but due to the structure of the model the disease would have died out more quickly
if it had only been introduced in the growers or finishers.
In the human model it is assumed that no initial infection is found in the occupational groups.
This is not realistic since some individuals would probably be infected if tests were made at
any given point in time. In the modelling framework this assumption is necessary to reduce the
amount of parameters to estimate, since there is not enough data available about the prevalence
in these groups. The assumption is presumed not to have a large impact on the results since
the pig herd also has a large initiation period.

5.5
Parameter estimations
Some of the fundamental results are the parameter estimates. In general, the estimations for
the farm and human model have the same problem. They are all estimated based on a few
fixed target values which have been found in literature. This means that the fluctuations and
seasonality observed in the model dynamics are not captured when estimating the parameters.
Furthermore, the parameters are optimized against a mean value instead of the development
over time.
It would have been more optimal to have some data which could describe the
temporal development.
When looking into the actual results of the parameters estimation there are also some noticeable
things. For the transmission parameters in the farm model it was expected that the transmission
rates would increase with the prevalence. This is only the case for the mature group whereas
this biological effect is not captured in the other transmission rates. One possible explanation
occurs when looking at the posterior distributions of the transmission rates. Compared to the
mature one the posterior for them are different, but they are not nicely closed. This means the
estimates of the parameters not very certain. This can also be seen on the quantile intervals
which are almost identical across the prevalence scenarios. Furthermore, it can also be argued
that the pigs spend too little time in the life stage groups, to which the rates are related, to
capture the biological effect. Overall this means that there is no fundamental problem with the
model even though the transmission rates deviate from the expected biological pattern.
The parameters in the human model are also relevant to look into. The transmission rates
for the occupational groups are identical and the transmission rate from human to human
does not differ much either. This suggests that the model could be simplified by combining
some population groups into one e.g.
combine the two occupational groups or combine all
groups into one population. This is in line with Dohmen, Van Gompel, et al. 2017 and Dahms
et al. 2015. The first study shows that there is no difference between the prevalence in Dutch
slaughterhouse workers and the general population. The second one could not find any evidence
of direct transmission from livestock to farmers.
Furthermore, the assumption that farmers have increased contact with certain life stage groups
seem insignificant since α is only just above 1. This could therefore likely be removed from the
current modelling framework without having a large impact. On the other hand the reasoning
behind implementing the parameter is still valid.
5.6
Sensitivity analysis
The results of the sensitivity analysis show that across all model components some of the most
influential parameters are the recovery rates. From a biological perspective this is expected since
the longer individuals are infected the longer they are able to transmit the disease to others. On
the other hand from a modelling perspective it is unfortunate that these rates are as dominant
in the systems as they are. Especially taken into consideration that the biological basis for the
values of these parameters is very scarce. Very little data related to spontaneous clearance of
carriage of ESBL E. coli in pigs and humans is available and large uncertainties are therefore
related to the rates. A solution to this problem could have been to differentiate transmission
over time, meaning that the amount of pathogen an individual excrete would decrease with time
and therefore so would the infectiousness.

5.7
The baseline scenario
Another relevant point of discussion is the definition of the baseline scenario. Throughout the
project the baseline has been defined as the medium prevalence scenario in the farm model and
the subsequent realisations of the human model, but this might not be the most optimal.
The problem with the selected baseline could be that the disease often dies out. This leads to
a risk that the mean prevalence, which is used to evaluate the results, is too heavily influenced
by the zero realisations. This might make it difficult to compare the baseline to scenarios, in
which the disease never dies out, without the impact appearing as significant.
On the other hand it could be argued that the diversity in the results of the baseline is positive
since it then represents the biological variation which is also observed in real life. Furthermore,
it thereby also captures, when compared to other scenarios, the large increase in prevalence that
can happen if disease becomes established on a farm which was previously disease free.
5.8
Model results
In the following sections the results of the different scenarios made for the farm and human
models are discussed. The discussion will focus on which dynamics have the largest influence
on the respective populations and if the found relations are realistic.
5.8.1
The farm model
For the farm model three sets of scenarios have been made. The first set investigated the impact
of the target values for the parameter estimation of the transmission rates, the second varied
the disease duration and decay rate and finally the impact of mingling finishers was assessed.
Baseline, low and high scenario
For this set of scenarios it was easily observed that the farm model has a prevalence limit. Below
this limit the disease cannot sustain itself and it dies out. This means that if the prevalence is
approximately 1.5 % in all life stage groups, expect sows and gilts, the disease dies out according
to the model. For the disease to survive half of the time this limit is approximately 6 %. This is
interesting since the model indicates that total eradication of the ESBL E. coli is not required.
Measures to bring down the prevalence might be enough to become disease free or at least
decrease the risk of having an endemic status of ESBL E. coli. For this observation it should be
noted that it applies to a closed system, such as the model, in which there is no "new" inflow
of disease and the transmission is assumed unidirectional (only from pigs to huamns).
The limit might even be higher than 6 %. In the baseline scenario the prevalence has a decreasing
trend even when the zero realisations are removed. This is likely very influenced by the definition
of a zero realisation. These realisations have been defined as no infected individuals at year 5 in
any of the life stage groups. This definition might not be optimal, since if the disease at a point
in time only exists in the growers and/or finishers, then it is only a matter of time before it dies
out. The definition could therefore have been no disease in the sows, gilts and piglets. Changing
the definition would have increased the number of zero realisations and thereby increased the
prevalence limit.
64
==PAGE:70==

DISCUSSION
On the other hand, the other scenario in the set shows that when the prevalence is increased to
an average of approximately 15 % across the groups the disease is always self-sustaining. The
turnover from disease-free to endemic is therefore, according to the model, quite sensitive and
a farm could probably quickly go from one status to another.
Lastly it can also be discussed if it realistic that the disease can die out if the mean prevalence
across groups is higher than 6 %. In one way coincidental transmission is likely to play a large
role in a pig herd since interactions are random. Furthermore, it has not been investigated
thoroughly how much ESBL E. coli an infected pig excretes to the environment and how long
it can survive. Therefore it is probable that disease survival can vary a lot. However it can
also be argued that other factors besides prevalence are more important in determining disease
survival as for example the disease duration.
The decay rate
Some of the unexpected results from the farm model occurred when the decay rate of ESBL E.
coli in the environment is decreased. According to theory of the model, a decrease in decay rate
should increase the environmental pressure and thereby also increase the prevalence. This is not
the case. As presented in the results the prevalence decreases and so does the environmental
pressure. This means that there is a part of the model which does not work as intended.It is
hypothesised that the mistake a sign error in equation for the environmental pressure which
means that the decay in the pressure is added instead of subtracted at each time point. This
part was adopted directly from Tuominen et al. 2022 which also means that the mistake was
found to late in the project period to correct all results. On a positive note the sensitivity
analysis and the results show that this parameters is not as influential as others. The obtained
results are therefore still considered useful.
A few realisations in which the hypothesised mistake has been corrected have been made. These
show that there at times are slight differences in the pig prevalence between the baseline and the
corrected scenario, but most of the time they overlap. Furthermore, the differences observed do
not have a clear trend or pattern. This means that the alternative scenario display lower and
higher prevalences than baseline at separate time points. For the plot showing these results see
appendix K.
5.8.2
The human model
The human model has been used to evaluate the impact of the different scenarios on the preva-
lence in the three populations. Some scenarios are used to evaluate the impact of changes in
the farm model whereas others are related to factors in the human model.
As previously mentioned, it cannot be concluded that a single transmission dynamic is respon-
sible for all transmission in the human population since multiple dynamics interacts across the
populations, but a few general observations can be made.
The occupational groups follow the development in the prevalence of the pig herd. This means
that for the scenarios in which the pig herd prevalence is either increased or decreased so is the
prevalence in these groups. An increased prevalence in the pig herd has a larger impact on the
groups than a decrease. This behavior can be attributed to the transmission dynamics from
human to human. In the scenario without occupational exposure the two groups quickly reach
a steady-state prevalence which is different from zero. This prevalence can, for both groups, be

interpreted as the prevalence which would be observed if the groups were a part of the general
population. This behavior shows that human to human transmission is a dominant pathway,
but also that all transmission cannot be attributed to it. The same pattern is observed for the
general population, when not accounting for the effect of the travellers. Without occupational
exposure the prevalence in the general population decreases slightly and increases in the pig
prevalence also affect the general population.
This observation in the dynamics is in line with Mughini-Gras et al. 2019. They found that
open community human to human transmission was attributed to approximately 60 % of ESBL
E. coli carriage, but that other sources also are important for sustaining an endemic status in
the open community. One of these sources is human to human transmission from farmers which
attributed with approximately 1 %.
For the general population the parameter with the largest influence is the travellers. The model
results clearly show that removing the impact from travellers leads to a significant decrease in
prevalence. The impact of the travellers is also evident in the seasonal pattern of the prevalence
in the general population. The prevalence rises each summer as a reaction to the increased rate
of travelling. The impact of the travellers is to some extent in line with Mughini-Gras et al.
2019 whom attributes almost 4 % of human sources to returning travellers. This amount is less
significant than the impact the travellers have in the model. Other studies have also shown
that travelling outside Europe is a proven risk factor for community carriage of ESBL E. coli
(Arcilla et al. 2020; Laupland et al. 2008). The influence of the travellers is possibly artificially
increased due to the exclusion of other sources such as food and other animals. If these had
been included, the burden would likely have been more spread out.
In summary the results for the human populations are in line with other studies conducted and
the results show that the most dominant transmission dynamic for the general population is
the travellers, but that occupational exposure also contributes significantly.
5.9
Recommendations
Based on the model results it is relevant to make recommendations on how the prevalence of
ESBL E. coli can be reduced in the pig herds and the human population.
The prevalence in the pig herds is highly influenced by the biological factors such as the disease
duration in the pigs. Factors as these are difficult to alter since it would require e.g. treatment.
In the case of carriage of ESBL E. coli treatment is not a viable option since the only current
treatment option is last resort antibiotics. Furthermore, there is no incentive to treat since the
pigs rarely have clinical symptoms and are otherwise not affected by the bacteria. If treatment
is used anyhow it would likely increase the risk of selection of antimicrobial resistant strains
and thereby make the problem worse. So even though altering this rate would have the largest
effect it is not recommended.
On the other hand the model results also showed that increasing the probability of mingling
the finishers to 10 % the prevalence increased with 36 % after 5 years. This indicate that strict
management strategies are effective in preventing spread.
A recommendation based on the
model is therefore to strictly follow the all-in-all-out principle to minimize interactions between
pigs from different pens. This observation is in line with the conclusion from study by Freitas
Costa et al. 2022. They developed a model, which describes ESBL E. coli spread within a broiler

flock and the zoonotic transmission to the human population. They conclude that to minimize
the prevalence in humans the most effective measure is to reduce the within-flock spread by
increasing the bio-security measurements. Other management strategies such as disinfection of
pens after use would therefore also likely have an influence, but this is a bit more difficult to
quantify with the model due to the mistake in the decay rate.
For the human population the most obvious recommendation is reducing the amount of infected
returning travellers. This is much easier said than done especially considering that the commu-
nity prevalence of ESBL E. coli is rising worldwide (Bezabih et al. 2021). The main problem is
that humans are often also asymptomatic and screening is therefore required for diagnosis. For
humans the treatment options are also limited, so it can also be discussed how much diagnosis
would help. The model results also show that eliminating occupational exposure also decreases
the prevalence in the general population. Reducing the number of infected travellers and the
occupational exposure could therefore in combination be the best strategy for effectively reduc-
ing the prevalence of ESBL E. coli in humans. Both recommendations would require increased
awareness to sanitation, hand hygiene and other protective measures.
The recommendations above could not have been deduced from any previously developed mod-
els. This is only possible for this model since it combines zoonotic transmission to occupational
groups and the influence of travellers in the general population. The model also demonstrates
the need for using a One Health approach when analysing the spread of ESBL E. coli since
interactions in-between species and the environment are important.
5.10
Future perspectives
As part of the model development it has also been considered how the model can be extended
to improve the understanding of ESBL E. coli transmission dynamics. Improving the model
can be divided into extending the farm and human model respectively, whereas some factors
may be relevant for both.
One of the best proven risk factors for promoting selection of antimicrobial resistant strains is
AMU. Implementing AMU in the pig herd and the human population is therefore very relevant.
A model developed for Danish finishers predicted that by replacing extended-spectrum penicillin
usage with another antibiotic type the beta-lactam resistance in the pigs was reduced more than
3 % (Vibe Dalhoff Andersen et al. 2020). Another model by McCarthy et al. 2022 also predicted
that the use of Amoxicillin would significantly increase the amount of resistant E. coli.
When this is combined with the fact that pigs in Denmark accounted for 83 % of all antimicrobial
consumption in animals (Ribeiro Duarte et al. 2023) in 2022 it only enhances the argument for
including this in the modelling framework. When taking the model results into consideration
the implementation of AMU also seems very beneficial. The model clearly showed that below
a certain prevalence level, the disease cannot sustain itself in the pig herd.
Maintaining a
prevalence below this level would be very dependent on AMU due to the strong association
described above. In addition it would be really interesting to be able to evaluate how a reduction
in AMU in pigs would affect the human prevalence. In humans the correlation between AMR
and AMU has also been proven and therefore implementing this relation in the human model
would also be relevant (Kim et al. 2021).

5
DISCUSSION
On a farm in real life there are also some variables which likely affects transmission dynamics,
but which are not implemented in the current model. These variables include, among others,
quarantine pens, flooring type and transmission between adjourning pens.
The potential impact of quarantine pens is straight forward. If an infected pig is isolated from
the rest the herd it minimizes the risk of spread. On the other hand the problem with ESBL
E. coli, as previously mentioned, is the lack of symptoms. Therefore quarantine pens might be
less effective for this pathogen compared to others, but it would be interesting to investigate
anyhow. The flooring type can influence the faeces built-up in a pen, since slated floor removes
the excrement partially in contrast to solid floor. Flooring type has previously been used in
a model for Salmonella transmission (Hill et al. 2016) and has been accounted for in a model
for ESBL E. coli transmission in a pig herd (McCarthy et al. 2022). Transmission between
adjourning pens would be the most simple to implement in the current model since the pens
already have a spatial attribute. The impact of cross contamination could contribute to a better
understanding of the importance of transmission from the environment.
For the human model there are also multiple different sources which could have been included
in the model. The first one is food. ESBL E. coli has been found in meat from poultry, pork
and cattle, but also in some vegetables (Kaesbohrer et al. 2019). Furthermore, Mughini-Gras
et al. 2019 could in their study attribute 6 % of transmission to seafood. This all indicates that
food is potentially a significant source which is not accounted for in the model.
Another potential source which is also mentioned in Mughini-Gras et al. 2019 is companion
animals such as dogs and cats. Companion animals are potentially an important source since
humans are in close contact with them for long periods of time.
Overall this section clearly shows that the model could be improved and extended in various
ways. It should on the other hand always be considered whether an expansion of a model is
feasible or if it makes the modle too complex. Sometimes an additional model which focuses on
some other dynamics might be the best solution.
No matter what approach they would likely all contribute to understand the transmission dy-
namics of ESBL E. coli even better. The amount of sources also demonstrates the need for a
One Health approach in which both human and animal health are considered along with impact
on ecosystems and environment.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
The purpose of this project was to develop a model which can be used to assess which trans-
mission dynamics are the primary drivers of spread of ESBL E. coli in the human population
in Denmark. The final model is a two component model in which each component describes
transmission in a pig herd and in the human population respectively. In the farm component
the pig herd is divided into different life stage groups based on the typical production setup at
a Danish farrow-to-finish farm. In the human component two population groups with occupa-
tional exposure (farmers and slaughterhouse workers) and the general population is modelled.
The model results are used to assess the transmission dynamics and which parameters have an
influence on the prevalence.
For the pig herd it can be concluded that the most influential factor for the ESBL E. coli
prevalence across the life stage groups is the duration of carriage (disease duration) in the pigs.
A 25 % increase in the duration more than tripled the prevalences. Another factor which had
an impact was random mingling of some pigs within the same life stage. This highlighted the
importance of management strategies in mitigating disease spread. The model also showed that
if the prevalence across the groups can be reduced to approximately 5 % the disease will die
out almost half of the time and if reduced even further ESBL E. coli cannot not sustain itself.
For the prevalence in the general population the most crucial parameter is the travellers. During
a five year period the prevalence, when removing travellers completely, is reduced with 64 %
and with time ESBL E. coli would be depleted. Furthermore, when occupational exposure is
removed the occupational groups, which are not directly exposed to travellers, still reaches a
steady-state prevalence. In conclusion this means that across the human population groups
human to human transmission is the dominant mechanism. On the other hand occupational
exposure from the pig herd also have an influence and increases in the pig herd prevalence does
lead to increases in the human populations as well.
Interventions to mitigate transmission is difficult due to the often asymptomatic nature of
intestinal carriage of ESBL E. coli. Besides this the model results show that adherence to strict
management strategies on the pig farm (such as all-in-all-out) and decreasing the returning
traveller prevalence through information campaigns are effective measures.
Based on the recommendations the most relevant extension of the model would be to include
AMU due to the strong association between AMU and AMR. An effective evaluation of pre-
ventive measures or reduction in prevalence would greatly improved if this association was
accounted for as well.
Lastly it can be concluded, as with other diseases, that the transmission dynamics of ESBL E.
coli are more complex than what is included in the model. There potentially exist many other
impactful sources such as food and companion animals which makes prevention and mitigation
of spread even more difficult. This demonstrates the enhanced need for a One Health approach
since no source can be analysed in isolation.
