Abstract
The overarching motivation of this master thesis is to examine the failure of the
closed circular hollow steel member with the evaporation of water content inside the
pipe due to the elevated temperatures as the pressure maker. During the galvanization
process of the steel, there is a chance of rare occurrence, of failure of the closed circular
hollow steel member during the high temperatures which are required for zinc to bond
with steel. To investigate the effect of steel member failing, hoop stress, which requires
smaller pressure to reach higher stresses in that direction compared to the axial stresses,
is analyzed. It was found that the water concentration, in the state of humidity of air,
cannot cause the failure at temperatures up to 600◦C, but in the cases where there is
1 % or more water content of the volume of the pipe, the failure can occur before 600◦C
in some cases.

Description of the project
Background and motivation(why?)
Galvanization of the steel is a process of submerging steel profiles into hot zinc (up to
600◦C) in the manufacturing process. The rare occurrence can happen, when close hollow
steel member fails due to the temperature increase during the galvanization process. In
order to avoid these types of failures, small holes are drilled in the hollow profiles that allow
evaporation of the water and provide an escape for the hot air to reduce the inner pressure.
However, zinc application is typically avoided on fire-protected steel profiles, which
means that these kinds of profiles do not have any holes to let hot air out and are closed at
the end to reduce the exposed surface to the fire and slow down the heating process. Since,
typically, the critical/design temperature of such profiles (400 −500◦C or more) is close to
the one of the melted zinc process, failure due to the water evaporation and air expansion
could in principle occur in a similar way observed during zinc application on closed hollow
steel profiles(CHS) without safety holes.
The possible failure reason for the closed CHS is the water concentration inside the
closed CHS, which with the elevated temperatures evaporates, and builds up pressure that
is sufficiently high to result in a failure of the member.
In Eurocode, there is no regulation regarding these types of failures of the hollow cross-
section which is believed that can occur in some particular cases and can lead to a failure.
Therefore, the cases where this type of failure occurs are not well documented and there is
no explanations regarding the kind of failures in closed hollow steel members.

Object and aim(what?)
Literature review and market research will be investigated first, with different variables
such as temperature, heating rate, moisture content, evaporation coefficient of water, the
expansion coefficient of air, etc., which will be taken into account as the amplitude of each
factor can be significant for the pressure development. All the calculations will be investi-

Firstly, the possible failure will be analyzed with only the moisture content inside the
air when the closed hollow member is manufactured. Also, the content of water needed for
a member to fail will be evaluated with respect to the temperature.
Secondly, different pipes for different purposes, such as structural pipes and sprinkler
system pipes, will be introduced and analyzed with a bigger concentration of water. All the
different methods that are going to be used are shown in the method section.
All of the different calculations that will be introduced in the method section are going
to be analyzed with different parametric as seen above, as well as different geometry(different
cross-section dimensions as well as different purposes of the members).
Method(how?)
All the analysis will be done using Matlab software as some calculations are too complex
to do analytically. Also, all the calculations will be done using the ideal gas law as both air
and water vapor are considered to be ideal gases.
Firstly, steel strength in elevated temperatures will be evaluated using the degradation
factors that predict the behavior of the material as the temperature elevates.
The first calculation will analyze the possible failure of the critical type of stress in
circular hollow steel members due to tensile pressure due to fire. As the temperature builds

Theory background
2.1
Thermodynamics
Thermodynamics is a science of energy.
It can also be documented as the relation
between heat, temperature, work, and energy. Energy can be viewed as the ability to cause
change and it can change from one form to another but the total amount of energy remains
constant. With that being said, the energy cannot be destroyed or created. [1]
An open system (control volume) is a system that allows mass flow and uses devices
such as compressors, turbines, etc. The flow in these types of systems is studied by selecting
the region within the device and using it as a controlled volume. Controlled volume allows
mass and energy to cross the boundaries of the system.
For this case, a closed system will be used. A closed system(control mass) consists of a
fixed amount of mass, and no mass can exceed the boundary of the body. Only the energy,
in terms of heat and work, can exceed the boundary.
2.1.1
Pressure pot
A pressure pot case is described as a closed system with a moving boundary or with
a no movable boundary but different parameters have to be included such as temperature
increase.
For that reason, in this case, most of the calculations can be referred to as a
pressure pot problem, with a closed system and with no movable boundaries (volume stays
the same). Pressure pot case can also be presented as a problem with 2 states as seen in
Figure 1. Where the changing parameter can be volume change or temperature, but the
mass of the content inside should always be the same. In the case where there is a mixture
inside the pot, the individual mass can change but the total mass of the system is constant.

For this case, relative

2.1.3
Water vapor expansion
Water vapor expansion is governed by the behavior of gases. Water vapor has the same
principles as any other gas. The behavior can be explained using the ideal gas law.
When water is heated, its molecules move faster, thus they gain energy. With respect
to the temperature rise, water molecules’ kinetic energy increases too, and the molecules
"break free" from the liquid state and enter a different phase as water vapor.
After the transition phase, water vapor molecules heat up, start to move faster, and start
to hit walls at a higher speed which introduces the increased pressure inside the cylinder.
If the temperature decreases, it can lead to a water vapor kinetic energy decrease and
it can lead to water vapor condensing back into the liquid state.

2.2.5
Elevated temperatures in steel
Elevated temperatures affect on properties of steel mostly changing the microstructures
of the steel components and the interactions between molecular components.
The strength of the steel decreases as the temperature elevates due to the increase in
mobility of the small particles inside the steel. The new phase makes it easier for particles
to move and plastic deformation can occur at lower stresses of the same material. Ductility
is also negatively affected by elevated temperatures. For the same reason as strength is
decreasing, ductility is also decreasing with elevated temperatures due to the mobility of
steel components, more plastic deformation is present which leads to an increased ability
of the material to stretch without fracturing. This process leads to an increase in plastic
deformations inside the material at lower stress levels.
Material stiffness (Elastic Modulus) is mostly constant at elevated temperatures. Thus,
at extremely high temperatures, a decrease in material stiffness can start to decrease.
Steel, like most materials, expands and contracts when temperatures increase/decrease.
This process can lead to stresses within specimens if different parts are at different temper-
atures. Rapid increase/decrease in steel temperature can also lead to the potential failure of
different parts of the specimen.

2.4
Sprinkler systems
The most common system usage of sprinkler systems is automatic sprinkler systems. It
is considered the most effective and cost-friendly type of water supply to areas where fire
can occur. There are 4 different types of automatic sprinkler systems[12]:
• wet pipe system - it is constructed of a network of pipes filled with water under pres-
sure. It is designed such that automatic sprinklers are connected to the piping system
and each sprinkler is assigned to a specific area to permit water discharge.
• dry pipe system - similar to a wet system except that water is held back by a dry pipe.
When sprinklers are in operation, they release the air pressure from the pipe and water
starts to fill the pipes and starts to go to the sprinklers.
• deluge system - used in places where rapid fire can occur as the system is not using
automatic sprinklers. It uses open sprinkles.
• preaction system - similar to a deluge system, but uses automatic sprinklers. It contains
small air pressure inside the pipes and it releases the water only in areas where sprinkles
are activated.
These are four basic sprinkle systems, widely used in the industry, but there are more
systems that can be used in specific cases which can be specified in these four cases[13].
For most of automatic sprinkler systems, especially dry pipe systems, the transitioning
tube (from water to air pressure pipe) is placed in a different room(in most cases inside the
basement) from the sprinkler system. Thus, the problem can occur when a fire occurs in the
region where there are no sprinklers (beginning of air-pressured pipe) and pressure inside the
pipe can develop without the system enabling as the higher temperatures will not reach the

Conclusion
4.1
Summary and highlights
From the analysis, we can conclude a couple of important points.
Firstly, the pressure due to water concentration inside the humid air, described in sub-
section 3.1, cannot lead to failure. As seen in Figure 9, the relative humidity of air due
to increasing temperature is meager and even pressure from air is contributing more than
pressure from water content inside the humid air.
Also, in Figure 12, it is seen that even the air does not make a big amount of inner
pressure for steel to fail until elevated temperature above 800◦C when steel loses most of its
properties.
From the analysis of the larger water concentration inside the pipe, the concentration
of water is described as a percentage of the whole volume of the pipe. Using the saturated
water table, step-by-step analysis can be implemented, which is done by the Equation 3
and based on two-step increments described in subsection 2.3. This step is repeated several
times until all the water is evaporated. After that, only the temperature increases and the
pressure development continues using Equation 18.
For several cases where percentages of water concentration are higher than 4%, it can
be seen in Figure 14 and in subsection 3.3, that this type of analysis can affect a result as
the critical temperature can be reached before the whole water is evaporated.
In the subsection 3.3 it can be seen how different parameters affect the critical temper-
ature of different concentrations of water. From Figures 15 and 16 it can be seen that an
increase in diameter causes the critical temperature to decrease as the content of the water
is bigger. On the other hand, increasing thickness has a much bigger influence on the critical
temperature. With increasing of the thickness the critical temperature increases as well.
The degradation factor has an important role as well. As seen in Figures16 and 17 the

Limitation and further studies
difference in critical temperature at the range between 350◦C and 500◦C has a big impact.
Comparing two different factors, it can be seen that most of the profiles at water concentra-

performs much better compared to the 0.2% degradation factor, which makes sense as it

4.2
Limitation and further studies
Limitation for this analysis, for structural members that are exposed to the initial
pressure, the ideal gas law cannot be implemented as the initial state has to be considered
and developed in a different way.
Furthermore, the limitation of this analysis is that this process can be done only with
the gases that are considered the ideal gases and all the different gases that are in touch
with the profile and are not ideal have to be done in a different, more complex, way[15].
Further studies regarding the galvanization process of the closed hollow steel member
and the incident that rarely occurs with the failure of the profiles, can be concluded that the
temperature increase due to the hot zinc bath has nothing to do with the moisture content
inside the pipe. It can also be concluded that there is a different factor that contributes to
the failure of the member which can be related to the chemical process of relation between
hot zinc and steel.
Also, further studies can be done experimentally, to see if there is a possible mismatch
between theoretical calculations and real-world scenarios. Experimental data can also give
the possible new factors that should be considered.
