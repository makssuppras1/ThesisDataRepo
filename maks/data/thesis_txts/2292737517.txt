Abstract 
 
 
Page | v of xx 
ABSTRACT 
This report is addressed to the department DEFU in the Danish Energy Association 
which provides consulting services for Danish power grid companies. The purpose of 
the project is to determine a method of estimation for the coincident peak power con-
sumption of single family households in urban areas with installed heat pump loads. 
The motivation for the project is a political agreement in Denmark to reduce the use of 
fossil fuels which includes an electrification of the residential heating sector. One of the 
most promising solutions to such electrification is the electrical heat pump. 
By studying the technology and the market of heat pumps it is found that the most 
common heat pump installed in single family households is a water/water or air/water 
heat pump with an average thermal capacity of 10 𝑘𝑊𝑡. Today, the use of such heat 
pumps is limited. As barriers such as high investment costs and taxation on electricity 
are lowered it can be expected that more heat pumps will replace existing oil and gas 
heaters. 
Hourly power consumption measurements for 2011 are collected from 1,000 customers 
with oil or gas heaters and from 1,000 customers with heat pumps. The statistical data is 
analyzed using quantiles to remove extremities in the power consumption for individual 
customers. It is found that the power consumption for customers with heat pumps is 
fluctuating significantly over the year. By investigating the daily power consumption for 
separate seasons and types of days, it is found that there are no differences between 
workdays or days off for estimation purposes, and that the peak power consumption 
occurs at 17:00 in days of the winter season. 
The coincident peak power consumption for customers with heat pumps is found to be 
almost linearly dependent on the number of aggregated customers. The resulting method 
applies a linear estimation of the yearly peak power consumption of an aggregated 
group of customers with heat pump loads. Using the thermal capacity of cables the 
method disregards the two highest consuming hours of the year and estimates the 3rd 
yearly peak power. By evaluating the method, it is found to be accurate within the per-
missible limits of the natural variance in the individual power consumptions. If a better 
precision is desired, more influential factors must be incorporated.

Resumé 
 
 
Page | vii of xx 
RESUMÉ 
Denne rapport er udarbejdet til afdelingen DEFU i Dansk Energi, som tilbyder konsu-
lentydelser til danske netvirksomheder. Formålet med projektet er at bestemme en me-
tode til at estimere den maksimale samtidige belastning for énfamiliehuse i byområder 
med installerede varmepumpebelastninger. Motivationen for projektet er en politisk 
aftale i Danmark om at reducere brugen af fossile brændsler, hvilket inkluderer en elek-
trificering af varmesektoren i boligområder. En af de mest lovende løsninger til en så-
dan elektrificering er den elektriske varmepumpe. 
Ved at studere teknologien og markedet for varmepumper konstateres det, at den mest 
almindelige varmepumpe installeret i énfamiliehuse er en vand/vand eller luft/vand 
varmepumpe med en gennemsnitlig termisk kapacitet på 10 𝑘𝑊𝑡. I dag er brugen af 
sådanne varmepumper begrænset. I takt med at barrierer, som høje investeringsomkost-
ninger og beskatning på elektricitet, bliver mindre, kan det forventes, at flere eksiste-
rende olie- og gasfyr bliver udskiftet med varmepumper. 
Timevise belastninger for 2011 er indsamlet fra 1.000 kunder med olie- og gasfyr og fra 
1.000 kunder med varmepumper. De statistiske data analyseres med fraktiler for at fjer-
ne ekstremiteter i belastningerne for enkeltkunder. Det er konstateret, at effektforbruget 
for kunder med varmepumper fluktuerer betydeligt over året. Ved at undersøge belast-
ningerne på daglig basis for separate sæsoner og typer af dage er det fundet, at der ikke 
er estimerende forskelle mellem arbejdsdage eller fridage, samt at den maksimale be-
lastning forekommer kl. 17:00 for dage i vintersæsonen. 
Den maksimale samtidige belastning for kunder med varmepumper er konstateret at 
være næsten lineært afhængigt af antallet af aggregerede kunder. Den endelige metode 
benytter en lineær estimering af den maksimale årlige belastning for en aggregeret 
gruppe af kunder med varmepumpebelastninger. Ved at benytte den termiske kapacitet i 
kabler ser metoden bort fra de to hårdest belastede timer i året og estimerer den tredje 
maksimale årlige belastning. Ved at evaluere metoden er det fundet, at den er nøjagtig 
indenfor de tilladte grænser fra den naturlige varians i de individuelle belastninger. Hvis 
en mere præcis metode ønskes, er det nødvendigt at tage flere betydelige faktorer i be-
tragtning.

INTRODUCTION 
The electrical power grid is one of the most important scientific inventions ever made. 
Modern life is highly influenced by appliances powered by electricity, which is trans-
ported through the transmission and distribution power grids. These power grids have 
grown into massive and complex infrastructures that are constantly supplying consum-
ers with electricity. Today, distribution system operators (DSOs) are paying special in-
terest to the development of the residential power consumption due to expectations of 
radical changes to the present load profiles. The changes are the result of non-electrical 
appliances being electrified, which creates new consumption demands. One of such 
appliances is the electrical heat pump. Heat pumps are expected to replace traditional 
electric heaters but even more importantly also oil and gas heaters. The purpose of this 
project is to analyze the influence of heat pumps on the power flow in residential low 
voltage feeder lines, and to develop a method to estimate the coincident peak power 
consumption for customers with heat pump loads. The principal idea of the method is to 
provide a tool for screening the residential low voltage power grid for weak points that 
are likely to experience problems with overloading resulting from an increasing number 
of heat pump installations. 
1.1 
Project background 
Distribution of power to residential consumers is maintained by DSOs. These compa-
nies are obligated by law to ensure that power is delivered safely and reliably to their 
customers [1]. This obligation implies careful operation and maintenance work of the 
power grids. DSOs are interested in dimensioning the power grids with sufficient mar-
gin to the demanded peak power to avoid congestions, and to allow for increase in de-
mand. Proper dimensioning of the power grid reduces the risk of having to replace func-
tional power lines with new lines that have larger transfer capacities. This is especially 
relevant in recent years where overhead lines are being substituted by underground ca-
bles, which increase the cost of replacement dramatically due to expensive excavation 
work. Expensive replacements are one of the reasons that power grids are built to be 
==PAGE:24==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Introduction 
 
Page | 2 of 128 
operational for long periods of time before significant maintenance work or complete 
replacements are needed.  
 
In the past years the consumption of electricity in households has declined which has 
lessened the burden on the power lines [2] [3]. The decline can be explained by an in-
creasing efficiency of domestic electrical appliances and lighting. However, significant 
changes to the electrical consumption are to be expected if new domestic sectors such as 
the transport sector or the heating sector become electrified. In the heating sector a 
promising possibility is the electrical heat pump, which uses electrical energy to provide 
hot water and space heating. The DSOs are interested in the influence of heat pumps on 
the residential power consumption. High penetration levels in the residential sector may 
increase the risk for high coincident power demand, which can exceed the transfer ca-
pacity for certain low voltage feeder lines. It is beneficial for the DSOs to investigate 
the power flow in the low voltage grid to get an understanding of the residual network 
capacity, and assess the risk of congestions in the event of heat pump installations. 
 
In order to observe the power flow in specific parts of the power grid, online current 
measurement devices have over the years been installed in the power grid to measure 
the transfer of current. Such information is essential in power grid planning to provide 
an indication of weak points in the power grid. Online current measurements are typi-
cally made on the outgoing 10-20 kV feeders at primary distribution substations. The 
0.4 kV low voltage power grid is so large and complex which have made it uneconomic 
to install online current measurement devices on the outgoing feeders from secondary 
distribution substations. Consequently the DSOs are not able to determine the exact 
power flow in the residential power grid. Locally read meters have historically been 
installed in households to determine the yearly energy consumption, primarily for bill-
ing purposes. The readings of the local meters are reported annually to the DSOs who 
calculate the yearly energy consumption of the customers based on last year’s reading. 
This annual energy consumption data does not directly provide information on the cus-
tomers’ peak power consumptions or their load profiles, both of which are useful quan-
tities in low voltage grid planning. However, by using estimation methods the annual 
energy consumption can indirectly be used for grid planning purposes. 
 
For ideal dimensioning of the power grid, the DSOs require knowledge of the coinci-
dent peak power demand on residential low voltage feeder lines. In 1952 Professor Sten 
Velander contributed to this subject by statistically deriving a correlation of the yearly 
energy consumption to an empirically estimated peak power consumption [4]. The 
equation of the correlation is applicable for different customer categories. The equation 
==PAGE:25==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Introduction 
 
Page | 3 of 128 
takes into account the coincident power consumption of several customers of the same 
category, and can provide an estimate of the peak power used on a residential feeder 
line. Since Velander’s contribution much development has occurred in the electric pow-
er sector, and the statistical data which Velander’s formula is based on may have 
changed to such an extent where it is no longer valid. The validity of the Velander for-
mula has been discussed in several research papers, and new methods to determine the 
coincident peak power consumption has been studied [5] [6] [7].  
 
Emphasis has in recent years been given to installing remotely read meters in the low 
voltage grid to measure the power consumption of residential customers on an hourly 
basis. The Danish Energy Agency has announced that DSOs must replace all local read 
meters in households with remotely read meters by 2020. The replacement is part of a 
smart grid strategy, where hourly readings will allow consumers to adjust their con-
sumption to electricity price signals [8] [9]. The complete transition to remote read me-
ters makes it possible to collect a vast dataset of hourly power consumptions. Such data 
will provide an opportunity to make statistical estimations of typical residential load 
profiles, which can be used in low voltage grid planning. Data from the first remote read 
meters have already been collected. In this project an analysis of hourly power data 
from residential households will be made with the purpose to identify a typical power 
consumption profile for customers with heat pumps. Such an analysis can provide valu-
able information to the planning of low voltage power grids.

Project thesis 
The aim of this project is to provide a method to estimate the dimensioning power con-
sumption for households with heat pumps on low voltage feeder lines given the infor-
mation available to the DSOs. The overall problem in the project can be stated as 
 
Determine a statistical method to estimate the coincident peak power consumption 
for households in urban areas with installed heat pumps. 
 
In order to determine such a statistical method, it is required to study the power con-
sumption of residential customers with heat pumps, and to investigate which types of 
heat pumps are expected to dominate the market. The heat demand and power consump-
tion from heat pumps are among other dependent the quality of thermal insulation of the 
individual households. In households with poor thermal insulation the power consump-
tion will be relatively high compared to households with good thermal insulation. Since 
the statistical method involves feeder lines with multiple customers, it is necessary to 
take into account how the peak power demand among neighboring customers coincides. 
These considerations can be formulated as three questions, which need to be addressed 
in order to determine a proper statistical method for the overall problem. 
 Which types and sizes of heat pumps are the most common on the resi-
dential market today, and which are expected to be installed in house-
holds in the future? 
 
 What is a representative power consumption pattern for residential cus-
tomers with heat pump loads? 
 
 How can the coincident peak power usage for neighboring households be 
expressed statistically? 
These questions will provide the necessary knowledge of typical power consumption for 
households with heat pump loads, which is a necessity for providing a method to esti-
mate the coincident peak demand on residential feeder lines.  
 
The penetration level of heat pumps at specific feeder lines has a large influence on the 
estimation of dimensioning peak power. The method of estimation should be able to 
take this level into account to provide a realistic result. The calculated peak coincident 
power can be used to quickly screen a large number of feeder lines to find where the 
low voltage network is likely to experience overloading.

Limitations 
Due to the limited amount of time set aside for the project there are aspects which are 
too time consuming or which are not relevant for the main focus. Such aspects have 
been excluded from the scope of the project. The limitations are presented below and 
their significances for the results in the project are assessed. 
 
 Analysis of all customer categories: 
It has been chosen to focus the analysis on single family households with heat 
pumps. The consumption data used in the project originate from single family 
households in an urban area. The results of the project are assumed to be repre-
sentative for single family households in other urban areas as well. The daily 
power consumption of a customer category is unique, and the results will not be 
applicable for other customer categories such as industry or apartments. 
 
 Heat pump type and size: 
The power usage of a heat pump is dependent on the rated capacity and on the 
type of heat source and sink media. In this project a representative type and size 
of heat pump will be identified. If other ratings or types of heat pumps are used, 
it may impact the results of the project significantly. 
 
 Detailed heat pump analysis: 
In this report a short presentation on heat pump technology will be given. Only 
the necessary information in regards to power grid dimensioning will be investi-
gated. Further details which are not related to power grid planning will not be 
investigated. This will not have an influence on the results of the project. 
 
 Load forecast: 
Forecasting of residential daily load profiles will not be a part of the project. 
Since the analysis of the project is based on the power flow in the distribution 
grid, future changes in residential power consumption can have a substantial in-
fluence on the results. 
 
 Thermal insulation: 
The power use of heat pumps in the residential sector is strongly dependent on 
the quality of thermal insulation in buildings. New households are generally 
built with good insulation, which lowers the necessary thermal energy for heat-
ing. In this project it is chosen to expect a quality of insulation comparable to 
households built in 1960-1970, since this type of household is very common in 
urban areas. This limitation does not impact the result since the thermal insula-
tions of the investigated households are unknown.

 Price signals: 
In the future, heat pumps are desired to be controlled by electricity price signals 
as part of a smart grid solution. This can have a large impact on the time periods

zero capacity. Since the size of the power consumption from heat pumps can be 
significant, the continuous switching will cause voltage flicker. Furthermore, the 
use of support heat production from immersion heaters will produce additional 
voltage flicker. An investigation of voltage flicker is left out of the scope of the 
project. This limitation will not have an influence on the results of the project.

1.4 
Approach 
This project aims at determining a method to estimate the coincident peak power con-
sumption for residential customers in urban areas with heat pump loads. This aim will 
be accomplished by investigating modern heat pump technologies, analyzing power 
consumption data of residential customers with heat pumps and by applying statistical 
calculation methods.  
 
The technology of electrical heat pumps needs to be studied to obtain a fundamental 
understanding of the possible influence that heat pumps can have on the power con-
sumption for residential customers. It is required to investigate the present heat pump 
market to identify the most common types and sizes of heat pumps used in single family 
households at the present time. Additionally, the expectations for the future heat pump 
market should also be investigated to uncover any probabilities for substantial changes 
to the heat pump market which will have to be accounted for in the estimation method.  
 
Power consumption data for customers in single family households with respectively 
traditional heaters and heat pumps must be collected. By analyzing such data it is possi-
ble to determine the changes in residential power consumption caused by the introduc-
tion of heat pumps. Since the estimation method will be used to screen the existing low 
voltage power grid to find areas that are likely to experience problems with overloading, 
a scenario with high power consumption must be considered. Therefore the dimension-
ing season of the year and type of day is examined as part of the daily power consump-
tion analysis. Additionally the dependency of the power consumption from heat pumps 
on the outdoor air temperature will have to be analyzed to identify if the estimation 
method will be valid for other temperature conditions than those of the measurement 
year. 
 
When the dimensioning season of the year and type of day have been determined, statis-
tical calculation methods to aggregate the power consumption of individual customers 
will be applied to the data. It is important that the simultaneity between the individual 
customers’ data is kept in the aggregation process in order for it to reflect the coincident 
aggregated power consumption for a group of customers on the same feeder line. Final-
ly a suitable method to estimate the aggregated power consumption from the measure-
ment data will be investigated and evaluated from a separate set of data.

2.1 
Structure of the Danish power grid 
The Danish power grid is divided into two distinctively operated power grids; the 
transmission power grid and the distribution power grid. The transmission power grid is 
operated by Energinet.dk, which is an independent public enterprise owned by the Dan-
ish Ministry of Climate, Energy and Building. The transmission power grid covers the 
400 kV, 150 kV and 132 kV voltage levels, as well as electrical connections to Norway, 
Sweden and Germany [10]. Large amounts of power are transferred over long distances 
at these high voltage levels. The distribution power grid is operated by many separate 
DSOs; DONG Energy, SEAS-NVE, Energi Fyn, EnergiMidt among others. The distri-
bution power grids cover the 0.4-60 kV voltage levels and are connected to the 132-150 
kV transmission grid through transformers with low voltage sides of 30-60 kV. At pri-
mary distribution substations the voltage level is further transformed to 10-20 kV. In 
some situations the 30-60 kV network is omitted and the 10-20 kV side of the primary 
distribution substations is connected directly to the 132-150 kV transmission grid. Fi-
nally at secondary distribution substations the voltage level is transformed to the resi-
dential 0.4 kV [11]. From the secondary distribution substations 0.4 kV feeder lines 
transport power to residential customers through cable distribution cabinets (CDCs). 
These 0.4 kV power lines which transport power from the CDCs to the households are 
referred to as service lines. 
 
The power grid in Denmark is split into a western and an eastern part which are not op-
erated synchronously. The western part includes Jutland and Funen, while the eastern

2.2 
Electrification of heating sector 
In March 2012 the Danish government passed an energy act describing initiatives and 
milestones for a vision of becoming completely free of fossil fuels by 2050 [14]. One of 
the key progresses is the production of renewable electricity from wind power, which is 
projected to account for roughly half of the Danish electricity consumption by 2020. 
The transition to renewable electricity will impact the residential heating sector, which 
presently uses both oil and gas as fueling commodities. The residential heating sector is 
expected to reduce the use of fossil fuels by phasing out oil and gas heaters [15]. One of 
==PAGE:34==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Project Basis 
 
Page | 12 of 128 
the ways to accomplish this milestone of phasing out oil and gas heaters is by introduc-
ing residential heat pumps and thereby use electricity for hot water production and 
space heating. Heat pumps can also utilize a surplus of electricity by converting electri-
cal energy to thermal energy and store it in hot water tanks. In periods of excessive elec-
tricity generation, the heat pumps can increase the temperature of the water tanks and 
remain passive in periods of low generation while using the thermal energy reserves. 
This can be achieved without the consumers experiencing a noticeable decrease in com-
fort, but it requires relatively large water tanks and intelligent control units that can act 
based on signals for the electricity price and generation as part of a smart grid solution 
[16].  
 
The number of installations of heat pumps in Denmark has in the past years been lim-
ited [17]. Two of the main obstacles for residential household owners are high invest-
ment costs and high taxation on electricity. The energy tax on electricity per unit of en-
ergy is significantly higher when compared to the energy tax on other commodities in 
the heating sector such as oil or gas. An equalization of the energy taxes to make heat 
pumps more profitable for household owners has been widely discussed and actions 
toward such an equality have already been made [18] [19]. 
 
In Sweden and Norway much of the electricity comes from dispatchable renewable en-
ergy sources such as hydropower. The electricity in these two countries is both renewa-
ble and cheap which has made heat pumps a popular investment to replace the previous-
ly used resistive electric heaters [20] [21]. The expansion of heat pumps in Denmark is 
different since traditional electric heaters have not been widely used as is the case in 
Sweden and Norway. Instead district heating, biomass, oil and gas heating systems are 
the primary sources of space heating in Denmark. Furthermore the renewable energy in 
Denmark primarily comes from wind and solar power which are non-dispatchable 
sources. As a result there has been less incentive to research and invest in heat pump 
technology. If market barriers such as the price of electricity for heating purposes de-
creases to the levels observed in the Swedish and Norwegian power systems, the incen-
tive for Danish household owners to install heat pumps may increase.

2.4 
Peak power estimation methods 
It is essential for DSOs to be able to estimate the peak power loading that may occur on 
the power lines even if heat pumps do not replace the existing heating systems. Power 
lines can only endure to be slightly overloaded for short periods of time and proper di-
mensioning of the power grid is important to avoid congestions. The combined peak 
power consumption of a group of customers is not necessarily equal to the sum of their 
individual peak consumptions, since it is unlikely that these will occur simultaneously. 
The concept of coincident peak power demand should be used for dimensioning purpos-
es. 
 
Over the years several methods have been used for estimating the coincident peak pow-
er consumption on residential low voltage feeder lines. The simplest methods are rules-
of-thumb approaches, which are based on experience [27]. Such methods assume a cer-
==PAGE:38==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Project Basis 
 
Page | 16 of 128 
tain peak power based on the type of residence and number of occupants. The individual 
peak powers are then aggregated and a coincident factor may be taken into account. 
Other methods, using empirical approaches, include the Velander formula and the use of 
characteristic load curves. 
2.4.1 
Concept of Velander’s formula 
The Velander formula provides a method for estimating the combined yearly peak pow-
er consumption of a group of customers. It describes a simple correlation between the 
yearly energy consumption for a group of customers and the combined yearly peak 
power consumption. The method is primarily used in Scandinavian countries to give an 
estimate for the peak power load based on readings for customers’ yearly energy con-
sumption, which are available to DSOs as discussed in section 1.1. The formula first 
appears in 1952 presented by Swedish professor Sten Velander [4]. It has been a very 
popular methodology for many Scandinavian DSOs since it is simple to apply and can 
provide quick estimates. 
 
The Velander method can be used for several customers of identical customer category. 
A customer category describes customers with similar characteristic consumption pat-
terns and levels, and specific Velander constants are attached to each category. The Ve-
lander constants can’t be calculated theoretically, but must be determined through 
measurements and statistical analysis for every customer category [28]. In the following 
section Velander’s formula for customers within the same category and of varying cate-
gories will be derived with statistical methods.

2.4.2 
Characteristic load curves 
Another method of peak power estimation is the use of characteristic load curves. Such 
curves describe the average load patterns for customers, and are determined from power 
measurements made directly at the households. The resolution of the measurements 
should be at least on an hourly basis to provide information on the peak power demand. 
If the measurements are made on less than an hourly basis, essential information may be 
lost when averaging. Since the characteristic curves describe average loading patterns, 
there are statistical variances which need to be accounted for. 
 
Characteristic load curves are based on different customer categories like the Velander 
formula. Each category has a unique consumption pattern and level due to their specific 
daily activities. The total peak power load for identical customers or for customers of 
varying categories is found by aggregation. Since the characteristic curves are time-
resolved it is possible to aggregate the hourly load more accurately than compared to the 
Velander formula. This significantly improves the calculation of the aggregated peak 
power load across different customer categories.

HEAT PUMP TECHNOLOGY 
In recent years the installation of renewable energy in the form of wind and solar energy 
has increased significantly [31] [32]. As a consequence electricity is becoming plentiful. 
Efforts are being made to converting fossil fueled sectors to consume electricity and 
thereby allowing for further increase in the number of renewable energy resources. As 
described in section 2.2 this change includes an electrification of the heating sector 
where oil and gas are presently used to a large degree. Heat pumps are an efficient and 
modern solution for electrical residential space heating and hot water production with a 
technical lifetime of approximately 14 years [33]. As opposed to standard electric heat-
ing, heat pumps do not generate heat directly, but transfers heat from one place to an-
other using little electricity [34]. In general, heat pumps can be used both for heating 
and cooling purposes. The temperature control depends solely on the direction of heat 
transfer. In this report only heat pumps used for domestic heating purposes will be in-
vestigated.  
 
When heat pumps are used for heating purposes, heat is moved against its spontaneous 
flow direction from a cold to a warm environment, usually from outdoor surroundings 
to indoor environments. Heat is extracted from the thermal solar energy in the outside 
air, soil or water. The outside thermal energy is processed through the heat pump and 
forced inside by an electrically driven pump system. The energy consumption to move 
heat in a heat pump is relatively low compared to other types of heating systems such as 
conventional electric heating, oil or gas heaters. For every kilowatt-hour of electrical 
energy used in the heating process, up to three to four kilowatt-hours of thermal energy 
can be delivered [17] [34]. 
In this chapter the different types of heat pumps will be presented along with a descrip-
tion of how an electrical heat pump works. Furthermore different methods of operation-
al control of heat pumps are investigated and the impact of the electrical consumption 
from heat pumps on the power grid is assessed. Hereafter statistics for the current types 
and sizes of the present heat pump installations are provided. Lastly, expectations for 
the future market situation are given.

3.1 
Types of heat sources and heat sinks 
A heat pump is classified by the media from which it collects heat (heat source) and the 
media to which it delivers heat (heat sink). Based on combinations of heat sources and 
heat sinks there exists four types of heat pumps. The categorization will be denoted in 
the form [heat source/heat sink]. The main types of heat pumps are 
 Air/Air 
 Air/Water 
 Water/Water 
 Water/Air 
Of the four listed types of heat pumps, the air/air, air/water and water/water heat pumps 
are the most common. The water/air heat pump is only very rarely used in practice.  
A short description of the three commonly used types of heat pumps based on their heat 
source media will be given in sections 3.1.1 and 3.1.2. Auxiliary heat supply, hybrid 
heat pumps and issues with Legionella bacteria are introduced in section 3.1.3. 
3.1.1 
Air source heat pumps 
Heat pumps using air as heat source media extracts heat from the outside air, and can 
use fans for active heat exchange to obtain an increased efficiency. Heat pumps based 
on air heat sources are cheap and easy to install relative to water source heat pumps, 
which has made them extensively popular [35]. When used in combination with an air 
heat sink, the heat pump functions similar to an air conditioner operating in reverse. 
Heat is extracted from the outside air and dispersed indoors by central heating elements. 
See Figure 3-1 for an illustration of a typical air/air heat pump [36].

An air heat sink can’t support hot water production, and is only used for space heating. 
In order to support hot water production it is required to have a water heat sink or so-
called hydronic systems. Hydronic systems can support both hot water production and 
space heating by dispersing heated water through radiators or floor heating. In air/water 
heat pumps heat is transferred from the hot air inside the heat pump to the hydronic sys-
tem through a heat exchanger. In households with oil or gas heaters a hydronic system is 
already installed, and air/water heat pumps are expected to be the preferred replacement. 
The primary use for air/air heat pumps are households without hydronic installations – 
typically holiday homes, which are primarily only in use in the summer season. This is 
because the effective benefit of having a hydronic heating system is lost if the residence 
is not in use in most of the year, which is the case for most holiday homes [23] [37]. It 
is unlikely that customers in households with hydronic systems will shift to an air dis-
persing system. 
3.1.2 
Water source heat pumps 
The concept of water source heat pumps can be confusing. Before discussing the basic 
principles of such heat pumps it is important to understand what the notation “water 
source” entails. In the group of water source heat pumps there are heat pumps using the 
ground water or nearby streams as heat source. However, the notation also covers heat 
pumps that use the ground soil as heat source. These are occasionally referred to as 
ground source heat pumps as an individual type, and are the most common of the water 
source heat pumps.  
 
Water source heat pumps utilize the solar energy stored in the soil, ground water or 
nearby streams, and are almost exclusively used with hydronic heat sink systems. Coils 
are buried in soil or suspended in water near the residence to extract the necessary heat. 
The coils require significant available space to obtain sufficient surface area to absorb 
enough heat. When the coils are buried in soil it is possible to have a horizontal layout 
at a depth of approximately one meter, or a vertical layout up to 200 meters [25] [38] 
[39]. In the deep vertical installations the coils will reach the ground water which neces-
sitates a lot of regulations in regards to risk of pollution. For illustrations of the two coil 
layouts see Figure 3-2 [40]. Water source heat pumps are more expensive compared to 
air source heat pumps due to the more complicated installations, and are commonly ac-
quired for their relatively higher efficiencies [35].

3.1.3 
Auxiliary heat supply 
Most heat pumps are not dimensioned to cover the highest heating demands that may 
occur under very cold temperatures. Typically air/water and water/water heat pumps are 
dimensioned to cover 80-85 % of the peak heat demand, while air/air heat pumps are 
dimensioned for slightly less than 80 %. Even though the heat pumps are not able to 
cover the entire peak heat demand, they are estimated to be able to supply 95-100 % of 
the yearly heat consumption depending on mean annual temperatures [34].  This method 
of dimensioning heat pumps below the peak heating demand is favorable because there 
are a limited number of hours in a year where the weather is so cold that the highest 
heating demands are reached. When these low temperatures occur, it is possible to sup-
plement the heat produced by the heat pump with additional heat from an auxiliary elec-
tric immersion heater, or in some situations from traditional electric heaters. The electri-
cal immersion heaters supply auxiliary heat in a number of stages based on simple resis-
tive heating. On very cold days the power consumption from auxiliary electric immer-
sion heaters and from traditional electric heaters can be significant. 
3.1.3.1 
Hybrid heat pumps 
One of the newest ideas in supplementary heat supply is the hybrid electric and gas heat 
pump. The auxiliary supply in hybrid heat pumps is a gas heater, which requires that the 
customer is connected to the gas grid. Such a solution may be attractive for customers 
that are presently using gas as the primary heating commodity, since the hybrid heat

pump can be connected to the existing gas heater. The hybrid heat pump continuously 
evaluates which of the two fueling commodities are most economic, and selects the en-
ergy source based on the evaluation. Most of the time the electrical part of the hybrid 
heat pump is most economical, and the gas part acts as auxiliary supply in cold tempera-
tures [41]. This is because the electrical performance is excellent under mild tempera-
tures but decrease significantly when the temperature decreases. In relation to this pro-
ject it may become relevant if this type of hybrid heat pump becomes popular, since it 
does not use electricity for supplying peak heat demand.  
3.1.4 
Water tank and Legionella bacteria 
In air/water and water/water heat pumps it is a possibility to install a hot water tank in 
connection to the heat pump in order to store thermal energy. Due to the large capacities 
in such water tanks it is possible that the water tank is not emptied regularly. This may 
result in the growth of Legionella bacteria, which can cause severe illness if inhaled. To 
remove these bacteria the water in the tank needs to be heated above 60℃ once a week 
[34]. The power consumption to sufficiently heat the water can be significant, especially 
during summer where the heat pump is not used for space heating. 
3.2 
Heating process 
As described in the beginning of the chapter, the process of heating in heat pumps is the 
transfer of thermal solar energy stored in the outdoor surroundings to thermal energy in 
the indoor environment. Two key principles are involved in the heating process of heat 
pumps. The first principle is Gay-Lussac's Law, which is one of the ideal gas laws de-
scribing the proportionality between temperature and pressure in gases of constant vol-
ume and mass [42]. The second principle is the thermodynamic phase transitions of va-
porization and condensation. 
 
These two principles occur under the heat transfer process and are performed by the 
four component elements listed below. 
 
 Evaporator 
 Electrically controlled compressor 
 Condenser 
 Expansion valve 
 
==PAGE:48==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Heat Pump Technology 
 
Page | 26 of 128 
The electric compressor is the only controllable element in the heat pump circuit [43]. 
There are three distinct systems used in the heating process of a heat pump.  
 
 The heat pump system, which uses electrical energy to force thermal energy 
from the heat source to the heat sink. 
 
 The heat source system that absorbs thermal solar energy stored in the outdoor 
surroundings. 
 
 The heat sink system, which distributes the thermal energy in the indoor envi-
ronment. 
The evaporator, electric compressor, condenser and expansion valve all belong to the 
heat pump system and form a closed loop where a refrigerant liquid circulates between 
the heat source and heat sink. The heat source system consists of an outdoor heat col-
lector component and of coils in which an anti-freeze liquid circulates. The heat collec-
tor captures solar energy while the coils transfer the energy to the evaporator of the heat 
pump system through a heat exchanger. The second law of thermodynamics states that 
heat spontaneously flows from warm regions to cold regions. As a consequence the anti-
freeze liquid in the coils must be kept at very low temperatures in order to extract heat 
from the cold outdoor surroundings. The heat sink system distributes the thermal ener-
gy obtained from the heat pump system through hot air or hot water in the indoor envi-
ronment. 
 
The principle heating process and the interaction between the three heat systems can be 
described in six steps [34]. 
 
Step 1. 
Solar energy is absorbed by the anti-freeze liquid in the heat source collector 
from the outside surroundings, and the temperature of the liquid increases 
slightly. 
 
Step 2. 
The anti-freeze liquid is cooled in the evaporator as it delivers heat to the re-
frigerant liquid in the heat pump circuit. The refrigerant evaporates in re-
sponse to the increase in temperature and changes from liquid to gaseous 
form. 
==PAGE:49==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Heat Pump Technology 
 
Page | 27 of 128 
 
Step 3. 
The pressure of the refrigerant gas is raised in the electric compressor caus-
ing a controllable increase in the temperature of the refrigerant as a result of 
Gay-Lussac’s Law.  
 
Step 4. 
The refrigerant gas delivers heat to the heat sink system at the condenser el-
ement by the second law of thermodynamics. The temperature decrease of 
the refrigerant gas causes it to condense and change from gaseous to liquid 
form. The temperature at which the condensation occurs is high due to the 
high pressure at the condenser. 
 
Step 5. 
The pressure is lowered at the expansion valve causing the temperature of 
the refrigerant liquid to decease significantly. The cooled refrigerant liquid 
can now extract heat from the anti-freeze liquid once again. 
 
Step 6. 
The sequence starts over and repeats. 
 
The described heating process is valid for water/water heat pumps. In air/water and 
air/air heat pumps the heat source system is integrated in the heat pump system. Instead 
of separate coils with anti-freeze liquid, the refrigerant liquid and the evaporator are 
directly connected to the outdoor heat collector component. At the heat collector a fan 
can be used to blow the outside air through the coils containing the refrigerant liquid, 
which absorbs heat from the air. Additionally for air/air heat pumps the heat sink system 
is integrated in the heat pump system. The condenser element in this type of heat pump 
consists of heating coils where a fan is blowing the hot air into the house. In Denmark 
the air/air heat pumps primarily have a ductless indoor element resembling an air condi-
tioning element as was shown in Figure 3-1. The principles of the heating processes for 
the three types of heat pumps are illustrated in Figure 3-3.

3.3 
Efficiency 
When describing the productivity of heat pumps it is not sufficient only to study the 
conventional efficiency factor. In connection to energy conversion processes efficiency 
describes the ratio of useful output energy to input energy. The efficiency factor is re-
stricted to the interval from 0 % to 100 % by the first law of thermodynamics, which 
states that energy in a closed system can’t be created or destroyed. In heat pumps the 
process is a movement of energy rather than a conversion. A more appropriate measure 
of productivity in heat pumps is a coefficient of performance (COP). The COP is a di-
mensionless quantity that describes the ratio of thermal energy output to electrical input

energy. The theoretical maximum COP can be described by the Carnot COP. This is 
based on the principle of the Carnot cycle which is the most efficient thermodynamic 
cycle that can be achieved [45].

outdoor coils extract heat from the surroundings. The temperatures are absolute and 
should be given in Kelvin [45]. Since the temperatures have an influence on the COP 
there are predetermined operating temperatures at which the COP is measured to allow 
comparison between heat pumps. For ground source heat pumps the COP is commonly 
determined at an earth temperature of 0℃ and a water flow temperature of 45℃ to hy-
dronic heating systems. For air/water heat pumps the COP is determined from an out-

practice there are losses present when the compressor has to establish operating pressure 
and maintain desired temperatures. The practical COP is therefore given as the product 
of the efficiency of the combined compressor system and the Carnot COP.

The efficiency of the compressor system can be approximated to roughly 50-70 %, 
which is a limiting factor of the productivity of heat pumps [45]. Furthermore the effi-
ciency of the compressor increases as the work load reaches full capacity. This is a 
common property of machine systems [47]. Typical COP values for residential heat 
pumps are approximately three [5] [34], dependent on the type of heat pump and the 
temperature differential between heat sink and heat source. This means that for one 
joule of electrical energy used in the electrical compressor three joules of thermal ener-
gy is moved from a cold environment to a warm environment. For a conventional resis-
tive electric heater the efficiency is 100 % since the applied electrical energy is convert-
ed to thermal energy for space heating as joule heating losses [48]. Thus heat pumps are 
more productive than traditional electric heaters.

It can be seen from the Carnot COP that the productivity of all types of heat pumps are 
very reliant on the temperature of the indoor environment and on the temperature at 
which heat is extracted from the outdoor surroundings. Heat pumps are most productive 
– and have the highest COP – when operating in warmer outdoor surroundings since the 
required work load is smaller. The COP decreases as the temperature of the outdoor 
environment decline and the work load increases [34]. In extremely cold weather the 
heat pump effectively works as a conventional electric heater. The COP of heat pumps 
with air heat sources is more temperature dependent than the COP of water source heat 
pumps. If the weather is cold and humid, moisture occurs when heat is extracted from 
the air. As a result of the cold temperatures the moisture freezes and causes the for-
mation of ice at the outdoor heat exchanger. The ice blocks the circulation of air and 
thereby prevents full functionality of the heat pump. In order to remove the ice, the heat 
pumps run defrosting cycles, which significantly reduces the COP. During a defrost 
cycle the direction of heat transfer reverses to move heat into the outside coils and melt 
the ice. To maintain the indoor temperature, the auxiliary immersion heater is activated 
meanwhile [49]. 
 
Unlike air source heat pumps, ground source heat pumps extracts heat from the ground 
and use the thermal storage capability of the soil. Dependent on the depth, the tempera-
ture in the ground soil is kept more or less constant throughout the year [39]. Therefore 
horizontal and vertical ground source heat pumps can achieve higher COP than air 
source heat pumps during cold periods. The burial depth is a factor of how good the 
storage capacity of the ground is. Since vertical heat pumps are buried deeper than hori-
zontal heat pumps, they can achieve the highest COP in cold periods. It has, however, 
been investigated how the thermal storage capacity of the ground soil can be depleted 
over time by prolonged use of ground source heat pumps. As a result, the COP during 
cold periods decline over the lifetime of especially vertical heat pumps [34]. Further-
more if the coils in either layout are placed close together, the amount of locally extract-
ed heat may cause the surrounding soil to freeze. This is known as permafrost. The frost 
acts as an insulator that reduces the heat potential and causes the COP to decrease. As 
the heat pump keep demanding that heat is collected, the permafrost gets worse and will 
eventually fully block the transfer of heat [50]. 
3.4 
Control strategies of heat pumps 
The basic thermal properties of the heat pump technology have now been studied. In 
this section, control strategies of heat pumps and thereby the influence of the associated 
power consumption on the power grid are investigated. The power consumption pattern 
of a heat pump is determined from the electrical energy input to the compressor unit. 
==PAGE:53==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Heat Pump Technology 
 
Page | 31 of 128 
The electrical compressor is driven by an induction machine that is connected to the 
power grid, and whose rotational speed is directly related to the grid frequency. The 
compressor can be controlled by two distinct operational strategies which determine the 
influence of the heat pumps on the power grid. The first and most traditional strategy is 
based on a two-state on/off relay, while the second strategy is based on frequency con-
trol by inverter technology. In the second strategy a frequency inverter is situated be-
tween the electric compressor of the heat pump and the power grid. 
3.4.1 
Two-state relay control 
Controlling a heat pump with an on/off relay limits the heat pump to operate either at 
full or at zero power. In contrast the heat demand in most residences varies considerably 
over time and does only rarely reach the full capacity of the heat pump. Therefore the 
compressor relay has to switch between states repeatedly to maintain a temperate indoor 
environment. In this type of control the induction machine is modulated by a duty cycle, 
which describes the active percentage of an on/off cycle. The modulation of the heat 
pump is determined by a permissible temperature band of a few degrees Celsius [43]. 
 
When a heat pump compressor is activated, the induction machine draws a very short 
but high magnetizing current and subsequently a lower current to accelerate the rotor. 
These currents and the acceleration of the rotor cause the windings in the induction ma-
chine to heat up. The compressor must establish an operational pressure difference be-
tween the evaporator and the condenser to control temperature and heat transfer as de-
scribed in section 3.2. Thus when the heat pump stops there exist a pressure difference, 
which requires a minimum off-period to equalize. Generally up to three on/off cycles 
per hour can be used. For each switching cycle there are power losses incurred in the 
heat pump as the windings heat up when reaching operational pressure. As a result of 
repeated switching the COP of relay controlled heat pumps is reduced [43] [51]. The 
number of switching cycles can be reduced with the use of a hot water tank, since there 
is a large amount of water to be heated in such tanks. In this way the change in water 
temperature is much less dynamic. 
 
If the minimum off-period is not maintained and there is an existing pressure in the heat 
pump when it is activated, the acceleration of the rotor is more difficult and will cause 
the windings to heat up quickly. This phenomenon is known as short cycling, and may 
cause the heat pump compressor to burn out [52]. Protection systems are incorporated to 
avoid short cycling by forcing the compressor to remain off for sufficient time to allow 
pressure equalization and to avoid critical temperatures by measuring the temperature of 
the windings and shut down the heat pump if the windings are dangerously warm [52].

3.4.2 
Frequency control 
Frequency control of a heat pump can be achieved by inverter technology in variable 
speed drives (VSD). A VSD can control the pressure and temperature of the electrical 
compressor continuously by adjusting the rotational speed up and down on the com-
pressor side of the induction machine. Consequently the heat pump can be driven at 
part-load and the pressure and temperature from the condenser can be controlled. VSDs 
are already used in large facilities, and are not a new invention. However, only in the 
past years, development has been given to implementing VSDs in small residential 
units. Such development occurs in connection with inverter technology becoming 
cheaper and available for small customers. By using an inverter to continuously control 
the heat pump’s output capacity the switching losses can be lowered since the number 
of switching cycles can be greatly reduced, and the initial surge of high input current 
can be avoided. However, there are additional losses associated with the active use of an 
inverter. The obtainable reduction in energy consumption using VSD has been meas-
ured to be 20-40% [34] [43]. 
 
In relation to this project it is interesting if heat pumps with VSDs become more popular 
on the residential market than heat pumps with relay control, since the continuous pow-
er balancing remarkably changes the power consumption pattern of the heat pumps. To 
increase the economic incentives for the individual household owner, the investment 
costs of the inverter technology in VSDs have to be weighed against the reduction in 
operational costs. If VSD heat pumps become popular in the residential heating sector it 
may cause the hourly power consumptions to change. In this case, it is necessary to 
measure new load curve data for heat pumps with VSDs and adjust the analysis of this 
project.

example of the power consumptions over an hour for both control strategies are shown

reality, the power consumption of frequency controlled heat pumps will vary up and 
down as the compressor adjusts for the heat demand. Small ramps on the power pulses 
from the relay controlled heat pump can be seen. This is contributable to the accelera-
tion of the rotor on the induction machine. For low capacity residential heat pumps this 
ramp will likely be so steep that it will not be noticeable.

In relation to the influence on the electrical power grid, it is observed that relay con-
trolled heat pumps can draw significant power in the periods they are active – see sec-

state can be very high compared to the traditional consumption for typical residential 
customers. In the summer period the demand for space heating is low, and water source 
heat pumps are only active for hot water production and weekly removal of Legionella 
bacteria. However due to the relay control, the peak power consumption is still very 
high and short bursts of high power demands may be seen on the feeder lines when heat 
pumps are activated. Between several customers the likelihood of coincident use will be 
low. In periods of cold temperatures the probability of coincident use is higher and may 
cause a considerable amount of power to be transported through the low voltage grid. In 
such periods the number of switching cycles is low, and the heat pumps may operate for 
hours at a time, which can cause congestions in the grid. For frequency controlled heat 
pumps the consumption of power is continuous depending on the heat demand. This can 
lessen the burden on the power grid in most hours of the year since heat pumps with 
VSD do not operate with peak power load during mild temperatures. In cold periods the 
consumption may almost reach full capacity and the same issues as for relay controlled 
heat pumps occur.

activated and the integrator is reset. The first step is active until either the temperature 
of the water flow reaches set point or the second step is required [53]. The compressors 
on newer heat pumps are dimensioned to be capable of supplying stand-alone space 
heating at outdoor temperatures down to −7℃ [54]. The immersion heaters are there-
fore not active for many hours each year and usually only a few minutes at a time, 
which limits the probability of coincident use. However, when the immersion heater is 
activated the power consumption increases dramatically. For dimensioning purposes 
this needs to be accounted for in the event of extreme cold temperatures.  
 
Another problem for the power grid in relation to heat pumps is a general phenomenon 
known as cold load pickup. For heat pumps the phenomenon occurs after power outages 
where the temperatures of all affected households have decreased below comfortable 
levels. When power is restored, all the heat pump units will activate their compressors 
and perhaps several stages of the immersion heaters in order to increase the indoor tem-
peratures [43]. This will result in an excessive demand for power which is likely to 
cause congestions in the power grid. One method to limit the severity of cold load 
pickup is to restrict the use of immersion heaters in the first hours after a considerable 
power outage, and allow the heat pump compressors to regulate the temperature slowly 
but at lower coincident power demand for the benefit of the power grid.

3.6 
Expectations 
As seen in section 3.5 the air/air heat pump is dominant on the residential heat pump 
market today. Excluding sanitary hot water heat pumps, the second and third most sold 
heat pumps are the water/water and air/water types.  
 
In single family households the heat pumps can be a suitable replacement for oil and gas 
heaters. This makes it likely that household owners will delay investments in heat pump 
technology until their existing form of heating is no longer functional. As a conse-
quence, there is a natural time limit on the number of heat pump installations per year 
even though heat pumps may be profitable to install as a replacement. Most holiday 
homes are not heated by oil or gas heaters, but by traditional electric heaters. This 
makes it easier and cheaper to convert early into air/air heat pumps. Therefore it is not 
unexpected that holiday homes will have the highest amount of installed heat pumps 
compared to single family households at first. 
 
Moving towards the aim to phase out oil and gas heaters it can be expected that wa-
ter/water and air/water heat pumps will be dominant in single family households that are 
replacing their old heaters. The choice between these two heat pumps will likely depend 
on the availability of sufficient ground area to install earth coils and the difference in 
investment costs. In combination with efforts to make heat pumps more attractive for 
household owners this will likely lead to an increase in the sales of these two types of 
heat pumps compared to previous years.

Data Protection Act 
As a part of the European Union’s Directive 95/46 EF, information of personal content 
is protected in Denmark according to the Danish Data Protection Act. The act was es-
tablished in 2000 and applies to all information which is stored electronically or in a 
registry and is traceable back to a specific physical individual [62] [63]. The act speci-
fies that personal data can only be collected with an approved warrant and briefing of 
the persons whom the data involves – referred to as the registered. Categorization of 
information sensitivity is defined in three levels; sensitive, semi-sensitive and non-
sensitive information. Based on the sensitivity level the warrant can be hard to obtain, 
and good arguments for the collection and use of the requested data is necessary. Only 
data that fits the purpose and arguments for which the warrant was granted can be col-
lected, and it can only be stored for as long as it serves that purpose. The registered has 
a number of rights for the collected data involving them [64]. 
 
 The right to be informed that data is going to be collected and for which purpose 
 The right to insight in the gathered information that is being processed 
 The right to protest against processing of the gathered information, and to pro-
test against the transfer of such data 
 The right to have incorrect or misleading data deleted or corrected

4.2.1 
Data cleansing 
Power consumption data have been collected from more than 100,000 traditional cus-
tomers and approximately 1,600 customers with heat pump loads. Occasionally the 
measurement readings of the energy consumption are incorrect and may be logged iden-
tically to the last correctly measured hourly consumption or as zero consumption. Due 
to uncertainty of the correctness in the data, it is chosen to apply two stages of data 
cleansing to remove suspicious time series from the data. The data cleansing stages are 
based on an algorithm, which searches for a given set of parameters and remove the 
time series that do not fulfill the requirements. The first stage searches through each 
time series, and apply the following four filtering parameters 
 
1. Number of hourly measurements equal to 8,760 
2. Positive yearly energy consumption and yearly peak power consumption 
3. Product of yearly peak power consumption and number of measurements should 
be larger than or equal to the yearly energy consumption 
4. Number of zero power measurements should be equal to or less than 1 % of the 
total number of measurements 
The first stage of the data cleansing removes time series that does not constitute an en-
tire set of data or which show incorrectly measured data. When the specific time series 
data have been filtered by the four parameters listed, the remaining dataset is further 
filtered. The second stage of the data cleansing searches through the overall customer 
categories 1210 and 1230. The filtering parameters in the second stage are 
 
1. The 0.01 % customers that have the highest yearly peak power consumption is 
removed 
2. The 0.01 % customers that have the highest total yearly energy consumption is 
removed 
The second stage of the data cleansing is used to sort out statistical outliers who have 
excessively large consumption within a specific hour or throughout the entire year. This 
is done to get a more representative estimate of the typical household consumption.

4.2.2 
Limitation of time resolution 
As previously stated, the readings of power consumption in the measurement data are 
made on hourly basis. The hourly measurements are made by continuously aggregating 
the energy used in the household and log the consumed energy once every hour. The 
power consumption during each hour is then found as the difference to the energy read-
ing of the preceding hour. The hourly time resolution poses an issue regarding the iden-
tifiable peak power and the actual peak power that is consumed. The issue occurs due to 
electrical appliances in households that have a high rated capacity, but which are not 
operated continuously for the most part of an hour. In such situations the peak power 
consumption will be obscured in the hourly readings of the household power consump-
tion. As an example an electric kettle can have a rated capacity of 2,400 W. When used 
to heat water e.g. for a hot beverage it only takes approximately three minutes. In this 
case the peak power consumption of the kettle will be 2,400 W, but the influence on the 
hourly measurement will be 120 
𝑊ℎ
ℎ. Another appliance with similar characteristic issue 
is a heat pump with supplementary immersion heater.  
 
In section 3.4 it was described how a conventional heat pump is operated in intervals by 
an on/off two-state relay. Furthermore it was described how an auxiliary immersion 
heater uses a significant amount of power in the short periods of time where it is active. 
The time resolution limitation is most severe in periods where the heat pumps and im-
mersion heaters only operate for short time, and less severe in periods where they are 
operated most of the day. Thus the limitation is not expected to be as severe in the cold 
winter season, where the heat pumps are likely to operate in many hours each day com-
pared to the summer season. However, the auxiliary heat supply from immersion heaters 
may not be active for large parts of an hour even in the winter period, and may therefore 
give a slightly underestimated impression on the yearly power peaks. In section 5.2 the 
use of immersion heaters in the data is investigated in detail.

4.2.3 
Data sampling 
The power consumption of individual customers can be hard to predict as it is depend-
ents on the specific activities of each day. However among a group of customers the 
combined behavior is more predictable because it will resemble a typical daily con-
sumption pattern on average. Therefore an accurate analysis of the power consumption 
in households can’t be based on individual data but should be based on data for a group 
of customers. 
 
After the cleansing of the measurement data there are many accepted customers remain-
ing. In order to reduce the computational time of the analysis, only a representative 
sample of customers belonging to each customer category will be used. When deciding 
on the sample size it is important to ensure that vital information in the data is kept by 
maintaining a sufficiently large sample group. Furthermore to be able to compare the 
results between the customer categories the sample groups should be of identical size. 
Thus the limiting size is set by the number of approved customers with heat pump 
loads. For the analysis it is chosen to make sample groups of 1,000 random customers 
from each category. For the traditional customers this is a significant selection process, 
whereas for the heat pump customers the sample selection only removes approximately 
200. The size of the sample groups are sufficiently large to provide statistical robustness 
in the dataset for customers with incorrect or extreme consumption that slipped through 
the data cleansing. These “bad” customers will be suppressed among the other custom-
ers that show more typical behavior. By analyzing groups of customers the natural vari-
ance that exists in power consumption is maintained while the statistical outliers are 
removed. These two sample groups will constitute the data basis for the analysis of typ-
ical consumption patterns within each category.

4.2.6 
Statistical treatment of data 
Before proceeding to the analysis of the power consumption data it is important to un-
derstand how the data is processed statistically in this project. The aim of the analysis is 
to determine a method for estimating the dimensioning peak power consumption of cus-
tomers on a random feeder line. For this purpose the data for the 1,000 customers in 
each customer category form the data basis for determining a statistical representation 
for the power consumption of such random customers. When working with power con-
sumption data, which has been measured directly at residences, it may contain extremi-
ties and power spikes. These spiking fluctuations occur as electrical appliances are 
turned on and off, which can have a sizeable impact on the power measurements for a 
single household depending on the appliances’ rated power and duration of usage. For 
individual households the fluctuations are random occurrences that can’t be predicted 
accurately. For an estimation method in power grid planning the randomness in demand 
is important to keep due to coincident demand between several households. However, 
heavily fluctuating and spiky curves are not suitable in the analysis of power consump-
tion. In order to circumvent this problem the measurement data has to be handled with 
such an approach that the extremities and spiky fluctuations does not influence the re-
sulting method. Two different techniques have been attempted throughout the project 
period to smooth the curves and to equalize the extremities in the data. The two tech-
niques are synthetic customers and quantile analysis. 
4.2.6.1 
Synthetic customers 
The first technique averages a small number of customers into a single theoretical syn-
thetic customer. Since the synthetic customers are based on data for a number of cus-
tomers, the extremities and spiky behaviors are attenuated depending on the number of 
real customers used to create a synthetic customer. The idea of the attenuation from

averaging customers into synthetic customers is that the obtained attenuation should 
reduce the spiking fluctuations while maintaining the statistical randomness of the pow-

In the first attempt the synthetic customers were made from the average time series of 
10 real customers, where the same customer was not averaged twice. It was found that 
important information of the random behavior and of the high consumption levels oc-
curring for some customers disappeared since the attenuation of the power curves was 
too high. As a consequence it was attempted to lower the group size continuously from

group size to two customers, the attenuation was still found to be too high. In the end it

tion without compromising with vital information on random consumption behavior. 
The technique was in the end rejected in favor of the quantile analysis technique used in 
the resulting method. 
4.2.6.2 
Quantile analysis 
The second technique involves applying quantiles on the customer data to remove the 
extremities and fluctuations found for individual customers. In this way, the random 
power consumption behavior is maintained for the benefit of the analysis of coincident 
power consumption in chapter 7. A quantile, or the quantile function, is a term com-
monly used in descriptive statistics. The quantile function is the inverse cumulative dis-
tribution function of a random variable X, while quantiles are values taken along the 
quantile function. The 𝑞𝑡ℎ quantile describes the value x where the probability of X be-
ing less than or equal to x is q [65].  
 
In other words, a quantile defines a boundary value of a given dataset below which a 
chosen share from 0 % to 100 % is situated. When calculating a quantile of e.g. 75 %, 
the data is initially sorted in ascending order. Subsequently the 𝐾𝑡ℎ observation is se-
lected as the boundary value, where 𝐾 is given as 0.75 × number of observations. Thus 
75 % of the observations have values that are equal to or lower than the boundary value 
of the 𝐾𝑡ℎ observation.

The understanding of the statistical treatment of the data is essential for discussing the 
procedure of the analysis, which is split into two parts. The first part investigates the 
power consumption of individual customers, while the second part investigates the ag-
gregated power consumption of a group of customers.  
 
The first part of the analysis serves to identify a representative yearly and daily power 
consumption pattern of a random customer. This will be used to determine which season 
of the year and which type of day is dimensioning – i.e. when the individual power con-
sumption is at its highest. In the first part it is therefore mostly the structure and the pat-
tern of the power consumption curve which is in focus. Here it is appropriate to study an 
average customer (Average ID) on an average day (Average day) such that the result is 
descriptive for any random sample of individual customers. In regards to the statistical 
treatment this is obtained by setting both TS and DS to the average values. 
 
The second part of the analysis seeks to determine the coincident peak power demand 
for a group of households on the same feeder line. This is done by considering the ag-
gregated power consumption for a number of customers. In this part the magnitude of 
the peak power consumption is in focus since it will be the determining factor in the 
estimation method. It is therefore not appropriate to study the average customer on an 
average day since it does not provide information on the dimensioning peak power dur-
ing a year. It is more appropriate to study a customer with high power consumption 
(Quantile ID) during a day where that customer’s consumption is high (Quantile day). 
This will result in a peak power which is associated with a worst case situation for the 
power grid. Regarding the statistical treatment this is obtained by setting both TS and 
DS to a quantile value. The percentages of the quantiles determine how close to the 
worst case situation the result will be.

5.1.1 
Yearly quantile intervals 
The power consumption between individual customers can be significantly different, 
which is an important aspect in power grid planning. This variation is reflected in the 
measurement data and must be taken into account. The yearly power consumption 
curves in Figure 5-1 only show the average yearly consumption and do not provide any 
information on the variation in power consumption between individual customers. To 
get an overview of the variation between customers, the quantiles of the yearly power 
consumption curves are investigated.  
 
The quantiles will be arranged to form bands that confine a certain share of the data 
population. The common approach for such an analysis would be to use confidence in-
tervals. The term confidence interval will for many statisticians refer to the determina-
tion of the interval by a specific equation. A confidence interval provides an interval 
estimate of a specific parameter based on a sample population. The limits of a confi-
dence interval describe the probability that a random sample from another population 
will lie within the bounds of the interval. However confidence intervals require that the 
population data is assumed to be approximately Gaussian distributed [65]. As shall be 
seen, this assumption is not applicable for the power consumption data, and it is not 
appropriate the use confidence intervals. Since the interval boundaries in this case are 
based on quantiles it will be slightly misleading to use the term confidence interval. 
Therefore in this report the term quantile interval (QI) will be used to denote a confi-
dence interval made from the quantiles of the data. The quantile intervals provide in-
formation on the share of data located within the boundaries of each interval. The upper 
==PAGE:78==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen

and lower limits are determined as the quantiles which are equally distributed on each 
side of the median (50 % quantile) of the data. As an example the 50 % quantile inter-
val for a population is confined by the 75 % quantile as the upper limit and the 25 % 
quantile as the lower limit, thus forming a 50 % interval in between.

5.2 
Immersion heaters 
In section 5.1 the yearly power consumption curves for customers with traditional heat-
ers and customers with heat pumps have been examined. It was found that the seasonal 
dependency of the power consumption for customers using heat pumps was twice as 
high as for customers with traditional heaters. It is unclear whether the auxiliary immer-
sion heaters have been used during the year of measurements and therefore if the related 
power consumption is contained in the measurement data. If this is not the case, the 
resulting method will not reflect the power consumption that may be related with the 
active use of the auxiliary heaters. The concept of immersion heaters as auxiliary heat 
supply in heat pumps was briefly introduced in chapter 3, where it was explained how 
the use of immersion heaters can cause very high consumption of power in rather short 
periods of time. Due to the hourly time resolution of the data, information on the use of 
immersion heaters can be difficult to extract from the combined power consumption. As 
an example, if an immersion heater is only used for 15 minutes during an hour it may 
appear identical to a situation where the heat pump itself is loaded slightly more over 
the hour.  
5.2.1 
Temperatures of 2011 
Immersion heaters are used in periods where the heat pump itself is not able to provide 
sufficient heating. As a result, the activation is based on several factors such as the type 
of heat pump, quality of thermal insulation of the house, air temperature and the tem-
perature setpoint for activation of the immersion heater. The air temperature is one of 
the important factors influencing the necessity to use the immersion heaters. At the 
same time it is the most varying factor among the listed, which makes it a determining 
factor for when auxiliary heating is required. Daily air temperatures for the winter 
months of 2011 are collected from the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) and 
shown in Figure 5-4 [66]. This is typically the coldest season of the year. From the tem-
perature data it appears that the winter of 2011 was only moderately cold. The lowest 
mean temperature measured across the country was −5 ℃ for five days in February. 
Local temperatures as low as −16 ℃ have been measured, but it is uncertain whether 
the location of the temperature measurements is consistent with the location from where 
the power consumption data was collected. When the air temperature decreases, the 
quality of thermal insulation for the household determine how quickly the temperature 
influences the indoor climate. Therefore the air temperature must be sufficiently low for 
an extended period of time before auxiliary heating is required. Based purely on the 
knowledge of the daily temperatures it is still uncertain whether immersion heaters were 
necessary during the winter of 2011 since the weather was only moderately cold.

customers with heat pumps. These will provide an overview of the structure of the daily 
power consumption for an average day. Additionally the customer data is analyzed to 
determine the period of the year where the daily power consumption can be expected to 
be at its highest for the average customer. The variation in daily power consumption 
between customers will also be studied for various periods of the year. 
 
The general structure of the power consumption curves for residential customers have 
been known for a long time. The power demand is typically moderate during the day 
hours from 08:00 to 16:00 where most people are working, and typically low at night 
from 23:00 to 06:00 when they sleep. During the morning from 06:00 to 08:00 the de-
mand increases slightly as breakfast is being cooked and people are getting ready to 
leave for work. The highest daily demand commonly occurs during the late afternoon 
and early evening in the hours from 16.00 – 20.00 when people return home from work 
and start turning on lights, electrical appliances and cooking dinner. During the evening

WEATHER INFLUENCE ON HEAT PUMPS 
In section 5.2 the relation between the power consumption from a heat pump related to 
the outdoor air temperature was briefly discussed. The weather has a large influence on 
the power consumption of a heat pump since it becomes increasingly difficult to extract 
heat from the outdoor surroundings when the effective temperature decreases. In this 
chapter the relation between the power consumption for heat pumps and the air tem-
perature is investigated in two parts. In the first part, the investigation is based on the 
measurement data from 2011 for the single family households with installed heat 
pumps, which is correlated to weekly weather data from DMI [68]. The use of weekly 
weather data limits the resolution of the relationship slightly in the first part of the in-
vestigation. In the second part, the investigation is based on heat pump power consump-
tion data and air temperature data from the project Styr Din Varmepumpe. Since the 
measurement setup introduced in section 5.2.4.1 is made to measure both the power 
consumption directly on the heat pump unit and the local air temperature on hourly ba-
sis, the second part of the analysis will be more accurate than the first part. 
6.1 
Temperature influence on weekly basis 
In the available measurement data from 2011, the traditional household power con-
sumption is not separated from the heat pump power consumption. Therefore it is not 
possible to exclusively compare the power consumed by the heat pumps to the variation 
in air temperature. An attempt to circumvent this issue can be made by taking the differ-
ence of the power consumptions for the two customer categories. In theory this differ-
ence in power consumption is only contributable to the power consumed by the heat 
pumps. However, since the customers in the two customer categories are not identical 
and due to natural variance in power consumption, this method can only provide a 
rough estimate. To obtain the weekly power consumption for each of the customer cate-
gories, the power consumption for an average customer is summed on a weekly basis. 
The weekly difference in power consumption between the two customer categories is 
shown in Figure 6-1.

As expected it is observed that the power consumption for customers with heat pumps is 
significantly higher than for customers with traditional heaters. The difference in power 
consumption between the two customer categories therefore have a similar pattern to the 
power consumption of the customers with heat pumps.  
 
Two different weather parameters describing the effective air temperature will be com-
pared to the estimated power consumption of heat pumps. The first parameter is the 
mean air temperature, and the second parameter is degree days. Degree days are a unit 
of measurement for the energy required for space heating in a certain period – day, 
week or year. The number of degree days for a day is determined as the difference be-
tween the average daily indoor and outdoor temperatures. It is commonly assumed that 
the desired average daily indoor temperature is 20-22℃. In the calculation of degree 
days the average daily indoor temperature is usually set to 17℃ to compensate for a 
3-5℃ heat addition from lighting etc. [69].

temperature for 2011. It is observed how the pattern of the two curves resembles, mean-
ing that the two parameters describe the same changes in effective temperature. The 
lowest weekly average air temperature measured in 2011 is −3℃. This consists with 
categorizing 2011 as having a moderate winter.

7.1.1 
Thermal overloading capacity of cables 
Though it is not the primary purpose in this project to give detailed explanations on the 
thermal overload capacity of power cables, a brief discussion on the subject will be pro-
vided in this section to understand why it is acceptable to estimate for the 3rd yearly 
power peak.  
 
All power cables are provided with a rated current. The rated current does not describe 
the absolute limit where the cable suddenly breaks down if an overcurrent is transferred. 
On the contrary, the rated current is based on a rated temperature of the cable conductor, 
which is usually 90℃ [70]. The rated current based on the rated temperature is calculat-
ed from the assumption that the current is continuous. It is therefore possible to transfer 
more than the rated current for a short time without any immediate damage to the cable. 
This is commonly referred to as overloading of a cable. The temperature of the cable 
conductor is influenced by the ambient temperature, and additional heat is generated by 
the transfer of current i.e. through joule heating [71]. The increase in temperature 
caused by the transfer of current is dependent on a thermal time constant, which is vary-
ing based on the type of cable, surroundings etc. Power cables are constantly affected by 
mechanical, chemical and electrical degradation. However, high temperatures can ac-
celerate the degradation processes considerably [71]. If the temperature becomes exces-
sively high, the cable will start to degrade rapidly which causes irreversible changes to 
the fundamental structure – especially the insulation – and significantly lowers the life-
time of the cable [71]. Since replacement of power cables is very costly, it is highly un-
desirable to unnecessarily reduce their operational lifetime. The rated temperature of 
power cables is selected at 90℃ due to a substantial increase in the temperature’s influ-
ence on the degradation processes above this temperature. Overload faults of power 
cables from exceeding the thermal capacities can occur as a consequence of high fault 
currents or by continuously transferring an overcurrent. 
 
In relation to the method of aggregation, it may not be necessary to dimension the low 
voltage cables to the absolute highest peaks of the year, since they may be occurring in 
two different days of the year such as one hour during Christmas and one hour during 
New Year’s Eve. The temperature increases caused by overloading the cables during 
these few very high peak hours in the year may not be significant enough to cause the 
cable temperature to exceed its rated value. This is of cause dependent on the prior load-
==PAGE:112==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Aggregated Power Consumption 
 
Page | 90 of 128 
ing level since the temperature is directly related to the earlier current transfer [72]. It 
should be noted that overheating of the cable can occur rapidly when the temperature 
equilibrium is exceeded, which occur when more heat is being generated than there is 
heat being removed. By utilizing the thermal capacity of the cables it is chosen to esti-
mate the aggregated peak power consumption for the 3rd yearly power peak. 
7.2 
Equalization of individual random power fluctuations 
When aggregating customers on the same feeder line there is not complete simultaneity 
between their individual power consumptions. In other words, the aggregated coincident 
peak power for two customers will be less than the sum of their individual peak powers 
since these are very unlikely to occur on the same day and in the same hour. In Figure 
7-2 the normalized aggregated daily power consumption per customer is shown for one 
to 20 aggregated customers. It is observed that as the number of aggregated customers 
increase, the combined power consumption per customer decreases. This is an indica-
tion that there is equalization of the power consumption between the customers. For the 
first few customers the degree of equalization is much higher than it is for larger num-
bers of customers. After approximately 10 customers any further addition of customers 
only changes the power consumption per customer slightly. The magnitude of the equal-
ization is dependent on the influence of the additional customer on the combined power 
consumption. Therefore the addition of a second customer will have a 50 % influence, 
while the third customer will have a 33 % influence, and so on. In this way the change 
in power consumption magnitude from an addition of customers above 20 aggregated 
customers will be less than 5 %. If there was complete simultaneity between all custom-
ers, the aggregated power consumption per customer would be equal for any number of 
aggregated customers. The equalization is also observed in Figure 7-3, which shows the 
percentage increase in power consumption as a function of the number of aggregated 
customers. Here it can be seen that the percentage increase in power consumption 
quickly diminishes as the number of aggregated customers increases from one through 
10. For five aggregated customers the increase in power consumption is roughly 20 %, 
whereas for 10 aggregated customers it is 10 %. At about 30 aggregated customers the 
increase in power consumption is only about 3 % per additional customer, which is in-
significant.  
 
The importance of these observations is that the variance in power consumption for dif-
ferent samples of only a few customers is very high in comparison to the variation in 
power consumption for samples of more than 10 customers on the same feeder line. 
This is a consequence of an increased equalization in the power consumption for higher 
number of customers contributing to the combined power consumption as observed

EVALUATION OF ESTIMATION METHOD 
A method for estimating the 3rd yearly power peak for a group of aggregated customers 
has been established. In this chapter the estimation method will be applied to power 
measurement data in order to evaluate its precision to correctly estimate the 3rd yearly 
power peak on average, based on information of the customers’ yearly energy consump-
tion. For simplicity, the newly determined estimation method for customers with heat 
pumps will be referred to as the heat pump load curve estimation method. 
8.1 
Holdout cross-validation 
In section 4.2.3 it was explained that approximately 200 time series for customers with 
heat pump loads were left out of the analyzed set of data. These remaining 200 meas-
urement time series will be used to evaluate the method. It is a common statistical ap-
proach to leave out a part of the measurement data for evaluation of a method that has 
been prepared from the rest of the data [74]. 
 
There exist several different evaluation methods in statistics. In the situation where only 
a single set of data is available, both for preparing a method and to evaluate it, the most 
typical method is the cross-validation technique. The principal idea of cross-validation 
is to avoid evaluating a method with the exact same data from which it was prepared. 
This would cause the evaluation results to be positively biased, since the data that has 
been used to prepare the method will always show better results than any set of uncon-
nected data. There are several variations of the cross-validation technique. In this pro-
ject the variation of the holdout method is used. This is a luxury variation, since it re-
quires a larger amount of data than the other variations. The holdout method is also the 
simplest of the variations. The principle of the holdout method is to divide the collected 
set of data into a training set and a testing set [74]. The training set is used to prepare 
the method, while the testing set is used to evaluate and verify the determined method.

Set of data used to prepare a statistical method

It is only possible to use the holdout method when the collected set of data is sufficient-

is available, a common division of the data is 80 % for the training set and 20 % for the 
testing set. This is roughly the division applied in this project for the customers with 
heat pumps, where 1,000 time series were used in the analysis and 200 time series were 
left out for evaluation. The accuracy of the holdout cross-validation method is depend-
ent on which data is used for the training set and which data is left out for the testing set 
[75].

A method to estimate the coincident power consumption for customers with heat pumps 
has been determined. The purpose of the method is to be a tool for screening the exist-
ing power grid to determine the number of customers on the same low voltage feeder 
line that can transition into heat pump heating systems before the feeder line will be 
unable to supply the demanded load. Therefore the estimation method can’t be used 
solely for customers with heat pumps but should also be applied for the other customer 
categories found on the same feeder line. This is required in order to find the combined 
coincident yearly peak power consumption on the feeder lines. 
 
The analysis presented in this report for customers with heat pumps will almost be iden-
tical for other customer categories. One of the most significant differences in the analy-
sis is that the power consumption profile for customers with heat pumps is not identical 
to the power consumption profile for other customer categories. Two examples are cus-
tomers with electrical vehicles for whom the power consumption profile is significantly 
different, and customers with photovoltaics for whom power is both generated and con-
sumed. The power consumption profiles can therefore be considerably different. Anoth-
er important difference is that the hour of daily peak power consumption for other cus-
tomer categories may be different from the peak hour determined for heat pump cus-
tomers. Therefore the estimation method must be made for all hours of the day for all 
customer categories. By adding the aggregated hourly power consumptions across all 
customer categories on the same feeder line, the combined aggregated power consump-
tion during all hours of a day can be determined as observed from the power grid. By 
comparing the combined aggregated power consumptions for all hours it will be possi-
ble to determine the hour of combined peak power consumption and thereby find the 
magnitude of the dimensioning aggregated power consumption.  
 
However, as previously discussed, there is an increased equalization in the power con-

APPLICATIONS OF ESTIMATION METHOD 
A suitable method of estimation for single family households with heat pump systems 
has been determined. In this chapter, considerations regarding the use of the method for 
screening purposes on the existing infrastructure of the power grid will be given.  
10.1 
Low voltage fuses in the power grid 
The application of the estimation method has primarily been focused on screening the 
loading of the power cables of the existing low voltage power grid. Such a screening 
process can be used to determine the parts of the power grid where problems may arise 
when residential customers living in single family households transition into heat pump 
heating systems. However, this is not the only limiting factor on a low voltage feeder 
line. Low voltage fuses in the residential power grid are installed at the origin of the 
feeder line, and on occasion between cables of different cross-sections. These low volt-
age fuses provide overload and short-circuit protection for cables and transformers. 
Since the fuses provide protection for the cables against high currents, the rated current 
of low voltage fuses are lower than for the cables [81]. As a result, the fuses will reach 
their rated currents before the cables. This is problematic since the low voltage fuses do 
not have the same thermal capacity properties as power cables, and therefore does not 
allow for overcurrents. Thus it is required to use the estimation method for screening the 
current ratings for the low voltage fuses in addition to those of the power cables. The 
determined method was chosen to accurately estimate the 3rd yearly peak power. A set 
of new coefficients for the estimation method must be determined for the 1st yearly peak 
power if it is to be used for low voltage fuses, which can’t allow for overcurrents. In this 
way, the screening process will indicate that more parts of the power grid are probable 
to show problems with the low voltage fuses than with the power cables.  
10.2 
Three phase current imbalance 
In the analysis of the estimation method it has implicitly been assumed that the power 
grid is operated as a perfectly balanced three phase system. In a perfectly balanced sys-
tem the current in the three phase conductors will be equal and there will not be any 
==PAGE:136==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Applications of Estimation Method 
 
Page | 114 of 128 
current in the neutral conductor. Since the power determined from the estimation meth-
od is a three phase quantity, it describes the required three phased power of a low volt-
age feeder line as equally divided between the three phases. In practice, the power con-
sumption from households, and the aggregated power consumption observed on a low 
voltage feeder line, is almost never perfectly balanced between the three phases. The 
severity of such phase current imbalances have been registered as high as a single phase 
being loaded roughly twice as much as the two other phases [82]. When DSOs apply the 
estimation method to screen their low voltage power grids to determine the required 
current ratings for the power cables and low voltage fuses, it is necessary to account for 
phase current imbalances. 
10.2.1 
Accounting for phase imbalances 
In order to account for phase current imbalances, the DSOs can set a limit to the amount 
of imbalance that they will permit in their power grids. The chosen limit can then be 
implemented in the screening process in two ways. One way is to reduce the rated cur-
rent values by the allowable limit for the low voltage fuses and power cables in the ex-
isting power grids prior to comparing with the estimated peak power from the estima-
tion method. This method of accounting for the imbalance can’t readily provide an indi-
cation of the absolute current difference between the corrected rated currents and the 
estimated load current since the rated currents have been reduced. Another, more practi-
cal solution, is to increase the estimated power from the estimation method with the 
allowable limit. In this way an estimate of the maximum allowable imbalanced currents 
in the conductors will be compared to the rated current values of the existing compo-
nents in the power grid and the difference in current is readily available. 
10.3 
Voltage variation 
The primary principle of the estimation method is to provide a tool for grid planning to 
account for the implementation of heat pump heating systems in the low voltage power 
grid. It has been seen that the method can be used to estimate the yearly peak power 
consumption on a feeder line with a number of aggregated customers. Such estimate can 
then be used to determine the required ratings of the low voltage fuses and power cables 
on a feeder line. The determination of the dimensions for low voltage fuses and power 
cables is one aspect in power grid planning.  
 
Another aspect in power grid planning is the decrease in voltage that occurs along a 
feeder line as a function of the increasing load caused by the transition into heat pumps. 
The use of the estimation method for this aspect of the power grid planning is compli-
cated. First the yearly peak power load on the feeder line must be determined with the 
==PAGE:137==
 
      
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen

estimation method. The estimated load can be distributed along the feeder line by sever-
al methods. One method would be to divide the estimated load among the households 
on the feeder line based on the size of their yearly energy consumptions. When the es-
timated load has been properly distributed on the feeder line, a load flow analysis must 
be made in order to determine the expected voltage drops that occur with the estimated 
yearly peak load. It is not the purpose in this project to provide detailed explanations of 
the use of load flow analysis, but it is not straightforward to make a load flow analysis 
using the estimated yearly peak load. Kirchhoff’s current law may not be theoretically 
fulfilled in situations where a low voltage feeder line branches into two or more seg-

CONCLUSION 
The purpose of this project was to determine a method for estimating the dimensioning 
coincident yearly peak power consumption on feeder lines with groups of residential 
customers with installed heat pump loads. It was chosen to focus the analysis on cus-
tomers living in single family households in urban areas.  
 
By investigating the basics of heat pump technology, the present heat pump market and 
expectations of future market changes it was possible to answer the question 
 
Which types and sizes of heat pumps are the most common 
on the residential market today, and which are expected to 
be installed in households in the future? 
 
It was found that the residential heat pump market is still in its early stages and only a 
limited number of heat pumps are installed in single family households at the present 
time. Market barriers such as high investment costs and high taxation on electricity must 
be lowered before the Danish heat pump market can develop and before significant 
numbers of heat pumps will be installed in single family households. In the present 
market air/air heat pumps with rated capacities in the range of 0-6 𝑘𝑊𝑡 was by far the 
most commonly installed type of heat pump. This is because air/air heat pumps are rela-
tively inexpensive compared to other types of heat pumps and has therefore been an 
ideal investment to replace electric heaters in holiday homes. For customers living in 
single family households water/water and air/water heat pumps were found to be prefer-
able as replacements for traditional oil and gas heaters. The typical rated capacity of 
these heat pumps were seen to be in the range of 0-16 𝑘𝑊𝑡 with an average rated ca-
pacity of 10 𝑘𝑊𝑡. On the present market these two types of heat pumps only represent a 
small part as the investment costs are very high. In the future market it is expected that 
more water/water and air/water heat pumps will be implemented in single family house-
holds if the market barriers are lowered and as old oil and gas heaters are phased out.

Conclusion

Power consumption measurements have been collected in order to find the differences 
in power consumption that arises as a result of transitioning into heat pump heating sys-
tems. The measurement data contained hourly power consumptions of an entire year for 
customers with traditional oil or gas heaters and for customers with heat pumps. By 
analyzing the data on a yearly basis it was found that customers with heat pumps have 
high and fluctuating yearly power consumption compared to customers with traditional 
heaters. Consequently customers with heat pumps were also observed to have larger 
variation between their individual power consumptions since the potential for variance 
is related to the magnitude of the general power consumption. By looking at duration 
load curves it was found that only a limited number of the analyzed customers had used 
auxiliary immersion heaters. Due to the limited usage, the immersion heaters did not 
cause any substantial coincident power consumption. Based on data from project Styr 
din Varmepumpe it was verified that heat pumps are very dependent on the effective air 
temperature. However, it was also determined that the temperatures during a typical 
Danish winter are not sufficiently low to cause significant coincident power consump-
tion from auxiliary immersion heaters. 
 
By analyzing the daily power consumption curve of an average customer it was possible 
to answer the question 
 
What is a representative power consumption pattern for 
residential customers with heat pump loads? 
 
It was found that a representative power consumption pattern for customers with heat 
pumps occurs during the winter season, where the power consumption was observed to 
be significantly higher than for the other three seasons of the year. There were no dis-
cernible differences in the peak power consumption between workdays and days off if 
the days of Christmas and New Year’s Eve were disregarded. A representative power 
consumption pattern was seen to be divided into three distinctive consumption periods. 
The power consumption was seen to be low during the night from 23:00 to 06:00, mod-
erate during the day from 08:00 to 16:00 and high during the evening from 16:00-20:00. 
During the morning from 06:00 to 08:00 the power consumption was seen to increase, 
while it was decreasing during the evening from 20:00 to 23:00. The daily power con-
sumption peak was found to occur in the hours 17:00 and 18:00 known as the cooking 
peak, when most people are cooking dinner and having lights and electrical appliances 
turned on.

Conclusion

The power consumption for individual customers was seen to include extremities and 
power spikes, which are statistical random occurrences that do not conform to the ex-
pectations of the average power consumption pattern. 
 
By investigating two approaches to remove the extremities and power spikes in the ag-
gregation of the power consumption data without compromising with the statistical ran-
domness of the individual power consumption, it was possible to answer the question 
 
How can the coincident peak power usage for neighboring 
households be expressed statistically? 
 
It was determined that the peak power consumption for customers on the same feeder 
line could be expressed by aggregating their individual power consumptions. The simul-
taneity in the power consumption data was taken into account by summing the custom-
ers’ individual time series. It was found that by using quantile analysis the extremities 
and power spikes were equalized in the aggregation process. Utilizing the thermal ca-
pacity of power cables it was possible to disregard the two most power consuming hours 
of the year, typically found during Christmas and New Year’s Eve. It was achieved to 
focus the aggregation process on the 3rd yearly peak power consumption on average by 
setting both the time series and daily series computations to the 90 % quantile. 
 
By answering the three questions, it has been possible to solve the overall project thesis 
 
Determine a statistical method to estimate the coincident 
peak power consumption for households in urban areas with 
installed heat pumps. 
 
Using the statistical aggregation process, the aggregated power consumption was de-
termined as a function of the number of customers on the same feeder line with 24 hour 
time resolution. It was found that the aggregated power consumption was linearly de-
pendent for more than 10 customers on the same feeder line. For less than 10 customers 
the aggregated power consumption was slightly deviating from a linear dependency. In 
situations where a small number of customers are present on the same feeder line, the 
predictability of the aggregated power consumption was found to be worse than for 
large numbers of customers. A linear approximation of the aggregated power consump-
tion was accepted despite the aggregated power consumption not being completely line-
ar. With the assumption that the smallest cable cross-section installed in the low voltage 
power grid is 50 𝑚𝑚2 𝐶𝑢 or 𝐴𝑙, the application range of the estimation method was 
limited to more than five customers on the same feeder line. 
==PAGE:142==
 
 
 
Mathias Christoffersen 
 
Conclusion 
 
Page | 120 of 128 
 
An evaluation of the estimation method was performed from a separate set of power 
consumption measurements. The evaluation results of the method were compared to the 
results obtained by using the alternative Velander method for customers with traditional 
heaters and customers with electric heaters. It was observed that the estimation method 
determined in this project was more accurate than the existing Velander method, and 
that it was most precise for the 3rd yearly peak power consumption. The accuracy of the 
method was constrained by the natural variation in the power consumption data due to 
its simplicity of only accounting for the customers’ yearly energy consumption. It was 
found that the method should be more complex in order to achieve more accurate esti-
mations.  
 
Based on quantile analysis of available hourly power consumption measurements and a 
linear approximation of the aggregated power consumption for customers with heat 
pumps, an accurate statistical estimation method for low voltage grid planning has been 
established.  
 
It must be noted that the determined estimation method is largely sensitive to the choice 
of quantiles in the aggregation process. The method determined in the project is there-
fore only valid for estimating the 3rd yearly peak power consumption with the given 
choice of quantiles.

Conclusion 
 
Page | 121 of 128 
11.1 
Future work 
This section will list certain aspects that may be interesting to investigate in relation to 
the determined estimation method but that have not been included in the scope of this 
project due to the limited time set aside for the project. Further work on the estimation 
method may take the following aspects into consideration. 
 
 Analyzes should be conducted for other customer categories to obtain estimation 
equations for all categories of customers that exist on low voltage feeder lines. 
This is required to calculate the aggregated power consumption for a feeder line 
with customers of different categories. 
 
 Estimation equations for all hours of the day should be determined. These will 
be useful when determining the aggregated power consumption for a feeder line 
with multiple customer categories, where the yearly peak power consumptions 
does not necessarily all occur at 17:00. 
 
 A suitable method to aggregate the power consumption for a feeder line with 
customers of different categories should be investigated. Simply adding the 
yearly peak power consumptions from the estimation equations will not consider 
the simultaneity in the power consumption for customers of different categories. 
 
 Estimation equations for dimensioning purposes should be found. The estima-
tion equation resulting from this analysis is useful for screening purposes of the 
existing power grid. When applied for dimensioning the method should estimate 
the 1st yearly peak power consumption, and have a low risk of underestimating. 
By changing the quantiles for the time series and daily series computations in 
the aggregation method it is possible to change the coefficients of the estimation 
equation making it useful for dimensioning purposes.  
 
 If power consumption measurements with higher time resolution than hourly ba-
sis become available, the analysis should be updated since new information on 
the yearly peak power consumptions can be found. 
 
 A more detailed study of temperatures recorded in Denmark should be made to 
determine the risk for high coincident power demand from immersion heaters. 
 
 The use of additional input parameters to the estimation method should be 
looked into in order to achieve a more accurate result.

Polynomial Regression for Aggregated Customers 
 
Page | D-1 of D-4 
D   
POLYNOMIAL REGRESSION FOR AGGREGATED 
CUSTOMERS 
In chapter 7 it was seen that the chosen approach to account for the nonlinear behavior 
of the aggregated power consumption for a small group of customers was to use a par-
tial linear approximation. During the project period, another approach using both a line-
ar and a polynomial approximation was studied. The principal ideas of this approach are 
given in this appendix chapter.  
 
The aggregated power consumption for up to 10 customers on the same feeder line was 
observed to be slightly nonlinear. For groups of more than 10 customers, the aggregated 
power consumption is roughly linear. The partial linear approximation overestimates the 
power consumption for a small group of customers. However, by dividing the approxi-
mation of the aggregated power consumption into a polynomial part and a linear part, it 
is possible to estimate the power consumption of a small group of customers more accu-
rately. Due to the slightly nonlinear behavior, a 2nd degree polynomial will provide a 
suitable approximation. In Figure D-1a the aggregated power consumption is approxi-
mated with a 2nd degree approximation from 1-10 aggregated customers and a linear 
approximation from 10-100 aggregated customers. There are two specific points of in-
terest when applying two different approximations for the aggregated power consump-
tion. The first point of interest is the size of the deviation from the 2nd degree polynomi-
al approximation to the aggregated power consumption for a single customer. The devi-
ation at this point will be the reference for the accuracy of the approximation for small 
groups of aggregated customers. The second point of interest is the deviation between 
the polynomial and the linear approximations at the point where the type of approxima-
tion changes. This point will be referred to as the crossover point. If there is a signifi-
cant deviation between the two approximations, then the resulting two estimations will 
be different for the same number of aggregated customers. A closer investigation of 
these two points of interest is made in Figure D-1b. It is observed that the deviation of 
the approximation at a single customer is negligible, whereas there is an obvious devia-
tion between the two approximations at the point of crossover.

It is investigated how the placement of the point of crossover affects the deviations at 
the two points of interest. The two point of interest is studied in Figure D-2 for a cross-
over point placed at 10, 20, 30 and 40 aggregated customers.  
 
The deviation between the polynomial and linear approximations at the crossover point 
is observed to become smaller as the point is moved to a larger group of aggregated 
customers. This is because the linear approximation is appreciably more accurate for 
larger groups of aggregated customers, while the polynomial approximation only be-
comes slightly less accurate. On the other hand, the deviation from the polynomial ap-
proximation to the aggregated power consumption for a single customer increases, as 
the point of crossover is moved to a large group of aggregated customers. When the 
polynomial approximation has to fit to a larger portion of the aggregated power con-
sumption, it attempts to approximate a larger part of the linear behavior. Therefore the 
polynomial approximation comprise with the accuracy for small groups of aggregated 
customers who behave nonlinearly, to gain a suitable accuracy for larger groups of cus-
tomers who behave more linearly.

Table D-1: Deviations of polynomial and linear approximations for two points of 
interest at varying crossover point. 
It is seen that the best compromise is found for a crossover point at 30 aggregated cus-
tomers, since it provides a good accuracy for both points of interest. To further increase 
the precision of the estimation, the method can use the average of the two approxima-
tions at the point of crossover since it appears that they are placed on either side of the 
aggregated power consumption.  
 
The increased accuracy obtained for small groups of customers on the same feeder line 
by using both a polynomial and a linear approximation is helpful if the estimation meth-
od is used for dimensioning purposes instead of screening. When dimensioning a power 
grid with multiple customer categories it may be required to provide an accurate esti-
mate for a small group of customers belonging to one of the categories. In this case the 
method of using two approximations in the estimation method is preferred. If the meth-
od is implemented in an estimation program, it is possible to simply evaluate the num-
ber of customers of the same category on the analyzed feeder line and choose the asso-
ciated equation of approximation with an if-statement.
