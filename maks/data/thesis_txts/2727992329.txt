Abstract
Scale-up of tablets from research to production scale still represents a challenge in the pharmaceu-
tical industry. A better understanding of the factors affecting this transfer would make possible the
better prediction of tablets’ properties manufactured at production scale using high speed rotary
tablet presses, based on the results from small research scale tablet presses.
This project aims to evaluate the effect of speed, tablet geometry and pre-compression during scale-
up of tablets. For doing so, the effect of these parameters is studied individually for each tablet
press, and also used to compare tablets manufactured by a single punch tablet press and a rotary
tablet press. Moreover, a compaction simulator is also employed to produce tablets mimicking both
tablet presses, being the main objective to determine if this tablet press could be used in the future
to predict the properties of tablets manufactured by this two types of tablet presses. Furthermore,
different characterisation techniques are employed, being especially relevant the use of Computed
Tomography to asses tablet’s porosity.
The results obtained could be used in the future to better evaluate the effect of speed, tablet ge-
ometry and pre-compression in the scale-up of tablets. It was observed that punch speed highly
affects the tensile strength of tablets, and a lower speed leads to higher values when employing
both machines, single punch and rotary tablet press. Furthermore, punch speed seems to be the
explanation to the higher tensile strength of tablets manufactured by the single punch tablet press,
when tablets manufactured by both machines are compared and same conditions are tested. A
higher deformation of components with plastic behavior, and a longer relaxation time is likely to
be the cause of this variation. Moreover, it was also observed that geometry of the tablets affects
the tensile strength, and convex tablets show higher values when analyzing tablets manufactured
by both, single punch and rotary tablet press. This variation could be caused by differences in
density distribution, as density distribution within convex tablets is less uniform compared to flat
ones, leading to higher stored stress within the tablets.
Compaction simulator was able to mimic the rotary tablet press with high accuracy. However, it
was observed that the effects of pre-compression using this type of machine can be challenging
to evaluate. In the case of simulating the single punch tablet press, the same cycle could not be
used, and consequently new experiments should be performed in the future mimicking the exact
same cycle. Furthermore, the porosity analysis obtained by using Computed tomography images
support the use of this technique for new applications regarding tablet characterisation. However,
further research is needed to evaluate this property with higher accuracy.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Scale up of tablets from research to production scale presents challenges related to the quality of
tablets when production increases. Predicting the properties of tablets during production based on
research-scale results is a challenging task. During research, small single punch tablet presses are
usually employed, which implies the use of a small amount of material and lower speeds. However,
when tablets are produced in a production stage, rotary tablet presses with the ability to produce
hundreds of tablets per minute are employed.
Oral route is the preferred route of administration, being tablets the main representative of this
route. However, numerous challenges arise when formulating certain drugs as solid dosage forms.
One of the challenges associated with tablet manufacturing is ensuring accurate prediction of the
properties of tablets based on early development results. During this project, factors affecting the
quality of tablets during their transfer from research to production scale are studied. In particular,
the study focuses on examining the impact of speed, tablet geometry, and pre-compression.
Moreover, assessment of certain properties, such as porosity, represents a challenge during the
development of tablets. The development of new techniques, using the latest technologies, such as
image analysis methods, are needed to elucidate how porosity could be evaluated with accuracy in
the future.
Overall, this project aims to determine which factors affect tablet properties in a higher extent
during scale up in tablet production. Furthermore, the correct evaluation of tablet properties is also
crucial. Prediction of these properties in the production stage based on small-scale experiments
is very important in order to ensure the successful development of various therapeutics in tablet
form.
1.2
Hypothesis
The hypothesis of this project is that single punch tablet press Korsch would be used to predict
properties of tablets manufactured by rotary tablet press Fette. By identifying the parameters
that influence this transfer, it would become feasible to manufacture tablets using a single punch
tablet press with the same properties as those produced on a larger scale with a rotary tablet press.
Moreover, a Compaction Simulator (CS), would also be employed to anticipate tablet’s properties
by mimicking the tablet press of interest.
Furthermore, the application of Computed Tomography (CT) would aid in determining the porosity
1
==PAGE:11==
Irene Rico Gomez
of tablets, and this investigated method would be very valuable for addressing the current challenge
assessing this property.
1.3
Objectives
The aim of this project is to determine how to predict the properties of tablets manufactured during
production stage based on results from research scale. Subsequently, various objectives are defined:
1. Predict tablet’s properties manufactured by rotary tablet press Fette based on results from
single punch tablet press Korsch.
2. Assess the potential of Compaction Simulator (CS) Styl’One, for mimicking the tablet presses
Korsch and Fette.
3. Determine which factors among those investigated, speed, tablet geometry, and pre-compression,
contribute to increased tablet variations when producing tablets using different tablet presses.
4. Evaluate Computed Tomography (CT) as a method for determining the porosity of tablets.
5. Study main characterisation techniques and determine which ones provide the most relevant
information necessary for determining tablet properties.

State-of-the-art
2.1
Drug discovery and development
It is estimated that every marketed drug requires an initial examination of over 100,000 candidates,
and around 90% fail after clinical trials [1][2]. The development of a new drug, from initial dis-
covery to approval, is estimated to be 10-15 years, and the average cost for each new drug is $1 to
2 billion [1]. The whole process can be divided into different stages. Figure 1 shows these stages
along with the success rate and years needed for the development of each of them.
Figure 1: Drug discovery and development process, including years and success rate for each
phase. Created with Biorender.com [3].
The first one, drug discovery, consists in target discovery, lead discovery, and lead optimization.
The main objectives are the identification of multiple compounds that module the activity of the
target of interest, the establishment of a set of related compounds with the desired activity, and the
optimization of the selected compound [1].
Next step involves preclinical studies for demonstration of the efficacy and safety of the compound.
This involves toxicology and pharmacology studies both in vitro and in vivo, using appropriate
animals. Next, the submission of an Investigational New Drug (IND) application takes place,
requesting the permission for starting clinical trials in humans [4]. During phase I, safety and
tolerability of the new drug is studied in a small number of healthy individuals (20-80), and the
right dosage is determined. Next, phase II is undergone. During this phase, drug is administered to
more individuals (100-300), and the main aim is to evaluate the desired therapeutic effects. Phase
III involves hundreds to thousands of patients, and the main aim is to determine the efficacy of
the new candidate, as well as possible adverse side effects. Moreover, during Phase III different
populations are studied, and results are compared to other treatment approaches. This phase is the
3
==PAGE:13==
Irene Rico Gomez
most expensive, as it requires more time and more individuals are tested [5]. Upon completion of
clinical trials, a New Drug Application (NDA) is submitted to the appropriate regulatory body. A
phase IV is usually required for analysing rare and long-term adverse effects, which can lead to
changes in labeling, contraindication for the co-administration of the new drug with other drugs,
or in the worst case scenario, the withdrawal from the market [1] [5].
Advancing a drug to clinical trials is a great achievement, but only 10% of drugs are considered
after this phase. Some of the reasons for this high failure rate are lack of clinical efficacy, un-
manageable toxicity, poor drug-like properties, and insufficient market demand and inadequate
strategic planning [6] [7]. Moreover, it is worth noting that this percentage of success refers only
to drugs that reach clinical trials. Consequently, if pre-clinical stage is also considered the fail-
ure rate is higher than 90% [8]. Figure 1 shows the success rates for each of the stages in drug
development. Data for success rate corresponds to a study including 798 drug discovery projects
developed between 1991 and 2015 at 36 academic institutions in the United States [3].
2.2
Oral route for drug administration
Oral drug delivery is the preferred method for administering therapeutics, with high patient accep-
tance and long term compliance, increasing the therapeutic value of the drug [9]. The oral route
presents many advantages that makes the administration of drugs though this route very desirable.
These benefits encompass the alleviation of pain and discomfort typically linked to injections, as
well as the prevention of potential infections resulting from improper needle usage or reuse. Ad-
ditionally, oral formulations prove to be more cost-effective to produce due to the absence of a
requirement for sterile manufacturing conditions [10].
However, oral route also presents challenges. One of the biggest problems is low bioavailability,
caused by poor solubility, slow dissolution rate, and low permeability, among other reasons [11].
Besides physicochemical barriers, there are also biological ones that affect the drug absorption
in the gastrointestinal tract (GIT). Oral drugs need to be soluble in gastric fluid so they can be
absorbed in the stomach, the small intestine or the colon. Moreover, the challenges in drug absorp-
tion do not only include barriers in the GIT, but they also include hepatic barriers once they are
absorbed, known as first-pass metabolism [12].
Besides these challenges, the main factors supporting the selection of the oral route are patient
adherence and cost-effectiveness, as previously mentioned. According to a report of the World
Health Organization (WHO), maintaining consistent adherence to long-term therapy is a global
problem, with developed countries experiencing an average adherence rate of approximately 50%
[13]. Consequently, roughly 60% of commercially available small-molecule drug products are
administered orally. Oral formulations make up approximately 90% of the total market share
4
==PAGE:14==
Irene Rico Gomez
for pharmaceutical formulations intended for human use worldwide [14]. Nevertheless, orally
administered drugs can also suffer from low patient adherence. This is particularly relevant when
treatment is intended to the elderly or children, being difficulties to swallow one of the reasons
for the low adherence [15]. Consequently, it is very important to manufacture tablets with an
appropriate shape and size. However, it is important not to compromise the quality of tablets, as
this would change the effectiveness and safety.
2.2.1
Peptides and proteins
Proteins and peptides play a crucial role in a great variety of processes that are vital for sustaining
life. Additionally, proteins and peptides are implicated in pathological conditions like cancer and
diabetes. Consequently, the use of proteins and peptides as therapeutic agents is an appealing
strategy to combat a multitude of diseases [16]. The main difference between peptides and proteins
is size and structure. Peptides are smaller than proteins and consist of between 2 and 50 amino
acids, while proteins are made up of 50 or more amino acids. Moreover, peptides’ structure is less
defined than proteins’ one, which adopt secondary, tertiary, and quaternary conformations [17].
The first therapeutic peptides were natural hormones, such as insulin. The discovery and develop-
ment of this hormone, a peptide with 51 amino acids, was a great achievement in drug discovery
[18]. It was isolated by Frederick Banting in 1921 and further developed by Frederick and Charles
Best [19], becoming in 1923 the first commercial peptide drug. More peptide hormones were de-
veloped from the 1950s to the 1990s. In the 21st century the discovery of new technologies have
led to the design of peptides with desirable biochemical and physiological activities [18]. A total
of 33 non-insulin peptide drugs have been approved since 2000, accounting for more than $70
billion in 2019 [20]. According to Njardarson et al. [20] the top three peptide drugs according
to sales were all GLP-1 analogues, indicated for treating Type 2 Diabetes, including dulaglutide,
liraglutide, and semaglutide.
Therapeutic proteins include monoclonal antibodies and fusion proteins. These type of compounds
are complex and cannot be fully synthesized through chemical processes alone, requiring living
cells or organisms to produce them [21] [22]. This also implies complex purification processes
[23]. The incorporation of innovative strategies and methodologies to alter protein-based pharma-
ceutical products has led to the development of several protein-engineering platform technologies,
employed to enhance the circulating half-life, targeting, and functionality of emerging therapeutic
protein drugs, while increasing purity [24] [25]. Since 2011, 62 therapeutic proteins have been
approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA), being half of them monoclonal anti-
bodies. Coagulation factors were the next largest class, followed by replacement enzymes. These
products are indicated for different therapeutic areas, being oncology and hematology the primary
5
==PAGE:15==
Irene Rico Gomez
ones [26].
The parental route is the preferred route for delivery of peptides and proteins. However, as pre-
viously mentioned patient adherence via parental routes is typically lower than adherence to oral
treatment, significantly limiting the therapeutic efficacy of the drugs, especially for conditions like
diabetes [10]. The development of oral delivery systems for peptides and proteins has posed a
persistent obstacle for pharmaceutical scientists, mainly due to the numerous unfavorable physic-
ochemical properties exhibited by these molecules. These properties include large molecular size,
vulnerability to enzymatic degradation, short plasma half-life, ion permeability, immunogenicity,
and a propensity for aggregation, adsorption, and denaturation [10] [27]. As a result, the absolute
oral bioavailability of the majority of peptides and proteins is less than 1%, being the primary
challenge enhancing the oral bioavailability to a minimum of 30-50% [10].
Significant advancements have been achieved in recent years regarding the development of inno-
vative technologies aimed at enhancing absorption of peptides and proteins across the GIT. Co-
formulation with permeation enhancers, such as sodium N-[8-(2-hydroxybenzoyl amino]caprylate
(SNAC), has been approved. The main effect of SNAC is preventing the destruction in the stomach
by decreasing the activity of digestive enzymes [28]. Furthermore, other permeation enhancers,
enzyme inhibitors, and hydrogels, are now under clinical trials to evaluate its efficacy with other
peptide drugs, such as octreotide and insulin [29] [30].
2.3
Tablet formulation strategies
Drug formulation involves combining the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) with different
inactive substances, called excipients, and selecting the best form of delivery [31]. During pre-
formulation studies chemical, physical, and mechanical properties of the drug are determined, with
the objective of selecting the most appropriate inactive ingredients. Moreover, these studies also
evaluate the candidate drug and potential excipients before developing a final formulation [32].
Different formulations are possible, and although solid dosage forms present many advantages,
and they are the final form for many drugs, it usually happens that at the beginning simpler formu-
lations, such as oral solutions, are selected [33]. Properties of the API and excipients are used for
determining the route of administration. In the case of selecting the oral route, solid dosage formu-
lations are usually developed. Tablets and capsules are the most common solid dosage forms [34].
In fact, 70% of all medication is administered as tablets, that range from simple, immediate-release
dosage forms that rapidly disintegrate and release drug in the GIT, to more complex modified re-
lease tablets. Figure 2 shows the main types of solid dosage forms [32], according to dosage form
and drug release mechanism.

2.3.1
Excipients
Tablets must resist operations such as packing, transport and patient handling, but they also need to
disintegrate and dissolve after been taken by the patient. Tablets consist of an API and excipients,
inactive compounds that are mixed with the API. Excipients are selected depending on the final
dosage form, and compatibility with the drug must be considered [35]. Main types of excipients
are:
• Filler/Diluent
Increase the bulk of the formulation, which is specially important when dealing with low-
dose drugs. A good filler will have good compactability and flow properties, creating ap-
propriate compression characteristics for tablet manufacturing [36]. Selection of the diluent
depend on processing, plasticity of materials and solubility of the drug. Common diluents
are Dibasic calcium phosphate (DCP), kaolin, lactose, mannitol, microcrystalline cellulose
(MCC), sorbitol, and starch [35]. Microcrystalline cellulose (MCC) is commonly used as a
diluent for direct compression, as it shows good flow and compaction properties [32].
• Binder
A material with a high bonding ability, able of increasing the mechanical strength of the
7
==PAGE:17==
Irene Rico Gomez
tablet [37]. A binder is usually a ductile material that undergoes plastic deformation. These
materials undergo plastic deformation during compression, filling the interparticular voids
and creating bonds. As a consequence, tablet porosity decreases, and tensile strength in-
creases [38]. If powder mixture is granulated before compression, this is done by adding
water or an organic solvent. Binders are added as a solution in the granulating fluid [35].
• Disintegrant
Tablets must desintegrate after been taken, ensuring dissolution of the API. Disintegrants are
included in the formulation for overcoming the strength produced during the compression
process [39]. Some disintegrants commonly used are Alginic acid, carboxymethylcellulose
calcium, MCC, polacrilin potassium, sodium alginate, and sodium starch [32].
• Lubricant
Required for increasing the free flow of powders and prevent manufacturing disorders. There
are three types: glidants enhance flow properties; antiadherent excipients reduce friction be-
tween the tablet punch faces and tablet punches; and die wall lubricant excipients reduce
friction between the tablet surface and the die wall during and after compaction by forming
a thin solid film at the interface of the die and the tablet, enabling easy ejection of the tablet
[40]. When including disintegrant and lubricant, the disintegrant must be blended with gran-
ules before the addition of the lubricant for minimizing the risk of a hydrophobic film around
the disintegrant [41]. Calcium stearate, magnesium stearate, mineral oil and stearic acid are
common lubricants [35].
• Coating materials
Coating is used for protection of the drug from the environment increasing stability, taste
masking, minimizing patient contact with drug substance, improving product identity and
appearance, and ease of swallowing [42]. It can also be useful for modifying drug release.
The main coating methods are sugar coating, film coating, and compression coating. Sugar
coating has been the most common method and involves coating with sucrose. This results in
an increase of the tablet weight and size. Film coating involves application of a polymer film
to the surface of the tablet. This method does not result in size increase. Last method, com-
pression coating, makes multilayered tablets by an initial compaction followed by additional
fill material and compression [32][35].
• Flavor, Sweetener, and Colorant Flavor
Primarly used to change or mask the taste of the drug. Its main purpose is enhancing patient
compliance. Coloring tablets can also improve tablet identification, especially when patients
are taking a number of different tablets [32].

2.4
Tablet presses
Tablet production systems include tableting machines for production and research as well as tablet-
ing machine simulators, mainly used for research as they allow precise and well adjustable mea-
surements during tableting with only a small amount of material [35].
Tablet machines operate differently and can produce between one and a million tablets per hour,
but the principle of manufacture remains the same. Powder is filled to a specified depth in a die
and compressed between two punches. The compression force is ended by removal of the upper
punch, and the lower punch then moves upward in the die to eject the tablet [43]. The main steps
for tablet compression are [43]:
• Filling: Transfer of powder from hopper to the die cavity, where it is compressed. This
cavity is formed by the die and a lower punch responsible for the cavity´s volume.
• Metering: Excess granules are removed. The right volume is determined by controlling the
position of the lower punch in the die, which is determined by the metering cam, or weight
control unit.
• Compression: During this stage the punches come together to form the tablet. The com-
pression rolls push the punches towards the die where compression happens. Thickness and
hardness of the tablet is determined by the distance between the punches.
• Ejection: This last step involves the removal of the tablet from the die cavity. The upper
punch rises, followed by the lower punch that pushes the tablet to the surface and out of the
cavity.

amounts of material are needed, which is very important during early stages of development. The
compression profile obtained for an specific formulation using this kind of machine usually differs
from the one obtained when same formulation is compressed in a rotary machine. This kind of
machines has a filling shoe which moves back and forth for filling the die cavity, and pushes the
tablet once is finished [35]. Figure 3 shows how these machines operate.

2.5
Direct compression
The processes of manufacturing tablets using wet and dry granulation methods involve multiple
stages, essential for transforming formulation components into a compressible state suitable for
tablet production. However, if a primary formulation component already exhibits the necessary
level of fluidity and compressibility, the granulation step may be unnecessary. In these cases,
direct compression is used. A diluent with good flow and compaction properties is required for
direct compression [49].
During direct compression, powder is compressed using rigid punches in tableting machines. Par-
ticle rearrangement occurs under a compressive force until further densification is not possible
without deformation. In the case further compression is applied, particles will deform elastically
and density of powder will increase. After surpassing the elastic limit, plastic deformation or brit-
tle fracture will happen. In both cases, the particles form bonds required for the cohesion and
compactability of the tablet [35].
Compression of powder is described by plotting compaction pressure, density and tensile strength.
Compressibility plots describe how increasing compaction pressure results in increasing solid frac-
tion of the tablet. A solid fraction of 1 represents a totally solid material. Other plots include
compactability plots, which shows solid fraction versus tensile strength, and tabletability plots,
showing compaction pressure versus tensile strength. The last type of plots, show the tablet´s
cracking point, which happens when increasing pressure does not result in an increase in tensile
strength, and cracking occurs [35]. Tabletability plots are very important when compaction pres-
sures are determined in a study, as it is important to reach a plateau face where no changes in
tensile strength are observed if compaction pressure increases. The solid fraction at this point is
around 0.85, which means a porosity of 15% [49].
2.6
Tablet compaction and compression
Tablets consist in powder that has been compressed using a tablet press. Two processes take place
simultaneously when the tablet is made, compaction and compression [50].
14
==PAGE:24==
Irene Rico Gomez
Compression consists in the decrease of the overall volume of a powder, primarily caused by
the displacement of air present between particles. This process leads to a reduction in the void
space among solid particles, resulting in a decrease in the porosity of a tablet. Compaction can be
defined as an increase in the mechanical strength of a powder, primarily due to the consolidation of
particles. Consequently, compaction exhibits a strong correlation with particle consolidation and
bonding, exerting a direct influence on tablet hardness and friability [35][50].
Particle rearrangement occurs during the compaction process until a point is reached where further
densification cannot be achieved without particle deformation. If further compression is applied,
the particles will deform to accommodate the induced stresses. Regarding the nature of the materi-
als, different phenomena occur. Brittle materials undergo fragmentation, resulting in the formation
of fine particles that will contribute to secondary packing. On the other hand, plastic materials
will distort to occupy voids and may also exhibit void filling through percolation once the limit of
plastic deformation is reached, leading to fracture [50].
A compaction event consists of four parts: consolidation, dwell, and relaxation times. Figure
8 shows a compaction event, where it can be seen that maximum compression force is attained
prior to the vertical alignment of the punches with the center of the compression roll. The time
between the peak of compression and the middle of dwell time is the peak offset time [51] [52].
This phenomenon arises from the plastic flow occurring within the compressed material, which
alleviates the pressure and leads to a decrease in force. Consequently, the shape of the compression-
time curve is influenced by the plasticity characteristics of the powder [50].
Figure 8: Compaction event, with main parts: Consolidation time, Dwell, and Relaxation times.
(UC reffers to upper compression, UPD to upper punch displacement, and LDP to lower punch
displacement) [50].

==PAGE:25==

Different time events regarding the compaction event can be defined:
Consolidation time (Ts): Time when punches are changing their vertical position, decreasing the
distance between the punch tips [50].
Dwell time (Td): Time when punches are not changing their vertical position [50].
Relaxation time (Tr): Time when punches are changing their vertical position and increasing the
distance between the punch tips before losing contact with the rolls [50].
Contact time (Tc): It is the time during which punches are moving, having their tips in contact with
the material and their heads in contact with the pressure rolls. It is the sum of consolidation time,
dwell time, and relaxation time (Tc = Ts + Td + Tr) [50].
2.6.1
Tablet failure
Many problems usually occur when scaling up from research tablet presses to high-speed produc-
tion ones. Some of these problems include capping, lamination, cracking, picking, and chipping
[50].
Capping is an observed phenomenon characterized by the stratification of tablet layers, leading
to a horizontal displacement. The likelihood of capping increases with compression force and
tableting speed [53] [54], with precompression force [55], and with punch penetration depth and
tablet thickness [56]. A high speed is considered the main cause, due to a slow process of stress
relaxation and consequently, stored internal stress [50].
Lamination occurs when a tablet splits apart in single or multiple layers. Over compression is
usually the cause of lamination, as it causes flattening of the granules, resulting in the loss of their
ability to interlock. Tendency of tablets to laminate increases with speed, compression force, and
pre-compression force [55] [57] .
Elastic recovery during decompression can cause stress cracking due to elastic recovery during
ejection. Cracking is a speed related phenomenon, while chipping may be caused by a too brittle
material or powder sticking to punch faces [50]. Figures 9 and 10 show how capping, lamination,
cracking and chipping look like in tablets.

2.7
Simulators for tablet production
2.7.1
Types of simulators
Tablet machines varies from small scale to industrial production. Due to the small amount of drug
in the early steps of development the use of small tableting machines is necessary. Tablet machine
simulators are an option to mimic tablet presses using a small amount of powder, reducing the
material consumption [35].
Compaction simulators are used during process development, serving as a valuable instrument for
enhancing theoretical knowledge of the compaction process. It offers distinct advantages, such as
emulating the operational sequence of numerous rotary tablet presses and capturing all significant
parameters throughout the cycle. Moreover, simulators work with a single pair of punches, reduc-
ing the consumption of material. This type of tablet presses consists in a single station equipped
with two punches and a die, that remains stationary [58]. There are two types of simulators:
1. Hydraulic Compaction Simulators
These simulators were the first developed and are still used due to a high speed control of
both punches, that can be monitored separately, as the punches move independently of each
other. The principal aim when using hydraulic simulators is mimicking the densification pro-
cess and the mechanical factors affecting it. Calculating the force-time profile of a tableting
machine is challenging, as numerous factors affect the measured force, such as the proper-
ties of the material being tableted, machine geometries, tableting speed, and tableting tools.
Similarly, determining the displacement-time profile of a tableting machine, particularly a
rotary tableting machine, is difficult using these type of simulators. Calculations based on
machine geometries are only feasible up to a certain extent. Consequently, it is not possible
to fully simulate the mechanics of a tableting machine [35] .
2. Mechanical compaction simulators
17
==PAGE:27==
Irene Rico Gomez
In this type of machine, compression pressure is generated by mechanical springs and con-
veyed on the compression rollers. The first developed was the machine simulator Presster,
in 1998 [59]. A single pair of punches move on a linear track, and compression wheels
reassemble the rollers of the rotary tableting machines. It is possible to simulate different
tablet machines, by adjusting dwell times from 5 to 80 ms. Special testing has shown that
rotary tableting machines can be accurately simulated with a precision level of 1-5%. One
disadvantage of the Presster is the fact that it works with a moving filling shoe, and cannot
mimic a rotary machine. However, new versions of this simulator allow pre-compression,
important to evaluate its effect in the final tablet properties [32].
In 2005, Stylcam was developed.
It consists in a simulator with a cam placed on the
lower compression wheel. Different dwell times can be simulated by adapting the com-
pression wheel speed. Pre-crompression can also be simulated, being the time between
pre-compression and main compression freely adjustable. This simulator works with a fixed
filling shoe, better reassembling a rotary tableting machine [32].
In recent years, a new compact simulator has appeared in the market, Styl’One, specifically
suitable for easy and fast compaction studies. The main difference with Stylcam is that the
pressure is transmitted only by the lower cam, while in the case of the Stylcam, the pressure
is applied by the upper and lower excentre cam. This simulator also offers production of
multi-layer and tab-in-tab tablets. It is easy to handle, and offers extensive data analysis
using a minimum amount of powder. Moreover, it is equipped with two sensors on each
punch that allow accurate measurements of compression force in a very short time from 0 to
50 KN [60]. Styl’One is the simulator used for this project.
2.7.2
Previous studies
Single punch tablet presses are suitable for small quantities of powder, and consequently are mainly
employed for research and development purposes. However, there are limitations leading to a great
risk for the scale-up to the high-speed rotary tablet presses. Main challenges arise from distinct
powder behavior and consecutive tablet properties when comparing results from single punch tablet
presses to rotary tablet presses [61].
Compaction simulators have been used since the end of the 20th century. These machines replicate
the compression process on tablet presses and offer benefits such as minimal material usage and
advanced instrumentation, while reproducing the conditions of the tablet press of interest [62].
The primary difficulties lie in accurately replicating punch movements, which in turn affects the
compaction profiles [61] .
Comparison studies of tablet properties produced with compaction simulators and original tablet

2.8
Effects of speed, pre-compression and tablet shape
2.8.1
Effect of speed
The compaction of powders into tablets involves the reduction of pores within a powder bed and
the formation of interparticulate bonds. Throughout the compression process, materials undergo
complex stresses. The configuration of the powder bed undergoes modifications, primarily driven
by particle rearrangement, plastic deformation, and fragmentation, resulting in consolidation [32]
[43] .
20
==PAGE:30==
Irene Rico Gomez
The deformation of pharmaceutical materials has been found to be time dependent according to
different studies. There are many factors influencing this dependence. One of the contributing fac-
tors to the consolidation process is the nature of the materials, which can either be brittle or plastic.
Brittle materials consolidate primarily through fragmentation, whereas plastic materials undergo
plastic deformation. Plastic deformation is considered to be more time-dependent due to stress re-
laxation following compaction, whereas fragmentation is less influenced as it is rapidly achieved,
and greater exposure to force has a diminished effect. Plastic materials undergo plastic deforma-
tion, reducing interparticulate distances and improving bond formation. Under compressive forces,
ductile materials deform and fill voids, creating interparticulate bonds, reducing porosity and in-
creasing the tensile strength of the compact [32] [43]. Microcrystalline cellulose (MCC) shows
its maximum plasticity at 18 KN, and increasing the compaction pressure does not lead to higher
plasticity. Once the limit is exceeded fragmentation of particles occurs. Figure 13 shows plasticity
at 18 and 25 KN for some of the main dry binders, including MCC [32].
Figure 13: Plasticity under 18 and 25 KN for some dry binders used in tableting [32].
A previous study looking for the effects of compression speed in compaction properties, compared
tablets produced under same compaction pressures and different dwell times (8 ms, 27 ms, 20 s,
and 90 s). The results concluded that tablets made from MCC were independent of speed [65].
However, breaking force was measured within one minute after compression, and consequently,
stress relaxation was not taken into consideration. Figure 14 shows compressibility profiles at
different dwell times [65].
An alternative investigation demonstrated that MCC tablets exhibited a 5% elevation in tensile
strength when the dwell time was extended to 10 seconds, as opposed to 0.09 seconds. Notably,
more significant variations were observed in tablets comprising MCC and compressible starch,
which primarily exhibit plastic behavior, in contrast to tablets composed of compressible sugar or
lactose. Plastic deformation involves the creation of interparticulate bonds, increasing the tensile

strength [66].

showed that the porosity of compacts was affected by different speed when the particle size of

2.8.2
Effect of pre-compression
Pre-compression is employed during compression processes to reduce the porosity in two stages.
The entrapped air causes defects such as cracks and laminations, consequently, by adding a pre-
compression step this air is reduced and defects are prevented [68]. However, pre-compression
could have other effects on the quality of tablets. In their investigation on the impact of pre-
compression using a rotary press on tablet strength, Vezin et al. [69] observed that tablets produced
with pre-compression and main compression exceeding a certain compaction pressure exhibited
indications of lamination at higher machine speeds (1200–1500 tablets/min). However, the tensile
strengths of these tablets did not appear to be significantly affected. The authors concluded that the
benefits of pre-compression vary based on the specific characteristics and response of individual
formulation components to stress, as well as the shape of the tablet and the operating speed of the
machine [70].
Akande et al. [69] studied how the tensile strengths of paracetamol-MCC tablets changed at dif-
ferent compression speeds when single compression and combinations of pre-compression and
main compression were used. The authors observed that the consolidation of paracetamol and
MCC was time-dependent, and sufficient time required for stress relaxation and plastic deforma-
tion to take place was not available at higher compression speeds. The plastic energies generally
exhibited an increase, showing that more energy is required to form the tablets at higher com-
pression speed, while elastic energy tended to decrease with higher compression speed. However,
the ratios of elastic energy to plastic energy showed an increase as the material displayed higher
elasticity. Consequently, an increase in compression speed resulted in decreased tensile strengths
and increased elastic recoveries. The latter could be attributed to reduced stress relaxation and
consequently build up of internal stress after compaction at elevated rates [69] .
The utilization of pre-compression and main compression in combination did not effectively mit-
igate the decline in tensile strength of tablets as the compression speed escalated. Nevertheless,
employing pre-compression and main compression resulted in compacts exhibiting higher tensile
strengths compared to tablets compressed through a single compression process. To illustrate,
when the compression speed reached 390 mm/s, the tensile strengths of tablets produced using
a pre-compression and main compression pressure combination of 160/80 MPa were compara-
ble to the tensile strengths of tablets formed through a single compression pressure of 320 MPa.
The reductions in tensile strengths, expressed as percentages, as the speed increased from 78 to
390 mm/s, were more pronounced when tablets were compressed with pre-compression and main
compression pressure combinations of 240/320 MPa compared to combinations of 80/160, 160/80,
160/240, or 240/160 MPa [69]. Figure 17 shows the decrease in tensile strengths of tablets caused
by an increase in compression speed, for the different combinations tested [69].

Overall, tablets made after the implementation of pre-compression and main compression resulted
in the doubling of contact periods with the applied pressure. This extended contact time allowed for
enhanced stress relaxation of the material, facilitating further consolidation and ultimately leading

2.8.3
Effect of tablet geometry
Modifying the geometry of a tablet can lead to significant variations in density distribution. Con-
sequently, accumulated stress and strain occur within the tablet, impacting both its tensile strength
and the potential for fracture upon ejection from the die. Furthermore, the stress relief process may
induce alterations in the compact’s geometry after compaction. Early researchers, Newton et al.
(1974) [71], have examined the relationship between the tensile strength of tablets and factors such
as shape, compaction pressure, and density distribution within the tablets [71].
A more recent study by B. Eiliazadeh et al. [72] studied the density distribution of flat and convex
face tablets after single ended compaction. All of them showed an uneven distribution, which
implies a different movement of the particles when compaction pressure is applied. In the case of

2.9
Tablet production scale-up
In contrast to other pharmaceutical operations, the scale-up of the tableting process from research
and development to production does not necessitate an expansion in volume, as the die size remains
unchanged. Matching the compression force is not either the problem. However, the main aspect
to consider during scale-up of tablets is the compaction rate, specifically the speed at which the
punches operate [74].
Nevertheless, even if the compaction rate is the same, the duration of tableting events is influenced
by various factors, including roll diameter, pitch circle diameter, number of stations, punch ge-
ometry, length of the feed frame, and the angle of the ejection ramp. Therefore, it is important
to follow some recommendations during scale-up. First, it is important to evaluate the minimum
consolidation time (time to reach maximum pressure), at which tablets of a satisfactory quality can
be produced. By knowing this minimum time it will be possible to establish a compaction rate
leading to good quality tablets. Besides, calculating what is the number of tablets produced per
minute (rpm) corresponding to this rate for different tablet presses is also crucial [74].
Previous studies by Pitt et al. [75] aimed to study how tablets produced from a single punch tablet
press relate to the ones produced in a rotary tablet press, using same formulation. Following these
studies, Garr et al. [76] studied two different formulations, one direct compression formulation
consisting of a blend of API with MCC, sodium starch glycolate, silicon dioxide and magnesium
stearate (named DC1), and the other consisting of a wet granulation of API, mannitol, MCC and
polyvinylpyrrolidone (named WG1) [76].
When using the single punch tablet press, GTP, compaction forces from 1 to 5 kN were used to
compress 100 mg of the direct compression blend, DC1, and 80 mg of the granulated compression
blend, WG1, to form flat face cylindrical tablets. In the case of using the rotary tablet press,
Fette, 800 mg caplet shaped tablets (for DC1) and 350 mg round convex tablets (for WG1) were
produced, and compaction forces from 6 to 30 KN were applied [76].
Despite the differences in size and geometry, the obtained tabletability, compressibility, and com-
pactability plots showed that tablets produced with the single punch and rotary tablet press were
congruent for both formulations, demonstrating the applicability of this scalability approach to

3.6.4
Porosity analysis
Porosity has been determined by using Computed Tomography (CT) analysis. This technique was
employed for studying tablets under four different conditions. Two tablets corresponded to convex
face tablets produced at a compaction pressure of 106.1 MPa, and speed of 50 rpm, using two
different tablet presses: single punch Korsch, and rotary tablet press Fette. The other two types
of tablets corresponded to a convex face tablet produced at a compaction pressure of 331.5 MPa
and speed of 50 rpm, using the simulator Styl’One mimicking the rotary tablet press, and a tablet
produced at same conditions but using the rotary tablet press Fette with a flat face punch. Figure
51 in Section 4.6 shows the tabletability profile with all the selected tablets for CT analysis.
For analyzing the stack containing the four selected tablets, the samples were placed in the same
device and scanned simultaneously. The purpose of this was to establish uniform conditions for
analyzing all tablets and enable a fair comparison during image analysis. For determining the
porosity of the tablets, software ImageJ and myVGL were used [85] [86]. First, images were
visualized with myVGL, and Imagej was used to process the images and assess the porosity by
the establishment of a threshold for analyzing the particles corresponding to air-filled voids. The
images analyzed corresponded to cross-sectional images generated after CT-scanning. Three dif-
ferent top section views, corresponding to each tablet were studied with ImageJ. Moreover, the
software myVGL was also used directly to determine the gray value and the porosity of the tablets.
Results from both software were compared. The main steps for porosity determination using each
method are included in Section 7.
Solid fraction was obtained by calculating the apparent density and true density, explained in 3.6.1.
When using ImageJ and myVGL the porosity of the tablet is represented by the area fraction, and
corresponds to the dark regions, which are air-filled spaces. Solid fraction can be determined by
subtracting the porosity from one. Therefore, after obtaining the values for porosity, it is straight-
forward to calculate the solid fraction.
The mean gray value was also employed as an indicator for evaluating porosity, as a higher mean
value signifies a greater number of voxels with elevated intensities. Intensity values increase from
black to white, with white denoting the highest values. Accordingly, a larger mean value indicates
a whiter image, implying a more compact and less porous tablet [87].

4
Results and Discussion
This chapter describes main results of the thesis, obtained by performing the previously detailed
methods. It includes main plots for visualizing how tensile strength and solid fraction varies with
compaction pressure when using the different tablet presses and changing the parameters under
study. Results from the various employed characterization techniques, including disintegration,
friability, and CT scanning, are also used to explain the disparities observed across different sce-
narios, supplemented by information from relevant literature sources.
4.1
Single punch tablet press
Flat and convex tablets were produced by using this machine, operating at 20 and 50 rpm and
selecting the same compaction pressures tested in the other tablet presses. The rotational speeds
correspond to punch speeds of 29 and 59 mm/s. These values were calculated by dividing the
distance from the tip of the upper punch in the upper-most position to the die table, which equals
36 mm, plus the average filling depth (7 mm operating at 20 rpm, 8 mm operating at 50 rpm), over
the time needed to complete half of a compaction cycle (1,500 ms at 20 rpm, and 750 ms at 50
rpm).
4.1.1
Punch effect
Tablets produced with Korsch were characterised to determine how the punch selection affected
the tablet properties. It was observed that when operating under the same speed, the convex face
punch, FET52, yielded tablets with higher solid fraction. The tensile strength of the tablets was also
higher when using this punch. Figures 28 and 29 show tabletability and compactability profiles for
Korsch when using both punches at low (20 rpm) and high rotational speed (50 rpm), respectively.
It is seen that convex tablets produced at 223 MPa and 50 rpm have a high variation, caused by a
tablet with lower breaking force than the others.
Overall, the variation in solid fraction could arise due to disparities in the dimensions of the two
punches. Moreover, when calculating the apparent volume, Equation 17, the height was measured
after relaxation, while the rest of parameters, such as cup depth, cup volume, and cup height
were theoretic. Consequently, the calculated volume is not exactly the real one, as the effects of
relaxation are not known in all directions. The volume could be different leading to a different solid
fraction. In fact, according to Eiliazadeh et al. a high density distribution appears in the corners
of convex tablets, and a higher stress is stored in these zones. Consequently, once the tablet is
ejected, during relaxation, the tablet is likely to expand in these regions, altering its volume [72].

A higher apparent volume, will lead to a decrease in the apparent density, and consequently the
solid fraction. Regarding flat face tablets, alterations in height were only considered; however,
the areas of elevated density in these tablets are primarily the top corners and the middle bottom
half [72]. Hence, when employing the actual height to compute the apparent volume, variations
in these specific regions are taken into account. Consequently, it is likely that the obtained solid
fraction for these tablets aligns more closely with reality.
Regarding tensile strength, different equations are employed when utilizing a flat face punch com-
pared to a convex face punch. The equation for convex face punches was derived experimentally
by Shang et al. [78], who established a correlation between the solid fraction of convex tablets
and the tensile strength of flat tablets exhibiting the same solid fraction, as explained in Section
3.6.5. However, it is important to take into account that Shang et al. used convex tablets only, and
the convex tablets produced during this project are compound cup tablets. Consequently, the de-
rived coefficients could be slightly different and this variation could account for differences when
calculating the tensile strength using this equation.

4.1.2
Speed effect
There are significant distinctions observed in tablets manufactured at varying operating speeds.
Figure 30 displays compactability and tabletability plots for both punches operated at low and
high speeds. It is seen that the solid fraction remains close when employing the same punch at
different speeds. However, differences in tensile strength are observed, and two trend lines can be
plotted for each punch, each for a different speed. Consequently, there are significant variations in
the tensile strength caused by different speed.
A lower speed leads to an increase in the compaction of the tablets and consequently their tensile
strength. The punch speed corresponding to 20 rpm is 29 mm/s, while for 50 rpm is 59 mm/s.
According to the literature, a lower speed can lead to higher tensile strength in tablets mainly
composed of MCC, the main component of the formulation used for this project [66]. The plastic
behavior of this excipient, leads to the formation of inter-particulate bonds when it deforms under
compression forces. Previous studies have highlighted that the influence of speed on plastic ma-
terials might be more significant due to stress relaxation occurring after compaction. This would
imply that a longer deformation time may lead to more stress relaxation once the pressure ceases,
allowing the material to reduce internal stress levels. Oppositely, if a material is compressed dur-
ing a shorter time, this may lead to a reduced relaxation, and a higher internal stress could lead
48
==PAGE:58==
Irene Rico Gomez
to defects and a reduction in tensile strength once the tablets are ejected [65]. The formulation
used consists of MCC (60%), but also sorbitol (39%). Sorbitol is a brittle material, and this type
of materials are less affected by differences in speed, because fragmentation happens faster and is
less influenced by time [65]. Consequently, if the content of MCC was increased, it is likely that
differences in tensile strength as a consequence of speed would be higher than the ones observed.
Previous studies used the dwell time to explain the differences in tensile strength observed when
operating at different speeds. However, as the dwell time of Korsch is considered to be negligible,
this argument is not applicable for our observations. As a result, the observed difference could be
likely attributed to variations in punch speed, when different rotational speeds are chosen.

4.2.1
Punch effect
Tablets produced with Fette using different punches were characterised to determine how the punch
selection affected the tablet properties. Figures 31 and 32 show tabletability and compactability
profiles when using both punches at low and high speeds, respectively. The tabletability plots show
two trend lines for each punch when operating at 20 and 50 rpm, showing that solid fraction is very
similar for tablets produced using both punches at the different compaction pressures, but tensile
strength is higher for convex face tablets.
The dissimilar geometry of tablets produced with different punches accounts for differences in
tensile strength. As previously mentioned when explaining observed differences in tablets made
with single punch tablet press, Eiliazadeh et al. determined that convex tablets show high density
regions in the corners, close to the die wall [72]. When determining the breaking force of tablets,
resistance to crushing was obtained by measuring the diametrical crushing strength. Consequently,
a higher density in the periphery of the tablet would also imply a higher breaking force, which as
a consequence will imply a higher tensile strength.
Convex tablets break in a more complex form, and this can be caused by different factors, including
a different distribution of stresses. A previous study by Pitt et al. [81], studied the stress distribution
in convex tablets under diametrical loading. The authors came to the conclusion that a stress factor
can be defined as the ratio of the maximum tensile stress in a convex tablet to that in a flat tablet
subjected to the same load and with the same W/D value, being W the belly band, and D the
diameter of the tablet. All the convex tablets considered for this study had a W/D between 0.06
and 0.30, and a D/R lower than 1.43, corresponding R to the radius of curvature and D to the
diameter of the tablet. The main conclusion was that for a constant D/R of 1.43, the defined
stress factor increased with increasing W/D [81]. Using the dimensions for the convex tablets
produced in this project, W/D equals 0.27. This value is very close to 0.30, the highest limit used
in Pitt et al.’s study, and the point at which maximum stress factor was obtained. The tablets
utilized in this project are compound convex tablets, contrasting with the convex tablets employed
in the referenced study. As a result, the findings of the study may offer insights into the potential
explanation for the observed higher tensile strength in convex tablets. However, determining the
complete applicability of Pitt et al.’s work remains challenging because of the differences in the
tablet’s geometry.

4.2.3
Pre-compression effect
The effect of pre-compression was determined by producing tablets under same conditions and
adding a pre-compression of 19.9 MPa. Same punches were used and tablets were produced at the
same low and high speeds, 20 and 50 rpm, respectively. It was expected that the main consequence
of pre-compression would only be the mitigation of trapped air, diminishing the likelihood of
defects within the tablets. However, it was observed that the addition of pre-compression lead to
more variations.
Tablets produced at 20 rpm did not show significant differences. Figure 34 shows tabletability
and compactability profiles for tablets produced at 20 rpm. Convex tablets show very similar
values for solid fraction and tensile strength. Flat face tablets show a slightly variation, but the
curves corresponding to tablets made with and without pre-compression are very close and overlap.
Consequently, it can be said that the addition of pre-compression do not affect the tensile strength
of tablets when operating at 20 rpm.
Flat face tablets manufactured at 50 rpm are more affected by pre-compression. Figure 35 shows
tabletability and compactability profiles for tablets produced at 50 rpm. Tablets manufactured with
53
==PAGE:63==
Irene Rico Gomez
pre-compression show higher solid fraction and tensile strength. Oppositely, convex tablets did not
show significant differences. According to the literature, at high speeds it was observed that the
addition of pre-compression could lead to higher tensile strength. This could be due to an increased
contact with the pressure, as when pre-compression is added two periods of contact occur. Con-
sequently, relaxation time also increases and the material can further consolidate and show higher
tensile strength [69]. This effect could become more evident when employing high compaction
rates since, at lower velocities, the material has more relaxation time, thereby minimizing the in-
fluence of additional time afforded by pre-compression. The reason for flat face tablets to show
higher variation when pre-compression is added, could be explained by a more even distribution
of compaction forces. Eiliazadeh et al. found that density distribution shows less variations in flat
face tablets. Furthermore, it was also concluded that compaction pressure could be correlated with
density distribution [72]. Consequently, there is a great possibility that pre-compression forces are
more homogeneously distributed in the flat face tables, as it happens with the density distribution.
Therefore, the effect of pre-compression is more evident.

Flat face punch
Simulator Styl’One was able to mimic Fette with high accuracy. Flat tablets produced at 20

tabletability and compactability plots for Fette and Styl’One operating at 20 rpm. It is seen in

aligned with Fette when pre-compression was applied. Nevertheless, it is difficult to know if pre-
compression makes a difference when using the simulator, as differences when using the real tablet
press at this speed were minor.
When operating at 50 rpm, flat tablets manufactured with Fette and pre-compression showed higher
solid fraction and tensile strength. Figure 43 shows the results for Fette and Styl’One operating
at 50 rpm using punch FET20, with and without pre-compression. This variaiton could be due
to an increased relaxation time as a consequence of doubling the contact with pressure [71]. Flat
tablets seem to be more affected by pre-compression than convex tablets, and this could be at-
tributed to a more uniform distribution of compaction pressure, explained by a consistent density
distribution within flat tablets [72]. However, simulator Styl’One did not show differences when
pre-compression was added. Therefore, it seems that predicting the effect of pre-compression with
Styl’One is challenging. A possible explanation for this variation could be that the relaxation time
between pre-compression and main compression for Fette was longer than for Styl’One. When
using Fette the distance between the point where pre-compression and main compression were
applied was obtained by using Equation 21, where r is the radius corresponding to Fette turret, 140
mm, and θ is the angle between the two points, 1.57 radians (90 ◦). Time between these points
was obtained by dividing this distance, which is 219.8 mm by the linear speed at 20 and 50 rpm,
which is 293 mm/s and 733 mm/s, respectively. The linear speed was obtained by using Equation
15. After this calculations, time between pre-compression and compression when using Fette at 20
rpm was determined to be 750 ms, and 300 ms when operating at 50 rpm. These relaxation times
when using Styl’One are lower, 470 and 175 ms, respectively. Relaxation times for Styl’One were
obtained from the raw data generated by the CS. Consequently, a longer relaxation time between
the two compaction events could explain the difference in tensile strength observed when using
Fette and the absence of such variation when employing the simulator.

Convex face punch
Figure 44 shows tabletability and compactability plots for tablets manufactured using both tablet

the convex face punch, FET52, at 20 rpm, showed similar solid fraction, but higher tensile strength
than those manufactured by Styl’One under same conditions when high compaction pressures were
applied. As previously explained, relaxation time between pre-compression and main compression
was longer when employing Fette, and this could explain the dissimilarity observed when pre-
compression was included. However, simulator Styl’One also showed lower tensile strength values
when pre-compression was not considered. The variations were only observed at high compaction
pressures, and over compression could be leading to these lower tensile strength values. The
fact that the difference was not observed at same speed when flat tablets were produced could be
explained by the difference in density distribution, and consequently internal stress, between flat
and convex tablets, studied by Eiliazadeh et al. [72].
Operating at 50 rpm same tendency was observed, slightly higher tensile strength was seen at
high compaction pressures for tablets manufactured by Fette, although the difference was very
small. Figure 45 shows tabletability and compactability plots for tablets manufactured using both
tablet presses and the same convex face punch, FET52, at 50 rpm. Again the possible cause
is over compression, supported by the fact that a deviation was observed at 331.5 MPa for the
tablets produced with Styl’One without pre-compression. This deviation was further analyzed by
performing a CT scan, where lamination was observed. Further information is included in Section
4.6.
Convex tablets have a higher extent of density distribution, with lower density regions in the center
of the tablet. A higher variation in density distribution implies more accumulated stress within
the tablet, which is released after ejection as stress relaxation. Consequently, convex tablets usu-
ally present more defects, and this explains why lamination occurs more often in convex tablets
manufactured above a certain compaction pressure [72].

4.5
Single punch vs. rotary tablet press
The main objective of this project was to determine the factors affecting the scale up of tablets
from research scale to production scale. Consequently, after analyzing the effect of these different
factors when using each of the machines and evaluating the ability of the simulator Styl’One to
reproduce both machines, the quality of tablets produced with the single punch tablet press Korsch
and the rotary tablet press Fette was evaluated. This analysis aims to determine if Korsch can be
used to predict the quality of tablets produced with Fette when same conditions, speed and punch
shape, are employed. In the case of using Fette, results using pre-compression are also included.
4.5.1
Flat face punch
Flat tablets produced at 20 and 50 rpm using both tablet presses showed very similar properties.
Figures 46 and 47 show the results for Fette and Korsch operating at 20 and 50 rpm using flat face
punch FET20. Three parallel trend lines, corresponding to Fette with and without pre-compression,
and Korsch, can be plotted in the tabletability plot.
Regarding tensile strength, both machines show similar values. However, it is observed that Korsch
show higher tensile strength values when operating at both speeds, specially relevant at the high
end of the compaction pressures when operating at both speeds. This difference is explained by
a difference in punch speed. As previously observed when evaluating the speed effect on both
machines, a lower speed leads to higher tensile strength values. Korsch speed is 29 mm/s when
operating at 20 rpm, while Fette speed is 65 mm/s. At a high speed, 50 rpm, Korsch speed is 59
mm/s, while Fette speed is 90 mm/s. At 50 rpm the tensile strength curve in the compactability plot
corresponding to Korsch is close to Fette with pre-compression. The addition of pre-compression
seems to counteract the effect of a lower punch speed when operating at high rotational speed. As
previously explained, an increase in tensile strength when pre-compression is included could be
due to an increased contact with the pressure, as relaxation time also increases and the material
can further consolidate [69]. This effect could be more evident in flat tablets, because compaction
pressure is distributed more evenly [72].
Overall, it was observed that a lower speed leads to a higher compaction, and tablets with higher
tensile strength. As previously explained, the plastic behavior of MCC, the main component, leads
to a higher deformation and consequently more bonding when compaction rate is lower [66]. A
longer relaxation time, as a consequence of a lower speed, leads to a reduction of internal stress and
higher tensile strength [67]. This observation is in agreement with the results obtained for Korsch
and Fette when studying the effect of speed in the tablets produced using each type of machine.
The lower the speed, the higher the compaction of the tablets, and consequently the higher the

4.5.2
Convex face punch
When using the convex face punch, the solid fraction operating at both speeds was higher for
Korsch tablets. The difference was specially relevant at high compaction forces. Three trend
lines are plotted in the tabletability plots for 20 rpm and 50 rpm, corresponding to Fette with
pre-compression, without pre-compression, and Korsch. Figures 49 and 50 show tabletability and
compactability plots for tablets manufactured using both tablet presses at 20 and 50 rpm.
Again, the possible explanation could be the different punch speed when using each machine. This
could explain a higher compaction of tablets at a lower speed, and as a consequence a higher solid
fraction and tensile strength. As previously explained, MCC is a plastic material that deforms
under compression forces. A longer deformation time could cause longer stress relaxation once
the pressure ceases, allowing the material to reduce internal stress levels, and as a consequence
show higher tensile strength [67].
Disintegration experiments were undergone for tablets made at a compression force of 238.7 MPa
at 50 rpm, using both tablet presses, to see if the observed differences in solid fraction affect the
tablet properties. The average disintegration time for Korsch tablets was 14 s, while the average
time for Fette tablets was 12 s. A higher disintegration time for Korsch tablets was expected, as
a higher solid fraction means less porosity. However, disintegration times for Fette tablets highly
varied, as it can be seen in Figure 48, where disintegration times for six tablets manufactured by
each tablet press are plotted. A t-test determined that the samples are not significantly different,
showing a p-value of 0.2571 and a t-value of 1.274. Same disintegration experiments were repeated
for tablets produced with Korsch and Fette under compaction pressures of 106.1 MPa at 50 rpm.
The average disintegration time for Korsch tablets was 6 s, while for Fette tablets was 7.42 s.
Difference is very low, and consequently no effect on disintegration properties may exist. However,
a t-test determined that samples are significantly different, with a p-value of 0.0004 and a t-value of
5.288. Overall, disintegration testing is highly variable, and results are not completely trustworthy.
However, differences between tablets manufactured under a compaction force of 106.1 MPa and
238.7 MPa are obvious, and consequently disintegration testing can be used as an approximation
when differences in tablet quality are relevant.
Friability experiments were also undergone for the tablets manufactured at 106.1 MPa with Fette
and Korsch. No changes were observed after weighting the tablets before and after performing the
test. Equation 19 was used to determine the percentage change. Consequently, none of the tablets
were affected by chipping or breakage.

5
Conclusion
The purpose of this master project was to determine if single punch tablet press Korsch could
be used to predict tablet properties manufactured during production stage employing rotary tablet
press Fette. The effect of speed, tablet geometry, and pre-compression, has been studied to de-
termine which factors have a bigger impact in tablet properties during scale up from research to
production scale. Furthermore, the applicability of compaction simulator (CS) Styl’One for pre-
dicting tablet properties was also assessed.
First, it was observed that a different tablet geometry led to variations in solid fraction and tensile
strength in tablets produced with both, the single punch tablet press Korsch, and rotary tablet
press, Fette. This variation could be caused by differences in density distribution as a consequence
of tablet shape. It was also observed that a lower speed led to a higher tensile strength, being
this effect especially relevant for tablets produced with the rotary tablet press. The increased
tensile strength could be attributed to an extended relaxation time. Moreover, the addition of pre-
compression resulted in higher tensile strength in flat tablets when operating at high speed.
Regarding the CS results it was observed that the simulator was able of reproducing the single
punch tablet press with high accuracy when producing flat tablets. However, it showed lower
solid fraction and tensile strength values when producing convex tablets. On the other hand, the
simulator Styl’One was able to reproduce the rotary tablet press Fette with high accuracy, although
the effect of pre-compression was not correctly simulated, which could be caused by a shorter
relaxation time between pre-compression and main compression when employing the simulator.
Differences at high compaction forces were observed for convex tablets, being the higher tendency
of this type of tablets to be affected by over compression the possible explanation.
Finally, it was observed that Korsch led to tablets with higher tensile strength than those manufac-
tured by Fette. A lower punch speed seems to be the reason for this observation, as the same trend
was observed when studying the speed effect on both machines. Consequently, when predicting
the quality of tablets produced with Fette by employing Korsch, the impact of speed must be taken
into account. It is important to understand that having the same rotational speed and producing
same number of tablets per minute do not imply having the same punch speed. Therefore, punch
speed must be the same to produce comparable tablets regarding tensile strength.
Additionally, Computed tomography (CT) has been proven to be an useful tool for evaluating
porosity. It is feasible to approximate the porosity and identify variations when comparing tablets
produced with different tablet presses manufactured at varying conditions. However, there is still a
need of advanced image analysis techniques and standardized protocols for accurately determining
and comparing the porosity of tablets when employing different formulations.

6
Future perspectives
The assessment of factors affecting the scale-up of tablets from research to production scale was
the aim of this project. The conclusions drawn from the results give a better insight and under-
standing of the effect of speed, tablet geometry and pre-compression on tablets manufactured by
different kinds of tablet presses. However, more experiments would be needed to determine how
these effects can be predicted with higher accuracy. Moreover, all experiments used the same for-
mulation, consisting of only excipients, and further research is required for evaluating formulations
including API.
First of all, experiments for evaluating the potential of the simulator to mimic the single punch
tablet press should be repeated by employing the correct cycle, it means, by reproducing the same
punch movement. This would possibly lead to tablets with more similar properties, and conse-
quently, the simulator could be considered in the future to predict the quality of tablets manufac-
tured by the single punch tablet press.
Other improvement related to the simulator could be the better approximation of relaxation times
when mimicking the rotary tablet press with pre-compression. It was observed that the simulator
was able to mimic the rotary tablet press Fette with high accuracy, but it could not predict how
pre-compression affects the tensile strength of tablets when reproducing this tablet press, although
the same compaction cycle was mimicked. Therefore, it is likely that differences in relaxation time
between the two compaction events are the reason for this variation. Tablets should be manufac-
tured with the simulator after the establishment of a compaction cycle with the same relaxation
times between pre-compression and main compression.
Furthermore, when evaluating the single punch tablet press to predict the quality of tablets man-
ufactured with the rotary tablet press, the punch speed should be the same for both machines. It
was concluded that a lower speed leads to tablets with higher tensile strength, and consequently it
is important to ensure that the same punch speed is used when manufacturing tablets at a research
scale, if we want to predict the quality of tablets at the production scale. Another option could
be establishing a correlation between the two tablet presses. For the latter, it would be needed to
do several experiments at different compaction rates, until it is possible to accurately predict the
properties of the tablets produced with the rotary tablet press by using the results from the single
punch tablet press.
Moreover, more advanced imaging techniques could be developed to determine porosity of tablets.
The software used has been proved useful to determine porosity of tablets made using the same
formulation, but it would likely lead to erroneous results if the blend is different. Consequently,
a more accurate method, able to differentiate materials with low density and air-filled voids is

Appendix A: Porosity determination
Porosity was determined by using software myVGL, needed for visualization of the images ob-
tained with Computed tomography (CT), and ImageJ. The main steps for analyzing each cross-
section image with ImageJ are:
1. Open the image and select Image, Color Split Channels.
2. Choose one channel. In this case, this step is not important, as all of them look the same.
Figure 58: Initial image opened in image analysis software ImageJ.
3. Use the rectangular section to select the biggest rectangular section possible. Crop selection
by Image Crop
Figure 59: Rectangular selection before cropping corresponding to the original image.
4. Select the areas of porosity by choosing Image, Adjust Threshold.
5. Drag both slider bars to the left until they are set to cero. Increase the value on the lower
slider until consider all the dark areas are red, and select apply. The image will become a
binary image.
86
==PAGE:96==
Irene Rico Gomez
Figure 60: Dark areas transformed into red while setting the threshold (on the left). Binary image
after applying a threshold, where all dark areas appear as black (on the right).
6. Select Analyze, Analyze particles, and click Summary box. Take the area fraction and trans-
fer it to an spreadsheet for further comparisons.
On the other hand, software MyVGL was employed for comparing gray values of the different
images. Main steps for obtaining gray values include:
1. Open the image with myVGL.
2. Select Analysis, Gray value. A histogram showing number of voxels over intensity grey
value is generated. Figure 61 shows an example for one of the tablets.
Figure 61: Histogram showing number of voxels over intensity, obtained from myVGL [86].
On the other hand, porosity was also obtained using this software. The steps are:

1. Open the image with myVGL.
2. Select Determine surface by clicking in the corresponding symbol in the tools bar.
3. Select Analysis, Porosity/Inclusion, and choose VGDefX/Only threshold.
4. In the emergent window, select Void in Analysis mode, and Use determined surface in Ma-
terial definition. Noise reduction is set to Medium, and Probability criterion to Pore. The
probability threshold is established as zero, because the size of the pores are not known. The
minimum size is determined as Volume equals 2 voxel. Figure 62 shows the window with
all selected parameters.
Figure 62: Emergent window with all selected parameters for porosity analysis, using myVGL
