Abstract 
III 
Abstract 
Geothermal energy is a renewable type of energy, which operates by producing the heat stored within 
the inner layers of earth, originated mainly by the yielded heat from the creation of earth and the current 
ongoing radioactive decay in its interior structure. Owing to the reduction of conventional hydrocarbon 
based fuels, the utilization of the alternative routes for fulfilling the energy demand of the world is crucial; 
among which, geothermal energy ,which is exempted from some of the main operational constraints such 
as season, and the weather condition that are stated as the most important limiting factors for some sorts 
of clean energies such as wind and solar based plants,  could play a significant role in fulfilling the future 
needs of human beings to a clean and trustable energy. 
Geothermal energy has various applications among which, district heating purposes are in great 
significance due to the lower requirement of temperature condition of the reservoir. Denmark as a 
European nation with a great emphasis on the utilization of green energy, has a proper potential for 
exploiting this clean sort of energy. The main area of interest with proper potential of geothermal energy 
has been placed in Jutland, which includes various geothermal reservoirs in different formations such as 
Frederikshavn, Gassum, Haldager and so on. In the current work an investigation has been made to 
evaluate the technical and economic feasibility of heat production and storage in Frederikshavn 
formation. The current job is made with the collaboration with Ross offshore company 
The technical and economic analysis of the investigated area illustrate the proper potential of seasonal 
heat production and storage with respect to district heating, which implies on the existence of reservoirs 
with proper technical properties including porosity and permeability that are placed in a feasible depth 
with respect to economic profitability of the project. After technically and economically evaluating the 
area, some locations have been stated as the areas with the highest potential for exploiting geothermal 
energy with respect to district heating.  
A considerable variation in the potential of heat production and storage was found by conducting the 
sensitivity analysis and adjusting some of the crucial technical parameters such as production rate and 
depth of reservoir (correspondingly temperature status of the reservoir). The cited parameters yielded a 
tremendous influence toward the economic feasibility analysis of the opted locations. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: 
Geothermal energy, heat production, Frederikshavn formation, Gassum formation, district heating

Preface 
V 
Preface 
The current work has been submitted as master thesis project to Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 
as a part of fulfilment the Master of Science degree in petroleum engineering. 
 
The thesis aimed for evaluating the technical and economic feasibility of heat production and heat storage 
in Frederikshavn formation. To fulfill the cited goal, Schlumberger Eclipse reservoir simulator has been 
used for conducting the reservoir simulation as a part of technical evaluation of the mentioned area. The 
thesis has been implemented with the consultancy of Ross offshore company (Ross DK A/S). The project 
represents 32.5 ECTS points, which has been carried out in a period of 5.5 month initiating from 28th 
January until 13th July 2019 under the supervision of Wei Yan (CERE, DTU), Duncan Paterson (CERE, DTU) 
and William Georg e Harrar Ross Offshore (Ross DK A/S).

1 Introduction 
 
’Geothermal’ by word consists of two Greek words including ‘gê ‘ and ‘thérm’ that stand for earth and heat 
respectively. This heat reveals itself on the surface of the earth in the form of volcanoes, hot springs and 
geysers. In the academic point of view, geothermal resources are assumed as the accumulation of the 
earth originated heat or the geothermal energy that can be economically extracted with the present 
technology or can be utilized in the near future [1].   
Geothermal energy originates from the earth and is sorted as a type of renewable energy, which is not 
directly or indirectly attained from the sun. The sufficiently high capacity factor which is a qualitative 
illustration of a facility working time together with the capability of operating in various conditions 
regardless of some of the common operational constraints including the season, day time or weather 
condition, could be stated as the striking technical privilege of the geothermal energy, which bolds it 
among other green energy resources [2]  
Earth is an infinite source of energy, which has saved a huge amount of heat within its internal structure; 
however, merely a significantly small part of this structure has been directly investigated. The Russian so 
called ‘SG-3’well with the approximate depth of 12 kilometer (12262 meter), which is placed in the Kola 
Peninsula, could be stated as the deepest well ever drilled. Taking into account the interior structure of 
the earth, which is illustrated in Figure 1, one can state that ‘SG-3’ covered less than 1% (0.2 % to be 
precise) of the earth’s radius. Owing to the cited illustration, the quantitative measurement of the earth’s 
heat cannot be fulfilled directly, which resulted in the application of indirect methods such as seismic 
activities and the analysis of the velocity and the transmissibility of the waves, conducting HPHT (High 
Pressure and High temperature) experiments to analyze the phase alteration of the minerals , and the 
model computation of the mineral phase conductivity, which yield an estimate of 6000 degrees Celsius 
for earth’s center temperature; however, due to the lack of empirical data, the cited methods suffer from 
the lack of accuracy, resulting the uncertainty of  1000

2.1 Utilization of Geothermal Energy 
2.1.1 Direct-use application of geothermal energy 
 
The direct-use application of geothermal energy is not limited to the present time and it has a long history. 
In the ancient time. Geothermal energy was usually utilized in the forms of steaming grounds and hot 
springs for various applications including cooking, bathing, medical usage and balneology. In the present 
time, the direct usage of geothermal energy is dedicated mostly to activities such as providing hot-water 
for inhabitants, space heating, snow melting for water supply in cold areas, swimming pools, food drying, 
green house cultivation in cold zones, fish and shrimp farms and sugar condensing. A brief overview of 
the current direct-use application of geothermal energy has been provided in the following. 
The most common traditional application of the geothermal energy in the ancient time is bathing. Japan 
could be cited as a pioneer in the bathing related applications as by 2010, nearly 30000 hot-spring sources 
(27825) were utilized as mean of tourist attraction[2, 7]. China as another Asian economic giant has also 
made a great investment in this field by the development of hot-springs in rather deep areas (3000 
meters) of the well-known Ordovician limestone strata zone since the beginning of 21st century [2, 8]. 
Native New Zealanders known as Maori people have an ancient tradition of steam cooking of their foods 
using steaming grounds in the area of Rotorua and Taupo [2, 9]. Application of green house for flower 
cultivation and Warm water shrimp farms in Monte Amiata (Italy) and Wairakei (New Zealand) 
respectively, using geothermal energy could be mentioned as another dimension of geothermal energy 
exploitation. The most famous country in the field of house heating application of geothermal wells is 
Iceland, whose residence have been receiving more than 90% of their hot water supply through 
geothermal plants[2]. 
2.1.1.1 Geothermal district heating  
 
The application of geothermal energy for district heating accounts for the utilization of the thermal energy 
of the earth for the aim of heating non-industrial and industrial buildings, including domestic water 
heating, space heating and industrial process heating [10, 11].In some areas, such type of energy is used 
for pedestrian road heating to prevent accumulation of snow during winter(Figure 2) [12].The applicability 
of geothermal energy for district heating would be less complex in areas located in the vicinity of the so-
called geothermal hot spots( Figure 3), which is owing to the higher underground temperature. The history 
of utilizing geothermal energy for such purpose traces back to 14th century in France[13].

geothermal wells drilled in the vicinity of the campus. As a summary of the technical description of the 
cited system, one can state the geothermal temperature of 89oC pumped through underground isolated 
pipes made out of fiberglass with the rate of 62 liters per second, capable of providing 0.53 MWt energy

2.1.1.1.1 Technical properties 
Temperature range 
 
In terms of technical characteristics, the areas with the temperature range of 40-150oC are appropriate in 
fulfilling the utilization of geothermal energy for district heating purpose. Among which, the areas with 
temperature range of 90-150oC are distinguished in the so-called moderate temperature range category 
whose main application is in low temperature power plants and heat demanding industrial applications 
[16, 19, 20].  
Components 
 
In general, the geothermal-based district heating system is consisted of three main components, including 
“heat production”, “Transmission and distribution system” and “Central pumping stations/ in-building 
equipment”, which have been depicted in Figure 4. The three aforementioned sections have been 
illustrated in the following [10]. 
==PAGE:25==

Figure 4: The visualization of the different components of geothermal-based district heating system (adapted from [10]) 
Heat Production 
 
Heat production is the initial component of geothermal-based district heating system, performing as the 
feed stuck of the rest of the sections (pumps, heat exchangers and so on). Such section contains the 
geothermal production/recharge field as the source of heat, wellhead heat exchangers and the ordinary 
fueled peaking station [10]. 
Transmission/distribution system 
 
This section acts as a bridge, supplying the heat extracted via geothermal wells to the costumers in the 
form of geothermal fluid or the injected fluid heated by geothermal source [10, 11].  
Central pumping stations/ in-building equipment 
 
The geothermal fluid delivered to the users could be either transmitted toward a central pumping 
station/heat exchanger or to be delivered directly to the heat exchangers placed in each user’s building. 
Moreover, with respect to the consumer’s demand, the utilization of thermal storage tanks can be also 
taken in to consideration [10, 11]. 
2.1.1.1.2 Benefits  
 
Geothermal-based district heating systems can provide a vast range of benefits covering various fields 
including environmental, technical and economic aspects, which have been listed in the following. 
Technical Aspects 
 
One of the most beneficial aspects of using geothermal energy for district heating is the reduction of 
demand on fossil fuels. By the application of geothermal energy in district heating, a huge amount of fossil 
==PAGE:26==

fuel in the forms of oil, gas and coal, which are traditionally burnt for the heating purposes, will be saved 
and can be set as an alternative during the peak demand period. Moreover, for the areas located in zones 
with appropriate geothermal potential such as “geothermal hot spots” with the average geothermal 
temperature exceeding 150oC, there would be a proper efficiency to use geothermal energy for both 
electricity generation and district heating rather than using fossil fuels [10]. 
Environmental Aspects 
 
The most striking feature of geothermal energy is to be among the environmentally friendly resources of 
energy supply. As noted, by the application of geothermal energy in district heating, the consumption of 
hydrocarbon-based fuels would be decreased resulting in less emission of greenhouse gasses such as 
carbon dioxide to the environment. Furthermore, by the reduction of combustion-based heating systems 
operating mostly by hydrocarbon fuels, the risk of ignition specifically within non-industrial zones would 
be highly diminished [10]. 
2.1.1.1.3 Geothermal District heating in Europe 
 
Currently there are more than 5000 systems for district heating in European Union that are mostly located 
in central and eastern Europe and Scandinavia region; however, fossil fuels still have the highest 
contribution. For instance, the conventional heat and power systems (CHP) fulfill nearly 80% of the 
demand for German district heating. The Polish government utilize coal for supplying nearly 80% (76% 
precisely) of its demand for district heating, while Italy and France utilize natural gas to manage 43% of 
their need; however, the application of geothermal energy in district heating (denoted as “geoDH”) has 
been increasing its portion among other types of supply by the current 450 projects, that are already in  
operation or planned to be started in European union(250 and 200 operating and planned projects 
respectively) , which can be highly effective to assist Europe in becoming independent of fossil fuels [11]. 
“geoDH” is not a novel route and its application traces back to the ancient times and the utilization of 
geothermal energy by Roman empire; in which, the discoveries of pipelines and water catchments in the 
ruins of bathrooms and houses have verified the application of geothermal heat. Chaudes Aigues city in 
France, could be stated as another old application of geothermal energy for district heating (currently 
operating); in which, the district heating system whose creation dates back to 1330, utilizes the energy 
extracted from Par hot spring with the approximate water temperature of 80oC (82 degrees centigrade) 
[11]. 
The progresses of technology have enabled the development of fields with reservoir temperatures as low 
as 50oC for district heating purposes. One must state that owing to the increase of hydrocarbon prices 
(peak in oil and gas price compared with 20th century) and the increasing trend among nations towards 
sustainability and independency in energy, a proper progress has been occurred in the “geoDH” field; 
however, still a huge gap remained between the current status and the potential in this field. Due to the 
proper potential of the utilization of geothermal energy in district heating, several east and central 
European nations such as Slovenia, Czech Republic, Poland, Slovakia and Hungary have been using 
“geoDH”; however, the current operating status is far behind from the real potential. Moreover, other 
countries such as France, Germany and Italy have set tremendous goals to be achieved by 2020; for which, 
big amendment in the policies regarding procedure of financial support has been made. Among European

2.1.2 Geothermal usage for power generation 
 
Despite the long history of direct-use application of geothermal resources, the implementation of 
geothermal energy for power generation has a short history, which traces back to early 20th century. Italy 
could be stated as the pioneer in the utilization of geothermal energy for power generating purposes in 
1904 in Tuscany, Larderello field [5]. The utilization of geothermal energy for power generation initiated 
as an experimental program in the cited field and then commercially approved by 1913 with the continues 
production until present time with the exception of second world war period [2]. In general, power 
generator-Geothermal application could be divided in two main categories, which are stated in the 
following [2]. 
• 
Steam flash 
• 
Binary cycle 
In the case of ‘Steam flash’ method, the operation can be made for the geothermal cases; in which, the 
reservoir fluid has the minimum temperature of 150oC. Even though the temperature in the geothermal 
fields, which are opted for power generating purposes, exceeds the boiling temperature of water, owing 
to the high amount of sub-surface pressure, most of the geothermal fields are merely consisted of water 
in liquid phase and are categorized as liquid-dominated. In such cases, some portion of the liquid phase 
might be turned to vapor due to the drilled hole, the production of water and the release of sub-surface 
pressure (the so called “borehole flash phenomenon”), resulting in the implementation of separators in 
the power plants for segregating the resulted phases. The attained steam would be used as the feedstock 
for the turbines and the liquid phase will be directed back to the geothermal reservoir. The cited technic 
==PAGE:38==
Chapter 2. Literature review 
20 
is stated as ‘Flashed-Steam’ power plant, which is economically approved and is illustrated in Figure 21 
[1, 2, 22]. 
 
Figure 21: The schematic of 'Steam flash' power generation method [2, 5] 
It should be noted that in some cases, the steam is the dominant phase and liquid phase is constrained to 
be found merely in the small pores and fractures due to the capillary pressure. In the cited case, the 
development of the geothermal power plant varies, which exempts the requirement of having a 
separator. It can be stated that in economic point of view, such systems are in a great value, albeit not so 
frequent to be found and merely Larderello and Geysers geothermal fields in Italy and United states are 
the current operating fields from this category [1]. 
By the introduction of ‘Binary Cycle’ technique in 1980s, the minimum temperature requirement of 150oC 
can be neglected. In the cited technique, by the application of a secondary fluid (usually pentane or 
ammonia) that has a low boiling-point (evaporating) temperature, the produced water that has a lower 
temperature compared with the previous case (‘Steam flash’), which is not capable of steam production, 
is utilized as a heat source for boiling the second fluid. Owing to the low boiling point of the second fluid, 
the ‘Binary Cycle’ technique enables the economic utilization of geothermal fields with lower reservoir 
temperatures (as low as 90oC). A schematic of the binary method has been illustrated in Figure 22 [5, 23]. 
==PAGE:39==

Figure 22: The visualization of binary cycle method for power generating geothermal application[2, 5] 
The ‘binary cycle’ method can be implemented in various routes; among which, the most frequently used 
one could be stated as the Rankin cycle that uses a single fluid as the so-called secondary fluid under the 
constant pressure and a fixed boiling (evaporating) temperature. In such case, the method is cited as 
Organic Rankin cycle (ORC) if the secondary fluid utilized in the system is a hydrocarbon (For instance, 
pentane or butane). On the other hand, the system designed by Aleksandr I. Kalina in 1980, which is called 
the Kalian cycle system, utilizes a mixture of water and ammonia as the secondary fluid under the constant 
pressure condition. Owing to the fact that in this case, the system is not comprised of a single secondary 
fluid, the boiling temperature (evaporation) can be continuously amended by altering the water/ammonia 
ratio, enabling the system to have a better efficiency with a more appropriate thermal efficiency 
compared with the ordinary Rankin cycle system. The originally designed system is working with water 
and ammonia; however, the system can be expanded using various mixtures as the secondary fluid [2]. 
2.2 Geothermal resources 
To have a proper assessment of the geothermal resources, conducting an economic feasibility 
investigation, which takes into account the technical point of view via considering various technical tests 
in different aspects such as geology, drilling and production, seems to be essential. Taking into account 
the illustrated assessments, geothermal resources can be categorized in the commonly named ‘McKelvy’ 
diagram, which is shown in Figure 23 [2, 24]. 
==PAGE:40==
Chapter 2. Literature review

Figure 23: The illustration of geothermal resources in the McKelvy diagram [2, 24] 
It must be stated that the economic range of geothermal resources might be altered with respect to the 
current and future progresses of technology. For instance, taking into account the utilized technology in 
the last decades (1978), the maximum feasible depth of a geothermal well to be economically approved 
was approximately 3 kilometers; however, the current progresses of technology have made it possible to 
drill and develop deeper wells economically. The geothermal resources utilized in Unterhaching 
(Germany), Cooper Basin (Australia), Soultz (France) with the depth range of 3.58, 4.42 and 5 kilometers 
successively could be stated as the main examples of attaining economic feasibility in deeper depth scopes 
by the progress of technology [2, 25, 26].  
A geothermal Resource base could be divided in to three main categories of ‘hydrothermal convection’, 
‘Thermal conduction’ and ‘high-level magma’ with respect to heat transporting medium. The term 
‘hydrothermal convection’ refers to systems; in which, most of the heat transfer occurs through the 
convection principles by the means of hot liquid or steam circulation. Convection mainly occur in the 
medium with high rock permeability owing to the heating buoyancy effect and the corresponding fluid 
thermal expansion. In such cases, hot fluid/steam tends to rise and as soon as it is cooled down, deposits 
to the lower sections (that will be heated again) [27]. On the other hand, heat conduction occurs via 
transmission of heat from hot zones to colder zone without fluid movement and in geothermal aspect 
occurs mostly through solid rocks and the effect of pore fluid is in less concern. The key parameters for 
heat flow through conduction could be stated as rock conductivity and temperature gradient [27-29]. It 
must be stated that the current conventional methods of power generation through geothermal 
resources including ‘steam flash’ and ‘binary-cycle’ are operating on resources of ‘hydrothermal 
==PAGE:41==
Chapter 2. Literature review 
23 
convection’ type. Operation through ‘Thermal conduction’ systems is assumed to be marginal in economic 
point of view. The high-level magma systems are not currently feasible to be developed and are entitled 
‘Future geothermal resource’; however, their significance as being the sub-volcanic source of heat for 
hydrothermal convection systems should not be neglected. The illustrated systems have been depicted in 
Figure 24; in which, the geothermal gradient yields the approximate result of 30, 40 and 18oC per kilometer 
for ‘Continent’ (younger sections), ‘Volcanic zones’ and ‘Older continent’ (older zones) respectively. A 
general fact that can be vividly noticed for all of the illustrated cases is that the deeper the well is drilled; 
the higher geothermal energy will be accessed [2].  
 
Figure 24: The visualization of depth and heat relationship. The red dots are added based on the experimental data attained 
from Japanese wells [2, 30] 
As stated previously, according to the geothermal gradient principle, the increase of depth leads to a 
higher temperature; however, in technical point of view, permeability decreases by the increase of depth, 
which has been depicted through Figure 25 [31].Permeability is a crucial parameter that controls the flow 
efficiency in the pores and is a matter of rock porosity and fracture density of rock layers. Considering the 
basic principle of heat convection (fluid circulation) and owing to significance of permeability for fluid 
transfer through rock pores, the hydrothermal convection resources that operate mostly based on heat 
convection are constrained to shallow depth. The depth at which, the so-called ‘Brittle-plastic transition’ 
is violated is the point that rock permeability is ceased, and all the pores and fractures will be blocked due 
to rocks viscous behavior. The cited issue confirms the limit of depth range for hydrothermal convection 
resources [2, 32].

By the recent progresses of technology, the feasibility of utilizing the geothermal resources in the deeper 
sections and in the zones of ‘thermal conduction’ has been fulfilled; for which, by the creation of artificial 
fractures and the circulation of water, the geothermal heat will be extracted. This method enables the 
utilization of geothermal resources for the depth and temperatures higher than the brittle-plastic 
transition point, which means, the applicability of using geothermal resources is not limited to special 
countries and it can be used worldwide. The cited method is entitled ‘Enhanced/Engineering Geothermal 
System’ and is also known as ‘Hot Dry Rock’, which has been initially investigated in the united states of 
America and Japan and its commercial applicability has been sought in Australia, France and Germany [1, 
2, 6]  
2.2.1 Distribution of geothermal resources 
2.2.1.1  Worldwide 
 
The global distribution of the conventional geothermal resources (Hydrothermal resources with the 
reservoir temperature above 150oC) has been depicted through Appendix-A 1,  in the appendix. It can be 
noticed that the geothermal resources are not regularly distributed. Generally, the hot zones, which are 
suitable for developing power plants for the utilization of hydrothermal resources, are in the vicinity of 
zones with volcanic activities and their heat source is the magnificently hot underlying magma [1]. The 
latter refers to the plates that move away from each other, which are also known as divergent boundaries. 
The driving source of such movement is provided by the convection of the plastic rocks placed below the 
lithosphere, leading to the evacuation of hot magma from the separation zone. The Midocean ridges could 
be stated as the most significant example; in which, such type of movement can be witnessed. Convergent 
boundaries refer to the boundaries that move toward each other and collide. An example of such plate 
==PAGE:43==
Chapter 2. Literature review 
25 
movement could be stated as ‘Hymalayas’ that has been occurred as a result of the convergent 
movements of Indian and Asian plates. The most frequent type of convergent boundaries occurs between 
an oceanic and continental plate through the so-called process of subduction. In which, the rather denser 
oceanic plate will be forwarded below the continental plate leading to its melting due the significant 
temperature of the subduction area. The most well-known example of such type of movement could be 
cited as the so-called ring of fire in the Pacific Ocean [1, 2].Owing to the fact that spreading boundaries 
mostly occur in marine environments and due to the high expenditures of submarine operations, the 
majority of geothermal energy is exploited from the areas of convergent boundaries; however, It must be 
noted that in some areas such as Great Rift Valley placed in the eastern section of Africa together with 
Salton Sea and Cerro Prieto in north America, the divergent boundaries have been occurred on land, which 
has eased their utilization. Iceland could be cited as another exception, which is located on the mid-
Atlantic ridge; for which, due to the significant volcanic activities of the hot spot, the ridge occurs onshore 
[1].

2.2.1.3 Geothermal Energy in Denmark 
2.2.1.3.1 History 
 
The history of geothermal energy utilization in Denmark is tracing back to 1983, when Dong Energy (Dansk 
Olie & Naturgas) initiated its investigation to find the possibility of geothermal energy exploitation in 
Denmark. DONG energy ceased its activities in the geothermal field by 2010 and since then the 
investigations and the development of geothermal resources have been implemented by the 
collaboration of GEUS and district heating companies. According to the current Danish legislations no 
funding is dedicated to geothermal plants; however, owing to the high fuels tax and the great interest in 
the reduction of carbon dioxide, there is an increasing willingness in the development of geothermal 
projects by district heating companies and the municipalities [33]. The initial studies regarding geothermal 
energy resources in Denmark initiated earlier by the comprehensive studies of Michelsen in 1981, which 
takes into account the petrophysical (seismic) and the recorded well data attained from onshore wells in 
Denmark, with the main focus on the sand stone aquifers of the depth range of 2000 to 3000 meters [34]. 
The cited results were used in the ‘Atlas of geothermal resources’ provided by the European commission 
that has signified the most appropriate areas with respect to the development of geothermal activities 
[35-37]. 
 In the beginning it was assumed that the deep and hot geothermal reservoirs would be an ideal case to 
be developed in Denmark; for which, 3 wells were tested in the depth of 3 kilometer in the sandstone 
reservoirs of Gassum formation placed in the northern Jutland. The results illustrated that despite the 
proper porosity of sand stone, the permeability was not found satisfying, leading to the change of the 
exploitation strategy of geothermal resources from deep hot reservoirs to rather colder reservoirs placed 
in the shallower section with more appropriate permeability [37]. 
2.2.1.3.2 Areas with the proper potential  
 
The potential geothermal reservoirs in Denmark are covered with four well known structural features 
including ‘Danish Basin’, ‘Ringkøbing-Fyn High’, ‘Sorgenfrei-Tornquist zone’ and ‘North German Basin’, 
which have been depicted in Figure 31 [37]. A brief description of the illustrated sections has been 
provided in the following.

Danish Basin 
 
As it has been depicted in Figure 32, Danish Basin is located in the north of ‘Jylland’ province, which is 
bounded by ‘Ringkøbing-Fyn High’ and ‘Sorgenfrei-Tornquist’ sections in the north east and south

Permian-Cenozoic basin- fill’ has approximately a uniform thickness over the basin with some thickenings 
up to 3 kilometers (9 kilometer) in the north east (toward ‘Sorgenfrei-Tornquist’).The layers with the 
geological age of ‘Triassic-Lower Cretaceous’ are distributed with the uniform thickness in most of the 
basin with some exceptions toward ‘Ringkøbing-Fyn High’. Owing to the uplifting in ‘Ringkøbing-Fyn High’ 
and ‘Danish Basin’ during the ‘Middle Jurassic’, the abbreviation of ‘Triassic-Lower Jurassic’ sequence has 
been occurred by the so-called ‘Base Middle Jurassic Unconformity’ that has been depicted in Figure

Due to the occurrence of subsidence in the ‘middle- late Jurassic’ time, the onlap of the younger geological 
sequences of ‘Upper Jurassic- Lower Cretaceous’ has been done onto the younger sections (‘Upper

reservoirs of ‘Lower Triassic’ in shallower depth in the region of ‘Ringkøbing-Fyn High’ [37]. 
Sorgenfrei-Tornquist Zone 
 
As has been depicted in Figure 31, ‘Sorgenfrei-Tornquist Zone’ crosses over ‘Kattegat’, north of Jutland, 
southern sections of Sweden and the north section of ‘Øresund’. This zone is highly ‘Block faulted’; in 
which, the tilted fault blocks of ‘Paleozoic’ has been overlain by the rather thick deposits of ‘Mesozoic’ 
time. As illustrated in Figure 32, the subsidence that has been occurred during the previously discussed 
uplift in the ‘Middle Jurassic’ led to the deposition of thick sand stone layers, which has formed highly 
appropriate reservoirs depicted as ‘Haldager sand’ [37].   
2.2.1.3.3 Reservoir with highest potential 
 
As can be seen through Figure 32, the reservoirs with highest potential can be placed between the 
geological time sequences of ‘Triassic- Lower Cretaceous’. The cited area has been chosen as a petroleum 
exploitation target since 1935, which is highly investigated with various drilled wells and the implemented 
seismic activities. Various reginal investigations have led to the identification of different areas with

geothermal potential including ‘Bunter’ sand stone in the ‘Lower-Upper Triassic’, ‘Skagerrak formation,’ 
‘Gassum formation’ placed in ‘Upper Triassic-Lower Jurassic’, sand stone formation of ‘Haldager’ in 
‘Middle Jurassic’ and the ‘Frederikshavn’ formation in ‘Upper Jurassic- Lower Cretaceous’[38-40].  Apart 
from the cited formations, potential reservoirs can be locally found in some other formations such as the 
Fine grained sand stone zones in the so-called ‘F-II’ member in the ‘Fjerritslev’ formation together with 
the ‘Arnager Grønsand’ formation, which is located in the eastern section of Zealand [37]. 
Bunter Formation 
 
The ‘Bunter’ formation with the lithology of ‘Sandstone’ is located in the southern section of ‘Ringkøbing- 
Fyn High’ and covers some sections of ‘Danish Basin’. As can be deduced from Figure 31, through the 
north east margin with the ‘Danish Basin’, the ‘Bunter’ formation is graded to ‘Skagerrak formation’[37-
39]. The lithology of ‘Bunter’ formation is mainly consisted of fine-grained sand stones, which were 
deposited in the dry continental territory with the dominance of aeolian dunes, fluvial channels and 
marginal marine facies [37]. 
Skagerrak Formation   
 
Skagerrak Formation suffers from the lack of information arising from the low level of investigation; 
however, owing to its distribution along the north and north east borders of ‘Danish Basin’ together with 
the coarse-grained sand stone lithology (improperly sorted), which is interbedded with some layers of 
claystone, one might anticipate the possible deposition in the lakes and alluvial fans [37]. 
2.2.1.3.3.1 Gassum Formation 
 
‘Gassum’ formation can be spotted through whole section of Danish area, whose thickness varies from 
100 to 150 meters with the highest thickness spotted in the ‘Sorgenfrei-Tornquist’ zone [40, 41].The 
lithology of the formation is mostly consisted of fine to medium grained sand stone (with some locally 
coarse-grained sections), which are interbedded with layers of heteroliths and claystone together with 
thin layers of coal. The deposition of sandstone has occurred via the continues shoreline progradation. 
With respect to the distribution of the lithology, one can mention the dominance of fluvial and estuarine 
type sand stone toward the ‘Lower-middle’ section of ‘Gassum’ formation in the area of ‘Sorgenfrei-
Tornquist’ zone [37]. 
Haldager sand formation  
 
‘Haldager sand stone formation yields the approximate thickness of 200 meter in the ‘Sorgenfrei-
Tornquist’ formation, which follows a decreasing trend toward north east and south west directions [40, 
41].In terms of lithology, the thick layers of fine to coarse-grained sand stone, replace with some layers of 
coal, contribute to the formation. The deposition has been placed in fluvial, shallow marine, estuarine and 
lacustrine environment [37].

Frederikshavn formation is one of the six formations of Danish basin, whose age traces back to the Upper 
Jurassic – lowermost Cretaceous (Figure 33). It can be interpreted that the cited formation was formed 
between 138 and 152 million years ago[37, 42]. 
History of the formation 
 
The early definition of Frederikshavn formation was made in 1966[43].Afterwards, in the next decade 
(1978), the cited formation was reduced in rank and redefined as a Member of Bream formation [44]; 
however, in 1984, the Bream formation was decided to have no place in the new sorting, resulting in 
Frederikshavn to be redefined back to a formation by the late of 1980th [37, 45]. 
 
Lithology and Environment  
 
The whole formation can be distributed in two major categories of sandstone and siltstone, which has 
been stated in more details in the following:

It must be stated that in the illustrated formation, the layers with the lithology of siltstone and sandstone 
are interbedded via narrow silty greyish black and calcareous clay stones, which form 2–3 generally 
coarsening upwards units separated by claystone-dominated intervals. In the lower and upper sections of 
the formation, Glauconite, bivalve and ammonites containing layers could be commonly found [37, 43, 
46, 47]. 
Sandstone can be frequently found in the middle part of the formation (in the so-called ‘Sorgenfrei–
Tornquist’). In the vicinity of the north-eastern basin, the rather thin layers of coal beds can be spotted 
(upper sections of formation) [37]. 
 
In terms of the environment of the sedimentation, it can be noted that the deposition occurred mostly in 
the shallow to deep marine areas. Non-marine conditions prevailed on the Skagerrak- Kattegat Platform 
and locally in the Sorgenfrei–Tornquist Zone during the deposition of the parts of the middle coarsening- 
upwards unit and the lower part of the upper unit [37, 43, 44]. 
 
Distribution and thickness  
 
The formation is located in the eastern section of the Danish basin; in which, the thickest zone could be 
found in Sorgenfrei–Tornquist Zone with the recorded thickness of 230 meters. On the other hand, the 
zones with the relatively thin thickness have been spotted in the central sections of Danish Basin, locating 
in the westernmost sections of Frederikshavn formation. 
The upper and lower boundaries of Frederikshavn formation (Vedsted formation and Børglum formation 
respectively) could be defined using the result attained via gamma ray and sonic log. The transition from 
Frederikshavn formation with the lithology of sandstone to the clay stones of Vedsted formation could be 
illustrated with an increase and a gradual decrease in gamma ray and sonic log results respectively. On 
the other hand, the transition from Børglum formation with the lithology of claystone to the rather coarse-

and sonic log results successively [37].

characteristic leading to a great deterioration in porosity and permeability. For instance, the investigations

Frederikshavn formation illustrates the decrease of the thickness of sand stone (as a lithology with 
appropriate potential for a geothermal reservoir) from east to west, occurring owing to the existence of 
clay [37, 48].

2.2.1.4.2 Copenhagen plant 
 
In 1999, a group comprised of the district heat supplying companies and heat and power producers known 
as ‘HGS’ was formed by DONG energy with the aim of constructing a geothermal plant in Copenhagen 
area.The initial location for implementing the drilling was set in the vicinity of Amager Combined heat and 
power plant (CHP).The drilling was made to the depth of 2.7 kilometer in 2002 that can cover the ‘Bunter 
formation at the depth of 2.6 kilometer. The second well was drilled with the step-out of 1.3 kilometer in 
2003. The geothermal water had the salinity of 19% and the temperature of 74oC, which was extracted

2.2.1.4.3 Sønderborg  
 
The initial investigations for ‘Sønderborg’ area was made through seismic explorations in 2007, concluding 
that the wells should be drilled outside the town. The production and injection well were drilled by 2010 
with the aim of extracting energy from ‘Bunter’ formation at the expected depth of 2.1 kilometer. Due to 
the failure in spotting ‘Bunter’ formation at the cited depth, the plans were changed by installing a gravel 
pack at the depth of 1.2 kilometer in ‘Gassum’ formation, which was followed by some operational issues 
due to high skin factor. Acid injection was implemented to solve the cited issue leading to the final 
geothermal water production with the temperature of 48oC and the salinity of 15% with the designed 
electrical generation of 12MW[33]. 
2.2.1.4.4 Future plans   
 
Some of the Danish companies working in the field of district heating have made a large-scale plan to 
establish a geothermal plant with 11 wells in Copenhagen; in which, each well deviates more than 1 
kilometer from the reservoir. By the implementation of 3 large-scale star-plants in Copenhagen, the 
attainment of 200MW heat could be fulfilled by 2050, which can reach up to 400MW as a long term goal 
[33].

4.1.10 
Simulation Period

4.3 Economic perspective 
4.3.1 General overview of geothermal plant for district heating 
 
As previously described, the main goal of a geothermal plant for district heating purpose is the economic 
transfer of the extracted heat of geothermal source (geothermal water) to district heating consumers and 
injecting back the cooled water to the reservoir. The cited plant is comprised of various sections such as 
wells, pumps, heat exchangers, pipelines and so on, whose application mostly depend on the type of 
geothermal reservoir, which is dealt with. For instance, in the cases that geothermal water temperature 
is lower than the required district heating temperature, heat pumps must be implemented to fulfill the 
cited necessity. A general overview of a district heating geothermal plant has been provided through 
Figure 95 [68]. 
 
Figure 95: General schematic of a district heating geothermal plant with the most important sections, more detailed than Figure 
4 [68] 
With respect to the provided illustration via Figure 95, cool water (water with lower temperature of 
geothermal water) will be injected to the reservoir, which will be heated and produced back to the surface. 
Afterwards, as previously illustrated, if the resulted fluid temperature is not sufficient, it must pass 
through heat exchanger and then through a heat pump to accelerate its heat and fulfill the demands. It 
must be noted that based on the consultancy of Ross offshore company, the application of electric pumps 
is more widespread compared with other types of heat pumps due to their technical and economic 
advantage. On the other hand, in the cases that extracted heat through geothermal system is higher than 
the required one for district heating application, the excess of heat could be extracted directly through 
heat exchangers. As previously noted, the type of design in the surface plant and the required facilities 
vary with respect to different parameters including technical and economics; among which, temperature, 
==PAGE:125==
Chapter 4. Simulation Results 
107 
production rate and depth of well play the key role in deriving a successful geothermal district heating 
plant [68, 69].  
As noted in technical section, In the current study, heat production and storage has been considered 
through a seasonal cycle; through which, during the cold season (considered as the first half of year) cool 
water with surface temperature (considered as approximately 10oC) will be injected to the reservoir and 
then after a shut in period of 15 days, which is implemented for assisting the heat equilibrium in the 
reservoir, injector and producer will be switched. It must be stated that during the second half of the year, 
which is known as the hot season (with less heat demand), injection temperature is 60oC. Based on the 
consultancy of Ross offshore, this study focuses on the investigation of reservoir capabilities considering 
all the rest of conditions to be equal for all the cases. As a result, the routs for supplying the cited energy 
for providing hot water with the temperature of 60oC, have not been considered in the current study. In 
the following, a brief overview of the most important surface facilities utilized for geothermal energy plant 
for district heating has been provided. Afterwards the economic discussion of the technically approved 
sections has been provided. 
4.3.2 Heat Exchangers 
 
Heat exchangers are types of facilities used with the aim of transferring heat between two (or more) fluids 
without allowing them to be mixed, which can be used for both cooling and heating purposes [70].In 
geothermal applications, heat exchangers are utilized in the system for fulfilling the efficient transfer of 
heat from geothermal water to the district heating fluid. Owing to the possible high percentage of salt in 
the extracted geothermal fluid, heat exchangers are usually constructed from corrosion resistant 
materials such as titanium. For boosting the efficiency of heat exchangers, they are usually made in series 
of sheets, which are placed close to each other for diminishing the energy loss(known as plate heat

4.3.3 Heat pumps 
 
Heat pump is a facility with the functionality of transferring heat from a source to a so-called heat sink. In 
easy words, they transfer thermal energy in a direction, which cannot be made spontaneously (such as 
extracting heat from a colder area and releasing it in a warmer region) [73]. The main applications of heat 
pumps in geothermal energy extraction for district heating utilization has been stated in the following 
[69]. 
• 
Boosting geothermal extracted fluid to the required temperature of district heating (when 
geothermal fluid has lower temperature than district heating fluid) 
• 
Maximum absorb of heat from the returned fluid before re-injection to the reservoir (due to the 
lower temperature than reservoir, heat cannot be transferred spontaneously) 
In terms of variety, heat pumps are mainly constructed in two types including absorption heat pumps 
(shown in Figure 95 ) and electrical heat pumps (also known as compressor heat pumps). The cited pumps 
vary in terms of governmental tax, required transfer of energy, operational costs (such as supplying 
electricity and biomass for compressor and absorption heat pumps respectively).The main key factor 
affecting the choice of utilizing the cited pumps is the efficiency of the pumps, which is symbolized as COP. 
The cited term implies on the ratio of internal energy supply to the delivered heat. It must be stated that 
the key parameter for determining the efficiency of pump is temperature difference of geothermal fluid 
and district heating fluid. The less the cited difference is, the higher the efficiency will be (due to the lower 
required input energy). In a general overview, electrical heat pumps deliver a rather higher efficiency 
compared with absorption heat pumps. Moreover, the application of absorption heat pumps requires the 
installment of boilers for fulfilling the required input energy, which will be complex to be set in urban 
areas. As a result, in the current study, Ross offshore company suggested the utilization of electrical heat 
pumps for the sake of economic evaluation.

4.5 Technical results 
4.5.1 First run 
 
As illustrated in the technical section, simulation of reservoir implemented for the areas that had a 
succession of 3 main geothermal formation in northern Jylland (Frederikshavn, Gassum and Haldager). 
Afterwards, the effect of depth change was investigated by relocating top of each formation with respect 
to the topography data, which were attained from the GEUS data source. After the end of the cited step, 
some of the locations including Vedsted-1, Oddesund-1, Mejrup-1, Mors-1 and Farsø-1 were exempted 
from the rest of analysis due to their poor performance. A more detailed overview about the implemented 
decision has been stated in the following.  
In Vedsted-1 well, With respect to Figure 64, the maximum production rate that could be yielded for 
Vedsted well for the first depth limit case (top of Frederikshavn formation of 800 meter) is nearly 400 
cubic meters per day, which would be less than 20 cubic meters per hour. Based on Ross offshore 
consultancy, in the cases with production rate of less than 100 cubic meters per hour, specific pumps must 
be implemented to boost the production above the economic limit of 100 cubic meters per hour. Owing 
to the fact that providing the required technical work including the supply of energy and technicians 
requires more budget investments and with respect to the rather similar temperature status with the 
other approved candidates (approximately 30oC), the preference would be focusing on other candidates. 
The condition in Oddesund-1 well is even harsher with the maximum production rate potential of nearly 
70 cubic meters of geothermal water per day, which is equal to less than 3 cubic meters per hour (Figure 
66).The cited value is too low to be considered as an economical choice to proceed with. Digging more 
into details, the illustration made through Figure 50 depicts the lack of proper reservoir transmissivity for 
the investigated zone (0 Dm). The very low reservoir transmissivity in Frederikshavn formation of the cited 
location is due to the very low distribution of highly permeable sandstone layers within the area 
(visualization of wells attached as an external file). As a result, despite the proper temperature status of 
the reservoir, which is owing to the placement in an appropriate depth, reservoir is not capable to deliver 
the energy in an economical route. Mors-1, Mejrup-1 and Farsø-1 wells yielded the maximum potential 
rate of 600,160 and 250 cubic meters per day respectively within their first run (Figure 68, Figure 69 and 
Figure 82 successively), which are equal to less than 25, 7 and 11 cubic meters per hour respectively. 
Owing to their rather low reservoir transmissivity, production rate is not expected to reach the economic 
limit of 100 cubic meters per hour in deeper depth sets. Moreover, in the case of drilling deeper wells, the 
drilling relevant expenditures would be higher, so the cited locations would not be an interesting aim to 
pursue. 
4.5.2 Second run 
 
For the remaining cases, the second set of simulation was conducted. The main reason for this step was 
to investigate the effect of well distance on the simulation results especially on temperature and rate 
conditions. As a general overview, decreasing the horizontal distance between the injection and 
production well had a positive effect on temperature and production rate status. As stated previously, the 
production rate in the simulations is set to be equal with injection rate. In the cases that producer and 
==PAGE:131==

injector are placed closer to each other, the movement of injected water would be eased toward 
production well. 
 The cited condition would be more vivid in cases with higher horizontal permeability such as Sæby-1 and 
Gassum-1 well; for which, injection rate experienced and increase of approximately 2000 cubic meters 
per day (from 11000 to 13000 and from 160001 to 18000 cubic meter per day respectively), which is 
illustrated through Figure 91 and Figure 92 respectively. Similarly, temperature status was positively 
affected with an average increase of nearly 5oC for the specified locations. The appropriate temperature 
response for the implemented sensitivity analysis is owing to the fact that by diminishing the distance 
between producer and injector, the cooling and heating effect of seasonal changing of the injection 
temperature would be more stabilized; however, setting the wells too close to each other should be 
avoided due to the fast break through of injection water to the producer, resulting in the lack of proper 
heating period for the injected water. Another vivid change of status occurred in Horsens-1 well, whose 
production rate increased nearly 700 cubic meters per day ;however, its temperature was not sensibly 
affected, which can be interpreted as its lower reservoir transmissivity with respect to other 
candidates(Figure 87). Comparing the intensity of results for Sæby-1, Gassum-1 and Horsens-1 implies on 
the significance of reservoir transmissivity (including horizontal permeability) on the quality of reservoir 
(Figure 84,Figure 87 and Figure 86 respectively). With respect to Figure 83 and Figure 85, the implemented 
sensitivity analysis has shown a positive effect in Voldum-1 and Hylleberg-1 well as well; however, its 
effect is negligible compared with previously stated locations. Reservoir transmissivity for Voldum-1 and 
Hylleberg-1 wells is 9 and 3 Darcy meters respectively (Figure 47 and Figure 55 respectively), while, 
Gassum-1, Sæby-1 and Horsens-1 wells depicted the reservoir transmissivity of 97, 84 and 60 Darcy meters 
successively (Figure 53,Figure 49 and Figure 54 respectively). Owing to the proper behavior of Gassum-1, 
Sæby-1 and Horsens-1 well, the cited locations have been chosen to pursue further investigations.

successfully fulfilled the technical criteria of production rate and temperature status (Sæby-1, Gassum-1

With respect to Figure 97, one can state that the general belief on the higher potential of the geothermal

rate and drilling/maintenance expenditures play a significant role as well. For instance, as depicted

(as a result of shallow depth) have covered the  defect of low reservoir temperature and the cited case 
has appeared more economically attractive compared with  Horsens-1 well.

5 Conclusion 
 
Based on the current study, one can state the proper potential of Frederikshavn formation for geothermal 
based district heating studies. The comprehensive investigation, which is performed in the initial part of 
thesis considering all of the currently investigated zones, whose data are available through the GEUS and 
Ross offshore database, was narrowed down to the zones with best performance regarding technical 
aspect. Later, the economic feasibility of the technically approved candidates was evaluated by 
performing business case analysis considering the current financial policies of Ross offshore company. 
According to the results, it can be stated that for a successful geothermal plant, temperature and 
production rate status play a vital role in the technical performance, which should be considered in line 
with the operational constraints of a project, mainly denoted as drilling and maintenance expenditures. 
The results attained from Sæby-1 well implied on the fact that the technical and economic feasibility of 
the project is not merely a matter of a specific constraint. In other word, if It must be taken into 
consideration that a geothermal reservoir could be still technically and economically approved even if 
temperature status is not completely satisfying. On the other hand, the cases with proper temperature 
condition, could not be always economically satisfying (such as Horsens-1 well). 
The other interesting fact, which was pursued in the current work, was the investigation of the possible 
heat transfer from the underlying formations (Gassum and Haldager) to Frederikshavn formation. The 
cited goal was analyzed owing to the fact that in terms of the possible existence of heat transfer between 
the aforementioned zones, the drilling/maintenance expenditures would be highly decreased. In other 
word, the exploitation of deeper zones, which are placed in Gassum and Haldager formation, that have a 
more appropriate temperature status, could become technically and economically more feasible, in case 
of heat transfer to the shallower zone. Unfortunately, the temperature analysis, which was performed in 
Sæby-1 well as the candidate with the lowest depth margin between Frederikshavn and the underlaying 
formation, depicted that during the 30 years of the implemented simulation, the temperature alteration 
on the top of Haldager formation (the formation below Frederikshavn formation) was merely 0.02 degrees 
Celsius. The attained results depict that shale marginal shale layer between Frederikshavn and the 
underlaying formation performs as a seal preventing heat to be transferred to the operating wells in 
Frederikshavn formation. 
In terms of heat storage, all of the zones performed properly, which is due to the fact that the injected 
heat to the reservoir was properly sealed by the overlaying shale layers.  In the cases with higher reservoir 
transmissivity, the cited stored heat was utilized more properly due to the better transmission to the 
cooled area, which was affected by cold water injection. In other word, the wells; in which, lower 
temperature difference between the production and storage period was recorded, have higher technical 
and economic potential to be used for   geothermal based district heating plant.

5.1 Future studies 
• 
The current study signified the areas with highest technical and economic potential for 
geothermal based district heating plants; however, more detailed subsurface studies would be 
necessary prior to decision making plan. 
 
• 
In the current study, the focus was made on assessing the capability of reservoirs in heat 
production and storage  and the routes for supplying the injected heat during the storage period 
was  not considered ; however, due to the significance of the cited      matter, further studies could 
be made using the current work as a base. 
 
• 
The geochemical status of the reservoir, including the precipitation and the minerals and the 
possible corrosion are significant parameters, which should be investigated to have a better and 
more precise technical and economic evaluation. 
 
• 
The design of the geothermal plant including the detailed analysis of the required facilities with 
respect to the status of the reservoir and the economic policies of the operating company requires 
a detailed study, which can be confirmed in line with the current results. 
 
• 
Considering all technical and economic aspects, one steps yet required to be taken, which is the 
investigation of the area with respect to the heat demand of the residents. In other word, one 
must take into account that whether the closest area to the geothermal plant require the cited 
energy, or the heat supply should be transferred to other regions
