Abstract 
The monopile carrying the DTU IEA 15-Megawatt Offshore Reference Wind Turbine [23] has been analyzed 
in relation to buckling in three different load cases. This buckling analysis was done by using the buckling 
approach in Eurocode 3 (EC3) and Det Norske Veritas (DNV) and through nonlinear buckling analysis in 
ABAQUS. It was concluded the monopile wouldn’t buckle in these 3 load cases according to these different 
buckling approaches. 
A nonlinear buckling analysis in ABAQUS was done with implementation of different dimple imperfections. 
The different dimple imperfections were a general dimple imperfection and an axisymmetric weld 
depression imperfection. It was concluded the axisymmetric weld depression imperfection was more 
comparable to the buckling approach in EC3 due to the similar effects of imperfection depth. 
The characteristic buckling capacities obtained through nonlinear buckling analysis with weld depression 
imperfections were very similar to design values obtained through the buckling approaches in EC3 and DNV. 
A parameter study was conducted, and the thickness was the most important parameter for the monopile.

Project description 
Thin-walled cylindrical shells made of steel are used in a lot of different structures since they are an 
effective use of material. One of the challenges using thin-walled cylindrical shells is buckling, since 
cylindrical shells is very imperfection sensitive, meaning the buckling capacity is drastically reduced if there 
are imperfections in the cylindrical shell. A lot of research has been made designing buckling equations for 
cylindrical shells which take imperfections into consideration. The buckling equations need to be 
conservative and accurate enough to make efficient designs. 
Some commonly used equations in Europe for buckling of imperfect cylindrical shells are described in the 
standard named Eurocode 3 (EC3). In EC3 the initial imperfections are assumed to be a single dimple 
shaped dent somewhere on the cylindrical shell and an initial ovalization of the cross-section caused by 
fabrication errors. A draft version of Eurocode 3 is currently in development with some modifications to the 
official buckling approach, this modified buckling approach will also be used and comparisons between the 
official version and the draft version will be made. 
This project will focus on implementing dimple shaped dent imperfections described by EC3 in the Finite 
Element Analysis (FEA) program ABAQUS. The imperfections will be implemented in a model of a monopile 
foundation for a reference wind turbine with reference geometry and loads. The project will analyze 
different load combinations for the monopile and obtain a critical load case. The project will analyze how 
different dimple geometries affect the buckling capacity. The project will compare buckling capacities for 
the monopile obtained by utilizing the buckling approach in EC3 and DNV with the results obtained by 
introducing the geometric imperfections described in EC3 into the FEA model.

0. State of the art 
General definition of buckling 
Buckling is a failure mode which occurs when a slender element is affected by a high compressive stress. 
Buckling failure happens when the membrane strain energy in a structural element converts into bending 
strain energy without any change in external load. The reason this conversion is critical, is because the 
bending stiffness of slender structural elements is much smaller than the membrane (axial) stiffness. 
Because the bending stiffness is dependent on the moment of inertia, which for slender structures is much 
smaller than the cross-sectional area, which the axial stiffness is dependent on [7] [3]. Buckling is therefore 
important to consider when designing slender structures, especially in essential elements with large 
compressive stress. 
Buckling failure can occur without a total collapse of the structure, but the stiffness of the structure changes 
and stresses are redistributed which affects the structural behavior if the load is increased [2]. 
Bifurcation buckling 
A bifurcation point is the point where the buckling load is achieved and the load-displacement path of a 
structural element under compressive load can branch out into different paths [2]. The deformations of the 
structural element can change into new patterns after the bifurcation load [3]. Bifurcation buckling can 
occur in for example cylindrical shells, beams, and plates [2]. There are different types of postbuckling 
behavior (behavior after the bifurcation point has been reached) depending on the structure it can either be 
stable, neutral, unsymmetric, or unstable [2]. 
Figure 0.1 displays the different bifurcation buckling behaviors in [2] and [4], where P is the load applied and 
w is the characteristic deformations. The characteristic deformations could for example be out-of-plane 
displacements in a column under pure axial compression. In this case if the column is assumed ideal, the 
out-of-plane displacements are zero until the load reaches the bifurcation point where the displacements 
can either remain zero and increasing load can be applied without any out-of-plane displacement (this is the 
mathematically unstable solution), or the out-of-plane displacements can increase in different patterns as 
shown in Figure 0.1 (this is the mathematically stable solution).

If the structure is imperfect the buckling behavior can be quite different from the idealized bifurcation 
behavior of a perfect structure. This behavioral change is especially important in imperfection sensitive 
structures since the maximum load in some cases are drastically lower than the bifurcation load. The 
maximum buckling load in imperfect structures can’t be described by the bifurcation load because 
bifurcation analysis assumes idealized behavior. If the maximum load capacity drastically decreases due to 
imperfections, the structure is described as imperfection sensitive. Cylindrical shells are for example 
imperfection sensitive [2] [4] [6]. This report calls the maximum load on the load-displacement diagram the 
limit load. This describes the maximum load applied before buckling failure occurs.

Buckling of unstiffened cylindrical shells and monopiles 
Most monopiles are either cylindrical shells with constant thickness or cylindrical shells with stepwise 
variable wall thickness. Therefore, it is essential to describe the buckling behavior of cylindrical shells. 
Cylindrical shells can buckle in two separate modes, either by local shell buckling or global buckling. The 
critical limit load can be found by calculating the local shell buckling limit load and the global buckling limit 
load and using the smaller value. In some rare cases, an interaction between the two buckling modes can 
occur if the buckling capacities are similar [5]. Global buckling only occurs in cylindrical shells that are very 
long compared to their diameter. Local shell buckling is very critical in thin-walled cylindrical shells [6]. 
Classical bifurcation analysis assumes ideal structures. This overestimates the limit load for realistic 
cylindrical shells. This overestimation comes from factors influencing realistic cylindrical shells that are 
neglected in classical bifurcation analysis. The most important factors are (1) geometric imperfections since 
cylindrical shells are imperfection sensitive, (2) prebuckling deformations and stresses, and (3) boundary 
conditions and non-uniform loading [6]. 
 
Geometric imperfections 
Geometric imperfections are the primary factor in reduction of buckling capacity [6]. These imperfections 
==PAGE:11==
6 
are created in fabrication, transportation and during the lifetime of structures made of cylindrical shells. 
Since monopiles have a very large diameter they can be difficult to manufacture and transport which can 
cause large geometric imperfections. 
There are many ways to implement imperfections in finite element models, such as axisymmetric wave 
imperfections, imperfections taken from the first few buckling mode shapes in a linear bifurcation analysis 
(LBA), and creating a local dimple shaped perturbation in the mesh [12]. 
One of the most used imperfection types is the imperfection taken from the displacement shape in the first 
few buckling modes in a LBA, because it is easy to implement and gives an understanding of how 
imperfection sensitive the structure is. The biggest advantage with this imperfection type, is it has the most 
critical imperfection shape for the structure, meaning it will likely give a conservative limit load. The biggest 
disadvantages with this imperfection type are it is unrealistic, because it is a purely theoretical imperfection 
shape which isn’t typically observed in realistic cylindrical shells. This can cause the implementation of 
buckling modes and their magnitude to be a bit arbitrary for the designer, and this can have a big effect on 
the expected buckling capacity [6]. The other big disadvantage is the pre-buckling stiffness can be 
significantly reduced by some buckling mode imperfections, which is a phenomenon not observed in 
experiments [6]. 
Prebuckling deformations 
Prebuckling deformations and stresses can be local bending deformations caused by support conditions 
restraining radial movement of the shell wall [6]. This forces the cylindrical shell to deform instead of 
moving. This can occur in a monopile close to where it is supported by the seabed. 
Local prebuckling deformations can also occur close to locations with large changes in stiffness, for example 
close to sudden changes in thickness. This is important to analyze in the case of monopiles made of 
cylindrical shells with stepwise variable wall thickness, because it can change the buckling behavior. 
Experiments has shown the buckling capacity of cylindrical shells in pure compression is reduced by 10%-
20% due to prebuckling deformations [6]. 
 
Boundary conditions 
Boundary conditions can in some cases affect the buckling response of cylindrical shells. This is important 
for analyses made in this report because the official version of EC3-1-6 assumes specific boundary 
conditions varying for each load case. These boundary conditions are not relevant for monopiles. EC3-1-6 
requires both ends to be supported for some loads [1] whereas a monopile is only supported at the bottom. 
Nonuniform loading 
Nonuniform loading is an issue that can be caused by manufacturing inaccuracies or unintended 
eccentricities in connections. In some experiments the nonuniform loading reduced the buckling capacity 
under axial compression by approximately 1% when combined with geometric imperfections and 3%-5% 
without geometric imperfections [6]. Analyses made in this report include geometric imperfections, 
therefore nonuniform loading is neglected. It should be noted the effects could affect the results differently 
for monopiles, due to the application of multiple different loads. 
 
Welding connections 
An additional important factor when designing monopiles are the weld connections that are created during 
fabrication of the monopile. These weld connections can cause local changes in stiffness and create 
geometric imperfections along the weld, which can cause changes in the buckling capacity and behavior [6].

In this report the effect of a weld is investigated. 
 
Knockdown factor 
The most common way to include the effect of these factors in theoretical analysis, is to introduce a 
knockdown factor which compensates for the discrepancy between bifurcation theory and experiments on 
realistic cylindrical shells. NASA introduced a knockdown factor they named the Shell Buckling Knockdown 
Factor (SBKDF) in 1968 with focus on space launch vehicle structures. This factor considers the effects of 
geometric imperfections and is dependent on radius and thickness [6]. Eurocode 3 (EC3) has introduced an 
analysis method to compensate for the discrepancy with focus on cylindrical shells used in general 
structures [1], this is the primary theoretical analysis method utilized in this report. This method is more 
complicated and will be described in the next section. Det Norske Veritas (DNV) also has an analysis method 
which will be compared with the EC3 method. 
1. EC3 and DNV buckling approaches 
Eurocode 3-1-6s suggested buckling design of cylindrical shells  
There is currently an official version [1] and a draft version of EC3-1-6 [9] with a few differences in buckling 
approach. The biggest differences are the draft version has changed some of the buckling parameters, 
especially with focus on moment capacity. Additionally, references are given in the draft version, making it 
easier to understand the basis and assumptions of the analysis method. 
This project will compare the buckling approach from the official version of EC3-1-6 [1] with the draft 
version. Since the draft version of EC3-1-6 is still subject to change and isn’t a European Standard, it has 
more uncertainty in relation to buckling capacities. 
The buckling approach in the official version of EC3 is described in the following sections and all use [1] as

EC3s suggested implementation of imperfections 
Imperfections are implemented in Eurocode 3, by introducing four different imperfection types [1], that can 
occur because of realistic manufacturing and fabrication errors. (1) A dimple imperfection which could be a 
weld depression or could be a dent caused by an accidental drop of a shell part. (2) An out-of-roundness 
imperfection which can occur because of errors when turning the flat shell segments into cylindrical shells. 
(3) Unintended eccentricities in joints that can be caused by misalignment in shell segments when they are 
welded together. (4) Interface flatness is a small circumferential slope at an interface of a structure 
continuously supporting a shell (for example a foundation) and the shell itself, this could be caused by a 
small misalignment between the shells. 
Dimple imperfection 
The dimple imperfection is the most important type of imperfections in EC3 for the buckling capacity of 
cylindrical shells. Dimple imperfections reduce membrane stiffness locally, since the changes in geometry 
caused by a dimple are unfavorable against membrane stresses compared to the perfect geometry. The 
dimple imperfections should be located at areas with significant stresses.  
The maximum allowable depth of a dimple Δ𝑤0𝑥 and the maximum allowable height and width of a dimple

In this report the unintended eccentricities are neglected, because a lot of assumptions must be made to

should be noted that unintended eccentricity could affect results differently, since there are multiple loads 
on a monopile, but this is out of scope of this report.

Since the cylindrical shell in a monopile is a supporting structure, the interface flatness imperfection could 
occur between the monopile and the wind turbine. The size of the slope shouldn’t be above 0.001 radians 
according to the interface flatness tolerance. The interface flatness is neglected in this report.

2. Theory behind finite element method 
The general purpose of static analysis in the finite element method (FEM), is obtaining an accurate 
prediction of the structural response to external loads through numerical analysis. This can be done for very 
complex geometries and loads compared to what can be calculated with theory. The finite element method 
does this by discretizing a continuous body into a mesh of elements and nodes, then calculating the field 
variables for each of these nodes, and then interpolating the field variables through the elements to find 
the structural response at any given point. Field variables can for example be displacements, stresses, and 
strains in static analysis. 
The discretization is very important for the FEM since a continuous body has infinite degrees of freedom 
which isn’t possible to calculate. The discretization also greatly affects the accuracy and speed of an 
analysis, where a fine mesh with a lot of nodes and elements is slower but more accurate compared to a 
rougher mesh with fewer nodes and elements [15]. The FEM always yield approximate results due to this 
discretization. 
The FEA is considered an accurate solution if the mesh is converged, therefore a mesh convergence study is 
very important to consider when utilizing FEM. It is also important to use the mesh convergence study to 
find a mesh which isn’t too fine to reduce computation time and storage usage as much as possible while 
still finding accurate results.

Linear bifurcation analysis 
The linear bifurcation analysis (LBA) is a FEA method that assumes no nonlinearities and perfect initial 
geometry. It is used to find the ideal buckling behavior of a structure in the same way as bifurcation 
buckling theory and obtains the load just before the bifurcation point [17].  
It is very important to note that LBA gives nonconservative results in imperfection sensitive buckling 
scenarios, especially for cylindrical shells due to initial imperfections and nonlinearities. LBA can be used as 
a comparison with Equation (0.2) to get an indication if the mesh is decent and the model parameters are 
correct. It can also be used to see where buckling likely occurs and as an upper bound solution. 
The assumption of linearity means (1) there is always only one solution for each load case. (2) The solution 
is scalable, meaning if every load is multiplied by a factor, the left-hand side of Equation (2.1) will be 
multiplied by the same factor [16]. (3) It means that superposition is possible. These assumptions are more 
accurate if only small displacements and rotations occur in the structure. Because of these assumptions the 
buckling load can be found for an arbitrary external load by multiplying with a factor 𝜆 dependent on the

Expected output from LBA 
Different buckling patterns can occur in cylindrical shells, dependent on the dimensions of the cylindrical 
shell and the loads applied. Since bending is the most important load for monopiles, this section will focus 
on the expected results for a cylindrical shell under pure bending. 
Bending causes compression in half the cross-section, and tension in the other half. The stress distribution is 
linearly distributed in elastic buckling. LBA assumes elastic buckling as part of the linearity assumption, 
since elastic-plastic behavior is nonlinear material behavior. Since buckling occurs in compression, only the 
compression side is relevant. The critical stress occurs at the compression fibre furthest from the center. 
The boundary conditions at both ends can affect the buckling shapes. Short ring-shaped cylindrical shells 
are expected to have 1 half-wave occur along the length on the compressive side. This increases at different 
rates dependent on the boundary conditions. For medium length cylindrical shells there are many half-
waves along the length [20]. It is important to note the Brazier effect is neglected as a nonlinearity, meaning 
the buckling shapes of longer cylindrical shells aren’t realistic. 
Nonlinear buckling analysis 
The nonlinear analysis allows nonlinearities. The primary sources of nonlinearities are the changes in 
geometry and load directions due to imperfections, large deflections, and large rotations. Material 
properties such as plasticity also causes plasticity [16]. The Brazier effect is important for longer cylindrical 
shells, and is a nonlinearity considered in nonlinear buckling analyses. 
It is generally very difficult to include nonlinearities in standard equations, because either a lot of 
assumptions and simplifications must be made, or a lot of experiments must be completed to make 
empirical equations.

The Riks method is an algorithm which is used if the load and/or displacement decreases during a load-

Circumferential pressure caused by waves 
Waves also cause circumferential pressure like wind does. 
It is assumed the wave loads have the same shape as the wind loads. Which means the equivalent 
circumferential pressure for waves can be found with Equation (3.2) and (3.3). This assumption is made 
since EC3 specifies how to convert wind external pressure load into an equivalent axisymmetric pressure 
distribution and doesn’t specify how to convert wave external pressure load, and a theoretical expression 
for such a conversion couldn’t be found.  
Hydrostatic pressure 
It is assumed the monopile is filled with sea water up to sea level, meaning there is no hydrostatic pressure.

Load and stress curves 
Model simplifications 
Perfect geometry and idealized elastic behavior with small displacements and rotations are assumed, 
meaning superposition can be utilized to add loads together, the structural stiffness isn’t affected by the 
loads and the loads are distributed perfectly through the cross-section with no plastic behavior. These 
simplifications are made to reduce the complexity of the problem, but they cause the mathematical model 
to have some discrepancies compared to the real behavior of the monopile. 
These load and stress curves are all made with the loads described in the section above. 
External load curves 
The normal force curve can be found by adding the load at the top of the monopile from the weight of the 
wind turbine with the load from the weight of the monopile which varies with depth:

Assumptions 
A few additional simplifications are necessary before a finite element model can be set up. 
It is assumed the top of the transition piece is working as an infinitely stiff flange because it is much stiffer 
than the monopile. This means the top boundary of the monopile can’t deform, and it can only rotate as a 
rigid body. This assumption is necessary to avoid local buckling at the top boundary. 
It is also assumed the yield curve for steel is bilinear elastic plastic with a very slight slope instead of elastic 
perfect plastic as seen on Figure 3.12. This is done because it is more realistic that some strain hardening 
will occur after yielding.

Nonlinear buckling analysis utilizing Riks method 
The moment buckling capacity found utilizing ABAQUS Riks method with the nonlinear geometry (nlgeom) 
option enabled for an imperfect cylindrical shell with maximum increments set to 500 since the Riks 
method sometimes require many increments [33]. The maximum increments set to 500 and the nlgeom 
option enabled has been used for all nonlinear buckling analyses in this report. The moment buckling 
capacity found through this method is compared with the moment buckling capacity according to the 
different standards. 
Imperfections 
Imperfections are introduced into the nonlinear Riks method as a combination of a weld depression in the 
middle (z=H/2=22.5 m) as a local imperfection, and a small buckling mode shaped imperfection based on 
the first four relevant eigenmodes, which is a more globalized imperfection in the area where compression 
has the highest effect. The implementation of the buckling mode shaped imperfection is done by 
multiplying a scale factor onto the displacements in the first four relevant buckling modes. The reason the 
buckling mode shaped imperfection is introduced, is to cause perturbations in a larger area the mesh, which 
can increase the mathematical stability of the Riks method in ABAQUS [16]. 
The depth of the weld depression is found from Equation (1.23) for each of the three fabrication classes 𝑈𝐴, 
𝑈𝐵 and 𝑈𝐶 respectively, also the height has been found from Equation (1.24): 
𝑈𝐴= 4√𝑟𝑡⋅0.006 = 0.012 m, 𝑈𝐵= 4√𝑟𝑡⋅0.01 = 0.020 m, 𝑈𝐶= 4√𝑟𝑡⋅0.016 = 0.031 m, 𝑙𝑔𝑤= 0.5 m 
To make the buckling mode shaped imperfection, the normalized displacement in each node in the first 
eigenmode is multiplied by a scale factor -1/50 𝑈𝐶 and the next 3 eigenmodes are multiplied by a scale 
factor -1/500 𝑈𝐶. The reason the scale factors are like this, is because the first eigenmode, which is most 
important, has the highest effect on the displacements. The scale is chosen to make the weld depression 
dominant in all cases. The sign has been changed so the peak in the middle of the first eigenmode is in the 
same direction as the weld depression. This buckling mode shaped imperfection has been used in all 
nonlinear buckling analyses in this report.

It is seen the load curve for the analysis named “No dimple” is going up into a single bifurcation point, and 
snap-back occurs due to strain localization. Strain localization is a phenomenon that can occur after 
buckling, meaning the strains increase in a localized area of the monopile but decreases for the rest of the 
monopile. This phenomenon occurs because it is more energy efficient to localize the strains than to cause 
globalized strains [22]. This load curve looks like the load curve for a perfect shell in Figure 1 [31]. 
It is seen the load curve “flattens” as the depth of the weld depression increases, and the moment limit 
load is reduced. This is expected due to the imperfection sensitive behavior of cylindrical shells. The load 
curve for the analysis made with dimple depth from fabrication class C is more like the imperfect shell in 
Figure 1 [31]. All the load curves have an unstable postbuckling behavior (see Figure 0.1 d)) as expected for 
cylindrical shells.

Comparison between FEA buckling analyses and buckling approaches in the standards

Since these results are so similar, it indicates the assumptions made are reasonable for comparison 
between FEA and the standards.

boundary conditions aren’t specified in DNV, the nonlinear buckling analyses in ABAQUS yield more reliable 
results. 
It should be noted the utilization ratios found through FEA are characteristic and the utilization ratios found 
with standards are design values. This is a source of error of unknown quantity since it depends on which 
factor of safety that should be utilized for the FEA. If the factor of safety is 𝛾𝑀𝐴≤1.3 the utilization ratio is 
still below 1, meaning the monopile won’t buckle in this load case according to these calculations if 𝛾𝑀𝐴≤
1.3. Since FEA yield very accurate results if converged, the factor of safety is mostly dependent on how 
accurately the geometry and material parameters and loads are modelled compared to reality. The factor of 
safety also depends on how reliable manufacturing and the steel quality is.

4. Conclusion 
The IEA 15-Megawatt Offshore Reference Wind Turbine has been analyzed in relation to buckling. It is 
concluded Load Case 1 is the critical load case analyzed in this report, because the utilization ratios are 
higher in Load Case 1 than it is in Load Case 2 and Load Case 3. In Load Case 1 the most conservative 
utilization ratio was found through Det Norske Veritas to be 0.772 for a monopile of constant thickness and 
0.85 for a monopile with realistic thicknesses used in documentation for [23], which means buckling won’t 
occur according to calculations made in this report.  
Nonlinear buckling analyses have been made for a model with constant thickness and a general dimple 
imperfection or a weld depression imperfection utilizing the requirements described in Eurocode 3. These 
analyses have been compared with the official Eurocode 3, the draft version of Eurocode 3 and Det Norske 
Veritas. It is concluded the characteristic values obtained through nonlinear buckling analyses made with a 
weld depression imperfection in ABAQUS yield very similar results to design values using the buckling 
approach in the draft version of Eurocode 3 for all fabrication classes, based on simplifications made in this 
report. For steel of fabrication class C Det Norske Veritas is a slightly better choice, since this yields 
conservative values with a deviation of 1.8%. 
Analyses have been made to observe the effects of varying dimple parameters and varying weld depression 
parameters. It was concluded that if a single general dimple is used as the imperfection, a factor of safety is 
necessary to introduce. This factor of safety would vary depending on which standard is used. It is also 
concluded that analyses made with a weld depression imperfection yield more similar results to EC3 than 
analyses made with a general dimple imperfection. This conclusion was based on the depth of the weld 
depression affects the buckling capacity in a similar way to EC3, compared to a general dimple, where the 
depth had almost no effect on the buckling capacity. 
It was concluded that a nonlinear analysis of a monopile with constant thickness didn’t represent the actual 
buckling behavior of a monopile with stepwise varying thickness, and multiple analyses were made to 
analyze this disparity. 
A parameter study has been made and the effects of different parameters have been written in Table 19. It 
was concluded the out-of-roundness introduced in EC3 had negligible effects on the buckling capacity in this 
case. It was concluded the effect of including torque was irrelevant in this case. The parameter most 
important to consider is concluded to be the thickness of the monopile as it affects both buckling behavior 
and the buckling capacity by a lot.
