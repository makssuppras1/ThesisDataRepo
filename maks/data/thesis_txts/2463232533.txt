Abstract
Energy is known to be the crucial, enabling factor for many development goals such as
health, education, poverty eradication and environmental sustainability. Yet, nearly
one billion people in the world currently still do not have access to electricity and
more than 95% of these people live in developing countries in Sub-Saharan Africa and
South-East Asia. In Sub-Saharan Africa, this number is expected to keep growing
unless the electrification efforts increase exponentially. The population in rural areas
has been the most left out in electrification developments because of their remoteness
making a possibility of grid extension very costly. The goal of this study is therefore
to explore how electricity could be brought to rural areas through means other than
the conventional fossil fuels, grid-centralized, means. This study was done in Senegal
and the methods used include interviews and surveys, a literature review, and an
optimization tool (HOMER). A renewable energy resources assessment is necessary
for this analysis and is carried out using online models. The assessment revealed a
very good solar radiation throughout the entire country, good wind speeds localized
on the Northern coast of Senegal, and a good hydropower potential along the main
rivers. These resources were hybridized to make an energy system meeting the needs
of communities and fostering the development of productive uses of energy.
The
practical way of introducing such a system in the rural communities is detailed, based
on the creation of local energy cooperatives to empower rural areas.
The study
provides policy recommendations leading to the development of a skilled workforce
through education, to the expansion of the presence of the private sector in that field
and to the rise of local initiatives.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Current energy situation in developing countries:
an overview
At the beginning of the millennium, world leaders introduced the Millennium Devel-
opment Goals in order to eradicate poverty by 2015 [1]. After that milestone year,
the Sustainable Development Goals were put in place, covering targets within the
health, education, poverty eradication, gender equality and environmental sustain-
ability fields, among others [2]. Energy is known to be the cornerstone of most of
these goals and yet more than 1.1 billion people in the world did not have access
to electricity in 2017 and 95% of these people lived in developing countries in Sub-
Saharan Africa and South Asia [3].
In those regions, the main sources of energy
used are kerosene and solid fuels and they are responsible for approximately 4 million
deaths each year because of the indoor pollution caused [4][5]. Therefore, if the en-
ergy access issue is not urgently addressed, the Sustainable Development Goals will
remain unachievable [6].
The energy deficit is particularly important in Sub-Saharan Africa. Of the 20 coun-
tries with the lowest electrification rates in the world, 19 are found in that region
[7]. According to the Energy Access Outlook 2017 projections, of the 674 million
people still without access to electricity worldwide in 2030, 90% live in Sub-Saharan
Africa. This means that the energy production in the region is expected to still be
dominated by inefficient fuels and costly diesel generators in the future. Furthermore,
a lot of African households and businesses connected to the grid do not have access
to reliable electricity and have to cope with severe power outage and rely on diesel
generators as well, when they can afford it. As stated by IRENA in [8], diesel power
generators alone cost some African economies between 1% and 5% of their GDP each
year, making the cost of having electricity, or rather the cost of not having electricity,
higher than in any other region of the world.
Globally, the rural areas are more affected by the lack of electricity than urban areas
as 84% of the people without an electricity access worldwide reside in rural areas
[3]. Remoteness, a dispersed demographic pattern and poverty make the connections
costly and as a consequence, since 2000, the progress made in electrifying urban areas
was double the one made in rural areas [3]. According to the World Bank, in Senegal,

1.2
Definition of the key terms of the topic
1.2.1
Techno-economic assessment
A techno-economic assessment is a way of evaluating the feasibility of a project by
analyzing the technological appropriateness and economic viability of a system [10].
This is usually done through a cost-benefit analysis which compares different costs
and benefits associated with the project. The costs assessment takes into account two
types of costs: the investment costs (CAPEX) and operational costs (OPEX). The
benefits assessment covers all the possible profits from different sources such as the
sale of electricity, the sale of a specific product, government benefits, carbon benefits
or the sale of green certificates [11][12]. This method generally takes into account the
time-value of money and therefore brings both costs and benefits to a present value.
1.2.2
Renewable energy
The International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) defines renewable energy as:
“Any form of energy from solar, geophysical or biological sources that is replenished
by natural processes at a rate that equals or exceeds its rate of use. Renewable energy
is obtained from the continuing or repetitive flows of energy occurring in the natural
environment and includes low-carbon technologies such as solar energy, hydropower,
wind, tide and waves and ocean thermal energy, as well as renewable fuels such as
biomass.” [13]
Renewable energy technologies can provide different types of energy such as elec-
tricity, thermal energy and mechanical energy. They can be installed at the point
of demand in a decentralized way or be deployed at a larger scale and be part of a
centralized large national network. Some renewable energy technologies have reached
a mature state, such as hydropower for example, and others are still subject to a lot
of research.
1.2.3
Rural electrification
Rural electrification is the process of providing electricity or energy as a whole to rural
areas. The meaning of rural electrification therefore relies strongly on the definition
of a rural area and the definition of energy access.
The areas that can be qualified as rural areas depend on the country they are in. The
definition can be based on the distance to an urban area, the number of inhabitants

and the population density [14].
There is less consensus on the definition of the term energy access. According to the
International Energy Agency (IEA), most definitions have criteria in common, such
as: a minimum access to electricity; access to sustainable and safe cooking and heat-
ing means; access to energy for productive use and public services. The definition
used by the IEA is as:
“a household having reliable and affordable access to both clean cooking facilities and
to electricity, which is enough to supply a basic bundle of energy services initially,
and then an increasing level of electricity over time to reach the regional average” [3]
The importance of having a definition covering the different aspects of energy de-
mand that does not limit energy access to a binary concept, as to simply evaluating
whether one is connected to a grid or not, is argued in [15], as this view could lead
to misguided policies.

1.3.1
Geographic and demographic situation
Senegal is a West-African country bordered by the Atlantic Ocean to the West, Mau-
ritania to the North, Mali to the East, Guinea and Guinea Bissau to the South and
Gambia in the middle. The northern part of the country belongs to the African zone
called Sahel, characterized by its semi-arid climate. However, Senegal also has a short
rainy season from June to October and its southern part is dominated by a tropical
forest. The geographic area of the country is approximately 197,000 km², with 2% of
water area.
The population of the country was 15.9 million in 2017 with a demographic growth
rate of 2.8% [16]. The capital city, Dakar, is also the biggest city with a population
of 3.5 million [17], representing almost half of the total urban population.

Economic situation
Senegal is a developing country with a GDP of USD 21.1 billion (as of 2017). The

1.3.3
Energy situation
Senegal is one of the countries with the lowest electricity consumption in the world.
In 2014, a Senegalese resident consumed 223 kWh on average, compared to 5,859
kWh for a Danish resident and 12,984 kWh for an American resident. The world

average is at 3,127 kWh/capita, so more than 14 times the Senegalese average [20].
This extremely low value can be explained by different phenomena. First of all, the
electricity consumption per capita is calculated dividing the total population electric-
ity consumption by the population. As in Senegal more than 2 people in 5 do not
have access to electricity, many people do not consume electricity at all resulting in
a very low electricity consumption per capita [9]. Secondly, the country has suffered
from recurrent power outages these past years as the electricity demand is growing
without being met by the same growth in power capacity and supply, leading to a
supply that is not reliable [21]. Senegal had a generation capacity of 548 MW in
2008 and 864 MW in 2018, thus the capacity saw an increase of 58% in 10 years [21].
However, as stated in [22], electrical capacity should grow at a rate of 13% each year
in order to reach universal electricity access by 2030 in Sub-Saharan Africa, meaning
Senegal should have had a capacity of 1,860 MW in 2018. As a consequence, people
cannot consume as much power as they need.
Senegal is greatly reliant on fossil fuels as the primary energy supply is based on
54% of biomass, 40% of imported oil products and 6% of coal and hydro power [21].
The heavy reliance on biomass leads to a very high energy intensity because of the
low efficiency of the type of biomass used.
Electricity access has increased significantly during the past decades, rising from
25% in 1990 to 65% in 2016. However, this progress has been uneven as the current
rural electrification rate is around 38% while the urban one is at almost 88% [9]. To
tackle this very slow progress, the Senegalese government created in 1999 an agency
whose sole focus is on rural electrification, ASER (Agence Sénégalaise d’Electrification
Rurale – Senegalese Agency for Rural Electrification). Three years later, a 20-year
program was introduced, PASER (Plan d’Action Sénégalais d’Electrification Rurale
– Senegalese Rural Electrification Plan of Action) with a target to reach a 62% elec-
trification rate in rural areas by 2022 [21]. The strategy of ASER has been to divide
the non-electrified rural areas into concessions, 10 in total, and to award those con-
cessions to private companies through public tenders. ERIL projects (Electrification
Rurale d’Initiative Locale – Local Initiative Rural Electrification) were created to tar-
get smaller villages, not covered by the concessions [23]. In 2004, the government
established a fund in order to support financially rural electrification with subsidies
[24].
Senegal has also been endorsing the development of renewable energy by setting
up targets for the share of renewables in the electricity mix and by opening the power
production market to Independent Power Producers (IPPs). In 2008, policies aiming
at the expansion of renewables were materialized in the LPDSE (Lettre de Développe-
ment du Secteur de l’Energie – Letter for the Development of the Energy Sector) and
two laws were adopted with an objective of reaching a share of 15% of renewable
energy in the energy mix by 2020 [21]. At the time these policies were introduced,
the only renewable power input on the national grid was from the Manantali dam in

1.4
Rationale for the study
1.4.1
Why a developing country? Why Senegal?
The reasons behind the choice of Senegal for this study are multiple.
First of all, it is well-known that the energy access issue is mostly a developing country
issue as all OECD countries have an electrification rate of nearly 100% [9]. Therefore,
increasing the energy access in well-industrialized and technology advanced countries
in order to close the minor gap between the current electrification rate and a com-
plete electricity access, for both urban and rural areas, would not necessarily have
an impact on the standard of living of the people in those countries. However, for
poorly industrialized, although rich in natural resources, developing countries, small
increases in the energy access field could significantly impact the conditions of living
[5]. It was consequently important to choose such a country to carry out this project.
Secondly, Senegal shares the same situation as many other Sub-Saharan countries
regarding demography and energy access. Indeed, the country currently has a low
electrification rate of 65.2%, particularly in rural areas where more than half of the
population lives [26]. This means the research could be useful to other countries in
the region.
Finally, Senegal has shown in the past years a strong will to improve the energy
system, increase the energy access across the territory and support the integration
of renewable energy technologies. This has resulted in a lot of efforts put in this sec-
tor from the government side, but also from the private side and non-governmental
organizations. In 2012, the country hosted the first pilot study of the Renewables
Readiness Assessment lead by IRENA [21].

1.4.2
Why rural electrification?
Rural areas are the poorest energy-wise. The electricity grid rarely reaches them be-
cause of some practical barriers raising the cost of grid extension such as remoteness,
lack of roads, dispersed settlements, rugged terrains and low energy demand com-
pared to urban centers, in the current state [27][28][29][30]. Many private investors
are reluctant to venture in those areas because the low income level among the rural
population and the usual absence of support from the government make the invest-
ment non-appealing [31]. As a consequence, rural areas are often left out in many
electrification schemes and the electrification does not increase as much as in urban
areas [6]. This lack of electricity and energy as a whole, coupled with other problems
related to poverty, fuels the desire for many rural inhabitants to leave their homes
for more developed cities [32]. This rural exodus brings an increasing pressure on
cities where dwellers still do not have access to reliable power in many Sub-Saharan
countries like Senegal. Therefore, solving the energy issue in rural areas should re-
duce that rural-urban migration and decrease the imbalance between rural and urban
areas [14][33].
In order to have universal access to energy in developing countries, sustainable electri-
fication efforts should be addressed to all the areas at once [4]. Reinforcing the power
grid in urban centers does not mean excluding rural areas because if such a thing is
done, the energy shortage issue will only be shifted from rural areas to urban areas.
Electricity from the grid is considered as a public good and by definition, cannot
exclude anyone, even if it means providing electricity by other means [34].
More importantly, most populations in rural areas in Senegal count an endless num-
ber of entrepreneurs whose main goal is to reach economic well-being for them and
their community. The fact is many families or communities specialize in farming, rais-
ing cattle and/or fishing, while others are engaged in specific trades, such as wood,
metal or leather work. Their micro-firms are usually already very productive with
the very few energetic resources they have access to. However, the lack of energy
prevents their business from flourishing and producing a significant income. Energy
access would therefore expand their efficiency and facilitate the industrialization of
their craft activities thus creating a stable economic dynamic that would be beneficial
for all.
1.4.3
Why renewables?
Some have argued that unlike more developed countries, Sub-Saharan African coun-
tries do not have to shift their energy sources to renewable ones because of more
pressing issues related to poverty, health and economic growth. The thought behind
that belief is that a majority of industrialized countries have used fossil fuels to reach
their level of development, regardless of how much they were polluting, and that devel-
oping nations now have the right to take the same path and deal with environmental
==PAGE:24==
8
1 Introduction
issues later [35][36]. However, this type of arguments fail to acknowledge that a sus-
tainable development cannot take place without sustainable energy and that without
it, people will keep deteriorating their health burning solid fuels in their homes or
spend a great amount of money to buy diesel to fuel inefficient diesel generators.
Sub-Saharan Africa does not have to take the same path as industrialized countries.
Indeed, thanks to all the technological innovations that have occurred, the region
could leapfrog to clean and efficient renewable energy technologies [8].
The costs
of such technologies have been decreasing sharply for years, making them more af-
fordable for an increasing number of countries. Solar photovoltaics modules are 80%
cheaper than they were in 2009 and onshore wind electricity 25% cheaper [37]. As
stated by IRENA, electrification in most rural areas in Senegal is like a tabula rasa,
little has been done and everything is yet to be built. Consequently, those areas have
the chance to be able to adopt cheaper sustainable energy right away.
Furthermore, solely depending on fossil fuels could have unpredictable repercussions,
especially when all the fuels are imported. A lot of Sub-Saharan countries decided
to build oil power plants in the past, because oil was cheap at some point in time
and benefited from subsidies in many cases. However, that choice made them depend
strongly on the price of oil, which is not constant. This led to a debt increase for
many nations that most of the time struggle to keep their power plants running [38].
Instead of building fossil fuel plants that have a very long lifetime, particularly in Sub-
Saharan Africa where those plants are operated even beyond their technical lifetime,
renewable energy technologies could be implemented [28]. In addition, polluting could
become very expensive in the years to come as it is very likely that power plants will
soon have to pay CO2 taxes, as a mean to abate pollution and fight climate change.
In that case, fossil fuels will be much less competitive than renewable sources that are
getting increasingly cheaper than the fossil fuel options, even without the CO2 taxes
[8][36]. This would be all the more true if the cost of all the externalities generated
by the use of fossil fuels were included in their true cost [39]. This is the reason why
even though Senegal recently discovered offshore oil and gas resources [40], it should
not based all its future energy system on their exploitation.
Regarding rural areas, renewable energy sources would be the most convenient to
use as they would allow the production of energy closest to where it is needed as
the entire territory is endowed with renewable resources. This would enable rural
communities’ access to energy based on their own resources and avoid the costly
transportation of power along very long distances [41].
1.4.4
Why off-grid?
The fact that rural and urban areas have very different characteristics leads to the
consideration of alternative ways of delivering electricity. Transporting the electricity
==PAGE:25==

through a grid is the common way of providing electricity. Since 2000, grid connection
has been the electrification mode for nearly all the 1.2 billion people who got an access
to electricity [3]. Therefore the first way that is thought of for the electrification of
rural areas is grid extension even though it is not always the most efficient and
cost-effective option [34]. Off-grid electrification with either stand-alone systems or
mini-grids has proven to be an efficient solution for rural areas and is gaining ground.
It is compatible with the production of energy from local renewable energy sources
and avoids all the technical losses occurring when transporting power from the main
grid over very large distances [42]. According to the International Energy Agency,
half of the new electricity access by 2030 will be through off-grid means [3]. It is
consequently an option to consider in the scope of this study.
1.5
Research objectives
The main goal of the study is to understand what the critical factors are to achieve
reliable, cost-effective and sustainable electrification in the rural areas of Senegal. As
each site considered is different and has its own characteristics and natural endow-
ments, the energy system has to be adapted to each case. One single general energy
system cannot work efficiently everywhere [6][7][43]. Solutions should be designed
depending on the site as they vary with the unique geographic characteristics, hence
each region has to be studied thoroughly.
The first sub-objective is to carry out a renewable energy resource assessment to
evaluate the potential of those energy resources and see what type of renewable en-
ergy technologies is to be used. This assessment will lead to a mapping of renewable
energy resources for Senegal showing where to find which type of resource. It is a
crucial step for the design of an energy system adapted to the resources of each region.
The second sub-objective is to estimate the electricity needs of the households and
identify the income generating activities of the different communities. This step will
be rudimentary as most of the villages have never had access to any form of electric-
ity and therefore do not have any electrical appliances. Consequently, estimating the
needs by assessing how much each electrical appliance consumes will not be possible
and some assumptions will have to be made.
Based on the results from the previous steps, the third sub-objective is to determine
different energy systems that meet the needs of the rural populations in an optimal
way, depending on the renewable resources available. A cost-benefit analysis will be
undertaken in order to choose a cost-effective solution in each case.
The fourth sub-objective consists of elaborating a practical implementation of the
energy systems in the communities, through a social impact assessment. If the so-
lution is designed regardless of the social environment, it will most certainly fail.

2.1
Systematic literature review
The first information canal used to gather knowledge, data and build a material ba-
sis for this work is systematic literature review. In order to carry out this task, a
research question has been formulated, based on the title of the thesis: how can ru-
ral electrification take place in Senegal through the deployment of renewable energy
systems, from a techno-economic point of view? Keywords, that are the input of
the databases, were identified from this question and are the following: rural elec-
trification, renewable energy, off-grid electricity, hybrid energy systems, mini-grids,
developing countries, Senegal, Africa, productive use. The documentation in other
developing countries was relevant to look at as certain methods and findings can be
applied from several places in the world, with similar backgrounds, to the situation
in Senegal.
Some limits had also to be introduced in the research in order to provide a frame-
work. Two different limits were put in place. The first one being the language of the
publications as only the documents written in French, official language of Senegal,
and English, most used language in scientific communication, were looked at. The
second one being a time limit, as only the papers written and published in the last
fifteen years were considered. Indeed, the renewable energy field is very dynamic and
evolves at a very fast pace, with the costs of technologies decreasing quickly. Further-
more, the electrification situation of a country can change significantly in a few years,
making it necessary to have the latest data. Ghana’s electrification rate has increased
from 25% in 1989 to 84.3% in 2017 and Morocco’s rural electricity access from 15%
in 1990 to 97.4% in 2017 [46][8][26]. These two reasons justified the necessity to focus
on recent research.
Once the keywords were defined and the time limits specified, scientific citation
databases have been used to find the relevant literature. Scopus and ScienceDirect
were used and the keywords written earlier were combined in several manners such
as: rural electrification in Africa; off-grid electricity for rural electrification; renew-
able energy and their productive use in developing countries; etc. Some papers, found
via the scientific database, were later obtained through the open-access library DTU
Findit, when they could not be downloaded directly from the database.

2.3
Renewable energy resource assessment tools
The wind resource assessment was undertaken using two different tools. The first
one is the Global Wind Atlas (GWA), a web-based application developed by DTU,
in association with the World Bank. The application enables the observation of the
wind power density and the wind speed at the heights of 50 m, 100 m and 200 m.
As only the application of small-scale wind power is studied in this project, the two
parameters were observed at 50 m and the corresponding maps were downloaded for
Senegal. The second tool used for the assessment of the wind power is an implemen-
tation of the WAsP software (Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program), called
Frogfoot. The advantage of Frogfoot is that it can map large areas in a short amount
of time, with a very good precision, based on the Global Wind Atlas generalized wind
data and topographical maps. The wind power density and wind speed were therefore
mapped this way and the maps were displayed using the software QGIS, a geographic
information system.
The solar resource assessment was carried out using a photovoltaic geographical in-
formation system called PVGIS. PVGIS is an online tool giving access to the solar
resource worldwide. The irradiation is given through mapping of the global horizon-
tal irradiation, direct normal irradiation, and global irradiation with specific angles.
PVGIS was therefore used to display the yearly sum of global irradiation for Senegal.

Optimization tool
The software used to perform the energy system optimization is HOMER (Hybrid Op-
timization of Multiple Energy Resources). HOMER is a simulation model, simulating
hundreds or thousands of possibilities to present the least-cost hybrid energy system
based on the components the user decides to include. The energy resources can either
be downloaded from the NASA Surface meteorology and Solar Energy database or
imported by the user. HOMER also needs other inputs such as the economic param-
eters corresponding to the country the hybrid energy systems are optimized for. In
this project, the discount rate was fixed at 4% and the inflation rate at 1.50%, based
on values of the previous years and the expectations for the years to come.

sources assessment
For a country, carrying out a renewable energy sources assessment is a fundamen-
tal step towards the transition to renewable energy technologies. It delivers a clear
mapping of the natural resources of each area thus putting to light exactly what kind
of technology could be used to harness power and where [8]. It helps in treating
each region as a special case and selecting the appropriate technologies for each one
of them, based on the different resources they have. Such an assessment also ad-
dresses the intermittency issue associated with the use of renewables as it identifies
the time variations of the resources. Consequently, the system can be designed based
on several sources of renewable energy that compensate each other time wise [47].
Moreover, knowing the renewable sources variations is important to the sizing of any
system as it is crucial to meet the people’s energy needs, even when the wind does
not blow strongly or the sun does not shine much. The system must be designed for
the worst-case scenario, making it necessary to know precisely what this worst-case
scenario is [48].
The renewable energy resources assessment for an area can be done through mod-
els or measurements, or both. The model gives a preliminary layout of the resources.
Then, ideally, the information derived from the model should be confronted to the
measurements in order to test its accuracy.
The following section gives an account of an attempt to map the renewable resources
of Senegal for an energy production purpose. The assessment was based on literature,
models and real measurements data when available.

4.1
Estimation of electricity needs
4.1.1
Definition and importance
One of the first steps that has to be taken in the design of an electrical system is
the assessment of the electrical load. It consists in determining how much electricity
people need and at what time they need it. This information is capital as it is a key
input to the optimization of the designed system, when it comes to choosing the size
and number of the electrical components for a dimensioning purpose [58][10][4].
It is of major importance that the estimation of the electricity needs be as close as
possible to reality. Therefore, the data necessary to that estimation must be collected
locally, at the area of interest [4]. Using a model that was predetermined without
any adaptation to the local environment and realities comes with a lot of flaws. A
predetermined model essentially disregards many elements as each area represents a
different case. In order to have the best chance of success and durability, the solution
proposed to a community must be designed to match that community’s own needs
and specific objectives [15][47].
The consequence of the implementation of a system that was designed without a
clear understanding of the local demand is an imbalance between the demand and
the supply. If there is a shortage in the power supply, the demand is not met and the
system is consequently unreliable. However, a system that is over-dimensioned drives
the costs up and results in having extra electricity that cannot be used in some cases
[4][47][48].
4.1.2
Different methods to carry out the load assessment
For the purpose of electrifying rural areas, the electrical demand has to be estimated
among communities who have never had access to electricity. It is therefore impos-
sible to measure the electricity needed by detailing the electrical appliances or by
==PAGE:48==
32
4 Design of an off-grid electrification solution
analyzing past bills because they do not exist. This difficulty makes it necessary to
find alternative ways of evaluating the electricity demand, presented in the following
section.
One way of finding the electrical demand is through what is called Substitutable
Energetic Expenses in [59]. Indeed, people who do not have access to grid electricity
find other ways of meeting their needs, particularly for lighting. They use candles,
batteries or oil powered diesel generators. The cost of each of these commodities can
be calculated. Added up, it would represent the amount of money households would
most likely be willing to spend on electricity. The electrical demand would, in that
case, be measured in monetary terms.
A second way of measuring the electrical load consists of evaluating it in the elec-
trified rural neighboring communities. An inventory of the electrical appliances used
in those communities would inform on the amount of electricity rural areas consume
once they are electrified [59][28]. This estimation could give a precise idea of the de-
mand as it is based on real consumption of areas similar to the study areas targeted.
A third way is to analyze what activities are undertaken in rural communities that
need electricity and, from that list of activities, deduce the power level that would be
needed. In rural areas, energy is mainly needed for lighting and cooking, as primary
needs [5]. Based on the size of the buildings, the amount of light penetrating the
building’s windows and glass walls, and such parameters, the amount of light bulbs
needed could be estimated [15]. Income generating activities should also be taken
into account as they guarantee a decent standard of living.
Last but not least, taking a look at previous studies in several parts of the world
can help make the estimation in local areas in Senegal. The electricity demand was
estimated in rural areas in Vietnam in [60], with the purpose of examining the pos-
sibility to electrify Vietnamese rural communities with household-sized renewable
energy technologies. In that study, the rural electricity demand for one household
was estimated at 0.3 kWh/day and the method used was mainly based on appliance
inventories and surveys. Results from a mini-grid model made for rural South-Africa
in [43] showed that a rural household needed 2.4 kWh/day to cover all their needs and
support income generating activities. A short review of some studies estimating the
electricity needs in remote areas was done in [61]: the needs were found to be between
0.3 kWh/day and 0.6 kWh/day in the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region (IMAR)
of China and between 0.5 kWh/day and 0.6 kWh/day in Mekong countries. Accord-
ing to this study, when a small refrigerator is included in the needs, the electricity
demand range is shifted to 1.2 – 1.6 kWh/day for IMAR and 1.0 – 1.5 kWh/day for
Mekong countries. In Cameroon, energy surveys revealed a demand in the range 0.2
– 1 kWh/day for needs covering lighting, radio and television sets and a similar study
in Nigeria found a load between 1.5 kWh/day and 1.66 kWh/day.

4.1.4

Beyond the value of the daily electricity consumption, the variations in time of the
load have to be studied and understood because they have an important influence
on the design of a renewables-based electrical system. Whether electricity is mostly
needed in the evening for domestic purposes or during the day, for productive uses,
changes the way the system functions.
Different possible load profiles were used in [51] and [62] in order to study the in-
fluence of the shape of the load on the optimal electrical configuration. The possible
load profiles were studied in Kayar belonging to the region of Thiès and Potou in the
region of Louga. Three loads are suggested in the study and can be seen in Figure

be operated during the day for productive uses such as mills, welding machines, etc.
The peak observed at night is explained by domestic electricity use in all the house-
holds and some commercial equipment. Profile 2 shows a high, relatively constant
consumption during the day, explained by the utilization of a desalination and water
pumping system and some domestic equipment. The night peak is explained by the
same factors as for the first profile. Profile 3 is typical of communities working in
the fields during the day: the electricity consumption is low and constant during the
day. As for the two other profiles, there is the same peak at night. Each community

4.1.5
Demand growth and seasonality
Based on past observations, it has been noticed that rural electricity demand is gen-
erally lower than the urban electricity demand [43][61]. However, from the moment
rural areas are electrified, their electricity demand is expected to grow for several
reasons. First of all, electrification naturally comes with growing economic condition.
As the households’ economic welfare will grow, they will want more than the basic
electricity service level provided and will aspire to a more comfortable living place.
Electricity penetration in different productive uses will increase the efficiency through
technological development, leading to a workforce that might start other activities
in need of energy, thus increasing the demand. Secondly, the infrastructural devel-
opment that should follow electrification will also increase the energy balance as a
better built environment will require more energy [4]. Thirdly, population growth is
an inevitable cause of load growth [30]. This increased energy demand will have to
be met as it is a necessity to enable people to meet their objectives [15].
Not considering load growth can have several consequences leading to the failure
of the projects undertaken. The first consequence is technical as enough capacity
==PAGE:52==
36
4 Design of an off-grid electrification solution
will not have been installed over time in order to meet the demand. The second
consequence leading to failure is financial as the costs associated with the design and
installation of a system that can meet the demand at all times, over its lifetime, will
have been miscalculated [4].
The load growth has been included in several different ways in the models.
It is
modelled as an extra household in [63] and is taken as a sensitivity analysis parame-
ter in [47]. The models developed with HOMER use the multi-year module allowing
to account for a yearly increase in growth. In the present study, load growth has
been accounted for in the sensitivity analysis.

4.2
Productive uses
As explained in 4.1.3, beyond the classic consumptive loads, productive uses have to
be included in the design of an electricity system. These productive uses vary locally
and each village, in each region, has specific ones. For the solution to be tailored
around the needs of each community, these have to be defined and studied carefully
in order to enhance the income of rural communities and empower them. Farming
is important in all villages. Some combine that activity either with fishing (coastal
areas) or with cattle raising (ethnic groups whose lifestyle is based on owning cattle).
4.2.1
Fishing villages
Fishing is one of the main economic activities in the country, Senegal having a 530
km long coastline. This activity has been observed in a local context, in the village
of Diel Mbam, in which the main income generating activity is fishing but where the
lack of electricity is a serious handicap for the safe preservation of the fish. Currently,
villagers have to go more than 20 km away in order to buy ice to be able to keep the
fish until it is sold. When the fishermen only come back in the middle of the night,
they are unable to buy ice and have to wait until the next morning. This village is
representative of all the other rural fishing villages, without electricity. When asked
about the main reason they would need electricity for, their unanimous answer was
to have freezers, before any other domestic need. Therefore, the main productive
use in fishing villages similar to Diel Mbam is to have a cooling system, to allow a
prosperous fishing business.

Another type of village with a very different type of economic activity is the farming

4.3.4
Results
Presentation of the results
The different cases presented previously have been modelled in HOMER and the
results given by the software, that are the most relevant to the analysis are the fol-
lowing economic and technical parameters:
• Net Present Cost (NPC): this value given by HOMER is equivalent to the
life-cycle cost and is the sum of all the costs incurred by the project over its
lifetime minus all the revenues. Therefore, economically speaking only, the lower
the NPC, the better.
• Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE): it is the average cost over the lifetime
of the project. It indicates the cost of producing one kilowatt-hour of energy.
• Unmet load: it is the amount of load the system is unable to meet. This
value can be controlled through the maximum annual capacity shortage value
in the model constraints. Setting this value to zero forces the system to be
==PAGE:65==
4.3 Centralized system and public lighting
49
designed in a way that ensures the demand is met at all times. However, as it
will be shown in more details further in the report, a certain capacity shortage
has to be allowed in some cases. Otherwise, for those cases the project would
be infeasible.
This value is used in this research to determine whether the
electrification solution is reliable.
• Excess electricity: the excess electricity is the electricity that has to be
dumped, and is therefore wasted, when the supply exceeds the demand. This
happens when there is no battery to store the excess energy or when the battery
capacity has been reached. Controlling that this value is not too high is a way
of making sure the system is not over-designed as it would increase its cost.
• Break-even grid extension distance: it is the distance for which the net
present cost of extending the grid and the net present cost of implementing an
off-grid system are equal. As explained before, this means that if the distance
between the community in question and the grid is higher than this distance,
an off-grid system would be optimal.

Table 4.7 shows that the NPC of the different configurations considered vary in a
large range, from $51,935 for the lowest to more than $2 million for the highest. It

The configurations that include a storage device are generally cheaper than the ones
without. Indeed, as in the configurations without a battery, the system still tries to
meet the demand completely, this results in over-sizing the components, leading to
an increase in the cost of the overall system. The systems without a battery only
become cheaper when the capacity shortage allowed increases. For example, in the
simple case in which only solar components are included, with a battery, the NPC
is around $52,000 (Case 3). For the same case, without a battery, the NPC is only
equal to or lower than $52,000 for a minimum unmet load of 22.9%, meaning there
would not be any power approximately a quarter of the time, minimum, which poses

a reliability issue. It can therefore be concluded that a system with a battery would
be more advantageous techno-economically.
Another issue stemming from the absence of storage is the excess electricity.
Ta-
ble 4.7 shows that Cases 5 to 8, corresponding to all the configurations without a
battery have much more excess electricity than Cases 1 to 4. This is due to two main
reasons. First of all, as the systems do not have a battery and as the demand does not

is consequently bigger.

shortage, which is not ideal.

tems. The NPC and LCOE of this base case are higher than the ones corresponding

system (Case 3) rather than a diesel only system. Having a hybrid system combining

in these three cases are displayed in Table 4.8. It can be seen that the reason for

to power diesel generators. Indeed, the capital needed to install a renewable energy
system is much higher than the one needed to set a diesel generator but renewable
energy systems become cheaper during the system total lifetime as the fuel costs of
the diesel only systems become important, when there are none for renewable systems.
Furthermore, a renewable energy system would be much better for the environment
and for the health of the people in the community as it would not emit any harmful
gases. In this case, a diesel only system would release more than 14,000 kg of carbon
dioxide each year and around 100 kg of carbon monoxide, a gas toxic to humans
and animals. Therefore, both from an economic and environmental point of view,

Mini-grid
4.4.1
Description of the system
Having a mini-grid would enable all the houses to be connected and to have electricity
everywhere, and not only in the power centre. This system could also be designed in
a way as to minimize the costs, in order to make it affordable to rural communities.
Indeed, it has been noticed in the two grid-connected villages visited during the
fieldwork that even though they had electricity, they were not able to afford certain
types of appliances like refrigerators, for example.
Instead of all having personal
refrigerators, some people in the community had some and others could come and
leave their food in exchange of a little financial compensation. The mini-grid could be
designed taking that observation into account. Some appliances could be shared and
placed in a central place powered by renewable energy systems. All the houses could
be connected between them and to that central place, in a mini-grid. The electrical
load in the houses would be lower as it would mainly consist in lighting, fans or TVs.

4.6
Limitations
4.6.1
Limitations of the electrification solution proposed
The first limitation of the centralized system is that it does not enable people to
have electricity in their homes, meaning only appliances that have a battery can be
used so they can be charged in the power center and then taken back.
Focusing
only on meeting people’s basic electricity needs may not fully correspond to what
communities are expecting but is a first step to the realization of a more complex
and complete electrification solution. Actually, the emphasis on productive needs is
a way to generate a virtuous circle. Firstly, it boosts economic activity by putting
the focus on income generating ventures. Secondly, the revenues thus gained serve
to empower the community, hence giving it the means to invest in renewable energy
technologies.
In the mini-grid solution proposed, electricity is available in all the households but
this system also has its limits. With a view to limiting the cost of the overall system,
the model was designed to cover a given load corresponding to the operation of the
refrigerated room and to the utilization of some appliances, as to say phones, lamps,
televisions and fans. The system is not able to support extra energy-intensive appli-
ances. For example, if some households decided to have personal fridges, it would
most likely destabilize the system as it was not planned in the design. However, as in
the centralized system case, this limitation is temporary. The capacity of the system
is something that can increase over the years along with the communities willingness
to pay and with load growth, which is a phenomenon that is inevitable.
Another limitation, common to the two off-grid solutions proposed, is the fact that
the excess electricity has to be thrown away, as the electricity system is not connected
to the national grid. In order to keep that electricity, the storage capacity would have
to be increased, which would increase the cost of the overall system.
Lastly, the solutions proposed did not take into account the possibility to have re-
course to biogas because of the lack of information regarding the availability of organic
waste. However, more comprehensive studies and surveys could be done in order to
assess the availability of this resource and include it in the power system. Undeni-
ably, the use of biogas could increase the reliability of the system as its use is flexible
and controllable, contrary to solar and wind resources. The use of biogas could also
decrease the cost of the system as it would reduce the storage capacity needed.
4.6.2
Limitations of HOMER for the design of the solution
Some limitations in the design of the solution are based on the software used to
perform the optimization. HOMER is an economic optimizer, meaning it has one
==PAGE:82==
66
4 Design of an off-grid electrification solution
objective function it seeks to minimize, which is the Net Present Cost. The other
aspects of the system related to the environment or related to the social structure
of the community are not taken into account. This is why all the decisions related
to the social aspect of the project or the environmental aspect are made separately.
Besides, there are many extra costs that do not appear in the overall cost of the sys-
tem. Some of these costs are significant enough to have an impact on the feasibility
of the project such as the importation costs as most or nearly all the components are
available neither in Senegal nor in the neighbouring countries. The transportation
costs to the villages are also not taken into account, nor the installation costs.
The fact that the software lets the user choose the components of the system, without
suggesting which manufacturer or which capacity would be better given the general
set up and the load characteristics is another limitation. Indeed, certain components
could be better suited than others for a small-scale configuration, in a rural back-
ground and this is an element that should be taken into account because the different
components vary in costs.
Finally, as explained in the renewable energy sources assessment, having long term
measurements of the renewable resources is essential to have the best possible siz-
ing of the system. Yet, the HOMER model uses meteorological data with monthly
averages to do the simulations. The design can therefore be more accurate after a
long-term measurement campaign is carried out.
4.6.3
Barriers to the expansion of off-grid systems and reasons for
its non-expansion
First of all, the main reason why renewable off-grid energy systems are not gaining
ground in Senegal is because most people are not aware of their existence. During
the field research, the majority of people asked were not very familiar with renewable
energy. In the village of Diel Mbam, it was actually impossible to do the survey
because the village council did not know what renewable energy systems were and
were therefore reluctant to allow a survey to be done in the village about something
they did not know. In the other villages, all the children asked said they had never
heard about renewable energy at school. This shows that the penetration level of
the knowledge on renewables is low and it is an important brake on the expansion of
renewable off-grid systems. Actually, the main issue is that the electrification solu-
tions are expected to come from the State and communities do not necessarily think
about collectively creating their own electrification projects. When asked about how
the community issues could be solved, people would generally answer that the State
should be the one approaching them. Few people would individually invest in solar
devices and no collective project was observed.
Secondly, the classic and long-time way of electrifying has always been through grid
==PAGE:83==

extension and communities expect the grid to eventually reach them. This belief in
this single mode of electrification is both the cause of the very slow development of
alternative options and the consequence of the lack of alternative energy projects in
the country. Quite naturally, as the Senegalese territory has not seen such projects
taking shape, neither policy makers nor target communities are completely aware of
their advantages over the grid in some cases.
Thirdly, the quasi-absence of entrepreneurs launching renewable off-grid projects is
due to several factors, the first one being the lack of open-source data on renewable
energy potential across the country. Renewable projects and their specifications are
equally insufficient. The scarcity of specialists in the renewable energy field also re-

to the village of Ourour L’Islam, a solar project had been realized but it stopped

to be checked up one month after installation, and then six months after installation.
The following regular maintenance needs to be done every two years [65]. The solar
panels need to be cleaned on a daily basis, especially given the fact that the areas
where they would be installed in are very dusty due to the climate.
It is this combination of factors that impedes the development of off-grid renewable
energy projects.

5.1
Guiding concept: building the base progressively
5.1.1
Theoretical foundation: from the current energy situation in
Senegal to the desired situation in the future, an efficient
transitional solution
The electrification process should be implemented from the current energy situation
in Senegal. As things are, Senegal does not have any Renewable Energy Systems
manufacturers and that must be taken into account when proposing an electrifica-
tion solution, even though having a mature energy market with manufacturers and
retailers is the desired situation in the future. Besides, non-electrified communities
cannot wait for two or three other decades until the market is developed enough to
have local retailers. Each community needs their basic necessities be met as fast as
possible. Therefore, an efficient transitional solution must be put in place. Such a
solution will provide communities with electricity right now while at the same time
making it possible to take the country to where it needs to be energy-wise in a few
decades. It means building the base progressively.
5.1.2
Practical approach: bottom-up approach
The deployment idea that is specified in the following section takes its roots from
the car garage market in Senegal. Even though the country does not have car man-
ufacturers, car sales keep increasing each year. There is a multitude of car garages
in the country who, even though they cannot make cars, know how to fix any car
brought by a customer. The foreign car makers have retailers in the country with
many businesses selling spare parts, meaning the reparation of a car does not need
==PAGE:88==
72
5 Practical implementation of the system
any element from abroad, as everything is generally available in the country. The
material is available and the know-how as well. This market dynamic is hereafter ap-
plied to the energy sector. However, this model will be updated, in order to include
the manufacturing in Senegal of renewable equipment and spare parts.
The surveys done in Senegal highlighted the way people organize themselves, as a
community. In all the villages visited, most inhabitants would set up cooperatives
or Economic Interest Groups to venture into a business together. This is the reason
why it would be an asset for any energy project to be a group project, belonging to
the community as a whole as this is the way they normally operate. Plus, people can
mobilize a lot of money together when needed, for a shared good or a common goal,
and this has also been observed during the fieldwork. For example, in the village
of Ourour L’Islam, in order to afford diesel-powered irrigation pumps for the fields,
farmers share the renting costs and then share the use of the irrigation pump. In sev-
eral villages, either women or men would put an equal amount of money in a common
pot in order to organize parties, finance gatherings or some other specific endeavour.
The old system of ”tontine”1 is also used to manage group savings. These concrete
examples show that people do invest in common projects that matter to them.
Therefore, elaborating on the car garage analogy, a renewable energy systems com-
pany A could sell renewable energy equipment to a village cooperative. It is impor-
tant that the company be the one approaching the community and not the other
way around because most of the communities are isolated and do not have the means
nor the incentive to do the research and contact companies. In parallel, the com-
pany A must organize a thorough training program to teach members selected by
the community how to do the maintenance of the systems so they are able to repair
them themselves whenever needed. This step is crucial as the life-cycle success of the
overall project depends on the empowerment of each community and their ability to
master the technologies implanted in their village. It is desirable that the repairs be
done locally, by a community member, to ensure the community is never at a loss to
know what to do and does not depend on the visit of someone from the outside to
perform the maintenance. In addition, the following question asked to men as well as
women, during the surveys, always had a positive answer: ”If an energy system was
installed in the village, would you be ready to take care of it?”. It means that people
are willing to be taught the necessary skills for the equipment’s maintenance.
At first, company A must have retailers in the country with available spare parts
so every community technician can obtain the parts they need to repair their system.
However, the medium-term objective of the company is to have a plant in Senegal.
That will serve the dual purpose of lowering the cost of equipment and spare parts,

5.2
Step-by-step guide
The purpose of this section is to suggest a practical guide for the concrete realization
of electrification projects in Senegal. It is meant to be a multi-use and scalable guide
to be used by policy makers, communities, private companies and entrepreneurs, from
the smallest entity, like the village of Ourour L’Islam visited during the field research,
to the bigger ones. The guide takes into account the respect for traditional author-
ities observed in all the villages as well as the ways in which communities operate.
Its objective is to help design a tailor-made electrification solution, which means a
solution that adapts to the needs of every single community.
Step 1: Identification of the target villages
The very first step is the identification of all the villages that do not have access
to electricity in Senegal. This step has to be achieved in close collaboration with the
entities that hold the information. For each village, it is important to know whether
there already are projects or campaigns underway or whether the villages already
belong to an electrification concession given by the Senegalese Agency for Rural Elec-
trification.
Step 2: Organization of regional seminars
The gateways to villages are the village chiefs, they are the first people to contact in
order to establish a relationship with a village. Therefore, company or organization
A must get in touch with them before trying to move further. A way of doing this
would be to organize regional seminars, at which all the village chiefs of a region,
or a department if the region is too big, are invited. These seminars would be like
focus groups, during which the project to electrify each village through the use of
renewable energy systems would be explained and during which all their questions
would be answered. Although French is the sole official language in Senegal, it is fully
==PAGE:90==

understood by less than 10% of the population2, hence these focus groups need to be
organized in the local languages. The use of national languages will facilitate com-
munication by removing the language barrier thus allowing everyone to understand
all the details of the project. Documentation should also be provided as the village
chiefs will, after the seminars, pass on the information to their community and relay
all the project details3. They will be the go-to people, both for the village and the
project organizers. During the seminars, each village chief should be asked whether
their village would be interested, a priori. If the answer is positive then they will
be asked to confirm their willingness to take on the project after having talked with
their community. It is only once the green light has been given by the villages that
it will be possible to go ahead to the next step.
Step 3: Organization of surveys
The third step is to do surveys, similar to the ones done for this project, in all the
villages who shared their interest in the electrification project. As the project will
already have been announced and discussed within the community, the survey teams
will be expected and they will be able to communicate with people in order to gather
all the required information and identify the communities true needs. These surveys
will be the opportunity to gather, evaluate and analyze the following information,
imperative to the adequate tailoring of the project:
• the level of energy demand;
• the scope of possible productive uses that could be associated with the electricity
generation;
• the workings of the community dynamic.

undertaken in the target villages to make sure each village receive the appropriate
energy systems. Long-term data is needed which means that this step should last
at least a year. During the measurement phase, the company/organization A has to
maintain dialog with the community and build a relationship of trust, transparency
and communication.

Step 5: Energy system analysis
Once the measurements are done, they have to be analyzed to know what resources

social and cultural needs and aspirations, through a business whose ownership and
management are collective and where power is exercised democratically and in accor-

Consequently, a cooperative company should be created in order to finance the project
and manage the maintenance of the energy system. This step is important for three
main reasons. First of all, empowering a community means giving them autonomy
and letting them operate their own affairs. Secondly, for communities to truly care
about the energy system and involve themselves in its sustainability, they have to
be the ones in charge. Thirdly, as it will be explained briefly in the next step, it is
necessary for the financing of the project because the onset assumption is the neces-
sity of making the project a group project as a guarantee of its success and durability.

5.4
Payment system
A fair payment system has to be put in place to guarantee everyone contributes
equally to the electrification project and then pays for the electricity they consume.
Indeed, a system in which each member of the energy cooperative invests up to what
they can and then never pays for electricity again would not be fair nor sustainable
because the member who invested $10 and the one who invested $1,000 would have
access to the exact same service. The following suggested payment system is an at-
tempt to solve that issue.
Once the design of the system has been completely done, the overall capital cost,
called C in this section, can be estimated more or less precisely, as well as the price of
one kilowatt-hour of energy. If the number of people participating in the project and
willing to invest by being a member of the energy cooperative is called N, for all the

C/N, upfront. However, in reality, people will not contribute equally because they do
not have the same financial means. The situation in which person A contributes up
to $10 and person B contributes up to $1,000 is taken as an example, assuming that
$10 < C/N < $1,000. Person A will pay for the electricity they consume, based on
the price of the kilowatt-hour until what they have paid equals C/N. The situation

amount of money will be paid back to them from the cash generated from the sale of

from the beginning to people who have more limited means and who would rather pay
progressively. For each person who has contributed up to C/N, a free and fixed elec-

will have to pay for each kilowatt-hour of electricity consumed above that allowance.
All the people in the community who are not members of the cooperative will have to

The money surplus from the sale of energy will be used to constitute an operation
and maintenance reserve, where money can be taken whenever new equipment has to

CHAPTER 6
Policy overview
Even when the energy projects are fully prepared technically and are socially em-
braced by communities, they cannot thrive without the right regulatory framework.
Accordingly, this chapter addresses the policy issue by taking as a starting point the
current policies and laws in the Senegalese energy sector and then analyzing how they
could better boost the renewable energy sector in the case of rural electrification.
6.1
Current policies and laws
The already existing policies and laws have been analyzed with the objective of finding
out whether they would enable the realization of the different concepts developed
earlier. They have started setting a framework and have clearly stated the desire
of the country to encourage the development of renewable energy and boost rural
electrification, among others.
6.1.1
Law regulating the sector of electricity
The law regulating the electricity sector in Senegal is the law n°98-29 of April 14th
1998. The objective of the law is to reform the electricity sector in order to guaran-
tee the country has access to electricity at the lowest cost, both in urban and rural
areas. The law states, in its explanatory statement, that the presence of the State is
not enough to develop the energy sector. It recognizes the importance of providing
incentives for the private sector through the legal and regulatory framework.
The main element of the law applying to the current project is the necessity to obtain
a license in order to produce, distribute or sell electricity. The criteria for obtaining
this license are as follows:
• the ability of the company that applies for a license to respect all its obligations;
• the ability to carry out all the activities for which the license is asked and the
experience of the company in this field, the integrity of the shareholders and of
the company executive;
==PAGE:98==
82
6 Policy overview
• the ability to ensure compliance with the rules of personnel and public security,
of protection of the environment and urban planning regulation;
• the ability to assume civil liability for the activities the license is asked;
• the development of energy production capacity from energy sources compliant
with the current sector policy;
• the development of energy distribution capacity that is compliant with the
current sector policy;
• the security of electrical systems, installations and equipment;
• the proper protection of the environment and proper usage of the land.
The only situation in which any natural or legal person is exempted from asking
for a license, is when the energy produced, transported and distributed is for self-
consumption, within the household or company buildings, as long as no equipment
stands in the State or national domain. A license is not needed, but all the safety
and insurance conditions must be respected. In any case, this law only applies to the
energy plants whose installed capacity is higher than 50 kVA.
The electricity sector law also establishes the creation of the Senegalese Agency for
Rural Electrification (ASER) whose role is, according to the law, to provide techni-
cal and financial assistance to electricity companies and individuals working for rural
electrification. As mentioned in Section 1.3.3, ASER organizes public tenders in order
to entrust the rural electrification of predefined concessions to private operators.
6.1.2
Law on renewable energy
A law on renewable energy, the law n° 2010-21, was adopted in 2010 to boost the
development of renewable energy throughout the country. The law aims at provid-
ing incentives for the sale and remuneration of electricity produced from renewable
energy sources, reduce the widespread use of fossil fuels and promote the spread of
renewable energy equipment, among others.
To build that regulatory structure, the renewable energy sector benefits from tax in-
centives in the acquisition of equipment for production, exploitation and self-consump-
tion of renewable energy, and also in the acquisition of research equipment in that
field. The acquisition of equipment for domestic self-consumption of renewable energy
benefits from full tax exemption and any natural or legal person is free to produce
their own renewable energy, for their own consumption1.

6.2
Policy recommendations
The policies that could be taken by Senegal are the ones that will set the path leading
the country to where it wants to be, energy wise, in a few years, as to say, energy
independent.
This means each issue standing in the way must be analyzed and
addressed separately with the formulation of adequate policies.
6.2.1
Changes in the existing laws
The existing laws, summarized above, related to energy and renewable energy in
particular, prove Senegal is determined to see the renewable energy field flourish.
However, some elements in the laws could be modified in order to facilitate the real-
ization of projects like the ones detailed in the present study.
First of all, the law n° 98-29 of April 14th 1998, regulating the sector of electric-
ity and presented in Section 6.1.1, demands that a company applying to obtain a
license have experience in the energy domain. The reason why this criteria is present
in the law is probably to make sure the company who would sell electricity to the
Senegalese National Electricity Company (SENELEC) is competent enough to honor
their contract and deliver the agreed amount of electricity. Nevertheless, in the rural
electrification project presented here, the framework differs as the village consumes
electricity produced by the village company cooperative. There would not be a power
purchase agreement between the village cooperative and SENELEC. Having the ex-
perience as a criteria in the law favors foreign companies over Senegalese companies
since so far, very few Senegalese companies have experience in the production of elec-
tricity from renewable energy sources. Besides, as the framework becomes different
with the appearance of energy cooperatives, this clause, which is a barrier to their
activity, should be removed.
Secondly, the term ”electricity self-consumption” has to be clarified to include new
ways of producing and distributing electricity. Both the law n° 98-29 of April 14th
1998, regulating the sector of electricity and the law n° 2010-21 on renewable energy
provide benefits for self-consumption that are the exemption from having a license
and tax benefits. The import of equipment intended for domestic self-consumption
of energy benefits from full tax exemption. The term ”electricity self-consumption”
in the law could therefore be broadened to include the consumption by a village of
electricity produced by the village energy cooperative. Making the rural electrifica-
tion projects benefit from full tax exemption when importing the energy equipment,
at least in the first years could boost their development.

6.2.2
Long-term policies
In the long term, the ideal situation for the country would be to do the energy system
analysis locally (engineering studies, site assessment, system design, environmental
study, etc), do the installation of renewable energy systems with a local workforce
and perform the operation and maintenance locally, in a few words, have renewable
energy expertise. In order to reach that point, a diverse and educated local workforce
should be constituted and the best way to do so is through education combined with
the setting up, in the country, of renewable energy equipment factories. During the
field research surveys, most of the people interviewed did not know about renewable
energy and almost none knew about mini-grids. When children were asked whether
they had heard about solar energy or wind energy at school, the answer was negative.
This means renewable energy should be introduced in schools curricula and more
sustainable energy branches should be opened in universities and technical schools.
This will create a local qualified workforce of engineers and technicians. Partnerships
could be established between Senegalese universities and foreign universities in or-
der to share knowledge and develop the Senegalese academia in that field. Keeping
that goal in mind, the law should be designed to create an enabling environment
that encourages foreign investors to establish renewable energy factories in Senegal
in partnership with the local Senegalese entrepreneurs. This would permit to manu-
facture renewable energy equipment locally as well as train a local workforce so the
knowledge and know-how can be transmitted.
Another important point, already argued for in Chapter 3, is to make renewable
energy sources assessments public so the data about the potential of Senegal in re-
newable energy is available to everyone. This will be a way of boosting entrepreneur-
ship and increasing efficiency in the phase of data collection, which is a long process.
Universities and research centers could be involved in carrying out measurements and
the main university in each region could be in charge of regional measurements. This
national collaboration would result in the creation of an atlas for each energy source,
with local measurements to have the most accurate data. Knowing the country better,
in terms of resources, is essential to be able to harness those resources.
To facilitate the financing of renewable energy projects, the State should incentivize
banks to offer free-interest or low-interest loans to the energy cooperatives.
This
would help fill the financing gap when neither the personal contributions nor the
money obtained through crowdfunding is enough to cover the capital expenditure.
As things are, as local renewable energy projects are not commonplace yet, they are
associated with a perceived high risk of investment so an impetus from the State
would help to remove that obstacle. Furthermore, according to the articles 160 and
161 of the Uniform Act relative to the Law on Cooperative Companies, different co-
operatives can regroup in cooperative networks [68]. They could therefore apply for
bank loans as networks, as it would reduce the risk for banks as instead of having one
renewable energy project, they would have a portfolio of projects. Lastly, it is impor-

to renewable energy projects on the one hand, and to rural electrification projects,
on the other hand. This will create a well-known climate for the development of

Senegal having two national agencies that are, on one side, the one for rural elec-

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
Several elements in this research project can be discussed, as the electrification of
rural areas in Senegal with hybrid energy systems is still a niche, where much is to be
improved and still needs to be discovered. Every solution comes with its drawbacks,
whether it be costs wise or technology wise. A discussion on some of the findings
of the research is presented below, followed by recommendations for possible future
work.
7.1
Discussion on the results
First of all, it is legitimate to wonder whether the renewable energy sources assess-
ment is accurate enough, as all the models were not validated by measurements. For
the wind resource assessment, the GWA and Frogfoot models have to be confronted to
measurements [55] but real measurement data were not available for this project. The
same is true for the solar resource assessment, where long-term measurement is also
needed [51][21]. This shows the necessity, whenever considering the introduction of a
renewable energy system in a specific area, to conduct those measurement campaigns.
Secondly, the hybrid solutions proposed in this study hybridize mainly the solar re-
source, the wind resource and a storage device. These solutions give a hint as to what
can be done in the villages but have flaws that can be eliminated. The first flaw is
that the solutions do not cover the entire range of renewable energy sources that can
be used. Biomass, for example, is a resource available in all the regions, in different
forms such as agricultural residues, animal excrement and food waste but was not
incorporated in this study because of the inability to estimate the resource. As a
matter of fact, one interviewee in the electrified village of Ross-Béthio said that her
dream was to have an integrated farming compound, combining agriculture and cattle
raising. She imagined it as a cycle: the machines, the irrigation machine in particular,
would be powered by biomass obtained from cow dung; irrigation would make the
fields flourish and produce hay that would feed the cattle. This anecdote shows the
power of biomass and the extent to which this resource could be used, locally. With
biomass, a biogas generator could be integrated to the design of the hybrid system
and counter-balance the variability of the wind and solar resources. The second flaw
of the design is the use of lead-acid batteries. Even though they have a big share
in today’s storage market, they come with health and environmental hazards, espe-
==PAGE:104==
88
7 Discussion
cially in developing countries where the recycling of the battery units is not always
monitored. Their use can consequently be controversial. They are nonetheless the
least-cost option.
Thirdly, the solutions can be criticized for being too basic or not equaling the quality
service the grid can offer. They however support productive activities and are the
first step towards a more complete electrification in which each individual is able to
consume as much electricity they need, at an affordable price. The decentralized, hy-
brid solutions given in this report, thanks to the revenues they will generate through
income-generating activities, will open the door to more complex systems that can
include health centers, multimedia centers, schools with sufficient resources such as
television sets, computers and scientific material.
Lastly, the fact that it is suggested herein that the people implement their electri-
fication themselves can lead to discussions. It is usually believed that electricity is a
basic commodity that has to be provided by the State or municipality. Nevertheless,
as many communities are still deprived of electricity at this time, the electrification
process has to be accelerated by other means. Community members being in the best
position to know their needs, should lead that process to ensure the solution is closest
to their interests.
7.2
Field research improvements and lessons
The field research of this project was limited by time constraints which is the rea-
son why no more than four villages could be surveyed. Increasing the sample size
would be a major improvement as it would give a better description of the general
picture. Selecting villages in each of the fourteen regions of Senegal would be ideal as
their characteristics are not necessarily the same. In each region, not only talking to
community members but to the authorities, in order to compare their opinions and
different views on the energy issue would help understand this issue better.
The field work provided several lessons that must be borne in mind when replicating
them. They are presented below.
Organization of the interviews
First of all, it is important to bring visual elements of the energy systems that are
talked about during the surveys. In the village of Diel Mbam, the village council said
they would have been more comfortable to talk about the renewable energy system
if they had seen it. If bringing a small model of a renewable energy system is not
possible, bringing pictures and videos are an alternative.
Secondly, it works best to be accompanied and introduced to the village community
==PAGE:105==
7.2 Field research improvements and lessons
89
by someone from the community or by someone from an NGO they already know
well. In the first two villages, both a villager and an ENDA worker were present
which directly created a feeling of trust. On the contrary, in the last two villages, no
coordinator was present and the interviewees were much more suspicious, making it
harder, or impossible to proceed with the surveys.
Thirdly, it has been noticed that individual interviews, in which people were taken
apart, were much more genuine than group interviews, or interviews in which people
were sitting close to each other, hearing each other’s responses. In the latter, people
were influencing each other and as a consequence, the answers were very similar. This
is what happened in the village of Ndiébène Gandiole, as everybody was sitting to-
gether. There was also a phenomena of censorship and self-censorship as some people
did not want to say certain things out loud, not wanting to be heard, or would be rep-
rimanded by others when saying “sensitive” things. Doing the interviews individually
are therefore probably best to ensure the authenticity of the responses and avoid bias.
Lastly, there were some questions people did not really understand or did not know
how to answer to. These questions should probably have been explained better to
make sure the interviewees understood them properly.
This was the case for the
questions regarding climate change, the question “How is your income spent between
energy, food, transportation, school, etc.?” and in some cases, the question “What
are the good things about living in the village?”.
Organization of the Senegalese society
The different surveys showed the communities were not necessarily organized in the
classic state way but in the traditional way. Even though there is an elected mayor,
there is still great respect for the traditional authorities who are the village chiefs
and the elders. In the village of Diel Mbam, even though the mayor had provided the
names of the households to go to, the village chief was still the one to decide whether
the survey could be done or not.
Impact of the research
In all the villages, the community complained about the fact that there had been
other surveys and that they had received many politicians who asked them questions
about their lives, but that nothing ever followed. The first person interviewed in
Ndiébène said there were too many surveys, which is the reason why they were re-
luctant to talk. They were tired of talking for nothing. This means that when a
research project is implemented, carrying out the pilot projects in the villages where
the surveys took place would be good, so communities still have faith in research and
do not feel cheated.

The extension of this research project should include all the cost elements related to
the energy systems, beyond the cost of the electrical components. Indeed, the policy
maker, community member or entrepreneur reading this paper should have a direct
and global idea of how much it would cost to implement a renewable energy system
in a village. Therefore, the costs of building the energy center, the school or the
cold storage room; the costs of the equipment associated with productive uses such
as irrigation pumps, grinding machines or cutting machines; the cost of importation
of the energy system components and transportation to the designated village should
all be exposed.
Additionally, case studies in all the regions of Senegal can be included, following
on the extension of the surveys explained earlier. Studying and analyzing various
applications of hybrid renewable energy systems in different environment and serving
different productive uses give a sketch of how adaptable and flexible those systems
are, and how they adapt to different setups.
Furthermore, having a pilot project would be a great way of confronting the study to
reality. It would be the opportunity to study the chosen community deeply, design
the electrical system in detail and see what the major pitfalls in the implementation
of the project are. The study would be adjusted and would have higher chances of
success on a bigger scale.
Lastly, the possibility to connect some of the villages between them could be studied.
It would permit the flow of electricity from one village to the other when needed,
when one village has extra electricity that would otherwise be wasted and when the
other village lacks electricity. The cost of connecting the villages depending on the
distance between them would have to be estimated, along with the money that would
be saved.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Through the completion of this project, the various objectives of the research, stated
from the beginning, have been met. A renewable energy sources assessment has re-
vealed an excellent solar resource, in most parts of the country, with an annual global
horizontal irradiation of more than 2,000 kWh/m²/year. Due to a thick cloud cover
during the rainy season, from July to October, the solar irradiance is lower during
that time of the year. The wind resource is not as widespread as the solar resource
and is however mainly concentrated in the northern coast, with wind speeds going
up to 5.7 m/s at a height of 50 m. Some local high wind spots have also been found
in the eastern regions of Kedougou and Tambacounda, with wind speeds higher than
5 m/s at some specific locations. The study also shows a seasonality for the wind
resource, with lower wind speeds during the rainy season from July to October, and
higher wind speeds during the dry season from October to June, with the months
from January to March being the period with the most wind. The hydropower re-
source can be found throughout the country, along the main rivers but its potential is
useful only for medium to large scale applications (> 30 MW installed capacity), and
is not suitable for the community-scale perspective which is of interest for this study.
The biomass resource is believed to be abundant in all the regions, under different
forms, but an exact estimation could not be done within the framework of this study.
However, the resource assessment of the communities selected for the field research
showed very good wind speeds especially as the communities are located along the
coast, specifically the coastal region of Saint-Louis. The communities of Diel Mbam
and Ourour L’Islam both have wind speeds around 5.3 m/s at a height of 50 m, which
is a value suitable for small-scale applications. They also have a good solar radiation,
with a yearly global irradiation of around 2400 kWh/m² for Diel Mbam and 2200
kWh/m² for Ourour L’Islam. The communities seemed to have a variety of biomass
resources available to them but these resources are yet to be estimated. Generally,
the study argues for the importance of assessing all the renewable resources of the
country through measurements and establish a clear mapping. This is a crucial step
to confirm the models results.
The energy needs of rural communities have been estimated, spanning from basic
electricity needs such as lighting and phone charging to productive uses like irrigation,
preservation of perishable goods and grain grinding. Specific values could be found in
==PAGE:108==

the literature but more explicitly defined and localized information was found during
the field research. In the surveyed electrified villages, the electricity demand was mea-
sured at 6.9 kWh/day at Ross-Béthio and 3.1 kWh/day at Ndiébène Gandiole (using
one calculation method). The load consumption of the income-generating activities
were added: 12 kWh/day for water pumping, 7.5 kWh/day for grain grinding and
54 kWh/day for a cold storage room. This information, coupled with the renewable
resource assessment, was at the base of the design of electrification solutions, meeting
the targeted and specific needs that were identified. Two main configurations were
suggested, one involves re-grouping the electricity services in a power center for the
farming village of Ourour L’Islam, and the second providing electricity to the house-
holds, through a mini-grid, in the fishing village of Diel Mbam. Findings from the
techno-economic assessment shows that the most economical and reliable solution for
Ourour L’Islam is based on a solar system in order to power the energy center, with
a net present cost of approximately $52,000, an internal rate of return of 25% and a
discounted payback time of 4 years. A solution based on a diesel-only system would
cost $155,000 and would therefore be much more expensive than the solar system. A
hybrid wind and solar renewable energy system appears to be the preferred option
for the mini-grid in Diel Mbam, with an estimated cost of $537,000, an internal rate
of return of 8.8% and a discounted payback time of 12 years. This hybrid renewable
energy system is economically more attractive than a diesel-only system, which would
cost $816,000. Both configurations, that is to say the hybrid wind and solar system
in Diel Mbam and the solar system in Ourour L’Islam, include a battery in order to
guarantee the reliability of the energy system.
In addition to technology, a major question this project aimed to answer was how
to concretely realize those electrification solutions, in a real context. Findings from
the study recommend anchoring the energy system in the Senegalese rural realities
and empowering the communities by using a bottom-up approach, designed in a way
analogous to the car market in Senegal. Indeed, renewable energy systems could be
bought and imported but spare parts should be sold in the country to ensure mainte-
nance and repairs can be done locally, anytime. This solution is only transitional as
in the long term, there should be factories in the country in order to build the energy
components locally to get rid of the importation costs and boost local employment
and expertise. In the implementation method suggested, rural energy cooperatives
are the ones driving electrification projects. They benefit from the technical and fi-
nancial help of the rural agency, ASER, and renewable energy agency, ANER, but
are the ones operating the system, managing how the electricity has to be distributed
and taking care of the maintenance. The organization of training sessions will guar-
antee the presence of all the necessary skills among the communities.
Lastly, the study provides recommendations of enabling policies needed for the re-
alization of these projects as well as the creation of a favorable market of renewable
energy for rural electrification. This would hopefully lead to having within the coun-
try, industries and expertise in that field, in order to be able to produce the equipment

locally. The development of a skilled and diverse workforce would naturally follow, to
take care of the entire electrification process, from equipment production to electricity
distribution. The policies include:
• more flexibility in the law to allow village energy cooperatives to thrive;
• tax incentives;
• renewable energy study lines in universities and technical schools;
• partnerships between foreign investors and Senegalese entrepreneurs;
• transparency in the renewable energy sources assessment;
• facilitated access to financing and creation of support schemes;
• collaboration between the energy cooperatives and State agencies.
This study provides guidelines that could be used by all relevant actors in the rural
electrification mechanism. It presents a multidisciplinary approach where technical
elements, economic estimations, social considerations and policy recommendations
are crossed to make quick and sustainable rural electrification a reality.

Survey questionnaire
English version of the survey questions
Questions to ask and things to say before starting the interview:
– Can the interview be recorded?
– Can I take your name or would you prefer to remain anonymous?
General questions about the respondent:
– How old are you?
– Do you work? If yes, where?
Questions about climate change in order for me to introduce renewables:
– Have you noticed any changes in your environment during the past years? Any
change in the usual seasonal pattern?
– Do you see any problems in the village related to solid waste, water pollution,
health degradation linked to a polluted environment?
– How do you think those issues should be solved?
– At this stage of the interview, explain that my project is based on renewables
only, so on natural resources they have around them. Give a bit more details
about the project.
– Has there been any similar project in the community before?
Questions about life in the household and in the village:
– What are your everyday activities?
