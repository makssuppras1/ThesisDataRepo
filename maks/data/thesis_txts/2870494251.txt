Abstract 
With global temperatures reaching critical thresholds and climate change accelerating, urgent 
action is required to reduce atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO₂) levels and mitigate its devastating 
impacts. The urgent need for CO₂ removal from the atmosphere has driven the development of 
Direct Air Capture (DAC) technologies. However, traditional DAC methods has energy 
consumption and efficiency limitations. This study explores enzyme-assisted DAC, leveraging 
carbonic anhydrase (CA) to enhance CO₂ capture efficiency in liquid-based systems. A 
comparative analysis was conducted using potassium hydroxide (KOH), potassium carbonate 
(K₂CO₃), and L-lysine as absorbents, both with and without enzymatic catalysis. Experimental 
trials were performed in a counter-current packed column system, where CO₂ absorption was 
evaluated by real-time pH monitoring and proton concentration analysis. Results demonstrate that 
KOH exhibits the highest CO₂ absorption efficiency due to its rapid reaction kinetics. However, 
its regeneration energy requirements pose challenges for large-scale deployment. In contrast, 
K₂CO₃ and L-lysine, particularly when combined, showed competitive performance with reduced 
energy demand. This research explores bio-inspired carbon capture strategies by demonstrating 
the viability of enzyme-assisted DAC as a scalable technology for atmospheric CO₂ mitigation. It 
highlights the potential of enzyme-enhanced solvent systems to provide an energy-efficient and 
sustainable solution for CO₂ capture, addressing both economic and environmental challenges 
associated with DAC. Future work should focus on integrating renewable energy sources for 
solvent regeneration, and scaling up the technology for industrial applications.

1. Introduction 
The global increase in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions poses a critical challenge for 
environmental sustainability. Climate change, driven predominantly by GHG emissions, poses an 
existential threat to ecosystems and human society. Among these gases, carbon dioxide (CO₂) is 
the most significant contributor, accounting for more than 73% of global greenhouse emissions. 
The CO₂ emissions come from a variety of sources including energy, agriculture, industry, and 
waste sectors (Ritchie, 2020). As one of the leading contributors to climate change, CO₂ emissions 
from these major sources require immediate attention. The consequences of CO2-driven climate 
change include rising global temperatures, sea-level rise, and increasingly severe weather events, 
all of which impose long-term and irreversible impacts (Baker et al, 2018). Innovations across 
various sectors are needed to reduce the effects of CO2 emissions (Ritchie, 2020). To mitigate the 
climate challenges, international efforts such as the Paris Agreement signed in 2015 aim to limit 
global temperature increases to 1.5°C above pre-industrial (1850-1900) levels (Falkner, 2016).  
However, according to Copernicus Climate Change Service, the surface air temperature of October 
2024 was 1.65°C above the pre-industrial level (Figure 1) (Copernicus, 2024). So the limit has 
been reached in 2024, which call for instant actions with green technologies.  
In recent years, significant advancements in clean energy technologies such as solar, wind, and 
nuclear power have contributed in reducing greenhouse gas emissions. However, given the 
constant dependence on fossil fuels and the challenge of addressing historical emissions, carbon 
removal and de-carbonization technologies have become essential components of climate 
mitigation strategies. These technologies can be broadly categorized into carbon capture, 
utilization, and storage (CCUS) and emerging ocean-based carbon removal methods. Carbon 
capture technologies primarily focus on capturing CO₂ from industrial processes and power 
generation. These methods include point-source carbon capture, which can be classified into post-
combustion capture (removing CO₂ after fuel combustion), pre-combustion capture (removing 
CO₂ before fuel combustion), and oxy-fuel combustion (burning fuel in pure oxygen to facilitate 
CO₂ capture) (Liu et al., 2023). Alternatively, ocean-based carbon removal strategies are gaining 
attention. Techniques such as ocean alkalinity enhancement aim to enhance the ocean’s capacity 
to absorb and store CO₂ (Yang et al., 2023). These approaches use the ocean's ability to absorb 
carbon to help to mitigate climate change. Once CO₂ is captured, it can be utilized or stored through

various approaches. Carbon utilization involves processes such as enhanced oil recovery (EOR) 
and the conversion of captured CO₂ into synthetic fuels and industrial products. Meanwhile, carbon 
storage includes geological storage, where CO₂ is injected into deep underground rock formations,

al., 2023). Additionally, Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS) integrates 
bioenergy production with carbon capture, allowing for the removal of atmospheric CO₂ while

Direct Air Capture (DAC) captures CO₂ directly from ambient air, making it useful for reducing 
emissions from industries like aviation and cement production (International Energy Agency, 
2022). DAC has been considered as a promising solution due to its scalability, flexibility, and 
ability to address both ongoing and legacy emissions. Unlike traditional CCUS, which is often

constrained by geographic and industrial limitations, DAC can be deployed in locations with 
access to renewable energy and suitable geological storage sites, making it a versatile tool in global 
de-carbonization efforts (McQueen et al., 2021). If deep emissions reductions are to be achieved, 
DAC may become an indispensable component of future climate strategies. 
 
 
1.1. Direct Air Capture (DAC) 
DAC is a technology designed to remove carbon dioxide directly from the atmosphere. It employs 
chemical processes to extract CO₂, which can then be permanently stored underground or utilized 
in various applications. This technology is primarily categorized into two types (Figure 2) based 
on the materials and processes employed: solid sorbent-based and liquid solvent-based systems. 
Emerging methods, such as cryogenic separation and humidity-swing adsorption, are also under 
development, though they are less advanced (McQueen et al, 2021). Solid sorbent-based DAC (S-
DAC) (Figure 2A) utilizes materials such as metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), zeolites, or 
amine-functionalized supports to bind CO₂, which is then released through the application of high 
heat or vacuum. Liquid solvent-based DAC (L-DAC), on the other hand, relies on solutions like 
potassium hydroxide (KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) to absorb CO₂, followed by a high-
temperature chemical regeneration process (Li & Yao, 2024). Liquid-DAC (Figure 2B) removes 
CO2 using a process involving solvent-based absorption and desorption. In the absorption phase, 
atmospheric air is drawn into the system using large fans, where it passes over chemical solvents 
that selectively react with CO₂ to form a carbonate compound. Once the CO₂ is captured, the 
system moves to the desorption phase, where the carbonate solution is subjected to heat, causing 
the chemical reaction to reverse and release concentrated CO₂ gas. After CO₂ release, the solvent 
is cooled, regenerated, and recycled back into the absorption system, making the process 
continuous and efficient (“Negative Emissions Technologies and Reliable Sequestration”, 2018).  
This feature allows DAC systems to be strategically sited in regions with abundant renewable 
energy, optimizing their carbon capture potential while minimizing land-use conflicts. 
Additionally, DAC technologies do not require large-scale agricultural land, which reduces 
competition with food production and preserves ecosystem services (World Resources Institute, 
2024). Furthermore, when integrated with renewable energy sources, DAC can operate

sustainably, offering a pathway for net-negative emissions. The scalability of DAC, alongside its 
ability to remove CO₂ directly from the atmosphere, positions it as a vital tool in achieving global 
climate targets (International Energy Agency, 2022).

However, DAC is not without its challenges. L-DAC is considered energy-intensive primarily due 
to the process of solvent regeneration. In L-DAC systems, carbon dioxide is captured by chemical 
solvents, which must be heated to release the CO₂. This regeneration step requires significant 
==PAGE:8==

amounts of thermal energy, as heat is applied to break the bond between the CO₂ and the solvent 
(International Energy Agency, 2022). The absorption and desorption cycles must be repeated 
continuously, further increasing energy consumption. The need for large amounts of heat and, in 
some cases, additional power for air compression, makes L-DAC a high-energy process (Kotowicz 
et al., 2025 & Ozkan, 2024). Due to this energy-intensive process, the costs are currently high. A 
report by the IEA estimated that projects like FOAK DACCS are likely to range from 
approximately $400-$700/ton CO2 Capture (IEAGHG, 2021). Moreover, large-scale use of DAC 
requires robust infrastructure, including storage sites and transportation networks. While solid 
sorbent and liquid solvent-based systems are relatively advanced, newer methods require 
significant research and development. When integrated with renewable energy, DAC offers a 
viable path to achieve negative emissions, making it a key tool for fighting climate changes (Qiu 
et al., 2022). 
 
1.2. Column Setup 
DAC using chemical absorption involves capturing carbon dioxide from ambient air through 
reactions with liquid solvents, typically strong alkaline solutions like potassium hydroxide (KOH) 
or sodium hydroxide (NaOH). These solvents react with CO₂ to form carbonate or bicarbonate 
compounds. The process takes place in an absorption column, where atmospheric air is passed 
through a contactor designed to maximize the interaction between air and the liquid solvent. This 
is achieved using packing materials that increase the surface area, enhancing CO₂ capture 
efficiency (Keith et al., 2018). The absorption column is generally set up as a vertical structure, 
allowing for counter-current flow air moves upward while the liquid solvent flows downward. 
This arrangement improves mass transfer efficiency. Alternatively, crossflow designs, where air 
moves horizontally across falling liquid films, are also used (An et al., 2024). After CO₂ is 
absorbed, the carbonate-rich solution undergoes a regeneration process involving heating or 
chemical reactions to release concentrated CO₂, which can then be stored or utilized, while the 
solvent is recycled (Mazzotti et al., 2013). Efficient column design and advanced materials are key 
factors for minimizing energy consumption and operational costs in DAC systems.

1.3. Chemical absorption in DAC technologies 
DAC technologies using potassium hydroxide solution has significant attention for their potential 
to efficiently remove CO₂ from ambient air. Hydroxide plays a significant role in DAC systems 
by serving as an effective chemical sorbent for CO₂ removal (Zanatta, 2023). In the liquid-based 
DAC systems, atmospheric CO₂ reacts with aqueous KOH to form potassium carbonate, which is 
then processed to release concentrated CO₂ for storage or utilization. This method works well due 
to the high reactivity and availability of KOH, enabling effective CO₂ capture (International 
Energy Agency, 2022). However, despite these advantages, KOH-based systems have limitations. 
KOH requires more energy because it absorbs CO₂ at lower temperatures but needs to be heated 
above 300-900°C to release the CO₂ (International Energy Agency, 2022). Energy requirement in 
KOH is even more than MEA (100-120°C) (Barzagli et al., 2016). This limitation has driven to 
explore alternative solvents and strategies to mitigate the high energy demands associated with 
KOH-based systems (Erans et al., 2022 & International Energy Agency, 2022). Recent 
advancements have focused on enhancing the efficiency and scalability of DAC systems while 
addressing these challenges. One notable example, Song et al (2024) has designed a new 
electrochemical reactor that not only captures CO₂ from the air but also directly converts it into 
useful raw materials. It reduces energy requirements and lowers the costs for both CO₂ capture and 
conversion, thereby improving the overall economics of DAC technologies. The high energy 
requirements for regenerating KOH in DAC systems highlight the need to explore alternative 
absorbents, such as potassium carbonate and amino acids. Researchers are also investigating the 
use of enzymes, like carbonic anhydrase, to enhance the CO₂ capture process, potentially reducing 
energy consumption and improving system efficiency (Zaghini et al, 2025). 
Potassium carbonate (K₂CO₃) has gained prominence as a solvent in CO₂ capture from flue gas 
due to its numerous advantages over traditional amine-based solutions. K₂CO₃ is less corrosive, 
exhibits greater thermal stability, and has a lower energy requirement for regeneration. These 
properties not only reduce operational costs but also make the process more environmentally 
sustainable (Stolaroff, 2013 & Ayittey et al., 2020). Additionally, potassium carbonate solutions 
can be enhanced with the introduction of enzymes such as carbonic anhydrase, which catalyze the 
hydration of CO₂, thereby increasing the overall absorption rate (Shao et al., 2024 & Zaghini et al, 
2025). K2CO3 solutions are economically advantageous than KOH due to their low vapor pressure 
==PAGE:10==
7 
 
but it has slower reaction rate than KOH or NaOH with CO₂. K₂CO₃ reacts slowly with low 
concentrations of CO₂ in ambient air, requiring longer contact times or larger equipment (Rouxhet 
et al, 2022). Additionally, regenerating K₂CO₃ from Potassium bicarbonate (KHCO₃) still requires 
significant thermal energy, increasing energy costs and potentially negating environmental 
benefits (“Negative Emissions Technologies and Reliable Sequestration,” 2018). Over time, 
K₂CO₃ degrades, leading to reduced performance, and its low CO₂ loading capacity requires more 
material, further driving up costs (Ramezani et al., 2018). These challenges highlight the need for 
innovations to enhance the efficiency and cost-effectiveness of K₂CO₃-based DAC systems. 
Amino acids have garnered attention for their potential in L-DAC systems to sequester carbon 
dioxide (CO₂) from the atmosphere. Recent research has described the potential of lysine-based 
systems in DAC technologies for sequestering atmospheric CO₂. In a study, alkaline salts of L-
lysine were investigated as green solvents for DAC applications. The research demonstrated that 
potassium and sodium salts of L-lysine effectively captured CO₂ under laboratory conditions, 
making them promising candidates for sustainable DAC applications due to their stable molecular 
structures and favorable chemical properties (Khan et al., 2025). Finding sustainable chemical 
solvent pairing them with enzyme-assisted DAC offers a key advantage over hydroxide systems 
by achieving comparable kinetics while requiring less energy for regeneration. This promising 
approach could lead to more sustainable CO₂ capture technologies, warranting further research 
into scalability, cost-effectiveness for industrial applications. 
 
1.4. Enzyme Enhanced CO2 Capture 
Carbonic anhydrase (CA) is an enzyme that catalyzes the rapid interconversion between carbon 
dioxide and water to form bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and protons (H⁺) (Figure 3). This reaction is 
fundamental in various physiological processes, including respiration and acid-base balance. This 
reaction occurs at a near-diffusion-controlled rate, with turnover rates reaching up to a million 
times per second (Silverman, 1991). In biological systems, CA plays a crucial role in maintaining 
pH balance and facilitating CO₂ transport. The enzyme's active site typically contains a zinc ion, 
which plays a crucial role in its catalytic activity (Deutsch, 1987). This enzymatic activity has been 
harnessed to enhance CO₂ capture processes, offering a biochemical approach to carbon 
sequestration. 
==PAGE:11==
8 
 
 
Figure 3:Carbonic anhydrase, catalyzing the conversion of CO₂ and water into bicarbonate and a proton. 
 
The incorporation of CA into liquid solvents in DAC systems significantly increases the rate at 
which CO₂ dissolves into the liquid phase, maintaining a high concentration gradient between the 
gas and liquid phases. By speeding up this conversion process, CA prevents the accumulation of 
CO₂ in the liquid phase, allowing for a continuous and efficient absorption process (Fradette & 
Ceperkovic, 2010). This rapid interconversion to mass transfer of CO₂ from the gas phase into the 
liquid solvent means that CO₂ is absorbed more quickly and efficiently, enhancing the overall 
performance of the DAC system (Fradette & Ceperkovic, 2010, Zaghini et al, 2025 & Xu et al, 
2021).  
Zaghini et al, 2025 demonstrated that even sub-micro molar concentrations of CA significantly 
enhance CO₂ absorption in the aqueous solvent K₂CO₃ used for DAC. The researchers found that 
the presence of CA accelerated the rate of CO₂ capture, highlighting the enzyme's potential to 
improve the efficiency of DAC systems. Another study investigated the integration of CA into 
micro algal systems has been investigated to enhance biological CO₂ capture from air. Microalgae 
can assimilate CO₂ through photosynthesis, and the addition of CA has been shown to facilitate 
the dissolution of atmospheric CO₂, thereby increasing the availability of inorganic carbon for 
algal uptake. This approach not only aids in CO₂ capture but also promotes the growth of 
microalgae, which can be utilized for biofuels or other valuable bio products (Xu et al, 2021). 
In summary, carbonic anhydrase presents a promising avenue for enhancing direct air capture of 
carbon dioxide. Its ability to rapidly catalyze the conversion of CO₂ to bicarbonate can significantly 
improve the kinetics of CO₂ absorption processes. Ongoing research focused on enhancing enzyme 
stability, developing synthetic mimics, and integrating CA into biological systems continues to 
advance the feasibility of CA-assisted DAC technologies.

1.5. Objective of the Study 
This study aims to explore the use of the enzyme carbonic anhydrase to enhance CO₂ capture in a 
DAC system. It will be investigated by continuous absorption of CO₂ using different aqueous 
solutions. The interaction of these gases with aqueous solutions under predefined conditions 
provides insights into how flow dynamics influence absorption efficiency. The novelty of this 
work lies in the comparative analysis of different solutions, including potassium hydroxide (KOH), 
Potassium Carbonate (K₂CO₃), L-lysine, and combination of carbonate and lysine with and without 
enzymatic enhancement. By quantifying CO₂ absorption across varying gas flow rates and solution 
compositions, the study seeks to identify optimal conditions for minimizing the cost to pave a path 
for sustainable DAC technologies. Such investigations are crucial for addressing challenges in 
global CO₂ mitigation efforts, aligning with international targets for reducing greenhouse gas 
concentrations.

2. Materials and Methods 
This chapter describes the materials, equipment and methodologies employed for the experimental 
investigation of a packed column-based L-DAC system. The aim is to provide a comprehensive 
overview of the experimental setup, operating conditions, and analytical techniques used to 
evaluate the system’s performance. 
2.1. Experimental Setup for CO₂ Absorption 
 
Figure 4:This figure represents (A) Visualization of enzyme assisted DAC and representation of the 
experimental setup from Zaghini et al, 2025,  and (B) The actual photo of the  Column setup. 
 
CO₂ absorption experiments were carried out using a vertical absorption column (Figure 4). The 
column was 33 cm in height, with an internal diameter of 3 cm, and was packed with Raschig glass 
rings (Karl Hecht glassware factory) to enhance gas-liquid contact. The setup was designed to 
allow countercurrent flow of gas and liquid, with gas introduced at the bottom of the column and 
liquid introduced at the top. Predefined gas flow rates were achieved using two mass flow 
controllers (CMOSens® SFC5500 mass flow controller, Sensirion, Switzerland) to regulate the 
==PAGE:14==
11 
 
partial pressures of CO₂ (PCO2) and N2 (PN2) where the gas flow was maintained with a mixture of 
99.6% nitrogen and 0.04% carbon dioxide. A closed-loop system was employed where 50 mL of 
the liquid absorbent was retained in a beaker and continuously stirred at 800 rpm with a magnetic 
stirrer to maintain homogeneity. 
 
2.2. Measurement of CO₂ Uptake 
The pH of the absorbent solution in the beaker was continuously monitored using a WTW 3310 
pH meter (Buch & Holm, Denmark). To assess the performance of various solvents and the role 
of enzyme catalysis in enhancing CO₂ absorption efficiency, experiments were conducted using 
fresh 10 mM KOH, solutions of 10 mM L-lysine, a mixed solution of 5 mM K₂CO₃ and 5 mM L-
lysine, and 10 mM K₂CO₃. Carbonic anhydrase was introduced to a final concentration of 1 µM in 
enzyme-assisted runs, immediately before starting the liquid flow. The variation of pH was 
continuously monitored during each experiment to calculate CO₂ absorption rates, and all runs 
were performed in duplicate to ensure data reliability. 
 
2.2.1. Variable Gas Flow 
Potassium Hydroxide’s benchmark performance as a CO₂ solvent was evaluated under gas flow 
rates ranging from 40 to 400 mL/min, with the liquid flow rate held constant at 25 mL/min. Each 
experimental run lasted 3000 seconds.  
L-lysine’s CO₂ capture performance was evaluated under varying gas flow rates ranging from 10 
mL/min to 400 mL/min, with the liquid flow rate held constant at 25 mL/min. To reach the lowest 
as 10 mL/min of gas flow rate compressed air served as the gas source. Each experimental run 
lasted 4000 seconds and the pH changes during the experiments were used to track CO₂ absorption 
and to calculate absorption rates. 
The combined effects of 5 mM L-lysine and 5 mM K₂CO₃ as a mixed solvent were assessed under 
gas flow rates of 80, 200, and 400 mL/min. Liquid flow rates were maintained at 25 mL/min 
throughout these experiments. Each run lasted 3000 seconds, and pH monitoring was utilized to 
evaluate the combined absorption behavior and catalytic enhancement by CA. 
==PAGE:15==
12 
 
 
2.2.2. Variable Liquid Flow 
For K₂CO₃, experiments aimed to investigate the impact of liquid flow rates on CO₂ absorption 
efficiency. Gas flow was maintained at 200 mL/min with a mixture of 99.6% nitrogen and 0.04% 
carbon dioxide, while liquid flow rates were systematically varied at 5, 15, 25, and 32 mL/min. 
Each experimental run lasted 5000 seconds, with continuous recirculation of the solvent. 
 
2.3. Enzyme assisted absorption 
The enzyme used in this experimental setup is thermostable carbonic anhydrase. This enzyme 
catalyzes the rapid conversion of CO₂ into bicarbonate ions in aqueous solutions, which 
significantly enhances the rate of CO₂ absorption. Carbonic anhydrase accelerates the reaction: 
 
CO₂ (aq) + H₂O ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ 
(Scheme 1) 
The enzyme was added in solution to a final concentration 1 µM just prior to starting the solvent 
flow. The enzyme-enhanced CO₂ absorption rates were recorded and compared with experiments 
conducted without the enzyme.  
 
2.4. Cleaning Procedures  
Cleaning steps are essential to ensure accurate and uncontaminated experimental results. Before 
starting an experiment, the column was purged with gas of the desired composition. After each 
experiment, the absorption column, tubing, and glass rings were cleaned thoroughly. For standard 
cleaning, Milli-Q water was flushed through the system to remove any contamination, followed 
by the absorbent solution intended for the subsequent experiment. During enzyme experiments, 
new sets of the same beads were used and during the cleaning those beads additional Milli-Q water 
was poured into the column to ensure complete removal of residual enzyme and reaction products. 
It ensures complete removal of any leftover materials, maintaining the integrity of subsequent 
experiments.

2.5. Data Processing 
When carbon dioxide (CO₂) gas is introduced into an aqueous solution, it dissolves to form 
aqueous CO₂: 
 
CO2 (gas) ↔ CO2 (aq) 
 
(Scheme 2) 
Upon dissolution, a fraction of CO₂ undergoes hydration, reacting with water to form carbonic 
acid (H₂CO₃), which quickly converts into bicarbonate and a proton:  
 
CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ 
 
(Scheme 3) 
This reaction sequence leads to the production of hydrogen ions (H⁺), consequently altering the 
pH of the solution. There by, the extent of pH change serves as an indirect measure of CO₂ 
absorption, establishing a crucial relationship for quantitative analysis. 
In this study, the CO₂ capture efficiency was investigated through pH monitoring and calibration-
based quantification of proton generation. Initially, a calibration curve was established by titrating 
the various chemical solutions with known amounts of protons, allowing for the correlation 
between pH changes and the corresponding proton concentrations. These curves were generated 
through titration experiments involving the addition of precisely 1M hydrochloric acid (HCl) to 
various solvent solutions, including 10 mM KOH, L-lysine and K2CO3) solutions and a mixed 
solution of 5 mM K₂CO₃ and 5 mM L-lysine (Figure 5). This calibration curve served as the 
foundational tool to accurately interpret pH data in terms of proton equivalents.

solvents, gas flow rates, and the influence of enzymatic enhancement. The result was measured 
through pH changes during CO₂ absorption and it was continuously recorded (Figure 6A). The 
underlying mechanism involves the dissolution of CO₂ into the aqueous phase, where it reacts with 
water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which partially dissociates into protons (H⁺) and bicarbonate 
ions (HCO₃⁻). This reaction leads to an increase in proton concentration, directly influencing the 
pH. By applying the calibration curve to the pH data, the concentration of protons generated during 
CO₂ absorption was quantified (Figure 6B; Scheme 3 and Scheme 4). Given the stoichiometric 
relationship between CO₂ capture and proton release, the calculated proton concentration was 
directly proportional to the amount of CO₂ absorbed. This methodology provides a robust, indirect 
approach to quantifying CO₂ capture rates, leveraging pH as a reliable indicator of chemical 
transformation within the liquid solvents.

4. Discussion 
This study provided key insights into optimizing CO₂ capture in DAC systems using various 
solvents and enzymatic enhancements. The research systematically evaluated the performance of 
a 10 mM KOH solution, 10 mM L-Lysine, and a hybrid 5 mM L-Lysine + 5 mM K₂CO₃ system, 
both with and without the catalytic addition of 1 μM CA. The findings highlight the interactions 
between gas flow rate, solvent chemistry, and enzymatic activity in achieving effective CO₂ 
capture.  
4.1. Experiment with Variable Gas Flow 
In this study, 10 mM KOH achieved the highest CO₂ capture rate among all of the solvents (Figure 
10) which confirms that hydroxide-based solutions have the potential to efficiently capture CO₂ 
from the atmosphere. In Figure 7, it is observed that CO₂ capture initially increased with gas flow 
rate (40–300 mL/min) due to improved CO₂ availability and reaction kinetics, followed by a 
plateau in the capture rate beyond 300 mL/min. These results represented a few key factors that 
are tied to the principles of gas-liquid mass transfer and reaction kinetics. The gas flow rate directly 
influences the availability of CO₂ in the gas phase, which is essential for absorption into the liquid 
phase. As the gas flow rate increases, more CO₂ is introduced into the system, enhancing the 
reaction rates of CO₂ dissolution and its subsequent conversion to bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and 
carbonate (CO₃²⁻) ions. The increase in capture rate aligns with the expected behavior of a gas-
liquid absorption system, where higher gas flow rates provide a larger CO₂ supply for reaction. 
Consequently, the CO₂ capture rate plateaus beyond 300 mL/min. Even though the gas flow rate 
is increased, the rate of the CO₂-KOH reaction was constrained by the limited availability of 
hydroxide ions to react with the excess CO₂. This may also be due to reduced gas-liquid contact 
efficiency at high gas flow rates. Almoslh et al. (2021) found that increasing the gas flow rate 
beyond a certain point led to a significant decrease in CO₂ absorption rate, due to reduced gas-
liquid contact efficiency.  
Mass transfer is another critical factor influencing the CO₂ capture rate. At lower gas flow rates, 
there is more time for CO₂ molecules to diffuse into the liquid, allowing for efficient absorption. 
As the gas flow rate increases, the time for CO₂ to interact with the liquid decreases, resulting in a 
reduction in mass transfer efficiency. At higher gas flow rates (beyond 300 mL/min), the increased 
CO₂ concentration in the gas phase is not effectively transferred to the liquid due to reduced contact 
==PAGE:28==
25 
 
time. This results in a diminishing return in CO₂ capture, and the capture rate plateaus. The inability 
of the system to efficiently handle the higher gas flow rates due to mass transfer limitations and 
the reaction kinetics of the CO₂-KOH interaction is a key reason behind the observed behavior. 
 
Amino acids, particularly L-lysine, are promising solvents in liquid-based DAC systems due to 
their ability to chemically react with CO₂. This study represents the first systematic evaluation of 
L-lysine with CA for DAC applications. The results showed a similar increasing trend as KOH but 
exhibit a decline at higher flow rates. It highlights the interplay between gas-liquid contact 
efficiency, reaction kinetics, and mass transfer limitations in DAC system. At lower flow rates 
(40–200 mL/min), the increased CO₂ capture rate is due to sufficient gas residence time, allowing 
effective diffusion into the liquid phase and reaction with L-lysine. As the gas flow rate increased 
from 40 mL/min to 200 mL/min, the region adjacent to the gas-liquid interface (boundary layer) 
became thinner (Karamah et al., 2021). This reduction in boundary layer thickness decreases mass 
transfer resistance, thereby enhancing the overall mass transfer coefficient. However, beyond 200 
mL/min, the decline in capture rate was due to by reduced contact time of gas and liquid phase. At 
very high flow rates, gas may bypass some sections of the packing material (Raschig rings), 
leading to inefficient utilization of the absorber volume. Studies showed that increasing gas flow 
rate could enhance mass transfer up to a certain point but when gas flows in higher rate, it actually 
reduces the contact between gas and liquid, causing a drop in absorption efficiency despite the 
higher gas flow (Kayahan et al., 2023). They have also stated that in gas-liquid absorption systems, 
the gas flow rate plays a crucial role in determining the efficiency of mass transfer. So, optimizing 
the gas flow rate is essential to balance the advantages of reduced boundary layer thickness against 
the potential drawbacks. Kierzkowska-Pawlak et al (2012) explained that the formation of the 
carbamate complex (Scheme 5) is kinetically slower compared to reactions with stronger alkaline 
solvents like KOH. Because of the faster reaction rates of CO₂ with hydroxide ions, which involve 
simple acid-base neutralization, while carbamate formation requires additional steps such as 
zwitterion stabilization.  It implies that in weak bases or amine, the reaction with CO₂ proceeds 
slowly, leading to a reduced capture rate.  
When carbonic anhydrase is added to the L-lysine solution, it significantly improves the rate of 
CO₂ capture. By speeding up the rate of CO₂ hydration to bicarbonate ensures faster availability 
==PAGE:29==
26 
 
of CO₂ for reaction leads to a significant boost in the CO₂ capture rate, it shows the maximum 
capture rate of 2.1 µM/s at 200 mL/min. Interestingly, both enzyme-assisted and enzyme-free 
systems peaked at 200 mL/min before declining at higher flow rates, likely due to reduced 
residence time for gas-liquid interaction. However, even with CA, the capture rate begins to 
decline at higher flow rates. The presence of CA not only catalyzes the reaction between CO₂ and 
water but also affects the transport rate in the boundary layer, as demonstrated by Zaghini et al. 
(2025). This suggests that CA influences the reaction kinetics, rather than being limited by 
diffusion and gas-liquid contact efficiency alone. At high flow rates, the gas phase moves too 
quickly for adequate contact with the liquid, and the residence time is reduced, leading to less CO₂ 
being absorbed into the liquid in the first place. Thus, even though the enzyme enhances the 
reaction kinetics in the liquid, it cannot overcome the physical limitations of gas-phase transport 
or the reduced contact time at higher flow rates, which causes the capture rate to decline beyond a 
certain flow rate.  
 
The combination of L-lysine and K₂CO₃ exhibited a synergistic improvement in CO₂ capture rates 
compared to L-lysine alone. The hybrid system exhibited increased performance at higher flow 
rates, achieving a maximum capture rate of 2.5 µM/s at 400 mL/min with 1 µM CA which is 
approximately 20% higher than its enzyme-free experiment and closely approaches the capture 
rate observed for KOH in the system (Figure 10B). The observed synergy between L-lysine and 
potassium carbonate in enhancing CO₂ capture rates can be attributed to the complementary 
mechanisms by which each compound interacts with CO₂, leading to improved overall 
performance. L-lysine, an amino acid with two amine groups, reacts with CO₂ to form carbamate 
complexes, facilitating rapid absorption. Meanwhile, K₂CO₃ captures CO₂ by forming bicarbonate 
ions in an alkaline medium. When combined, these two compounds create a dual absorption 
pathway where L-lysine ensures fast reaction kinetics, and K₂CO₃ contributes to CO₂ capture 
capacity within the system. Studies have shown that certain additives can significantly improve 
the performance of K₂CO₃ solutions. For instance, the addition of piperazine has been observed to 
create a synergistic effect, resulting in a higher CO₂ absorption rate than when using K₂CO₃ alone. 
This synergistic interaction is not universal and appears to be specific to certain solvent pairs 
(Zhuang & Clements, 2017). Experimental results from this study indicate that the L-lysine + 
==PAGE:30==
27 
 
K₂CO₃ mixture, in the presence of CA, achieves a capture rate comparable to or exceeding that of 
L-lysine alone, reinforcing the presence of a synergistic effect. Zaghini et al. (2025) reported that 
under similar experimental conditions, K₂CO₃ exhibited a CO₂ capture rate of 0.6 µM/s at a flow 
rate of 200 mL/min and 1 µM/s at 400 mL/min in the presence of an enzyme. Given that their 
study was conducted using the same apparatus and under analogous conditions, a direct 
comparison with the present results is justified. At 400 mL/min, K₂CO₃ alone captured CO₂ at a 
rate of 1 µM/s, while L-lysine achieved a rate of 1.82 µM/s. The combined system attained a 
capture rate of 2.55 µM/s, exceeding the sum of the individual contributions of its components. 
This finding strongly suggests a cooperative interaction between K₂CO₃ and L-lysine, reinforcing 
the hypothesis that their combined use, particularly in the presence of carbonic anhydrase, 
facilitates a synergistic enhancement in CO₂ capture efficiency. At a reduced flow rate of 200 
mL/min, the combined system exhibited a capture rate of 2 µM/s, closely aligning with the 
individual rate of L-lysine (2.13 µM/s), whereas K₂CO₃ demonstrated a significantly lower capture 
rate of 0.78 µM/s. These observations suggest that the cooperative effect is more prominent at 
higher flow rates, likely due to improved mass transfer dynamics, which enhance the interaction 
between CO₂ and the absorption medium.

4.2. Experiment with Variable Liquid Flow 
A study by Zaghini et al. (2025) observed a plateau in CO₂ capture efficiency at high gas flow 
rates in aqueous K₂CO₃ solutions without CA. This aligns with expectations that higher gas flow 
reduces CO₂ retention time, limiting un-catalyzed CO₂-K₂CO₃ reactions. In this study, the effect 
of liquid flow on CO2 capture is investigated in order to verify if the same trend is observed. Here, 
CO₂ capture efficiency increased with liquid flow rate even in enzyme-free systems, suggesting 
that factors beyond retention time influence performance. At lower liquid flow rates, solvent 
saturation limits CO₂ absorption, while higher flow rates replenish unsaturated solvent, 
maintaining absorption capacity. 
 
Although conducted in the same lab, this study and Zaghini’s work highlight key differences in 
flow dynamics. Gas flow-dominated systems rely on efficient gas-liquid interaction, which can be 
constrained at high flow rates. In contrast, controlled liquid flow mitigates retention time 
limitations by refreshing the solvent, optimizing absorption conditions. CA primarily accelerates 
CO₂ hydration at the gas-liquid interface, explaining its strong enhancement in both studies. In 
enzyme-free systems, un-catalyzed CO₂ hydration depends more on physical mass transfer 
mechanisms. Interestingly, this study contradicts Zaghini’s assumption that lower flow rates favor 
CO₂ capture due to increased retention time. Instead, higher liquid flow rates improved CO₂ 
capture even without CA, indicating that mass transfer not reaction kinetics is the limiting factor 
in un-catalyzed systems. Increased liquid flow enhanced CO₂ diffusion compensating for shorter 
retention time. In enzyme-assisted systems, where reaction kinetics are already optimized, mass 
transfer limitations become more noticeable.

5. Conclusion 
The findings of this study demonstrates the potential of enzyme-assisted DAC as an effective and 
energy-efficient strategy for mitigating atmospheric CO₂. By incorporating carbonic anhydrase 
(CA) into traditional liquid-based DAC systems, this research demonstrates significant 
improvements in CO₂ absorption kinetics while addressing the high energy demands of 
conventional solvents. Potassium hydroxide (KOH) exhibited the highest capture efficiency 
among the tested absorbents, but its substantial regeneration energy requirements limit scalability. 
On the other hand, potassium carbonate (K₂CO₃) and L-lysine, especially when used together, offer 
a more energy-efficient alternative with competitive absorption rates. The introduction of CA 
enhanced CO₂ uptake, increasing absorption efficiency by up to 20%, particularly in hybrid solvent 
systems. This study also highlights the importance of optimizing gas and liquid flow rates for 
maximum capture performance. Increasing the gas flow rate brings more CO₂ into contact with 
the liquid, improving capture efficiency. However, beyond a certain point, high gas flow rates can 
hinder CO₂ transfer, reducing overall performance. The presence of CA can help lower liquid flow 
rates while maintaining or even improving capture efficiency. This can reduce the cost of pumping 
the liquid, making the system more energy-efficient and cost-effective. Adjusting liquid flow rates 
remains critical for ensuring effective gas-liquid contact and maximizing solvent use. Enzyme-
assisted systems showed higher capture rates across various operational conditions, showing its 
potential for large-scale DAC applications. However, challenges remain including enzyme 
durability, large-scale implementation and integration with renewable energy sources. Combining 
enzyme-assisted DAC with carbon utilization pathways, such as synthetic fuel production or 
mineralization, could enhance its sustainability and practicality. With continued optimization and 
innovation, this bio-inspired approach has the potential to significantly contribute to global climate 
targets and ensure a sustainable future.
