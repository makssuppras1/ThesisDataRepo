Abstract
This project aims to design and model new type of solid oxide cell that can convert H2 and CO2 at the
same time without mixing them. This model is being designed in Engineering Equation Software (EES)
software. This could make it easier and cheaper to produce carbon monoxide, a key ingredient for synthetic
fuels and green chemicals, by using less electricity and avoiding mixing of steam with the CO2 stream.
The new cell design is based on conventional and a well-known ceramic material Nickel-yttria stabilized
zirconia (Ni-YSZ), which makes the fabrication process simple. The cell works by balancing two reactions:
H2 is oxidized to H2O on one side, while CO2 is reduced to CO on the other side. This setup uses lower
energy than traditional CO2 electrolysis. The software analysis will explore how it could fit into existing
fuel production systems. The results of the project indicate that the novel cell consumes less energy than
traditional CO2 electrolysis at 750 ◦C (0.91 W vs 25.39 W) but requires atleast 87.5 % more electrode area
which would increase the capital expenditure of the Novel electrolyser system. The economic analysis shows
that the Novel CO2 electrolysis cell is feasible when the cost of H2 is less than or equal to the cost of the
electricity supply.

INTRODUCTION

Introduction
The escalating levels of greenhouse gases (GHGs) in the atmosphere have emerged as one of the most
pressing global challenges of our time. These emissions are driven primarily by human activities such as
fossil fuel combustion, industrial processes and deforestation. These activities have significantly altered the
planet’s climate, resulting in rising temperatures, extreme weather events, and environmental degradation.
To address the profound climatological impacts of GHG emissions, there is an urgent need for innovative and
strategic technological solutions. Among these, the development of a robust pipeline for cost-effective carbon
capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) methodologies has gained significant attention. CCUS technologies
offer a pragmatic approach to not only curtail GHG emissions but also repurpose carbon dioxide (CO2) as a
valuable resource for industrial and energy applications, paving the way for a sustainable and carbon-neutral
future.
One promising pathway within the CCUS framework is CO2 electrolysis, a transformative technology that
converts electrical energy into chemical energy. In this process, CO2 is electrochemically reduced to carbon
monoxide (CO) at the cathode, while oxygen (O2) is released at the anode. The utilization of solid ox-
ide electrolysis cells (SOECs) for CO2 electrolysis offers distinct advantages over alternative technologies.
SOECs which are built upon material advancements and research in solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC), operate at
high temperatures, which significantly lower the activation overpotential and ohmic losses which is required
for CO2 reduction as the materail conductivity increase with increase of temperature. This high-temperature
operation not only enhances reaction kinetics but also allows for the co-electrolysis of CO2 and steam to
produce syngas which is a mixture of CO and hydrogen (H2). Syngas is a versatile intermediate that can be
further processed to produce liquid hydrocarbons, including renewable fuels and sustainable chemicals.
The potential applications of CO2 electrolysis are vast and transformative. By increasing the availability
of carbon monoxide in a sustainable way, CO2 electrolysis can enable the production of renewable fuels,
green chemicals and advanced materials as illustrated in Figure 1. For instance, Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, a
well-established method for converting syngas into liquid hydrocarbons relies heavily on CO as a feedstock.
Enhancing the production of CO through efficient CO2 electrolysis can reduce downstream energy losses
and improve the overall efficiency of process such as the production of sustainable aviation fuel (SAF). This
is particularly important in the context of decarbonizing the aviation sector, which is a significant contributor
to global GHG emissions[1].
The importance of advancing CO2 electrolysis infrastructure lies not only in its potential to mitigate emis-
sions but also in its ability to integrate with existing industrial systems. Beyond fuels, CO2 electrolysis can
be utilized to manufacture green chemicals, such as methanol and ethanol, and support applications in met-
allurgy and advanced carbon capture systems. These innovations align closely with the global drive toward
achieving a carbon-neutral environment and transitioning to a circular carbon economy.
1.1
Objectives
The objective of this project is to explore the development of a Novel SOEC design to enhance the efficiency
and cost-effectiveness of CO2 electrolysis. The proposed design leverages a conventional ceramic material,
nickel-yttria-stabilized zirconia (Ni-YSZ) to create a cell that can simultaneously convert hydrogen and
CO2 without intermixing. By balancing two reactions i.e hydrogen oxidation at one electrode and CO2
reduction at the other, this innovative configuration offers significant technical and economic advantages

2.1
Solid oxide fuel cell
A fuel cell is an electrochemical device that directly converts chemical energy from fuel into electricity and
heat. A solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) is a type of fuel cell that offers many benefits over other types due to the
high efficiency and stability it offers. The use of high-temperature SOFC has been of great interest to the sci-
entific community due to the number of advantages it offers. SOFCs are recognized for their high efficiency
in producing electricity (roughly 50%) [3] and fuel flexibility. Its fuel flexibility allows the conversion of bio-
fuels, especially biogas and syngas. Furthermore, SOFC can also convert CO electrochemically, thus, with
hydrogen, can contribute to the overall energy production [4]. The SOFC model consists of three main layers
contributing to its overall functionality, a solid oxide electrolyte in between an anode and a cathode. To make
the fuel cell operational, fuel is supplied at the anode side where hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR) takes
place, whereas air is led to the cathode, where oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) occurs. At the anode, H2
reacts with oxygen which aids its dissociation into hydrogen protons and electrons. The hydrogen protons
further combine with oxygen molecules to form water vapour, which is then released as a byproduct of the
fuel cell. The electrons circulate through an external circuit, which then generate an electrical current. The
electrolyte then selectively allows the entry of oxygen ions from the cathode to the anode. This movement
of oxygen ions facilitates an electrical potential difference across the cell, which pushes the electron flow
through the external circuit. At the cathode, oxygen from air combines with electrons and oxygen ions to
form oxygen molecules and heat [3]. These electrochemical processes are highly exothermic, thus raising the
overall tem peratures of the SOFC. Generally, high temperatures are beneficial for the operation of the SOFC
as it promotes the reduction of total voltage losses [5]. Ordinary SOFCs must work at 800-1000 °C to provide
optimal electrolyte and technical performance [4]. Heat is also beneficial as it makes it possible for the SOFC
to work in cogeneration and deliver high temperature exhaust gases, that can be processed for heat recovery,
thereby delivering thermal energy [5]. Current models of SOFC stacks are made of zirconium-oxide stabi-
lized with small amounts of yttirum-oxide (YSZ) for the electrolyte; a nickel-YSZ cermet (ceramic-metal
composite) for the anode; and porous ceramic material made of lanthanum strontium manganite (LSM) for
the cathode. Unlike other fuel cells, the SOFC eliminates the need for expensive catalysts such as platinum
or ruthenium, bringing its advantages forward [3]. However, the use of an SOFC still features some draw-
backs, which causes its relatively slow deployment. One of which is the brittleness of ceramic components,
which limits its size to at most 0.2 m2. This presents a major problem towards the scale-up of SOFC power
plants to megawatt capacities. Additionally, introducing this technology to the current energy market would
involve high capital-to-cost ratio [3]. One way to improve the SOFCs competitiveness in generating electric-
ity is through oxyfuel combustion. Unlike the traditional use of air as the oxidiser, oxyfuel combustion relies
on (relatively) pure concentrations of oxygen. Due to the absence of molecular nitrogen from air, oxyfuel
combustion can cause increased energy efficiencies and reduc tion of emissions, as a concentrated oxygen
flow allows for more effective capture and treatment of CO2.[6]. Oxyfuel combustion works by pumping
high purity O2 in the oxygen compressor which is then combined with H+ ions and electrons that have trav-
elled through the SOFC. This produces water and excess heat. The excess heat generated from the anode
and cathode reactions can be recirculated in the form of steam to further help maintain the high-temperature
requirement for the operations of the SOFC [6]. However, inadequate regulation and exposure to elevated
temperatures in SOFCs can result to detrimental effects. These conditions can cause thermal expansion and

2.2
Solid oxide electrolysis cell
Electrolysis technologies are expected to play a key role in future energy systems, as the ‘core’ of PtX
solutions and the link between electric, gas, and thermal grids, as well as providing synthetic fuels for
transport.Therefore, the demand for large-scale electrolysis technologies is likely to be high in the future.
Although there are various electrolysis technologies to consider, it is generally agreed that solid oxide elec-
trolyser cell (SOEC) is the most suitable option for wider-scale adoption. In the past, SOEC has been deemed
an immature technology still under research and development. However, the recent changes and improve-
ments on the SOEC suggest that it is now a proven technology ready for an industrial scale-up, and that
scale-up has been, in fact, already happening [7]. SOEC has experienced immense improvements within the
past decade, including doubled electrochemical performance and durability improved by a factor of roughly
100 [7]. In addition, SOEC is a scalable technology and does not require any precious metals unlike some
other electrolyser cells. One of the most significant reasons for choosing SOEC for large-scale adoption
is the exceptional conversion efficiency it offers in comparison to other electrolyser cells. The theoretical
electrolysis efficiency of SOEC is around 90%, whilst the overall efficiency is over 55% [8].
The reason behind these high efficiencies is the high operating temperatures, generally varying between 600-
900°C [9]. whereas other electrolyser cells usually operate below 100°C. The high operating temperatures
of SOEC offer some advantages such as favourable thermodynamics and faster kinetics. From an overall
efficiency perspective, electrolysis systems should be operated near thermoneutral potentials (1.29 V for
steam electrolysis), so that the cooling effect from the endothermic electrolysis process is balanced by the
Joule heating from the cell resistance, which enables higher current density operation [10].
In turn, higher current density yields higher H2 production rate. With a lower thermoneutral voltage com-
pared to other cells, SOEC in fact requires less electricity per produced H2 gas, which translates to lower
operational costs [7].
In addition, SOEC also obtains much higher current densities at thermoneutral voltages than other electrol-
yser cells. A higher current density means that fewer electrolysers are needed to achieve a certain capacity,
which therefore leads to lower capital costs. Hence, it is economically viable to choose SOECs for wider
adoption of electrolysers, provided the main challenges are successfully addressed in the future. Some of the
challenges include the shorter state-of-the-art lifetime compared to other electrolysers, and the stack com-
ponents’ susceptibility to corrosion. In addition, even though the scale of SOEC has increased over the past
years, the large-scale applications of SOEC are still under development [11]. Therefore, the costs associated
with large scale SOECs are relatively high today, but the projected cost scenarios show a significant decrease
in capital costs. A typical SOEC operates as the reverse of a SOFC [9]. SOECs electrochemically convert
electricity into chemical energy in the form of H2 or other compounds, unlike SOFCs which do the opposite.
The by-products of the SOEC depend on the feedstock of the cell. A water-fed SOEC, which also takes
electricity as an input, generates H2 and also O2 and excess heat. The most relevant output from this unit
for the synthesis of methanol is hydrogen, as hydrogen is a key reactant in the chemical reaction for the
production of MeOH. The excess heat can be used to produce steam, which can then be used as an input for
further SOEC operations [11].

2.2.1
High Temperature electrolysis
High temperature electrolysis in a solid oxide cell (SOC) can be used to electrolyse carbon dioxide or steam,
producing carbon monoxide or hydrogen, respectively, or to co-electrolyse the two simultaneously. One
of the advantages of co-electrolysis is that syngas (CO + H2) is produced. This is a precursor to synthetic
fuel and is readily converted to methane, methanol or dimethyl ether using existing technology, some with
minor adjustments or to longer chain hydrocarbons via the Fischer-Tropsch process. The fuel produced can
be utilised directly as either a product or combusted or used in a fuel cell to produce electricity. An SOC
converts electrical energy into chemical energy and therefore can be considered as an energy storage device.
Large scale storage of chemical compounds is significantly easier than that of electricity, therefore this type
of technology has significant potential for future energy strategy[12].
Figure 2: A Solid Oxide Cell operating in Electrolysis mode (left) and Fuel cell mode (right)
An SOC can operate in both fuel cell mode (as an SOFC), producing electricity and oxidising the feed and
in electrolysis mode (as an SOEC), utilising electricity and reducing the feed. Oxide ions are transported
across the dense electrolyte from cathode to anode. In electrolysis mode the cathode is the fuel electrode
and the anode the oxygen electrode whereas the reverse is true for fuel cell operation [12]. While a large
proportion of the SOFC research translates directly to SOECs depending on the operating scenario chosen
for the SOC (e.g. reversible operation or just electrolysis mode) the optimum materials and configurations
are not necessarily the same.

2.3
Thermodynamics of High-Temperature CO2 Electrolysis
Compared to CO2 electrolysis at low temperatures, high-temperature CO2 electrolysis can utilize the inter-
nal heat produced by the ohmic resistance of the electrolysis cell and the industrial waste heat to reduce the
ohmic resistance and activate the electrode reactions which further leads to higher electrochemical perfor-
mance. Meanwhile the reduced electrical energy demand will lower the cost of high temperature CO2 elec-
trolysis and improve its economic competitiveness in the market. However when the product of CO2/H2O
co-electrolysis is CH4 or C2H6, the electrical energy demand and heat demand are almost independent of the
temperature [19].

Besides this the electrode materials are essential for the SOEC performance by mastering the polarization

large amount of TPBs or 2PBs is crucial for the excellent electrochemical performance of CO2 electrolysis
at high temperature. [19]

Modelling of co-electrolysis has gain length scales from micro modelling of the transport of ions, electrons

3.6
CO2 Electrolysis model
The model developed for steam electrolysis was adopted to simulate the electrolysis of carbon dioxide (CO2).
The foundation for this transition relied on the same theoretical framework provided by the Modeling Manual
for Steam Electrolysis from DTU Energy, with modifications made to account for the unique characteristics
of CO2 electrolysis.
The chemical reactions, key parameters, and equations used for modeling steam electrolysis were also ap-
plied to CO2 electrolysis, as the electrochemical principles remain consistent. However, certain modifica-
tions were necessary to reflect the change in reactants and products.
The primary reactions in CO2 electrolysis is the conversion of CO2 into carbon monoxide (CO) instead of
the generation of H2. The key reactions are as follows:

3.6.1
Experimental Data of CO2 Electrolysis
To validate the EES model, the data was compared with experimental data sourced from the research done
here at DTU.[2]
In the research paper a single-cell tests were conducted on planar-supported SOECs manufactured by Haldor
Topsoe A/S. The cell design includes a Ni-YSZ porous support, a Ni-YSZ active layer (fuel electrode), a YSZ
electrolyte, and a lanthanum strontium cobalt ferrite (LSCF)-based oxygen electrode with a contact layer. A
gadolinia-doped ceria (CGO) layer is also present between the YSZ electrolyte and the LSCF electrode.
The tests, carried out at DTU Energy (Technical University of Denmark, Risø campus), used cells with
dimensions of 5.3 cm × 5.3 cm and an active area of 16 cm2. The cells were mounted in a test rig with uniform
contact and gas distribution provided by corrugated Ni and Au meshes for the fuel and oxygen electrodes,
respectively. Experiments used a 50% CO/ 50% CO2 gas mixture as the fuel and air as the oxidant, with
gas inlet temperatures ranging from 750 ◦C to 850 ◦C. Current density-voltage (i-V) measurements were
performed under these conditions to validate the model, as described in various research papers.
The data from this published experiments was compared with the results generated by the EES model of
CO2 electrolyis to ensure the accuracy of the simulation.
The experimental data from this I-V curve Figure 3 was used to compare the data obtained from the EES
CO2 electrolyis model. The I-V curve in Figure 3 investigates the performance of CO/CO2 mixtures with
a composition of 50 % CO under both the electrolysis and fuel cell operation modes at varying gas inlet

4
Results and discussion
This chapter presents a comprehensive analysis of the key findings from the study of steam electrolysis,
CO2 electrolysis, and the development of a Novel CO2 electrolysis. The evaluation of previous models
for steam electrolysis, developed from the fitted parameters [25] is being discussed initially highlighting
its limitations. The improvements made to this models were explored, with emphasis on incorporating
material-specific characteristics to enhance theoretical consistency. Secondly, the performance of the CO2
electrolysis model is analyzed, including comparisons with experimental data to validate it under varying
operational conditions. Finally, the Novel CO2 elecrolysis is introduced and compared to the conventional
CO2 electrolysis in terms of voltage efficiency, power consumption, and economic feasibility.
4.1
Steam Electrolysis
Previous studies, particularly the work of Leonidas[25], utilized an optimization approach based on a trial-
and-error method to determine parameter values that best fit the experimental data for steam electrolysis.
While these values were effective in modeling the experimental results, they lacked any clear physical mean-
ing. Instead, they served as empirical parameters that worked well for data fitting. For instance, discrepancies
such as one contributing factor being disproportionately larger than another arose, highlighting inconsisten-
cies in the physical interpretation of these parameters.
For the model, Rafael Nogueira Nakashima, Postdoc (DTU Energy) provided the framework of the model
based on the Leonidas parameters [25] but it lacked a stronger connection between the model parameters
and the physical properties of the materials as it is essentially used in the electrochemical cell. Further this
model was refined by incorporating material-specific characteristics directly into the parameters. Since the
properties of the materials in the cell (e.g., electrodes and electrolytes) had been independently measured, ad-
justing the model parameters accordingly rendered the model both technically more robust and theoretically
consistent.
Although this improvement is largely philosophical and cannot be measured quantitatively, it provides a crit-
ical advantage that the ability to independently measure and validate cell specific values. This in turn facili-
tates the development of Novel CO2 electrochemical cells with improved performance. The key parameters

However, it is essential to address some potential sources of uncertainty that could contribute to the observed
error. First, the experimental data extracted from the research paper may not be entirely precise due to
limitations in the extraction process itself. Web Plot Digitizer, though a powerful tool is subject to minor
inaccuracies when extracting data points from plotted curves particularly when the curves are not clearly
defined or properly labeled. Secondly, the reported gas input temperatures (750 °C, 800 °C and 850 °C)
might not reflect the exact temperatures at which the experiments were conducted leading to slight deviations
in the thermodynamic and electrochemical behavior of the system. Furthermore, there is the possibility
that the electrochemical cell used in the experiments as predicted in the model may not perform optimally
introducing another layer of uncertainty in the comparison.
Given these potential sources of error it is important to recognize that achieving a perfect fit between the
experimental and model data is neither necessary nor realistic. The observed error margin of less than 5
percent is technically acceptable and aligns well with the inherent variability expected in experimental and
modeling studies of this nature. Thus the results validate the model’s predictive capabilities without the need
for further adjustments underscoring its utility for similar applications.

4.2.1
Thermodynamic and kinetic considerations
The kinetics of the electrochemical reactions were assumed to be similar for steam electrolysis and the CO2
electrolysis model. This assumption was based on the hypothesis that the limiting factor for the reaction ki-
netics in both systems is the same i.e. the transfer of charge carriers (e.g., oxygen ions) across the electrolyte
and the reaction rates at the electrodes. In theory the new parameters should be fitted for the CO2 electrolysis
but it turns out that it was not required to get the necessary result. This is because the fuel electrode has a
minor contribution in the voltage compared to other losses (air electrode and electrolyte).
The same kinetic model was applied to CO2 electrolysis based on the assumption that the same electrolysis
cell design was utilized. It is important to note that the system’s overall performance is not governed by a
single factor but rather by a combination of interdependent processes.
In both steam and CO2 electrolysis the limiting step is primarily dictated by phenomena occurring within the
electrochemical cell such as the transport of oxygen vacancies through the porous electrolyte or electrode
materials. These vacancies play a crucial role in facilitating the movement of oxygen ions but their mobility
can be sluggish particularly within the complex microstructure of the porous medium.
Furthermore, the electrochemical reactions consist of multiple sequential and parallel steps including ad-
sorption, charge transfer, and ion transport. Among these the limiting steps typically arise within the cell
itself, irrespective of whether the reactant is H2O or CO2. As a result the kinetic behavior remains funda-
mentally similar between steam and CO2 electrolysis as it is governed by the inherent material and structural
properties of the cell rather than the specific gas phase reactant.

4.4
Carbon Deposition
The CO2 electrolysis at high temperatures is a promising solution but is also hindered by degradation mecha-
nisms such as carbon deposition, which can even destroy the cell especially during electrolysis where carbon
formation is electrochemically driven at the electrode-electrolyte interface. This carbon deposition phe-
nomenon was not studied during this development of Novel CO2 electrolysis process because of the time
constraint but we are aware that it is one of the limiting factor for this process. Also carbon deposition will
affect both the conventional cell as well as the novel cell so it is assumed it will impact similarly on its
results and processes. From the research paper [2] it was found that CO/CO2 gas diffusion gradients and
cooling from the endothermic electrolysis reaction are important driving forces for carbon deposition to oc-
cur locally when it is not expected based on the outlet CO concentration. Furthermore, correlation with a set
of experimentally determined threshold operating points indicated that carbon deposition occurs primarily
by the Boudouard reaction rather than by direct electrochemical reduction of CO or CO2 to carbon. But
variation of fuel electrode porosity and thickness showed some interesting results that reducing gas diffusion
layer widened the operating window that avoids carbon deposition.

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
A Novel CO2 electrolysis cell was successfully modeled in EES software based on experimental data avail-
able from DTU. The novel cell can convert H2 and CO2 into CO without mixing the inlet streams. The
voltage efficiency, power consumption,area and economic feasibility are evaluated, which lead to the follow-
ing conclusions:
1. The thermodynamic and kinetic considerations worked similar for both the cell i.e H2O electroly-
sis and CO2 electrolysis cell in the EES. In theory the new parameters should be fitted for the CO2
electrolysis, but it turns out that it was not required to get the necessary result.This can lead to sim-
ply because the fuel electrode has a minor contribution in the voltage compared to other losses (air
electrode and electrolyte).
2. The comparison of I-V curve of the experimental CO2 electrolysis and modeled CO2 electrolysis
revealed that the error between the two datasets was less than 5% for the calculated temperature ranges.
3. From the polarization curves for the Novel CO2 cell it is observed that starvation occurs at current
densities less than -1.561 A/cm2 and more than 1.624 A/cm2 for the given temperature ranges.
4. The Novel cell consumes only 3.6% to 6.2% of the power required by conventional CO2 electrolysis
process. However, the area required for production of same amount of CO is between 1.87 to 3.37
times more in Novel CO2 cell as compared to conventional CO2 cell.
5. This Novel CO2 cell electrolysis process is economically viable when cost of hydrogen is equal to or
less than the cost of electricity otherwise this proces is not feasiable.
