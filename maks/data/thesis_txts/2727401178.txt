Abstract
In search-based problem solving uninformed search algorithms often lack behind when
working in large state spaces, especially in problems with high complexity and large
branching factors. IW is a well known search algorithm that performs very well in limited
state spaces.
However, it struggles keeping up when the state spaces get sufficiently
large. Traditional solutions to this challenge has been applying heuristics to the search
algorithms allowing them to prioritize the most promising states. Designing effective
heuristics across many different planning domains can however be a challenge.
The purpose of this thesis is to propose an alternative strategy to heuristics in addressing
search algorithms for large state spaces. We introduce attention-based algorithms which
relaxes the level and search algorithm to place its focus towards the most relevant objects
to a given goal. We have developed two algorithm types, Novelty and Attention, and
Obstruction-based attention.
The Novelty and Attention algorithm utilizes the aggressive pruning strategy of IW while
maintaining focus on the most relevant objects to the goal. It tries to solve any goal by
maintaining a width of 1. This can be achieved by relaxing the level sufficiently until the
goal is solvable at a width of 1. The IW algorithm performs very quickly at a width of
1. Obstruction Based Attention takes a different approach, by relaxing the entire level
until only the minimum relevant objects for a goal are left. It then attempts to solve the
goal in the shortest fashion possible, and if an obstruction is met, the obstructing object
is then incorporated into the attention set and the search resumes.
Our experimental results indicate that the attention based algorithms improves the overall
time performance of the search algorithms, although with a slight compromise of solution
quality. In addition, some problems that were unsolvable by the search algorithms became
solvable when attention was incorporated into the search.

Introduction
When working with Artificial Intelligence or planning algorithms, the general theme of
both is about decision making.
Likewise, decision making plays a significant role in
everyday life for humans whether they are conscious or unconscious decisions. Based on
large amounts of input from our sensory network, we are able to make informed decisions
about our actions. In a world with copious amounts of information or data input it is
a marvel that people can subconsciously filter through it and select the most relevant
pieces of information for their current objectives. AI agents function quite similarly to
humans in this regard, establishing planning strategies to make the best decision based
on the provided information. This is quite evident in the field of classical planning where
the environment is fully-observable, finite, static, and deterministic.
In classical planning, generally algorithms are divided into two different types: Unin-
formed and informed search. In the context of uninformed search, the algorithms are
considered blind and know nothing of their domain except for their possible actions and
the goal state. An informed search, generally employs a heuristic to evaluate the current
state’s proximity to a goal state based on some chosen factor. Some of the best known
and most used types of uninformed search algorithms, are Breadth-First Search (BFS)
and Depth-First Search (DFS). Many of the more modern algorithms usually bear re-
semblance to one of them, if not an attempted combination of both strategies. One such
algorithm, Iterated Width (IW) [1] has been developed on the principles of BFS, and can
be used along other popular search strategies to produce state-of-the-art results [1].
IW functions as a forward state BFS algorithm that prunes states based on their nov-
elty measure. The algorithm maintains a current width, and any state with a novelty
that is higher than the current width is pruned. IW has laid foundation to a number
of variations including a serialized version to solve multiple goals with low complexity,
and other variations such as IW+ [2], or IW++ [3] that try and make the pruning less
aggressive and preserve good solutions. IW, its variations, as well as general uninformed
search algorithms can only perform so well until the constraint of dealing with sufficiently

large data sets. Navigating these large data sets is often where the informed search is
introduced, using heuristics to prioritize certain states over others. While heuristics can
certainly help solve problems, they bring with them their own set of challenges; the pri-
mary of which is the generalization of heuristics. Many domains exists across planning
problems, and developing a heuristic to be effective in all of them is often an incredible
challenge. It’s to this end, in dealing with large data sets that we propose an alternative
of utilizing attention inspired algorithms.
In dealing with a specific problem, the human attention span is a dynamic structure
capable of keeping track of the most immediately relevant objects required to solve the
problem[4]. It is capable of adding objects to the structure as they appear as obstacles,
and it is able to disregard the remainder of the world around it. In a large state space,
a search algorithm would be immediately more effective if we could direct its search to
only maintain the most relevant objects for it to find its goal.
Our hypothesis is that introducing attention based algorithms on top of recognized pow-
erful search algorithms, will enable them to be utilized on substantially large state spaces
for which they would not be able to find solutions on their own.
Before we get into the functionality of Attention we will in section 2 introduce some
background information regarding planning, graphs and the domain we selected. In sec-
tion 3 we will briefly cover the search algorithms BFS, DFS, IW, IW+, and IW++ in
order to establish how they work as a baseline. In section 4 we will then introduce atten-
tion, how we have applied it based on the principles of attention and which algorithms
we have developed with it. Section 5 will cover how we intend to carry out the exper-
iments, along with what implementation decisions we have made. Section 6 will cover
our observed results and in section 7 we discuss their implications and relevance to our
hypothesis. Lastly section 8 will summarize our findings as well as provide suggestions
to where future work can be done.

2.1
The search problem
We will be using the classical model for planning [1] Σ = {S, s0, SG, A, f}, where S is
a set of states, s0 ∈S is the initial state, SG ⊆S is a set of goal states, a ∈A(s) is
the set of actions that are applicable in the state s, and f is a function that determin-
istically maps a state and an action to a new state, s′ = f(a, s), and is the result s′
of applying action a to the state s. A solution to the planning is a sequence of actions
π = a0, a1 . . . am that generate a sequence of states s0, s1 . . . sm+1 such that sm+1 ∈SG,
and where ai ∈A(si), si+1 = f(ai, si). We define S as our state space, as it contains
all the states we could possibly be in. Additionally we define the branching factor of a
problem to be the number of applicable actions in each state, |A(s)|.
We will also be using the STRIPS [1] language to represent our problem P = {F, I, O, G},
where F is a set of boolean variables called atoms, I ⊆F is the set of atoms representing
the initial state, O is a set of actions, and G ⊆F is a set of goal atoms.
In the STRIPS language, a state, s ∈S, is represented by a set of atoms that are true in
s, s ⊆F. Any atom that is not present in s is assumed to be false in s.
Each action a is represented by a tuple of operators {Pre(o), Add(o), Del(o)}. Pre(o)
is the set of atoms that must be true for o to be performed, and as such it is called
the precondition of o. Add(o) is the set of atoms that are made true by performing this
action, and Del(o) is the set of atoms that are made false by performing this action. This
means that
s′ = f(s, o) = {s ∪Add(o) \ Del(o)}
For an action to be applicable in a state, o ∈A(s), its precondition must be met in s,
which means that

2.2
Graphs
Graphs are fundamental in representing structures across various fields of study. They
are particularly relevant in network analysis, logistics, and countless areas in computer
science. By providing a mathematical representation of these structures we can utilize
graph theory to discover solutions to problems that are not inherently clear. A graph
consists of a collection of vertices, also called nodes, and edges that connects pair of
vertices.
2.2.1
Directed Graphs
Directed graphs, or Digraphs are one type of graph where the edges maintain an associated
direction. This means an edge is an ordered pair of vertices where one vertex points
towards the second vertex, often called an arc. A directed graph D is an ordered pair
D = (V, A) where V is a set of vertices, and A is a set of arcs. Each vertex in a directed
graph has both an in-degree and an out-degree. The in-degree of a vertex is the number
of arcs ending at that vertex, while the out-degree is the number of arcs originating from
that vertex. A path in a graph is a sequence of vertices v0, v1...vi where each adjacent
pair is connected by a directed edge and no vertices or arcs appear more than once. In
our domain we will be working with directed graphs, particularly bidirected graphs.

Domain Description
A level in the hospital domain is made up of a 2D grid of cells, where each cell contains
either a wall, box, agent, or an empty field. Each agent has a number and each box has
a letter. Both boxes and agents have a color. Agents are drawn as circles and boxes are
drawn as squares. Walls are black squares.
Certain cells are marked as goal cells. Goal cells have either a letter or a number depend-
ing on whether the goal is for an agent or a box. An example level can be see in figure 1.
Each level defines both an initial state and a goal state. The goal state is the state where
all goal fields are occupied by a box or an agent of the same letter as the goal field at the
same time.
A solution to a level is the series of actions that the agents can take to go from the initial
state to the goal state.
Figure 1: A simple level in the hospital domain
The domain is simulated in a series of discrete steps. At each step, each agent must
perform one action. An agent has a total of 29 actions available to them. These actions
can be split into 4 categories, Move, Push, Pull, and NoOp. An agent has 4 move actions,
one for each cardinal direction.
An agent has 12 Push actions available to push a box, where each action describes the
direction the agent is moving and the direction the box that the box being pushed is
moving. For example the action Push(N, W) moves the agent to the north and the box
to the west. This action requires that an agent has a box present on the north side of the
agent, and that the cell west of the box is free, as neither boxes nor agents can be moved

only one agent, there is no reason to use the NoOp action.

Breadth & Width Algorithms
The search problem Σ can be represented by a graph where the nodes are states, and the
edges are actions. Representing the problem like this means that we can use common
graph search algorithms, such as breadth first search (BFS) and depth first search (DFS)
to solve the problem.
3.1
BFS
Breadth-First Search (BFS) is a graph traversal algorithm[6] that explores the vertices
in a graph level by level, starting with an initial state. First the initial state is explored,
and then its adjacent vertices, then their adjacent vertices and so on, this continues
until all vertices in the graph has been searched. As we are working in a goal oriented
environment, the algorithm would return once a goal state has been found, else it will
not return a solution.

BFS is a relevant algorithm to consider as it finds optimal solutions to graph problems.
However, if the problem is too complex the algorithm will run out of memory available
before finding the solution. The time complexity of BFS is O(|V | + |E|), where |V | is
the number of vertices in the graph and |E| is the number of edges. This is because BFS
visits each vertex and edge at most once. The space complexity of BFS is O(|V | + |E|)
in the worst-case scenario. This is because BFS needs to store the exploration status and
parent information for each vertex in the graph as well as the queue of vertices to be
visited, which in the case of a connected graph could include all vertices and all edges.
BFS only eliminates states that have already been visited before which will represent

3.2
DFS
Depth-First Search (DFS) offers an alternative approach to BFS for graph traversal. As
its name implies, DFS explores the most recently generated nodes first by utilizing a LIFO
queue. This allows DFS to deal with large state spaces in situations where a solution’s
optimality is not a requirement. The space complexity of DFS is O(dm) where d is the
maximum depth of any node, and m is the branching factor. At any given time DFS only
needs to keep track of one path from the root to a leaf node. This is superior to BFS space
management which makes DFS a good contender as a search algorithm in problems with
large state spaces. When looking at the time complexity, neither algorithm outperforms
the other as in the worst case both algorithms have to traverse the entire graph. Meaning
both algorithms have a O(|V | + |E|) time complexity. The primary drawback of DFS
compared to BFS is that it lacks optimality. Meaning if multiple solutions to a problem
exists at various depths of a graph, DFS may not find the optimal solution first, and
instead discover a subobtimal solution deeper in the graph.
3.3
Iterated Width
Iterated Width[1] (IW) is a width-based algorithm we decided to look at, and it works
slightly different than BFS. As BFS traverses the adjacent edges of a vertex, it merely
checks if the adjacent vertices have been explored before, if so it disregards the vertex
and continues on to another adjacent vertex.
IW works by instead representing a state s as a set of atoms T ⊆F. In this context an
atom is a boolean variable that represents a specific property of the state. In our domain
we used the simple atoms BoxAtPositionXY and AgentAtPositionXY as mentioned ear-
lier. We only regard the positive values in our implementation, meaning if an atom is not
12
==PAGE:18==
Andreas Handberg
Carsten Andersen

included in a state, it is assumed to be false. IW then uses the concept of a novelty to
decide if a state should be pruned. The novelty of a state s is the size of the smallest tuple
t ⊆T where s is the first state in the search to make t true. If no such t exists in the state,
the novelty is set to n + 1 where n = |F|. IW works by calling IW(0), IW(1) . . . IW(n)
until it finds a solution or it reaches the last iteration IW(n). For the iteration IW(i), i
is the width of that iteration. IW(i) prunes a state s if novelty(s) > i. The complexity
of IW(i) is O(ni)[1], which means that if a problem P is solvable with a width of w, then
IW will solve P in O(nw) time.

With this in mind a few observations can be made regarding the IW algorithm. First,
When i = n, IW(i) is functionally the same as BFS, as it only prunes duplicate states.
Second, while searching at lower widths, the algorithm may find a solution, but it is not
guaranteed to be optimal. This can either be because of no optimal path existing at the
current width, or it can be attributed to the exploration order of the algorithm.
Consider two different states, s1 and s2, each contains the same atom-tuple t and not

other new tuples. Assume these states are found at the same depth within the graph. If
s1 is discovered before s2, then s2 will be pruned as we have already seen t in s1. Similarly
if the algorithm simply explores a poor path first, where a tuple t is made true, and an
optimal path exists that would discover t later in the graph, then the optimal path will
also be pruned if there are no other new tuples in any of these states. This can lead to
suboptimal solutions, or even no solution being found when i < n. What we end up with
is an algorithm that prunes more aggressively than BFS, but with its own inherent flaws.
To this end, we decided to explore other possible variants of IW dealing with these flaws.
3.4
Variants
3.4.1
IW Plus
As mentioned above, IW represents a state as a set of atoms. However, if multiple actions
result in a state where the same or some of the same atoms are generated, then these sim-
ilar states will be pruned. This is good when the domain has a large state space, but can
also cause problems when a ”good” state is pruned. Consider for example the level shown
in figure 6. If the state where the box is moved right is explored before the state where the
box is moved down, then the problem is not solvable with a width of 1. If the exploration
order of the states at a depth of 1 is swapped, the level becomes solvable with a width of 1.

3.4.2
IW PlusPlus
Both IW and IW+ offer more aggressive pruning strategies than BFS, but they also
compromise the feature of BFS being an optimal algorithm. As an alternative variation
of IW, IW++[3] has been developed with the intent of performing similarly to BFS with
width-based performance. IW++, like IW and IW+ is a complete algorithm, but does
not guarantee optimal solutions. IW++ works by adding a depth, d, to the atom-tuples
t to get the tuple (t, d). An atom-tuple is now considered new if the tuple has not been
made true in any previous depth of the graph. The algorithm still adheres to the width
restraint of IW, meaning that the new atom-tuples must also be smaller than or equal to
the current width. IW++ only prunes states that has the same atoms at deeper depths,
and duplicate states. Effectively this algorithm maintains many more paths that have the
same atoms made true in the same depth. This allows IW++ to keep the optimal paths
at the cost of maintaining more of the graph at any given time compared to IW or IW+.
This suggests that IW++ performs worse on problems with high branching factors, and
as covered in section 2 the domain we are working with can have a significant branching
factor. However, the possibility of close to optimal solutions makes IW++ an interesting
contender and possibly a combination of the algorithm and attention will produce great
results in large state spaces.

4
Attention
4.1
Background
When humans solve larger problems, we will usually only pay attention to a few things at
a time. When choosing what we pay attention to, we tend to choose based on experience,
or what seems immediately relevant to the problem. At certain points, our attention
can switch to other objects as we progress with a problem. All of this helps reduce the
complexity of the problem to be more manageable, and means that we can sometimes
solve problems with very large state spaces. We want to use the concept of attention
to increase the performance of search algorithms, and potentially allow them to solve
problems with large state spaces.
We can define attention from 6 core principles [4]:
1. Attention is limiting and selective
2. Attention asymmetrically biases mental processing
3. Attention can be captured or directed
4. Attention can be directed inward or outward
5. Attention facilitates integrated mental processing
6. Attention facilitates conscious access
As we are more interested in the concept of attention with regards to planning algorithms,
and not general human attention, we will choose to focus on the first three points. We
can interpret these three points as wanting to limit ourselves to a subset of objects, and
focus our processing on these objects.

4.2
Applying attention
To apply these principles to our domain, we need to limit the amount of objects that we
include when we do a search in the state space. To do that we need to figure out which
objects are important to the subgoal we are currently working on. Here we consider two
basic subgoals, moving a box to a goal, and moving an agent to a goal. Both goals have
a minimum set of objects, m(g), that need to be included in the search for the search
to succeed in any level. These objects are usually the objects that are present in the
predicates that are part of the goal. For example, if g = {BoxAtPosition(A, 1, 1)}, then
m(g) must contain a box with the letter A.
When moving an agent to a goal, the bare minimum we need to include in our search is
the agent that is moving to the goal. When moving a box to a goal, the bare minimum
we need to include in our search is the box that is being moved to the goal, and the agent
that will be moving the box. When choosing which box to move to a goal, we will always
use the box that is closest to the goal.
4.2.1
Algorithm 1 - Combining Novelty and relevance (NA)
The idea of this algorithm came around when we observed a feature of the MAvis domain.
In a given scenario where there is an agent, a box and a corresponding solvable box goal
that goal would always be solvable with a width of 1 given the proper exploration order as
described in figure 6. Figure 7 provides example levels that can all be solved with a width
of 1 by the IW search algorithm. The idea then became, if we can relax our problem,

with the minimum relevant objects, to the point where it can be solved with a width of
1, then we could potentially create a highly efficient algorithm. This is attributed to the

fact that running an iteration of IW with a width of 1 is computationally inexpensive.
This observation also establishes a fundamental prerequisite to a domain that for any
goal to be addressed using this algorithm, it must be solvable with a width of 1.
Given our attention set of minimum relevant objects m(g) we continuously relax the
initial state s0, by removing boxes from it that are not in m(g), until the level is solvable
by the IW search algorithm with a width of 1. At this point the objects used in the
relaxation are then added to our total set of relevant objects M(g). The IW algorithm
works as mentioned in section 3.3. This Attention algorithm adds a layer of pruning to
IW, the atom-tuples generated in each state are compared against the relevant objects
in m(g). If the atom tuples do not contain any new tuples in regard to the relevant
objects, then the state is pruned. Effectively this makes IW with Attention an even more
aggressive pruning algorithm.
For the MAvis domain, we established that any goal can be solved with a width of 1 if
only the minimum relevant objects exist in the level, given the proper exploration order.
This suggests if a goal exists with many additional objects present in the level, we will
eventually be able to solve the goal if we relax the level by removing the additional ob-
jects. However, the goal might also be solvable even without removing these additional
objects, suggesting that we do not need to consider all additional objects as relevant,
but only those that hinder us from solving our goal. This implies that the better we can
identify the hindering objects and add them to our relevant objects m(g), the better the
algorithm will perform. However as performing an IW based search with a width of 1 is
inexpensive, this method does not need to be perfect. For our implementation we decided
to find the hindering objects, by simply removing random objects in the level until it was
solvable. Starting by removing a single object at a time, then increasing the number of
removed objects removed at a time, until eventually, all objects in the level are removed
at once.
This algorithm performs particularly well when dealing with levels that do not have
a large number of boxes that are obstructing the current goal. An example can be seen

4.2.2
Algorithm 2 - Obstruction based attention (OBA)
Given a goal g, this algorithm starts by removing all objects in the level, except objects
in m(g), from the initial state s0, producing a relaxed state sr0. We then solve our goal
using sr0 as our initial state, producing a solution π0 = a0, a1 . . . an and a state sequence
sr0, sr1 . . . sr(n+1). We then take the actions in our solution and apply them one by one on
the original state, si+1 = f(si, ai) until we get to an action that would be illegal to apply,
ai /∈A(si) for any i, or until we have applied all actions in π0. If we can successfully
apply all actions in π0, then π0 is the solution to g.
If we instead get to a state sk where ak /∈A(sk), we know that sk contains an object that
is not present in srk that is blocking us from applying ak. We then add this object to srk
and start a new search using srk as our initial state, which produces a solution π1. Doing
the same as with π0, we apply the actions in π1 one by one to sk. We continue this until

4.2.2.1
Using a dynamic attention set
Some objects are only important temporarily, but with this algorithm they stay in our
attention until we solve our goal, and the attention set is reset. To prevent objects from
lingering in our attention after they are no longer relevant, we change our attention set
to be a FIFO queue of size q. Now when we add objects to the start of our attention
queue, we also check the size of the queue. If the size is larger than q, we remove the
last object in the queue from our relaxed state and attention queue. This ensures that
old objects that might not be relevant anymore are removed from our attention once we
focus on new objects, while still allowing these objects to be re-added to our attention
in case they are still important to achieving our goal. The objects in m(g) do not get
affected by the attention queue, and are always present in the relaxed state, as we assume
these objects are always relevant.
The question now is what q should be? If we try different levels with a different q we can
find the optimal q for each level, but this size might be different for different levels. We
need a way for the algorithm to change q depending on the need of the level.
One of the signs of the attention queue being too small is that the same objects keep
being added and removed from the queue. We want to be able to detect that this is
happening, and increase q.
To do this we introduce another FIFO queue, R, for objects that have recently been re-
moved from the attention queue. Once an object is removed from the attention queue, it
is placed in R. This essentially makes the attention queue and R into one long queue with
two parts. Now, when an object is added to the attention queue, we also check if this ob-
ject is present in R. If this is the case, we increase q by one. This is illustrated in figure 15.

With this solution we still have to figure out the size of the R queue, but this value now

more likely to get repeat objects and increase q, while a smaller queue means that we
only increase q if the objects we remove are needed again immediately. For our testing,
R will have a size of 3, as this seemed to give results that were close to the best results
we got with a fixed q.

Methodology
5.1
Domain
As stated in section 2 we selected the hospital domain in MAvis for a variety of reasons.
The selection of domain introduced the requirement of creating a client capable of solving
the levels with the algorithms described earlier. In this process we encountered a number
of challenges and problems which we overcame with specific implementation choices, some
of which may have impacted the overall performance of our solutions.
In section 2 we mention that we use atoms such as BoxAtPosition(A, x, y). In practice,
we decided to give each object a unique ID instead of using the objects letter. This means
that for the IW algorithm, even if an A box has already been in position (x, y), it is still
treated as a new atom if another A box is moved to position (x, y), as the two boxes
will have different IDs. This choice significantly increases the size of the state space for
some levels when running IW, but we found that for levels such SADeepurple, where a
lot of box-shuffling can occur, this meant that the agent was less restricted in where it
can move boxes without having important states be pruned.
As a level can consist of many goals of the types BoxGoals or AgentGoals, we decided to
solve the collection of these goals separately in a prioritized solving manner. We chose this
approach to try and limit the size of a search graph at any given time. We hypothesized
that solving the individual goals must produce a smaller graph than solving all levels at
the same time. Solving goals individually introduced a series of new problems that we
needed to address. There were three factors we kept referring to when dealing with these
issues:
1. Allow previous solved goals to be destroyed
2. Guarantee previously solved goals are still solved after each goal is solved
3. Maintain a low attention set
Firstly we had the issue that once a goal had been solved it could in future exploration be
27
==PAGE:33==
Andreas Handberg
Carsten Andersen
Master Thesis
June 29, 2023
destroyed, meaning it had to be solved again. Secondly we had to deal with the scenario
of a level containing multiple goals with the same character. The issue in that scenario is
the same box could be selected to solve both goals, resulting in a never ending loop. And
lastly specific to our attention algorithms we wanted to maintain as small of an attention
set as possible. Every approach we conceived to solve these issues only allowed for two
of the three factors to be true. We then hypothesized that in this domain, levels must
have a sequence of which goals can be solved in which no goals are broken. And this led
to the creation of our GameGraph.
5.1.1
GameGraph
The GameGraph we introduced divided a level into two types of nodes goal nodes and
area nodes. Goal nodes could neighbor other goal nodes and area nodes, and area nodes
could only neighbor goal nodes. An area node represents the full neighboring set of empty
fields in a level. Representing the levels with this graph allowed us to develop a method
for identifying which goals can currently be solved, and which should be solved at a later
point. A goal node could be classified as one of the following types:
1. A leaf node
2. part of a complete cycle
3. a connecting node
If a goal is part of a cycle or a leaf node it can be solved immediately. However, special
considerations are made if a goal node is a connecting node, meaning it connects two
areas together. In these cases the goal nodes can only be solved when one of the two
areas has the connecting node as its sole neighbor. Additionally there must be no other
boxes or agents left in the area. These connecting goals are always solved after all leaf
and cycle goals have been solved. This approach effectively creates a sequence of solvable
goals that never break a goal once its been solved.

Heuristics
To increase the performance of the algorithms, we are using two heuristics additively
to determine how good a state is. The first heuristic is the depth of the state we are
looking at, and the second heuristic is how many goals are currently unsolved in the
state. In practice, this means that we are using A-star with a goal count heuristic. We
chose general heuristics because we wanted to make sure that our algorithms could be
generalized to other domains, and to ensure that the increase in performance (if any)
from the algorithms we presented were due to the strengths of the algorithms and not
because of a very domain specific heuristic. In our results, all results that uses these
heuristics have ”heuristics” as part of their name.

without diminishing the solution quality. This hope is founded on the idea that if a
solution exists for a given goal, it must include the minimum relevant objects of the goal,
and therefore we assume that any solution found would ultimately be included in the
isolated subgraph containing the minimum relevant objects.

7.4
Heuristic impact
One might have noticed that in all of the results we have talked about thus far, we
avoid much mention of the heuristic based solutions. We find that the heuristic based
solutions almost appear random, there is no clear trend in what kind of solutions they find.
Sometimes they find better solutions, but most of the time they don’t. And generally we
see that the solutions are slower, and with more actions used. Considering we have used
A* with a goal count heuristic, this inconsistency is a bit of a challenge to explain. One
possible explanation is attributed to the nature of IW’s atom generation pruning possible
good states due to their repetition of atoms before the heuristic influences the selection
of next state to explore.
7.5
Exploration Order
One of the results we found that surprised us, is the fact that OBA finds a solution to
all 16 levels, and NA doesn’t. In fact, NA doesn’t find a solution to one particular level
when it’s used in conjunction with the IW Algorithm. That level is SATowersOfSaigon04.
When we look more closely at the level alongside the functionality of NA, we managed

As explained in figure 6 we find that the exploration order of
the possible moves can determine whether a level can be solved with a width of 1 or
not. When looking at TowersOfSaigon04 in appendix A we see that each goal is actually
solvable with a width of 1, but it requires that the correct exploration order is used. If
the agent pulls box C towards the goals rather than down into the middle tunnel, then
the atoms depicting C in the top right have already been generated. Therefore the level
cannot be solved with a width of 1 if that exploration order is always used. NA utilizes
the IW search algorithm at a width of 1, as the method of finding swift solutions which
means in this particular type of level, and levels that have a similar exploration order
issue NA will not be able to find a solution in conjunction with IW.
7.6
Interpretation of Results
Restating our hypothesis we estimated that with attention, state of the art search algo-
rithms will be able to find solutions in large state spaces that they otherwise wouldn’t be
able to find. And given the results shown above we can say that attention in conjunction
with a search algorithm, is capable of solving large state spaces. Not only that, but we
can also say that on less complex state spaces attention significantly improves the time
quality of a search algorithm. Generally the algorithms also slightly worsens the solution
quality by a low margin. This opens the discussion of what is the primary purpose of
using these algorithms. If the purpose is to find the best solution, then attention probably
isn’t the best choice. But if we’re looking to work within large state spaces, or work at a
high pace within complex state spaces, then attention is a viable option. Levels with a
higher branching factor generally seem to be levels that Attention performs better in.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, we aimed to create attention based algorithms in order to solve problems
with large state spaces. To this end we implemented search algorithms within the hospital
domain of the MAvis tool. Here we showcased that the algorithms, IW, IW+ and IW++
performed rather well within the domain. As our primary focus is solving large state
spaces, we can say that IW was the best performing algorithm on its own as it solved
the most levels. In addition it also had the best solution quality, and second fastest
solution time when comparing with the other two algorithms. When adding attention
to all three algorithms we found that on average, they found solutions faster than the
baseline algorithms. This result came at the cost of the solutions being a slightly worse
quality. But for all algorithms, when use in conjunction with attention were capable of
solving more levels than they could on their own. Adding Attention to IW in particular,
allowed it to solve all levels in the sample levels with the exception of a single level for the
Novelty and Attention algorithm. This is explainable because of the exploration order
making that particular level not solvable by IW with a width of 1. We conclude that
attention based algorithms has large potential when it comes to dealing with large state
spaces. Additionally attention shows great promise in less complex problems, as adding
attention to the search algorithms does not seem to worsen the solutions by a significant
amount.
8.1
Future Work
8.1.1
Saving the best state in each iteration
In all of the presented variations of IW, when we start a search with width i, we are
throwing away all the work we did in the iterations 0...i −1. We can instead keep track
of the best state we find in iteration i, and use that state as the initial state in iteration
i + 1. The ”best” state in this case is the state with the lowest heuristic value, and as
such this method depends heavily on a good heuristic. One could potentially use one of
46
==PAGE:52==
Andreas Handberg
Carsten Andersen
Master Thesis
June 29, 2023
the heuristics found in this[7] paper. If we use a heuristic this way, we can potentially
re-use some of the work we did in earlier iterations. The benefit of this change is that
the later iterations will likely have an initial state that is closer to the goal (given a good
heuristic), and as such will require less processing time. This can be very important as
the later iterations of IW can take a very long time.
The disadvantage of this method is that earlier iterations of IW are more likely to be
suboptimal, as more states are pruned. This means that at least part of the solution
found using this method is likely to be suboptimal as well. However, As our primary
focus has not been on creating a specific and well performing heuristic, this became an
approach we decided to not explore further.
8.1.2
Completion of Novelty and Attention
As mentioned in the discussion, OBA and NA manages to solve all of the levels except
NA doesn’t solve the TowerOfSaigon04 level. We attributed this flaw to the exploration
order of possible moves where a bad path would be explored before a good path. As a
obvious possible future improvement point of the algorithm, would be creating a way to
deal with this exploration order. A possible way to do this could be to pass through a
move-set order to keep it deterministic and shuffle up the order, should the algorithm
not find a solution. This could sound expensive in terms of performance, but should not
require a lot of repetitions. In addition we have shown that running the IW algorithm
with a width of 1, is fairly inexpensive. Making this a possible solution of the problem.
8.1.3
Dynamic attention size reduction in algorithm 2
As mentioned earlier, OBA has no way of lowering the size of the attention queue again
after increasing it. This is mainly a problem in cases where a large attention queue is
required at the start of solving a goal, but not towards the end. It would be interesting
to try and find a way to dynamically lower the size of the attention set, although the
performance increase would only be noticeable in specific scenarios.
