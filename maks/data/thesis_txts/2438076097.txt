Abstract
Reverse osmosis is used within the dairy industry to concentrate dairy liquids. Many components
present in the dairy solutions can aﬀect the performance of the ﬁltration system. Arla Foods Ingredients
(AFI), a leading Danish whey ingredient supplier observed calcium phosphate scaling on their reverse
osmosis systems during the manufacture of lactose. Te calcium deposits decreased the production time
by 4-6 hours and increased the costs associated with cleaning. Tis thesis focuses on using ultrasound as
an alternative methodology to remove calcium deposits from dairy fouled reverse osmosis membranes.
Te potential of ultrasound to be used as an independent cleaning procedure and during ﬁltration
operation has been investigated in this work. A 24 kHz, 200 W nominal power transducer was used as
the ultrasonic source. Te whey (UF) permeates and reverse osmosis membranes used industrially were
provided by AFI for this study.
A ﬂatsheet polyamide reverse osmosis membrane was repeatedly fouled using the UF permeate in a
dead-end ﬁltration unit. Te fouled membranes were cleaned ex-situ using an ultrasonic power of 200
W or 40 W for short periods of 5, 10 and 20 minutes. Te performance of the ultrasound for cleaning
was evaluated and compared with the conventional chemical cleaning procedure and in the absence
of sonication. Cleaning eﬃciency and water ﬂux recovery, which were used to quantify the cleaning
performance was found to be independent of sonication period but strongly dependent on the ultrasonic
power. Te membrane structure was not aﬀected from the repeated fouling and intermitent ultrasonic
irradiation used for cleaning. Te rejection of lactose and calcium ions was greater than 99% and was
found to be unaﬀected in the presence of ultrasound. In another study, the performance of ultrasound
to remove fouling was assessed during a 10h batch ﬁltration carried out in the dead-end ﬁltration unit
with ex-situ sonication. Te average solution ﬂux increased by 82% in the presence of sonication and
the lactose was not degraded during repeated sonication of the feed. Both the lactose and calcium ion
rejection were greater than 99%. Lactose rejection was unaﬀected due to ultrasound, whereas calcium
ion rejection in the presence of ultrasound was signiﬁcantly higher than in the experiment without
sonication. Te higher rejection of calcium ions may have been due to the positive eﬀect of ultrasound
in reducing the concentration polarization in the sonicated feed. Te results from this thesis suggest that
short periods of sonication can be eﬀective as a cleaning procedure as well as during ﬁltration. However
a more suitable use for ultrasound would be during ﬁltration, since it has been found to increase the
solution ﬂux without degrading the lactose while inhibiting the precipitation of calcium ions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Arla Foods Ingredients (AFI) located in Videbæk (Denmark) is a subsidiary of Arla Foods, a global
dairy co-operative owned by dairy farmers in six countries. AFI is a leading whey ingredient supplier
for infant nutrition, baking, dairy, medical applications and sports nutrition all over the world. Tey
supply whey proteins, milk minerals, lactose and whey permeate to pharmaceutical and other allied
food industries.
Lactose, a simple milk sugar is one of the key ingredients processed in AFI. It is manufactured from the
up-concentration of permeates obtained from the ultraﬁltration of whey. Whey is a by-product formed
during cheese making. Te permeate from whey ultraﬁltration is referred as whey permeate or UF
permeate. During the the demineralization of the UF permeates through several reverse osmosis units,
calcium phosphate scaling decreased the processing volume and subsequently aﬀected the quantity of
lactose manufactured. Tese calcium phosphates deposits were periodically removed from the membrane
surface using complex and expensive chemical cleaning agents. However, the cleaning procedure reduced
the production period by approximately 4-6 hours. Moreover cleaning with chemicals generated high
volumes of cleaning wastes, which increased the costs associated with disposal. Alternatively the
pressure was ramped up on the feed side during the production period to increase the production
volume. Tis increase in operating pressure further contributed to increased operational cost of the
reverse osmosis units.
1.2
Objectives and outcomes
Tis thesis aims to investigate the ultrasound usage as an alternative cleaning methodology to remove
calcium deposits from dairy fouled reverse osmosis membranes. Te potential of ultrasound will be
evaluated as an independent cleaning procedure and during ﬁltration.
Te experiments in this work were designed as a proof of concept to check the feasibility of ultrasound
usage for fouling control and removal in reverse osmosis membranes. It was hoped that the results from

this study will cater to the development of an eﬀective strategy in controlling and removing fouling at
AFI, which can bring down the operating costs and increase the production time. Tis would not only
directly beneﬁt AFI, but also the knowledge gained on the possible usage of ultrasound for cleaning
could be used for a wider range of reverse osmosis applications in the food industry.
1.3
Motivation for project
Tis project was formulated to understand if ultrasonic irradiation can be applied to improve the
performance of reverse osmosis by cleaning the membrane surface. Ultrasonic waves showing a
positive eﬀect on ﬁltration and cleaning of microﬁltration and ultraﬁltration systems have been well
documented. Some researchers have also reported that ultrasound can be eﬃcient in removing fouling
in low pressure membrane operations, while considerably reducing the time required for cleaning.
However the ultrasonic eﬀect on reverse osmosis operations is not well known.
1.4

Literature Overview
2.1
Membranes in the dairy industry
Four membrane operations are widely used in the dairy industry, namely microﬁltration (MF), ultraﬁltra-
tion (UF), nanoﬁltration (NF) and reverse osmosis (RO). Te common application of the operations are as
follows. MF can be used to removes bacteria and macromolecules. UF is widely used in the processing of
whey and milk products. NF can be used to concentrate organic components by partial demineralization
and RO systems can be used to concentrate solutions by removal of water [2][4]. In the dairy industry,
starting, equilibriating, interrupting or stopping any of the processes generate large amounts of eﬄuents.
Tese process waters with dilute fractions of milk components contribute signiﬁcantly to the wastewater
production. NF and RO systems have been extensively used in the industry to treat these process water
and the treated water can be reused elsewhere in the production process [5][6][7].
2.1.1
Reverse Osmosis
Reverse osmosis (RO), also called hyperﬁltration is used when low molecular weight solutes such as
inorganic salts or small organic molecules have to be separated from a solvent (usually water). Te
membranes retain most of the dissolved solid and selectively permeate water[8].
During the process, a feed is run through the membrane active layer. Te stream that passes through is
called permeate whereas the remaining components on the feed side is termed concentrate or retentate
[9]. Denser membranes with very high hydrodynamic resistance is required since very small solutes are
involved. Due to the high membrane resistance a high pressure must be applied to force the solvent
through the membrane. In order to allow water to pass through the membrane the applied pressure
must be higher than the osmotic pressure. In a reverse osmosis operation, 10 to 100 bar is normally
applied on the feed side[10].
Te pressure diﬀerence between the applied pressure and the osmotic pressure of the feed is the driving
force for reverse osmosis. Te transport of solvent takes place due to the result of driving force acting
on the individual components in the feed. Te rate of permeation is usually proportional to the driving

2.2
Membrane fouling
Fouling results in a continuous drop in membrane permeation rate, an increased elimination of low
molecular weight solutes and blocking of ﬂow channels. Tis results in a signiﬁcant reduction in
separation eﬃciency by decreasing permeate ﬂux and increasing the pressure drop across the membrane.
Flux decline can be caused by several factors such as concentration polarization, adsorption, gel layer
formation and plugging of pores [10]. A reduction of 10-30% in the membrane permeation rate is
commonly noticeable in UF processes afer a few minutes of operation [12]. Tis is ofen followed by a
steady decrease. Fouling is partly due to the blocking of membrane surface, reduction in the eﬀective

diameter of pores or development of a slowly thickening layer on the membrane surface commonly
known as concentration polarization [13]. Concentration polarization is commonly observed in reverse
osmosis systems. It refers to the accumulation of rejected solute in the membrane causing a concentra-
tion gradient where the solute decreases from the membrane interface to the bulk solution. Te feed
components are transported to the membrane surface by convection while the rejected species diﬀuse
away from the membrane surface. Since diﬀusion is slower than convection, the rejected solutes are
accumulated on the membrane surface resulting in a stagnant ﬁlm at the membrane boundary layer.
Fouling could be classiﬁed as reversible and irreversible. Reversible fouling can be removed by a strong
shear force such as backwashing or physical cleaning method whereas irreversible fouling requires a
combination of physical and chemical cleaning processes. Fouling can be minimized by controlling and
cleaning methods. Controlling methods include feed pre-treatment, membrane material selection and
membrane surface modiﬁcation.
Foulants can be categorized into 3 groups as organic precipitates (macromolecules and biological
substances), inorganic precipitates and particulates. Inorganic compounds responsible for fouling are
Ca2+, Mg2+, CO3
2−, SO4
2−, silica and iron [14]. As water recovery in the RO system increases, the
concentration of these constituents in the reject stream also increases.
2.2.1
Foulants in the dairy industry
Proteins and calcium phosphate are the common foulants encountered in the reverse osmosis units
in the dairy industry. Milk is highly saturated with calcium and phosphate ions, which exists in
dynamic equilibrium with undissolved or colloidal forms. According to Fox and Mcsweeny [15][16],
the proteins insoluble at a pH of 4.6, called casein precipitates as micelles in the presence of calcium
concentrations above 6mM and temperatures above 20◦C. Calcium phosphate exceed their solubility
limit (1.8 g/100 ml) under the conditions in milk and scales the adjacent surfaces. Calcium exists as
two forms in dairy solutions, the soluble Ca(H2PO4)2 and CaHPO4 as well as in a colloidal form as
casein calcium phosphate micelles. Cheese whey permeates typically contains 375 ppm calcium and 400
ppm phosphorous. Phosphates can form many polymeric ions, but the majority of calcium phosphate
deposits are amorphous [17]. According to Durham [18], calcium phosphate precipitate is favored by
increase in concentration, elevated temperature and alkaline pH. During lactose production, calcium
phosphate scaling is inevitable since the crystallization of lactose results in increased saturation of the
dairy solution leading to the precipitation of calcium phosphate as hydroxyapatite (HAP).

Common membrane cleaning techniques
Membranes in industry are maintained and cleaned in place and cleaning cycles can either totally
or partially remove clogged particles from the membrane. Membrane cleaning techniques typically
interrupt the continuous ﬁltration process resulting in longer processing times. Te most common
cleaning techniques involve hydraulic, mechanical, chemical and electrical methods [19]. Turbulence

2.3.1
Physical cleaning
Hydraulic cleaning such as back washing or backﬂushing is an important procedure to reduce mem-
brane fouling. Backwashing is performed with a reversed ﬂow from the permeate to the feed side of
the membrane. Tis dislodges the deposits from the insides of the pores and loosens the cake on the
membrane surface [27]. Te back pressure can interrupt continuous ﬁltration and may result in longer
processing times. Backwashing does not remove strongly adherent ﬁlms or material trapped within the
porous substructure of the membrane. Flux degradation typically occurs afer repeated backwashes [11].
Backwashing can be easily applied to tubular and hollow ﬁbre membrane modules. However, it has
limited application in the case of spiral wound modules due to the very high probability of mechanical
damage when pressure is applied on the permeate side [2]. Backpulsing or backshocking is a rapid
backwash (0.1 s or shorter), where a ﬁltration step is followed by a reverse ﬁltration step, that can
eﬀectively remove non-adherent foulants from membranes [25]. In pneumatic cleaning (commonly
referred as air sparging) of membranes, air is passed through the membranes to enhance the ﬂux in the
ﬁltration step [28]. Air sparging and backﬂushing have been applied together in spiral wound NF to
remove biofouling [29].
Some of the non-conventional methods include ultrasonic and electrical cleaning. Ultrasonic membrane
cleaning requires high frequency sound waves to agitate aqueous medium which acts on the foulants
adhering to the surface [26]. Application of ultrasound has an eﬀective approach to enhance ﬂux
in ultraﬁltration or microﬁltration processes and to improve the cleaning of fouled membranes [30].
Ultrasound produce cavitation and induce acoustic streaming, which provide vigorous mixing to reduce
concentration polarization and break the cake layer on the membrane surface [31]. Electrical cleaning
or electroﬁltration [32] uses an electric ﬁeld generated across a membrane by placing two electrodes on
either side to enhance permeation ﬂux and control fouling. Electrical cleaning is limited to the removal
of ionic foulants.
2.3.2
Chemical cleaning
Chemical cleaning can be carried out either in-situ, called cleaning in place (CIP) by replacing the feed
solution with the cleaning solution or ex-situ, also called cleaning out of place (COP) by placing the
membrane in an oﬀ-site tank [31]. Chemical treatment involves large volumes of surfactants, acids
and/or caustic soda, which can reduce the life span of the membrane by damaging the membrane
material as well as cause secondary pollution. Alkaline cleaning followed by acid cleaning is commonly
used in the dairy and surface water industry to eﬃciently remove macromolecules and mineral foulants

2.4
Ultrasonication
Ultrasound can be eﬀective in enhancing both the production and cleaning cycles of dairy membrane
processes [2][39][40]. Te application of ultrasound is atractive since no chemical cleaning reagent is
required and a high permeate ﬂux can be maintained when applied during the ﬁltration process, not only
immediately afer cleaning. Ultrasonic energy can reach crevices that are not easily cleaned by other
physical methods, disperse a solid or liquid ﬁlm and increase the dissolution rate of the surface ﬁlm
[41]. Ultrasound prevents membrane fouling and cleans fouled membrane surfaces via the high velocity
ﬂuid movement associated with ultrasonically generated microstreamers, microstreaming, microjets
and acoustic streaming [42]. Ultrasound increase permeation by either decreasing the foulant cake layer
thickness or increasing turbulence in the concentration polarization layers. Low frequency ultrasound
(20-50 kHz) has been reported to maximize membrane permeation and can be used as an eﬀective
cleaning method [42][38][43].
2.4.1
Mechanism
Ultrasonic waves create vigorous mixing which causes increased turbulence and cavitation. Ultrasound
propagates through a medium, a series of compression and refraction cycles results in the formation
of cavitation bubbles [44] (Figure 2.3). Compression exerts a positive pressure and pushes the liquid
molecules together, while refraction creates a negative pressure and pulls the molecules apart. Tis
negative pressure gives rise to cavitation bubbles, when greater than the tensile strength of the liquid.
Cavitation bubbles are small vapor ﬁlled cavities, that pulsate in size under the propagation of ultrasound
[45].

2.4.2
Usage in membrane operations
Power ultrasound (20-500 kHz) has been widely researched to control fouling in ultraﬁltration mem-
branes since it causes cavitation with sound intensities of 10 to 1000 W/cm2 [47]. Additionally, ultrasound
irradiation does not aﬀect the intrinsic permeability of polysulfone membranes. Flux recovery has
been known to be independent of length of sonication time but strongly dependent of the ultrasonic
power[38]. Also a cross-ﬂow system signiﬁcantly reduces the potentiality of membrane damage at
higher ultrasonic frequencies. Ultrasonic irradiation at low power levels can enhance permeate ﬂux by a
factor of 1.2 to 1.7 [40].
Te eﬀect of ultrasonic waves is sensitive to frequency, output power, irradiation angle and the position
of ultrasonic vibration plate in the membrane module [48]. Cavitational eﬀects are maximized at low
pressures (35 kPa), hence ultrasonic cleaning is employed at similar pressures [2]. Ultrasound at 20 kHz
or 50 kHz and the highest nominal power (commonly 300 W in case of ultrasonic baths and 200 W for

2.4.3
Challenges
Tere has been contradicting reports on the integrity of membrane material in the presence of ultrasound.
Composite membranes get easily disintegrated in the presence of ultrasound due to diﬀerent temperature
dependency of the polymers involved [26]. Mechanical forces created from cavities can erode the mem-
brane surface. Li et al [49] have reported that polysulfone hollow ﬁbre UF membranes were damaged
when exposed to ultrasonic irradiation with low frequency and high intensity. Another challenge
involved is the generation of uniform acoustic ﬁeld across the entire membrane surface. An uneven
application will likely result in membrane damage and will also cause ineﬃcient energy consumption[40].

4.1
Preliminary tests
Te preliminary tests were performed to evaluate the impact of ultrasound (US) on the whey permeate
(UF permeate) samples of AFI and the reverse osmosis (RO) membranes. According to Fox [15], in a multi
ion solution such as the UF permeate tested, the crystallization of lactose can aggravate the precipitation
of calcium phosphates by increasing the saturation of solution. It was also suspected that ultrasonic
irradiation might aﬀect the structural properties of the membrane. So, it was crucial to understand
whether the application of US aﬀected the following before usage in the cleaning or ﬁltration studies
1. Mechanical properties of the RO membrane
2. Concentration of lactose, the valuable product to be recovered by the RO membrane
3. Precipitation of calcium, which was a common scaling ion present in the UF permeate samples
4.1.1
Eﬀect of sonication on the UF permeates
Lactose degradation
Te inﬂuence of US on lactose concentration was tested for 5, 10 and 30 minutes at an ultrasonic power
of 40, 100 and 200 W corresponding to amplitudes of 20, 50 and 100% at 24 kHz. If sonication were
to be eﬀected continuously on the UF permeate samples for longer periods of time, it was crucial to
understand if the application of US degraded lactose in the samples. Te initial concentration of lactose
in all the experiments was 24.5±0.1 g/l. Figure 4.1 shows the variation in concentration of lactose at
diﬀerent combinations of ultrasonic powers and sonication periods. Te lactose concentration in the UF
permeate samples remained constant at 29.4 g/l with a maximum standard deviation of 0.42% at the
various ultrasonic powers and sonication periods tested. Less than 1% of lactose was observed to be
lost during the sonication process. Tis loss could be atributed to the experimental error of handling
samples than to degradation of the material from ultrasonic irradiation. Hence it can be ascertained that
lactose did not degrade at the sonication conditions tested.

In dairy solutions such as the UF permeate samples containing lactose, calcium and phosphates, calcium
phosphate is known to occur as soluble Ca(H2PO4)2 and CaHPO4 [15]. According to Whitier [57]
when the amount of water becomes limiting or in other words if the solution approaches saturation,
calcium phosphate can crystallize as (Ca)3(PO4)2 which can form a scaling layer on surfaces. Ultrasonic
waves have been reported to cause spontaneous supersaturation which can result in the precipitation of
calcium ions. However at the the sonication conditions tested, calcium ions were kept suspended in the
presence of ultrasound. According to Fox [15], McSweeney [16] and Alkhati [58], in a multi-ion solution
such as the UF permeate samples, the precipitation of calcium ions can decrease due to chelation with
lactic acid or citrates to form soluble calcium lactates or calcium citrates respectively. Furthermore the
presence of peptides and impurities can retard the precipitation of calcium ions according to Durham
[18].

In this work, defouling refers to the removal of scaling mineral ions, especially calcium. Ashokkumar et
al., has reported that low frequency ultrasonic source (∼20 kHz) can generate strong physical forces
such as shear and turbulence, which can be utilized for cleaning [59]. So, a 24 kHz ultrasonic source
was chosen to sonicate the membrane material in the current study. Sonication was carried out ex-situ
due to the pressure limitations of the experimental setup. A dead-end ﬁltration unit was employed for
the preliminary assessment of ultrasound usage either as a cleaning procedure or during ﬁltration for
defouling.

sections discuss the ultrasound usage for defouling ﬂatsheet polyamide RO membranes as an independent
cleaning procedure and during ﬁltration.

Chemical cleaning had the highest cleaning eﬃciency and ﬂux recovery of 98.46% and 94.44% respectively.

been shown that cleaning eﬃciency has been independent of sonication time. Hence shorter sonication
periods even less than 5 minutes could be used eﬀectively for cleaning the membrane surface. Te
downtime for cleaning can be considerably reduced by opting an ultrasonic source instead of chemical
cleaning. Further, chemical cleaning introduces additional mineral ions from the acid as well as base

Economical considerations
Te capital cost associated with transducers and maintenance must be considered if ultrasound has
to be used in an operation. Te major operating cost to incorporate ultrasound to assist cleaning and
ﬁltration is associated with electricity. Ultrasound might be eﬀective when employed intermitently for
short periods throughout a longer production period rather than continuously. Tis would reduce the
electricity consumption to a more viable level. Furthermore the short periods of sonication could extend
the production time between cleans and further reducing operating costs.

Conclusion
Tis thesis summarizes the results from the investigation of ultrasound usage for the removal of calcium
ions from whey permeate fouled polyamide reverse osmosis membranes. Te potential of ultrasound as
an independent cleaning procedure and during the ﬁltration operation has been investigated in this
study. A 24 kHz, 200 W nominal power transducer was used as the ultrasonic source. Te performance
of the reverse osmosis membranes were assessed in a dead-end ﬁltration unit. Test samples (whey/UF
permeates) and the polyamide membranes used industrially were provided by Arla Foods Ingredients
(AFI). Tis work has been divided into evaluating three parts (1) the eﬀect of sonication on the membrane
properties and stability of the UF permeate samples (2) ultrasound assisted membrane cleaning and (3)
ultrasound assisted ﬁltration.
In a dairy solution such as the whey permeate samples from AFI, the reduction in concentration of
lactose can increase the precipitation of calcium phosphate from the solution. So the eﬀect of sonication
on the stability of lactose and calcium ions in the whey permeate samples was assessed. Lactose was not
degraded and calcium ions were not precipitated when ultrasound was applied. Furthermore it was also
suspected that structural changes might occur on polyamide reverse osmosis elements when subjected
to ultrasonic treatment. However, the values of water permeation and membrane resistance did not
change signiﬁcantly before and afer sonication at 200 W for 1h. Te active layer of the membrane
was not damaged from sonication in this case. Moreover, a prolonged continuous and direct exposure
of ultrasound for a period of 24h at 200 W mechanically damaged the active layer of the membrane
element. Several factors such as elevated temperature, localized erosion and increased shearing may
have led to the membrane failure.
A ﬂatsheet polyamide reverse osmosis membrane was repeatedly fouled using the UF permeate in a
dead-end ﬁltration unit. Te fouled membranes were cleaned using an ultrasonic power of 200 W (100%
amplitude, 24 kHz) or 40 W (20% amplitude, 24 kHz) for short periods of 5, 10 and 20 minutes. Te
performance of the ultrasound for cleaning was evaluated and compared with the conventional chemical
cleaning procedure and in the absence of sonication. Cleaning eﬃciency and water ﬂux recovery,
which were used to quantify the cleaning performance was found to be independent of sonication
period but strongly dependent on the ultrasonic power. Te membrane structure was not aﬀected
from the repeated fouling and intermitent ultrasonic irradiation used for cleaning. Te rejection of lac-
tose and calcium ions was greater than 99% and was found to be unaﬀected in the presence of ultrasound.

prolonged use of the ultrasound have to be considered before scaling-up these procedures to industrial

Future work
Tough this work has advanced the understanding of the application of ultrasound as a cleaning proce-
dure and during ﬁltration, the study can be continued with the following suggestions.
From this study, ultrasound has been found to inﬂuence cleaning and ﬁltration under the process
conditions tested in a batch dead-end ﬁltration unit. However the results cannot be readily implemented
in the industry. It might be desirable to understand if ultrasound can remove calcium deposits during a
continuous ﬁltration under similar conditions as the industrial ﬁltration of whey permeates. It might
also be desirable to evaluate the extent to which ultrasound can increase the production period between
cleaning cycles. It may be beneﬁcial to investigate the fouling control capacity of ultrasound under
reduced fouling conditions such as the cross-ﬂow ﬁltration operation. In a cross-ﬂow system, the feed is
run tangentially across the membrane surface at high velocities. Te turbulent ﬂow caused by the high
velocities continuously washes away the scales and other foulants from the membrane surface during
operation.
It has been found at through this work that ultrasound can be used as an independent cleaning pro-
cedure. However, it was not as eﬃcient as the chemical cleaning in fouling removal. A judicious use
of ultrasound during a chemical cleaning cycle may be cost-eﬀective in reducing the total chemical
consumption. Tis would also reduce the quantity of chemical solutions requiring disposal into the
environment. Terefore a combination of cleaning procedures, such as using chemicals and sonication
alternatively may be carried out to evaluate the synergy of the diﬀerent cleaning procedures.
Eventhough lactose and calcium ions were not aﬀected from direct sonication of the whey permeate
samples in the current study, indirect application of ultrasonic irradiation on the solution might be
preferred due to hygiene requirements in the industry. So It may be useful to evaluate the depth of
penetration of ultrasonic waves in the solution since it might aﬀect the ability of ultrasound in reaching
the membrane surface. Additionally, the experimental conditions have to be optimized to ﬁnd the least
energy demanding process conditions.

A.5
Possible fouling and cleaning mechanism in US assisted ﬁltration
To represent industrial ﬁltration, 10h of ﬁltration was carried out with the UF permeate at 15±2◦C and
30 bar. Afer every hour afer ﬁltration, the membrane element was sonicated ex-situ for 10 minutes
at an ultrasonic power of 200 W (100% amplitude, 24 kHz) in the feed solution (concentrate from the
ﬁltration). Te sonicated concentrate was subsequently reused as the feed to the ﬁltration unit in the
next hour of operation. However, the reader should note that ﬁltration was carried out at 30 bar while
the sonication was eﬀected at atmospheric pressure. For the control experiment, ﬁltration was carried
out for 10 hours at 30 bar without intermediate sonication.
From the results of the experiments, the solution ﬂux (J) was found to continually decline in the absence
of sonication. J decreased during every hour of ﬁltration, but signiﬁcantly improved when the sonicated
feed was reintroduced as the feed solution. Te possible fouling mechanism for the decline of J can
be found in Figure A.5.1. Te ﬁgure in the lef represents the ﬁltration setup and the accumulation of
foulants due to the continual applied pressure on the feed side. Te decrease in solution ﬂux (J) during
ﬁltration may be an indication of the decrease in driving force (i.e the applied pressure) across the
membrane. Te decrease in driving force may be associated with the increasing osmotic pressure, which
is known to be proportional to the concentration of foulants on the membrane surface. Te increase
in foulant concentration on the membrane surface in comparison to the bulk is commonly referred as
concentration polarization. It results in the accumulation of rejected solute in the membrane causing a
concentration gradient where the solute concentration decreases from the membrane interface to the
bulk solution. Te feed components are transported to the membrane surface by convection while the
rejected solutes diﬀuse away from the membrane surface. Since diﬀusion is slower than convection, the
rejected solutes are accumulated on the membrane surface resulting in a stagnant ﬁlm at the membrane
boundary layer. Additionally, some foulants may have adhered to the membrane surface due to the high
pressure applied on the feed.
Figure A.5.1: Representation of the ﬁltration operation and the possible fouling mechanism during the
10h batch ﬁltration performed at 30 bar. Lef end: representation of experimental setup; Right: (enlarged)
representation of the membrane surface during ﬁltration
Te concentration polarization signiﬁcantly lowered the calcium ion rejection in the case of ﬁltration
without sonication. Te reduction in calcium rejection could be atributed to the increase in calcium
concentration in the feed near the membrane surface in comparison with the bulk. Te rapid rise
in concentration may have allowed the divalent calcium ions to pass through the membrane which
otherwise would have been retained due to its large hydrated diameter (114 picometer (pm)). Te slight
decrease in rejection values occurs to maintain the calcium ion balance on both sides of the membrane.
More than 2% of the calcium passed through the membrane as the ﬁltration time increased in this case.

Hence, concentration polarization may be regarded severe in the absence of ultrasound, which was also
responsible for the stark decline in solution ﬂux as explained earlier. Calcium was marginally lost in
the presence of ultrasound, indicating up to 1% of the in the permeate. Tis could be associated with
the positive eﬀect of ultrasound reducing concentration polarization when the sonicated feed was run
through the membrane during the subsequent hour of ﬁltration. However, the rejection of lactose which
was a relatively large molecule was not aﬀected by concentration polarization and the rejection values
remained constant in the presence of ultrasound.
When the membrane element was taken out of the ﬁltration unit and transferred to a beaker containing
the concentrate from ﬁltration, the foulants tend to setle on the membrane surface. Tis is graphically
represented in Figure A.5.2 as ’during transfer’. Te possible ultrasonic cleaning mechanism is also
depicted in Figure A.5.2. During sonication, ultrasound has been known to dislodge the foulants by
shearing the membrane surface. Te shearing is usually accompanied by turbulence in the solution
being sonicated. Te turbulence created by the ultrasound most likely suspends the dislodged foulants
in solution. Te removal of foulants from the membrane surface by turbulence and shearing, reduces the
resistance induced by the increased foulant concentration on the membrane surface thereby increasing
J in the subsequent ﬁltration hour.
