Abstract
N6-methyladenosine (m6A) is an essential RNA modiﬁcation that plays an essen-
tial role in post-transcriptional gene regulation. In Arabidopsis thaliana, YTH domain-
containing proteins (ECT2, ECT3, and ECT4) act as m6A readers and are necessary
proteins for normal plant growth and development.
This study investigates whether ribosomal proteins (RP) and oxidative phosphory-
lation (OXPHOS) genes are key targets of ECT2/3/4 and whether their regulation is
associated with phenotypic rescue in te234 mutants. Additionally, the study explores the
enrichment of URUAY/URUA-like motifs around m6A sites and investigates the role of
PUM proteins in this enrichment.
Diﬀerential expression analysis of several RNA-seq datasets showed that RP and OX-
PHOS genes are signiﬁcantly downregulated in te234 mutants, suggesting that they con-
tribute to growth defects. Furthermore, the analysis of poly(A) binding proteins (PAB4)
overexpression in te234 mutants showed partial phenotypic rescue, highlighting a potential
link between ribosomal function and m6A-mediated gene regulation. Motif enrichment
analysis conﬁrmed that URUAY is not an m6A-enriched motif, contradicting previous
studies. While PUM proteins appear to increase URUAY enrichment around m6A sites,
statistical analyses suggest that this occurs independently of m6A modiﬁcation.
This
ﬁndings create new questions about the indirect mechanisms where PUM proteins may
contribute to motif enrichment. Overall, this study provides insights into m6A-mediated
regulation of ribosomal function and expands our understanding of URUAY motif enrich-
ment in plant mRNA regulation.

Introduction
3.1
N6-methyladenosine (m6A) in mRNA
Genes are sequences of DNA that have all the information needed to build functional
proteins or, in some cases, RNA. Active protein-coding genes are transcribed from DNA
to mRNA in order to be translated into proteins with the help of the ribosome.
In
Arabidopsis thaliana there are approximately 27,500 genes whose tight regulation deﬁnes
the diﬀerent expression patterns observed in diﬀerent cells and tissues. [8].
Regulated gene expression is a result of multiple mechanisms acting at both tran-
scriptional and post-transcriptional levels. One of these mechanisms involves covalent
modiﬁcation of nucleotides in the mRNA. These modiﬁcations include methylation that
may act as a chemical tag and serve as a docking station for RNA-binding proteins that
control nuclear and cytoplasmic properties of the mRNA, including translation [2]. The
most widespread internal covalent mRNA modiﬁcation is N6-methyladenosine (m6A).
N6-methyladenosine (m6A) is the most abundant internal modiﬁed nucleotide in eu-
karyotic mRNA bodies and it is key for the eukaryotic mRNA biogenesis and functionality
[10].
In plants and mammals, this modiﬁcation can aﬀect embryogenesis [20, 35]. In other
eukaryotes, like yeast, it aﬀects the meiosis process. It has been studied that, in Saccha-
romyces cervisiae, the mRNA methylation is necessary to start the process of meiosis and
sporulation [25]. On the other hand, in Drosophila melanogaster, m6A has an essential
role in sex determination and nueronal function thanks to the regulation of the Sex-lethal
(SXL) splicing [19].
While the m6A modiﬁcations spread across eukaryotes, it is not universally conserved.
For instance, in Caenorhabditis elegans, m6A are present but have diﬀerent functions than
with other species. That indicates that, while this process is essential for mammals and
plants for the embryonic development [20], it is not essential for all species.

3.2.1
m6A methyl transferase ("writer")
m6A methylation of the RNA normally takes place in the nucleus of the cell by a
highly conserved methylase METTL3/METTL14 (MTA/MTB in plants) [5] and highly
conserved proteins. In plants, a catalytic heterodimer formed by MTA-MTB (METTL
Complex (MAC) and the multi-subunit m6A-METTL Associated Complex (MACOM),

==PAGE:9==
3.2
Molecular frame-work for m6A functions
will allow the full function of the m6A writer proteins. This multi-subunit MACOM is
composed by multiple proteins — FIP37, VIRILIZER (VIR), HAKAI, and HIZ2 (Figure
1). Knock-out studies show that when losing FIP37, VIR and HIZ2, more than 80% of
the m6A levels in mRNA drop leading to developmental problems [6]. However, only
removing HAKAI showed a loss of 35% with only mild developmental problems [28].
The fact that highly conserved factors are needed for the methylation of the m6A,
suggests that m6A distribution should follow a pattern in all eukaryotic cells. Studies
show that m6A is found in a consensus site RRACH (R = G/A, H = A/C/U) enriched in
the 3’UTR of mRNA of organisms that have the METTL3/METTL14 methylase [4]. In
plants, while the RRACH motif is also prevalent, additional motifs such as GGAU have
been identiﬁed as plant-speciﬁc [1].
3.2.2
m6A demethylase ("eraser")
m6A modiﬁcation is reversible with the action of m6A demethylases, also called "erasers".
Eraser proteins remove the methyl group to regulate the RNA metabolism (Figure 1).
FTO and ALKBH5 are the main eraser proteins in mammals, where they catalyze oxida-
tive demethylation of m6A back to adenosine, regulating RNA stability and translation
[17, 34]. In plants, these m6A demethylases are less understood, but some plant homologs
like AlkB are know to have similar demethylation functions [9].
3.2.3
m6A binding proteins ("readers")
m6A can impact the function of cells thanks to the creation of binding sites for reader
proteins, between others [2]. The principal m6A reader proteins contain the YT521-B
homology domain (YTH).
This YTH domain has a highly conserved aromatic cage that accommodates m6A with
roughly 20-fold higher aﬃnity than unmethylated adenosines. YTH domain proteins can
be separated into two diﬀerent phylogenetic groups: YTHDC and YTHDF (Figure 1).
YTHDC proteins are often found in the nucleus and contain long Intrinsically Dis-
ordered Regions (IDRs), which are frequently present in multidomain proteins. These
regions may regulate nuclear RNA decay, splicing, and mRNA export.
Meanwhile, YTHDF proteins are normally found in the cell cytoplasm and the domain
is placed at the C-terminus after an IDR [10]. This position of the YTH domain is in

a highly conserved zone and receive the name of Evolutionary Conserved C Terminal
Region (ECT) [24] with a total of 11 ECT sited in Arabidopsis thaliana (ECT1-11). In

plants [6]. More details about ECT proteins in plants will be explained in Subsection 3.3.
3.3
m6A readers in plants
In Arabidopsis thaliana, three out of the eleven ECT (ECT2, ECT3, ECT4) are ex-
pressed in cells with a high rate of division, specially in organ primordia [29]. Mutations
in these proteins (named ect2, ect3 and ect4) cause a defect in the plant growth resulting
into signiﬁcantly slower plant development. The triple mutant (te234) show a speciﬁc
phenotype where there is a growth inhibition and delayed organogenesis (Figure 2) [10].
Figure 2: Comparison of the phenotype of the Col-0-WT with the te234 after 9 DAG
(Days After Germination) and 19 DAG. There is a clear diﬀerence in the delayed growth
of the true leaves in the triple mutant compared to the wild type. This growth delay
continues even after 19 DAG. Image from Due Tankmar et al..
ECT2/3 are the so-called "eﬀector" proteins that bind to mRNA and control the cell
division in primordial cells, ensuring proper cell functions. If these functions are not con-
trolled, there can be a delay in organogenesis, leading into growth defects. Interestingly,
ECT4 has no eﬀect when suppressing it alone, but when combined with the previous
action of the ECT2/3 mutations, intensiﬁes the inhibition of the organogenesis and the
growth delay [10].

3.4
Mechanisms of ECT-stimulated growth
ECT proteins also help with antiviral defense and stress adaptation.
The loss of
ECT2/3/4/5 proteins increases the possibility for the plant to be harmed by the Alfalfa
mpsaic virus (AMV), highlighting their role in plant immunity [23]. As an environmental
stress response, ECT2/3/8 relocate to the processing bodies and stress granules with the
purpose of regulating mRNA. ECT8 acts as a stress sensor, promoting stress responses
such as mRNA decay and plant adaptation [7].
3.4
Mechanisms of ECT-stimulated growth
3.4.1
Target identiﬁcation
ECT2/3 promote cell proliferation during organogenesis of Arabidopsis thaliana and
bind to a large overlapping set of mRNA targets [2] identiﬁed using HyperTRIBE and
iCLIP technology. Previous studies suggested that ECT2/3 are localized in the nucleus
and involved in alternative polyadenylation, but more recent research found that their
primary function is in the cytoplasm [2].
A Gene Set Enrichment Analysis showed a decrease in ribosomal protein (RP) and
oxidative phosphorylation genes (OXPHOS) expression in te234, explaining this decrease
with the activation of stress responses in these mutants. The activation of these stress
responses could lead to alterations in the ribosomal function and energy metabolism,
showing a decrease in RP and OXPHOS genes considering them a marker. [2].
3.4.2
Relation to uL11b proteins
uL11b gene in plants is a ribosomal protein found in the large subunit of the ribo-
some, which is an essential part of protein synthesis. A mutation in this gene can lead to
ribosome dysfunction and, consequently, defective growth and division [30].
uL11b mutants show phenotypic similarities to ECT mutants, highlighting a possi-
ble connection between ribosome activity and m6A-mediated mRNA regulation in plant
growth [3, 2]. Unpublished data presented in the 17th Microsymposium on RNA Biology,
suggest that mutations in the uL11B gene in the nucleotides G141E and P149L in te234
plants could restore the phenotype with the over-expression of ribosomal proteins (Figure
3). In this thesis, a reanalysis of this unpublished data will be performed to get a better
insight of how the ribosomal proteins and the OXPHOS aﬀect the plant growth and de-
velopment.

3.4.3
Relation to ALBA proteins
Acetylation Lowers Binding Aﬃnity (ALBA) proteins are a very conserved family of
nucleic acid binding proteins present in multiple species. In Arabidopsis Thaliana, ALBA
proteins help with RNA binding, regulation of gene expression, and it is also involved in
stress responses. It also helps with mRNA stability and translation. [27]
ALBA proteins play a very important role in m6A and ECT mediated gene regulation.
These proteins interact with ECT2, facilitating its binding to the m6A in mRNA in vivo.
The pyrimidine-rich sequences adjacent to the m6A sites are recognized by ALBA proteins,
helping the ECT2 proteins bind to the methylated mRNA (Figure 1). The cooperation
of these two proteins is essential for mRNA regulation for plant development and stress
responses [27].
3.4.4
Relation to poly(A) binding proteins
Polyadenylate-binding proteins (PABPs) are RNA-binding proteins that attach to the
poly(A) tail (3’UTR region) of the mRNA, and play an essential role in RNA metabolism,
translation and biogenesis [22].
PABPs have multiple recognition sites across the N-
terminal for the poly(A) interaction in the 3’UTR, and can also attach to the C-terminal
end for protein to protein interactions [10]. When binding to the poly(A) tail, PABPs
protects the mRNA from degradation and improve translational eﬃciency, attracting the
ribosomes for a higher protein synthesis. [22]

3.4
Mechanisms of ECT-stimulated growth
In Arabidopsis Thaliana, PABPs play a very important role in mRNA regulation, not
only protecting the RNA through the poly(A) binding, but also interacting with other
RNA-binding proteins such as ECT2/3/4 (Figure 1). Mutations in the interactions be-
tween PABP and ECT proteins lead to developmental problems [10].
Since PABPs signiﬁcantly impact the interaction with ECT2/3/4 and the protection
of mRNA through poly(A) binding, thereby enhancing translational eﬃciency, it was
interesting to investigate whether increasing PABP levels in the plant could reverse the
reduced developmental growth observed in the te234 mutant through the overexpression
of PAB4 [10]. To assess how PABPs aﬀect the te234 phenotype, a Diﬀerential Expression
Analysis including triplicates of Col-0-WT, te234, and two diﬀerent PABP overexpressing
cell lines used in the study by Due Tankmar et al. will be performed as part of this project.
3.4.5
Relation to PUMILIO proteins
PUMILIO/Fem-3 Binding Factor (PUF) are RBP that consist of a long N-terminal
IDR followed by a PUM homology domain (PUM-HD) which is very conserved across
species. These proteins predominantly bind to the 3’UTR regions [33] of the genome with
a total of 26 homologs of the protein (PUM1-PUM26) in plants [11].
Six of these proteins (PUM1-PUM6) belong to a canonical group where they share
more than 50% of their amino acid sequence, which is conserved between species. PUM
proteins recognize and ﬂexibly bind to the RNA sequence with the motif UGUANAUA
(N=A/G/T/C) in the 3’UTR [11]. Some PUM proteins have diﬀerent known functions,
for example, PUM5 has antiviral functions against the positive strand of the Cucumber
Mosaic Virus (CMV) [16]. However, very little is known about the functions of PUM
proteins in plants, and although this family of proteins was studied in mammals, yeast,
Drosophila, and C. Elegans, they do not have clear plant homologues except for PUM1-6
[15, 12, 26].
In plants, the URUAY motif (R = A/G, Y = U/C) is enriched around the m6A sites
in the 3’UTR and has been described as plant-speciﬁc due to its abundance near the
m6A sites. It was concluded that URUAY serves as a binding site for the ECT2 proteins
in the YTHDF domain [32]. More recent studies showed that ECT2 proteins primarily
bind to the RRACH context, but not URUAY. However, despite the weaker enrichment
of URUAY around the binding sites, it remained signiﬁcant [1].

3.4
Mechanisms of ECT-stimulated growth
The technique used to obtain these results in the article by Wei et al. was Formaldehyde-
based Cross-Linking and Immunoprecipitation (FA-CLIP), which involves cross-linking
RNA and proteins with formaldehyde, immunoprecipitating the ECT2 proteins, and se-
quencing the RNA fragments [14]. FA-CLIP may introduce bias, as creating artiﬁcial
interactions. Therefore, the conclusion that URUAY is a plant-speciﬁc motif [32] might
be inﬂuenced by the limitations of the technique used.
Part of this project aims to help understand the enrichment of URUAY around the
m6A sites in the context of PUM proteins. The ﬁrst ﬁve nucleotides of the PUM pro-
tein consensus binding site, UGUANAUA, ﬁt within the URUAY motif, leading to the
hypothesis that PUM proteins may be responsible for the enrichment of the URUAY con-
text. PUM binding sites are ﬂexible because of the structure of the PUM domain. The
last three nucleotides (AUA) are less conserved, allowing for variations in motif that can
recognize. This ﬂexibility allows PUM to bind sequences such as URUAY, rather than
requiring a strict UGUANAUA match.
To test this hypothesis, the comprehensive single nucleotide resolution m6A sites map-
ping using the m6A-SAC-seq in the Arabidopsis Thaliana dataset will be used [31]. The
Sac-seq data from Wang et al. gives insights about transcriptomic, single-base resolution
map of m6A modiﬁcations, identiﬁcation over 200,000 m6A sites across more than 20,000
genes in Arabidopsis Thaliana, oﬀering valuable material to investigate new roles of m6A
in plant development.

Aim of study
This project has the purpose of investigating a potential role of ribosomal protein
(RP) and oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) genes as key targets of ECT2/3/4, and
their relationship with phenotypic rescue in te234 mutants. Additionally, the study exam-
ines the enrichment of URUAY/UGUA-type motifs around m6A sites and the potential
involvement of PUM-mediated targeting around m6A-ECT2/3 binding sites. These two
main topics lead us to the following research questions:
Q1: Are RP and OXPHOS particularly important targets of ECT2, ECT3,
and ECT4, and can they help with phenotypic rescue of te234 mutants?
To explore this, we will perform diﬀerent Diﬀerent Expression Analysis of multiple
RNA-seq datasets including protoplast data (Subsection 6.1), early leaves with riboso-
mal proteins mutants data (Subsection 6.2), and early leaves with PAB4 overexpressed
mutants data (Subsection 6.3). The analysis focuses on identifying gene sets (GSEA)
with particular interest in ribosomal proteins and oxidative phosphorylation genes and
evaluating the relationship with phenotypic rescue in te234 mutants.
Q2: Can we conﬁrm that URUAY/UGUA-type motifs are enriched around
m6A sites?
We will investigate the presence and enrichment URUAY/UGUA motifs around m6A
sites through an analysis of motif enrichment from sac-seq datasets [31]. This involves
generating enrichment percentages and a bar plot to visually represent motif enrichment
patterns (Subsection 6.4).
Q3: Is PUM targeting responsible for the URUAY/UGUA-like enrichment
around m6A-ECT2/3 binding sites?
To answer this, we will evaluate PUM-binding site distribution and enrichment around
m6A-modiﬁed regions and ECT2/3 binding sites using overlap analyses and statistical
signiﬁcance tests.
Visualizations are produced to demonstrate potential relationships
between PUM and ECT2/3 (Subsection 6.5).

Materials and methods
5.1
Experimental methods
5.1.1
Seed sterilization and spotting
For seed germination on Murashige and Skoog (MS) agar plates, Arabidopsis thaliana
seeds were ﬁrst surface sterilized in a sterile environment. Seed sterilization started with
an addition of 1 mL of 70% EtOH to 1.5 ml of Eppendorf with seeds and left it incu-
bating for 2 minute. The process was followed it a 10 minute incubation with the 1.5%
NaClO/0.05% Tween 20, and ﬁnally, two 1 mL sterile H2O washings. For the plate spot-
ting, the seeds were resuspended in 700 µL of MS-agar media. The plates with around
100 seeds, were sealed with 3M microporous surgical tape, and cold-stratiﬁed at 4 °C in
darkness for 4 days. After cold stratiﬁcation, plates were transferred to a standard growth
environment with temperature of 21-22 °C, light intensity of 120 µmol m-2 s-1, 70% rel-
ative humidity and a 16 hour light / 8 hour darkness photoperiod. Samples Col-0-WT,
te234 and PAB4-Venus 2.4 and 6.5 will be grown in the Aralab for 7, 8.5, and 9 days,
respectively.
5.1.2
Total RNA extraction
The early leaves of approximately 30 plants per sample were collected and frozen with
liquid nitrogen. In order to have a record of the times the samples were collected and to
be able to consider that time in the PCA, the time between the start and the end of the
collection was annotated.
When starting the RNA extraction, the tissue was ground with metal beads and the
tissue lyser to have tissue powder. The samples were shaken with the beads in two in-
tervals of 30 seconds in the tissue lyser to get the tissue powdered. After the tissue was
thoroughly ground, 1 ml of TRIzol reagent was added homogenizing immediately after
the addition with the vortex. It is extremely important that the samples are always in a
cold environment. The centrifuge was set during all the process at 4 °C and at a maxi-
mum speed of > 13.000 g. After the addition of the TRIzol reagent, 200 µL of chloroform
was added and homogenized immediately after with the vortex. The tubes were then
centrifuged for 10 minutes. Centrifugation allowed a separation between phases; aqueous,
interface and inorganic. The aqueous phase was carefully transferred into a new tube

with one volume of cold isopropanol (approximately 400 µL) and 1 µL of diluted Glycan
Blue, very carefully to not get any interface during the transferring process. The mixture
needed an incubation of 30 min at room temperature, and a centrifugation afterwards of
15 min to get the pellet. Supernatant was removed using pipetting and 800 µL of 70%
ethanol was added twice to wash the pellet.
To make the sample more pure, it was decided to continue the process with polysac-
charide precipitation. The process starts with the resuspension of the pellet with 300 µL
of nuclease-free water, continued with the addition of 1/10 volume (30 µL) of cold 99%
ethanol and 1/30 volume (10 µL) of 3 M NaOAc pH 5.2. The mixture was incubated 30
minutes on ice and afterwards centrifuged at 4 °C 13,000 g for 15 min to precipitate the
polysaccharide. The supernatant was transferred to a new, pre-cooled Eppendorf tube
with 2.5 volume of cold 99% ethanol and 1/10 volume (33 µL) of 3M NaOAc pH 5.2,
followed by incubation at -20 °C for at least an hour and centrifugation for 30 min. The
RNA pellet was washed with 800 µL of cold 70% ethanol twice and residual ethanol was
carefully removed with a pipette. The RNA pellet was then air-dried for 5 min and resus-
pended in 10 µL nuclease-free water. Sample concentrations were measured afterwards
using Nanodrop.
5.1.3
RNA Agarose Gel Electrophoresis
After RNA extraction, it is important to check the quality of the samples and assess
if RNA is degraded through agarose gel electrophoresis. An agarose-TAE mixture was
given beforehand and 1 µL of Ethidium bromide (EtBr) was mixed with the solution.
The solution was poured into a casting tray with a comb and left it solidify at room
temperature for at least 15 minutes. Once the gel was solidiﬁed, it was retired from the
casting and the comb was removed. The gel was placed inside the electrophoresis chamber
and loaded with 1 µL of Ladder and 5 µL of the samples mixed with loading dye (5:1
ratio). The gel was run at 100 V for 40 minutes and checked the bands under the UV
light. To be conﬁrmed that no RNA was degraded, two clear rRNA bands corresponding
to the 28S and 18S subunits with an approximate intensity of 2:1.

5.2
Bioinformatics methods
5.2.1
Quality Control and Read Trimming
Raw reads were assessed using FastQC to evaluate read quality. This process is very
important to see if the sequences need any kind of trimming or processing before starting
with the alignment. Some parameters that indicate a trimming need are the presence of
adapters or the bad quality of the reads at the end or the beginning of the sequences.
Fastp software is a good tool to trim the sequences. Default parameters were used
where reads with fewer than 40% of bases having a Phred quality score ≥15 and discards
reads shorter than 15 base pairs after trimming. Adapter trimming, quality trimming from
the front and tail of the sequences, and overrepresentation and duplication analysis were
also automatically performed. Post-trimming quality was veriﬁed again with FastQC.
5.2.2
Alignment to Reference Genome
After trimming, the sequences were aligned to the reference genome using STAR
(Spliced Transcripts Alignment to a Referece).
The ﬁrst step needed for STAR, was the indexing of the genome using the annotation
ﬁle (Araport11_GFF3_gene_transposons.201606.gtf), followed by the actual alignment
of the sequences. After the alignment, it was necessary to check the percentage of mapped
reads present in the autogenerated ﬁle log.ﬁnal.out, considering an optimal alignment
greater than 85%.

5.2.3
Gene Count Quantiﬁcation
When quantifying the gene expression, the software HTSeq from Python was used.
HTSeq counts the number of mapped reads to each gene corresponding to the annotation
ﬁle. This process will give the hit counts per each one of the genes in the Arabidopsis
thaliana genome.
Another method for gene count quantiﬁcation is Salmon. This software can give an
estimating transcript abundance without the need of a full alignment, facilitating the time
of processing. However, HTseq is preferred considering the high level of accuracy.
5.2.4
Data Filtering and Normalization
Gene count quantiﬁcation ﬁles from HTSeq were processed in R studio. First step was
creating a comprehensive table containing genes, samples, and counts.
Low expressed genes were discarded since they would give noise to the analysis. A
density graph (ggplot2) from all the genes was visualized, and the minimum gene count
was set so we were working with 50% most expressed genes.
5.2.5
Diﬀerential Expression Analysis (DEA)
DESeq2 is an R package commonly used to analyze RNA-seq data and ﬁnd genes that
are expressed diﬀerently (Diﬀerentially Expressed Genes) between conditions.
The ﬁrst step in this analysis was to deﬁne the comparisons we wanted to make
and create a matrix using the DESeqDataSetFromMatrix function from the package. To
ﬁnd diﬀerentially expressed genes, the DESeq and results functions were used. The out-
put provides the log2FoldChange and the adjusted p-value (p-adj), calculated using the
Benjamini-Hochberg method. The thresholds used to determine diﬀerential expression
were a log2FoldChange of 1 and a p-adj of 0.05 to ensure proper statistical signiﬁcance.
5.2.6
Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
Variance stabilizing transformation (VST) was applied to reduce the impact of vari-
ance heterogeneity across the data and normalizing the data so the eﬀect of the outliers
was minimum [21]. Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on the VST-
transformed data using the prcomp function. This allowed the calculation of each principal
component and the PCA was plotted using the ﬁrst two principal components.

5.2.7
Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA)
Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) is a method that determinate if predeﬁned
groups of genes, so-called gene sets, show any statistically signiﬁcance in expression be-
tween two conditions of the dataset. The analysis was conducted with the gseGO function
in R and plotting the top 10 enriched Gene Ontology biological pathways (down and up
regulated) and the gene set size.
5.2.8
Motif Enrichment Quantiﬁcation
The following pipeline was created to check if for an motif enrichment around the
m6A in the sac-seq data [31]. Sac-seq data was loaded in the R environment, as well as
the Arabidopsis thaliana reference genome (BSgenome.Athaliana.TAIR.TAIR9 package in
R) and the previously given background data (BGA) for methylated adenosines in the
genome. The background data consisted in the coordinates of all the adenosines in the
genome, but some base-calling errors happen when creating big datasets like sac-seq, so a
strict ﬁltering to only have adenosines in the 3’UTR was conducted. All datasets needed
to be deﬁned as GenomicRanges before proceeding.
Motif windows were generated around these sites (considering the methylated adeno-
sine as the anchor point) using shifting (shift) and resizing (resize) procedures to align
(getSeq) with the expected positions of various motifs. It was very important to consider
the shifting and the resizing in both positive and negative strands. Once the motif size
was set in the ﬁltered sac-seq data, the last step was to check which of the motifs that
were found did match with the motif we wanted to calculate the enrichment for.
5.2.9
Visualization of motif enrichment around m6A
plot_ol function was designed to calculate the density of motifs overlaps near methy-
lated adenosines within a speciﬁc nucleotide window and plotting a density graph for
easier visual interpretation.
The function computed the overlap hits between the genomic regions speciﬁed and
a target motif dataset, in this case, sac-seq in the 3’UTR. A sliding window of 100 nu-
cleotides was set for each one of the regions. The function shifted the genomic regions
incrementally, counted the number of overlaps with the motif, and returned a vector
representing the overlap density across the current window. A normalization was also
performed and the counts were scaled to 1000 sites.

==PAGE:21==
5.2
Bioinformatics methods
Once the overlap density was calculated, a line plot was visualized for a better com-
parative visualization between the foreground or target gene sets, and the corresponding
backgrounds. The line plot showed the density of the previously calculated overlaps with
the m6A site or random adenosine (when working with the backgrounds) in the center of
the plot.
5.2.10
Logistic Regression Model for Model Enrichment Testing
A statistical signiﬁcance test of the motif enrichment around the m6A sites was neces-
sary to conﬁrm whether the observed enrichment in the visual plot was indeed signiﬁcant.
The analysis focused on a set speciﬁc window, in this case, -35 to -5 nucleotides from
the methylated adenosine position. All GenomicRanges objects were adjusted to fall in
those coordinates: target sets and their corresponding backgrounds. A binary counting
method was applied adding 1 if there was a hit for the motif on the speciﬁc coordinate,
or a 0 if the motif was absent. This process was performed across the sac-seq 3’UTR
data from seedlings, generating binary vectors for each condition (m6A sites vs. random
adenosines) and gene set (ECT2/3 with and without PUM targets).
The ﬁnal step was to perform a logistic regression model using the R function glm(y
∼condition * set, family = "binomial or poisson"): y corresponding to binari hits, set to
ECT2/3 with PUM and ECT2/3 without PUM, and condition to m6A sites or random
adenosines.
The logistic regression model evaluates whether m6A methylation and PUM targeting
have interactive/mutual-dependent eﬀects on deﬁning the abundance/density/count/en-
richment of the motifs of interest.

6.4
URUAY/UGUA-like Motif Enrichment Analysis around m6A Modiﬁcations
6.4
URUAY/UGUA-like Motif Enrichment Analysis around m6A
Modiﬁcations
For this analysis, the goal was to see the enrichment of URUAY motifs and its variants
around the m6A sites within the 3’UTR of Arabidopsis thaliana using the sac-seq data
[31]. 3’UTR was selected to examine whether the statement from Wei et al. explained in
the introduction 3.4.5 saying that the URUAY is a plant speciﬁc motif enriched in m6A
sites, was true.
The Motif Enrichment Calculation (5.2.8) was performed for the motifs: RAC, DRACH,
GAU, GGAU, URUA, URUAY, URUGUAY and UGUAWY, and their corresponding
background sets. Backgrounds set the threshold of how many motifs are possible ﬁnd
matching random adenosines of the genome. This allows us to see whether the motifs are
truly enriched at m6A or if they are also high in the background.
RAC and DRACH are eukaryotic m6A motifs, and GAU and GGAU are considered
plant speciﬁc motifs (Subsection 3.2.1). While wanted to check the already conﬁrmed m6A
enriched motifs, URUAY and URUA-like motifs (URUA, URUGUAY and UGUAWY [32])
were also explored due to their potential relevance.
After calculating the enrichments and plotting the data in a bar plot (Figure 17), a
clear enrichment from the RAC, DRACH, GGAU and GAU motifs were observed at the
m6A sites. Background sets (BGA) for these motifs showed a very signiﬁcant drop of
occurance compared to the m6A sites, conﬁrming that the motif enrichments were bio-
logically signiﬁcant and not random.
In contrast, for the URUAY and URUA-like motifs, the enrichment around the m6A
sites compared to the backgrounds was signiﬁcantly lower. These results suggest that even
though URUAY is present at 2.38% around the m6A sites, its presence in the genome is
higher at 4.13%. Therefore, URUAY is not an m6A motif but rather the opposite, as its
occurrence at m6A sites is lower than the genomic average.6

the observed enrichment, not by directly binding to the ECT2/3 targets but potentially
through interactions occurring elsewhere within the 3’UTR.

7
Conclusions
This study investigated the role of ribosomal protein (RP) and oxidative phosphory-
lation (OXPHOS) genes as potential targets of ECT2/3/4 and how they contribute to to
phenotypic rescue in te234 mutants. The results show that these genes are signiﬁcantly
downregulated in te234, suggesting that their misregulation may contribute to the ob-
served growth defects. The overexpression of PAB4 in te234 cell lines led to a partial
rescue of the phenotype, supporting the idea that ribosomal function plays a key role in
m6A-mediated gene regulation.
Regarding motif enrichment, the analysis conﬁrmed that URUAY is not enriched
around m6A sites, contradicting previous claims that it is a plant-speciﬁc m6A motif.
Furthermore, the enrichment of URUAY and URUA-like motifs does not appear to be
directly driven by PUM proteins around m6A sites. This suggests that PUM proteins
may inﬂuence motif enrichment through indirect mechanisms rather than direct interac-
tion with m6A sites.
Overall, this study improves our understanding of m6A-dependent gene regulation
in Arabidopsis thaliana and remarks the importance of ribosomal proteins and oxida-
tive phosporylation genes function in plant development. Future work should investi-
gate whether PUM proteins inﬂuence motif enrichment indirectly, and investigate how
ribosomal proteins (te234-uL11B-G141E and te234-uL11B-P149L) and plant growth are
connected.
