Summary
After the ﬁnancial crisis of 2008, counterparty credit risk (CCR) has been a key
element of the ﬁnancial regulations. For CCR, a quantiﬁcation of the credit
exposure was developed under the name of Internal Approach. This is done by
generating future states of scenarios and revaluing all the trades in the future. In
order to generate the future states of scenarios, it is necessary to assume that a
market variable comes from a certain model as well as to assume that the trades
can be priced with a robust pricing model. The latter assumption has been
researched extensively in recent decades by the quantitative ﬁnance community
and there is not much to test out. However, the former is the most important
driver in a credit exposure simulation and it is not as well researched and tested
as the latter. Therefore, in this project three diﬀerent continuous time stochastic
models were compared; Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM), Heston Stochastic
Volatility Model and a Geometric Brownian Motion with Jumps (GBMJ). The
models have been used as future states scenario generators for equity prices
and speciﬁcally the simulation, calibration and backtesting of the 3 models was
investigated. The models were calibrated by using the historical equity prices.
For the calibration of the GBM model, an analytic expression was derived as the
likelihood function was possible to obtain. For the Heston model, 2 diﬀerent
calibration methods were investigated. One was used to extract the realised
volatilities by using Extended Kalman Filter and applying a normal maximum
likelihood to the tracked volatility process.
The other method was used to
create a joint likelihood approximation as the exact likelihood expression is not
possible to obtain for the Heston Model. Lastly a jump diﬀusion process named
GBMJ with uniformly distributed jump sizes was explored and its calibration
was done by detecting the extreme returns beyond a certain number of standard
deviation.
To assess the performance of the models a backtesting was performed on 20
equities for 1 month, 2 months, 6 months and 1 year horizons. The equity data
included 20 diﬀerent single names and indices from US, Denmark, Japan and
Germany. Based on the backtesting results, the quality of the calibration results
and the computational complexity of the calibration methods, it was concluded
that the GBM and GBMJ models with the given calibration methodology were
ﬁt-for-purpose.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Banks play a crucial role in the creation of new capital in a country. In case
a bank defaults depending on its size, not only it aﬀects the other ﬁnancial
institutions in the same country but may also threaten the ﬁnancial institutions
of the whole world.
In 2008 some ﬁnancial institutions failed including the
extremely high proﬁle bankruptcy of the investment bank Lehman Brothers.
Even more ﬁnancial institutions would have failed were it not for government
bailouts. One important risk area that may trigger these defaults and needs
special attention is that of counterparty credit risk (CCR), often known simply
as counterparty risk.
Counterparty risk arises from the credit risk in securities ﬁnancing transactions
such as repos and the vast and often complex OTC (over-the-counter) derivatives
market. During the 2008 crisis Lehman Brothers had a notional amount of $800
billion of OTC derivatives at the point of bankruptcy. The complicated and
bilateral nature of derivatives means that the ﬁnancial instability of a large
institution can easily cause major shockwaves and aﬀect the connected entire
ﬁnancial system. Therefore, it is important for a bank to measure and manage
its default risk regarding to these complex products (Canabarro [2009]).
In order to avoid unexpected losses a certain amount of capital is put as a
buﬀer. Regarding both the credit and the counterparty risk, in 2006 the Basel
Committee for Banking Supervision has inserted in Basel II reform the Stan-
dardized approach and the Internal models method. Both these approaches are
for capital buﬀer purposes in order to avoid a potential unexpected loss. In the
standardized approach, the banks apply standard risk weights to their assets.
It is based on the use of ratings agencies such as SP, Moody’s and Fitch. On
the other hand, the internal models allow banks to estimate the risk themselves.
They are tailored to individual banks and therefore allow risks to be measured
more accurately based on the evaluation of the certain risk measures such as
Exposure At Default (EAD) for an OTC derivative portfolio. This results to a
better risk management, comprehensive assessment of risks and more eﬃcient
use of capital. For these reasons, more and more banks decide to implement
internal models.
The steps to derive the EAD are speciﬁed in ECB’s regulations, European Cen-
tral Bank [2013], Article 283(1). In order to derive the EAD, future states of the
underlying variables are simulated and the future portfolio values are obtained

1.1
Credit Exposure Modelling
In this section we illustrate the concept of credit exposure modelling. One use
case is utilising them in internal approach of a regulated ﬁnancial institution
in CCR area. The main goal for this approach is to get a realistic exposure
proﬁle for each counterparty that the bank conducts business with so that the
institution can put aside a certain amount of capital accordingly. As explained
in Zhu and Pykhtin [2007], this is done by simulating the inherently random
market variables and using their future market scenarios in order to price the
assets that are in its portfolio. Later, all the valuated derivative products are
aggregated with a certain hierarchy of rules. The steps are as follows,
1. Scenario Generation. Future market scenarios are simulated for a ﬁxed set
of simulation dates using evolution models of the risk factors(Underlying
market variables of the derivative products).
2. Instrument Valuation. For each simulation date and for each realization
of the underlying market risk factors, instrument valuation is performed
for each trade in the counterparty portfolio.
3. Portfolio Aggregation. For each simulation date and for each realization of
the underlying market risk factors, counterparty-level exposure is obtained
by applying necessary netting rules
The scenarios that are generated in step 1, are usually speciﬁed via stochastic
diﬀerential equations (SDE). The scope of this thesis is to assess several of the
industry practice SDEs. Later, the chosen model’s parameters are estimated and
the equity prices are simulated so they can be used as the future scenarios. The
main focus of this project is not elaborating on the credit exposure modelling
but to focus on the scenario simulation methods. Note that the simulated prices

Chapter 2
Related Work
The most common model for the CCR simulation of the stock prices is Geometric
Brownian Motion (Ruiz [2012] and Brigo et al. [2008]). However, Canabarro
[2009] argues that when simulating the evolution of a risk factor the correlation
to other risk factors should also be considered as there can be infrastructure
obstacles while simulating the equity prices since in a bank’s portfolio there
might be thousands of diﬀerent equity derivatives from all around the world. Not
all of these equities are moving independently. It is possible for a stock in certain
country to be correlated with other equities in the same sector/region/rating.
In this case a multi factor model which has less risk drivers than the number of
equities in that country might be useful. This is because the correlation matrix
may easily become too big if one would simulate all the equities separately with a
correlation to the other equities, FX rates or interest rates. Therefore, one of the
models that can be considered next to GBM is multifactor GBM. Another way
to decrease the dimension of the simulations is to simulate the relevant indices
and using the CAPM to obtain the future state of the equities. The purpose of
the multifactor GBM and the CAPM approach is to reduce the dimension of the
correlation matrix. However, they might cause a poor back testing performance
since we will be working with a lower number of risk drivers than the number
of equities.
On the other hand, Ricardo Pachon [2012] emphasizes the importance of the
stochastic volatility models because i) in the volatility sensitive portfolios such
as variance swaps and options, the netting beneﬁt can not be fully enjoyed with a
constant volatility model, ii) constant volatility models do not provide a realistic
distribution as the emprical studies show that the spot stock prices have fat tails
and skewness.
Therefore, they relax the constant volatility assumption and
propose a stochastic mean reversion process for volatilities as known as Black
Karasinki process. They show the skewness and kurtosis of S&P500 is better
captured with a stochastic volatility model rather than a GBM. The problem
of the fat tails is also investigated by Brigo et al. [2008] and their solution is to
relax the constant volatility assumption with GARCH(1,1) model, introduced
by Bollerslev. Furthermore, they consider including stochastic jumps by adding
a time homogeneous Poisson process into the dynamic equation of the GBM.
Regarding the backtesting methodology, because of the lack of a strict guidelines
for the backtesting of the CCR models, there are more than one way of assess-
ing the performance of the models. Ruiz [2012] proposes a framework with the
red-green-yellow traﬃc light status similar to the one in market risk VaR back-
testing. Their methodology is based on ﬁnding the p-values of the realisations

Chapter 3
Methodology
Some dynamical systems do not follow a deterministic process. Stochastic mod-
els may represent their behaviour more appropriately. Under many disciplines
the diﬀusion models are utilised extensively like physics, biology or ﬁnance. Ex-
amples of such processes can be the motion of the pollen particles as being
moved by individual water molecules(Einstein (1905)), diﬀusion of pollutants in
the air, coal dust particles on the surface of alcohol or the stock prices dynamics.
Even though there are some rebuttals against to the stock prices may or may
not be modelled by a stochastic model, there seems to have been an agreement
on the usage of SDEs in derivative pricing, risk management etc. Therefore,
in this thesis only the continuous time models that are already proven to be
helpful at describing the stock price dynamics are selected and compared. Most
stochastic models used in practice are driven by Wiener processes and have the
following structure,
dS(t) = f(t, S(t), Θ)dt + σ(t, S(t), Θ)dW(t)
(3.1)
The selection of the drift, f(t, S(t), Θ) and diﬀusion, σ(t, S(t), Θ) is the part
where a model is diﬀerent than another one. This selection is important but the
parameters calibration of such process is also as important.
3.1
Real world or risk-neutral?
In mathematical ﬁnance, the models to describe the movement of an underlying
process is widely used for the derivative pricing and hedging. However, in this
work they are meant to be used for counterparty risk simulation in real world
measure which means that the intention is to forecast the future distributions
based on the historical prices of the equities rather than prices of the traded
derivatives. It is theoretically possible and justiﬁable to calibrate the models by
using the historical equity prices and the derivative prices. Using the derivative
prices to calibrate a model means that we are implying the model parameters
from the derivative prices.
There are pros and cons of each approach.
The
reactivity to the changes in the market is better captured by the market implied
but would lead to a more volatile and frequently updated parameter estimations
which would lead to a volatile regulatory capital requirement for an institution.
The risk neutral versus the real probability discussion is elaborated in Canabarro
[2009] section 4.3.2. The author is in favor of using the real world probabilities
when stating “For a risk management application, such as exposure generation,
==PAGE:14==

one does not need to use the risk-neutral measure and should be focused rather
on the real measure, estimated using historical data and common sense”. At the
same time they suggest to use the market implied paradigm for VaR calculations
but not for the CCR exposure simulations. The main argument boils down to
the long horizon vs short horizons of CCR (usually more than 10 years) and
Market Risk (2 weeks). For example it is common that the forward FX rates
and spot FX rates diverge far from each other. This can lead to a big diﬀerence
at the end of the exposure proﬁle and can cause a signiﬁcant underestimation
or overestimation of the exposure for the long horizons. Therefore, the author
argues that scenario generation generally should focus on the real parameters
whereas pricing should focus on market implied.

3.2.3
Arbitrage free pricing with GBM
This section explains how the GBM process is used in order to price a ﬁnancial
derivative.
The ﬁrst part is not only restricted to options.
It is a general
formula that is valid for all the derivatives as in principle the contract function
can be replaced with anything. The main logic of the derivation of the arbitrage
free pricing framework is based on the assumption that a ﬁnancial derivative is
expected to have the same return with the short term interest rate. Otherwise,
there would be arbitrage opportunity and it would disappear soon. As explained
in Rouah [2013], there are multiple ways of deriving the Black Scholes PDE.
Some of them are as following,
• By hedging argument.
• By replicating portfolio.
• By Capital Asset Pricing Model
• By deriving as a limit of the binomial model

3.5
Backtesting
In principle, the testing of an exposure model needs to be done for all three steps
explained in section 1.1. Risk factor models, pricing models and the aggregation
rules can be tested holistically (Ruiz [2012]). The plot at the bottom in ﬁgure
1.1 emphasizes the materiality of the risk factor models by using two diﬀerent
parameter for GBM and keeping everything else the same. Because of the fact
that the pricing models are not as material as the risk factor models and the
aggregation rule testing is relatively straight forward, in this project as well as
in others the attention is concentrated on the risk factor model testing only.
The performance of a risk factor model is assessed by comparing the histori-
cal observations and the forecasted distribution of the risk factor given by the
model we are testing. In other words, two distributions are compared within a
time interval. Forecasted distribution will be updated periodically after every
calibration. If we leave the periodic calibration out of this issue then the prob-
lem boils down to comparing two distributions and checking if they come from
the same probability distribution or not. In the area of goodness of ﬁt usually
a sample is compared to a theoretical distribution. There are multiple ways
of handling this issue such as histograms, empirical cumulative density func-
tions, percentile-percentile plots, quantile-quantile plots (Wilk and Gnanade-
sikan [1968]). However, this time the question is slightly diﬀerent as we want to
test if the two data sample come from the same probability distribution or not
without knowing which theoretical distribution they come from. One sample
data is the realised stock prices and the other one is the forecasted prices. The
main tool that is used in this work is percentile-percentile plots.
3.5.1
PP-plots
In order to understand the backtesting methodology used in this project, one
needs to understand how and why to use the QQ/PP plots because the main
logic is the same with a QQ/PP-plot. Both of the questions are "how closely two
data sets agree?" by plotting the quantiles or more speciﬁcally in this case the
percentiles of two distributions against each other. QQ/PP plot take a sample
data, sort it in ascending order and then plot them versus quantiles/percentiles
from another distribution. If the distributions being compared are exactly the
same, then the points in the QQ/PP-plot will be in the x = y line because nth
percentile will also correspond to nth percentile of our second distribution. The
perfect line can be seen in ﬁgure 3.3. This perfect line is not possible to obtain
unless we have inﬁnite amount of data or the two data sets are exactly the same.
Therefore, instead we get a line similar to the one visualised in ﬁgure 3.4 even
when we sample the data from the same model. Note the diﬀerence between the
perfect line and the imperfect line between the two pp-plots. This variability

Our backtesting framework is a modiﬁed version of QQ/PP-plot for our unique
problem because (1) the predicted distribution moments are changing over time,
(2) forecasted distribution is conditional on the observed price. In other words,
in QQ/PP-plot the theoretical distribution’s moments do not change but this is
not the case for our problem because we estimate the parameters of our model
periodically to capture latest market dynamics.
Hands-on implementation speciﬁcally tailored for CCR modelling is proposed
in Ruiz [2012]. We choose a backtesting window which starts at tstart = 2016
and ends tend = 2021 for a backtesting time horizon of ∆(e.g. ∆= 1 month).
The steps are as follows,
1. The ﬁrst time point of measurement is t1 = tstart. We calculate the model
risk factor distribution at a point t1 + ∆subject to the realization of xt1.
We then take the realized value xt1 + ∆of the time series at t1 + ∆and
observe where that value falls in the risk factor cumulative distribution
calculated previously. This yields a value F1. In this case F1 is merely
the percentile of our observed equity price in our predicted probability
distribution by the model.

The data shown in ﬁgure 4.1 are used to generate the results of this work. For

As a

ticker BAX and ISIN code of US0718131099 has gone through its 4th split on

The company does not actually lose value because of this stock split
action. Another example of a corporate action is the dividend payment. In
this case the log return for the dividend payment day is simply replaced with
log((Sk + D)/Sk−1) where D is the dividend amount.

and Denmark in their respective currencies without converting to a base cur-
rency. This is because FX rates are usually simulated as another risk factor to
the base currency. If the currency is desired to be converted to a base currency,
then this conversion is done with the simulated FX rates at a future point of
the time grid.
In this work all simulations and calibrations are done in the
underlying asset’s own currency and their historical prices are not converted.
Apart from the corporate actions, it can be seen that the equity prices are
sometimes subject to sudden shocks. The most apparent example of this is the
COVID-19 medical crises. The sudden drop in March 2020 is a good reason
to consider other models than the Geometric Brownian Process. In ﬁgure 4.3
weekly and monthly returns of S&P 500 is shown. It is common to observe
returns that are greater than 2-3 standard deviations such as 2008 and 2020-
Covid19 crises both of which caused the similar shock sizes. Another observation
that can be done by looking at the S&P 500 is that the returns do not look so
stationary. Even though the average return seems to be constant, the variance
sometimes increases and sometimes decreases. Therefore, this is a good visual
reason for considering a model like Heston stochastic volatility model.

Chapter 6
Discussion
Throughout the thesis, some decisions were made such as the selection of the
calibration methods (market-implied vs historical), the selection of the hyper
parameters in calibration and backtesting (data frequency, data length, back-
testing horizon, simulation starting frequencies etc), selection of the models to
be implemented and selection of the suitable calibration methodologies. These
decisions were based on the existing research, and the experiment results of
this project. The calibration methods were chosen as historical due to the long
horizons in CCR exposure proﬁles. Besides, it is signiﬁcantly harder to obtain
a market data of an equity derivative compared to the equity’s directly ob-
served price. Another reason is that the market-implied parameters tend to be
more volatile than the historically calibrated parameters which leads to a more
volatile capital requirement for an institution.
The calibration methodologies were chosen by paying attention to the execution
time and accuracy. Ideally, one would be able to derive the functional form of
the likelihood but it is rare that this is explicitly known for the models. There-
fore, approximations and simpliﬁcations from the literature were utilized. It is
not a big issue for the derivation of the GBM’s likelihood function as the closed
form solution of GBM can be found easily. This was not the case for the He-
ston model. Therefore, 2 diﬀerent approximation/simpliﬁcation methods were
investigated and implemented. The chosen method was the joint approximated
likelihood function of Atiya and Wall [2009] as the calibration results were bet-
ter than the Kalman Filter solution. Another method could be to incorporate
the Monte Carlo based estimation methods. In Lindström [2012], the Monte
Carlo Expectation Maximization method is used. By looking at ﬁgure 2 in the
article, the accuracy of the Monte Carlo Optimization Maximization method is
performing relatively well. However, the execution time is too extensive and in-
feasible for a project like this as the backtesting requires 20 calibrations for each
equity and each backtesting horizon all of which are done by using a Monte Carlo
simulation. Therefore, the chosen methods explained in the relevant calibration
section were the ones that have a reasonable time complexity.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and future work
The scope of this project was to: (a) explain how a credit exposure simulation
model works which is an example use case of the simulation models investigated
in this work, (b) to investigate the candidate underlying simulation models, (c)
to implement and simulate a subset of the chosen models, (d) to estimate the pa-
rameters of the chosen models and (e) to implement a backtesting methodology
in order to quantify the performance of the models.
In the Introduction, we focused on the steps to simulate a credit exposure and
explaining the motivation behind this work by showing a use case of the risk
factor simulation. This was done with a hypothetical portfolio with 3 diﬀerent
option positions. The section was ﬁnalised with a materiality test for the simu-
lated portfolio in order to emphasize the importance of the risk factor simulation
models. In the test we observed that the capital requirement for the portfolio
increased twice only by changing a parameter slightly. This was the proof why
the work load should be allocated primarily to the risk factor model testing
rather than the pricing model testing. In Related Work we highlighted the ex-
isting research that was used as sources of inspiration for choosing the relevant
models for the project. In the Data chapter we presented the input data and
some insights and take aways from the data. In the Methodology we elaborated
the chosen three stochastic continuous time models for the simulation of the
equity prices. All of their calibration and simulation methods were explained in
detail or by giving reference to the detailed article. Additionally the use case of
the models for pricing purposes was also presented by deriving an arbitrage free
pricer with the given underlying models. In Experiments & Results the chosen
simulation models were calibrated and backtested. The number of green lights
from the GREEN/AMBER/RED traﬃc light results were highest for the GBM
and the GBM with jump diﬀusion models. Not only their backtesting results
were ﬁne but also their calibration methods were computationally less expensive
and easier to understand than the Heston Model. In the Heston Model there
were 2 diﬀerent equations that needed to be ﬁtted and one of them is inher-
ently missing data to ﬁt. Therefore, this adds another layer of complexity to
our problem. One potential solution was to apply the Kalman Filter method
to trace the unobserved state variable, volatility. However, this did not yield
an accurate result. Therefore, the approximate joint likelihood process was in-
vestigated, which gave us more accurate parameter estimates. Lastly, a jump
diﬀusion model was proposed as we have observed some extreme jumps through-
out the last 10 years as can be seen from the Data section. The GBMJ model
also performed as well as the GBM model and its calibration was also found
==PAGE:49==
43
to be stable, understandable and computationally less expensive compared to
the Heston Model. It is concluded that the GBM and GBMJ models are ﬁt for
purpose in the CCR modelling context.
For future work within CCR modelling, the stochastic volatility jump diﬀusion
model (SVJD) can be investigated. SVJD will be even more challenging to cal-
ibrate as the number of parameters are more than both the GBMJ and Heston
SV models, Craine et al. [2000]. Therefore, the calibration technique of Lind-
ström [2012] can be utilised for this problem as they are already supposedly
giving a good accuracy in their research.
The Heston Model is widely known as a promising model but it may have per-
formed poorly in this project because of the insuﬃcient calibration methodology.
Therefore, an investigation could be performed on the inaccurate parameter es-
timation of the Heston Model and the same backtesting methodology can be
performed with a more accurate calibration methodology.
