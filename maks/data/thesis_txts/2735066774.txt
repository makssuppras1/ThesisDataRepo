Abstract
In this thesis, the utilization of a novel fault­tolerant control architecture with a dynamic in­
put reconstruction­based super­twisting sliding mode controller (STSMC) is investigated
for a conventional wind turbine (WT) hydraulic pitch system (HPS). Detailed pitch system
dynamics are included in the design process which provides the opportunity for systematic
design, as well as, for testing fault­tolerant capabilities of the proposed architecture in the
presence of a wide range of fault scenarios. The content of the thesis offers a general de­
scription of WTs and a comprehensive picture of the investigated hydraulic pitch system.
This is followed by the design of a conventional PID controller variant, a PPI cascade con­
troller, used as a baseline controller for comparison purposes. In Chapter 3, the design
concept of the proposed control architecture and its related considerations used in the de­
sign process are explained. Chapter 4 details the design process of a dynamic mapping­
inversion­based fault­tolerant super­twisting sliding mode controller (MI­STSMC). Later,
the performance of the proposed controller in the presence of various fault scenarios with
metric evaluation in comparison with the baseline controller is presented. In Chapter 6,
a summary of the findings and insights gained from this study are outlined. Last, but not
least, the final chapter concludes the potential future research directions of the project
introduced in the thesis.

Introduction
The pace of technological advancement in human society is unmatched in history. Al­
though artificial intelligence is at the forefront of scientific hot topics and there are newer,
more novel, more sophisticated solutions developed from day to day, several intricate is­
sues are yet unsolved where human expertise seems to be the only feasible path to go.
Such a major field with complex problems that call for urgent solutions are the sustainabil­
ity goals. Since the year 2030, when many of the sustainability milestones defined by the
World Climate Action Summit and the European Union are hoped to be attained, there
has been a huge demand for innovative, sustainable, and effective solutions regarding
energy production.
To date, as a consequence of efforts to follow the sustainability goals of the energy sector,
the power produced by renewable energy sources has increased tremendously in the
last 20 years. According to Our World in Data’s [1], by the end of the year 2022, wind
energy became the second largest renewable energy generating power source. In 2023,
GWEC’s (Global Wind Energy Council) Global Wind Report 2023 [2] stated the installed
global capacity to be 906GW worldwide which represents a 9% Year­on­Year growth.
However, to exploit most of the installed capacity several aspects should be taken into
consideration when wind turbines (WT) are designed. Therefore, ensuring proper control
action for a WT is a major field of interest both in the industry and in the research domain.
1.1
Motivation
Given the complexity of WTs, the control architectures implemented in them are corre­
spondingly varied. However, as a result of the latest developmental trends and the fact
that a significant portion of the already installed WTs have been reaching the end of their
service time, modern challenges are emerging in certain subsystems of turbines. One
with key importance is the pitching system of which the main objective is to ensure the
required pitch angle for the blades to maximize power generation.
A major challenge arises from the inherent attribute of current trends. Conventional WTs
are built with a horizontal rotor to which three rotating blades are attached, hence, there
are different control approaches for pitching: individual pitch control (IPC), collective pitch
control (CPC), and hybrid IPC & CPC. Even though each method has its advantages and
disadvantages, as a result of the latest developments, IPC seemed to become more at­
tractive due to its fatigue mitigation attributes. However, the utilization of IPC architectures
imposes additional points of failure in the system. First of all, an IPC structure consists
of more components than their CPC counterparts. With the increase of the number of
components, it is inevitable to multiply the possibility of pitch actuator faults.
Another is the consequence of aging. It is by nature a characteristic for any mechanical
system to encounter faults by their service time. Since, the most common pitch systems
currently used in the industry are hydraulic pitch systems (HPS), besides mechanical
aging of actuators, other faults regarding the pipes or their internal liquids can contribute
to unsatisfactory operation, loss of power, or even shutdown.
Although these challenges are clear, previously developers had less focus on the insur­
ance of proper pitch control action under faulty operation. The obsolete objective of con­
troller design was to ensure the highest accuracy possible while minimizing cost and op­
timizing complexity. But mid­term operational experiences revealed that operation and

maintenance costs can surge up to 10­50% of the total energy generation cost of WTs, [3]
which can be an indicator of the importance of utilizing controllers that are not only capa­

1.2
Research review
To define the novelty and the relevance of the proposed project regarding future WT con­
trol designs, a non­exhaustive but thorough research has been conducted. During this
research, the objective was to gain comprehensive knowledge about currently developed
fault­tolerant control (FTC) schemes for hydraulic actuators and specifically for wind tur­
bine pitch systems.
1.2.1
Data­based wind turbine pitch control
Mousavi et al. in [4] proposes a fractional order PID solution with a firefly algorithm for
controller gain optimization, which was tested on three types of HPS fault scenarios. An
adaptive FTC approach is represented in [5], where the faults and uncertainties are coun­
teracted by a neural network.
1.2.2
Wind turbine pitch control with advanced techniques
An early work from 2013 in [6] shows a linear FTC approach for a WT with IPC when pitch
position faults occur. One of the most cited publications is from Lan et al. in [7], where the
authors investigated a conventional PID controller with a sliding mode (SM) step­by­step
observer for pressure actuator faults. Liu et al. in [8] proposed a fault­tolerant (FT) IPC
scheme for load mitigation purposes based on a subspace predictive repetitive control
(SPRC) approach which was tested on two actuator fault scenarios. In their other work in
[9] the robustness of a Bayesian optimisation­based pitch controller with PI control was
tested with similar faults. The authors in [10] introduce a specific model­free adaptive
control strategy that is tested with three actuator fault scenarios. Habibi et al. in [11]
proposes a passive FTC solution with a modified nonlinear adaptive constrained control
approach. Another active FTC control strategy is presented in [12], where a continuous
adaptive time delay control is designed based on estimated uncertainties. An adaptive
observer­based method with a state feedback FTC is proposed in [13].
1.2.3
Wind turbine pitch control with sliding mode control
A switched linear system adaptive sliding mode control (SMC) for floating WT systems
is tested for actuator efficiency and additive faults. Noshirvani et al. in [14] propose
an active FT pitch control design for 2.5MW WT with a fractional­order terminal SMC.
The design demonstrated favorable results, although only hydraulic actuator faults were
investigated. In [15] and [16] Mazare et al. introduce two similarly complex active FTC
approaches to handle five different HPS faults. In the former, an adaptive fractional­based
terminal back­stepping super twisting (ST) SMC, while in the latter, a nonsingular fast
integral type terminal sliding mode controller is designed. Both are supported by time
delay estimation­based methods. Likewise, an STSMC is utilized in the presence of pitch
actuator faults in [17]. Another work in [18] addresses HPS actuator faults by the design of
an adaptive SMC. The authors of [19] tested on an experimental 5kW WT prototype their
SMC for efficiency enhancement under actuator faults utilizing a time­varying exponential
reaching law. An integral sliding mode observer (SMO) based integral SMC in [20] is
applied to detect and ensure the proper control of a WT pitch system under healthy and

two faulty scenarios. A highly related paper to this thesis is presented in [21], in which an
exponential rate reaching law­based SMC is designed for a similar HPS with leakage and
actuator­type faults.
1.2.4
Similar hydraulic system controls
Since the challenges of HPS control may resemble those of similar systems, it is reason­
able to investigate whether kindred techniques have been implemented in other industrial
fields. Maddahi et al. in [22] proposes a practical approach to design an FTC for hy­
draulic actuators using a fractional­order PID controller suitable for internal leakage faults.
For a hydraulic manipulator, Dao et al. present an active FTC scheme with a full­state
backstepping controller for internal leakage faults in [23]. A multi­model­based adaptive
control is proposed in [24] for an electro­hydraulic position servo system. In [25] & [26]
the authors proposed two observer­based FTC architectures for similar electro­hydraulic
systems. The first includes dynamic surface control, while the latter presents a command­
filter backstepping control solution. Sun et al. in [27] worked with an active suspension
system where they designed a second­order SM­based super­twisting controller. A ter­
minal SMC­based solution is proposed in [28] for an electro­hydraulic system with internal
leakages and bias faults. For a redundant aviation hydraulic actuation system, an adap­
tive continuous non­singular fast terminal sliding mode FTC is developed in [29] to tackle
faults caused by servo­valve leakage.
1.2.5
Research conclusions
Based on the author’s comprehensive review, numerous papers have tackled the issue of
FTC of wind turbine HPS. However, the methodological approach presented in this thesis
appears to be distinct from the approaches found in the surveyed literature. The most
common differences from the projects introduced in this thesis are as follows:
• The HPS is frequently modeled as a second­order system. This approach excludes
the possibility of identifying and analyzing the direct effect of faults on the system.
• The majority of the literature focuses on a few specific fault cases and does not pro­
vide a comprehensive solution for a wider range of fault scenarios.
• While it is common practice in many instances, not every paper conducts tests of
their results using a high­fidelity simulator.
1.3
Hypothesis
Following the above discussion and research findings, the standard industrial control ap­
proach of wind turbines gravitates towards the utilization of PID controllers. While PID
controllers offer a certain degree of robustness, their effectiveness hinges on information
on the error profile, which renders them susceptible to performance degradation in the
presence of faults and system uncertainties.
HPS is of crucial importance regarding the proper operation of a WT, hence neglecting
its internal dynamics and considering it a black box with second­order dynamics does not
provide the possibility to systematically counteract its internal faults. Including a detailed
description of the HPS in the control architecture, can potentially increase the reliability
and safety of the turbines. However, the incorporation of the nonlinear and discontinuous
dynamics of the HPS may introduce challenges, particularly regarding the accessibility of
the input for mathematical derivation.

In light of these considerations, a robust nonlinear FTC solution is desirable which requires
achievable knowledge about error and uncertainties, in addition to an effective and reliable
input reconstruction method that possesses the capacity to estimate the required input for
the controller. Therefore, the hypothesis of the author integrates these two aspirations as
follows:
• It is possible to systematically design a super­twisting sliding mode controller (STSMC)
for the detailed dynamics of an HPS.
• It is possible to utilize a dynamic mapping­inversion (MI) algorithm to reconstruct
proper input for the designed STSMC.
• It is possible to achieve superior control performance in the presence of system
faults with the proposed MI­STSMC architecture to a conventional PID controller.

System description
To establish a foundational understanding regarding the topics of this thesis, the following
chapter is entitled to provide a general description of the structure and components of a
modern wind turbine, as well as about the investigated hydraulic pitch system topology
and its related faults considered.
2.1
Wind turbine architecture and components
Wind turbines are energy­converting machines that aim to exploit the tremendous kinetic
energy of the wind and transform it into electrical power. Despite the simplicity of the
objective, designing a properly functioning and efficient WT is a multi­domain engineer­
ing challenge where the synergy arising from the cooperation of structural, mechanical,
electrical, and control specialists is essential. Figure 2.1 presents the structure and main
components of a conventional three­bladed horizontal axis wind turbine.

The primary focus of the proposed thesis is located in the hub of the turbine, where the
pitch control unit and the pitch system are located.

First, it is necessary to understand how pitching action is executed and why the supporting
system is required for these actions. Figure 2.2 shows a cross­sectional schematic view
of a turbine blade. The root of the blade is affixed to the hub via a rotating element known
as bearing. This bearing is linked to a hydraulic cylinder, which facilitates the rotation of
the blade, thereby enabling the pitching process.
Coherently, from the name itself, the cylinder is attached to a hydraulic system that sup­
plies the required pressures for pitching. The reference HPS for the proposed thesis can
be seen on Figure 2.3. The system consists of two subsystems: the pump station and
the actuator system.
2.2.1
Pump station
The pump station, which serves as a pressure source for the actuator system, is located
in the nacelle of the turbine. It is composed of a fixed displacement pump powered by
a motor, an accumulator, and a pressure relief valve ensuring that the system pressure
does not exceed a certain limit. The pump charges the accumulator to the nominal sys­
tem pressure and its flow is circulated through a filter back to the tank. Consequently,
under nominal conditions, the accumulator fulfills the role of being the pressure source.
Nevertheless, the thesis delimits its scope to the actuator system, thus the supply circuit
is neglected further on and the supply pressure (ps) is considered to be constant. 1

3
Baseline controller design
While advanced fault­tolerant controllers are explored in literature, the industrial standard
leans towards PID controllers due to their several advantages. The foundation of PID
is more than a hundred years old, therefore their design process is well­developed and
several software solutions simplify their implementation to a high extent, including their
tuning. Consequently, no high­level understanding of non­linear controllers is required,
which simplifies the task for companies to locate individuals adept in working with PIDs.
Regarding hydraulic pitch systems, standard PID solutions usually rely on a single posi­
tion measurement, thereby they count as a relatively cheap solution for control purposes.
Although they are not strictly considered in the category of fault­tolerant controllers, they
possess a certain degree of robustness that provides moderate fault­tolerance. However,
it is important to note that to achieve proper robustness against faults, knowledge is re­
quired about the profile of the given disturbance which is a cumbersome task and data is
not always available for such scenarios.
In light of the above discussion, it is a reasonable decision to first implement a similar
controller for the HPS which will serve later on as a baseline controller for performance
comparisons.

Sliding mode control
The objective of the proposed thesis is to provide an FTC solution that can subdue or
at least maintain acceptable performance under faulty operation of the nonlinear HPS
described in section 2.2. Following these objectives, a robust nonlinear type controller
method called sliding mode control (SMC) is a reasonable choice, due to its theoretical
foundation which makes it an almost perfect candidate for systems with uncertainties and
perturbations.

4.1.3
Limitations and challenges
As it is reflected in the above discussions of subsection 4.1.1 and subsection 4.1.2, SMC is
a powerful tool mainly for two reasons: it guarantees finite­time convergence to the origin
and to achieve that, only knowledge about the bounds of the disturbance is required.
Although this particular attribute is overly desirable for the HPS, SMC has its limitations
as well.
Up til now, the time spent between two consecutive control actions was assumed to be
infinitesimally small. In real­life applications, when state measurements are digital sig­
nals and digital controllers are used, continuous control action, i.e. approximately zero
time between two control signals, can not be achieved. As a result, the controller can­
not react at the exact moment when the state diverges from the sliding surface, thus the
control action constantly overshoots the sliding surface back and forth (Figure 4.3). This
phenomenon stands for the main issue that encumbers the practical implementation of
SMCs, the chattering.2
Chattering can be mitigated by approximating the discontinuous control signal by arctan
function and therefore providing a smoother and continuous control signal, or by introduc­
ing higher­order SMC, as it is described in subsection 4.1.4. Moreover, these mitigation
strategies can be utilized simultaneously. It is important to mention, however, that while
arctan function provides a more favorable control signal, it is achieved at the expense of
loss in control performance.

2Chattering is a high­frequency oscillation that occurs due to the discontinuous control signal. This can
excite undesired high frequencies in the system and may cause wear and tear on physical components.

4.1.5
Advantages of STSMC
When contrasted with the traditional first­order SMC, the STSMC presents several ad­
vantages. As it has been discussed in subsection 4.1.3, the main issue of SMC is the
chattering phenomena caused by its discontinuous control action. With the integrator aug­
mentation, the control signal of the STSMC becomes continuous, which not only makes
the controller more suitable for practical implementations but at the same time reduces
chattering significantly. If one also decides to approximate the signum function in (4.10)
and (4.11) with arctan functions, chattering can be further mitigated at the expense of
reduced performance.
Furthermore, in contrast to other sophisticated non­linear controllers, such as adaptive
backstepping controllers, STSMC is capable of providing superior performance with only
two tunable parameters (k1 & k2). This tuning simplicity combined with its moderate chat­
tering attribute made STSMC the ideal controller for the proposed control problem of this
thesis.

5.4
Closed­loop stability
In the realm of control systems, the stability of the individual elements is often a primary
focus. However, it is equally important to consider the stability of the closed­loop sys­
tem as a whole. The interaction between the components can lead to dynamics that are
not apparent when examining the elements in isolation. These dynamics can potentially
destabilize the system, even if the individual elements are stable.
Given the vast scope of this project, comprehensive proof of the closed­loop stability was
beyond its purview. The focus was primarily on the design and implementation of the
control architecture, and the stability of the individual elements therein. However, this
does not diminish the importance of closed­loop stability in the overall performance and
reliability of the control system.
Drawing from the discussion of Loria in [40], it is indeed possible to establish the stabil­
ity of the closed­loop system, as it was demonstrated in [41]. While detailed proof is not
included in this thesis, the theoretical groundwork laid by Loria provides a promising direc­
tion for future work. It is hoped that subsequent research will build upon this foundation,
conducting a thorough investigation into the closed­loop stability of the proposed control
architecture.

Test conclusion
The first simulations of the proposed control solution in the nominal system revealed un­
expected results. Although, based on the discussion of subsection 6.1.3, a more accu­

input signal, the designed controller performed satisfactory reference tracking. Hence, a
large­scale simulation was conducted with a practical reference signal exported from a
high­fidelity simulation.
Since the controller was able to provide similar control performance for a realistic refer­
ence signal as in the case of the step change and sinusoidal tests, the potential of the
proposed architecture is reinforced, and the setup defined in this chapter is considered to
be suitable for further testing in the presence of various fault scenarios.

7.1
Fault scenario desriptions
Although Table 2.2 presents the list of the considered faults in the system, it is important
to see how the individual faults were implemented for the simulations.
7.1.1
Sensor faults
To properly control a system, it is inevitable to gather reliable measurements on the states
of the system. In the case of the HPS system, three sensor measurements are directly
fed into the controller: piston and rod side pressures (ypp,ypr) and the cylinder position
(yxc). Their importance is crucial from a controlling perspective, as Table 2.2 indicates
it with a high severity level. To test whether the proposed controller design is capable
of providing FTC when such critical elements of the architecture are faulty, a bias was
assumed separately on each of the state measurements based on Table 2.2.
The bias was implemented in the simulator as a change in the mean value of the sensor
noise. Its amplitude was defined concerning simulation data under nominal conditions
as a percentage of the maximum value of the given state. For example, in the case of
position, when the bias is assumed to be 10%, it means the following:
∆yxc = 1.1 · max(xc) = 0.1 · 1.35 = 0.135 m

as defined in Table 2.2. Larger friction can lead to loss of efficiency and in extreme cases
– e.g. when the cylinder gets stuck – to shutdown.

calculation of Ffr was modified as follows:

7.1.3
Change in bulk modulus
Generally, oil in an HPS is considered to be incompressible. However, as the system
experiences wear during operation, the oil in the system becomes contaminated, which
can mean increased air content in the oil and/or sludge formulation. In most cases, con­
tamination increases the compressibility of the given fluid, i.e. decreases its bulk modulus
β. For the investigated HPS, β of the oil is used in the system dynamics, which is a key
descriptor of elastic properties. Therefore, a change in its value is considered as a fault in
the system. β is a complex descriptor that depends on many factors, hence its variation
in the system is implemented as a generic change in magnitude concerning its nominal
value.
βfault = (1 −∆β)βnom
(7.3)
7.1.4
Check­valve fault
The check valve is a vital component of an HPS, ensuring unidirectional fluid flow, blocking
pressure spikes, and preventing oil line drainage, thus contributing to the overall efficiency
and safety of the system. When it gets stuck, the HPS can experience detrimental effects,
even shutdown. Due to its impact on the system, check valve­related faults are classified
with a high severity level.
Due to its unidirectional nature, the dynamics in (2.7) are modeled with a Heaviside func­
tion. However, when a stuck fault happens, the check valve either stays completely shut,
or some flow is present bidirectionally. Hence, fQcv is implemented in simulation as:
fQcv = −Kcv(pr −ps −pcv)H(pr −ps −pcv) + ∆Qcv
(7.4)
7.1.5
External leakage
During operation, each WT component experiences aging/wear. One of the most crucial
components where wear can cause high­severity faults is the hydraulic cylinder. The most
undesired scenario is when external leakage occurs in the system, i.e. the oil from the
cylinder chamber starts to leak outside. In case of external leakage, the cylinder pressure
dynamics are badly influenced which leads to loss of efficiency or even shutdown. To
test the system with realistic external leakage values, the work of Asmussen et al. in [45]
served as a basis for defining Qle,p and Qle,r 1. During simulation, the external leakage
fault enters the system dynamics as described in (2.2) and (2.3).
7.1.6
Internal leakage
In addition to external leakages in the hydraulic cylinder, it is also possible to experience
leakage between the chambers of the cylinder due to wear. This fault is referred to as
internal leakage since there is no oil loss towards the outside. Internal leakage has only
an impact on the damping of the pitching, therefore its severity level is significantly lower
than its external counterpart. Similarly to subsection 7.1.5 the values represented in [45]
were used as a basis to choose relevant Qli for fault simulation. The internal leakage,
similarly to external leakages, enters the system in the pressure dynamics described by
(2.2) and (2.3).

Simulation results under fault scenarios
To provide an extensive evaluation of the control performance achieved by the proposed
controller, each fault scenario was tested in comparison to the baseline PPI controller of
chapter 3 with five to six severity levels. The FTC performance of the compared controllers
is presented on Figure 7.1 ­ 7.9. Furthermore, a performance metric was calculated by
employing the Mean Squared Error (MSE) method to evaluate the tracking precision for
each fault (Table 7.1 ­ 7.9). 2
During the 52 simulated fault scenarios, there were six occasions when the MI­STSMC
was not able to stabilize the system, while the PPI – although with poor performance – was
still capable of providing control. Therefore, each figure displays the reference tracking
results under the minimum and maximum severity level of the investigated scenario where
both controllers stabilized the system after the occurrence of the given fault.
7.2.1
Biased position measurement
Since both controllers are reliant on the sensor measurement in their feedback loop, a
bias in the position signal provides an opportunity to investigate how the controllers react
to the failure of a critical component in their control architecture. By observing the findings
Table 7.1 and Figure 7.1, it can be seen that the baseline PPI controller is more robust
against bias in a state feedback signal. While the MI­STSMC was capable of controlling
the system in the presence of a 5% bias, the PPI maintained control up to 10% bias.
It is pertinent to underscore that the proposed MI­STSMC was more sensitive to negative
than positive bias. Furthermore, its reference tracking performance was demonstrated to
be sufficient, but even in the presence of the lowest fault level, it fell short of surpassing
the PPI.
7.2.2
Biased pressure measurements
In comparison to position measurement­related fault, a biased pressure signal imposes
a challenge only for the proposed controller by design, since its control action relies on
information about the piston and rod side pressures of the hydraulic cylinder as well.
Therefore, the results presented in Table 7.2 ­ 7.3 and on Figure 7.2 ­ 7.3 contains the per­
formance of the PPI controller only for demonstrative reasons. The values and reference
tracking response of the baseline controller during these fault scenarios are equivalent to
nominal conditions.
By investigating the reference tracking signals of the STSMC in both cases, it can be
observed that the proposed controller is more sensitive to piston­side pressure measure­
ment faults. While control was maintained in the presence of a 50% bias in the rod side
pressure signal, the same fault level on the piston side resulted in an unstable response.
Even though the STSMC was more reliable in terms of providing control in the presence
of rod side pressure bias, the fault induced a moderate increase in chattering as can be
observed on the reference tracking and error signals of Figure 7.3.

control input. Although smoother reference tracking can be observed on Figure 7.7 from

Closing remarks
8.1
Conclusions
This thesis intended to investigate the feasibility of systematically designing a fault­tolerant
super­twisting sliding mode controller for a wind turbine hydraulic pitch system with a
detailed dynamic description, as hypothesized at the outset of this manuscript. The thesis
also aimed to explore the utilization of a dynamic mapping­inversion (MI) algorithm to
reconstruct proper input for the designed STSMC and to assess the potential superiority
of the proposed MI­STSMC control architecture compared to a conventional PID controller
in the presence of various system faults.
The findings indicate a significant process in achieving the objectives outlined in the hy­
pothesis. Experiments and tests conducted in this thesis demonstrated the feasibility
of the proposed fault­tolerant STSMC. Furthermore, the utilization of the dynamic MI al­
gorithm for reconstructing the control input from the virtual input of the STSMC showed
promising results under the influence of system uncertainties during preliminary testing.
While the research has yielded auspicious outcomes in terms of feasibility, the perfor­
mance of the proposed control architecture implemented in a high­fidelity simulator was
less effective than expected. Although the combined operation of the MI algorithm and
the designed STSMC is capable of achieving favorable reference tracking, the occurring
chattering in the input signal emerges as a challenge in terms of practical implementation.
To ensure the validity of the design and its anticipated fault­tolerant capabilities, the MI­
STSMC was tested on a wide range of system fault scenarios in a comparative analysis
to a baseline PPI controller. Albeit the compromises made in terms of control perfor­
mance, the proposed MI­STSMC with properly tuned gains demonstrated sufficient refer­
ence tracking results which displays the potential of the proposed control architecture.
The author views the completion of this thesis as a significant achievement, primarily
due to the introduction of an innovative control architecture in the realm of pitch control.
Initiated from the ground up, this work encompasses a broad spectrum within the field. It
offers a substantial overview, thereby enriching the overall understanding of the subject
matter. The findings and methodologies presented in this thesis not only contribute to the
existing body of knowledge but also pave the way for future explorations in this field.
8.2
Further work
The journey of refining and perfecting the proposed control architecture does not end with
this thesis. Several avenues for future work have been identified throughout this study.
The unexpected performance of the MI algorithm indicates a need for a meticulous review
of both the design and implementation. This comprehensive examination will ensure that
any discrepancies or errors are identified and rectified, thereby enhancing the reliability
and effectiveness of the algorithm.
Once the dynamics MI has been thoroughly supervised and refined, the next step would
be to implement the system in a complete high­fidelity simulator, given that up until now,
testing has been conducted solely on the HPS control subsystem. This simulator, which
replicates the workings of all subparts of the wind turbine control, provides an environment
that closely mirrors real­life implementation. Applying the proposed controller in such a
Nonlinear Fault­Tolerant Control for Wind Turbine Hydraulic Pitch System
59
==PAGE:72==
setting will allow for robust testing and fine­tuning, ensuring that the system possesses
potential for industrial utilization.
Finally, exploring the incorporation of adaptive functionality into the proposed architecture
emerges as a promising direction for future work. Such an enhancement could augment
the system’s performance, enabling it to adapt and respond effectively to diverse pertur­
bations. This exploration is made possible by the robust foundation established for the
MI­STSMC through this thesis. The work done here not only creates a path for extensive
further development but also serves as a stepping stone for subsequent research, thereby
fostering continued innovation and progress in this field.
