Abstract
This thesis aims to investigate scour development, through 3D Computation Fluid
Dynamics (CFD) calculations in OpenFOAM. The purpose is to evaluate scour and
backﬁlling rates for ﬁxed scour holes around a cylindrical vertical structure. This is
done for varying hydrodynamic conditions and sediment characteristic. The purpose
is to populate a database which can be used to fast-evaluation of long term scour and
backﬁlling for oﬀshore monopile structures.
Almost all oﬀshore wind turbines are bottom ﬁxed. These are all vulnerable to changes
in the seabed level. All oﬀshore constructions placed on the seabed must be designed to
accommodate changes and deformations in the seabed. Scour begins to develop when a
structure is placed on the sea bottom. The sea bed material is a cofactor of the time it
takes do develop the scour hole. It is important to evaluate the development of the scour
holes. Currently the foundations are dimensioned for completely developed scour holes.
It is interesting to investigate, if this is actually necessary, or if the presence of waves
backﬁlls the scour holes and thereby reduces the need of dimensioning foundations
after fully developed scour holes.
The method consists of tables of scour rates, which is used to predict the scour devel-
opment around the structure at diﬀerent stages of the scour hole. When changing the
governing parameters for the ﬂow, which are causing the scour development around
the structure, the erosion rate or backﬁlling rate can be calculated from the mass bal-
ance of the sediment. This leads to the scour rates tables that are used to analyse
the development of the scour hole under diﬀerent current, waves and combined current
and wave conditions. The method has been tested against experimental scour data
and shows very promising results.
The thesis shows that it is possible to set up scour rate tables for a ﬁxed scour hole.
The results shows that the scour rates are in the right order of magnitude compared
to earlier studies and experiments from Dixen et al. (2012)[10] for pure current.
The thesis shows that scour occurs when the bed is ﬂat, or a small scour hole is
present, and decreases when the scour hole becomes bigger. At one point the scour
hole reaches its equilibrium, and it starts to be backﬁlled. The thesis shows that this
happens around a non dimensional scour depth, S/D = 2 for the validated current, at
S/D = 1 for the validated wave, and S/D = slightly over 1 for the validated combined
current and waves. Only a few simulations have been made. An actual database would
need extended work on top of the presented data in this thesis to be valid.

Introduction
Background
Almost all oﬀshore wind turbines are bottom ﬁxed. Either as a gravity based founda-
tion placed on the seabed, a monopile driven into the seabed, or in the future tripods
or jackets as illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of diﬀerent windmill foundations
These are all vulnerable to changes in the seabed level. This could be from foundation-
induced scour (local erosion), or by natural changes in the seabed morphology. All
oﬀshore constructions placed on the seabed must be designed to accommodate changes
and deformations in the seabed. Scour begins to develop when a structure is placed
on the sea bottom. When the seabed consists of sand, the scour develops rapidly,
while on a clay bed it develops much slower. In case of a monopile, the scour hole
can be up to two times the monopile diameter deep (typically 8 - 10 m if there is
time enough for the equilibrium scour depth to be attained). This often results in
the need of longer and more costly piles. In the case of gravity based foundations
the scour holes are often smaller, but because it is placed directly on the sea bottom,
it can easily be undermined by the scour, which may lead to failure. Therefore it is
important to evaluate the development of the scour holes. Currently the foundations
are dimensioned for completely developed scour holes. It is interesting to investigate,
if this is actually necessary, or if the presence of waves backﬁlls the scour holes and
thereby reduces the need of dimensioning foundations after fully developed scour holes.

==PAGE:13==
Project approach
The idea with this project is, through 3D Computation Fluid Dynamics (CFD) cal-
culations in OpenFOAM, to evaluate scour and backﬁlling rates for ﬁxed scour holes
around a cylindrical structure for varying hydrodynamic conditions (current, waves
and combined current and waves) and sediment characteristic.
This will hopefully
serve to populate a database used to serve for a fast-evaluation of long term scour and
backﬁlling for oﬀshore monopile structures. The model will be developed in Open-
FOAM, where bed load and suspended sediment transport will be implemented.
First, a 2D model is set up. The model is used to simulate conditions at the seabed
when current, waves, and combined current and waves are added. The model is vali-
dated by comparison with practical experiences. Next, a vertical pile is placed in an
area with the same water depth as the 2D model. This model is used to simulate the
conditions in 3D at the seabed, in the area around the vertical pile, when waves and
current combined with waves are added. With the 3D model, the conditions regarding
the scour hole that occurs at the pile, are investigated when sediment is included in
the model.
Anticipated results
The project will hopefully lead to a database of scour rates under diﬀerent conditions.
This can be used to analyse long time development of scour holes.

General Model
This chapter describes the theory behind the model, and set-up of the numerical model
in OpenFOAM, as well as the computational mesh set up. The model is set up as two
standard cases. One for the 2D model, described in section 3, and one for the 3D
model, described in section 4. The hydrodynamic model for both the 2D- and 3D case
are build up the same way, described in 2.1.
2.1
Hydrodynamic model
The hydrodynamic model is based on the Navier-Stokes equations, with a turbulence
model.
2.1.1

Top of domain (water surface) - TOP
The top of the domain is treated as a frictionless slip wall with horizontal velocities
and scalar hydrodynamic quantities having wall-normal zero gradient (∂/∂n = 0).
This is speciﬁed as a slip in OpenFOAM. The top boundary does not represent the
free surface of real waves in the cases where waves are included. The ﬂow beneath the
top is approximated by an oscillatory ﬂow when waves are included in the simulations.
The application of a ”rigid lid” at the top boundary rather than a free surface facility
is a commonly used and valid approach in numerical simulations of ﬂow and scour
around structures Fuhrman et al. (2014)[14] and Lian and Cheng (2005)[15].
Sides of domain - SIDE
In the two dimensional cases, the sides of the domain are speciﬁed as empty boundaries
in OpenFOAM, which means the sides won’t inﬂuence the simulated parameters in the
streamwise or vertical direction.
In the three dimensional cases the sides are speciﬁed as zeroGradient (∂/∂n = 0),
except the velocities, they are speciﬁed as slip.
Sea bed - BED, and pile surface - PILE
At the friction wall boundaries (sea bed and pile surface) no-slip conditions are im-
posed. This is speciﬁed in OpenFOAM as Wallfunctions. Here the velocity components
are set to zero, and a generalized wall function approach developed by Fuhrman (2017)
et al. Fuhrman et al. (2014)[14] is used.
Inlet - IN
The velocity is driven in the two dimensional model by a constant horizontal pressure
gradient based on the desired undisturbed friction velocity Uf (In OpenFOAM by us-
ing cyclic). Over time the velocity proﬁle is developed to a logarithmic distribution,
which is used as the input for the velocity in the 3D case. In case of waves, the velocity
is driven in the 2D model by an oscillatory ﬂow instead of the pressure gradient (in
OpenFOAM by using oscillatingFixedValue, while the rest of the boundaries here is
cyclic), and in the combined cases both above velocity are driving components. Here
zeroGradient is used as boundary in OpenFOAM instead of cyclic.
The boundary conditions for u, k and ω at the inlet are for the 3D case obtained
from a preliminary hydrodynamic simulation for a domain with the same water depth
and vertical cell distribution without a pile (The 2D model), where the only non-
zero component for the velocity is the stream wise x-direction. In OpenFOAM this is
applied with the boundary condition timeVaryingMappedFixedValue.

2.2.3
Computational mesh
A ﬁne mesh is used, which then leads to a smaller time step according to the Courant
number (the Courant number is deﬁned in equation 2.2.3.1).
A ﬁne mesh results in long simulation time. The preferred mesh size is a compromise
between accuracy and computational time. In principle the mesh should be so ﬁne
that a reﬁnement doesn’t change the result. However such reﬁnement is not always
possible to get.
The preferred cell size is as coarse as possible, but still with enough accuracy close
to the pile where the ﬂow parameters changes rapidly. Therefore diﬀerent meshes are
simulated to ﬁnd a resolution suitable for the sediment transport (total amount of
cells: 10.000 to 16.000.000).

Turbulence modelling of ﬂow close to the sea bed
The boundary layer plays an important role in a turbulent ﬂow. The ﬂow is divided
into four layers. The viscous sublayer, the buﬀer layer, the logarithmic layer, and the
outer layer.
The inner layer (the viscous to the logarithmic layer) is modelled with the rough wall
function approach in OpenFOAM to allow for a coarser mesh in the CFD model. If
wall functions are not used, a much ﬁner mesh would be required to solve the boundary
layer. The distribution of the velocity in the inner layer is seen in 2.2.3.1.
Figure 2.2.3.1: Sketch of diﬀerent ﬂow layers
It is seen that the viscous and logarithmic layer behaves diﬀerently. As the sea bed is
approached, the viscous eﬀects will dominate the ﬂow, while away from the sea bed,
the turbulent eﬀects will dominate the ﬂow.
In between these are the buﬀer-layer
which gradual transitions from viscous to turbulent dominated ﬂow. The idea of using
a wall function, is that this part of the ﬂow is modelled in the ﬁrst cell layer next
to a wall boundary. This allows a coarser mesh, because the mesh doesn’t need to
resolve this region. The coarser mesh then allows for a larger time step, due to the
relation between the ﬂow velocity, the time step and the cell size in the deﬁnition of
the Courant number. The Courant number is deﬁned as
Co = ∆t|U|∆x
(2.2.3.1)
The Courant number should be less than 1, to ensure a stable solution Summer and
Mutlu (2013)[4].

The combined case is set up with the same inputs as both the previously explained

Since U for the inlet is not cyclic, the current will not be build up in the domain, if the

from the middle of the domain, to make sure no mistakes due to the boundaries are
incorporated in the 3D model. The current model was run for 600 sec. to be stable.

5.1
The ﬁxed shape of the scour hole
By studies of shapes for scour holes and development of scour holes around piles, a
ﬁxed shape for the scour hole is chosen to be a frustum of a circular cone. The radius
of the monopile, r, is kept constant as seen in ﬁgure 5.1.1. The angle of the slope in
the frustum is taken to be equal to the sediments angle of repose, which was taken to
be 32 degree from Summer et al. (2014)[5].
By choosing a ﬁxed angle of 32 degree, the downstream angle of the slope is too steep
compared to what has been observed in experiments and ﬁeld data (23 degree Summer
et al. (2014)[5]), see ﬁgure 5.1.1, the dotted line.
Figure 5.1.1: Sketch of ﬁxed shape of the scour hole.
Figure 5.1.1 shows a sketch of the ﬁxed shape of the scour hole. The dashed line at
the downstream side shows the angle of a typical downstream slope observed in model
test and ﬁeld data for a circular pile Dixen et al. (2012)[10]
There are several reasons to not choose a ﬁxed shape which would ﬁt more correctly
to the observed downstream slope of a scour hole in a constant current. The ﬁxed
shape is chosen, ﬁrstly because the shape should not only be valid for steady current,
but also for other ﬂow conﬁgurations such as waves and combined current and waves,
where this angle would change. Secondly, the direction of marine and coastal currents
is not constant, and therefore the cone shape is assumed to represent the real area.
The idea with the ﬁxed scour hole is to see how well the scour predictions will be with
a simple approximation of the scour hole. With the chosen ﬁxed shape of the scour
hole the scour depth can easily be changed, simply by changing the depth, h, of the
scour hole seen in ﬁgure 5.1.1, since the slope angle and the monopile radius, r, are
kept constant.

5.2
Sediment transport and morphological model
This section describes the sediment transport and morphological model used in this
project.
5.2.1
Sediment transport
According to Deigaard and Fredsøe (1992)[7], the sediment transport can be divided
into three components as listed below.
• Bed load
• Suspended load
• Wash load
Where the wash load is consisting of very ﬁne sediment which is not present at the
bed. This is not accounted for, because it is not related to the sediment transport at
the seabed.
The suspended load is not in contact with the bed, and is driven by the turbulence in
the ﬂuid, while the bed load is in contact with the bed, and is determined by the eﬀec-
tive bed shear stress acting on the bed. When the critical bed shear stress is reached,
the sediment starts to come into motion. In this project, only the bed load is taken
into account. Therefore the model makes use of only the bed load description.
The CFD model is equipped with a sediment transport model, which calculates the
bed load transport Dixen et al. (2012)[10]. The bed load theory by Engelund and
Fredsøe (1976)[18] has been extended to the case of any arbitrary sloping bed using
the vectorial approach of Kovac and Parker (1994)[19], where the bed load transport
model is based on a fully non-linear vectorial formulation for the force balance on
a sediment particle. The force balance on a sediment particle can be seen in ﬁgure
5.2.1.1.

7
Discussion and improvements
Section 5.4 shows, that the scour rate tables are pretty coarse. An improvement to the
result would ﬁrst of all be to make more simulations over diﬀerent non dimensional
scour depths S/D.
The model which is set up, only includes bed load. The sediment transport also in-
cludes the suspended sediment, and that part of the sediment is not included. This
part might change the scour rate tremendously (depending on the shields parameter).
To get a more accurately and useful model, suspended sediment should be included.
Suspended sediment could be included by using the ”two meshes” approach described
in Liu et al. (2013)[16].
One of the simpliﬁcations in this model was regarding the scour hole shape. In practice
the scour hole does not have the same angle of repose in all directions. In practice the
scour hole would have a shape as shown in ﬁgure 7.1.
Figure 7.1: Sketch of scour hole from Summer et al. (2014)[5]
It would be interesting to investigate how the shape of the scour hole would aﬀect the
results of the simulated scour rates.
As described earlier, a mistake or deviation occurred in the S/D = 2 simulation of cur-
rent. A reason for that could be a too short run time for the 3D model, and therefore
would a longer simulation time probably be an improvement.
The results for waves and combined current and waves would also be improved if the
mean value of the bed load could be found from for example 20 wave periods instead
of only one. That could probably also resolve the problem for the wave result, where
the wave is shifted a little to one of the sides (seen in ﬁgure 6.2.2.2a).
The model has only been run for one ﬂow condition for each situation: current, waves,
and combined. It would be interesting to examine how the diﬀerent ﬂow parameters
aﬀect the scour rate.

8
Conclusion
A method based on table lookups to predict scour around monopiles has been devel-
oped. The tables consist of scour rates around a monopile, for the three cases, current,
waves and combined current and wave. All with a shields parameter, θ of 0.24. The
scour rates have all been plotted as a function of the non dimensional scour depth S/D,
for each of the three ﬂow conditions.
The scour rates have been obtained from simpliﬁed CFD calculations, where the shape
of scour hole was kept ﬁxed. A total of 20 simulations were conducted for:
8 diﬀerent S/D for current, ranging from 0 ≤S/D ≤2.0
6 diﬀerent S/D for waves, ranging from 0 ≤S/D ≤0.16
6 diﬀerent S/D for combined current and waves, ranging from 0 ≤S/D ≤0.5.
The method is based on a simple scour hole shape, which not totally resembles the real
scour shape around a monopile. The shape of a scour hole was taken as a frustum with
side slopes equal to the angle of repose. This is not entirely correct for the downstream
slope of the scour hole which normally is 9 degree less steep than the upstream slope
( upstream = 32 degree, downstream = 23 degree). The purpose of choosing a shape
of the scour hole which does not entirely resemble the real scour shape was due to
future perspectives of the method, where the method should be used for more complex
structures where the shape of the scour hole is not as well-known as it is for the case
of a monopile.
The scour can easily and fast be predicted by using the scour rate tables. The method
for current has been tested against experimental data for scour development around
a pile. The predicted scour development showed very promising results. However the
magnitude of the scour rate in current was so small compared to the magnitude of the
scour rate in waves, that the combined case was almost expressed completely by the
wave.
The thesis shows promising results in making lookups for prediction of scour and
backﬁlling, but further investigation is necessary to evaluate the accuracy of the results.
The simulations should be done for more ﬂow conditions, and more non dimensional
scour depths S/D should be run to clarify the results. The few simulations which has
been run shows that the model can simulate the behaviour of the bed load, but the
results are too coarse with this few values.
