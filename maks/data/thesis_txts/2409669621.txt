Abstract
In order to mitigate rain and wind induced vibrations of stay cables on bridges it is often a
necessity to install external dampers on the cables due to their low inherent damping.
This project concerns semi active damping of stay cables with focus on semi active dampers
with apparent negative stiffness. Apparent negative stiffness can be attained by controlling
the damper such that it is only active when the cable is moving towards its resting position
rather than when its moving away from it. Thus, the cable motion at the damper position
increases which allows the damper to extract more energy from the system.
The subject of this study is one of the longest stay cables on the Forth Replacement Crossing
which is currently under construction, where passive friction dampers are installed at a
relative distance of 2 % from the cable support.
The lowest natural frequency of the cable is found to be 1.975 rad/s and the relative stiffness
of the cable was found to be very low, for which reason optimal damping of taut cables can
be applied.
In this project, dampers are modelled as power-law dampers, which is a mathematical
damper model that can attain properties of both linear viscous dampers and friction
dampers. Using harmonic averaging, equivalent linear stiffness and viscosity parameters
are derived for the power-law damper model with no stiffness as well as apparent positive
and negative stiffness and optimal damping parameters are determined.
The power-law dampers with different properties are tested on a non-linear numerical ca-
ble model where results are obtained from a time integration over a free decay analysis.
It is found from this analysis, that a viscous damper proves slightly more efficient than a
friction damper. More importantly it is found that a damper realising apparent negative
stiffness leads to a damping ratio more than twice the damping ratio of a damper with no
stiffness.

Resumé
For at undgå vibrationer i skråstagskabler på broer fremkaldt af vind- og regnlaster er det
ofte nødvendigt at installere eksterne dæmpere på grund af kablernes lave naturlige dæmp-
ning.
Det forhåndenværende projekt omhandler semiaktiv dæmpning af skråstagskabler med
særlig fokus på semiaktive dæmpere med tilsyneladende negativ stivhed. Negativ stivhed
i dæmpere opnås ved at kontrollere dæmperen således at den kun er aktiv når kablet
bevæger sig mod dets ligevægtsposition i stedet for når det bevæger sig væk fra den. Således
øges kablets bevægelse ved dæmperens position, hvorved dæmperen kan trække en større
mængde af energi ud af systemet.
Grundlaget for analyserne udført i dette projekt er en af de længste skråstage på Forth
Replacement Crossing broen, der på nuværende tidspunkt er under opførelse, hvor passive
friktionsdæmpere er installeret i en relativ afstand på 2 % fra kablets nedre understøtning.
Den laveste egenfrekvens i kablet er bestemt til 1,975 rad/s og den relative stivhed af er
kablet er fundet til at være meget lav, hvilket gør, at optimal dæmpning af et taut kabel kan
anvendes på kablet.
I dette projekt er dæmpere modelleret som power-law dæmpere – en matematisk dæm-
per model der kan antage egenskaber af både en viskos dæmper og en friktionsdæmper.
Ved harmonisk midling er ækvivalente lineære stivheds- og viskositetsparametre samt op-
timale dæmperparametre udledt for power-lav dæmpere uden stivhed og med henholdsvis
tilsyneladende positiv og negativ stivhed.
Power-law dæmpere med forskellige egenskaber er testet på en ikke lineær kabelmodel,
hvor resultater er opnået ved tidsintegration over en fri henfaldsanalyse. Denne analyse
viser, at en viskos dæmper er en smule mere effektiv end en friktionsdæmper. Endnu
vigtigere viser analysen at en semiaktiv dæmper med tilsyneladende negativ stivhed med-
fører mere end dobbelt så meget dæmpning som dæmperen uden stivhed.

1
Introduction
Recent modern stay cable bridges rise taller and span wider than ever before, requiring
very long and slender stay cables to support the weight of the bridge deck [1]. Long and
slender stay cables typically has low natural frequencies within the first 1 to 3 vibration
modes, making them prone to vibrations induced either by wind or rain loads or by a
combination of both [2, 3]. In worst case, the stay cables can be introduced to vibrations
of their resonance frequency in which case they are likely to vibrate with a high amplitude
causing fatigue in the cable over time [3]. Consisting of strands of high strength steel, stay
cables typically has very low inherent damping at roughly 0.10-0.15 % [4] making it neces-
sary to install a damper system in order to mitigate resonance frequency vibrations in the
cables. A stay cable damping system is often implemented as a transverse damper close
to the lower cable support at a relative distance of 2-4 % [5]. The closeness of the damper
to the support severely limits the efficiency of the damper due to the relatively small cable
amplitude at this location, making it highly important to tune the damper to attain maxi-
mum energy dissipation [6]. Especially shallow cables with high relative stiffness develop
regions of reduced motion close to the cable support [2].
In general, dampers can be divided into three main categories: Passive dampers, semi ac-
tive dampers and active dampers [7]. A passive damper is a mechanical device that delivers
either a constant damping force or a damping force proportional to the magnitude of the
velocity of the cable at the damper position. The passive damper has the disadvantage that
it can only be tuned to optimally damp one resonance frequency of the cable whereas the
other two can be tuned to damp multiple frequencies [8]. The semi active damper is sim-
ilar to the passive damper except for the important difference that its damping properties
can be changed in real time depending on either displacement, velocity, acceleration of the
cable or a combination of the three. This makes it possible for the damper to always adapt
to the current vibration situation. The last type, the active damper, as the name implies is
similar to the semi active damper in the way that it can also change its properties in real
time to adapt to the current vibrations of the cable. The difference from the semi active
damper is that the active damper can provide energy to the system in order to damp it
more efficiently [9]. Recent years has seen a vast expansion in application of the semi ac-
tive damper as it is very cost efficient compared to the active damper while still being able
to greatly outperform the passive damper [6, 9, 10]. In particular, semi active dampers
utilising apparent negative stiffness has proven to be roughly twice as effective as an op-
timally tuned passive viscous damper [6, 11, 12] mainly due to increased cable motion at

Damping
In the previous chapter the general theory of free vibrations in shallow cables have been
presented. The following addresses the theory regarding damping of cables as the purpose
of the project is to efficiently damp vibrations in stay cables. First, the three main damper
categories will be presented after which the different types of applied damping is discussed.
In the end of the chapter, the theory regarding equivalent linear damping and negative
stiffness will be presented as the main focus of the project is to investigate the effect of
negative stiffness.
Stay cables usually consist of strands of high strength steel, which leads to an extremely low
inherent damping, ζst, of the cables usually within a range of 0.10-0.15 % [2, 4, 17]. Due to
the low structural damping of the cables it is often necessary to apply external damping to
stay cables in order to mitigate large vibrations which can lead to discomfort for the users
of the structure and in worst case structural fatigue [1]. For practical reasons, external
dampers on stay cables are often applied as transverse dampers attached close to the lower
cable support at a relative distance, a, of about 2-4 % of the cable length [4, 7]. The focus of
this project is on such a transverse damper. The motion of a transverse damper however, is
limited by its closeness to the support where the amplitude of the cable is small, especially
for shallow cables with a relative elastic stiffness that leads to a natural frequency around
the frequency crossover, as explained in Section 2.2.2, where the cable motion is extremely
small close to the supports [2]. Thus, the amount of extractable energy for such a transverse
damper is limited and it is therefore highly important to tune the damper to extract as much
energy as possible [4, 5]. If the damping force is too small vibrations will not be reduced
sufficiently. On the contrary, if the damping force is too large it will lead to locking of
the cable at the damper position which will neither reduce vibrations but instead slightly
increase the natural frequencies of the cable [5, 18].
As mentioned, the purpose of the damper is to reduce cable vibrations by extracting energy
from the system. Thus, the effect of the damper depends on the energy dissipation which
is the amount of energy loss in the system due to damping, typically measured over a full
vibration cycle [8].

3.1
Damping Categories
Different types of dampers with different tuning and configuration dissipate energy from
the system in different ways. Generally, dampers are divided into three main categories
depending on their ability to be controlled in real-time [7]. Below is a brief presentation
of the three main damper categories.
3.1.1
Passive Damping
A passive damper is, as the name implies, a damper that is not controllable in any way.
The damping properties of a passive damper are ideally constant with small deviations due
to for instance nonlinear oil behaviour in an oil damper [3]. For this reason the passive
can only be tuned to efficiently damp one vibration mode of the structure [19]. A great
advantage of the passive damper is that it requires no external power supply as it relies solely
on the motion of the structure in order to deliver a dissipative damping force. Though the
passive damper can for many systems prove more than sufficiently efficient, it has distinct
performance limitations due to the lack of controllability [20].
3.1.2
Active Damping
An active damper is, in contrast to the passive damper, fully controllable and can at any
time deliver ideally any force in any direction independent of the motion of the structure
up to a certain limit depending on the specific damper [7]. This makes it possible for the
damper to provide energy to the system. The advantage of the active damper is that it
can be tuned to dissipate energy from the system for multiple vibration modes. The active
damper typically delivers a force using electro-hydraulic or electro-mechanical actuators
based on feedback provided by censors located either on at the damper position or at a
remote location on the structure [9]. One of the drawbacks on the other hand is that the
active damper typically requires a large power source to provide the sufficient energy to
the system [9]. Furthermore, it is obvious that if the damper is tuned incorrectly or if there
is a system fault resulting in wrong censor feedback returned to the damper it might in
fact increase vibrations rather than reduce them as it is able to generate forces in the same
direction as the motion.
3.1.3
Semi Active Damping
A semi active damper can be considered a combination of the passive and active dampers.
It is a mechanical device that, like the active damper, requires an external power supply
to control its damping properties but opposed to the active damper it is unable to provide
energy to the system which is the main drawback of the semi active damper as it can, like
the passive damper, only deliver dissipative forces. The semi active damper however holds
great advantages over both the passive and the active dampers. Because the semi active
damper does not need to provide energy to the system it requires very little external power
to be controlled compared to the active damper causing it to be highly cost-efficient in
comparison [10, 21]. The ability to control the damping properties makes it possible to
tune the damper to extract energy from various vibration modes as opposed to the passive

3.2.2
Friction Damping
Another simple damper mechanism is the dry friction damper which is broadly applied in
mechanical engineering for motion reduction as it is both a very effective and economic
method [22]. In recent years dry friction damping has also been introduced for motion
reduction in larger scale structural engineering where it is often applied as damping mech-

tion. First it is evaluated in the very beginning where it is based off of the tangent stiffness
matrix in the reference state of the system. In this evaluation the inherent damping, ζst is
set artificially high in order to kill off any vibrations for the static equilibrium analysis as
further described in Section 5.1.2. When the static equilibrium with self weight is obtained
the inherent damping matrix is re-evaluated using the tangent stiffness matrix of the static
equilibrium state of the system.
4.3
Time Integration
This section covers the time algorithm used in the project to simulate non linear stay cable
vibrations. Time integration of the equation of motion can be a powerful tool used to in-
vestigate dynamic problems especially in non-linear systems where the dynamic behaviour
of the system is unpredictable.
Overall there are two different types of time integration algorithms used to solve dynamic
problems, the collocation-type methods and the momentum method. Traditionally, the
collocation-type methods (the Newmark algorithm for instance) have been and are still
being widely used. In the collocation-type methods the equation of motion is matched
at selected points in time under suitable assumptions regarding the relations between dis-
placement, velocity and acceleration. However, in recent years there has been rapid devel-
opment in the momentum-based method. The idea behind the momentum-based method
on the other hand, is to consider the development of the motion, integrating the equation of
motion over a time interval [tn, tn+1] whereby the inertial term becomes a finite increment
of momentum over the interval and the internal and external forces can be represented by
suitable mean values.
The state of the system at any given time, t, can be described completely by the loading f(t)
and the state vector [u(t), ˙u(t)] at the time. The simplest time integration algorithms are
the single-step methods where displacement and velocity calculated based on the previ-
ous displacement and velocity and the load history between the two states. In collocation
methods, the equation of motion is matched at selected time within the time step accom-
panied by additional equations relating displacement, velocity, and acceleration.
In this project momentum-based energy dissipating algorithm by Krenk [13] is used to
analyse cable vibration. Below is a brief presentation of key equations leading to the algo-
rithm.

viscous damper.
As mentioned in Section 3.2.3, a power-law damper model can be used to describe both as
friction damper and a linear viscous damper as well as a combination of the two, depending
on the value of αp. For this reason the main focus of the studies in this project addresses
the power-law damper.
The friction damper turned out to be extremely time consuming to perform numerical
time integration over. This is due to the abrupt changes in the damper force which makes
it extremely difficult for the time integration algorithm to achieve equilibrium within a
time step where a force change occurs. In addition, since the damper control function of
a friction damper contains the term |˙ud|−1, it is seen that the stiffness contribution given
in (4.38) becomes infinitely high for velocities around zero, causing the effective stiffness
matrix to become singular. By slightly modifying the pure friction damper by replacing
the abrupt transitions between force levels with steep linear transitions it was possible,
though very time consuming, to perform some simple integrations with a friction damper,
corresponding to αp = 0.
Investigation of the different damper types implemented is carried out using to separate
analysis methods. In the first analysis, a dynamic load is applied to the cable causing it to
vibrate in it’s first vibration mode. Once vibration are stable, the dynamic load is removed
and the decay of the vibrations are measured. The goal of the second analysis is to measure
the dynamic amplification of the cable this is done by applying a dynamic load with a very
low frequency relative to the natural frequency to obtain the reference static deformation
of the cable. The frequency is then increased in steps, and the amplitude of the cable is
measured when vibrations are stable within every step.

6
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to investigate semi active stay cable dampers numerically with
a special focus on dampers with apparent negative stiffness. The subject of this study was
one of the longest stay cables on the Forth Replacement Crossing, with a horizontal span of
390 m and a relative damper position of 2 %. The static and dynamic behaviour of the cable
has been studied. The lowest natural frequency of the cable was found to be 1.975 rad/s.
Moreover it was found that the cable has a very low relative elastic stiffness, making the
distinction between a taught and a shallow cable very small.
Different types of dampers has been studied with a special focus on the power-law damper.
The equivalent linear stiffness and viscous terms have been derived for this damper along
with an optimal control strategy.
A momentum-based energy-dissipating non-linear time integration algorithm was imple-
mented to study the vibration response with a number of dampers. A free decay analysis
has shown by adjustment of the power-law damper exponent, that a viscous damper proves
slightly more efficient than a friction damper. More importantly, the analysis showed that
a power-law damper utilising apparent negative stiffness is more than twice as efficient
as an optimally tuned passive linear viscous damper. The damper with apparent negative
stiffness resulted in a damping ratio of 2.10 % compared to 1.02 % for the linear damper.

Further Work
At the end of this project there is still a lot of work that can be done in the future. One
apparent thing is to further investigate the dynamic amplification of the cable with the
applied damper types as the results of the analysis does not comply well with theory.
It could also be of great interest to further study friction dampers, as the dampers cur-
rently employed on the Forth Replacement Crossing are passive friction dampers. This
will however require an improvement in the friction modelling due to the extremely long
computation time of the friction dampers modelled in this project. An interesting property
of semi active friction damper to be studied is the energy spillover phenomenon caused by
switching the friction force levels. For this purpose, and in general, it could be interesting
to study more than just the first vibration mode of the cable.
Furthermore it could be very interesting to study negative stiffness further to for instance
determine if there is a finite amount at which point the negative stiffness is no longer prof-
itable for a damper or if the damper keeps improving with increasing negative stiffness.
From a commercial point of view it would also be of great interest to study if different types
of dampers are more efficient on some cables while other dampers may be more efficient on
other cables. If that proves to be the case it would be very profitable to create an algorithm
that can predict the best damper type for a cable with a given set of properties.
