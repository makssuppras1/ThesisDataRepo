Abstract
The natural heterigeneity of guaiacyl (G) and syringyl (S) compounds from lignin processing
hampers their direct use as plant-based chemicals and materials. A new group of short fungal
polyphenol oxidases (PPOs) exhibit the potential to reduce the heterogenity of lignin-derived
mixtures. In this study, two short fungal PPOs from lignocellulose-degrading ascomycetes are
heterologously expressed in Pichia pastoris and subsequently puriﬁed; MtPPO7 from Myce-
liophthora thermophila and CgPPO-473 from Chaetomium globosum, both able to catalyse
ortho-hydroxylation of G- and S-compounds, generating identical products.
Herein, we show that heterologous expression in P. pastoris yields extensively N-glycosylated
MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473, proving crucial for the solubility of CgPPO-473, while MtPPO7
remains soluble upon deglycosylation. MALDI-TOF assessment of the deglycosylated MtPPO7
indicates a truncation of 1.7 kDa, measuring 43,166 Da compared to the predicted molecular
weight of 44,869 Da, whereas mass photometry analysis indicates an average molecular weight
of 50 ± 2.4 kDa.
Applying mass photometry on the glycosylated PPOs indicates average
molecular weight of 67 ± 3.1 kDa for MtPPO7 and between 50 ± 2.4 to 71 ± 3.4 for CgPPO-
473, respectively, and shows that both PPOs are monomers in 20 mM sodum acetate pH 6.0.
The puriﬁed PPOs exhibit copper-deﬁcient active sites, partially occupied by other divalent
cations. A copper saturation method is developed, achieving near-complete copper loading of
91% and with 87% copper content for MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473, respectively. The presence
of the type-3 copper centre typical for PPOs is conﬁrmed by UV-Vis spectroscopy, measuring
the characteristic band at 345 nm upon H2O2 saturation.
MtPPO7 displays high thermal
stability assessed by nDSF with a thermal midpoint of approximately 75◦C, which is una↵ected
by extensive N-glycosylations, while glycosylated CgPPO-473 exhibits a thermal midpoint of
60◦C.
Lastly, we demonstrate PPO activity on G lignin-units assessing MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473
activities on guaiacol and vanilic acid substrates. Both PPOs oxidise vanilic acid by a two-
step mechanism, releasing the intermediate o-diphenol, 3,4-dihydroxy-5-methoxybenzoic acid.
The pH optimum of the monophenolase activity is pH 7.0 for MtPPO7 and pH 6.0-7.0 for
CgPPO-473.
For diphenolase activity MtPPO7 displays pH optimum at 6.0, while pH has
limited impact on CgPPO-473. Both PPOs display a monophenolase-to-diphenolase ratio of
approximately 0.45 at pH 7.0.
The application of fungal short PPOs opens for new routes to reduce the heterogenity and
methoxylation degree of mixtures of G and S lignin-derived compounds, presenting unique
opportunities for lignin valorisation.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Lignocellulosic biomass is the most abundant renewable organic material on earth and presents
an obvious natural resource to exploit through green biotechnology. It is a complex matrix
primarily composed of three biopolymers: the two polysaccharides, cellulose and hemicellulose,
and a heteropolymer, lignin [1, 2]. Until recently, the primary purpose of bioprocessing ligno-
cellulosic biomass was extracting value from the abundant polysaccharides, compromising the
aromatic lignin structure by disrupting the native chemistry. Consequently, lignin has become
an inert low-value byproduct [3].
Lignocellulose processing is facing a new era, often referred to as ”Lignin-First”, where lignin
valorisation is recognised as essential to enable economic viability of the lignocellulosic biore-
ﬁning industry [4]. Thus, current research centres around developing methods to extract native
lignin from the lignocellulosic biomass and exploit its remarkable properties in future applica-
tions [5]. A central challenge in lignin utilisation is the intrinsic chemical heterogeneity of lignin;
depolymerisation yields, regardless of the approach, a mixture of compounds.
To overcome this, biocatalytic conversion of lignocellulose constitutes a key technology for green
and sustainable production of biochemicals and biofuels and can, in turn, facilitate a fossil-free
future [6]. A promising green approach is utilising enzyme technology to e↵ectively valorise
lignocellulosic biomass, while preserving the native chemistry of both the polysaccharides and
lignin. Drawing inspiration from natural lignocellulose-degrading organisms, research is directed
towards the fungal kingdom. Fungi have evolved strategies to degrade and metabolise lignin and
while various lignin-modifying enzymes have been isolated from fungal secretomes, the exact
mechanism of lignocellulose degradation remains ambiguous [7, 8, 9]. A recently recognised
class of sequence-related short polyphenol oxidases (PPOs) from ﬁlamentous fungi exhibit the
potential to reduce the heterogenity of lignin-derived mixtures [10]. The application of fungal
short PPOs opens for new routes to reduce the heterogenity and methoxylation degree of these
lignin-derived mixtures, presenting unique opportunities for lignin valorisation.
1.2
Lignocellulosic Biomass
In nature, lignin is the largest renewable source of aromatic carbon, accounting for approxi-
mately 30% of all organic carbon in plant biomass [11]. The lignin polymer is one out of three
major constituents of lignocellulosic biomass and is considered the resin that holds the ligno-
cellulosic matrix together, as it covalently cross-links to the polysaccharides (Figure 1.1). The
three biopolymers, cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, are intertwined to form a complex cell
wall structure in vascular plants. Together, they comprise up to 80% of the total weight of raw
plant material, while small amounts of pectin, salts, and ash is present [2]. The proportion of

Lignin cross-links to various polysaccharides, primarily hemicellulose, forming lignin-carbohydrate
complexes (LCC) through covalent, intermolecular anchoring points (Figure 1.1) [14]. The in-
termolecular linkages exists in di↵erent chemical conformations, but the main type found in
wood are phenyl glycoside bonds, benzyl ethers, and esters. These are typically formed be-
tween an aliphatic alcohol of the lignin polymer and glucuronoxylan, the primary constituent
of hemicellulose [14, 15]. Altogether, the complex structures of lignocellulose provides rigidity
to the cell wall and serves as a resilient barrier towards biodegradation, in which lignin plays a
signiﬁcant role. Lignocellulosic biomass is generally divided into three main types: softwoods
(gymnosperms), hardwoods (angiosperms), and grasses or herbaceous plants (graminaceous)
[2, 16].
1.2.1
Lignin Structure
The natural role of lignin is reﬂected in the hydrophobic, amorphous structure. Lignin is a cross-
linked phenolic heteropolymer composed of methoxylated phenylpropanoid units [17]. The main
monomeric precursors consist of three cinnamyl alcohols: coniferyl alcohol, p-coumaryl alcohol,
and synapyl alcohol. Upon incorporation into lignin, the monomeric units are referred to as
p-hydroxyphenyl (H), guaiacyl (G), and syringyl (S) units (Figure 1.2) [18]. Monolignols are
linked through multiple carbon–carbon and aryl–ether linkages, resulting in a complex structure
that display varying chemical structure, high heterogenity, and a low degree of order [19].

It is believed that in vivo lignin synthesis is an enzymatically initiated chemical reaction, in-
volving oxidative polymerisation of the precursors [17]. Monolignols are oxidised to phenoxy
radicals in the cell wall by oxidative enzymes and, consequently, the formed radicals couple
through random radical-radical coupling reactions, contributing to polymer growth [16, 18].
Ligniﬁcation starts with a dehydrogenative dimerisation of two oxidised precursors and con-
tinues with further cross-coupling of dimers, oligomers, and incoming monolignols [20]. The
formed phenoxy radicals are relatively stable owing to electron resonance stabilisation of the
aromatic ring and conjugated side-chains (Figure 1.3). The ﬁnal structure of the lignin polymer
is predominantly dictated by chemical reactions, where parameters such as pH and concentra-
tion of precursors inﬂuence the generated linkages and ratio of monolignols [18]. Hence, the
composition and chemical structure of lignin is inﬂuenced by the origin and growth conditions
of the plant.
Figure 1.3: Enzyme catalysed formation of resonance stabilised phenoxy radical of lignin precursor, p-coumaryl alcohol.
Monolignols couple through inter-unit linkages and are linked by multiple carbon-carbon bonds,
often referred to as condensed linkages, and aryl-ether linkages. Due to the stability of the
phenoxy radical, electron delocalisation provides single-electron density to sites around the
aromatic ring, which varies depending on the subunit, and to the conjugated side-chain in β-

The most abundant linkage in native lignin is the β-O-4 ether bond which constitutes more than

the inter-unit linkages 4-O-5 and 5-5; these linkages have only been demonstrated for higher
molecular weight oligomers and polymers [18]. Typically, native lignins consist of 80-90% non-
phenolic subunits and 10-20% phenolic subunits [16].

1.2.2
Biocatalytic Conversion of Lignocellulosic Biomass
The ”Lignin-First” approach emphasises extraction and exploration of lignin, declaring lignin
the most valuable constituent in lignocellulose [4]. Currently, 95% of the worldwide lignin is an
underutilised by-product of the paper and pulp production with the remainder being used for
low value applications [11]. This current industrial processing of biomass entails structural mod-
iﬁcation of lignin, in which common functional groups, like hydroxyl groups and ethers, become
condensed, leaving non-phenolic substructures and an overall more recalcitrant and inert lignin
polymer [22, 16]. Therefore, ”Lignin-First” calls upon a renewed assessment of lignocellulose
processing with a focus on mild treatments that preserves the native, high-quality lignin prop-
erties. Separation and accessibility of the lignin polymer accommodates the polysaccharides as
well, allowing for all three major components of the biomass to be fully valorised [6].

1.2.3
Fungal Oxidoreductases Involved in Natural Lignocellulose Conversion
In nature, fungi and other microorganisms have evolved strategies to degrade and metabolise
lignin but the exact mechanism is not fully understood [8, 24]. Biological depolymerisation of
lignin has been studied extensively over the last decades, discovering several lignin active oxi-
doreductases secreted by fungi [25]. Since lignin is invariably found intertwined with polysac-
charides, the lignolytic enzymes act in concert with hemi- and cellulolytic enzymes, reﬂecting
the complexity of the biomass [9, 7].
Natural degradation of lignin is believed to entail a range of synergetically working lignolyti-
cal enzymes. These extracellular enzymes often display wide substrate speciﬁcity and utilise
radical intermediates for lignin depolymerisation.
The smaller substructures are eventually
absorbed by the microorganism, in which they are modiﬁed and incorporated into the central
carbon metabolism [8, 26]. Heme peroxidases, like manganese peroxidase, lignin peroxidase, and
versatile peroxidase, and the oxidase laccase are recognised as key enzymes in natural lignin
depolymerisation [25, 16, 27]. They contribute directly to oxidative modiﬁcation of lignin and
display a common characteristic in their ability to catalyse one-electron oxidations of electron-
rich substrates, producing radicalised products and water (Table 1.1).

1.3
Polyphenol Oxidases
Polyphenol oxidases (PPOs) are a family of enzymes comprising tyrosinases (EC 1.14.28.1),
catechol oxidases (EC 1.10.3.1), and aureusidin synthases (EC 1.21.3.6). They are ubiquitous
and can be found in all domains of life from bacteria, fungi, and plants to humans [29]. PPOs
are coupled binuclear copper enzymes with a type-3 copper centre that use molecular oxygen
to oxidise small phenolic compounds. They catalyse ortho-hydroxylation of monophenols via a
monooxygenase reaction (referred to as monophenolase activity) and a subsequent two-electron
oxidation of the resulting ortho-diphenols to the corresponding ortho-quinones (diphenolase
activity) [30].
Depending on the substrate, PPOs display either monophenolase activity or
dipholase activity, catalysing catechols via dehydrogenation reactions (Figure 1.5). Both re-
actions yields o-quinones which are highly reactive and undergo spontaneous, non-enzymatic
reactions, polymerising to pigments [30].
Figure 1.5: The two oxidative reactions catalysed by PPOs.
PPOs are classiﬁed based on their activity; those possessing both monophenolase and dipheno-
lase activities are referred to as tyrosinases, while PPOs possessing only diphenolase activity are
named catechol oxidases. This classiﬁcation was originally based on enzymatic activity towards
L-tyrosine and L-DOPA, however, recent studies show that previously classiﬁed catechol oxi-
dases possess monophenolase activity towards their natural substrates, despite not catalysing
the oxidation of L-tyrosine or L-DOPA [31, 32, 33, 34]. Organisms from di↵erent kingdoms
utilise di↵erent substrates for their pigments and, consequently, organism express PPOs with
aﬃnity for speciﬁc precursors [33].
1.3.1
Fungal PPOs
In 2010 Gasparetti et al. discovered a clade of extracellular short PPOs in ﬁlamentous fungi [35].
They distinguish between two clades of sequence-related fungal PPOs based on sequence length.
The new clade, short PPOs, comprises 350-400 residues compared to the long, canonical PPOs
with 400-600 residues [35]. The canonical, fungal PPOs are typically categorised as tyrosinases,
displaying activity towards L-tyrosine and L-DOPA, and comprise three regions: a N-terminal
domain, a linker region, and a C-terminal domain. The N-terminal domain is considered the

catalytic domain, in which six highly conserved histidine residues coordinates the two copper
ions, while the function of the C-terminal domain remain elusive [30, 36]. In literature, the
C-terminal domain is mostly annotated to act like a shielding domain of pro-tyrosinases [37].
The primary di↵erence arises from the lack of the C-terminal domain in short PPOs. Stud-

1.3.2
Type-3 Copper Centre and Catalytic Cycle of PPOs
Type-3 copper proteins display highly conserved active sites comprising two copper atoms,
referred to as CuA and CuB, each coordinated by three histidine residues: HA1 to HA3 and
HB1 to HB3, respectively (teal sticks in Figure 1.6 B) [40, 33, 37]. The two copper atoms can
reversely bind oxygen, giving rise to di↵erent copper conﬁgurations with varying monophenolase
and diphenolase activities [30]. Despite the high similarity that all type-3 proteins display in
the active sites, their substrate range di↵ers notably [33, 40].

The type-3 copper centre is often utilised for veriﬁcation and identiﬁcation of PPOs and has
been thoroughly characterised in literature. PPOs are characterised by exhibiting four discrete
oxidation states: deoxy-, oxy-, met-, and deact-state (Figure 1.6 A). The deoxy-PPO, a reduced

dicopper(I) state, binds molecular oxygen in a side-on bridging geometry to yield the oxy-state,
µ-⌘2:⌘2-peroxo-dicopper(II) (Figure 1.6 A) [41, 42, 43]. Upon oxygen saturation, the oxy-state
exhibits two characteristic ligand-to-metal charge transfer transitions that absorb at s345 nm
and s580 nm [44]. The absorption band at 580 nm is less intense and reported up to 20 fold
weaker than the band at 345 nm [41, 30, 42]. Hence, the O22- Cu(II) charge transfer transitions
enables identiﬁcation of type-3 centre with UV-Vis spectroscopy [41, 42, 43].
PPOs are primarily found in the met-state with approximately 10-15% in the oxy-state [45, 30].
In the met-form, a hydroxyl ion or water molecule is bound to dicopper(II) in a bridging
geometry (Figure 1.6 A) and yields no characteristic absorption. This state serves as a resting
state for type-3 copper centres and gives rise to a pronounced lag phase during in vitro utilisation
on monophenols [40, 46, 36]. The oxy-state is the primary oxidising state of PPOs, catalysing
oxidation of phenols by a monophenolase mechanism and catechols by a diphenolase mechanism
(Figure 1.7) [40, 47]. Given the majority of the enzymes are in the met-form, little to no activity
on monophenols is observed initially. To obtain the oxy-state a two-step reaction is required,
causing the lag period: initial conversion of the met-state to the deoxy-state through diphenolase
activity and subsequent binding of molecular oxygen (Figure 1.6 A) [48, 45].
Previously, the lag phase has posed challenges in accurately identifying and assessing monophe-
nolase activity in PPOs. However, in 2013 Garcia-Molina et al. showed that addition of hydro-
gen peroxide eliminates the lag phase, pushing the met-PPO into the oxy-PPO (Figure 1.7).
This way, the oxy-form can artiﬁcially be generated directly from the met-state, facilitating
assessment of monophenolase activity and UV-Vis identiﬁcation [49].
Phenols bind both the met- and oxy-state but are only oxidised by the oxy-state, while catechols
bind by both states and are oxidised by a two-electron oxidation. During oxidation of catechols,
the oxy-PPO is reduced to met-PPO that subsequently bind a new catechol, releasing an o-
quinone, water, and reduced deoxy-PPO. In the deoxy-form both copper atoms are reduced to
Cu(I) and is rapidly converted to the oxy-state by binding molecular oxygen (Figure 1.7) [40].

In the presence of molecular oxygen both phenols and catechols are oxidised by oxy-PPO to
the corresponding o-quinones through separate oxidative cycles, releasing either met-PPO or
deoxy-PPO. Whether the monooxygenation of phenols follows a single step mechanism, where
the oxy-PPO activity results directly in the release of the o-quinone, water, and deoxy-PPO, or
a two-step mechanism is unclear and studies show that the structure of the substrates inﬂuences
this [44, 10]. The two-step mechanism comprises release of the intermediates o-diphenol and
met-PPO followed by subsequent diphenolase step with a separate binding event by met-PPO
(Figure 1.7) [40]. The diphenolase reaction occurs much faster than the monophenolase reaction,
thus the hydroxylation of phenol is considered the rate-limiting step [41].
The catalytic cycle of PPOs is unconventional. Besides displaying dual catalytic activities and a
lag period in vitro, PPOs are slowly inactivated by the catechol substrates [50, 49]. Occasionally,
a catechol substrate is treated as a phenol during the oxidation by oxy-PPO. Consequently,
the catechol is oxidised following a monooxygenase mechanism, leading to formation of an
irreversible, deactivated copper site, deact-PPO (Figure 1.7). In the deact-state, one copper
atom is reduced to a Cu(0) state and may di↵use out of the active site, while the other copper
atom remain in the Cu(II) oxidation state [50]. This is a minor pathway that occur slowly,
however, over time it leads to enzymatic inactivation [40].

degrading ascomycetes display activity toward guaiacyl-like substrates [10]. Furthermore, three
of the six PPOs displayed the ability to demethoxylate and hydroxylate S-lignin type substrates,

activities remain unclear. Other approaches can be pursued to mitigate o-quinone formation,
such as the presence of reduction agents, reducing the formed quinones to catechols, or coupled-
enzymatic processes [10, 33, 55].

The scope of this project is to investigate the protein properties of three short fungal PPOs,

- Recombinant expression in Pichia pastoris of up to three short fungal PPOs and subse-

3.1
Recombinant Expression
In this project, we pursue two di↵erent expression strategies of the selected PPOs. Starting with
recombinant expression of a His6-tagged version of the enzymes, we aimed for high yields and
simple puriﬁcation. This approach proved unsuccessful, instead expression of the non-tagged
version of the proteins is pursued.
3.1.1
Expression of His-tagged PPOs
The three His6-tagged PPOs, MtPPO7, CgPPO-473, and MtPPO-809, are recombinantly ex-
pressed in Pichia pastoris X-33. We conduct three expressions attempts, evaluating the bu↵er-
exchanged crude extract each time with activity assays using 3-methoxycatechol, SDS-PAGE
analysis, and Western blotting.
Both MtPPO7 and MtPPO-809 are successfully expressed and visible on the SDS-Page gel
(Figure 3.1 left). MtPPO7 appears as a smear ranging from 40 kDa to approximately 100 kDa,
while MtPPO-809 displays a more deﬁned band at around 45 kDa. Both MtPPO7 and MtPPO-
809 appear above their predicted molecular weights, (Table 2.1) possibly due to glycosylation.
No band is observed in the crude protein for CgPPO-473 (Figure 3.1 left, lane 4).

Molecular Weight Determination with MALDI-TOF
To elucidate whether post-translational truncation of MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 has occurred,
part of the characterisation involves molecular weight determination of each PPO. SDS-Page
indicates that both SEC-puriﬁed PPOs exhibit signiﬁcant heterogeneity due to varying degree
of glycosylation. To ascertain if the PPOs undergo proteolytic cleavage, we employ MALDI-
TOF. Furthermore, we aim to obtain a relative measure of the glycosylation degree based on
the di↵erence between predicted mass and the detected masses of the glycosylated PPOs. It
is noted that the standards used for calibration are within the 5-17 kDa range; standards with
larger molecular weights are not available within the timeframe of the project.
For both glycosylated MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 no mass signals are detected, although we
assessed a range of di↵erent concentrations (Appendix A.7). Consequently, we conjecture that
the number of ionised proteins with the same molecular weight is too low for detection or that
extensive glycosylation limits ionisation. Despite CgPPO-473 precipitates upon deglycosylation,
a small fraction of the deglycosylated, soluble protein is applied to MALDI-TOF analysis but
yields no mass signals.
The deglycosylated MtPPO7 is successfully detected using MALDI-TOF, displaying a mass of
43,165.6 Da corresponding to [M + H]+ and a mass of 21,558.9 Da corresponding to [M + 2H]2+

3.3.2
Biophysical Studies with Mass Photometry
To investigate the heterogeneity of the glycosylated PPOs, we perform mass photometry (MP)
of MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473. Furthermore, this study investigates oligomeric state of PPOs in
solution and aims for molecular weights determinations to complement MALDI-TOF analysis.
In simple terms, MP measures a number of landing events (spots) originating from a measured
contrast, protein particles, which is subsequently converted to molecular weight. A Gaussian
curve is ﬁtted over the data to calculate the average molecular weight of each sample and
entails calculation of the standard deviation i.e., describing the heterogeneity of the sample.

copper-deﬁcient active sites.
The puriﬁed enzymes display low speciﬁc activities compared
to literature.
Additionally, UV-Vis spectroscopy shows weak absorption signals at 345 nm,
indicating low copper occupancy of the type-3 copper site and addition of copper sulfate to
activity assays is not optimal, as it entails slight chemical oxidation of the substrate, observed
in the enzyme-free control reactions.
As an alternative to direct copper sulfate addition, a brief literature-based copper saturation
procedure has been applied but yields limited improvement of the enzymatic activity [63]. This
protocol involves incubation of CuSO4:PPO in a 6:1 ratio for 30 minutes at 4◦C with subsequent
removal of free copper ions by diaﬁltration.

expression and puriﬁcation. Therefore, the protein solution is treated with ethylenediaminete-

3.3.9
E↵ect of pH on Monphenolase and Diphonalse Activity
In the literature, PPO activity and pH dependency are primarily determined by spectrophotom-
etry, quantifying the change in absorption due to formation of the product, often oligomers (Sec-
tion 3.3.8) [33, 65]. However, this assessment does not allow for distinction between monopheno-
lase and diphenolase activity of PPO-catalysed oxidation of monophenols. Therefore, we assess
the monophenolase and diphenolase activity of MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 based on substrate
consumption using HPLC-MS. We utilise the guaiacyl-like structures vanilic acid (VA) to assess
monophenolase activity and 3,4-dihydroxy-5-methoxybenzoic acid (DHMBA) to assess diphe-
nolase activity. These structures enable MS identiﬁcation with detected m/z ratios of 167 m/z
for VA and 183 m/z for DHMBA (structures depicted in Figure 3.26). To mitigate a lag phase
upon assessment of monophenolase activity, the reaction mixture comprised either 30 µM or 20
µM H2O2 for MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473, respectively.
Whether the monophenolase activity follows a single step or a two-step mechanism is under
discussion.
The single step mechanism entails direct formation of the o-quinone from the
monophenol (VA), whereas the two-step reaction releases the intermediate diphenolic com-
pound (DHMBA) before diphenolase activity forms the o-quinone (Figure 1.7). Both MtPPO7
and CgPPO-473 exhibit release of o-diphenol (DHMBA) indicating that the enzymes follow a
two-step mechanism using VA as substrate (Figure 3.26). In 2022, Kipouros et al. show that
the release of the intermediate o-diphenol is directed by the R-group in the para-position of the
substrate [44]. They demonstrate that the diphenol product of the monooxygenation reaction
is more likely to dissociate from the active site of the met-PPO when the substrate comprise a
electron-withdrawing para-substituents, such as -COOCH3. This aligns with the observations
of this study where the R-substituent in the para-position of VA is -COOH.

of the enzymes. The MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 catalysed consumption of VA display similar

sumption. The activities determined from the formation of DHMBA is approximately 50% of
the activities of the consumption; at pH 7.0 the activity of MtPPO7 is 8.5 ± 1.0 U/µmol and
for CgPPO-473 it is 7.2 ± 0.8 U/µmol. The lower rate of DHMBA accumulation than the rate
of VA consumption can be attributed to substrate competition, since the diphenolase activity
of the PPOs further converts DHMBA to the corresponding o-quinone. As more DHMBA is
formed, the PPOs can bind either VA or DHMBA depending on the oxidation state of the cop-
pers. For conversion of VA to occur, the oxy-PPO is required, which can either be formed due
to diphenolase activity of the met-PPO or artiﬁcially generated by H2O2. These data suggest
that MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 follow a two-step reaction mechanism (depicted in Figure 1.7),
conﬁrming the observations of Silva et al. (2024) [10].

3.4
Further Studies
3.4
Further Studies
The ﬁndings of this project give rise to various intriguing studies for future research. Pursuing
crystallisation of MtPPO7 aims to elucidate protein folding, residue orientation, and glycosy-
lation sites, while also identifying any truncations. A particularly interesting aspect would to
compare a crystal structure of a G- and S-active PPO, MtPPO7, with a crystal structure of a
G-active PPO, such as MtPPO-809. This might enable identiﬁcation of di↵erences in structural
folding and architecture of the active site. Understanding which residues determines selectivity
and speciﬁcity, the chemical environment of the active site, as well as binding of oxygen and sub-
strates would prove valuable for unlocking the potential of PPOs. Additionally, determination
of a PPO crystal structure in complex with its preferred substrates could facilitate enzymatic
engineering to improve monophenolase activity.
In this study, we show that MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 catalyse the G-like substrate VA by a
monophenolase activity that follows a two-step reaction, releasing the diphenolic intermediate.
A highly interesting approach is exploring how the presence of reducing agents may favour the
initial conversion of monophenols to o-diphenols, reducing the formation of the corresponding
o-quinones, as shown by Silva et al. (2024) [10]. It is further proposed to investigate perox-
ygenase activity of short fungal PPOs. As shown for guaiacol and VA, introduction of H2O2
shortens the lag phase, potentially through peroxygenase activity. Using 18O-labelled H2O2 en-
ables investigation of the peroxygenase activity and should be pursued. Finally, the mentioned
optimisations points of the performed studies in this project could be addressed.

Conclusion
4
Conclusion
In this project, we perform heterologous expression, puriﬁcation, and characterisation of two
short fungal polyphenol oxidases from lignocellulose-degrading ascomycetes, MtPPO7 from
Myceliophthora thermophila and CgPPO-473 from Chaetomium globosum, both able to catal-
yse ortho-hydroxylation of G- and S-type lignin units, generating identical products. Charac-
terisation of a third short fungal PPO active on only G-type lignin units, MtPPO-809 from
Myceliophthora thermophila, is attempted but prove inactive upon heterologous expression.
Initially, we show that heterologous expression in P. pastoris yields extensively N-glycosylated
MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473. N-glycosylation prove crucial for the solubility of CgPPO-473, as
the PPO precipitates upon deglycosylation, while MtPPO7 remains soluble.
MALDI-TOF
assessment of the deglycosylated MtPPO7 indicates a truncation of 1.7 kDa , measuring 43,166
Da compared to the predicted molecular weight of 44,869 Da, whereas mass photometry analysis
indicates an average molecular weight of 50 ± 2.4 kDa. Applying mass photometry on the
glycosylated PPOs indicates average molecular weight of 67 ± 3.1 kDa for MtPPO7 and between
50 ± 2.4 to 71 ± 3.4 for CgPPO-473, respectively, showing that both PPOs are monomers in
20 mM sodum acetate pH 6.0.
Utilising ICP-OES, we show that heterologous expression in P. pastoris produces MtPPO7 and
CgPPO-473 with copper-deﬁcient active sites, partially occupied by other divalent cations. A
copper saturation method for the puriﬁed enzymes is developed, achieving near-complete cop-
per loading of 91% and 87% copper content for MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473, respectively. The
presence of the type-3 copper centre typical for PPOs is conﬁrmed by UV-Vis spectroscopy,
measuring the characteristic band at 345 nm upon H2O2 saturation, converting the enzyme
to the oxy-state.
MtPPO7 displays high thermal stability assessed by nDSF with a ther-
mal midpoint of approximately 75◦C which is una↵ected by extensive N-glycosylations, while
glycosylated CgPPO-473 exhibits a thermal midpoint of 60◦C, indicating their potential for
high-temperature bioreﬁning processes.
Lastly, we demonstrate PPO activity on G lignin-units assessing MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473 on
guaiacol and vanilic acid and that both PPOs oxidise vanilic acid by a two-step mechanism,
releasing the intermediate o-diphenol, 3,4-dihydroxy-5-methoxybenzoic acid. The pH optimum
of the monophenolase activity is pH 7.0 for MtPPO7 and pH 6.0-7.0 for CgPPO-473.
For
diphenolase activity MtPPO7 displays pH optimum at 6.0, while pH has limited impact on
CgPPO-473. Both PPOs display a monophenolase-to-diphenolase ratio of approximately 0.45
at pH 7.0.
To conclude, the performed characterisation contributes with increased understanding of the
two short fungal PPOs, MtPPO7 and CgPPO-473.
