Abstract
Contamination of groundwater and surface water is a widely occurring issue due to human
activities. In order to comply with the legal requirements for assessment of risk from a con-
taminated site to surface water, it is essential to obtain a thourough understanding of the
contaminanted site and spreading of the contamination.
A multiple contaminant plume from a factory site discharging to Grindsted stream was mod-
elled by means of a conceptual site model (CSM) and simulations by a numerical model. For
construction of the CSM, existing geological, chemical and hydrological data were assessed
in combination with new data for hydraulic head (h) and hydraulic conductivity (K) acquired
through ﬁeld investigations. A conceptual model was built for redox species, chlorinated ethenes
(CEs) and pharmaceuticals. This lead to the formulation of a hypothesis for the propagation
of the plume, which suggested that the presence of a low permeability layer, most likely lignite,
causes the plume to behave diﬀerently over depth. While the upper plume discharges to the
stream in the expected location, the deep plume is deﬂected and migrates horizontally in the
western direction.
A numerical model was built to further investigate the ﬂow of the plume. From review of previ-
ous models, it was decided to build a local scale steady state model in COMSOL Multiphysics
to simulate the ﬂow by particle tracing, and to include groundwater-stream water interactions.
The best simulation of the observations for h was made with ﬁxed-head boundary conditions
on the western and eastern boundaries, allowing ﬂow through these boundaries, and implemen-
tation of a low permeability layer. The numerical model largely conﬁrmed the ﬁndings from
the CSM. Furthermore, it assisted the understanding of the medium depth plume, for which
the data was insuﬃcient. The results suggested that the plume is continuous across the entire
transect at the stream, which in turn implies that a very large mass of contamination is present
in the subsurface.
A risk assessment was conducted for stream water and groundwater resources, respectively.
This suggested that the stream is at risk from contamination of vinyl chloride (VC), and to a
minor degree also cis-dichloroethene (cis-DCE) and total sulfonamides. Regarding groundwater
resources, both cis-DCE, VC and ethyl carbamate constitute a risk. Although the observed
concentrations of total barbiturates and total sulfonamides in the transect are high, risk cannot
be assessed since no quality criteria are deﬁned for these compounds.
The project shows how a CSM and a numerical modelling tool applied in combination can
provide the foundation needed for performing a risk assessment for stream water as well as
groundwater resources. Furthermore, this approach can assist the formulation of an improved
CSM.

Sammenfatning
Forurening af grundvand og overﬂadevand er et udbredt problem som følge af menneskelige
aktiviteter. For at imødekomme lovmæssige krav om risikovurdering af forurenede grunde i
forhold til overﬂadevand, er det centralt at opn˚a en god forst˚aelse af den forurenede lokalitet
samt mulig spredning af forureningen.
En kompleks forureningsfane, der udsiver til Grindsted ˚a, blev modelleret vha. en konceptuel
model for lokaliteten samt simuleringer med en numerisk model. For at konstruere den kon-
ceptuelle model blev der anvendt eksisterende geologiske, kemiske og hydrologiske data, samt
nye feltm˚alinger for hydraulisk potentiale (h) og hydraulisk konduktivitet (K). En konceptuel
model blev lavet for hhv. redoxarter, chlorerede ethener og farmaceutiske stoﬀer. Dette førte
til formulering af en hypotese for udbredningen af forureningsfanen, der antydede at tilstede-
værelsen af et lag af lav permeabilitet, sandsynligvis lignit, for˚arsager forskellig udbredning af
fanen over dybden. Mens den øvre fane udsiver til ˚aen p˚a det forventede sted, bliver den dybe
fane afbøjet og migrerer horisontalt mod vest.
En numerisk model blev konstrueret for yderligere undersøgelse af fanens strømning. P˚a bag-
grund af evaluering af tidligere numeriske modeller blev det besluttet at konstruere en lokal
stationær model i COMSOL Multiphysics for at simulere strømningen vha. partikelbanebereg-
ninger. Endvidere blev interaktioner mellem grundvand og ˚aen inkluderet. Den bedste simu-
lering af h blev opn˚aet med en fastholdt rand mod vest og øst, samt ved implementering af et
lavpermeabelt geologisk lag. Den numeriske model bekræftede i store træk konklusionerne fra
den konceptuelle model. Desuden bidrog den til forst˚aelsen af den mellemdybe del af fanen,
hvor data var utilstrækkelige. Resultaterne indikerer, at fanen strækker sig over hele transektet,
hvilket igen tyder p˚a, at en stor masse af forurening ﬁndes i undergrunden.
Risikovurdering blev udført for hhv. ˚avand og grundvandsressourcer. Disse tydede p˚a, at ˚aen
er under risiko fra forurening med vinylchlorid (VC), samt i mindre grad cis-dichloroethen
(cis-DCE) og totale sulfonamider. Mht. grundvandsressourcer vurderes s˚avel cis-DCE, VC og
ethylcarbamat at udgøre en risiko. Selvom koncentrationerne af totale barbiturater og totale
sulfonamider var høje, er det ikke muligt at vurdere risikoen fra disse stoﬀer, eftersom intet
kriterie er fastsat.
Dette projekt viser, hvorledes en konceptuel model for en forurenet lokalitet i samspil med en
numerisk model kan udgøre basis for risikovurdering for˚avand s˚avel som grundvandsressourcer.
Desuden kan denne tilgang støtte formuleringen af en forbedret konceptuel model.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Chapter 1
Introduction
All around the world, ecosystems and water resources are under pressure due to human activi-
ties (Hinsby et al., 2008; Essaid et al., 2015). For instance, contamination of soil can lead to a
number of adverse eﬀects on the environment and human health. If contaminants are released
from soil to groundwater, transport of solutes may occur, which in turn may result in the re-
lease of pollutants to surface water. This has detrimental eﬀects on stream ecosystems (Roy
and Bickerton, 2012). Even compounds of low ecotoxicity have been observed to have negative
impacts on stream macroinvertebrates (Rasmussen et al., 2016).
Moreover, where multiple
contaminants are present, there is a risk that they may interact (McKnight et al., 2012). In
response to the threat to ecosystems and human health arising from surface water contamina-
tion, the European Commission has issued the EU Water Framework Directive (WFD), which
speciﬁes a number of quality criteria for lakes and streams that the member states must comply
with (European Commission, 2000; EQS Directive 2008/105/EC, 2008). Therefore, there is a
legal requirement for risk assessment of possible threats to surface water quality.
The formulation of a conceptual site model (CSM) is key to studying contamination and its
transport in an environmental system. The US EPA deﬁnes a CSM as:
”a three-dimensional picture of site conditions that conveys what is known or sus-
pected about the sources, releases and release mechanisms, contaminant fate and
transport, exposure pathways, potential receptors, and risks.” (US EPA, 2012).
Hence, it serves to summarise the current knowledge, while at the same time supporting the
identiﬁcation of what further information is required. Fulﬁlling the purposes of both a planning
instrument, a tool for modelling and data interpretation, and a means of communication, a
CSM constitutes an important element in any contaminated site investigation (US EPA, 2010;
Albright, 2012). Several studies have looked into the use of conceptual models as hypotheses
for transport of contaminants, applying modelling tools to evaluate the likelyhood of each
hypothesis (Troldborg et al., 2010; Thomsen et al., 2016). Pirot et al. (2015) have looked into
the use of CSMs for contaminant transport forecasting where geological data is sparse, which
is a common problem within the ﬁeld.
This study aims to construct a conceptual model to form a hypothesis for the distribution and
transport of contamination in Grindsted, Denmark. Grindsted stream is subject to extensive
and complex contamination from various sources. These include an old landﬁll and a factory
site on either side of the stream (NIRAS, 2009). The contamination of the stream is predom-
inantly pharmaceutical compounds and chlorinated ethenes, which were formerly produced
1
==PAGE:18==
(e.g. barbiturates, sulfonamides) or employed in production processes (e.g. perchloroethene,
trichloroethene) at the factory site (Rasmussen et al., 2016), or degradation products formed
in the plume (e.g cis-dichloroethene, vinyl chloride). These chemicals can be considered risk
drivers for stream water and groundwater resources.
Due to the severity of the contamination, both the stream and the sources have been subject
to numerous investigations, starting in the early 1970s. Recent examples include COWI A/S
(2011), Orbicon (2013) and Orbicon (2017), along with several studies by Department of En-
vironmental Engineering, DTU, and academic partners (Rønde et al., 2016; Rasmussen et al.,
2016). However, the comprehensive data arising from recent ﬁeld campaigns has not until now
been synthesised into a CSM, describing the current state of the contamination.
As a part of the previous research, a number of groundwater models were developed with the
purpose of describing the migration of contaminants in groundwater and their discharge to
the stream (Alectia, 2015; Petersen et al., 2010; Balbarini et al., 2017; Nicolajsen, 2014). The
present study will adapt an existing numerical 3-D model describing the inﬂuence of meanders
on groundwater discharge to streams (Balbarini et al., 2017). Adaptations will be made ac-
cording to the ﬁndings from the CSM. Furthermore, ﬁeld measurements will be conducted to
estimate the hydraulic conductivity (K) and hydraulic head (h) in a control plane adjacent to
the stream. Since especially the parameter K is of major importance for groundwater ﬂow in
aquifers, the population of the model with K-values veriﬁed by observations will improve the
reliability of the model. Finally, the model results will form the basis for assessing the risk for
stream water and groundwater resources.
Aside from improving the understanding of the speciﬁc site investigated, this study also has a
broader relevance within the ﬁelds of soil contamination and groundwater-surface water inter-
action. It provides an example of how a conceptual model can be constructed and applied in a
case with the complicating factors of a highly sinuous stream, complex geology and a multiple
contaminant plume.
The objectives of this thesis are to:
• Assess existing data to built a conceptual site model for Grindsted, which combines the
hydrogeology and the contamination from the factory site.
• Acquire new data for hydraulic conductivity and hydraulic head. This has the purpose of
supporting the hydrogeological model and populating the numerical model with reliable
hydraulic properties.
• Review previous numerical models of Grindsted, and evaluate which approaches are most
suitable for a new numerical model.
• Build a numerical model to describe the groundwater ﬂow and transport of the contami-
nant plume, and its discharge to Grindsted stream.
• Perform a risk assessment of the stream and groundwater resources based on the concep-
tual model and the simulations.

The site Svinget 12 (black star) formerly served as a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP)

2.1
History of contamination
Production of a wide range of chemicals has taken place at the factory site at the north eastern
outskirts of Grindsted since 1914. These have included explosives, pharmaceuticals and en-
zymes for the food industry. The bulk of the contamination is expected to have occurred over
approximately two decades starting around 1960, due to high production and limited regulation
in this period (Kr¨uger A/S, 1992).
Aside from pollution originating from production activities on the site itself, emission and
deposition of waste at external locations also constitute a threat.
In the area relevant to
Grindsted stream, two such sites are of relevance: A waste deposit north of the factory site
(Banegravsdepotet), and a wastewater ditch connecting the factory site with Grindsted stream
(Aﬂøbsgrøften) (Kr¨uger A/S, 1992; NIRAS, 2009). This implies that contaminants discharging
to the stream through groundwater may originate from a large upstream area.
In total, at least 1160 diﬀerent compounds have been employed or produced over the course
of the production. Of these, NIRAS (2009) estimated that 306 compounds potentially pose
a risk to groundwater, while this is true for 349 compounds in stream water.
Hence, the
contamination sources are highly complex. According to Ejlskov A/S (2005), the factory site is
not well-characterised in terms of composition and source strength, in spite of being the main
contamination source.
2.2
Geology
Grindsted is situated west of the former location of the Weichsel glacier, and the upper geology
is therefore characterised by the sandy deposits of an outwash plain. At a regional scale, the
upper 100 m can be divided into three overall formations, based on a thorough investigation
conducted near Grindsted landﬁll. From the top down, the formations consist of: Quarternary
Weichselian sand, Tertiary Miocene sand and ﬁnally Tertiary Miocene clay. The latter forms a
barrier of high hydraulic conductivity, and hence constitutes a separation between the upper and
lower aquifer (Heron et al., 1998). This overall geological structure is illustrated in Figure 2.2.
As shown, an additional Miocene sand deposit exists under the conﬁning clay layer (Bastrup
formation) (Petersen et al., 2010). At a local scale, heterogeneities are found in the sand bodies,
consisting of gravel, clay/silt lenses and lignite (Heron et al., 1998).
Kr¨uger A/S (1992) have composed a geological model with a focus on the area north of Grind-
sted stream by studying the bore logs of particularly deep wells between the factory site and
the stream. This is a less detailed study than Heron et al. (1998), but of more relevance for the
study site. The ﬁndings are largely in accordance with the illustration in Figure 2.2. However,
the top of the Arnum formation is estimated to be somewhat higher in the 1992 investigation,

Grindsted stream has a catchment of about 200 km2 (Rasmussen et al., 2016). The sandy

a lignite layer is aﬀecting the groundwater ﬂow.

TCE can be further broken down to dichloroethene (DCE), which exists in the three isomers:
1,1-DCE, cis-1,2-DCE and trans-1,2-DCE. The cis-form has been related to microbial action,
and is most frequently observed in contaminant plumes. cis-DCE can in turn degrade to vinyl
chloride (VC), whose degradation product is ethene. The entire series of degradation steps
can occur through a process known as reductive dechlorination, which involves the stepwise
replacement of a Cl atom with an H atom. This is a microbial process where the CE is used
as a terminal electron acceptor during respiration, and is probably most important for PCE
and TCE. Alternatively, cometabolic reactions may occur, where no energy is obtained by the
microbe (Alvarez and Illman, 2005). As the name suggests, reductive dechlorination occurs
in anaerobic aquifers, and hence the redox conditions should be assessed when considering the
degradation of CEs in a contaminant plume. The level of redox condition necessary for reductive
dechlorination varies among the CEs, with more reducing conditions needed to degrade the
lighter CEs. The conditions are listed in the rightmost column of Table 2.1 (Stroo and Ward,
2010).
As the molar weight of the CEs decreases, and thereby the level of chlorination, the water
solubility increases and the log Kow decreases. This implies that the degradation products are
less hydrophobic than the mother compounds, and thereby more mobile in aquifers.

quality criteria are deﬁned for both drinking water and surface water (with the exception of

2.4.2
Pharmaceuticals
Barbiturates are a group of medical drugs which were widely employed in the past as narcotics,
sedatives, and anaesthetics. Their consumption was high in the period 1920-1950, but they
have now largely been phased out (Fourati et al., 2016). Three species which are detected in
high concentrations in Grindsted are listed in Table 2.1.
Sulfonamides have been widely employed as antibiotic agents for about 50 years (Bia lk-Bieli´nska
et al., 2012). Currently, they are mainly used as veterinary drugs, for which they remain in
frequent use (Vila-Costa et al., 2017). The top three sulfonamides are selected due to their
high occurrence at the study site, while sulfadiazine is included because it has a limit value in
surface water.
Ethyl carbamate, also known as ethyl urethane or simply urethane, has been used as an anaes-
thetic and as a neoplastic agent in the treatment of leukaemia. However, due to its mutagenic
and carcinogenic eﬀects, this practise has ceased, however, the compound remains in use as
an anaesthetic for laboratory animals. As a side note, it has been found to occur naturally in
fermented food products, such as bread and alcoholic beverages (Schlatter and Lutz, 1990).
Barbiturates, sulfonamides and ethyl carbamate generally have high water solubilities in the
range of several thousand mg/L. This implies that they are highly mobile in groundwater.
Sulfadiazine represents an exception with a reported water solubility of only 77 mg/L. Fur-
thermore, they all seem to be moderately to highly persistent in the environment, based on
degradation models (not shown) (DTU Food, 2017). Experimental results indicate that some
sulfonamides may be sensitive to photolysis, and they have also been demonstrated to degrade
both in lake water (Zhang et al., 2013) and in setups where benthic stream bioﬁlm was in-
troduced (Vila-Costa et al., 2017). However, neither of these pathways are relevant during
transport in an aquifer. According to (Bia lk-Bieli´nska et al., 2012), some species of sulfon-
amides can be sensitive to hydrolysis, but due to pH-speciation, this is especially true at low
pH. The rate is therefore not expected to be high in a groundwater aquifer. Hence, it seems
probable that only very limited degradation of the pharmaceuticals will occur before discharge
to Grindsted stream.
2.4.3
BTEX
Benzene, toluene, ethyl benzene and the three isomers of xylene constitute the category ’BTEX’.
They are typically associated with petroleum products such as gasoline and diesel (Bedient
et al., 1999), and are therefore not speciﬁc to activities at the factory site, as opposed to the
pharmaceuticals, for instance. Therefore, it is part of the delimitation of this study that they
are not directly included in the CSM construction. They are, however, present in the focus area
and would aﬀect the redox conditions when degrading in the aquifer. Hence, they are relevant
for the discussion of CE occurrence and fate, and a brief background will therefore be provided
in this section.

BTEX were probably formerly present in the factory site plume. A spill of toluene has been
recorded in the mid-western part of the factory site. Also, investigations of the factory site
in 1990 found several zones where respectively benzene, toluene and BTX-compounds were
detected in the pore air (Kr¨uger A/S, 1992). Hence, there is evidence that BTEX have been
released from the factory site. Other sources could be accidental releases from service stations
or other places where car fuels are handled (Bedient et al., 1999).
Similarly to chlorinated solvents, BTEX are poorly soluble in water and have high log Kow
values. They can therefore be classiﬁed as NAPLs. However, since their density is lower than
that of water, they are light nonaqueous phase liquids, LNAPLs, and to not sink below the
water table. Rather, when encountering the groundwater table, they form a pool on top of
it and release a dissolution plume from here. Biodegradation is a key process for the fate of
dissolved BTEX in aquifers, as they are readily biodegradable under both aerobic and anaerobic
conditions (Bedient et al., 1999). This would in turn contribute to more reducing conditions in
the aquifer.

CHAPTER 3. DATA COLLECTION AND PROCESSING METHODS
Chapter 3
Data collection and processing methods
The following chapter will explain the processing of existing as well as new data to construct a
conceptual model for the study site.
Building a conceptual model of the contaminant plume involves two major components: ground-
water ﬂow pattern and solute concentration. Hydraulic head (h) data can provide insight into
groundwater ﬂow directions, since water will tend to move down any pressure gradient. How-
ever, the hydraulic conductivity (K) is governing for ﬂow, since it varies by many orders of
magnitude in geological sediments.
Data for solute concentrations can provide information
regarding the current distribution of the plume. Furthermore, it can indicate the occurence or
absence of certain transformation processes.
Regarding chemical parameters, much data is already available for both groundwater and
stream water. Furthermore, hydraulic head data exist from manual soundings of wells, and
in some cases from data loggers permanently installed in the wells. The stream water level was
formerly measured continuously at two gauging stations: Tingvejen (station 31.28), about 3
km upstream of the focus area, and Eg Bro (station 31.14) at about 7.5 km downstream. Eg
Bro station is still in operation, whereas Tingvejen station closed in 2015.
Estimates of K-values for sediments near the landﬁll have been conducted by Bjerg et al. (1995)
among others, however, no thorough analysis of hydraulic conductivity in the focus area has
been done previously. It is therefore the aim to validate or reﬁne the K-values suggested by
Bjerg et al. (1995) through ﬁeld investigations at the stream site.

Previous modelling of Grindsted
A substantial body of research and models for Grindsted already exists, regarding both the
landﬁll, the factory site and the stream. The following section will brieﬂy outline which mod-
elling approaches have been implemented so far for the factory site plume and its discharge to
Grindsted stream. Five previous models are described, and key features and ﬁndings from each
are listed in Table 4.1 at the end of Section 4.2. Finally, it will be discussed which shortcomings
the current project can address.
4.1
Regional scale models
A transient hydraulic model was set up by Petersen et al. (2010) (Grontmij). It covers the
entire town of Grindsted as well as some of the surrounding area, and includes 9 geological
layers in the vertical direction.
Hence, it can be considered a regional scale model.
The
grid size ranges from 50 x 50 m near the boundaries to 25 x 25 m in the central area of the
model domain. No low-permeability layer between the Quarternary and Tertiary deposits is
included. After calibration, the hydraulic conductivity of the two upper layers are 13003 m/yr
for the Quarternary deposit, and 14496 m/yr for the Odderup formation. This corresponds to
respectively 4.12 · 10−4 m/s and 4.60 · 10−4 m/s. Particle tracing was conducted to identify the
most likely location of discharge zones of contamination.
The results indicated that the plume from the factory site mainly travels in the upper 14 m of
groundwater, assuming that the source zone is at ground level. In the case of free-phase DNAPL
inﬁltration, the plume would be much more distributed over depth. For the ﬁrst scenario, the
discharge zone was from the upper end of Engsøen to a few hundred metres upstream, while the
estimated travel time (retardation disregarded) would be 30-40 years. For the second scenario,
the discharge zone would be further downstream, mainly in Engsøen, and the travel time was
estimated to be within the range of 35-40 years.
The hydraulic model built by Alectia in 2013 is likewise used for particle tracing from the
known sources in Grinsted town (Alectia, 2013). The model domain is somewhat smaller than
the previous model, only about 3 km across, in contrast to almost 10 km for Grontmij’s model.
It actually consists of two interlinked models, one of regional scale with grid sizes of 100 m,
and a local scale model with grid sizes of 10 m. Hence, this model is more spatilly reﬁned
than the previous, but can still be considered of regional scale. It is built as both a stationary

and transient model, where the latter uses time series of input data for the period 1990-2010,
for instance data for water abstraction. This includes abstraction from the factory site itself,
which was probably mainly relevant during the 90s (Alectia, 2013).
According to the results, the discharge zone of factory site contamination seems to be further
upstream than suggested by Petersen et al. (2010), starting just upstream of Vestre Boulevard
and ending upstream of Søndre Ringvej. The same two scenarios for source zones are investi-

Alectia (2013) also ﬁnd that it does not penetrate the Arnum formation.

is found that the uncertainty from parameter estimation exceeds the variation of the dynamic
input. In terms of improvement of the model, it is recommended to model the propagation
of the plume by solute transport rather than particle tracing, since this approach can incor-
porarate processes such as dispersion, retardation and degradation, whereas particle tracing
can only simulate conservative transport. Furthermore, it is suggested to implement geological
heterogeneity.
The suggestion of incorporating solute transport into the model was realised by Alectia (2015)
two years later. In this model, the domain boundaries are designed based on a hydropotential
map, and hence have a more ’organic’ shape than in the previous two models. This design
is also reﬂected in the boundary conditions. Similarly to the previous model, the new model
is also set up to be transient.
The geology is accounted for to great depth and is highly
reﬁned, containing sub-layers of several of the major formations. For instance, the Arnum clay
is subdivided into three layers. In total, 11 layers are deﬁned in the regional domain, with
an additional 19 geological layers deﬁned locally. It is noted that a layer of moraine clay is
included within the Quarternary sand deposit. The grid is even ﬁner than the previous model
from Alectia, the local model cells being in the range of 2-3 m.
Again, it is found that the ﬂow directions in the Quarternary deposit and Odderup formation
mostly remain the same regardless of groundwater levels, which vary over the course of the
year. Also, the vertical ﬂux patterns around the stream were looked into for geological layers
at various depth. It is seen that the stream functions as a drainage pathway at a regional scale,
and that the magnitude of the upward ﬂux diminishes with increasing depth, while the area of
upward ﬂux increases. Travel times are not reported by Alectia (2015).
4.2
Local scale models
The transient model built by Nicolajsen (2014) deviates from those described above in a number
of ways.
Firstly, it is of stream reach scale, which is much more local than the previous
models. The focus is on groundwater-stream interactions, and the eﬀect of the meanders is of
particular interest. Hence, the area of interest is focused around known zones of groundwater
discharge to the stream (Nielsen et al., 2014), and the sources are not included in the model
domain. Furthermore, the model is restricted over depth to only include the geological layers
of the Quarternary deposit and the Odderup formation. That is, the Arnum clay formation is
16
==PAGE:33==
CHAPTER 4. PREVIOUS MODELLING OF GRINDSTED
considered to constitute a barrier to the contamination, which is in line with ﬁndings from the
previous models and also ﬁeld observations Orbicon (2013).
The aquifer is assumed to consist of Quarternary sand. One scenario furthermore explores the
eﬀect of implementing a clay disk in the centre of the domain at depth 10 m below terrain.
Hydraulic anisotropy is incorporated by deﬁning two separate hydraulic conductivities for hori-
zontal and vertical ﬂow. For Quarternary sand, the horizontal hydraulic condictivity (Kh) is set
to 30 m/d, corresponding to 3.47 · 10−4 m/s, while the vertical hydraulic conductivity (Kv) is
one order of magitude lower. For the clay layer, Kh is set to 5·10−3 m/d, or 5.8·10−7 m/s, and
Kv is likewise an order of magnitude lower. Similarly to Alectia’s 2015 model, the boundaries
are designed based on a hydropotential map, with boundary conditions of respectively time
varying head or no-ﬂow. Solute transport is not included.
The results suggest that the eﬀect of the stream meanders are of major importance for the
ﬂow direction when the plume approaches the vicinity of the stream. By investigating the
ﬂow velocity through the stream bed and -sides, it is observed that a higher proportion of the
incoming ﬂux seems to enter the stream on the outside of meander bends than on the inside.
Particle tracing from diﬀerent depths shows that the streamlines tend to concentrate on the
outside of the meanders. Furthermore, the presence of the clay layer is found to alter the ﬂow
patterns. The trajectories of particles above the clay layer are barely aﬀected, while those of
particles below the clay layer are deﬂected and enter the stream much further downstream.
The model by Nicolajsen (2014) has been further developed by Balbarini et al. (2017) for the
purpose of elucidating the eﬀect of stream meanders on groundwater ﬂow. The model outputs
are studied in the context of three synthetic cases containing streams of various sinuosity.
In addition, the computational eﬃciency of the transient model is drastically improved by
the implementation of a coordinate transformation method which eliminates the need to re-
create the mesh for every time step. This is necessary with an unconﬁned aquifer, where the
groundwater table is subject to changes between each time step. Aside from making the model
more computationally sophisticated, this study also resulted in an improved understanding
of the 3-dimensional ﬂow patterns around the stream, and how these are aﬀected by annual
variations in groundwater hydraulic head and stream water level.

4.3
Approaches for a new model
As demonstrated in this summary of previously conducted modelling work, many diﬀerent
approaches have already been skilfully applied in the modelling of contamination transport in
Grindsted. However, a number of opportunities for further work remain. Since the work of
Nicolajsen (2014) and Balbarini et al. (2017), a large amount of new data has been produced,
including contaminant concentrations in groundwater and surface water between the factory
site and the stream. No such chemical data was considered by Nicolajsen (2014) or Balbarini
et al. (2017). In combination with hydraulic head data, these chemical data provide a new and
improved framework for formulating a conceptual site model for the propagation of the plume
and its discharge to the stream.
Other conceptual changes could also be of relevance. For instance, none of the above models
work with a conceptual model which includes a continuous lignite layer, in spite of such a layer
appearing in most bore logs of the area. This is particularly the case near the stream, where
the layer also seems to be of greatest thickness. Lignite diﬀers from clay in having a hydraulic
conductivity about one order of magnitude higher than the clay implemented by Nicolajsen
(2014) (Lønborg et al., 2006). If it does indeed extend over the entire model domain, as the
bore logs indicate, then omitting the lignite layer would result in high conceptual uncertainty.
Therefore, an approach similar to that of Thomsen et al. (2016) and Troldborg et al. (2010)
can be employed to investigate the most likely geological conﬁguration: several CSMs are
formulated, where each constitutes a hypothesis for the geology at the site. Then, it can be
tested which model best reproduces the observations, indicating that this geological model best
describes the site.
Regarding the choice of boundary conditions, the works of Nicolajsen (2014) and Balbarini
et al. (2017) both employ no-ﬂow boundaries at the eastern and western end of the model
domain. This is meaningful considering that the model domain is based on a hydropotential
map. However, knowing that the head is far from constant over depth near the stream, it could
be argued that the no-ﬂow condition is not valid over the entire depth of the model domain.
As also observed by Balbarini et al. (2017), the eﬀect of the stream seems to vary with aquifer
thickness, such that the ﬂow at the bottom of a relatively deep aquifer may be characterised
mainly by the regional ﬂow. In the case of Grindsted, the regional ﬂow would exit through
the western side of the model domain, but a no-ﬂow condition prevents this from happening.
Hence, such a condition may force a ’false’ ﬂow to the stream even from the deep layers of the
aquifer.
According to Alectia (2013), the most accurate description of the plume is produced by im-
plementing solute transport rather than particle tracing, since many contaminants are subject
to degradation, dispersion and retardation. However, since these processes are not considered
important in a local scale steady state model, particle tracing will be used. This decision will
be a point for discussion.
As demonstrated, previous modelling work has included stationary as well as transient models.
The conceptual model of this thesis builds on chemical data from October 2016. However, the
solute concentrations found in the water samples are not simply a reﬂection of the hydraulic
conditions at the time of sampling, but are a result of much more long-term processes, consid-
ering that the plume travel time is in the order of 50 years (Petersen et al., 2010; Alectia, 2013),
and that the factory site has been in operation for over 100 years Kr¨uger A/S (1992). There-
fore, in conceptual terms, the factory site can be considered more of a constant source than a

At a local scale, Balbarini et al. (2017) found that the discharge may vary in magnitude over
the year, but that the location of the main zones is consistent.

5.6
Mesh size and water balance
The ﬁner the mesh in a numerical model, the ﬁner the spacial resolution of the results. However,
reﬁning the resolution also increases the computational time. Therefore, deciding the grid size
is always a compromise between accuracy and time required. As an additional challenge, the
resolution of the top boundary of the model domain is observed to aﬀect the accuracy of the
water balance. In a stationary model, the water balance dictates that inﬂow must equal outﬂow,
since there can be no storage. Hence, it has the form of equation 5.17:
QGRWin + R −QGRWout −Qstream = 0
(5.17)
where Q [m3/s] is a ﬂow rate through the outer boundary, and the subscripts denote respectively
incoming groundwater ﬂow, outgoing groundwater ﬂow, or discharge of groundwater to the
stream. R is the recharge rate, speciﬁed to 0.5 m/yr. When the grid is coarse, the model
cannot handle the ﬂow in the uppermost layer where the ﬂow lines are close together. Water
therefore escapes out of the top of the model domain before reaching the stream. This leads
to an apparent deﬁciency when calculating the incoming ﬂux through the top boundary, since
the forced overﬂow cancels out part of the recharge condition. However, reﬁning the grid in the
top boundary of the model domain improves the modelling of ﬂow in the uppermost aquifer.

6.1
Hydrogeology
Review of previous investigations of Grindsted indicates diﬀerences in the interpretations of
geology. In particular, there is contradictory information regarding the nature and extent of
a possibly conﬁning layer at depth 15-25 m. This corresponds to the interface between the
Tertiary and the Quarternary deposits (Heron et al., 1998; Kr¨uger A/S, 1992). Such a layer,
consisting of lignite or sometimes clay, is observed in many boreholes of the area, but seems to
be absent at the former wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) at Svinget 12 (Orbicon, 2017).
Two boreholes are reported to merely contain pieces of lignite. However, since no geological
description of the samples was conducted at this site, it is possible that the lignite layer also
exists at this point, but has simply not been reported. This is supported by a large diﬀerence
in observed hydraulic head between the two screens in well 114.2567. Over a vertical distance
of only 5 m, the hydraulic head increases by about 1 m with depth. This could be explained by
the presence of a low permeability layer in between the screens, and this coincides with where
’lignite pieces’ are reported in the bore log. However, a similar sudden change in hydraulic
head is not observed in well 114.2568, although pieces of lignite are also reported in this bore
log.
Due to the nature of well F1 at Svinget 12, which is a geoprobe well, no geological
report is available, but it was observed that no water sample could be taken at 12-13 m below
ground surface (mbgs), This conﬁrms the presence of low permeability sediment. This in good
accordance with the position of the lignite layer in well 114.2567, and supports a geological
model which includes a low permeability layer in the top of the Tertiary deposits.

The assessment of hydrogeological data suggested that the ﬂow directions diﬀer on either side

is to some extent ’shielded’ from the inﬂuence of the stream. This would in turn imply that the
plume behaves diﬀerently depending on the depth. In the upper part of the aquifer, it would

However, in the deeper part of the aquifer, the plume would be ’dragged’ further to the west,

the plume is currently discharging to the stream.
Since the groundwater ﬂow is known to be mainly horizontal at well 114.1447, see Figure 6.4,

dissolved PCE release, and additionally, pools on top of clay lenses may have functioned as a
more substantial reservoir of separate phase PCE. As stated before, however, these reservoirs
are probably depleted at this point in time.

6.2.3
Pharmaceuticals
Figure 6.7 shows a conceptual model for the plume of pharmaceuticals, also illustrated by a
plan view, a longitudinal cross-section and a transectional cross-section. The occurrence of
pharmaceuticals generally matches the shape of the CE plume, with high concentrations in the
focus area above the lignite layer, and lower concentrations below the lignite layer.
As stated in Section 2.4.2, the pharmaceuticals discharging to Grindsted stream are not consid-
ered to undergo signiﬁcant degradation during transport. Furthermore, since they are highly
hydrophilic (DTU Food, 2017) and the aquifer is poor in organic matter (Heron et al., 1997),
retention by sorption is not expected to be of signiﬁcance. This implies that the pharmaceutical
plume is mostly subject to conservative transport.
Barbiturates and sulfonamides are distributed similarly, indicating that they originate from the
same source(s). Therefore, they are conceptually synthesised and shown as one colour (red).
Below the factory site, they are distributed over the entire depth. It is not obvious why this
is the case, since they are not DNAPLs (see properties in Table 2.1 on page 8), but travel
dissolved in the water phase. As the ﬂow direction below the factory site has been shown to
have practically no vertical component, the distribution over depth cannot be explained by
transport by advection under the present conditions. However, in a study of leachate migration
from Grindsted landﬁll, Kjeldsen et al. (1998) found that the high density of the leachate seemed
to cause substatial vertical transport in the aquifer. A similar eﬀect is most likely involved at
the factory site.
Another factor which may inﬂuence the plume propagation is alterations of the groundwater
ﬂow itself.
As reported by Alectia (2013), substantial groundwater abstraction took place
within the factory site during the 1990s. This may have caused a disturbance in the regional
groundwater ﬂow suﬃcient to aﬀect the vertical distribution of contaminants at the site.
Regarding ethyl carbamate, the distribution varies from that of barbiturates and sulfonamides.
In Figure 6.7, it is displayed as purple. It is almost entirely absent in the deep plume at well
114.1495, see transect diagram in Figure 6.7. In the focus area, however, it is detected at
concentrations of several hundred µg/L, and even at 1600 µg/L in two well screens. When
comparing to the CE plume, the high ethyl carbamate concentration in well 114.1448-5 stands
out. This could indicate that ethyl carbamate originates from a diﬀerent source zone.

7.1
Assumptions and applicability
A number of assumption must be made in order to build any model. The assumptions for the
current CSM and COMSOL model, as well as the applicability of the models, will be discussed
in this section. Also, the numerical model will be compared to previous models for Grindsted.
7.1.1
Conceptual model
The interpretation of bore log data to formulate a conceptual geological model for the focus area
leads to some of the most important assumptions in the conceptual site model, since geology
highly aﬀects the ﬂow. The designation of the low permeability layer as lignite rather than
partially or entirely clay is perhaps debatable. However, in the present model, deﬁning this
layer as a particular sediment type only serves the purpose of selecting a suitable set of K values
for the numerical model, and these are anyway calibrated in the numerical model. The extent
and thickness of the layer is therefore more crucial for the behaviour of the plume. Based on
the currently available geological data, it is uncertain whether the layer is continuous between
Svinget 12 and well 114.1447, and its extent is also unknown between well 114.1447 and the
factory site. The presence of a continuous layer starting near the factory site would lead to a
higher degree of separation of the plume over depth. In consequence, there may be diﬀerences
in ﬂow velocity which are not considered in the current CSM.
As stated in Chapter 6, the conceptual delineation of the plume is rather weak.
In order
to establish a stronger foundation for determining the width of the plume, more chemical
data would be necessary.
The location of well 114.1447 between the factory site and the
stream seems to be appropriate for additional samples. It has a suﬃcient distance from the
source that substantial contaminant concentrations can still be found, and it is located far
enough from the stream that the groundwater ﬂow is horizontal throughout the entire aquifer.
Additional boreholes placed at the same isopotential line as 114.1447 would therefore improve
the knowledge of the plume width.

7.1.2
Numerical model
The conceptual site model has been successfully applied to built a numerical model in the
software COMSOL Multiphysics. In this process, simpliﬁcations of the study site are necessary
in order to model it numerically. One such simpliﬁcation is that of the geological geometry,
which is particularly relevant for the lignite layer.
It is modelled as consistently 10 m in
thickness, but in reality, it is probably much thinner in some places, or possibly altogether
absent. Such heterogeneities would greatly aﬀect the local ﬂux across the lignite layer. If, for
instance, holes or thinner zones exist, the deep plume might cross to the upper aquifer more
easily than expected. It would therefore be beneﬁcial for furture modelling projects to have
access to more comprehensive geological information. This could be obtained by additional
boreholes or, perhaps by geophysical methods.
The previous Grindsted models covered in Chapter 4 vary greatly in scale. The models by
Petersen (2012) and Alectia (2013, 2015) can be characterised as regional, whereas those of
Nicolajsen (2014) and Balbarini et al. (2017) are more local. The new model is closer to the
latter, although the model domain is extended to accomodate assimilation of data from wells
114.1447 (north) and 114.1495 (west). It therefore has the beneﬁt of higher accuracy than the
regional scale models due to a ﬁner resolution, which results in more reliable modelling of the
focus area. Furthermore, it includes the eﬀects of stream-groundwater interactions on the ﬂow,
58

similarly to the local scale models, and is thereby able to model the discharge to the stream. In
contrast to the previous local models, the new model focuses only on the ﬂow from the factory
site. Therefore, in its current form, it should only be applied for simulation of transport of
contaminants from the northern direction.
The scale of the numerical models described also applies in the vertical direction. The early,
regional models tended to include many more geological formations than the more recent local
models.
Since investigations have shown that the Arnum clay forms an eﬃcient barrier to
contamination (Orbicon, 2013), treating this formation as a model domain boundary saves an
immense amount of computational power, without compromising the reliability of the results.
In contrast to most previous models, the current model is built as steady state. In reality,
however, it is more accurate to describe it as a ’pseudo steady state’ model, since a fundamental
assumption is that the plume does not change signiﬁcantly over a 5-year period. This is the
time frame over which the chemical and hydrological data was produced. If the purpose were
to model the movement of the plume over time, this would not be suﬃcient, however, it seems
appropriate for modelling the current ﬂow and discharge of the plume.
Concerning the temporal applicability, the model should be valid as long as no major changes
in precipitation or abstraction alter the groundwater ﬂow or the stream water level from that
of 2014. However, the model may lose its relevance when the plume has passed. This limits
the practical applicability to in the order of 40 years into the future. By this point, the plume
has most probably passed beyond the model domain.
All investigated previous models employed particle tracing, but it was suggested by Alectia
(2013) that solute transport would be more appropriate. Particle tracing assumes conservative
transport with no degradation, dispersion or retardation of the plume. In a steady state model,
such as the current, retardation is not relevant. Regarding degradation, it is highly relevant
for a number of the contaminants.
These include the CEs, which are observed to degrade
to a large extent. Furthermore, BTEX and other readily biodegradable hydrocarbons would
also be relevant to model by solute transport, especially considering their impact on the redox
conditions. On the other hand, the pharmaceuticals are expected to be highly persistent during
groundwater transport.
Finally, the process of dispersion could also be included with solute transport. Dispersion causes
the plume to spread in all three directions to diﬀerent degrees. Usually, the following sequence
of dispersivity magnitudes can be assumed: longitudinal ≫transversal > vertical. Bjerg et al.
(2011) compared the results from several landﬁll plumes, and found that the longitudinal dis-
persivity is generally in the order of metres, while the transversal and vertical dispersivities are
in the order of milimeters. In the context of transport between the factory site and the stream,
other factors are probably more signiﬁcant for the spreading of the plume. Most prominently,
the character of the factory site as a long-term source implies that contaminants are released
continuously over a long period of time, rather than as a pulse. Therefore, it is not really
meaningfull to include longitudinal dispersion. Regarding transversal and vertical dispersion,
it does not seem relevant until the plume has been delineated more accurately.
In conclusion, solute transport is probably most relevant for the degradable compounds, or in
case of building a regional scale model, where dispersion would have greater impact. At the
scale of the current model and due to its quality as steady state, particle tracing should suﬃce
for modelling transport of the contaminant plume.

7.2. COMPARISON OF CONCEPTUAL AND NUMERICAL MODELLING RESULTS
7.2
Comparison of conceptual and numerical modelling
results
Four versions of the numerical model were built. These diﬀer in complexity of the geology and in
the deﬁnition of the boundary conditions. The model version with the combination of complex
geology and ﬁxed head boundaries is chosen as best in accordance with the CSM. It is able to
reproduce the conceptual plume very well, and explains the observed distribution of the plume
across the transect. However, irregularities in the ﬂow are observed near the eastern boundary,
where the numerical model results are in discord with the CSM. The simulation results suggest
that the eastern part of the deep plume turns and exits the model domain through the eastern
boundary. This would correspond to a ﬂow against the regional ﬂow direction, and indicates
that the design of the eastern boundary somehow causes problems. To remediate this, the
hydraulic head could be increased at the boundary, or perhaps, the shape of the boundary
should be redesigned all together. Since the isopotential map from 2012 (Orbicon, 2013) was
created for the upper aquifer, basing the shape of the model domain on this map may cause
the problems encountered regarding the lower aquifer.
The proposed shape of the plume within the model domain is largely conﬁrmed by the simula-
tions, especially for the upper plume, but some diﬀerences are also observed in the case of the
deep plume. In the CSM, the path of the deep plume is sketched as bending very softly towards
the west, whereas the simulation results indicate a sharper turn. Furthermore, the simulations
place the deep plume entirely north of the stream. The point of convergence of the northern and
southern ﬂow is expected to be situated below the stream itself, so the shift to the north could
be related to the boundary conditions along the southern boundary. It would be interesting
to see where the deep plume would be if the southern boundary was moved further away from
the stream, such that constant head could be assumed over the entire depth. Unfortunately,
no deep wells are currently installed south of the stream in the area of interest, as it is the case
to the north (well 114.1447).
The processes of conceptual and numerical modelling are interlinked, and in investigations of
contaminanted sites, the process is not linear, but rather iterative. Therefore, Sections 7.3
and 7.4 consider further development of the CSM. Section 7.3 will review the conceptual cross-
sectional shape of the plume, whereas Section 7.4 will discuss the possible fate of the deep
plume west of the current model domain with the support of stream water and hyporheic zone
data.
7.3
Cross-sectional shape of the plume
The results from the numerical model cast new light on the cross-sectional shape of the plume
near the stream, which could not be determined based exclusively on the observations. This
is best shown in the transect. In the original conceptual model of the transect, the detections
of contaminants near point S6 were not graphically connected with those at well 114.1495.
However, the modelled results suggest that the two are probably connected, and that the
transversal shape of the plume is a result of the diﬀerences in groundwater ﬂow above and
below the low conductivity layer.
A revised suggestion for the plume distribution in the transect is presented in Figure 7.1.

Finally, part of the plume remains below the lignite layer until reaching the transect. A dis-

further west than the top of the lower plume. This shape is suggested due to diﬀerences in
the ﬂow pattern over depth in the lower aquifer, where a decreasing inﬂuence of the stream on
the ﬂow patterns is expected with increasing depth. In the deepest section of the aquifer, the
regional ﬂow patterns would therefore dominate, resulting in the lower plume travelling more
west than the upper plume. This eﬀect is also responsible for the observed shift of the lower
plume relative to the upper.
Concerning the ﬂow in the transect plane, a hypothesis can also be formulated from the nu-
merical model results. Below the lignite layer, some horizontal ﬂow in the western direction is
expected, as seen from Figure 6.13, p. 52. Furthermore, the numerical model indicates a slight
upward component. When this ﬂow meets the lignite layer, the ﬂow switches direction because
it is refracted. Upon emergence from the lignite layer to the upper aquifer, the ﬂow is refracted
once more. Due to the strong inﬂuence of the stream in the upper aquifer, the ﬂow here will
be highly dependent on locations of meanders, such as that at point S6. The groundwater will
tend to discharge to the stream here.
Connecting the separately observed parts of the plume to form one large plume results in a
simpler model, where the possible existence of separate sources at the factory site are not of
importance. However, it also implies that the mass of contaminants in the underground is very
large indeed. The possible risks which the remaining plume may impose on stream water and
groundwater resources are discussed in the following section.

towards a risk regarding cis-DCE, VC and ethyl carbamate, whereas PCE and TCE can be
ruled out. However, the likelihood of exposure is uncertain. Considering that the fate of the
plume is poorly determined west of Grindsted town, the possibility of exposure cannot be
disregarded. Therefore, cis-DCE, VC and ethyl carbamate are designated as constituting a risk
for groundwater resources, but this is deﬁnitely a ﬁeld for further research.

CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
Chapter 8
Conclusion
This study had the purpose of modelling a contaminant plume discharging to a stream in
Grindsted, Denmark. This is highly relevant in order to comply with EU requirements for risk
assessment of surface water, and was achieved by the combination of a conceptual site model
(CSM) and a numerical model.
Geological, chemical and hydrological data was assessed together to formulate the conceptual
site model (CSM) for the factory site plume and its discharge to Grindsted stream. Along
with existing data, hydraulic head (h) was sounded in a number of wells in a transect near the
stream, and hydraulic conductivity (K) was determined by means of mini slug tests. The K
values determined by ﬁeld investigations veriﬁed the properties of the sand deposits suggested
in previous studies of the site. A hypothesis for the shape and propagation of the contaminant
plume was produced based on the data collected, focusing on redox species, chlorinated ethenes
and pharmaceuticals.
A number of previous numerical models were reviewed. This lead to certain decisions regarding
the construction of a new model for the contamination plume in Grindsted. Similarly to two of
the previous models, the current model was built using the software COMSOL Multiphysics.
The focus of this study is the contaminant plume and its interaction with the stream, and
hence the scale of the numerical model is local rather than regional. The model domain was
designed based on an isopotential map from 2012, and the the boundary conditions for the
outer boundaries as well as the stream were deﬁned from mean values of time series data from
the year of 2014.
In contrast to the previous numerical models, the current model is steady state. This was
chosen since it is not the purpose to model the movement of the plume over a long period of
time, and because the source is characterised by long-term contaminant release. Furthermore,
the relevance of particle tracing versus solute transport was discussed, and in conclusion, it
was found that solute transport is not relevant in the current model. The review of previous
numerical models also indicated that the presence of a low conductivity geological stratum
aﬀects the ﬂow signiﬁcantly, while the assessment of geological data suggested that a layer of
lignite is present in the model domain. Therefore, it was decided to implement this layer in the
current model.
Two diﬀerent geological scenarios were tested, as well as two diﬀerent sets of boundary condi-
tions. This resulted in a total of four versions of the numerical model. These were calibrated for
K and the stream water gradient, i. The two versions which incorporate a layer of low perme-
71
==PAGE:88==
ability had R2 values above 0.9, whereas the model versions which represent a simple geological
scenario had R2 values below 0.2. The complex geological scenario was therefore selected as
most accurate. Furthermore, the version which employs ﬁxed head boundary conditions was
found to produce a ﬂow which best corresponds to that of the CSM. The model which included
a low permeability layer and had ﬁxed head boundary conditions was therefore selected for
further studies.
The simulation results from the selected model were generally in good accordance with the
conceptual model, supporting the hypothesis that the upper plume ﬂows straight to the stream
and discharges, while the deep plume is deﬂected in the western direction. In addition, the
simulation results indicated that the plume immediately below the lignite layer is ﬁrst deﬂected
to the west, but upon crossing the lignite layer changes direction and ﬂows to the stream, where
it discharges within the model domain.
The ﬁndings from numerical simulations lead to a revised suggestion for the shape of the
plume in the cross-section near the stream. With the proposed delineation of the plume at
the northern model boundary, the results suggest that the plume could be continuous between
wells 114.1495 and 114.1448. Furthermore, it is proposed that the deep plume is shifted in the
western direction, relative to the upper plume.
The combined results from the CSM and the numerical model formed the basis for a risk as-
sessment of stream water and groundwater resources. In stream water, the detections of vinyl
chloride (VC) were found to exceed the quality criterium by a factor 440, and was therefore as-
sessed to constitute a risk to the stream. Furthermore, the concentrations of cis-dichloroethene
(cis-DCE) and total sulfonamides exceed the guideline values, but by a small margin. They were
therefore not estimated to represent a high risk. Perchloroethene (PCE) and total barbiturate
concentrations were below those speciﬁed in the (suggested) quality criteria.
Regarding groundwater resources, cis-DCE and VC were both determined to constitute a risk.
The peak concentrations in the transect were respectively 640 µg/L and 1200 µg/L, while the
quality criteria for drinking water are 1 µg/L and 0.5 µg/L. On the other hand, PCE and
trichloroethene (TCE) were not considered to represent a risk, although the quality criterium
of 1 µg/L is just exceeded for both compounds. However, natural attenuation in the form of
dilution and degradation will most likely cause a decrease in the concentration. Concerning
barbiturates and sulfonamides, the peak concentrations were respectively 635 µg/L and 1740
µg/L, which is rather high.
However, no quality criteria is deﬁned for drinking water for
these compounds, so it is uncertain whether there is a risk. Based on a virtually safe dose
from literature, a suggestion for a drinking water quality criterium for ethyl carbamate was
calculated to 0.5 µg/L. In the transect, it was detected in concentrations up to 1600 µg/L, and
was therefore assessed to represent a risk for groundwater resources.
The approach of combining the formulation of a CSM and simulations by a numerical model
to study a contaminant plume proved very eﬀective in the case investigated.
The site of
study is characterised by a number of complicating factors, such as groundwater-surface water
interaction with a highly sinuous stream, complex geology and multiple contaminants in the
plume. In spite of these factors, it was possible to reproduce hydraulic head observations and
formulate a new hypothesis to explain chemical observations.

CHAPTER 9. FURTHER STUDIES
Chapter 9
Further studies
9.1
Future ﬁeld investigations
A conceptual site model helps to identify important gaps in the knowledge of the site. Sugges-
tions for future ﬁeld investigations which may strengthen the foundation for modelling of the
site are therefore provided in this section.
The suggested delineation of the plume in the CSM, Figures 6.6-6.5, is not very well-founded.
The main reason is the lack of chemical data between the factory site and the stream. Near
the factory site, concentrations of contaminants are generally low due to the depletion of the
source. At the stream, the variability in ﬂow patterns over depth complicates the prediction
of the plume origin. However, at the distance of well 114.1447, contaminant concentrations
are higher, resulting in a clearer identiﬁcation of the plume extent. Also, analysis of h and
isopotential lines over depth indicates that the entire plume is propagating in the same direction,
unlike its behaviour near the stream. Additional deep boreholes at the same isopotential line
as 114.1447 would therefore supply useful information for improved delineation of the plume.
Another problem regarding delineation of the plume is the insuﬃcient width of the transect
adjacent to the stream. The limits of the plume were not found based on the chemical data
available. It would therefore be interesting to extend the transect in the eastern and western
directions, and investigate the content of pharmaceuticals and CEs. This would be possible if
new boreholes were installed. Particularly the western end of the transect is a point of interest,
since more data here would help to describe the westward migration of the deep plume.
The classiﬁcation of redox conditions would be strengthened if more data for CH4 were avail-
able. The concurrent presence of CH4 and high levels of SO2−
4
in the transect complicates
the classiﬁcation of redox conditions. With more data for CH4 in the plume upstream of the
transect, the origin of the CH4 found near the stream could be better understood.
Aside from chemical data, the model would also beneﬁt from more observations of hydraulic
head.
Near the location of the main discharge zone, h is quite well studied.
This is not,
however, the case along all model boundaries, as they are deﬁned in this study. As mentioned
previously, irregularities in the deep ﬂow in the eastern side of the domain were observed. More
comprehensive data for h would facilitate the formulation of more suitable boundary conditions.
It would beneﬁt the geological model to have more spatially distributed data points across the
model domain. In this project, the geological model is based exclusively on borehole data,

which gives very accurate data for a few particular points. Many of these points are clustered
in a small area around the discharge zone. By application of geophysical methods, one could

In connection with the ﬁeld work in March 2017, and from aerial photographs taken previously,

9.2
Future modelling
The risk of exposure from the deep plume through groundwater resources could not be deter-
mined with certainty within the framework of the current model. In order to do so, a new
numerical model would have to cover a much larger part of the stream reach. Although being
more regional in scale, it is also crucial that the new model could include groundwater-stream
water interaction, since the stream is the possible sink for the deep plume.

Appendix C
Field methods
A ﬁeld investigation of the Grindsted site was carried out on March 15th to 16th. The purpose
was to provide further data for the model in terms of hydraulic conductivity, and moreover
to conduct synchronised sounding of hydraulic head and measurement of stream water level.
Finally, the ﬁeld investigation includes levelling of a reference point for measurement of stream
water level. The following sections describe ﬁeld methods and subsequent data treatment.
C.1
Well sounding
As the ﬁrst task upon arrival at the site, all geoprobe wells were sounded using a Solinst
water level meter, Model 101, seen in Figure C.1a. The depth from the top of the pipe to the
water level was recorded. The remaining wells were sounded in connection with slug testing.
Also, it is of interest to record the stream water level at the time of the well soundings. To
enable comparison with previous measurements, this was measured from one of the previous
wastewater treatment plant outlet pipes, marked with a red cross in Figure C.1b. The elevation
of this point was later measured by levelling.

C.2
Levelling of a new reference point
In levelling, the height of a certain point relative to a reference point is measured. The equip-
ment is shown in the photograph in Figure C.2. At the site in Grindsted, a point on the bridge
(background of photograph) has a known elevation above mean sea level.
To conduct levelling, the instrument is placed within 100 m distance of a known reference
point. The tripod of the instrument is adjusted such that the spirit levels indicate a completely
horizontal position. Then, the levelling staﬀis placed upright upon the known point, and the
automatic reading is initiated by pressing a button. This measures the height of the instrument
above the reference point. Then, the staﬀis moved to the new point, which should likewise be
within 100 m distance of the instrument, and a new reading is done. Now, the elevation of the
new point can be calculated.
In Grindsted, readings were conducted for the bridge, the water level by the bridge, the top
of the S1-1 geoprobe pipe, and the wastewater treatment plant outlet. A ﬁnal reading was
done for the bridge, in order to ensure that the instrument had not been disturbed during the
previous readings. The two readings were in accordance to within 1 mm.

C.3
Slug tests
In a slug test, one measures the response in hydraulic head over time to a sudden change in
water level. The hydraulic conductivity can then be estimated based on this data. Two types
of wells were slug tested in this ﬁeld investigation: 63 mm diameter wells and 3/4” geoprobe
wells. Due to the diﬀerence in diameter, two separate approaches were needed.
Slug tests of the 63 mm wells were conducted in a ’traditional’ fashion, similarly to the method
described by Hinsby et al. (1992): a pump induces a vacuum/pressure inside the well, which in
turn raises/lowers the water level, and the change in head over time upon release is measured
by a pressure transducer immersed in the well. A photograph of this setup is shown in Figure
C.3a for well 114.2570. Due to the hydraulic conditions in the area of study, the hydraulic head
of a number of the wells was above the level of the well tops. Therefore, an extension of these
wells was required in order to perform a slug test. For the 63 mm wells, the white PVC pipe
seen in Figure C.3a was attached to the top of the existing well pipe.
The experimental procedure for the 63 mm wells was as follows: The extension pipe with top
piece was attached to the well pipe. Then, the transducer was lowered through a hole in the
top piece. When reaching the desired depth, the transducer cable was ﬁxed with a rubber
stopper, which aside from holding the transducer in place also tightens the hole (see Figure
C.3b). The metal component of the top piece has three outlets including a valve. If leaving
the valve slightly open, the speed of obtaining the initial displacement is reduced, and thereby
easier to control. An air pressure gauge was connected to one outlet, while the other outlet
was reserved for the pump tube. Since the return to static water level was digitally logged, a
new ﬁle was prepared before inducing the initial displacement. The pump was started and the

therefore more eﬃcient than using a pump, provided that a suﬃcient volume of water is easily
available. Also, the assumption of instantaneous initial change of the water level is probably
best fulﬁlled by this method. As a drawback, the change in water level would be smaller than

Due to the small diameter of the geoprobe wells, the presence of the transducer could potentially
obstruct the free ﬂow of water inside the well. Also, based on experiences from previous ﬁeld
campaigns, the geoprobe screens seem to be situated in low conductivity soil types. Therefore,
a lower temporal resolution of data will suﬃce, and hence, digital logging is unnecessary. In
consequence, the approach was as follows: the water level meter was inserted into the well pipe
to the point where the tip of the electrode was exactly level with the pipe top. In this way,
the water displaced by the bottom half of the device was constant for all measurements, and
would not be a source of error. The well was then quickly ﬁlled with water, and using a stop
watch, the decline of the water level was manually recorded. If the decline was slow, it was
suﬃcient to record the water level at regular time intervals. With a fast declining water level,
it was easier to ﬁx the water level meter at a known level, and then record the time when the
noise from the device stopped. Then, the water level meter was moved e.g. 10 cm down and
repeat the new time recorded. For geoprobe wells S1-1 and S3-1, the static water level was so
high that an extension pipe was needed in order to be able to induce an initial displacement of
signiﬁcance. The extention pipe is a metal pipe of the same dimensions as the geoprobe pipes,
and is attached by matching threads. When screwed on tightly, the top of the extension is
1.004 m above the top of the existing pipe.
Several sources of uncertainty is associated with the procedures described above. Firstly, in the
cases of applying an extension pipe, there is a risk of leaking in the connections, which would
result in a false increase of estimated hydraulic conductivity. However, such leaking was not
observed, neither for 63 mm wells nor geoprobe wells. Another potential source of error is the
presence of the top piece of the 63 mm extension pipe, which might aﬀect the air ﬂow into/out
of the well upon release of the initial displacement. Ideally, the air should ﬂow freely in and out
of the extension pipe, such that air pressure does not aﬀect the movement of the water table.
Furthermore, when manually timing the decline of the water level in the geoprobe wells, there
is a risk of being mistaken by as much as a couple of seconds. However, considering that most
of the geoprobe slug tests lasted several tens of minutes, this does not seem to be a signiﬁcant
uncertainty.
In well 114.2570-1, a strongly oscillating behaviour was observed. The initial displacement was
induced by means of pressure, meaning the water level was lowered. Upon release, the water
columm surged so high that it reached the top piece of the extension pipe, and some water was
forced out of the outlet. After two trials starting with about 2 bars of pressure, a third trial

C.4
Field data processing
The following section describes the procedures for processing of raw ﬁeld data to determine the
hydraulic conductivity.
The software AQTESOLV is employed for data analysis of both the digitally logged and the
manually recorded data sets. The raw data ﬁles are adjusted to express head above static water
level. Also, if necessary, the data are converted to falling head curves. In practice, this means
that measurements obtained using pressure for initial displacement are inverted with respect
to the static water level.
Depending on the type of aquifer, diﬀerent solutions are applied to the data to estimate the
hydraulic conductivity, K. In Grindsted, wells screened both above and below the lignite layer
are slug tested. It is debatable whether this layer separates the aquifer. On the one hand, a clear
pressure gradient exists across the boundary, but on the other hand, solute transport does seem
to occur from deeper groundwater up into the stream, indicating a connection. Furthermore,
the extent of the lignite layer appears to be limited when moving away from the stream, so a
connection certainly exists at a distance perpendicular to the transect. Keeping this in mind,
the aquifer is considered conﬁned below the lignite layer and unconﬁned above it.
Also, each screen should be classiﬁed according to which type of forces dominate the system.
This is seen in the response of measured head over time. The ’usual’ response where the water
level simply returns to the static level is termed overdamped, whereas an oscillatory behaviour
implies that the aquifer is underdamped.
Examples of each behaviour are shown in Figure
C.4. The underdamped behaviour is caused by inertial eﬀects, and is sometimes observed in
high conductivity aquifers. On the contrary, viscous or Darcian forces dominate overdamped
systems (Weight and Wittman, 1999). Table C.1 summarises which solution is employed to
analyse data sets from each type of aquifer.
The Bouwer-Rice (1976) solution is preferred for unconﬁned aquifers without inertial eﬀects,
according to Duﬃeld (2004). This is validated by comparing results obtained by respectively
Bouwer-Rice and Hvorslev to results from previous investigations of the site, see Appendix
C.5. The Bouwer-Rice solution is a semi-analytical solution, and is valid for both fully and
partially penetrating wells. All wells investigated in this study belong to the latter category.
Also, Bouwer-Rice is a quasi-steady-state model, implying that elastic storage of the aquifer

C.5
Validation of ﬁeld method and data processing
As stated in Appendix C.3 describing the ﬁeld methods of slug testing, diﬀerent means were
employed to induce the initial displacement of the water level, depending on the circumstances:
raising the water level with a vacuum or by pouring water into the well, or lowering the water
level by applying an air pressure. To test for any diﬀerences in resultant K, all three methods
were employed at well 114.1495-3. Furthermore, both the methods of pressure and vacuum
were used at well 114.1495-4. Figure C.5 compares 3 diﬀerent outputs from analysis of each set
of results. Two experiments were conducted with each method.
Both well screens gave oscillatory data sets, hence the Butler (1998) solution was applied in
data processing.
Aside from K, the outputs from the Butler solution also include Le, the
eﬀective water column length, and C(D), a dimensionless damping factor. When well matched,
the estimated Le should be similar to the actual water column length, L, which is the height of
the static water level above the top of the screen (Duﬃeld, 2004). C(D) expresses the degree
of damping. By default, C(D) = 1 correponds to critical damping, meaning that the system is
in transition between the two states (Weight and Wittman, 1999). When C(D) is closer to 0,
it indicates that the system is underdamped to a higher degree.
Regarding the results obtained for K, all methods produced output values nicely within the
same order of magnitude for both wells. For 114.1495-3, the standard deviation is 2.67 · 10−4
m/s, which is an order of magnitude smaller than the results. The same is true for 114.1495-4,
where the standard deviation is 3.10·10−5 m/s. Considering the range of possible K values, this
is quite satisfactory, and suggests that the three methods are largely equivalent. As a possible
exeption, the K values obtained using the water method seem to be higher than the others.
Qualitatively, these data sets were also more diﬃcult to ﬁt, espicially trial (a).
Looking at Le values, trial (a) and (b) for well 114.1495-3 are also furthest from the actual L,
suggesting a less accurate ﬁt. The best ﬁt for 114.1495-3 is attained with vacuum, whereas it
is pressure for in the case of 114.1495-4.
The readings for well screen 114.1495-3 were observed to be highly oscillatory during ﬁeld
work. This is reﬂected in low estimated C(D) values, with a mean of 0.23. In contrast, well
screen 114.1495-4 showed less oscillatory behaviour in its response, and in fact, the data sets
consistently produce higher C(D) values with a mean of 0.53.
In conclusion, the three methods for inducing initial displacement appear to be mostly equiva-
lent in output, although there are indications that the water method may stand out as producing
data which is slightly more diﬃcult to ﬁt using the Butler solution.
