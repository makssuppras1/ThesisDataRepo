Title: Turbulence estimation from a continuous-wave scanning lidar 
(SpinnerLidar) 
 
DTU Wind Energy-M-0102 
July 2016 
 
 
Summary: 
 
One of the current challenges using  lidars for wind energy measurements is 
the inability of lidars to accurately measure turbulence. There are several issues 
that contribute to this such as: 
1) the spatial averaging over the lidars measurement volume (leading to smaller 
eddies being averaged out) and 
2) the mixture of the 3D velocity wind components into the lidar's line-of-sight 
measurements. 
 
This thesis will attempt to develop a methodology based on horizontally swept 
scanning measurements of the turbulent wind to quantify the three turbulent 
quantities < u’ >, < v’ > and < u’v’ > from a fast continuous-wave horizontally 
scanning lidar mounted on the nacelle (or in the spinner) of a wind turbine. 
 
After filtering out erroneous spectra occurring due to for example hard target 
returns caused by the rotor blade passage in front of the lidar, and the nacelle 
of the turbine and subsequently preprocessing the Doppler spectra measured 
by the lidar, the spectra are used to estimate the full unfiltered variances of the 
radial line-of sight speed in order to circumvent the issues of spatial averaging. 
 
The second issue that occurs when measuring turbulence with lidars is the 
mixing of the three wind speed components (a vector) into the (scalar) line-of 
sight measured wind speed. Because lidars only measure the line-of-sight 
component of the wind speed, parts of the horizontal wind speed and its 
fluctuations that are being measured using cup anemometers are not measured 
when using lidars. 
Additionally, lidars will, if not horizontally aligned, also measure (part of) the 
vertical wind component, adding another ambiguity to the measured turbulence 
and thus modifying the lidar measured variance relative to the turbulence 
measured using a cup anemometer. 
 
A methodology based on symmetric scanning and a correspondingly simplified 
equation for the line-of sight measured speed variance will be described to 
extract < u’ >, < v’ > and < u’v’ > using least square fitting to the line-of sight 
turbulence measurements obtained using a continuous-wave lidar mounted on 
the nacelle of NREL's CART3 turbine at the test site of the National Renewable 
Energy Laboratory (NREL) in Colorado, USA. The longitudinal (mean flow) 
turbulence component < u’ > will then be compared to measurements from a 
cup anemometer mounted at hub height in front of the turbine.

1 — Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Turbulence is an important topic in wind energy as it inﬂuences both the energy
production of a wind turbine and the loads on its components. Traditionally, atmo-
spheric turbulence is being measured using cup or sonic anemometers mounted on a
measurement tower. However, due to evolving circumstances (larger turbines, wind
farm development further oﬀshore and need for measurements over a larger area) it
has become increasingly diﬃcult and expensive to use met mast anemometry.
Li-
dars have the potential to provide more eﬃcient solutions under these circumstances,
but the technology is relatively new and still requires further development of analysis
procedures in order to fully exploit the inherent potential.
One of the current challenges using lidars for wind energy measurements is their in-
ability to accurately measure turbulence. There are several issues that contribute to
this, such as the spatial averaging over the lidars large measurement volume (lead-
ing to smaller eddies being averaged out) and the cross-contamination of the diﬀerent
turbulence components [11].
For lidars to be considered as an industry standard for, for example, wind resource
assessment or power performance testing, it will need to be demonstrated that lidars
can measure turbulence with an accuracy at least comparable to the way turbulence
is currently being measured using cup anemometers.
1.2
Project Scope
In this thesis, an algorithm that can estimate atmospheric turbulence from measure-
ments performed by a horizontally mounted, continuous-wave lidar will be investigated.
This algorithm will be based on the similarities between the probability density func-
tion of the wind speed and the Doppler spectra measured by the lidar. These similar-
ities were ﬁrst demonstrated by Branlard et al. using wind tunnel measurements [4].
This thesis will mainly focus on the practical applications of this principle in the ﬁeld
and will investigate its possibilities and limitations.
The algorithm will be veriﬁed using measurements obtained by a SpinnerLidar in-
stalled on top of the nacelle of NREL’s CART3 turbine located at the National Wind
Technology Center in Colorado.
Before these measurements can be processed using the turbulence estimation algo-
rithm, the available data has to be preprocessed in order to construct a high-quality
data set. This includes ﬁltering out the measurements that are aﬀected by the tur-
1
==PAGE:11==
bine blades rotating in front of the lidar and ﬁltering out the noise in the measured
spectra.
To verify the algorithm, wind speed variances over 10-minute intervals will be deter-
mined and compared to turbulence measurements obtained from a cup anemometer
installed at hub height on a tower in front of the turbine.
1.3
Background Theory
This section will explain some of the ideas and principles that should be understood
when reading this thesis.
It mainly focuses on the theory behind lidars and lidar
measurements, but also includes some information on cup and sonic anemometers as
they can be used as reference instruments to validate lidar measurements against.
1.3.1
General Lidar Theory
Lidars (Light detection and ranging) are capable of measuring wind speeds by emitting
a laser beam into the atmosphere and measuring the Doppler shift in the light wave that
is reﬂected back when the beam hits aerosols (i.e. small particles in the atmosphere
like dust, water droplets, pollen etc.).
Because these aerosols travel at the speed of the wind, their reﬂection causes a Doppler
shift in the frequency of the laser beam, proportional to the wind speed at the location
of the aerosol. Therefore it is possible to determine the wind speed in the direction of
the laser beam (the so-called radial- or line-of-sight velocity) from the Doppler shift
in the backscattered light [14].
Due to its dependency on the Doppler principle, a lidar can only measure the line-of-
sight velocity, not the entire wind speed vector. This means that the measured line-
of-sight velocity is a combination of the longitudinal (u), tangential (v) and vertical
(w) components of the wind speed vector. The ratio in which these three velocity
components are mixed, which depends on the diﬀerence in azimuth (θ) and elevation
(φ) angle between the laser beam and the wind vector, is given by equation 1.1.
vr = u cos(θ) cos(φ) + v sin(θ) cos(φ) + w sin(φ)

1.3.2
Continuous-Wave Lidars
The lidar systems currently in use in the wind industry can be divided into two cate-
gories: pulsed lidar systems and continuous-wave lidar systems. This thesis will only
focus on continuous-wave lidars, the principles and characteristics of which will be
described in this section.
In contrast to pulsed lidars, continuous-wave (CW) lidars continuously emit a laser
beam into the atmosphere. Simultaneously, the backscattered signal is being acquired
and analyzed. The measurement distance of a CW lidar is determined by the optical
2
==PAGE:12==
system which focuses the laser beam at a certain distance away from the lidar. This
means that continuous-wave lidars can only measure at a single distance at a time. In
order to measure at a diﬀerent distance, the focus of the laser beam has to be adjusted
accordingly.
The maximum range of a continuous-wave lidar is mainly determined by its optical
system. As the focus distance of the laser beam increases, the probe volume (the
volume of air over which the lidar is measuring) increases. Increasing the probe volume
means that the measurement is averaged over a larger area and the resulting line-of-
sight velocity therefore contains less of the small scale ﬂuctuations. This means that
the lidar’s optical system has to be capable of keeping the laser beam suﬃciently
focused over the desired measurement range of interest. For most modern CW lidars
this means that they can achieve a maximum range of around 200 m without the
measurement volume becoming too large to obtain suﬃciently localized measurements.
CW lidars have no problems measuring at short distances. However, for eye-safety
reasons, their minimum range is usually capped at around 10 m [10].
1.3.3
Lidar Signal Acquisition & Processing
After the backscattered signal is detected by the lidar, it has to be processed before
it can be used. This is usually done using a method called heterodyne detection. In
this process, the backscattered signal is mixed with a small portion of the emitted
signal, which leads to an oscillation in the signal power of the resulting mixed signal
known as the beat phenomenon. The frequency of this oscillation is known as the beat
frequency and is equal to the shift in frequency of the backscattered signal compared
to the frequency of the emitted signal.
Figure 1.1 illustrates the beat phenomenon. Figures 1.1a and 1.1b show the signal
power of two sinusoidal signals with a constant amplitude and frequency (20 Hz and
21 Hz, respectively).
Figure 1.1c shows the signal power of the sum of these two
signals. It can be seen that the signal power of the summed signal ﬂuctuates at the
diﬀerence frequency of the two individual signals (i.e. 1 Hz).

This beat frequency can be determined using a photodetector that can measure the

outputs a current that can be converted into a digital signal using a analog to digital
converter.
This digital signal is then split into small blocks that are Fourier transformed. Because
the resulting spectra typically contain a lot of noise, they are ﬁrst averaged over a
number of spectra. These averaged spectra will be used for turbulence estimation
further on in this thesis.
1.3.4
Cup Anemometers
One of the oldest and most commonly used methods to measure wind speeds is using
a cup anemometer. Its principle is quite simple: the wind blows against the cups,
causing the shaft to start rotating due to the diﬀerence in pressure on both sides of
the cup. The number of rotations per second can be counted, from which the wind
velocity then can be derived as it is linearly dependent on the rotational velocity of the
shaft. As they have been extensively studied and found to be very robust due to their
simple design, cup anemometers are considered to be a staple in the wind industry
when it comes to performing outdoor measurements and industry standards heavily
rely on them.
When attempting to measure turbulence using cup anemometers there are two impor-
tant considerations to be made:
1. Assuming correct, horizontal mounting of the instrument, the ﬁrst order pertur-
bation equation of a cup anemometer only depends on the longitudinal compo-
nent of the ﬂow [6].
4
==PAGE:14==
2. A cup anemometer ﬁlters out smaller ﬂuctuations due to the inertia of the in-
strument. The amount of turbulence being ﬁltered out is characterized by the
distance constant ℓ0 of the particular cup anemometer type and the height above
the ground at which it is mounted [7].
1.3.5
Ultrasonic Anemometers
Ultrasonic anemometers measure wind speeds by sending a sound pulse from one
transducer to another. Based on the time it takes the pulse to reach the receiving
transducer, the wind speed can be calculated. Sonic anemometer have several advan-
tages compared to cup anemometers when it comes to turbulence measurements. Due
to their high sampling frequency and small probe volume, sonic anemometers ﬁlter out
less turbulence than cup anemometers. Additionally, depending on their conﬁguration,
they are capable of measuring two or three wind speed components.
1.4
NREL 2015 Measurement Campaign
The lidar data used for this thesis was collected during a measurement campaign
performed at the National Wind Technology Center in Colorado, USA in 2015. In
section 1.4.1 the SpinnerLidar that was used to measure the incoming wind ﬁeld is
described. Section 1.4.2 describes the CART3 turbine on which the lidar was mounted
and section 1.4.3 describes the test site where the experiment was performed.

2 — Preprocessing
2.1
Noise Filtering
During the acquisition of the signal and its subsequent processing, a signiﬁcant amount
of noise is introduced into the spectra. Before the measured spectra can be analyzed
any further, this noise needs to be ﬁltered out.
The ﬁrst step in this ﬁltering process is the ﬂattening of the spectra. This is done
by dividing the individual spectra by the so-called ’background spectrum’. The back-
ground spectrum can be interpreted as the mean spectrum not containing any actual
signal, i.e. the mean noise spectrum.
2.1.1
Background Spectrum Estimation
To determine the background spectrum, a way has to be found to determine the noise
intensity per frequency bin. Taking the mean spectrum is not an option as this has
to be done without any a priori knowledge of the presence of any measured signals in
the spectra, which would inﬂuence the mean spectrum.
This can also be seen in ﬁgure 2.1. Figure 2.1a shows the probability density function
of the spectral power for a frequency bin that corresponds to a (very high) radial
velocity that does not occur in the given time series. This means that there is only
noise being measured in this bin, which is also suggested by the pdf that follows a
normal distribution. Consequently it would for this bin be possible to estimate the
mean noise intensity by simply taking the mean of the bin’s spectral power.
Figure 2.1b on the other hand shows the probability density function of the spectral
power for a frequency bin that corresponds to a radial velocity that does occur a
signiﬁcant amount of times in the given time series. As a consequence, the distribution
is skewed, which means that the mean of the distribution in this case does not represent
the mean noise intensity. However, it can be seen that the pdf still follows a normal
distribution for the majority of the distribution, including around the peak.
This
suggests that the peak of the pdf is still determined by the intensity of the noise. It is
thus assumed that the peak of the pdf does not shift when a Doppler shift is introduced
into the signal and that the mean noise intensity of a frequency bin can therefore be
found at the peak of the pdf.

2.3
Blade Return Filtering
When using a turbine-mounted lidar to measure the incoming wind ﬁeld in front of a
turbine, it is desirable to have the lidar installed in the spinner of the turbine in order
to have an unobstructed view on the wind ﬁeld that is approaching the turbine’s rotor
plane. However, this is not always feasible. An alternative position to place the lidar
12

is on top of the turbine nacelle, but this means that the laser beam will periodically hit
the blades of the turbine when they are rotating. These blade reﬂections will result in
incorrect measurement values and have to be ﬁltered out. In this section an algorithm
will be presented to identify these blade return signals. This method is based on the
work presented by Angelou & Sj¨oholm [3].
The idea behind this method is to model the radial velocity measured by the lidar
if the laser beam would hit a blade and then compare this to the actual measured
value. If these two values are found to be within a certain margin of each other, the
measurement is assumed to be a blade return signal and marked as such.
As explained by Angelou & Sj¨oholm, the expected radial velocity measured from a
blade return signal can be predicted using equation 2.2. Here Sx and Sy are the x-

beam emission, ω is the rotational speed of the rotor blade and h and l are the vertical
and lateral displacement of the origin of the lidar’s laser beam transmission compared
to the center of the rotor [3].
Vr = −Sx · ω · h + Sy · ω · l

3 — Spatial averaging
One of the challenges that has to be addressed when attempting to measure turbulence
using lidars is the spatial averaging that occurs within the lidar’s probe volume. This
stems from the fact that a lidar measurement is not a point measurement, but rather
an average of the wind speeds in a measurement volume centered on the lidar’s line-of-
sight pointing direction and having an approximately Lorentzian weighting function,
which is centered around the lidar’s set measurement range by focusing the laser beam
around the lidar’s set measurement range.
Due to this line-of-sight probe volume
averaging, wind ﬂuctuations on scales smaller than the probe volume will be averaged
and thereby ﬁltered in magnitude. As a result a lidar on average only measures 80%
of the standard deviation measured by a cup anemometer [13].
Using an upwind pointing wind lidar, it has been demonstrated in wind tunnel exper-
iments that the intrinsic unﬁltered probability distribution function of the wind speed
ﬂuctuations in a turbulent ﬂow is contained by the Doppler spectrum averaged over
the sampling period [4]. Hence, the variance of this mean spectrum is representative
of the total (unﬁltered) turbulence of the turbulent ﬂow. This means that the en-
semble averaged lidar spectra can be used to estimate the full unﬁltered variance in a
turbulent ﬂow.

The same idea can be applied to the instantaneous individual lidar measurements.
Each lidar measurement consists of a single line-of-sight velocity measurement, but
this measurement is in fact the Lorentzian-weighted average of wind speeds within the
measurement volume.
As it can be assumed that the ﬂow within the measurement volume does not change
while sampling an individual lidar spectrum (as a SpinnerLidar scans at a sampling
rate of 312.5 Hz and an individual measurement therefore only takes 3.2 ms), the vari-
ance of an individual spectrum is caused only by the velocity diﬀerences experienced
by the backscattering aerosol particles within the probe volume. This means that the
width (calculated as the standard deviation) of the instantaneously measured individ-
ual spectra is representative for the small-scale turbulence ﬁltered out by the lidar’s
probe volume.
It also means that the mean of the variances of the individual spectra combined with
the variance of their corresponding line-of-sight mean velocities is equal to the total
variance of the ensemble averaged mean spectra sampled over the same time interval.
In this way, we now have two diﬀerent ways to access the total (unﬁltered) variance
of the line-of-sight projected wind velocity vector ﬂuctuations measured at the focal
point set along the lidar’s line-of-sight pointing direction.

4 — Variance Contamination
Another issue that occurs when attempting to measure turbulence using lidars is vari-
ance contamination. Because a lidar only measures the line-of-sight component of
the wind speed (ﬂuctuations), it will usually not just measure one single turbulence
component, but a combination of all three components (u′, v′ and w′). The exact
composition of this combination depends on the orientation of the lidar’s laser beam
as well as the orientation of the wind vector (see equation 1.1).
The orientation of the laser beam is known from the lidar’s internal measurement
system, while the orientation of the wind vector and the turbine (and thus the lidar
system) can be estimated from the available SCADA (Supervisory Control And Data
Acquisition) data. The obtained yaw misalignment can then be used to estimate the
angle between the laser beam and the wind vector.
4.1
Spatial Binning
Another issue that has to be taken into account when measuring turbulence using
lidar Doppler spectra is noise. As noise leads to a higher variance, it is important to
ﬁlter out any noise in the spectra as much as possible. The noise in the frequency
bins that do not contain any measured signal can already be ﬁltered out during the
preprocessing phase (see section 2.1), but the spectra themselves still contain noise.
To minimize the eﬀect of this noise, the mean of a series of spectra can be taken in
order to average out the eﬀect of the noise in the individual spectra.
In order to average the spectra while at the same time preserving the spatial orien-
tation of the scanning pattern, the measurements are grouped into square bins. The
resulting pattern of 5◦by 5◦bins is visualized in ﬁgure 4.1.

5 — Results & Discussion
5.1
Lidar - Cup Anemometer Comparison
In this section, the turbulence measured by the lidar will be compared to the turbulence
measured by a cup anemometer installed at hub height in the met mast in front of
the CART3 turbine. In order to be able to compare the two data sets, the variance
of the wind speeds measured by the cup anemometer was calculated over 10-minute
time intervals coinciding with the available lidar time series. This was then compared
to the longitudinal variance ⟨v
′2⟩measured by the lidar.
As mentioned in section 1.3.4, cup anemometers ﬁlter out some of the smaller length
scale perturbations. The amount of turbulence ﬁltered out depends on the distance
length constant of the cup anemometer and the height above the ground at which it
is mounted. The Met One Instruments SS-201 cup anemometer that was used in this
experiment has a maximum distance constant ℓ0 of 4.6 m and was placed at a height
of 36 m, which means that it is expected to ﬁlter out approximately 8.3% of the total
variance (or 4.3% of the standard deviation) [7]. It is therefore expected that the
variances measured by the lidar will be 8.3% higher than the variances measured by
the cup anemometer.

6 — Conclusions & Recommendations
It was shown that it is possible to overcome the problems commonly associated with
turbulence measurements using lidars and obtain the longitudinal variance ⟨u
′2⟩by
using the information contained in the lidar spectra to retrieve the ﬁltered out turbu-
lence and by measuring straight into the wind vector to avoid mixing of the tangential
and vertical wind speed components.
It was also shown that, although problematic using the available data, it should be
possible to also retrieve the tangential variance ⟨v
′2⟩by measuring the turbulence in
multiple directions and ﬁtting the obtained line-of-sight variances to an equation that
relates them to the longitudinal and tangential variances as a function of the azimuth
angle.
This method has several advantages compared to more traditional measurement de-
vices like cup or sonic anemometers. In addition to the advantages generally associated
with lidars over ﬁxed devices like cup or sonic anemometers that can only measure at
one single location, using lidar spectra to estimate turbulence is in theory more precise
than the aforementioned methods as it measures the full turbulence upto the smallest
length scales, whereas cup and (to a lesser degree) sonic anemometers ﬁlter out some
of the smaller length scale ﬂuctuations.
Although this study shows the potential of the method, there is still a signiﬁcant
amount of work and research to be done before this technique can be adopted as an
industry standard for such applications.
One important step forward would be to obtain data from a lidar installed in the
spinner of a turbine to eliminate erroneous measurements resulting from hard targets
such as the turbine’s blades and nacelle. This would not only signiﬁcantly simplify the
preprocessing of the data, but would also allow for measurements at lower wind speeds
where the blade return signals would otherwise be too similar to actual wind speed
measurements to be able to accurately ﬁlter them out. Data from such an experiment
could be used to prove that estimating ⟨v
′2⟩using a least-squares data ﬁtting method
is possible using real atmospheric measurements.
Another possible improvement in the experiment setup would using a horizontal scan-
ning pattern (i.e. a Plan Position Indicator or PPI scan at zero elevation angle) instead
of the rosette pattern. This could increase measurement accuracy by providing a larger
number of measurements that is more equally spread over the measurement bins. This
would also reduce the uncertainty due to vertical ﬂow components being mixed with
varying elevation angles.
This can be achieved relatively easily using the existing SpinnerLidar by adjusting the
gearbox ratio such that both prisms are counter-rotating at the same speed [9].
28
==PAGE:38==
Unfortunately the sonic anemometer in the CART3 met mast was not functioning
properly during this experiment and a cup anemometer had to be used as reference
instrument instead. Using a sonic anemometer would have two signiﬁcant advantages
over a cup anemometer; namely the ability to measure all three components of the
wind speeds, giving the ability to also verify the tangential variance ⟨v
′2⟩, and a smaller
probe volume, resulting in a more accurate estimation of the total turbulence. There-
fore, it would be beneﬁcial for future research to use a sonic anemometer as reference
instrument.
