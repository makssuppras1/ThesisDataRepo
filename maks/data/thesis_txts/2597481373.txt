Abstract
The electroreduction of CO2 to hydrocarbons and alcohols using renewable electricity oﬀers a promising avenue to
produce carbon-neutral fuels and chemicals while also storing surplus electricity from solar and wind energy as chemical
bonds. However, this technology cannot be implemented on a large-scale yet since Cu, which is the only monometallic
electrocatalyst capable to reduce CO2 to multicarbon products, is still not active and selective enough. Thus, novel
electrocatalysts are needed.
Cu is able to reduce CO2 to multicarbon products because it has an intermediate CO binding energy as a result of
its unique electronic structure. In this project Zn-modiﬁed Pd electrocatalysts were synthesized in the light that the
electronic structure of a Pd(111) single crystal can be modiﬁed to resemble that of Cu when sub-monolayer amounts of
Zn are deposited. To this end, epitaxial Pd(111) thin ﬁlms were synthesized by magnetron sputtering and characterized
by X-ray diﬀraction (XRD). The deposition of Zn on Pd(111) was conducted by underpotential deposition at pH 7 in
order to ensure that sub-monolayer amounts of Zn were deposited on Pd(111). The near-surface composition and
resulting structure of the synthesized electrocatalysts were studied by angle-resolved X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy
(ARXPS) and ion-scattering spectrosocpy (ISS). It was found that Zn UPD on Pd is an unusual UPD system in that it
is not electrochemically reversible and the Zn content displays a linear relationship with the applied potential. By a
thorough analysis of the ARXPS and ISS data it was found that this abnormal behaviour occurs because the resulting
structure of the electrocatalyst is an overlayer with subsurface alloy. Crucially, it was shown that the amount of Zn
deposited on Pd can be systematically controlled by potential.

Introduction
The electrochemical reduction of CO2 (CO2R) is a technology capable to convert CO2 to fuels and chemicals using
electricity as an input. As such, it is an attractive solution to address many of the challenges faced by society nowadays.
One challenge is climate change. The world population is expected to grow as a result of rising levels of prosperity [1].
Accordingly, it has been forecast that the global energy demand will increase from 562 quadrillion BTUs in 2017 to 675
quadrillion BTUs in 2040 [1]. This represents an associated increase of CO2 emissions from 33 billion tonnes in 2017 [1]
to 44 billion tonnes in 2040 [2] which means that climate change will be accelerated unless appropriate actions are taken.
By using CO2 as the feedstock CO2R can address this challenge.
Another problem is the storage of renewable energy. Wind and solar energy have witnessed important technological
advancements so that now wind energy is cheaper than natural gas for electricity production and solar energy is devel-
oping in the same direction [3]. However, an intrinsic problem of these sources of renewable energy is their intermittent
nature. Sun needs to shine and wind needs to blow in order for solar panels and wind turbines to produce electricity.
Therefore, energy storage techniques are needed. While batteries are an option, CO2R is a more attractive alternative
since it can store electricity in the form of synthetic fuels and in contrast to batteries, fuels have a higher energy density
and do not require a new transportation infrastructure.
Lastly, the chemical industry is currently unsustainable. On the one hand, it heavily relies on gas and oil as a feedstock
for its processes and on the other hand, it is a major contributor of CO2 emissions [1]. CO2R can address both problems
by oﬀering the chemical industry the option to use its own CO2 emissions as the feedstock and thereby converting its
processes to carbon-neutral technologies.
The potential of CO2R as a technology to drive a sustainable future is thus evident. However, in the light that it is not
ready for commercial applications yet [4], further research on it is necessary.

CO2 electroreduction (CO2R) to multicarbon products
2.1
Activity and selectivity trends of Cu for CO2R
Copper is the only monometallic electrocatalyst capable of reducing CO2 to fuels and chemicals. This was ﬁrst docu-
mented when CO2R was studied on several metals [5] a couple of decades ago. In this study the metals were classiﬁed
into 4 groups according to the major reaction products (H2, CO, HCOOH and multicarbon products, i.e. alcohols and
hydrocarbons). This classiﬁcation is shown in Figure 2.1 where the metals are presented in a periodic table and the
faradaic eﬃciencies for the corresponding major product are also exhibited. Roughly speaking each group has the same
product selectivity. Since each group has elements with a similar valence electronic structure, it can be observed that the
major reaction product is determined by the electronic structure of the metal.
Figure 2.1: Classiﬁcation of metals for CO2R according to major reaction products. Reprinted from [6]
The activity and selectivity of Cu in CO2R changes as a function of the applied potential. Figure 2.2 shows this dependence
for polycrystalline copper at pH 7 where diﬀerent trends can be observed. First, Figure 2.2a shows an increasing total
current density (activity) with more cathodic potentials. Since at more cathodic potentials the driving force for reduction
increases this is an expected behavior except when mass transfer limitations are reached. In that case the current levels
oﬀand/or declines. According to Figure 2.2b the major reaction products of CO2R on Cu are methane, formate, carbon
monoxide, ethylene and ethanol. Undesired hydrogen is also produced by the competing hydrogen evolution reaction

2.2
Key intermediate
CO is the key intermediate for CO2R. This was established early on by observing that the reduction of CO (COR)
and CO2R on Cu have similar product distributions [8]. Later on, the CO binding energy was used to rationalize the
selectivity of diﬀerent metals in CO2R, as shown in Figure 2.3. It can be observed that it is the intermediate CO binding
energy of Cu which makes it an unique electrocatalyst for the reduction of CO2 to fuels and chemicals.
Figure 2.3: Selectivity of diﬀerent metals in CO2R rationalized in terms of the binding energies for CO and H. Reprinted
from [6]
Since CO is the key intermediate there is a lot of interest in the investigation of COR. On the one hand, COR on Cu could
aid the understanding of CO2R by reducing the number of reaction steps to consider. In addition, it makes it possible
to study CO2R on a basic environment since CO2R is constrained to a neutral pH (see Section 2.4 for a more detailed

explanation) and ﬁnally, COR can split CO2R in two reaction steps: a) Conversion of CO2 to CO and b) Conversion of
CO to the desired product.
2.3
Dependence of CO2R on surface structure
The surface of a catalyst can be described as a collection of terraces (planar areas that expose only one lattice plane),
steps, kinks and other defects at the interface of diﬀerent crystallites. Thus, the measured catalytic activity is the sum
of the activities of all these diﬀerent structures. [7]. Given that their contribution is diﬀerent there is an interest to
individually study each element.
The eﬀect of Cu low-index planes, i.e. Cu(100), Cu(110) and Cu(111), on its electrocatalytic performance for CO2R is
shown in Table 2.1. It can be observed that each plane shows a diﬀerent selectivity towards CO2R products: Cu(111)

towards multicarbon products (i.e. products containing 2 or more C atoms) which is observed to increase in the following
order: Cu(111), Cu(100) and Cu(110).

Figure 2.4 exhibits the CO binding energies of Cu(111), Cu(100) and Cu(110). By comparing them with the trend of
selectivity towards multicarbon products displayed in Table 2.1 it can be observed that the stronger the CO binding
energy the more multicarbon products are produced. Thus, the selectivity trends discussed above can be rationalized in
terms of the CO binding energy which in turns determines the CO and H coverage. The low CO binding energy displayed
by Cu(111) leads to a low CO/H coverage which favors hydrogenation and this is the reason Cu(111) is more selective
towards methane. As the CO binding energy increases for Cu(100) the CO/H coverage also increases and this favors C-C
coupling so that molecules like ethylene and ethanol are preferentially produced. By further increasing the CO binding
energy Cu(110) reaches an even higher CO/H coverage which favors the production of carbonyl-containing products.

2.4
Dependence of CO2R on pH
The study of CO2R is constrained to a neutral pH since in acidic conditions HER is dominant due to the availability of
more protons and in basic conditions CO2 reacts with the electrolyte to be converted to bicarbonates and carbonates.
However, COR can be studied instead of CO2R on a basic environment because CO does not react with the electrolyte.
Thus, a comparison of CO2R on neutral and basic environments can be made. Such comparative study was recently
conducted by studying CO2R at pH 7 and COR at pH 13 [13]. The results of the investigation are shown in Figure 2.6.
The most remarkable feature is that products are formed at lower applied potentials on the RHE scale when the pH is 13.
This means that basic electrolytes can lead to a major enhancement of the energy eﬃciency of the process. An additional
interesting observation is that for COR the onset potentials between C1 and C2 products are diﬀerent which indicates
they are formed through diﬀerent pathways.
Figure 2.6: CO2R and COR activity on polycrystalline Cu at pH 6.8 and 13, respectively - RHE scale. Reprinted from [13]
In order to gain more insight into CO2R on Cu, Figure 2.7 re-plots the results of Figure 2.6 on the SHE scale. The fact that
C2+ products have almost the same onset potentials in CO2R and COR indicates that the production of C2+ products
are pH independent. In contrast, the C1 product (CH4) still displays a shift which implies that CH4 formation is pH
dependent. Assuming the reactions for these product have a rate-determining step (RDS), this means that the RDS for
C2+ products is either a chemical reaction or an electron transfer step while the RDS for the C1 product is a proton or
proton/electron transfer step [13].

2.5
Modiﬁcation of Cu electrocatalysts
A large-scale implementation of CO2 reduction to fuels and chemicals requires electrocatalysts that more active and
selective than planar Cu [4]. Thus, a lot of research has been carried out to try to improve the electrocatalytic performance
of planar Cu. There are two strategies to increase the activity of an electrocatalyst: a) increase its number of active sites
and b) increase its intrinsic activity (see Figure 2.8). The ﬁrst can be accomplished by increasing the catalyst loading
and/or surface area but it is limited by mass and charge transport eﬀects; in addition, it can only achieve 1 to 3 orders
of magnitude of catalytic improvement [4]. The latter is a more eﬀective strategy since the degree of improvement in
electrocatalytic performance can be more than 10 orders of magnitude [4].
Figure 2.8: Schematic illustration of catalyst development strategies. Reprinted from [4]
Both strategies have been applied to enhance the electrocatalytic performance of Cu for CO2R. The increase of number
of active sites has been mostly achieved by nanostructuring [7]. When studies about Cu nanostructuring were ﬁrst
published [14, 15, 16] their authors claimed superior activity compared to planar Cu but they asserted this based on the
geometric current density. However, when nanostructured and planar Cu were compared in terms of current density
normalized to the electrochemical surface area (ECSA), it was found that none of the materials were superior to planar
Cu [7]. The apparent higher current density arised instead from an increased roughness factor. Thus, a fair comparison
of electrocatalysts needs to be made with current densities normalized to the ECSA [17].
While nanostructuring does not lead to an improved activity it does display changes in product distribution [7]. For
example, CH4 formation is suppressed to some extent and the selectivity toward multicarbon products is increased. The
latter is explained as an optimal balance between an increased local pH at the surface electrode (which arises under mass
5
==PAGE:11==
transport limitations) vs the buﬀer behaviour of CO2.
Increasing the intrinsic activity as a strategy to improve the electrocatalytic performance of Cu has been studied in the
form of bimetallic systems using metals that were selective towards other products [7]. In these studies the main changes
were observed in the electrocatalytic selectivity with the most interesting feature being the increased selectivity to C2+
oxygenates (ethanol and carbonyl products) which was observed for Cu combined with CO-selective metals [7].
3
Modiﬁcation of electronic structure
3.1
The d-band model
The adequacy of a transition metal to catalyze a reaction is determined by its ability to bind the key intermediate correctly;
the bond needs to be neither too strong nor too weak. Thus, adsorption energies are responsible for trends in surface
reactivity which is why it is important to understand what determines them. The d-band model states that adsorption
energies are governed by the energy of the d-band center relative to the Fermi level. As such it is a useful model to
understand how changes in the electronic structure of transition metals relate to changes in chemical reactivity.
The valence state of a transition metal is divided in s-electron (empty) and d-electron states. In a simple explanation of the
model [18], when an adsorbate interacts with a transition metal it interacts with the d-states which gives rise to bonding
and anti-bonding states. The occupancy of these states determines the strength of the bond; the more anti-bonding
states are occupied the weaker is the bond. It was postulated and validated experimentally that the occupancy of the
anti-bonding states depends on the energy of the d-band center relative to the Fermi level in the following form: as the
energy of the d-band center gets closer to the Fermi level less anti-bonding states are occupied; as a result, the bond is
stronger.
In a more detailed explanation of the model [19], the energy of the adsorbate-surface bond, ∆E, is described as the result
of two contributions: the coupling of the adsorbate levels to a) the free s-electrons, ∆E0 and b) the d-electrons, ∆Ed, of
the transition metal as shown below:
∆E = ∆E0 + ∆Ed
(3.1)
The two most important assumptions of the model are: a) ∆E0 is independent of the transition metal and b) ∆Ed can be
estimated in a non-self-consistent fashion. Assumption a) can fail when for example the adsorbate states do not interact
with the d-states of the transition metal. However, having assumed that ∆E0 is constant, the only way to change the
energy of the adsorbate-surface bond is by changing ∆Ed which in turn depends on: a) the energy of the adsorbate states,
ϵa, b) the density of the d-states of the transition metal, nd(ϵ) and c) the coupling matrix element. The density of d-states
is described by the energy of the d-band center, ϵd.
Based on the above description the strength of adsorption of an adsorbate in a speciﬁc metal can be changed when nd(ϵ),
or in other words, the electronic structure of that metal is modiﬁed. This can be achieved in a number of diﬀerent ways:
by changing the surface structure of the metal or by interaction of the metal with other metals or compounds. In the
next section a discussion of surface structure and bimetallic interaction are thoroughly discussed in terms of the d-band
model.
3.2
How to modify the electronic structure?
3.2.1
Surface structure
The d-band center of a transition metal can be changed by modifying its surface structure which in turn depends on the
coordination number of the surface. Thus, diﬀerent lattice planes and defects have diﬀerent d-band centers as shown in
Figure 3.1. It can be observed that as the coordination number decreases (Pt(100)-hex low >9, Pt(111) = 9, Pt(211) = 7,
Pt(1185) = 6) the energy for the d-band center increases.
6
==PAGE:12==
Figure 3.1: Calculated density of d-states for Pt as a function of decreasing coordination number. Reprinted from [19]
The relationship between adsorption energy and the energy of the d-band center is shown in Figure 3.2 where CO was
used as the probe molecule. It is observed that the higher the energy of the d-band center (the less negative the energy is)
the higher the CO adsorption energy (the more negative the adsorption energy is). Therefore, the lower the coordination
number the higher the adsorption energy for CO which is in agreement with the observation that kinks and steps bind
CO more strongly than ﬂat surfaces.
Figure 3.2: CO adsorption energy vs. d-band centers. Reprinted from [19]
3.2.2
Intermetallic interactions
The modiﬁcation of the electronic structure of one metal can be achieved by its interaction with another metal whose
electronic structure is very diﬀerent, these materials are called intermetallics. The interaction can be accomplished by
overlayer structures and surface alloys although other types of structures are also possible. In an overlayer structure a
monolayer of one metal (overlayer) is deposited on top of another metal (substrate). On the other hand, surface alloys
incorporate both metals on the surface. In both cases the electronic structure of the interacting atoms is modiﬁed by
ligand and strain eﬀects. The ligand eﬀects come from the plain interaction between the diﬀerent atoms and the strain
eﬀect comes from their diﬀerent lattice constants and crystal structures.
In order to illustrate how intermetallic interactions change the adsorption strength of one metal and show that this is
related to the d-band center, Figure 3.3 shows the change in H2 and O2 adsorption energies (presented on the y-axis)
when Pd(111) is modiﬁed in overlayer structures with diﬀerent substrates (presented on the x-axis by the energy of
their d-band centers). The modiﬁcation of the electronic structure in this case is mainly dominated by strain eﬀects.
The binding energy for pure Pt(111) is indicated by the dotted line. Since the change in adsorption energies, ∆E, was
calculated by substracting the adsorption energy of Pt(111) in the overlayer structure, EM, from the adsorption energy of

values represent a weaker binding energy. Thus, in agreement with the d-band model, it is observed that as the d-band
center gets closer to the Fermi level the adsorption energy increases. Interestingly, Figure 3.3 can be used to determine
what substrate needs to be used to change the adsorption properties of Pt(111) in a desired fashion. For example, if a

required Pt(111) should be modiﬁed with Ni as the substrate.

4
Project goal and strategy
Since Cu was ﬁrst discovered as the only monometallic electrocatalyst capable to reduce CO2 to fuels and chemicals
there have been ongoing research eﬀorts to enhance its electrocatalytic performance (see Section 2.5) but, as of today, Cu
is still not active and selective enough for commercial applications. Thus, there is a need to ﬁnd novel electrocatalysts.
It was established that CO is the key intermediate in the conversion of CO2 to fuels and chemicals and that Cu is
capable to carry out this reaction because it has an intermediate CO binding energy (see Section 2.2). Thus, since the
chemical properties of a metal are determined by its electronic structure, an interesting avenue to come up with novel
electrocatalysts is to modify the electronic structure of another metal to resemble that of Cu. This can be accomplished
by intermetallic interactions (see Section 3.2.2).
Figure 4.1 shows experimental evidence that the modiﬁcation of the electronic structure of Pd induced by sub-monolayer
amounts of Zn can result in similar CO binding energies as those of Cu. This claim is done by comparing this ﬁgure with
Figure 2.4 where the common temperature region from 170 to 240 K indicates that Pd(111) with a Zn coverage from 50 to
95 % can have the same CO binding energy as Cu (45-60 kJ/mol).
Figure 4.1: CO TPD from Zn/Pd(111), reproduced from [20]
On a pure form, Pd has a strong CO adsorption energy. This is why, as shown in Figure 2.1, Pd is mostly selective towards
H2 since the strongly adsorbed CO molecules poison the electrocatalyst. In the intermetallic system presented in Figure
8
==PAGE:14==
4.1 the strong CO adsorption energy of pure Pd is being reduced by the addition of Zn. In terms of the d-band model, this
means that increasing amounts of Zn move the energy of the d-band center of Pd away from the Fermi level so that more
anti-bonding states are occupied. In a more detailed description, Rodriguez [21] explained that the increased d-band
center of Pd weakens the Pd(4d)-CO(2 π*) interaction (π-backdonation) which is the most important interaction for the
CO-Pd bond. The weaking of the π-backdonation means, in turn, that it is harder to transfer an electron from Pd to CO
and as result the bond becomes weaker.
The modiﬁcation of the electronic structure of Pd by its interaction with Zn is shown in Figure 4.2 where the valence
band of Cu is also exhibited for comparison. It is observed that the intermetallic system of Pd and Zn has a Cu-like
valence band density of states as evidenced by the same peak positions and widths. Comparing the Pd and PdZn valence
bands the main eﬀect of Zn is the suppression of the 4d states of Pd near the Fermi level (EF) which basically means that
the electron population of these states was reduced. It is the suppression of 4d states that makes the π-backdonation and,
therefore, the CO-Pd bond weaker [22].
Figure 4.2: XPS valence band spectra for Cu, Pd and Zn/Pd. Reprinted from [22]
The equivalent chemical reactivity that Cu and intermetallics of Pd and Zn should have given that they have similar
electronic structures (Figure 4.2) has been experimentally validated for methanol steam reforming [22]. Thus, on the basis
that intermetallics of Pd and Zn are expected to have the same CO binding energy as Cu and the fact that their catalytic
activity has been shown to be equivalent, the goal of the project will be to synthesize Pd electrocatalysts modiﬁed by
sub-monolayer amounts of Zn and test them as novel materials for the conversion of CO2 to fuels and chemicals. For
this purpose, the project will be divided into the following 3 stages:
• Synthesis and characterization of epitaxial ﬁlms: Given that surface structure plays a role on the selectivity
to CO2R products (see Section 2.3), the surface structure of Pd will be studied by the synthesis of Pd(111), Pd(100)
and Pd(110) epitaxial ﬁlms. Epitaxial ﬁlms expose only one lattice plane on the surface, like single crystals. One
disadvantage over single crystals, however, is that they have more defects (e.g. grain boundaries). On the bright
side, epitaxial ﬁlms make product quantiﬁcation easier.
• Synthesis of intermetallics: In order to resemble the electrocatalysts synthezised in Figure 4.1, the synthesis of
intermetallics of Zn and Pd will be achieved by Zn underpotential deposition (UPD) on Pd since UPD is a technique
capable to deposit sub-monolayer to monolayer amounts of one metal on another. Moreover, given that there is
a possibility for Zn segregation to the surface upon exposure to air (since the formation energy of Zn-O bonds,
-1.8 eV/atom [23], is higher than that of Zn-Pd bonds, -0.58 eV/atom [23]) the synthesis of the structures will be
conducted in-situ (i.e. the structures will be synthesized in the setup for testing of electrocatalytic activity).
• Assessment of electrocatalytic activity and selectity: The electrocatalysts will be tested for COR at pH 13
due to the improvement in energy eﬃciency that basic environments display for the reduction of CO2 (see Section
2.4). Although the electrocatalysts could be tested for CO2R potential issues such as formate poisoning or a lower
selectivity towards desired products due to formate production discourage this option.

Theory on experimental techniques
5.1
Magnetron sputtering
Sputtering is a physical technique to deposit thin ﬁlm layers on a substrate. A simple sputtering system is shown in
Figure 5.1. Basically, particles of a solid material (the target) are ejected from its surface by bombardment with the ions
9
==PAGE:15==
of a plasma. The plasma is initiated by applying a large voltage across a gap containing a low pressure gas; the resulting
ions can be accelerated towards the target because the target is set to a negative charge. The ejected atoms then travel
the gap between the target and the substrate which are closely spaced to collect as many of the ejected atoms as possible.
The gas pressure in the chamber is held at a level that is neither too low so that negligible sputtering occurs nor too high
so that the deposition rate is low.
Figure 5.1: Simple sputtering system. Reprinted from [24]
Compared to evaporation, sputtering has a better ability to cover surface topology, induces less radiation damage than
electron beam evaporation and is better at producing layers of compound materials and alloys [24].
Depending on the type of pre-treatment of the substrate diﬀerent types of thin ﬁlms can be synthesized: polycrystalline,
textured and epitaxial. In a polycrystalline ﬁlm more than one lattice plane is exposed on the surface and the crystallites
are tilted and twisted. In textured and epitaxial ﬁlms only one facet is exposed on the surface. For the ﬁrst the crystallites
parallel to the surface are twisted but not signiﬁcantly tilted and for the latter the crystallites on the surface are neither
signiﬁcantly tilted nor considerably twisted. When crystallites are misoriented (tilted or twisted) grain-boundaries are
formed. This means that the amount and types of grain boundaries that are formed is the biggest for a polycrystalline
ﬁlm and the smallest for an epitaxial ﬁlm.
5.2
Underpotential deposition
Underpotential deposition (UPD) is a phenomenon by which a monolayer of a metal (M) is deposited on a diﬀerent metal
(the substrate, S) and occurs when potentials more positive than the equillibrium potential of the metal being deposited
are applied. The positive overpotential at which the process occurs is a consequence of a stronger interaction of the metal
with the surface of the substrate than with the surface of the same metal. The stronger interaction of the metal with the
substrate, in turn, is a result of the charge transfer from the metal to the substrate due to a higher electronegativity of the
substrate with respect to the metal.
After studying many UPD systems, Kolb and coauthors [25] proposed the following model to relate the potentials of bulk
and monolayer deposition using work functions instead of electronegativities (since work functions are also a measure
of electronegativity and have been determined more accurately):
VM,mon −VM,bulk = α(φS −φM)
(5.1)
where VM,mon is the peak potential for desorption of the monolayer of the metal, VM,bulk is the peak potential for
desorption of the bulk of the metal, α is a constant (0.5 V/eV), φS is the workfunction of the substrate and φM is the
workfunction of the metal. Basically the power of Equation 5.1 lies in its ability to predict the potential at which the UPD
of a metal on a substrate will occur.
5.3
X-ray techniques
5.3.1
X-ray diﬀraction
X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) is a bulk structure characterization technique that can identify the crystallographic phases that
are part of a catalyst. The advantage of XRD as a characterization tool for catalysts is that it gives unequivocal structure
information on particles that are suﬃciently large and is capable of providing this information under reaction conditions
due to its penetrating power. One of the drawbacks of the technique is that it cannot detect particles that are either too
small or amorphous [26] and other is that it is diﬃcult to get surface sensitive information.
10
==PAGE:16==
In this technique the sample is mounted in a goniometer which positions the sample at selected orientations while it is
illuminated with a beam of X-rays. A typical conﬁguration for XRD measurements is shown in Figure 5.2 where ω is the
incident angle and 2θ is the glancing angle. The goniometer positions the sample by rotating it (φ axis) or tilting it (ω
and χ axis).
Figure 5.2: Schematic illustration of XRD conﬁguration. Reprinted from [27]
The X-rays are usually generated by bombarding a metal with high-energy electrons. When a collision expels an electron
from an inner shell of the sample an electron of higher energy drops into the vacancy emitting the excess energy as
an X-ray photon. If the electron falls into a K shell (shell where the principal quantum number, n, is 1), the X-rays are
classiﬁed as K-radiation. Similarly for transitions to the L (n=2) and M (n=3) shells. Distinct lines are labelled as Kα, Kβ
and so on (see Figure 5.3).
Figure 5.3: X-ray emission from a metal. Reprinted from [28]
Condition for reﬂection and Bragg’s law
The result of the technique is a diﬀraction pattern which consists of spaced peaks known as reﬂections. Each reﬂection is
associated to a lattice plane and occurs, as shown in Figure 5.4, whenever the normal to that plane bisects the incident
and diﬀracted beams (diﬀraction is the interference caused by an object in the path of waves, it occurs only when the
dimensions of the diﬀraction object are comparable to the wavelength of the radiation).
(a) Reﬂection occurs
(b) Reﬂection does not occur
Figure 5.4: Condition necessary for reﬂection of a lattice plane. Reprinted from [29]

==PAGE:17==
Mathematically, a reﬂection is observed when Bragg’s law is fulﬁlled:
nλ = 2dsinθ
(5.2)
where λ is the wavelength of the incoming radiation and d is the spacing between lattice planes.
Characterization of epitaxial ﬁlms
The characterization of epitaxial ﬁlms can be completed by conducting symmetric, omega and phi scans and also
pole ﬁgures. Symmetric scans, also known as θ/2θ scans, identify the lattice planes parallel to the sample surface. To
accomplish this both the source and detector move so that the diﬀraction vector remains parallel to the surface. Omega
scans, also known as rocking curves, determine the average degree of tilting of a lattice plane parallel to the surface. In
this measurement the intensity of a single reﬂection (2θ is ﬁxed) is measured and the sample is tilted around the ω axis
(see Figure 5.2). Phi scans determine the average degree of twisting of a plane parallel to the surface by measuring the
intensity of a diﬀerent lattice plane. In this technique the detector is ﬁxed at an speciﬁc angle and the sample is rotated
around the φ axis (see Figure 5.2).
A pole ﬁgure provides information about the orientation distribution of a lattice plane. In this technique the intensity of
a single reﬂection (2θ is ﬁxed) is measured as a function of the orientation of the sample. The orientation is varied by
tilting (χ axis) and rotating the sample (φ axis). Thus, the pole ﬁgure is a combination of ω and φ scans.
X-ray optics
Focusing and parallel-beam optics are the alternatives for X-ray optics. Focusing optics employs a divergent incident
beam for high intensities and high resolution. The axial divergence can, however, lead to peak displacements and
asymmetrical peak proﬁles among others. Thus, it is not used for thin ﬁlm analysis.
In parallel beam optics (see Figure 5.5) the incident divergent X-ray beam is converted into a parallel beam by using
optical elements both on the incident and diﬀracted beam sides which limit axial divergence. The parallel beam makes it
possible to obtain accurate diﬀraction angles [30].
Figure 5.5: Parallel-beam optics for XRD. Reprinted from [30]
5.3.2
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is a catalyst characterization technique capable of yielding information about: i)
the elemental composition of the near-surface region, ii) the oxidation state of the elements and iii) the dispersion of one
phase over another. As such it is also capable to determine if the surface is contaminated and detect layers of adsorbed
materials.
In this technique the binding energy of the sample, which is characteristic of the element from which it comes, is inferred
when a valence or core electron is ejected by absorption of an X-ray photon. Because energy is conserved when a photon
ionizes a sample, the following equation must hold:
hν = Eb + Ek + φ
(5.3)
where ν is the frecuency of the exciting radiation, h is Planck’s constant, Eb is the binding energy of the ejected electron
(photoelectron), Ek is the kinetic energy of the photoelectron and φ is the work function of the spectrometer. Normally,
Mg Kα and Al Kα are the sources X-ray radiation. The kinetic energies of the photoelectrons are measured using an
electrostatic deﬂector. When electrons of diﬀerent speeds reach the detector the electron ﬂux is recorded and plotted
12
==PAGE:18==
against the binding energy, a plot known as the X-ray photoelectron emission spectrum.
The information provided by XPS comes from a penetration depth of a few nanometeres. Angle-resolved XPS (ARXPS),
on the other hand, can provide information from diﬀerent penetration depths by varying the angles at which electrons
are collected [31].
In addition to photoelectrons, Auger electrons are also observed in an XPS spectrum. An Auger electron originates
because the atom from which a photoelectron was ejected has a hole an is therefore unstable. In order to relax, an
electron of a higher shell ﬁlls the hole thereby releasing energy. This energy is taken up by another electron, the Auger
electron. Thus, Auger electrons have a ﬁxed kinetic energy. Then Auger electron peaks can be recognized by recording
spectra at two diﬀerent X-ray energies: XPS peaks appear at the same binding energy while Auger peaks shift their
position.
Photoelectron peaks are labeled according to the quantum numbers of the level from which the electron is ejected. A
level depends on the principal quantum number, n, and the angular quantum number, l. Moreover, taking into account
that the electron spin momentum, s, may be up (s = 1/2) or down (s = -1/2) each level (except those for which l=0) has two
sub-levels and they are labeled according to the total momentum j (j = l + s). Thus, each level gives rise to two peaks. For
example, the peaks of a 2p level (n=2, l=1) are labeled 2p1/2 and 2p3/2. In contrast, Auger electrons are labeled according
to X-ray spectroscopy terminology. A KLM Auger electron originates from a transition where the hole is in the K shell
and is ﬁlled by an electron of the L shell. The Auger electron is emitted from the M shell.
A problem in XPS measurements is that the sample acquires a positive charge due to the ejection of electrons. This
causes peaks to shift to higher binding energies but by the same amount. Thus, the problem can be overcome if the
sample contains an element in a state for which the binding energy is known. Alternatively, a ﬂood gun, which sprays
the sample with low energy electrons, can be used.
5.4
Ion spectroscopic techniques
5.4.1
Ion scattering spectroscopy
Ion scattering spectroscopy (ISS), also known as low energy ion scattering (LEIS), is a characterization tool capable of
yielding information of the outermost layer of a sample. In this technique a beam of noble gas ions with an energy Eo is
shot to the sample which are then scattered back from the surface with a reduced energy E since energy is lost in the
collision with surface atoms. The energy of the scattered ions is measured by an ion energy detector and the result of the
technique is an energy spectrum (plot of intensity vs kinetic energy). The energy lost by the ion depends on the masses
of the interacting atom and ion. Thus, the kinetic energy of the scattered ion reveals the mass of the atom from which it
scattered. The ions lose less energy to heavy than light atoms, as a result, higher kinetic energies in an energy spectrum
correspond to heavier atoms. Finally, it is important to note that only atoms heavier than the ion can be detected.
5.5
Electrochemical techniques
5.5.1
Cyclic voltammetry
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) is an electrochemical technique to investigate the reduction and oxidation processes of elements
and molecules. In a typical experiment one starts at a given potential which is ramped down linearly up to a switching
potential (also known as the vertex potential) at a constant rate, ν, called the sweep rate. The sweep in the negative
direction is called the cathodic scan. At the switching potential, the sweep rate is reversed and the potential is returned
to its original value. In the positive direction the scan is called the anodic scan. At all potentials the current is recorded.
A typical shape for a CV is shown in Figure 5.6
13
==PAGE:19==
Figure 5.6: Typical shape of cyclic voltammograms. Reprinted from [32]
Various phenomena aﬀect the shape observed in Figure 5.6: the kinetics of the reaction, the mass transfer of reactants and
products, the scan rate and the double-layer charging. The ’duck’ shape is mainly due to mass transfer eﬀects. To have a
clearer picture about what happens, let’s imagine we have a bulk solution which initially has equal amounts of a reduced
(R) and an oxidized (O) species that react according to: R ↔O + e−. When reduction is promoted (cathodic scan) O
is depleted on the surface of the electrode but it is also replenished from the bulk by molecular diﬀusion. However,
at some point a maximum rate of replenishment is reached and thus a further increase on potential does not result
in an increase in current (the limiting current is reached). As the scan proceeds the volume of solution containing
the reduced species (the diﬀusion layer) will continue to grow and this slows down the mass transport of O to the
electrode surface, thus the current decreases. When the switching potential is reached, the scan direction is reversed
and the previously formed reduced species are oxidized back. The two peaks are separated due to the diﬀusion of
the analyte to and from the electrode. It is important to realize that when the potential is being scanned the surface
concentration of both reactant and product change; thus, the reaction kinetics and equilibrium potential change constantly.
When the kinetics of the reaction is suﬃciently fast (electrochemically reversible) it is referred as a Nernstian process
and can be described by the Nernst equation. Nonetheless, when the reaction is more sluggish the kinetics is described
by the Butler-Volmer equation. Figure 5.7 shows the inﬂuence of kinetics on a cyclic voltammogram.
Figure 5.7: Eﬀect of kinetics on a cyclic voltammogram. Reprinted from [32]
As can be observed, the slower the kinetics the farther apart the peak potentials are. The inﬂuence of the scan rate is
shown in Figure 5.8. It is seen that as the scan rate is increase the peak currents also increase. This happens because the
diﬀusion layer thickness depends on the square root of time. Thus, the higher the scan rate the thinner the diﬀusion
14
==PAGE:20==
layer and the higher the limiting current.
Figure 5.8: Eﬀect of scan rate on a cyclic voltammogram. Reprinted from [32]
Finally, the charging of the double-layer gives rise to a ’non-faradaic’ current since no electron transfer is involved. The
equation that describes this process is:
ic = νCDL
(5.4)
where ic is the current associated with the double-layer charging and CDL is the speciﬁc double-layer capacitance of the
electrode. The double-layer current is constant for a given sweep rate and direction, but changes sign when the sweep
direction changes.
5.5.2
Chronoamperometry
Chronoamperometry (CA) is a potential step experiment in which the potential is changed instantaneously and the
current is measured as a function of time. In the context of this project CA is used to evaluate the activity of the
electrocatalyst.
5.6
Chromatographic techniques
5.6.1
Gas chromatography
Gas chromatography (GC) is an analytical technique to quantify the composition of gas and liquid samples. If the sample
is liquid it is vaporized and then mixed with an inert gas. The mixture is then passed through a column where the
components are separated by a liquid stationary phase that has diﬀerent aﬃnities for the components of the gas. Then
the components are quantiﬁed in a detector. The thermal conductivity detector is used as an universal detector which
has a detection limit of 500 pg/mL. When the sample contains hydrocarbons a ﬂame ionization detector (FID) can also be
used which has an improved detection limit of 1 pg/s. The output of this technique is a chromatogram which displays a
series of spaced peaks: the position of them is used to identify the components of the sample and the area under the
peaks is a measure of the quantity of each species.
5.6.2
High-performance liquid chromatography
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is a technique used to separate and identify the components of less
volatile samples compared to those analyzed in GC. The principle of HPLC is the same as that of the GC: heavier molecules
will elute more slowly than the lighter ones with polarity also playing a role. However, for HPLC the mobile phase is a
mixture of an organic solvent, ultrapure water and other components.

Experimental section
6.1
Synthesis of epitaxial ﬁlms
6.1.1
Magnetron sputtering
The synthesis of epitaxial thin ﬁlms was investigated by sputter deposition of Pd (99.999 % Kurt J. Lesker) and Ni (99.999
% Kurt J. Lesker) on Si wafers with (111) and (110) orientations (1-10 Ωcm Silicon Materials) as well as (100) orientation
(1-20 Ωcm Topsil). Before deposition the native oxide layer of the wafer was removed by etching the surface with a
5% HF solution (BASF) and the wafer was then transferred into the load lock of an AJA ATC-Orion 5 UHV sputtering
system in less than 5 min. The deposition was performed at a rate of 1 A/s that corresponded to 10 sccm and 2 mbar of
Ar and 30 W for Pd and 92 W for Ni, both of them regulated with a DC power supply. Deposition temperatures in the
range from 23 to 250 oC were used with the heating being achieved by an IR lamp with a control loop. The ﬁlm thickness
was 100 nm unless otherwise stated.
For the samples where the oxide layer of the Si wafer was removed by Ar sputtering the power was set to 40 W and the
substrate was sputtered for 5 min.
6.1.2
X-ray diﬀraction characterization
The epitaxial characteristics of the thin ﬁlms were analyzed with a Panalytical Empyrean X-ray diﬀractometer (XRD)
using Cu Kα radiation (45 kV, 40 mA). The diﬀraction measurements were conducted using parallel beam optics. The
optical elements employed on the incident beam side were a parallel beam mirror, a 1/8” divergence slit, a 0.04 rad Soller
slit and a beam mask narrower than the sample being analyzed. On the diﬀracted beam side, a parallel plate collimator, a
0.04 rad Soller slit and a Ni ﬁlter were used.
Symmetric scans were conducted to identify the planes grown parallel to the surface and were collected in the 2θ range
from 20 to 100 o with a step size of 0.005 o. Omega scans were carried out to determine the degree of tilting of the
crystallites parallel to the surface and were conducted, unless otherwise stated, in the ω range of +/- 5 o (step size of
0.01 o) with respect to the θ value of a given facet. Phi scans were conducted to determine the degree of twisting of the
crystallites and were carried out with a step size of 0.01 o in the φ range from 0 to 360 o.
6.2
Synthesis of overlayer structures
6.2.1
Underpotential deposition
All UPD experiments were carried out in a three-electrode setup as depicted in Figure 6.1. The solution reservoir was a
polypropylene beaker and the cap, also made of polypropylene, had 4 holes, 3 to ﬁt the electrodes and 1 to degass the

before every experiment and the reference electrode was a Ag/AgCl (3.5 M KCl) electrode (Innovative Instruments). The
electrolyte solution was prepared using 18.2 MΩcm deionized water. 60 mL of electrolyte were used for the experiments
which were degassed for 20 min prior to each measurement and also during the experiment.
Figure 6.1: Experimental setup for UPD experiments
A Biologic VSP-300 potentiostat was used to perform all the electrochemical experiments. The potential of the working
electrode was recorded against that of the reference electrode and then converted to the RHE scale. The resistance of
the electrolyte solution was determined by a current interrupt (CI) experiment with a set current of -1 mA that was
16
==PAGE:22==
interrupted after 0.05 s and repeated for 10 times. 85 % of the resulting averaged resistance was compensated in situ and
the remaining 15 % was corrected in the post-treatment of the data.
For the experiments carried out in a basic environment the electrolyte to study the Pd electrochemistry was 0.1 M KOH
(99.999 %, Sigma Aldrich) which was stored with Chelex 100 (analytical grade, Sigma Aldrich) in order to remove trace

stable CVs in the potential range from -0.43 to 1. 45 V vs RHE (full range CV) at 20 mV/s.
The time necessary to dissolve the Zn foil to the saturation concentration of 1.66x10−4 in 0.1 M KOH was estimated
from a corrosion-related polarization experiment. The setup was as usual except that the working electrode was the Zn
foil and the solution was not deaerated to resemble the actual conditions of dissolution. The potential was varied +/- 0.10
V vs OCV and the current was recorded.
In neutral conditions the electrolyte to study the Pd electrochemistry was 0.05 M KClO4 (99.99 %, Sigma Aldrich) while
that for Zn UPD on Pd was 0.05 M KClO4 with 400 µM Zn(NO3)2 (99.998 % Alfa Aesar). The CVs of Pd with and
without Zn were collected in 2 potential ranges: from -0.55 to 1.95 V vs RHE (full range CV) and from 0.1 to 1.1 V vs RHE
(UPD region). The electrochemical behaviour of Pd with and without Zn was studied by recording stable CVs at 100
mV/s.
The overlayer structures were synthesized by conducting a cathodic sweep in the UPD region. A time study was
carried out to determine the time for Zn coverage at steady state and a potential study to determine the Zn coverage
corresponding to diﬀerent potentials was also performed. First, the beaker was ﬁlled with 60 mL of the 0.05 M KClO4
solution and a stable CV was recorded to ensure usual behaviour. Then 600 µL of a concentrated solution of Zn(NO3)2
was added in-situ to reach a concentration of 400 µM Zn(NO3)2 and CVs were also recorded until stable. Afterwards,
the electrode was sweeped cathodically up to the potential of interest and held at this potential for a given time. The
time study was conducted by holding the electrode at 0.10 V vs RHE for 2, 6, 10 and 14 min and the potential study was
carried out by holding the electrode at 0.10, 0.35 and 0.60 V vs RHE for 10 min. An additional sample to determine the
remaining Zn content upon desorption was also prepared. In this case an anodic sweep was run after the cathodic scan
and the electrode was held at 1.1 V vs RHE.
6.2.2
XPS and ISS characterization
The near-surface composition of the overlayer structures was measured by ARXPS with a Thermo Scientiﬁc Theta Probe
X-ray photoelectron spectrometer using Al Kα radiation (15 kV, 15 mA) and a pass energy of 200 eV. Pd was quantiﬁed
using the Pd3d region (329 to 347 eV) and the amount of Zn was measured using the Zn2p region (1015 to 1060 eV). The
emission angle at which electrons were collected were in the range from 20 to 80 o relative to the surface normal. Survey
scans to ensure that the electrocatalysts were synthesized without impurities were also conducted; only peaks related
to Pd, Zn, C and O were identiﬁed in all the samples. Also, scans of the C1s and Zn Auger regions were collected to
verify the oxidation state of Zn. All the samples displayed an oxidation state of +2 for Zn indicating that Zn-O bonds
were formed. Lastly, a sensitiviy factor analysis was carried out to make sure that the calculated compositions were correct.
In order to study the structure of the synthesized electrocatalysts depth proﬁle experiments by ISS were conducted in the
same equipment. Seven ISS spectra were collected using a He ion beam with an energy of 1 keV.

7.1
Synthesis of epitaxial ﬁlms
7.1.1
Eﬀect of pre-treatment
Si wafers have an amorphous native oxide layer (Figure 7.1 a) that needs to be removed before epitaxial growth [24].
Techniques for oxide layer removal are Ar sputtering and HF etching. While the ﬁrst produces a roughened surface as
shown in Figure 7.1 b the latter produces a more even surface (Figure 7.1 c). In this section the choice of HF-etching as
the pre-treatment technique to synthesize epitaxial thin ﬁlms is justiﬁed.
Figure 7.1: Schematic representation of eﬀect of pre-treatment of Si wafer: a) None, b) Ar sputtering, c) HF etching
The ﬁrst step to verify a thin ﬁlm is epitaxial is to carry out a symmetric scan. In a symmetric scan only the planes parallel
to the sample surface are detected and epitaxial ﬁlms expose only one plane on the surface. Thus, the symmetric scan of
an epitaxial ﬁlm should display only one reﬂection corresponding to the deposited ﬁlm. Exceptions correspond to lat-
tice planes that have a parallel plane or a lattice plane (or planes) that are at the interface between the ﬁlm and the substrate.
Figure 7.2 shows the symmetric scans of Pd thin ﬁlms deposited on Si(hkl) for the cases when the substrate is not
pre-treated, Ar-sputtered and HF-etched. The sample whose substrate is not pre-treated is included for comparison
purposes. For this case the symmetric scan (Figure 7.2 a) shows that Pd(111) and Pd(002) are parallel to the sample
surface although the latter displays a reduced relative intensity. This, however, suggests that the sample is not epitaxial.
Conversely, for the cases with substrate pre-treatment (Figures 7.2 b and 7.2 c) only the reﬂection for Pd(111), and the
corresponding parallel plane, Pd(222), are observed. When compared to the peak of Si (hkl) the intensity of Pd(111) is also
seen to be more intense for the cases when the substrate is pre-treated than when not. This means that pre-treatment of
the substrate favors the growth of more Pd(111) planes parallel to the surface. Although there is a ﬁrst indication that the
Ar-sputtered and HF-etched samples are epitaxial further characterization is needed to conﬁrm it.
Figure 7.2: Symmetric scans of Pd/Si(hkl) thin ﬁlms grown at 23 oC with the following pretreatments: a) None - Si(002) b)
Ar sputtering - Si(002) and c) HF etching - Si(111). Film thickness: 100 nm Pd
The next step to conﬁrm a thin ﬁlm is epitaxial is to carry out an omega scan. As discussed in Section 5.3.1, an omega scan
determines the degree of misorientation of a lattice plane relative to the sample surface so it is a measure of its tilt breadth.
For a lattice plane that is perfectly parallel to the surface the result of the scan would be a line; however, due to growth
18
==PAGE:24==
defects, thin ﬁlms display a peak. The quality of the thin ﬁlm is judged by the FWHM of that peak, the narrower the better.
Figure 7.3 shows the omega scans of the Pd(111) reﬂection for the diﬀerent evaluated pre-treatments of the substrate. In
the case of Ar-sputtering (Figure 7.3 b) the FWHM is 1.9 o indicating that, on average, the Pd(111) lattice plane is 1.9 o
tilted with respect to the sample surface. For the HF-etched sample (Figure 7.3 c) this degree of misorientation is 2.2 o.
The omega scan for the sample without pretreatment (Figure 7.3 a) was also included for comparative purposes. All 3
scans were recorded at the same conditions, i.e. by tilting the sample by 10 o (5 o counterclockwise and 5 o clockwise) and
what is evidenced from Figure 7.3a is that when the substrate is not pre-treated the Pd(111) planes are tilted well beyond
this range indicating that the corresponding ﬁlm displays a minimal preferred growth orientation which conﬁrms that
epitaxial ﬁlms cannot be grown in substrates that are not pre-treated. In brief, what is concluded from Figure 7.3 is that
the thin ﬁlms synthesized with Ar-sputtering and HF-etching as the substrate pre-treatment could still be epitaxial but a
ﬁnal test is needed to conclude this.
Figure 7.3: Omega scans of Pd(111) in Pd/Si(hkl) thin ﬁlms grown at 23 oC with the following pretreatments: a) None -
Si(002) b) Ar sputtering - Si(002) and c) HF etching - Si(111). Film thickness: 100 nm Pd
The ﬁnal step to conﬁrm a thin ﬁlm is epitaxial is to conduct a phi scan. While the omega scan is a measure of the tilt
breadth of a lattice plane, the phi scan is a measure of its twist breadth. In this technique a lattice plane diﬀerent from
the lattice plane that is parallel to the surface is probed while the sample is rotated. The result is a series of reﬂections
that should correspond to the symmetry of the lattice plane parallel to the surface. As in the case of the omega scan, the
FWHM of these peaks is also a measure of the epitaxial quality of the thin ﬁlm.
Figure 7.4 exhibits the phi scans of the Pd(111) planes for the considered pre-treatments of the Si wafer. Only in the
case of HF-etching (Figure 7.4c) 3 periodic reﬂections are observed. These reﬂections have 120 o intervals and display
the three-fold symmetry characteristic of a (111) plane. Moreover, the reﬂections are very sharp almost corresponding
to straight lines which indicates that the Pd(111) crystals have minimal crystal twist and therefore a minimal amount
of grain boundaries. In the case of Ar-sputtering (Figure 7.4b) no periodic reﬂections are observed which indicates the
corresponding thin ﬁlm is textured. Finally, as expected, the ﬁlm with no pre-treatment of the substrate (Figure 7.4 a)
also does not display epitaxial characteristics.

This section presented a thorough discussion of how epitaxial ﬁlms are characterized and the eﬀect of the pre-treatment
on the type of ﬁlm that is obtained. In summary, to verify epitaxial growth symmetric, omega and phi scans need to be
conducted. Regarding the eﬀect of the pre-treatment it was demonstrated that HF-etching of the substrate is necessary
for epitaxial growth. When the substrate was Ar-sputtered a textured ﬁlm was obtained and when no pre-treatment of
the substrate was applied the thin ﬁlm did not display a preferred growth orientation.
7.1.2
Synthesis of Pd(111)
The diﬀusivity of a metal deposited on a Si wafer is an important parameter in epitaxial growth since the formation
of silicides is necessary for epitaxial ﬁlms and has been evidenced in the growth of Cu ﬁlms [33]. The diﬀusivity of
the metal, D, can be controlled by changing the deposition temperature, T, and both are related by an Arrhenius-type
expression as follows:
D = AeE/kT
(7.1)
where A is a pre-exponential factor, E is the activation energy and k is the Boltzmann constant. Cu is known to grow
epitaxially at 20 oC [34] temperature at which the diﬀusivity on Si is 6.4x1022 cm2/s (calculated from experimental
data compiled in [35]). Thus, assuming other metals require the same diﬀusivity to grow epitaxially, a proxy for the
deposition temperature can be obtained as shown in Figure 7.5 (the calculations for the plot were also completed using
the experimental data compiled in [35]). It is observed that, theoretically, epitaxial Pd is obtained at 40 oC. In practice,
however, the deposition of Pd was carried out at room temperature (23 oC) due to limitations of the UHV sputtering
chamber for temperature control.
Figure 7.5: Theoretical estimation of deposition temperature for epitaxial growth of diﬀerent metals on Si
The symmetric scans of 100 nm Pd thin ﬁlms deposited on HF-etched Si(100), Si(110) and Si(111) at room temperature are
shown in Figure 7.6. The scans indicate that no matter the orientation of the Si wafer Pd(111) is the only plane that is
20
==PAGE:26==
grown parallel to the surface. These experimental results can be rationalized in terms of the surface energy of the lattice
planes of Pd which in turn depend on their coordination number. Pd has a face-centred cubic (fcc) crystal structure and
the coordination number of the planes for an fcc metal is as follows: (111) = 9, (100) = 8 and (110) = 7 [36]. A higher
coordination number means that the plane is missing less bonds and this results in a lower energy surface. Therefore,
(111) is the most stable plane in an fcc structure and this could explain why Pd(111) is grown on all the considered
substrate orientations.
Figure 7.6: Symmetric scans of Pd/Si(hkl) thin ﬁlms grown at 23 oC: a) Si(111) b) Si(100) and c) Si(110)
The omega scans of the Pd(111) planes grown on the diﬀerent substrate orientations are shown in Figure 7.7. Based on
the FWHM of the peaks the ﬁlm grown on Si(100) exhibits the least tilt breadth (2.3 o) while that grown on Si(110) has
the largest (3 o). For all 3 cases, however, the results are indicative (although do not conﬁrm) epitaxial growth.
Figure 7.7: Omega scans of Pd(111) in Pd/Si(hkl) thin ﬁlms grown at 23 oC: a) Si(111) b) Si(100) and c) Si(110). Film thickness:
100 nm Pd

epitaxial Pd(111) while the ﬁlms grown on Si(100) and Si(110) are textured Pd(111).

7.2.1
Palladium electrochemistry in basic conditions
It was decided to ﬁrst study Zn UPD on Pd at pH 13 so that the conditions of electrocatalyst synthesis and testing can be
kept uniform (COR is conducted at pH 13 to ensure an stable local pH on the electrode). As a ﬁrst step to understand Zn
UPD on Pd the electrochemistry of pure Pd at pH 13 was investigated.
Figure 7.20 shows the experimental CV of Pd(111) in the potential range from hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) to
oxygen evolution reaction (OER), referred henceforth as the full range potential, when the electrolyte is 0.1 M KOH.
The thermodynamic potentials of relevant reactions are also exhibited. According to a literature review [38, 39, 40] the
observed currents correspond to the following processes : The anodic current starting at ca. 1.5 V vs RHE is a result of
the formation of Pd oxides and is a convoluted process with OER. The cathodic peak starting at about 0.8 V vs RHE is the
corresponding oxide reduction. Further in the cathodic region, the large current starting at ca. 0.3 V vs RHE corresponds
to HER and adsorption and absorption of H, the latter being a unique property of Pd. Finally, the anodic peak starting at
ca. 0.3 V vs RHE is assigned to the desorption of H and possibly to the adsorption of OH−ions. However, due to the
27
==PAGE:33==
ability of Pd to not only adsorb but also absorb H, the current density for H desorption could signiﬁcantly obscure that of
OH−adsorption.
Figure 7.20: CV proﬁle for Pd(111) in 0.1 M KOH (pH=13) recorded at 23 oC and 20 mV/s. Dotted lines represent equilibrium
potentials for relevant reactions
The equilibrium potentials displayed in Figure 7.20 were plotted to verify a logical assignment of processes to peaks and
were calculated using the Nernst equation. The electrochemical reactions and equations for the equilibrium potentials (V
vs SHE) for the HER, OER and formation of PdO (in hydroxide form since Pd has an oxidation state of 2+ [41]) in basic
solutions are shown below:
H2O + 2e−↔H2 + 2OH−
U = −0.0591pH
(7.2)
4OH−↔O2 + 2H2O + 4e−
U = 1.229 −0.0591pH
(7.3)
Pd + H2O ↔PdO + 2H+ + 2e−
U = 0.897 −0.0591pH
(7.4)
At potentials greater than U the anodic reaction is favored. Thus, the assignment of the furthest anodic current to oxide
formation and OER is thermodinamically feasible. On the other hand, when the applied potential is below U the cathodic
reaction is promoted. In this regard, the partial assignment of the furthest cathodic current to HER and the assignment of
the cathodic peak to PdO reduction are also thermodinamically feasible.
Further veriﬁcation that the peak pairs were correctly identiﬁed was carried out by studying the eﬀect of both the
anodic and cathodic vertex potential on the CV of Pd(111) and is shown in Figure 7.21. As the cathodic vertex is
increased (Figure 7.21a) it is observed that both the current for H absorption and desorption increase proportionally.
This unequivocally indicates that both peaks are related and that the more cathodic the vertex potential is the more H is
absorbed and subsequently desorbed. Likewise, as the anodic vertex is increased (Figure 7.21 b) a proportional increase
in the currents for oxide formation and reduction is evidenced conﬁrming that both peaks are related. Thus, the more
anodic the vertex potential is the more oxide is formed and therefore the more oxide is reduced upon the cathodic sweep.
Regarding the formation and reduction of Pd oxides, the repetitive cycling between these processes has been reported to
increase the electrode surface area by e.g. formation of defects such as the rearrangement of atoms at grain boundaries [40].

H adsorption can be classiﬁed as H underpotential deposition (H UPD) or H overpotential deposition (H OPD) and both

While H UPD occurs at more anodic potentials H OPD happens at more cathodic potentials than the equilibrium potential

and HER. H absorption by Pd has been linked to crack formations and embrittlement of the electrode due to the formation
of interstitial hydrides [40].
7.2.2
UPD in basic conditions
It was decided to use Zn foil to prepare the electrolyte required for Zn UPD on Pd. The main advantage of this approach

Kinetics of dissolution of zinc foil in basic conditions
Although thermodynamics oﬀers an important insight into corrosion it cannot predict its rate. In order to assess if the
Zn foil can be dissolved in a reasonable time an experimental kinetic study was performed. The corrosion rate was
determined by recording a corrosion-related polarization curve. In practice the electrolyte for UPD was prepared by
leaving a Zn foil sitting in a quiescent 0.1 M KOH solution. Thus, in order to resemble these conditions, the electrolyte
for the experimental setup was not deaerated.
In a corrosion-related polarization experiment the potential is varied above and below the OCV (which corresponds
to the corrosion potential) and the current is measured. When the potential is increased the current due to the anodic
reaction (dissolution of zinc) is measured and when the the potential is decreased the current due to the cathodic reaction
(ORR) is recorded. Then at high enough overpotentials (> 0.1 V) the kinetics of the reactions can be described by a Tafel
approximation and the corrosion current can be estimated by linearly extrapolating the current of the controlling process
which at pH 13 the is the anode kinetics [43]. The polarization curve at pH 13 is shown in Figure 7.22. The potential was
varied +/- 0.10 V vs OCV in order not to drive additional reactions.

7.2.3
Palladium electrochemistry in neutral conditions
The considerations to choose the salt for the new electrolyte include not only that the salt can render a neutral solution
upon dissociation but also, very importantly, that the corresponding ions do not strongly interact with Pd so that it
could be detrimental for Zn UPD. It was also important that the salt be readily available to start the new experiments.
For all these reasons, 0.05 M KClO4 (pH 7) was chosen as the new electrolyte. KClO4 gives rise to a neutral solution
because neither of its ions undergoes hydrolysis to an appreciable extent (generally salts that contain an alkali or alkaline
earth metal ion and the conjugate base of strong acid render neutral solutions [41]). Regarding the interaction of ions in

solution with Pd, neither K+ nor ClO−4 adsorb strongly to the electrode surface [40]. The concentration of the electrolyte
was set at 0.05 M due to the low solubility of KClO4 in water (13.9 g/L or 0.1 M at 20 oC). The lower concentration
compared to the basic conditions should not be a problem though since it only impacts the resistance of the electrolyte
which is compensated by the potentiostat software and corrected manually afterwards.

Conﬁrming UPD region by XPS
As discussed above, the CVs in Figure 7.26 suggest that Zn UPD occurs in the potential region from 0.1 to 1.1 V vs
RHE. Thus, when the electrode is sweeped cathodically Zn should be deposited (up to monolayer coverage) and con-
versely, when the electrode is sweeped anodically Zn should be desorbed. Considering that the UPD region might
span a potential range a bit larger than that shown in Figure 7.26, this means that on the cathodic vertex (0.1 V vs
RHE) the most Zn is expected while on the anodic vertex (1.1 V vs RHE) the least Zn should be observed. In order
to quantify the Zn composition in the near-surface region as a function of potential angle-resolved XPS (ARXPS) ex-
periments were conducted. Here it is important to note that the calculated compositions do not correspond to those
at the surface but to an average composition in the near surface region which is heavily inﬂuenced by the upper lay-
ers. Thus, high Zn contents in the near-surface region are indicative that more than 1 ML (monolayer) of Zn was deposited.

From a fundamental point of view the driving force for an overlayer structure is the surface energy diﬀerence between
Pd and Zn and that for alloying is the energy for alloy formation. Pd has an fcc structure and exposes the (111) facet
which corresponds to a surface energy of 0.54 eV/atom (see Appendix A.3). Assuming that Zn adopts the same crystal
structure and exposes the sample plane as Pd the surface energy for Zn is 0.40 eV/atom (see Appendix A.3). In that
Zn has the lowest surface energy Zn is favored to remain on the surface and the driving force for this process is 0.40 -
0.54 = -0.14 eV/atom. On the other hand, the energy for PdZn alloy formation, is -0.575 eV/atom [23]. Thus, there is a
driving force for the adsorbed Zn to enter the surface and this fundamentally validates the model proposed in Figure 7.33a.
Further experimental validation of the structure elucidated in the simulation (Figure 7.33a) was carried out by ISS depth
proﬁling. Although the technique is not quantitative it was used to give a qualitative picture of the relative amount of
elements on the surface and the near-surface region by removing atoms from the surface in sequential scans. Figure
7.34 shows how the Pd intensity evolves with each scan for all samples in the potential study and compares them to
that of pure Pd. It is observed that the intensity of pure Pd evolves quickly and this is due to the ease of removal of C
and O atoms. Comparatively, the intensity of the cathodic and anodic samples increases more slowly which is due to
removal of not only C and O but also Zn which has a much lower sputter yield due to its heavier atomic mass. It is also
observed that as the sample is more cathodic the Pd intensity evolves more slowly which means that more Zn is present
and further supports the conclusion that the Zn content can be systematically changed by potential.

On balance, this section shows that the Zn content deposited on Pd can be systematically controlled with potential which
is pivotal for the synthesis of Zn-modiﬁed Pd electrocatalysts with diﬀerent compositions. It also revealed that Zn UPD
on Pd is an abnormal UPD system that results in an structure more complex than a Zn overlayer (a Zn overlayer with
sub-surface alloy). The 2 metrics that make this system exceptional is that Pd and Zn have diﬀerent work functions (this
is the driving force for UPD) and the energy for alloy formation is greater than the surface energy diﬀerence (this enables
alloying). Thus, using this insight a prediction about what other metal systems might behave in the same way can be
made.

Conclusions
The electrochemical reduction of CO2 is a promising technology to store intermittent renewable electricity in the form
of carbon-neutral fuels and chemicals. However, to bring this technology to commercial scale novel electrocatalysts are

==PAGE:46==
needed since Cu, the only monometallic electrocatalyst capable to reduce CO2 to multicarbon products, is still not active
and selective enough.
The ability of Cu to reduce CO2 to fuels and chemicals is a consequence of its unique electronic structure which results
in an intermediate CO binding energy that enables C-C coupling. Thus, a promising approach to synthesize novel
electrocatalysts is to modify the electronic structure of one metal so that it displays a CO binding energy similar to that of
Cu. In this project, the synthesis of Zn-modiﬁed Pd electrocatalysts was investigated in the light that it has been shown
that the CO binding energy of Pd(111) can be modulated to resemble that of Cu when it interacts with sub-monolayer
amounts of Zn.
For this purpose, the synthesis of Pd(111), Pd(100) and Pd(110) epitaxial ﬁlms was investigated by magnetron sputter-
ing and the resulting ﬁlms were characterized by XRD. The synthesis of Pd(111) was successfully conducted at room
temperature using HF-etched Si(111). In contrast, the synthesis of Pd(100) and Pd(110) proved to be more challeng-
ing. The synthesis of Pd(100) requires an optimization of the Ni interlayer provided that the the Pd/Ni/Si(004) ﬁlm
synthesized with an interlayer of 20 nm Ni at 200 oC and 100 nm Pd at 23 oC is not epitaxial Pd(100). On the other
hand, the synthesis of Pd(110) using a Ni interlayer seems unlikely and therefore other approaches need to be investigated.
The deposition of Zn on Pd(111) was carried out by UPD to ensure sub-monolayer amounts of Zn were deposited on Pd
and was investigated at pH 7 and 13. The near-surface composition and the structure of the synthesized electrocatalysts
were investigated by ARXPS and ISS. Zn UPD on Pd(111) on basic conditions (pH 13) was non-reproducible and this is
likely attributed to the low solubility and slow dissolution kinetics of Zn on a basic environment. On the other hand, Zn
UPD on Pd(111) on neutral conditions (pH 7) was more stable.
At pH 7 it was shown that the amount of Zn deposited on Pd can be systematically controlled by potential which is
crucial for the synthesis of Zn-modiﬁed Pd electrocatalysts with diﬀerent compositions. During the project it was also
discovered that Zn UPD on Pd is an unusual UPD system in that it is not electrochemically irreversible (i.e. it does not
occur instantaneously) and the Zn content displays a linear relationship with potential. By a thorough analysis of the
ARXPS and ISS data it was shown that this strange behaviour occurs because Zn is not only deposited on the surface but
is also incorporated as an alloy. The 2 metrics that make this system particular is that Pd and Zn have diﬀerent work
functions and the energy for alloying is greater than the surface energy diﬀerence. Thus, this insight can used to predict
what other metal systems might behave in the same way.
