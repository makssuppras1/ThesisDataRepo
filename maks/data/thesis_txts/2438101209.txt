Abstract 
Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is a metabolic disease that affects millions of people worldwide disrupting the 
body’s ability to regulate blood glucose. Multiple risk factors are associated with the development of the 
disease, such as excess body weight, and it is hypothesised that the human gut microbiota takes part in 
and adds to the development of the disease. The gut microbiome assists in digesting otherwise indigestible 
plat polysaccharides and complex carbohydrates, thereby generating excess energy together with 
monosaccharaides and short-chain fatty acids, which interacts with and within the host’s metabolic 
processes and immune system. The most widely used pharmacotherapy for T2D is metformin, which 
assists in regulation of the blood glucose. Recent studies have suggested that treatment with metformin 
alters the composition of the T2D gut microbiome, and that microbiome studies should exclude the 
metformin treated individuals from studies when focusing on defining the T2D microbiome. 
This study aimed to identify microbial signatures of the T2D gut microbiome with different statistical 
methods as well as investigating metformin’s plausible effects on the T2D gut microbiota. The 
microbiome dataset was investigated at both the Operational Taxonomic Unit (OTU) and taxonomic 
Genus levels with three statistical tests; Fisher’s Exact Test, Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test and DESeq2, for 
both prevalence and abundance differential analyses of the sample populations. Additionally, a Bayesian 
logistic regression method was used for predictive modelling of the microbial composition, determining 
the host’s disease phenotype. The study provided insight into the different tools for microbiome analysis 
as well as worked with setting up statistical null hypotheses for differences in microbial compositions of 
different sample populations. 
The study found that excluding metformin treated individuals is a necessity when searching for true T2D 
microbial signatures, as the statistical tests found multiple taxa differentially expressed between the 
metformin treated and metformin naïve T2D samples. Furthermore, when the metformin treated samples 
were included, differently abundant taxa turned out to be a signature of the metformin treated T2D gut 
microbiome. A group of taxa having the same Genus level Lachnoclostridium were the strongest signal for 
a microbial signature of the T2D gut microbiome. Furthermore, another set of taxa, including multiple 
taxa of the Family Ruminococcaceae and Genus Dialister, were found more often and more abundant in 
the normal glucose tolerant (NGT) individuals in comparison with the metformin naïve T2D individuals. 
The study found the taxa with Genus Escherichia/Shigella were more abundant in the metformin treated 
T2D samples, which supports findings of analogous microbiome studies.

Resumé 
Type 2-diabetes (T2D) er en metabolisk sygdom, der rammer millioner af mennesker verden over ved at 
forstyrre kroppens evne til at regulere blodglukose. Flere risikofaktorer er forbundet med udviklingen af 
sygdommen, såsom kraftig overvægt, og det antages, at den humane tarmmikrobiota tager del i og 
påvirker sygdommens udvikling. Tarmmikrobiomet hjælper med at fordøje ellers ufordøjelige 
plantepolysakkarider og komplekse kulhydrater og derved generere overskydende energi sammen med 
monosakkarider og kortkædede fedtsyrer, som interagerer med og i værtens metaboliske processer og 
immunsystem. Den mest anvendte farmakoterapi til behandling af T2D er metformin, der bistår i 
regulering af blodglukosen. Nylige studier har antydet, at behandling med metformin ændrer 
sammensætningen af tarmmikrobiomet hos patienter med T2D, og at mikrobiomstudier bør udelukke de 
metforminbehandlede personer fra undersøgelsen, når der fokuseres på at definere mikrobiomet hos type 
2-diabetikere. 
Dette studie havde til formål at identificere mikrobielle signaturer af tarmmikrobiomet hos type 2-
diabetikere via forskellige statistiske metoder, samt at efterforske en mulig påvirkning af metformin på 
tarmmikrobiotaet. Mikrobiomdatasættet blev undersøgt på både Operational Taxonomic Unit (OTU)-
plan og taksonomisk genusniveau ved tre statistiske tests; Fisher's Exact Test, Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test 
og DESeq2, både for ’prevalence’- og ’abundance’-differentialanalyser af populationerne. Desuden blev 
en Bayesiansk logistisk regressionsmetode anvendt til prædiktiv modellering af den mikrobielle 
sammensætning til bestemmelse af værtens fænotype. Undersøgelsen gav indsigt i de forskellige værktøjer 
til mikrobiomanalyse, såvel som arbejdet med at opsætte statistiske nulhypoteser for forskelle i 
mikrobiomsammensætningen af forskellige populationer. 
Studiet viste at udelukkelse af metforminbehandlede individer er nødvendig, når der skal søges efter sande 
T2D-mikrobielle signaturer, da de statistiske tests fandt flere taxa differentielt udtrykt mellem de 
metforminbehandlede og metforminnaive T2D-populationer. Når de metforminbehandlede prøver var 
inkluderet, viste det sig endvidere at være signatur af den metforminbehandlede T2D-tarmmikrobiom. 
En gruppe af taxa med det samme genusniveau, Lachnoclostridium, var det stærkeste signal for en 
mikrobiel signatur af T2D-tarmmikrobiomet. Endvidere blev et andet sæt taxa, herunder flere taxa fra 
familieniveauet Ruminococcaceae og genusniveauet Dialister, fundet oftere og i hyppigere forekomst hos 
normal glukosetolerante (NGT) individer i sammenligning med metforminnaive individer med T2D. 
Studiet viste, at taxa med genusniveauet Escherichia/Shigella havde en hyppigere forekomst hos de 
metforminbehandlede individer med T2D, hvilket understøtter resultaterne af analoge mikrobiomstudier.

1 Introduction 
From the moment we are born, we rely on microbes to help us establish a stable health system. The 
human body consists of trillions of microbes interacting in both constructive and destructive ways in a 
battle for symbiotic balance [1]. The awareness and understanding of the microbes residing inside the 
human body has evolved dramatically within the last century, going from ignorance of their existence, to 
unravelling their involvement in infections and diseases, allergies and sensitisation, and continuing into 
this moment, where the gut microbiota is being studied and acknowledged as a key player in the human 
health system. Research is not only looking for the involvement of the microbiome, which is the collective 
genome of the gut microbiota, in human health, but also the environmental factors affecting it, such as 
diet, exercise, disease status, medication etc. [2], [3]. Figure 1 illustrates a simplified version of the 
biological system that the microbiome is part of, and it also 
represents the general research hypothesis that the 
microbiome is involved in a complicated and dynamic 
system [4].  
This master’s thesis “Statistical Analysis of Microbiome 
Data for Disease Signatures” is an in-silico study of the 
human gut microbiome looking for microbial signatures of 
the disease type 2 diabetes (T2D) and any possible effects 
of the anti-diabetic agent metformin. A better understanding 
of the interaction between the microbiome, metabolic processes, environment, and pharmacotherapy, 
would make it possible to provide and improve treatment of patients with T2D for which there still seems 
to be an unmet medical need [2]. The thesis was conducted as a sub-study to a larger gut microbiota 
study, which was initiated in 2013 by the University of Copenhagen (KU). The original cohort includes 
around 450 individuals from Denmark, and this sub-study employs a subset of data from that cohort, 
including two thirds of the subjects. The dataset will be explored and analysed with different statistical 
methodologies in order to gain knowledge on how to conduct a microbiome study. In the following 
sections, the human gut microbiota will, together with the tools for analysing microbiome data and how 
to conduct a microbiome study be presented. Acknowledgement goes to Oluf Pedersen, Torben Hansen 
and Henrik Vestergaard for the data on the Danish samples.  
1.1 The human gut microbiota 
The human body contains trillions of microbes existing both on the inside and outside of the body, and 
the largest concentration of microbes is found within the gastrointestinal tract (GIT), having a huge 
surface area of around 250-400 m2 for living cells and microbes to interact and exchange nutrients and 
other substances [1], [2]. The majority of gut inhabitants are of bacterial origin though virus, archaea and

fungi also are found in the GIT and contribute to the balance of the gut microbiome [2]. The capacity of 
the gut community is beyond imagination with the number of microbes exceeding 1014, which is 
approximately 10 times more than the amount of human cells that the body consists of, furthermore, the 
genomic pool of the community microbes is 100 times greater than that of the human genome [1]. The 
two most predominant Phylum groups of bacteria residing in the gut are Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, 
though the microbiome (the genomic pool that the microbiota consists of) is like a fingerprint and can 
even vary significantly among family members, as the changeability of gut composition is so great that 
acute change of diet or a sudden infection have been observed to alter the gut composition within just 24 
hours of initiation [3], [5]. Both animal models and human studies have linked alterations in gut 
microbiota with changed immune response, induced inflammation and differences in metabolic processes 
such as the lipid and glucose metabolism [2]. Furthermore, experimental studies, where microbiota from 
disease donners and controls have been transferred into germ-free hosts (such as mice), have provided 
evidence that the composition indeed can affect the host and drive disease or health conditions [2], [6].  
Thus, the human gut microbiota seems to play an important role in many processes in the body, where 
the main ones are: (1) energy harvesting, (2) host metabolism and (3) immune response and homeostasis, 
and thereby influence the human health state [2], [3], [7]. An animal-based diet is said to promote the 
abundance of bile-tolerant microbes such as Bacteroides, thus reducing the abundance of Firmicutes that 
metabolise dietary plant polysaccharides [3]. The gut bacteria express carbohydrate-active enzymes, 
which assist digesting otherwise indigestible plat polysaccharides and complex carbohydrates thereby 
generating excess energy together with monosaccharaides and short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) [1], [8]. 
SCFAs absorbed by epithelial cells in the GIT are involved in various cellular processes [1]. SCFAs, such 
as acetate, butyrate, and propionate, take part in synthesis of triglycerides in the liver, and can become 
incorporated into adipocytes (also known as lipocytes and fat cells) and thereby increase the risk of 
developing obesity, which of now is a worldwide epidemic and one of the contributing risk factors for 
development of T2D [2], [5], [8], [9]. However, the SCFAs do also help the immune system by 
influencing the production of cytokines [1]. Another important function that the gut microbiota takes 
part in is synthesis and absorption of vitamins and essential amino acids which cannot be produced by 
the host (both humans and animals) [1]–[3], [5]. Examples of contributing bacteria are Lactic acid bacteria 
as key microbes in the production of vitamin B12, Bifidobacteria in the production of folate, while other 
bacteria are involved in synthesis of vitamin K, riboflavin, biotin etc. [1]. The gut microbiota influences 
the maturation and adaptation of the immune system together with defending intrusion of pathogens or 
aggressively opportunistic microbes and regulation of intestinal hormone secretion [1], [2]. The 
production of excess nutrients or compounds together with the composition of the gut microbiota have 
been associated with the development of diseases, such as obesity, T2D, cardiovascular disease and 
inflammatory bowel disease [2], [5].

1.2 Type 2 diabetes and metformin 
Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is a chronic metabolic disease where the body has developed insulin resistance 
and cannot efficiently use the insulin that it produces [10]. Pancreas secretes insulin, which is a hormone 
that regulates blood glucose, and opposite to T2D, people with type 1 diabetes suffers from deficiency in 
insulin production [11]. The risk of developing T2D is caused by both metabolic and genetic factors, 
though the main risk factor is excess body weight (and obesity), which most often is caused by 
malnutrition and physical inactivity. Other risk factors are high waist size, high body mass index (BMI), 
smoking and early childhood malnutrition [10], [11]. Suffering from T2D can cause a number of 
additional diseases, some which are related to damage of the macro- and microvascular system of the 
heart, eyes, blood vessels, nerves and kidneys, leading to disability and premature death [11]. The World 
Health Organisation (WHO) stated in their global report on diabetes in 2016 that in 2014 T2D affected 
422 million people worldwide and that the prevalence of diabetes is growing rapidly. T2D, insulin 
resistance, and obesity have all been correlated with alterations in gut microbiota composition, though 
sufficient studies proving clear evidence are still to come [2], [12]. Different treatment strategies involving 
the gut microbiota have been suggested, such as dietary changes, ingestion of probiotics, anti-diabetic 
medication, such as metformin, and bariatric surgery [12].  
Metformin is the most widely used pharmacotherapy for T2D, as it has shown beneficial effects on blood 
glucose, is low in cost and relatively safe to use [12], [13]. The anti-hyperglycaemic effects of metformin 
is mediated by supressing the hepatic glucose output. It is known to have gastrointestinal side effects, 
though its full mechanism of action and effects on the gut microbiota remain not well-defined [2], [12], 
[13]. A recent study conducted by Wu et al. published by Nature Medicine in 2017, provided evidence 
that metformin treatment in newly diagnosed T2D individuals resulted in significant dramatic alterations 
in the composition of their gut microbiota over a two- to four months period, especially Escherichia and 
Intestinibacter abundances changed significantly [13]. These findings support the highly cited cross-
sectional study conducted by Forslund et al. and presented in Nature in 2015, who also found that T2D 
patients not treated with metformin had less bacteria known to produce butyrate, which is in line with 
other studies showing an increase in butyrate-producing bacteria in the faeces of healthy individuals [12]–
[14]. Other studies have provided similar findings, which is to be investigated further [2], [12], [13]. The 
aim of this thesis is to identify possible bacterial signals of either T2D or metformin from a large 
microbiome dataset.

1.3 The analysis of the microbiome 
The human gut microbiome is a dynamic environment, with constant shifts following the rhythm of the 
body [5]. For years scientists have explored the composition of the microbiome with general cultivating 
methods, though it has been difficult to capture and grow the true microbiotic community as many of the 
microbes exist under complex conditions [2]. The number of microbiome studies has therefore grown 
considerably since the advancement of next generation sequencing technologies, which made it possible 
to analyse without culturing and cloning the microbiome [15]. When conducting microbiome studies, 
stool samples are currently the most general and easy way to access and analyse the state of the 
microbiome besides from colonic mucosal biopsies. From the stool samples microbial DNA is extracted, 
which in theory should represent the microbiota well, though there are still issues with reflecting the true 
composition [2], [8]. The purified microbial DNA is sequenced using one of the high-throughput 
sequencing technologies available and hereafter the data is analysed with computer-based tools. As 
sequencing technologies have developed and the quality of the reads has gone up together with reduced 
prices of analyses, it has become possible to investigate the microbiome in more detail. Several different 
ways to sequence microbiome data exist, and some of the most widely known methods are meta-
transcriptomics (RNA-seq), 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) sequencing and whole-genome shotgun (WGS) 
metagenomics [15], [16]. Table 1 lists a brief description of each of these sequencing methods and 
emphasizes their pros and cons.

The two most widely applied methods are the WGS and 16S rRNA sequencing methods, as 16S rRNA 
offers good information on the sample composition and WGS provides a deeper functional insight [2], 
[3]. RNA-seq provides a new and even deeper insight into the state of the microbiotic environment, 
though the sequencing method faces different challenges, such as the fact that mRNA has a short half-
life, and its presence does not always follow the protein activity [16]. This project investigates data 
generated from amplicon sequencing, targeting the variable regions V3-V5 of the 16S rRNA subunit gene, 
as these variable regions are some of the most stable parts of the microbial RNA evolutionary-wise [7]. 
The amplicon sequencing allows grouping of microbial genomic sequences by dividing them by 
taxonomic classification based on reference database sequences and phylogenetic mapping [7]. However, 
sequenced microbes without reference genomes in the reference database are rarely classified deeply, 
though it depends on the choice of reference database and method.  
Going further with 16S rRNA analysis, the sequence reads are often clustered into bins based on a 
minimum of 97 % sequence identity called Operational Taxonomic Unit (OTU) (also sometimes often 
referred to as “phylotypes”, though in this report as “taxa”), which works as “surrogate microbial strains” 
and gathers (bins) all the reads very similar to that of the reference database [6], [7]. However, there is no 
rigorous species concept for bacteria, although the term OTU is widely used in microbiome studies and 
is the most standard way to share and discuss sequence-based observations [6]. A collection of different 
reference databases are available for classifying 16S rRNA sequenced microbiomes, which brings in some 
uncertainty when comparing between studies. The main databases used for binning of 16S reads are: 
SILVA, RDP, Greengenes or NCBI, and there are dissimilarities between them arising because of the 
differences in size and structure of the databases together with the fact that they are periodically updated, 
so different versions can lead to different taxonomic information [6], [17]. The OTUs are classified most 
often following classical Linnaean taxonomy with seven taxonomic levels (from highest to lowest): 
Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus and Species [18]. For an overview of how such a 
taxonomic linkage could look like, please refer to Table 6. The clustered OTUs can provide vital 
information on the taxonomic characterisation of the bacterial community, although they are in general 
well-defined at the top taxonomic levels, they are often less precise at the Species level [15]–[17].  
The objective of this thesis is to explore different statistical methods used for the analysis the human gut 
microbiome, and evaluate the application of them by conducting a study of two sample populations, one 
population suffering from T2D and the other being the control group with normal glucose tolerance. 
Furthermore, the plausible effects of the anti-diabetic medication, metformin on the composition of the 
gut microbiome will be investigated. The statistical tests hypothesise that there are no difference in the 
microbiome composition of the sample populations and they will be further described in the Methods 
section 2.4.

1.4 The importance of study methods 
Each of the sequencing methods used for microbiome analysis have a workflow for handling data, and 
those can overall be divided into three main steps: (1) Pre-processing of reads, e.g. demultiplexing of 
sequence reads and quality filtering if needed, (2) creation of OTU abundance tables or assembling data 
into a dataframe, phyloseq object or similar for easy access of the data, (3) statistical analyses and 
interpretations of the data [7]. The chosen workflow is to be reported with the study so the results can be 
comped with other studies and reconstructed if needed. 
Illustrative methods for analysing the microbiome can provide a clear overview of a dataset, and be used 
for comparing sample groups. It is a general assumption that the diversity of the microbial community 
reflects characteristics of the environment that it was sampled from [19]. For the study of the microbiome, 
two types of taxonomic diversity measures are explored: (1) Alpha Diversity, which represents the 
number (richness) and distribution (evenness) of taxa within a sample, whereas (2) Beta Diversity is the 
comparison of taxa between multiple samples [6]. Alpha diversity can be estimated by different measures 
such as “OTU richness”, which is the amount of different OTUs found, or “observed species count”, 
which is the number of unique OTUs found in each sample. Beta Diversity can be measured 
quantitatively using the sequence abundance or qualitatively when considering only presence-absence of 
sequences, and can be phylogeny based [6]. A principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) plot can be used to 
illustrate the beta diversity among samples, as each of the principal coordinates explains a certain fraction 
of the variability observed in the dataset  [6], [7], [20], [21]. Illustrative methods tend to show few 
complete clear patterns, and therefore statistical null hypothesis methods are explored.  
In statistical studies, a null hypothesis about two sample groups being the same or having the same sample 
microbial composition is set up, and then rejected or accepted (not rejected) based on a level of 
confidence, which is often illustrated by a cut-off for significance with a p-value at or less than 5 % [4]. 
Depending on the statistical methods, the null hypothesis concerns a specific aspect, such as Fischer’s 
Exact Test, where the proportion of one sample population is compared with the proportion of another 
sample population, or for Wilcoxon’s Rank-Sum Test, where observations in two sample populations are 
ranked together and then compared [22], [23]. These statistical tests provide knowledge on single points 
(observations) in the dataset, whereas other statistics can be used for modelling the data and get an 
understanding of the complete effect and differences between sample groups. Generalised linear models 
(GLM) are often used for modelling data, and can be applied with the R tool DESeq2, furthermore there 
is an probabilistic modelling alternative with Bayesian statistics, though it is often not chosen due to its 
complexity [24], [25]. The choice of statistical method often depends on to familiarity and ability to 
compare studies. Another factor could be the access to computational power and know-how for some of 
the more complex modelling methods.

Another method to analyse the microbiome with is through functional analysis. When having an 
overview of the bacterial genome pull in a sample, then all the predicted proteins from a reference genome

2.4 Statistical methods 
For the purpose of this project, various statistical methods have been used in the bioinformatic analyses 
of the dataset in search for microbial signatures of T2D and the effect of metformin. Depending on the 
statistical test at hand, the assumptions on the sample distribution varies. However, a False Discovery 
Rate (FDR) adjusted p-value with a significance level at α = 0.05 (5%) was applied across all tests to filter 
out type I errors (false positives), which is when the null hypothesis is being rejected, although it is 
actually true [52], [53]. Figure 3 illustrates an example of a sample distribution, where tests are accepted 
under the null hypothesis or rejected and therefore has an alternative hypothesis describing the 
distribution [stats1]. Type I errors falls in the area marked with light purple, whereas type II errors (false 
negatives) are marked in dark purple and represents the tests result where the null hypothesis could not 
be rejected even though it should have been [52], [53].  
 
 
 
 
 
The significance level at 0.05 results in a confidence interval covering 95 % of the sample distribution 
[54], [55]. The p-values are ranked by how confident their significance levels are, and three *** signifies 
a p-value below 0.001, two ** a p-value below 0.01, and one * a p-value below 0.05, ranking from very 
significant to a little significant. By setting the FDR adjusted threshold at a p-value below 0.05, means 
that the proportion of false positives expected among the differentially expressed OTUs are 5%, e.g. 
having 500 OTUs differentially expressed with an FDR threshold at 0.05, the expected number of false 
positives are 25 [DESeq2]. The general theories on the statistical methods applied in this project are 
presented in the following sections 2.4.1 to 2.4.6.

2.4.1 Fisher's Exact Test 
When working with smaller sample sizes, it can be advantageous to choose a statistical method, which 
uses exact small-sample distributions rather than large-sample approximations [54]. Fisher’s exact test is 
a statistical significance test for a 2 X 2 contingency table, which is a cross-tabulated matrix showing the 
frequency distribution of the variables at hand [23], [56], [57]. The test follows a hypergeometric 
distribution, which is also known as sampling without replacement. This type of distribution is applicable 
for datasets where both row and column margins are naturally fixed and for sampling binary outcomes 
without replacement from a fixed population [54], [56]. For example would the binary outcomes be the 
observed OTUs in a sample population, where the null hypothesis predicts number of observations to be 
the same between the sample populations. Though, if the proportion of OTUs differ significantly between 
the two groups tested, then the null hypothesis is rejected [54], [55]. Fisher’s exact test is a single point 
test and can be used for differential prevalence analysis, as the proportion of an observed OTU can be 
found significantly different between two sample groups. However, the test does not take confounding 
factors into account, which can affect a result with false positives representing another variable or state. 
If this is the case, other statistical hypothesis tests might be more applicable.   
2.4.2 Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test 
Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test (equivalent to the Mann-Whitney test) is a hypothesis test and the non-
parametric alternative to a normal two-sample t-test, as its sample mean and standard deviation is less 
susceptible towards outliers [22], [54], [55], [58]. Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test applies a ranking method for 
testing if two sample groups are significantly different, without considering if the sample observations are 
normally distributed [58]. This is done by first combining the two sets of data and turning the values into 
ranks, beginning with the smallest observation having rank 1 and the second smallest observation having 
rank 2 etc. The combined dataset with ranks is then separated back into the two sample groups and the 
sum and average rank is computed and rank-sum tested [55], [58]. The null hypothesis claims that there 
is no difference between the rankings of observations in the two sample groups, and that the observations 
in each group are drawn from the same distribution. Evidence against the null hypothesis (the alternative 
hypothesis) will claim that one sample group has a larger proportion of its observations ranked higher 
than the other sample group, leading to a significantly difference in distribution of rank sums [58]. This 
statistical ranking method can be applied for differential abundance analysis testing if the relative 
abundances of an OTU between two sample groups, e.g. sick and healthy, are ranked significantly 
different.

2.4.3 DESeq2 
As mentioned in the R package section 2.3, the DESeq2 package was developed for differential 
expression analysis of RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data, which in the beginning of its time only was used 
for detection of variance in gene expression [25]. Nowadays, the package is used for more than RNA-seq 
data, as the structure of the tool allows for hypothesis testing and differential analysis of any set of count 
data, e.g. from a microbiome or environmental study [4], [43]. DESeq2’s greatest advancement is the 
strength of normalising differences in library size and library composition [59]. The null hypothesis tested 
by DESeq2 for a comparative analysis will state that the logarithmic fold change (LFC) between two 
sample groups for a gene's expression is exactly zero, meaning that in this case the phenotypic 
circumstances separating the two sample groups does not affect the abundance of specific OTUs [25]. 
DESeq2 works with a count matrix, where the read counts of a gene or OTU, are listed in columns across 
the sample that they were observed in which are listed in rows. A DESeqDataSet object can be obtained 
from a phyloseq object as described previously in section 2.3.  
The DESeq analysis in itself runs by default trough three steps: (1) estimation of size factors, (2) 
estimation of dispersion and (3) negative binomial generalised linear model (GLM) fitting following 
Wald statistics with two parameters: a mean and a dispersion [25], [43]. The normalisation of the library 
in step 1 and 2 means that differences in sequencing depth between samples will be taken into account 
which makes counts of a OTUs in different samples directly comparable. In this study the estimation of 
size factors has to deal with a lot of zero counts, therefore the type of size estimation is set to “poscounts” 
which calculates a modified geometric mean. The estimation of dispersion (change in mean–variance 
relationship) is dealt with by default settings to obtain dispersion estimates for the negative binomially 
distributed data [43]. For extracting the desired results from the DESeq2 analysis, it is important to apply 
the contrast argument, which specifies what is to be compared, e.g. two sample groups by c("condition", 
"level to compare", "base level"), and because the contrast is a linear combination of the estimated log2 
fold changes it is used to test if differences between groups are equal to zero and thereby equivalent to 
the null hypothesis [43]. By default the p-values in the DESeq2 analysis achieved by the Wald test are 
corrected for multiple testing using FDR adjusted method mentioned earlier with a significance cut-off 
threshold. The choice of threshold was set at 0.05, meaning there is a 5% risk that the significant OTU is 
a false positive [DESeq2]. The log2 fold change is the effect size estimate reported on a logarithmic scale 
to base 2, meaning that e.g. a log2 fold change of 1.67 for the expression of the OTU “u__357312” (seen 
in Table 9) is increased by a multiplicative factor of 21.67 ≈3.18. This also means that if the NGT score 
is smaller than T2D then will the fold change be positive, which also points in the direction that the taxa 
has been more expressed in [DESeq2], [43].

2.4.4 Bayesian Linear Models 
Besides working with single point hypothesis testing, where each OTU are compared between sample 
groups, another way to analyse a dataset is by modelling it and hereby try to predict a combination of 
observations resulting in an outcome, such as a combination of OTUs as a signature for a disease. A 
standard linear model can be described by the following equation (1):  
𝑌𝑖= 𝛽0 + 𝛽1𝑥1 + ⋯+ 𝛽𝑖𝑥𝑖+  𝜀 , for 𝑖= 1, … , 𝑛 
Where 𝑌 is the outcome (response/dependent variable) of the observation 𝑖. The 𝑥 represent one or more 
predictors (explanatory/independent variables) of the outcome. 𝜀 is a random error, which e.g. could be 
measuring deviations in an experiment, and is often assumed to have a normal distribution. 𝛽0 is the 
intercept and together with the other 𝛽s are these the regression parameters of interest (the predictors) 
[24]. However, if the data is not assumed to be normally distributed, then generalised linear models 
(GLM) can be of help to model the outcome. Working with differential prevalence analysis, the response 
is binary (between 0 and 1), which for the GLMs can be modelled with a logistic regression. Logistic 
regression works in the number space of a sigmoid function, where 0 and 1 corresponding to e.g. a 
“success” or a “failure”, which in relation to this study could be “sick” (1) or “healthy” (0). With a binary 
outcome, the regression of 𝑌 on 𝑥 is called a conditional probability, meaning that the estimated 
regression function is conditioned on the predictor variables the 𝛽s, which tells something about the 
probability that the outcome will be a “success” (1) or “failure” (0) [24]. GLMs works based on a 
probability function and the assumed sample distribution can be specified in the syntax of the model.

the values of 𝑝(𝑥) are restricted to the binary outcome between 0 and 1. The 𝛽s on the other hand are as 
mentioned above the parameters of interest, just as the intercept and slope parameters of a simple linear 
regression model. A maximum likelihood estimation method can be used to determine the values of the 
𝛽s for which makes the observed data most likely to have occurred [24]. If 𝑝 is the probability that a 
randomly selected individual has the disease, then then 1 −𝑝 is the probability the individual is healthy. 
So for equation (2) 𝑘 individuals out of a total 𝑛 people in a sample group, will be sick. The main focus 
of maximum likelihood estimation is to find the value of 𝑌, which maximises the probability of 𝑃(𝑌= 𝑘), 
the likelihood [24]. Iterative algorithms are needed to determine the maximum likelihood estimates of 
the 𝛽s for larger models, and for this a Bayesian statistical approach with Markov Chain Monte Carlo 
(MCMC) method can be applied, which allows the approximation of the posterior distribution as 
calculated by Bayes' Theorem [54]. Thus, an approximation for the mode of the posterior distribution 
approximates the maximum likelihood estimation. The standard linear model assumes that 𝛽 is fixed 
parameter, however, for the Bayesian approach 𝛽 is assumed random and its distribution tells something 
about how the other 𝛽s might be. By using the sample data to estimate parameters of the prior 
distribution, then the prior knowledge can be updated by computing the conditional distribution also 
known as the posterior distribution of 𝛽s given the observed data. From the posterior distribution it is 
possible to report a credible interval of the outcome [55]. It is through this analysis possible the compute 
the prediction of the response variable, when having observed some data (prior knowledge). So given a 
dataset of OTUs observed in a sample group, in theory, it should be possible to predict certain outcomes, 
such as if an individual in the sample group suffers from a disease or not. It is not enough to create a 
predictive model without validating its predictive skills, this can be done in different ways, though for 
this study manual cross-validation was applied. 
2.4.5 Cross-Validation 
Cross-Validation (CV) methods are used in general for two main things: (1) for model fitting and 
selection, when working with more than one model and trying to decide on the best on; (2) for model 
validation, when working with one observation and validating the goodness of the model [60]. In the 
case of this study, CV was used to validate how good the Bayesian generalised linear model was at 
predicting true “successes” based on the dataset. A good and reliable way to get a sense of the 
performance of a predictive model is to make it give a prediction for a sample it has never seen, and in 
this way CV helps reduce variability, overfitting any unwanted bias of testing data on a model created 
from that same data. It is appropriate always to use some kind of CV to test the prediction strength and 
credibility of the fitted model [55].  
==PAGE:32==

For this study manual CV was applied, as the dataset was randomly split into three subsets (or folds) 
before modelling and as shown in Figure 4, which is illustrating the three different models fitted with the 
training datasets and afterwards tested with the left out testing set. 
 
 
This method is called 3-fold cross-validation, and from the testing of the three predictive models different 
performance indicators of the predictive accuracy can be generated. For the purpose of this study, the 
cross-validation was conducted with randomly divided subsets and models created with Bayesian GLMs.  
2.4.6 Random Forest modelling 
Random forest (RF) classifiers are often seen as one of the state-of-the-art methodologies applied in most 
machine learning studies of large metagenomic datasets [61]. RF can be applied for both classification 
for categorical response variables or regression for a continuous response [50]. The method creates a 
forest of trees with each tree depending on sampling of random variables for predictive measures. The 
main purpose of the method is to determine a prediction function for a response variable and it is 
relatively fast to train and can easily be implemented in parallel with another study method [50]. The RF 
method was used to evaluate and compare the predictive power of the conducted Bayesian GLM 
analysis. For the purpse of this study, two different but similar indicators was used to indicate how well 
the Bayesian statistical method did modelling the dataset. The first one was a general calculation of the 
percentage corretly predicted “succeses” across all three models into one overall percentage of 
perfromance. The other indicator was an area under an receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC , 
ROC), which is a more illustrative way to validate the performance, as the ROC tests different cut off 
thresholds for the predictive values and by that an AUC can be calculated to see the performance of the 
predictive model. 
A standard method for gaining insight into obvious differences in the microbial sample composition is 
by measuring the Bray-Curtis distance for beta diversity of the microbial communities as described in the 
introduction. PCoA plots were used to illustrate the beta diversity among the sample groups.

3.2.1 OTU level analyses 
The OTU level analyses for the T2D signal were based on 1822 observed OTU's in 61 samples from 
individuals with T2D (metformin naïve) and 138 samples from the NGT control group and therefore the 
phyloseq object was reduced to a subset including the total of 199 samples. The information column on 
metformin was excluded, being a constant variable in this subset. 
3.2.1.1 Differential prevalence analysis with Fisher’s Exact Test 
The Fisher’s Exact Test was used for testing differences in prevalence of OTUs among the two sample 
groups T2D and NGT. The test was conducted for an OTU at the time, comparing the proportion of 
samples where the OTU was observed in-between the sample groups. No extra library was needed to be 
loaded as the function fisher.test() was a build-in function in the automatically loaded R statistic space. 
The phyloseq object was manipulated into a dataframe with three columns (OTU name, T2D 
observations, and NGT observations) and 1823 rows representing the OTUs and the number of times it 
was observed in each of the sample groups. The last row listed the total number of samples for each 
group, which was used for calculating the proportion of the sample population that the OTUs were 
observed in. The analysis ran across all the OTUs, however, no significant prevalence of any taxa were 
observed with the FDR adjusted p-value at 0.05, meaning that among the two sample groups there were 
no significant differences in the proportion of prevalent OTUs. A cut out of the table showing no 
significant hits is listed in Appendix B. 
3.2.1.2 Differential relative abundance analysis with Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test 
For the Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test, the package coin was loaded to R together with the phyloseq object 
including the 199 samples. Phyloseq build-in functions were used to calculate the relative abundance of 
the OTUs counts and they replaced the read counts in the OTU table. The analysis ran with the coin 
function wilcox_test(), ranking the relative abundance of the observed OTUs in the complete set of 199 
samples and hereafter dividing them into the sample groups. The Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test resulted in 
two OTUs significantly different in relative abundance between the sample groups with the FDR adjusted 
threshold for significance at a p-value of 0.05. The two OTUs are listed in Table 8 and the Phylum, Class 
and Order of both OTUs are respectively Firmicutes, Clostridia and Clostridiales, furthermore were none of 
the OTUs annotated at Species level, this information was left out of the table.

3.4 Bayesian modelling 
When constructing the Bayesian GLMs for logistic regression on the dataset, the rstanarm package was 
loaded into the R programming space. The phyloseq object was manipulated into a simple dataframe and 
the additional sample information, age, BMI and fat percent, were added to it. There were two main 
predictive analyses conducted: one predicting the phenotype of a T2D gut microbiome and the other 
predicting the phenotype of a metformin treated T2D gut microbiome. Depending on which sub-study, 
the columns portraying constant variables were removed together with person ID, as the Bayesian 
predictive method can predict differences based on constant or very little variation in the sample 
information, as constant variables contribute with no constructive information. The Bayesian logistic 
regression models were to predict the phenotypic composition of the microbiome profiles depending on 
the prevalence of the OTUs identified in the dataset.  
The read counts listed in the dataframe were transformed into presence-absence measures and the 
factorised variables, such as gender, disease status or metformin intake, where also transformed into 
binary measures, as the logistic regression model works within the binary space of a sigmoid function 
(between 0 and 1) as described in the Methods section 2.4.4. This meant that for the gender, men were 
noted as 1 and women were 0, for disease status, T2D subjects were 1 and NGT subjects were 0, and for 
metformin intake, yes was 1 and no was 0. OTUs with either observations in almost all samples or 
observations in less than four samples were removed using the dplyr select() function, as they did not 
contribute with any predictive power to the logistic regression model. The numeric variables were kept 
unchanged, because the Bayesian model is able to handle the numeric values. In order to build reliable 
models, manual 3-fold cross validation was used together with random sampling for splitting the dataset 
as shown in Figure 4, which meant that three different predictive logistic regression models had to be 
constructed. For these models a standard prior was set at 7 degrees of freedom and applied as described 
in the Methods section 2.4.4. The predictive logistic regression models were built with stan_glm()  for 
generalised linear models and the family type distribution set at binomial. For both analyses the Monte 
Carlo simulation ran for 4000 iterations in 4 chains, where the first 2000 iterations are warm up to tune 
parameters of the MCMC algorithm and they are therefore thrown away ending with 2000 iteration for 
the predictive values of the predictors (𝛽s). 
3.4.1 The predicted T2D gut microbiome 
To construct the three Bayesian logistic regression models for predicting the microbial phenotype of a 
T2D gut microbiome, 25 OTUs were removed as the models would not run with constant variables or 
variables with very little constructive information. 15 of the OTUs were removed across both Bayesian 
analyses, however, 10 of the OTUs were unique for this analysis and not removed in the metformin 
phenotypic analysis. This was due to the difference in sample group composition, as some OTUs might 
==PAGE:45==
 
36 
 
be unique to the 80 metformin treated individuals not included in this analysis resulting in the OTU being 
few or zero times observed in the sample population and therefore had to be removed. The data was 
divided into three sets, which with 3-folds CV each acted twice as a trainingset and once as a testset as 
illustrated in Figure 4 section 2.4.5. Based on each fold, three logistic regression models were created and 
tested following normal manual 3-fold CV and equation 1 and 2 in the Methods section 2.4.4, using the 
logistic link function to calculate the predictive values. From the logistic regression for each value of the 
predictors, a probability is given for the response variable Y equal to 1 (T2D), and this value is within the 
sigmoid function. Then a threshold for the probability is applied, indicating which one predicts a success. 
If the threshold is at 0.5, then the probability of a predictive value of an OTU that is more than 0.5 will 
be predict in the direction of the T2D phenotype (a “success”), otherwise below 0.5 towards the direction 
of NGT phenotype (a “failure”). With a threshold of 0.5 for predicting the phenotype of T2D in the 
sample group with T2D and NGT subjects, the three models predicted in average 72.2 % correctly. To 
compare the predictive performance of the Bayesian logistic regression modelling, a random forest (RF) 
classifier was used to predict the phenotype based on the same dataset. With a threshold at 0.5, the RF 
model predicted 70.7 % correctly. For further investigation of the predictive performance of the two 
methods, a ROC curve was created based on the predictive values from each of the tests.   
The ROC curve tests a range of thresholds from 0 to 1 or 0 to 100 % being predictive of the T2D 
phenotype, starting with the threshold for the predictive value set a 0.0, the sensitivity set at 0 and the 
specificity at a 100. The sensitivity then goes up and the specificity down, as the threshold is screwed 
towards a 100 % sensitivity and 0 % specificity. Based on the number of correctly predicted response 
variables, at the set threshold, the ROC curve is created and an AUC score calculated. In the AUC plot, 
the number of samples predicted successfully are indicated by the specificity measure and how many of 
them are correctly predicted is measured by the sensitivity. If the AUC is around 50 % and the ROC is a 
diagonal line, the model has no predictive power, as the values of the predictors randomly predict the 
response variable. So when the threshold is at 0, every value of the predictors will point the response 
variable in the direction of the T2D phenotype, and as already shown, only 80 of the 199 cases have that 
phenotype, which would mean that 199 samples would be predictive wrongly. From the R package 
pROC, the function plot.roc() was applied on the predicted values against the true values which created 
the ROC curves and estimated the AUCs. The results are shown in Figure 9.

4 Discussion 
This study was conducted as a master’s thesis project focusing on a subset of data from a larger 
microbiome study with the aim of finding microbial signatures of T2D in the gut microbiome as well as 
looking for possible effects of the anti-diabetic agent metformin on the T2D gut microbiome. 
4.1 Signatures of the T2D gut microbiome  
A number of statistical methods tested different hypothesis about the data, all aiming to identify microbial 
communities of significance between population phenotypes. Three statistical methods were used to 
analyse the microbiome dataset both at OTU and Genus level. One Bayesian statistical method was used 
to model the dataset, aiming to predict the population phenotypes at Genus level, which will be discussed 
in section 4.3. When looking for the signatures of T2D on the gut microbiome, it was not possible to 
reject the null hypothesis test of the Fisher’s Exact Test, as there was no difference in prevalence of OTUs 
among the two sample groups. The test did not find any significant taxa at either OTU or Genus level. 
The Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test was at Genus level not able to reject the null hypothesis and find any 
significantly different abundant taxa between the two sample groups. However, at OTU level it was 
possible to reject the null hypothesis for two taxa (please refer to Table 8), and provide statistical evidence 
for their significant different abundance between the two sample groups with a FDR adjusted threshold 
for significance at a p-value of 0.05. Both taxa u__185883 and u__189899 were classified down to Genus 
levels Lachnoclostridium and Negativibacillus and with a FDR adjusted p-value at ~0.0375 (*), which is not 
the best p-value, but it past the significance threshold discussed in the beginning of section 2.3. By 
inspection of Table B1 in Appendix B, these two taxa were also the two highest ranked taxa by the 
Fisher’s Exact Test at the OTU level, however, the FDR adjusted p-value was not significant. It can thus 
be discussed whether these taxa are truly significantly different between the two sample groups.  
The DESeq2 analyses provided sixteen different taxa at OTU level and one at Genus level that all were 
significantly differently expressed between the two sample groups (please refer to Table 9 and Table 10). 
For the OTU level analysis, six of the taxa were more abundantly expressed in the T2D gut microbiome, 
and they were all annotated at the Genus level Lachnoclostridium. The other ten taxa were more abundant 
in the NGT gut microbiome, and five of them were annotated at the Family level Ruminococcaceae, and 
the other five were divided under the Family levels Veillonellaceae, Barnesiellaceae and one only at the Order 
level Mollicutes_RF39. For the DESeq2 analysis at Genus level, one taxa was identified, which was a taxa 
annotated at Genus level to Lachnoclostridium, and which were more abundant in the T2D sample 
population. Based on the results of the three statistical analyses, the signatures of the T2D gut microbiome 
were all of the Genus Lachnoclostridium taxa, which belongs to the Family Lachnospiraceae that has 
earlier been found significantly increased in the diabetic group of a mouse study [62]. The number of 
genes encoding the taxa have also been observed significantly enriched in individuals with T2D in a 
==PAGE:52==
 
43 
 
genome-wide association study of the human gut microbiome [63]. The taxa with the Order Mollicutes 
RF39 have earlier been reported more abundant in the metformin treated T2D gut microbiome compared 
to NGT individuals, which is not consistent with the findings of this report [64]. However, the same study 
also provided support of the findings of higher abundant taxa of the family Ruminococcaceae in the NGT 
gut microbiome, which supports the findings of this study. Other taxa that were more expressed in the 
NGT sample group were of the Genus Butyricicoccus, which is known to produce butyrate [65], together 
with taxa of the Genus Dialister (including Species succinatiphilus), which belongs to the Class Negativicutes 
and have been observed contributing to the succinate pathway by formation of propionate [66], and the 
Genus Barnesiella, which have been shown to prevent the spread of highly antibiotic-resistant bacteria 
[67]. Combining the findings of the many different bacteria related to the NGT gut microbiome with the 
knowledge from other studies, provides statistical evidence for signatures of a healthy gut microbiome, 
as hoped for. The signature of T2D on the gut microbiome is still vague, though this project have provided 
one clear Genus Lachnoclostridium with multiple OTUs observed just in the T2D gut microbiome samples.  
4.2 Effects of metformin on the T2D gut microbiome 
A part of the T2D individuals had been treated with metformin, which has been suggested to have an 
effect on the composition of the T2D gut microbiome as presented in the Introduction section 1.2 [2], 
[12], [13]. Therefore, the second part of the study was aiming to find any signs on the effects of metformin 
on the T2D gut microbiome. The first statistical test, the Fisher’s Exact Test, was used to look for 
differences in prevalence of OTUs between the two sample populations. The tests did not find any 
significant taxa at OTU level, though at Genus level there was one hit; the OTU u__555945, which was 
annotated at the Genus level Romboutsia ( please refer to Table 13). The Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test looked 
for taxa that were differently abundant and ranked among the two sample groups, and found the same 
two taxa at both OTU and Genus level to be significant different with the FDR adjusted p-value (please 
refer to Table 11 and Table 14). The two taxa detected were u__1111294 with Genus Escherichia/Shigella 
and u__555945 with Genus Romboutsia (as the Fisher’s Exact Test also found). Both taxa were annotated 
at Species level in the OTU level analysis, though for both, more than one Species (Multi-Affiliation) was 
suggested by the SILVA reference database (please refer to Table 11). The largest difference between the 
two Wilcoxon analyses were the degree of significance that the taxa were scored with. The Genus level 
analysis pointed at the two being quite significant both with a FDR adjusted p-value around 0.004 (**). 
Romboutsia ilealis/timonensis have before been identified in the human gut microbiota [68], [69], and 
contributes with production of H2, CO2, acetate, formate and lactate to the gut [70], [71].  
The DESeq2 analysis looking for OTUs differently expressed between the two sample groups, identified 
three significant taxa with the OTU level analysis and two with the Genus level analysis. The OTU level

and immune system, Alloprevotella has the capabilities to ferment carbohydrates and produce SCFAs 
(acetate and butyrate) [5], [72]. As metformin has an impact on the disease state of the body suffering 
from T2D, it seems plausible that bacteria observed in the healthy gut microbiome would reappear, 
assisting in the general processes of the GIT. 
An additional set of analyses of the complete dataset were conducted in order to emphasize the 
importance of stratification of the T2D individuals based on their metformin treatment, as metformin 
acts as a confounding factors. The three statistical tests, Fisher’s Exact Test, Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test 
and DESeq2, at OTU level each identified 13, 32 and 20 significantly different taxa, respectively. Fisher’s

different between the metformin naïve T2D and NGT.  
Therefore these OTUs cannot with certainty be identified as signatures of either metformin naïve or 
metformin treated T2D gut microbiome compared with the NGT controls. For the Wilcoxon Rank-Sum

factor, influencing the true signature of the T2D gut microbiome. Furthermore, people with late stage 
T2D are often treated for other conditions as well, and when conducting microbiome studies looking for 
differences between the healthy and sick microbiota, it is important to take other factors into account. 
For the study of the true signatures of T2D individuals, a way to go about it would be to include pre-
diabetics in the study, to see how the microbiota evolves from the healthy state to the sick state. Pre-
diabetics were not included in this sub-study, but are interestingly part of the larger study that this dataset 
belongs to.  
4.3 Bayesian predictive modelling 
As part of the statistical analyses, Bayesian logistic regression was used to model and predict the 
composition of the metformin naïve T2D gut microbiome and the microbial signatures that defines it. 
Furthermore, the same was done trying to model the signatures of the metformin treated microbiome 
compared to the metformin naïve T2D microbiome. The two Bayesian models were setup to seek for 
differences in prevalent OTUs among the sample groups, and was done with manually 3-fold cross-
validation (CV). Most literature on CV for Bayesian modelling suggest 10-fold as a good optimum 
[kaggle][50]. This would have been implemented if more samples had been included, as it meant that a 
lot of OTUs were being observed zero times in some of the folds and thereby prohibited the analyses form 
running, as there were not enough predictive information. The three Bayesian models created were 
compressed and their predictive performance evaluated with a fixed threshold at 0.5, where the T2D 
models predicted the samples’ disease status 72.2 % correctly, so almost every three out of four sample 
was “diagnosed” correctly. For the prediction of the metformin treated T2D samples, this number was 
only 63.6 %, not as precise as for the T2D models. One of the main differences between the two Bayesian

the T2D models were based on

age, BMI and fat percentage, as they all add some predictive value to the pile of information. In order to 
validate the performance of the Bayesian models created, a random forest classifier was used to predict 
the disease and metformin treatment status, respectively. The RF was treated in the same way as the

cases the RF models performed worse than the Bayesian. In order to validate these performances further,

modelling the system. The Bayesian models did quite well predicting the disease status of three out of

To investigate the most informative microbial predictors of the Bayesian models, two extra models

toxins [74]. Of the 12 informative predictors at Genus level, five of them were in the direction of the

refer to Table 19). The two most informative taxa listed in Table 19 were the Genus Howardella and

meaning that they with most certainty do not pull the microbial composition in the direction of the T2D 
gut microbiome. The taxa with Genus Intestinibacter has before been observed depleted in the T2D gut

metformin is was expected that Intestinibacter was depleted in the metformin treated gut microbiome, 
whereas the Genus Escherichia/Shigella was observed with a higher abundancy. Looking further into to 
the search for predictors of the metformin treated T2D gut microbiome, only six taxa were identified,

though it resulted in some interesting findings, it is understandable that this type of statistics is often 
overlooked or deselected for the analysis of the composition of the gut microbiome. When that is said, 
the opportunity to model and predict the factors determining which type of microbiome that is being 
investigated is amazing and for larger datasets, or even better, multiple datasets the Bayesian method 
could contribute to new ways of looking at the gut microbiome composition and which microbes are 
signs of a sick or healthy gut. 
4.4 Conducting a microbiome study 
As a sub-study of a larger microbiome study, this project focused on analysing the microbiome data with 
different statistical tools in order to identify signatures of the T2D gut microbiome as well as exploring 
the effects of metformin. Although being a sub-study, it included 279 samples from different individuals, 
which is considered a good sized cohort. The amount of data supports the statistical confidence, as larger 
cohorts are more likely to convey a picture of the true microbial community. A microbiome study consists 
of several steps, from collection of samples, choice of sequencing technology, selection of methods to 
processing the data and then using a number of bioinformatic tools for analysing the data, to having the 
final results. Multiple approaches are available at each step, depending on the aim of the study, costs and 
machines/tools present. Working with 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing it primarily provides information 
about microbial identity and not function. In order to investigate the microbiome’s functions, many 
researchers use a shotgun metagenomic approach in which the whole bacterial genome is sequenced [5]. 
It is however possible to compare the genomic content of the OTUs found by the 16s rRNA sequencing 
by using their representative sequences and searching against a database composed of 16S rRNA 
sequences and locating the best matches for the inferred genome for the OTUs. Based of those and the 
genomic content in the sample, their total metagenomics content is generated and can be expressed in 
terms of orthologs and pathway counts using e.g. the KEGG genome database [26], [75]. However, the 
time was limited in this study, as for most master’s thesis projects, and working with the functional 
content would be appropriate for further analyses. 
==PAGE:57==
 
48 
 
This sub-study did not take part in any of the previous step of the work-flow, as the data was received as 
a phyloseq object, already quality processed etc. In order to get a sense of the data, PCoA plots, based on 
the Bray-Curtis distance measure for beta diversity, were used for illustration. The PCoA axes 
summarised the variability in the dataset, but did not capture much of the variation in the data, as they 
all explained less than 10 %. The PCoA plots did therefore not portray any clear groupings of microbial 
communities, which suggested the need for more in-depth statistical analyses. 
The three main statistical tests analysed the microbiome data at two levels: OTU and Genus level. When 
looking at the results from the different statistical analyses, there are quite large differences in the number 
of taxa or which taxa were found differently prevalent or abundant between the sample groups. For the 
T2D signatures, the Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test null hypothesis could be rejected for two taxa at OTU 
level, however not a Genus level. The biggest differences are based on the fact that the Genus level taxa 
might be a grouping of different Species, which in reality are quite different (i.e. not that related), but due 
to the way of annotation and the choice of reference database, they were compiled within the same Genus 
level. At OTU level it is possible for the taxa to be separated into different not yet classified Species levels 
with a 97 % identity. The reliability of using the Genus level can be discussed as taxa with the same 
Genus level can be very different at Species level. On the other hand, only around 11 % of the OTUs 
were annotated at Species level or at a Species level with multiple taxonomic names included, which also 
leaves the analysis with uncertainty and blank answers [6]. Even for the OTUs, the species can influence 
the gut microbiota differently, such as different strains of Clostridium difficile, where some produces one 
or two types of toxins and others not at all [74]. 
The differential analysis of the OTUs was conducted at two levels: 1) with a prevalence measure reflected 
by the presence-absence observations, where the read count does not matter, only that the taxa has been 
observed or not in the sample; 2) with a count relationship measure reflected by the abundance of taxa in 
a sample, which is often measured in relative abundance, where all the read counts in a sample are 
summed into 1. The relative abundance measure takes the whole sample into account, whereas the 
prevalence measure makes it impossible to known in how large quantities a taxa was observed, which 
leaves a lot of uncertainty when comparing sample groups. This means that a taxa could have been 
observed in high numbers (relative abundance) in one population, but in few numbers in the other, and 
by the prevalence measure, this will not be evident in a statistical test as Fisher’s Exact Test. However, 
working with relative abundance can also be tricky, not knowing if the whole microbial community was 
sampled or if only part of the sample was more condense with certain taxa, and this reflects the 
environment that was captured by the microbiome analysis. Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test and DESeq2 both 
work well with read counts, though in different ways, as DESeq2 applies a method for adjustment of 
library size and Wilcoxon does not. 
==PAGE:58==
 
49 
 
The choice of R packages for the statistical analyses has been complicated, as R is an open source 
programming language and for which many interesting and useful packages and tools have been 
developed. The most valuable packages used in this project were: phyloseq, vegan, plyr/dplyr, coin, 
DESeq2 and rstanarm, without those it would have been a difficult project to accomplish, having to code 
the complex solutions that was provided by these packages. Other packages were tried, and only a handful 
has been chosen for presentation in this report. However, the choice of statistics and analysis methods 
was hard, as the three statistical tests set up the null hypothesis in different ways, and thereby provided 
different significance outputs. It is worth thinking about what you aim to get out of an analysis, and if 
the tool chosen can provide that. 
The Fisher’s Exact Test was used for differential prevalence testing, and in this study, the test had trouble 
locating the difference in the gut microbiome composition between the sample populations, and did only 
once identify an OTU to be proportioned significantly different in prevalence between the two sample 
groups. For future microbiome studies, this tool would probably not be the first go-to tool, as it is not 
able to catch many of the differences in the microbiome dataset. The Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test is widely 
used in microbiome studies to compare continuous variables between two groups, and for this study it 
was used for differential relative abundance testing between the two sample groups [4]. The test was quite 
useful and brought some insight into the differences in the read counts observed of an OTU between the 
two sample groups. In three out of four analyse, it did locate significantly different OTUs, and supported 
with information about the T2D gut microbiome. The test can be implemented in R in different ways, 
both manually and by the package coin, however, using coin made it very easy to setup. The DESeq2 
package in R employs the null hypothesis test that there is no difference in expression of the abundance 
of OTUs between the two sample groups. The build-in test provided unique library adjustments, by 
estimation of size factors and dispersion, which for the analysis of the gut microbiome brought some 
interesting results. As presented earlier, the DESeq2 was originally made for detection of differential 
expression of genes, however, it was easy to apply the tool for differential OTU abundance analysis. The 
amount of information that can be withdrawn from the result object is very useful and especially the log2 
fold change, presenting which sample condition had the highest abundance of the OTU and thereby 
directing the OTU as a signature of that sample group. The rest of the results were not presented in this 
project, as this project focused on finding signatures of the gut microbiome and not deep analyses of the 
tools used, but more a general understanding of how to apply them for microbiome analysis.  
The Bayesian prediction modelling was the most interesting and challenging part of this project, as 
Bayesian statistics opens up for new ways to look at and use data. The Bayesian methods are old, 
however, it has not been until the advancement of computers that the methods could be implemented 
and used more widely. The model used in this project was at one level, as the prior was not readjusted 
==PAGE:59==
 
50 
 
based on the posterior outputs, and interactions between the variables were not implemented in the model 
as predictive factors, which could have been done and is known as hierarchical modelling. Scientists tend 
to avoid too many complications when they are at the final stages of a research project, and starting with 
exploring the possibilities with Bayesian statistics is like opening Pandora’s Box: it brings a ton of 
uncertainty, as it is complicated to both apply and understand. The “old” and well tested methods still 
provides useful information and the Bayesian methods are computationally demanding. The Random 
Forest classifier on the other hand, is well used among scientists, though the way it works is by creating 
a forest of trees looking at possible ways for the data to be arranged and connected randomly in every 
circle is difficult to understand. Even in this analysis, the performance of the three predictions varied a 
few percentages up or down for each run.  
The use of AUC as a measure of performance of predictive distribution models for presence-absence 
bacterial data have been criticised as there is a higher level of uncertainty attached to absence than 
presence. This is due to the fact that the degree of species detectability can reflect false absence of taxa 
that might actually be present [76]. Taxa might be present in very low numbers, or just not captured when 
the sample was taken, which can lead to data analyses portraying ideas about the microbial community, 
but not capturing the true essence of it. Collection of samples, protocols for DNA extraction and choice 
of sequencing can affect the outcome, and especially the choice of bioinformatic tools for analysis, which 
all have different ways of handling the observed counts and tackle differences in the library size between 
samples (DESeq2), or just work with presence-absence as the measure for differences. As the microbiome 
is being studied more efficiently and both the sequencing methods together with computational power 
and development of tools for microbiome analysis are advancing, scientists are becoming more aware of 
how to deal with biased data and how to balance the use of the different methods.

5 Conclusions 
The gut microbiota is a complex system with a lot of interactive factors playing different roles in keeping 
the microbiota in balance. The microbiota may have causative effects on the human body, as it assist in 
harvesting energy from SCFA, and interacts with the human metabolic processes and the immune system 
[4]. The bacterial composition of the gut microbiota is like a fingerprint and can be highly divergent even 
between family members. During the lifespan of an individual, the gut microbiota varies by habitats and 
can be dramatically altered, either briefly or long term by diseases, such as infections or medical 
interventions, such as antibiotics. T2D is a worldwide pandemic, influencing the lives of millions of 
people with numbers only growing. It is therefore one of today’s most studies diseases, as no cure exists. 
The disease state of T2D is often a result of a metabolic syndrome leading to excessive obesity and it 
influences the body heavily being a risk factor for blindness or damage to heart and kidneys. The human 
gut microbiome and T2D might influence the state of one another, and understanding their interplay can 
assist in the treatment of T2D patients worldwide.  
The aim of this master’s thesis project was to identify microbial signatures of the T2D gut microbiome 
with different statistical methods as well as investigating the anti-diabetic medication metformin’s 
plausible effects on the T2D gut microbiota. Three main statistical tests both for prevalence and 
abundance were applied together with one method for predictive modelling, in order to test their 
applicability for differential analysis at both OTU and Genus level. The study provided insight into the 
different tools for microbiome analysis as well as worked with a concrete aim of searching for microbial 
signatures of T2D by setting up statistical null hypothesis for differences between sample populations. 
The strongest signature of the T2D gut microbiome that was found in this study, was the colonisation of 
taxa by the Genus Lachnoclostridium, which were found significantly different in abundance between 
sample groups by both Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test and DESeq2. In addition to this, multiple taxa, such 
as earlier reported Family Ruminococcaceae and Genus Dialister, with beneficial abilities for the gut were 
found at increased levels in the NGT sample group. The Bayesian logistic regression model was used to 
predict the microbial phenotype for the T2D gut microbiome, though many different taxa were suggested, 
only a few were in favour of the T2D gut microbiome, and with the 90% confidence interval close to 
crossing the threshold at zero. On the contrary, there were two taxa with Genus Howardella and 
Intestinibacter both being strong predictors of the NGT gut microbiome with predictor value observed far 
from the zero threshold.  
The confounding effects of metformin treatment of the T2D gut microbiome were investigated, both by 
conducting statistical differential analysis of the metformin treated versus the metformin naïve T2D 
microbiome, and by statistical differential analysis of the complete set of T2D subjects including the 
metformin treated versus the NGT subjects. The analyses of the complete T2D versus the NGT 
==PAGE:61==
 
52 
 
individuals identified taxa as being significantly different between these sample groups, though the 
differential analyses of the metformin treated versus the metformin naïve T2D individuals turned made 
those results untrustworthy, as the identified taxa turned out to be signals of metformin treatment. The 
main example of this incidence was the taxa with Genus Escherichia/Shigella being identified as most 
significant differently abundant between the complete T2D and the NGT sample groups, but had earlier 
in the project been found differently abundant between the metformin treated and metformin naïve T2D 
individuals. As for this dataset, it was proven that metformin acts as a confounding factor by effecting 
the results with false positives. Metformin treated and untreated individuals should for future studies of 
the T2D gut microbiome be kept separate. The Bayesian predictors of the metformin treated T2D gut 
microbiome did not observe the taxa with Genus Escherichia/Shigella as the other statistical tests. Instead 
it supported other findings, such as the taxa with Genus Romboutsia being a signature of the metformin 
naïve T2D gut microbiome in comparison with the metformin treated ones, as this taxa was also found 
significantly different between sample groups by Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test and Fisher’s Exact Test. 
Few but clear microbial signatures of the T2D gut microbiome were detected in this project together with 
evidence of metformin as an active confounding factor when investigating T2D samples. The statistical 
methods did rarely find the same taxa as significantly different between sample groups, though when they 
did, they had consistent results. In conclusion, the group of taxa having the same Genus level 
Lachnoclostridium were the strongest signal for a microbial signature of the T2D gut microbiome. 
Furthermore, the multiple taxa of the Family Ruminococcaceae and Genus Dialister, were found more often 
and more abundant in the normal glucose tolerant (NGT) individuals in comparison with the metformin 
naïve T2D individuals. The study found the taxa with Genus Escherichia/Shigella were more abundant in 
the metformin treated T2D samples, which supports findings of analogous microbiome studies. 
As next generation sequencing methods are optimized and reduced in price, together with the growing 
computational power, the microbiome communities will be sequenced even deeper and patterns, 
variations and signatures of certain states will become more evident. The field of microbiomics is still 
new and standardisations and large revolutionary studies will be a prerequisite for landmark 
achievements within this field of science. Research in the microbiome field will provide further 
understanding not only of the association, but also on the causative influences of bacteria in human health 
and diseases.

5.1 Future perspectives 
This section is dedicated to the future perspectives of this project as well as within the field of studying 
the microbiome. To work further ahead with this dataset functional analysis could bring some more 
insight into which processes that the microbes engage in, as described both in the introduction and 
discussion sections. Development of the statistical methods and setting them up in a user-friendly way 
for researchers, who are not bioinformaticians. This is being done at the moment, with many new R tools 
are being developed for microbiome analyses; hopefully they will be of great use for the field. There has 
been a boom in annotation of species, though very little of the assumed world’s microbe-population has 
been annotated by now. Furthermore, development and expansion of reference databases and platforms 
for sharing microbiome data will make it easier to classify sequence data at share useful information. 
Comparing the metformin treated T2D group with the NGT group might show some significantly 
different taxa that would be more in relation to reality, as most T2D patients are prescribed metformin 
shortly after diagnosis, and this microbiome is a large part of the T2D population. Furthermore, by 
including pre-diabetic individuals would assist with the understanding on how the microbiome changes 
in early stages of the disease. Collection of more samples and conducting large cohorts, would support 
the statistical uncertainties. Furthermore, to investigate the relationship between the microbiota and its 
host is suggested to make causal inference models and conduct longitudinal studies, especially for 
improving the understanding of the changeability of the gut microbiome. Standardisation of the methods 
used in microbiome studies, especially for data analysis is needed, as researches tend to choose the 
methods and thresholds showing what they wish to see, and in the end, the results will be biased by the 
influence of the researcher. People eat and live differently, the composition of the gut can change 
dramatically due to medicine, food and exposure to other people. For treatment of disease, e.g. obesity 
which can lead to T2D, dietary interventions (personalised nutrition) based on gut microbiome 
composition could be tried out instead of treating with medicine.
