Summary (English)    
The purpose of this report is to analyse the current implementation and use of BIM as an 
integrative project management tool associated with managing and integrating processes on 
complex construction projects involving multiple internal and external stakeholders. The 
research also aims at identifying improvements and developing suggested "best practises" for 
the implementation of BIM as an integrative project management platform for large 
engineering organizations such as COWI.  
The thesis focuses on the structural and organizational aspects of using BIM to integrate project 
management and uses Leavitt’s Diamond Model to structure both the theoretical and practical 
sections. The theoretical perspective is derived from literature research whereas the practical 
perspective is obtained through qualitative interviews and meetings. The theoretical sections 
defines the mechanisms of project integration management, including the necessary structural 
and BIM related components whereas the practical section seeks to illustrate the extent to 
which COWI is using BIM to support project integration management as well as the practices, 
barriers and challenges specific to COWI. By doing a comparative analysis of the theoretical and 
practical perspectives, a list of specific suggested improvements to achieve better BIM support 
for integrative project management at COWI is listed. Finally an approach for implementing 
these improvements is suggested with a focus on the organizational and structural 
improvements specific to projects, which is the focus of this report.  
The collected data indicates that COWI already has an efficient strategy in place to realise the 
benefits of BIM as a project management platform, but the primary challenge for COWI is the 
execution of this digital strategy. This is a challenge COWI shares with most Nordic companies 
across industries according to recent study performed by Accenture (Accenture, 2016). The 
less digitally adapt project managers at COWI are struggling to adapt to the use of BIM and the 
successful execution of the strategy depends on these project managers becoming more 
comfortable and effective in their use of BIM as a project management tool. They need to lift 
their current digital capabilities and associated expertise in the use of BIM. The best-practice 
section illustrate ways to achieve this, either by motivating the project managers or by 
simplifying the current BIM related project management processes.  
Raising the level of BIM skills and understanding and making sure that all project managers are 
comfortable with the use of the BIM related processes and practices, will not only eliminate 
further fragmentation issues but will also strengthen the communication and information 
sharing between discipline across construction projects.  
The business case for COWI and other large construction companies for investing to realize 
these benefits is significant and would be an obvious area for further study and research.

Summary (Danish)    
Formålet med dette speciale er at analysere den nuværende implementering og brug af BIM 
som et integrerende projektledelsesværktøj til at styre og integrere BIM relaterede processer 
og mekanismer på komplekse byggeprojekter. Opgaven har også til formål at identificere 
forbedringer og foreslå "best-practices" for implementeringen af BIM som en integrerende 
projektledelsesplatform i store ingeniørvirksomheder som COWI. 
Afhandlingen bruger Leavitts Diamond Model til at strukturere både de teoretiske og praktiske 
afsnit. Det teoretiske perspektiv er opnået ved litteraturforskning, hvorimod det praktiske 
perspektiv er opnået gennem kvalitative interviews og møder. De teoretiske afsnit definerer 
integrerende projektledelse inklusiv de nødvendige strukturelle og BIM relaterede 
komponenter, mens afsnittene med de empiriske data tilstræber at illustrere omfanget af 
COWIs anvendelse af BIM til at understøtte integrerende projektledelse samt de 
projektledelsespraksisser, barrierer og udfordringer som er specifikke for COWI. Ved at udføre 
en komparativ analyse af det teoretiske og praktiske perspektiv, opstilles en liste af specifikke 
forbedringsforslag til at opnå bedre BIM understøttelse af integrerende projektledelse hos 
COWI. Afslutningsvis foreslås en fremgangsmåde for implementering af disse forbedringer med 
fokus på de organisatoriske og strukturelle forbedringer, som er hovedfokus for denne 
afhandling.  
De indsamlede data viser, at COWI allerede har en effektiv strategi på plads, der sigter op at 
realisere fordelene ved at udnytte BIM som en projektledelsesplatform, men den primære 
udfordring for COWI ligger i selve eksekveringen af strategien. Dette er en udfordring COWI 
deler med de fleste Nordiske virksomheder på tværs ad brancher i følge et nyligt studie udført 
af Accenture (Accenture, 2016). De projektledere, der ikke er digitalt kurante, kæmper med at 
tilpasse sig brugen af BIM. Den succesfulde eksekvering af COWI’s digitale strategi afhænger 
således af, at projektlederne bliver mere komfortable og effektive i deres anvendelse af BIM. De 
skal hæve deres nuværende digitale kompetencer og tilhørende ekspertise i brugen af BIM. 
Best-practice afsnittet illustrerer, at dette kan gøres ved enten at motivere projektlederne eller 
at forenkle de nuværende BIM relateret projektledelsesprocesser og -værktøjer.  
Ved at hæve BIM kompetenceniveauet og sikre at alle projektledere er komfortable med brugen 
af de BIM relaterede projektledelsesprocesser og -arbejdsgange, kan risikoen for yderligere 
fragmentering reduceres og kommunikation og informationsdeling mellem discipliner på 
tværs af store byggeprojekter vil kunne forbedres. 
Værdien for COWI og andre store ingeniørvirksomheder ved at investere i at realisere disse 
fordele er høj og repræsenterer et indlysende område for videre studie og analyse.

Preface    
This thesis was prepared at the department of Management Engineering at the Technical 
University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements for acquiring a M.Sc. in Civil 
Engineering. The project was started in January 2016 and finished in June 2016.   
The thesis deals with the subject of deploying new BIM tools in construction and engineering 
organizations and utilizing BIM to support an integrative project management approach. The 
thesis does not intend to cover the technology, infrastructure, software and data aspects of BIM 
deployment, but rather focuses on the organizational and structural aspects of integrative 
project management using BIM. The thesis focusses specifically on the project specific aspects 
of BIM deployments as opposed to companywide or business unit aspects.  
The topic of this Master’s thesis was formulated based on my interest in looking beyond the 
technical and solution specific questions relative to BIM deployments and understanding the 
structural and organizational impacts and barriers for effective BIM deployment and use in 
complex construction projects.  
I have found it challenging and interesting to use my background in Civil Engineering and apply 
my problem-solving mind-set to what is essentially a change and organizationally orientated 
topic. Although this is a topic with little prior research, I have aimed to conclude the theory and 
the empirical results of this thesis in the form of practical and implementable recommendations 
as outlined throughout the thesis.

1 Introduction

1.1 
Background 
Construction projects are constantly challenged to deliver successful results on tight schedules 
and deadlines with limited resources. Because of these pressures and driven by the increasing 
competitiveness of the global construction industry, the effective coordination and sharing of 
information between project members on large construction projects, is becoming increasingly 
critical and a competitive differentiator in its own right. Construction projects are also 
increasingly affected by fragmentation (Alashwal, A. and Fong, P., 2015). This fragmentation is 
caused by the more specialized nature of each construction component and the large number 
of highly specialized participants in modern construction projects. The requirement for highly 
specialized actors is driven by the increasing scale, complexity and optimization objectives of 
modern construction projects. This in turn drives an even bigger need for an effective and error 
free communication and integration between the many different actors in all phases of the 
projects including planning, execution, management and operations. In order to respond to the 
challenges associated with the effective communication and integrated management across 
many specialized stakeholders both inside and outside the company, leading construction 
companies must adapt and change the core of their business organization, processes and 
technology to enable and support this new way of delivering and managing large construction 
projects. This is a fundamental change facing the industry and the leading construction 
companies must embrace this change quickly to maintain their competitive advantage moving 
forward.    
As discussed in a McKinsey & Company article (2016a) all industries are undergoing some level 
of digital disruption. This disruption and the increasing degree of digitalization is very 
noticeable in the construction industry. The way complex construction projects are being 
planned, designed, built and managed is undergoing a significant transformation triggered and 
enabled by new digital technologies and platforms. The emergence of sophisticated Building 
Information Modelling (BIM) tools is one of the important new digital triggers and enablers of 
this transformation. These are intelligent model-based tools that makes it possible to build a 
detailed digital representation of the physical and functional characteristics of a construction 
project early in the planning and design phase of the project. This enables radical 
improvements not only in the way projects are planned, modelled and designed, but also in the 
way construction is managed and executed. The use of BIM-tools can not only increase the 
productivity of the main contractor, it can also improve the collaboration, productivity and 
insight of all the participating stakeholders during all phases of construction cf. figure 1 
(Autodesk homepage, 2015):

BIM tools are also used to develop and share information between multiple stakeholders 
throughout the lifecycle of a construction project. The concept of BIM has existed since the 
1970s (Eastman, C., Tiecholz, P., Sacks, R., Liston, K., 2008), but the tools have now matured and 
become more sophisticated and accessible and broader in scope. The BIM tools are now also 
available on cloud platforms providing easy access to shared project data across the many 
project stakeholders and reducing the up-front infrastructure investment to license and deploy 
the tools.  
The BIM tools are therefore increasingly being considered as a fundamental and indispensable 
project management tool. The significant benefits of using BIM have changed the way 
construction projects are managed and BIM is considered a significant milestone in 
construction project management, due to the wide variety of disciplines that benefits from its 
use (Bips, Digital constructions, 2006).  This is also evidenced by the fact that most BIM 
solutions are shifting their development focus from the modelling functions to increasingly 
focus on the stakeholder integration and project management areas. 
To derive the benefits from using BIM tools across the project lifecycle however, companies are 
required to implement proper management rigor in all construction phases and ensure that 
they understand the internal organizational changes that are required to deploy BIM effectively 
across all internal and external stakeholders. If correctly managed and implemented, BIM 
greatly reduces the risks associated with project fragmentation, because BIM can greatly 
improve the planning, communication, integration and transparency across project 
stakeholders (Viig, O. J., 2014).   
Despite these benefits, the adoption of BIM as a project management tool is only in its early 
stages at the bottom of the maturity curve. This way of using BIM has not yet been fully 
exploited at scale by the leading Danish construction companies. There are still many 
challenges and barriers associated with its implementation and use. In order to address these 
challenges and achieve the full benefits from using BIM, it is critical to change the way projects

are managed and specifically how the integration of all the project components is achieved 
effectively.  
The modern BIM tools represents an exciting new world of opportunities to leverage new 
digital technologies and platforms. While the effective deployment of these technologies and 
the functions and features of BIM would represent an interesting topic for research in itself, the 
purpose of this report is to study the organizational impacts of using BIM as a project 
integration platform, especially as it relates to project organizations. The report will attempt to 
double click on the organizational aspects of using BIM as a tool to integrate project 
management across the internal and external stakeholders of large construction projects. In 
this context, the report will also look at potential current and future areas where BIM could 
drive further benefits for Danish construction companies as the BIM tools become more 
sophisticated and accessible.  The research of this report will be based on the processes and 
project organisation structure of the Danish construction company; COWI and the impacts and 
possibilities of using BIM on a specific ongoing construction project and associated project 
management practises. A further objective of the research in this report is to serve as an input 
for determining the future strategy and approach for implementing BIM more extensively in 
COWI and in large Danish engineering companies in general. 
 
1.2 
Problem statement 
The first step in developing the problem statement is to decide the type of research that will be 
conducted. The research question, the associated sub-questions and the hypothesis is then 
formulated based on the selected type of research problem. Subsequently the method is chosen 
and the expected results are defined.  
The research is focused on the organizational impacts of deploying and using BIM effectively 
and is as such change-orientated. At the same time, the research aims at evaluating and 
developing suggestions for future improvements to the way BIM is used to integrate and 
manage construction projects.  
Through a preliminary understanding of digital disruption in the construction industry caused 
by the modern BIM technologies as well as the organizational and process related challenges 
associated with implementing and using these technologies as an integrated project 
management platform, the following hypothesis was derived:  
“The current use of BIM in engineering companies today is primarily focused within each of the 
vertical functions in engineering projects (e.g. architecture, services, structural engineering, 
facility management, ...) and the use is still mostly centred in the early project stages using 
traditional BIM functionality within modelling and visualization. This causes further 
fragmentation between disciplines. To improve the impact and benefit of using BIM, it should 
instead be considered to implement BIM horizontally, enabled by a central project management

function in the organizational structure specialized in the use of BIM to integrate across 
disciplines. This way the real benefit of using BIM can be realized by strengthening the 
communication between project disciplines and providing an improved understanding and 
transparency of the key project data across these disciplines. This will in return greatly 
strengthen the competitive edge of large engineering companies such as COWI.” 
Thus the purpose of this report is to analyse the current implementation and use of BIM as a 
tool to integrate project management processes across the disciplines and stakeholders in large 
construction projects. The research also aims at identifying improvements and developing 
suggested "best practises" for the implementation of BIM as an integrating project management 
platform in large engineering organizations such as COWI.  
This is achieved by assessing the extent to which BIM is integrated into the business processes 
and project organizations of COWI and analysing the organizational and project related 
challenges. The expected results should indicate that COWI has not been able to fully deploy 
BIM as an integrative project management tool across the business and that it would represent 
significant value to COWI if they could close this gap. Furthermore, the analysis is expected to 
find, that structural changes is required to enable a more effective use of BIM as an integrative 
project management platform.  
 
1.3 
Methods 
To address the objective of the report, it is necessary to develop a general understanding of 
three key topics: 
 An understanding of the need for (and benefits associated with) the integration across 
stakeholders in construction projects 
 A general understanding of Building Information Modelling 
 An understanding of what structural changes within an organisation are required to 
achieve specific business outcomes (e.g. better integration) 
The theory behind these topics will initially be researched individually and then combined into 
a theoretical perspective. The data is collected using a qualitative research method [Borrego, 
M., Douglas, E. P.; Amelink, C. T., 2008]. The theoretical perspective is developed from literature. 
The practical perspective is developed by interviewing key business unit and project level 
managers in the large Danish Engineering company, COWI and through a case study selected in 
co-operation with COWI. The case study is an on-going COWI construction project, which is 
followed by attending meetings and conducting follow-up interviews, to further develop and 
substantiate the practical perspective and eliminate or minimize any biases.  
The data will then be analysed using a casual-comparative research approach [Johnson, R. B., 
Christensen, L., 2013]. The research method has been chosen to compare the theoretical and

improvement suggestions can be identified and presented. 
 
1.4 
Scope 
The objective of this research report as previously stated is to analyse the current 
implementation and use of BIM to support and enable integrate project management and to 
identify improvements and suggest "best practise" improvements specific to COWI.  
Integrative project management based on BIM platforms touches most processes and 
disciplines in large construction companies and involves many interdependent components 
including technology infrastructure, software solutions, data repositories, organizational 
support structures and internal and external stakeholders. In order to limit and define the 
scope of this research report, the scope has been narrowed to focus primarily on the 
organizational and structural aspects of integrative project management using BIM and has also 
been focussed primarily on the project specific aspects as opposed to companywide or business 
unit wide aspects.  
For completeness however, the list of suggested "best practise" improvements also includes 
technical and process related recommendations, but these have not been detailed to the same 
extent as the organizational and structural recommendations and they also do not have a 
theoretical foundation to the same extent as the other recommendations. 
 
1.5 
Structure 
The report is divided into seven main sections: Introduction, Theory, Empiricism, Analysis, 
Implementation, Discussion and Conclusion including a list of references and appendices.  
The theory section is further divided into three sub-sections. The first sub-section presents the 
basics of BIM, its practices and how it has affected project processes. The second sub-section 
aims at describing the integration between project stakeholders, with a focus on the 
communication between construction project participants. The third sub-section presents the 
theoretical basis for effective structural changes within an organisation. In this section different 
organizational approaches are presented to address and enable digital disruption.  
The empiricism chapter presents COWI and an overview of the data collected. This includes a 
description of the DTU life science and bioengineering project, which is an on-going COWI 
project. This project has served as the case and as the source of practical information 
throughout this research report. The empiricism section also summarizes the interviews that 
have been conducted and their associated agendas and results. Additional sources of 
information and data is also described in this chapter.

Theoretical framework 
This research report aims at assessing and suggesting ways to improve the use and 
implementation of BIM as an integrative project management tool. BIM is a headline concept 
under which falls a wide array of tools and methods. These tools are all digital by nature and 
have evolved very significantly in the latest wave of digital evolution; the so called "fourth 
industrial revolution" (Weforum.org, 2016), which is characterized by a fusion of technologies 
that are blurring the lines between the physical, digital, and biological spheres. The new 
generation of BIM tools are radically changing the way engineering companies operate and 
therefore impacts the way these companies are organized and structured. In order to fully 
understand and illustrate this change and its potential process and organizational impacts, it is 
important to understand the theoretical background of organizational change and behaviour 
and its underlying components. 
Leavitt offered a new approach to consider organizational behaviour, in which every 
organization consists of four interdependent components: 
 People 
 Tasks 
 Technology 
 Structure 
This approach is widely used for considering organizational changes and making them more 
effective: This model will be used as the theoretical and analytical framework for this research 
report. The four components are part of Leavitt’s diamond model, which was published by H. J. 
Leavitt in 1965 (Leavitt, H.J., 1965) and is illustrated in the figure below:

2.2.2 Defining BIM: Building information modeling 
In order to understand the value and potential of BIM, the mechanisms of BIM has to be defined. 
There are many different definitions of BIM as it is a new and evolving concept. The National 
Institute of Building Sciences defines BIM as: 
“A BIM is a digital representation of physical and functional characteristics of a facility. As such 
it serves as a shared knowledge resource for information about a facility forming a reliable basis 
for decisions during its lifecycle from inception onward. 
 
A basic premise of BIM is collaboration by different stakeholders at different phases of the 
lifecycle of a facility to insert, extract, update, or modify information in the BIM to support and 
reflect the roles of that stakeholder” – BuildingSMART, 2007 
 
In similar fashion, according to the National Building Specification (NBS, 2015), BIM is the 
means by which project stakeholders can understand a building through the use of a digital 
model.  
 
“BIM is a way of working; it is information modelling and information management in a team 
environment, all team members should be working to the same standard as one another. BIM 
creates value from the combined effort of people process and technology” – NBS, 2015 
 
Both definitions of BIM see it as a tool to share knowledge resources and information that 
creates a reliable basis for decision-making during the project lifecycle.  
The concept of BIM has existed since the 1970s (Eastman, C., Tiecholz, P., Sacks, R.; Liston, K., 
2008), but was only commercially available as a tool in the early 1990s with the ability for 
==PAGE:24==
2 Theory 
 
Technical University of Denmark

computers to handle the size and processing requirements of 3D CAD models. As BIM became 
more mainstream in 2007-2010, the series of follow-on applications, services, and hardware 
that supported BIM grew significantly. These follow-on applications and services had led to 
entire new ways of using BIM, which is becoming less focused on its initial 3D modelling 
capabilities and more focused on generating value that align to processes, information and 
consolidating functions.  
Project organizations are exploring new ways of using BIM and the information it produces to 
eliminate waste in redundant data entry and to identify issues early in the design phase that 
were impossible to identify before the introduction of BIM (Hardin, B. and Mccool, D., 2015). As 
a result, many industry organizations host presentations and discussions that has moved 
beyond introducing BIM as a modelling concept to an integration platform used to create better 
connected projects with a more simple agile result-orientated approach based on the use of 
information contained in the common project wide BIM repository. Although the use of BIM has 
previously been associated with the design team, the true long-term-value of BIM lies in the 
field of project management (NBS, 2015).  
 
2.2.3 The value of BIM 
Construction projects are constantly being challenged to deliver successful and effective results 
with limited resources on tight schedules and deadlines. The coordination and sharing of 
information is critical to deliver these results. Construction projects are also heavily impacted 
by an increasing degree of fragmentation (Alashwal, A. and Fong, P., 2015) caused by the large 
number of specialised actors that are typically involved in complex large scale construction 
projects. This drives an even larger requirement for effective communication and integration 
between the different project stakeholders. BIM is a solution that addresses these 
fragmentation issues since it greatly improves the integration and collaboration of project 
stakeholders as schematically illustrated in the figure below.

It is the collaborative, project-integrative approach of BIM that allows teams to function more 
efficiently and use BIM to achieve better results more quickly. When a construction 
management team is allowed to better communicate with the client and design teams using the 
same data, they can quickly determine what factors are important to the client and design team 
and use that knowledge going forward as they prepare to build, hence eliminating many of the 
fragmentation issues as stated by George Elvin (Wiley, 2007):  
“Integration enables a team of designers and constructors to work together toward a common 
goal, allowing design and construction activities to unfold in the best way for the project, rather 
than locking them into separate phases required in over-the-way delivery” – George Elvin 
BIM has evolved and the construction industry is seeing a shift from the 3D visualization aspect 
of BIM to workflow-specific tools that are being applied to solve real-world problems, such as 
installation verification, estimating, clash control and sequencing. BIM continues to redefine 
the way the construction companies build and collaborates. The core value of BIM in the 
construction industry is the ability to take model information and extend its use by giving it 
meaning for other related workflows and processes (Hardin, B. and Mccool, D., 2015).   
These new capabilities has opened doors to faster and more efficient projects, but has also put 
an even larger pressure on managing the communication channels and information ecosystems 
required by modern BIM solutions. Too much information can create risks associated with 
diluting the focus of the project and ultimately leading to a less efficient source of information 
sharing and work-flow (Hardin, B. and Mccool, D., 2015).   
BIM as a project management tool is a promising instrument to address the fragmentation 
issues and achieve better, more efficient and integrated construction projects (Viig, O. J., 2014). 
The use of BIM as a project management tool however, is still in the early stages of development, 
and there are still many challenges and barriers associated with its use. 
 
2.2.4 Challenges and limitations of BIM 
There are many advantages and benefits associated with using BIM as a tool to achieve better 
and more efficient integration in construction project management. Despite these benefits 
there are also difficulties and limitations that should be factored in when considering the use 
of BIM. These limitations mostly relate to the communication between the various participants 
and stakeholders and their level of BIM experience and expertise. Having gaps in the experience 
level of the different BIM users, will create issues in the effective communications between the 
project participants and stakeholders, as illustrated in figure 7 (Vestergaard, F., Karlshøj, J.,

2.2.6 Brief summary 
Digital disruption is influencing all industries including the engineering and construction 
industry. It is important to understand both the source and outcome of the disruption to 
respond accordingly as an organization. BIM is one of the main sources of digital disruption in 
the engineering industry as it impacts most of the demand and supply levers that characterize 
disruptive technologies. This disruption has resulted in an urgent requirement to implement 
BIM related processes and integrating BIM with the existing project management mechanisms 
in large engineering companies. BIM has resulted in a broad array of new possibilities such as 
early visualization, improved communications, improved quality control etc., but has also 
brought on a new set of challenges that have to be addressed. With the new tools and the 
increasing customer expectations and requirements also come an increased pressure on the 
management of communication, information sharing and integration throughout the project 
lifecycle. This represents a new focus of BIM usage, which has previously primarily been 
focused on modeling and visualization in the early stages of construction projects.

Project integration management 
 
2.3.1 Defining project integration management 
Project integration Management is a critical function in construction projects. These projects 
involve many disciplines and participants that have to work together to achieve a common 
objective. The tasks and deliverables of each participant are highly interdependent and 
therefore require a high degree of cooperation and information sharing. The information flow 
and communication between the different participants is highly impacted by the use of BIM. 
Using BIM not only promotes better collaboration internally within a project, but also

2.3.2 Communication in construction teams 
According to Hargie (1999) an understanding of the communication process is a requirement 
to improving communication. Understanding communication in both organizational and 
private settings is difficult. A review conducted by Dance and Larson in 1972 (Dance, F.E.X. and 
Larson, C.E 1972) revealed 126 different definitions of communication, and many more have 
been formulated since then.  
Group participation and collaboration are two of the key characteristics of project-orientated 
communication in organizations.  Participation is the extent to which individuals are involved 
in group interaction, being colored by the group norms and group development (Wallace, 1987; 
Anderson et al. 1999). Project participation and interaction includes turn taking, the initiation 
of conversation, how individuals interrupt others and if, and how, the interaction intensifies 
(Emmitt, S. and Gorse, C.A., 2007). During project interaction, actors’ participation is regulated 
by the feedback received from their contribution. The amount of contributions in association 
with discussions and participation within the project group is consequently controlled by the 
behavior of other group members. During construction projects, the project manager will be 
exposed to  communication and pressures from many different multidisciplinary actors that all 
want their needs addressed. All this communication has to be managed and incorporated into 
the project efficiently. The project manager needs to control the communication dynamics by 
controlling the amount of individual contributions and must be aware of the different turn-
taking dynamics. Controlling the turn-taking dynamics is an important process of creating an 
efficient and successful project. The figure below illustrates the typical turn-taking dynamics of 
construction projects:

2.3.3 Interpersonal and group communication 
As mentioned, one form of communication that takes place within the construction project 
industry is interpersonal interaction. Interpersonal communication generally involves the 
process of communicating between two or more people, whereas more than two people is 
considered a group (Emitt, S. and Gorse, C.A., 2007). An interpersonal channel is one that 
involves a face-to-face exchange between a source and a receiver (Rogers, E.M., 1976). 
Interpersonal communication enables individuals to establish and maintain relationships. The 
process in which one person will attempt to communicate with another will depend upon how 
they assume that the recipient will interpret and respond to the information they wish to 
transmit to them.  
Another important form of communication that takes place in the context of construction 
projects is group communication. Group communication involves more than two people but is 
limited to a single group of people (Emitt, S. and Gorse, C.A., 2007). Participants of group

2.3.4 The importance of effective multidisciplinary communication 
Effective communication in decision-making is critical to business and is required to share 
understanding of problems and discuss the various solutions efficiently (Gouran and Hirokawa, 
1996). The importance of effective communication to organizations is succinctly summarized 
by Armstrong (2001):  
 Achieving coordinated results – organizations function by means of the collective 
actions of people but independent actions lead to outcomes incongruent with 
organizational objectives. Coordinated outcomes therefore demand effective 
communications. 
 Managing change – Most organizations are subject to continuous change. This, in turn, 
affects their employees. Acceptance of and willingness to embrace change is likely only 
if the reasons for this change are well communicated.  
 Motivating employees – the degree of which an individual is motivated to work 
effectively for their organization is dependent upon the responsibility they have and 
the scope of achievement afforded by their role. Feelings in this regard will depend 
upon the quality of communications from senior managers within their organization.  
 Understanding the needs of the workforce – for organizations to be able to respond 
effectively to the needs of their employees, it is vital that they develop an efficient 
channel of communication. This two-way channel must allow for feedback from the 
workforce on organization policy in a way that encourages an open and honest 
dialogue between employees at all levels and the top-level managers of the 
organization.  
Given the benefits of effective communication outlined above, the consequence of poor 
communication for an organization is that employees will misread management decisions or 
react to them in a way that was not intended. Similarly, managers will misunderstand the needs 
of employees and will therefore suffer from lower engagement and performance.  
Given the level and pace of change in the engineering industry, the ability to communicate and 
share information effectively is critical. Efficient communication and knowledge sharing has

The communication between the design management and the multidisciplinary groups found 
in the engineering industry functions as both group and interpersonal interaction. 
Multidisciplinary group communication occurs within systems that are made up of 
interdependent groups that share the performance of tasks needed to achieve a common goal 
(Kreps, 1989). According to Hare (1976) a general observation tends to suggest that decisions 
made by a group are more accurate and effective than those made by an individual, which 
increases the necessity of effective communication between the multidisciplinary groups in 
order to fully profit from the knowledge and expertise of the different group participants. It is 
therefore paramount to both form and manage the vital communication interface between the 
different multidisciplinary groups and the wider project in which they work, in order to fully 
profit from the benefits of efficient communication and is an area where the engineering 
industry can highly benefit from the use of Information and Communication Technologies 
(ICTs) 
 
2.3.5 ICT and Multidisciplinary communication 
An increasing degree of multidisciplinary communication and sharing of information are taken 
place using ICTs such as BIM. This involves the digital exchange of information and data 
between internal and external participants. This is a new critical communication channel on 
projects and a type of communication, which is directly enabled and supported by BIM. The 
challenges in this area evolve around aligning standards, so that the parties can effectively 
exchange information and maintaining a rigorous process to manage the communication, which 
is often asynchronous and not real time. Despite the increasing degree of communication and 
information sharing taking place using ICT’s, it is however, impossible to divorce interpersonal 
and inter-group communication from the construction process (Dainty, A. and Moore, D. and 
Murray, M., 2006).  
Effective multidisciplinary communication and realizing the full potential of ICT depends on  
adapting to its use by changing attitudes, cultures, structures and processes. It is also important 
to realize, that although ICT’s can improve the current efficiency of engineering project 
communication and sharing of information, it can also cause further fragmentation if not 
properly managed.  
In order to realize the full potential of ICT, the organization needs to develop a culture of 
accepting and adapting to new technologies. ICTs must be designed so they consider the 
existing processes and mechanisms within the industry (Dainty, A. and Moore, D. and Murray, 
M., 2006). It is therefore crucial as a project manager to be aware of the mechanisms and

2.3.6  The role and tasks of project managers 
The role of the project manager is to manage project scope, project organization, quality, costs 
and duration of the project. Effective communication is vital to achieve these objectives. The 
need for the project manager to function as an effective communicator and facilitator is self-
evident when considering the number of occasions that communication has been cited as a 
primary cause of fragmentation and project failure (Higgin, G. and Jessop, N., 1965).   
It is important as a project manager to understand and resolve the various barriers associated 
with effective communication. According to Huczynski and Buchanan (2001) there are five 
principal barriers to effective communication that can be generalized as: 
 Power differences – Employees distort upward communication and believe that 
superiors have a limited understanding of subordinate’s needs. 
 Gender differences – men tend to walk more whereas women tend to listen and reflect 
more. 
 Physical surroundings – issues such as room layout, noisy equipment and physical 
proximity affect communication effectiveness. 
 Cultural diversity – different cultures harbor dissimilar expectations as regards formal 
and informal expectations.  
These barriers stems from human interaction and relations, where people interact with 
colleagues influenced by their socialization and cultural values. There are however also barriers 
associated with listening, which is also highly related to the position as project manager. 
Sheldric-Ross and Dewdney (1998) summarized the typical barriers to effective 
communication from the listener’s perspective as: 
 Selective perception – the listener only hearing those messages that support their 
model of the world and filtering out others. 
 Making assumption – the listener assuming what the sender means or feels rather than 
listening to what they say.  
 Giving unsolicited advice – the listener giving unwanted advice or providing advice 
before listening carefully to the problem.  
 Being Judgmental – the listen being critical of another person’s point of view n a way 
that distances them from the transmitter’s point of view 
 Acting defensively – the listener defending a position rather than listening to the 
position of another person 
 Failing to understand cultural differences – subtle but significant differences in 
language of pronunciation often lead to miscommunication. For example, silences have 
==PAGE:39==
2 Theory 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
Page | 28 
distinct meaning and require specific etiquette in some cultures that must be respected 
for effective communication to take place.  
These barriers infer that effective communication is reliant on effective listening skills as much 
as it is communication skills (Baguley, 1994).  It is therefore highly relevant for a project 
manager to have both high communication and high listening skills in order to not be blighted 
by the barriers listed above while yielding the most efficient degree of communication in all 
phases and disciplines of a project.  
 
2.3.7 Brief summary 
Project integration management is an extended view of project management with a focus on 
the integration challenges relating to managing and effectively communicating with multi-
disciplinary internal and external stakeholders in large construction projects.  
To successfully deliver large construction projects, effective interpersonal and group 
communication is critical. On large projects it is typically the multidisciplinary group 
interaction that is most problematic due to the high level of fragmentation between disciplines. 
An increasing degree of the multidisciplinary communication and sharing of information is now 
done using ICTs such as BIM. This is a new critical communication channel on projects and a 
type of communication, which is directly enabled and supported by BIM.  
Effectively managing these new communication and information sharing channels is a critical 
part of being a project manager. It is therefore important not only to understand the 
multidisciplinary communication and information sharing mechanisms, but also to understand 
how these mechanisms are enabled by BIM tools and processes. If the project manager cannot 
use the new tools to achieve effective multidisciplinary group communications, this will 
translate to poor integration management.

2.4

2.4.1 Digitalization and structural change 
As mentioned earlier, the construction industry is changing rapidly due to the increasing degree 
of digitalization and digital disruption. Digitalization should not simply be considered in terms 
of doing things differently. Companies must also consider doing different things. As an example 
many construction companies now have the opportunity to define and offer completely new 
value added services by introducing connected embedded sensors and other smart 
technologies in the constructions and defining new services that leverage this information that 
can be layered onto the traditional construction products. Such levels of change will have 
significant impacts to the organization and skills required. Most Nordic and Danish companies 
across industries recognize the potential of digital to transform their business, however most 
of them are struggling to realize that potential and put their strategy to use (Accenture, 2016).  
Change readiness and a willingness to implement required structural changes is a necessity for 
the engineering industry conditioned by the surrounding environment and pace of digital 
disruption. The leading companies have to acknowledge and act to be the digital disrupters 
themselves and avoid becoming digitally disrupted.  
In order to make efficient structural changes, it is key to understand the definition of 
organizational structure and the associated management processes. It is also important to 
understand the baseline of the current state of the organization as well as what defines effective 
structural change. Since the focus of this research report is centred on the organizational 
impacts of BIM as a range of digital tools, the structural change methods presented in this 
section will focus on digital operating models.

2.4.4 Structural change: Monitoring and adaptation 
Making the right structure work in practice depends on having the right behavioural 
capabilities in place. These go beyond technical skills and should be more readily thought of as 
a prevailing digital mind-set and attitude for the whole organization. The most important 
capabilities with their associated degree of urgency in a digital enabled environment are 
(Accenture, 2016):

2 Theory

 Urgent capabilities 
o Seek, sense and understand disruption: To succeed in a digital environment, 
businesses need to develop a wider sense of disruption, looking beyond their 
own industry to identify new digital trends, wherever they come from, and 
assess how (and when) they may have a material impact. 
o Build digital IQ: The whole organization – from the board to the frontline – 
needs to operate with a high digital IQ: a ‘digital first’ mentality to sense, predict 
and act to build digital services and capture new opportunities. Training and 
coaching are fundamental to this, but other routes to building a digitally savvy 
team, extending even to acquiring relevant talent, should all be up for 
consideration. 
o Unleash ideas, fail fast, scale what works: Operating at digital speed means 
developing and launching new idea faster. Some will fail (and must be allowed 
to do so quickly); others will be suitable for scaling up. But it’s only by having a 
constant pipeline of ideas and experimentation that businesses will find 
winning new products and services. That may even mean a business becoming 
its own source of disruption in the search for new value. 
 Less urgent capabilities, still important 
o Make the most of all data, old and new: Data’s at the heart of the digital 
business. It requires dedicated analysis to fully exploit its potential. What’s 
more, the sources of data that can generate value may be beyond today’s 
boundaries. Businesses therefore need to make sure that they expand their data 
horizons. 
o Create pervasive digital trust: Without trust, businesses cannot operate 
effectively. And that’s especially the case for digital businesses. Trust needs to

o Think (and act) ecosystem: The digital world is connected and collaborative. 
Digital businesses therefore need to participate in partner ecosystems, 
leveraging APIs(Application programming interface), working with others to 
execute non-core activities and sharing and exchanging new sources and 
inspiration for innovation. 
Changing these behavioural capabilities, with focus on building digital IQ, can be challenging, 
especially as it relates to older more experienced employees who have developed deep-rooted 
daily routines through extensive practice. Understanding human behaviour and behavioural 
change is necessary in order to adapt an organizations employees to newly developed 
strategies. BJ Fogg from Stanford University has developed a model, which aims to understand 
human behaviour and behavioural change, namely The Fogg Behaviour Model (FBM). The FBM 
asserts that for a person to perform a target behaviour, three factors must occur at the same 
time or else the behaviour will not occur. These three factors are referred to as: 
==PAGE:46==
2 Theory 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
Page | 35 
 Motivation  
 Ability 
 Trigger 
The interdependency between motivation and the ability to do a behaviour and the trigger 
required for the behaviour to occur can be seen in the figure below: 
 
Figure 18 Illustration of the interdependency between the FBM components (Fogg, BJ., 2015) 
The FBM implies that motivation and ability are trade-offs. Employees with low motivation may 
perform a behaviour if the behaviour is simple enough (high ability - easy to do) (Fogg, BJ., 
2015).  If a sufficient degree of motivation to perform a behaviour is matched with the ability 
to do that behaviour, all that is then needed for the behaviour to occur is a call-to-action (a 
trigger). Meaning that a behaviour will not occur without a trigger despite both the ability and 
motivation being high enough. In the digital environment the organization’s digital strategy can 
work as a trigger towards employees adapting to the strategy.   
When a behaviour does not occur, at least one of the three elements is missing.  There are 
several mechanisms an organization can use to motivate or trigger employees or to increase 
their associated ability to do a behaviour. If the employee has the required ability to do a 
behaviour but is lacking the motivation, an organization can exploit the three core 
fundamentals of motivation namely, sensation, anticipation and belonging. Each of these has 
two sides: pleasure/pain, hope/fear and acceptance/rejection 
==PAGE:47==
2 Theory 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
Page | 36 
If the employees are lacking the required ability to do a behaviour, an organization can optimize 
the ability threshold by either increasing the employee ability through training or by 
simplifying. Simplifying is the process of identifying the ability blockers of the behaviour and 
act accordingly. Typical ability blockers of behaviours are defined as (Fogg, BJ., 2015): 
 Money 
 Time 
 Physical effort 
 Brain cycles (Mental effort) 
 Social deviance 
 Non-routine 
As an example, if a target behaviour requires money and an organization’s limiting resource is 
money, then the behaviour is not simple (Fogg, BJ., 2015). Understanding these ability blockers 
and motivational fundamentals is paramount in order to ensure that employees will adapt to a 
desired behaviour, which is an essential part of fulfilling an organizations development 
strategy.  
 
2.4.5 Brief summary 
The construction industry is facing an increasing degree of digitalization and digital disruption. 
In order to capitalize on and leverage digital disruption, companies must change and adapt their 
organizational structure and project processes to be able to use the new digital tools effectively.  
The organizational structure is a subset of the management processes and determines how the 
roles, power and responsibilities are assigned, controlled, and coordinated, and how 
information flows between the different levels of management. 
Structural change must address all these components. To achieve structural change, businesses 
must have an appropriate strategy, which they can also execute. Many Nordic companies are 
struggling with the execution of their digital strategy. The execution of a digital strategy 
depends on having the right behavioural capabilities in place and having a prevailing digital 
mind-set and attitude in the whole organization. To achieve this mindset change, companies 
must understand the relationship between individual triggers, motivation and abilities.

interdependent components are adjusted together. When an organization introduces new 
technologies such as BIM, it should understand that the processes and behaviors of the 
employees will need to change accordingly. The same notion of interdependency between these 
components is also the basis for analyzing organizational change using Leavitts diamond model.

BIM usage is slowly moving beyond being simple modeling usage to becoming a more 
integrative tool used to create better-connected projects driving a need for integrated digital 
platforms. Given that the integrated BIM approach across disciplines and projects is still 
relatively new, the BIM platform and usage on projects needs to be managed and supervised by 
BIM specialists because the necessary level of BIM knowledge and expertise has not yet been 
established widely in the project organizations. This therefore means that the center of 
excellence (CoE) model is an appropriate model at least in the early stages of adopting BIM as 
an integrative project management platform. The lack of skills of the project managers, is also 
the reason why the centralized model cannot be adopted in the first instance until a deeper 
understanding of BIM processes can be embedded into the organization and specifically into 
the project managers. This CoE should then also be responsible for standardizing the 
application, processes and information shared through BIM.  
For engineering projects it is important to establish a common digital strategy for the use and 
deployment of BIM across project disciplines. The execution of the digital BIM strategy requires 
a combination of shared and individual execution. It requires shared functions to manage the 
standardization of applications, processes and data across business units and it requires 
discipline specific execution to cater for the different nature of each discipline.

4.3 
Data summary and analysis 
The data collected documents some of the practical challenges and barriers as well as the 
benefits associated with the use of BIM. The data gave an insight into the project processes and 
mechanisms related to the use of BIM. The first two meetings focused solely on the 
organizational challenges and general benefits of BIM and introduced the organizational and 
project structure of COWI as well the project processes across all disciplines cf. appendix D. The 
four subsequent interviews focused on the project practices of typical engineering projects of 
COWI. A variety of different roles were covered by the interviews in order to yield a wide array 
of different perspectives cf. appendix F. The attended project meeting was included to further 
enhance the focus on project integration and the associated flow of information and 
communication between project disciplines.  
The benefits of BIM were more apparent in certain disciplines compared to others. The project 
managers perceived the benefits of using BIM quite differently. This depended to some extent 
on the age of the project managers. Age was therefore found to be an important factor for the 
acceptance and understanding of the benefits of using digital tools. This was caused by the older 
and more experienced project participants having deeply rooted daily routines that they found 
difficult to change. It is however, the older and most experienced project managers that are 
most critical in the construction industry, which depends heavily on specialized skills and 
practical experience. 
The challenges of using and propagating BIM at an organizational level in COWI focused on the 
complexity of implementation and adapting its use. The economical aspect of the 
implementation due to the cost of educating employees was said to be one of the main barriers 
at an organizational level as well as changing and integrating new practices and project 
processes. For project processes, the main barriers related to lack of experience and the 
requirement for early planning.

As mentioned in the empirical section, the data consists of meetings and interviews, which were 
split into three different focus areas that reflect the overall focus of this research report:  
 Digitalization and BIM 
 Project integration and the influence of BIM

4.3.1 Digitalization and BIM 
One of the first steps towards using and implementing a new tool is understanding its 
importance and why and how it should be integrated with the current project processes. It is 
also important to acknowledge barriers and challenges associated with the implementation and 
use of new tools as well as the targeted benefits.  
BIM tools are being using in an increasing degree across engineering disciplines at COWI. As 
mentioned by Peter Autzen, BIM is being used during projects to coordinate, communicate and 
share information between disciplines through an integrative BIM platform established by a 
specialized BIM team. Better coordination and integration between disciplines makes it 
possible to eliminate many of the complications that were previously dealt with on-site.  
The adaption process associated with using a new array of digital tools such as BIM has put a 
larger pressure on the need for early planning. This was consistently brought up in all 
interviews. Entrepreneurs and clients have put higher demands on engineering projects and 
much of the work that was previously done on-site, now has to be done in the early phases of a 
project and delivered as a finished product model before the actual building phase starts. Due 
to the project manager being the primary connection between the client and the project as 
mentioned by Henrik Olsen, it is important that the project manager is aware of these BIM 
related contracts and deliverables associated with new customer expectations and extensive 
requirements.  
The difference in the level of expertise is also an apparent challenge in ongoing projects such as 
the DTU life science and bioengineering project. The difference in the level of expertise causes 
complications when more disciplines and project members are included in the BIM processes. 
Peter Autzen mentions the importance of good integration and coordination as a critical aspect 
of including more project members in the BIM processes. He states that BIM has the tendency 
to make good integration and coordination better but at the same time it also has the opposite 
effect if there is poor integration and coordination in a project.  
In the DTU life science and bioengineering project, Peter Autzen handled the BIM related 
contracts and deliverables, while other management processes was handled by Henrik Olsen. 
Despite Peter Autzen handling the BIM processes, he acknowledges the importance of having 
the project manager as a primary part of these processes due to the increasing need for better 
BIM related communication and integration during the early phases of a project. Peter Autzen 
also states that it is important for the project manager to be aware of the challenges related to 
different BIM processes of a project and to continuously collect the necessary experience in 
order to improve these processes in the future. It is therefore important, according to Peter 
Autzen, that the project manager establishes a foundational understanding of BIM, which is 
good enough to collect and share experience from project to project, since BIM is such a new 
tool, which has not yet been implemented in all disciplines and in all projects. The project 
manager is therefore the link between projects in terms of gathering and utilizing experience.

but as all interviewees state, the benefits of BIM are clear and once the use of BIM can be 
streamlined and well-integrated onto current project processes, the benefits will be realized.  
 
4.3.2 Project integration and the influence of BIM 
According to Henrik Olsen the role of the project manager is to lead and integrate project 
disciplines, which includes streamlining processes, to communicate with external stakeholders 
and to ensure a good interdisciplinary communication and sharing of information. Weekly 
meetings and awareness of the information shared between disciplines are used to enhance the 
integration of the project and to eliminate the risk of fragmentation.  
Fragmentation issues have always plagued the engineering industry, which is putting a large 
pressure on effective handling of project integration. Casper Zerlang-Illum mentions this cause 
of fragmentation in relation to the use of BIM and that one of the most important tasks of BIM

is the identification of communication lines between disciplines and the handling of the 
associated flow of information. Peter Autzen further emphasizes the importance of good project 
integration management between disciplines. He also mentions that while BIM has the 
potential to enhance project integration it can also have the opposite effect and lead to a more 
fragmented working environment.  
Simplifying the BIM processes and involving more project participants is one solution towards 
eliminating fragmentation issues and creating more well integrated and profitable projects. 
Simplifying the processes can also lead to the project managers becoming a more central part

The clients and entrepreneurs are also becoming more involved in the engineering project 
processes, since BIM has made it possible to tailor a project that fits to the clients’ demands 
more accurately, as mentioned by Kim Bundgaard. This requires further efficient

which as mentioned by Henrik Olsen and Kim Bundgaard, is one of the more important roles of 
the project manager. An increasing degree of these interactions relate to BIM processes, which 
has in turn developed a further emphasis on the project manager being able to understand and 
use BIM to establish the associated contracts and deliverables.   
BIM has developed entire new project processes that have to be integrated into the current 
processes and systems. Different understandings of these project processes can cause 
uncertainty and discomfort in the project environment as mentioned by Kim Bungaard and 
Peter Autzen. Creating a shared understanding of these processes is therefore critical when an 
organization develops best practices and project templates for BIM.

The overall digital strategy of COWI is to raise the expertise in the use of BIM and other digital 
tools across all disciplines, which is expected to lead to more efficient and better-integrated

and its associated tools and processes as mentioned by Michael Blom and Jørgen Emborg. The 
strategy covers all disciplines and specifies the degree to which they should, use BIM and what 
associated tools are required. The template for these processes and tool sets can be seen in 
appendix A. The template was reviewed during the preliminary organizational meetings with 
Jørgen Emborg and Michael Blom.  
COWI has redeveloped project processes and practices to include and adapt the use of BIM. New

 Project BIM manager 
 Project BIM coordinator

BIM has developed new project processes that have to be adapted and integrated with the

The primary focus of this report directly reflects the primary concern of Michael Blom, Jørgen

Specifically in this case to include the project manager much more actively in the processes and 
mechanisms of BIM and not relying on the project BIM coordinator and the discipline BIM 
coordinator for this. Given the central nature of the project manager role, this must be the role 
to also ensure that BIM is leveraged as a integrative project management platform. This can't 
be "outsourced" to BIM coordination roles. Including the project manager and effectively 
leveraging the associated vast amount of integration management experience could lead to a 
more efficient and well-integrated BIM usage and potentially eliminate or reduce the need for 
BIM coordinators.

4.4.1 Using BIM as an integrative project management tool 
COWI recognizes the importance of BIM and is implementing it across all project disciplines as 
shown in appendix A. According to the collected data, BIM is also being adapted increasingly 
for project processes. A number of new BIM roles have been introduced to COWI's project 
organizations: BIM manager, Project BIM coordinator and Discipline BIM coordinator. During 
typical COWI projects such as the DTU life science and bioengineering the information and 
communication channels associated with these newly added roles have yet to be efficiently 
used.  
The data from COWI show, that the lack of involvement of the project managers in the BIM 
processes leads to a less efficient use, since many of the project integration practices and 
mechanisms are overlooked and the experience of the project managers is not applied to the 
way BIM is being used. This will in return lead to many issues caused by not acknowledging 
these integrative mechanisms of communication and information sharing as presented in the 
theoretical analysis. Properly managing these integrative mechanisms could eliminate many of 
the barriers and challenges associated with the use of BIM as experienced by COWI.  
The communication and integration mechanisms rely on interpersonal and group 
communication mechanisms as described in sections 2.3.3 and 2.3.4. An increasing degree of 
both interpersonal and group communication on COWI projects occurs using ICT’s such as BIM. 
Controlling the ICT participation and collaboration between project members will be critical 
for project managers at COWI to ensure more efficient project processes as discussed in 
sections 2.3.5 and 2.3.6.  
Some project managers at COWI are able to realize the benefits of using BIM and are therefore  
more motivated to use and implement BIM as mentioned in some of the interviews. This largely 
depends on the age of the project managers, where the younger are more digitally adept and 
find it easier to understand and use the BIM tools. Having the project manager aware and 
knowledgeable of the BIM processes, will result in more efficient and well-integrated projects,

4.4.3 Adapting to the use of BIM in COWI 
COWI has established a digital strategy which specifically addresses the use of BIM. COWI 
however, is struggling with the execution of the strategy and to realize the potential of using 
BIM and creating a more integrated BIM platform. COWI has established BIM related 
requirements in all disciplines which also involves the project managers. These requirements 
work as the digital goal and trigger within the different disciplines in adapting and using BIM.  
The main challenge in executing the strategy is that the BIM requirements are perceived as time 
consuming with no immediate value, especially as it relates to the project management 
processes. The project managers are aware of the BIM requirements but are lacking in adapting 
and using its tools and processes as stated in section 4.3.2. As outlined in the previous sections, 
the primary challenges and barriers of BIM lies in its implementation and including the project 
managers in the processes and practices of BIM.  
One of COWIs solutions is based on simplifying and reducing the BIM related tasks of the project 
managers by introducing project BIM coordinators and discipline BIM coordinators in the 
projects. This solution is only working to a limited extent, as it prevents and "insulates" those 
project managers that are not digitally adept from understanding the potential of BIM through 
hands-on use. This means that the older and more experienced project managers are still 
struggling in adapting and using BIM as stated in section 4.2.3.

Motivating the project managers as well as the rest of the organization by using the

5.1  
Up-skilling the project managers at COWI 
One of the best ways to achieve a use of BIM as an integrative project management platform is 
to increasing the involvement of the project manager as outlined in the analysis. This is one of 
the main improvement areas. COWI's digital strategy should therefore include specific 
approaches  to achieve this. This can be achieved in several ways, where the most obvious  is to 
invest in training and up-skilling all the project managers to a level where the project managers 
are capable of mastering the communication and information sharing tools and processes in 
BIM to achieve a significantly improved project level integration. Although this is considered a 
significant investment at COWI, it is a foundational action that must be taken in order to support 
all of the other improvement actions. They all rely on extending the capabilities and 
responsibility of the project managers to extend the use of BIM as an integrative project 
management tool.  In order to pave the way for a decision to make this investment, a business 
case should be developed to quantify the specific value that COWI can realize by making this 
investment as outlined in section 5.1.5. 
To develop a training curriculum and program, COWI should engage the few project managers 
who already have a good understanding of how BIM can be used to support the integrative 
project management objectives as well as the current project and discipline BIM coordinators. 
The training should be shaped around actual BIM project management use cases and processes 
at COWI and should not be based on standard BIM product training. The training should also 
be an ongoing effort and not just a "one off" activity. It should ideally be carried out as part of 
the execution of an overall BIM program, which also includes the other actions listed in this 
section. This would mean that the effectiveness and outcomes from the up-skilling and 
structural changes could be measured and followed up based on key performance indicators 
(as outlined in section 5.1.3) and could also be included in the individual performance 
management process (as outlined in section 5.1.4).  
The up-skilling of the project managers would also serve to increase the motivation of the 
project managers as explained by the Fogg Behaviour Model described in section 2.4.4. As 
project managers become more versed and comfortable with the use of BIM as an integrative 
project management tool, their motivation to further extend the use and apply their experience 
would automatically increase and amplify the outcomes even further.  
This will enable the accumulation and application of management experience, which is required 
to use BIM effectively, not only as a modelling tool but also as a project management tool to 
integrate project management practises across stakeholders on complex construction projects.

As the project managers build their BIM skills (through up-skilling and other related actions), 
the project managers  as well as the rest of the project organization, will enhance their level of 
BIM skills and understanding over time, which then means that the current COWI operating 
model as described in section 4.4.2 can then be changed to a centralized model as illustrated in 
figure 25. This will introduce the project managers as a more central part of the BIM governance 
and processes. This suggestion has already been described in sections 4.2.2 and 4.4.2.  
In this model there is no need for a specialized CoE and the project managers take more direct 
ownership to achieve a new level of integrative project management by combining their 
practical experience with a deeper understanding of BIM tools and processes. The centralized 
operating model avoids fragmentation issues associated with having multiple overlapping roles 
and reduces costs as it eliminates or reduces the need for project and discipline BIM 
coordinators.  
Once the project managers have a deeper and more consistent level of BIM knowledge, they  
should be able to transition over into a more BIM central management role that includes many 
of discipline BIM coordinator functions and responsibilities such as coordinating BIM related 
discipline communication and information sharing. The project manager should be able to 
ensure that the project team is working according to the BIM execution plan throughout the 
project and execute BIM collaboration with project partners, while ensuring a common 
understanding of the different BIM related communication and information channels. The 
project BIM manager will still be responsible for creating an integrated BIM platform in 
collaboration with the project manager. The Center of Excellence will then consolidate into the 
integrated digital platform function, which defines and supports BIM standards and usage 
across project disciplines and potentially across projects. 
 
5.3 
Defining Key Performance Indicators to measure and track BIM usage 
Jan Leschly, the famous Danish tennis player and subsequent CEO of the global pharmaceutical 
company SmithKlineBeecham, once said: "If you don't keep score then you're just practising". 
Applying this logic to the issue of extending BIM usage in large engineering companies, there is 
a need to "keep score". This is what Key Performance Indicators do.  
This suggestion involves setting up management level and project level scoring to track the 
effectiveness and the results of the improvement actions. The intent is to define a set of key 
performance indicators (KPIs), that reflect how COWI is progressing against its BIM objectives. 
Key performance indicators are specific measures defined to evaluate the success of strategic 
==PAGE:73==
5 Improvement suggestions 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
 
 
Page | 62 
initiatives.  They can be quantitative as well as qualitative and can be used to track inputs (e.g. 
how much money or resource is invested to achieve an outcome), process efficiency (e.g. how 
productive are the resources involved in a project) or outputs (e.g. what direct benefits can be 
associated with the an activity or project). 
Examples of KPIs that could be considered by COWI to track progress against the BIM strategy 
execution could include: 
 Level of direct investments in BIM infrastructure and support.  
 Number of project managers who has received BIM training. 
 Average BIM proficiency level score by role and project discipline based on self 
assessments.    
 Volume of work and number of projects where BIM is used as an integrative project 
management platform. This would require well defined criteria for when a project can 
be considered to leverage BIM in this way. 
 Value realized against defined business case objectives (as outlined in section 5.1.5) 
 Progress against specific BIM process and technology simplification initiatives.  
This is a non-exhaustive list of example KPIs that could be considered by COWI. The KPIs would 
serve to keep track of progress against plan and could potentially become part of the 
management reporting scorecard at COWI, considering the strategic importance COWI has 
placed on improving and extending the use of BIM. It should also be noted that KPIs also serve 
as a motivational factor and positive reinforcement, as they quantify and directly reflect and 
report on progress and results achieved.        
 
5.4 
Including BIM usage as a performance objective 
One of the most effective ways to motivate project managers to take personal ownership for 
extending the use of BIM as an integrative project management platform, is to define individual 
performance management objectives for the project managers, that directly relate to the effort 
they invest personally and the results they achieve. This directly relates to the motivation axis 
in the Fogg Behaviour Model described in section 2.4.4.  
If project managers become more adept in their BIM understanding and capability through 
training and hands-on experience (ref. section 5.1.1) and they have a direct performance 
objective that can potentially trigger increased bonus and/or career progression, then COWI 
would effectively have addressed both dimensions of the Fogg Behaviour Model, and 
significantly increased the chance of success in achieving the desired objectives.  
The specific performance objectives would be included as one of many individual objectives for 
the project managers. They could be based on individual and project based KPIs (e.g. taking 
required training curriculum and reaching required level of average project team BIM 
proficiency). The performance objectives would have defined and agreed targets that if met

Technical University of Denmark

could factor into the variable compensation (bonus) calculation and be considered when 
project managers are assessed for promotions. Those project managers who have successfully 
met their targets for extending the use of BIM as an integrative project management platform 
and demonstrated results in doing so, would be prioritized for bonus payouts and promotions 
over those who were less successful in achieving these results. Obviously this would just be one 
parameter that would be considered in the performance management process alongside the 
more traditional performance objectives such as successful project completion on schedule 
within agreed quality and cost boundaries.  
 
5.5 
Developing a quantified BIM business case   
In order to motivate COWI to continue and possibly increase the investments in BIM, COWI 
should develop a specific quantified business case to understand the actual savings and value 
that can be achieved by extending the use of BIM to become an effective integrative project 
management platform.  No such business case exists in COWI today.  
This will involve carefully considering and valuation of the associated costs and benefits. This 
should include both direct costs and benefits such as investments, expected savings and value 
from increased win-rates as well as indirect costs and benefits such as increased productivity 
caused by increased employee engagement and motivation.  
This will not be an exact science, but it is very important to carefully contemplate and articulate 
the cost and value drivers and to have a precise expectation of the business case which progress 
can be tracked against (ref. section 5.1.3). The business case is a living document which should 
be continuously updated as underlying assumptions and cost/benefit drivers change. The 
business case should also include a view of the qualitative benefits of extending BIM usage. 
The business case should be presented and signed off at the executive management level and 
should be form the basis for investment decisions relative to BIM and the improvement actions 
as suggested in this section.  
 
5.6 
Assessment of process enabled simplifications and improvements   
The final suggestion relates to the BIM processes and technology. BIM has its origin in the 
modelling and visualization disciplines mostly related to the early phases of construction 
projects. Given that the new generation of BIM solutions have evolved its support for 
integrative project management and that the enabling technologies have also evolved 
significantly (e.g. cloud based technologies, handheld and wearable technologies, internet of 
things, etc.), the suggestion is to define and launch a project to assess the BIM requirements for 
COWI as they relate to integrative project management and compare the requirements to the 
recent progress of the modern BIM tools and enabling technologies. The focus of this project

6 Discussion  
 6 
This section discusses and interprets the results of the research.  
The purpose of the research was to analyse the current implementation and use of BIM as a tool 
to integrate project management processes across the disciplines and stakeholders in large 
construction projects. The research has also assessed the organizational and structural impacts 
of extending the use of BIM as an integrative project management tool and developed specific 
suggestions for how this can be achieved.  
The research hypothesis was that the current use of BIM in engineering companies primarily 
focuses within the vertical functions in engineering projects and that the use is still mostly 
centred in the early project stages using traditional BIM functionalities within modelling and 
visualization.  This leads to fragmentation between disciplines and sub-optimal use of BIM 
tools. To improve the usage of BIM, it should instead be implemented horizontally enabled by 
a central project management function in the organizational structure specialized in the use of 
BIM to integrate across project disciplines. This way the communication and management 
across project disciplines is significantly strengthened leading to better project integration and 
results, which improves the competitive edge of large engineering companies such as COWI.  
The scope of the research was deliberately narrowed to focus on the organizational and 
structural impacts of implementing BIM as an integrative project management platform. This 
meant that process and technology impacts were only considered at a high level but not 
discussed in any detail. The organizational and structural impacts are arguably the more 
important aspects of project integration management, but given that the hypothesis has largely 
been confirmed by this research as discussed later, it could be very relevant and interesting if 
a similar study would be conducted to focus on the other aspects of project integration 
management as illustrated in figure 12. 
To establish the theoretical basis for the paper, literature research was performed to 
substantiate the theory behind three key topics, that were critical to understand in order to test 
the hypothesis: 
 The need for integration across stakeholders and disciplines in construction projects 
 A general understanding of Building Information Modelling (BIM) 
 An understanding of how specific business outcomes (e.g. better integration) are 
achieved through structural changes within an organisation. 
Leavitt’s expanded model (Leavitt, H.J. 1965) was used as a framework for the research and 
analysis of each of these topics.  The literature research was mostly used to develop insights 
into the mechanisms of project integration management, and defining necessary components 
whereas the practical research was used to develop an understanding of the barriers and

challenges of the engineering industry (e.g. effective structural change) in the face of digital 
disruption as represented by the emergence of new digital BIM tools. Methods and models 
developed by large global consulting companies have been used to understand the barriers and 
challenges that the engineering industry is facing. These models combined with data from the 
COWI case study and interviews, demonstrated why BIM is such a necessary tool and also 
highlighted the scale of organizational changes required to implement BIM as an integrative 
platform n an engineering environment.   
The data used for this research report was collected qualitatively by identifying an ongoing 
COWI project as a case study and interviewing key COWI project team members. It would be 
interesting to conduct a similar study based on quantitative data. This would require a different 
approach, where various indicators measuring the usage of BIM would have to be defined and 
collected in order to decipher and interpret the quantitative data. Collecting the data 
quantitatively could possibly provide a broader perspective of BIM usage across the industry 
and contribute to understanding how BIM application and usage depends on factors such as the 
project managers background, culture, age and digital expertise and proficiency level.  
As previously stated the objectives of this research report was to understand the importance of 
BIM and assess if it should be considered an integrative project management tool. The objective 
was also to assess the extent of organizational and structural change required to enable its use 
in this way. 
The theoretical analysis confirmed the importance of BIM to the engineering and also 
confirmed that BIM is not only a modeling and visualization tool, it is also a tool that can be 
effectively used to integrate communications and management across disciplines and 
stakeholders in construction projects. In fact modern BIM solutions are increasingly evolving 
in this direction. The theoretical analysis also identified that organizational and structural 
changes would be required to support this use of BIM and also suggested conceptual operating 
model. The comparative analysis was then used to analyze the practical situation of COWI 
compared to the theoretical perspectives. The comparative analysis confirmed the hypothesis 
mentioned earlier and was also used to develop a list of improvement suggestions to support 
COWI in implementing a strategy to extend BIM usage in COWI to realize value from using BIM 
as an integrative project management platform with a focus on the organizational and 
structural changes.   
The topic and the result of this research report is highly relevant for leading engineering 
companies. Despite that the comparative analyses and associated improvement suggestions 
has been based upon the use of BIM as an integrative project management tool, most of the 
suggested improvements and actions can be used across disciplines and industries and would 
also be relevant for other digital tools, where the adoption is facing similar challenges and 
barriers.

If engineering companies want to remain competitive, they must understand their vulnerability 
to digital disruption and they must realize that structural changes are necessary to embrace 
disruption and not be victimized by it.  
Companies such as COWI need to understand the real importance and potential value they can 
derive from using new digital tools such as BIM. They should understand that one of their main 
assets is the collective experience in the minds of their project managers, so the project 
manager should be central in any effort to extend the use of BIM as an integration platform for 
project management across project stakeholders and disciplines.

7 Conclusion  
 7 
Digital disruption is a challenge for all industries. The engineering industry has been disrupted 
with the introduction of digital technologies and solutions. BIM is one such technology which 
affects all the economic demand and supply levers normally associated with digital disruption. 
It is therefore a very disruptive technology which would indicate that structural and 
organizational change would be required to effectively enable its use. 
With the introduction of ICT tools such as BIM, the multidisciplinary communication and 
information sharing mechanisms become even more critical as new and previously unknown 
processes and procedures are being introduced to the engineering industry. Effectively 
managing these communication and information sharing channels is a critical part of the 
project manager role. It is therefore important to understand the multidisciplinary 
communication and information sharing mechanisms and to ensure that these mechanisms are 
used effectively when new ICT tools (e.g. BIM tools) are being introduced. 
Most companies and organizations such as COWI already have a strategy in place to respond 
and capitalize on the potential of digitalization, but they are struggling to put their strategy to 
use. Many have yet to move forward to achieve the outcomes they believe digitalization can 
deliver. For them to achieve their organization’ goals they need to develop the right digital 
operating models, that not only focuses on the strategy, but also on the required structural 
changes, the execution and the ongoing monitoring and adaption 
The hypothesis of this research report was that the current use of BIM in engineering 
companies is primarily focused within each vertical discipline in engineering projects. Through 
interviews and meetings, it was found that this is to a large extent true for COWI. Although the 
need for horizontal management through the usage of BIM is clearly defined in COWI's strategy, 
COWI is struggling with realizing the strategy and achieving horizontal project management 
integration through the use of BIM. The recently implemented BIM functions at COWI are 
mostly focused within each vertical discipline, and although they are perceived as a current 
necessity due to the lack of BIM knowledge at the project manager level, these roles actually 
cause fragmentation issues for COWI and represent a barrier to a more integrated approach 
driven by the project managers.  
The recommendation is to change project organization in this area, and equip the project 
managers through training and an up-skilling programs with the skills and support that enables 
them to take a much more central role in extending the usage of BIM as an integrative project 
management platform. The recommendation is also to implement KPI's to track progress and 
to motivate the project managers (and other key BIM roles) by defining individual performance 
objectives and incentives aligned to these BIM objectives.  Finally it is recommended to 
establish a specific business case for BIM and to assess other process and technology related 
BIM initiatives to extend BIM usage at COWI.

Appendix B: Summary of Digital operating models 
Option 1. Decentralized Model 
  
Pros: 
 
• Digital strategy, resources and assets remain very close to the individual business unit’s or function’s needs  
 
Cons: 
 
• Companies have little opportunity to reuse or scale-up digital assets or content  
 
• Development cycles are long with little flexibility  
 
• IT costs can explode with growth  
 
Option 2. Shared Services Model 
  
Pros: 
 
• Digital strategy remains close to the business unit’s needs  
 
• Helps develop a scaled pool of skilled resources to quickly and flexibly ramp-up digital technology needs across different 
business units  
 
• Provides a degree of cost efficiency  
 
Cons: 
 
• Accountability between business and  
 
IT is split, and success is dependent on the effective collaboration between the two organizations 
==PAGE:92==
9 Appendix 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
 
Page | 81 
 
 
Option 3. Center of Excellence 
 
(CoE) Model 
  
Pros: 
 
• Central access to digital skills and delivery help improve IT and business alignment  
• Improved consistency by adopting best practices across business units  
 
Cons: 
 
• Dual reporting may limit business unit control  
 
• Requires heavy governance  
 
Option 4. Centralized Model 
  
Pros: 
 
• Drives business agility, growth, cost reductions and innovation  
 
• Provides a dedicated focus of resources and mandate to execute digital across the organization  
 
Cons: 
 
• Smaller business units’ priorities may not get the dedicated focus and attention of larger business units
