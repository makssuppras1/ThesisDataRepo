Abstract
This thesis investigates the application and evaluation of dimensionality reduction techВӯ
niques, with a particular emphasis on UMAP, to address the challenges of analysing and
evaluating dimensionality reduction visualizations. A comparative study of different methВӯ
ods was conducted for the recognition of handwritten digits and facialВӯbased images to
assess each techniqueвҖҷs ability to preserve critical structures and patterns.
The UMAP hyperparameter analysis was conducted to evaluate its impact on both, inter
and intraВӯcluster relationships, examining the influence of n_neighbours and min_dist on
projection variability and cluster compactness. It involved computing qualitative and quanВӯ
titative comparisons, including evaluations of cluster separation, compactness, stability,
and density. Showing the difficulties of assessing how well a dimensionality reduction
technique is working, and how sensitive the technique is to certain hyperparameters. FurВӯ
thermore, it highlighted the interplay between local and global structures and the tradeВӯoffs
between prioritizing local relationships versus capturing broader global structures. ProВӯ
viding insights into the parametrization of the algorithms for different data characteristics.
Moreover, this research studied whether it is beneficial to create a pipeline combining mulВӯ
tiple dimensionality reduction techniques with different characteristics, or if it is better to
use a single technique. In the domain of imageВӯbased facial emotion recognition, this reВӯ
search explored the impact of preprocessing techniques, including LLE, PCA and Gabor
filters, on the quality of the lower dimension space. These methods showed their ability
to enhance clustering metrics and refine data representation, particularly when combined
with supervised learning. Additionally, an exploratory analysis investigated UMAPвҖҷs caВӯ
pacity to identify image sources in merged datasets, illustrating its potential for domain
identification and transfer learning applications.
The objectives of the study included enhancing visualization techniques through hierarВӯ
chical architectures and analysing the role of global parameters. The findings serve as
a foundation for understanding UMAPвҖҷs behaviour and capabilities across various apВӯ
plications, providing insights and orientations for researchers to optimize the algorithm
for improved stability, interpretability, and effectiveness in diverse highВӯdimensional data
scenarios.

Chapter 1
Introduction
TodayвҖҷs biomedical data, marked by its unparalleled scale, diversity, and intricacy, includВӯ
ing sources like genomic sequences, medical imaging, and realВӯtime wearable device
data, is crucial in numerous fields, such as personalized medicine, disease research and
emotion recognition. However, the analysis and evaluation of such heterogeneous, highВӯ
dimensional datasets present significant challenges. One of the most effective ways to
tackle them is through data visualization in lower dimensions, which enables researchers
to explore and interpret the underlying structures within the data. Commonly used techВӯ
niques, such as tВӯDistributed Stochastic Neighbour Embedding (tВӯSNE) and Uniform ManВӯ
ifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP), are widely employed to reveal clusterВӯlike
structures, feature extraction, and visual analysis. Despite their utility, these methods
are often sensitive to global parameters, where small adjustments can lead to different
outcomes.
For these reasons, the interplay between local and global structures required extensive
experimentation and careful analysis to understand how each hyperparameter influences
and changes dimensional visualization. In this project, these topics are explored and
experimentally analysed, providing new insights and a comprehensive understanding.
Objectives Вӯ Research Questions
вҖў Enhance visualization techniques by applying a hierarchical architecture.
The investigation related to this goal consisted on a robust research of frameworks
composed by a preВӯphase module and the transformation of high dimensional data,
ensuring the preservation of critical data structure and patterns.
вҖў Study and analysis of certain algorithmвҖҷs principal parameters.
It was analysed the impact of tuning specific parameters using parametrized and
heuristic methods, to enhance interpretability of the visualizations.
вҖў Evaluate supervised and unsupervised UMAP performance in clustering and
visualization quality.
This goal refers to exploring the role of label guidance in UMAP in improving the
reliability of clustering metrics and visualizations, with a focus on applications in
imageВӯbased emotion recognition and domain identification.

These objectives can be also expressed more globally as the following research quesВӯ
tions.
вҖў How can data preparation techniques like Principal Components Analysis (PCA),
Isometric mapping (Isomap), Locally Linear Embedding (LLE) and Multidimensional
Scaling (MDS) improve the effectiveness of UMAP in preserving the critical strucВӯ
tures and patterns within highВӯdimensional data?
вҖў How does the incorporation of supervised and unsupervised UMAP learning imВӯ
pact clustering performance and low dimensions reliability, and what preprocessing
strategies can further enhance its effectiveness across datasets, such as emotion
recognition and domain identification?
To address the previous points, this thesis explores a comprehensive analysis of dimenВӯ
sionality reduction techniques, with a particular focus on UMAP and its behaviour under
various parameter configurations. By conducting experiments on benchmark datasets
such as MNIST for general dimensionality reduction evaluation and RAFВӯDB and FER2013
for imageВӯbased emotion recognition, the study aims to uncover the strengths, limitations,
and potential enhancements of UMAP. A special emphasis is placed on understanding the
tradeВӯoff between preserving local versus global structures, and the role of preprocessing
steps to enhance data representation and clustering performance.
Furthermore, towards the end, the study delves into a comparison between results obВӯ
tained in supervised and unsupervised environments. By leveraging label information, the
supervised UMAP technique aims to refine visualizations and improve clustering metrics,
particularly in datasets where classВӯspecific relationships are critical. The exploratory
evaluation of preprocessing techniques, adds another layer to the investigation, showВӯ
casing how these steps can amplify UMAPвҖҷs capabilities, especially for applications in
emotion recognition and domain identification.
Structure of the thesis
The rest of this report is structured to provide a comprehensive flow over examination of
the methodologies, experiments, and findings.
Chapter 2 outlines foundational concepts and techniques, including mathematical definiВӯ
tions and notations for the metrics and dimensionality reduction methods used. EstablishВӯ

2.1.1
Supervised learning
Supervised learning involves training models on labelled datasets, where each input has a
corresponding output. Through this, models learn to predict outputs for new, unseen data.
Common algorithms include regression and classification techniques, such as Support
Vector Machines (SVM), Decision Trees, and NN.
The SVM algorithm is popular in supervised learning, especially useful for classification
tasks and can be understand as a regularization approach.
It aims to find the bestВӯ
separating hyperplane that maximizes the data division into distinct classes, making it
an excellent technique for binary classification. According to [5] the key concept behind
this method is the margin maximization principle. The margin refers to the distance beВӯ
tween the separating hyperplane and the nearest data points from each class, known
as support vectors. By maximizing this margin, SVM focuses on improving the modelвҖҷs
ability to generalize to unseen data, helping in preventing over fitting. SVMs can be comВӯ
putationally intensive, especially for large datasets, as its complexity increases with the
number of support vectors.
On the other hand, neural networks are inspired by the structure and function of the human
brain and are a powerful tool for supervised learning, particularly in complex pattern recogВӯ
nition tasks [8]. A neural network is made of interconnected layers of nodes, neurons, that
transform input data into outputs. Each neuron receives inputs, applies a weight, sums
the result, and passes it through a nonВӯlinear activation function, allowing the network to
model nonВӯlinear relationships.
In the case of supervised learning, the output space Y may be continuous or discrete.
When Y is continuous, the supervised problem is referred to as a regression problem.
Conversely, when Y is discrete, the problem is referred to as a classification problem,
where the task is to assign each input to one of a finite number of categories, called

The architecture of NN generally includes: input layer (one), hidden layers (one or more)

2.2
Dimensionality Reduction
In todayвҖҷs world, most of the information and data collection is organized and stored toВӯ
gether for analysis or processing, containing multiple variables that describe each detail.
In fields like biomedical research, for instance, data can include numerous features, such
as gene expressions, biomarkers, and imaging metrics. While this abundance of informaВӯ
tion brings the possibility for depth analysis, it also introduces challenges likeНҫ difficulty in
processing, visualizing, and interpreting. Furthermore, it can suffer from issues like noise
and redundancy, as not all variables contribute in the same way to the analysis. This probВӯ
lem, known as the вҖңCurse of DimensionalityвҖқ [13], highlights the difficulty of finding hidden
structure when the quantity of variables is large. As the number of dimensions increases,
data points become sparse, and the computational cost of analysis rises significantly.
In order to address these challenges, Dimensionality Reduction (DR) methods transform
high dimensional data into a more manageable, lower dimensional space while preservВӯ
ing essential patterns and structures [14, 15]. By simplifying data in this way, it is possible
to identify significant trends, improve computational efficiency, and reduce noise. AddiВӯ
tionally, regarding visualization, dimensionality reduction facilitates the representation of
complex data in two or three dimensions, allowing clearer understanding of underlying
relationships. For these reasons, DR is an essential pre processing step that enhances
the overall effectiveness of ML models and data analysis workflows [5].
Formally, a DR method is a mapping g : X вҶ’Z such that the dimensionality of Z that
is denoted as m is lower than the dimensionality of X (p). Note that, a DR method also
can be applied in supervised contexts. In this case, the dimension m can be equal to the
dimension of the output space d, or larger than d (which corresponds to the most common
scenarios).
Dimensionality reduction and clustering are closely connected as they address compleВӯ
mentary aspects of highВӯdimensional data. By applying dimensionality reduction before
clustering, the inherent structures of highВӯdimensional datasets become more apparent,
facilitating the identification of clusters. For instance, DR techniques like PCA, enabling
clustering methods to operate more effectively in a reduced space [16].
Conversely, clustering methods can guide DR by uncovering latent structures that inform
the selection or transformation of features. This bidirectional relationship is particularly
useful in exploratory data analysis, where the interplay between these two techniques
provides deeper insights into data organization [17]. For example, clustering results might

2.5
Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection method
2.5.1
Introduction
Dimensionality reduction techniques are increasingly being applied across various fields.
Thus, it is desirable to find algorithms that are scalable to massive datasets and capable
of handling diverse data types. Generally, DR methods fall into two categories: those
aiming to preserve the pairwise distance structure among all data points (e.g., PCA, MDS)
and those prioritizing local distances over global distance (such as tВӯSNE and Isomap
algorithms). Techniques falling into the former category preserve overall geometry of the
data in the reduced space by maintaining the similarities between all data points as much
as possible. On the other hand, those who fall into the latter one focus on preserving the
neighbourhood structure of the data, meaning the relative arrangement of points within
local clusters or groups [39].
As it is explained in [39], the development of UMAP was focused on the challenge of
uniform data distributions on manifolds by combining principles of Riemannian geometry
with methods inspired by categoryВӯtheoretic techniques for the geometric realization of
fuzzy simplicial sets. While tВӯSNE is currently considered stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart for dimensionalВӯ
ity reduction in visualization, UMAP offers similar visualization quality, arguably preserving
more of the global structure while delivering superior runtime performance. Additionally,
it scales effectively to much larger datasets compared to current methods. Moreover, it
imposes no computational restrictions on the embedding dimension, making it a versatile
and practical tool for general purpose dimensionality reduction in machine learning.
As mentioned, UMAP aims to preserve the global and local structure of high dimensional
datasets [39]. Over the last years, it has gained significant popularity thanks to its ability
to produce visualizations easy to interpret and the potential to retain the global structure
of data while revealing hidden patterns in lower dimensions.
In this section the algorithm is introduced, providing also a brief description of its mathВӯ
ematical theory, main parameters and computational aspects. Furthermore, its principal
advantages are described, and is presented a discussion regarding its limitations.

UMAP is capable of operating in both supervised and unsupervised contexts, which highВӯ

On one hand, with the unsupervised mode the algorithm builds a graph representation

the embedding. In supervised learning:
вҖў Label proximity is used to adjust the weights of the highВӯdimensional graph, ensuring
that points with similar labels are drawn closer together in the embedding space.
вҖў Enabling UMAP to create projections that align with the underlying labels, making it
suitable for tasks such as classification and data stratification.

2.5.7
Critical view: limitations and challenges of UMAP
While UMAP is a versatile and powerful tool for DR, it is not without its limitations. Below,
scenarios where UMAP may face difficulties or is not fully recommended are described.
вҖў Tasks requiring precise pairwise distance preservation. UMAP focuses on preВӯ
serving the local and global structure of the data manifold rather than exact pairwise
distances. This makes it unsuitable for tasks such as metric learning, where precise
distances between all points are critical. Unlike methods like MDS or PCA, which exВӯ
plicitly aim to preserve distances, UMAP relies on the graph structure and optimizaВӯ
tion for embedding, which can distort pairwise distances on the side of maintaining
neighbourhood relations [39].
вҖў Data distribution. UMAP can be less effective for datasets with linear or GaussianВӯ
like distributions [43]. In these cases, techniques such as PCA are computationally
simpler and more interpretable.
вҖў UMAP sensitivity to noise and outliers. They can disrupt the construction of the
neighbourhood graph. Since this technique relies on the local manifold structure,
anomalous points can influence on the graph weights and distort the embedding.
Preprocessing steps such as de noising a filtering are often required to ensure reliВӯ
able results. If noisy data is not handle properly, it can lead to fragmented embedВӯ
dings or misleading cluster formations [39].
вҖў Sparse datasets may lead to scatter representation. Sparse datasets, such as
documentВӯterm matrices in Natural Language Processing (NLP) or gene expression
data in bioinformatics, pose challenges for UMAP. A dataset is considered sparse
when most of its elements are missing or zero, leading to a highly scattered data
representation. UMAP could not perform well in these cases unless an appropriate
distance metric (e.g., cosine or Jaccard) is chosen [39].
вҖў UMAPвҖҷs sensitivity to parameter choices. The quality of the embeddings can
vary significantly depending on the selection of parameters such as n_neighbours
and min_dist. This sensitivity can lead to different visualizations and interpretations
of the same dataset, making it challenging to ensure consistency and reproducibility
in results [44].
Finally, from the most notable studies that utilize UMAP for singleВӯcell RNA sequencВӯ
ing (scRNAВӯseq) data analysis, is worth mentioning the application of the algorithm for
visualizing high dimensional singleВӯcell data, preserving both local and global data strucВӯ
tures [45]. Additionally, in [46] the authors introduce joint embeddings that better repreВӯ
sent the complex relationships in multimodal datasets, with tВӯSNE and UMAP, enhancing
the analysis of scRNAВӯseq data combined with other modalities.

As previously commented, UMAP is designed mainly for DR and visualization, preserving

they are in the original highВӯdimensional space. Both evaluations are crucial for underВӯ
standing how well UMAP performs at preserving the intrinsic structures of the data, and
they help guide decisions on parameter settings for optimal visualization [55].

Minimum Spanning Tree analysis
To investigate the structure of the data further and understand how different hyperparamВӯ
eters influence the local and global relationships in UMAP lowВӯdimensional visualizations,
the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) technique was applied to the distance matrices comВӯ
puted from the cluster centroids.
This analysis helped to visualize and quantify the relationships between clusters by proВӯ
viding an optimal tree structure that connects all the centroids with the minimum possible
edge weights, which correspond to the distances between the centroids.
A fundamental concept in graph theory is the MST. Given a connected, undirected graph
G = (V, E), where V represents the vertices (or nodes) and E represents the edges (or
connections) between the vertices, a MST is a sub graph that connects all the vertices
with the minimum possible total edge weight, without forming any cycles. All nodes are
connected in the graph while minimizing the total weight of the edges used in the connecВӯ
tion [56]. For graphs where there are not repeated weights in the edges, then the MST is
unique.
In a nutshell, a MST of a weighted graph G is a tree that contains:
вҖў All vertices of the graph.
вҖў The minimum possible sum of edge weights to connect all vertices.
вҖў No cycles.
The procedure for computing the MST was as follows. For a specific set of hyperameters,
T random trials of the same experiment were computed, producing the distance matrix
described in the above section 3.3.1. Therefore, per each configuration of hyperparametrВӯ
ers, T distance matrices were obtained. Then, the average matrix among those matrices
was computed (that represents a graph), and for it a MST was calculated. Hence, resulted
in one tree per each evaluated hyperparameter configuration that was computed among
the results of T experiments.
A visualization of the MST facilitates quickly assessing how the connection is and how
relative distances affect the overall structure.

The process involved calculating the centroids of the clusters for each run and assessing

the runs. These, represent the spread of the centroid positions and are important for
understanding the variability of the cluster locations across runs:

вҖў Вөk= (Вөk, Вөk) mean coordinates of centroid over all trials вҲҖk вҲҲ[0, 9].

Consecutively, the results of all centroid coordinates in a given trial were evaluated within
the acceptable interval. Cases where all clusters satisfied this condition, were considered

Adaptable radius
Another component of the intraВӯcluster evaluation was to compute the density and comВӯ
pactness of clusters in the lowВӯdimensional space, for this reason an adaptable radius
was estimated for each cluster.
Note that the radius was not defined as the maximal distance from the centroid to any
point in the cluster. Instead, it was determined based on the mean intraВӯcluster distances
across multiple UMAP runs.
The idea of using an adaptable radius is to account for varying cluster sizes across difВӯ
ferent runs, making the analysis less dependent on fixed value of it. By calculating the
number of points within this radius for each cluster, can be better understood how their
distribution works and the quality of clustering assessed.
The adaptable radius for each cluster was defined as follows.

It was computed as the average of the mean distances between cluster points and their
respective cluster centroids across all UMAP runs.
This method ensures that the radius reflects the actual spatial distribution of points within
the cluster, adapting to the intrinsic cluster geometry.
The calculation begins by computing the centroids of the clusters as the mean of all points
belonging to a specific cluster for each run. For each cluster, the Euclidean distances
between the cluster points and their centroid were calculated.

the radii was computed for each cluster, providing a stable and representative adaptable
radius.

the clusterвҖҷs intrinsic size and shape, the approach avoids arbitrary or fixed thresholds,
ensuring a more dataВӯdriven evaluation.

вҖў The adaptable radius is sensitive to the variations in cluster size and density across
different runs, capturing the diversity of cluster geometries.
вҖў By averaging radii across runs, the method reduces the impact of anomalies or noise
in individual runs, ensuring more consistent and reliable measurements.
вҖў The methodology emphasizes intraВӯcluster properties, reflecting the compactness
and density of clusters without being influenced by interВӯcluster separations.
This calculus represents a significant improvement over fixedВӯthreshold methods, enВӯ

DR techniques. The primary goal was to assess the effectiveness of various preprocessВӯ
ing and embedding methods, both in supervised and unsupervised learning settings. All
experiments were performed with UMAP as the primary DR method, using 10 iterations
to account for randomness, with min_dist = 0.1 and n_neighbours = [10, 50, 100].
UMAP
Initially, UMAP was applied directly to the datasets, using emotion labels in the supervised
setting and excluding them in the unsupervised case. This provided a baseline to evaluate
the visualizations. UMAP excels at preserving local relationships in highВӯdimensional
data, making it suitable for visualizing emotion clusters in twoВӯdimensional space.
Despite UMAPвҖҷs ability to form clusters, the initial results indicated room for improvement
in their separation. This motivated further investigation into preprocessing methods to
enhance the quality of the results.
PCA and UMAP
PCA was incorporated prior to UMAP as a preprocessing step. By transforming the 2304
dimensional pixel space into fewer components (retaining 95% variance), PCA provided a
more manageable and noiseВӯreduced input. This combination improved UMAPвҖҷs cluster
separability, as UMAP benefited from the more compact and representative input provided
by PCA.
GaborвҖҷs filters with PCA and UMAP
To further enhance the quality of the results, the Gabor filter technique was introduced
before PCA and UMAP. This method is widely used in image processing to extract texture
and edge information. Each filter is a linear operator sensitive to specific orientations and
spatial frequencies, making it effective for capturing facial features like the shape of the
mouth, eyebrows, or eyes.
Gabor process involved:
вҖў Creating a set of Gabor kernels with different orientations and scales.
вҖў Applying these kernels to each image, generates textureВӯbased features.
вҖў Flattening the filter responses into feature vectors for each image.
These GaborВӯfiltered features were then passed to PCA and to subsequently UMAP. Its
inclusion led to betterВӯdefined clusters in the final visualizations.
LLE and UMAP
The LLE algorithm was also tested as an alternative to PCA for preprocessing. By foВӯ
cusing on local neighbourhoods, LLE can uncover intricate patterns in the data that PCA
might miss. The LLE embeddings were subsequently used as input to UMAP, providing
an additional perspective on cluster separability.
Supervised UMAP vs. Unsupervised UMAP
For each preprocessing combination, UMAP was run in both, supervised and unsuperВӯ
vised modes. In the first one, the emotion labels were provided during the embedding

process, allowing the algorithm to use the labels to influence the embedding structure.
This resulted in tighter and more distinct clusters, as observed in the visualizations. In
contrast, the latter one relied solely on the intrinsic structure of the data, resulting in visuВӯ
alizations where cluster separability was more influenced by local similarities rather than
global class distinctions.
While unsupervised UMAP is valuable for discovering latent structures, the supervised
approach consistently provided a clearer separation of emotion categories, as it leveraged
the label information to guide the embedding process.
Merged datasets
Finally, the RAFВӯDB and FER2013 datasets were merged, and an additional identification
column was introduced, indicating the source dataset from each image. This new dataset

showed that UMAP, particularly in its supervised configuration, could effectively separate
the datasets, highlighting its utility for domain identification and transfer learning tasks.

Conclusions
Throughout the thesis, advanced methods and parameters for the visualization and analВӯ
ysis of high dimensional data were explored, with a focus on dimensionality reduction and
clustering techniques.
Evaluating the results and tuning hyperparameters in UMAP presented significant chalВӯ
lenges. The interplay between local and global structures, influenced by hyperparameВӯ
ters such as n_neighbours and min_dist, required extensive experimentation and careful
analysis, as no single configuration consistently yielded optimal results. To address this,
this study was conducted to try to understand how UMAP responds to different hyperВӯ
parameter configurations. This approach not only revealed the nuanced effects of them
on visualization outcomes but also underscored the need for dataset specific tuning in
highВӯdimensional data analysis.
Based on the above, the objectives outlined at the outset guided the research to address
three critical challenges: examining robust preВӯprocessing frameworks for the UMAP alВӯ
gorithm, understanding and tuning UMAP hyperparameters for interpretability, and inteВӯ
grating supervised and unsupervised learning in biomedical data studies.
A hierarchical architecture was applied to ensure the preservation of critical data structure
and patterns in highВӯdimensional biomedical data.
Building on this foundation, facial images have emerged as a valuable tool in medical reВӯ
search and healthcare applications, particularly in diagnosing health conditions and recВӯ
ognizing emotions. For these reasons, the analysis explored the application of UMAP for
emotion recognition using facial image datasets.
The experimental study was conducted on three datasets from two distinct domains to
evaluate the performance and applicability of DR techniques. Firstly, the MNIST dataset,
which consists of handwritten digits was used to test and compare the strengths and
weaknesses of various DR methods. This served as a basis for a more inВӯdepth exВӯ
ploration of the UMAP algorithm, focusing on the impact of two specific hyperparameters:
n_neighbours and min_dist. Secondly, facialВӯbased images for emotion recognition were
analysed using the RAFВӯDB and FER2013 datasets. This evaluation aimed to assess
UMAPвҖҷs ability to handle biomedical image data, explore the effects of preprocessing
steps, and compare supervised and unsupervised approaches for dataset identification.
While several DR techniques were tested on the MNIST dataset ( for hand written digits
recognition) PCA, LLE and were chosen as preВӯprocessing steps for facialВӯbased emotion

features while reducing computational complexity before UMAP was applied.

essential information while minimizing computational complexity before to applying UMAP.

Throughout this work, a systematic exploration of parameter optimization was undertaken,

On the one hand, for lower values of these hyperparameters, visualizations exhibited
greater variability across runs, as observed from the standard deviation of the projecВӯ
tions.
Reflecting UMAPвҖҷs sensitivity to randomness when prioritizing local structures.
Conversely, higher values produced more consistent results with reduced standard deviВӯ
ations of cluster centroids, smoothing out local fluctuations and emphasizing global strucВӯ
ture. Albeit, the average number of points within a cluster radius did not directly correlate
with estimated radii size, revealing a subtle relationship between point density and spread.
On the other hand, the MST displayed that moderate min_dist values decrease the proВӯ
jections variability, which is crucial for stabilizing the projections and reducing sensitivity
to randomness during UMAP optimization. However, the global and local analyses on
MNIST revealed that, for the case of the study, no single hyperparameter configuration
can consistently outperform others, highlighting the challenge of tuning global parameters
in complex datasets.
Extending the analysis also to SammonвҖҷs stress, where UMAP and MDS were compared.
Stress values decreased as n_neighbours increased, indicating that larger values of this
hyperparameter effectively preserved global structures, leading to visualizations that betВӯ
ter approximated original distances.
The methods and analyses conducted in this thesis have practical implications for the
visualization and interpretation of highВӯdimensional data. Particularly, in research based
on images. By consistently comparing DR techniques and addressing the challenges of
parameter tuning, this work hopes to provide a roadmap for researchers grappling with
similar field datasets. The integration of preВӯprocessing techniques, along with the evaluВӯ
ation of stability across runs, highlights the importance of methodological rigour in develВӯ
oping reliable visualization frameworks. Even though, the values of the metrics obtained
were low, the integration of Gabor Filters, PCA, and UMAP significantly improved results,
particularly for supervised learning in emotion recognition tasks. When applied to facial
image datasets, Gabor filters were able to enhance feature extraction by capturing texВӯ
ture, edge details, and facial expression patterns. In contrast, running only UMAP often
produced overlapping or ambiguous clusters with poor separation among emotion cateВӯ
gories, while the addition of Gabor Filters outperformed other combinations by enabling
the identification of more distinct and meaningful patterns. However, there is room for imВӯ
