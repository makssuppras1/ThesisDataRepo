Abstract
In recent years, Hyperspectral Imaging (HSI) technology has demonstrated significant im­
portance in fields such as remote sensing, environmental monitoring, agriculture, medical
imaging, and military reconnaissance. HSI offers richer spectral information compared
to traditional Red­Green­Blue (RGB) imaging, playing a crucial role in numerous appli­
cations. However, the collection of HSI data is challenging, facing issues such as high
equipment costs and complex acquisition processes. To address these challenges, this
study explores the reconstruction of HSI from readily available RGB data, aiming to re­
duce both time and cost.
This research utilizes deep learning methods, specifically Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs), Residual Neural Networks (ResNets), Unet networks, and various Generative
Adversarial Networks (GANs), for the reconstruction of HSI. Particularly, the study focuses
on the use of Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) to tackle this problem
and attempts to employ novel methods to solve the issue of exposure invariance.
A notable innovation of this study is the reliance on color information and the image itself
for HSI reconstruction, diverging from traditional methods that heavily depend on spatial
information. Additionally, the study includes a comprehensive literature review covering
the development of HSI, methods for hyperspectral reconstruction from RGB data, and
the latest advancements in deep learning within this domain.
In terms of the dataset, this research utilizes data from the NTIRE Workshop and Chal­
lenges 2020, specifically selecting the dataset from the ”clean” track. This dataset in­
cludes 450 randomly selected RGB images, each corresponding to a hyperspectral image
of the same size.
In summary, this study proposes a deep learning­based method for HSI reconstruction,
particularly focusing on cGANs, demonstrating potential in addressing the challenges of
HSI reconstruction and offering insights into solving the problem of exposure invariance.

1
Introduction
1.1
Problem Description
In recent years, Hyperspectral Imaging (HSI) has become a vital tool in fields such as
remote sensing, environmental monitoring, agriculture, medical imaging, and military re­
connaissance[1][2][3]. HSI technology provides richer spectral information than traditional
Red­Green­Blue (RGB) images by capturing a wide range of wavelengths, from near ul­
traviolet to far infrared. This spectral information is critical for accurately identifying and
classifying substances and plays a pivotal role in many applications[4].
However, collecting HSI data poses significant challenges. Typically, a two­dimensional
sensor is required to collect three­dimensional signals, adversely impacting the quality of
data collection and making it difficult to simultaneously meet spectral and spatial require­
ments. Additionally, the high cost of HSI equipment significantly increases the financial
burden. To address these issues, researchers have started exploring the reconstruction
of HSI from readily available RGB data, aiming to reduce time and cost.
With the advancement of deep learning in image processing, researchers have begun ex­
ploring its application in HSI reconstruction. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have
been particularly effective in improving spatial resolution[5]. Though this marks a founda­
tion for further research, reconstructing HSI from RGB is fundamentally an ill­posed prob­
lem. Existing techniques still struggle with complex issues such as exposure invariance,
where consistent reconstruction effects under varying exposure conditions and accurate
color reproduction cannot be guaranteed.
Our research attempts include:
• Exploring existing solutions such as CNNs, Residual Neural Networks (ResNets),
Unet networks, and various Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs).
• Utilizing Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) as a core approach
to address HSI reconstruction.
• Investigating new methods to resolve the problem of exposure invariance.
Notably, in the NTIRE2020 competition, the more effective solutions significantly relied
on spatial information. In contrast, our exploration focuses on color information and the
image itself, which forms the crux of our innovative approach.
1.2
Related Work
1.2.1
Hyperspectral Imaging (HSI)
Hyperspectral imaging techniques, developed since the mid­20th century, have under­
gone several stages of evolution.
Early HSI heavily depended on spectrometers to measure light intensity at various wave­
lengths, yet they struggled with obtaining direct spatial resolution. This limitation meant
that detailed information about the location of objects was often missing. NASA’s AVIRIS
system addressed this issue using ”whisk broom” technology[6], utilizing optical fibers and
mirrors to collect electromagnetic signals on a pixel­by­pixel basis.
As technology progressed, the ”push broom” technique was introduced[7]. It involved us­
ing dispersive optics and photosensitive sensors. Laboratories also began employing full

2D acquisition, capturing target images through interchangeable filters, sacrificing tem­
poral resolution but providing more comprehensive spatial information.
Computational methods also played a significant role during this period. For instance,
Computed Tomography Imaging Spectrometers (CTIS) used special diffraction gratings
to project 3D hyperspectral data cubes onto 2D sensors[8], necessitating complex pro­
cessing for hyperspectral reconstruction but representing an important technological ad­
vancement.
Today, real­time acquisitions are possible, such as ”Hyperspectral fovea” systems com­
bining high­resolution RGB with central hyperspectral images[9]. There is also increasing
interest in reconstructing HSI from RGB images.
1.2.2
Spectral Reconstruction (SR)
The reconstruction of hyperspectral images from RGB data has gained popularity recently,
with significant explorations in this field. Antonio Robles­Kelly suggested solving this prob­
lem using sparse coding[10]. Boaz Arad and Ohad Ben­Shahar extended this approach
by integrating data sparsity with spatial and spectral properties[11]. However, these were
relatively primitive hyperspectral reconstruction methods based on a priori information and
often lacked generality.
As deep learning gained prominence, researchers like Zhiwei Xiong leveraged it for in­
formation extraction and reconstruction using CNNs, leading to the HSCNN model[5].
Building on this, Shizhan removed manual up­sampling and utilized residual blocks and
density blocks, proposing improved models like HSCNN­R and HSCNN­D[12]. These
methods made strides in effectiveness but still relied on point­to­point mappings, not fully
capturing the relationships within the image.
In the realm of deep learning, the attention mechanism has become increasingly signifi­
cant. Yuanhao Cai, utilizing this mechanism, proposed the MST and its advanced version
MST++, based on the Transformer architecture. These models achieved first place in the
NTIRE 2022 competition[13].
1.3
Dataset Description
Our research utilizes data from the NTIRE Workshop and Challenges 2020[14], featuring
a challenge to reconstruct HSI from RGB images. The challenge was divided into ”clean”
and ”real world” tracks, with two different datasets provided for these tracks. We chose
the dataset from the clean track for our study.
This dataset, collected outdoors in Israel using a Specimen IQ mobile hyperspectral cam­
era (similar in dimensions to a standard DSLR camera), includes RGB images derived
from real hyperspectral data. The process involved applying known response functions to
hyperspectral data stored as MAT files, transforming them into RGGB mosaic images, and
simulating a digital camera’s photoreceptor. Noise was added to these mosaic images
to approximate actual camera captures, followed by de­mosaicing to produce complete
RGB images.
The dataset contains 450 randomly selected RGB images in ”.PNG” format, each with
a resolution of 512*482 pixels, 3 channels, and pixel values ranging from 0­255 (Unit8
format). Corresponding to each RGB image is a hyperspectral image of the same size
in ”MAT” format, with data as floating­point numbers between 0 and 1 (float64 numbers).
Each hyperspectral image comprises 31 channels, covering wavelengths from 400nm to
700nm.

2.1
Image Theory
During our introduction in Chapter 1, we mentioned that the RGB images in our dataset
were reconstructed from known HS images by a specific method, where providing ac­
curate spectral response parameters was crucial. The acquisition of RGB images in our
dataset mimics the process of capturing RGB images with a standard visible light camera.
In the field of Remote Sensing, meaningful hyperspectral data typically includes the Near­
Infrared (NIR) bands. Some commonly used bands are documented in table 2.1[15].
Conversely, RGB imagery requires only the visible component of the spectrum, which is
the 450 nm ­ 680 nm band as recorded in table 2.1. Our hyperspectral data also spans
the 400­700 nm range, slightly beyond the visible spectrum, but this does not affect our
reconstruction efforts.
For the RGB data, it contains information only about the bands corresponding to the three
colors red, green, and blue, stored in separate channels (dimensions)[16]. The data from
these channels are superimposed by the model to form colors recognizable by the human
eye.
Typically, in a standard light camera, the three RGB bands are arranged in a Bayer array
on the sensor, and each color pixel is covered with a color filter of the corresponding
color[17]. Since the color filter is only effective for visible light, it cannot filter out NIR
light. Therefore, when acquiring the image, the sensor is usually used in conjunction with
an IR filter and a Low Pass Filter, resulting in an image that contains only information
from the visible spectrum. During this process, NIR is often considered as noise and is
artificially removed. It is thus impossible to obtain HS images from RGB images through
image processing, as ordinary visible light cameras cannot capture information beyond
the visible spectrum.
In the field of remote sensing, the method of acquiring HS images differs fundamentally
from that of acquiring RGB images[18]. When capturing an HS image, as light enters the
lens of a device, a photoelectric sensing device converts the light signal into an electrical
signal and sets an intensity threshold. Photons that reach this threshold are quantized
into an electrical pulse signal, thus forming a pixel value. The number of photons entering
the lens depends on two factors: the limit on the number of photons that can pass through
the lens and the size of the device’s receptive field.
For instance, for the same beam of light, a multispectral sensor receives significantly fewer
photons than a panchromatic photoreceptor due to filtering[19][20]. To capture more pho­
tons, it may be necessary to expand the camera’s receptive field. These factors together
determine the quality of remote sensing imagery.
In remote sensing imaging, the device’s more detailed beam splitting allows it to capture
spectral information by bands, storing these bands in channels, thereby creating a hy­
perspectral image. In the image, since each channel represents a band, extracting the
values of a pixel point in different channels allows us to generate a spectral map of that
pixel point.
The most significant difference between RGB and HS images is the number of channels.

2.2
Deep Learning Theory
We have abstracted the reconstruction problem into a solution for relational mapping.
The most popular and efficient tools for such problems are various deep learning models.
These models excel in analyzing relationships between vast amounts of data from a ma­
chine’s perspective, and some even exaggerate by describing them as the best fitters[22].
In our task, we used the following models: HSCNN­D, ResNet, Pix2pix models, and other
customized cGAN models.

In deep learning, the convolutional neural network (CNN) is the most fundamental net­
work. It extracts features from images through convolutional layers, reduces the number
of parameters using pooling layers, and finally outputs the desired results through fully
connected layers[23]. During the training process, it continuously updates the weight pa­
rameters through forward and backward propagation until the network can fit the input
and output well.
Building upon this, Xiong and others developed HSCNN and its variants[5]. In this paper,
we adopted HSCNN­D. Compared to traditional CNNs, HSCNN­D incorporates density
blocks, enhancing the depth and learning capacity of the model. Specifically, HSCNN­D
is a convolutional neural network optimized for hyperspectral image processing[24]. It
introduces the concept of dense connectivity, where each layer receives the output of all
previous layers as additional input, enhancing feature transfer and reducing information
loss. This structure allows the network to learn image details more deeply, especially
excelling in delicate texture and complex pattern recognition of hyperspectral images. The
main goal of HSCNN­D is to precisely reconstruct hyperspectral images by minimizing
reconstruction errors, such as mean squared error (MSE).
Simultaneously, in this project, we also utilized a classic network: ResNet. ResNet intro­
duces a residual learning framework to address the gradient vanishing problem in deep
neural networks[25][26]. It allows signals to bypass some layers directly through skip con­
nections. This structure enables the network to deepen its layers while maintaining lower
training difficulty and good gradient flow. ResNet primarily optimizes network parameters
by minimizing the loss function, such as cross­entropy or mean squared error, thereby
improving the model’s accuracy and generalization ability.
The models mentioned above fall into supervised learning. In recent years, GAN models
and their variants have also achieved excellent results in unsupervised learning.
The GAN model, based on CNN, introduced an adversarial concept[27]. A standard GAN
model consists of two parts: a generator and a discriminator[28]. The generator is re­
sponsible for predicting the target output from noise, aiming to generate outputs that can
deceive the discriminator. In contrast, the discriminator needs to distinguish whether the
data comes from the generator’s prediction or the real value from the dataset. These two
parts will reach a Nash equilibrium through mutual opposition[29].
The GAN model’s target function is usually defined as eq. (2.3), the gradient update infor­
mation of G in GAN does not come from the dataset but from the discriminator D, which
is an important feature of the GAN model.
The disadvantage of the GAN model is also apparent: instability. For GAN models, reach­
ing Nash equilibrium is not an easy goal, leading to often unstable training of GANs.
As generative networks, GANs are commonly used to predict single­category images.
Based on the GAN model, we can add category information to the random noise, enabling
the network to perform multi­categorization tasks. This network, which adds information
constraints to the generator, is known as cGAN[30]. Its target function can be expressed
as eq. (2.4).

From eq. (2.4), it can be seen that cGAN embodies the feature of utilizing prior knowledge
to guide the generator’s direction of generation[31]. In the first chapter, we mentioned that
some people have attempted to use prior information for HSI reconstruction and achieved
meaningful results. cGAN, in concept, aligns with their attempts. This method is worth
trying.
To attempt this method, we first tried the Pix2pix model. Pix2pix is a variant of the cGAN
model, a conditional generative adversarial network model for image­to­image transla­
tion[32]. Its core lies in the adversarial training between the generator and discriminator,
where the generator attempts to produce outputs as close as possible to real images,
while the discriminator strives to distinguish between the generated and actual images.
The Pix2pix model combines GAN loss with L1 loss, ensuring that the generated images
are not only visually realistic but also pixel­accurately consistent with the target images.
Pix2pix typically uses a Unet structure as the generator and a PatchGAN model as the
discriminator.
The Unet network, as the generator in Pix2pix, shares a similar philosophy with the
previously mentioned ResNet, both utilizing skip connections to integrate original infor­
mation[33]. In the Unet network, features are extracted through multiple downsampling
processes and then reconstructed through the same number of upsampling processes.
During upsampling, each expansion of data utilizes skip connections to merge features
extracted during previous downsampling.
PatchGAN, used as the discriminator in Pix2pix, improves the blurriness commonly found
in traditional GAN­generated images[34][32]. By dividing the image into multiple small
patches and feeding them sequentially to the discriminator, PatchGAN enhances the clar­
ity of the images. In this process, PatchGAN can be seen as a loss sensor for image
textures.
The combination of Unet as the generator and PatchGAN as the discriminator makes
Pix2pix highly adept at image translation and style transfer, which matches well with our
reconstruction tasks.
Additionally, based on Pix2pix, we explored the effects of different combinations of cGAN
networks by replacing the generator, such as using CNN or ResNet instead of Unet.

Implementation
3.1
Dataprocess
In the first chapter, we detailed the source and characteristics of the dataset. Before
starting the training, we conducted necessary processing on the data.
Firstly, we divided the data. As mentioned in the first chapter, the dataset provided for the
competition was divided into three parts: the training set contained 450 RGB data and
corresponding HS data, the validation set contained 10 RGB data and corresponding HS
data, and the test set contained only 10 RGB data. The competition rules stipulated that
the HS data in the test set were used to evaluate all participating models, so this part
of the HS data was not disclosed to the participants. Since we did not participate in the
competition and for the ease of visualizing the results later, we did not use the data in the
way expected by the competition. In our actual training, we discarded the test set data
provided by the competition and selected the last five RGB data and their corresponding
HS data from the training set to form a new test set. The RGB images of these five data
are shown in fig. 3.1.
Next, we checked the data. We found no anomalies in the RGB data, but in the HS data in
MAT format, there were 67 pixels with values less than 0. HS data records spectral data,
and in physics, spectral data cannot have negative values, which means the HS data
needed manual adjustment. During our training, we forcibly converted these negative
values to 0 to ensure the accuracy of the data. The final data distribution is like fig. 3.2.
Then, we processed the data itself. As mentioned in the first chapter, the data collection
size was 486 x 512 pixels. To expand the dataset through cropping, we needed to address
this size issue. Therefore, we padded the data, filling the original size from 486 x 512 to
512 x 512 with zero values. After that, we cropped each piece of data into 16 small­sized
data of 128 x 128. Thus, our training set was formally expanded to 7120 pieces of data,
which was more than sufficient for our training.
In the formal training process, we divided the data into three separate datasets: a training
set consisting of 450 RGB images along with their corresponding HS images, a valida­
tion set consisting of 10 RGB images and their corresponding HS images, and a test set
consisting of 5 RGB images and their corresponding HS images. During the training pro­
cess, we used several evaluation metrics to assess the model’s performance, including
PSNR (Peak Signal­to­Noise Ratio), SSIM (Structural Similarity Index), SAM (Spectral
Angle Mapper) measured in degrees, and MSE (Mean Squared Error). These evalua­
tion metrics are represented by the respective formulas in eq. (3.1), eq. (3.2), eq. (3.3),
eq. (3.4).

image. Generally, the PSNR range corresponding to high­quality reconstructions should
fall between 30dB and 50dB. Higher PSNR values are desirable, theoretically approach­
ing infinity. When the PSNR exceeds 30dB, it implies that the human eye finds it difficult
to distinguish differences between the reconstructed image and the ground truth. If the
PSNR ranges from 20dB to 30dB, it means that the human eye can perceive differences
between images, while a PSNR below 20dB indicates that differences between the recon­
structed image and the ground truth are easily discernible. In eq. (3.1), MAXI represents

3.2.3
Pix2pix
Next, we will present our Pix2Pix model. The model consists of two parts: the generator
and the discriminator. In this model, the Unet model serves as our generator, and the
PatchGAN structure is used as our discriminator.
In the generator part, the basic unit of our UNET structure is the Unet Block. Specifically,
each Unet Block contains two parts: an encoder and a decoder. If we consider a com­
bination of a convolutional layer + BN layer + ReLU activation function as a unit, then
the entire encoder part completes the downsampling through two such units, while the
decoder similarly completes the upsampling through two units. The structure is shown in
fig. 3.8.
The final Unet model in the downsampling stage has four layers composed of Unet Blocks,
each followed by a pooling layer. The result of the upsampling is input into the Unet central
module, followed by the same upsampling process to restore the original size. The final
model is shown in fig. 3.9. In this structure, each yellow block signifies a Unet Block as
shown in fig. 3.8 combined with a max pooling layer. Meanwhile, the horizontal arrows
indicate that features obtained during downsampling are merged using skip connections
during the upsampling process. The data size changes are detailed in table 3.6.
In the discriminator part, we used PatchGAN. Since the discriminator in GAN models
should not be too strong, we simplified the PatchGAN model. The model is shown in
fig. 3.10. In this network, a series of convolutional layers (Conv2d) and activation func­
tions (LeakyReLU) progressively extract features. During this process, we set a crucial
parameter called ”ndf”, which represents the number of feature channels. Specifically,
in the first convolutional layer, we map the input features to ’ndf’ feature channels, then
double the number of channels to ’ndf × 2’ in the second layer, and increase to ’ndf × 4’
Exposure Invariant Deep Neural Networks for Hyperspectral Super­resolution from RGB images

==PAGE:22==
in the third layer. Finally, in the last layer, we restrict the channel number to 1, outputting
a feature map of size 1×1. This approach allows the discriminator to judge different local
areas of the input image rather than just globally, enhancing the model’s sensitivity to
image details and local information.
The loss function of the Pix2Pix model consists of two parts: Generative Adversarial Net­
work (GAN) loss and L1 loss. In the PyTorch framework, we use ”BCEWithLogitsLoss” as
the GAN loss function, which combines a Sigmoid layer with binary cross­entropy loss.
This choice is beneficial as it internally applies the Sigmoid function, providing a more
stable version of binary cross­entropy loss when dealing with logits.

For the L1 loss, we use ”L1Loss” in PyTorch, which calculates the average absolute error
between the predicted and actual values. This loss is particularly useful for reducing
blurriness, as it penalizes pixel­level differences between the generated and real images.

The GAN loss is calculated using the discriminator’s predictions for the generated images
and the real label values. This part of the loss encourages the generator to produce
images that are indistinguishable from real images to the discriminator.
The L1 loss is calculated between the generated images and the real images. This result
is then scaled by the coefficient λ to balance the contribution of the L1 loss relative to the
GAN loss.
Finally, the total loss for the generator is the sum of the GAN loss and the weighted L1
loss. This combined loss function guides the generator to produce images that not only
deceive the discriminator but also closely resemble real images in terms of pixels. The

For method 1, we have obtained the color information in the RGB image by extracting the
color histogram of the picture in practice. In particular, since the RGB image has three
channels, we initialize a 256­size histogram for each color channel, and then for each
color channel in the image, we calculate the distribution of values of the pixels by the
”torch.histc” method, and then plan the histograms. The histograms provide a constant
insight into the composition of the colors in the image by capturing the distribution of pixel
values for each color channel. We then normalized the histograms for each channel in
order to make the feature representation more standardized as well as to increase its com­
parability. Finally, we rung merge the three normalized histograms into a 1­dimensional
vector, so that we obtain a 1­dimensional vector of length 3*256, which is the color charac­
teristic of the RGB image. By this method, we hope to capture the global characteristics of
the image color and actively ignore the spatial characteristics of the objects in the picture,
thus making the extraction of color information more efficient.
As for method 2, we will extract the features of the RGB data in the form of convolution
and then merge the features into the model.

HS image reconstruction
As the Baseline model, HSCNND represents the performance of traditional convolutional
neural networks when dealing with the task of HS image reconstruction. From fig. 4.1, we
can see that the loss curve of training eventually stabilizes, as well as the SSIM metrics
in the test set, which means that the model has stabilized under the hyperparameter
conditions we have set and agradually reached the convergence state.
fig. 4.2 shows the visualization of the reconstructed results. Comparing the grayscale map
of channel 7 of the HS image in the second row with the original RGB image, we can find
that the HSCNND model is acceptable for the overall reconstruction of the image. From
the perspective of the human eye, the shapes of the objects contained in the grayscale
map, as well as the contrast between light and dark, are very close to the original image.
However, if we look carefully at the absolute error in the third row, we see that the error is
somewhat overstated from the data’s point of view. We have set up color bars on the right
side of the error map, with the range of the color bars mapping the range of the errors. It
is clear that although the five images as a whole are shown to be low error (blue overall),
there is a significant difference in the scale of the error as well as the effect of the error.
For example, the maximum error for the first image is slightly above 0.2, but the maximum
error for the third image is above 3.0. We don’t see any deep red dots in the image just
because the number of such extremes is too small, but the extremes are real.
And for results like the first image, where the overall error range is relatively small, we can
also observe some meaningful phenomenon. First of all, we can see the obvious grid on
the image, this is because during training, we fill the 482*512 image to 512*512 size first,
and then crop it to 16 small images of size 128*128, so the trained model still predicts the
small pieces after segmentation. The grid lines in the figure are actually the edges of the
small size images. This means that the model learns the edge pixels poorly.
Also, for the edges of the objects in the image, such as the railings in the upper part of
the first image and where the wood meets the ground, you can see more obvious white
lines. This means at this location, our reconstruction results perform relatively poorly. In
other words, it means that the model’s ability to learn at the edges of the contents and the
edges of the image needs to be strengthened.
Also from fig. 4.3, we can see that the reconstructed spectra perform differently depending
on the pixel location. The spectral map in the upper right corner shows that for the part
of the image with more balanced colors, the result of the spectral reconstruction can be
consistent with the real value in the overall trend, but with some deviation, and for the part
with less balanced colors in the lower right corner, the reconstructed pixel points show
a very poor spectral curve. Meanwhile, we have labeled the three visible light bands of
blue, green and red in the spectral map, in fact, the performance of HSCNND in all three
visible light ranges is not very satisfactory.

5.1.2
Exposure invariance
As seen in table 4.2, the image quality deteriorates when we make adjustments to the im­
age’s brightness. When we decrease the brightness, both the PSNR and SAM increase.
However, the model still manages to learn the image’s content effectively, resulting in
high­quality outcomes. On the other hand, when we increase the brightness, the gener­
ated results degrade significantly as the brightness level rises. For instance, at a bright­
ness factor of only 2, the PSNR drops to 32.2898, which is already comparable to the
performance of our Baseline model. When the factor reaches 10, the PSNR plummets to
16.2625.
It’s worth noting that the PSNR is bounded by 30. Values higher than 30 indicate that
differences are challenging for the human eye to discern, whereas values below 30 imply
that differences can be easily noticed. Both the error plot and the spectrum plot support
our conclusion.
Based on the images and data presented above, we can conclude that our model is not
well­suited for addressing the exposure invariance problem.
5.2
Conclusion
The analysis of the visual results and a deeper understanding of these outcomes re­
veal that the cGAN model significantly outperforms the traditional HSCNND and Resnet
models in overall performance. As an improved version of the GAN, the cGAN inherits
many advantages of the GAN, such as finer attention to deep details through adversarial
learning between the generator and discriminator. In other words, the generator is more
effective at capturing complex data distributions.
In contrast, while HSCNND has achieved significant results in reconstructing HS images,
its nature as a convolutional neural network limits its potential. Although density blocks re­
duce parameter loss while decreasing data loss, the layer­by­layer transfer of information
increases the learning burden, turning theoretical advantages into practical disadvantages
of information redundancy. Moreover, this mode reduces the number of parameters but
increases the volume of information processing, significantly demanding computational
resources, and the final scale of the generated feature map may be too large, affecting
the model’s practicality.
As for Resnet, its introduction of residual links optimized the gradient explosion problem
and accelerated the convergence process. However, optimization becomes more difficult
for deep networks, and there may be an information redundancy phenomenon similar to
that of HSCNND. As seen in [Insert Figure], the dataset’s distribution is relatively con­
centrated, indicating limited data complexity. This restricts our choice of deep networks
because excessive model capacity leads to a waste of resources without performance
improvement. Therefore, although Resnet’s performance is better than HSCNND’s, it still
falls short compared to other models.
Regarding the Pix2pix model, it is suitable for pixel­level tasks, especially excelling in
capturing local features of images, which is why it is widely used in tasks such as style

transfer. As an end­to­end model, it does not require complex feature engineering or
preprocessing. However, a major drawback is its high dependency on the construction of
the dataset. Fortunately, our dataset is of high quality, effectively avoiding this risk, which
is key to obtaining good reconstruction results. However, we also found that Pix2pix is
difficult to tune and lacks stability.
About the Pix2pix model, as a type of cGAN, its generator and discriminator mainly handle
the image itself as conditional information. This explains why we did not provide additional
color information to the discriminator when exploring Pix2pix cGAN(Color). Usually, con­
ditional information needs to be provided to both the generator and discriminator, but in
GAN networks, to avoid making the discriminator too powerful when it is sufficient to de­
termine the generated results, we choose not to further enhance the discriminator. The
results prove that this decision was correct; after adding color information, the perfor­
mance of the new Pix2pix model still improved.
For CNN cGAN(Color), its results were surprising, outperforming both Pix2pix and Resnet
cGAN. The conditional information we chose was the color histograms of the RGB images.
For CNN, this type of conditional information is difficult to learn through convolutional lay­
ers alone during training, so the color information we provided compensated for the CNN’s
deficiencies. For Resnet, since it can pass the image’s own information through short con­
nections, the additional color information may lead to information redundancy, decreas­
ing model performance. Observing the results of Resnet cGAN(RGB), we found that this
structure was even worse. In Resnet cGAN(RGB), the image information we provided
overlapped highly with the image information passed by Resnet itself, further proving the
negative impact of redundant data on model performance. In Pix2pix cGAN(Color), due
to the Unet structure and the characteristics of two downsamplings, the added color in­
formation still promoted the improvement of training effects.
Typically, the conditional information used in cGAN is category information, which is why
many cGAN models are used for classification tasks. Since our task is generative, the
choice of conditional information is not diverse. Practice shows that avoiding information
redundancy is an important foundation for achieving good expectations.
In the last two types of models, whether using color consistency constraints Pix2pix or
CNN cGAN, both show better generation results. If we reverse the presentation order,
i.e., first show the cGAN trained using color consistency constraints, then show the cGAN
guided by color information, and finally show the ordinary Pix2pix or CNN network, we
could form a presentation path of an ablation study. However, due to time constraints, our
experiments were not sufficient and failed to fully control variables.
Our study builds upon the foundations laid by the NTIRE 2020 competition, where the
prevalent trend among participants was to incorporate attention mechanisms into their
models. Although effective, this approach significantly increased computational costs.
As we highlighted in Chapter 2, our model training requires relatively few epochs, typi­
cally completing within 50 epochs. This underscores our research direction, which leans
towards making models more trainable without compromising their efficacy.
The research involved two sub­tasks: reconstruction of HS images and ensuring exposure
invariance. For HS image reconstruction, the results were generally satisfactory. How­
ever, our attempts at exposure invariance were less successful. The inherent darkness
of our dataset meant that it lacked detail in simulating shooting conditions, an important
aspect often overlooked. In OpenCV, brightness adjustments are made by directly alter­
ing pixel values. If an image’s brightness leads to uniform pixel values, this can result in

a failure to accurately reflect the spectral differences between real objects.
For cameras, varying exposure conditions can be achieved by altering the number of
photons reaching the sensor. Our discussion in Chapter 2 about remote sensing the­
ory touched upon how multispectral cameras, after spectral separation, require a larger
field of view due to insufficient photon capture. This applies to optical cameras as well;
more photons mean richer spectral information. However, adjusting the brightness of an
already­formed RGB image cannot alter the contained information. Excessively brighten­
ing an image can lead to loss of meaningful pixel value distribution, rendering the advan­
tages of deep learning in fitting massive datasets futile. This suggests an incompatibility
in our dataset for this specific task.
Throughout this project, our progress was hindered by limited equipment capabilities,
preventing us from exploring more complex models. Additionally, the scarcity of hyper­
spectral datasets posed a challenge. Despite the high quality of available datasets, they
were more suited for reconstruction tasks than exploring exposure invariance, misalign­
ing with our research focus. Given the opportunity, I would prefer to create a customized
dataset and further refine our model.
