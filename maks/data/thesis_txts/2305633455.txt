Abstract 
Based on a review ICT-Regulations in Denmark if was found that effects on the actors 
level of 3D model based working methods had increased from a very limited use in 
2008 to a much greater use of BIM in projects in 2014. It showed that 2/3 architects 
and engineers used BIM in most- or all projects, 1/3 of constructors used BIM in some- 
or most projects, 2/5 building owners didn’t have BIM projects and 1/2 of the material 
producers had no BIM projects at all, so a great gap of BIM skills among the actors in 
the Danish Building sector was created.  
Studies on the effect on the design quality showed a much higher quality of the 3D 
model based design material compared to 2D based design, and 3D based material 
with an ICT-Service specification was found to score twice as high, compared to the 
average in all projects. There was however still problems with the design material as 
67% of the assembled discipline models contained collisions after the constructor had 
received the IFC model, which means that the 3D model used for the tender bidding 
phase possibly contains uncertainties with a negative effect on the build quality.  
An interview with actors in the Danish Building industry study showed that it was a 
general problem for the designers to fulfill some demands in the ICT-Service 
specification, and the building owners should be more careful and verify that the 
demands stated in the ICT-Service specification, was also performed to a satisfactory 
level.  
A case study on an actual ongoing project showed and verified that the 3D discipline 
models contained collisions that would have effect on the economy, design- and build 
quality of the project 
A proposal for an IDM to fit into the Danish ICT-regulations was made, to try and 
improve the quality of the collision check and make it easier for the building owner to 
verify if the task is performed to a satisfactory level.

1 Introduction  
 
The Danish building sector is one of the larger trades in Denmark, and employs 
156.900 people in the private sector alone (Danmarks Statestik, 2016) with a revenue 
of 255,7 billion DKK (Danmarks Statestik, 2013). It has long been blamed for not being 
productive or innovative and delivering the expected build quality. An analysis of the 
productivity in Denmark from 1970-1995 showed that the building sector just had an 
increase of merely 20 % where most other big trades in Denmark had doubled or more 
(By- og Boligministeriet, et al., 2000). When compared to the productivity of the building 
sectors in other countries (Sweden, Holland and Germany), in the same time period, it 
showed that the building sector in general does not develop as fast as other trades, but 
all other countries have had a bigger increase with Sweden almost reaching 100 %. 
(By- og Boligministeriet, et al., 2000). One of the suggestions from the report that found 
these problems with the lack of development in the Danish building sector, was to take 
greater advantage of the use of IT and implement a standard for 3D aided design. The 
report recognized the need to create a knowledge center for the building sector to 
facilitate innovation and creating common standards. It was also recognized that a 
demand from the authorities and public building owners could create a demand for 
implementation of IT and 3D aided design, and “kick start” the implementation in the 
building sector (By- og Boligministeriet, et al., 2000). 
As a result of the recommendations to increase the productivity in the building sector 
by implementing IT and 3D aided design, a work group of experts in the building sector 
investigated the problem further and came to two conclusions (Erhvervsministeriet, 
2001): 
 
Develop IT-guidelines for public building owners 
The public building owners represents half of the demand for building projects in 
Denmark (in 2000), but are divided into many independent smaller fractions. By 
creating common IT guidelines and specifications for all public building projects, it was 
believed to remove to investment uncertainty for rest of the building sector such as 
architects, engineers, material producers etc. 
 
Develop standards to work across the building sector 
In order to utilize the data- and information flow between the different actors in a 
building process, different standards for communication must be established. Cases 
with “best-practice” must afterwards be distributed to the building sector as a whole. 
 
Later, in 2003 a government funded initiative named “The digital construction”, was 
launched with the main purpose of creating an industry effort to implement digital 
processes and producing cases with “best-practice” information to the whole industry. 
The main strategy was to force the necessary implementation by having the public 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
 
 
7 
building owner making specific ICT demands, and thus creating the necessary effort 
from the industry as a whole, to create new methods and standards. This effort lead to 
the first ICT-Proclamation 
The first “ICT regulations” in the Danish building sector came into force the 1th January 
in 2007, and made demands for implementation of ICT for building projects involving 
the state as the building owner. The first ICT-Proclamation involved demands for the 
application of a digital project web, classification of building elements and the use of 3D 
building models wherever it was deemed positive for the clarification of the project. The 
3D model was to be used in the early stages of the project for visualizations and later 
for clash control, tendering and for the handover of data to the building owner when the 
projects where completed (Bygge- og Erhvervsstyrelsen, 2006). It was later revised to 
facilitate the use of DBK classification and made further and more specific 
requirements for the handover of data to the building owner (Bygge- og 
Erhvervsstyrelsen, 2010). 
The newest edition (BEK. 118 and BEK 119) came into force the 6th and 7th February in 
2013 (Klima-, Energi- og Bygningsministeriet, 2013) (Ministeriet for By, Bolig og 
Landdistrikter, 2013) and now contains seven demands for the building owner to fulfill: 
 
ICT coordination 
 
Handling of digital building elements 
 
Digital communication and project web 
 
Use of digital object based building models in project competitions, 
design and building phase 
 
Digital tender 
 
Digital handover  
 
Digital information about construction errors 
With the newest addition of a new ICT proclamation (BEK 119), where social housing 
projects is also obliged to fulfill the same obligations as government funded buildings, it 
involved a lot of new actors and building owners. There are 750 housing cooperatives 
in Denmark, having 20 % of the total housing market which are now implied to use ICT, 
so the level of government forced ICT implementation in Denmark was further 
increased. (Ministeriet for by, bolig og landdistrikter, 2013).  
The wording and definition in the newest ICT-Proclamation in BEK 118 and 119 
changed from being a demand-based approach, to an approach where the building 
owner has more freedom to choose from his knowledge or best practice to fit into his 
organization’s own paradigms.  
The bias with this approach is though that the building owner, often has a low 
implementation of BIM and other digital tools within his own organization, and therefore 
has no best practice or sufficient knowledge to gain the benefits of this increase in data 
exchange in the lifecycle of his project (BIPS, 2014).  
==PAGE:8==
 
 
 
 
8 
The need to standardize and ensure consistency and validation in information 
exchange in the lifetime of a  construction project, is still very much needed to improve 
a productive and beneficial communication between all related actors.  
A way to standardize and improve information deliveries in ICT regulated BIM projects, 
could be to introduce the concept of an Information Delivery Manual from 
BuildingSMART (shortened to IDM).  
“An Information Delivery Manual defines an industry process that involves at least two 
types of software applications, and the information that should be exchanged between 
those applications” (BuildingSMART, 2012, p. 5). 
An IDM can also be seen as a methodology for interdisciplinary exchange of data 
between two or more participants and create a standardized framework for data and 
information exchange in the project organization. 
And IDM could be a tool to break down projects in a cluster of packages each 
representing a working process or a delivery. (Treldal Niels, Mondrup Thomas Fænø, 
Karlshøj Jan, Vestergaard Flemming, 2015) On a project management level this could 
ensure transparency and an overview of the processes needed at a specific time, to 
fulfill the demands of the specific data, for all related actors. The building owner could 
also use specific IDMs to ensure which data is necessary to deliver at given phases in 
the project. The Architectural, Engineering and Construction (AEC) actors will know 
when to deliver or complete an activity and what kind of deliverables are expected.

2 Problem definition 
This project aims to answer the following problem definition: 
After reviewing and evaluating the current Danish ICT regulation, it is investigated how 
building owners can benefit from using IDMs to describe work processes to streamline 
and simplify information exchange within a project and to benefit from BIM information 
generated during construction projects. 
By developing IDMs to fit into the Danish ICT regulation, by a case study on an actual 
project, is it possible to optimize current workflows, support implementation of BIM in 
projects, and thereby get the right information needed during different project phases, 
with focus on deliverables. 
2.1 Delimitation 
This section deals with the delimitation of the current project. The investigation of 
current working methods when working with the Danish ICT regulations is done by a 
case based study undertaken on a project in phase 6 – Coordinated Design (ISO, 
2012). The case aims to clarify whether whether or not the data and BIM models 
contains the relevant information regarding the need of data described in the ICT-
service specifications used in the project. Essential is whether the information is ‘nice-
to-have ´’ or ‘need-to-have’. This investigation of coherency in the data will also be the 
base for a development or investigation to create a working and functional proposal of 
a simple IDM

3 Method 
3.1 Review of the Danish ICT regulation 
The aim for the review of the Danish ICT regulation is to provide the reader with an 
understanding of the course the ICT regulations has undergone from the first BEK 
1365 from 11/12 2006 to the current BEK no. 118 and 119 from 06/02/2013. It will be 
investigated and illuminated how the different scopes and methods might have 
changed and how they have influenced the working processes of the stakeholders in a 
building project. 
3.2 Review of the current IDMs 
The aim for the review of the current IDMs used in the Danish building sector, is to 
provide the reader with an understanding on how and if IDMs are used, and to what 
purpose. It will also cover which actors that are trying to implement IDMs in their own 
organizations and to what success. The conclusions for the review is to enlighten the 
author and reader if IDMs have been fully or partially implemented. This is to be used 
in Production phase of this report, where the author will try and use the positive effects 
and outcomes from previous implementations of IDMs.

4 General terminology and subject areas  
This chapter aims to briefly describe some of the main terminology used in this report 
in order to provide the reader with the necessary knowledge and understanding when 
reading this report. The main focus will be to explain some of the terms used in the

4.1 CCS 
Cuneco Classification System or CCS is a development project managed by BIPS in 
cooperation with other Danish actors in the Danish building sector from 2010-2015 
(Cuneco, 2015). The aim and goal for the project was to create a common “digital 
language” for actors in the Danish building sector and seeks to completely replace the 
former Danish Classification System (DBK) and other used systems such as 
Samarbetskomitén för Byggnadsfrågor(SfB), BIM7AA and Forvaltnings Klassifikation 
(FK). There is no specific demand in the latest ICT regulation on which classification 
system to use, but as CCS is the recommended system by BIPS, it could most likely be 
the most commonly used. 
 
This report will not go into full detail of CCS, but merely an introduction to understand 
the basics.

4.1.2.5.1 % Identification ID 
 
The identification ID is used to differentiate a specific group of objects within the same 
class of objects in a project. The identification ID is often used in the tender phase, 
where it creates the link between the 3D model and the tender quantity list. As an 
example, DaluxProject can export single level ID [%], via a plugin to a quantity list in 
Excel (Dalux, 2016). This way, all objects in the 3D model assigned with an ID, can be 
transferred to a quantity list and synchronized automatically any time the 3D model is 
updated.

4.1.3 Information levels 
 
Information levels is not only a concept known in CCS. There are many other 
standards for describing the level of detail of an object or a 3D model as a whole, such 
as the Level Of Development specification(LOD) (BIMForum, 2015). Information levels 
are usually used to describe what the expected level of information in the 3D model at 
specific building phase. This serves to avoid over- or under-modelling for the designer. 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
21 
The aim for the CCS information level specification is: 
“CCS information levels is used to group information of an object in levels from the 
idea- phase to it is realized” (CUNECO, 2014). 
 
The information levels in the CCS classification are divided in 7 information classes: 
 
Figure 8 - Information levels in CCS classification 
 
 
The CCS information level is a way to describe a minimum amount of information for 
objects, construction entities, spaces and equipment in a 3D model in relation to a 
specific time in a project phase. The information levels are meant to describe single- or 
groups of objects in the 3D model and not the whole 3D model itself. (CUNECO, 2014). 
The general aim is then to be able to combine classes of objects with properties e.g.

easy understandable understanding of the goal and process. 
4.2.2 Process map(PM)

gateways. Data objects provides information about what activities require in order to be 
performed or what the activity produce. Data objects used in IDMs would typically be 
input and outputs of data or data storage. Connection objects are connectors used to

5 Review of the Danish ICT regulation 
 
The initial development for the first Danish ICT regulation came from three primary 
investigations and reports from around the year 2000. The first report: IT i byggeriets 
fremtid (Hauch, et al., 2001) was a summation from private actors in the Danish 
building of their experience with project webs, object based 3D models, digital 
catalogues and quantity takeoff. 
The report concludes that the potential for IT and CAD in the building sector is in 
general high, but there is a lack of productive IT coordination between the actors in the 
building projects. It is also emphasized, that there is a great need for a coordinated 
effort in the IT infrastructure development.  
 
The second report: Ressourceområdeanalysen Bygge/Bolig (Erhvervsfremmestyrelsen, 
2000) investigated the lack of development in the building sector. The report found 
three main issues which needed to be addressed: 
 
The demand of the end user is not fulfilled to a satisfactory level. The end user 
is not able to estimate the end price for the product, as the end price is often 
different from the offered price in the early stages. 
 
The price for houses and building projects are too high compared to other 
countries, that Denmark often compares itself to. This is a result of a stagnated 
productivity development in the building sector. 
 
There are too many errors that needs to be rectified. Byggeskadefonden1 found 
in 1999/2000 that there were severe errors in 26 % of all building projects at the 
5-year inspection. (Byggeskadefonden, 1999/2000). The total cost can be 
hard to fully evaluate, but Byggeskadefonden paid a sum of 113 Mill. DKK in 
1999 to cover damage in public funded building projects. (Byggeskadefonden, 
1999/2000) 
 
The last of the three reports: Byggeriets fremtid - Fra tradition til innovation was the 
product of a task-force with the aim to produce practical advice to the Danish 
government to try and solve the problems found in the report from 
(Erhvervsfremmestyrelsen, 2000). It was found that one of the great potentials lies in 
the interface between actors in the building projects. This could achieve an effective 
resource use, minimizing waste time/stand still time and better quality. This is achieved

by having the right information, accessible to all, at the right time when it is needed. 
(By- og Boligministeriet, et al., 2000)   
 
 
The solution for the lack of innovation and productivity in the Danish building sector led 
to the initiative “Det Digitale Byggeri” (Jespersen, 2008) by Erhvervs- og 
Byggestyrelsen in 2002. The goal and aim for the initiative was to create a platform of 
IT- standards and common guidelines for which government building owners could use 
to make ICT demands for architects, engineers and contractors and thereby force the 
wanted innovation in the building sector (BIPS, u.d.). The result after the 4-year 
initiative from 2003-2007 with “best practice” cases and practical knowhow was the first 
Danish ICT proclamation.

Classification

The demand for the use of DBK is no longer in the proclamation, and the building 
owner can now decide any particular classification method. However, the Ministry of 
city, housing, and rural areas has decided in regard to facility management, that all 
housing must be classified with FK classification (Ministeriet for by, bolig og 
landdistrikter, 2012). This was due to the fact that CCS was not completely finished at 
the time of the release of the proclamations, and BYGS did not want to make the same 
mistake, as with making a demand for the unfinished DKB in prior proclamations (BIPS, 
2013).   
Tender with Quantities 
The demand of a tender with a quantity list, is also removed and the text is replaced 
with: “If the tender is with quantities…” This was removed as the method of 
extracting the quantities from the model, was not developed to such an extent, that 
is was possible to automatically extract all the numbers  
 
Morten Steffensen (BIPS, 2013) 
“It was an illusion to think, that the press of one button would just spit 
out the quantity list” 
There was also demands in the earlier proclamations that certain BIPS standard 
should be applied, but this is also removed. This was done due to the fact, that it is 
not important which standard you use, as long as any standard is used, and this 
would also to open up for international companies (BIPS, 2013).  
3D Models 
The use of 3D models is now limited to be used for the design- and construction 
phase, whereas the earlier version demanded a use of the 3D models for the 
design competition bidding. 
ICT-Coordination 
There is a new addition in BEK 118 and BEK 119, and that is §3 – ICT-
Coordination. The previous proclamations had demands for a “responsible person 
for DBK, responsible person for project web, responsible person for digital 
handover and responsible for 3D model coordination, but now it states that this 
should be one on the same person responsible for all ICT coordination. This was 
made to ensure that one person throughout the whole building project is 
responsible for the coordination and collaboration between all actors in the project 
(Steffensen, 2013).

I use the terms “first-“, “second-“ and “late movers” which are used in relation to

One of the reasons why we see constructors and material producers as late movers,

not, in the same way as designers and engineers, forced to use ICT by the building

and best practices and thereby enjoying lower costs and making fewer mistakes as a 
result. It would seem like this only work in the particular group of actors in the building 
industry, and the lessons learned by the architect, is not distributed to the constructor 
or material producer.

6 Interviews 
 
This chapter will cover an industry survey with a selection of industry professionals. 
The aim for doing interviews is to gather information from the person and to get an 
insight to his perception of world view (Kvale, 1994). This is done by using a form 
called “semi structured interview” (Bryman, 2008), and is viewed as a conversation with 
a purpose. It is the best suited interview structure to engage interviewees or groups 
when the goal is to capture all the views and ideas of the participant concerning the 
treated subject.  
 
It is very important, when performing this kind of interview, to have a clear and 
structured form planned out form the beginning, in order to be able to compare results 
from different participants. This is done by creating an interview guide, where questions 
and themes are prepared beforehand and is used dynamically during the interview to 
steer the participants in the right direction, and make sure you get all the important 
questions answered. This does not mean, that the participant is not able to speak freely 
and move around the subject, so you are able to get a perception of the full spectrum 
of his views. The interviews lasted approximately 60 minutes.

The aim is the acquire views and opinions regarding the Danish ICT proclamation from 
key persons in the Danish building sector. Therefore, key persons from respectively

6.5.2 3. Review of ICT-Proclamations 
 
There is a general acceptance and agreement of the ICT-proclamation as a positive 
and necessary tool to get the Danish building sector to innovate and improve. The 
building sector has a notorious reputation of being hard to move away from well proven 
methods, and the ICT-proclamations opened a new market. The first two ICT-
proclamations had demands for the use of DBK, which were not fully operational, and 
may have damaged to buildings sectors own involvement as a lot of momentum was 
lost in the first years because of the failure to complete a working national classification 
system.  
There was a lot of optimism in the industry at first, and the 3D model approach was by 
the designing industry seen as a tool with endless possibilities, an easy way to optimize 
work processes. A lot of bigger architectural companies invested heavily in new 
software and equipment, but forgot it is not the tool, but the methods and workers that 
needed to be invested in with time and money, “it was is like trying to hammer nails 
with a screwdriver”.  
 
All actors can agree on that the two newest ICT-proclamation’s similarity, is a very 
positive thing. As more building owners have similar demands, the easier and more 
effective the actors will be, as they don’t have learn new methods or processes for 
each separate building project.  
MS see a big ambiguity in the current ICT-service specifications, as they both serve as 
a service specification and also a guidance, and it can be hard to differ between those 
two. It can be very hard to tell when it serves as a manual, and when it is a specific 
demand. This makes it difficult the match the expectations between the involved parties 
if it is not addressed in the beginning of the project.

6.5.3 4. Scope on the current industry 
 
There is a consensus about there being a big momentum in the Danish building sector 
regarding BIM projects and investing time and money right now. They both recognize 
that the active parts in the industry is now the bigger constructing companies, the 
building owners and facility managers. They have now recognized the positive gains 
made by the first movers of their own part of the industry, and they now have to step up 
if they want to be a part of the leading actors. MS recognized the big gap in the industry 
being a problem as the material producers and constructors can be years behind with 
the use of BIM compared to architects and engineers. This creates a big gap between

the collaboration throughout the entire project. This often leads to the creation of a 
separate 2D project being created, in order to get the work process going. MS

6.5.4 5. Future of ICT – IDM 
MS view on the future of having a regulation of ICT by the government, is seen as a 
transitional phase, where the industry at some point will be the frontrunner and 
regulations would only be a constraint. Every time there is a constraint you prevent 
development. In a shorter perspective, the two ICT-proclamations and their guidelines 
should be made into one, so they have the exact same wording and descriptions. This 
will make it easier for the industry as you can capitalize on earlier experiences even 
more. Introducing IDMs could be a good idea as the actions and deliveries you need to 
make is made very specific. This is a very good idea to as it serves as a match of 
expectations between all actors, and if they are accompanied with the ICT agreement 
from the beginning, it will increase the smoothness of workflow as you know what is 
expected of you to perform. MS mentions that the designers often have to guess what 
the building owner expect, as there is near clear definition of expectations.  
SA view on ICT regulations is a long term perspective. It is not a very good idea to 
have a “Yo-Yo” regulations where you change the basics too often, or change it at all. 
When you decide something in the building industry you have to be in it for the long run 
as it is a very slow starter. It is a good idea to have simple and long term demands in 
the building sector, so everyone has a chance to learn new methods and become 
familiar with them, as errors in the building sector are always very expensive errors. 
You have to take what you got and become better at it, and ICT as proven to be a good 
method, we just have to become better. The way to become better right now is to 
highlight the positive experiences we already got from ICT implementation on tour with 
these results so everyone has a change to learn from previous positive experiences.

6.5.5 7. Conclusion 
 
The interviews were done of two actors in the industry, one being an architect and one 
being a building owner. One of them is paid by the other to deliver a product to satisfy 
the demands of the other, so they probably would have two different opinions on what 
a satisfactory product would be.  
There is a general understanding, that the ICT-demands made by public building 
owners, and with the addition of BEK 119, served to force the industry to evolve and 
make efforts to implement new working methods. That idea to force the industry to 
evolve is seen as a partially success. Its only seen as partially as only parts of the 
industry has evolved, as described by MS, where the material producers and some 
contractors are 4 years behind the consulting part of the industry which create a gap in 
knowledge and work methods. SA does not share the same opinion and see it as a 
problem, as they just have to embrace and evolve like the rest of the industry. MS see 
an internal problem in the industry where not all actors have evolved in the same pace, 
which creates problems when exchanging information. This difference can be seen in 
Chart 6 which shows an imbalance in BIM implementation of different parts of the 
industry.  
They both have a big emphasis on there being a greater standardization in the 
industry, and standardization would make all processes easier for all involved parties 
as they can benefit from earlier cases and best practices.  
SA believes that the current ICT-Proclamations are solid for the future, as the demands 
a very general, and they should continue to be at force for many years. The changes 
needed to be done, can be made effective in the specific ICT-service specifications as 
the building owner has the right to demand whatever he wants as he is the buyer of a 
product and should not take its suppliers skills or needs into account. MS believes that 
the supplying part of the industry will at some point be the leading actor regarding ICT 
services and would naturally have the best ICT methods, and should not be forced to 
perform certain actions. That would only serve to stop further development in the area.  
 
Regarding using IDMs in the building sector, it seems like the industry is familiar with

Discussion on collision check 
Another important very important thing, when a design material is provided to the 
constructor as a basis for the tender, contains questionable design solutions. The 
contractual basis on this project is the General Terms AB92, and is between the 
building owner and the constructor. It states in §2:  
“Bids shall be made on the basis of the information contained in the tender documents. 
The contents of these documents must be unambiguous and presented so as to make 
quite clear the extent and nature of the services to be provided.”  
The key words in the paragraph here is “unambiguous” and “presented so as to make 
quite clear the extent and nature of the services to be provided”. 
What this means in general terms is that the project material the constructor makes his 
bidding on, must not contain any questionable design solutions, and the design 
material must appear buildable. So what happens when a design solution, is not 
ambiguous and not picked up during the bidding phase, and the contract is signed? 
The constructor will say, that the design material is not unambiguous and presented as 
to make quite clear the extent and nature of the services provided, and redirect this 
complaint to the building owner, who then direct this complaint to the designer. This will 
again involve a lot more persons to solve this problems, compared to if the problem 
had been solved before the tender. What I am implying here is, that a relatively easy 
task of doing a collision detection and solved the problem earlier on, now involves a lot 
more persons, involves more communication between different actors, and could delay 
the building projects, and put an economic burden on all actors across the project.

8.2 Conclusion on the project specific ICT-Service specification 
 
After reviewing the project case, it could be seen that the “de facto” ICT work methods, 
such as project web, and digital tender was satisfactory fulfilled. This falls in line with 
some of the conclusions SA from BYGS also noticed after doing an internally review in 
their organization. That is, that the building owners should be a lot better, to check the 
demands made in the ICT-service specification and if those demands were met. This 
project case investigation clearly showed, that there is a lot to be desired when it 
comes to all of the other demands made, such as 3D modelling and classification. The 
collisions detection done on the tender design material, showed that there was still 
issues that should have been solved before it was sent into bidding. These issues such 
as missing recesses in the load bearing structures and collisions between the various 
installations could have been solved with a relatively small effort, but if not, it has a 
potential to cause complications in the future building process, and be the cause of 
wasted man hours for all implicated actors. As there still was unsolved important 
collisions, even though the ICT-service specification contains deliveries for collision 
detection, we must conclude that the designer has failed to deliver what was stated. 
The collisions in the load bearing structures found with Tekla Bimsight, could however 
also have been detecting when the designer receives the 2D material from the 
concrete material producer for quality assurance, but that again involves a manual 
piece of work, equally possible more time consuming. 
We saw that there was consistency between the 2D design material and the tender 
quantity list, but no consistency with the 3D models, even though this was also stated 
in the ICT-Service specification. The classification was done “manually” with a great 
risk potential as there were humans involved, and could have been even more time 
consuming compared to using a software to perform the task.  
It seems like the designers are, in some areas, willing to take chances on not 
performing a task, if they feel it has no value for the building owner, or for the quality of 
the design material and general building process. It can simple not be the fact that the 
designer does not have to necessary skills to apply a CCS classification in the building 
models if they wanted, and the skill and expertise to deliver 3D models with no 
collisions and an unambiguous design material. It can though be discussed if the 2D 
drawings with CCS classification tags, did create consistency between the design 
material and the quantity list and work descriptions etc. but that would all depend on 
the BIM skill level of the constructor, as he could have wanted used the CCS to various 
quantity take-offs and sorting of the building objects.  
This however does very much comply with some of the statements that the designer 
MS mentioned in during the interview. MS mentioned that it was sometimes hard to 
guess what the building really want, and the ICT-service specifications fail to make a 
clear match of expectations as some of it is written as a manual, and not based on very 
clear demands of what is expected by the designer.

to improve. This means that all these digital BIM software and new ways to improve the 
quality of the design material and ways to increase the productivity in the Danish 
building sector is not fully utilized. There can be many reasons of this failure to deliver, 
but one of them was the failure to match expectations between one or more actors, and 
especially between the building owner, and the designer. This was partly due to the 
fact that the ICT-Service specifications is partly a service specification and partly a 
manual. I think an IDM could be an answer to provide a very clear match of 
expectation, as an IDM provides a very clear structure of what is needed, who needs to 
perform it, how to perform it and when to perform it.  
By ensuring a thorough collision detection would make sure that the tender bidding 
material and especially the 3D model will have a higher quality with lesser 
uncertainties. Lesser uncertainties would probably mean a lower cost for the building 
owner, when the contractor will calculate the prize involved with doing a particular task 
if the design material provides an unambiguous and clear overview of the project 
(Vestergaard, et al., 2012).  
This suggestion for an IDM, should be seen as attachment in an ICT-service 
specification, and only relate to a collision detection. This IDM differ from the regular 
perspective of an IDM, as this will enforce or dictate a certain process that should have 
occurred naturally in the designer’s workflow as a specific service. The IDM will serve 
as matching of expectations, and emphasize the importance of the collision check, and 
also that the building owners wants to be involved in the process. The building owner is 
defining the requirements and demands for his product, so why would he not ensure 
that he gets the best product when the process, technology, experience and software is 
easy available.  
 
IDM – Collision control before tender bidding phase in projects with 
an ICT-Service specification 
 
The Suggested IDM is supposed to be generic, so it could work across many types of 
building projects with different styles of ICT-service specifications. It is also constructed 
so the ICT-Coordinator still needs to use his professionalism to decide how the best 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
 
 
78 
and must functional way of conducting the collision check and coordination between 
the associated actors in his project. The IDM is therefore not very technical, as this 
level should be decided in regard to the BIM skill level in the specific project. 
 
Use case 
 
A collision detection is an important BIM process, where a software is used to collect 
and assemble various discipline models from all actors involved in the design phase. 
These actors would be the architect and various engineering disciplines such as 
construction, ventilation, electrical and plumbing. The process provides an occasion to 
discover collisions that might have a negative impact on chosen design solutions and 
build quality, and creates the opportunity the act upon these in due time. These 
collisions will be of various character, and the designated ICT-Coordinator will be 
forced to take action according to the nature of the collision whether to be ignorable or 
critical, and the ICT-Coordinator will then assign the collision to the implicated actors 
who will then take the necessary action. It is important that the collision check is 
planned in the project time schedule, with sufficient time to coordinate and rectify the 
critical collisions before the tender phase. 
The main outcome for this process is a coordinated design material and BIM models 
whereupon the constructor must make his bid, where the BIM model is consistent and 
unambiguous. This provides solid foundation for the constructor to make his bid upon 
and for the future building process with a higher quality of information, which should 
result in a smoother building process with lesser disputes.

10 Discussion 
 
This report aimed to do a review the ICT-Proclamations in Denmark, and it seemed to 
be it bit more complex than initially expected. The review revealed how complex the 
Danish Building industry really is, when trying to isolate the effect of one small, single 
initiative such the implementation of an ICT-regulation. There is a lot of esoteric 
opinions about the lack of will to innovate in the building sector, and the investigation 
showed that they seem to be true. Innovation and the will to try out new methods in 
order to gain a greater opportunity does not come naturally in the building sector, and 
adaptions of new methods such as 3D modelling is not something that occur over a few 
years, rather it is probably going to take a decade, before we see the full spectrum of 
the building sector with a uniform implementation. This was the result of the 
comparison of the surveys done by BIPS in 2008 and 2014. In the timeline of 6 years 
only the architects and engineers showed a significant change in the adaption of BIM, 
while contractors and material producers lacking behind with almost half the material 
producers working with BIM at all. This diversity seems to be a problem with the 
conception of a BIM process where there the data and information is stored in a central 
BIM model, that develops and receive more information throughout the whole building 
process which is one of the corner stones in the digital process where data can be 
stored and reused thus saving time. It probably won’t affect the constructor or material 
producers, but the main designers job to deliver “as-built” is job of maintaining the 
correct information during the building process is probably more difficult, and the 
building owner could miss out on a lot of valuable data and information of his product.  
The interview study was a bit narrow, and probably should have included more actors 
in the industry. The two persons that were willing to participate were however some key 
players in the industry through their positions in various committees regarding ICT and 
their job positions.   
The data from the MT Højgaard whitepaper, only ranked 37 % of the design material 
they had received in “high quality, which leads a lot to be desired from the architects 
and engineers in the Danish building sector, but the really interesting fact was, that the 
3D based project material ranked a lot higher than 2D, and projects with and ICT-
Regulations had 77 % scoring high quality. This gives an unambiguous answer, that 3D 
design material is the definitive best method especially when there is an ICT-Service 
specification used in the project. 
One of the main thesis was to investigate how the building owner might benefit from 
the use of IDM to get the right information during the various project phases and 
deliveries. The investigations into the use of IDMs in Denmark showed, that IDMs for a 
long time have believed to be the prodigy to BIM processes as it is a very powerful tool 
to describe process with multiple actors in a simple and precise way, so all deliveries of 
data and information is explicitly described from start to end. This way a process with 
multiple actors would now exactly what to deliver, when to deliver it, how to deliver it 
and to whom. The Danish building industry does not however seem to have picked it 
==PAGE:84==
 
 
 
 
84 
up, and the governing body BuildingSMART seemed to have given up on the idea to 
standardize them and further develop IDMs, as their list of IDMs was last updated in 
2013, with no other news on their webpage. It seems like, if there is going to be a 
development and greater implementation of them in the industry, it has to come from 
the industry itself. Especially if it comes from the buyer, that is the building owner, and 
we would probably see the same evolution as we saw with the implementation of BIM, 
when the public building owners starting to make demands for ICT processes. It seems 
like the idea of providing cases with positive results is not enough to convince actors in 
the building sector, to try new ideas, as the DIKON IDM for clash detection from 2010 
have not gained foothold anywhere. An ICT specific IDM might solve that problem, and 
it could become a de facto standard in the industry thus ensuring that the design quality 
of ICT based building projects would become uniformly better. An IDM could also serve 
as a match of expectations if the building owner had a range of IDMs to ensure 
important processes and would also give a uniform design material every time, as you 
can imagine that in different projects, the building owners project manager might have 
different views of what areas of the ICT-service specification that are important, or 
simply don’t have the skill or knowledge to verify whether or not the designer has 
fulfilled a delivery or a task to a satisfactory level. This could be the case in BYGS, as 
they are a very large organization with many project managers, and that could explain 
why their internal survey on fulfillment of the ICT-service specification performed so 
poorly. 
The investigation of the case project in regard to classification, showed that 
classification of models, necessarily is not a natural process by the designer, as most 
of the discipline models did not contain a single CCS classified object, and the models 
that contained some degree of CCS, appeared to have happened randomly as some 
Revit families contained CCS. The building owner’s project manager, in the case 
project, did not emphasize on the need to fully classify all objects, but neither told that it 
could be left out. Classification does not have a good track record in Denmark, as the 
first two ICT-proclamation demanded the use of a not functional classification system, 
and could be the reason why it is not an integrated process yet, and as long as 
classification is still some form or another a manual process, it’s not going to be very 
successful. Another negative factor, is of course that you as a designer experience a 
building owner, that does not have a specific purpose with a fully classified model as 
many does not yet have FM system to take advantage if these data, and those who 
does are probably better at demanding this service. 
It does not seem like there is going be any new ICT-Proclamations anytime soon in 
Denmark, as SA from BYGS clearly emphasized during the interviews, the focus was 
now for the industry to become better at performing those demands it contains now 
which make a lot of sense from their perspective. The idea that the industry would 
outperform the demands made in the current ICT-Proclamation that MS presented 
during the interview would most likely happen someday, but that would require some 
form of governing body such as DANSKE ARK or FRI.

11 Conclusion 
 
In order to develop a proposal for a specific IDM, that would fit into the Danish ICT 
regulations, a review of the earlier- and current ICT-Proclamations was conducted and 
reviewed with a focus on what changes and differences there have been made from 
the first BEK 1365, to BEK 1381 and to the current BEK 118 and BEK 119. The effects 
on the actors level 3D model based working methods was found in studies to have 
increased from a very limited use in 2008 to a much greater use of BIM in projects in 
2014. It showed that 2/3 architects and engineers used BIM in most- or all projects, 1/3 
of constructors used BIM in some- or most projects, 2/5 building owners didn’t have 
BIM projects and 1/2 of the material producers had no BIM projects at all, so a great 
gap of BIM skills among the actors in the Danish Building sector was created.  
Studies on the effect on the design quality showed a much higher quality of the 3D 
modelb based design material compared to 2D based design, and 3D based material 
with an ICT-Service specification was found to score twice as high, compared to the 
average in all projects. It must be concluded that having an ICT-Service specification in 
3D based design material will provide the building owner with the best foundation for a 
successful building project. There was however still problems with the design material 
as 67% of the assembled discipline models contained collisions after the constructor 
had received the IFC model, which means that the 3D model used for the tender 
bidding phase possibly contains uncertainties with a negative effect on the build quality. 
These collisions occur even though it is the designers responsibility, as it is a demand 
in the ICT-Service specification to perform a collisions detection before the bidding 
phase, why it can be concluded that somehow these are not performed to a 
satisfactory level.  
An interview with actors in the Danish Building industry study showed that it was a 
general problem for the designers to fulfill some demands in the ICT-Service 
specification, and the building owners should be more careful and verify that the 
demands stated in the ICT-Service specification, was also performed to a satisfactory 
level.  
A case study on an actual ongoing project showed, among other things, that the 3D 
discipline models contained collisions that would have effect on the economy, design- 
and build quality of the project. It can therefore be concluded by various and 
independent studies that there is a general problem with especially collision check of 
discipline models. 
A proposal for an IDM to fit into the Danish ICT-regulations was made, to try and 
improve the quality of the collision check and make it easier for the building owner to 
verify if the task is performed to a satisfactory level.
