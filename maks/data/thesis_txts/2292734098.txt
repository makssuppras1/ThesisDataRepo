Abstract
In this thesis we have investigated the origin of Bayesian Principal Compo-
nent Analysis (B-PCA) its relation to Bayesian Factor Analysis (B-FA). We
demonstrate how moving the Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) prior
allows for the development of a fast Variational Bayesian Factor Analysis Model,
which is exclusively formulated as matrix-matrix multiplications, provide a scale
methods for both ordinary CPU computation and for utilizing the massive par-
allelization of Graphical Processing Units (GPU). We also attempted to develop
a Variational Bayesian Sparse Principal Component Analysis, but were unable
to validate its performance.
We tested the B-PCA and B-FA on synthetic datasets against other latent com-
ponent methods, these were Archetypal Analysis (AA), Independent Component
Analysis (ICA) and Probabilistic Principal Components (PPCA). We showed
that the developed models were able to determine the intrinsic dimensionality
of the data, investigated of homo- and heteroscedastic noise settings. B-PCA
and B-FA achieved comparable or superior performance in all synthetic test.
Next we applied B-FA to fMRI data from 29 subjects performing a block-design
motor-task experiment, where we concatenated the temporal dimension of the
data. This approach were compared to Multi-Subject Archetypal Analysis (MS-
AA) and Group-ICA. B-FA discovered functional brain networks resembling
well known brain areas such as the left-right sensorimotor cortex, visual cortex
and part of the default mode network. But interpretation of the results were
ambiguous as the ARD prior did not prune superÔ¨Çuous latent components.
Group-ICA and MS-AA both clearly captured the experiment design but had
no way of identifying the intrinsic dimensionality.

We conclude that more realistic noise models are a necessity in fMRI modelling,
but that B-FA needs a strong ARD-prior to actively prune superÔ¨Çuous functional
brain networks (latent components) in fMRI.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Neuroimaging is the study of brain structure or function, more commonly de-
noted as structural and functional imaging. There exist a wide array of imaging
modalities oÔ¨Äering diÔ¨Äerent views of the brain, each with their advantages and
disadvantages. We focus on study of functional connectivity in the human brain
using functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) ([Ogawa et al., 1990]).
The advantage of fMRI lies in being a non-invasive technique providing a de-
tailed image of the brain down to a few millimeters (1-6mm), spatial resolution,
but limited to a temporal resolution of a few seconds,. Determining activity
in the brain is done through statistical analysis due to high spatial resolution
and relative poor signal-to-noise ratio of fMRI images. A collection of highly
used statistical models can be found in the Statistical Parametric Mapping
(SPM) toolbox. A popular method to Ô¨Ånd brain activation is the General Lin-
ear Model (GLM) where measured fMRI is compared directly with experiment
design (stimulus timing) to identify brain networks. Where a brain network is
a collection of voxels as grouped by some statistical method, due to share some
characteristics. However, this approach is limited to the study of stimuli acti-
vated networks and can not used in an experiment without a predeÔ¨Åned stimuli.
Remaining antagonistic to the experiment design is advantageous in order to
detect signal variability not stemming directly from the task design. Research
into design-free experiments has yielded promising resting-state information and
has lead to the characterization of the default mode network.

Latent variable methods can be used to extract functional brain networks (latent
components) without knowledge of experiment-design but their application is
an ongoing challenge in fMRI. Two widely used approaches are decomposition
and clustering models.
This thesis will focus on latent variable decomposi-
tion methods and the open problem of determining the number of active latent
components, also called the intrinsic dimensionality. Solving this problem is
essential for providing interpretable networks and improving further analysis
as redundant information is discarded. We also investigate the eÔ¨Äect of het-
eroscedastic noise due to critique of poor noise models leading to erroneous and
irreproducible results in fMRI ([Eklund et al., 2012][Lund et al., 2006]).

The acquisition of functional MRI is closely related to that of MRI, a rough
explanation of how MRI and fMRI are acquired follows. As the human body is
largely composed of water it by extension contains a large amount of hydrogen
atoms.
Hydrogen atoms have one positively charged proton, which revolves
around its own magnetic axis.
Now by introducing a strong magnetic Ô¨Åeld,
denoted B0, the proton of the hydrogen atom will partially align along the B0-
Ô¨Åeld. This equilibrium position can then be distributed by a radio frequency
(RF) wave, perturbing (changing) the net magnetization of the proton away
from its equilibrium at B0. Now when switching oÔ¨Äthe RF pulse the proton
will gradually return to its equilibrium at B0, emitting a measurable radio
wave in the process. The emitted signal has a frequency proportional to the
magnetic Ô¨Åeld strength of B0, known as the Larmor Frequency [Hanson, 2009].
The emitted signal forms the basis of MR imaging and by measuring the time
between an RF pulse and the emitted signal, a contrast signal can be measured.
To get a spatial location of this signal, gradient coils are introduced providing
linear variations in the magnetic Ô¨Åeld strength of B0, providing the MR signal
a frequency and phase encoding used to generate a MR image.
The BOLD response or contrast comes from measuring changes in the MR-
signal following changes in the oxygenation level of blood (haemoglobin). Due
to the physical properties of blood, that oxygenated blood is diamagnetic while
deoxygenated blood is paramagnetic properties. These magnetic eÔ¨Äects can be
detected by the MR signal, producing a measure of the level of oxygen in the
measured areas. The resulting image is referred to as a BOLD fMRI image,
which we will simply denote as fMRI in this thesis.

1.2
Statistical Modelling in fMRI
Statistical modelling in fMRI has being going on for decades and was initially
conÔ¨Åned to parametric models and classical statistical signiÔ¨Åcance test. This
type of modelling assumes that voxels are independent and lead to so-called
mass-univariate modelling approaches. The General Linear Model (GLM) was
and possibly still is the most widely used statistical model in fMRI, with a
sound theoretical background and several well-developed hypothesis tests. But
the assumptions of GLM are violated and methods developed to correct any vi-
olations have been under critique. Recently [Monti, 2011] provided an overview
of the validity of assumptions, showing that violations introduce bias into the
estimates of variance, in turn aÔ¨Äecting test statistics, power calculation and
false positive rates in experiments. However, these problem are likely persist in
models were similar assumptions are made.
At the turn of the century a new way to model neurological data appeared and
has been growing in momentum ever since, namely the use of decomposition
methods to construct latent components of functional networks from observed
data. These methods represent fMRI as a linear mixture of functional networks
components, usually as brain maps of voxel loadings (latent variables).

Common methods include Principal Component Analysis (PCA) ([Andersen
et al., 1999]), which seeks to minimize reconstruction error by Ô¨Ånding the la-
tent components accounting for the most variance and Independent Component
Analysis (ICA) ([McKeown et al., 1997]), Ô¨Ånding latent components with maxi-

used for pre-whiting data before an ICA approach is used to Ô¨Ånd latent compo-

latent components, which is an open problem in fMRI modelling and latent

Another approach to identifying functional networks in fMRI is the use of clus-
tering or region-of-interest (ROI) methods. In clustering each network is based
on a prototypical voxel, usually deÔ¨Åned by the mean of voxels in the functional
network, see [Goutte et al., 1999] for a clustering application to fMRI. The ad-
vantages of this approach is its interpret-ability as mean voxels are close to the
observed, but suÔ¨Äers from the simplifying assumption of binary assignment, i.e.
a voxels only belongs to one network. In ROI or seed-based analysis a single
set of voxels is selected, the correlation coeÔ¨Écient between this set and the re-
maining voxels is then investigated. The aim is to see how the seed region is
connected to other parts of the brain. Disadvantages of ROI analysis is its sensi-
tivity to the selected region and that the region must be predetermined, thereby
limiting its ability to Ô¨Ånd the true underlying functional networks. [Poldrack,
2007] provides a detailed description and overview of ROI use in fMRI.
FMRI has traditionally been restricted to experiments with a small number
of participants having a low temporal resolution. However, the continued im-
provements of data acquisition, due to stronger magnetic Ô¨Åelds and more ad-
vanced scanning protocols. Is bringing about large datasets with high temporal
(milliseconds) and spatial resolution (sub-millimeters) [Feinberg et al., 2010].
The scale of data is futher increased by the rise of open data movements in
fMRI research, where the initiatives such as the Human Connectome Project
(cf.
[Van Essen et al., 2013]) aims to provide scannings of over a thousand
subjects.

while being a computational eÔ¨Écient approach. However it suÔ¨Äers from only
approximating a local optimum which might be far from the global optimum.

Factor Analysis (FA) and Principal Component Analysis are a lot alike, they

This turns out to be the eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which are the squared
variances and orthogonal directions of maximal variance in the data, respec-
tively. In FA the observed data is assumed to be aÔ¨Äected by heteroscedastic
noise and the factors, using PCA in this setting would distort the true latent
components. This similarity of PCA and FA is clear from the deÔ¨Ånition of FA
is,

Due to the wealth of matrix properties which can be imposed there exist an
enormous amount of diÔ¨Äerent FA scheme, and the Ô¨Åeld overlaps with that of
Matrix Factorization (MF), with the critical diÔ¨Äerence is MF usually assumes
homoscedastic while FA assumes heteroscedastic noise.

Results
In this chapter we investigate the develop methods and compare their per-
formance to Independent Component Analysis and Multi-Subject Archetypal
Analysis.
First we compare the development methods and their performance in case of
underlying homo- or heteroscedstic noise, then we asses their ability to identify
the intrinsic dimension of the data.
4.1
Synthetic Experiments
We will now compare performance of the developed methods on synthetic datasets,
against the following methods,
‚Ä¢ Mean-Field Independent Component Analysis (ICA-MF):
A Bayesian ICA proposed by [H√∏jen-S√∏rensen et al., 2002] where the la-
tent components are found through a variational mean-Ô¨Åeld with a linear
response correction [Opper and Winther, 2001].
‚Ä¢ Maximum-Likelihood Independent Component Analysis (ICA-
ML):
==PAGE:48==
38
Results
A Maximum likelihood implementation by [Nielsen, 2001] where the num-
ber of ICs can be determined using the Bayesian Information Criterion
[Hansen et al., 2001]. This algorithm is equivalent to the InfoMax by [Bell
and Sejnowski, 1995].
‚Ä¢ Archetypal Analysis (AA - Homoscedastic):
We use the implementation by [Hinrich et al., 2016] which for a single
subject with homoscedastic covariance is equivalent to the original AA
model proposed by [Cutler and Breiman, 1994].
‚Ä¢ Archetypal Analysis (AA - Heteroscedastic):
Single subject AA with heteroscedastic noise, again the model by [Hinrich
et al., 2016].
‚Ä¢ Probabilistic Principal Components (PPCA):
We use the PPCA implementation of Matlab 2015a, which is based on
the PPCA method proposed by [Tipping and Bishop, 1999]. A solution
is found by Expectation-Maximization which includes improvement pro-
posed by [Roweis, 1998], [Ilin and Raiko, 2010].
Both ICA methods assume homoscedastic noise and can be found in toolboxes
developed by the Cognitive Systems Section at DTU, see1. The parentheses con-
taining either homoscedsatic or heteroscedastic in connection with the methods
name indicates what type of noise the method models, not what type of noise
is present in the synthetic data.
All methods are known to be susceptible to local optima, so for a fair com-
parison all methods are run 10 times. For each method the run with highest
log-likelihood is selected.

5.1
Noise Modelling and Intrinsic Dimensionality
In synthetic experiments we showed that VB-PCA and VB-FA recovered the
intrinsic dimension of data, which was also true for Probabilistic PCA. The
dimensionality was recovered in synthetic experiments for both homo- and het-
eroscedastic noise. This recovery was observed in two settings, one where the
intrinsic dimension was known equal to the latent dimension, and another where
it was and unknown.
In case of known intrinsic dimensionality, all the tested methods except PPCA
recovered 3 or 4 out of 4 true components, in both noise settings. The best
result in case of homoscedastic noise was achieved by the AA model assuming
homoscedastic noise, showing almost exact recovery of the subspace covered
by the true sources, closely followed by VB-PCA and VB-FA. With underly-
ing heteroscedastic noise the performance of AA-Homo. and AA-Hetero. were
equal, but lower than VB-PCA and VB-FA. VB-PCA was slightly aÔ¨Äected by
the heteroscedastic noise, while VB-FA remained unaÔ¨Äected.
When the intrinsic dimensionality was treated as unknown duplicate compo-
nents were found by both AA, both ICA and the PPCA method. The correct
intrinsic dimensionality were determined by PPCA, VB-PCA and VB-FA.

Discussion
Our results show that in synthetic experiments the dimensionality can be identi-
Ô¨Åed regardless of a homoscedastic or heteroscedastic modelling approach. How-
ever, more exhaustive testing should be done to understanding why VB-PCA
works so well even with heteroscedastic noise. We suspect one can Ô¨Ånd a noise
threshold where VB-PCA will fail while VB-FA wont, which would be of inter-
est to inorder to determine situations were simpler VB-PCA approach works
equally well.
Applying the methods in a real setting to a block-design task-experiment we
found that the ARD-prior was unable prune inactive latent components, making
determining the intrinsic dimension impossible. Choosing a Gamma-prior with
broad hyper-parameters kept inactive latent components active as the values of
‚Üµwere made to exhibit a uniform distribution. To re-mediate this we suggested
inferring the hyper-parameters of the ARD-prior, enabling faster pruning of
inactive latent components, as they would not be kept artiÔ¨Åcially "alive" by the
preset hyper-parameters. Another approach would be to use a diÔ¨Äerent prior,
such as a non-informative JeÔ¨Äreys prior as suggested by [Guan and Dy, 2009].
[Nielsen, 2004] previously applied a VB-FA model to fMRI data from a visual-
design experiment, and were not able to infer the intrinsic dimension precisely.
Their use of individual hyper-parameters for ‚Üµand less broad ARD-prior con-
tributed to a more successful pruning. Further, we note that the experiments
in this thesis are an order of magnitude larger in both voxels and timesteps,
contributing to a much slower deactivation of inactive components.
We showed that heteroscedastic noise is present in fMRI, especially in areas
close to the ventricles. Modelling heteroscedastic noise is essentially to learn
the scale of each voxel. So if the heteroscedastic noise was known, it could be
used to scale the data as a preprocessing step and then similar results would
be found by a homoscedastic model. Unfortunately the heteroscedastic noise is
unknown.
A major beneÔ¨Åt of heteroscedastic noise models in general, is they provide the
model with robustness to outliers, as dimensions/voxels with high noise variance
have low impact on the objective function. This in-turn gives a more stable
estimate of model parameters.

Local optima are an ever persistent challenge in probabilistic models (and op-

solutions by comparing their shared subspace. The degree to which two solu-
tions overlap were determined by the RV-coeÔ¨Écient, which gives a scale and

We showed that VB-FA implementation consistently Ô¨Ånds subspaces of high
similarity, given random initializations within the same number of latent di-

runs with diÔ¨Äerent number of latent components would also be interesting. This
could be used to investigate if the identiÔ¨Åed intrinsic dimensionality of the data
is consistent when varying the starting criterion. But due to the ineÔ¨Éciency of
the ARD process, this has not been possible.
5.3
The Challenge and Promises of Sparse PCA
Starting out optimistic, we know that sparse PCA provides superior inter-
pretability on fMRI to regular PCA and its performance is comparable to that of
ICA [Sj√∂strand et al., 2006]. We also noted that it provides a bridging of latent
variable and seed-based (Region-of-Interest) methods. A variational Bayesian
approach to SPCA would allow elegant handling of uncertainties in the pa-
rameters, more robust to outliers, treatment of missing values and of-course
automatic relevance determination in both component and voxel space. The
X = SCX factorization also provides an interesting coupling in multi-subject
fMRI analysis, with subject-speciÔ¨Åc latent components CX(b) based on a shared
seed generation C, and subject-speciÔ¨Åc latent variables (brain-maps) S(b), as
shown by [Hinrich et al., 2016].
Unfortunately we were not able to deliver on this, but it is our Ô¨Årm belief that
the eluding factor is based on numerical precision issues, but have been unable
to resolve within the time-frame of this thesis.
We note that our factorization of Q(C) = Qq

T ‚á•T matrix, which is prohibitively expensive when modelling multi-subjects
by concatenation in the time domain. There are two approaches to mitigate

5.4
Future work
There are multiple future avenues of research and here list those we expect to
be fruitful (in no particular order),
‚Ä¢ Bayesian Sparse Principal Component Analysis:
Investigating its applicability to fMRI modelling as well as a multi-subject
approach similar to MS-AA with subject-speciÔ¨Åc archetypes generated
from shared seed components.
‚Ä¢ More realistic multi-subject modelling:
Properly accounting for inter-subject viability is crucial to discover shared
latent components between subjects. We suggest extending the VB-FA
with subject-speciÔ¨Åc spartial S maps but shared latent generators, while
accounting for subject-speciÔ¨Åc mean and noise.
‚Ä¢ Investigating diÔ¨Äerent ARD-priors:
As repeatedly mentioned, we expect a JeÔ¨Ärey prior to provide a more
active pruning of the inactive latent components, inspired by [Guan and
Dy, 2009]. Other priors could also be investigated, but we note that the
hyper-parameters of any ARD-prior should be inferred as well, to avoid
initial settings keeping inactive components "alive"
‚Ä¢ Complex noise processed for latent variable methods:
Here we are intrigued by auto-regressive processes, but other noise pro-
cesses that account for noise in the temporal domain are also of interest.
‚Ä¢ Scale to thousands of subjects: As the volume and viability of fMRI
dataset increases so does the need to scale to thousands of subjects. In turn
this leads to the need for multiple-GPU computing or cluster computing.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In thesis we have investigated the origin of Bayesian Principal Component Anal-
ysis (B-PCA) its relation to Bayesian Factor Analysis (B-FA). We demonstrated
how moving the Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) in B-PCA leaves its
modelling capacities unchanged and enables a fast Variational Bayesian (VB)
approach to B-FA (VB-FA). The models were implemented in Matlab exclu-
sively using matrix-matrix multiplications providing a scalable implementation
for both CPU and GPU computation. The developed VB-FA algorithm has
time and space complexity O(q3 + q2T + q2V ) and O(V T + qT), respectively.
Initially B-PCA and B-FA were tested on a synthetic dataset against other
methods, showing they achieved comparable or superior performance. We also
showed the models ability to identify the corrected intrinsic dimensionality of
the data using an ARD-prior.
Next the developed models were tested on real fMRI data, a block-design motor-
task experiment with 29 participants. In this setting the ARD prior was not
able to identify the intrinsic dimensionality, which we attribute to the choice
of prior and lack of hyper-parameter inference. We suspect that using a non-
informative JeÔ¨Äreys prior instead could mitigate this problem. The discovered
functional brain networks resembled well known brain areas such as the left-
right sensorimotor cortex, visual cortex and part of the default mode network.
But the results were ambiguous as the ARD prior did not prune superÔ¨Çuous
==PAGE:86==
76
Conclusion
latent components. The results were compared with Group-ICA and MS-AA.
Both approaches clearly captured the experiment design but had no way of
identifying the intrinsic dimensionality.
We conclude that Group-ICA and MS-AA outperform the proposed VB-FA
model on real fMRI data. However, the VB-FA method did not model inter-
subject variablity of the components nor underlying noise processes.
A worthy attempt to develop a VB Sparse-PCA model was made. The attempt
was to bridge the interpretable components of MS-AA, eÔ¨Écient representation
of Group-ICA and Ô¨Çexible assumptions of FA/PCA. Furthermore, we sought
infer the model parameter utilizing the fast variational Bayesian framework.
But this attempt was unsuccessfully due to its complexity, numerical instability
and a lack of time.
Finally we note that while VB-FA may not always Ô¨Ånd interpretable compo-
nents, it can serve as a useful method to Ô¨Ånd the correct subspace, from which
further analysis can be made.
