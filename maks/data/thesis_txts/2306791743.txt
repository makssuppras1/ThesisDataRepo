ABSTRACT 
The Danish construction industry have serval challenges with productivity and clarity on 
project, where quality of the project design is varying. As BIM has a beneficial effect on 
project, and support a clearer project basis, is it essential to optimize the utilization of BIM. 
Since models is the core of the BIM, such an optimization would be in upgrading the quality of 
the models. 
The thesis focus area was utilization of BIM models in a VDC project, with the main focus on 
the tender estimation phase. Two two side of a problem with the current process of model 
validation: 
First problem is the time used on model validation, which consumes about 20-30% of the total 
modeling time. This is a lot of working hours, which costs a large amount of money. In 
addition to that, there are still lack of quality in the BIM models, and this is a general thing 
for the industry.  
The second problem is the way of using VDC (Virtual Design Construction) in a company, 
requires continuous correct models much earlier in the project phases. In addition to that, is 
that tender phases are commonly short in periods.  
A shortening in the time used on developing and improving the model, gives the tender team 
an extra gap of time to estimate a more precise project where risks are less. 
 
An alternative method of validation of method was developed and descried in the thesis, which 
is based on the two fundamental study areas: 
 
The requirements to high quality models in a VDC project, based on interviews, 
theoretical research, workshops and model analyzes. 
 
The method of an alternative process, based on studies from other industries (Test 
Driven Development), literature research and software testing. 
The research resulted in the development of the Process of Continuous Automatic Model 
Validation, which both focus on process and content to the method. The method works by 
continuously pre-checking and correcting the model, while modeling the model in the original 
software. Furthermore two test cases was carried out, where one of them had this process 
implemented, while the other used the common method of validation. The results showed a 
reduction from 4 to 1 hour in time used on validation, which indicates an acceptable process 
with clear improvement. Challenges and concerns using the method was further described and 
discussed.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Integration of digital technology is changing the world, and with it various industries. One of 
them is the construction industry, where drawings and information exchange are being 
optimized and changed to technological methods.  
BIM (Building information models) are more and more common in projects, and models are 
being basis for different tasks and services. Tasks as model based estimation, simulations, 
visualization, 3D print and even the newer technologies as VR (Virtual Reality). This changes 
the requirement for model quality, which depends on the purpose the model is created for. For 
that there is a need for a quality control and management, resulting in a model with a specific 
quality.  
Definition of quality and quality management is described by PMI (Project Management 
Institute) as following (Project Management Institute, 2004):  
 
Quality: The degree to which the project fulfills requirements. 
 
Quality Management: It includes creating and following policies and procedure in 
order to ensure that a project meets the defined needs intended. 
1.1 PROBLEM STATEMENT  
Today the validation of models happens in the end phase of the modelling process. After many 
hours of modelling, the designers go through a model validation process. It is estimated (by 
MT Højgaard BIM office in Vietnam) that the model validation time, consumes about 20-30% 
of the whole modelling time (Berard, 2016). This is a lot of working hours, which cost a large 
amount of money. 
In addition to that, BIM models still lack quality, and this is a general thing for the 
Construction industry. While some models may require many hours of work, the human factor 
may also play a role in the results of errors. 
There are two sides of a problem with the current process of model validation, wherein the 
first is the above mentioned with the large consumption of working hours. The second problem 
is the way of using VDC (Virtual Design & Construction) in a company, requires continuous 
correct models much earlier in the project phases. 
Workshops are held continuously with the use of the models, where the information in the 
elements must correspond with the project specific requirements and in a correct model or it 
may negatively affect the project meetings. This process changes the modelling time to shorter 
periods, yet the models does not need to have the high level of detail.  
Depending on the phase of the project, the information and details of the models can vary to 
the specific VDC task.

1.2 THESIS COVERAGE 
The thesis will cover the process of continuous model validation, as an alternative to the 
common way. The process is analyzed, while method, consequence and challenges are 
described. The use of automatic model validation would further be looked at as a main method 
of the continuous model validation process. Solution suggestions for the process, rulesets, 
contents and changes the method will contribute are described and analyzed. 
The thesis theoretical part will also cover the Test-Driven Development, which is a method 
that is used in the software development. The method is an open source test system, which 
keeps testing the codes "live" until the code works after described rules. The idea of 
implementing a similar system to the modelling will be described. Furthermore, the BIM 
quality control software's, and the general quality of models will be analyzed for the use in the 
VDC process. 
A study case of the effect of using the automatic technology in the process, where there will be 
tests and performance measurements on a project using Continuous Automatic Model 
Validation (CAMV), is also part of the scope of this thesis. The same project, with the same 
modelling disciplines in two phases, where the first follows the standard model validation in 
the end of the process, and the second follows continuous automatic model validations between 
each modelling discipline. The process, differences, consequences and time consumed will be 
analyzed and discussed. 
The conclusion will add the perspective of the above-mentioned points as an overall process, 
the efficiency, dilemmas and the "perfect way" such a process can work. 
As the requirement for models can vary from project to project, the focus of the models 
application will be on the tender phase. In VDC projects MT Højgaard works with iTWO, a 5D 
software that is used for QTO (Quantity Take-Off), Estimation and Simulation in tender 
phase.

2 HYPOTHESIS 
This thesis addresses the aspect of changing the model validation method, as an alternative or 
addition to standard validation method. The use of Continuous Automatic Model Validation 
could benefits the output of higher quality of models that helps the contractor to a more 
accurate estimation with lesser risk. Furthermore it is expected to have shorter design and 
validation phases of BIM models, at the same time as increasing the quality. 
Higher quality of models may also contribute transparency of the project basis, which all 
stakeholders may benefit from. 
Developing a Process of the Continuous Automatic Model Validation is the goal of this thesis, 
so it possible to further develop an implement in future work.

4.2 ITWO 
MTH uses different software to estimation, quantity take offs (QTO), model validation, 
simulation etc. however iTWO have been chosen to be the main software for VDC projects.   
“iTWO is a fully integrated solution spanning the whole lifecycle of asset creation projects, with 
an interactive Building Information Model (BIM) based graphics process that integrates 
planning and execution phases from end to end.  
iTWO integrates traditional estimating, packaging, controlling and execution, revolutionary 5D 
capabilities (3D Integrated model + time + cost) and project collaboration into a single software 
platform.” (RIB Software, 2016)   
iTWO was initially a calculation software for tender estimation and construction economic 
control, which was later developed to have the functionality of model based estimation. MTH 
only uses iTWO to estimate, when it is a BIM model to the project, so the estimate can be 
model based. 
As seen on Figure 3. iTWO Process the model is the starting point of the project, and from the 
model the QTO, estimate and calculation for bidding are made. This make it necessary that 
the model is correct, or the estimation would be incorrect.

6.1.1 Typical Errors and Problems when Modelling  
 Typical Errors in Models  
In the first part of interviews the interviewees were asked about typical errors and problems 
with BIM models, so it was possible to get an overview over the repeating errors that show up. 
The interviews transcriptions can be found in the Appendix section 16.3 Interviews 
transcription 
Three problems were mentioned by four out of the five interviewees as common problems with 
models, while the fifth indicated the same problems in the other questions: 
 
Data errors and inconsistency: Missing or wrong classifications on elements, data 
in the wrong properties and different naming of objects. Some of these data errors 
would have showed up if there were a good QA on the models, but can be challenging if 
there for example are 5 different interdisciplinary models, and each of the designer 
have put the data in a different property. 
 
Coordination and collisions: Models ware not coordinated and collisions were very 
common between interdisciplinary models, and even in a single model. Collision of 
models may be very hard to correct in a late phase, and in some projects a whole new 
redesign of interdisciplinary systems such as ventilation may be needed because of the 
large number of errors. 
 
Geometric errors: When two objects occupies same space. This is necessarily not the 
same as a collision, but more of a soft clash. Geometric errors can also be incorrect form 
of elements. 
These errors would all be possible to have been avoided by rule based checks, if they were run 
before handing the model to tender or execution. Different designers may use different type of 
rule checks, and while architects, engineers or other designers focus the checks on something 
specific the contractor focus on constructability on the site. According to the interviewed VDC-
Consultant a uniform ruleset could help on such type of problems, where different 
stakeholders can get a better understanding of quality of models.  
Splitting of elements which is a problem that showed up various times during the research on 
modelling problem was also mentioned and confirmed by interviewed Tender Manager and 
System Consultant. This is especially a problem for the tender estimation, as prices can 
changes depending on the method of constructing which is affected by the size of the elements.

6.3.1 LOD and LOI 
As a part of specifying element geometry and information in components on different levels, as 
the requirements can vary depending on purpose, project specifications, phase, type of models 
and others, MTH defines the components in a  Building component catalog.  
The purpose for the catalog is described as following (MT Højgaard, 2016): 
• To assist project participants in our construction projects to define the content to be 
included in a BIM delivery, and to get a clear picture of the contents of a BIM delivery. 
• To help design managers and BIM coordinators in MT Højgaard to communicate the 
information to be included in a BIM model at a given time. 
• To provide a reference for contracts and project’s BIM strategy. 
The latest version of the catalog (March 2016) defines LOD (Level of development) as both the 
geometry as LOD (Level of detail), and properties as LOI (Level of information). The 
components are defined in different levels, where LOD100 is the lowest and LOD500 is the 
highest. It should be noted that a higher LOD level also includes the properties from the lower 
levels.

6.3.2 Modelling guidelines 
MTH has formulated BIM modeling practice in a form of 12 guidelines, which aims to improve 
the BIM model for a better utilization. Some of the rules are basic modelling techniques, while 
some others have some requirements that when fulfilled contributes to a better management 
of building projects.  
The 12 guidelines are translated from the original best BIM modelling practice (MT Højgaard, 
2015): 
1. The models consists of objects 
2. Identical coordinate system, gridlines and reference points in all models 
3. BIM objects must be used for their purpose 
4. No overlap of objects 
5. No duplication of objects for the same purpose 
6. Object naming must be consistent 
7. Drawings are retrieved from the model 
8. Models have properties 
9. Models are divided for production 
10. Objects are connected to the correct floor 
11. The models have roomobjects with information 
12. Objects have a classifications code  
The previous studies and analyzes shows that a variety of the issues would have not occurred 
if the guidelines was followed, such as for example the error described in section 6.2.3 
Modelling errors by simple review, where objects are connected to the correct floor (practice 
10). Most of the guidelines are also ruleset in the SMC, as a part of the BIM model validation 
which is described in section 11.1 Design QA in MTH Vietnam Office.  A focus would be on 
creating rulesets that checks the above, so the errors can be found in a pre-checking in the 
model. This would contribute to less errors, and improvement in the quality of the models as 
studies have proved in BIM based pre-checking systems (Shin, et al., 2015) (Zuppa, et al., 
2009).

7 TENDER ESTIMATION AND MODELS 
Research was conducted regarding the challenges with models based calculation and finding 
out which requirements that should be for the information in the model, so the tender 
estimator could make best use of the model when making a tender estimation.  
There are to sources for this research: 
 
The interview part from the tender estimators, who were asked what information that 
was need to have in a model 
 
Information observed and gathered from talking with tender estimators and managers, 
when it was possible to ask them in different occasion.  
7.1 CHALLENGES AND UNCERTAINTIES 
It was clear that tender estimators did not have one specific way of calculating a tender, they 
had in fact several different methods of estimating. While some calculate in pieces of elements, 
others used area or volumes. This was also reflected in the various ways some of the BOQ was 
built. Measurement rules, the stage of the design or tender and the experience of the tender 
had all influence on the estimation method. Furthermore the supplier that some of the 
material is sentfor pricing for (as for example concrete walls), measure and prices in their own 
way. This could be gross/net area of walls and slabs, area of slabs including or excluding 
grouts or when to include cut. All this contribute to challenge in mapping a specific 
requirements when pricing, and standardization of a BOQ method. 
Secondly there is mistrust to the models from the tenders, which have roots in various 
reasons: 
 
Modelling customs that do not match the desired purpose the estimators want. This 
can be errors as mentioned in previous chapters, which would result in wrong 
quantities and prices. And can also be that the model is not build as an estimator 
prices the project, which wouldn’t necessary mean that the model is bad. Such 
problems lay in the purpose and requirement to the model. 
 
According to the interviewed Tender manager, different measurement rules, can give 
big quantities difference. As a QTO of elements may take exact area or volume of an 
element, the element could include grouts or other material connection that one should 
be aware of. It is estimated that depending on what such element contains, and which 
measurement rule that is used – the quantities can differs between 5-10%.  
 
Many tender estimators and managers have many years of experience of tender 
estimating, by manually finding quantities by ruler and a simple calculator or spread 
sheet they have created. It is very hard to change such a method which they trust, to 
using software that output such quantities by “the click of a bottom”.  
 
Finally, what was mentioned by both tender estimators in the interviews, was 
understanding the quantity output. It is important to understand the model, and what 
the quantities represent from the model.

8 PARTIAL CONCLUSION 
The research of models quality shows that are challenges regarding the use of model based 
projects, where some of them may be solved by simple checks and remodeling or correction 
while others is derived from working method that is engrained in the industry.  
Some of these problems may be solved by higher requirements for projects and models, such as 
BOQ and model most be built with the same breakdown structure, elements most be divided 
as constructed on site and levels of geometry and information. While others may need a 
change in industrial level, as for example standardization of BOQ, agreements about the 3D 
models is the basis of the quantities and drawings, and the time for design, tender estimation 
and procurement. The use of VDC projects may contributes to some of these changes, both on 
industrial level and on the agreements for requirements of models and projects.  
As some errors that contributes to these challenges can be solved, a list of these must compiled 
and prioritized to be solved by automatic model validation. This should be errors that can be 
translated to a computer language in form of ruleset, so validation software can find the errors 
in an early phase. It must be mentioned that some of these ruleset already exists in model 
checking software as SMC, Naviswork and such. 
From a VDC project perspective analyses shows that the geometry must be correct, 
classification system or naming system must be valid and connected to a BOQ if possible, 
location, rooms, materials must be in the models and the elements in general must be build in 
a correct way by connection and similar for correct quantities and estimation. These general 
problem can be solved rulebased, but a method must be setup for that. The next chapters will 
clarify how this is done as a process, the Continuous Automatic model validation system, 
challenges and contribution to the industry of using that.

10 VALIDATION IN OTHER INDUSTRIES (TEST DRIVEN DEVELOPMENT) 
This section describes a validation method that is used in the software coding sector, which 
was some of the inspiration to the development of the automatic continuous model validation. 
The method is called TDD (Test Driven Development), and is developed by Kent Beck. (Beck, 
2002) 
The TDD differs from the standard method of coding, by continuously implementing partial 
testing and coding of partial code of the whole system.  
Before TDD the natural way of the software development process was by creating a design 
which consists of various codes, implementing it and then testing it as illustrated on Figure 
40. Software Development Process. 
 
Figure 40. Software Development Process 
This method had challenges, especially in the test phase. As a design or a system is built of 
various codes that are dependent, as the codebase grows bigger the more and more fragile will 
it be. A code can contain a bug, which would need to be located and fixed in the code base if 
there are errors in the system. This method resulted in the tests phase took a large amount of 
time, and schedule time for testing was usually overrun.  
The worst case scenarios were with regression bugs, which is when something used to work 
stop working because of some new feature or bug fix. This required manual testing, that takes 
very long time as all part must function.  
While system products grow bigger, the amount of dependencies grows – which results  in the 
testing phase actually is not testing, but a “finding bugs and fixing” phase. In additional to 
that cost of change raises and can get high, when the design and code base grows. See Figure 
42. Cost of Change With and Without TDD . 
The TDD development process works by designing the systems by writing tests, where a test 
for every code is carried out with a pass/fall output. This would results in loops of testing and 
designing before implementing. The process is illustrated on Figure 41. TDD Process: 
 
Figure 41. TDD Process 
By running such an iterative design, it makes the system easier to test, locate and fix bugs in 
the codes. As long bugging codes are harder to fix, because new bug may rely on the bug 
behavior from an earlier code, which will make it necessary to break up in the dependencies 
until the error is found. This is not needed in the TDD as the development of the codes have 
not continued, before the previous code has passed the test.

The description on the process is as following: 
 
Write a test: A test is created/written, with rules regarding when to pass and when to 
fail. The test must fail in first run, to be sure it works.  
 
Run the test: A check if the test pass the criteria, where the output is fail or pass.   
 
Fail: If the test does not pass, then the code is changed, by trying to fix the failure code 
and send back to “Run the test”.  
As mentioned earlier the tests should be run continuously, so the code in itself is small 
and any big change would be regarded as a new code.  
 
Pass: If the test passes, there code must be checked for refactoring if possible. 
 
Refactoring: When the test are passed, there should be checked if the code can be 
optimized and improved by cleaning the code. 
o Yes: The code is improved by minimal change and send back to “Run the test”. 
o No: A new code can be added to the system, and the development can continue. 
 
All tests: After codes are developed, the system must be tested as a whole system. 
When every function works and have been tested, it is expected to give lesser bugs for whole 
system than the previous mentioned method where all system is written the tested.

early, reduce the time and cost of change, get rid of extensive time for correcting systems and 
the necessity to act on errors in an early stage are the ideas that CAMV got from TDD.

12 CONTINUOUS AUTOMATIC MODEL VALIDATION - CAMV 
12.1 INTRODUCTION OF THE IDEA 
The idea of the continuous automatic model validation comes from the need for better models 
in earlier phases, while shortening the overall modelling time. The need and desired results 
can be described as following: 
 
Higher Quality: The need of better models, as the model has to be correct in both 
geometry and attributes. From the tender estimators perspective there is mistrust to 
models, brought about by various reasons described in earlier sections. A generation of 
better quantities may change that. Furthermore when using model based estimation, 
errors in information or geometry contributes to incorrect quantities and pricing.  
The research conducted in chapter 6.1 Quality of models, indicated that there indeed 
are errors in models that need to be addressed. 
 
Time for Validation: There are two aspects of time used on validation of models.  
o Current validation method including correcting errors, is estimated to take 
about 25-30% of the whole modelling time in the MTH BIM Department 
(Berard, 2016). This time is desired to be shortened.   
o As VDC projects may require part deliveries or earlier deliveries for models, 
time pressure can be a challenge. This may be a factor for either a less thorough 
validation phase where some errors may not be corrected because of delivery 
time, or exceeding time schedule because of unexpected errors which first shows 
up in the final phase of the validation process. 
From the TDD method that is described in section 10. Validation in Other Industries (Test 
Driven Development), the idea of CAMV was developed, where continuous iterative checks 
contribute to better system (in this case models) and an overall shorter design phase. Even if 
the many checks may seem to take more time, they will contribute to a shorter validation and 
correcting phase in total and result in an earlier delivery. Figure 46. Validation Methods 
Timeline, illustrates on the top how the current modelling method takes place, while the 
bottom timelines illustrates how the CAMV method would look like.

12.5 RULESETS, AND SCRIPT PACKAGES 
12.5.1 Runtime for Ruleset 
The continuous model validation method requires running checks much more frequently, 
instead of few times in the end of the modelling phase. Because there is a large number of 
rulesets for model checking, this can be very heavy to run all ruleset gathered constantly. To 
create an optimized flow for running different ruleset check, the rulesets are divided into 
packages.  
There are various basis ideas for this: 
1. The research done in previous chapters and interviews showed that there are some 
type of errors that are more common than others, and those are the first rulesets to 
focus on and to continuous check. 
2. Some of these errors are bigger problems than others. To prioritize time used on 
checking errors, the most commonly errors are checked more frequently than others.  
3. A structural overview over the rulesets, which can be monitored and analyzed.  
4. Creating a flow of model check that focus on the severity of errors, with time and 
incidence of running checks. See 12.5.2 Ruleset PDCA/PDSA 
5. Continuously running check on something specific, and correcting the errors makes the 
BIM designer aware of the errors before it happens.  
6. Better performance, as fewer scripts are run on the same time. 
The validation checks are divided in to package groups, where every ruleset package group is 
run to between specific runtime.  A suggestion to the packages (RS.1-3), and the rulesets that 
contains is illustrated on Figure 50. Ruleset Package groups.

12.5.2 Ruleset PDCA/PDSA 
With inspiration in PDCA (Plan, Do, Check, Act) or also called PDSA (Plan, Do, Study, Act), a 
plan should be set for monitoring ruleset packages and optimizing them. As the Ruleset 
packages are created as suggestions with the basis of the research that is described in the first 
part of the thesis, the packages can be optimized when there have been tested on a larger 
number of models. 
PDSA is a 4 step cycle circle used continuous improvement, by quality controlling in loops. The 
iterative way of continuous quality control, brings the output closer and closer to the desired 
goal. PDSA is used in various industries as quality control managing tool, and was extended 
from a 3 step cycle to the following and made popular by Quality managing guru Dr. W. 
Edwards Deming. Figure 51. PDSA Cycle, shows the PDSA cycle idea.  
 
Figure 51. PDSA Cycle (The W. Edwards Deming Institute, 2016) 
 
As seen in the Figure 61, there are 4 steps in this process: 
 Plan: Plan how to carry out the cycle, the objective of the cycle and what the expected 
output is. 
 Do: Carry out the plan, and implement the design. Data, problems, errors and other 
observations must be documented.  
 Study: Analyze the data, compare results from the execution to the expected output. 
Summarize what was learned. 
 Act: Act and change the needed in the process and plan, so the next cycle gets closer to 
the desired output. (Moen, 2009) 
This system can be implemented in the continuous model validation, to have the best ruleset 
packages with the best flow when modelling.   
The rulesets which gives most frequent errors should be checked daily. While ruleset with a 
lesser frequency should be checked weekly and ruleset with minimum number of error 
frequency every third week. An example how this would look like, is shown in the Figure 52.

12.6 CHALLENGES IN THE INDUSTRY  
The continuous model validation method may have advantages and benefits, but there are 
several of challenges that may show up when implementing it. The following sections is 
conducted from the interview parts, regarding the challenge of implementing CAMV. 
12.6.1 The Software and Ruleset: 
As main test of the Dynamo software was done in a three week special course, a conclusion 
was described regarding the use of Dynamo as the CAMV tool: 
The use of automatic validation with Dynamo script makes it possible to rapidly test if the 
model has the correct data, but the limits are that a computer only does what it is setup to do.  
While SMC is a very popular software to use for model checking, the use of Dynamo have pros 
and cons as an alternative or additional use. Dynamo makes it possible to correct model while 
modelling, where Solibri outputs in reports. In some tasks the engineer, BIM coordinator or 
others may want a report as documentation.  
The challenge in DYNAMO is the constant update in both releases and user made packages, 
which because of the change in the “infrastructure” of the software may consequence in a 
script that stops working.  
When script is full working and all nodes are setup, it can save lot of time instead of manual 
work. This is especially relevant to a script that is used on a large number of projects, because 
developing scripts may take a lot of time. The time is depended of the desired output, must be 
proportional with the sequence of use. One must consider the time used on creating and 
maintaining the script, because if it is only used a few time then the time of creating the script 
may be higher than the time saved of using it. (Al-Nachar, 2016) 
There are of course different aspects of how much that can be checked, as there is a variety of 
problems that cannot be solved by automatic QA. This is for example if there is a 
miscommunication in the delivery, or the data for the rulesets checkup data are input 
wrongly. Such quality assurance must be validated manually, as for an example if all element 
serial numbers in project are connected to a classification. Furthermore some checks must be 
done visually (manually), because the human factor can be an uncertainty. 
12.6.2 The Architect, Engineers and Designers: 
There may be two aspects of the challenges with the architect and designers, where the first is 
time for designing a project and the other is the working method. Both method is related with 
each other, because the time pressure on project have affected the working method. 
As designers, engineers and architects may have a limited time for finishing work, 
continuously running scripts may disrupt the way of working. Furthermore a common way of 
working now is drawing, designing and postponing some errors for solving later – early 
continuous validation may force them to make decision or act early on. There may be no time 
for this in the design phase.

12.6.3 Tender Team: 
Challenges of using CAMV pertain more to general tendering/bidding practice than 
problematic challenges. Depending on the phase of the tender, the estimators diverge in their 
requirements to the model. As for the early stage, interviews alluded to rather want a model 
with lower quality earlier than a good model later.  
As in theory many checks seems like it will delay the model, which may frustrate the tender 
estimator if waiting for one.  This is especially in very early stages, where an estimator can 
give a relatively accurate price to the actual bidding price, from rough geometry of a project. 
That means correct details may be less relevant.   
In the detailed tender estimation with many different elements, a good model is important 
when making QTO and pricing. Errors found early can change the price drastic, depending on 
the type of errors. 
In regarding to these two point must be made: 
 
It is expected that CAMV will give an earlier end phase, even if there a more frequent 
checks as results showed in the tests. 
 
Continuous parallel modelling while tender team works with architects, may give a 
good trust to the model because of the ongoing communication. When mistakes are 
found, which are not BIM designers fault – the mistake must be communicated to the 
rest of the team, and solved by the architect, designer or other responsible. This gives 
the tender team a good overview of details in the project.

12.6.4 The Process and the Culture: 
One of the big challenges in changing the way of modelling, and the validation method is the 
indoctrinated culture around the use of 2D drawings and 3D models in the construction 
industry.  
It was observed by author that even if there are models where a lot of resources are used on, 
the agreement (ICT contracts) on the majority of the tender contracts says that the 3D models 
should not be the quantity giving basis. There have been seen examples of contracts like that, 
meanwhile the pricing quantities given from the client consultant was exactly the same 
quantities as the models. This seems like a general problem, especially in Denmark where the 
BIM models are not trusted – even from their designers.  
This was confirmed when interviewing, as the VDC Specialist indicated that the focus on 
delivering was the finish materials in 2D. The 2D materials are the legally binding basis, and 
are always checked and validated – for the reason of the above mentioned regarding contracts.  
This problem was however not in the steel models and industry, as the 3D model was the 
main material which was so detailed that the model was used on site in addition to working 
drawing and cutting plans are directly created from the models.  
An important point was made by the BIM Consultant regarding part hand in of models, which 
is one of the ideas with CAMV. Many people generally, may have some sort of perfection on 
their work. As a part design hand in, may seem like a “not finished” work, even if the elements 
in the work are error free. This could be compared to serving a food dish. One would rather 
serve the food dish finished, instead of people seeing how it is cook and prepared ongoing. 
In addition the CAMV may increase the dependencies and communication between different 
parts of the project, whether it is between BIM designer and tender estimator, architect and 
project manager or others.  
If the BIM designer finds missing information when running check, which also are missing in 
the project materials – he/she will need to contact the project manager and request this 
information. The same applies for missing information about elements splits, for the time plan 
simulation.  
This early back and forth communication would increase the understanding of the project 
which would have shown up later. However it may be a challenge to work on that way, if the 
responsible wants to work concentrated and focus while waiting with solving some of the 
problems in the end of the design phase. 
If the previous mentioned is set, there should not be a challenge with daily and weekly check, 
as long as there are a process with a clear method. Furthermore there must be a clear 
understanding of who are responsible of what.

14 CONCLUSION 
The study suggested an alternative method for validation of BIM models, as research showed 
that the industry still lacks in the quality of the models. The Continuous Automatic Model 
Validation method was developed from the idea of Test Driven Development, the requirements 
of VDC models and the construction industries need for a collaboration method that 
contributes to better construction projects. 
Some of the problems that the BIM models are affected of, can be solved by rule based pre-
checking, while others need to be solved by requirements, contractual agreements in form of 
ICT-agreements and coordination between respective designs responsible.  
Studies indicates that BIM have a beneficial effect on projects, while improvement and 
optimization of BIM models will result in a better project basis and a higher trust to the 
models. Such progress effects the construction industry to advance towards a higher 
utilization of BIM.  
The CAMV method of work will contribute that the designers to be extra aware of design and 
models flaw, and compel to deal with such issues in an early phase. This will save both time 
and money, while give a better project basis between different stakeholders on a project.  
The CAMV process and description are expected to be a basis for future validation of elements, 
with focus on VDC projects. The method is not expected to replace the common way of 
checking models after design finish, but may be an addition and support to it - that reduces 
time used on validation and correction of models, while results in a higher quality of models.

 
Where is the biggest risk in model based estimation? 
 
o Biggest risk if you trust you quantities to much – a press on a bottom and all the 
quantities are outputted.  
o You need to analyze the model, where you sit down and look what the model 
contains both geometric and attributes. 
 
 
Do you see any challenges by continuous model validation instead of the 
standard method? 
 
o It may seem like that it takes longer time to check, however it results in a project 
which have been check and validated early. One may find errors early on, which 
is much easier to solve early than later. In some projects you can give a relative 
close price to the actual price, from rough geometry of a project – that means 
correct details may be less relevant. This depends on tender phase. 
 
 
 
Will it optimize the modelling process? Will a tender estimator could make 
better use of correct a model that are continuously being updated, or is it 
better to wait for the finished product? 
 
o When calculating a tender, I would like to know which type of “house” I am 
calculating. An earlier rough model may be more desired for me, than a better 
model that is received later. This is because I can always change the detailed 
quantities, when reviewing the finished model later. It is however hard to say, 
because this may vary in project.

Other comment thought or inputs? 
 
o I like to get a model that is finished, where it is possible to QTO – but one must 
say that this is not always necessary as earlier mentioned.
