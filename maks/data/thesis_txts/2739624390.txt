Abstract
Nowadays waste-to-energy technologies have made it a priority to use sewage sludge
(SS) for the production of renewable energy due to the growing requirement to handle
larger volumes of SS in an energy-efficient manner. Using H2 from electrolysis and a
Low-Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed (LT-CFB) gasifier, this study examines
two different gasification approaches for turning SS into biomethanol. In case 1, an
oxygen blown LT-CFB is used and in case 2 an electrically heated LT-CFB. The research
assesses the syngas composition, process efficiency, heat requirements, and economic
feasibility. The results show that while the syngas yields were the same, the electrically
heated gasifier produced syngas of greater quality with 9% more H2 and 10.2% less
CO2 than case 1. The cold gas efficiency (CGE) of case 2 was better both before and
after partial oxidation (POX) (86.3% vs 82.2% before POX and 76.8% vs 71.4% after
POX), while the methanol efficiencies were found to be identical (80%).
The total
heat requirements for case 1 were found to be slightly higher as well as the necessary
electrolysis capacity (5 vs 3 MW H2). That was reflected on the total annualised system
cost, which was cheaper by 0.79mEUR24/y (approximately 9%) for case 2 in comparison
to case 1, resulting in a levelised cost of methanol (LCOx) cheaper by 0.07 EUR/kg in
case 2 (0.90 vs 0.83 EUR24/kg).

Introduction
As shown in figure 1 and reported in references [4, 5], the global population is growing
exponentially, and urban populations in developing nations are increasing at an annual pace
of roughly 3%. According to references [6, 7], this rapid expansion is expected to continue
along with the rise in industrialisation and living standards, imposing considerable strain
on natural resources, which are at risk of depletion in the near future. These reserves are
further depleted by the growing demand for fossil fuels, mainly driven by the expansion of
emerging economies. This emphasises the need for alternative energy sources.
Figure 1: World population growth 1950-2024 and projections to 2100
[8]
The volume of sewage sludge (SS), an inevitable by-product of running municipal
wastewater treatment plants (MWTP), is increasing, and disposing of it has adverse en-
vironmental consequences that make it a significant problem in many nations. Between 35
and 85 g of dry matter (DM) per person per day are estimated to be produced globally via
SS generation [9]. Figure 2 illustrates the yearly production in various countries. The Euro-
pean Union, represented by the EU27, has the largest annual generation at nine million dry
metric tons (mt). Germany provides more than half this volume, with an annual production
of 5.5 dry mt. With 6.514 and around 3 dry mt, respectively, China and the United States
produce the next-highest amounts of SS annually.
Figure 2: Sewage sludge annual production per country
[10]

Disposing of sewage sludge using traditional methods like landfilling and agricultural use
is no longer viable due to strict environmental regulations, limited land availability, and the
increasing prevalence of harmful contaminants like pathogens and heavy metals [11] [12]. A
comparison among the disposal practices used in Europe, the USA, and China is presented in
3. While land application and composting are the most common practices in Europe and the
USA, with over 50% utilisation, China’s main approach is improper dumping, constituting
more than 80% of its total disposed sludge. Landfilling and incineration are less common in
all regions, with Europe and the USA demonstrating moderate usage.
Figure 3: Sewage sludge disposal practices between Europe, USA and China
[13]
Water and wastewater treatment procedures (WWTPS) account for about 5% of green-
house gas (GHG) emissions, of which wastewater treatment (WWT) alone accounts for 70%
[14]. One effective way to reduce these emissions is to use thermochemical conversion, which
is used by an increasing number of facilities globally to treat SS and convert it into fuel
and energy [15]. This method can provide valuable products to assist in reducing resource
consumption and emissions from fossil fuels in addition to providing new sources of alter-
native energy. Thermochemical processes can also lead to significant volume reduction as
well as the recovery of essential nutrients and metals [16]. Figure 4 shows the potential SS
conversion routes.
Thermochemical conversion, an established method for high-grade coals, has been adapted
for use with lower-rank fuels like lignite and biomass, and many of these applications have
been made commercially accessible. Recently, interest in applying this technology to SS has
surged. While there has been experience with coal and biomass, sewage sludge provides
particular hurdles because of its specific features, such as high moisture, nitrogen, and heavy
metal concentration. These aspects need to be adequately considered to successfully harvest
energy from SS thermochemical reaction [13].
As illustrated in Figure 4, the gasification of SS presents multiple pathways, including
the production of liquid fuels. The world is currently dealing with two main issues: the de-
pletion of fossil fuel reserves and the acceleration of environmental degradation. Depending

on how they are produced, liquid fuels, also referred to as green fuels, biofuels, renewable
fuels, or alternative fuels, are made from renewable resources to lessen their negative effects
on the environment, especially regarding greenhouse gas emissions. These fuels, which in-
clude ethanol, biodiesel, biogas, hydrogen, methanol, and dimethyl ether, are necessary to
progressively replace fossil fuels and create a sustainable energy future. MeOH, often known
as methanol (CH3OH), is an essential raw material used in the chemical and pharmaceuti-
cal industries and in the formation of synthetic hydrocarbons [18]. About 90 million tons of
methanol are generated annually worldwide; 65% of this is obtained from natural gas through
steam methane reforming, and 35% is produced from coal through gasification processes [19]
[20]. The interest in producing methanol from renewable sources is rising quickly due to the
growing need for low-carbon fuels[21]. Currently, despite this interest, renewable methanol
production remains minimal, coming from biomass gasification and amounting to less than
0.2 million tons per year, or 0.2% of the overall output [19].
The aim of this study is to investigate the production of biomethanol via SS gasifica-
tion, which could almost eliminate sulfur oxide and particulate matter emissions and reduce
nitrogen oxide emissions by 60–80%, and carbon dioxide emissions by 60–95%, according to
Methanol Institute [22]. The ability to blend biomethanol with conventional fossil fuels like
gasoline or diesel makes it more appealing. In addition to being a fuel for transportation,
CH3OH demonstrates great versatility since it may be used as a feedstock for the synthesis
of other valuable chemicals and materials [22],[18].
For the purposes of the present analysis, a Low-Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed
(LT-CFB) gasifier is chosen to facilitate the synthetic gas production, which is then mixed
with renewable hydrogen in a methanol-synthesis plant to produce bio methanol production.

2.1
Sewage Sludge Treatment and Composition
The essential component of an urban wastewater infrastructure is the wastewater trea-
ment plant (WWTP). WWTPs process contaminated water that is collected from many
sources in a city, such as commercial, industrial, residential, and agricultural activities.
A complex mixture of materials, including sediments, detergents, medications, and differ-
ent contaminants, are present in this effluent. At every stage of the treatment process in
WWTPs, significant amounts of solid, semi-solid, or liquid SS are unavoidably produced
[23]. Volume and nature of the generated sewage sludge are influenced by the composition of
the incoming wastewater, but also by specific variations in the treatment processes of both
the wastewater and the sludge.
As zero waste production is not achievable, proper pre-treatment of SS should be man-
dated by law prior to its discharge. One of the water sector’s most energy-intensive operations
is the treatment of SS, which requires a substantial amount of heat and electrical energy
[24]. Though, there are efficient and eco-friendly ways to utilise it. Although WWT facilities
vary, they typically include primary, secondary, and preparatory treatment stages [10]. An
overview of the most popular treatment methods is shown in Figure 5.
When raw SS enters a wastewater treatment facility, it first passes through bar screens
to remove big debris. After the initial treatment, the organic matter is left suspended and
the grit and sand are separated in grit chambers [13, 25].
Grease and oil are separated by sedimentation in sizable tanks, where they accumulate
to a surface layer that is skimmed off. At the same time, the heavier solids produce primary

sludge by settling to the bottom of the tank. About 50–70% of the suspended particles in

Biological processes are used in secondary treatment to eliminate suspended particles
and stabilize organic materials. This is typically accomplished by combining sedimentation
with aerobic systems, such as rotating biological contactors, activated sludge, and trickling
filters. The resultant sludge is known as waste-activated sludge or secondary sludge. Water
can be further purified by adding tertiary treatment as a last step which focuses on nutrient
removal and improves the effluent’s quality even more [10, 13, 25].
SS is processed via a number of steps to lower its weight, volume, and hazardous con-
tent. Usually, this treatment entails dewatering, stabilization, and thickening. The main
objectives are to minimize the risk of environmental contamination, minimize odors, and
enable handling, transportation, and disposal while deactivating germs. stabilization meth-
ods, such as anaerobic digestion, can produce energy as a useful byproduct. Thickening
concentrates solids to 3–6% by applying techniques like flotation or gravity. Dewatering
uses techniques like centrifugation or filtration to further lower the water content to 10–30%.
Stabilization inactivates infections, lessens odors, and stabilizes organic matter. It can be ac-
complished physically, biologically, or chemically. The final sludge product applied depends
on the particular treatment techniques used [10, 13, 25].

The complex, water-rich material that makes up SS is made up of a variety of inorganic
and organic materials, including microorganisms. It resembles a Bingham plastic in terms
of consistency and behaviour. When it comes to composition, SS is far more variable than
resources like coal or conventional biomass because of factors like wastewater properties,
treatment methods, seasonal variations, and environmental regulations. Properties that can
vary significantly between sludge samples and even within the same plant over time, such
as moisture content and organic matter, are indicative of this variability. Therefore, unlike
other solid fuels, managing and processing sewage sludge calls for a customized strategy
[13]. Rulkens [27] states that the components of SS are divided into six groups: organic
matter (around 60% of dry weight), nitrogen and phosphorous compounds, toxic organic
and inorganic pollutants including heavy metals), pathogens, inorganic minerals and water.
Sewage sludge primarily consists of water (>90%) and suspended solids. Water exists

Proximate and ultimate analysis give critical information about a fuel’s composition
and behavior during thermochemical conversion. Proximate analysis indicates the relative
amounts of moisture, fixed carbon (FC), volatile matter (VM), and ash, whereas ultimate
analysis determines the elemental composition, which includes carbon (C), hydrogen (H),
oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), and sulfur. These analyses play an essential role in determining
a fuel’s compatibility for various thermochemical conversion processes. Calorific values are
also an important factor in determining the efficiency of SS conversion [29, 30, 31].
The composition of SS is notably different from that of traditional fuels such as coal and
biomass. Table 2 shows the proximate and ultimate analysis of SS and it is compared with
various fuels of coal (Lignite 1-5) and biomass (Wood pellet 1-2, Switchgrass, Hazelnut husk,
Sawdust) at table 24. SS has higher ash content and lower FC on average than coal and
biomass. On average, SS contains more ash and less FC than coal and biomass. The mix of
various coal and biomass fuels includes more VM than coal but less than biomass. SS has
a substantially greater VM-to-FC ratio, suggesting its ability to produce gaseous products.
Inorganic components, on the other hand, have the potential to increase ash content and
underestimate FC during analysis. The ultimate analysis found that it is notably rich in
N, mostly derived from proteins and other organic substances, and has a slightly greater H
content, distinguishing it from coal and biomass. Although the S and O concentration is
equivalent to lignite, it exceeds that of biomass, while the C value lies somewhere between
coal and biomass. The average higher heating value (HHV), or calorific value, for SS samples
ranges from 11.10 to 22.10 MJ/kg, according to a study of 32 values reported across 18
separate literature articles [13].

2.2
Gasification and Gasifier technologies
Gasification is a thermochemical process that transforms organic materials into a flammable
gas mixture largely made up of hydrogen, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, and light hy-
drocarbons. This process takes place at high temperatures (typically ≥800 −900° C) in
oxygen-deficient conditions and produces syngas or producer gas [13]. Choosing a gasifica-
tion agent, such as air, oxygen, or steam, has a considerable impact on syngas composition
and the process efficiency [33, 34].
Air gasification is cost-effective, however the syngas produced has a decreased calorific
value due to nitrogen dilution. Oxygen gasification produces a higher calorific value gas but
has higher expenses. Steam gasification enhances hydrogen generation and is often chosen
for Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, but it requires additional heat input [35, 36, 37, 38].
Syngas is a multipurpose energy carrier with applications encompassing power gener-
ation, fuel production, and chemical synthesis. It can be utilized directly in combustion
engines, gas turbines, and fuel cells or transformed into liquid fuels or chemicals using
techniques such as Fischer-Tropsch synthesis. Gasification transforms low-value carbona-
ceous materials into valuable products, promoting a more sustainable and circular economy
[39, 35, 36].
A gasification system typically has four operating stages or zones: drying, pyrolysis/de-
volatilization, oxidation, and reduction/gasification ([39]). The chapter will provide an ex-
tensive analysis of the individual zones and reactions related to sewage sludge gasification.
Choosing the right gasifier technology is critical for effectively conducting any gasifi-
cation project. To optimize the process, the feedstock properties, intended product, and
operational restrictions must all be carefully considered. Gasifier type, size, and operating
conditions have a considerable impact on syngas quality, energy efficiency, and overall system
performance. Identifying the right gasifier technology allows for optimal energy recovery, re-
duces environmental effects, and ensures project economic feasibility [40]. A representation
of the 12 major gasifier technologies is shown in Figure 19.
The various gasifier technologies in figure 19 are categorized into 4 main types: fixed bed
(updraught/downdraft), fluidize bed (bubbling/circulating), entrained flow and transport.
A review of 50 gasifier manufacturers in Europe, the United States, and Canada revealed
that 75% of the designs were downdraft fixed beds, 2.5% were updraft fixed beds, 20%
were fluidized beds, and 2.5% other types [41]. All four gasifying methods have relative

The updraft gasifier, named for its counter-current flow of biomass and gasifying agents
(such as air, oxygen, or steam), is one of the most basic types of gasification technology.
Biomass enters the reactor at the top and progresses through a succession of phases as it
descends. The material dries at 100°C before undergoing pyrolysis at 300°C, resulting in
char and gas production. The char reacts with the gasifying agent at high temperatures,
up to 1400°C, producing CO2 and H2O. The heated gases mix with the remaining biomass,
turning it into hydrogen (H2) and carbon monoxide (CO) as they drop to 400-750°C. The
gasses then exit the reactor as syngas which concludes the gasification process. Updraft
gasifiers usually handle biomass particles between 2 to 50 mm in size [2, 1, 41].
The downdraft gasifier is fed with the gasification agent (air, oxygen, or steam) at or
above the oxidation zone at the same time as the feedstock, and both move in the same
direction to the bottom. The particles and vapors produced in the pyrolysis zone (300°C)
react with the gasification agent at the "throat" under air pressure. The reaction occurs
at a temperature of approximately 1000°C. The acid and tarry feedstock components flow
through a charcoal bed, producing and collecting sygnas from the reactor’s bottom (°700).
The feedstock particle size ranges from 1 to 30 cm [2, 1, 41].

continuous flow of gas (usually air or steam). This fluidized state is obtained by injecting
gas at a sufficient velocity through the bed.

Bubbling fluidized bed (BFB) gasifiers are reactors that convert feedstock (in this ex-
ample, biomass) into gas. Air or oxygen is injected from the bottom, suspending biomass
particles in a fluidized bed. Intense mixing inside the bed allows for rapid heating and pyrol-
ysis, which produces char and gasses. These products further react, producing syngas. To
optimize operational conditions, the bed temperature is normally adjusted between 700°C
and 1000°C by altering the air/feedstock ratio. Ash control is critical to avoiding operating
concerns and an alternative bed material, such as alumina, can be employed to keep the ash
from softening and causing defluidization. The resultant syngas is recovered from the top of
the reactor. [2, 1, 41].
The circulating fluidized bed (CFB) gasifier is a cutting-edge gasification system that
operates by constantly circulating bed material and volatile gases between a reaction vessel
and a cyclone separator. In this process, the feedstock (in this example, biomass) is placed
into a fluidized bed of heated sand or other inert particles inside the reaction vessel. The

in coal gasification, with a significant majority of operational and planned projects worldwide
employing this method and therefore dominating the market.While entrained flow gasifica-

In a single-stage entrained flow gasifier, biomass and oxidizing agents (such as air, oxy-
gen, or steam) are pumped into the reactor at high speeds. The quick infusion results in
a very short residence period (1–5 seconds) while the biomass is gasified at high tempera-
tures, typically 700–1500 °C. The high temperature completes the conversion of biomass into
syngas, while any mineral matter in the feedstock is eliminated as slag [2].
The two-stage entrained flow gasifier enhances the single-stage design by dividing the
gasification process into two sections. In the first stage, biomass is gasified with superheated
gas, and in the second stage, steam and biomass are added to continue the gasification
process. This staged approach reduces exit temperature, increases syngas yield, and improves

less reactive feedstocks, guaranteeing high syngas quality while efficiently handling slag and

Notably, product gas quality varies significantly throughout different analyses. Several
essential parameters influence these features, including equivalence ratio (ER), steam-to-
biomass ratio (S/B), gas residence time, temperature, and the use of catalytic materials
[13].
The equivalency ratio (ER), which is defined as the ratio of actual to stoichiometric
air supplied, has a considerable influence on gasification results.
Low ERs (<0.2) lead
to incomplete combustion, resulting in more tar and char. Excessively high ERs, on the
other hand, promote complete combustion, producing more CO2 and H2O while decreasing
syngas production and quality.
Increasing ER in the 0.2-0.4 range improves gas output
while decreasing CO, H2, CH4, and total heating value. This is due to increased oxidation
reactions that generate CO2 at the expense of important syngas components. Higher ERs can
minimize tar production through combustion and thermal cracking, although optimization
is needed to balance tar reduction with syngas quality [13].
Steam addition greatly improves sewage sludge gasification compared to air. By raising
the steam-to-biomass ratio (S/B), hydrogen and carbon monoxide generation increase while
tar and char creation decline. This results in increased gas heating values and enhanced
cold gas efficiency. Steam contributes in important reactions (R12, R10, and R11), which
promote char conversion and reduce tar. Steam gasification is endothermic, therefore requires
external heat input or efficient heat recovery within the gasifier [13].
Increased gas residence time, achieved through factors such as reactor bed height, im-
proves char conversion efficiency in SS gasification. Longer exposure to high temperatures
promotes more thorough char gasification, resulting in larger syngas yields and better prod-
uct quality. Furthermore, increased residence time encourages secondary processes, such as
steam reforming of volatiles, further enriching the syngas with valuable elements such as
hydrogen [13].

cation by reducing tar and increasing process efficiency. According to studies, dolomite has

However, due to the complex composition of sewage sludge and the specific demands
of gasifier technologies, as discussed in the previous chapters 2.1 and 2.2, the gasification of
sewage sludge poses several challenges, such as tar in the outlet gas, high moisture content,
high N and S content, and high inorganics content [13].
The challenges that tar (hybrocarbon volatiles) poses in the gasifier can cause issues
like clogging, fouling, corrosion, and reduced efficiency in downstream processes if the gas
temperature drops below the tar dew point. That can affect the use of the producer gas
(heat/power generation/chemical production). Due to the fact that there are specific ac-
ceptable levels of tar in producer gas, its removal is essential for advanced applications.
That can be achieved through primary methods (within the gasifier), secondary methods
(post-gasifier) or a combination of both [13].
The high moisture content in SS can have a major influence on gasifier performance
and producer gas quality by increasing energy consumption and creating challenges with the
feeding feedstock process of sewage sludge into the gasifier. The latter may have a direct
impact on mass and heat transport during gasification. It is generally beneficial to keep the
moisture content below 30% to avoid energy losses from heating and water vapourisation.
Given that the ash content of SS could be at unfavorable levels (>50%), it is recommended
to examine moisture limitations on an ash-free basis [13].
The high nitrogen and sulfur levels in SS pose substantial issues during gasification due
to the higher potential of secondary environmental pollution. During the pyrolysis phase,
they are volatilized, resulting to the creation of hazardous compounds such as ammonia
(NH3), hydrogen cyanide (HCN), and hydrogen sulphide (H2S). Smog, acid rain, and ozone
depletion are all possible environmental consequences of combusting producer gas. Further-
more, H2S is corrosive and may harm process equipment and catalysts. Sulfur in sewage
sludge is primarily organic and can be released as H2S at different temperatures, according to
available literature. During pyrolysis, nitrogen in sludge largely forms HCN and NH3, with
NH3 released through various mechanisms depending on the temperature. The presence of
steam and certain metals can further influence the formation of these nitrogen-containing
compounds [13].
Although certain inorganic species in SS may have advantageous catalytic effects, the
gasification process may face additional difficulties due to their high concentrations. Signif-
icant ash and heavy metal content in sewage sludge result in severe operational challenges
in a fluidised-bed gasifier, particularly because the ashes tend to sinter and agglomerate.
Even at moderate gasification temperatures (770–850 °C), some inorganic ash compounds
may volatilize and deposit on downstream equipment. Phosphorus is a major problem due
to its high concentration in suspended solids. Organic phosphorus is primarily volatilized
during pyrolysis, while inorganic phosphorus remains stable until temperatures near 1200°C.
Furthermore, the gasification process can result in the release of trace heavy metals via at-
trition, entrainment, and volatilization, with each metal’s individual behavior determined

Methanol overview and production via syngas utilisation
Extensive research on methanol synthesis has shown that methanol (CH3OH), which is
the final product of the process, offers a wide range of applications and can be utilised as
fuel, solvent, energy storage medium, as well as industrial feedstock. Badische Anilin and
Soda Fabrik (BASF) created the first commercial methanol synthesis factory in Germany
in 1923, which was notable for its high-pressure process. This process used a chromate-
based catalyst and operated at temperatures ranging from 320 to 450 °C and 250 to 350 bar.
The discovery of sulfur–free synthesis gas in the 1960s made low-pressure methanol synthesis
feasible, allowing for the use of more effective copper-based catalysts. This modern technique
runs at lower pressures and temperatures, especially 60–80 bar and 250-280 °C. Since then,
Imperial Chemical Industries (ICI) and Lurgi have dominated the market with their low-
pressure processes [48].
Renewable methanol may be produced using two methods: bio-methanol and e-methanol.
Bio-methanol is formed from organic waste sources (agricultural waste and by-products, bio-
gas, sewage sludge etc.), whereas e-methanol is synthesised from CO2 and H2, which must be
produced using renewable electricity in order to be deemed renewable. Figure 12 provides
an overview of the numerous methanol synthesis pathways. The technologies for produc-
ing renewable methanol are similar to those used for producing traditional methanol, with
the main steps being feedstock pretreatment, gasification, water gas shift (WGS), syngas
cleaning, methanol synthesis, and purification [48, 49].

The first attempts at water electrolysis were conducted in the late 18th century us-

industrial-scale water electrolysis did not become practical until the late 19th century due
to technological limitations [52].

Electrolyzers are classified into three primary types based on their electrolyte: solid oxide
electrolyzers (SOEs), polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) electrolyzers, and alkaline water
electrolyzers (AELs). An overview of the characteristics of each electrolysis technology is

AEL: Its the most mature and a well-established technology using a potassium hy-
droxide or sodium hydroxide solution as the electrolyte. It operates at low temperatures
(50-90 °C) and uses nickel electrodes with an asbestos diaphragm. While efficient, AEL has
limitations such as low current density (less than 0.4 A/cmÂ˛) and high power consumption
(4.5–7 kWh/NmÂł). To prevent safety hazards, pressure on both sides of the diaphragm
must be balanced. Its slower response to changing power inputs makes it less suitable for
integration with renewable energy sources [52, 54].
PEM: PEM electrolysis uses a proton exchange membrane instead of asbestos. This
membrane allows protons to pass through while minimizing gas leakage, making it more
environmentally friendly.
They offer several advantages, including faster response times,
higher efficiency, higher current density (0.6–2 A/cmÂ˛) and similar power consumption
(4.5–7 kWh/NmÂł). These characteristics make them well-suited for industrial hydrogen
production [52, 54].
SOE: A promising but still under pilot trials technology, offers high energy efficiency
which can be utilized as heat. Using a zirconia-based electrolyte, SOECs operate at high
temperatures (500–1000 °C), reducing the required voltage for water electrolysis (0.7–1.5 V).
This results in lower energy consumption (2.5–3.5 kWh/Nm3) and higher thermal efficiency,
potentially reaching 100%. However, high temperatures can lead to material degradation
and limit the lifespan of SOEC systems. The hydrogen produced often contains water vapor,
requiring further purification [52, 54].
In short, AEL’s main advantages are: the high stability, the low cost and maturity of
the technology, no catalyst needed and notably longer lifetime. However, gas permeation,
corrosive electrolyte as well as its lower density and dynamicity are the main disadvantages.
PEM’s primary advantages are its high current density, compact design, production of
high-purity hydrogen and simple design that allows for quick response and start times. On
the downside, PEM relies on precious and expensive catalysts which increases costs and the
durability of the system is lower especially in the acidic medium used in the membrane.
That can lead to potential issues over time.
SOEC’s main advantages are the low energy requirement and capital loss, no need for a
catalyst while achieving a very high efficiency. However, the technology faces significant dis-
advantages such as the delamination of electrodes, safety issues, instability of the electrolytes
and unsuitable sealing which can complicate its operation and maintenance.

Methodology
The LT-CFB gasifier, a type of circulating fluidized bed gasifier discussed in section
2.2, is specifically designed to gasify agricultural residues, which often have high levels of
alkali metals that can cause issues in conventional gasification systems. As shown in figure
13, the LT-CFB gasifier has two main reactors: a pyrolysis chamber and a char reactor.
The unique aspect of the LT-CFB gasifier is its operating temperature, typically between
650–750 °C, which helps minimize problems with ash and alkali metals. The feedstock first
enters the pyrolysis chamber, where it is heated to around 650 °C, causing it to decompose
into pyrolysis gas, char, and inert particles. The heat needed for this process comes from
recirculated sand which is used as a bed material and ash particles, which are heated in the
char reactor. The char and inert particles from the pyrolysis chamber are then sent to the
char reactor, where they are gasified with the presence of a gasifying agent at around 730 °C.
This gasification process releases heat, which is transferred back to the pyrolysis chamber.
The system also uses cyclones to separate fine ash particles, including alkali metals, from
the product gas. This setup helps avoid problems like agglomeration and slag formation,
ensuring efficient gasification and clean product gas. In addition to processing agricultural
residues, the LT-CFB gasifier produces valuable bio-as which can be used as fertilizers, soil
amendments, and for carbon sequestration, helping to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from
agriculture [3, 48, 55]. The study considered two cases: one utilizing oxygen as the gasifying
agent and the other employing an electrically heated LT-CFB gasifier. The simulation in
AP was divided into two hierarchical stages (gasification and electrolysis) and the methanol
synthesis cycle.

In the simulation of the LT-CFB gasifier in AP, several key assumptions were made. The
model assumed steady-state conditions, meaning all process variables such as temperature,
pressure, and composition remain constant over time. A zero-dimensional approach was
used which simplifies the model by assuming uniform conditions throughout the gasifier
as well as that all the gases are ideal.

The component used in AP was a RADFRAC which performs rigorous rating and design cal-
culations for single columns. Based on equilibrium, the first distillation column "DISTCOL1"
removes lighter gases and by-products, while the second column "DISTCOL2" separates liq-
uid methanol from water and heavier by-products. Their operating conditions is shown in
table 14. Distilation columns include a condenser at the top and a reboiler at the bottom.
The first columns have 10 stages while the second have 20, A stage represents a point where
the liquid and vapor phases reach equilibrium, and the more stages there are, the better the
separation that can be achieved. The Partial-Vapor condenser indicates that only a part of
the vapor is condensed while allowing both liquid and vapor streams to exit the condenser.
The reboiler Kettle heats the bottom product to create vapor that rises through the column
while it is consider Vapor-Liquid phases for the seperation process. The distillate rate is the
flow rate of the product that is collected from the distillation column. The ratio between
the liquid returned to the distillation column (reflux) and the liquid taken out as the dis-
tillate (product) is called reflux ratio. A high reflux ratio with a high distillate rate might
require more energy to maintain both the quality of separation and the production rate.
Conversely, a low reflux ratio with a high distillate rate might lead to a decrease in product
purity. In this research, reflux ratio was set at 1 and the distillate rate in "DISTCOL1" was
calculated through a design specification in which was set as the desired outlet temperature
from the "GASES1" stream to be at 30 °C (DISTRATE, table 10). The reason is that
cooling water can be used in the top condenser instead of having very low temperatures.

In process design and optimization, composite curves are essential tools, especially when
it comes to heat integration studies. The combined hot and cold temperature-dependent en-
thalpy changes within a process are graphically represented by these curves (figure 19(a),19(b)
as were created by AP Energy Analaser. It has been mentioned that the feedstock here was
assumed being 100kg/s input instead of 1kg/s and for the conversion of enthalpy’s units
KJ/h to MW, it can be considered that 1 kJ/h = 2.77778 x 10ˆ-7 MW. The red curve repre-
sents the heat curve while the blue one the cooling curve. Based on the figures, both cases
are showing that there is a significant amount of heat that can be used for heat recovery.
Conversely, the minimum cooling requirement is very low. In terms of case 1 % case 2 find-
ings, case 2 appears to have less overall heat demand for the process. Pinch point, the least
energy requirement for the system is determined by the closest approach between the hot
and cold composite curves, a key area where the temperature difference between the hot and
cold streams is the smallest. In this case, it seems to be in very low temperatures (below
25°C).
To get a better view of the flows that they can not supplemented from hear or cooling
recovery, the Heat Exchanger Network (HEN) is illustrated in figure 19(c),(d).
HEN is
an integrated system of heat exchangers that optimizes heat recovery within an industrial
process. As in the composite curves figure, red lines represent the hot streams while the
blue lines the cold streams. It is divided into 3 distinct sections. Left section, All the flows
represented by the vertical lines can occur in that zone. The left section has an excess of
heat available from hot streams and any heat recovery can only occur in this segment (above
pinch point). Heat has to be injected to utility coolers while cooling has to undergo to a

4.5
Economic evaluations
The performance evaluation of the 2 analysed cases is structured on the basis of the
Levelised Cost of their respective output products (LCOx). Given the context of the present
study, a full lifetime cashflow evaluation is deemed out of scope for the analysis. On this
backdrop, the respective LCOx of each technology is calculated on the basis of its annualised
expenditures (including Capital expendidure (CAPEX)) for the deployed technological ca-
pacities.
The annualisation of capital expenditures takes place via the use of a Capital
Recovery Factor (CRF), usually referred to as annuity. Its utilisation aims to distribute
the initial investment of each technology in an equal series of payments across its lifetime,
while also accounting for the time value of money. Of course, the present value of all annual
investment segments (discounted to the evaluation year) sums up to the same amount of
CAPEX that was practically paid up front during the Financial Investment Decision (FID).
In this way, the summation of all annual costs in each distinctive year results in the value
hereafter referred to as Total Annual Economic Cost (TAEC), as thoroughly discussed in
[66]. With the evaluation period for the present model stretching to 1 operational year, the
discussed LCOx practically represent an annual snapshot and not the representative lifetime
LCOx. Nevertheless, such a metric will serve the purposes of the targeted assessment. A
cost-based LCOE can be calculated via TAEC for each technology’s nameplate energy carrier
output.
TAEC consists of the sum of the annualised capital costs and all other annual
expenditures for each plant’s operation. When TAEC is divided by the energy produced
annually, it results in the cost per unit of energy, also known as LCOx.
The Capital Recovery Factor (CRF) is calculated using the following formula:
CRF = WACC · (1 + WACC)lifetime

5
Discussions and Conclusions
The completion of this report provided answers for all the questions that have been
addressed in section 1. More specifically the findings to every research question are:
What is the syngas composition and yield derived from each gasification
case?
The electrically heated gasifier in case 2, resulted in the production of a higher
quality syngas with more H2 (40 vs 31%) and less CO2 (6.4 vs 17.2%) while the producer
gas yields of both were identical. The superior syngas in case 2 as expected had higher LHV
(11.01 vs 8.53 MJ/kg) and HHV (12.08 vs 9.24 MJ/kg).
Which case showed higher efficiency? It was found that both gasification tech-
niques had high efficiencies, with case 2 exceeding case 1 (86.3 vs 82.2% before POX and
76.8 vs 71.4% after). Their overall efficiency in producing methanol was demonstrated to be
roughly the same (80.5 vs 80.0%).
What is the total heat requirement for each case? Even though both gasification
procceses were designed to be alothermal, the total heat requirement for the gasification
process in case 2 was found to be slightly higher by 0.24MW. The reason for that is the steam
that was introduced to the char gasifier for the elimination of C in the output gasesm unlike
in case 1, where there was no need for steam addition. When it comes to the electrolysis
process, case 1 reflected higher needs for electricity and water input (6.76 vs 4.67 MWe
and 0.35 vs 0.24kg/s water) and that is explained from the composition of the derived
syngas. Consequently, the waste heat from the electrolyzer was significantly higher (1.12 vs
0.77MW). Finally the methanol production cycles, which included compression and cooling,
methanol synthesis, recycling loop and distillation, had similar amount of heat generated
and electricity consumed.
How do the resulting system costs compare for each case? The present techno-
economic evaluation has been based on the optimised system layout(s) utilised to treat
an inflow of sewage sludge of 1 kg/s, turning it into bio-methanol in combination with
electrolytic (green) hydrogen.
The set-up results in the generation of approximately 75
GWh bio-methanol per year (13.5 ktons/y) in both evaluated cases, through a 9 MW MeOH
plant. The more electrified gasification pathway (case 2) manages to yield annual savings of
0.79 mEUR24/y, according to the accounted cost elements.
Which process demonstrated lower methanol production costs? In both cases,
the sewage sludge to methanol process is assisted by the presence of a 5 and 3 MW H2,
ultimately resulting in a levelised cost of bio-methanol between 0.83 and 0.90 EUR24/kg,
placed closer to the higher end of international expectations.
Overall, the case 2 looks more favorable and promising in terms of the findings. That can
be clearly said regarding syngas yield, efficiency of the process, captical costs and LCOx of
methanol. Furthermore there is enough heat generated to supply the processes that require
heat.
However the study icnorporated several limitations and simplifications that should be
mentioned. Firstly, the biggest concern regarding LT-CFB gasifier is the tar formation. In
this research, tars presence was assumed to be in the CH4 and wasn’t modelled as C6H6
(benzene) as in the vast majority of literature. Also, the main possible products that were
Page 53 of 54
==PAGE:61==
identified in the Gibss reactors were H2, CO, CO2, CH4, N2, and H2O while products such as
CH3OH, CH3OCH3, and NH3 weren’t taken into consideration. The general system doesn’t
consider any heat loss and the electrically heated gasifier in case 2 is modelled by considering
1:1 conversion of electricity to heat. The electrolysis produced waste heat is not utilized as
well as the surplus electrolytic O2. The C added to char gasifier in case 2 is a simplified
modelling choice instead of adding some recycled syngas after POX. A typical drying of SS
that was modelled in a belt dryer (80% efficiency), was assumed that it can be heated from the
extra heat at the system by 50% and for the financial case the remaining 50% was included
in the costs. Finally, the financial data that was used for the calculations were obtained
from literature’s similar plants configurations instead of using in-depth dimensioning for
each simulated component.
The research revealed a significant potential especially for the electrically heated LT-
CFB gasifier and it could be extended in future work. First of all, a more detailed look
at energy integration techniques like heat recovery and combined heat and power (CHP)
systems must be done. Under the two cases, another possible future work could be a thorough
life cycle assessment (LCA) to evaluate the environmental effects of the gasification process,
including greenhouse gas emissions, resource utilization, and energy consumption. It could
be also examined the effects of utilizing more sophisticated electrolyzer technologies, such
as SOECs, to increase energy yield and H2 generation during the production phase of the
process. The effect of using a different feedstock and possible new bio-fuel production along
with the use of a different gasification agent can be analyzed as well. In terms of financial
modelling, both simulations can be analyzed in a profit maximization way where waste heat
from electrolyzer and other unused surplus utilities such as O2 can be utilized to bring extra
revenues to the already low bio-methanol price in market.
