Abstract
The physical mechanisms behind extreme weather and climate extremes are complex,
and often these events are the results of interactions between many diÔ¨Äerent climate
drivers. Classical multivariate statistical approaches to study such interactions are gen-
erally limited by a lack of data, thus having to extrapolate heavily on the tails of the
distributions. In this study, we use a novel multivariate method from dynamical systems
theory, building on chaos theory and extreme value theory, to explore the link between
warm and cold extremes in Denmark and the large-scale patterns of atmospheric cir-
culation and temperature. Using sea level pressure (SLP) and 2m temperature (T2M)
variables as input, we compute for each time step three dynamical systems metrics in
a larger domain in northern Europe: The local dimension d, the inverse persistence
őł‚ąí1 and the co-recurrence ratio őĪ. Considering anomalies of these metrics, we explore
how they deviate from normal winter and summer days, when conditioned on extreme
warm or cold days in Denmark. We also explore the opposite link, whether it is possi-
ble to recover extreme temperatures by conditioning on extreme coupling. The results
show: (i) The relations between the diÔ¨Äerent metrics are highly complex and can be
explained by diÔ¨Äerent seasonal cycles; (ii) In the winter, extreme warm days show
higher-than-normal coupling, and extreme cold days show lower-than-normal coupling.
Both warm and cold days are, however, associated with a coherent large-scale SLP and
T2M pattern. In the summer, the metrics are to a high degree insensitive to extreme
temperatures, although a small higher-than-normal coupling is seen for both warm and
cold days. (iii) By conditioning on extreme coupling, warm and cold days in Denmark
can be recovered to some degree in the winter, although these coupling values lead to
more extreme temperatures in other parts of northern Europe. In the summer, we do
not recover extreme temperatures when conditioning on extreme coupling.

Introduction
Extreme weather and climate events are generally the results of complex interactions
between many diÔ¨Äerent climate variables. In order to prepare for and better adapt
to climate change in the future, we need to have a sound knowledge basis on hazard
risks, requiring a better understanding of the physical processes, driving these extremes.
When multiple climate drivers in their combination cause an extreme impact on so-
ciety, the variables may not necessarily be extreme on their own but be ampliÔ¨Āed by
their joint occurrences. An extreme event, caused by multiple drivers, is an example of
a so-called compound event, Ô¨Ārst introduced in 2012 by the Intergovernmental Panel
on Climate Change (IPCC) in their special report on the risks of extreme events and
disasters (SREX)[26]. The working deÔ¨Ānition today of a compound weather and cli-
mate event is, in the Sixth Assessment Report by IPCC, that it is ‚ÄĚa combination of
multiple climate drivers and/ or hazards that contribute to societal or environmental
risk‚ÄĚ, adopted from the review article by Zscheischler et al. (2018) [49].
Compound extremes are usually studied, using multivariate statistical methods, by
modelling the correlation between variables in multivariate distribution functions. The
most dominant approaches are copula-based, in which the modelled dependence struc-
ture can be asymptotically focused on the tails of the marginal distributions. However,
compound extremes are by deÔ¨Ānition rare, and a limitation to pure statistical methods
is a lack of data when studying the tails of the multivariate distributions. Observations
of extreme values of multiple climate variables jointly are even more sparse than extreme
observations in the univariate case, and thus we need to extrapolate heavily on the tails.
In this thesis, we will apply an alternative multivariate approach to studying the interac-
tion of multiple climate drivers, relying on a recently-developed method from dynamical
systems theory. The approach builds upon extensive research on extreme value theory
for dynamical systems, taking its starting point in ergodic theory and chaos theory.
Exploring the chaotic nature of the atmosphere, the method computes local dynam-
ical properties in phase space of the underlying chaotic attractor, which also in the
univariate setting can be linked to extreme weather and climate patterns [15]. In this
approach, the dependence between diÔ¨Äerent climate variables is measured through a co-
recurrence measure, which can be computed for each local state in phase space. Hence,
the co-recurrence measure is unique for diÔ¨Äerent value combinations of the diÔ¨Äerent
variables, and thus it diÔ¨Äers from statistical dependence measures, computed as overall
measures for the multivariate distribution function. The method relies on extreme value
theory, and so the method also requires suÔ¨Écient data to compute the local metrics.
However, as no multivariate distribution is Ô¨Ātted, some of the limitations from the pure
multivariate statistical approach are overcome. A further motivation for the use of this
approach is the potential of exploring the links between diÔ¨Äerent dynamical measures in
phase space such as the local dimension, interpreted as the degrees of freedom around a

Extreme temperatures pose a signiÔ¨Ācant risk to society, especially over a persistent

Extreme weather is closely governed by large-scale atmospheric circulation patterns,
although also local low-scale factors can be attributed to extremes. The atmospheric

‚ÄĘ How do the dynamical systems metrics deviate from their seasonal cycles for ex-
treme warm and cold temperatures in Denmark in the boreal winter and summer,

‚ÄĘ By conditioning on extreme high and low re-recurrence values in northern Europe
in the boreal winter and summer respectively, is it possible to recover extreme
temperatures in Denmark?

Mathematical Framework
In this chapter, we will present the mathematical foundations of dynamical systems
theory for extreme events, on which our methodology to study compound events is
based.
First, we will explore dynamical systems and chaos theory from the point
of view of ergodic theory, allowing us to study deterministic systems using statistics.
Next, we will review some important results from extreme value theory Ô¨Ārst from a
classical setting and subsequently from a dynamical setting. Lastly, a method to study
local properties of a dynamical attractor is presented for a speciÔ¨Āc class of dynamical
systems, Axiom A systems, which has shown to be useful for the characterization of
extreme climate events.
2.1
Dynamical Systems and Ergodic Theory
Dynamical systems theory is the mathematical formalization of the study of the be-
haviour of a system over time. Any man-made or natural system, subject to change,
can be described as a dynamical system, and therefore dynamical systems play a vital
role in predicting real-world situations. A dynamical system consists of a phase space,
in which all possible states are represented as points, and a rule, determining all fu-
ture states either deterministically or stochastically [27]. In the continuous case, the
dynamical rule is usually described by diÔ¨Äerential equations, while in the discrete case,
the next iteration of a state is described by a mapping rule.
Atmospheric circulation is chaotic by nature, and the general assumption is that the
dynamics of climate data will settle on a strange chaotic attractor [32]. In a chaotic
system, the long-term behaviour is impossible to determine from an initial value due to
sensitivity to initial conditions. However, using ergodic theory, which is concerned with
the statistical behaviour of a dynamical system over a long time-period, it is possible
to predict the average behaviour. Originally, ergodic theory and chaos theory emerged
independently as two diÔ¨Äerent Ô¨Āelds, with a focus on respectively the topological prop-
erties and the statistical properties of dynamical systems. However, the two Ô¨Āelds have
converged over the years, creating fruitful results for both mathematicians and physi-
cists to study the atmosphere.
Chaos theory is the study of certain deterministic dynamical system that show appar-
ent random behaviour in the long run. The Ô¨Ārst pioneer in chaos theory was Henri
Poincar¬īe, who in the late 1900‚Äôth century found that even the smallest changes in his
initial conditions in the three body problem would lead to vastly diÔ¨Äerent trajectories
[41]. However, the phenomenon of chaotic behaviour did not get its oÔ¨Écial name until
much later in the 1960‚Äôs when Edward Lorenz discovered chaos in his model for weather
forecasting [31]. He demonstrated that diÔ¨Äerences in his initial values would amplify
in an exponential manner, contradicting the until then accepted assumption that small
diÔ¨Äerences in initial conditions lead to small diÔ¨Äerences in the results. Lorenz, further-

Ergodic theory has its roots in the statistics of thermodynamics and was Ô¨Ārst introduced
by Ludwig Boltzmann in 1909 with his hypothesis that the time a particle spends in
some region in phase space will in the long run be proportional to the volume that this
region occupies in phase space [6]. The mathematical foundations of ergodic theory is
usually ascribed to George BirkhoÔ¨Äand John von Neumann that almost simultaneously
in the 1930‚Äôs proved slightly diÔ¨Äerent versions of the celebrated ergodic theorem, based
on the study of measure-preserving dynamical systems [5, 40]. As ergodic theory has
evolved from thermodynamics, originally the theory has been applied to the study of
conservative systems, i.e. volume-preserving systems of phase space. However, with the
introduction of the Sinai-Ruelle-Bowen (SRB) measure [14], ergodic theory has shown
to also have physical relevance for certain chaotic and dissipative, i.e. non-conservative,
systems such as the atmosphere.
Ergodic theory takes its starting point in measure theory. A measure-theoretic approach
to deterministic dynamical systems will therefore be adopted.

2.1.2
Ergodicity and Observables
The intuition behind an ergodic system is that we cannot compose the system into
multiple parts.

DeÔ¨Ānition 2.2. For a dynamical system (X, ¬Ķ, T), we say that T is ergodic with respect
to the measure ¬Ķ if for every A ‚ąąB,
T ‚ąí1(A) = A ‚áí¬Ķ(A) = 0 ‚ą®¬Ķ(A) = 1.

a system is ergodic [14].

2.3
Local Dynamical Indicators
When studying atmospheric dynamics, local properties rather than global properties
of the dynamical attractor have shown to be of importance in the characterization of
certain weather patterns linked to extreme climate events [46, 15]. The local properties
of a state ő∂ on the underlying attractor are determined by the indicators: the local
dimension d(ő∂) and the local persistence őł‚ąí1(ő∂). In the multivariate case, we will also
consider a third indicator, the local co-recurrence ratio őĪ.
In the previous section, we have presented criteria for when the BM approach to ex-
tremes is justiÔ¨Āed for dynamical systems.
For a large class of dynamical systems,

considering distance observables [36]. Interestingly, these systems do not necessarily
comply with the conditions Dq(un) and D‚Ä≤

In this chapter, the results and method are discussed. First, we will discuss how the
applied method illustrates the link between extreme temperature events and large-scale
atmospheric circulation. Next, we will discuss theoretical assumptions, and Ô¨Ānally we

5.1
Atmospheric Circulation and Temperature Extremes
In the present thesis, we have investigated the link between extreme temperature events
in Denmark and the large-scale coupling between atmospheric circulation and temper-
ature, using local dynamical systems metrics. We have found that the degree, to which
atmospheric circulation is a driver of local extreme temperature, is highly dependent
on the seasons of the year.
In the winter season, the NAO is the most dominant mode of atmospheric variability
over Europe [25], and previous studies have shown that the NAO has an eÔ¨Äect not only
on the regional mean annual temperature but also on extremes of daily temperatures
[28, 47]. It is therefore encouraging that our study leads to similar Ô¨Āndings. Indeed,
Kenyon and Hegerl (2008) [28] Ô¨Ānd a strong statistically signiÔ¨Ācant increase in extreme
warm and cold days for the NAO in its two phases, peaking around Scandinavia, which
is in accordance with our identiÔ¨Ācation of typical extreme winter temperatures in Den-
mark as NAO events. In the summer season, our Ô¨Āndings point to local factors as the
main drivers of warm and cold extremes in Denmark, although anticyclonic patterns
also have an important role. This result is also in alignment with multiple other studies,
identifying the two main drivers of heat extremes in Europe as local thermodynamical
eÔ¨Äects of moisture limitation and large-scale anticyclones. However, it is generally un-
clear which of these drivers is strongest, and diÔ¨Äerent studies point to diÔ¨Äerent results
[44, 42, 37].
Our dynamical systems approach provides a further perspective to this relation between
atmospheric circulation and temperatures, as we for each day aÔ¨Éliate the large-scale
patterns to dynamical conÔ¨Āgurations on the chaotic attractor. The change of these
metrics can be studied for speciÔ¨Āc types of extremes locally, thus elucidating the real
advantage of the method. In addition to the co-recurrence ratio őĪ, which we consider
as a measure for the dependence between SLP and T2M, we have also linked extreme
temperatures in Denmark to the local dimension d as well as the inverse local persis-
tence őł, informing on the intrinsic predictability of the atmospheric system.
Using a dynamical systems approach to study drivers of extremes is, however, not always
simple which our results demonstrate for extreme winter temperatures in Denmark. For
warm winter days, we Ô¨Ānd stronger coupling than normal, and for cold winter days we
Ô¨Ānd weaker coupling than normal. A natural conclusion to draw for warm days is that

5.2
Theoretical Assumptions
Using dynamical systems theory to describe the atmosphere highlights a complex inter-
play between mathematics and physics. Mathematics relies on the deductive method,
only accepting strict results that can be proved formally. The atmosphere is, however,
of a complex size and cannot fully be described by rigorous mathematics, and we thus
have to rely on a number of assumptions. This interplay between the two disciplines
create fruitful results which we have seen exempliÔ¨Āed in the present thesis. Eckmann
and Ruelle (1985) [14] put it in the following way:
‚ÄĚOne might thus be tempted either to disregard rigorous mathematics and
go ahead with the physics, or on the contrary to wait until the mathematical
situation is suÔ¨Éciently clariÔ¨Āed before going ahead with the physics. Both
attitudes would be unfortunate.
We believe in the value of the interplay
between mathematics and physics, although either discipline oÔ¨Äers only in-
complete results.‚ÄĚ (p. 621)
In the thesis, we have silently assumed that the atmospheric dynamics are stationary.
Subscribing to the chaotic hypothesis by Gallavotti and Cohen (1995) [23], we assume
that chaotic systems with many degrees of freedom behave essentially as Axiom A sys-
tems. Thus, we perceive the atmosphere as having the Axiom A property, considering
it as an autonomous and ergodic system. However, in reality the forcing of the climate
system is not constant, and the atmosphere should be viewed as a non-autonomous
dynamical system. Indeed, the external conditions are highly time-dependent, both on
short time scales such as seasonality and long time scales such as decadal variability of
sun radiation and slow-evolving anthropogenic climate change.
The results in this thesis support the fact that the atmosphere is indeed not Axiom
A. For ergodic systems, Young [48] has proved the result that the local dimension of
almost all states in phase space is constant and equal to the average value D. We
obtain, however, a distribution of local dimensions, highly dependent on the seasons of
the year, contradicting that the system should be ergodic. One might raise the question
if it is thus reasonable at all to apply the proposed method for climate data.

As seen in the present study, it is the variation in the local metrics rather than the
average value which is of interest when studying extremes. Furthermore, computa-
tions of the average dimension D have shown to be unreliable for systems with many
degrees of freedom, speciÔ¨Ācally for atmospheric dynamics [24, 33]. Multiple other stud-
ies have conÔ¨Ārmed the eÔ¨Äectiveness of computing local dynamical metrics in order to
study climate extremes, demonstrating that atmospheric dynamics seem to lie on a
non-stationary attractor, changing with the seasons [18, 17, 15, 16, 38, 10, 11]. The
applied method relies on the recurrences of states, and although the atmosphere is not
Axiom A, diÔ¨Äerent weather patterns do return to similar patterns over time. We might
say that non-stationarities of the atmosphere are weak in the sense that the dynamics
are not too far away from a stationary attractor. We do not obtain constant values
for the local dimensions, but we do however get distributions that are centered around
stable values in time. Under long-time scale forcing, such as anthropogenic climate
change, these central values change [17]. The drifts in time are, however, small, and
as long as the non-stationarities do not lead to bifurcations, we may assume that the
dynamics are close to an Axiom A system. Thus, the computed local dynamical metrics
might be meaningful, although not in a mathematical strict sense.
On a Ô¨Ānal note, another important theoretical assumption which deserves to be dis-
cussed is the normalization of the variables in the joint logarithmic return, introduced in
Section 3.2.2. The norm used in the present thesis is distanced-based, deÔ¨Āned for each
Ô¨Āxed state {ő∂x, ő∂y} and chosen to be in accordance with the study for North America
[18]. Hence, we subscript the norm by the Ô¨Āxed state, i.e. ‚ą•x‚ą•ő∂x. However, we could also
have considered an absolute-value-based norm, such as the average root mean square
norm, not depending on the distance to a Ô¨Āxed state {ő∂x, ő∂y}. In such case, the norm
would be the same for each observable, and we would write ‚ą•x‚ą•, not subscripting by
a Ô¨Āxed state. It might be argued that it is more natural to remove physical units by
normalizing before introducing observables. However, a norm not depending on the
Ô¨Āxed state, would give greater weight to the variable with highest variance in the joint
logarithmic return, and thus the computed co-dimension d and inverse co-persistence őł
would follow a pattern closer to that variable. This shows that the choice of norm has
the potential to aÔ¨Äect the computed values of the metrics. There is no universal way
to handle normalization in a dynamical system. However, the fact that diÔ¨Äerent norms
can lead to diÔ¨Äerent results elucidates the importance of making such choices carefully.

temperature events. However, the categorization of compound events is not strict, and
as we study large-scale co-occurrences of SLP and T2M, we might argue that we study
multivariate events, characterized by multiple drivers and/ or hazards occurring in the

same geographical region. The dynamical systems approach is not limited to the study

existing conditions, and temporally compound events, characterized by a cascade of
hazards, can be studied by introducing a time-lag between the considered variables.
For example, warm temperatures in autumn and wet conditions in the following spring
is an emerging type of compound event with severe consequences for agriculture [4].
These types of extremes could be examined from a dynamical systems perspective by
coupling temperature and precipitation with a lag of some few months and relating the
coupling to the persistence of both variables. Finally, we can study concurrent extreme
events in diÔ¨Äerent geographical locations, spatially compound events, by deÔ¨Āning dif-
ferent domains of the diÔ¨Äerent variables. Large-scale atmospheric circulation can cause
diÔ¨Äerent weather patterns in diÔ¨Äerent locations such as heavy precipitation events and
wind extremes. By coupling pressure, precipitation and wind, deÔ¨Āned in three diÔ¨Äerent
regions, these relations can be examined.
In addition to studying other types of compound events, the deÔ¨Ānition of extremeness
could furthermore be adapted in future studies. To link large-scale atmospheric circula-
tion and temperature to extreme temperature events in Denmark, we have in this thesis
deÔ¨Āned extremes on a daily basis. Often, we are, however, more interested in clusters
of extremes such as extreme warm temperatures over an extended period of time. In
such case, the selection procedure of extremes should be accommodated such that only
consecutively extreme days are selected. Yet another approach to the deÔ¨Ānition of ex-
tremeness is to deÔ¨Āne extreme values for each grid point in the large domain instead
of deÔ¨Āning overall extremes from area-averaged anomalies locally. This approach does
not allow us to compute plots of the anomaly means of the metrics as a function of the
degree of extremeness as in Figure 9 and Figure 12. Instead, composite maps of the
metrics anomalies could be computed, conditioned on extreme temperatures in each
grid point. Such maps would provide local information, in the whole domain, on how
extreme days dynamically deviate from average days.
Finally, the changes in driving mechanisms of extreme events under anthropogenic
forcing could be studied. In continuation of the present study, an interesting future
aspect would be to use climate model data to examine whether the link between extreme
temperatures locally and large-scale patterns will change in a future climate.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
In order to better prepare for and adapt to extreme weather and climate events, a
better understanding of the driving mechanisms is needed. For this purpose, pure sta-
tistical approaches are usually applied, studying the tails of multivariate distributions.
However, extreme events are by deÔ¨Ānition rare, and thus data limitation forces such
methods to extrapolate heavily on the tails. In this thesis, we have applied an alter-
native multivariate approach from dynamical systems theory, combining chaos theory
with extreme value theory, to study the link between warm and cold temperatures in
Denmark and the large-scale patterns of SLP and T2M. Three local dynamical systems
metrics have been computed for each day in the data period, revealing instantaneous
information on the dynamical properties of the atmosphere. The local dimension d
is a measure for the active number of degrees of freedom on the underlying chaotic
attractor, the persistence őł‚ąí1 informs on the stability, and the co-recurrence ratio őĪ
is a dependence measure. The metrics d and őł‚ąí1 have been computed for both SLP
and T2M individually and for the two variables jointly. The metric őĪ has only been
computed for the joint variables as it is bivariate by deÔ¨Ānition.
Examining the relations between the computed dynamical systems metrics, we have
found that d and őł show a positive correlation for both univariate and bivariate met-
rics. However, for SLP as well as joint SLP and T2M we have found an increasing
variance for increasing metrics values, which is in contrast to other studies. We suggest
that this is due to a large-scale pattern, not capturing the complete region of the NAO.
The relations between the metrics can be complex, and while őĪ is negatively correlated
with d and őł for SLP and the joint variables, the őĪ values seem randomly distributed
according to d and őł for T2M. We have found that the complex links between the met-
rics can be explained by very diÔ¨Äerent seasonal cycles.
Considering anomalies of the dynamical system metrics, restricted to extreme warm
and cold days in Denmark, we have examined how these days diÔ¨Äer dynamically from
other days in the same season. Our Ô¨Āndings suggest that a typical warm winter day
occurs when SLP and T2M are strongly coupled, and the persistence is lower, or at
least not higher, than normal, and furthermore that a typical cold winter day occurs
under lower coupling and higher persistence than normal. We associate warm and cold
winter days with the NAO pattern in its positive and negative phase respectively. There
is an undeniable ambiguity in a coherent large-scale SLP and T2M pattern together
with a lower-than-normal coupling for cold winter days. This might be interpreted as a
negative NAO being a driver for cold days, but that other circulation patterns generally
have a greater coupling to T2M, although not resulting in extreme cold temperatures
in Denmark. In the summer season, we have found that the metrics anomalies are
generally highly insensitive to temperature extremes, although the coupling of SLP and
T2M is slightly higher than normal for both cold and warm days. This points to local
factors as the main drivers for extreme temperature events in the summer, although

large-scale anticyclonic behaviour also seems to have an inÔ¨āuence.
Finally, conditioning on extreme high and low coupling between SLP and T2M, we
recover warm and cold days in Denmark to some degree in the winter. However, these
strong and weak couplings lead to more extreme temperatures further northeast from
Denmark.
Hence, although the coupling is lower and respectively higher than nor-
mal for warm and cold days in Denmark, these days are not the strongest respectively
weakest coupled days. We associate days with most extreme coupling with a dipole
pattern, similar to that for the NAO, though with a high pressure center further east.
Again, we see an apparent contradiction in associating a lower-than-normal coupling
with coherent large-scale patterns. In order to understand this ambiguity better, it is
suggested to not only examine the mean of the SLP and T2M anomalies in composite
maps but also the sign agreement between these anomalies. In the summer season, we
do not recover extreme warm nor cold days in Denmark when conditioning on extreme
high and low coupling. This is indeed as expected since the coupling is slightly higher
than normal for both warm and cold summer days.
This study has highlighted that linking results from dynamical systems theory to phys-
ical interpretation is not always an easy endeavour. Indeed, the atmosphere is complex,
and thus the applied method relies on a number of assumptions which are not fully met
by the climate system. Subscribing to the chaotic hypothesis, we have assumed that
the atmosphere behaves like an Axiom A system, provided with an SRB measure. Since
the atmospheric dynamics are non-stationary due to e.g., seasonality and anthropogenic
forcing, this is clearly not true. However, the method relies on recurrences of states on
the underlying attractor, and though the atmosphere is not Axiom A, diÔ¨Äerent weather
patterns do return to similar patterns over time. Although not strict in a mathematical
sense, the applied method might therefore be meaningful in a physical sense. Indeed,
this study has shown that it is the variation in the dynamical systems metrics that allow
us to study weather extremes, which is in contrast to a constant local dimension in a
true Axiom A system. Instantaneous dynamical conÔ¨Āgurations provides us with daily
information on the coupling between variables and the internal predictability, which we
link to speciÔ¨Āc weather patterns.
The applied method in this study is not restricted to multivariate events but could
be extended to other types of compound events. Indeed, in future studies diÔ¨Äerent
geographic regions could be studied or a time-lag between the variables could be in-
cluded.
Finally, changes in the dynamical conÔ¨Āgurations could be examined under
anthropogenic forcing, in order to map changes in the driving physical processes of
extreme climate events.
