Abstract
In the present study, Action-based Computation Tree Logic(ACTL) for Labeled
Transition Systems(LTS) was inspected and analysed. The investigation has
begun with a research about Transition Systems(TS) and the Computation Tree
Logic(CTL) applied to them. Because of the lack of model-checkers to simulate
systems mainly based on actions, the goal of the thesis was discovering rules to
convert LTS's in TS's and ACTL formulae in CTL formulae. The conversion
rules got round the problem of the lack of LTS model builders, and created a
way to exploit the usual TS's as representation and reection of LTS's. Then,
the functional programming language SML was used to encode functions for the
conversion. In order to test the working modes of the translation, the software
PRISM was used. For this purpose, specic SML functions were created to give
them as input the LTS's representative elements, and obtain text les whose
content represents the TS for the PRISM environment. The same was done for
the ACTL formulae. The transformation let us to obtain TS's which fully reect
the behaviour of the analogous LTS's, and CTL formulae which fully reect the
logic of the analogous ACTL formulae. Finally, as running example the Health
Care system has been chosen since it lends itself well to the investigation of
action-based systems. The LTS Health Care has manually been modelled and
some ACTL formulae have been tested on it. The SML program was then used
for the conversion. The obtained TS has been built in PRISM, and the previous
tested ACTL formulae have been transformed in CTL formulae and checked
against the TS. The results of the testing were positive since they reected the
behaviour of the LTS and of the ACTL formulae. Thanks to this investigation,
a rst approach to the use of LTS's as new modelling systems has been realized
and the comparison between TS's and LTS's and between CTL and ACTL
allowed the realization of the conversion.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The use of computers and electronic devices has begun essential in our day life
as the years have gone by. Almost everything around us is computerized: from
the smallest household appliance to our expensive car, from the smart phone
to the credit card and everything is connected as a huge distributed system.
Not everybody thinks about how important is that all these systems work as
they are supposed to, without any margin of errors. A tiny little mistake in an
aeroplane controller checking can be disastrous and all the digital machines that
can bring risks to human lives can provoke tragedies and vast harms. Testing
and checking of software's and algorithms have always been the most crucial part
in the making of any electronic device. It is the longest and most demanding
part of a software development product. During the years, a lot of dierent
techniques have been formulated in order to nd all possible errors however
small they can be. Although all the eorts spent in the analysis and control
of the systems functioning, crucial errors still aict many systems especially
when the checking part has to be manually done. One of the most promising
and eective approach ever realized is the use of formal verication methods.
This approach is based on the fact that all systems in the real life can be
represented as mathematical objects whose behaviour can be expressed through
specic properties. In this study we are going to analyse one of these methods
which is nowadays widely used during the design of safety-critical systems. The
technique we are referring to is known as Model Checking. It is a model-based
approach and it works on the fact that the system to analyse is expressed as
==PAGE:10==

graphs with a nite number of states. The key point of this method is that it uses
automatic and ecient algorithms to go through all the states of such systems
and checking weather or not the ongoing behaviour described by a specic logic
is satised on it. After evaluating the properties, the model checker produces
counterexamples for debugging, if the system does not encounter the specics as
requested. The systems we are going to analyse are called Transition Systems
and among all the dierent existing logics we are going to use CTL. The study
in fact starts with the investigation of the mathematics and logics behind these
systems and the verication method applied to them. Transition systems are
very spread when it comes to model the behaviour of a system in the study of
computation. The clarity and simplicity with which a system can be represented
with the use of TS's led to an increase in the application of this method in the
eld of model checking.
The nodes in the graph represent the states of the
system, while the edges model the transitions. In this way the graph models the
evolution of the system which is carried on by taking a transition and changing
the state. The presence of innite paths in these kind of systems allows to avoid
deadlocks which are one of the aspects of a bad checking phase. CTL is then
used to check the correct behaviour of transition systems. It has been chosen
because it is a powerful branching-time logic which combines path and state
operators to check the satisfaction of a property over all the states of a system.
Afterwards, the analysis moves on another kind of systems representation called
Labeled Transition System. This is the core of the study. The dierence between
the usual transition systems and the labeled ones is, as the name suggests, the
presence of labels on actions. These models are useful to describe systems that
change through actions of some kind.
We actually realized that it is more
natural to think about a system that change by doing some actions instead
of a system that change by switching their states. The representation is the
same of the usual transition systems but in this case the transitions are seen
as real actions thanks to the presence of labels on them. The logic to check
properties on these models change, and it is called ACTL. ACTL is an extension
of CTL with the introduction of operators on actions to check properties both
on states and on transitions. Unfortunately, there is no model checker which
can be used to build an LTS and automatically verify its behaviour. For this
reason, the project goes on with the identication of mathematical rules to
switch from an action-based model to a state-based model. After identifying
the transformation rules, the study continues with the use of the model checker
software PRISM. It is a software used to model transition systems and to check
properties on it by using CTL among other logics. It has its own environment
and it needs a specic language to build a system and to validate its functioning.
Since there is no way of modelling a system as an LTS in PRISM, the study
continues by nding a way to derive from a given LTS, the correspondent TS
which respect the transformation rules. In order to automatically obtain such
models in PRISM, an expression-based functional programming language is used
to realize a prototype implementation of the translation of the systems and the

Afterwards, the SML functions are used to transform the system in a TS model

are tested and veried.

Chapter 2
State-Based Modeling
The rst step for the study of a formal verication method is to have a good and
reliable model to represent a system. In this chapter the state-based modelling
approach is described. The approach is based on the creation of specic diagrams
called Transition Systems which are used to represent the behaviour of a system
as a set of states. The chapter goes on with the description of the syntax and
the semantics of Computation Tree Logic (CTL) used to specify properties on
such systems.
2.1
Transition Systems
A Transition System TS is a graph composed by states and transitions. The
states are drawn as circles and the transitions as arrows between states. A tran-
sition system is used to model the behaviour of a specic system by representing
all possible states in which the system can be. In a transition system, nothing
happens outside the states. The transitions are just used to show the connec-
tion between states, meaning show which state can be the next in the execution.
There are many denitions used to describe a transition system. The one we
are going to use is taken from [BK08] but it was rearranged for our purpose. In
fact we did not include actions in the tuple as in [BK08], since they are going

to identify the LTS's and distinguish them from the TS's.
Definition 2.1 Transition System[BK08]
A transition system is dened as a tuple TS = (S, R, S0, AP, L) where:
• S is a set of states
• R ⊆(S × S) is a set of transitions
• S0 ⊆S is a set of initial states
• AP is a set of atomic propositions
• L : S →2AP is a labeling function
A transition system has no nite paths, which means there is no possibility of
reaching a terminal state and getting into a deadlock. This means that, for all
states in the system there will always be the possibility to move to a next state.
It is also assumed that every state should be reachable, meaning that there
are no states without ingoing transitions, except for the initial states that are
considered the beginning of the path. Moreover, there should not exists isolated
groups of states meaning that from the initial state there exists at least a path
which leads to all the states of the system. Formally:
Lemma 2.2 Given a TS = (S, R, S0, AP, L) must hold that:
∀s ∈S : ∃s′ ∈S such that (s, s′) ∈R and
∀s ∈S : ∃π = {s1, ..., sn} ∈Paths(s1) such that s1 ∈S0, sn = s and ∀i :
(si, si+1) ∈R

lead us through all the exploration of the new logic.

2.2
CTL
CTL, or Computation Tree Logic, is a branching-time logic used to formulate
set of properties to be checked over a transition system.
The denition of 'branching-time' means that it is a logic based on a transition
system tree-view. The tree is obtained by unfolding all possible paths in the
system. In this way all the properties over all possible future decisions can be
checked by using CTL.
A model checker can follow some transition s →s′ only if all the needed prop-
erties are satised over the state s. Only after checking the satisfaction of the
properties, the system can change its state by moving to the next one s′.

2.2.1
Syntax of CTL
CTL is used to describe mathematical formulae by using specic signs. The
innovation of CTL, compared to the other linear temporal logics, is that it
is possible to separate the state formulae from the path formulae giving the
possibility to analyse the system from a tree-time structure point of view. The

2.2.2
Semantics of CTL
The CTL formulae represent properties that have to be satised over the states
and the paths of a transition system. The semantics of the CTL formulae is
dened by the symbol |= which identies the satisfaction relation between the
state or the path in the system and the state or the path formula.
Before explaining the dierent semantics let's dene some notation:
• s ∈S is a state in the TS.
• π = s0, s1, s2... is an innite path of the TS.
• Path(s) is the set of all paths starting from the state s.
• π(i) is the state with index i.
• |= is the satisfaction relation for a CTL formula. It can be read 'satisfy'.

Chapter 3
Action-Based Modeling
The core of the project starts with this chapter wit the analysis of Labeled
Transition Systems. The modelling approach changes and it become action-
based thanks to the introduction of labels on transitions. The focus moves on
such systems whose behaviour is better explained by looking at the actions.
These systems are seen as more dynamic since every transition identies an
action which, if taken, changes the state of the system. The analysis continues
with Action Computation Tree Logic(ACTL) as the logic used to check the
validity of the LTS's.
3.1
Labelled Transition System
A Labeled Transition System LTS is a graph composed in the same way of
a transition system, with states and transitions. The dierence is that each
transition of the system is labeled with a specic action that is executed during
the transition. While in a transition system nothing happens outside the states,
in a labeled transition system nothing happens outside the transitions meaning
that a label on a transition represents an action of the system, which is seen as
a change, and a label on a state represents a state of the system. To formally
dene an LTS we modied the denition from [RMG04] to compare it with the
==PAGE:22==

denition 2.1.
Definition 3.1 Labeled Transition System[RMG04]
A labeled transition system is dened as a tuple LTS = (S, Act, R, S0, AP, L)
where:
• S is a set of states
• Act is a set of actions
• R ⊆(S × Act × S) is a set of transitions including actions
• S0 ⊆S is a set of initial states
• AP is a set of atomic propositions
• L : S ∪Act →2AP is a labeling function
A labeled transition system is composed by innite paths, and it does not contain
'non-reachable' states. Formally:
Lemma 3.2 Given an LTS = (S, Act, R, S0, AP, L) must hold that:
∀s ∈S : ∃l ∈Act : ∃s′ ∈S such that (s, l, s′) ∈R and
∀s ∈S : ∃π = {s1, l1, ..., ln, sn} ∈Paths(s1) such that s1 ∈S0, sn = s and
∀i : (si, li, si+1) ∈R

tions to transform it into an LTS.

used to represent the actions to be taken to reach the states 'light on' and 'light
o'. For a more realistic interpretation, the labels can be expressed as follows:
• a=light o
• b=light on
• χ=turn on the light
• η=turn o the light
3.2
ACTL
ACTL, or Action Computation Tree Logic,[RMG04] is a branching-time logic
which extends CTL by expressing properties either on states and on transitions.
It applies to such systems whose behaviour is mainly expressed by actions and
whose semantics is expressed by LTS's.

3.2.1
Syntax of ACTL
The syntax of ACTL is very similar to the CTL syntax. The dierence is that
the properties can now be checked either on the states and on the transitions.
In fact, in order to go from state s to state s′ by taking a transition l, a model
checker has to verify properties both on the state s and on the transition l.
In addition to the previous formulae we are going to consider action formulae.
Because of the introduction of action formulae, the syntax for the path formulae
has to be changed as well.

• χ is an action formula

Semantics of ACTL
The semantics of the ACTL formulae is dened by the symbol |= which identies
the satisfaction relation between the states, the paths or the actions in the
system and the state, the path or the action formulae respectively. In other
words it is not sucient to verify properties on the states but it is necessary to
verify properties on the actions as well.
Let's redene some notations:
• s ∈S is a state in the LTS.
• l ∈Act is an action in the LTS.
• π = s0, l0, s1, l1, s2, ... is an innite path of the LTS.
• Paths(s) is the set of all paths π starting from the state s.
• π(i) is the state with index i.
• π[i] is the action with index i.
• |= is the satisfaction relation for an ACTL formula. It can be read 'satisfy'.

In order to explain how does ACTL works, we will give a formal explanation
of the satisfaction sets by using both logic formulae and common words. The
semantics for the state formulae are the same of CTL.
Definition 3.8 Semantics of ACTL state formulae[BK08]
• s |= a i a ∈L(s)
• s |= Φ1 ∧Φ2 i s |= Φ1 ∧s |= Φ2
• s |= ¬Φ i s ̸|= Φ
• s |= Eφ i there exists a path π ∈Paths(s) with π |= φ
• s |= Aφ i for all paths π ∈Paths(s) we have that π |= φ
Definition 3.9 Semantics of ACTL action formulae[RMG04]
• l |= a i a ∈L(l)
An action l satises a if the action l is labeled a.
• l |= ¬χ i l ̸|= χ
An action l satises not χ if the action l does not satisfy the formula χ.
• l |= χ1 ∧χ2 i l |= χ1 ∧l |= χ2
An action l satises χ1 and χ2 if the action l satises the formula χ1 and
the formula χ2 as well.
Definition 3.10 Semantics of ACTL path formulae[RMG04]
• π |= XχΦ i π(1) |= Φ and π[0] |= χ
Considering starting from the state s0, the formula is satised if in the
next state of s0, Φ is satised (π(1) |= Φ) and this state is reachable by
the action χ (π[0] |= χ).
• π |= Φ1χUΦ2 i there exists some i ≥0 such that π(i) |= Φ2 and for all
0 ≤j ≤(i −1) we have that π(j) |= Φ1 and π[j] |= χ
Considering starting from the state s0, the formula is satised if Φ2 is
satised either on the initial state of the path (π(0) |= Φ2) or at some
point i along the path(π(i) |= Φ2). In case of this last situation, all the
states π(j) before π(i) satisfy Φ1 (π(j) |= Φ1) and they are reachable by
the action χ (π[j] |= χ), including the action before the state π(i).

Chapter 5
A prototype implementation
In the following chapter a prototype implementation for the translation rules is
presented. SML has been chosen as programming language for the denition
of the syntax and for the conversion. The SML code is used to translate an
LTS in the correspondent TS and an ACTL property in the correspondent CTL
property ready to be checked on the new TS. Three les have been realized, the
rst for the syntax of LTS and TS, and the second for the syntax of ACTL and
CTL. Both les are composed by two sections: the rst is used for the denition
of the syntax such as every state, path and action formula creates a specic type
and every element of the LTS and TS does the same. The second part is used
for the denition of some functions used for the translation and other purposes.
The third le is the main which includes the two previous les and combines
the transformation of LTS and ACTL.
5.1

transition systems in order to obtain one from the other. The le starts with
the denition of the data-types.
For our purpose we had to use two data-
types, one used to dene the elements of a transition system and the other

The labeling function is dened as list of the tuple (string ∗string), so that
the rst element represents the state or the action and the second element

The data-types are
named: LabelledTransitionSystem, TransitionSystem, lts and ts. They reect
the denition of TS and LTS as in 2.1 and 3.1. In order to explain the use of
the data-types we are going to show the use of some of them.
• StateLts of string list
It represents the set of states of an LTS. Every state in the LTS has to be
expressed as a string with an unique name.
We can dene the set of states SLT S = {s0, s1, s2, s3} as:
LTS(StateLts(["s_0","s_1","s_2","s_3"],...))
• RelationLts of (string*string*string) list
It represents the set of relations of an LTS. Every relation in the LTS has
to be expressed as a tuple of three strings where the element in the middle
represents the action taken to go from the state represented by the rst
element to the state represented by the third element of the tuple.
We can dene the set of relations RLT S = {(s0, l0, s1), (s1, l1, s2)} as:
LTS(StateLts([...]),ActionLts([...]),InitialStateLts([...]),
RelationLts([("s_0","l_0","s_1"),("s_1","l_1","s_2")])).
• LabelTs of (string*string) list
It represents the set of labels of a TS. Every label in the TS has to be
expressed as a tuple of two strings where the rst element represents the
state, and the second element represents the correspondent label on it.
We can dene the labeling function LT S = {L(s0) = φ1, L(s1) = φ2} as:
TS(StateTs([...]),InitialStateTs([...]),RelationTs([...]),APTs([...]),
LabelTs([("s_0","phi1"),("s_1","phi1")],...))
As we can see the lts and the ts data-types are dened such that you cannot
skip the insertion of one element in the tuple. Since the transition system is the
combination of all elements, the lts and the ts data-types have been expressed

model checker
There exists dierent model checking tools used to build a transition system and
verify its behaviour. In this project we are going to use PRISM, a probabilistic
model checker which use its own PRISM modelling language to construct a
system and a property specication language to describe some temporal logics
including CTL. The purpose of this chapter is to understand how PRISM can
be exploited to represent a transition system and nd a way, if there exists, to
represent a labeled transition system as well.
6.1
Prism
"PRISM is a probabilistic model checker, a tool for the modelling
and analysis of systems which exhibit probabilistic behaviour. Prob-
abilistic model checking is a formal verication technique. It is based
on the construction of a precise mathematical model of a system
which is to be analysed. Properties of this system are then expressed
formally in temporal logic and automatically analysed against the
constructed model." [DoCS]
==PAGE:58==
50
Exploiting the PRISM model checker
As we can see from the denition, PRISM uses its own language to dene a
transition system. The concepts to build a TS are really easy. PRISM needs
the number of the states of the TS and the initial states. In order to describe the
behaviour of the system, PRISM uses commands which include guards that have
to be satised to take a transition, and one or more updates which describe what
happens after the transition is taken. The main types used to dene variables
are int(integer) and bool(boolean). Every transition system can then be dened
by a dierent number of modules each describing a specic process. PRISM is
called probabilistic model checker since every command has a certain probability
to be executed. If no probability is dened, a probability of 1 is assumed.
After dening the system, PRISM has a section used to dene the labels and
the properties. The label section is used to identify some states that have a
particular behaviour. The property section is used to dene properties on the
system. After dening the model and its labels, PRISM allows to build the
model and if no errors are found a simulation can be done to check if properties
are satised over the dened transition system. PRISM uses a property speci-
cation language which can dene dierent logics. For the purpose of this project
we are going to analyse the use of this language to dene CTL. The property
specication language uses specic symbols to dene state and path formulae.

Chapter 8
Conclusions and Future
Work
The aim of this project was to investigate the action-based modelling view of
systems and the exploitation of a model checker to build and verify them using
Action Computation Tree Logic.
Thanks to the analysis of TS and CTL, it was possible to nd a way for trans-
forming Labeled Transition Systems in Transition Systems and ACTL formulae
in CTL formulae.
The conversion from LTS to TS was quite easy to realize and many examples
have been done to prove its validity. For the LTS transformation, it would have
been better to use a dierent name for the transformed TS's to distinguish them
from the 'non-derived' TS's. The 'squared states' graphically dierentiate it but
they make the system look strange from a modelling point of view. Moreover,
even though we didn't go through a deeper investigation of a big system, we
realized that the conversion from an LTS to a TS can easily bring to a state-
explosion due to the increase in the number of states because of the addition of
actions. We didn't deal with this problem but it is something which probably
needs a further investigation.
The time spent to nd a good conversion from ACTL to CTL lasted a bit longer
but at the end a short solution for the ACTL formulae transformation was dis-
covered by exploiting the existing operators of such logics. The conversion for
the Next Operator is quite straightforward. The same cannot exactly be said
for the Until Operators whose translation has been changed a lot during the
==PAGE:82==
74
Conclusions and Future Work
project. The syntax seems nally good and valuable but it would be better to
continue with some insights about it.
The study of SML language helped a lot in the denition of LTS and ACTL
due to the fact that SML has to work with specic data-types. Because of the
lack of experience in any kind of functional programming language, the nal
coding-style is not excellent and many functions could have been written in a
more clever way. However all the functions work as expected and the denition
of the syntax is clear and readable.
Finally the health care example was good to explain the aim of Labeled Tran-
sition Systems. The graph after the translation was represented with 'squared
states' on purpose, to better show how a transformed TS would look like. In
a further investigation the heath care system should be expanded to deal with
the problems of big and more realist systems.
The investigation of the outcome of this project generated many ideas for future
studies. The rst step could be the creation of a completely new model checker
used to dene LTS and ACTL formulae without using any transformation rules.
In this regard a possible model checker could be realized by starting from the
PRISM environment as model. The representation of LTS's could be done by
writing the usual PRISM commands to show the set of relations but with the
introduction of the name for the commands as identication of actions. The
model checker must be able to count the actions as separate sets of elements
and distinguish one action from the other by the dierent command names.
