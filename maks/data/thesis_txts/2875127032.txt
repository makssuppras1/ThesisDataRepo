Abstract
In this thesis, a fully decentralized and distributed collaborative localization (CL) algoôÙ
rithm was developed for use with unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) equipped with ultra
wideband (UWB) transceiver nodes. Using time of flight (ToF) and angle of arrival (AoA),
these UWB nodes provide rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements of both anchors and other
UAVs. Furthermore, exploiting the fact that both UWB nodes record AoA during a ranging
exchange, a pseudo angle of departure (AoD) was obtained. This allowed pose to be
fully determined from a single anchor measurement. The accuracy of the measurements
performed by these nodes at different ranges under line of sight (LOS) conditions was
investigated experimentally. A big challenge of decentralized CL is keeping track of interôÙ
robot correlations in order to avoid overconfident estimates. The implemented algorithm
approximates these correlations in a distributed extended Kalman filter (EKF), where each
UAV has its own individual filter. Furthermore, the algorithm was also enhanced to resolve
the frontôÙback ambiguity of the AoA measurements by selecting the most likely AoA based
on the prior belief. The CL algorithm using UWB transceivers was tested in simulation by
simulating six UAVs collaborating under various conditions. From the simulation results, it
was found that the localization algorithm can achieve a converging estimation error when
faced with limited measurement range and ambiguities, though overconfident estimates
could not be fully prevented. It was also found that collaboration improved the localization
accuracy of the UAVs in conditions where anchor measurements were limited.

Introduction
Collaborative groups of Unmanned Aerial vehicles (UAV) have been projected to see
widespread use in industry and other aspects of society. This includes surveillance, inôÙ
spection of infrastructure, search and rescue, environmental mapping and entertainment
[1]. In order for these groups of UAVs to perform optimally, each UAV must be able to
correctly determine where it is located within its environment and relative to other UAVs.
Typically, UAVs localize by first utilizing measurements from interoceptive sensors, such
as inertial measurement units (IMU), to perform dead reckoning. This prediction is then
fused with exteroceptive sensor measurements that provide absolute positioning inforôÙ
mation, such as global navigation satellite systems (GNSS) or measurements of anchors
with known position, to ensure a converging position estimate [2]. Collaborative groups
of UAVs provide additional sources for localization information, as each robot in the group
has a belief of its positioning in the environment. Through mutual relative measurements
between the UAVs in the group, the UAVs can correct their position estimate according
to the belief of others. This essentially enables sensorôÙsharing and belief propagation
among the UAVs. This paradigm is called Collaborative localization (CL) or is sometimes
also referred to as collaborative navigation (CN). Figure 1.1 illustrates how in CL, the
UAVs localize not only based on measurements to the anchors but also other UAVs. CL
provides many benefits over conventional localization, such as improved localization acôÙ
curacy [3]. Furthermore, it is also seen to be effective in environments where only certain
robots in the group have access to absolute positioning, such as anchors or GNSS [4].
For example, even though the topmost UAV in figure 1.1 cannot detect any anchor, it still
has access to absolute positioning through the other UAVs in the group. This makes for
a more robust localization, as not all UAVs in the group need direct access to absolute
position measurements.
Figure 1.1: Collaborative localization is performed with multiple UAVs and anchors. By exploiting
exteroceptive measurements of both anchors and other UAVs, illustrated with the dashed lines,
higher localization accuracy can be achieved.
However, one significant challenge of CL is properly accounting for the interôÙrobot corôÙ
relation. After a relative measurement between two UAVs has been used for correcting
their beliefs, their individual position estimates are now correlated. This correlation has

to be accounted for when fusing measurements in the future in order to avoid the doubleôÙ
counting problem [5]. DoubleôÙcounting occurs when shared information is assumed inôÙ
dependent which results in an overestimation of how much new information is gained.
This can lead to overconfident position estimates where the error between the estimated
position and actual position is larger than the associated estimated error covariance of
the position. This lack of consistency between the actual error and estimated error coôÙ
variance is disastrous for the performance of the localization. Therefore, it is important to
properly manage interôÙrobot correlations during CL.
In order to employ CL on the UAV, a sensor package that allows for absolute measureôÙ
ments and relative measurements between the UAVs participating in the CL is needed.
Sensors measuring both range and bearing are a common choice [6, 5, 7] and include
cameras and radio frequency sensors, such as UltraôÙwideband (UWB) radios. In particôÙ
ular, UWB is promising for shortôÙtoôÙmedium range realôÙtime localization purposes. UWBôÙ
based sensors can estimate the range between devices from the time of flight (ToF). By
employing an array of multiple antennas, the angle of arrival (AoA) or bearing can be esôÙ
timated from the phase difference of arrival (PDoA) of the incident signal received by the
antennas. Due to the high timeôÙresolution of UWB, the sensors exhibit very high localôÙ
ization accuracy on the order of decimeter, which is much higher than other comparable
shortôÙrange radioôÙbased techniques, such as Bluetooth [8]. Besides rangeôÙandôÙbearing
measurements, UWB radios are also capable of wireless communication which is reôÙ
quired for CL when communicating beliefs. Furthermore, they are lightweight, lowôÙcost
and powerôÙefficient [9]. This makes them ideal candidates for onôÙboard sensors on an
UAV with limited power and computational budget.

1.2
Related work
Both collaborative localization and localization with UWB technology have been extenôÙ
sively covered in the literature. This section introduces the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart developments
within both topics, which form the background for the UWBôÙbased CL algorithm developed
for this thesis.
Collaborative localization
The most direct approach to CL is that of centralized CL. In this scheme, all robots commuôÙ
nicate to a single fusion center that runs the CL algorithm for the entire system. However,
this approach requires significant communication, assumes that all robots can communiôÙ
cate with the fusion center at all times and is vulnerable to attacks that render the fusion
center inoperable. Decentralized CL or distributed CL, seeks to distribute the localization
out among the robots to reduce communication, increase robustness and make better
use of the onboard computational resources of the robots. Especially with decentralized
CL, special care must be taken to maintain interôÙrobot correlations. Usually, one of three
different approaches are taken for implementing decentralized CL: Kalman filters, particle
filters or optimization [11].
Optimization involves minimizing a cost function, such as the error between estimated
position and measured position, from historical data in order to determine the current state.
The optimization problem can then be solved with a least squares (LS) [12, 13], maximum
likelihood (ML) [14, 13] or maximum a posteriori (MAP) [15, 13] estimator. These methods
can be computationally expensive and require large amounts of communication as the
optimization has to happen over the entire network of states to be effective and properly
account for interôÙrobot correlations.
A popular approach to optimization is factor graphs (FG) [16, 13, 15, 17]. Factor graphs
define graphs of nodes representing the states at different times connected by probabilisôÙ
tic factors. These factors include motion model factors that connect two nodes, private
measurement factors that connect to single nodes and relative factors that connect nodes
across different robots. The states in the chain can then be solved as a MAP problem. To
reduce communication, marginalization can be employed to reduce the size of the node
chains received from other robots [15, 17]. While it yields good performance, FG optiôÙ
mization is still computationally expensive, especially compared to a Kalman filter [16].
The particle filter (PF) estimates the prior probability distribution from clouds of particles
generated from the prediction model. The posterior distribution is then found by weighting
the particles according to how good they fit exteroceptive measurements. The advantage
of PF is that it can handle systems with nonôÙlinear prediction and measurement models as
well as nonôÙGaussian probability distributions. However, it is computationally expensive
and most detrimental of all for CL, it has a hard time tracking interôÙrobot correlations as
discussed by Chang et al. in their literature review [7]. As a result, many methods assume
no correlation [18, 19]. Alternatively, the PF has to be augmented by other means, such
as using covariance intersection to estimate interôÙrobot correlations and resampling the
particles from the resulting posterior distribution [20].
Finally, the Kalman filter (KF) approach is one of the more widely adopted. While it is
constrained to modeling uncertainties in the system as unimodal Gaussians, it is compuôÙ
tationally efficient due to its closed form nature. Many decentralized KFôÙvariants for CL
have been developed with the focus being to reduce communication while still correctly
accounting for interôÙrobot correlations. Through bookkeeping of multiple filters, the overôÙ
confidence problem can be avoided. Bahr et al. [21] used interleaved updates (IU), where
each robot kept a bank of multiple filters and only used those filters that were not already
MultiôÙRobot Collaborative Localization using UltraôÙWideband Technology
3
==PAGE:12==
correlated with a robot j for updating said robot. However, their approach required that
up to 2n filters had to be maintained for each robot, where n is the number of robots in the
group, and thus this method does not scale well.
A more popular method for estimating interôÙrobot correlations is covariance intersection
(CI) or variants thereof [7, 22, 23]. The problem with the CI approach is that while it preôÙ
vents overconfidence and only requires communication between the robots involved in a
relative measurement, it is very conservative in regards to the estimated error covariance.
Some authors have attempted optimization techniques to improve upon CI, at the cost of
computational efficiency [4].
Roumeliotis et al. introduced a fully decentralized distributed CL, where the robots could
maintain interôÙrobot correlations during the prediction step and private measurements [3].
Only when two robots made a relative measurement, did they need to communicate the
updated interôÙrobot correlations to all other robots. While it required communication to
all robots after a relative measurement, the scheme has identical performance to a fully
centralized EKF. Luft et al. [5] continued with this framework, and through a blockôÙdiagonal
approximation in the update step, they cut down information significantly so only the two
robots involved in the measurement needed to communicate. While the block diagonal
approximation means this algorithm cannot guarantee the consistency of the filter, it still
closely matches the centralized CL in performance and outperforms CIôÙbased approaches
in regards to localization accuracy [7].
Sensing with UWB
UWBôÙbased sensors have seen widespread use in realôÙtime localization (RTL) systems,
where ToF, AoA or both are used to localize UWB nodes within an environment [24, 25,
1]. In the literature, Qorvoãs DW1000 IC is a popular choice when developing UWBôÙbased
sensors. However, this IC only supports a single antenna port which means that on its own
it cannot measure the PDoA required for estimating AoA. Multiple ICs connected to the
same clock are therefore needed to estimate AoA [26, 27, 24]. The newer DW3000 family
introduced a second antenna port to the chip, which means AoA can be estimated from the
PDoA between the antennas on a single chip [28]. Furthermore, this family of transceivers
promises high accuracy of up to ôÝ5 cm for range measurements and ôÝ5ãÎfor angular
measurements [29]. Margiani et al. utilized the DW3220 chip to develop a compact UWB
sensor node with the capability of performing both ranging and AoA measurements [10].
However, as the sensor node estimates AoA from only two antennas, frontôÙback ambiguity
is inevitable and needs to be resolved using other measurements [28]. This can be done
by considering rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements from multiple sources [14] or by utilizing
interoceptive motion measurements [30]. Alternatively, more antennas can be employed
in specific configurations to resolve these ambiguities. Mathew et al. did so with their
UWB sensor featuring a sensor array with four antennas, enabling full localization within
3D space [24]. Usually, transceivers from the DW3000 family have a maximum range of
about 50 meters, but studies have shown that this range can potentially be extended up
to 300 meters, while still complying with local regulations [31].

cation technology and decentralized collaborative localization to develop a robust UWBôÙ
based CL algorithm for a group of collaborating UAVs. This leads to the following problem

1.3
Problem statement
A group of UAVs seeks to localize itself within an environment. To do so they have acôÙ
cess to interoceptive IMU measurements and can perform exteroceptive measurements
of other UAVs and anchors using onboard UWB nodes capable of rangeôÙandôÙbearing
measurements and communication. Each individual UAV has no prior knowledge of the
size of the group or the number of anchors, which might also change over time. FurôÙ
thermore, the onboard UWB nodes have limited communication range and their bearing
measurements suffer from frontôÙback ambiguity. The goal of this thesis is to develop a
scalable and robust collaborative localization algorithm that is able to provide satisfactory
performance under the aforementioned conditions. Furthermore, firmware for the UWB
nodes enabling the rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements required for the CL has to be deôÙ
veloped.
When approaching this problem formulation, the following assumptions regarding the sysôÙ
tem of UAVs that the CL algorithm is targeting, are made:
ãÂ All UAVs in the group maintain line of sight (LOS) at all times.
ãÂ All UAVs movement is restricted to the 2D horizontal plane and all move at the same
height. It is assumed that the only rotation they experience is around the azimuth
axis.
ãÂ If an UAV is able to make a measurement of another UAV/anchor, it is also able to
successfully communicate with it.
ãÂ The beliefs of all UAVs are properly initialized at the start of a run.

2.1
Overview of ultraôÙwideband technology
UltraôÙwideband (UWB) technologies utilize a wide band of at least 500 MHz for wireless
communication. This wide bandwidth allows for extremely short radio frequency impulses,
on the order of nanoseconds [9]. This is used in impulse radios (IR) UWB systems, where
the transmission is defined by a series of short pulses with very low duty cycle. Data can
be embedded in the pulse trains by modulating the position of the pulse (burst position
modulation or BPM) within a frame, or modulating the polarity of the pulse (binary phaseôÙ
shift keying or BPSK). An example of the resulting sequence of such modulation is given
in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Example of how information is modulated in an IR UWB transmission. The position of
the pulse is modulated with BPM, so it occupies one of multiple positions in each frame. In the
figure, each frame is separated by the dotted lines. The polarity of the pulse can also be modulated
with BPSK. In the above figure, positive polarity is illustrated by an upwards arrow and vice versa
for negative polarity. A number of frames can make up one information symbol, which is used to
transmit data.
The wide bandwidth comes with a number of advantages. The wide bandwidth improves
the penetration capabilities of the signal, as it contains both low and high frequencies.
The high bandwidth also results in a high time resolution, which allows for more accurate
timestamping of signals. According to Shannonãs capacity formula [9], the capacity of a
channel increases linearly with the bandwidth
C = B log2(1 + SNR)
Where C is the capacity and B is the bandwidth, both in bits per second. SNR is the
signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratio. UWB communication systems are thus capable of fast data rates,
while still keeping the signal power low. This also reduces the interference they have on
other narrowôÙband communication systems, as the signal power is spread over a wide

spectrum. Finally, UWB transceiver architecture is significantly simpler than its narrow
band equivalent, as the signals are carrierôÙfree. This allows for lowôÙcost implementations.
UWB systems are also very secure and resistant to detection, interception, and jamming
[8].

Whenever a UWB node wishes to transmit to another node, these transmissions need to
follow a certain packet structure, so the receiving node knows how to acquire and interpret
the data correctly. The IEEE 802.15.4z standard defines the UWB packet structure shown
in table 2.1 [32].

The functionality of these five fields of the packet is as follows:
ãÂ Preamble: The Ipatov preamble consists of a predetermined sequence of pulses.
The receiver is able to recognize this sequence and knows it is acquiring a preamôÙ
ble of a packet. From the preamble, the CIR (channel impulse response) can be
estimated. From this CIR, further information regarding the incoming signal can be
extracted, such as timestamp, phase of arrival, and signal quality.
ãÂ SFD: The SFD (start of frame delimiter) is a known set of symbols that marks the
end of the preamble and the start of the PHR.
ãÂ PHR: The physical header contains information regarding data rate and frame length.
ãÂ PHY payload: The data payload. According to the IEEE 802.15.4ôÙ2015 standard,
this can be up to 127 bytes, though the IEEE 802.15.8ôÙ2015 standard optionally
extends this to 1023 bytes [32].
ãÂ STS: Optionally a STS (scrambled timestamp sequence) can be included in the
frame. This is an encrypted sequence of pulses, where a secure CIR can be reôÙ
trieved if both transmitter and receiver share the same key. This STS can be placed
after the SFD, after the payload, or completely replacing both PHR and payload [32].
In table 2.1, the STS is placed after the SFD.
The IEEE 802.15.4ôÙ2015 standard for UWB packets is the one used for messaging in
this thesis. They are used for determining the angle of arrival and range, as well as for
communication between devices. How UWB technology can be used for localization will
be covered in these next sections.

2.4
Network protocol
UWB radios are halfôÙduplex, meaning they can only operate in a transmit or receive mode,
not both at the same time. Furthermore, in a system of single channel UWB radios,
only one radio can be transmitting in a shared channel at a time to avoid message colliôÙ
sion. Therefore, a network protocol is needed to control which UWB radio can transmit
in the channel at any given time. For adôÙhoc wireless networks with varying node topolôÙ
ogy, CarrierôÙsense multiple access with collision avoidance (CSMA/CA) [36] is a popular
choice. In basic CSMA, a transceiver first checks (or senses) if there is any activity in the
channel before transmitting in it. If the device senses activity in the channel, it backs off
for some time, before attempting to use the channel again. UWBôÙbased CSMA senses
channel activity by looking for any preambles present in the channel, as UWB does not
make use of any carrier waves.
For wireless systems, CSMA is often enhanced by augmenting it with collision avoidance
(CSMA/CA). Here, the transmitting node first transmits a short requestôÙtoôÙsend (RTS)
message, requesting access to the channel. When the message is received by the inôÙ
tended receiver, it responds with a clearôÙtoôÙsend (CTS) message, which then initiates the
exchange between the nodes. This RTSôÙCTS handshake announces the transmission to
all other nodes, so they may hold off on attempting transmission for some time.

An important aspect of the board design is the distance between the two AH086M555003ôÙ

The transceiver IC used for the UWB nodes is the DW3220 IC developed by Qorvo [43].

4.2
Firmware
The firmware used for the nodes is based on the firmware developed by Margiani et al. [10]
and the API for the driver functions in Qorvoãs driver package version 04.00.00 [45]. The
Qorvo driver functions implement a lot of useful functions for writing and reading registers
on the DW3220 IC, which makes programming the IC significantly easier. Included in the
package are also platformôÙspecific drivers for running the firmware on a Raspberry Pi.
The firmware by Margiani et al. was capable of performing DS TWR while simultaneously
measuring the PDoA, allowing for rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements. However, it still had
some serious issues and shortcomings that had to be circumvented in order to use it for
this project. The most obvious issue is that it was hardcoded so one node was only able
to perform ranging with another specific node. For this project, it is of interest to perform
ranging between multiple nodes, so the firmware had to be expanded to support that.
The other more significant issue was that the DS TWR had been implemented incorrectly
in the firmware. This resulted in range measurements that had a large offset of about
seven kilometers, drifted over time, and had a noise covariance that was about five times
larger than what was expected for the DW3220 IC. In their paper, they circumvented these
issues by using a machine learning (ML) approach, where a model was fed data such as
the CIR, PDoA, and TDoA and thus achieved a high accuracy for their measurements.
This model could not be used in this project, as it was only trained on the impulse response
of a specific room at ETH. Furthermore, it would have added unnecessary complexity to
the project to include a ML model, as the base accuracy of the DW3220 IC of ôÝ5 cm for
ranging and ôÝ5ãÎfor AoA is sufficient for the purpose of this project. The ML model also
required that large amounts of diagnostic metrics be read from the IC over SPI, which
significantly slowed down the rate at which measurements could be made.

4.2.1
Communication between the Raspberry Pi and DW3220
The communication between the Raspberry Pi and the DW3220 IC is handled by the
platformôÙspecific drivers developed by Qorvo [45]. It makes use of the WiringPi library to
set up the SPI, the interrupt input pin, and the reset output pin on the Raspberry Pi. The
SPI is set up to use chip select 0 for the DW3220 IC, with a clock speed of 32 MHz and
SPI mode 0. The Raspberry Pi is configured as the master and the DW3220 IC as the
slave in the exchange.
4.2.2
Configuration of the DW3220 IC
The DW3220 IC supports different configurations, which affects its performance in reôÙ
gards to range and measurement throughput. In general, lower data rate and higher
preamble length allow for longer range at the cost of longer packets, which also take
longer to transmit [31, 32]. For this project, a preamble of 256 symbols was used for both
the Ipatov preamble and the STS. The PAC (Preamble acquisition chunk) was configured
to 16 symbols, in accordance with the DW3000 manual [32]. For the shortôÙdistance meaôÙ
surements, the data rate was configured to 6.8 Mbs. For longôÙdistance measurements,
the data rate was configured to 850 kbs. Furthermore, in order to retrieve the PDoA from
the received STS of each antenna, the IC was configured to enable STS, and the PDoA
mode was set to 3.

Transmitting and receiving messages
Transmission and reception of messages can be easily handled with the functions proôÙ
vided by the API. Before transmission, the byte array representing the PHY payload is
written to the transmission buffer with dwt_writetxdata(). The TX frame control registers
can then be configured with dwt_writetxfctrl() before finally initiating the transmission
with dwt_starttx().
The IC supports different transmission modes, of which the DS TWR implementation uses
DWT_START_TX_IMMEDIATE and DWT_START_TX_DELAYED. The first of these starts the transôÙ
mission as soon as possible. The latter schedules the message to be transmitted at a
given time set by dwt_setdelayedtrxtime(). This is used for controlling the length of
the reply times in the DS TWR implementation. If the IC misses this scheduled time,
the transmission will fail. Ideally, one would go with the shortest delay time possible, in
order to maximize the throughput of the range measurements and reduce the effects of
clock drift. In order to make sure the hardware meets the realôÙtime constraints imposed
by the delayed transmission, the number of expensive operations between reception of
the previous message and the delayed transmission of the reply should be minimized.
Especially SPI transactions between the Raspberry Pi and DW3220 IC can have relaôÙ
tively large overhead, so in the implementation of the DS TWR, all SPI transactions that
can be made in advance, such as dwt_writetxfctrl(), are performed while waiting for
the reception of a message. Using these optimizations, a delayed transmission time of

around tdelay_reply = 1.2 ms was found to be a good middle ground between speed and
minimizing missed transmission errors. tdelay_reply for both devices is kept identical to
further reduce the effect of clock drift on the measurements, as described in section 2.3.
Handling reception of messages is also fairly simple. Received payloads are stored in
the receive buffer and can be read with dwt_readrxdata() when a message has been
successfully received. Afterwards, the receiver will automatically turn off.
The driver API supports callback functions for four kinds of interrupts: Successful transôÙ
mission of a message, successful reception of a message, reception error, and timeout
error. The successful transmission and reception interrupts are used in the implementaôÙ
tion to cut down on SPI communication between the Raspberry PI and DW3220 IC, as
the Raspberry Pi otherwise would need to constantly poll the status register of the IC over
SPI.
Obtaining timestamps
The round and reply times used to obtain the ToF are calculated from the timestamps
recorded by the IC. Whenever a message is successfully transmitted or received, the
timestamp can be retrieved with the API functions dwt_readtxtimestamp() and
dwt_readrxtimestamp(). The timestamps are stored as 40ôÙbit unsigned integers and are
given in device time units, where the resolution of these device time units is approximately
15.65 ps per unit.
The timestamps wrap after approximately 17 seconds. If the 40ôÙbit timestamps are stored
as unsigned 64ôÙbit integers, this might cause problems when subtracting timestamps in
order to obtain reply and round times, due to the timestamps wrapping. To avoid this, the
timestamps are cast to unsigned 32ôÙbit integers, before the subtraction is done. As long
as the reply and round times are not larger than 67 ms, the truncation of the upper 8 bits
should not cause issues.
One important thing to note about the timestamps is that there will be a slight delay beôÙ
tween when the timestamp is received by the antenna and when it is processed and
recorded by the IC, and vice versa for transmission. While this delay is very small, it
causes huge offsets in the calculated ToF. This is mitigated with the functions
dwt_settxantennadelay() and dwt_setrxantennadelay(). These take as input a deôÙ
lay value in device time units, that is then automatically added to the timestamps when
retrieved. For this project, a TX and RX antenna delay of tant_delay = 16387 device time
units was used. This antenna delay was mainly found through experimentation, but is not
far from the standard delay of 16385 device time units.

4.2.4
Retrieving PDoA
If the DW3220 is configured to include STS in the packets and the PDoA mode is set to
3, the IC will automatically estimate the PDoA between antennas from their respective
STSs every time it receives a signal. The IC then automatically stores this value in a
dedicated register as a signed fixed point 16ôÙbit value, with the unit being radians. The
Qorvo API allows for easy retrieval of the PDoA stored in this register with the function
call dw_readpdoa(). From here, the PDoA can be used to calculate the AoA or AoD.
Obtaining a function that maps between the two is detailed in section 4.3.5.
In the implementation used in this project, both nodes measure the PDoA during a DS
TWR. The responder node reads the PDoA stored locally when it detects that it has reôÙ
ceived the response message, while the initiator node reads the PDoA when it detects it
has received the final message.
4.2.5
Network protocol for multiple nodes
As noted in section 2.4, a network protocol is needed to manage and distribute the airtime
between the nodes. Since the localization is completely distributed among the UAVs, the
network protocol must also have to be ad hoc in nature and assume no prior knowledge of
which nodes are involved in the localization. The protocol takes inspiration from CSMA,
by leveraging the fact that the firmware has been set up to generate an interrupt each
time a message is successfully received. This way, the nodes can keep track of when the
channel is free, by keeping the receiver on.
As seen in figure 4.2, a node can either be in initiator mode or responder mode. All nodes
start out initially as responders. As a responder, the node awaits a sync message from
the initiator. This sync message has a similar function to the RTS message of CSMA/CA,
since it requests to start a DS TWR. When it receives one, it enables the receiver and
checks if the channel is busy. It does so by checking for reception of any messages. If
there is no activity in the channel within a random time period tpoll_wait, then the node
transmits a poll message targeted at the initiator node, thus initiating the DS TWR, similar
to the function of the CTS message. The randomness of tpoll_wait lowers the risk of two
nodes attempting to start a DS TWR at the same time. When the DS TWR is complete,
the node in responder mode waits for the next sync message.
The CSMA approach is also used for deciding when a node can enter initiator mode. The
node in responder mode first checks if any activity in the channel has happened within a
time window tto_resp. If the channel is clear, the node changes to initiator mode. tto_resp
is set randomly, to more evenly distribute which node gets to be initiator. In initiator mode,
the node immediately sends a single sync message. It then waits for any poll message
from a node in responder mode. When it receives a poll, it completes the DS TWR with the
node that sent the poll message and then goes back to waiting for the next poll message.
If no more poll messages are received within the time window tto_init, it changes back to
responder mode.
The timings of tpoll_wait, tto_resp, and tto_init are important to maximize the performance
of the network protocol while still avoiding message collisions. For the current implemenôÙ

Simulating collaborative localization
This chapter presents the final collaborative localization algorithm used by the group of
collaborating UAVs. Furthermore, the simulation used to test the localization algorithm
is also presented here. The experimental results from the UWB nodes are used to best
represent the behavior of the nodes in the simulation, including error covariance, sensor

5.1
The localization algorithm
The CL algorithm used in this project for the group of collaborating UAVs is based on
the one developed by Luft et al. [5], which was presented in detail in section 3.2. In the
context of the framework laid out by Luft et al., the private measurements are the rangeôÙ
andôÙbearing measurements to an anchor. The relative measurements are the rangeôÙ
andôÙbearing measurements between UAVs. All the measurements and prediction models
used for the algorithm are presented in section 3.5. The localization algorithm developed
in this project supports both the normal AoA rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurement and the
variant that features both AoA and pseudoôÙAoD. An overview of the localization algorithm

Due to issues with the numerical stability of the algorithm causing the system covariance

Another modification to the algorithm is the ability to handle ambiguous measurements.
As seen in section 4.3, the bearing measurements from the nodes can feature up to four

very simple approach to this by simply selecting the rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurement with

the benefit of being lightweight in regards to computation, storage, and especially in the
communication step between UAVs, as they only need to send and receive a single belief.

to 16 when AoD is included in the rangeôÙandôÙbearing, due to both AoA and AoD featuring
up to four ambiguities each.

5.2.1
Trajectories and simulated IMU measurements
The different reference trajectories that the simulated UAVs can follow are given by polyôÙ
nomials generated from a number of preôÙdefined states that the UAV must assume at speôÙ
cific times. Quintic polynomials are used to solve for the position, velocity, acceleration,
and jerk trajectories, while cubic polynomials are used to solve for the orientation, angular
rate, and angular acceleration trajectories. The calculated position and orientation trajecôÙ
tories are used as the reference, while the acceleration and angular rate trajectories are
used to simulate the IMU measurements the UAV makes while it is moving between poôÙ
sitions. ZeroôÙmean Gaussian noise, random biases and random outliers can be injected
into these simulated IMU measurements to better model the measurement noise that the
actual sensors might experience.
For this project, six different distinct trajectories were defined for the simulated UAVs to
follow. They all simulate the UAV sitting still for 30 seconds, before taking off and flying
around for 10 minutes. These trajectories, along with all the possible anchor locations,

Discussion
The discussion presented in this chapter is split up into two separate parts ôÙ one for the
UWB nodes and one for the localization algorithm.
6.1
The UWB nodes
6.1.1
Range measurements
The accuracy of the range measurements of the UWB nodes developed in this project
closely matched that of the expected ôÝ5 cm advertised by Qorvo at shorter ranges dref ãÊ5
meters. At longer ranges, the standard deviation and mean error are larger, but they are
also consistent over all distances. The antenna delays were calibrated based on the
short range data, but it seems that this calibration does not carry over well to long range
measurements, as these all have a consistent positive bias of 18ôÙ28 cm.
Higher accuracy in the ranging measurements can be achieved by properly calibrating
both the TX and RX antenna delays of each individual UWB node according to Qorvoãs
calibration application note [48]. Furthermore, the received signal level also introduces a
ranging bias on the order of centimeters and needs to be taken into account in order to
obtain higher accuracy [35]. These two methods were not applied in this project, as the
base accuracy seen in table 4.3 was sufficient for the purposes of this thesis.
The accuracy of the range measurements presented in this thesis assumes that the nodes
are stationary. In a real localization application with moving UAVs, this will obviously not be
the case. In the simulations conducted in this project, it was assumed that the movement
of the UAVs was slow enough so as to not introduce too much movementôÙbased noise
into the measurements.
The nodes were able to perform DS TWR at ranges up to 50 meters. At this range howôÙ
ever, the nodes were only able to range at specific angles, as the STS quality was otherôÙ
wise too poor. Longer range should be achievable by increasing the Ipatov preamble and
STS of the packets sent by the node, though this also lowers the measurement throughôÙ
put. It should be possible to achieve ranges of up to 300 meters using DW3000 hardware
according to Han et al. [31].
6.1.2
PDoA and AoA measurements
The PDoA measurements and the fitted PDoA to AoA model leave much room for imôÙ
provement. The PDoA wrapôÙaround and odd antenna behavior significantly complicates
obtaining AoA from the PDoA. It was out of scope to redesign the UWB nodes for this
project, but removing the wrapôÙaround by moving the antennas closer together would
greatly simplify the PDoA to AoA model at no cost. Furthermore, replacing the antennas
on the board might help in obtaining more consistent PDoA measurements in different enôÙ
vironments, as it seems the current antennas have trouble estimating the PDoA at certain
angles. The consistent PDoAôÙAoA relationship seen in figure 4.5 only appeared when
using the indoor testôÙsetup seen in figure 4.3a ôÙ attempting to measure the PDoA in any
other environment yielded results closer to those seen in figure 4.6. However, for the few
angles where the nodes were actually able to obtain a proper PDoA, the measurements
were fairly consistent over all ranges, hinting that with proper hardware the UWB nodes
are able to measure AoA over longer ranges.
The current linear fit between the PDoA and AoA fails to capture the curvature at the peaks
and thereby introduces biases in the error. This could perhaps be mitigated by using a
MultiôÙRobot Collaborative Localization using UltraôÙWideband Technology
63
==PAGE:72==
continuous piecewise linear function, with a linear fit for the curved part at the peaks and
another for the straight part between the peaks. Alternatively, a lookup table could be
used for a more accurate PDoA to AoA mapping, but this would also come with the risk of
overfitting. In the end, the linear fitting was chosen for its simplicity, even though it means
the AoA can only be determined within ôÝ18ãÎ, which is far larger than the advertised error
of ôÝ5ãÎby Qorvo.
6.1.3
Network protocol
The current CSMA/CA inspired network protocol used for managing channel access could
be more efficient. As it turns out, the current method of sending out a sync message and
waiting for all potential delayed poll messages is slower than just sending a sync message
and then stopping expecting anymore poll messages after the first one. That is because
with the current method, the node always has to wait for tto_init after performing DS TWR
with all available nodes, to make sure there are no more delayed poll messages left,
instead of just directly returning to responder mode. Expecting only one poll message per
sync would also allow for a simpler and more robust network protocol that is more inôÙline
with how the standard CSMA/CA network protocol works, with only a single CTS per RTS.
Other optimizations could also be employed to decrease the number of failed DS TWRs.
In the current implementation, any message received during a DS TWR that is not part of
the exchange triggers the DS TWR to fail. As the nodes have to wait at least tdelay_reply
before a reply or final message is sent back to them, the receiver might as well be turned
off during this time to reduce the risk of receiving a wrong message. The DW3220 actually
already supports a momentary receiver timeout right after a transmission, which can be
exploited for this purpose.
At the time of testing the network protocol, there was only enough hardware to test three
nodes using the same channel. This was just enough for testing all the basic features
of the network protocol, such as the ability for the two nodes responding to the sync
message to properly manage which node should perform the DS TWR first. However,
it would have been very beneficial to test it with more nodes to see if access could still
be evenly distributed beyond three nodes and how it affects the number of collisions and
throughput. Right now tto_init, tto_resp and tpoll_wait are mainly chosen empirically based
on the current setup with three nodes and it is very likely they would have to change with
a larger amount of nodes.
The firmware developed in this project does not strictly follow the IEEE 802.15.4z stanôÙ
dard. This makes it incompatible with other UWB devices. This was not seen as a probôÙ
lem for this project, as the localization system developed here is envisioned to be selfôÙ
contained.
6.2
The localization algorithm
6.2.1
Assumptions underlying the localization algorithm
As the localization algorithm builds on the EKF, the noise sources are assumed zeroôÙ
mean, Gaussian and white. This assumption has not been formally tested in this project,
but it is clear that the measurement noise does not meet all these requirements. As
seen in table 4.3, the range measurements all feature a bias. More severe is the AoA
measurements, where the imperfect mapping between PDoA and AoA results in quite
severe biases, especially at the peaks at ôÝ90ãÎ. This affects the optimality of the filter.
The current solution to this problem is simply to make the measurement noise larger to
account for modeling errors, but an alternative improvement would be to employ more
detailed models in the filter.

6.2.2
Motion and measurement models
It can be argued that the IMU bias states are perhaps not needed to be estimated and that
a quick occasional automatic calibration, similar to what is done in the first 30 seconds
of the simulation would be sufficient. According to the data and the datasheet for the
IMU, the change in the biases is not noticeable during shorter time spans of operation.
Not tracking these bias states would reduce the size of the state space to five. Smaller
state spaces improves both the runtime of the algorithm and the memory usage, but also
significantly reduces the size of the messages communicated between UAVs.
Similarly, it was deemed unnecessary to estimate the biases of the range measurements
and the bearing measurements. This is either because they appear random as in the case
of the ranging results in table 4.3, or because they are not drifting significantly over time
in the case of the PDoA bias. However, the error in the range caused by nonôÙline of sight
(NLOS) conditions is often modeled as a positive bias [49] and it can be very relevant to
include this augmentation if the UAVs were to be employed in an environment with more
obstacles. But for this thesis where LOS is assumed at all times, such state augmentation
is not necessary.
The EKF linearizes the prediction and measurement models, which can lead to linearizaôÙ
tion errors, if the measurement and prediction models are very nonlinear. An alternative
Kalman filter that reduces the linearization error is the unscented Kalman filter (UKF) [37],
which has been shown to yield superior results compared to the EKF in other CL systems
using only range measurements [23]. Considering the nonlinear measurement functions
for the rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements, an UKF would perhaps be a better fit than the
conventional EKF. While Luft et al.ãs CL algorithm is expressed for EKFs only, it should
be possible to convert it to use UKF, as the update equations for EKF and UKF are the
same.
At the moment, it is assumed in the simulation that the rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements
occur synchronously with the prediction updates, that is, in time slots of 10 ms. However,
the network protocol used in this project is fully adôÙhoc and is not restricted to time slots
ôÙ this means for example that in the real world, a measurement can arrive 5 ms after a
prediction update has happened. This needs to be accounted for on a real system. For
example, the integration time öÇt of the prediction model could be adjusted adaptively to
obtain a prediction at the time the measurement was made. Either that, or a slotted version
of the CSMA/CA network protocol must be used, which schedules the rangeôÙandôÙbearing
measurements so the measurement and prediction update can happen in the same time
slot.
Right now, the entire system is formulated in the 2D plane. Of course in a real application
the UAVs are expected to move in a 3D space and the models presented in this thesis
would also then have to be reformulated. However, with only two antennas, the UWB
nodes can only be used for computing the planar bearing. This means either 3D trilaterôÙ
ation or additional sensors that return the height of the UAV would have to be introduced
to the UAV in order to estimate the 3D position. As this thesis was mainly interested in
exploring the possibilities of collaborative localization with UWB, a detailed 3D description
of the problem was deemed out of scope.
6.2.3
Handling of ambiguous measurements and robustness
In its base form, the EKF framework used in this project only supports unimodal beliefs.
This can be a bit of a problem considering that the rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements are
multiôÙmodal in nature, due to the ambiguous measurements. The EKF can be enhanced
to support multimodal beliefs with frameworks such as multi hypothesis tracking [6], where
MultiôÙRobot Collaborative Localization using UltraôÙWideband Technology
65
==PAGE:74==
every possible belief created by ambiguous measurements is tracked in a Gaussian mixôÙ
ture model. As the number of beliefs increases exponentially whenever the ambiguous
measurements are being made, aggressive pruning of less important mixture components
is often used to keep the memory requirements down. Still, the problem with this frameôÙ
work appears when relative measurements are being made: In this case, up to 16 new
beliefs that need to be tracked can be created from a single measurement, if both AoA and
AoD are used for the rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurement. Furthermore, all these beliefs and
all the interôÙrobot correlations between all the possible beliefs need to be communicated
between the UAVs, which means much heavier traffic over the single channel available
to all UAVs. As an alternative, the factor graphs framework supports multimodal beliefs
and is widely used in collaborative localization in conjunction with marginalization to cut
down on communication. These two frameworks might be better suited for the localizaôÙ
tion problem at hand. However, in the simulations carried out for this project, the simple
method of resolving ambiguous measurements by simply selecting the measurement with
the smallest NIS seemed sufficient in most cases. As seen in the results conducted with
measurements featuring ambiguities, the UAVs did not seem to get lost or follow the wrong
ambiguous measurements as long as a certain level of communication connectivity beôÙ
tween the UAVs and anchors was maintained. The only exception seems to be UAV 6
in table C.7. Even in the communication outage test, all UAVs, both collaborative and
nonôÙcollaborative, seemed to recover over time. Nevertheless, more testing with longer
simulations would be required before drawing any conclusive remarks on the algorithmãs
robustness to the ambiguous measurements.
A way of increasing the robustness that was considered for the localization algorithm
would be to skip measurement updates completely if the NIS is too large. If the estimate
were detected as being inconsistent, the estimated covariance P (k) could potentially be
inflated by a large amount in order to recover a consistent estimate. However, the problem
in the collaborative localization is actually detecting which UAV has a wrong estimate. In
appendix C it is seen that plenty of UAVs displays overconfidence based on the ANEES
but not the ANIS. Thresholding both anchor and UAV measurements based on NIS might
then result in the UAVs ignoring all anchor measurements, as the absolute positioning is
incorrect but the relative pose between UAVs still holds. The same problem also applies
to resetting P (k) ôÙ if the UAV makes a measurement to a UAV with an incorrect estimate
and triggers the reset, the UAV with the wrong estimate might then correct the reset UAV
afterwards and degrade the pose estimate of the other robot. For these reasons, and
the fact that the current localization algorithm seemed to recover fine, these recovery
mechanisms were not implemented in this project.
6.2.4
Performance
Throughout the simulation, the localization system has exhibited very high accuracy, on
the centimeter scale for the position and subôÙdegree for the orientation. This is an imôÙ
pressive accuracy, considering the scale of the simulation space is 100 by 70 meters. It
is unlikely however that employing the algorithm on a real system would yield such high
accuracies. The simulation results in this project does not take into account more adôÙ
vanced noise sources such as biases and outliers in the measurements. Furthermore,
only the smaller variance of the short range measurements was considered, when definôÙ
ing the measurement noise covariance. Finally, the simulation did not simulate how the
measurement range and accuracy are affected by the angle between nodes. Thus, the
simulations provide something more akin to best case performance. For any conclusions
regarding the true performance of the system, an experimental campaign would have to
be carried out.

With regards to consistency, the CL algorithm does not perform as well.
In general,
when the UAVs are collaborating, their estimates show significant signs of overconfidence
based on the ANEES. The nonôÙcollaborating UAVs suffer from overconfidence to a much
lesser degree, except for the cases where the the measurement range is limited. In most
cases, the estimate of the collaborative UAVs suffers from overconfidence momentarily
and then recovers as seen in figure 5.5 and figure 5.7 ôÙ only when the measurement range
is too short does it stay overconfident. A variety of factors can be the cause of these inconôÙ
sistent estimates. It might be due to the nonlinearities of the measurement and prediction
models, where the linearization error often becomes more pronounced as the covariance
of the estimate error grows. The effect of the linearization errors does not seem to affect
the ANEES in the case of nonôÙcollaborative UAVs in table 5.1, but here the errors are also
kept small. Luft et al.ãs algorithm does not guarantee consistency of estimates and could
very well also contribute to the consistency issues. However, the results from table 5.3
shows that for a low number of collaborative UAVs, the estimate is consistent. Comparing
Luft et al.ãs algorithm with a guaranteed consistent CL algorithm such as CI would be the
best way to investigate the cause. A last and very likely cause could be that the collabôÙ
orating UAVs are correcting too much based on the relative measurements compared to
the absolute measurements, which results in precise but not accurate pose estimates.
Thus, having too many sources of relative information (UAVs) compared to sources of
absolute information (anchors) can cause the estimate to become overconfident. This
is again indicated by the results in table 5.3 and also further with the fact that the ANIS
calculated from the measurements often shows better signs of consistency.
The benefit of collaboration becomes more questionable when comparing the results of
the collaborative UAVs with that of the nonôÙcollaborative UAVs, when the measurement
range is unlimited. In these cases the accuracy is hardly affected by collaboration, as
seen in table 5.1 and table 5.2. The benefit of collaboration first becomes clear when
not all UAVs have direct access to anchors, but can propagate beliefs among each other.
In a practical application, this is useful because it means that total anchor coverage of
the entire space the UAVs fly in is not needed, which can save on the number of anôÙ
chors needed to be deployed. It also means that the UAVs can localize in areas that the
UAVs can access but where anchors cannot be deployed or other sources of absolute
positioning information are denied.
Including the additional pseudo AoD allows the UAVs to better make use of the available
anchor information. In cases, where more than one anchor is available at all times, it does
not make much difference to the accuracy or ANEES if the AoD is included or not. The
biggest benefit is that the UAV is able to fully determine its position if it only has access to
a single anchor as seen in table 5.1. Including the AoD however means that the anchors
must also be equipped with dual antennas in order to determine the incident angle of the
incoming message. Furthermore, the orientation of the anchors also suddenly matters, as
certain orientations can introduce more ambiguities to the measurements. Thus including
AoD in the anchor measurements complicates both anchor design and placement.

Conclusion
In this thesis, a fully decentralized CL algorithm based on the EKF was developed for
localizing UAVs in a 2D plane. The CL algorithm was fully distributed, requiring no central
infrastructure, but required only that each UAV store an interôÙUAV correlation matrix for
each encountered UAV, making it easily scalable.
UWB nodes allowing for rangeôÙandôÙbearing measurements were successfully prototyped.
Experiments showed that the nodes were capable of performing range measurements up
to 50 meters with a mean error of 28 cm and a standard deviation of 4.5 cm. The AoA
measurements were not as consistent at long range, but at ranges of five meters and
below, an accuracy of ôÝ18ãÎwas achieved. The pseudo AoD retrieved by the nodes during
the DS TWR exchange allowed the nodes to obtain additional positioning information ôÙ
simulation results showed that, assuming no bearing ambiguities, this allows a node to
fully determine its position from a single anchor measurement. Furthermore, a CSMA/CA
inspired network protocol allowed the nodes to successfully distribute channel access
between three nodes in an adôÙhoc fashion, without any central infrastructure governing
access or prior knowledge of how many nodes were attempting to use the channel.
These UWBôÙbased sensors were used together with the IMU measurements made by the
UAVs for the CL algorithm. In simulations, the collaborating UAVs managed to achieve
converging pose error when the rangeôÙandôÙbearing sensors featured bearing ambiguities
and limited range. However, the collaborating UAVsã estimates often exhibited overconfiôÙ
dence based on the ANEES, which was more pronounced the more UAVs were involved
in the collaboration.
It was found in simulation that collaboration between UAVs would not improve the accuôÙ
racy of the pose estimate if all UAVs already had access to anchors at all times. The
true benefit of collaboration is in cases where only some UAVs occasionally had access
to anchor measurements and could propagate the absolute positioning information these
provided to other UAVs through collaboration. This, together with the pseudo AoD enôÙ
hancement of the measurements, means the CL algorithm developed in this thesis would
be most useful in environments where access to anchors is sparse or reduce the number
of anchors necessary for successful localization.
7.1
Further work
For further work, it is highly encouraged to rework the UWB node hardware. This would
include moving the antennas closer to each other, thus eliminating the phase wrapping
artifacts and perhaps redesigning the antennas for more stable PDoA measurements.
Alternatively, using Qorvoãs QM33120WDK1 development boards1 as UWB nodes could
be considered, as they also support range and AoA. The ReLoki nodes by Mathew et al.
[24] could also be considered, as they feature no bearing ambiguities at all and allow for
full 3D positioning.
A proper experimental campaign that tests the localization algorithm in a real environment
would also be very beneficial for exploring the actual performance of the algorithm under
nonôÙideal circumstances. Additionally, integrating the CL algorithm into an actual Pixhawk

4 drone with proper ROS 2 nodes for the UWB nodes and the Kalman filter could then
also be done.
Right now, the models and CL algorithm assume the UAVs are restricted to a 2D plane.
Future work could be focused on expanding the measurement and prediction models to
a 3D space, in order to make the system more useful for UAV localization.
Furthermore, other types of measurements besides rangeôÙandôÙbearing could also be conôÙ
sidered incorporated into the localization algorithm as well, such as GNSS.
The CL algorithm used in this thesis was based on the one developed by Luft et al. in
their paper [5]. It would be interesting to see how other frameworks would compare to
the one used in this thesis, such as an UKF with CI or factor graphs. Moreover, more
advanced approaches to handling the ambiguous measurements, such as multiple hyôÙ

Design and implementation of the test
platform
The test platform developed for this project is shown in figure A.1. The node PCB is
fastened to the top of a metal rod in an upright position, placing the bottom of the node 34
cm above the ground. The rod is attached to a wheel bearing at the base, which allows
for full rotation around the yaw axis. A small magnet is glued to the bottom of the rod,
which interacts with an AS5048A magnetic encoder [50]. This encoder provides the true
orientation reference of the module, represented by a 14ôÙbit integer ü14bit. From this, the
angle üref can be found with

The magnetic encoder is wired to the Raspberry Pi, which provides the required 3.3 V to
the encoder, as well as communicates with it through SPI. The communication operates
in SPI mode 1, with a clock speed of 2MHz. As the UWB node is also connected to
the Raspberry Pi, both the node and the encoder share the same clock, MOSI and MISO
lines, but have separate chip select lines. While the node uses chip select 0, the magnetic
encoder is selected with chip select 1. In order to read the encoder value, an appropriate
command package has to be sent to the encoder over the SPI. Each command package
