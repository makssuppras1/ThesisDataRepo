Abstract 
Global warming forecasts suggest an increase in extreme heat events, posing challenges 
to the building sector in terms of energy consumption and climate change-related 
hazards such as heatwaves and power outages. Traditional Heating, Ventilation and Air-
conditioning (HVAC) systems can fall short in energy efficiency and occupant 
satisfaction. Personalized Environmental Control Systems (PECS) offer an innovative 
solution, focusing on conditioning occupants' immediate surroundings, allowing for 
relaxed HVAC temperature setpoints and thus, substantial energy savings. Despite these 
benefits, the number of commercial PECS products remains sparse. At the International 
Centre for Indoor Environment and Energy (ICIEE) of the Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU), the thermal comfort performance of a new PECS prototype was 
evaluated via human subject and thermal manikin experiments. The results showed that 
the cooling effect of the PECS prototype was limited, and therefore in this study, two 
axial fans were incorporated for additional cooling, positioned symmetrically on and 
beneath the desk.  
 
A series of experiments were thus designed to ascertain the cooling efficacy of the 
system, while ensuring that ventilation performance remained unaffected and 
evaluating local thermal sensation.  
 
The cooling effects of the PECS with added fans were analyzed using a thermal 
manikin in a climate chamber with ambient temperatures ranging from 23 to 30℃. The 
system was tested under various air terminal device (ATD) and fan settings, with results 
evaluated using manikin-based equivalent temperature.  
 
The local thermal sensation across distinct body parts of the manikin was evaluated 
using Zhang's model, specifically designed for non-uniform and transient environments. 
Based on data obtained from prior human subject experiments conducted at DTU, a 
classification model was built by machine learning. This model also served to compare 
with the results derived from Zhang’s model in the context of manikin experiments. 
 
The air profile in the occupant's vicinity was studied using anemometers to measure 
air speeds around the thermal manikin. This was conducted at an ambient temperature 
of 23℃. 
 
The personalized ventilation performance of the PECS was scrutinized by combining 
a thermal manikin with an artificial lung and using it for tracer gas measurements. The 
ventilation performance of the PECS was evaluated with the metrics of personal 
exposure effectiveness and ventilation effectiveness. 
==PAGE:6==
Abstract 
iv 
 
 
The study revealed several key findings: the enhanced PECS prototype, equipped with 
two additional fans, demonstrated improved cooling effects regardless of their location 
above or below the desk. As ambient temperature increased, the cooling effect, as 
represented by the manikin-based equivalent temperature (𝑇𝑒𝑞), diminished. In terms of 
local thermal sensation, fans located under the desk yielded a more uniform segmental 
thermal sensation. Analysis of airflow profiles indicated a complex interaction in the 
breathing zone between the manikin's thermal plume, the airflow from the ATD, and 
fan-generated airflows. The ventilation effectiveness assessment showed that additional 
fans did not significantly affect personal exposure effectiveness or ventilation 
effectiveness under isothermal supply air conditions. However, in non-isothermal 
conditions with lower supply air temperature from the ATD, personal exposure 
effectiveness declined. 
 
Key words: PECS, Thermal Comfort, Ventilation Performance, Local Thermal 
Sensation, Machine Learning.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Global Background 
The sixth assessment report of Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) 
indicated that the global surface temperature was 1.1 ℃ higher between 2011 and 2020 
than that in 1890-1900 period. This change in global temperature is projected to 
precipitate significant changes in future weather patterns, resulting in more climate 
extremes, such as heatwaves, heavy precipitation, among others. The planet is 
witnessing more hot extremes and less cold extremes. On the other hand, urban areas, 
where humans are intensively populated, are particularly susceptible to these intensified 
hot extreme events. The escalation of such events has resulted in corresponding 
amplified rates of human mortality and morbidity [1].  
 
In terms of the energy utilization in the building sector, the International Energy Agency 
reported that building operations accounted for 30% of the global final energy 
consumption and contributed 27% of the total energy sector emissions in 2021. There 
is a need for the building industry to decrease its energy consumption to mitigate the 
effects of global warming, aligning with the Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Scenario [2]. 
Owing to the expansion of building floor area and the escalation of global warming, the 
demand for space cooling has globally intensified, marking a growth of over 6.5% in 
2021. Updated cooling equipment is needed for mitigating the energy consumption [3]. 
 
In the European Union (EU), the building industry has been considered as one of the 
key vulnerable sectors when facing climate change. Many mitigation and adaptation 
strategies have been addressed to minimize the impact of climate change. These 
strategies are primarily targeted at reducing greenhouse gas emissions and enhancing 
building resilience [4]. 
 
With respect to the cooling phase, International Energy Agency (IEA) – Energy in 
Buildings and Communities Program (EBC), through its research project Annex 80, has 
conducted a series of studies on the resilient cooling of buildings. Attia et al. [5] 
indicated a timeline for the resilience process, which includes stages of vulnerability, 
resistance, robustness and recovery. Moreover, they proposed a definition for the 
resilient cooling for buildings as follows:  
 
“The cooling of a building is resilient when the capacity of the cooling system

integrated in the building allows it to withstand or recover from disturbances due to 
disruptions, including heat waves and power outages, and to adopt the appropriate 
strategies after failure (robustness) to mitigate degradation of building performance.” 
(Attia et al., 2021 [5]) 
 
Zhang et al. [6] further proposed four indicators corresponding to the timeline of the 
resilience process, aimed at qualitatively assessing different cooling strategies. 
Additionally, they highlighted that Personal Comfort Systems (PCS) can offer both 
comfort and energy advantages. In the context of building cooling resilience, PCS 
demonstrates robust restorative and recovery capabilities during heatwave and power 
outage events.  
 
In the context of this paper's discussion on thermal comfort, Personal Comfort Systems 
(PCS) is considered as a parallel concept to Personalized Environmental Control 
Systems (PECS). Compared with traditional heating, ventilation and air-conditioning 
(HVAC) systems, PECS emphasizes the environmental control of an individual micro-
environment. A newly developed PECS prototype (3rd version, v3) was tested by both 
thermal manikin and human subject experiments [7][8]. However, the findings 
indicated that the cooling effects produced by the system was inadequate [9]. This 
suggests the need for further improvements to boost the cooling capabilities of the 
system without compromising ventilation performance. Improving cooling effects via 
PECS could also be applied to relax room temperature setpoints, thus enhancing 
building resilience. 
1.2 Objectives 
The objectives of this paper were as follows： 
⚫ 
Examine the cooling effects of the prototype, PECS v3, with the addition of two 
fans.  
 
⚫ 
Assess the thermal comfort of each body part during the cooling effects 
experiments. 
 
⚫ 
Develop a segmental thermal sensation model for the evaluation of thermal 
comfort for each body part. 
 
⚫ 
Investigate the air movement interactions around the occupant surroundings.  
 
⚫ 
Evaluate the ventilation performance of the PECS with added fans. 
 
⚫ 
Explore the potential for energy savings and resilience offered by the PECS.

Chapter 2 
2 Literature Review 
2.1 Overall Background of PECS 
The supply air from PECS can be categorized into conditioned air (i.e., temperature- 
and humidity- controlled) [10], [11], recirculated room air [12], [13] and fresh outdoor 
air [14]. Pasts research have investigated various aspects of PECS, including thermal 
comfort, indoor air quality, perceived air quality, and the system's potential for energy 
savings [15].  
 
To control occupants' immediate surroundings, numerous studies on Personalized 
Environmental Control Systems (PECS) have been undertaken, predominantly 
focusing on thermal comfort and IAQ [8]. Fanger [16] discussed the potential of the 
personalized air system to provide clean inhale air while improving indoor air quality 
and individual thermal comfort. Personalized Ventilation systems (PV) have showed 
benefits in their capability to supply clean air efficiently to the breathing zone, 
improving individual thermal comfort [17]. Toftum [18] evaluated the human 
perception of air movement and discussed the potential benefits of air movement in 
enhancing individual thermal satisfaction during the cooling phase. For example, a 
relatively high air velocity (1.6 m/s) was found permissible for occupants, when the 
ambient air temperature was around 30 ℃ [19]. Higher indoor acceptable air speeds 
imply the potential of increasing cooling effects driven by air movement. Personalized 
air-conditioning (using Fan-coil) system also has been studied in comparison with 
traditional centralized air-conditioning systems, offering alternative solutions for 
conditioning the working environment [10]. In addition, innovative personal comfort 
equipment has been developed to control individual micro-environments, including 
solutions such as internet-connected, heating and cooling chairs [13].  
2.2 Thermal Comfort of PECS 
Pan et al. [10] investigated a personalized air-conditioning system, specifically a 
partition-type fan-coil unit (PFCU), using a thermal manikin under ambient conditions 
of 30 ℃ and approximately 50% relative humidity (RH). The central system under 
consideration was a water-cooled fan-coil unit. The findings showed that the thermal 
comfort of the personalized system, as assessed by the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV), 
outperformed that of the central system. Additionally, personalized systems 
demonstrated a relatively quicker response to fluctuating cooling loads in comparison

with the central system. 
 
Arens et al. [12] evaluated the thermal comfort and perceived air quality of a personal 
environmental conditioning (PEC) system with the help of 18 subjects in a climate 
chamber. The ambient condition was 28 ℃, 50% relative humidity (RH). Each 
workstation was equipped with two nozzles, positioned on either side of the occupants. 
The nozzles, powered by two 4W fans, were designed to supply re-circulated room air. 
The results regarding thermal comfort showed that recirculated air supplied by two 
nozzles had better performance compared with that of air supplied by the single nozzle. 
With the assistance of the PEC system, the perceived air quality was deemed fully 
acceptable. Moreover, the comfort afforded by the PEC system was sustained during 
10-minute breaks, even as occupants stood and engaged in conversations. 
 
Kim et al. [13] carried out a field study in an open-plan office with a chair-type Personal 
Comfort System (PCS). The PCS were designed to cater to individual occupants' 
comfort needs by providing localized heating and cooling, without influencing the 
conditions experienced by others in the same space. The internet-connected PCS chairs, 
supplying heating and cooling, were utilized in the study as a supplementary measure 
to the central HVAC system. The cooling effect was facilitated by three integrated fans 
in the chair, whereas heating was provided by heating strips. The results demonstrated 
that 96% of the subjects deemed the environment acceptable (including slightly 
acceptable), significantly surpassing the ASHRAE Standard 55-2020 [20] thermal 
comfort requirements. Furthermore, the research suggested the potential to use PCS 
control signals to guide the control strategies of the central HVAC system.  
2.3 Energy of PECS 
Schiavon and Melikov [21] employed IDA-ICE, a software tool for building 
performance analysis, to conduct simulations of an office room in cold climates with 
the aim of predicting the energy-saving potential of a Personalized Ventilation (PV) 
system. Mixing ventilation was used as the base case. The results indicated that an 
optimal supply air temperature is 20 ℃ for energy savings, which is also the minimum 
acceptable temperature for occupants. Energy savings of up to 60% were achieved with 
the PV systems in comparison to using only a mixing ventilation system. The potential 
for energy saving was attributed not only to a reduced ventilation flow rate but also to 
more relaxed room temperature comfort limits. It is noteworthy that the PV system 
should be associated with a workstation or areas where occupants primarily remain 
sedentary. Furthermore, the reduction of the central system's ventilation flow rate has a 
threshold, considering that ventilation can provide free cooling during certain seasons.  
 
Another simulation study was conducted by Hoyt et al. [22] on extending cooling 
temperature setpoints of a Variable Air Volume (VAV) system. A medium office model, 
==PAGE:19==

based on the Department of Energy's (DOE) reference, was constructed and analyzed 
using version 7.2 of Energy Plus, a comprehensive building energy simulation program. 
The simulation encompassed seven distinct climatic conditions. Maximum and average 
HVAC savings reached 77% and 50% respectively, when the cooling setpoint was 
expanded to 30 ℃, corresponding to 10% of the VAV's Minimum Volume Setpoint 
(MVS) fraction. Compared to the base model, the more relaxed cooling setpoint (30 ℃) 
was 7.8 Kelvin higher. Moreover, the study highlighted the usefulness of efficient 
Personal Comfort Systems (PCS), operating under 25 W, in widening the temperature 
setpoint dead band, thereby contributing to energy conservation. 
2.4 Indoor and Perceived Air Quality of PECS 
Melikov et al. [11] evaluated the personal exposure effectiveness (𝜀𝑝) of various air 
terminal devices within a Personalized Ventilation (PV) system, utilizing a breathing 
thermal manikin and tracer gas. The results revealed that the Vertical Desk Grill (VDG) 
exhibited the highest 𝜀𝑝 value of 0.6, achieved by directing air upwards towards the 
occupant's face. The Moveable Panel (MP) also demonstrated a high proportion of 
personalized ventilation air in the inhaled mix, achieved by directing air diagonally 
downwards towards the occupant's breathing zone. Furthermore, the study concluded 
that the 𝜀𝑝 did not exhibit a consistent increase with the escalating flow rates (L/s) of 
the ATDs, under both isothermal and non-isothermal conditions.  
 
Sekhar et al. [14] conducted several evaluations of a personalized ventilation (PV) 
system regarding thermal comfort, indoor air quality and energy saving aspects. The 
PV system utilized fresh outdoor fresh air. The experiments were conducted under 
ambient air temperatures of 26 and 23 ℃, with PV flow rates of 7, 11, and 15 L/s. In 
both thermal comfort and perceived air quality (PAQ) studies, the results demonstrated 
that the subjects' thermal comfort and PAQ were improved with a combination of 
relatively low PV air temperature and higher PV flow rates. Optimal conditions were 
achieved with a PV temperature of 20 ℃ and an airflow rate of 15 L/s. Based on the 
ventilation effectiveness study, the research demonstrated that a PV flow rate of 7 L/s 
per person, combined with a PV supply air temperature of 23 ℃, achieved 50% higher 
of the ventilation effectiveness of a comparable standalone mixing ventilation system 
with a ventilation rate of 15 L/s per person. Furthermore, the study indicated that the 
PV system could potentially achieve about 30% in energy savings.

Personalized Ventilation (PV) also has the capability to mitigate the risk of airborne

2.5 Previous Tests of PECS Prototype  
A range of studies has been conducted at DTU regarding PECS prototypes. Previous 
experiments primarily emphasized the assessment of thermal performance. The earlier 
version of PECS tested a Peltier element in the personalized air supply via an Air 
Terminal Device (ATD) to facilitate cooling, however, it could not provide efficient 
cooling demand [9].  
 
In the previous thermal manikin experiments, manikin-based equivalent temperature 
(𝑇𝑒𝑞 ) was employed to indicate the cooling effects generated by PECS. The 𝑇𝑒𝑞 
variations between the test cases (with both ATD and Peltier element active) and the 
reference case (with neither ATD nor Peltier element operational) were calculated. The 
differences of 𝑇𝑒𝑞 was used to illustrate the cooling effects of each experimental 
settings. The findings indicated that the cooling effects of PECS were limited across the 
entire body, with the 𝑇𝑒𝑞 being approximately -0.5 K when the room temperature was 
set at 28 ℃. Complementarily, human subject experiments were performed using 
identical PECS. The outcome, as gauged by the thermal sensation vote (TSV), 
demonstrated that there was no statistical difference in the TSV between cases with and 
without PECS when the room temperature was 26 or 28 ℃. Therefore, there is a visible 
necessity to enhance the cooling effects of the developed PECS prototype. [7], [9].

element for enhancing the cooling performance of the PECS prototype. The interactions

4 Local Thermal Sensation  
The cooling effects observed in the manikin testing of PECS were not evenly distributed 
across the body. Consequently, each body segment perceived a distinct thermal 
sensation. In order to predict these varied thermal sensations across different body 
segments during the manikin experiments, Zhang's model was used in this study. This 
model, with its focus on segmental body analysis, provides a more precise prediction 
of thermal sensations, acknowledging the inherent variability among different body 
segments. 
 
4.1 Why Zhang’s Model? 
Zhang's model presents a refined approach to evaluating human thermal sensation. In 
contrast to traditional thermal comfort models like PMV and PPD that regard the human 
body as a homogeneous entity, Zhang's model perceives the body as an assembly of 
segments, acknowledging that different body parts may encounter distinct thermal 
sensations even in identical environmental conditions. This distinctive methodology 
enables a more thorough understanding of thermal comfort, thereby improving the 
accuracy of comfort forecasts. Consequently, it facilitates more precise control and 
regulation of heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems. The model's 
inherent flexibility proves particularly valuable in multifaceted environments where 
disparate body parts are subjected to varying thermal conditions, such as inside vehicles 
or at localized heating/cooling workstations. In essence, Zhang's model significantly 
enhances the accuracy and sophistication of human thermal sensation analysis, 
promoting enhanced comfort and wellbeing across a range of environmental conditions. 
[32]–[35].

6 Energy of PECS  
Energy use in buildings, in terms of the HVAC system, could be classified as Air 
Handling Unit (AHU) cooling and heating energy consumption, and room-specific 
cooling and heating load [21]. PECS has the potential to promote energy savings by 
relaxing the upper limits of room temperature (𝑇𝑟) setpoints and optimizing ventilation 
flow rates.  
  
 
The cooling setpoint of a conventional HVAC system is typically around 22 ℃ [40]. 
Based on the thermal experiments in this study, the whole-body cooling effects were 
assessed by the manikin-based equivalent temperature (𝑇𝑒𝑞 ). The 𝑇𝑒𝑞  difference 
between each operational case and the reference case, as introduced in the data 
assessment section, could be considered as an indicator. It can show the ability to 
increase the cooling setpoint of the HVAC System. Figure 6.1 presents the cooling 
effects provided by the PECS under different ambient temperatures. The Predicted 
Mean Vote (PMV) during the experiment was calculated as 0.44 without PECS, with 
𝑇𝑟 at 26 ℃ and assuming a relative humidity of 50%. Applying PECS with strongest 
cooling effects settings in 𝑇𝑟 of 28 ℃ (PMV = 1.06) is expected to lower occupants’ 
perception of the environment as if it were approximately 25.5 ℃ (in terms of sensible 
heat), corresponding to a PMV of 0.28 [20]. Owing to the cooling effects provided by 
PECS, it is possible to increase the cooling setpoint without compromising occupant 
thermal comfort.

PV does not always contribute to building energy savings. Firstly, the temperature of 
the outdoor air sometimes is lower than 20 ℃, which is the limit for PV supply air 
temperature, considering the perceived air quality and energy efficiency [41]. Thus, in 
such case the supply air needs to be heated, which increases the energy use of the AHU. 
Conversely, the total volume ventilation system may provide air at 16 ℃. Therefore, 
the traditional system could save more energy in certain cases [41][21]. Moreover, 
reducing the ventilation flow rate might waste the "free cooling" provided by outdoor 
fresh air during shoulder seasons (transition periods between heating and cooling 
seasons). Compared with increasing the cooing setpoint, PV system need to be designed 
carefully.

7 Resilience of PECS  
Building upon the cooling effects demonstrated by the PECS during testing, this section 
discusses how the PECS enhances building resilience in conjunction with the central 
HVAC system. Considerations were made for hot extreme events and power outages in 
summer, deemed as some of the primary hazards encountered by buildings. Zhang et 
al. qualitatively defined four characteristics corresponding to the stages a building faces 
during a heatwave or a blackout [5], [6], [42]. The roles the PECS could play under 
these circumstances are described as follows: 
 
Absorptive capacity: The first capacity relates to the ability of a building to withstand 
disruptions, such as heatwaves and power outages. Currently, the PECS alone is unable 
to absorb or remove excess heat in these scenarios. However, the incorporation of 
certain cooling techniques into the PECS could potentially add thermal mass or enhance 
its heat removal capability. Examples of such enhancements include the use of Phase 
Change Material (PCM) panels. 
 
Adaptive capacity: The second capacity pertains to a building's ability to maintain 
robustness by adjusting cooling strategies during disruptions. Given that the PECS 
requires an active power connection to function, it will not contribute to the building's 
adaptive capacity by itself in the event of power disruptions. However, PECS could 
show a high adaptive capacity during a heatwave (without power outage). 
  
Restorative capacity: The third capacity is defined as the ability of a building to return 
to normal conditions following disruptions. In this context, the PECS displays 
considerable potential to aid the building's recovery process. By providing 
supplementary cooling effects, it can enhance the thermal comfort of individual 
occupants and make the building habitable earlier [42], thereby facilitating a return to 
normal conditions. 
 
Recover Speed: The fourth characteristic illustrates how fast the buildings encountered 
disruptions could be back in the normal condition. 
 
Enhancing the adaptive capacity of the PECS within a power outage could significantly 
improve building resilience. A potential solution could involve the integration of battery 
energy storage systems [43], [44]. The peak energy use of the testing PECS (ATD and 
Fans), is currently at 39W (under ATD 10 and Fans at maximum power, as shown in 
Table 7.1. It could be reduced by further design optimization. By combining the use of

8 Overall Discussion  
8.1 Discussion of Manikin Experiments 
8.1.1 Cooling Effects 
Generally, there was no significant difference in cooling effect based on the fan location, 
whether placed above or below the desk. However, it appears that fans situated on the 
desk provided a slightly greater cooling effect compared to when they were positioned 
beneath the desk in the reference case, which ideally should yield the same values. This 
discrepancy may be due to slight variations in the manikin's or ATD's positioning, 
which can influence the outcomes. 
 
It should be noted that using manikin-based 𝑇𝑒𝑞 ignored the sweating in the body 
surface. In this study, cooling effects declined as ambient temperature increased. This 
can be attributed to the diminishing difference between the manikin's surface 
temperature and the surrounding temperatures. However, in other studies involving 
human subjects, it was found that the cooling effects were greater with a higher room 
temperature [19], [45]. This is due to increased skin wettedness on the surface of the 
human subject's body, leading to an enhancement Evaporative heat loss Thus, the 
cooling effects of the PECS may have been underestimated in this study.  
8.1.2 Air Profile 
The air speed measured around the manikin's breathing zone was a significant point of 
interest. Apart from the mouth area and one instance of chest measurements, the air 
speed recorded in the chest, trunk, thigh (when fans were placed under the desk) and 
forearm (when fans were placed above the desk) showed a linear relationship with the 
power of the fans. However, in the mouth area, air speeds fluctuated and reached the 
highest level when the fans were at minimum power and the manikin was operational.  
 
This phenomenon could be explained by the interaction of the thermal plume, which 
moves upward around the thermal manikin, and the airflow from the ATD, which is 
directed diagonally downward, and the airflow driving by fans. This interaction creates 
a complex airflow pattern. Furthermore, regardless of whether the fans are placed under 
or above the desk, some of the air generated by the fans rises when it hits the trunk of 
==PAGE:65==

52 
 
the manikin, as shown in Figure 8.1. 
 
When the thermal manikin is switched off, increasing the fan setting reduces the air 
speed in the mouth area linearly, without the influence of the thermal plume. This 
demonstrates the influence of the thermal plume on the air speed in the mouth area. 
 
Draught can be a significant concern with cooling strategies that utilize increased air 
movement, particularly in conventional HVAC systems where occupants often have 
limited control over their immediate environment. However, with PECS, individuals 
have direct control over their microclimate, giving them the flexibility to adjust 
parameters such as airflow and temperature according to their personal comfort. In 
addition, the concept of alliesthesia plays a role. Alliesthesia refers to the changing 
perception of a stimulus as either pleasant or unpleasant depending on the internal state 
of the individual, For instance, a cool breeze may be refreshing when a person is warm 
but feel uncomfortable if they are already cold [46]. With a PECS, individuals can 
adjust their environment in real-time to better match their current thermal state, 
increasing the likelihood that the cooling effect will be perceived as pleasant.

8.1.3 Ventilation Performance 
The purpose of conducting experiments to test ventilation performance was to 
investigate the ability of the PECS to deliver clean air through the ATD directly to the 
manikin's breathing zone. For these tests, the fan setting was fixed to the minimum level. 
This setting was based on previous air profile experiments, which indicated that the 
highest air speeds in the breathing zone were achieved when the fans were set to their 
minimum setting. In the context of a PECS, the ability to provide effective ventilation 
at a low power setting can also help mitigate potential issues related to draught and 
noise.

8.2 Discussion of Thermal Sensation 
Two models were compared: Zhang's local thermal sensation model and a newly 
developed machine learning-based classification model. 
 
In the context of the manikin experiments, segmental body thermal sensation was 
analyzed using Zhang's model. This was because the manikin was set to the "comfort" 
mode, implying that the overall thermal sensation of the manikin should be neutral. By 
investigating the thermal sensations of different body parts, it was found that fans 
placed under the desk exhibited smaller segmental differences. This result aligns with 
the findings obtained from the manikin-based 𝑇𝑒𝑞 temperature analysis.  
 
The previous human subject experiments used the same PECS prototype, but without 
the additional fans. This means the cooling effects generated by PECS influenced 
mainly on the upper body of the participants. Therefore, after training the classification 
model, the developed model was only used to predict the results of Zhang’s model when 
fans were placed on the table. According to the confusion matrix in Figure 5.5, it shows 
the results employing the trained model (Predictions) to predict the results of Zhang’s 
model (Truth) according to the data collected from the manikin experiments. The 
==PAGE:67==

weighted f1-score of some body parts, which experienced greater cooling effects due 
to the additional fans, was low. This could potentially be explained by the training 
dataset from the human subject experiments where the PECS did not involve fans to 
provide additional cooling to occupants. Therefore, the trained model may have 
underestimated the cold sensation in the body parts of the hand, lower arm, and neck, 
when PECS is incorporated with fans. Conversely, this could also suggest that the fans 
provide significant cooling power that disrupts the predictions of the trained model. 
 
Multinomial logistic regression was chosen for the logistic model mainly for potential 
future studies. As mentioned, the questionnaire includes subcategories for both warm 
(acceptable/unacceptable) and cold (acceptable/unacceptable) sensations. When 
considering the acceptability of thermal sensations, these features would be mutually 
exclusive. Using multinomial logistic regression ensures that the sum of the 
probabilities of each class label equals 1, which wouldn't be the case otherwise. 
Furthermore, a weighted f1-score was utilized in this study since the distribution of 
different classes was not uniform. The potential of this trained model lies in combining 
PECS settings and occupants' thermal sensations to avoid local thermal discomfort.

Chapter 9 
9 Conclusion  
Cooling effects: the newly developed Personalized Environmental Control Systems 
(PECS) version 3 (v3), with two added fans, demonstrated enhanced cooling effects. 
The positioning of the fans—either above or beneath the desk—made minimal 
difference to the cooling results according test cases. According to the manikin-based 
equivalent temperature (𝑇𝑒𝑞), the cooling effects decreased with the increase of ambient 
temperature when considering the sensible heat only. The difference between the 𝑇𝑒𝑞 
of reference and testing cases, which was represented as 𝛿𝑇𝑒𝑞, reach the lowest at 4 K 
with fans above and 3.7 K with fans under, when the room temperature (𝑇𝑟) was 23 ℃. 
The 𝛿𝑇𝑒𝑞 increased to 2.5 K with fans above and 2.0 K with fans under, when the room 
temperature was 30 ℃. The 𝛿𝑇𝑒𝑞 of previous PECS without fans provided a cooling 
effect up to 0.3 K to the whole body at ATD 10 and 𝑇𝑟 at 28 ℃. With the help of fans, 
in the same experimental setting, the whole-body cooling effects represented by 𝛿𝑇𝑒𝑞 
was up to 2.5 K with fans under and 2.7 K with fans above. 
 
If the wettedness of the body surface were to be taken into account, the cooling potential 
could increase due to evaporative cooling.  
 
Local thermal sensation: Based on segmental thermal sensation analysis using 
Zhang’s model, the placement of fans under the desk proved more effective in achieving 
a uniform distribution of local thermal sensation. The most critical thermal sensation 
difference occurred between the upper arms and hands for the fans positioned 
underneath the desk (1.3), whereas it occurred between the foot and hands for those 
positioned above (2.6). The trained logistic model performed well when classifying 
labels with appropriate training dataset of the human subject experiments, evaluated by 
the f1-score. However, there is room to expand this model to include additional labels, 
such as “acceptable” or “unacceptable”, augmenting the existing warm, cold and neutral 
classifications. 
 
Air profile: Experiments showed that the breathing zone reflects the interactions of air 
among the manikin thermal plume and airflows from the ATD and fans. To analyze the 
impacts of the thermal plume, experiments were also conducted with the thermal 
manikin turned off. In both ventilation settings of ATD in 7 and 10, the results illustrated 
that increasing fans power will decrease the air speed measured in the mouth area. 
Moreover, when the fan setting was to a minimum, the air speed measured in the 
breathing zone was the highest.

Conclusion

Ventilation effectiveness experiments: the results showed that additional fans have 
not influenced the personal exposure effectiveness and ventilation effectiveness, in the 
isothermal condition in which 𝑇𝑝𝑣 and 𝑇𝑟 were at 26 ℃, when fans were placed under 
the desk. In the non-isothermal condition with 𝑇𝑝𝑣 at 20 ℃ and 𝑇𝑟 at 26 ℃ without 
fan operation, the personal exposure effectiveness decreased. The highest personal 
exposure effectiveness (𝜀𝑝) was found in both isothermal conditions with and without 
added fans, with a value of 0.45. 
 
Overall, the tested PECS prototype with fans beneath the desk was recommended for 
further study, since it shares the similar cooling effects of PECS with fans above the 
desk, while performing better uniform segmental thermal sensation. In addition, the 
additional fans placed beneath the desk proved to exert few impacts on PV performance 
of the PECS.

Future Studies 
57 
 
Chapter 10 
10 Future Studies  
Future investigations should concentrate on the PECS prototype featuring fans 
positioned beneath the desk. Further studies could modify the vertical position and 
angle of the fans towards the occupant. Moreover, to enhance realism and precision, 
body surface wettedness should be incorporated, either by incorporating human 
subjects or using a sweating manikin [19] [45].  
 
Multinomial logistic regression practice has shown a great potential of application for 
improving the segmental thermal sensation prediction of PECS. However, to refine this 
method, more human subject experiments are necessary, particularly those involving 
the PECS and additional fans. 
 
For further understanding of the airflow profile surrounding the manikin, future 
research should consider using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations. This 
approach allows for a wider variety of case studies, and it could provide valuable 
insights to optimize the PECS design. For instance, CFD could assist in determining its 
capacity to provide both cooling effects and fresh air.
