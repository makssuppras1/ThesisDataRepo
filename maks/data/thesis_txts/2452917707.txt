Vandrammedirektivets krav om fri passage for fisk, er det blevet vedtaget, at Langvad Å i Roskilde 
Fjord  bliver omlagt. Åen vil fra udgangen af 2019 blive lagt over i Gedebæk renden (Figur 1). 
Herefter vil udløbet fra Langvad Å ikke længere løbe ud i Kattinge Vig, men i stedet løbe ud i den 
sydlige del af Roskilde Fjord (Figur 1). Kattinge Vig har siden 2005 været et marint beskyttet område 
(engelsk MPA). Dette betyder at trolling, dørgefiskeri og brug af nedgarn er forbudt inde i dette 
område. Årsagen til at forvaltere indførte et marint beskyttet område var at havørred bestanden i 
Langvad Å ikke kunne opretholde sig selv. Ved at ulovliggøre visse typer fiskeri, forsøgte forvaltere 
at mindske fiskeri trykket og herigennem øge den marine overlevelse for havørred. På sigt skulle 
dette føre til at havørred bestanden i Langvad Å blev selvreproducerende. I dette studie har jeg sporet 
50 havørreds vandring i Roskilde Fjord ved brug af akustiske mærker. Jeg har, ud fra 
vandringsmønsteret estimeret, i hvilket omfang det marint beskyttede område beskytter havørrederne 
som det første studie af sin slags i Danmark. Ligeledes har vi analyseret og anslået effekten af at flytte 
Langvad Å samt evalueret den potentielle effekt af omlægningen af Langvad Å. Slutteligt har jeg på 
baggrund af resultater fra dette studie og eksisterende litteratur udarbejdet alternative 
løsningmuligheder. 
Resume og konklusion 
Et fundamentalt element, for at marint beskyttede områder kan beskytte fisk, er, at fiskene opholder 
sig inde i det beskyttede område. Flere kritikere mener derfor, at anvendelsen af marint beskyttede 
områder til at beskytte mobile fisk, så som havørred, ikke er muligt eller hensigtsmæssigt, da netop 
mobile fisk er tilbøjelige til at bevæge sig uden for det beskyttede område. Herved er fiskene ikke 
beskyttet. Resultater fra dette studie viser, at alle havørreder har brugt minimum 50% af tiden inde i 
det lille marint beskyttede område (3,82km-2) placeret i Kattinge Vig (Figur 1). Undersøgelsen af 
havørredernes marine overlevelse indikerede en marin overlevelse på 23%. I alt forsvandt 14 
havørred inde i det beskyttede område, og 22 havørred forsvandt uden for det beskyttede område. 
==PAGE:8==
 
8 
Undersøgelsen indikerer, at 11 ud af 14 havørreder, som forsvandt inde i det beskyttede område, 
sandsynligvis blev fanget af fiskere. Da der ikke er fortaget en undersøgelse af havørredens marine 
overlevelse forud for implementeringen af det marint beskyttede område, har det ikke været muligt 
at konkludere, hvorvidt det marint beskyttede område genererer højere marin overlevelse for 
beskyttede havørreder. Dog har andre studier påvist, at garnfiskeri, som er forbudt i det marint 
beskyttede område, står for omkring 10% af den samlede mængde af hjemtagende havørred på 
årsbasis. På dette grundlag kombineret med resultater, som viser at havørrederne opholder sig inde i 
det marint beskyttede område en stor del af tiden (>50%), estimerer jeg, at det beskyttede område 
sandsynligvis forhøjer den marine overlevelse.  
Tidligere studier har vist, at smolt dødeligheden i å-systemer, som løber gennem søer, er 
mellem 74% til >90% (Jepsen, Aarestrup, Økland, & Rasmussen, 1998; Schwinn, Aarestrup, Baktoft, 
& Koed, 2017). Resultater fra Schwinn et al. (2017) indikere også at forhøjet smolt dødelighed kan 
kompromittere en havørred bestand evne til at være selvreproducerende. På nuværende tidspunkt 
løber Langvad Å igennem søerne Svogerslev sø, Lille Kattinge sø og Store Kattinge sø. Resultater 
fra Henriksen (1998) viste en smolt dødelighed på 88% for smolt som vandrede gennem de tre søer. 
Henriksen (1998) og Henriksen (2016), argumentere at årsagen til at havørred populationen i Langvad 
Å ikke er selvreproducerende skyldes den høje smolt dødelighed. Den nuværende plan om at 
omlægge Langvad Å tager afsæt i at sikre fri fiskepassage til og fra Langvad Å.  Derfor er det ikke 
planlagt at omgå de tre søer i Langvad Å. Da Langvad Å efter omlægningen til Gedebæk renden 
stadigvæk vil passere de tre søer (Figur 1), estimere jeg at smolt dødeligheden i Langvad Å vil forblive 
omkring 88%. Efter omlægningen vil udløbet af Langvad Å ikke længere være beskyttet af det marint 
beskyttede område. Dette medfører at havørred som opholder sig omkring udløbet, ikke vil være 
beskyttet for fiskeri. Da smolt dødeligheden forventes at forblive ca. 88% kombineret med at havørred 
som opholder sig området omkring udløbet mister beskyttelsen fra det marint beskyttede område, 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
forventes det ikke at den nuværende omlægning af Langvad Å vil opnå god økologisk tilstand jf. 
DFFVø ørredindeks. Dog vil den nuværende løsning sikre fri fiske passage, og derved leve op til 
kravene under Vandrammedirektivet. På baggrund af resultater fra dette studie har jeg fremsat en 
løsningsmulighed, som både vil sikre fri passage og potentielt set muliggøre en selvreproducerende 
bestand af havørred i Langvad Å – og herigennem opnå god økologisk tilstand jf. DFFVø ørredindeks. 
Løsningen bygger på, at lede vandet fra Langvad Å over i Lejre Å. Herved omgås de tre søer, samt 
der sikres fri passage. Ved at gøre dette, forventes smolt dødeligheden at falde (Jepsen et al. 1998; 
Boel and Koed, 2013; Schwinn et al. 2017; Schwinn et al. 2018). Det forventes at en reducering i 
smolt dødelighed, vil forsage øget returnering af gydemodne havørred de kommende år (Crozier & 
Kennedy, 1993; Elliott, 1993). Herved mener jeg, at densiteten af ørred yngel i Langvad Å har 
potentiale til at stige. Herved kan det blive muligt at øge havørred yngeldensiteten fra de nuværende 
17 yngel pr 100 km2 til 80 ørred yngel pr 100 km2 (I overensstemmelse med DFFVø ørredindeks). 
Resultater fra dette studie viste, at 62% af alle havørreder (inklusiv havørreder der blev fanget m.m.) 
blev i den sydlige del af Roskilde Fjord (syd for Frederikssund; figur 3). Ligeledes indikerer data, at 
det rekreative fiskeri står for mindst 23% af den marine dødelighed. På baggrund af dette vurderer 
jeg, at en udvidelse af det marint beskyttede område samt skærpelse af regler for lystfiskeri i samspil 
med de nuværende planer om omlægningen af Langvad Å til dels vil sikre en bæredygtig bestand af 
havørreder i Langvad Å.

Mærkning af ørred 
Forud for evalueringen af det marint beskyttede område blev 50 havørreder mærket med én akustisk 
sender pr. havørred. I sammenarbejder med Foreningen for ophjælpning af fiskeriet i Roskilde Fjord 
og ROLK blev ørrederne fanget i fisketrappen i Langvad Å. Ørrederne blev mærket hhv. d. 14. 
december 2017 og d. 12. januar 2018. Det var imidlertid planlagt, at alle fisk skulle mærkes og 
udsættes d. 14. december 2017, men grundet ringe opgang af gydemodne havørred i Langvad Å var 
dette ikke muligt. Dette medførte imidlertid at havørrederne blev mærket og sat ud af to omgange. 
Forud for mærkningen blev alle havørreder strøget for sperm og æg (figur 4). Æg og sperm bruges til 
at producere ørredyngel, som udsættes som yngel i Langvad Å og mundingsudsætning af smolt i 
Langvad Å systemet. Havørrederne blev mærket af Jon Christian Svendsen (Figur 4A). 
 
Figur 4 viser Jon C. Svendsen, som indopererer de akustiske transmittere i en havørred (foto A). På foto B ses Jonn Poulsen stryge en 
havørred for æg. Disse æg bruges til at opdrætte ørredyngel og smolt til udsætning i Langvad Å.

Indsamling af data, vedligeholdelse og kontrol af udstyr  
Lytteposterne anvendt i dette studie oplagrer information (data) fra mærkede havørreder, som er 
blevet registreret. Med typen af lytteposter som er anvendt i dette studie (VR2W), er det nødvendigt 
at indsamle data manuelt fra en båd. Ved hyppig indsamling af data fra lytteposterne undgik vi at 
tabet af lytteposter kompromitterede datasættet. Data blev indsamlet i tæt samarbejde med Jonn 
Poulsen og Kim Jørgensen. Igennem projektets løbetid mistede vi seks hydrofoner. Med hjælp fra en 
lokal dykkerforening blev den ene lyttepost fundet igen, og en anden blev fundet på land af en 
lystfisker. Dette resulterede i, at kun fire lytteposter gik tabt. Grundet hyppig indsamling af data fra 
lytteposterne, gik vi gennemsnitligt ”kun” glip af data for 9 dage pr. tabt lyttepost. Da der inden 
opsætning af lytteposter blev taget højde for eventuelt tab af udstyr, blev der på alle lyttepoststationer 
sat ekstra lytteposter i vandet. Herved kompromitterede tabet af lytteposter ikke dækningsgraden af 
lytteposterne. Det vil sige, at havørreder som passerede forbi eller opholdt sig nær 
lyttepoststationerne, uafhængigt af tab af enkelte lytteposter, blev registeret af andre lytteposter. Den 
ene af de to lytteposter, som vi fik tilbage, havde spor af at være blevet sejlet ned af en motorbåd. 
Flere faktorer i Roskilde Fjord gjorde det nødvendigt at kontrollere og vedligeholde udstyret ofte, 
blandt andet på grund af kollision mellem lytteposter og både, kraftig strøm som tyngede lytteposterne 
ned, fiskeredskaber som sat fast i udstyret (gællegarn, ruser og fiskeliner), kraftig vækst af rur 
(Balanidae) og isdække. På trods af ovenstående problemstillinger, blev det ved brug af test-
transmittere bekræftet, at samtlige lytteposter inkluderet i projektet var fuldt ud funktionsdygtig 
igennem hele projektet. Data fra lytteposterne blev indsamlet med VUE-software (VEMCO, 2019c).

Koordinering og sikring af udstyr 
Undervejs i projektet opstod flere komplikationer som krævede kreative løsninger. Heriblandt 
var de to største problemstillinger tidsforskydning på lytteposternes interne ur samt isdækket i 
Roskilde Fjord. Tidsforskydningerne på lytteposternes interne ur varierede fra 4 sekunder til 23 
minutter. Dette medførte f.eks., at fisk, som i reel tid blev registreret kl. 12:00, ville fremgå som 
værende blevet registreret kl. 12:23. I sammenarbejde med VEMCO (lyttepostproducenten) 
udarbejdede jeg en løsning, som fik rettet op på fejlen. Undervejs i projektet, vinter 2018, frøs dele 
af Roskilde Fjord. Erfaring fra lignende projekter har vist, at isdække kan skade og sænke 
lytteposterne og herved forårsage tab af udstyr. For at imødegå denne problemstilling blev der 
udarbejdet en handleplan for, hvorledes vi fortsat kunne samle data i måneder med isdække og undgå 
at miste udstyr i fjorden. Det blev vedtaget, at alle flagbøjer skulle afmonteres og erstattes med 
undervandsbøjer. Undervandsbøjerne, blev monteret således at undervandsbøjen samt lytteposten var 
ca. 1 meter under havoverfladen. Dette gjorde at isflager, ikke ødelagde vores udstyr. For at sikre fuld 
funktionalitet og forebygge eventuelle tab, foretog jeg i disse perioder minimum en felttur om ugen 
(i de isfri områder af Roskilde fjord). Efter at have testet størstedelen af alle lytteposter kunne vi 
bekræfte, at lytteposterne var fuldt ud funktionelle i disse perioder.  
Manuel pejling 
Ved at anvende en håndholdt manuel pejler (VEMCO, 2019c) var det muligt at spore 
havørredernes vandring i områder, hvor der ikke var lytteposter. Manuel pejling af mærkede 
havørreder blev fortaget inden- og uden for det marint beskyttede område. Dette var med henblik på 
at registrere havørreder, som var uden for de stationære lytteposters rækkevidde. Herved kunne 
dødelighed estimeres når fik holdt op med at bevæge sig. Ved at anvende den manuelle pejler på 
månedligbasis kunne vi bestemme, om der var havørreder tilstede inde i det marint beskyttede

Databehandling og analyse 
Data fra den de stationære lytteposter og den manuelle pejling blev behandlet i R (R Core Team, 
2019). Helt specifikt blev der anvendt komponenter fra R pakken (Flávio, 2019). De specifikke 
delkomponenter, som blev anvendt fra R pakken, var data sortering og estimering af 
bevægelighedsmønstre. For at verificere korrekt databehandling og undgå fejl og mangler i de 
udarbejdede scripts, blev der med ca. to ugers mellemrum afholdt møder med R pakkens udvikler 
Hugo M. Flávio. Udover behandling, analyse og beregninger fortaget i R blev dele af data 
gennemgået manuelt. Dette var blandt andet for at estimere dødstidspunkt for havørreder, som gik 
tabt inde i det marint beskyttede område. Ved at anvende R kunne vi på baggrund af de individuelle 
havørreders bevægelser visualisere og analysere havørredernes tilstedeværelse og tid brugt inde i det 
marint beskyttede område. For hver enkelt havørred blev der udarbejdet et ”bevægelses datasæt”. 
Dette datasæt indeholdt den samlede tid, som hver enkelt havørred brugte i det marint beskyttede 
område. På baggrund af dette datasæt blev der lavet en visualisering af alle mærkede havørreders 
==PAGE:15==

bevægelsesmønster i fjorden. Et visuelt eksempel heraf kan ses i figur 6 nedenfor, hvor 
bevægelsesmønsteret for havørred med identifikationsnummeret A96-9006-2045 er præsenteret.

marint beskyttede område), lyttepost 7 til 12 (gul skravering) er i udmundingen af Kattinge Vig (det marint beskyttede område),

lytteposter gennemgået manuelt. Herigennem var det muligt at estimere dødstidspunktet for 
havørreder, som døde inde i det marint beskyttede område.  
GIS kort 
Alle kort præsenteret i det skriftlige udkast til artiklen er produceret i ArcGIS (figur 1 og 3). Samtlige

Abstract 
20 
Globally, policy makers are creating legislation to protect aquatic life. Within the European Union, 
21 
the Water Framework Directive (WFD) targets aquatic life and highlights the importance of 
22 
eliminating migration barriers and ensuring free passage for riverine organisms. This study 
23 
investigates a case where free passage is ensured for brown trout (Salmo trutta; termed trout) by 
24 
diverging a stream (Langvad Stream) to a different stream channel. While diverging the stream will 
25 
circumvent a migration barrier, it also means that the stream mouth will be diverged away from an 
26 
area inside a marine protected area (MPA) to an area outside of the MPA. This could hamper the 
27 
marine survival of the trout; however, the marine survival and the impact of the MPA to protect the 
28 
trout are largely unknown. This is particularly pertinent, because trout is a migratory species that may 
29 
leave the MPA shortly after entering the marine environment. Using acoustic telemetry, this study 
30 
investigated the efficacy of the MPA to protect post-spawning trout and estimated 1) the time spent 
31 
inside and outside of the MPA, and 2) the loss of brown trout inside and outside of the MPA. Overall, 
32 
tracking data revealed that trout were present inside the MPA during all seasons and spent on average 
33 
67.4% (± 4.5% SE) of the time inside the MPA. Brown trout surviving the entire study period spent 
34 
on average 50.6% (± 8.4% SE) of the time inside the MPA. In total, 77% of the brown trout were lost 
35 
during the study period and did not return to the Langvad Stream to spawn. The loss of brown trout 
36 
was unevenly divided inside (n = 14) and outside (n = 22) of the MPA. The Langvad Stream is not 
37 
reaching the goal of the WFD (80 juvenile trout 100m-2), suggesting that further measures are required 
38 
to enhance trout survival and production. Effects of the planned stream diversion on trout survival 
39 
and production are uncertain, and the Langvad Stream is unlikely to meet the WFD goal after the 
40 
diversion. We recommend that alternative solutions to meet the WFD are considered, including a 
41 
different diverging route and expansion of the MPA both in terms of area coverage and associated 
42 
fishing restrictions

Introduction 
44 
Extensive habitat degradation and overexploitation of living marine resources affect many marine 
45 
coastal areas (Lotze et al. 2006; Kristensen et al. 2017). As a result, conservation and restoration of 
46 
fish and fisheries are major environmental challenges (Balmford et al. 2005). To protect coastal 
47 
marine species and ecosystems, implementation of marine protected areas (MPAs) is recognized as a 
48 
promising management tool (Lester et al. 2009; Gaines, Lester, Grorud-Colvert, Costello, & Pollnac, 
49 
2010). MPAs are areas in the ocean where fishing and/or other extractive activities are restricted or 
50 
prohibited as no-take zones (marine reserves) (IUCN & WCPA, 2018). Embraced by high level 
51 
international bodies as a tool to achieve biodiversity goals, MPAs have been subject to rapid growth 
52 
in recent decades. Currently, approximately 6.4 % of the world’s ocean is covered by MPAs (IUCN, 
53 
2017), largely due to international treaties such as Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) (CBD, 
54 
2017). Under the CBD, the goal is that 10 % of the global coastal and marine areas will be covered 
55 
by MPAs in 2020 (CBD, 2017). However, MPA area coverage alone will not necessarily optimize 
56 
protection for marine biodiversity, nor reflect the MPA’s conservation and protective efficacy 
57 
(Kerwath et al. 2009; Edgar, 2011; Edgar et al. 2014). Instead, optimal MPAs efficacy depends on 
58 
various factors, including degree of fishing permitted inside of the MPA, level of enforcement, MPA 
59 
age, and the presence of continuous habitat allowing fish movement across MPA boundaries and 
60 
positioning of the MPA (Edgar, 2011; Edgard et al. 2014). Effects of MPAs have been widely 
61 
documented showing increase in fish abundance, biomass, individual size and egg production both 
62 
inside the MPA (Lester et al. 2009) and outside the MPA as a spillover effect (Goñi, Hilborn, Díaz, 
63 
Mallol, & Adlerstein, 2010; Abesamis & Russ, 2005). Because individual fish survival is positively 
64 
related to the time spent inside an MPA (Palumbi, 2004), exit from the MPA often causes elevated 
65 
individual mortality (Edgar, 2011). MPAs are mainly expected to protect less mobile and sedentary 
66 
fish species (Pilyugin, Medlock, & Leenheer, 2016), but positive MPA effects have also been 
67 
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
documented for mobile and migratory species (Kerwath et al. 2009; Claudet et al. 2010; Knip, Heupel, 
68 
& Simpfendorfer, 2012). 
69 
Migration is considered an adaptation to spatiotemporal fluctuations in resources, where 
70 
synchronized movements between distinct habitats occur at different life stages (Dingle & Drake 
71 
2007; Lucas & Baras, 2001). Anadromous fish, such as the brown trout (Salmo trutta; henceforth 
72 
termed trout), perform feeding and spawning migrations between freshwater and marine 
73 
environments (Thorstad et al. 2016). Trout may adapt different strategies to optimize individual 
74 
fitness, including variable periods spent at sea (Del Villar, Aarestrup, Skov, & Koed, 2014; Eldøy, 
75 
2015). Specifically, some trout may spend a few weeks at sea, whereas other trout may spend several 
76 
years at sea (Klemetsen et al. 2003). Therefore, measuring and predicting the efficacy of an MPA to 
77 
protect trout remains difficult.  
78 
European Union (EU) member states act in agreement with the Water Framework Directive 
79 
(WFD). By employing and implementing EU management measures into national legislation, the 
80 
purpose of the WFD is to prevent deterioration, protect and enhance surface water bodies (European 
81 
Commission, 2003). By integrating the WFD management measures into national policy, EU member 
82 
states are currently conforming with the legislative goals described in the second management cycle 
83 
(2015-2021) of the WFD. This entails protection and enhancement of surface water bodies, including 
84 
removal of waterway obstructions and to achieve good ecological status (GES) for surface 
85 
waterbodies (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2016; European Commission, 2003). Each member state 
86 
is required to define reference conditions (i.e. assumed pristine conditions) for surface water bodies. 
87 
Once reference conditions have been established, the ecological status of similar national surface 
88 
waterbodies is evaluated. The ecological status is classified by various biological, chemical, hydro 
89 
morphological and physical elements (Bonde et al. 2006). Good ecological status (GES) is by default 
90 
the objective for all WFD waterbodies. The ecological status of many rivers and streams in Denmark 
91 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
must comply with measures as stipulated in the DFFVø trout index. To achieve GES in compliance 
92 
with the DFFVø trout index, the density of juvenile trout must be at least 80 individuals 100m-2 in 
93 
small streams (i.e. below 2 m in stream width) (Nielsen, Sivebæk, & Baktoft, 2016). In addition to 
94 
the DFFVø trout index requirements, Denmark is required to remove all obstacles restricting fish 
95 
migration (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2016; European Commission, 2003). 
96 
In Denmark, trout are predominantly caught for recreational purposes using a diversity of 
97 
gears, including gillnets and rod and line fishing (Hayes, Ferreri, & Taylor, 2012; Gislason et al. 
98 
2014). In the past two decades, many Danish trout populations have experienced a steady increase, 
99 
primarily due to restoration of spawning and rearing habitats, removal of obstacles, small scale MPAs 
100 
and stocking with indigenous juvenile trout (Sivebæk, 2018). A range of regulative measures protect 
101 
trout, including a seasonal harvest ban on spawning colored trout (November 16th – January 15th) and 
102 
a 500-meter closed zone surrounding many river mouths (Fiskeristyrelsen, 2019). Seasonal harvest 
103 
ban and closed zones are employed to protect trout, primarily during the spawning season when trout 
104 
abundance increases near river mouths and in the rivers.  
105 
In 2005, the Danish fisheries agency implemented an MPA in the Roskilde Fjord, covering 
106 
the entire Kattinge Bay area and the outlet of the Langvad Stream (Figure 2). The trout population in 
107 
the Langvad Stream had deteriorated because of several factors, including; 1) a weir near the outlet 
108 
of the Langvad Stream despite the presence of a pool and weir fish passage (Clay, 2017; Figure 2), 
109 
2) the Langvad Stream running through three separate lakes (Figure 2) and  3) estimates of trout 
110 
experiencing high marine mortality (Fiskeridirektoratet, 2003). The weir limited fish passage and the 
111 
lakes caused elevated fish mortality, particularly relevant for trout smolts passing through the lakes 
112 
(Jepsen, Aarestrup, Økland, & Rasmussen, 1998). The MPA implementation in 2005 meant that gill 
113 
netting and lure trolling became prohibited fishing techniques throughout the year.  
114 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
To comply with the WFD and ensure GES (in accordance with the DFFVø trout index) in the 
115 
Langvad Stream, the stream is scheduled to be diverged (Figure 2) into the Gedebæk Stream (Figure 
116 
2). The objective of the stream diverging is to circumvent the weir near the stream outlet (Figure 2) 
117 
to provide free passage and to reach GES in compliance with the DFFVø trout index. The new outlet 
118 
of the Langvad Stream will be located outside of Kattinge Bay, thus outside of the MPA (Figure 2). 
119 
After the stream diverging, trout leaving the Langvad stream will no longer enter directly into the 
120 
MPA. Instead, the trout will enter a marine area where both lure trolling and gill net fishing are 
121 
permitted fishing techniques. The impact of the stream diverging on trout marine survival is uncertain 
122 
as the protection currently provided by the MPA is unknown. 
123 
Using telemetry, this study examined the ability of the Kattinge Bay MPA to protect adult 
124 
trout in the Roskilde Fjord. Specifically, the individual trout residence times inside and outside of the 
125 
MPA were estimated by tagging and tracking trout for a 1-year period. This was accomplished using 
126 
stationary receivers combined with manual tracking of the trout in the Roskilde Fjord. Using the 
127 
tracking data, the loss of individual trout was estimated inside and outside of the MPA. If trout leaving 
128 
the Langvad Stream are also quickly leaving the Roskilde Fjord for foraging elsewhere (e.g. Kattegat 
129 
Sea; Figure 1), the fishing restrictions inside the MPA are probably providing limited protection of 
130 
the trout. This migratory pattern would be in agreement with recent trout studies in neighboring fjord 
131 
systems (Kristensen, Birnie-Gauvin, & Aarestrup, 2019). On the other hand, trout could be residing 
132 
in the MPA for a substantial duration (i.e. months) in which case the MPA is expected to protect the 
133 
trout from gill netting and lure trolling. If the trout tend to remain in the fjord, but both inside and 
134 
outside of the MPA, expanding the MPA might be a useful tool to enhance the protection of the trout 
135 
from fisheries and enable a viable population of adult and juvenile trout in agreement with the WFD.

Methods 
137 
Study site 
138 
The study was conducted in Roskilde Fjord (55° 48' 36" N, 12° 03' 36” E; 117 km2) in eastern 
139 
Denmark. The Roskilde Fjord is approximately 40 km long and is draining into the southern part of 
140 
the Kattegat Sea (Figure 1). The Roskilde Fjord is generally shallow with water depths rarely 
141 
exceeding 6 meters. The Kattinge Bay (3.82 km2) is located in the southern part of the fjord (Figure 
142 
1). Comparable to the rest of the fjord, Kattinge Bay is shallow, except in a limited area where the 
143 
water depth is reaching 17 m (55° 40’ 37" N, 12° 01’ 08" E). The Kattinge Bay was appointed as an 
144 
MPA in 2005 based on estimates of high trout abundance in autumn and spring. The Langvad Stream 
145 
drains into the south-western part of Kattinge Bay. Importantly, smolt mortality surveys revealed 
146 
88% mortality for seaward migrating smolts passing the three interconnected lakes in the Langvad 
147 
Stream system (Henriksen, 1998; Figure 2). These findings are in agreement with other studies 
148 
investigating impacts of lakes on smolt mortality (Schwinn, Aarestrup, Baktoft, & Koed, 2017; Jepsen 
149 
et al. 1998). Near the outlet, stream discharge from the Langvad Stream is controlled by a sluice, 
150 
regulating water levels in the three lakes further upstream, and draining parts of the water into the 
151 
pool and weir fish passage (Figure 2). To recover to trout population, juvenile trout are released in 
152 
the Langvad Stream and smolts are released in the outlet of the Langvad Stream. Approximately 8000 
153 
smolts are released in the outlet of the Langvad Stream on a yearly basis, whereas approximately 
154 
3000 juvenile trout are released in the Langvad Stream every third year.  
155 
Stationary receivers 
156 
To track tagged trout, a network of 32 VR2W Vemco receivers were deployed throughout the 
157 
southern and central parts of the Roskilde Fjord (Figure 1). All receivers were positioned 1 m below 
158 
the water surface and deployed at water depths varying between 1.7 – 17 meters. The receivers were 
159 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
all attached to two coupled moorings (according to VEMCO standards (VEMCO, 2019b)) and kept 
160 
afloat and in place by surface buoys. In addition, one receiver was located upstream of the weir in 
161 
Store Kattinge Lake (Figure 2). Receivers were cleaned and data were downloaded on a bimonthly 
162 
basis. Test transmitters were used to confirm that all receivers worked correctly throughout the 1-
163 
year study period. To determine individual residence time and loss inside and outside of the MPA, 
164 
the hydrophones were deployed to cover key points in the fjord (Figure 1). Utilizing geographically 
165 
narrow passages (170-830 meters), the deployment of hydrophones ensured detection of trout 
166 
emigrating from the MPA. This was confirmed using test transmitters (range tests; VEMCO, 2019a) 
167 
and manual tracking of the trout, both inside and outside of the MPA.  
168 
Manual tracking from a boat 
169 
Using a portable receiver (VR100; VEMCO, 2019b), manual tracking was performed on a 
170 
monthly basis to locate tagged trout both inside and outside of the MPA. Manual tracking mainly 
171 
targeted areas that were outside of the detection range of the stationary receivers (Figure 2).  Thus, 
172 
trout were often detected by the manual tracking if the fish were located outside of receiver detection 
173 
range. By repeatedly returning to the same GPS locations, it was estimated if the trout were moving 
174 
or had become immobile. Combining VR100 data and data from the stationary receivers (Figure 2), 
175 
it was estimated if trout were lost inside the MPA without emigrating from the MPA. 
176 
Fish capture and tagging 
177 
Upstream migrating trout (n = 50, mean total body length = 54.9 ± 5.8 cm; range = 45 to 72 
178 
cm) were caught using a Wolf trap deployed in Langvad Stream between 1st November 2017 and 12th 
179 
January 2018. The trap was situated in the stream, 100 m from the Kattinge Bay. Due to low numbers 
180 
of trout entering the Langvad Stream, tagging and release were conducted on two separate days: 14th 
181 
December 2017 and 12th January 2018. During the trapping period, 56 trout entered the Langvad 
182 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
Stream. Captured fish were transferred to two flow-through (5000 liter each) holding tanks with 
183 
oxygenated water and kept until tagging (on 14th December 2017 and 12th January 2018). As fish 
184 
matured in the holding tank, fish were stripped for sperm and eggs and returned to the holding tanks. 
185 
Prior to tagging, fish were anesthetized according to standards described previously (Geertz-Hansen, 
186 
Koed, & Sivebæk, 2013). Fish were tagged using 69 kHz V13T coded Vemco transmitters 
187 
(Dimensions = 13x48mm, weight in air = 13 grams, power output = 152dB, estimated battery life 
188 
502 days, time lag between signal emission (nominal delay) = 120 ± 60 s). Transmitters were 
189 
equipped with a thermal sensor to record and transmit temperature data. The transmitted temperature 
190 
data meant that mammalian and avian predation could be detected by spike in transmitted temperature 
191 
data, if the predator ingested the transmitter and remained in the water (Wahlberg et al. 2014). All 
192 
transmitters were surgically implanted into the abdominal cavity following previous studies 
193 
(Aarestrup, Jepsen, Rasmussen, & Økland, 1999). Once fully recovered from anesthesia (Geertz-
194 
Hansen et al. 2013), fish were returned to Langvad Stream downstream of weir (Figure 2). After 
195 
release, trout entered the MPA directly when they left the stream. 
196 
All experimental procedures were approved by the Danish Experimental Animal Committee 
197 
and carried out in accordance with present regulation. All handling, anesthesia and tagging procedures 
198 
were performed in agreement with a license provided to the Technical University of Denmark for 
199 
animal experimentation (license 2017-15-0201-01164). 
200 
Acquisition and analysis of fish telemetry data 
201 
Analyses of tracking data included telemetry data covering a 1-year period spanning the 
202 
period 12th of January 2018 - 12th of January 2019. A total of 50 trout were trapped, tagged and 
203 
released in the Langvad Stream. Data analysis was divided into two fish groups: 1) analysis of all the 
204 
released fish (n = 50) and 2) analysis of the fish that survived the entire 1-year study period (n = 11).  
205 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
Rules for estimating trout presence inside and outside the MPA 
206 
Analyses of fish movements inside and outside of the MPA were conducted in agreement with 
207 
previous MPA studies examining fish residence times (Kerwath et al. 2009). To determine if the fish 
208 
stayed inside the MPA or left the MPA area, the receiver network was divided into three groups 
209 
(Figure 1). Group 1 covered receivers deployed inside the western part of the MPA in Kattinge Bay 
210 
(n = 6), group 2 covered receivers deployed at the exit of the MPA (n = 6) and group 3 covered 
211 
receivers deployed in three areas in the Roskilde Fjord outside of the MPA (n = 19, Figure 1). In 
212 
addition, one receiver was located upstream of the weir in Store Kattinge Lake (Figure 2).  
213 
Trout were estimated to be inside or outside of the MPA according to a simple set of 
214 
movement rules, similar to Kerwath et al. (2009). The rules were developed using test transmitters 
215 
applied in numerous locations in the Roskilde Fjord combined with known fish locations revealed by 
216 
the manual tracking inside and outside of the MPA. The movement rules were: After release into the 
217 
outlet of the Langvad Stream, trout were assumed to be inside the MPA until first detection by 
218 
receivers in group 2 (Figure 1). Upon detection by the receivers in group 2, fish were considered as 
219 
outside of the MPA until they were detected again by the receivers in group 1 (Figure 1). Trout 
220 
detected on the receiver in Store Kattinge Lake (upstream of the weir) were considered outside the 
221 
MPA. Thus, upstream migrating trout entering the Langvad Stream were not considered inside the 
222 
MPA.  
223 
Time spent inside and outside of the MPA 
224 
The analyses of time spend inside the MPA resembled previous telemetry studies determining 
225 
time spent inside MPAs (Knip et al. 2012). To calculate the amount of time each trout spent inside 
226 
the MPA, the periods when fish were present inside the MPA were summed.  
227 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
To calculate the proportion of time each fish spent inside the MPA, the time spent inside the 
228 
MPA was divided with the total time in which the trout was detected (time spanned between first 
229 
detection inside the MPA and last detection). For all trout entering the Langvad Stream, time spent 
230 
in the stream was subtracted from the total time spent inside the MPA. For trout that were lost during 
231 
the 1-year study period, the total time in which the trout was detected span from the first detection 
232 
inside the MPA (i.e. any receiver inside the MPA) until the estimated loss occurred (inside or outside 
233 
of the MPA). Importantly, this approach may overestimate the time spent inside the MPA. For 
234 
example, if a trout emigrated from the Langvad Stream and remained inside the MPA but was 
235 
removed from MPA after 60 days (e.g. by fishing), the proportion of time spent inside the MPA 
236 
approached 100% (60 days divided by 60 days and multiplied by 100%).  
237 
Trout survival throughout the 1-year study period was assumed when the fish was still moving 
238 
actively towards the study termination. For example, trout entering the MPA in October and moving 
239 
actively between receivers located inside or outside the MPA 72 hours prior to study termination were 
240 
estimated as survivors.  
241 
For trout surviving the full 1-year study period, the total time in which the trout was detected 
242 
span from the first detection inside the MPA (i.e. any receiver inside the MPA) and until the last 
243 
detection shortly before the study termination (12th of January 2019). For example, if a trout surviving 
244 
the entire 1-year study period spent 5 months inside the MPA, residence time inside the MPA was 
245 
estimated to 41.7% (i.e. 5 months divided by 12 months and multiplied by 100%).  
246 
Estimating trout loss inside and outside of the MPA 
247 
Similar to previous studies (Aldvén, Hedger, Økland, Rivinoja, & Höjesjö, 2015; 
248 
Thorbjørnsen et al. 2018), absolute trout loss was quantified for fish residing inside and outside the 
249 
MPA. Trout were considered lost when transmitters were returned by local fishermen, when fish 
250 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
failed to return to the Langvad Stream during the subsequent spawning season (starting in October) 
251 
or if horizontal movement completely ceased for more than four months. For trout that ceased to 
252 
move for at least four months, thereby considered dead, time of mortality was estimated and 
253 
remaining data were removed from the dataset. Time of mortality for the individuals that ceased to 
254 
perform horizontal movements (n =3) was estimated based on last evidence of active movement. To 
255 
confirm trout movements as revealed by the stationary receivers, data from manual tracking were 
256 
included and analyzed. The manual tracking revealed if trout were outside of stationary receiver 
257 
detection range, and if so, manual tracking data revealed if trout outside of stationary receiver range 
258 
were moving or immobile.  
259 
Statistical analyses and applied software 
260 
Fishers exact test was applied to compare mortality, respectively inside and outside the MPA. 
261 
Results were considered significant if α < 0.05. Data were downloaded from all receivers using the 
262 
VUE software (Version 2.6.1, Vemco Ltd., Halifax, Canada). All values are reported as means ± 
263 
standard error of the mean, unless noted otherwise. All calculations and analyses were performed in 
264 
R (R Core Team, 2019). 
265 
Results 
266 
In total, 50 trout were captured, tagged and released in the Langvad Stream (Figure 2). Three trout 
267 
were excluded from the study, as they were not detected during the 1-year study period. The 
268 
remaining 47 trout generated 2,523,761 detections, after accounting for transmitter collisions and 
269 
false detections.  
270 
Trout utilization of the MPA was substantial but also versatile. Trout were present inside the 
271 
MPA throughout the 1-year study period. On average, all trout spent 67.4% (± 4.5%) of the time 
272 
inside the MPA. For trout that did not survive the full study period, this estimate included the period 
273 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
of time spent inside the MPA and until the estimated loss occurred. As indicated above, this approach 
274 
may inflate the percentage of time spent inside the MPA. Data for trout that survived throughout the 
275 
full study period (n = 11) were used to produce more accurate estimates of the percentage of time 
276 
spent inside the MPA. The surviving trout spent on average 50.6% (± 8.4%) of the time (i.e. 
277 
approximately 6 months) inside the MPA. Utilization of the MPA among the surviving trout tended 
278 
to vary with season. Specifically, the highest percentage of the trout were present inside the MPA 
279 
during winter, spring and the late autumn, with fewer trout present inside the MPA during summer 
280 
and early autumn (Figure 3).  
281 
Trout adopted different behavioral strategies. Throughout the 1-year study period, 62% (n = 
282 
29) of the trout remained south of Frederikssund whereas 38% (n = 18) migrated north of 
283 
Frederikssund (Figure 1). These estimates included both surviving and lost trout. For the surviving 
284 
trout (n = 11) alone, the distributions were as follows A) five trout migrated north of Frederikssund 
285 
and spent on average 38.2% of time inside the MPA, B) four trout remained south of Frederikssund 
286 
and spent on average 38.1% of time inside the MPA and two trout resided inside the MPA throughout 
287 
the entire 1-year study period, performing only minor excursions from the MPA. These trout spent 
288 
on average 97.5% time inside the MPA. None of the tagged trout re-entered the Langvad Stream 
289 
during the 1-year study period. 
290 
The total trout survival throughout the entire study period was 23% (11 surviving and 36 lost 
291 
trout). Trout loss was higher outside of the MPA (47%, n = 22) than inside of the MPA (30%, n = 
292 
14). Fishers exact revealed no significant difference between fish mortality inside and outside the 
293 
MPA (Fisher’s exact test; p = 0.297).  
294 
Among the 14 trout that were lost inside the MPA, data scrutiny revealed that three of them 
295 
ceased to perform horizontal movements. The remaining 11 trout that were lost inside the MPA 
296 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
disappeared from the area inside the MPA. Manual tracking data confirmed that none of these 11 
297 
transmitters associated with trout lost inside the MPA were present inside the MPA after the last 
298 
detection by the stationary receivers. These findings indicated that three trout died inside the MPA 
299 
whereas 11 were removed from the MPA, presumably as a consequence of fishing activities.  
300 
Three transmitters from recaptured trout were retrieved from local fishermen, providing 
301 
evidence of fishing induced mortality. All three recaptured trout where caught using gillnets outside 
302 
of the MPA. 
303 
By the end of the 1-year study period, 9 of 11 surviving trout were present inside the MPA, 
304 
the remaining two trout were still moving outside of the MPA (Figure 2). Data analysis revealed that 
305 
the two trout residing outside of the MPA at the end of the study period, reentered the MPA between 
306 
October and November 2018, but left the MPA before the study termination. Illegal gillnetting was 
307 
observed inside the MPA on at least three occasions, but catches were not quantified. Similarly, illegal 
308 
angling was observed in the river mouth of the Langvad Stream (i.e. within the 500 m closed zone 
309 
surrounding the stream mouth).  
310 
Discussion 
311 
The use of MPAs as a mean to protect aquatic life is growing globally (Gaines et al. 2010), 
312 
however, but it remains controversial whether MPAs are protecting mobile species (Kerwath et al. 
313 
2009). Being the first study to investigate MPA efficacy using fish telemetry in Denmark, the present 
314 
study evaluated whether trout may benefit from protection afforded by a small MPA and estimated 
315 
the marine mortality inside and outside of the MPA. The present study found that trout spent on 
316 
average 67% time inside the MPA. It is important to note that this average number could be inflated 
317 
by the fact that trout that were lost inside the MPA could have a 100% residence time inside the MPA, 
318 
even if they only spent a relatively short period of time inside the MPA (e.g. weeks). This contrasts 
319 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
with the data representing the surviving trout spending on average 50% time inside the MPA, where 
320 
the residence time inside the MPA could be estimated more accurately and was performed in 
321 
agreement with methods described by Knip et al. (2012).  
322 
Previous studies have revealed that the protection afforded by MPAs for mobile species 
323 
depends on the time spent inside the MPA by the individual fish (Palumbi, 2004; Kerwath et al. 2009; 
324 
Knip et al. 2012). The present study found that all trout on average spent 67% time inside the MPA 
325 
and that surviving trout spent on average 50.4 time inside the MPA. These percentages   exceed 
326 
percentages reported by a previous trout study that quantified time spent inside an MPA. Specifically, 
327 
Thorbjørnsen et al. (2018) reported that trout spent 32% of the time, during a 1-year period, inside an 
328 
MPA in Norway. 
329 
It remains controversial whether MPAs may protect mobile species. It has been suggested that 
330 
many target species are too mobile to benefit from the protection afforded by area-based management, 
331 
such as MPAs, and that only resident species benefit from protection afforded by MPAs (FSBI, 2001; 
332 
Polunin, 2004; Botsford, Micheli, & Hastings, 2003; Sale et al. 2005; Pilyugin et al. 2016). The 
333 
general preconception is that highly migratory species disperse outside the boundary of MPAs, 
334 
indicating that large MPAs are required to protect mobile species (FSBI, 2001; Polunin, 2004; 
335 
Palumbi, 2004; Micheli, Halpern, Botsford, & Warner, 2004; Sale et al. 2005). Using mathematical 
336 
models, the preconception that mobile species may only benefit from large MPAs has demonstrated 
337 
by several studies (Guenette & Walters, 2000; Holland, 2000; Botsford et al. 2003; Stefansson & 
338 
Rosenberg, 2006). On the other hand, mounting evidence based on acoustic fish telemetry reveals 
339 
that MPAs may provide at least partial protection to mobile species. For example, average 
340 
percentages spent inside small MPAs range between 32% per year for trout (Thorbjørnsen et al. 2018) 
341 
to >50% per year for white stumpnose (Rhabdosargus globiceps) (Kerwath et al. 2009). The present 
342 
study revealed that the average trout spent >50% of the time inside a small MPA, indicating that the 
343 
==PAGE:33==
 
33 
small MPA provided substantial protection to the fish. Although fishing may be inflated outside of a 
344 
MPA, potentially eliminating any protective benefits afforded by the MPA (Palumbi, 2004), these 
345 
findings add to the mounting evidence supporting that small MPAs may provide protection, even to 
346 
mobile species (Morris & Green, 2012; Kerwath et al. 2009; Thorbjørnsen et al. 2018).  
347 
The present study found that 77% of all trout were lost in the 1-year study period. In total, 
348 
30% (n = 14) and 47% (n = 22) of all trout were lost inside and outside of the MPA, respectively. 
349 
Potentially, the estimated loss could be affected by uncommon fish behaviors. In anadromous species, 
350 
straying (Keefer & Caudill, 2014; Thorstad et al. 2016) or non-consecutive repeat spawning behavior 
351 
(Rideout, Rose, & Burton, 2005; Jonsson & Jonsson, 2009) may occur and could affect the present 
352 
results, but we assume that the marine loss (77%) largely corresponds to marine mortality (including 
353 
fishing induced mortality). Previous salmonid studies have provided mortality estimates that vary 
354 
substantially between years and locations (Jonsson and Jonsson, 2009; Aldvén et al. 2015). Annual 
355 
marine mortality may differ between 15% (Thorstad et al. 2016) and 85% (Berg & Jonsson, 1990; 
356 
Aarestrup et al. 2015; Kristensen et al. 2019). The marine mortality estimated by the present study 
357 
(77%) reflects an investigation lasting one year and in one location (Roskilde Fjord), indicating that 
358 
the estimated annual mortality should be interpreted with caution.  
359 
Marine mortality of salmonids may be influenced by several factors (Sobocinski, Greene, & 
360 
Schmidt, 2018), including environmental conditions (Gosselin et al. 2018), predation by birds (Wiese, 
361 
Parrish, Thompson, & Maranto, 2008), predation by seals (Eero et al. 2011), predation by  pike (Esox 
362 
Lucius) (Frost, 1954) and fishing (Kerwath et al. 2009; Morris & Green, 2012; Thorstad et al. 2016). 
363 
Whether pike occur in Roskilde Fjord remains uncertain, but harbour seals (Phoca Vitulina) are 
364 
known to reside in Roskilde Fjord and may target salmonid species (Thomas, Nelson, Lance, Deagle, 
365 
& Trites, 2017; Wright, Riemer, Brown, Ougzin, & Bucklin, 2007). Previous studies have 
366 
documented that temperatures recorded by fish telemetry equipment may increase if the tagged fish 
367 
==PAGE:34==
 
34 
are consumed by mammalian predators (Wahlberg et al. 2014). This was not observed in the present 
368 
study. In fact, transmitted temperatures never approached 37 degrees C as would be expected if the 
369 
transmitter was inside a mammalian or avian predator remaining in the water. These observations 
370 
indicate that predation by harbour seals is an unlikely mechanism explaining the loss of tagged fish 
371 
in the present study. Previous studies have documented that cormorants (Phalacrocorax carbo) may 
372 
consume trout and other salmonid species (Thorstad et al. 2012; Jepsen, Flávio, & Koed, 2018; Čech, 
373 
Čech, Kubečka, Prchalová, & Draštík, 2019). However, the predation risk inflicted by cormorants is 
374 
low for fish that are exceeding 370 mm in body length (Skov et al. 2014). In terms of salmonids, 
375 
Jensen et al. 2017 found cormorant-induced mortality to be size dependent with fish larger than 380 
376 
mm experiencing no predation from cormorants. Based on such results, Čech et al. (2019) indicated 
377 
that large body size may provide predation refuge. The smallest fish tagged in the present study was 
378 
45 cm and the average body length of trout that disappeared inside the MPA was 56 cm, suggesting 
379 
that predation by cormorants is an unlikely mechanism explaining the losses of the tagged fish.  
380 
This study revealed trout disappearing from inside the MPA. During the 1-year study period, 
381 
illegal and legal fishing was observed inside the MPA. Previous studies have documented that fishing 
382 
may affect marine mortality of trout (Rasmussen & Geertz-Hansen, 2001; Kerwath et al. 2009; 
383 
Sparrevohn, Storr-Paulsen, & Nielsen, 2011; Morris & Green, 2012; Thorstad et al. 2016). 
384 
Sparrevohn et al. (2011) showed that recreational fishery on trout in Denmark constitute 99% (600 
385 
tones) of the total annual harvest (609 tones). The study further revealed that approximately 90% (538 
386 
tones) were caught using rod and line and that 10% were caught using gillnets (Sparrevohn et al. 
387 
2011). The minimum size for legal harvest of trout in Denmark is 40 cm (Fiskeristyrelsen, 2018). The 
388 
length of trout tagged in the present study ranged between 45 and 72 cm. Because trout is the most 
389 
important species to the recreational fishery and is often harvested when caught (Rasmussen & 
390 
Geertz-Hansen, 2001), the present study indicates that the trout that disappeared from inside the MPA 
391 
==PAGE:35==
 
35 
may have been harvested by legal or illegal recreational fisheries. Quantifying the relative effect of 
392 
the recreational fisheries for trout inside the MPA is troublesome, primarily due to lack of data on the 
393 
extent of both fisheries, as also highlighted by Kerwath et al. (2009). Future projects should aim to 
394 
acquire data on fishing activity inside the MPA to investigate recreational fishing as a driver of 
395 
mortality.  
396 
Since the 1990s, numerous conservation and restoration projects have aimed to recover a 
397 
sustainable trout population in the Langvad Stream (e.g. restoration of spawning and rearing habitats 
398 
and stocking of juvenile trout). Despite these efforts, the abundance of mature trout and density of 
399 
juveniles remain low in the Langvad Stream (Henriksen, 2017). For example, Henriksen (2017) 
400 
revealed poor numbers of mature trout (n = 50 to 100 per year) and low density of juvenile trout (18 
401 
juveniles per 100 m2). To support the trout population in the Langvad Stream, managers aimed to 
402 
reduce trout marine mortality by implementing the MPA in Kattinge Bay in 2005 (Fiskeridirektoratet, 
403 
2003). Implementation of the MPA entailed prohibition lure trolling and gillnet fishing inside the 
404 
MPA (Fiskeridirektoratet, 2003). Although not the most important factor, Sparrevohn et al. 2011 
405 
revealed that gillnet fishing constitutes 10% of the annual harvest of trout. Based on such findings, 
406 
combined with the results that trout spend on average >50% time inside the MPA, the present study 
407 
may indicate that the marine mortality observed in this study could have been higher if the MPA had 
408 
not been established. However, evaluating the direct effects of the MPA on trout annual marine 
409 
survival is troublesome, largely due to a lack of reference conditions prior to the implementation of 
410 
the MPA. These considerations are consistent with previous studies calling for data on reference 
411 
conditions and specific management goals to evaluate MPAs (Nickols et al. 2019). This is particularly 
412 
relevant in terms of mobile species where time spent inside the MPA is often unknown, and mortality 
413 
outside of the MPA may vary depending on migration routes, foraging areas, locations of main 
414 
predators etc. Importantly, the trout population in the Langvad Stream is not sustainable, and although 
415 
==PAGE:36==
 
36 
the MPA offers some protection of the fish, the impact of the MPA is inadequate to recover a 
416 
sustainable trout population in the stream.  
417 
Getting the Langvad Stream to meet the goals of the Water Framework Directive 
418 
Free fish passage and partial compliance with the WFD are ensured by circumventing the weir 
419 
located in the outlet of the Langvad Stream (Figure 1). Previous studies have documented that weirs 
420 
and barriers often limit migration of various fish species, including salmonids (Olesen & Aarestrup, 
421 
2006), even when fish passages are installed (Noonan et al. 2012).  
422 
The current (2017) density of juvenile trout in the Langvad Stream (18 juveniles per 100 m2) 
423 
is not meeting the GES WFD goal of 80 juveniles per 100 m2 (Nielsen, 2016). Several factors may 
424 
affect the density and production of juvenile trout, including the abundance of spawning individuals 
425 
in the stream (Henriksen, 2017; Boel & Koed, 2013), predation (Riley & Marsden, 2009) and mature 
426 
trout marine mortality (Harris & Milner, 2006). Previous studies have documented low numbers of 
427 
upstream migrating trout in the Langvad Stream, spanning from 50 to 100 individuals every year 
428 
(Henriksen, 2017). Abundances of spawning trout are influenced by several factors, including barriers 
429 
(i.e. weirs; Olesen & Aarestrup, 2006), abundance of smolts emigrating from the river (Crozier & 
430 
Kennedy, 1993; Elliott, 1993) and the marine mortality (Harris & Milner, 2006). Thus, several factors 
431 
may explain the low numbers of mature trout and low density of juvenile trout in the Langvad stream.  
432 
 
Previous studies have demonstrated elevated smolt mortality when fish are passing 
433 
through lakes or reservoirs. Specifically, the mortality of smolt passing one lake or reservoir may 
434 
range between 74% (Schwinn, 2018) and >90% (Jepsen et al. 1998). Several studies have surveyed 
435 
smolt mortality before and after the establishment of a lake (1.12 km2) (Boel & Koed, 2013; Schwinn, 
436 
2017; Schwinn et al. 2018). Prior to the establishment of the lake, low smolt mortality ranging 
437 
between 0% and 8% was observed while 74% to >90% mortality was observed after the establishment 
438 
==PAGE:37==
 
37 
of the lake (Boel & Koed, 2013; Schwinn et al. 2017; Schwinn, 2018). These findings highlight the 
439 
dramatic influence of lakes situated within stream systems. Further studies by Schwinn et al. (2017) 
440 
and Schwinn et al. (2018) documented that elevated smolt mortality caused by lakes within stream 
441 
systems may eradicate self-sustaining trout populations. Currently, the Langvad Stream encompass 
442 
three lakes (Figure 2). All trout spawning areas are located upstream of Lake Kattinge (Henriksen, 
443 
2017). Thus, seaward migrating smolts pass through three lakes before reaching the marine 
444 
environment. Consistent with other studies (Jepsen et al. 1998; Schwinn et al. 2017, Boel & Koed, 
445 
2013), Henriksen (1998) documented 88% mortality for smolts passing through the lakes situated in 
446 
the Langvad Stream. Collectively, these findings suggest that a self-sustainable trout population in 
447 
the Langvad Stream is dependent on a reduction in the mortality experienced by the smolts migrating 
448 
between the spawning areas and the stream mouth where there is access to the marine environment.  
449 
For many salmonid species, the number of mature trout returning to a stream is proportional 
450 
to the number smolt emigrants from the stream (Crozier & Kennedy, 1993; Elliott, 1993). For 
451 
example, if the abundance of smolt emigrants is reduced by 50%, then the return of mature trout is 
452 
also reduced by 50%. Based on these findings, it is likely that the 88% mortality of seaward migrating 
453 
smolts in the Langvad Stream (Henriksen, 1998) constitute an 88% reduction in the number of 
454 
returning mature trout. Thus, elevated smolt mortality through the lakes (Figure 2) plays a crucial 
455 
role in terms of explaining the poor number of adult trout spawning in the Langvad Stream. To ensure 
456 
a sustainable trout population in Langvad Stream, and meet the goal of the DFFVø trout index, I 
457 
suggest that circumventing the lakes is a paramount element.  
458 
==PAGE:38==
 
38 
Finding the best route past the lakes in the Langvad Stream to enhance trout 
459 
survival 
460 
Free fish passage and partial compliance with the WFD are ensured by circumventing the weir 
461 
located near the outlet of the Langvad Stream. The present study indicates, however, that the existing 
462 
plans to diverge the Langvad Stream past the weir may not result in WFD compliance in terms of 
463 
juvenile trout densities in the Langvad Stream as stipulated by the DFFVø trout index. To achieve 
464 
GES and compliance with the WFD with regard to juvenile trout density, I suggest that circumventing 
465 
the three lakes is inevitable.  
466 
This study presents an alternative solution to the existing diverging plans. The novel solution 
467 
will ensure free fish passage and circumvent the three interconnected lakes situated in the Langvad 
468 
Stream (Figure 2). As indicated, interconnected lakes cause severe smolt mortality (Jepsen et al. 1998; 
469 
Boel & Koed, 2013; Schwinn et al. 2017) and may eradicate trout populations (Schwinn et al. 2018). 
470 
Based on such findings, I suggest an alternative solution where smolts can pass from the spawning 
471 
areas to the Roskilde Fjord without passing through any lakes (Figure 2). Specifically, the Langvad 
472 
Stream should drain into the Lejre Stream and eventually into the Gevninge Stream. This solution is 
473 
likely to reduce the smolt mortality from 88% to 5%-26% (Jepsen et al. 1998; Boel & Koed, 2013; 
474 
Schwinn et al. 2017; Schwinn, 2018). Currently, the distance between the Langvad Stream and the 
475 
Lejre Stream is about 10 m, suggesting that it is feasible to allocate water from the Langvad Stream 
476 
to the Lejre Stream. Water levels in the existing lakes may be maintained by allocating a limited 
477 
amount of water from the Langvad Stream to the lakes, while allocating the majority of the water to 
478 
the Lejre Stream (Figure 2). This alternate solution entails a relocation of the Langvad Stream outlet 
479 
from inside the MPA to Lejre Bay (Figure 2). The Lejre Bay is not protected by the MPA inside 
480 
Kattinge Bay and therefore, trout will not benefit from the protection from the MPA. However, the 
481 
number of smolts emigrating from the Langvad Stream is anticipated to increase, thus the proportion 
482 
==PAGE:39==
 
39 
of mature trouts returning to the Langvad Stream is also expected to increase (Crozier & Kennedy, 
483 
1993; Elliott, 1993). Although further research is required, these changes might alleviate the need for 
484 
an MPA to protect the trout in the marine environment.   
485 
Relocating and improving the MPA 
486 
By implementing the MPA in Kattinge Bay in 2005, managers aimed to enhance trout marine 
487 
survival (Fiskeridirektoratet, 2003). The current diverging plans entail that the outlet of the Langvad 
488 
Stream will be located outside of the MPA. Therefore, trout emigrating from the Langvad Stream 
489 
will not receive protection from the MPA (figure); thus, the protective benefits afforded by the 
490 
existing MPA are likely to diminish. As the present diverging plans for the Langvad Stream will 
491 
encompass all three lakes, smolt mortality is expected to remain at approximately 80-95% (Henriksen, 
492 
1998).  
493 
The present study revealed that 62% of all trout resided in the southern part of the Roskilde 
494 
Fjord throughout the entire 1-year study period (i.e. spent 100% of the time south of Frederikssund). 
495 
Specifically, 62% of the tracked fish resided south of Frederikssund, whereas the remaining 38% 
496 
went north of Frederikssund. For the surviving trout, 55% (n = 6) remained south of Frederikssund, 
497 
whilst 45% (n = 5) were detected at the most northern receivers located at Dyrnæs (Figure 1). These 
498 
findings suggest that a large component of the trout population reside in the southern part of the 
499 
Roskilde Fjord. These considerations suggest that expanding the MPA may protect a larger proportion 
500 
of the trout population for an extended period. However, present results indicated that the current 
501 
MPA regulations allow fishing techniques that are accounting for a large part of the mortality 
502 
observed inside the MPA. The extent of legal or illegal fishing leading to trout mortality inside the 
503 
MPA remains unknow, thus enhancing both regulation and enforcement may further reduce marine 
504 
mortality. Specifically, converting the current MPA into a no-take marine reserve (i.e. no harvest) 
505 
may ensure enhanced marine survival. Importantly, previous studies have revealed that marine 
506 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
reserves provide benefits superior to partially protected MPA (Lester & Halpern, 2008). Combining 
507 
the alternative solution of diverging Langvad Stream to Lejre Stream (Figure 1) with a future MPA 
508 
in Roskilde Fjord to protect trout, I suggest that a no-take marine reserve is established in the Lejre 
509 
Bay if Langvad Stream is diverged into the Lejre Stream (Figure 1). This solution would reduce smolt 
510 
mortality in the stream as well as the mortality of the trout in the marine environment. A no-take 
511 
marine reserve in Lejre Bay would protect trout emigrating from the Lejre Stream. If the no-take 
512 
marine reserve is extended beyond the Lejre Bay, it is likely to provide additional protection of the 
513 
fish.      
514 
Using fish telemetry to investigate effects of marine protected areas. 
515 
 
In the present study, the receiver network was divided into three groups (figure 2). Group 2 
516 
located outside of the MPA was positioned 15-40 meters from the MPA boarder. Trout detected at 
517 
group 2 were considered outside of the MPA. Combined with the detection range (up to 400-500 
518 
meter, depending on the weather etc.), the present study may have considered trout situated inside the 
519 
MPA as located outside of the MPA. Thus, employing this conservative approach, trout presence 
520 
inside the MPA may have been underestimated.  
521 
Small sample sizes influence whether extrapolations to the entire population can be made. 
522 
The Wolf trap deployed in the Langvad Stream (Figure 2) captured all trout performing upstream 
523 
migration between 1st November 2017 and 14th December 2018. Across this period, 56 mature trout 
524 
were caught and 50 relatively large individuals were tagged. Therefore, despite a small sample size, 
525 
it is likely that a large part of the trout population was tagged. 
526

Conclusion 
528 
Using acoustic telemetry, this study estimated the effects of a small MPA in terms of 
529 
protecting anadromous trout. Trout residence time inside the MPA was relatively high (> 50%), 
530 
indicating that small MPAs may protect mobile anadromous species. The present study indicated that 
531 
a substantial proportion (23% of all trout) may have been harvested by legal or illegal recreational 
532 
fishing inside the MPA. Thus, implementing further fishing restrictions, such as 100% catch and 
533 
release or prohibition of all fishing inside the MPA, may reduce the marine mortality.  
534 
Circumventing the weir in the Langvad Stream will comply with the goal of ensuring free fish 
535 
passage as stipulated by the EU WFD. The present examination revealed that a key element to ensure 
536 
a sustainable trout population and achieve GES in compliance with the DFFVø trout index is to 
537 
circumvent the three interconnected lakes situated in the Langvad Stream. The current diverging plans 
538 
of the Langvad Stream will not circumvent the three lakes, therefore smolt mortality in the diverged 
539 
Langvad Stream likely remains unchanged. Combined with the fact that trout emigrating from the 
540 
diverged Langvad Stream will not receive direct protective benefits afforded by the existing MPA, 
541 
this study concluded that the planned attempt to comply with the WFD may provide a limited 
542 
improvement of the juvenile trout density in the Langvad Stream.  
543 
 Instead, I suggest that the Langvad Stream is diverged into the Lejre Stream combined with 
544 
the establishment of a no-take marine reserve in the Lejre Bay, potentially expanded to cover the 
545 
southern part of Roskilde fjord (i.e. south of Frederikssund). By diverging the river to Lejre Stream, 
546 
the Langvad Stream may comply with both goals stipulated under the WFD (i.e. ensuring free fish 
547 
passage and 80 juvenile trout per 100 m2).
