Abstract
The availability and quality of prediction methods were investigated for a number of pure compound
thermodynamic properties of high relevance for industrial production processes comprising the formation
of fatty amines from fatty acids. This study presents a variety of different methods, described in literature,
for estimating the properties of Normal boiling point and Enthalpy of vaporisation for construction of the
vapour pressure curve and Liquid heat capacity. The included methods involves simple Group Contribution
methods, Corresponding States Principle methods, PC-SAFT and more. The results obtained from the
different methods are compared against experimental data collected from primarily the DIPPR database.
The compounds for which the predictions are made for are the compounds within the linear series of
aliphatic nitriles, amides, primary and secondary amines and fatty acids with emphasis on a C8-C22
chain length of the alkyl chain.
For the prediction of the normal boiling point, nine group contribution methods were examined. Not
surprisingly, the various methods performed with ranging success with regards to the component family
they were applied to.
However, looking across all the 62 compounds with experimental NBP, some
methods that showed an overall good performance were methods by Constantinou-Gani (1994), Stein-
Brown (1994) and original and modified versions of Marrero-Gani (2001, 2012, 2015). These gave AADs
(kelvin) of 2.1%, 1.5%, 2.4%, 1.8% and 1.6% in order as mentioned across all series of concern. When
applying these methods to the series of C8-C22 alkanes AADs of 2.1%, 1.9%, 2.0%, 1.2% and 1.4% were
obtained.
Seven methods were examined for the prediction of the enthalpy of vaporisation for 395 data points at
various temperature. Group contribution method by Constantinou-Gani (1994) seemingly gives the best
representation of the experimental data for the series of nitriles, primary amines and fatty acids with
AADs (kJ/mol) of 3.2%, 3.9% and 9.4% kJ/mol respectively for the heat of vaporisation at 298 K, and
3.8%, 5.5% and 8.6% when scaling to other temperatures. For the series of secondary amines, the modified
group contribution method by Marrero-Gani (2012) gave the lowest AAD, 3.2% at 298 K and 4.2% when
scaled to other temperatures. Severe lack of reliable experimental property data made evaluation of the
methods on the series of amides inaccessible.
For the prediction of the liquid heat capacity for 901 data points at various temperature, six methods were
considered. Generally, considerable deviations were observed in combination with an insufficient amount
of reliable experimental data available for most of the components of concern, as the majority of the data
are from the series of fatty acids alone. Group contribution method by Rùzicka and Domalski (1993),
however provides acceptable estimates for the series of fatty acids and primary amines with average
deviations of 1.5% and 0.7% respectively with reasonable results all the way up to C20 in the series of
fatty acids.

Resumé
Tilgængeligheden og kvaliteten af metoder til estimering af udvalgte termodynamiske egenskaber for rene
forbindelser af stor relevans for industrielle processer omfattende dannelsen af aminer fra fedtsyrer er
blevet undersøgt. Dette studie præsenterer en række forskellige metoder, beskrevet i litteraturen, til at
estimere normal-kogepunktet og fordampningsentalpi til konstruktion af damptrykskurven og varmefylden
ved flydende tilstand. De inkluderede metoder involverer simple gruppebidragsmetoder, CSP-metoder, PC-
SAFT med flere. Resultaterne opnået fra de forskellige metoder sammenlignes med eksperimentelle data
indsamlet primært fra DIPPR-databasen. Forbindelserne, som estimaterne udføres for, er forbindelser i
de lineære serier af alifatiske nitriler, amider, primære og sekundære aminer samt fedtsyrer med vægt på
en kædelængde af C8-C22 for alkylkæden.
Til estimering af normal-kogepunktet blev ni gruppebidragsmetoder undersøgt. Ikke overraskende blev
de forskellige metoder udført med varierende succes med hensyn til de forskellige kemiske familier. Men
ser man på alle de ialt 62 forbindelser, der har et tilgængelig eksperimentel normal-kogepunkt, så var der
nogle metoder, der viste en overordnet god repræsentation. Dette involverede metoder af Constantinou-
Gani (1994), Stein-Brown (1994) og originale samt modificerede versioner af Marrero-Gani (2001, 2012,
2015). Disse gav AAD’er (baseret på temperaturen i kelvin) på 2,1%, 1,5%, 2,4%, 1,8% og 1,6% i nævnt
rækkefølge på tværs af alle serier undersøgt. Ved anvendelse af disse metoder til serien af C8-C22 alkaner
kunne der opnås AAD’er på 2,1%, 1,9%, 2,0%, 1,2% og 1,4%.
Syv metoder er blevet undersøgt til estimering af fordampningsentalpien for 395 datapunkter ved forskel-
lige temperaturer. Gruppebidragsmetoden af Constantinou-Gani (1994) giver tilsyneladende den bedste
repræsentation af de eksperimentelle data for de lineære serier af nitriler, primære aminer og fedtsyrer
med AAD’er (kJ/mol) på 3,2%, 3,9% henholdsvis 9,4% for fordampningsvarmen ved 298 K og 3,8%, 5,5%
henholdsvis 8,6% ved skalering til andre temperaturer. For serien af sekundære aminer gav den modi-
ficerede gruppebidragsmetode af Marrero-Gani (2012) den laveste AAD, 3,2% ved 298 K og 4,2%, når
den blev skaleret til andre temperaturer. Mangel på pålidelige eksperimentelle data for serien af amider
gjorde evaluering af metoderne her utilgængelige.
Til estimering af varmekapaciteten i væskefasen for 901 datapunkter ved varierende temperaturer blev
seks metoder forsøgt. Generelt set blev der observeret betydelige afvigelser fra eksperimentelle data i
kombination med, at disse data også er af en utilstrækkelig mængde og pålidelighed for de fleste af de
relevante forbindelser, da størstedelen af dataene er fra serien af fedtsyrer. Gruppebidragsmetoden af
Rùzicka og Domalski (1993) giver dog acceptable estimater for serien af fedtsyrer og primære aminer med
gennemsnitlige afvigelser på 1,5% henholdsvis 0,7% og med rimelige resultater helt op til C20 i serien af
fedtsyrer.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The use of synthetic surfactants has continually increased since their introduction in the early 20th
century, and it is among the highest volume synthetic chemicals produced globally. Surfactants can be
made up by many substances and have a wide commercial and industrial applications as detergents,
emulsifiers, and wetting agents. They are high-production-volume chemicals with an estimated volume of
productions of 15 million tons per year [1], with an expected annual growth of 3-4% [2]. The worldwide
demand of especially fatty amines was 800,000 tons in 2011 [3].
Surfactants have historically been produced using petrochemical, accounting for roughly two thirds of the
organic carbon embodied in the final products. However, an increasing interest is seen in using a variety
of other renewable substrates as feedstocks for surfactant production [2], in order to not rely on finite
fossil fuel reserves. Surfactants of a natural origin are normally obtained from vegetable oils or animal
fat, which appear in the form of triglycerides [4, 5].
N-alkylanines are used without further transformation as surface active agents [6], and fatty amines
represent an important class of renewable hydrophobes [2]. Amines can be produced conventionally from
fats of different sources. Depending on the source of the fat, mixtures of different alkyl chain length as
well as saturated/unsaturated compounds are obtained. The nitrile process is the most common route to
primary and secondary fatty amines [2]. Here, fats are converted through hydrolysis to fatty acids, thus
in reaction with water the triglycerides are split into fatty acids and glycerol. The fatty acids can then be
reacted with ammonia over a catalyst and at high temperatures to form amides (reactive intermediate)
that reacts to form nitriles. Water is produced in the process, which should continually be removed to
move the equilibrium reaction toward nitrile production. The nitriles are then hydrogenated to form
amines [7]:

If it is wished to suppress this formation of primary amines to secondary amines, an excess of ammonia

The entire process comprises several steps, including the reactions and subsequent separations processes,
the temperature and pressure ranges are quite wide and will naturally also be dependent on the catalyst
used. Shirazi et al. (2017) reports for the nitrile reaction, involving the formation of nitriles from fatty
acids through amides, that it is performed at 280-360°C in the liquid state for several hours. They further
reports that the nitriles then typically are hydrogenated in the presence of Raney nickel [7]. Using this
type of catalyst for the hydrogenation, Bellefon and Fouilloux (1994) suggest temperatures below 100°C
and at hydrogen pressures of 50-100 atm, as they reports that general higher temperature favors the
formation of secondary and tertiary amines. It was seen that selectivity to primary n-octadecylamine
in hydrogenation of octadecanitrile on Ni/Al2O3 catalyst is increased from 29.7% at 280°C to 82.5% at
130°C [6]. On contrary, Volf and Pašek (1986) reports surprisingly formation of considerable amounts of
secondary amines, when using a copper catalyst at 160°C, seeing that at lower temperatures relatively
higher rate of condensation in comparison to hydrogenation occurs leading to a higher content of secondary
amines, where the content of primary amine increased with temperature within the interval 210-300 °C.
The nitrile hydrogenation was here lower by a factor of about 20 than that observed with nickel [8].
Uncertainties in the simulation of chemical processes and plants including effects of thermophysical prop-
erties on estimated process performance have been the subject of many publications. Wakeham et al.
(2001) demonstrated that, even small errors produced with the current first-order group contribution
methods available in simulation software, can dramatically mislead the designer [9].
For the estimation of the properties of mixtures, a series of characteristic parameters of the pure species
is used as the initial input in design simulators. Often we find that these parameters are not already
available from experiments, leaving two options. First option is to perform the necessary measurements,
which most often is very time consuming and costly, and it can itself also be a very challenging task
to produce data of a reliable quality. The second option is to estimate the desired chemical properties
through a property prediction method.
Methods for predicting chemical properties can be used to reduce the scope and number of experiments
needed, find better alternatives and can shorten the design phase. It also enables us to see how for
instance chemical structure affects the performance of a molecule in its specific application, and it may
help in assessing potential risks.
However, uncertainties in estimated parameters can propagate through to the final design of a process
plant, making it of great importance to have correct predictions of the input characteristic properties.

Project statement

1.1
Project statement
Prediction of pure component thermodynamic properties is of high relevance for the design and opti-
misation of industrial processes, when the necessary experimental data is not available. The predicted
properties needs to be reliable to ensure a minimal error when used in calculations. A variety of methods
for these purposes can be found in literature.
This present project therefore aims to present the availability and quality of prediction methods of pure
compound properties for specific component classes.
Available prediction methods and relevant data
will be compiled, and a comparison study shall be carried out with the purpose of selecting appropriate
prediction methods for the application.
The pure compound properties of concern are following:
• Vapour pressure curve
– Boiling temperature
– Heat of vaporisation
• Liquid heat capacity
The component classes of interest are following (in order of relevance):
• Nitriles e.g. CH3(CH2)nCN
• Amides e.g. CH3(CH2)nCONH2
• Primary amines e.g. CH3(CH2)nNH2
• Secondary amines e.g. CH3(CH2)nNH(CH2)nCH3
• Acids e.g. CH3(CH2)nCOOH
All considering both saturated and unsaturated compounds with emphasis on C8-C22 chain length of
the alkyl chain. The selected physical properties are of high relevance for industrial production processes
comprising these specific components
This thesis is purely modelling-centered, thus no experimental work is carried out.
The project can
be divided into three major bullets: collecting and storing property data, searching in literature for
prediction methods and examination of property estimates for the various component series.

Chapter 2
State of the art
This chapter provides a review of the current available property prediction methods of relevance for the
present work in literature. The review is based on scientific peer reviewed papers, technical reports and
other relevant material.
For the prediction of normal boiling point (NBP), Tb, and enthalpy of vaporization at normal boiling
point numorous methods has been proposed. A group contribution based approach is presented by Joback
(1984) [10] and Joback and Reid (1987) [11] These methods are considered acceptable for preliminary
calculations, but are not very accurate, as they are limited by their oversimplification of the molecular
structure representation as a result of the use of a simple group-contribution approach, which also makes
them unable to distinguish among isomers. Several attempts have been to presented in literature to
overcome these challenges [12] [13].
Constantinou et al. (1993, 1994) [14] proposed a method that provides accurate estimations of several
properties of pure compounds and allows capturing the differences among isomers. This method is based
on conjugate forms (alternative formal arrangements of valence electrons), and is rather complex, as the
generation of conjugate forms requires a symbolic computing environment [15].
Constantinou and Gani (1994) presented a less complex method which performs the property prediction
at two levels: at the basic level contributions from first-order simple groups is used. In the second level,
the first-order groups are used as building blocks for a small set of second-order groups which are used.
The purpose of the second order groups is to include the proximity effects and to distinguish among
isomers.
This method utilizes additive group values based on UNIFAC-defined groups to obtain the
first-order contribution to NBP. [16] [13]
Following, Marrero-Marejón and Pardillo-Fontdevila (1999) presented the group-interaction contributions
method, which considers the contributions of interactions between bonding groups in the molecule instead
of the contributions of simple groups. This allows the distinction of a large number of isomers [17].
Another well-known method today for constructing functional groups is the approach by Marrero and
Gani from 2001. The model extended the earlier first- and second-order models, to include even higher
level descriptions through additional second-order and third- order contributions [13].
Besides prediction based on group contribution (GC), as the one presented above, various other methods

have been reported for property estimation involving Corresponding States Principle (CSP) methods,
Quantitative Structure–Property Relations (QSPR) based methods, quantum chemistry based methods
and, more recently, Machine Learning (ML) based methods [18].
2.1
Property functions from Group Contribution methods
By using a GC method, the property of interest of the pure compound is estimated as a function of
structurally dependent parameters. These parameters are found from the frequency of the groups the
compound are made up from and their contributions [19]. These methods may be seen as reevaluations
of the original group contribution scheme of Lydersen (1955) by addition of new functional groups with
new contribution values, and by broaden the application to other properties beside critical properties
[20].
Appropriate regression methods are required to estimate the model parameters for the GC-models, the
property model functions as well as the associated model parameters [18].
2.1.1
Joback (1984, 1987)
The methods presented by Joback (1984, 1987) is a group contribution method using only a first-order
approximation. It is here assumed that there is no interaction between groups, which means that the
structurally-dependent parameters are found by summing the products of the number of each group in
the molecule times its group contribution. These methods does not claim overall high accuracy, but
accuracy at the level of or better than earlier methods. Joback’s method uses small very simple groups,
which allows it to covers the broadest range of compounds, at the expense of accurately. [10, 11]

Liquid Heat Capacity estimation

2.4
Liquid Heat Capacity estimation
The liquid heat capacity can be expressed in several ways, but the following methods represents the
change in enthalpy with temperature at constant pressure, denoted CpL.
Besides, it can commonly be represented as the variation in enthalpy of a saturated liquid with temper-
ature, CσL, or it can be the the energy required to affect a temperature change while maintaining the
liquid in a saturated state, CsatL. Experimentally measured liquid heat capacity often yields CsatL, while
CpL and CσL are the quantities that most estimation techniques produce. Below Tr ∼0.8, CpL, CσL,
and CsatL may be considered to have the same value [20].
At low reduced temperatures liquid heat capacities are not considered strong functions of temperature,
but above Tr = 0.7 to 0.8 Cp values are large and strong functions of temperature, approaching infinity
at the critical point. Also, near the normal boiling point, pressure is not considered to have an essential
effect [20].

Chapter 3
Property data
In this chapter the availability of experimental property data is presented.
As stated, the properties of interest in the present work is liquid heat capacity, Cp,L, normal boiling point
(NBP), heat of vaporisation, ∆Hv, which is also related to the vapour pressure, P sat.
DIPPR [35] is a database that contains critically evaluated thermo-physical properties, and this database
has been used as the main source for property data. This is accessed through DIADEM Professional
under the licence of CERE. In addition to this, the NIST Chemistry WebBook [36], a web application
that provides free access to a collection of critically evaluated thermodynamic property data for pure
compounds with a primary focus on organics, has also been used.
The scope of this study is to find methods of appropriate prediction of pure component properties for
rather large linear nitriles, amides and amines having a size of 8 to 22 carbon atoms for the alkyl chain
(denoted C8-C22 in this work). However, it is found that the extent of experimental data available for
these kind of compound is rather limited in the literature. Therefore compounds of a shorter alkyl chain
length are also included in the search as well as branched and cyclic compounds. As unsaturated com-
pounds also are of interest, these are also included, but otherwise poly-functional or aromatic compounds
have been excluded, as the effect of such groups would probably have a to big influence on the chemical
properties. Only the data overview of the linear series are included in this section, for supplementing
data please refer to Appendix D. As an addition, 30 normal boiling points and 1367 data points of liquid
heat capacity are also compiled for the series of alkanes.

Method comparison
In this chapter, the previously presented methods for prediction of pure component properties are applied
to series of compounds for which experimental data for the respective property is available. Compounds
of the series are referred to by their carbon number.

the property estimates by the various methods are plotted together against experimental data to give
an overall qualitatively judgement. This is supplemented with a quantitative evaluation based on the
Percentage Average Absolute Deviation (%AAD) determined for each data point and taken as an average
of all data points within a series:

4.3
Heat of vaporisation
Estimations of the heat of vaporisation of the series of linear aliphatic nitriles, amides, p-amines, s-amines
and fatty acids have been made. All the applied methods provide estimates based on either the molecular
structure or the Corresponding State Principle (CSP) as presented in section 2.3. These methods are
used to predict ∆Hv at 298 K or at the respective normal boiling point and hereafter the Watson relation
(1943) are used to scale to the temperatures of the experimental data points. Input properties such as
NBP, critical point and acentric factor is calculated using Marrero-Gani from ICAS and can be found in
Table D.6 in Appendix D. The choice of using Marrero-Gani (ICAS) is based on the acceptable results
for NBP presented in the previous section 4.2 in this present work, and because of the convenience of
using one methods that is available in computer aided software. The properties of the critical point
are, compared to the present properties, considered highly investigated, as many authors have suggested
group contribution methods for the critical properties in several publications throughout time [9], and
ICAS uses one of the more recent methods, a modified version of Marrero and Gani from 2012 [19].
In some cases the methods cannot be used for the first members of a homologous series, which is quite
common for group contribution methods in general [31]. In this sections this only involves hydrogen
cyanide (C1 for the nitrile series) for both Constantinou and Gani (1994) and Marrero and Gani (2001,
2012) for both ∆Hv,298K and critical properties and acentric factor.
The plots shown in the following sections are the predictions as a function of temperature for the specific
compounds where experimental data for more than one temperature are available. When only a single
data point is present, it is not really possible to see a trend and it may be difficult to evaluate whether
the data point is representative or an outlier. Some of these plots can be found in Appendix A, or are
included in the series and parity plots at 298K.

Liquid heat capacity
Estimations of the liquid heat capacities of the series of linear aliphatic nitriles, amides, p-amines, s-

molecular structure or Corresponding State Principle (CSP) as presented in section 2.4.
In some cases the methods cannot be used for the first members of a homologous series. This involves the
group contribution methods Rùzicka and Domalski (1993) and Zábranský and Rùzicka (2004) but also
the CSP by Poling et al. (2001) method as the ideal gas heat capacities used as input variables are here
also predicted by group contribution methods. The method used here is developed by Nielsen (1998) as
an addition to the methods of Constantinou and Gani (1994) [20]. This is based on the recomendations
of Poling et al. (2001) with regards to both reliability and simplicity.
For the methods of Tarakad and Danner (1977) and Chueh and Swanson (1973) all input parameters and
variable are from DIPPR [35]. The correlations proposed by DIPPR is also included in the following plots;
these are in most of the cases based on the experimental data, but in a few cases based on predictions
by Rùzicka and Domalski (1993).
The plots shown in the following sections are the predictions as a function of temperature for the specific
compounds where experimental data for more than one temperature is available. When only a single
data point is present, it is not really possible to see a trend and it may be difficult to evaluate whether
the data point is representative or an outlier. Some of these plots can be found in Appendix B, or are

PC-SAFT
This chapter investigates the use of an Equation of State, PC-SAFT, for prediction of the same properties
as already presented. The focus is on the Liquid heat capacity, as this property is considered important
in the context of the process, and not sufficiently described by the previous presented methods.
5.1
Liquid heat capacity with PC-SAFT
It is usually group contribution or corresponding states methods which are used for predicting liquid heat
capacities [20], however, large errors are seen for some of the investigated compounds when using the in
literature recommended methods. It is attempted to use PC-SAFT EoS for the prediction of the heat
capacity. This is an equation of state based on statistical associating fluid theory (SAFT).
The properties are calculated using MATLAB with a simplified PC-SAFT EoS available in a MATLAB
tool provided by CERE, DTU [40, 41]. With specified temperature and pressure (and composition if it
is a mixture), derived properties can be calculated to determine the residual heat capacity. The liquid
heat capacity is then determined from this and the ideal gas heat capacity:

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The main goal of this project was to investigate the availability and quality of prediction methods of pure
compound thermodynamic properties. More specific, this included prediction of the properties Normal
boiling point and Enthalpy of vaporisation for construction of the vapour pressure curve and Liquid heat
capacity for the compounds within the linear series of aliphatic nitriles, amides, primary and secondary
amines and fatty acids with emphasis on C8-C22 chain length of the alkyl chain. This also included
the objective of compiling experimental property data in the literature for the validation of the available
methods. A crucial part of the work was to collect a sufficient amount of property data for the evaluation
of the estimates. This was found challenging, as it turns out very few experimental investigations have
been done for some of these series, especially the heavy end which were of most interest for this study.
Normal boiling point was collected for a total of 62 components within the series, 395 data points were
collected of the heat of vaporisation at various temperatures, 901 data points of liquid heat capacities
and a total of 3,055 data point of vapour pressures at various temperatures were also collected for the
components within the series. Noted that for the series of nitriles and amines the data is primarily for the
lighter end compounds, making that extrapolation based on more commonly investigated components,
e.g. alkanes or alkanols can be necessary. Therefore, as an addition to this, 30 normal boiling points and
1,367 data points of liquid heat capacity were compiled for the series of alkanes, as well as a number of
data on branched and cyclic compounds with the respective functional groups. However, for especially
the series of amides, a severe lack of reliable experimental property data makes evaluation of the methods
unobtainable.
For the prediction of the normal boiling point, nine group contribution methods were examined. Not
surprisingly, the various methods performed with ranging success with regards to the component family
they were applied to. For the series of nitriles and amides, the method by Stein and Brown (1994) gave an
overall deviation of 1.2% and 3.2% kelvin respectively. For the series of primary amines the lowest overall
deviation of 0.8% kelvin was obtained with method by Marrero and Pardillo (1999). For the secondary
amines it was the method by Ghasemitabar and Movagharnejad (2016) which gave the lowest average
deviation of 0.7% kelvin. And last, for the series of fatty acids, the method by Constantinou and Gani
(1994) gives an overall deviation of just 0.6% kelvin. All in all, some methods could be pointed out with
acceptable result to all or most of the component families. This involves methods by Constantinou-Gani
(1994), Stein-Brown (1994) and original and modified versions of Marrero-Gani (2001, 2012, 2015), which
shows average deviations below 3% kelvin based on all the data points for the series of concern. Of course
as this is primarily based on the lighter end members of the series, the methods also need to be able
to extrapolate for the heavier compounds up to C22 for the scope of this project. Modified versions of
==PAGE:93==
76
Marrero-Gani are considered to give the best representation when extrapolating to heavier compounds.
Seven methods were examined for the prediction of the enthalpy of vaporisation as a function of tem-
perature. For the series of nitriles, primary amines and fatty acids, the group contribution method by
Constantinou and Gani (1994) seemingly gives the best representation of the experimental data with
deviations of 3.2%, 3.9% and 9.4% kJ/mol respectively for the heat of vaporisation at 298 K, and when
scaling to other temperatures the average deviations are 3.8%, 5.5% and 8.6%. If assuming a constant
value of the enthalpy of vaporisation predictions with Constantinou-Gani could be acceptable, but it
would depend on the application how great errors are allowed and if temperature dependence can be
disregarded. For the secondary amines it was the modified group contribution method by Marrero and
Gani (2012) which gave the lowest deviations, 3.2% at 298 K and 4.2% when scaled to other temperatures.
The purpose for the predictions of NBP and enthalpy of vaporisation was for the application of producing
the vapour pressure curves. In an attempt to exclude the estimates of the enthalpy of vaporisation,
method by Voutsas et al. (2002, 2006), a simple method for the prediction of vapor pressures of pure
compounds from their normal boiling point temperature, was compared against experimental data. And
as it have been found that reliable methods for prediction of NBPs for these series are available such
method based on NBPs seems reasonable. It was however seen how these uncertainties in estimated NBP
can propagate through to the vapour pressure curve, though the predictions for many of the compounds
still were reasonable. Problems are expected for members of the series above C13 has deviations seems to
be significant and increasing. The best representation is generally seen above the normal boiling point.
For the prediction of the liquid heat capacity initially five methods comprising both group contribution
and Corresponding State Principle methods were considered. Many issues were seen for the prediction of
the liquid heat capacities; generally very large deviations were observed, as well as an insufficient amount
of reliable experimental data available for all the components of concern. Somewhat acceptable estimates
are obtained for the series of fatty acids and primary amines from the group contribution method by
Rùzicka and Domalski (1993), with average deviations of 1.5% and 0.7% respectively covering all the
data points available in the two series. Even up to C20 in the series of fatty acids the this method
were able to provide estimates comparable with the data, though at temperature below 400 K. Thus,
it seems achievable to extrapolate this method to heavier end members of the two series, but it will
become questionable if applying higher temperatures, as it is not able to capture the strong temperature
dependence at high reduced temperatures.
Subsequently PC-SAFT, was also tested for prediction of liquid heat capacity in an attempt to improve
the estimates. while it was able to predict some data point quite accurately, overall the deviations were
comparable to, though not better than, the previously examined methods. An advantage of this kind of
method is that it would more likely be able to capture the temperature dependency at higher reduced
temperatures, likewise a CSP method, without the need for critical pressure and acentric factor.

7.1
Degree of completion according to scope
Different main objectives have been aimed from the beginning of this project. This have included:
• Literature review
• Compilation of available prediction methods
• Compilation of relevant data
• Comparison study of methods and data
• Selection of appropriate prediction method
The literature has been searched for available methods for prediction of properties of interest: NBP, ∆Hv
and CpL, primarily of the Group contribution types, where only chemical structure is used as input. The
literature search did also involve the background of the project for the specific applications and process
conditions involved.
Experimental data for the relevant properties for the series of linear fatty nitriles, amides, p-amines,
s-amines and fatty acids have been compiled, mostly from DIPPR. However, the scope of the project was
compounds of alkyl chain length of C8-C22, but the data on this heavy compounds are scarce, so mainly
compounds lighter than specified in the scope has been compiled.
The various methods have been used to estimate the properties of the compounds within the series, for
which data are available. This is mainly done for compounds lighter than the scope, but it is discussed
==PAGE:95==

how/if extrapolation is possible to heavier compounds where data is missing.

Predictions presented in this report are only for the saturated compounds, and not unsaturated.
7.2
Additional work not included in final report
During the 5.5 months of writing, a big amount of intermediate work and supplementary material have
been made, which have not been included in the final report due to lesser importance. This includes
spread sheets, code, plots and figures, unsuccessful or unfinished calculations and compilation of unused
data.
Time have also been spent on preparing an abstract and poster of some preliminary results as contribution
for the KT Consortium Annual Meeting 2022.
Furthermore, time have been spend on working with sensitive data not allowed for use in thesis or defense.
7.3
Future work
The suggestions for future work would be of a kind to fulfill the purpose of this present project. First
thing, which time did not allow, would be to apply the prediction methods to unsaturated compounds of
the linear series, as it was wished in the project statement. Which kind of unsaturated compounds (chain
length, placement of double bond, number of double bonds) would depend on the feedstock (fat/oil) used
for the nitrile process.
Another way the project statement did not end up completely fulfilled was due to the lack of experimental
property data of the compounds of interest. Perhaps an extended search could add a few more data points,
but it is not believed that it would be very successfully. It would seem more meaningful to continue the
work on extrapolating methods that do well for the lighter members of the series, while having a qualified
conception of how the behaviour of the compounds might change for the heavier members of the series.
However, for the series of amides, there would not even be enough data for this purpose.
Perturbation theories expressed in the form of thermodynamic models, like PC-SAFT which was briefly
examined in this work, could be of interest, especially where association might take place. If the work
were to continue on PC-SAFT better ’physical’ parameters would be needed instead of the ones used
here calculated assumable from group contributions in ICAS. Also, in the case of amines and especially
the carboxylic acids addition of association parameters might improve the results.
Another area that might be interesting for future work is the use of Machine learning for property
prediction. These kind of approaches can based on a large dataset be used for deriving statistical models
of quantitative structure–property relationships. ML methods also need data for training and testing of
the model, so here it might be necessary to broaden out the scope to also include non-linear compounds
to have a sufficient amount of data.
As a final notice, it would be relevant to enlighten how uncertainties in the various used input parameters
influence the predicted properties.
