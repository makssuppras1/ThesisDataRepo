Abstract
In this study six supported Ga-based intermetallics and one supported Cu cata-
lyst have been synthesized using IWI. To determine the success of the synthesis,
TEM, XRD, SEM and EDS have been used on three of the catalyst Ga2Pd/TiO2,
GaPd/TiO2 and GaPd2/TiO2. The results show dispersed nanoparticles, a Ga-
rich elemental composition and slightly lower loading than expected. Only the
crystalline phase GaPd2/TiO2 is actually confirmed, whereas the crystalline phases
of the samples GaPd/TiO2 and Ga2Pd/TiO2 remain unknown.
The stability of the GaPd intermetallic has been studied to determine the
best reaction conditions for eCO(2)R. The stability has been investigated by
comparing bulk characterization with surface characterization. The results show
there is a discrepancy between the two due to Ga leaching to the surface and
oxidizing, creating a surface shell of Ga2O3. It is shown that the Ga2O3 layer can
be partially removed by exposing GaPd/TiO2 to a strong alkaline electrolyte,
such as 0.1 KOH. As a result, the GaPd intermetallic should only be tested for
eCOR.
CO induced potential shifts have been measured on all the Ga-based inter-
metallics to predict the activity towards eCO(2)R. The results show that based
only on the CO induced potential shift GaPd/TiO2, Ga2Pd/TiO2, CoGa/TiO2
and Co2FeGa/TiO2 show promising electrochemical behaviour towards eCO(2)R.
eCOR has been conducted on a chip EC-MS for a Cu thin film, Cu/TiO2,
pure TiO2 and a GaPd thin film. The results of eCOR on Cu thin film has been
used to benchmark chip EC-MS, and as expected it is observed that Cu reduces
CO to ethylene. The intention of the supported Cu catalyst Cu/TiO2 has been
to have it as a reference to the supported Ga-based catalysts. Unexpectedly,
high HER activity is observed during eCOR on Cu/TiO2. Likewise, high HER
activity is observed for the pure TiO2 sample. The high activity is ascribed to
both impure copper-precursor and impure nanopowder TiO2. For this reason,
supported Ga-based intermetallics were discarded. Finaly, a GaPd thin film has
been synthesized to test for eCOR in stead of the impure GaPd/TiO2. The results
however, show only hydrogen an no gaseous hydrocarbons.
In parallel to the investigation and testing of Ga-based intermetallic, a con-
tamination problem of an EFC-cell has been solved by using a number of methods

to identify the contaminants. First, XPS results clearly show Au contamination
which is avoided by replacing the Au anode with a carbon anode. As contam-
ination continues in the EFC-cell, a study of Ni and Fe carbonyls is conducted
showing minimal traces of Fe and Ni, if the standard procedure of venting the
CO lines for 60 min at 20 sccm is followed. The carbonyl study also shows the
presence of Zn in unexplained concentrations. To solve the continuous problem
of contamination an electrochemical procedure consisting of CA, OCV and UPC
coupled to XPS has been developed. This procedure allows to discover the cor-
relation between time with the amount of Zn contamination. Finally this insight
constraints the possible contaminant sources, and by conducting ICP it is found
that the pump is contaminated due to a metal flake insight the pump.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
1.1.1
CO2 recycling
In the past decades Earth has experienced extreme climate changes as a result
of increasing global average temperature. In 2014 the Intergovernmental Panel
on Climate Change (IPCC) stated that the rise in the global average surface
temperature from pre-instustrial age (before 1951) to 2010 can, with extreme
likelihood, be attributed to anthropogenic greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions to-
gether with other anthropogenic forces[1]. To mitigate these climate changes, the
Paris Agreement was signed by 186 countries responsible for more than 89 % of
global GHG emission[2]. Amongst the goals in the Paris Agreement, is limiting
the global average temperature to increase more than 1.5° above pre-industrial
levels. In 2010 CO2 from fossil fuels and industrial processes contributed with
65 % of the annual anthropogenic GHG emissions (in CO2-eq/yr) making it the
primary driver of today’s climate change.
Different strategies exist to reduce GHG emissions to the atmosphere: de-
carbonization via conversion to renewable energy sources, carbon sequestration
and carbon recycling. Photovoltaics and wind power are amongst the renewable
technologies that have reached competitive pricing and ability for upscaling[3] in
comparison to fossil fuels. However, these technologies can only accomplish de-
carbonization of power generation by electrification. In the transportation sector
(21% of the total anthropogenic GHG emissions) decarbonization is possible by
electrification of vehicles. For sectors such as industry and agriculture not all
GHG emissions emanate from energy consumption. In the industry sector 12 %
of GHG emission are from chemical reactions that have CO2 as its byproduct
and are not readily decarbonized.

1.1.2
Intermetallics
The electrocatalytic conversion of CO2 (eCO2R) is however not straight forward.
The best single-metal catalyst in the field, Cu, capable of producing both desired
products ethylene (C2H4) and ethanol (C2H5OH), struggles with both a high
overpotential and selectivity issues[7]. The products formed during eCO2R are
multiple and diverse both in carbon backbone length (C1, C2 and C3 species)
and functional groups (aldehydes, ketones, alcohols, carboxylic acids, etc)[8].
The product diversity complicates the mapping of the reaction pathways of the
different end-products. Hence, understanding the fundamentals of the catalytic
properties of Cu is necessary to engineer better catalysts.
Predicting good candidates for eCO2R via quantum chemical calculations
requires prior knowledge of the optimal physical and chemical properties of the
reaction system. In the process of predicting good catalysts, model materials
with adjustable properties is beneficial. For this purposes, intermetallics are very
well suited. They provide the desired adjustability of both physical and chemical
properties by the possibility of varying the number of elements (binary, ternary,
etc.), the crystalline phase and the elemental stoichiometry. The intermetallics
not only allow for tailoring of physical and chemical properties but also geometric
properties that may influence potential reaction sites [9].

2.1.2
Heterogenous electrocatalysis
Many electrochemical reactions are thermodynamically favourable but have poor
kinetics, which requires an expensive and inefficient overpotential to run the reac-
tion at the desired current densities. Thus, the purpose of a catalyst is to provide
alternative activation pathways and ideally have high current densities close to
the thermodynamic equilibrium potential. Electrocatalysts provide alternative
pathways by adsorption of intermediates.
Because electrocatalysts alter the kinetics, Tafel plots (Figure 2.1) are used
as a measure of comparison. However, the complexity of electrolysis in general
(adsorption mechanisms, chemical reactions, etc) coupled with the complexity of
catalysts (stability of the elemental composition, crystalline phase, surface area,
etc) complicates the objective study of the intrinsic properties of the electrocat-
alysts and hence the interpretation of the Tafel plots. Especially, in eCO2R, as
will be shown later, the electrochemical environment plays a determining role.

transport of the electroactive species. The transport of electroactive species to
and from the electrode is influenced by mainly by three types of mass transport.
Migration, diffusion and convection.

potential gradient arises from the two electrodes which causes charged species
to migrate. However, the introduction of a large excess of an inert electrolyte,
will dissipate the electric field between the electrodes over all the ions from the
electrolyte, and thus not only on the electroactive species. This way, migration
effects are minimized.

In heterogeneous electrocatalysis, adsorption of intermediates on the electrode
surface plays a major role. Adsorption as a phenomena, is a bonding between
the adsorbate and the electrode. The bonding may be purely electrostatic, due
to a charge-dipole, due to surface energies or due to the formation of a cova-
lent bond (chemisorption)[13]. In electrochemistry in general, adsorption should
be viewed upon as a replacement process. The solvent will be polarized due to
the electrode charge, and thus polarized solvent molecules adsorb on the elec-
trode surface. Moreover, the electrode charge will attract counterions from the
electrolyte. Uncharged species such as organic compounds also adsorb on the
electrode surface in aqueous media. Their surface energy is lower than that of
water, favouring their adsorption. Finally, chemisorption occurs, when an elec-
tron transfer enables the adsorption. In electrocatalysis, chemisorption and disso-
ciative adsorption, are the important mechanisms that enable alternative energy
pathways. The latter, is the dissociation of the adsorbate upon adsorption. Thus
it is important to understand, that in electrocatalysis, there is a competition for
active adsorption sites, and thus all types of adsorption can interfere and impact
the catalytic effect.
The amount of adsorbed species is described by the adsorption coverage θ.
θ is the fraction of the electrode surface that is covered by the adsorbate. The
adsorption coverage depends on multiple parameters. First, it depends on the
nature of the adsorbate, whether it is neutral or charged. Consequently, it de-
pends on electrolyte, the adsorbate concentration c and concentration of other
species.

Electrochemical mass spectrometry (EC-MS) combines electrochemical half-cell
experiments with mass-spectrometry. The technique enables real-time detection
of gaseous or volatile reactants, intermediates and products. Mass spectrometry
(MS) is a method used for identification and quantification of species. In a mass
spectrometer the gaseous species are ionized and separated according to their
mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). Both the ionization and separation process requires
high vacuum (HV). To avert the discrepancy between the a liquid eletrochemi-
cal environment and the requirement for high vacuum batch methods have been
developed [22]. However, batch techniques complicates the investigation of reac-

tion kinetics and catalytic mechanistic pathways. Enabling in-situ and real-time
detection, will facilitate these investigations greatly.
For this technique to be useful it requires a fast, sensitive and quantitative
delivery of analyte species from the liquid electrochemical environment to high
vacuum[23]. Different techniques have been developed separate the liquid testing
environment from the high vacuum detection environment. In membrane inlet
mass spectrometry (MIMS) a semipermeable membrane protects the vacuum.
However, the thickness required to prevent the permeation of solvent limits the
response time to several minutes[23]. In differential electrochemical mass spec-
trometry (DEMS) a porous polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) membrane is used

flux into the MS such that no differential pumping is needed. Both DEMS and

optimal sensitivity, Trimarco et al. have developed a chip EC-MS with a 100 %

In catalysis, characterization plays a vital role in understanding the activity,
selectivity and durability of the catalyst. Understanding the properties of the
catalyst will allow for optimization and discoveries of better catalysts. In hetero-
geneous catalysis, the catalytic properties are determined by surface composition

3.1
Material Characterization Methods
3.1.1
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is a surface sensitive technique used
to detect elements, quantify the relative composition between elements and to
provide knowledge of the chemical state of the detected element. The technique
is based on the photo-electric effect. When a surface is bombarded with high
energy photons (X-rays), the energy might be absorbed by an electron which
enables it to escape the solid. The kinetic energy of the electron will depend on
the binding energy of the electron in the solid, which is a characteristic for the
solid. The kinetic energy (Ekin) is described by,
Ekin = hν −Eb −Φ
(3.1)
where hν is the photon energy, Eb is the binding energy of solid and Φ is the
work function of the solid. In practice, the sample and the detector will be in
ohmic contact which aligns the fermi levels of the sample and the detector, and
thus the work function to account for is that of the detector. Changes in the
==PAGE:50==
32
3 Characterization Methods
chemical state of an element are observable in a shift of the binding energy. In
general, as an element donates an electron (e.g. oxidation), the binding energy
will shift towards higher binding energies. For more information about XPS see
[26].
In this thesis, XPS has been used to monitor and detect surface impurities
and to qualitatively and quantitatively characterize the supported GaPd/TiO2
catalyst. Survey spectra of the Cu and Ag thin film used to benchmark the EFC-
cell have been conducted to control for impurities. For GaPd/TiO2 elemental
spectra where acquired to analyse the chemical state of the metals in the inter-
metallic and to quantify the ratio between Ga and Pd, and their respective ratios
to the support material Ti.
All XPS spectra for GaPd/TiO2 have been acquired using an Ar neutralizer
to compensate for charge accumulation, as it was initially observed, that with-
out the neutralizer, the peaks where distorted and shifted substantially. This is

as recommended by the supplier. A Theta Probe (Thermo Scientific) equipped

the corresponding Avantage Data System.
3.1.2
X-ray diffraction
X-ray diffraction (XRD) method is a technique used primarily to identify crys-
talline phases and structures of materials. Each crystal compound will have a
characteristic spectrum. X-ray diffraction occurs when X-rays interact with the
periodic nature of a crystalline material. Constructive interference arises when
the elastically scattered X-rays are in phase. The Braggs equation describes the
relation between diffraction angles θ and lattice spacing d of the crystal,
nλ = 2d sin θ; n = 1, 2, ...
(3.2)
where λ is the wavelength of the X-rays. XRD has many practical advantages.
The method is non-destructive, requires no sample preparation, is performed un-
der atmospheric pressure and has a large probe size. Additionally, the analysis
depth can be controlled by the incident angle α. The latter is known as graz-
ing incident X-ray diffraction. Figure 3.1 shows the schematics of asymmetrical
GIXRD. In asymmetrical GIXRD the incident angle α is kept fixed and the exit
angle β is moved in the 2θ axis. The collected diffracted X-rays are from crystal
lattice planes which are inclined to the surface. As a result, the penetration depth
of the X-rays is shorter than for symmetrical reflection where the diffracted X-rays
are from the lattice planes parallel to the surface. Consequently, for a thin film,
the substrate signal is suppressed and hence, the thin-film signal is enhanced.

nucleus and kick out a core electron. When an electron from a higher energy level
fills out the electron hole the energy difference will be released in the form of an
X-ray. The emitted X-rays are thus element characteristic. The atomic electronic

3.1.4
Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry
Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) is a analytical method
used to quantify concentrations at ppb level. Almost all elements in the periodic
table can be quantified by ICP-MS. The ICP-MS works by introducing the liq-
uid sample into a plasma as aerosol droplets. Inside the plasma, the molecules
are dried, dissociated and ionized. The single-charged ions are directed into mass
filter, typically a quadropole mass spectrometer, which is capable of rapidly scan-
ning a range of masses. To determine the concentration based on the measured
intensity from mass filter, a calibration curve is required. Due to the sensitivity
of ICP-MS (ppb), new calibration curves are required every time. The calibra-
tion curve is made from standards with very well-defined concentrations of the
desired element(s).
In this thesis, ICP-MS has been used as method to monitor the purity and
to detects impurities descending from any of the components and/or chemicals
used for eCO(2)R. In particular, alkaline electrolyte (0.1 M KHCO3) has been
measured upon. Because the matrix for the standards is acidic and the electrolyte
is alkaline, exact quantification has not been possible by ICP-MS. Instead, a
reference sample has been included for all ICP-MS measurements, thus enabling
a relative comparison with the reference sample.
The ICP-MS equipment used here is a Thermo Fischer Scientific iCAP-QC
ICP-MS. The standard used was a multi-elemental standard solution mix (10
ppm in 10 % HNO3) from Sigma Aldrich. For more details on ICP-MS see [30].

3.2.1
CO Induced Potential Shift
The CO induced potential shift refers to the potential shift observed when CO
is introduced during HER. When CO is introduced during HER, HER will be
suppressed due to CO adsorbing on the catalyst surface. The suppression of HER
will thus require a higher potential to reach the same current densities as before
introducing CO. The magnitude of this potential has been proposed by Hori et al.
to be directly proportional to the CO adsorption energy [15]. Because CO is a
key descriptor for eCO2R it means that the CO induced potential shift can be
used as an indirect measure of activity towards eCO2R.

4.2.1
Supported Nanoparticles by IWI
The procedure of the catalyst preparation on support was adapted from [32] and

The following is a general procedure for incipient wetness impregnation
(IWI).
1. The incipient volume of the support TiO2 nanopowder is determined by
pipetting 100 µl of Millipore water at a time. The incipient volume was
found to be VTiO2 = 700 ml g−1.
2. Metal-nitrate salts are weighed in a weighing boat and mixed accordingly
to the desired stoichiometry in a glass vial container.
3. The precursors are dissolved in water or 4.5 M HNO3 (depending on its
solubility) using a pipette. The glass vials are stirred manually to dissolve
all the metal-nitrate. The final concentrations of the precursor solutions
are adapted to meet the desired loading of 16.7 wt%.
4. 1 g of support material TiO2 is weighed in an aluminum weighing dish (due
to the electrostatic behavior of TiO2) and poured carefully into a ceramic
rectangular dish where it is impregnated with the incipient volume of the
precursor solution using a pipette. To ensure the optimal dispersion of the
precursor mix on the support material the mixture is stirred manually with
a spatula until the TiO2 looks equally moist all over.
5. The mixture is left drying on a hot plate at 150 ◦C for min. 2 h in air and
subsequently carefully poured into ceramic crucible boats.
The ceramic
boats are carefully pushed into a quartz glass tube and the glass tube is
placed into the tube furnace.
6. All samples except from the Cu-sample are reduced in a pure hydrogen gas
flow (10 ml/ min) in 550 ◦C for 4 hours. The Cu-sample was reduced in
230 ◦C for 2 hours only.

CO Induced Potential Shift
As previously mentioned, CO is a key descriptor and intermediate for eCO2R
and it has been proposed that the CO induced potential shift might be directly
proportional to the CO adsorption energy. Hence, the intention for this experi-
ment has been to predict the activity of the supported catalyst for eCO(2)R by
the potential shift induced by CO during HER.
Preliminary CO induced potential shift experiments were conducted in 0.1
M KOH (pH≈13) for all supported catalyst except Ni2FeGa. The glassy carbon
stub that Ni2FeGa/TiO2 was drop-casted on was not compatible with the rotating
disc electrode, and thus the Ni2FeGa/TiO2 sample could not be tested. Cyclic
voltammetry was used in a potential window from 0.0 Vvs.RHE to the necessary
potential to obtain a current density of j ≈5 mA cm−2. The rotation speed of
the rotating disc electrode was kept constant at ω = 1600 rmp. An Au foil was
used as counter electrode and Hg/Hg2SO4 as reference electrode. First, the CV
were recorded during Ar purging to stabilize the supported catalyst. After the
CV had stabilized, CO was purged and the shift in potential induced by CO was
recorded, denominated CO induced potential shift.
The results are shown in Figure 5.9 where the CO induced potential shift is
plotted as a function of number of cycles after replacing Ar gas flow with CO
gas flow. For all catalysts except the Cu/TiO2, the voltammograms never really
stabilized after introducing CO. The general trend observed for the supported
catalysts (apart from GaPd2/TiO2) was a CO poisoning of the surface causing
a positive potential shift. However, for GaPd2/TiO2 (Figure 5.9a) the potential
shift decreased over the number of CO cycles, thus enhancing HER. It was ob-
served for some of the supported catalysts, that catalyst material had stripped off
during the cycling. This observation is expected to induce an increase of the ap-
plied potential as less catalytically active surface area will be present. No further
investigation was carried out to understand the instability nor the enhancement
of HER by GaPd2/TiO2, as the measurements were intended to be repeated in
the electrolyte-flow-compression cell (EFC-cell).

5.2.3
Zn contamination
Subsequently to almost all electrochemical measurements, the cathode thin films
have been analyzed using XPS. Whereas ICP-MS can reveal information about
the dissolved species, what is really important, is what deposits on the surface
during the electrochemical experiments. However, XPS requires a coverage of
the order 1 at% which might explain why no impurities has been detected. The
expected contaminants Ni, Fe, Zn and Pb are all less noble than Cu, and thus
will reduce in the reductive potentials used (-0.8 to -1.6 V vs. SHE) but dis-
solve again, when potential is no longer applied. With this in mind, experiments
using chronopotentiometry (CP), open circuit potential (OCV) and semi under
potential control (UPC) manoeuvre was designed with the purpose of accumu-
lating the less noble metal contaminant over time and assuring that the reduced
metal-contaminant would stay on the surface. For this purpose, Cu was replaced
with Ag because Ag has a lower HER activity. A lower HER activity will help

can be detected. Finally, the actual crystalline structure for the GaPd thin film

forms) remains unanswered.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this thesis project, Ga-based supported nanoparticles have been investigated
for eCO(2)R while in parallel solving a contamination problem in an EFC-cell.
It has been shown by TEM imaging that IWI can be used to synthesize
GaxPdy nanoparticles on nanopowder TiO2 in the size range of 3-30 nm in diam-
eter. However, obtaining the desired crystalline phases has been less successful.
Nonetheless, the previously reported GaPd2 was successfully synthesized while
the crystalline phases of the intermetallic compounds with the nominal compo-
sitions Ga2Pd and GaPd remain unclassified. Classifying the crystalline phases
was partially complicated by the predominant XRD spectrum of the crystalline
support TiO2. Elemental composition by EDS revealed at systematic deviation
of the Pd content and a general loss Ga and Pd during synthesis.
Surface characterization of GaPd has shown that GaxPdy intermetallic should
be tested for COR as they require alkaline conditions to remove an oxidized
surface layer of Ga. This was deduced from investigation of the 1:1 GaPd in-
termetallic. For GaPd a discrepancy between bulk and surface elemental was
demonstrated by EDS and XPS. After storage in ambient conditions for 4 months
the bulk composition was found to be Pd-rich whereas the surface composition
was completely dominated by Ga (96 at%). XPS confirmed the Ga surface layer
on the surface of the nanoparticles was oxidized. It was shown that exposing the
Ga2O3 layer to 0.1 M KOH (pH≈13) for 8 min. partially removed the Ga2O3
layer.
CO induced potential shifts for all the supported intermetallics except Ni2FeGa
were determined in 0.1 KOH. The supported intermetallics showed very different
behaviour. It was however later found, that the support material TiO2 contained
impurities that influenced electrocatalytic behaviour. Nonetheless, CO induced
potentials shifts were close those reported for Cu in both 0.1 M KHCO3 and 0.1
M KOH, which suggests that they have similar catalytic behaviour as Cu towards
eCO(2)R.
Extensive work has been carried out to identify the contamination sources of
the EFC-cell. It was shown initially by XPS, that using Au a counter electrode
deactivated the cathode due to corrosion even at the presence of an anion ex-
change membrane. Moreover, Zn contamination was detected in the EFC-cell.

6 Conclusion
It was shown that using a sequence of CP-OCV-UPD could identify the con-
taminant both electrochemically and by XPS. By this method it was confirmed
that the Zn contamination increased over time, thus indicating that the source of
contamination originated from a cell component. Eventually, a metal flake was
found inside the pump connected to the EFC-cell.
Lastly, it was shown on the chip EC-MS that Cu/TiO2, which was intended
as reference sample, was highly active for towards HER. The same was shown for
pure TiO2. Both results were attributed to the lack of purity of both Cu(NO3)2
and TiO2. Finally, GaPd thin film was compared to a copper thin film. The
copper thin film behaved as expected for eCOR producing ethylene. The GaPd
on ther other hand showed no presence of hydrocarbons but only HER. However,
the GaPd thin film was not operated under ideal conditions, as the chip EC-
MS used cannot operate strong alkaline solutions required to dissolve the Ga2O3
formed on a GaPd surface.

APPENDIX H
Project Plan
H.1
Auto-evaluation of Project Process
Due to a variety of unforeseen obstacles, the project plan has very much devi-
ated from the intended plan. The idea of this project was to test a series of
Ga-based nanoscale intermetallic for eCO(2)R and correlate the activity with de-
tailed characterization. This was supposed to be accomplished within 2-3 months
allowing for 1-2 months of optimization of experiment and/or results. However,
the cell intended for activity measurements had problems with contamination
that had to be solved first. The latter ended up taking five months to solve,
partially due the difficulty of determining the origin of contamination and par-
tially due to equipment failure for characterizing and identifying contaminants.
The project has thus been shaped by the constant hope, that the contamination
problem would readily be solved. This means that some minor side experiments
have been carried out in parallel with solving the contamination and thus this
thesis is characterized by a number of minor independent experiments that were
never really prioritized as I was waiting for the electrochemical cell to be fully
operational.
