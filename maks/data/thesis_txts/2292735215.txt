Abstract
The present thesis investigates membrane forces in slender RC and FRC beam elements ex-
posed to large deformations. To analyse this phenomenon several 3-point-bending tests is
performed, where membrane forces are introduced by restraining the supports against hori-
zontal movement and rotation. Additional tests are carried out with horizontally free supports
and load-deformation diagrams are constructed for all experiments in order to compare and
identify the eect of compressive membrane forces. The executed work shows a clearly ten-
dency of increasing bearing capacity when membrane forces are introduced. Furthermore it is
revealed that short beams can obtain far greater capacity increase due to higher compressive
stiness.
Beside experimental work dierent calculation methods is examined and modied with the
purpose of describing the plastic behavior of the beam elements exposed to large deformations.
The change of geometry due to the deformation is taken into account through a series of upper
bound calculations, where each step of calculation represents a nite increment in displacement
and in plastic strains. This sti-plastic calculation method results in a overestimate of the
bearing capacity indicating that the supports not being innite sti.
Calculations taking movement of the xated supports into account are developed by treating
the supports as axial spring with no rotation allowed. The spring-contribution is calculated
in two ways. A deformation determined analysis and a normal-force determined analysis. In
the deformation based analysis a linear relation between midspan deformation and horizontal
movements of the supports is assumed. Thereby the spring force is estimated by the spring
position, which is evaluated from the beam midpoint displacement.
In the normal-forced
based spring analysis the force in the spring is estimated from the membrane force in the
beam found from the sti-plastic calculation.
Thereafter the spring position is estimated
from the membrane force. Both spring approximations results in a better description of the
load-deformation behavior in the plastic beam state. The deformation based analysis is still
an overestimate of the bearing capacity. The normal-forced based spring analysis successfully
described parts of the beam behavior and therefore is the best of the applied analysis. However
non of the approaches can describe the entire work curve and especially in the region of the
top point the calculations becomes invalid.
Finally a picture based software named ARAMIS is used to analyse the crack propagation in
the dierent reinforced beams. The program shows a main dierent between RC-beams and
FRC-beams in the number of cracks developing doing these identical bending tests. For Fiber
reinforced beams few cracks forms and only one or two cracks are visible for the naked eye,
where as far more cracks is observed in the conventional reinforced beams.

Introduction
In conventional calculation of bearing capacity of a beam element it is assumed that the beam
only undergoes small deformations. For extreme load cases like explosions, impulse or re loads
this is not necessarily a valid assumption. In these loadcases it can be necessary to include
large deformations in the calculations. For a beam with horizontally restrained supports this
means that the load will be carried both by bending and by membrane forces, which develop
within the beam. These membrane forces will help carry the applied load and can thereby
increase the capacity. In plate-theory this eect has already been observed and taken into
consideration [13: Braestrup. 1980]. In the work by Baestrup a concrete slap restrained at
the supports against horizontal movement is considered. The article proposes a rigid-plastic
calculation for the plate behavior.
In this thesis the same sti-plastic calculation is converted into beam-theory. The calculations
are performed in small deformation steps, where each step is an upper bound solution. By
this method it should be possible to include large deformations in the calculations. When the
assumption of small displacement is no longer valid, the geometry of the beam will change
as the beam deforms. In the proposed calculations this change of geometry is found in each
step. The bearing capacity can therefore be estimated in each step and the work-curve can be
predicted. The theoretical calculations are tested against concrete beams that are restrained
against all movement at the supports. The beams are casted with varying length and rein-
forcement, to test each parameter's eect on the capacity of membrane forces and how well
the theory ts each beamtype.
Three dierent types of reinforcement are used, conventional steel reinforcement bars, steel
ber reinforcement and plastic ber reinforcement. The goal for the beam tests are to in-
vestigate how much bearing capacity the dierent types can gain from compressive mem-
brane forces and if the beam has sucient ductility to gain additional capacity from ten-
sile membrane forces, for future references called catanary action, as seen for concrete slaps
[13: Braestrup. 1980]. A combination of both ber and conventional reinforcement is made
using the strength of the conventional reinforcement and the ductility of the ber reinforce-
ment, [1: Naaman. 2007], and hopefully a combination will thereby be able to establish extra
tensile membrane forces.
Because a sti-plastic behavior of a beam is dependent on a sti support, another type of
analysis method is presented in this thesis. In this alternative method the horizontal sup-
ports are modeled as axial springs thereby given the possibility for membrane forces, but
also the possibility for the supports to move. A solution for this statical system is made in
[17: Izzuddin. 2005]. In the work by Izzuddin the subject is a simple supported steel beam
with axial springs applied in the supports. Thereby this work does not take compressive mem-

Theory
In normal beam theory one usual assumption is that the deformations are small. Therefore
the beam length is assumed constant while the beam deforms. However for extreme loadcases
the assumption of small deformations are no longer valid. Once large deformation are taken
into account membrane forces will be introduced in the beam.
In slabs and plate theory it can be possible to include membrane forces in the bearing capacity
calculations, which can increase the capacity signicantly. For beams however the eect of
membrane forces are not taken into account in the calculations. Even for plates it is not an
easy task to add the membrane forces in the calculation [13: Braestrup. 1980].
Three dierent calculation methods is presented in this section. They dier by having dif-
ferent support conditions. The rst is modeled by assuming the supports are totally xed
against horizontal movement. Secondly and third two type of analysis are made where axial
springs are applied to the static system. All of them will depend on the moment-normalforce
diagram. This diagram will be presented both for the conventional reinforced concrete beam,
the ber-reinforced concrete beam and the mixture of these where both conventional and ber
reinforcement is present.

After the tests are performed all the images are loaded in to the ARAMIS-program for further

ARAMIS procedure:
- Create project: A 2D project is created with the default settings applied.
- Image series: The images for the analysis is loaded into the program and sorted so
that the calibration image with the ruler is placed at the end of the image series. A
stage for each image is generated.
- Dene mask: Dening the mask tells ARAMIS which part of the image should be
included in the calculations.
- Stage data: Dierent additional data such as deformation at midspan and load con-
nected to each image (stage) is loaded into the program as a text-le.
- Add start point: Start points are added within the dened mask. These start points
denes where the computation process is started and several points may be necessary
due to cracks. Every little point within the dened mask has to be connected to a start
point in order to be included in the calculation. If parts of the mask is cut o by cracks
an additional start point is selected at the separated side of the crack.
- Compute: ARAMIS coputes the project according to the dened mask and the start
points.
- Calibration: ARAMIS assumes distances during the rst calculation. The calibration
image with the ruler is used to indicate a real distance by drawing a line and type in the
real distance. ARAMIS then recalculate all the stages ones more with this real length
in mind.
- Analysis: The calibrated project and the added stage data is used to generate work
curves for the dierent beams. Furthermore crack propagation and movements of the
supports are investigated.

The work curves at gure 4.16 and 4.17 reveals relatively large deviations in strains at failure

Regarding the position based work curves at gure 4.17, various factors could be a part of
these dierences in maximum strain.
Firstly imperfections in the material would lead to
dierent fracture strains. Secondly the diameter changed signicantly along the bars which
denitely inuences the results. A third reason for the deviations may be due to the mounding
of the reinforcement bars between the clamps. The force at which the jaws clamped around

the 6 mm and 8 mm reinforcement bars respectively were elongated at a rate of 1 mm/min

Conclusion
In this thesis slender concrete beams restrained in both supports for movement and rotation in
all directions and with varying reinforcement types have been tested. The support conditions
introduce membrane forces in the beams. The clear tendency from all the beam tests were
that when compressive membrane forces can be build up the bearing capacity increase. The
eect of this is larger for shorter beams due to more compressive stiness while longer beams
can undergo more deformation and thereby ultimately build up tensile membrane forces.
However the beams do not have enough ductility to gain any important capacity increase due
to catanary action. Therefore the eect of this on concrete beams can be neglected. The tests
show that larger steel reinforcement size introduce more ductility and thereby increase the
change of observing catanary action and large displacements.
Fiber reinforced beams have a considerable lower bearing capacity than conventional rein-
forced beams. However they can possibly gain relatively more capacity when compressive
membrane forces are introduced. The FRC-beams can undergo more deformation than the
regular reinforced beams. Even though the beams can undergo larger deformation and thereby
could have increased ductility, the beams does not show signs of catanary action. This is due
to the fact, that the yield points for these beam types are more compressed than for regular
reinforced beams. The beams therefore obtain the deformation in only one cross-section and
thus reducing the ductility and thereby the possibility of catanary action. A good vibration
method is especially important for ber reinforced concrete, because it is crucial that the bers
are evenly distributed in the mix. Otherwise the yield points will be misplaced and the beam
behavior harder to predict. The dierent ber concrete types showed that the compressive
strength of the concrete type directly eect the capacity of building up membrane forces. A
weaker concrete type will therefore have a lower eect when compressive membrane forces are
introduced.
A beam reinforced with both ber and conventional reinforcement has an increased bearing
capacity, but the same eect from membrane forces compared with the RC-beam of the same
length. The beam with both reinforcement types actually show less ductility than the conven-
tional reinforced beam. The bers in the beams compress the yield points so the deformation
is made in a single cross-section. Thereby even though the beam has a higher steel percentage,
the ductility is lower and therefore no catanary action can be seen.
Together with the beam-tests several analyze methods have been proposed in this thesis. First
of the calculations presented is a sti-plastic analysis used to describe the post top-point beam
behavior. In this analysis the supports are assumed totally sti against all movements. This
calculation method highly overestimates the bearing capacity, especially for the weaker beams,
such as the ber reinforced beams. Especially the plastic ber beams behaved dierently than

anticipated by the theory. This was because these beams concentrated the yield points and
thereby made hinged which actually made the beams act more like a simple supported beam
than a xed beam. Therefore the analysis for the xed beam would overestimate the bearing
capacity since a wrong support condition was assumed. When the analysis was made with
simple supports the steel ber reinforced beams were well predicted by the theory.
One of the reasons for the overestimation of the sti-plastic analysis is that the horizontal
supports, which are actually causing the membrane forces, are not totally sti. Therefore two
dierent types of plastic analysis with axial springs at the supports were proposed. Firstly
a deformation based method, where the spring position was estimated as a function of the
displacement at beam midpoint. This method did not improve much on the results compared
with the sti-plastic calculation. This was due to the fact that the horizontal deformation had
to be chosen so small, in order to avoid introducing an theoretical compression in the beam,
that the eect was too small. Therefore the eect of this calculation method only aected the
last part of the work-curve, which moved closer to the observed test result. However the total
result of the analysis were disregarded as not being precise enough.
The second spring type analysis were normal-force based. The spring position was estimated
from the normal-force in the beam found from the MN-curve. Thereby satisfying horizontal
equilibrium at the supports. This method proved more successful than the latter in describing
the beam behavior. Especially the conventional reinforced beams were well predicted using
this method. For the ber reinforced beams however the prediction was better than for the
other methods, but still not able to precisely describe the beam behavior, until the support
conditions were changed.
The rise of the curve calculated from an elastic calculation on the plastic beam however show
more consistency with the test results, especially for the longer beams.
In the transition
between the rise and the fall of the curve none of the theories were successful. This is because
the beam at this point behaved as a mixture of the two elastic and plastic calculations.
Therefore none of the calculations could describe this part of the curve.
All in all by applying the normal-force based spring calculation method together with the
elastic analysis for a plastic beam the work-curve of a conventional reinforced concrete beam
can be described. For the ber beams, where the yield point were compressed it is impossible to
restrain the beam from rotation in the supports and therefore these beam must to some extend
be calculated as a simple supported beam. To properly predict the beam behavior for these
type of beams a further investigation to what extend the beams are xed. The ductility is also
eected by compressing the yield point. For tensile membrane forces to increase the bearing
capacity doing large deformations additional ductility must be introduced to the cross-section
around the yield points.

Future work
For future work several aspects could be interesting to make a further investigation of. Firstly
the spring stiness of the support is highly interesting due to its large impact on the the-
oretical work curve. The plastic based calculation method with spring support used in the
present thesis showed partly conformity between theory and practices for realistic values of the
spring constant. Additional experiments with measurements of the horizontal force applied
to the supports together with the movement could provide more accurate values of the spring
constant. Furthermore an analyse of the spring stiness doing the test could be performed in
order to investigate if it remains a constant value or if it changed throughout the test.
Secondly future work with test specimen addressed to analyse catenary action could be of
interest. Starting tensile membrane forces were observed in this project but insucient duc-
tility of the reinforcement lead to failure. Beams containing adequate ductile reinforcement,
especially around the yield points would provide the basis for a deeper analysis of increasing
bearing capacity due to tensile membrane forces as indicated by some of the test performed in
this project. Reinforcement bars with a lager diameter could be investigated simultaneously
for an analysis of their eect on the ability of introducing catenary actions.
For the ber beams, where the yield point were compressed and thereby acted more like hinges
it could be possible to investigate to what degree the supports should be analysed as xed
or simple supported. A connection between the length of the yield zone and the degree of
rotational freedom necessary for a correct analysis could be investigated.
Finally ber reinforced slabs could be tested and analysed. The performed tests for FRC-
beams showed the largest relative capacity increase due to compression membrane forces but
no catenary actions were observed. It could be interesting to investigate the anticipated more
eectiveness of membrane forces in FRC-slabs and possibly observe catenary actions.

The linear-motion potentiometer at gure A.2 has a measurement range of 100 mm. and
consists basically of a thin straight shaft with uni-axial movement allowed. The POT's op-
erates on the principle of a variable voltage divider known from light faders. An electrically
conductive wiper slides on a resistive element providing more or less resistance according to
the sliding direction. When the wiper is positioned at one end the resistance is zero and
the output will be equal to the supply voltage. At the other end total resistance is applied
whereby the output becomes zero [5: Antonelli et al. 1999]. A simple sketch of the principle
behind the linear-motion POT's is presented at gure A.5 At places where relatively large

(see gure 4.3)

A dierent type of POT called String-POT can be seen at gure A.4. The same theory applies

increases as the rotation becomes larger. The disk is connected to a string by e.g. a shaft,
meaning that the more the string is pulled away from the POT the more rotation of the disk,
thus lager resistance. Thereby the output voltage represents the movement of the string. In
general this kind of POT has a larger measurement range than the linear-motion POT's and
therefore it is xed to the middle of the beam, where the largest deformation appears.

The displacement range of the piston is 198 mm. going from 99 mm to -99 mm The displace-
ment of the piston is logged at the data-logger together with all the other measurements in
order to make later data processing and calculations easier. A piston displacement le is also
generated by the test machine which is assumed to be the real deformation. The logged defor-
mation represented by a voltage value is calibrated up against the assumed real deformation
from the test machine by linear interpolation:
x2 −x1
v2 −v1
· (v −v1) + x1
(A.24)
Here x1 and x2 is the minimum and maximum deformation respectively given from the test
machine. Likewise v1 and v2 is the min and max voltage output from the data-logger. v is the
variable voltage output taking values from v2 to v1. The deformation from the test machine
is zeroed due to the change of sign in order to ensure only increasing deformation. Thereby
the minimum deformation x1 becomes zero.
The load is also detected by the INSTRON load cell and logged at the data-logger simulta-
neously. Like for the piston displacement the logged output values is calibrated up against
the load from the load cell and recalculated from voltage to load by use of equation (A.24)
with the x-values representing the load. Equation (A.24) can be used together with the test
result outputs to make a load-voltage diagram and a position-voltage diagram for each exper-

load cells are presented in appendix A.5.

allowed. Measurement results from this test showed output values equal to huge tensile forces.
This indicated that the cable had fallen out for tests with enormous tensile forces. The sudden

doing the tightening of the hammerhead were all within a range of 19 kN to 40 kN which is
unacceptable high and treated as errors. It is uncertain what caused these fail measurements
but the tendency matches the expectations and the ARAMIS-results for the movement of the
hammerhead. The results from the load cell are rejected due to the inconsistency and clearly
impossible output values.
