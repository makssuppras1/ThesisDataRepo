Abstract
For the past decades, the standard for in vitro drug delivery and drug discovery, have
been static two-dimensional cell cultures.
While proving to mimic the physiological
environment well, the standard cell cultures lack important features of the human tissue
in vivo. Previous studies have succesfully diﬀerentiated these static cell cultures to be
more in vivo like, by applying ﬂuidic shear-stress using microﬂuidic devices, while the
cell cultures are grown on PDMS-chiplets.
Microﬂuidic systems haven proven eﬀective, but are expensive and very complex, making
it challenging to be used in high throughput studies. This is in part due to expensive
laboratory equipment, as well as expensive manufacturing processes of PDMS-chips.
This study introduces a novel system for applying ﬂuidic shear-stress on cell cultures
grown on inexpensive 24 well plates, using a single inexpensive stepper motor.
The
components of the system are printed using oﬀthe shelf 3D-printers, while still being
easy to setup and use. The system is modular, making it easy to customise for speciﬁc
projects.
The system has been tested on caco-2 and HUVEC cell cultures, showing a clear dif-
ferentiation compared to static cell cultures. The caco-2 cell cultures were proven to
express a goblet-like phenotype, with mucus and MUC2-antibodies being present, while
the HUVEC-cell cultures showed a clear spindle-like morphology.
In summary, the system has proven capable of diﬀerentiating cell cultures under shear-
stress.
Since it is aﬀordable and easy to use, the system has potential to become a
cost-eﬀective alternative to microﬂuidics for high throughput drug delivery studies.

Resume
Gennem de seneste årtier har standarden for in vitro drug delivery og drug discovery
været baseret på todimensionelle cellekulturer.
De har haft en god gengivelse af det
fysiske cellemiljø, men egenskaberne for menneskeligt væv er mangelfulde in vivo.
Tidligere studier har haft succes med at diﬀerentiere statiske cellekulturer til at ligne
in vivo, under anvendelse ﬂuid shear-stress ved brug af mikroﬂuide apparater, imens
cellekulturerne bliver groet på PDMS-chips.
Mikroﬂuide systemer har vist sig at være eﬀektive, men er dyrere og meget komplekse,
hvilket gør det svært at bruge dem i high throughput studier. Det er delvist på grund af
dyrt laboratorieudstyr og dyre fabrikationsprocesser af PDMS-chips.
Dette studie introducerer et nyt system til at bruge ﬂuid shear-stress på cellekulterer,
der er groet på billige plader med 24 brønde, ved at bruge en enkelt stepper motor.
Systemets komponenter er printet ved brug af 3D-printere, som kan købes i almindelige
butikker, alt imens det stadig er nemt at opstille og anvende. Systemet er modulært,
hvilket gør det nemt at tilpasse speciﬁkke projekter.
Systemet er blevet testet på caco-2 og HUVEC cellekulturer, som viste en klar diﬀer-
entiering sammenlignet med statiske cellekulturer. Caco-2 cellekulturerne viste sig at
udtrykke en goblet-lignende fænotype med mukus og MUC2-antistoﬀer, mens HUVEC
cellekulturerne viste sig at have en tydelig spiralformet morfologi.
Alt I alt har systemet vist sig at kunne diﬀerentiere cellekulturer under shear-stress.
Siden det kan betale sig og er nemt at bruge, har systemet potentiale til at blive et billigt
alternativ til mikroﬂuider til high throughput drug delivery studier.

The circulatory system
The circulatory system is the primary transport organ system in the body, permitting
blood to circulate and transport nutrients, gasses and other signalling compounds around
the body between the diﬀerent organs.
The circulatory system also includes the lymphatic systems, circulating lymph ﬂuids
around the body. The lymphatic system will not be explained into detail in this report.
The circulatory system has ﬁve main functions:
1. Transport of blood: Blood is transported from the heart to the body tissues and
back to the heart.
2. Exchange of nutrients/material: Nutrients and oxygen exchange with tissues by
diﬀusion from blood vessels to cells and waste materials exchange vice versa.
3. Transportation of substances: Substances such as hormones, cells of the immune
system, coagulation molecules etc., are transported to diﬀerent tissues in the body
via the blood stream.
4. Regulation of blood pressure: Together with the heart, the circulatory system
maintains the blood pressure within the normal range.
5. Directing of blood ﬂow: When needed, the circulatory system can direct blood to
certain tissues if needed to maintain homeostasis.
The circulatory system is mainly comprised of two main type of vessels, pulmonary vessels
transporting the blood from the right ventricle to the lung and back to the left atrium of
the heart, and the systemic vessels transporting the blood from the left ventricle around
the body, connecting most tissue, and back to the right atrium of the heart. There are

Histology of the blood vessels
Arteries and veins are generally comprised of the same three layers, the tunica intima,
the tunica media and the tunica adventitia. A graphical representation of the histology
of a blood vessel can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1.1. The tunica intima is the inner most layer,
and consists of simple squamous epithelial cells called the endothelium, being continous
with the endocardium of the heart wall. The endothelium lies on a thin tissue basement
membrane, then a thin layer of connective tissue called the lamina propria as well as a
thin layer of elastic ﬁbers called the internal elastic membrane. The elastic membrane
separates the tunica intima from the tunica media, which is mainly comprised of smooth
muscle cells arranged circular around the blood vessel. The muscles help control the
blood ﬂow, by either introducing vasoconstriction when the smooth muscle is contracted
around the blood vessel, or decreasing the diameter of the inner blood vessel, creating a
greater ﬂow, thus a larger general blood pressure. When the smooth muscle is relaxed,
vasodilation is introduced, increasing the inner diameter of the blood vessel, which in
return lowers the blood ﬂow, decreasing the blood pressure. Apart from smooth muscle
cells, the tunica media is also comprised of a range of diﬀerent elastic and collagen ﬁbers,
depending on the size of the veseel. The external elastic membrane separates the tunica
media from the tunica adventitia. The tunica adventitia is made of connective tissue,
being mostly dense connective tissue closer to the tunica media, converging towards more
loose connective tissue in the outer part of the blood vessel, merging with the surrounding
connecting tissue [1].
Arteries carry blood away from the heart, forming a continuum from large elastic arteries,
through to smaller muscular arteries ranging from 40µm to 300µm, and ﬁnally becoming
the smaller arterioles ranging in diameter from approximately 40µm to as small as 9µm.

Elastic arteries have a higher amount of elastic tissue compared to other types of arteries,
which have a relatively thick tunical intima. The tunica media is largely comprised of a
meshwork of elastic ﬁbers infused with circular smooth muscle tissue as well as collagen
ﬁber. The tunica adventitia is relatively thin. The muscular arteries have hence its name
a relatively higher amount of muscle ﬁbers compared to other types of arteries. The
larger muscular arteries have 25-40 layers of smooth muscle in the tunica media, while
the smallest muscular arteries have three or four layers of smooth muscle. The smooth
muscle in the muscular arteries make them able to partially regulate blood supply to
diﬀferent parts of the body in the larger muscular arteries, using vasoconstictrion or
vasodilation [1].
Capillaries are the smallest form of blood vessel in the body, ranging from 7µm to
9µm in diameter, and typiccally approximately 1 mm long. The capillary wall consists
primarily of the aforementioned squamos epithelial cells resting on a basement membrane.
Outside of the basement membrane is layer of loose connective tissue merging with the
surrounding tissue. Nutrients and oxygen diﬀuse over the thin cappilary walls into the
surrounding tissue, while substances and carbon dioxide diﬀuse back into the capillaries
over the thin capillary walls. The blood is then carried over the larger veins from the
capillaries [1].
Veins carry blood to the heart from the body tissue, and generally has similar features
as the arteries do. The smallest veins are called venules, and can be up to 50µm in
diameter. Venules generally have less smooth muscle, and have very thin walls, with a
structure generally similar to the capillaries. Substances can diﬀuse over the thin walls
of the venules, but exchange of substances decrease as the venules become larger and the
venules increase in thickness, becoming small veins and subsequently medium to large
veins. In medium and large veins, the tunica intima is thin, and apart from a thin layer
of endothelium also consists of layers of loose collagenous connective tissue, as well as a
few elastic ﬁbers. The tunica media has a thin layer of circular smooth muscle cells. The

The caco-2 cell line
The caco-2 cell line is an immortalised human cell line, isolated from a colon carcinoma,

Mechanotransduction by YAP/TAZ
Tissue diﬀerentiation is known to be controlled by genes, morphogenes and signalling
molecules such as hormones, while the physical microenvironment also has proved to
have inﬂuence in the cell diﬀerentiation and gene expression [25]. The physical properties
such as the stiﬀness of the ECM has shown to have an inﬂuence in the cellular gene
expression as well as involving rapid remodelling of the cytoskeleton in certain cells.
More speciﬁcally, variations in ECM-stiﬀness have shown to impact cellular behaviour in
both human mesenchymal stem cells (hMSCs), muscle stem cells as well as endothelial
cells [25–29].
Cells have mechanotransduction, enabling them to sense their environment, by having a
nuclear relay of mechanical signals, called the Yorkie-homologues YAP (Yes-associated
protein) and TAZ (transcriptional coactivator with PDZ-binding motif) [26]. The YAP/TAZ
pathway was ﬁrst discovered in 1994 [30], and is a part of the Hippo signalling pathways,
which in short controls organ size in animals through the regulation of cell proliferation
and apoptosis [31]. YAP and TAZ work as transcriptional co-activators associated with
DNA-binding proteins, ultimately impacting gene transcription in the nucleus. During

YAP/TAZ has a cytoplasmic localisation and shows proteosomal degradation. In the
aforementioned case of a soft ECM, cells are known to undergo apoptosis, growth ar-
rest and hMSCs are known to diﬀerentiate into adipocytes. If cells have e.g. a large
adhesive area as well as a stiﬀECM, YAP/TAZ are transferred into the nucleus of the
cell, binding to Transcriptional enhanced associate domain (TEAD), directly involved
in the regulation of cell growth and tissue homeostasis. In this speciﬁc case, it leads to

Additive 3D-printing
3D-printing, also known as rapid prototyping, layered manufacturing etc., refers to a type
of additive manufacturing, where a geometrical representation of a three dimensional
object, typically in the form of a Computer-aided design (CAD)-drawing, is created
by successive addition of material [37]. This is unlike conventional manufacturing or
machining, forging or casting processes, where material is removed to form an item,
poured into a mold or shaped [38].
The idea of additive manufacturing is not new,
but has becoming increasingly popular over the last few decades and has in most cases
become synonymous with the 3D-printing proccess fused deposition modeling (FDM),
were a polymer material is heated, and extruded onto a moving platform, to create
layers of plastic. Over the last few years, 3D-printing has become more aﬀordable and
accesible.
This is both due to the increasing availability of computers being able to
run computing intensive programs, as well as 3D-printers becoming more aﬀordable as
the technology has become more mature, which includes the increased precission and
decreased price of stepper motors.
3D-printing is often used as an approach for "Rapid prototyping", since a CAD-model is
quickly fabricated, in a full scale or a scaled model, leading to an improved or even faster
development of a product. In recent years, 3D-printing in the industry has not only been
limited to rapid prototyping, but to manufacturing as well. If a part is not fabricated in a
large enough volume for mass production to be cost-eﬀective, some companies now chose
to 3D-print certain parts, to be able to lower production costs. Boeing, an American
manufacturer of airplaines, have chosen to 3D-print parts for their Dreamliner-airplane
in titanium as a cost-eﬀective measure [39].
Several technologies for additive manufacturing exist, but the technologies FDM and
SereoLithogrAphy (SLA) will be further discussed in this section.
1.5.2

Fused Deposit Modelling
FDM, also known as Fused ﬁlament fabrication, is a 3D-printing technique where a
continious ﬁlament of a thermoplastic is heated, and extruded onto a moving platform,
depositing material to create a three-dimensional object.
A continous ﬁlament of a
thermoplastic material, e.g. Polylactid Acid (PLA), is fed through a moving extruder
head, where the plastic is heated to above its melting temperature. The heated polymer
is then deposited on a moving stage layer by layer, creating the three dimensional object.
Each axis controlled by a stepper motor, is capable of moving the stage and the extruder
very precisely under computer control, deﬁning the printing shape. The extruder head
may also be controlled to start and stop depositing material, as well as controlling the
speed of the extrusion of the material.

Stereolitography
SLA is an additive manufacturing technique, in which light causes chemical monomers
to form polymers, in a photochemical process. Commercial available SLA-printers have
a tank ﬁlled with a liquid photopolymerising resin, with a build platform lowered to the

Microﬂuidic experimental setups
Both HUVEC and caco-2 cell cultures have been proven to diﬀerentiate into in vivo
like cultures under the inﬂuence of ﬂuidic shear stress, as mentioned previously in this
chapter. A popular approach, is to grow the cell cultures in microﬂuidic PDMS-channels,
while applying ﬂow using microﬂuidic pumps, because diﬀerent parameters for the micro-
environment can be tightly regulated in such an experimental setup. As an example, the
ﬂuidic shear proﬁle can be changed, by changing the design of the PDMS-chip, while
a microﬂuidic pump tightly can regulate the exact ﬂow velocity, being as close to the
in vivo microenvironment as possible. Even peristaltic pumps can be added to such a
system, e.g. to mimic the peristaltic blood in the bloodstream, or to mimic the peristaltic
movements of a the small intestine. As mentioned previously, the cell diﬀerentiation is
highly dependent on the stiﬀness of the ECM. Since the stiﬀness of PDMS can be tightly
controlled, a PDMS-ﬂuidic chip can be tailor-made to suit the exact microenvironment
needed for the speciﬁc cell culture.
Although being well suited for in vivo like microenvironment studies, microﬂuidic setups
are seen as tedious to use, and are moreover very expensive. PDMS-chips cost between
10 and 80 USD, depending on the volume, while the prices of peristaltic and microﬂuidic
pumps range from 1000 to 2000 euros for a sigle pump. Microﬂuidic systems are also
very diﬃcult to setup and use, and one needs a thorough training to be able to use such
a system for experiments.
An aforementioned functional microﬂuidic system used for cell diﬀerentiation, is the mi-
croﬂuidic system developed by the group of Donald E. Ingber at the Harvard University.
Caco-2 cells are grown in PDMS-channels, embedded in a custom-made PDMS-chip, with
a porous permeable channel beneath the caco-2 cell cultures. Shear stress is applied by
creating a ﬂow thorugh the microﬂuidic channels using microﬂuidic pumps.
For the latter reasons, alternative approaches to microﬂuidics are being discovered, both
to lower the costs, while making it easier for the end-user to operate such an experimental
setup.

Aim of study
The aim of this study is to develop a new device for inducing cell cultures under in vivo
like ﬂuidic microenvironments. By inducing static cell cultures under in vivo like ﬂuidic
microenvironment, the goal is to diﬀerentiate the cultures into being more physiological
like in vitro, creating better models for in vitro cell studies. The aim is to develop a device
which can act as an alternative to current microﬂuidic setups, while being cheaper and
easier to setup and use. The proposed system uses relatively cheap and commercially
available 3D-printing techniques, while creating ﬂuidic shear stress using motor propelled
rotors as opposed to microﬂuidic pumps. The aim is also to develop a universal modular

General design of the system
The goal of this project was to develop a system, to induce ﬂudici ﬂuidic shear stress on
cell cultures, while avoiding the current issues with microﬂuidic systems, including cost,
ease of use and complexity.
The system was built on the foundation of a previous developed system, which the author
of this thesis made in the fall in 2018 under the supervision of Martin Dufva and Morten
Leth Jepsen.
The system is described in the report "Mechanical inducced stress on
endothelial cell cultures" [47], and is built on already existing laboratory equipment, in
this case cell culturing well plates. A simple stepping motor would then drive a set of
underlying mechanical gears, that in return would drive a number of rotors, creating a

2.2.1

Gear
In the aforementioned original design, a simple mechanical gear was used to drive the
rotors in the diﬀerent culturing well plates. Though the gears were functional, a signiﬁ-
cant wear of the gears was observed over prolonged use, preventing the gears to be used
for more than a week at a time. The simple mechanical gears also didn’t "interlock",
meaning the gears could move on the vertical axis to eachother, either spontaneously, or
due to external forces being applied when moving the system in the incubator.
To overcome this issue, a set of "herringbone" gears were designed, with a set of gears
corresponding to the distances of wells in a 12 well plate and a 24 well plate. A comparison
of the old and the new gear design can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2.1.
As a result of the change in design of the gears, the system proved to be less prone to
wear over time, as well as adding signiﬁcantly stability to the use of the system. Since
"herringbone" style gears interlock, two sets of corresponding gears had to be made.
Assembly of this design was more diﬃcult than the aforementioned simple gear design.
The simple gears were originally 3D-printed using a Formlabs Form 2 printer in the Clear
v4 resin. The new design proved to be robust enough to work when being printed on a
less accurate FDM printer, either the Original Prusa i3 Mk2.5 or the Original Prusa i3
Mk3.

Connection between gears and rotors
The mechanism mounting the rotors on the gears had previously proven to be unstable

mounting, The gears were simply mounted using a rod from the gears that would ﬁt

Design of the ﬁnal system
The ﬁnal systems were designed to work on a Nunc 12 well plate (Thermoﬁsher cat.
150628), as well as a 24 well plate (Sarsted cat. 83.3922.005). Both systems includes the
aforementioned reinforced motor lid, as well as the aforementioned "herringbone" style
gears, for optimised longevity of the system. Technical drawings of the system can be
seen in the appendix section 4.8.
The system uses a set of standard wheel bearings with an outer diameter of 11mm and
an inner diameter of 4mm. The herringbone gears were designed with a shaft, ﬁtting
tightly inside of the wheel bearings, while having a smaller rod on the end, connecting
with a corresponding hole in the rotors. The outer diameter of the rotor shaft is 5mm,
making it slightly bigger than the inner diameter of the wheel bearing. This ensures that
the rotors and the gears form a tight ﬁt around the wheel bearing.
The system was designed to be used with the Lego Mindstorm NXT system, while using
the Lego Mindstorm NXT servo motor. The Mindstorm NXT system was chosen due
to it’s ease of use, it’s well packaged components, and it’s relative low price compared
to other industrial stepper motors. The system was easily programmed using the Lego

Printing materials used in the system
Since the system had to be used with cells, in a sterile environment, speciﬁc materials
used for printing the system was chosen. The only part of the system in direct contact
with cells or cell medium, was the rotor, which subsequently was printed using SLA in
the Formlabs Dental LT clear resin, known to be a biocompatible resin used in dentistry
as well as other experiments involving human cell lines. A major drawback to the Dental
LT clear Resin is its relative high price at 399 euros per liter, and it is therefore not
suitable to print the whole system using this particular resin. Dental LT clear resin also
has signiﬁcant drawbacks compared to other plastics, in which it is substantially more
brittle, and cracks easier while being under mechanical stress, making it less suitable for
other moving parts of the system. The biocompatible material PLA was also considered
as a suitable plastic, but not chosen due to the lower resolution of available FDM-printers.
The rest of the system was not in direct contact with cells or cellular growth medium,
and did not have to be printed using material that was biocompatible to the same degree
as the Formlabs Dental LT Clear resin. The gears where under a signiﬁcant mechanical
stress while running the system, and had to be printed in a suitable material, in this case
either formlabs Clear v4 resin or Formlabs Grey Pro engineering resin was chosen. SLA-
printing technologies were preﬀerred, due to the higher printing resolution, compared to
other printing techniques. Grey Pro Engineering resin proved to be very usable, as it has
can endure higher mechanical strains than the Clear V4 resin, while in some cases being
better at printing desired smaller details in the prints.
The lids, both the lids containing the wheel bearings as well as the top lid for the
motor, were printed in PLA, due to its low cost, as well as the ease of use of a FDM
printer. These prints did not have ﬁner details, nor were needed to be as precise as
the aforementioned parts, and could be printed at the lower resolution FDM printers
oﬀers. These parts were initially printed in PETG polymer, as opposed to PLA, due to
the better mechanical properties of PETG, combined with its comparably ease of use in
3D-printing. After the initial experiements didn’t show any results, with imminent cell
deaths, the material was changed to PLA, even though the material shoudn’t have any
impact on biocompatibility since the aﬀected prints where not in direct contact with the
cells. One might assume that the cyclohexane dimethanol added to the polymer, might
make the polymer release a gas toxic to the cells.

PIV measurement of the system
Particle image velocimetry (PIV)-meaurements were made of the system, at diﬀerent
speeds using diﬀerent rotors, to better be able to describe the system and it’s ﬂuidic
proﬁle, depending on the position in the well, as well as the ﬂuidic velocity depending
on the height of the well.
PIV is method of visualising ﬂow optically, by measuring how particles ﬂow in a given
system or state. In this case, PIV-measurements were undertaken using ﬂourescent platic
beads, that were diluted in water, and measured in diﬀerent heights, at diﬀerent speeds,
at 3 distinct positins in each well. Furtermore PIV-measurements of 4 diﬀerent shapes of
rotors were made, to establish wether shape would have a signiﬁcant eﬀect of the ﬂuidic
velocity and ﬂuidic ﬂow proﬁle.
The 4 diﬀerent rotors are each shown in ﬁgure 2.4.1, and for the ease is called respectively
the "normal" rotor, "fermenter", "small" and "prop". The "normal" shape refers to
a long elongated ﬂat surface, which is expected to create the most predictable ﬂuidic
proﬁle. The "fermenter" design is based on a tank fermenter, and is used for mixinga
given substance well, without creating a large ﬂuidic velocity. The "small" shape, is a
version of the "normal", but with a signiﬁcantly smaller ﬂat surface, which is expected
to create a signiﬁcant smaller ﬂuidic velocity in the PIV-measurements.
The "prop"
design is a simple propeller based design, and is believed gives a more turbulent ﬂow,
with signiﬁcant less ﬂudic velocity, thus also a signiﬁcantly less ﬂuidic shear stress.

Chapter 3
Discussion
A new system for applying ﬂuidic shear stress on in vitro cell cultures was developed,
with the objective to diﬀerentiate static 2D-cell cultures into being more in vivo like.
The goal was to develop a novel system mainly based on 3D-printed parts, while using
commercially available stepper motors as well and wheel bearings. The developed sys-
tem was tested by applying ﬂuidic shear stress to caco-2 and HUVEC cell cultures, to
diﬀerentiate the cultures into having an in vivo like phenotype of respectively the small
intestinal wall and the endothelial walls of the blood vessels. The system was designed
to be printed on commercially available 3D-printers, with only some parts having to be
printed on more expensive biocompatible materials. The aim was to develop a system
that would be applicable to high throughput drug delivery and drug discovery studies.
3.1
|
Design and manufacturing of the experimen-
tal system
The experimental system was designed as a modular system, to be printed on standard
commercially available SLA and FDM-type 3D-printers. The system was designed to be
used with equipment already being used in the lab, making it as simple and aﬀordable
as possible. The system was also designed to be as easy to use as possible, requiring
minimal amounts of expertise in 3D-printing or microﬂuidic systems. By designing the
system around commercially available 3D-printers, the system became very aﬀordable.
The system design had to be as simple as possible, to avoid any 3D-printing issues,
enabling multiple systems to be printed and assembled at the same time.
The system was also designed to be modular, meaning that the rotors could be redesigned
and exchanged, independent of the rest of the system.
This also adds a signiﬁcant
value in itself, since diﬀerent rotors with diﬀerent properties can easily be designed and
deployed, without having to change the entire experimental setup.
When using this
system on a standard 24 well plate, 24 diﬀerent rotors could be deployed, creating an
45
==PAGE:60==
Dagnæs-Hansen
Master thesis
experimental setup with 24 diﬀerent ﬂuidic shear proﬁles. By already having invested
in a 3D-printer, as well as 3D-printing material, the turn-around time for redesigning,
printing and deploying new rotors, would eﬀectively be less than 24 hours.
The proposed system is designed to work on a standard 24 well plate, already used
in cell laboratories. Because each well can contain a separate cell culture with sepa-
rate growth/experimential conditions, the system can eﬀectively run 24 diﬀerent sub-
experiments at a time. The system does not take up much more space than a regular
24 well plate in an incubator, meaning that multiple systems can be run at the same
time. This allows upscaling without the necessity to manufacture a signiﬁcant amount
of PDMS-chips, and without having to invest in expensive microﬂuidic pumps.
In this study, a Lego Mindstorm NXT servo motor was used as a simple stepper motor to
drive the gears. The system can easily be redesigned to accommodate any other stepper
motor, depending on the preference of the operator.
The system was further optimised, due to mechanical instability, and prevention of op-
erational errors. The design of the gears were changed from conventional simple gears,
to more complex and stable herringbone-style gears. The top plate for motor mount
was further optimised, preventing unexpected mounting errors, as well as stabilising the
whole construction.
The system was tested to mount all 24 gears to create a fully functional 24 well setup.
It turned out that the array of mounting holes for the wheel bearings were not spaced
correctly, where the rightmost holes were completely misaligned with the 24 well plate.
This issue is both due to errors in the design, as well as changing tolerances of the
3D-printed plates.
The system had issues with biocompatibility due to unexpected problems of materials
not being in direct contact with the cells. Printing the top plates in PETG caused severe
biocompatibility issues, where all of the cell cultures would die imminently upon being
used with the system.
3.2
|
Diﬀerentiation of cell cultures
The system was tested with caco-2 cells as well as HUVEC cells, to show whether the
system could promote any considerable cell diﬀerentiation by applying ﬂuidic shear stress
to the cell cultures.
The aim of culturing caco-2 cells under shear stress was to achieve a culture, which had
a better in vivo like phenotype than current 2D-static cultures. Speciﬁcally, previous
studies have shown that caco-2 cell cultures grown in the right microenvironment could
express mucus, indicating a clear diﬀerentiation into cells with a goblet-like phenotype
[17]. The cultures were examined using alcian blue staining, as well as anti-muc2 antibody
stains. The results clearly indicated a mucus growth when examined using alcian blue
46
==PAGE:61==
Dagnæs-Hansen
Master thesis
staining, with a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between cultures grown under ﬂow and a static
culture. Staining the cultures for MUC2 showed a clear diﬀerence in the two scenarios,
conﬁrming the theory that caco-2 cells do in fact diﬀerentiate into a more in vivo like
culture when grown under ﬂuidic shear stress.
This diﬀerentiation was shown after
culturing the cells under shear stress for 9 days.
Previous studies have shown that HUVEC-cell cultures resembles a clear spindle-like
morphology when grown under conditions with ﬂow [20]. HUVEC-cells, cultured on top
of hydrogel casts, were induced under ﬂow 24 hours after the cells had been seeded, and
were induced under ﬂow for a total of 48 hours. Both phase-contrast images, as well
as ﬂourescence images of the F-actin ﬁlaments, showed a clear distinction between the
cultures grown under shear stress and the static control. The morphology observed in
the phase contrast image shows a clear unidirectional orientation. A similar pattern is
observed in the ﬂourescence images of the F-actin ﬁlaments as opposed to the culture
grown under static conditions. This clearly indicates a change in the phenotypic ex-
pression of the HUVEC-cells, that had previously only been achieved using microﬂuidic
setups.
Although only two cell types were shown to diﬀerentiate when cultured under shear
stress, the application of the system is believed to be universally applicable whenever a
static culture is presumed to diﬀerentiate when grown under ﬂuidic shear stress.
3.3
|
An alternative for microﬂuidic setups
Former studies of cell culture diﬀerentiation under ﬂuidic shear stress had almost exclu-
sively been done by microﬂuidic setups, using custom made PDMS-chips and expensive
microﬂuidic pumps. The advantage of these microﬂuidic systems is the tight control
over the microenvironment. This is done by controlling the ﬂow velocity through the
microﬂuidic channels, by making customised PDMS-chips with distinct designs, as well
as alter the stiﬀness of the PDMS-chiplet.
Kim et al. proposed a microﬂudic system for a gut-like microenvironment, to diﬀerentiate
caco-2 cell cultures to be more in vivo like, by expressing mucin, as well as the spontaneous
formation of villi [17]. This system used a custom-made PDMS-chip, where caco-2 cells
were grown in a channel.
Below the caco-2 cells were a porous membrane, enabling
transport studies of drugs over a layer of caco-2 cells. DeStefano et al. uses a similar
system to diﬀerentiate HUVEC-cells, by culturing the cells in microﬂuidic channels on a
PDMS-chip, while creating a ﬂow over the cell cultures with a peristaltic pump.
Both systems have shown to be functional, and to succesfully diﬀerentiate the cell cultures
in a in vivo like direction, by controlling the microenvironment. The system proposed by
Ingber et al., can be used for drug transport studies over the layer of caco-2 cells, making
it relevant for in vitro drug delivery and drug discovery studies. The disadvantage of
such a system is mainly the high cost as well as the overall complexity. As mentioned
47
==PAGE:62==
Dagnæs-Hansen
Master thesis
in section 1.6, a microﬂuidic system with one pump costs 1000-2000 euros, excluding the
cost of tubing and manufacturing of PDMS-chips. The manufacturing of PDMS-chips
has to be done in a cleanroom, or by using an external contractor. This process is both
time-consuming and expensive.
The overall turn-around time to manufacture a new
PDMS-chip is long, because the manufacturing process can be complex. A chip usually
has to go through many design-iterations before being fully functional, and certain chips
may also be tedious and expensive to manufature. Moreover, the he operator of such a
system needs a certain level of expertise to set up and carry out experiments. As soon
as more experiments are added, the costs increase many-fold, since more pumps have to
be purchased. As a result, the overall complexity of the system grows.
The system developed and examined in this study, is mainly comprised of 3D-printed
parts, manufactured using oﬀthe shelf 3D-printers and 3D-printing materials. The sys-
tem is designed to be used with equipment already applied in laboratories, to further
decrease the cost for new equipment speciﬁcaly necessary for microﬂuidic experiments.
Additionally, the system is designed to be as simple as possible, allowing a relatively
untrained user to 3D-print and assemble the system on their own. The system is also
modular, which enables implementation of rotors with speciﬁc ﬂuidic proﬁles. This makes
it possible to perform ﬂexible studies, which can still be designed to a speciﬁc experiment
and speciﬁc needs. The overall turn-around time for designing a chance, while putting it
into use, will be less than 24 hours, due to the speed of modern commercially available
3D-printers.

Future studies
The printing tolerances and the overall design of the system needs to be further optimised.
While the system would have no issues running when only 4 to 12 gears were applied,
the system showed issues when mounting a full 24 well plate. This needs to be adressed,
before the system can run on all 24 wells. The study have shown a clear diﬀerentiation
of caco-2 and HUVEC cell cultures, when used in this system. Further cell lines can be
tested, to see any phenotypical change when grown under shear stress. For the system
to be used in drug transport studies, a viable solution for a transwell system needs to be
developed.

Concluding remarks
In this study, a novel approach to microﬂuidic systems was described, and a new 3D-
printable system was proposed. The system was succesfully tested on HUVEC and caco-2
cell cultures, proving a clear phenotypic diﬀerentiation in both cases. Caco-2 cell cul-
tures grown under ﬂuidic shear-stress were proven to produce mucus, while expressing
MUC2 antibody, indicating a diﬀerentiation of the caco-2 cell culture into expressing in
vivo like goblet cells. HUVEC-cells cultured under shear-stress showed a clear change in
morphology, as the morphology turned spindle-like during the experiment. Results also
indicated an increase in cell proliferation. Cell cultures diﬀerentiated under shear-stress
have previously only proven possible using expensive and complex microﬂuidic setups.
The proposed system is mainly comprised of 3D-printed parts, while being signiﬁcantly
cheaper and easier to use. The study shows that the proposed system is a viable alter-
native to current microﬂuidic setups. In addition, it also has potential to be applied in
larger high throughput drug delivery and drug discovery studies.

3D-printing of the system
4.3.1
|
Designing and preparing the 3D-model for printing
3D-models were designed using the CAD-design program Fusion 360 by Autodesk. The
designs were made to ﬁt a 24 well multidish (Sarstedt, TC Plate 24 Well, Standard, F
Order number: 83.3922.005). The completed 3D-models were exported as STL-ﬁles, for
importing into the proprietary program for each 3D-printer.
4.3.2
|
FDM Printing
Parts of the system was printed in PLA using a FDM-type printer. The Original Prusa
MK3 printer was used, with a 0.4 mm nozzle head. Before printing, the STL-ﬁle was
imported in software slic3r, where support was added, while the proprietary g-code ﬁle
was generated and exported. The g-code ﬁle was exported to an SD-card, and the SD-
card was inserted into the printer. After the print had ﬁnished, the print was removed
from the heated surface, support structure was removed, and the printing surface was
cleaned with ethanol or IPA. The PLA-ﬁlaments used were mainly generic brand black
ﬁlament.
4.3.3
|
Resin-based SLA Printing
4.3.4
|
Preparing the 3D-printer and printing the models
Models printed using a resin-based SLA were printed using the Formlabs Form 2 SLA
printer, using a UV-light laser and photopolymer resin. The Photopolymer resin used,
was primarily of the type clear resin v4 (SKU: RS-F2-GPCL-04) and Dental LT clear
54

resin (SKU: RS-F2-DLCL-01) by Formlabs. Due to the photopolymer resin being skin
irritant, work with the Formlabs SLA had to be done using lab coats, nitrile gloves and
eye protection according to work environment regulations.
Before each print, following had to be observed: The build platform had to be cleaned
using IPA, the right resin cartridge had to be inserted according to the material that
had to be used in the print. Upon insertion of the resin-cartridge, the vent cap was
pressed open. The 3D-printer was in a well ventilated area, to ventilate the fumes from
the 3D-printing material.
4.3.5
|
Post treatment and curing
After the 3D-printing was done, the printed 3D-models were removed from the building
platform in a fume hood and cleaned using IPA before the build-platform was inserted
back to the printer. For prints using the Formlabs Clear resin v4, the prints were rinsed
in a bath of IPA using the Form Wash by formlabs for at least 40 minutes. After the IPA
had evaporated, the prints were transferred to the UV crosslinking oven, (Form Cure
by Formlabs), where the prints were cured at 60 °C for 60 minutes. For the Dental LT
clear resin, the prints were rinsed for one hour in new IPA, in clean containers. This was
done separately due to the risk of cross contamination of other non-biocompatible resins.
After rinsing the prints in IPA, the prints were transferred to the UV crosslinking oven,
(Form Cure by Formlabs), where the prints were cured at 60 °C for 120 minutes. After
curing the prints, support structure was removed, after which the print was washed in
water until the day of the experiement. This was to prevent leftover unvured residue
from contaminating the cell culture, causing biocompatibility issues.
The print was
subsequently cured for 30 minutes again in the UV-oven at 60 °C to sterilise the print
for experiments.

Since the complete assembly of case, lid, gears and rotors where used in an experiment

Before the prints were transferred to the LAF-bench, the prints where thoroughly washed
in 70% ethanol. After the transfer to the LAF-bench, the prints were placed on the surface

minutes, the orientation of the prints where ﬂipped, to ensure that all surfaces would be
sterile. After being exposed to UV-radiation, the diﬀerent parts where assembled into the
experiment assembly. A lego mindstorms NXT motor was used as a DC-motor, along
with a Lego Mindstorms NXT, acting as a controller unit. The NXT had a program
pre-transferred to the device, allowing continuous revolution of the motor and the gears,
until power was disconnected.

PIV measurements
Measurement of the PIV, to characterise the system, was done using ﬂourescent plastic
beads, at a concentration of 10,000 beads/mL. A solution of 1.5 mL with beads was
added to each well, prior to measuring the velocities using a ﬂuorescent microscope. The
rotors were then added to the system, and the system was placed in the light-proof case
around the microscope.
The motor was pre-programmed to run at either 25%, 50%, 75% or 100%. The microscope
was pre-programmed to take photos in three positions of each well, to make sure that
the measurements could be done again with the same measurements. A Z-stack of 10
pictures were taken at each position, with a z-distance of 50µm to be able to measure
the beads in the depth. The beads were excited using a laser at a wavelength of 580 nm.
Pictures were taken with an exposure time between 15 ms and 200 ms, depending on the
speed of the beads, to create a measurable trail at each measurement. This was repeated
in all three positions of all wells being examined.

Risk assessment
As mentioned in section 4.1, all HUVEC and caco-2 cells and the following experiments
were made under sterile conditions in a Class 1 Genetically modiﬁed organism (GMO)-
laboratory. The caco-2 cells themselves were not harmful to humans, and in general
not harmful to the environment. Since other experiments where made in the laboratory,
with organisms that could potentially harm the environment, all cell-related experiements
where made in a LAF-bench, while wearing disposable nitrile gloves, and using lab coats
designated to be used in the laboratory.
Diﬀerent harmful chemicals were used during this project, including PFA and methanol.
PFA is a releasing formaldehyde, and is a known carcinogen.
Methanol is known to
cause blindness if ingested, it can act as a depressant in the central nervous system
(CNS) and can be converted to the aforementioned chemical formaldehyde in the liver,
causing various metabolic disturbances.
Chloroform was also used to sterilise the gels casted for the inserts. Chloroform can cause
depression of the CNS, leading to coma and deep respiration depression.
If not handled correctly, the aforementioned chemicals could be fatal to the operator.
Assesments were made if any alternative chemical could be used, and if not, measures
were taken to minimise exposure. This included working with lab coats, wearing suitable
gloves to handle each chemical, as well as working in a fume hood with each chemical.
IPA was used to clean the Formlabs Form 2 3D-printer, washing the resin-based 3D-
prints, as well as cleaning the Prusa Mk2.5 printer. IPA is known to be skin irritant,
highly ﬂammable, as well as CNS-depressant, eventually causing ﬂushing, headache,
dizziness etc.
Formlabs Clear v5 resins, as well as the Formlabs Dental LT clear resins were used, as
mentioned in section 4.3. Before post-curing, the resins are known to cause skin irritation,
eye irritation, allergy or asthma symptoms or breathing diﬃculties if inhaled. The resins
are known to be harmful to aquatic life. The resin was always handled with extreme
care, wearing lab coats, protective glasses, nitrile gloves, and the work was performed in
a ventilated area.
Parts of the system was sterilised using UV-light in a LAF-bench. Even though the
LAF-bench was closed, the glass wall did not entirely protect the operator from the UV-
radiation. Measures were taken to avoid direct exposure of UV-light, to avoid any severe
skin burns or increased risk of skin cancer.
