Abstract
Heat pumps has shown to be an attractive way to electrify the district heating
sector in Denmark. CO2 emissions of the heating sector can be greatly reduced, as
heat pump becomes migrated with power from renewable power sources. The heat
source can have multiple origins and waste water was found to have great potential
in Denmark. Waste water treatment plants can utilize the cleaned waste water to
create a waste water heat pump for district heating. Deposit of fouling is expected to
occur in a waste water heat exchanger, which can cause operational and performance
issues. Kalundborg Utility has built an heat pump which uses waste water as a heat
source. A general wish is to gain more knowledge on the impact and detection of
fouling. This thesis investigates how fouling impacts the performance of a waste
water heat pump, and which indicators can give indicators of fouling. The impact
investigation revolves around both the thermodynamic and economics.
From Kalundborg was heat pump data used to find fouling indicators, by using
general theory about fouling and heat exchangers. A detection strategy was found
- measuring the temperatures around the fouling heat exchangers together with a
flow to calculate the overall heat transfer coefficient or the thermal fouling, was
found to indicate fouling build up in the heat exchanger. Furthermore, a thermo-
dynamic model was created to show how fouling would act on a waste water heat
pump. Model for asymptotic and linear fouling was used in order to show dynamic
differences.
The results showed that indicators such as UA value changed from 1200 kW/K to
600 kW/K during a period between CIP. The COP showed, on the other hand, close
to no changes in regards to fouling. The thermodynamic model was designed to
replicate the heat pump unit at Kalundborg Facility and reached a relative error
in COP of 1.6 % compared to the design point. The thermodynamic model was
also used to replicate the fouling seen in the data and showed good agreement
between the two. The model however had a harder time replicating the rest of the
system with the condensing pressure and compressor work deviating the most. The
thermodynamic model also showed a decrease in capacity as more fouling occurred.
The economic impact was investigated for the influences of interrupting, and shutting
off the heat pump to clean the fouling surface. The economic investigation showed
an increase in profits when interrupting production. It also showed there was an
optimum amount of cleaning but the loss of performing additional minor.
The
investigation did not consider any extension of life time for components.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Denmark’s policies on climate change mitigation is driven by national and inter-
national agenda. The UN Paris agreement set a climate target to keep the global
warming temperature increase below 2 ◦C (UN, 2015). One of sub goals of this tar-
get is reducing the total emissions by 40 % compared to 1990. The member states
of the EU made a ratification in 2016 which set goals for its individual members.
To achieve the goals at EU level, the goals for the member countries have been
distributed based on GDP. The goal for Denmark is to reduce the non-quota emis-
sions by 40 % compared to 2005 (Ministry of Climate & energy & utilities, 2020).
Denmark has additional goals set for itself with 70 % reduction by 2030 compared
to 1990 (ENS, 2020). One direction Denmark are taking to fight climate change is
to increase electricity production by renewable sources, and following close electrifi-
cation of the heating sector and transport sector. In Denmark, around 64 % of the
heat demand is supplied in district heating networks of various sizes.
District heating in Denmark is undergoing a green revolution, which is emphasized
in the Climate agreement of 22. June 2020 (Ministry of Finance, 2020).
Sector
coupling with the electrical power production is one of the driving forces of the
new agreement, and the Danish district heating sector electrification can be done by
renewable power, therefore reducing the sector’s emission of CO2. The reduction of
emitted CO2 could be 2.6 million tons yearly, which can be compared to almost a
quarter of emission from road transport in Denmark (fjernvarme, 2018).
Heat pumps are a good match for this sector electrification, as it utilizes electricity
to produce heat in an effective way. For the renewable power production, one of
the challenges is to counter large fluctuating power and to use the energy produced
effectively.
A study, (Lund and Mathiesen, 2009), showed that especially large
scale heat pumps are promising to counter the intermittency of renewable power
production.
The heat pump also needs a heat source which can be delivered at even low tempera-
tures. Sea water and waste water are some of the available heat sources in Denmark.
It is not without technical issues using such heat sources. Some issue are clogging,

scaling and fouling within the heat exchangers, resulting in downtime and issues
with not meeting the expected efficiency and heat delivery of such systems.
Existing heat pumps which utilize these types of heat sources are already in opera-
tion. One of these are the peak-load heat pump placed in Kalundborg for district
heating.
The heat pump use cleaned waste water from the nearby waste water
treatment plant.
In the present master thesis project, the heat pump from Kalundborg Utility will
be analyzed in order to see how waste water acts as a heat source. Technical is-
sues such as fouling in the waste water heat exchanger has been discovered by
Kalundborg Utility. An investigation of how to detect the fouling and how the im-
pact of fouling acting on the peak-load heat pump will be done. Moreover, a model
with fouling prediction shall be analyzed and hopefully show tendencies which can
be used for future waste water heat pump units.
1.2
Thesis statement
The overall goal for the thesis is to investigate fouling for waste water heat pumps
as the following statement describes:
How does fouling impact the performance of a waste water heat pump and which
indicators for fouling can give clear signs during operation?
The thesis desires to answer the following:
1. How does fouling impact the system and are there any indicators of fouling?
-
by analysis of data from an actual heat pump using waste water as a
heat source
- by developing a heat pump model with the implementation of theory for
fouling and using technical data from a waste water heat pump to have a
controlled environment
2. Economic impact of clean in place for waste water heat pump system.
- by using the results from the two first questions the economic impact is
investigated
The thesis investigates the thermal influences of fouling.

1.3.2
Fouling
Fouling is the gathering of unwanted particles against a wall that is of detriment of
function. The presence of a fouling layer makes it harder to absorb heat from the
heat source. The challenge of fouling has existed for a long while; since the indus-
trial revolution, issues with fouling have affected steam boilers, combustion engines,
heat exchangers, etc. This has been troublesome for the operational stability and
performance. A study showed that 2.5 % of the total amount of human made CO2
emission can be traced back to fouling (Müller-Steinhagen, Malayeri, and Watkin-
son, 2009). The same study showed that the economic impact of all heat exchanger
fouling worldwide corresponds to 0.25 % of the global gross domestic product.
Understanding how fouling forms and acts on any given system therefore has both
environmental and economic incentives. From designing to operational involvement
fouling should be of concern for an otherwise successful system. Figure 1.4 shows
how fouling can look in a heat exchanger; to the left, fouling has built up, and to
the right, the fouling deposit has been removed.

The fouling challenge lies within two main issues: Thermal conductivity and re-
duction of flow. In general, the thermal conductivity of the fouling layer is lower
than that of metals and often the thermal fouling resistance is on just one side
greater than the total thermal resistance of the metal wall (Melo, T. R. Bott, and
Bernardo, 1988). The issue with reduction of flow area is it leads to a higher pres-
sure drop. Both of these problems lead to increased energy requirements, which
results in increased operation costs. The fouling mechanics happen due to different
physical phenomenons, e.g. scaling, particulate, corrosion, chemical, solidification,
biofouling and composite fouling.
In this master thesis, focus will be on waste water, which is cleaned sewage water.
Properties of fouling in waste water and sewage water has been investigated. A
fouling composition from sewage water was tested in order to figure out the decom-
position of the fouling material (Zhang, Yang, and Sun, 2012). Drying the fouling
material at 105 ◦C showed a weight loss of 33.42 % which was mainly water. Fur-
ther burning at 520 ◦C showed a weight loss of 62.73 % mainly consisting of organic
matter. The fouling material from sewage water showed to consist of 96.15 % water
and organic matter.
Waste water is treated sewage water and will contain some amount of residue and
can therefore be expected to have the same kind of fouling material. The physical
phenomenon of organic fouling is known as biofouling.
1.3.3

Different types of fouling exist, and, in practice for waste water, most fouling occurs
because of biological material build up on the waste water side of the heat exchanger.
Biofouling is the deposition or growth of organisms on a surface. The material can
consist of microorganisms such as bacteria, algae, moulds and more.
The thickness and area of fouling film develops over time. The chemical composition
of biofilm commonly consist of 85 % to 97 % of water, which approximately gives

first two years. After the intial two year period, the peak-load solution was scheduled
to run around 901 hours a year. The peak-load plant has a design load of 10 MW.
The plant consists of 3 heat pump units with 4 reciprocating compressors in each.
The total number of compressors are 12. The refrigerant is ammonia (R717). The
heat pump was the largest of its kind in Denmark at time of completion.
After the waste water has been used as a heat source, the water is let out to the sea.
To reduce leakage risk of ammonia into sea water, an intermediate circuit between
the heat pump and waste water flow was devised. Ammonia leak precautions of this
nature was determined to be necessary. Ammonia leakage into the sea water would
have negative environmental consequences for the local area.
The intermediate

water pump in a closed loop. This precaution has an impact on the Coefficient Of
Performance (COP) (AEA, 2016). The temperature reaching the heat pump unit

heat up the district heating water. The first cycle heats up the district heating water

66 ◦C to 82 ◦C. The two cycles are combined in series to form one heat pump unit.
On the heat source side the first cycle cools the down the intermediate circuit from
12.8 ◦C to 7.8 ◦C, and the second cycle cools it further down from 7.8 ◦C to 3 ◦C.

The vapor compression cycle
In this section, the basic heat pump cycle is described.
A heat pump’s mode of operation is to transfer low temperature energy to a high
temperature reservoir or sink. To do this, the laws of thermodynamics dictates that
work has to be applied. A traditional term for this type of cycle is called the vapor
compression cycle.
A common example of the cycle consists of 4 components - a compressor, condenser,
expansion mechanism and evaporator. These are all connected as seen in figure 2.1.
A refrigerant circulates in the system between a high pressure and low pressure
stage. At the low pressure stage, the refrigerant absorbs heat in the evaporator and
begins to vaporize. At the high pressure state, the refrigerant rejects heat to the
high temperature sink, resulting in condensing of the refrigerant.

2.1.1
Evaporator
Evaporators comes in many shapes and forms; it handles the heat transfer between
the heat source and the refrigerant. With reference to the simple cycle, the refriger-
ant enters the evaporator as a vapor-liquid mixture and is evaporating isothermally
as it absorbs energy from the energy source. When the refrigerant reaches the dew
point curve, the quality is x = 1, heating the refrigerant further superheats the re-
frigerant. Superheating increases cooling capacity of the evaporator and the specific
volume at the outlet. Superheating the refrigerant also helps to ensure no vapor
droplets are entering the inlet of the compressor. This describes a Direct Expansion
(DX) heat exchanger. Another very common type used is a flooded evaporator.
A flooded evaporator, as seen on figure 2.3, utilizes gravity to separate the vapor-
liquid mixture. The outlet from the expansion valve connects to a vessel, which
works as a liquid and vapor separator. The heat exchanger is placed beneath the
separator and filled with refrigerant.
As heat is absorbed, the refrigerant boils,
and because of the difference in density, is transported to the separator. From the
separator, the compressor has its suction line connected such that 100 % gaseous
refrigerant enters the compressor.

2.1.5
Refrigerant
Depending on the application, different refrigerants are more suitable than others.
Environmental and climate related issues also need to be taken into account when
choosing a refrigerant.
Global warming potential (GWP) and Ozone Depletion
Potential (ODP) are factors to consider.
GWP is an expression for how the environmental impact of the refrigerant has on
global warming the next 100 years. The factor is relative to 1 kg of CO2. The GWP
number therefore needs to be as low as possible. Danish legislation dictates that
a system with over 10 kg of refrigerant should use a refrigerant with a GWP value
under 5 (Kølemidler.dk, 2020).
ODP express how the refrigerant has an impact on ozone layer depletion. The factor
is an relative value to the refrigerant Freon R11, which is set to 1.
Ammonia (R717) is a natural inorganic refrigerant and has GWP and ODP of zero.
Safety precautions should be in place when handling R717 due to its toxicity and
flammability. The smell of R717 is quite distinguishable even at low concentrations,
but detectors and emergency ventilation for leakage should be in place in any room
where ammonia is used.
The Ammonia’s isentropic exponent, κ, is around 1.3, which causes high discharge
temperatures after compression of the gas. Other alternatives like R1234yf has a
isentropic exponent of 1.01. The isentropic exponent results in an increase of work

the discharge channel from the low pressure compressor, to the suction line of the
high stage compressor, the outlet of the high-stage valve, and the inlet of the low-

PI-controllers
For operation in off design temperatures, pressures and the like are controlled by the
Industrial Control System (ICS) (Danish: SRO) to maintain orderly operation. To
be able to control the heat pump in a simulation model, the controls also need to be
determined. The aim with controllers are to achieve set targets during variations in
inputs. In heat pump systems components such as compressors, valves and pumps
are managed by controllers. A system should have certain degrees of freedom when
in operation in order to control the variation in operation inputs.
For a simple
refrigeration cycle, five degrees of freedom is found to work well from a control and
operational point of view (Jensen and Skogestad, 2007). For two-stage system the
degrees of freedom goes to 7 which corresponds to the added valve and compressor.
Methods for control of heat pumps can be split into three different classifications:
Conventional, advanced controls and intelligent controls (Goyal, Staedter, and Garimella,
2019). In order to ensure the simplicity of the controls for the heat pump system,
one of the most commonly used controls is PI feedback control. The PI-controller
works as a feedback loop having two control terms: proportional and integral. The
proportional term takes the error between the set point and the current measure-
ment. The proportional term is controlled by the gain factor and the error. The
integral term takes the error between the set point and the measured value and in-
tegrates it over time. If any residual error is left after the proportional control, the
integral term seeks to reduce the residual error to zero. Figure 2.8 shows a feedback
control loop, where the set value for y inputs to an error, where the PI controller

calculated as in equation (2.21).

2.4
Detection of fouling
Build up of fouling can have different impacts on a given system. In the food industry
it is an issue for bacteria interfering with the food process. For piping, fouling can
severely decrease the lifetime and performance of the pipes, as well as cause clogging.
The dairy industry uses various heat treatment operations for pasteurization and
sterilization. Fouling occurs in the heat exchangers of such heat treatments due to
solid milk depositions on inner surface.
Indicators and impact of fouling in large-scale waste water heat pumps
29
==PAGE:42==
For waste water heat pumps, fouling may impact different variables such as the
performance, pinch temperatures, capacity, cause flow restrictions etc. Methods for
detecting fouling can be a valuable tool for system operation of any system with a
fouling build up. Determining indicators of fouling can be difficult due to varying
operation conditions. Indicators of fouling can be split into two categories: direct
and indirect (Prakash, Datta, and Deeth, 2005).
Direct indicator would involve disassembling the fouled component and then weigh-
ing it before and after cleaning. The composition of the fouling mass could also
be analyzed in order to determine the thermal resistance of the material and the
origin of the foulant. This method for determining fouling would be difficult and
time consuming for a system. Changes to properties of the delivered waste water
may also result in this indication to be incorrect.
Indirect indicators are measurements of parameters in the system during operation.
Sensors and transmitters can be used to measure differences in pressure, temperature
and flow rate. Heat flux sensors can also be used via ultrasonic transducer. The
sensor can be used for pipes and other easy access areas, but is not relevant for plate
heat exchangers (Schmidt et al., 2007).
Monitoring the heat pump while in operation will use the indirect indicators in order
to detect fouling. This will allow for considerations regarding initialization of CIP
for performance optimization.
Monitoring the pressure drop for a heat exchanger can be done by measuring the
pressure before and after the heat exchanger. The principle is that the fouled deposit
will affect the passage of flow within the channels. If the flow rate is kept constant, it
is expected that an increase in pressure will occur. Monitoring the pressure is fairly
important, as it gives forewarning of excessive pressures on the components, which
can cause damage or destruction. Monitoring pressures can also give an indication
of leakages and faulty pumps. Fouling can be detected by using the pressure drop,
but it has its limitations. Measurements of pressure is relatively insensitive to small
changes in the system. Fouling would be seen as fairly small changes for the overall
pressure drop. Therefore, it requires accurate measurements, which requires a good
and often serviced calibration (Prakash, Datta, and Deeth, 2005) (Niras, 2020).
Measuring the temperature before and after the heat exchanger is another way to
detect the occurrence of fouling deposits. The fouling layer would result in a ther-
mal resistance that would reduce the heat transfer efficiency of the heat exchanger.
This would result in a change of pinch temperature and, therefore, a viable way of
detecting fouling. In order to keep the same heating capacity, the heat source needs
to be a higher inlet temperature. Otherwise the heat transfer will not be as intended.
Calculating the logarithmic mean temperature difference (LMTD) equation (2.17),
can be used as a way of detecting fouling (Prakash, Datta, and Deeth, 2005).
Monitoring the overall heat transfer coefficient, U (or UA) equation (2.18), can also
be used as an indicator for fouling, since an added resistance will cause a decrease in
the coefficient. Using the heat transfer coefficient will eliminate the issue of different
operation temperatures, which can be expected in waste water (Prakash, Datta,
and Deeth, 2005). Furthermore, the monitored heat transfer coefficient has a direct

Chapter 4
Modelling
In this chapter the dynamic heat pump model and its elements are described. The
heat pump model is described in its entirety, which includes control methods and
determination of these.
4.1
Numerical heat pump model
The heat pump model is developed in Dymola. Dymola is based on the Modellica
programming language, which is an open source language. Modellica is an object
oriented programming language in which each component is governed by a set of
equations.
Dymola offers an environment for building each component indepen-
dently and afterwards to drag and drop components to build complex systems. The
third party company, TLK-Thermo GmbH makes a component based library for
Dymola, named TIL.
TIL can be used for stationary and transient simulations of thermodynamic sys-
tems. Configurations of existing components such as heat exchangers, compressors,
valves and so on can be modified. Fouling can therefore be implemented into the
thermodynamic model and simulated.
Figure 4.1a illustrates a vapor compression cycle with its four main components in
the model. Each component has its own environment in which heat transfer models,
geometry etc. are called from their respective libraries as seen in figure 4.1b. In the
outer layer the fluid properties are called and off-design controls are implemented.
In the outer layer ports/connections, handling information transmission such as heat
and mass transfer between components are also present.
All components in Dymola are modelled by using dynamic mass and energy balances.
Momentum balances are applied by using steady state equations.

4.1.1
Heat exchangers
The heat exchangers in the heat pump cycle in Kalundborg are of the shell and
disc type. The condenser and evaporator in this thesis are modelled as chevron
plate heat exchangers (PHE). The heat exchangers are 1-d discretized staggered
grid, parallel flow heat exchangers where one flow is in the opposite direction. The
heat exchangers are divided into 15 equal size volumes for the condensers and 10 for
the evaporators. More were chosen for the condenser because of the larger difference
in heat transfer rate of gas compared to condensation. The number of plates is taken
from the technical description of the plant, and the size is compared to a disc with
the same cross-sectional area. The corrugated pattern for the heat exchangers are
determined from targeting key figures in the design point; heat transfer rates and
inlet and outlet temperatures in that order.
The correlations for heat transfer coefficient are based on Holger-Martin for 1 phase
flow (Martin, 1996). For condensation the Yan correlation (Shi et al., 2010) is used.
For evaporation Ayubs correlation is used (Ayub et al., 2019).
The intermediate circuit heat exchangers are made by Sondex and is a part of their
series called Free Flow, which are plate heat exchangers with another corrugation
pattern suited for fouling operation and with more spacing than usual (Sondex,
2020).
The heat exchanger model is however based on the same chevron plate
as the evaporators and condensers. In appendix C, the geometry of all the heat
exchangers can be found.
Flooded evaporator
Flooded evaporators are in industrial settings usually constructed such that a density
difference together with gravity drives the flow across the heat surface area. The
modelling of a thermosyphon is out of scope for this thesis. As a simplification,
an auxiliary pump is used to circulate the liquid refrigerant. This is also seen in
some systems (SWEP, 2019). The circulating flow was determined from having an
estimated circulation number between 1.3 and 1.5 (SWEP, 2019), which corresponds
to a quality at the outlet of the evaporator of ≈0.65 to 0.77. For off design operation,
the auxiliary pump was set to have a fixed pressure increase to try and replicate the
thermosyphon.
Valve
The thermal expansion valve is modelled as an Orifice valve, which is a simple valve.
It determines an effective flow area which the refrigerant passes. A PI-controller is
connected controlling the opening degree of the valve. The PI-controller is described
in section 2.2. For off-design the valves are controlled by subcooling of 0.2 K to make
sure it is liquid to replicate the real method.
4.1.2
Implementation of fouling
Fouling in a heat exchanger has an impact on its performance in various ways.
Thermal resistance, pressure drop and flow.
In this thesis, the main focus has been on the thermal resistance of fouling, which
results in a simplification of fouling as an property.
The implementation of the
fouling resistance was done as a conduction model. The conduction model should
be seen as an addition to the wall resistance in the model. It was assumed that the

4.1.3
Assumptions
The model had some simplification made that could have minor impact on the
system performance. Assumptions of efficiency for pumps was set to 1. The district
heating pump and waste water pump can be seen as part of the system boundaries.
Data from Kalundborg Utility did not indicate any calculated power consumption
of these pumps. The pump in the intermediate circuit can be seen as a circulation
pump and operates in a closed loop.
The separator and intercooler were both modelled as an ideal separator, meaning
equilibrium between the gas phase and liquid phase are uniform in pressure and
temperature within the separator.
It is assumed that the influence of amount of refrigerant migration has minor impact
on the dynamic behavior for the simulation model. The PI-controllers design was
found to have a strong influence on the dynamic behavior for model (Meesenburg
et al., 2019).

5.3.7
Summary of detection parameters
Finding a way to be certain fouling has occurred in a heat exchanger can be difficult
to do without taking it apart. Variations in operational parameters such as temper-
atures, properties of the foulant, pressure, load, flows and so forth have a role in the
fouling build up in a given system. The geometry of the heat exchanger also has
an impact on the fouling build up behavior as earlier described. Kalundborg Utility
used pressure difference and pinch temperatures as an indicator for fouling (AEA,
2016), which was found to be valid, but better alternatives exist, as discovered. Ta-
ble 5.2 summarizes the advantages and disadvantages of each indicator investigated.
It was found that some indicators gave a more clear sign of fouling build up than
others. COP did not show significant changes in regard to fouling - from a cleaned
to a fouled heat exchanger, COP changes were only of the magnitude 0.2, whereas
calculating LMTD, UA or Rf showed clear changes in between periods of CIP.
For Kalundborg Utility, the recommendation is therefore to implement calculations
of LMTD, UA and Rf into the monitoring system. Using pinch temperature as they
do now can lead to faulty fouling analysis and result in a performed CIP that is not
needed or too late. They should continue to monitor pressure difference, as it can
be vital in detection of clogging or other damaging incidents.

The low stage compressor’s speed will have to compensate for the decreased evapo-
ration temperature. This can be seen by looking at the low stage compressors speed.
Figure 5.20a shows the speed in RPM of both the low stage compressors (C01 and
C03) and high stage compressors (C02 and C04).
Figure 5.20b shows the work needed for each compressor to overcome the added
fouling resistance.
C01 runs with maximum RPM (1800) but the needed work
decreases. This is due to the lowered evaporation temperature increasing the density
of the refrigerant, resulting in a lower mass flow and therefore slightly less work. C03
increased in speed compared to the design point, and the compressor work increases
by doing so. The compressor, C03, reaches maximum speed whereas it follows the
same pattern of decreased work. The low stage compressor can therefore, to some
extent, compensate for the added thermal fouling resistance in the system.
The design of the compressors can be adjusted to run beyond the otherwise non-
fouled design point. Increasing the speed is found to be a way to compensate for
the increased inlet temperature in a heat pump system. The solution of having
compressors compensate for the fouling is not optimal, but if capacity control is a
priority for the system, it could be a solution. The COP will be affected, as the
condensers deliver less heat and the compressor work increases. This compensation
only happens by the low stage compressors, which might be due to how the high
stage compressors are controlled.
The high stage compressors are controlled by the intermediate pressure that results
in both speed and work decreases as the fouling resistance increases. The control
set point of the high stage compressors are not changed in the simulation or at
Kalundborg Utility. The control setup and the reduced flow from the low stage
compressors result in less work for the high stage compressors. A suggestion would
be to use an adjustable set point for the intermediate pressure. This way the added
work needed to overcome fouling could be shared between both low and high stage
compressors.

Waste water heat pump without IMC
Similar systems to the modelled heat pump exist. The company, HOFOR A/S, has
as part of a development project, named SVAF, built a waste water heat pump
without an intermediate circuit. The system is replicated in the model by removing
the intermediate circuit and applying the same asymptotic fouling build up model
to each evaporator. A study, (Fryer et al., 1988), about fouling in heat exchanger
networks used the same fouling model for each heat exchanger when they are in
series. It is therefore assumed in the model that each evaporator will experience the
same amount of fouling. The system was run with the same period of 42 days and
an interval of 1 hour.
Figure 5.25 shows the compressors speed and work. The compressors run initially at
lower speed as the inlet temperature is higher. This is due to the missing intermedi-
ate circuit, which caused a higher temperature difference between the waste water
and evaporators. The low stage compressors increase its speed when fouling begins
to be present.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The steady state of the thermodynamic model at the design point, showed good
agreement with the technical data available. The largest deviations were seen in
the compressors seen in table 5.1. This was due to the compressor polynomials for
the real compressors were not available for direct use. The compressor polynomials
were instead produced from a simulation tool available from the manufacturer by
performing an ordinary least squares regression of the tool’s result. The output from
the simulation tool was however an adiabatic process resulting in no polynomial for
the heat loss. The deviation was in relative terms 7 % to 8 %.
The validation of the model was done with data from Kalundborg Utility. Certain
parts of the model showed good agreement, especially the fouling model. The com-
pressors however showed that simplifications such as heat loss, could be implemented
to improve the model. The model validation does leave a desire for improvement,
but can show trends in regards to the focus area of the thesis. The fouling found in
the data analysis was used as an input to the model, which showed to behave as in
the data. The temperature surrounding the waste water showed that the difference
between the real free flow heat exchanger were small. The model could then be used
to show the system impact of fouling.
The data analysis was done by resampling data and making some assumptions that
might have an impact on the model, and therefore the results. Specific heat capacity
of water was set to 4.19 kJ/kgC, which was used for waste water, deionized water and
district heating waster. The simplification may have an influence on the calculated
capacities in the system. The data also have unexplained spikes, that might influence
the system. The spikes may come from externalities to the system. It was however
still possible to see the thermal trends caused in the system. The assumption of
waste water having the same properties as regular water may be of concern and is
an unknown factor for the results.
The controls of the system were also difficult to understand, as some data set points
left some information to be desired. Set points for the low stage compressors was
challenging to analyze as fluctuations in the set point was present, and not easy
to understand. The set points were chosen based on the data and then set to be
constant. The set point for the first low stage compressor had a lower limit of 3 ◦C

Another simplification of the model was to choose only one heat exchanger to repli-

for better control of subcooling or quality. In reality is a condenser not split into
thee units, but in order to simulate what happens within the condenser is easier
with a desuperheater, condenser and subcooler. The model it self was fairly com-
plex and required a large amount of computational power. The model consist of
around 60,000 equations at its simplest form. Furthermore was it already an issue
of running out of computational power, and it was therefore decided to keep just
one condenser unit per cycle in the model.
The waste water heat exchanger was modelled as an Chevron plate heat exchanger,
which does not correspond with the heat exchanger at Kalundborg Utility, which
was a Free Flow plate heat exchanger. It was assumed to be sufficient with using
correlations for chevron plate heat exchanger for the model.
This may have an
impact on the results and validation of the model. Additionally the calculated heat
transfer area from the model was used in the data processing, as the heat transfer
area was not available elsewhere.
The thesis focus only on thermal resistance aspect of fouling, which may be too
simplified. Fouling impacts both pressure and flow. The data analysis could there-
fore have included even more details of how pressures impacts the system. It may
explain some of the fluctuations in the measured data, that is otherwise not seen in
the model.
The two models created to show fouling build up, asymptotic and linear, was done
by approximations of parameters from data. The parameter, β, was used to show
trends in asymptotic fouling behavior and was calculated with a lower asymptotic
steady state fouling resistance, Rf∞. A SVAF configuration of the model was also
simulated with a fouling model, which may be difficult to evaluate as the evaporators
may not be designed with fouling considerations.
The economics surrounding CIP during operation showed to mostly dependent on
the sell price of district heating and showed that the gain for the first couple of CIPs
are large even with low capacity loss where after the benefit of additional converges
slowly toward clean operation. The economics does not include the extended life
time or the extended time between off-line cleaning. Adding these benefits to CIP
even greater differences between cleaning and not cleaning a fouling system would
be seen.
The finding of the thesis may be applicable for other low temperature heat sources
such as sea water heat pumps. Especially the detection of fouling should be consid-
ered used.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
A large scale heat pump using waste water as heat source was investigated, regarding
the influences and detection of fouling. The data analysis of the heat pump unit
at Kalundborg Utility showed indicators and impact of fouling. Of the detection
methods investigated the UA and fouling resistance showed the best promise, while
using pressure difference as an indicator showed less promise. The COP was seen
to be the least promising for detection of fouling. Operational differences influenced
the COP too much. On the day of a CIP only a 0.2 difference was seen. The UA
value showed differences throughout, but was very clear when looking at a period
between two CIP. For a period the UA value changed from 1200 kW/K to 600 kW/K
with 1200 kW/K being just after a CIP and 600 kW/K just before. The pressure
in the same period fluctuated between 1 bar and 1.5 bar having increased pressure
difference for a longer time, which drops just before CIP. For the day of the CIP the
pressure difference fluctuates between 1.3 bar and 1.4 bar before CIP and shows a
small drop in pressure difference afterwards with constant fluctuations to previous
CIP levels.
The thermodynamic model was designed to replicate the heat pump at Kalundborg
Utility using real components. The thermodynamic model was used to investigate
fouling impact on a heat pump system. At the design point, the relative error of the
system COP was 1.6 %. Compressor work had the largest relative error ranging from
1.18 % to 8.05 %. Validation showed some deviations from the received data from
Kalundborg Utility. The normalized root mean square error for the heat transfer
rate in the waste water heat exchanger was 2.05 %. The model was then simulated
with using an asymptotic and a linear fouling model. The result showed changes in
heat capacity and COP, both decreasing. A linear trend between the heat capacity
and the thermal fouling resistance was a result of the model. The heat pump was
responded by a lowered evaporation temperature, which initially caused low stage
compressors to increase in speed and work. The waste water heat exchanger reduced
in UA value, and LMTD was increased to try to achieve the same overall heat
transfer.
This caused the intermediate circuit to have lower heat transfer from
the waste water. Furthermore, the heat pump was configured to run without an
intermediate circuit, which showed that the impact is greater when an evaporator
are influenced by waste water fouling.
