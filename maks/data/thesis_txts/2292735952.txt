Abstract
Master thesis
Rail passenger route choice modelling at European level
by Alejandro Gómez Zarza
and supervised by
Otto Anker Nielsen,
Marie Karen Anderson and
Thomas Ross Pedersen.
This thesis presents a route-choice model of all major rail passenger lines in Europe.
The model is an integral part of the TRANS-TOOLS 3 project. The goal of the thesis work
was to provide a model, where trafﬁc volumes could be obtained in a simple, yet realistic
way, in the current network or in an enlargement of it. One of the main characteristics
of the data was the lack of timetables and frequencies, which implied that both the
transfers between trains and the average running speed had to be assumed. Throughout
the literature there are few examples of rail models with this sparse data, so the main
challenge of the thesis work has been to make the most of the limited available data.
The model returns acceptable results, that ﬁt better the higher the speed (above 200
km/h), and the longer the distance (above 250 km) of the analysed line. This model
could be useful as the very ﬁrst step in the long process of planning a new railway line
in Europe.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The construction of new passengers long-distance railways, mainly high speed, has be-
come popular in the last decades all around the world. New high-speed lines have been
or are being constructed in countries such as China, Japan, France, Spain, Germany,
Italy and also Denmark. The advantages of this mode of transport are many, like the
high speed, the sustainability, the energy efﬁciency and the comfortability. However,
it is a very expensive infrastructure to construct, due to the high turning radius in the
geometric design, in order to keep a high speed along the whole line. This causes that
many tunnels and viaducts have to be built, mostly in regions with a curvy orography.
Therefore, a good trafﬁc forecast prior to the construction is extremely important.
Sometimes, politicians, political parties, governments or associations propose the con-
struction of a new high speed line, and the ﬁrst issues that everybody wonders about
are whether it is needed, whether it is worth it, how many people will use it, etc. It is a
peculiar matter, because people usually have a very clear opinion about it, even though
nobody actually has any objective information.
The goal of this thesis is to make a rail passenger route-choice model that provides
a fast, cheap and approximate answer to those questions, and helps to be a decision-
making tool to either go forward with the trafﬁc forecasts studies for the project, or to
discard it. It pretends to be the ﬁrst step in the long trafﬁc forecast process, prior to the
construction. This route-choice model may be applied in almost any region in Europe
and forms part of the European project Trans-Tools 3 (‘TT3’).
The thesis is divided in the following chapters: the literature review; a chapter intro-
ducing all the data used in the model; a chapter describing the model itself, including
the assumptions, the validation and the results; an application of the model in a real

Chapter 2
Literature review
The TRANS-TOOLS 3 project ("TOOLS for TRansport forecasting ANd Scenario test-
ing") is a transport model covering the whole Europe for both passengers and freight
transport and for all the modes. TRANS-TOOLS 3 is an ongoing project funded by the
European Commission Joint Research Centre’s Institute for Prospective Technological
Studies (IPTS) and the Directorate-General DG TREN. The partners in this project are
a combination of universities, companies and institutes all across Europe. The coordi-
nator of the project is the Technical University of Denmark. (JRC IPTS, 2008)
TRANS-TOOLS 3 is destined to be a validated, well documented and user-friendly model
that provides policy makers with a tool for assessing and developing better transport poli-
cies. (TRANSTOOLS3, 2011)
TRANS-TOOLS 3 is divided into ﬁve different networks. These are the road network,
the rail-passenger network, the air network, the rail-freight network and the waterways
network. The rail-passenger network, that is what this thesis deals with, is character-
ized by Hansen (2011) in the following way:
• Trips are segmented by purpose.
• The assignment is stochastic-based.
• There are no data regarding either timetables or frequencies.
• The high speed rail is not considered a separate mode, although different costs
apply.
• Air access/egress trips by rail are added to rail passenger assignment.
3
==PAGE:14==
Chapter 2. Literature review
4
This model is signiﬁcantly different than the typical public transport model, described
by Brems (1999). There, the author divides a trip in access and egress times, in-vehicle
time and waiting and transfer times. In-vehicle, access and egress times are repre-
sented in the network by the links (or "arcs"), while the waiting and transfer times are
represented by the nodes. The waiting and transfer times are calculated directly from
schedule data, and this is why this thesis model becomes atypical.
Other important aspects that are usually modelled are described by Brems (1999) as
well, such as the passengers arrival to stations (either random or timed), the boarding
strategy (that depends on the available information for the passengers), the common
line problem (that happens when two or more different public transport services run in
parallel along the same links) and the hidden waiting time (the time to be waited at the
end of the trip for passengers with a ﬁxed arrival time due to the restriction of public
transport services).
Nielsen (2000) pointed out that many models lack timetables, containing only line-
frequencies, due to the extensive work needed in coding and in modelling. However,
the author also says that timetables are not practical at all in the case of strategic fore-
casts, as the TRANS-TOOLS 3 models are, because each plan-proposal would have its
own timetable and should be changed under examination. These are the two main rea-
sons why the TRANS-TOOLS 3 rail-passenger model does not have any schedule infor-
mation.
Nielsen (2000) also justiﬁes the use of stochasticity in the public transport assignments
based on the facts that the users do not have full knowledge of the network, that travel
times vary from day-to-day, and that different people have different preferences or they
change routes just for variation.

Chapter 3
The data
This chapter deals with all the different kinds of data that take part in the model, mainly
divided in the existing data at the beginning of the thesis and the new data required
for the model. The main software used is ArcGIS, although combined with Microsoft
Access.
3.1
Existing data
The base of the modelling is the data from the ETIS+ project, which are used in TRANS-
TOOLS 3. The different elements that form part of the database are:
• TRANS-TOOLS 3 zoning system, formed by the zones themselves and their cen-
troids.
• Railway network, formed by links and nodes. The stations are nodes, although
not all the nodes are stations.
• TRANS-TOOLS 3 parameters for rail assignment.
• TRANS-TOOLS 3 rail trip matrix.
The area covered is the whole of Europe. However some parts are incomplete (with-
out stations), such as Russia, Belarus, Albania and some islands in the Mediterranean
(Sardinia, Corsica and Mallorca).

3.2
Edits and new data
Several errors were found in the database and they needed to be solved. Some of these
errors did not let the model run if they were not ﬁxed.
One of the errors was that 64 links (out of 9 909) did not have any ID-number assigned.
The ID-number is the attribute that identiﬁes each link by a unique code. Those links
were given new IDs from the automatic attribute OBJECTID, to make sure the new IDs
are unique. They were located in Ukraine, Ireland and the United Kingdom. The list of
these IDs may be found in table B.1, in appendix B.

correct. All stations must be coincident with the endpoint(s) of the link(s), because
the model would not work otherwise. This also happened in Ukraine, Ireland and the
United Kingdom, plus Moldova and Russia. In order to solve this, these links were split
in the stations they were crossing and renamed adding an extra number at the end of
the previous ID. The list of these IDs may be found in table B.2, in appendix B.

3.2.2
Generation of connectors
The database did not have connectors. The connectors are a line feature class that con-
nects the centroids with the stations. This is a key part of the model because it connects
the point where trafﬁc is generated (i.e., the centroid) with the network (through the
stations). Therefore, the connectors had to be made.
The basis for creating the connectors is that all the centroids must have, at least, one
connector, even in the case that there is not any station in its zone. Like this, all the
zones will generate trafﬁc to the network, and not only the zones with stations on them.
==PAGE:20==

Also, every station is connected to the centroid of the zone where the station is, even
if there is a closer centroid from a neighbouring zone.
The connectors were given the ID of the station that it is connecting. This is because
almost all the stations have only one connector. Only in the case when a station has
more than one connector (from a neighbouring zone without stations), the connector
ID is the station ID, plus one more digit at the end.

The creation of the connectors was performed as stated below (see ﬁgure 3.6 to see this
as a ﬂowchart):
1. Add X and Y coordinates to the centroids and the stations, with the tool ‘Add
XY’. The Stations are obtained from the nodes with the attributes TypeOfPassen-
gerService=1 or TypeOfPassengerService=2 (see section 3.3 for the entire list of
attributes).
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 3. The data
11
2. Make a new table (‘Table1’) from the joining (‘based on the spatial location’) of
Stations with the Zones. The purpose of this joining is that the stations are con-
nected to the centroid of the zone where they are.
3. Join the previously created table with the Centroids, using the common attribute
ZoneID. The new table is called ‘Table2’.
4. Now, ‘Table2’ has each station and its correspondent centroid in the same row.
Also, the XY coordinates of both centroids and stations are in this table (created
in step 1). The last step is to make a line between each station and its centroid
with the tool ‘XY to line’. The output of this is the Connectors feature class.
5. However, more connectors have to be created because there are zones without
any station. These zones’ centroids have to be connected to some station outside
the zone, because every zone generates trafﬁc, even if there is not a station inside.
Therefore, a table containing the centroids without connector has to be created.
This is done by making a topology with the rule ‘Centroids must be covered by end-
point of Connectors’. The errors from this topology form the “centroids without
connector" table needed (‘Table3’).
6. These centroids have to be connected to the closest station. For that, the tool
‘Near’ is used, with ‘Table3’ and Stations as input.
7. Now, ‘Table3’ has the centroids without connector and the correspondent station
together. With the tool ‘XY to line’, the connectors are created.
8. Finally, the two connector tables (from steps 4 and 7) are appended each other to
the deﬁnitive ‘Connectors’ feature class.

3.2.3
Generation of turns
Another element created for this model was the turns. A turn is a line feature class with
length attribute 0 that connects two intersecting links. The purpose of a turn is to add
an extra time to the travellers that go through that intersection. In this model, that
extra time represents the transfer time, i.e. the time that a passenger needs to change
from a train to another.
This subsection deals with the process for the generation of the turns. The transfer
time is dealt in subsection 4.1.1.1.
This process is done with the Turn Generator tool, from the ArcGIS extension Trafﬁc
Analyst (Rapidis). The full procedure is the following:
1. Create a new network dataset with the four feature classes (links, nodes, centroids
and connectors) and click ‘Yes’ on the question Would you like to model turns?.
2. Run the tool Create turn feature class.
3. Run the tool Update by geometry, so the topology sets in the network dataset.
4. One of the needed inputs of the Turn Generator tool is a feature class called Inter-
section Polygons, which has to be created. This consists of polygons in which there
is one station in each. It does not matter either the size or the shape, only that
there is one station inside each polygon. The Turn Generator tool creates turns
between all the ingoing and outgoing edges of the polygons [Rapidis (n.d.)]. The
polygons created for this model had a circular shape, with a radius of 300 m (see
ﬁgure 3.7). Polygons may be generated with the tool Buffer.

Chapter 4
Construction of the model
This chapter covers the construction of the route-choice model, including also the as-
sumptions, the validation and the results.
4.1
Model description
This section develops all the aspects of the model itself and its construction process.
4.1.1
Assumptions
The main tool that is used to run this model is Trafﬁc Analyst’s Road Trafﬁc Assignment,
that, despite its name, is just a regular network assignment tool. Another tool called
Public Assignment exists, but it cannot be used for this model because it needs schedule
data. However, several assumptions were taken to make this rail model realistic using
the Road Trafﬁc Assignment tool. These assumptions are divided in three main groups:
assumptions for the transfers, assumptions for the free speed and assumptions in the
connectors.
4.1.1.1
Assumptions for the transfers
The biggest difference between the road models and the public transport models is the
transfers that occur between lines and/or modes. In this model, even with the lack
of timetables, transfers cannot be ignored. The rail model cannot be performed as a
road model, like if trains could freely choose their way as they want. The transfers are
represented in the network by the turns feature class.
23
==PAGE:34==
Chapter 4. Construction of the model
24
The considered aspects were (1) where a transfer occurs and (2) how long that transfer
takes.
(1) Where a transfer occurs
In real life, passengers have to do a transfer from a train to another train when they
change from a line to another line. This is very easy to see in a network whose lines
are clearly differentiated among them, like in a typical metro system. However, long-
distance rail lines are not so easy to differentiate. Usually, many lines run in parallel
along the same tracks, or sometimes there are not even lines deﬁned.
However, in this model, we do not even know the long-distance lines, so a realistic
method to guess the lines had to be found, and with them be able to simulate the trans-
fers. This method was:
A transfer occurs in the station where there is a big difference between the speed limits of
the incoming link and the outgoing link.
In this way, it is assumed that lines are classiﬁed according to the speed limit, and
therefore, a transfer occurs when a passenger changes from a line with a speed limit to
another with a different speed limit.
Of course, this does not always occur in reality, but it is true that it often does. In the
following lines, an example can be found that shows that this assumption is realistic.
Before that, it is needed to deﬁne what a big difference exactly is, as stated in the above
statement. After the validation procedure, the deﬁnition of big difference was taken as
a ratio of 1.39 (section 4.2). Analytically, it looks like:

4.1.1.2
Assumptions for the free speed
Rail links are modelled as road links, due to the use of the Road Trafﬁc Assignment, as
already mentioned. Again, some considerations were taken into account, in order to
make a realistic model.
The differences between a pure road assignment and this model assignment are mainly
that there cannot be congestion in rail, and that the average speed is not the free speed.
Congestion is a very important aspect to be modelled in road assignments, with spe-
ciﬁc attributes for it, and usually a key part of the results and discussion. However, in
railways, congestion is something impossible to occur, for obvious reasons, and there-
fore there are not attributes regarding congestion aspects. This model only calculates
the volume of passengers in each link, and then the next step would be to calculate the
frequency of trains needed for such volume of passengers.

4.1.1.3
Assumptions in the connectors
The connectors represent the trips from the departure point until the closest train sta-
tion. These trips may be done by car, bus, local train, etc. The free speed to be used in
the model is 30 km/h, representing the average of these possible trips. The trip along
the connectors is included in the model assignment.
4.1.2
The model itself
The route-choice model is performed executing the Road Trafﬁc Assignment tool by
the Danish company Rapidis. Let’s imagine a single traveller, and how this tool would
model his/her trip.
This person wants to travel from town A to town B. These two towns are several hundred
kilometres away. The main mean of transport is the long-distance train, even though
he/she has to use an alternative transport in the very beginning and in the end of the
trip, because the long-distance trains do not arrive to either the town A or B.
So ﬁrstly, he/she leaves town A towards the closest long-distance-trains station (called
Alpha station). He/she may do this trip by private car, bus, local train, ferry, etc. or a
combination of them. The equivalent of this ﬁrst part of the trip in the model is the
combination centroid-connector-station.
Secondly, he/she takes the long-distance train in Alpha station. He/she wants to travel
until the closest long-distance station to town B (called Beta). However, there is not
any train that does the trip Alpha-Beta directly. He/she has to change to another train
in a station somewhere in between (called Gamma). The trip Alpha-Gamma is modelled
by the combination of rail links that connect them as fast as possible.
Thirdly, he/she has arrived to Gamma station. There, he/she has to get off the train,
walk around the station to another platform, and wait until the train he/she has to take
leaves. This is modelled by a turn, that the only thing it does is to add an extra time to
the total trip time.
Fourthly, he/she has taken the train in Gamma that goes directly until Beta. This part is
modelled exactly as the second part, by the combination of rail links that connect the
two stations as fast as possible.
Fifthly and ﬁnally, he/she has to travel from Beta station to town B by an alternative
mean of transport (like in the ﬁrst part). This is again modelled by the combination
station-connector-centroid.

All the needed input to the model is summarised as follows (Rapidis (n.d.)):
• The network, that is formed by:
– Links.
– Nodes.
– Centroids.
– Connectors.
– Turns.
– Network dataset.
• The parameters, that describe the preferences for the route choosing. They are:
– Link types. This is relevant for road assignments. In this case, there is only
one possible link type, with the parameters α = 1, β = 1 and γ = 0. These
parameters model the speed-ﬂow curve in road assignments, i.e. the travel
times depending on the ﬂow and capacity.
– Trafﬁc types. This is also relevant for road assignments, in order to deﬁne
the different vehicles (cars, trucks, etc.). In this case, the only trafﬁc type
is passenger trains, deﬁned with the values: MaxSpeed = 350 (km/h) and
==PAGE:48==
Chapter 4. Construction of the model
38
PerCarUnit = 1.0. PerCarUnit converts the actual vehicles to cars units in
the assignment; in this case is simply one passenger = one car unit.
– Categories. The trips are classiﬁed by different categories. This is some-
times useful when interpreting the assignment results. The categories in
the database are: (1) business, (2) private, (3) vacation and (4) commuting.
– Route cost deﬁnitions. This deﬁnes how many different costs are used in
the utility cost function. They are four, the minimum that the tool allows
(distance, time of free-driving, time of congestion driving, and stochastic
error).
– Route choice parameters. This deﬁnes the cost values of each of the previous
cost deﬁnitions,
∗The distance cost is 0. This cost is relevant for cars, because it is assumed
that the number of kilometres to be driven matters in the choice (tyre
wearing, oil, etc.). However, in rail, it does not matter how many kilo-
metres are travelled because the passengers do not have to drive, nor
maintain the train.
∗The time of free-driving cost (ct) simply applies a cost to the amount of
time that the trip takes, counting the driving along the links, the turns
(i.e. transfer times) and the connectors. The value of this cost is log-
normal distributed in order to let the possibility to make the assignment
stochastic.
∗The time of congestion driving applies a cost to the time spent during con-
gestion. As previously said, there is no congestion possible in railways,
so this cost is 0.
∗The stochastic error. Stochasticity was not added to the model, although
it is a requirement, as mentioned in chapter 2. The addition of stochas-
ticity is included in the further work list (chapter 7). Therefore, this
model so far is an all-or-nothing assignment, as deﬁned by Brems (1999).
Hence, the value for the stochastic error in this model is 0.

The output ﬁles are:
• Links, turns and connectors loads tables, that contain the trafﬁc volumes for each
direction and total, separated by categories, as well as the speed for each direction
and total. The speed is simply the same value as the Free Speed used in the input,
because there is no congestion that decreases it. An extra tool is used to obtain
the trafﬁc in each link for the four categories summed.
• Filter loads, in the case a ﬁlter is deﬁned. The possible ﬁlters that can be deﬁned
are the routes of the users of a chosen link (like in ﬁgure 5.6) or the best route
between two zones.
• Trafﬁc cost matrix and trafﬁc cost matrix extended, that are tables providing the
calculated costs between each zone pair, classiﬁed by categories and cost deﬁni-
tions.
Depending on the chosen output ﬁles desired, the computational time for running the
model varies. The maximum time it can take, with all the cost matrices as output, is
between 20 and 30 minutes. If only the loads tables are desired, the computational time
is only between 2 and 5 minutes.

Chapter 5
Application of the model
The main utility of this model is to apply it to some kind of network enlargement
project, in order to get a ﬁrst approximation of how much trafﬁc the enlargement would
have. This chapter provides an example of application of the model, to show what kind
of conclusions may be obtained. However, it is important to note that this application
example here presented does not have induced trafﬁc, but just the same trip matrix
than in the current network.
Induced trafﬁc is a key part when a network enlargement is tested. It tries to model
how many new users the enlargement would have, due to the improvement it provides.
However, this was not the scope of this thesis. The purpose of the application done
here is to show what kind of results and conclusions may be obtained when the model
is applied, but not to make an actual reliable application.

Chapter 6
Discussion
Several aspects in the thesis are open to discussion. Here there are some of them.
One of the most important issues of the thesis was the modelling of the transfers. It
was very clear from the beginning that transfers had to be somehow modelled. The
problem was the lack of data regarding timetables, frequencies, lines, etc. The ﬁnal
system chosen (section 4.1.1.1) is not perfect, of course, but the results achieved were
acceptably realistic.
The average travel speed was also a delicate issue. Again, it was very clear from the
beginning that the speed used in the model could not be the speed limit. The chosen
system is probably not very rational (the equation 4.5), but it empirically works, some-
how. A good option could have been to make the travel speed dependent on the number
of stations crossed. However, this method is not perfect either because not all the trains
stop at every station, and also not all the stations are included in the database. More-
over, it would be something that might not be possible to model.

The results, in section 4.2, show that the model works very good for long distance and
high speed lines, i.e. the typical high-speed projects in Europe. It is important to note
that this model gives very imprecise results in the short distance trips. This is mainly
due to the large size of the zones. For example, the trip from Madrid-Chamartín to
Ávila (table 4.7) has a deviation in travel time of -81.70%. The reason is that the model

speed line, 300 km/h) because Ávila and Segovia-Guiomar are located in the same TT3
zone, despite they are separated by 70 km. Actually, we cannot tell the model that we

Chapter 6. Discussion
58
To ﬁnish the discussion, there is an aspect in which this model simulates wrongly. This
is in the railway junctions. This model assumes that a train can take any direction
in a rail junction. To avoid this happening, the solution would be to add turns in the
model to all the intersections (and not only in the stations) and apply a minimum angle
between links, where below it, the turn is not allowed. However, this would make the
model much more complex, and the computational time would increase signiﬁcantly.
Anyway, the wrong modelling due to this is only noticed locally, i.e. in the link loads
near the junction. The choice of long-distance routes is not severely affected by this.
The only issue is that some trips may return a lower travel time than what they should,
because they avoided a longer route when ‘jumping’ in the junction.

Chapter 7
Conclusions and further work
As a conclusion, it can be said that the route-choice model returns very realistic results
in the lines with a high speed (approx. above 200 km/h) and a long distance (approx.
above 200 or 300 km). It can be applied to future enlargements projects in Europe with
those characteristics, being sure that the results will be, at least, acceptable.
On the other hand, it is important to know that the model might return very bad re-
sults when applied to short distances and/or slower speeds. Maybe this aspect can be
improved in a further work, changing the assumptions related to the transfers and the
average speed.
Anyway, the model is not completely ﬁnished, and therefore there is room for further
work. This can be summarised in:
• validate the network in all the countries, to avoid more mistakes like the one
found in the Madrid high-speed entrance,
• try to improve the results for short distances and slower speeds,
• include the missing stations in Russia and Belarus,
• add stochasticity to the model,
• apply different costs to the high-speed lines,
• add the missing counts data, to be able to make a more reliable validation.

Chapter 8
Contribution of the thesis
The contribution of the thesis can be summarised in:
• The critical errors in the network are solved. The network is ready to be used,
even though a validation of several countries is probably needed.
• Proposal of a rail passenger route-choice model for the TRANS-TOOLS 3 project.
As explained in the previous chapter, the model lacks some aspects, like the stochas-
ticity, but the main work is done.
• Proposal of a rail model without timetables nor frequencies. This kind of model is
not common, and there is not much literature about it, so this thesis has humbly
tried to contribute to ﬁll this gap.
