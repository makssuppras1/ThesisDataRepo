Abstract 
The theory of the Hue Heat Hypothesis is based on the association between colour and 
thermal perception. If this theory is proven concerning CCT of LED lighting, the temperature 
range of occupants’ thermal comfort can be expanded and potential energy savings can be 
obtained. This project aimed to investigate this association by completing laboratory 
experiments with LED lighting. 
 
The experiments were performed at the Technical University of Denmark where 44 test 
subjects were exposed to a changeable CCT at different room temperatures. The 
temperature exposures were 19°C, 22°C and 27°C. The illuminance level was kept at a 
constant 1000lx on the work plane and thus the only variable during the experiment was 
the CCT. At each temperature, subjects were exposed during a one and half hour timeframe 
to a change in CCT from 2700K to 6200K to 2700K and vice versa 6200K to 2700K to 
6200K. During each experiment session, the comfort and satisfaction of the subjects were 
tested through a repetitive questionnaire of 19 questions while the subjects’ performance 
level was evaluated via a d2 performance test. 
 
The project confirmed the Hue Heat hypothesis for a room temperature of 22°C, which 
resulted in a 0.42 change in thermal perception. The change in CCT of 6200K to 2700K 
gave leigh way to decrease the heating set point of up to 2°C during winter. This was 
equivalent to a decreased energy consumption of 16.2% of the buildings entire energy 
consumption. Experiments at 19°C and 27°C showed no association between CCT and 
thermal comfort. Furthermore, as the CCT increased so did the perception of lighting 
intensity along with the perceived air quality. Hence the experiments proved the theory of 
Spatial Brightness and verified an association between air quality and thermal perception. 
 
The project concluded that there was an association between the change in CCT and 
thermal perception, and hence the project verified the Hue Heat hypothesis.

Introduction

The Hue Heat hypothesis describes the theory that warm and cold colours have an 
influence on the human thermal perception. Hence humans subconsciously associate red 
or warm colours with warmer temperatures and blue or cold colours with colder 
temperatures. This tendency is observed for people living in warmer climates who prefer 
cooler lighting while in the north, warmer lighting is preferred. Previous research projects 
and papers indicate a correlation between the sense of thermal comfort and the correlated 
colour temperature of lighting. This project is based on the assumption that the perception 
of thermal comfort can be influenced by controlling the correlated colour temperature of 
LED lighting. 
 
If the perception of thermal comfort can be influenced by a change in the correlated colour 
temperature of lighting, then the operative temperature can be decreased during the heating 
season or increased during the cooling season and hence energy can be saved. This study 
aims to prove the theory by performing laboratory experiments investigating the influence 
of correlated colour temperature on subjects’ thermal perception. 
 
1.1 
PSO 348-030 
ELFORSK is a funding organisation that supports research concerning energy saving or 
transfer of energy consumption. Thus, they support several research projects financially, 
along with the project described in this report. The project title is PSO 348-030, “Warm or 
Cool? Lights influence on thermal comfort”. 
 
1.1.1 
About the Project 
The PSO 348-030 project is conducted as a cooperation between SBi, DTU Civil 
Engineering Department and the DTU Photonics Engineering Department. The goal of the 
project is to demonstrate if adjustable LED lighting can be used to expand the thermal 
temperature interval that users find comfortable. The project is based on the theory that a 
connection exists between the perceived ambient temperature and the correlated colour 
temperature that users prefer for the lit environment.  
 
The project is founded on previous research of coloured light, in which it became apparent 
that in warm climate, the use of blue lighting is more common than in a colder climate where 
people prefer red light sources. Presumably, the use of differently coloured light is due to 
the experience of a cooler climate with blue/cool light sources and the impression of warmth 
by the use of a red/warmer light source. With new LED technology, these warm and cool 
light sources can easily be obtained by controlling the correlated colour temperature of the 
artificial light. 
 
The PSO project spans over a timeline of 2.5 years. During this time span, the project has 
been divided into different stages. The first stage was the start up and the beginning of the 
initial studies, in which the theoretical planning of the project was conducted. The next stage 
was the laboratory studies, which aimed to verify or disprove the theory through 
experiments. Subsequently, field studies shall be conducted to test the theory in real life 
scenarios in an office building. The timeline of the PSO project is illustrated in Figure 1-1.

This chapter reviews the theory behind the project and relevant topics, terms and concepts 
will be described briefly. The chapter primarily concerns indoor climate. Hence, theory and 
recommendations about artificial lighting, thermal environment, air quality and noise will be 
examined. Furthermore, the Hue Heat hypothesis is explained.  
 
2.1 
Artificial Lighting 
We spend an average of 90% of our time indoors in buildings with limited transparent area 
– compared to the outdoor view. This situation minimizes the exposure to natural sunlight. 
Human beings need light, both for practical reasons to sustain, but also for psychological 
reasons to ensure wellbeing. Because of these many hours indoor, a high demand for the 
artificial lighting arises. 
 
2.1.1 
Photometry 
In physics, light is described as electromagnetic radiation. Light that is visual for the human 
eye is within the spectrum of 380-780 nm, going from colours of violet and blue to orange 
and red. In the following, relevant expressions of how to describe or measure light will be 
explained.   
 
Luminous Intensity is a quantity which describes the power of a light source to emit light in 
a specific direction. The luminous intensity is given in the unit candela. 1 candela 
corresponds approximately to the intensity of a single candlelight.  
 
Luminance is defined as the luminous intensity of a surface in a specific direction, divided 
by the projected area as viewed from that direction. Unless the surface is perfectly diffused, 
the luminance will vary at the angle from which it is viewed. Light reflected by the surface is 
registered by the human eye and thus luminance is the most relevant parameter in terms 
of communicating the human perception of light quantities. In other words, the eye responds 
to the light reflected from an object rather than the light falling onto it. (Jacobs, 2001) 
 
Luminous Flux is a measure of the power of visible light from a luminaire. The flux of a 
luminaire can only be measured in specially equipped laboratories and is therefore normally 
only a term used by manufacturers (Jacobs, 2001). 
 
Illuminance is used to describe the intensity of the lighting. Illuminance is defined as the 
luminous flux that is received per unit area of a surface. In other words, it can be described 
as the flux density on the receiving surface. The unit is defined as lux or lm/m² (Jacobs, 
2001).

These four photometric terms are used to describe a light source and its relations to the 
surroundings or how it is perceived. However, illuminance is a quantity that can be 
measured very easily and affordable. Accordingly, most recommendations for lighting in 
offices and other work environments are based on illuminance (Jacobs, 2001).   
 
Colour Rendering of a light source is an indicator of its ability to realistically reproduce the 
colour of an object. This is defined by the difference in lighting of a coloured object for 
daylight and for an artificial light source. The difference in colour perception with the two 
light sources is quantified by the Ra-index. The Ra-index goes from 0 to 100 Ra, where 100 
Ra is perfectly accurate colour rendering. LED lighting normally delivers a colour rendering 
above 80 Ra (Jacobs, 2001). 
 
The Correlated Colour Temperature (CCT) describes the colour of light sources. It indicates 
the equivalent temperature that a black body radiator (Planckian radiator) would need to 
produce light of the same colour. The unit of CCT is Kelvin (K). The scale of CCT goes from 
0K to 10.000K. The lower the CCT, the more reddish the light seems. The higher the CCT, 
the more blueish the light is perceived. To be more exact; light below 3300K is classified as 
warm reddish light, and light above 5300K is defined as cool blueish light. The light in 
between is defined as white light without any significant colour. For example; an 
incandescent bulb normally has a CCT value of 2.700-3.200K, while daylight is around 
4.000-5.000K. Figure 2-1 illustrates the CCT value of different light sources (Jacobs, 2001).

Reciprocal Colour Temperature, abbreviated to RCT, is an alternative metric to explain the 
colour temperature of lighting. When describing the colour temperature of a light source, 
the CCT definition has an unequal distribution of the spectrum and how humans perceive 
the corresponding colours. By the definition, RCT distribution is balanced, causing a more 
equivalent parallel to the colour perception of the human eye. The unit of RCT is reciprocal

2.1.2 
Human Perception of Light 
The human eye is a visual system that can detect light, which is transferred to the brain via 
electrical impulses. Therefore, the eyes are the lens of seeing, while the brain is the engine 
of understanding. A given lit object will first be given colours when the signals have gone 
through the lenses of the eyes to the brain. The colours that the brain detects depend on 
the surfaces of the object and accordingly the wavelengths of the reflected light from the lit 
object. To put it in other words; colour is a concept resulting from the interaction of light 
source, object, eye, and brain (Jacobs, 2001). The part of the eye that observes the vision 
of light is called retina and is a light-sensitive membrane located in the back of the eye, see 
Figure 2-2. Retina is a part of the brain and consist of many photoreceptors that delivers 
the electrical impulses of vision directly to the rest of the brain.  
 
 
Figure 2-2 – The human eye. Source: (Jacobs, 2001) 
 
Human beings feel better both psychologically and physically when exposed to light 
corresponding to the outdoor environment. This is due to the impact light has on the 
melatonin production in the brain. Melatonin is a hormone in the brain that controls our 
sleepiness or alertness. Melatonin suppression is controlled by the pineal gland, which is 
our internal biological clock. When the eye is exposed to cool light or light at high CCT 
values, the human brain decreases the melatonin production. Vice versa, the brain

increases the melatonin production when the eye is exposed to warm light  or light with low 
CCT values (Chellappa et al., 2011). This relates to human biology where the body gets 
sleepier when reddish light appears at sundown – opposite, when the sun rises and the cool 
daylight appears, the body needs to be alert. 
 
Chromatic adaptation describes the ability to adapt the vision to new lighting scenarios. In 
this way, the term indicates how well the vision of the human eye adapts concerning 
changing illuminance or CCT. Many studies have been conducted regarding this definition 
but with different outcomes (Fotios, 2006). Hence, there is no clear definition or time limit 
on how the chromatic adaptation appears. One of the colour appearance studies concludes 
two stages of adaptation; a rapid stage, giving approximately 60% chromatic adaptation in 
the first 5 seconds, and a slower stage where approximately 90% chromatic adaptation is 
reached after 60 seconds (Fotios, 2006).  
 
Spatial brightness expresses how e.g. the CCT of the lighting can influence the human 
perception of brightness in the room even though the illuminance is constant. Many 
experiments have tried to prove or disprove the theory, all resulting in mixed conclusions. 
In this way, some studies prove that the CCT spectrum has an influence on the perception 
of spatial brightness, while other studies found no correlation. (Fotios et al., 2015)

2.2 
Thermal Comfort  
DS/EN 7730 defines thermal comfort as the state of the mind which expresses satisfaction 
with the thermal environment (Dansk Standard, 2006). This covers a state of mind where 
the occupant feels satisfied or dissatisfied with the current indoor climate conditions. Any 
discomfort with the thermal environment can be expressed as comfort of the body as a 
whole or caused by cooling or heating of a specific body part. Thermal comfort is defined 
by several elements but is primarily influenced by temperature, humidity and air velocity, 
along with activity level and clothing insulation. 
 
2.2.1 
Thermoception 
Thermoception is the technical term for human perception of thermal change. Physiologists 
are still attempting to define the specifics of how humans perceive the thermal environment. 
A simplified definition is that thermal receptors located on bare nerve endings create 
thermoceptic-pathways to the thalamus in the human brain, which results in responses to 
the thermal environment (Craig, 2009).

2.3 
Previous Studies Concerning Hue Heat Hypothesis 
The Hue Heat hypothesis is based on the concept that colours with wavelengths towards 
the red and orange end of the visual spectrum feels warm, while colours with wavelengths 
towards the blue end feels cool. Accordingly, the hypothesis claims that colours may 
influence the human thermal perception. Since the idea of Hue Heat Hypothesis was 
discovered, several projects and experiments have been conducted to prove the influence 
of colour on thermal comfort. 
 
The first studies, investigating this theory, examined the thermal influence of coloured 
objects. In 1926 the very first study was conducted by Mogensen and English, where 
various coloured pieces of paper were wrapped around rheostats with a constant surface 
temperature of 42°C. The test subjects were asked to look at the cylinders while judging the 
thermal property. Hence, they saw the colour of the paper while determining the 
temperature. The principle of this combination was for the subjects to judge “warm” colours 
thermally hotter than “cold” colours. However, the green paper was judged as the warmest, 
while the purple as the coldest. Thus the Hue Heat theory was not confirmed by this 
experiment.  
In 1940 Houghten, Olson and Suciu investigated whether a respectively red, white or blue 
screen with a corresponding red, white or blue light could influence the thermal perception 
of two test subjects facing the screen. However, the conclusion showed that no correlation 
between the thermal perception and the colours was present. In 1961 Berry was the first to 
actually investigate how coloured artificial lighting influenced the human perception of 
temperature. During the experiment five different colours of light where used, two “cool” 
colours, green and blue, and two “warm” colours, yellow and amber, along with a single 
white colour. The test subjects were asked to indicate their tolerance of the room 
temperature as it increased while exposed to the five colours. The experiment indicated that 
the test subjects did not perceive any intolerance to change in room temperature as 
dependant on the colours of the light. In 1972 Bennett and Rey tried to prove the Hue Heat 
hypothesis using red, blue and clear goggles under different wall temperatures in a test 
room. However the experiments did not prove the theory either. Finally in 1977 Fanger, 
Breum and Jerking proved the Hue Heat hypothesis. During this investigation, the test 
subjects were exposed to two noise scenarios and two lighting scenarios, extreme red and 
extreme blue. During these experiments the test subjects preferred a slightly lower (0.4°C) 
ambient temperature in the test room during the extreme red lighting than during the 
extreme blue lighting. The Hue Heat hypothesis was therefore confirmed. After this 
successful study, Greene and Bell conducted an experiment in 1980 where the walls in 
three different laboratories were painted respectively red, blue and white. However, the 
studies concluded that the colours of the walls did not influence the perceived temperature. 
 
These first six studies carried out in the 19th century were not that successful at proving the 
Hue Heat hypothesis. Accordingly, the hypothesis was not further explored for several years 
after 1980. First in 2014 did interest of the theory increase. From 2014 and until present 
four studies proved the Hue Heat hypothesis. Three of these studies used coloured light to 
influence the thermal perception. In 2014 and 2015 two coherent studies about coloured 
lighting in aircraft cabins were conducted by Albers, Marggraf-Micheel, Winzen and Maiers. 
During both studies the test subjects were exposed to blue and yellow light. The first study 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
16 
examined the thermal perception regarding lighting during a constant temperature of 22°C, 
while the second examined the same principle at six temperatures ranging from 21°C to 
26°C. Both studies proved the Hue Heat hypothesis. In 2016 Huebner et al. verified the 
hypothesis by investigating the thermal perception at respectively 6500K and 2700K with a 
varying temperature either decreasing or increasing from 20°C to 24°C. The last study to 
confirm the Hue Heat hypothesis was executed by Ziat, Balcer, Shirtz and Rolison in 2016. 
This study utilized a heated or cooled cup that test subjects were prompted to hold while 
exposed to different colours via virtual reality goggles.  
 
The three remaining studies in the 20th century did not confirm the hypothesis. Ho et al. 
actually proved the opposite of the Hue Heat hypothesis and found that a red object, relative 
to a blue object, raised the lowest temperature required for an object to feel warm, indicating 
that a blue object was more likely to be judged as warm despite the same physical 
temperature. In 2014 Vitali tested the hypothesis in a real work environment where the test 
subjects were exposed to warm and cool light at different times of day, but the results did 
not confirm the Hue Heat hypothesis. In 2016 Baniya, Tetri, Virtanen and Halonen made an 
experiment where test subjects were exposed to respectively a high room temperature of 
25°C and a low room temperature of 20°C. At these temperatures, they were adapted to 
light of 2700K, 4000K and 6200K. However, the experiments failed to prove the Hue Heat 
hypothesis. The results showed that the test subjects thermally preferred the medium CCT 
value of 4000K.

Based on the previous studies that both proved and disproved the Hue Heat hypothesis, 
some assumptions can be made concerning further studies.  
The Hue Heat hypothesis tended to be proved under conditions where coloured light was 
the variable factor that influenced the thermal perception – and not the colour of objects or 
surfaces. Studies by Houghten et al. (1940) and Vitali (2014) conducted experiments with 
only 2 test subjects. Both studies did not confirm the Hue Heat hypothesis, which indicated 
that more test subjects give a more representative result.

In the study by Houghten et al. (1940) they concluded that it might have been better to run 
the experiments with additional tests in the outer boundaries of the thermal comfort zone. 
Besides, the study by Bennett and Rey (2016) indicated that the illuminance of lighting must 
be stable during different colour temperatures. If the illuminance varies, it can interrupt the 
thermal perception. In the study by Baniya et al. (2016) they found that the thermal comfort 
was perceived highest at a middle CCT value of 4000K. This tendency was likely found 
because the CCT was rated as a colour preference rather than as a thermal judgement. 
Accordingly, there may exist CCT values that people find more comfortable than others, 
which can interrupt the results regarding thermal perception. 
When investigating the Hue Heat hypothesis by Heubner et al. (2016) found it necessary 
for further studies to examine the performance or the mood of the test subjects. In this way, 
any influence between the light and the awareness of the test subjects could be clarified. 
None of the studies which proved the Hue Heat hypothesis concluded an actual energy 
saving. In the studies by Albers et al. (2014) and Huebner et al. (2016) they mentioned that 
it was important to clarify if any energy saving was possible when using the Hue Heat theory.

3.2 
Experimental Planning 
Before the experiments could be conducted, they were planned carefully to assure that the 
execution would run smoothly and without unnecessary problems. The planning was a 
continuous process and many decisions were changed several times. The following chapter 
will therefore describe the final outcome of the planning. 
 
3.2.1 
Test Subjects  
Based on time and economic restrictions, the number of test subjects was set to 48 subjects 
with an even distribution of 50% females and 50% males. To standardize the subjective 
perception of colour temperature, subjects of northern European origin with no colour 
deficiencies (colour blindness) were recruited. The common hypothesis is that in southern 
warmer climates blue/cold light is preferred and oppositely in northern cold climates. Thus, 
subjects from southern Europe were not accepted. 
 
3.2.2 
Lighting Scenario Design 
Lighting scenarios were defined to visualize the CCT scale during the experiments. The 
lighting scenarios were set to begin and end at the same CCT values, as the difference 
between the high and the low CCT would be less noticeable to the test subjects. 
Furthermore, any uncertainties in the first test results, caused by thermal influence by the 
transition from the acclimatisation area to the test room, could be omitted. 
Additionally, to balance the exposures, half of the test subjects would start and end with the 
low CCT value and the other half of the test subjects would to start and end with the high 
CCT value. Hence, bias due to the order was avoided. The two different lighting scenarios 
are described in Table 3-1.

3.2.3 
Thermal Scenario Design 
Støttrup and Christensen (2017) determined that the experiments should be performed at 
three different temperature scenarios. Two of them should correspond to the outer thermal 
limits for indoor climate category C (Dansk Standard, 2007). This included the low limit for 
the winter scenario (19°C) and the upper limit for the summer scenario (27°C). The third 
temperature scenario was set to 22°C, as it corresponded to the optimal temperature for 
the heating season. 
The two outer temperatures were expected to show a significant influence of CCT on 
thermal sensation. It was predicted as the thermal environments was more likely to be 
perceived thermally uncomfortable where the lighting may have an influence. The tendency 
was indicated by previous studies of Houghten et al. (1940). Hence, it was probable to 
determine whether the CCT influenced the thermal perception during these scenarios.

half of the test subjects would be exposed to the middle thermal scenario, 22°C.

3.2.4 
Experimental Conditions 
Acclimatisation Procedure 
Before the subjects could enter the test room, they needed to achieve calm breathing and 
a stable body temperature before the experiments. This was to assure that all subjects 
would be at the same activity level despite their means of transport to the experiment. 
Therefore, a time slot of 30 minutes was devoted to the acclimatisation phase. The 
acclimatisation took place in a room next to the test room. The environment in this room 
was not controlled. During the acclimatisation phase, the test subjects were asked to fill out 
a personal questionnaire regarding their gender, age, height, weight, clothing and physical 
conditions. The questionnaire for the acclimatisation can be found in Appendix B. 
 
Questionnaire 
Throughout the experimental sessions, the test subjects were prompted to fill out the same 
questionnaire repeatedly. The questions were based on subjective judgement scales. The 
scales entailed perceptual scales, evaluative scales, and preference scales, all with 
emphasis on the subjects’ temperature and lighting perceptions. The questions were 
defined based on DS 10551 (Dansk Standard, 2001). Aside from the questions dealing with 
thermal and lighting conditions, supplementary questions were added regarding noise, 
alertness and air quality. These additional questions were added to ensure that the 
questionnaire covered all topics regarding indoor environment, but also mislead subjects 
so they would not uncover the purpose of the experiment. The questions in the 
questionnaires were based on three different types of answering.

Performance Test 
To test the influence of change in CCT on the performance ability of the subjects, it was 
determined to produce an additional measuring unit, a performance test. The performance 
test titled d2 test was selected. The test was constructed of a piece of paper with 14 rows 
of the characters d and p with a varying amount of marks above or beneath the letters. As 
the test is accordingly named, the purpose is to find the d2’s which are indicated by two 
marks either above, below or one above and one below, totalling 2 marks. The subjects 
would be given 20 seconds per line to find and mark all the d2’s. When the 20 seconds has 
expired, the subjects must move on to the next line regardless of, if they have completed 
the previous line. The setup of the d2 test is illustrated in Figure 3-8 below. 
 
 
Figure 3-8 – Set up of the d2 test 
 
The performance test was performed three times throughout an experiment session and 
always after a questionnaire. As the d2 test was an additional measuring unit it seemed 
trivial and time consuming to repeat it more than three times. Nine different performance 
tests were available, all with the same structure, but with a different order of characters and 
marks. As a result, the test subjects were presented with different performance tests both 
during one experiment, but also from experiment to experiment. Hence, the test person 
would not recognize a system in the test. One of the performance test is illustrated in 
appendix D.

Communication 
During the experiments, the communication to the test room was performed through walkie-
talkies. The test subjects were told when to fill out questionnaires and were guided through 
the performance tests step by step. 
 
Other conditions 
Besides the tables, chairs, tablets and measuring units, the test room was equipped with 
newspapers (Ingeniøren) and Sudoku’s. Thus, the test subjects could entertain themselves 
during the time between questionnaires and performance tests. The test subjects were not 
permitted to bring their own reading material due to possible influence on the perception of 
the lighting. The strong colours from e.g. computers or magazines can influence sight and 
were thus omitted. The Sudoku’s, questionnaires and performance tests where all printed 
on light yellow paper to assure there would not be unpleasant glare from the paper that 
could influence their response. The newspaper was printed on light grey paper. 
 
Water and glasses were provided for the test subjects and they were not allowed to talk to 
each other during the experiments. This avoided that they would talk about their answers 
to the questionnaires.

3.2.7 
Introduction Meeting 
To ensure that the test subjects were completely ready for the experiments, a compulsory 
introduction meeting was set up. Here the subjects were tested for colour deficiency with 
the Ishihara test and via this, it was concluded that they all had normal eye sight regarding 
colour perception. The Ishihara test can be found in appendix G. Furthermore, they were 
introduced to how the experiments would transpire, and were guided through a test session 
with a questionnaire and a performance test. Consequently, they all had the opportunity for

personal effects were allowed in the test room, and that they would receive an email 
reminder the day before each experiment regarding the amount of clothes they had to wear.

Data processing 
After the experiments were completed, the data was processed. The method for analysing 
the data was to focus on responses regarding the thermal environment, lighting and air 
quality. Primarily focussing on documenting the influence of CCT. 
 
Subjects 
The subject pool was analysed regarding age, physical conditions and clothing. The 
clothing insulation of the test subjects was calculated by Equation 2-10 in chapter 2.2. 
 
Questionnaires 
All answers to the questions in the questionnaire were translated into numbers. Thus the 
output of the questions could be comparable. The codebook can be found in appendix H. 
The answers to the questionnaires were registered into datasheets in Microsoft Excel. Once

association between responses and the change in CCT.

3.4 
Statistics 
Statistics is efficient when dealing with large amounts of data with multiple variables. The 
type of analysis depends on the character of the variables that are measured or 
investigated. This report dealt with three types of response variables: nominal, ordinal and 
continuous. A nominal variable represents categories with no intrinsic ranking, e.g. gender 
or yes/no answers - this type of variable is often referred to as categorical. Ordinal variables 
represent categories with intrinsic ranking, e.g. levels of satisfaction from highly dissatisfied 
to highly satisfied. Continuous variables arise e.g. from a scale with a mark between two 
poles that is graded continuously (JMP, 2017). 
 
Linear regression is primarily used when dealing with the relationship between one 
continuous dependent variable and one continuous or categorical independent variable. In 
many cases, linear regression, however, is not sufficient as the data collection can be too 
complex and involve more than one independent variable. To handle this scenario, multiple 
linear regression is utilized. Here the independent variables can be either continuous or 
categorical. Many different models exist in dealing with linear and multiple linear regression. 
 
For the continuous data, a generalised linear model (GLM) is used. Linear regression is a 
sub-model of the GLM. This may take the form shown in Equation 3-3. The model considers

Energy simulation 
The possible energy savings were calculated with the simulation tool IDA ICE. The IDA ICE 
model was built as a sample office room, which had been set up by Støttrup & Christensen 
and was based on a master thesis project from 2014 (Kaplan and Kalkerup, 2014). The 
room included windows, but no daylight control was used for the lighting installation. The 
model is illustrated in Figure 3-13. 
 
Figure 3-13 – Office dimensions used in IDA ICE model 
 
To calculate an actual energy saving, the room was first run as a reference model to 
investigate the energy consumption when nothing was changed. Secondly the set point of 
the heating or cooling was changed in regard to the influence of the CCT. Accordingly, the 
energy saving could be calculated as the difference between the energy consumption of 
the reference model and the CCT influenced model.

4.1 
Lighting Measurements 
Setting up lighting scenarios in the app Casambi required defining the CCT in kelvin (K) and 
the lighting intensity set in percent (%). To determine the accuracy of the app and the 
corresponding control of the luminaires, it was necessary to control whether this Casambi 
input was representative of the actual illuminance and CCT of the luminaires. This was 
investigated through several measurements of the Casambi input versus the actual 
illuminance and CCT. The analysis was carried out with a Metrue SIM-2 Spectral Irradiance 
Meter to determine the illuminance and CCT at every 100lx step in the interval 2700-6000 
K in Casambi.

4.2.1 
Ventilation System 
Early during the PSO project phase, three investigative temperatures were determined; 
19°C, 22°C and 27°C. During this controlled “laboratory” experiment it was possible to 
create an indoor environment with a constant temperature and air velocity rendering the 
CCT as the only factor that varied. It was essential before the execution of the experiment 
to find the optimal setting for the laboratory’s ventilation system to achieve this.  
 
The ventilation system needed to maintain a constant operative temperature with a constant 
airflow while four people were present in the room performing low-activity work with a 
metabolic rate of 1.2 met. This scenario was set up “artificially” by placing four heating 
mannequins emitting 70W each to mimic the four test subjects. Early investigations were 
performed to find the optimal inlet temperature and airflow for the three room temperatures, 
these can be seen in Table 4-1. The ventilation system had a central heating coil which 
could be activated to assist in achieving a higher room temperature. The central heating 
coil was only necessary at 27°C. The airflow was set to ensure a max. CO2 level of 800 
ppm (Støttrup and Christensen, 2017).

Discussion  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
76 
This project resulted in several results associated with the Hue Heat hypothesis. This 
chapter aims to discuss the experimental study and concludes with a few reflections and 
recommendations for the pending field study. 
 
8.1 
Experimental Findings 
The objective of this study was to investigate potential associations between CCT and 
thermal perception and perception of lighting intensity. 
 
8.1.1 
Thermal 
The Hue Heat hypothesis was verified at a temperature of 22°C, where the thermal 
sensation was significantly influenced by the CCT. A difference of 3500K CCT 
corresponded to a difference of 0.42 on the nine-point evaluation scale. During the planning 
stage of the study, it was expected that the more extreme thermal environments at the 
upper and lower temperature limits of the comfort zone, 19°C and 27°C, would instigate 
higher sensitivity to the change in CCT. However, the study showed that at 19°C and 27°C, 
no significant association between the CCT and thermal sensation could be documented. 
Probably at these temperatures, corresponding to slightly cool and slightly warm thermal 
sensation, thermal perceptions may be dominated by actual heat balance effects more than 
the brain’s interpretation of the CCT. Hence the only association between thermal sensation 
and CCT was found at 22°C, where the subjects were thermally comfortable and thus more 
sensitive to the change in CCT. 
 
The difference in thermal perception of 0.42 corresponds to a temperature decrease of the 
heating season temperature by 1-2°C by changing the CCT to 2700K. This result posed 
new investigative questions. How long can this effect last? The thermal influence of the 
lighting could possibly fade and thus the occupants would begin to feel cold at the 
decreased room temperature. Besides the clothing insulation of occupants at an office is 
difficult to control. Perhaps the 1-2°C temperature change can be translated into a piece of 
clothing, e.g. a sweater.  
 
Based on the findings, it would be beneficial to perform additional investigations with 
supplementary temperature exposures at the middle of the scale, to increase the validity of 
the results. As 22°C corresponds to the recommended temperature during the heating 
season for category C, it is advised to perform experiments also at 24.5°C which is the 
corresponding recommended temperature during the non-heating season for category C.  
 
8.1.2 
Lighting 
The subjects had perceived the lighting as more intense as the CCT increased, despite the 
constant illuminance of 1000lx through all experiments. Upon reflection, it was personally 
noted by the authors, that the lighting appeared brighter as the CCT increased. This 
phenomenon, called spatial brightness, was confirmed by the subjects’ responses. This 
result may also pose as a possible energy saving solution. An increased CCT would allow 
a decreased illuminance in the offices and thus a decreased electricity consumption. 
Accordingly, it is beneficial to do further investigations regarding the correlation between 
==PAGE:90==
Influence of Correlated Colour Temperature on Thermal Comfort 
Chapter 8 – Discussion 
 
 
77
CCT and perceived lighting intensity. It is preferable to examine whether the decrease in 
lighting intensity will affect the test subjects in additional aspects besides lighting perception. 
 
8.1.3 
Air Quality 
The results of the experiments indicated that the subjects experienced the room air stuffier 
at the high temperature and fresher during the low temperature despite the constant airflow. 
However, it was noted by the authors during the initial phase of the project that the air quality 
in the room seemed stuffier at 27ºC. The poorer air quality could be caused by emission 
from materials in the room at higher temperatures. Yet, the room only had low polluting 
materials. The correlation between air temperature and perceived air quality was 
investigated in previous studies by Fang et al. This study verified that the air was perceived 
as less acceptable by increasing temperature and humidity (Fang, 1997). Toftum et al. 
verified the exact same theory where the inhaled air of the test subjects was rated warmer, 
more stuffy and less acceptable at increased air humidity and temperature (Toftum, 
Jørgensen and Fanger, 1998). 
 
At 22°C the perceived air quality improved as the CCT increased. Accordingly, the room air 
was perceived fresher at a high CCT. This also confirms the association found for thermal 
sensation that a higher CCT is experienced as cooler and hence a higher CCT was 
perceived as fresher. This result was also verified by the study by Winzen et al. (2014). 
 
The better air quality in relation to the air temperature in the room can also be translated 
into energy savings. When the operative temperature is decreased, e.g. during the heating 
season, the ventilation rate can possibly be decreased without a compromised perception 
of air quality. However, this theory must be investigated through additional experiments to 
verify exactly how much the ventilation rate can be decreased and whether it may influence 
the performance or physical well-being of the occupants.  
 
8.1.4 
Experimental Notations and Observations 
A few observations were noted throughout the execution of the experiments, which could 
have interfered with the results. These observations may be taken into planning for future 
experiments.  
 
Subjects 
The aim was to recruit an equal number of males and females. However, only a third of the 
subjects were female. This could bias the results as males and females perceive 
temperature differently. From the results, it was clear that women react negatively to low 
temperatures, while men react negatively to high temperatures. 
 
Questionnaire 
The questionnaires were written in English, as the project recruited Northern European 
subjects. However, the primary subject pool was Danish. As there was no requirement to 
their English skills, the possibility of them misinterpreting the questions was present. Also, 
many subjects tended to answer the questions rather quickly. This may have been an effect 
of their familiarity with the questions or it could indicate that they had become restless and 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
78 
therefore monotonously repeated their answers. The latter was sustained by the subjects’ 
inclination to answer categorical response options with a circle around the poles instead of 
marking the line in between poles. These three notations were presumed avoided by the 
authors, as an introduction to the questionnaire was performed in advance to the 
experiments. Additionally, the subjects were reminded before each experiment, to consider 
the environment momentarily before answering, while also marking on the scale between 
poles.  
 
Performance Test 
The performance test did not show any association between the CCT and the performance 
of the subjects. Thus the change in CCT does not influence the performance of occupants. 
It however may also suggest that the test was not sufficiently sensitive. Baddeley's 
Grammatical Reasoning Test is more sensitive, and is recommended for future studies 
(Baddeley, 1968). 
 
Experimental Procedure and Execution 
The subjects acclimatised in another room before entering the experiment room. This 
possibly influenced their perception of temperature and lighting as they adjusted from one 
climate to another. During the analysis of the questionnaires, the answers to the first 
questionnaire were omitted as they seemed influenced by the subjects’ lack of adjustment 
to the environment in the test room. Therefore, the first completed questionnaire was 
omitted from the analysis. A future way to account for this is by letting subjects acclimatise 
in the test room. Furthermore, the subjects’ clothing insulation varied, although only 
modestly, but this may have influenced their thermal perception, and hence a stricter 
guideline for their clothing insulation should have been enforced. 
 
The duration of the experimental sessions can be argued were too long and thus the 
subjects became “bored” and unfocused. It could also be argued that they were too short 
and perhaps the influence of the CCT would decrease with time, which would render the 
findings obsolete. 
 
Additionally, the analysed results indicated that the lighting illuminance, 1000lx, was too 
high as many test subjects experienced glare and eye irritation during the experiments. 
Thus, it is recommended to perform new experiments with a decreased illuminance of 500lx.

8.2 
Field Study 
The next stage in the PSO 348-030 is a field study, where the Hue Heat hypothesis will be 
tested under realistic conditions. A potential test office is located at DTU Photonics or the 
library at DTU Lyngby, which is currently being equipped with LED lighting with advanced 
control opportunities.  
 
The laboratory conditions were carried out under well-controlled conditions, but in the field 
study this will not be possible. First, the daylight may interfere with the CCT of the artificial 
lighting. Besides the daylight, the light from electrical equipment like computers, tablets, 
smartphones and desk lamps can disturb the CCT as well. Lighting at the offices of DTU 
Photonics is not controlled by daylight, but manually by the occupants. Hence there is a risk 
that the occupants will turn off the lighting during the day. The illuminance is presumably 
~500lx corresponding to recommendations by Danish Standard. The illuminance during the 
field study is therefore expected to be lower than the one tested in the laboratory study.  
 
Another factor that can disturb the field studies, is the variable metabolic rate and clothing 
insulation of the occupants. The activity level will undoubtedly vary during a work day, which 
will affect their thermal perception of the office. Also, the clothing insulation may differ from 
occupant to occupant and from day to day, which can affect their thermal perception. 
 
Multiple experimental solutions can assist in tackling the variables that may influence the 
association between CCT and the perception of thermal comfort. A solution could be to 
modify the CCT or lighting intensity from computer screens and tablets to minimize their 
disturbance. Furthermore, an ongoing study, by Giorgio Chinazzo, modifies the CCT of 
daylight by applying filters to the windows to mimic the CCT of lighting. 
 
Despite the above-mentioned variables which can influence the association between the 
CCT and thermal perception, it is recommended to complete the field study as close to real-
life conditions. The Hue Heat hypothesis has been tested and verified in a controlled 
laboratory, and now the field studies must determine the bearing of the theory in a realistic 
office environment.

Conclusion 
  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
82 
The aim of the project was to investigate the Hue Heat hypothesis in an office scenario and 
the potential energy savings. In summation three main results were obtained. 
 
The first confirms an association between the correlated colour temperature of LED lighting 
and thermal perception at a room temperature of 22°C. Thus, the Hue Heat hypothesis was 
confirmed under controlled thermal conditions at a moderate temperature. Based on the 
results, the thermal sensation was affected by 0.42 scale units by changing the correlated 
colour temperature of lighting from 6200K to 2700K. This change in thermal sensation was 
equivalent to a decreased heating set point temperature of 22°C to 20°C. This results in an 
energy saving of 16.2% of the energy consumption. Experiments conducted under more 
extreme temperatures, 19°C and 27°C, did not show any association between the 
correlated colour temperature and thermal perception. Hence, the Hue Heat hypothesis, for 
this experiment, was only applicable under neutral thermal conditions. 
 
The second finding during the experiments, confirmed the association between the 
perception of brightness and correlated colour temperature. As the correlated colour 
temperature increased, the lighting was perceived as brighter, even though the illuminance 
was constant. Therefore, the theory of Spatial Brightness was verified. 
 
The third main finding of the experiments, verified the association between the perception 
of air quality and room temperature. The air quality was perceived as fresher under cooler 
temperatures. Additionally, a tendency indicated that the correlated colour temperature 
influenced the perception of the air quality at 22°C. With a higher correlated colour 
temperature, the air was perceived as fresher. 
 
In summary, the project confirmed the Hue Heat hypothesis, proved the theory of spatial 
brightness and verified the association between air quality and thermal perception.

Appendix A - Project description 
 
 
This project is based on the basic assumption that people’s perception of thermal comfort can be 
influenced by colour tampering. It is a general notion that warmer climates utilize cooler lighting while 
in the north, warmer lighting is often used. Many research projects and papers have proven a 
correlation between the sense of thermal comfort and the colour temperature of lighting. This 
investigation is based on the principle of the hue-heat hypothesis, which perceives humans to 
subconsciously associate warm colours and colour temperatures (red, orange) with warmer 
temperatures and vice versa cold colour temperatures (blue, violet) with cooler temperatures. 
 
The hypothesis provides a large possibility for energy savings, as the thermal comfort temperature 
interval can be extended up to 1 degree Celsius, and thus, results in a possible energy saving of 
10% energy usage of an entire office building’s energy consumption. This project will through 
experiments in a controlled testing room, prove or disprove the bearing of the hypothesis’ relevance 
to thermal comfort.  
 
Furthermore, based on the findings of the experiments, preparation for field surveys and experiments 
will be carried out. This will be accomplished via virtual environment simulation tools, such as IDA 
ICE, to determine influence of parameters such as user behaviour and daylight. However, this part 
of the project is dependent on the results of the experiments and is thus subject to change.
