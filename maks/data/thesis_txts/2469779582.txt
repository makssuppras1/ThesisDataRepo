Resume
Undersøgelsen blev udført for at evaluere og m˚ale den tekniske gennemførlighed af, og eﬀek-
tiviten ved, anvendelse af elektrolyse til justering af pH i RAS, og hvorledes denne justering
kan p˚avirke afgasningseﬀektiviteten af en cascade column afgasningsenhed. Testning fandt
sted i et laboratorium under en røghætte, med en lille elektrolytisk cellenhed under an-
vendelse af to adskilte strømme af vand, som hver gik fra et bægerglas, over enten anoden
eller katoden i cellen, og derefter tilbage til det respektive bægerglas. pH, ilt- og temper-
aturm˚alinger fandt sted i bægerglassene. Et større antal parametre (saltholdighed, alkalin-
itet, temperatur, spænding, elektrodetype, vandprøve placering i RAS, ion koncentration og
sammensætning samt membrantype) blev testet for at bestemme deres indﬂydelse p˚a syre-
og baseproduktion, produktionshastighed, eﬀektivitet og omkostninger. Omkostninger blev
sammenlignet med kemisk dosering med HCL (saltsyre). Statistiske tests (studerendes t.test
samt Anova) blev udført p˚a relevante parametre for at bestemme om der var signiﬁkante
forskelle inden for grupper, og om elektrolyseprocessen var duelig.
De ﬂeste parametre
havde betydelige forskelle inden for grupper, hvilket betyder at de næsten alle havde indﬂy-
delse p˚a produktionshastighed, eﬀektivitet og samlede omkostninger. Den eneste undtagelse
var temperatur, hvor forskelle var ikke-signiﬁkante. Valg af membran-type p˚avirkede ikke
processen med syre/baseproduktion signiﬁkant, men dette skyldtes den speciﬁkke eksperi-
mentelle opsætning i denne undersøgelse. Nødvendigheden af specielt en kation-bytte mem-
bran (semi-permeabel membran) er sandsynlig ved en større produktionsskala. Spænding
viste sig at være den mest indﬂydelsesrige parameter, m˚alt p˚a eﬀektivitet og omkostninger,
hvor b˚ade høje og lave spændinger var ineﬀektive og dyre. Spændinger mellem 3.5-4.5 volt
var mest omkostningseﬀektive. Alkalinitet og saltholdighed var ogs˚a vigtige med hensyn
til omkostninger, hvor saltvand sandsynligvis er dyrt p˚a grund af den lave eﬀektivitetsgrad
(15.7 %), som kan tænkes at være p˚a grund af de side-reaktioner der kan ﬁnde sted i salt-
vand. Stigende alkalinitet havde et lineært forhold til omkostningsgrad. Valg af elektrode
var meget signiﬁkant med hensyn til produktionshastighed, men var similære med hensyn
til eﬀektivitet, hvilket viser at valg af elektrode muligvis er mest vigtigt med hensyn til den
tid, det vil tage at justere pH. Test af vandprøver udtaget fra en RAS viste, at selv ved lave
ﬁskedensiteter, er der sandsynligvis nok ioner til r˚adig til, at processen kan ﬁnde sted uden
tilsatte elektrolytter. Testen viste ogs˚a partikel ﬂokkulering, hvilket indikerede, at processen
kan bruges til at fjerne kolloidale partikler samtidig med pH-justeringen. Alle tests viste
sig at være billigere end ved kemisk dosering med HCL, hvilket indikerede at anvendelse af
elektrolyse til en større justering af pH potentielt kan være omkostningseﬀektivt.
Afgasningseﬀektiviteten af en cascade column afgasningsenhed blev ogs˚a undersøgt ved
forskellige saltindhold (0 o/oo, 17.5 o/oo og 35 o/oo) og alkaliniteter (TA 0, 4, 6, 8).

forskel mellem første og sidste CO2 m˚alinger med en m˚aletidsforskel p˚a 10 min). Den pro-

pH management in recirculating aquaculture systems
Technical feasibility study of the use of an electrolysis module for active pH
control, in order to maintain optimal rearing conditions, and improve
carbon dioxide degassing eﬃciencies in a trickling ﬁlter
David Johansen
Abstract
The study was undertaken to evaluate and measure the technical feasibility and eﬃciency
of using electrolysis to adjust pH in a RAS, and how this adjustment might aﬀect the de-
gassing eﬃciency of a stacked column degassing unit. Testing took place in a laboratory
setting under a fume hood with a small electrolytic cell, using two separated ﬂows of water
that each went from a beaker, over either the anode or the cathode in the cell, returning to
the respective beaker, were pH, oxygen and temperature measurements took place. A num-
ber of parameters (salinity, alkalinity, temperature, voltage, electrode type, sample location,
ion availability and composition, membrane type) were tested to determine their impact on
acid and base production viability, rate, eﬃciency and cost. Cost was compared to chemical
dosing with HCL (Hydrochloric acid). Statistical tests (student t.tests and Anova) were per-
formed on relevant parameters to determine if there were signiﬁcant within-group diﬀerences
and whether the electrolysis process worked. Most parameters had signiﬁcant within-group
diﬀerences, meaning that they all inﬂuenced production rates, eﬃciency, and overall cost of
the process, excepting temperature were diﬀerences were non-signiﬁcant. Membrane type
did not signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the process of acid/base production, but this was due to the
speciﬁc experimental setup in this study, and the necessity of a cation-exchange membrane
is likely for a full scale production. Voltage was found to be the most inﬂuential parameter
on overall eﬃciency and cost, with both high and low voltages ineﬃcient and costly, but
voltages between 3.5-4.5 V are likely fairly cost-eﬀective. Alkalinity and salinity were also
found to be important in terms of cost, with highly saline water unlikely to be cost-eﬀective
because of the low degree of eﬃciency associated with it (15.7 %) (likely due to undesir-
able side-reactions taking place), and increasing alkalinity having a linear relationship with
cost. Electrode choice was highly signiﬁcant in terms of production rates, but were similar
in overall eﬃciency, showing that electrode choice may be most important in terms of time
to adjust pH. Testing water sampled from a RAS showed that even at low ﬁsh densities,
there is likely enough ions available for the process to occur without any added electrolytes.
The test also showed ﬂocculation, indicating that in RAS the process can be simultaneously
used to remove colloidal particles. All tests using adjusted parameters were cheaper than
chemical dosing with HCL, indicating the promise of using electrolysis for this purpose.

==PAGE:8==
The degassing eﬃciency of a stacked column degassing unit was also investigated at dif-
ferent salinities (0 o/oo, 17.5 o/oo and 35 o/oo) and alkalinities (TA 0 4, 6 and 8). The TA
0 trial was a pH reduced (acid-reduced) trial using chemical dosing. The cascade column
contained bio-block type packing material, was 1.81 m high, and only measured CO2 in the
collection sump using a single infrared probe. CO2 concentrations were measured over 2-50
mg L−1, and eﬃciency was measured in terms of mass transfer coeﬃcient KLa (The log con-
centration of CO2 over cascade height), as well as CO2 stripping eﬃciency (post equilibrium
diﬀerence in CO2 concentration after approximately 10 minutes between initial and ﬁnal
CO2 measurements). The percentage availability of inorganic carbon that could be stripped
over time was also evaluated. Eﬃciency of stripping in terms of cost in DKK to strip from
20 mg L−1 to 10 mg L−1 were compared for just the degassing unit vs one that had reduced
pH through electrolysis pH adjustment. KLa values were similar in group for all alkalinities
and salinities, and quite low as compared to previous studies. The low values were probably
because the stripping unit was not adequately air-tight, so that counter-current ﬂowing air
did not ﬂow up and and out of the unit, but instead ﬂowed out immediately through the sides
of it. Lack of adequate ventilation in the laboratory resulting in an unknown atmospheric
CO2 concentration probably also had an impact. A positive relationship between inlet CO2
concentrations and stripping eﬃciency was observed, ranging from 23.4-32.4 % for alkaline
trials and 11-15 % for saline trials for a single (10 min) pass through the system. The lower
eﬃciency for saline water for both eﬃciency measurements is likely due to diﬀerences in
ionization fractions of the carbonate species between saline and freshwater, indicating that
saline water will be harder to degas. Availability of carbon that could be degassed was low
for both fresh and saline water, and dropped to near zero over a single pass, except for the
acid-reduced trial where it remained at almost at 100 %, indicating that the reduction of
pH with acids is a viable way to increase total Ct reduction in a RAS. The cost of using
electrolysis to reduce pH and decrease CO2 concentration were much higher than simply
using a degassing unit, indicating that the technology is not currently economically feasible,
but additional beneﬁts to the process, such as the simultaneous production of oxygen, and
continued research may change this in the future.

Synopsis
1
Background
1.1
A brief overview of drivers of technological innovation in west-
ern aquaculture
The world is projected to grow in terms of both population size and economic output con-
tinuously, and with only mild signs of abatement, until the end of the century (UN 2017,
IMF 2019).
At least in the short and medium term, the majority of the share of both
types of growth will take place in what the IMF calls “. . . emerging markets and developing
economies. . . ” (IMF, 2019) and the UN calls Least Developed Countries (LDCs) (UN, 2017).
For these markets, economic growth share will increase from 76 percent this year (2019) to
about 85 percent in 2024 (IMF, 2019). One of the major trends likely to accompany this
concentrated and rapid wealth increase, is that consumption patterns in these markets and
LDCs will change, so that an increasingly large part of daily energy intake will be from ani-
mal sources (Gerben-Leenes, Nonhebel and Krol, 2010). This trend has been observed to be
fairly universal (though still subject to cultural inﬂuences and other factors) (Gerben-Leenes,
Nonhebel and Krol, 2010). As a consequence, global demand for animal protein will keep
increasing for the foreseeable future. Currently, this demand increase is being projected to
be met both by large increases in livestock (FAO, 2011), but also by an increase in aquatic
organism production (FAO 2018).
There is a possibility that western land-based aquaculture, as a producer of aquatic based
animal protein, will grow more than projected as it possesses several distinct competitive
advantages. Firstly, the industry is still fairly small, and stands to proﬁt from economies
of scale and continuous eﬃciency improvements in all areas of production (Rosten et al.,
2013).
Secondly, being a wholly artiﬁcial environment, land-based aquaculture can take
place on unproductive, cheap land. Thirdly, land-area requirements and the environmental
footprint for terrestrial production of aquatic organisms are generally much smaller than for
conventional livestock (cows, pigs, chickens, sheep) for the same functional unit (Ellingsen
and Aanondsen, 2006; Clune, Crossin and Verghese, 2017), which provides an avenue for
the industry to brand itself as the ethical choice in a time of increasingly environmentally
conscious consumers.
However this competitive advantage is threatened by low costs associated with low
wages, lax environmental regulations, and cheap land for producers in growing non-western
economies. In order to stay competitive, development of new technologies, novel designs and
incremental improvement of existing ones will be essential (Varadi et al., 2000; Rosten et al.,
2013).
Recirculating aquaculture systems
A particularly technology-intensive part of the
aquaculture toolbox, recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) are land-based, and seek to
optimize growth parameters while (increasingly) minimizing environmental impacts through
intensive water reuse and control of production made emissions (Odd-Iver Lekang, 2013).
RAS facilities often rely on a few, critical modules, designs and technologies but may diﬀer
1
==PAGE:19==
from other facilities by reason of species farmed, water-quality-relevant operating parame-
ters, organism life stage, overall design and choice of technological solutions (Lekang, 2013).
This great variety means that a one-size-ﬁts-all design approach to RAS is wholly untenable,
but optimized design choices are generally hampered by gaps in knowledge in regards to op-
erating eﬃciencies of diﬀerent technologies, modules and designs under diﬀerent operational
conditions. Due to the competitive threat and the current gaps in knowledge, it is vital
to the industry that novel technological approaches are tested alongside existing technology
under a variety of conditions to ﬁll the gaps and develop new solutions. One such technology
may be electrolysis and the potential beneﬁts it provides to the industry in the form of pH
adjustment, that may help degas CO2 at a greater eﬃciency, than what can be achieved
now.
1.2
Fish physiology and tolerance of carbon dioxide and pH
One of the many water quality parameters that RAS facilities must include in their design
considerations is carbon dioxide concentrations and pH. Carbon dioxide is naturally produced
as an end product of ﬁsh and bioﬁlter metabolic activity, and has been identiﬁed as a
potentially limiting factor for production when oxygen is of no concern i.e when it is being
continuously added to a system (Fivelstad, 2013). Chronic levels of exposure, which are
common in RAS facilities, may lead to detrimental physiological eﬀects in ﬁsh, even though
uncertainties remain in regards as to the magnitude of eﬀect in both the short term and long
term (Khan, Johansen and Skov, 2018).
Evidence suggest that carbon dioxide has a direct eﬀect on ﬁsh health. In the case of
chronically elevated levels of carbon dioxide, direct eﬀects are associated with hypercapnia,
acidosis, and the production of stress hormones, which may lead to issues with feed intake
(Cech and Crocker, 2002), growth performance (Good et al, 2018), and diseases such as
nephrocalcinosis (Hosfeld et al., 2008).
A number of reasons may be responsible for the
observed eﬀects. Hypercapnia decreases the aﬃnity in blood for oxygen through the Bohr
eﬀect, which reduces the maximum oxygen carrying and delivery capacity, thus reducing
metabolic scope (MS). Usually, more frequent ventilation is also observed, directly result-
ing in increased energy expenditure and also indirectly through increasing osmo-regulatory
energy costs. Increases in stress hormones, such as cortisol, may result in stress-induced
struggling, causing a lack of blood ﬂow to the gut (Cech and Crocker, 2002). CO2 is also
widely used as an anaesthetic in ﬁsh (Bernier and Randall, 1998; Marx et al., 1996). Although
the concentrations with which CO2 are usually applied are very high (between 150-500 mg L
during short exposure)(Erikson et al., 2006). the eﬀect might be replicated to a mild degree
with a sudden, small increase. The possible anaesthesizing eﬀect on ventilation and cardiac
relevant muscles will result in a decrease in their activity, and so too the ﬁshes scope of
metabolic activity (MS) beyond standard metabolic rate (SMR), as also speculated by Khan
et al. (2018).
The overall impact of elevated levels of carbon dioxide is determined not only by con-
centration level, as tolerance has been shown to be species-dependent (Ben-Asher et al.,
2013; Cech and Crocker, 2002; Fivelstad, 2015; Good et al., 2018; Steinberg et al., 2017).
Life stage also plays an important role; For Atlantic salmon, a number of studies indicate
that chronic and acute exposure to elevated levels of CO2 are more detrimental at earlier
2
==PAGE:20==
life stages. (Mota et al., 2019; Khan et al., 2018; Good et al., 2018).
Khan et al. (2018),
investigating the physiological and growth performance in Salmon smolts, found that there
was no level of carbon dioxide in which respiratory physiology and associated growth was not
aﬀected. In contrast to this, Good et al. (2018) did not ﬁnd any signs of growth impairment
in fresh water for Atlantic salmon parr.
The eﬀect that species and life stage have on carbon dioxide tolerance and impact is
further complicated by the inﬂuence of water water quality parameters such as alkalinity,
temperature, salinity, DO, and dissolved metals (Eschar et al, 2006; Fivelstad, 2013; Fivel-
stad et al., 1999, 2007, 2015, 2017; Good et al. 2018). These parameters may also have
interactive eﬀects that have not yet been uncovered which make it exceedingly diﬃcult to
identify the range or limit beyond which the ﬁsh will experience some form of physiological
impairment.
Despite the diﬃculties in identifying carbon dioxide sensitivity, the industry usually ob-
serves some limits to concentration - standard operating procedures are usually thought to
be around 8-10 mg L−1 [CO2] (Fivelstad, 2015). These limits are likely to be highly ar-
bitrary (as another example, in Norway, concentration limits may not exceed 15 mg L−1
[CO2])), because they are derived from a speciﬁc set of operational conditions. It is likely,
as shown, that diﬀerent conﬁgurations of operational parameters (species, life-stage, water
quality, etc.) would require very diﬀerent concentration limits. The total range could be
very wide indeed. As a consequence of this, for some facilities, the limits are likely to be far
too high for what is desirable. This would make management of carbon dioxide a much more
pressing concern and may mean that technologies conventionally thought expensive could
be economical.
In a recirculating system, pH is usually tightly regulated between 6.5-9 (dependent on
species)(Randall, 1991; Lekang, 2013). pH has previously thought to be responsible for many
of the problems that are also ascribed to carbon dioxide concentrations. Recent research
suggests however, that the impact of CO2 are greater than the impact of pH (Khan et al.,
2018).
pH is also of pressing concern when it comes to preventing the solubilization of
metals, such as aluminium, because of their toxic eﬀect (Fivelstad et al., 2003b; Skogheim
and Rosseland, 1986).

1.4
pH and carbon dioxide control in RAS
The previous sections establishes the complex mechanisms in RAS by which carbon dioxide
and pH are regulated, and their importance from a ﬁsh health and production optimization
perspective. This section will look into current control and management practises.
pH is usually tightly regulated around neutral, with a substantial buﬀering capacity,
which reduces the risk of sudden swings in pH in the system (Mydland et al., 2010) that
could lead to acute toxicity problems (Skogheim and Rosseland, 1986). This regulation needs
to be continuous as alkalinity is consumed in the bioﬁlter as a carbon source for the bioﬁlter
bacteria. Because it is a carbon-based source of alkalinity, its removal results in a drop in the
pH, and not just buﬀering capacity. Biesterfeld et al., (2003) identiﬁes the minimum bioﬁlter
requirements of carbon-based alkalinity for nitriﬁcation processes to occur as a concentra-
tion that must be no less than 45 mg/L, while Jin et al., (2008) operates with a mass based
theoretical minimal requirement at 7.1g CaCO3/NH+
4 (calcium carbonate per ammonium
ion). Management is almost universally done by replacing alkalinity by the addition of a
carbonate source such as CaCO3 or sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) (Timmons and Ebeling,
2010; Lekang, 2013). Besides the consumption of carbon in the bioﬁlter, pH may also need to
be adjusted at the MUW (make-up water) inlet so that it ﬁts the the operating parameters
in the facility (Lekang, 2013), in particular biological requirements, and for the avoidance of
the formation of toxic metal compounds, such as aluminium, that may form at low or high
pH (Skogheim and Rosseland, 1986). Finally biological activity, i.e the production of CO2
by bacteria and ﬁshes, may decrease pH (Colt, 2006).
Besides keeping pH stable, it is important to regulate the carbon dioxide concentration
6
==PAGE:24==
so that it is as low as can be, but operators must also take into the economic costs of
doing so. In RAS, there are usually three ways to go about this. The ﬁrst is dilution. By
increasing the water ﬂow rate, concentration levels will decrease in an inversely proportional
relationship, but will also increase costs related to pumping, MUW and ﬁxed equipment
(Summerfelt and Vinci, 2004). The second way of regulating carbon dioxide levels are by
chemical dosing. Because RAS can be considered closed for practical purposes, hydroxides
(OH−) or carbonate compounds such as (CaCO3) are commonly added, indirectly reducing

1.5

Among the many opportunities for innovation in recirculating aquaculture systems is in-

or stripping. In this section, the eﬃciency and current limitations of degassing technologies
will be examined.

mechanism as a whole. From the air ﬁlm, gas enters the air by a mixture of diﬀusion and
convection.

than what can be achieved with other gases. Figure 2 shows this process.

In order to achieve the optimization goals outlined in the previous paragraph, there are is
one fundamental design choice to be made; Either water can be moved through a medium of
air, or air can be moved through a medium of water. The two options result in the creation
of bubbles of air or droplets of water respectively, that are surrounded on all sides by the
other medium. Which option is more desirable depends upon the type of gas. For carbon
dioxide in a RAS, it will always be better to move water through a layer of air, as the gas
will be so highly concentrated in water - often 20-100 times the saturation concentration
in air - that a large volume of air is needed for the degassing process to occur eﬃciently
(Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). A low volume of air would result in the air being saturated
before a signiﬁcant amount of carbon dioxide had been removed from the water. For this
reason, degassers have high gas to liquid volume ratios (G/L) - between 5-20 is common
(Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). Often, the air is blown through the degassing unit counter-
currently to the incoming water droplets. Counter-current designs are widely used because
they maximize concentration gradients and therefore increase the eﬃciency of the system.
A common design for a degassing unit is having water pumped to the top of a vertical
tower, and breaking up this water either by letting it ﬂow through a perforated plate or
a spraying nozzle, and then letting the water cascade down through the tower. The tower
is either ﬁlled at speciﬁc intervals with perforated plates, or is cascade with large surface
area media, that break the incoming water droplets up into smaller units. The smaller these
droplets are, the larger the total surface area A exposed to the air. Figure 3 shows how these
webbed structure plastic packing media can look when placed on top of each other to form
a vertical tower. The shape of the media allows air to percolate through while still breaking
up the water eﬃciently. This approach to breaking up water droplets has the disadvantage
of also creating surface area for growth of bacteria, which may eventually lead to clogging of
the ﬁlter, increasing the maintenance costs of the system (Summerfelt et al., 2015). Letting
water droplets ﬂow downward also helps create turbulence in conjunction with the counter-
current ﬂowing air, which usually exits somewhere at the top of the tower. Turbulent ﬂow
will help minimize the thickness Zfilm of the boundary layer.

Temperature: inﬂuence on degassing eﬃciency
A higher temperature is likely to be conducive to increased eﬃciencies in CO2 degassing; Colt
and Bouck (1984) found that the KLa value increases with temperature, tripling between
5 ◦C and 30 ◦C , but that this for oxygen corresponds to a lower solubility that counteracts
the eﬃciency, because absolute concentration will be lowered. However, as operators are
summarily interested in degassing CO2, and this compound also has a lower solubility at
higher temperatures, this should mean an enhancement of degassing - not the opposite.
Increased temperature will translates to a decreased requirement in column height, which
lowers operating costs associated with pumping (Colt and Bouck, 1984).

Alkalinity and pH: inﬂuence on degassing eﬃciency
The concentration of carbon dioxide is proportional to TA at a given pH (Timmons and
Ebeling, 2010), and thus it is likely that alkalinity has a large eﬀect on [CO2] degassing
eﬃciency. It is diﬃcult to estimate to what extent because of the relative complexity of the
carbonate system.
A few attempts have been made - notably, the eﬀect of alkalinity on carbon dioxide stripping
in a moving bed bioreactor was investigated by Summerfelt et al., (2015). The study focused
on TIC (total inorganic carbon - synonymous with Ct) removal as a concentration (mg/L),
as a percentage of total TIC, (%) as well as the apparent CO2 removal. The study found
that alkalinity had a large eﬀect on the percentage of total TIC being removed, with low
alkalinities showing a large percentage drop and high alkalinities showing a low drop. Total
TIC removal increased from 0.95 mg/L to 1.65 mg/L but only between treatments 70-200
mg/L TA, with the lower alkalinities (70 and x mg/L TA) being highly similar. The apparent
eﬃciency of the degassing unit was similar across alkalinity treatments, ranging from 54 %
to 63 %.
These results ran contrary to the authors expectations that degassing eﬃciency should de-
cline with increasing alkalinity. They expected the TA to function as a kind of reservoir -
replenishing carbon dioxide to the water. As the authors did not show pH data, it is hard
to quantify exactly what the reason for the observed behaviour was, but it may be that the
large apparent eﬃciency is explained by a rapid increase in pH during the degassing process.
This would explain why there is between 3.78 and 4.41 mg/L reduction in [CO2] between
treatments, but only a 0.95-1.65 mg/L actual loss of carbon out of the system.
Other studies, namely Terjesen et al. (2013) reported similar apparent degassing eﬃciencies,
and betray the inherent focus in the studies on using the coupling of pH and carbon dioxide
to reduce [CO2] while minimizing actual carbon removal from the system, so that alkalinity
is not impaired for bioﬁlter purposes. A problem with the focus on this mechanism is that
it doesn’t address the build-up of TIC over time. Eventually, pH may increase to a point
where it is detrimental to other processes, but decreasing it would result in problematic CO2
concentrations. A balance must be struck between alkalinity needs - for the bioﬁlter and
pH-swing risk reduction - and the amount that can be removed to decrease carbon dioxide
concentrations.

A diﬀerent approach to degassing
There is an opportunity to improve the eﬃciency of not just apparent, but actual CO2 re-
moval in a degassing unit. Figure 1 shows that, at neutral pH, free carbon dioxide (dissolved
carbon dioxide) makes up a low fraction of the carbonate species. Because carbon dioxide
makes up a low fraction of the total carbonate species in a system at neutral pH, its removal
usually require large amounts of work, expended as head loss, and as shown by Timmons
et al (2015), will likely only remove a small fraction of the TIC stored at most alkalinities.
This could potentially be circumvented by decreasing the pH before degassing, so that a
large fraction of TIC were initially in [CO2] form. The loss in actual eﬃciency as a natural
consequence of degassing, (explained in the carbonate system section, also shown in ﬁgure
2) could be mostly negated by a large enough reduction in pH. The total concentration of
carbon dioxide would also increase with a reduction in pH- meaning that the concentration
gradient across a degassing unit would be higher - in itself resulting in a higher total removal
(mg/L).
A large enough drop in pH and initial CO2 concentration increase taken together should
result in a much higher actual eﬃciency (rather than apparent eﬃciency) in a degassing
unit. However, this approach will also decrease the alkalinity in the system, a potential issue
for the bioﬁlter as previously detailed. It is therefore necessary to make sure that it does
not reduce alkalinity below bioﬁlter-viable levels.
Any reduction in pH would also have to be both temporary and spatially separated from the
bioﬁlter and rearing tanks to prevent impairment of biological functions.
Having established the theoretical interest in pH reduction before degassing, a question
of how to proceed presents itself. A reduction may be done by dosing of acids, although this
would entail certain disadvantages, such as cost of reagents and risks of overdosing (Spine
et al., 1998; Herreros, 2017). As an alternative to chemical regulation, an electrochemical
approach may present additional advantages, but such systems are largely untried in RAS
and are only widely used in wastewater treatment for speciﬁc applications that go beyond
pH adjustment. However, it is theoretically possible to use a speciﬁcally designed electrolysis
module as an acid/base generator, as shown by Spinu et al. (1998). Such a module may
provide signiﬁcant advantages for pH-management, such as ease of control, simplicity, and
minimal overdosing risk, but there are large gaps in knowledge and optimal application that
needs to be addressed.

Basics of electrolysis
By placing two electrodes, connected to an electrical power supply, into two diﬀerent aque-
ous solutions containing electrolytes that are connected by a salt bridge, or separated by
an ion-exchange membrane, water can be split. This is known as electrolysis and is a non-
spontaneous redox reaction.
The two electrodes are known as an anode and a cathode
respectively. The anode facilitates oxidation, i.e the loss of electrons, and the cathode facil-
itates reduction, i.e gain of electrons. Since the splitting of water is non-spontaneous (the
electric potential is negative), an external electromotive force is applied using a DC cur-
rent, where electrons travel from the negative terminal of the power source to the cathode,
and electrons travel from the anode to the positive terminal of the power source. Using
chemically inert electrodes, the electrons are ideally supplied by water or ions present at
the anode. With non-chemically inert electrodes, the anode electrode may supply electrons
itself, becoming oxidized in the process (i.e degraded) over time.
The full redox reaction for the splitting of water is:
2 H2O(l) −−→2 H2(g) + O2(g)
{6}
A full redox reaction can be viewed in terms of its half-cell reactions. For the splitting of
water, these reactions will be useful for pH adjustment, because they involve the production
and reaction of H+ and OH−ions. As such they can be exploited to produce acidic and
basic solutions. The half cell reactions depend on the pH of the aqueous solution. In an
acidic solution, they are:
Anode
2 H2O(l) −−→O2(g) + 4 H+(aq) + 4 e−
{7}
Cathode
4 H+(aq) + 4 e−−−→2 H2(g)
{8}
In a basic solution the half-cell reactions are:
Anode
4 OH−(aq) −−→O2(g) + 2 H2O(l) + 4 e−
{9}
Cathode
4 H2O(l) + 4 e−−−→2 H2(g) + 4 OH−(aq)
{10}
Here, the reactions have all been balanced. What reactions 7-8 and 9-10 show, is that if
the half cell reactions can be separated, the pH will drop in a volume of water containing
the anode, and vice-versa for the volume of water containing the cathode, no matter the
initial pH of the solution. Separating the reactions in practise will require enabling a ﬂow
of ions between the two aqueous solutions as electric charge neutrality needs to be upheld
or the reaction will summarily stop. The process also needs to exclude ions OH−and H+
14
==PAGE:32==
from ﬂowing from one solution to the other. This can be done through the use of a ion-
exchange membrane, as proposed by Spinu et al., (1998), who also established a working
theoretical framework for the deployment of such as system in hydroponics. Before this
thesis, something similar had yet to be tried in RAS.
On the importance of ion-exchange membranes and acid/base generation design
The use of electrolysis for pH adjustment necessitates the separation of the products of the
half-cell reactions, otherwise the reaction runs to completion (see reaction 6). Separation can
be done by placing the anode and the cathode in two separated volumes, or compartments, of
water. Complete physical separation of the compartments is not possible, as the immediate
charge imbalance that arises at both electrodes when power is supplied needs to be balanced
by the migration of ions to complete an electric circuit. Without a continuous migration
of ions - essentially the migration of electrons - the process will quickly cease to function.
Migration can be achieved by connecting the two compartments by either a salt bridge, or
an ion-exchange membrane.
Figure 5 – The ﬁgure shows how a cation exchange membrane functions under electrolysis.
Two diﬀerent solutions of water containing electrolytes are separated by an ion-exchange mem-
brane. In either solution, an electrode is present. In the rightmost solution, H+ is generated
at the anode. OH−is generated in the leftmost solution at the cathode. This results in an
immediate charge imbalance in either solution, that must be sated by migration of ions. Only
cations are able to migrate across the cation exchange membrane, as it holds a negative surface
charge that drives away anions. The result is net migration of cations across the membrane
from the anode solution compartment to the cathode solution compartment.
A salt bridge essentially only diﬀers from an ion-exchange membrane by not presenting
15
==PAGE:33==
any physical barrier between compartments, instead connecting them directly as a container
running from one compartment to the other. The bridge itself contains a solute with elec-
trolytes. Being so, it adds to the total amount of ions present while ”bridging” the two
compartments.
An ion-exchange membrane is a thin barrier separating two solutes. It allows immediate,
speciﬁc travel of only ions across it and does not add any extra. The advantage of using
an ion-exchange membrane over a salt bridge partly lies in this direct connection of com-
partments, simplifying design.
Ionic exchange membranes are used instead of other kinds
of membranes because of their high speciﬁcity and the passive charge and concentration
gradients by which they function, signiﬁcantly reducing the risk of clogging of the mem-
brane and the need to drive the reaction with pressure, which can be costly. A non-selective
ion-exchange membrane allows both anions and cations to cross, whereas a cation or anion
speciﬁc membrane only allows the crossing of one type. An example of such a process is
illustrated in ﬁgure 5.
The charge speciﬁcity of a cation/anion exchange membrane is likely to be useful for the
purposes of pH adjustment of discrete volumes of water, otherwise known as a ”batch”.
A batch volume of water separated by a cation exchange membrane will only be able to
adjust the pH to a certain level. Assuming that the chamber with the anode (the anodic
compartment from now on) has a higher concentration of electrolytes than the cathodic
compartment, consider ﬁgure 5, where H+ is produced. Immediately after this production,
cations will be in surplus in the anodic beaker. There will be a net positive charge. Electro-
static forces are very strong, and will almost immediately force surplus cations to ﬂow across
the membrane from the anode compartment to the cathode compartment, so net electro-
neutrality can be reached.
The H+ is also a cation, but unless the pH is very low in the anode compartment(which
would make the electrolysis process redundant), it is likely to have a much lower concen-
tration than the sum of all other cations present in the solution.
Initially, at least, the
chance that a H+ will migrate across the membrane will then be very low. The pH will drop
continuously during the process (the concentration of H+ becomes ever greater) while the
concentration of other cations is depleted as they migrate across the membrane. Eventu-
ally an equilibrium is reached. The other cations will have depleted to the point where the
cation crossing the barrier will be H+ itself. The pH will then no longer drop in the anode
compartment.
Simultaneously with this, the concentration of electrolytes alongside a rise in pH has
taken place in the cathodic compartment, because here OH−will have been produced. In a
two compartment design using a cation-exchange membrane, the maximum capacity of pH
adjustment is dependent on the electrolyte concentration in the anodic compartment.
A charge speciﬁc ion-exchange membrane is absolutely essential for this process. If a
non-speciﬁc ion-exchange membrane had been used, anions as well as cations could have
crossed the barrier. Electrolytic concentration diﬀerences would quickly diminish until an
equal amount would be found in each compartment. As the reaction continued, pH would
16
==PAGE:34==
become the same in each compartment, and essentially the full reaction 6 would take place.
The thermodynamics of electrolysis
The opposite of reaction 6 is the formation of water.
2 H2(g) + O2(g) −−→2 H2O(l)
{11}
The formation of water has a standard gibbs energy of 237.13 kJ at 25 ◦C - i.e it is exother-
mic (releases energy). Naturally, this means that the splitting of water is endothermic. From
this, the minimum voltage required to run the electrolysis of water can be calculated as the
standard electrode potential using equation 17
∆G = −nFE
(17)
Where ∆G is the standard gibbs energy of formation of water, n is number of moles (each
mole of water requires two moles of electrons), F is Faradays constant and E is the standard
electrode potential. Inserting the known quantities yields a standard electrode potential of
-1.23 V (volt).
As the decomposition of water is non-spontaneous, at least 1.23 V must be supplied from
an external power source. Regner (1957) however, saw visible evolution of gases (H2 and O2)
at 1.7 V. This is due to the phenomenon of overpotential, where a higher voltage is required
to practically drive the reaction than what is thermodynamically ideal. Overpotential is
understood as heat loss.
This heat loss can be attributed to a number of factors such
as resistance in wiring, ion mobility, bubble formation on electrode surface and the poor
conductivity of water itself (Light et al., 2005). Often, there will be competing side reactions
if there are ions present in high abundance and with undesirable reduction potentials. These
side reactions will result in further eﬃciency losses, and increase the amount of power needed
to produce a given quantity of acids or bases. Sometimes, these side reactions may even come
to dominate, rendering the desired production of acids and bases untenable.

The maximum concentration of acids and bases in a given solution can be determined as an
alternative to calculating the required amounts of electrolytes for a given pH adjustment.

On the importance of electrolytes for the electrolysis process
Besides the necessity of electrolytes as ionic charge substitutes in the proposed acid/base
generation design, they are also needed to conduct electricity, as pure water will do so poorly.
An electrolyte will dissociate in water into its constituent anions and cations. As electrol-
ysis is taking place, a local build-up of H+ will take place at the anode - the anions from
the electrolyte will congregate towards this, and neutralise it. Similarly cations will rush
18
==PAGE:36==
toward the cathode to neutralise the build up of OH−. This enables much greater conduc-
tivity. Electrolytic concentration/conductivity is one of the most important parameters for
electrolysis for this reason (Feng et al., 2003).
The kind of electrolyte present is of great importance for two reasons; ﬁrstly, the elec-
trolyte will compete for electrons with the proton and hydroxide ions. Secondly, the elec-
trolyte may react readily with the electrode material, and degrade it. An electrolyte competes
based on the reduction potential of its constituent ions. As reduction potential measures the
tendency to be reduced (i.e accept electrons) a cation with a greater reduction potential than
H+ will accept electrons and be reduced instead of it, if both are present at the cathode.
Similarly, an anion with a smaller reduction potential than the hydroxide ion will give up
an electron at the anode. These diﬀerences are the reason for side reactions. There can be
many side reactions, if there are many ions present, such as is the case in salt-water. The
ratio of the side reactions to the main reaction in terms of mol/mol (a massless unit) will be
governed by their relative concentrations and the charge of the ions, as their ﬂux towards the
electrode will be driven by local charge and concentration diﬀerences. When side reactions
take place, some of the energy spent driving the electrolysis is naturally lost to them, which
of course reduces the eﬃciency of the process. The electrode may also be degraded because
the electrolyte is highly reactive - such as chlorine in a salt-water solution. Thus, care need
to be taken in the choice of an electrode so that it is resistant to degradation or replacement
could be a costly aﬀair.
If electrolytes are not already present in suﬃcient numbers in a solution to eﬃciently drive
the electrolytic process, it may be necessary to add them manually. There are a number of
suitable cations with lower reduction potentials than H, and one of the most used is sodium
Na+ (Harris, 2007), because of its inexpensiveness. There is also a number of suitable anions
with higher reduction potential than hydroxides, and one of the most used is sulfate (SO−2
4 )
(+2.010)(Harris, 2007). These two ions can be added as one (Na2SO4, a fairly inexpensive
salt).
As H+ and OH−are also electrolytes, their production in an acid/base generation setup
will increase conductivity as their concentrations increase.
On the availability of ions in RAS
Most RAS are freshwater, and freshwater by itself does not hold suﬃcient ions to function as a
good conductor of electricity without added electrolytes. The extent to whether the addition
of ions is necessary in freshwater RAS for acid/base electrolysis (both for conductivity and
for pH adjustment purposes), should depend upon the concentration of ions as a result of
ﬁsh metabolic activity. This could be determined experimentally. There is likely no need to
add electrolytes to RAS in saline water, as it is already highly conductive at 47.9 mS/cm at
20 ◦C using the formula from Janz and Singer (1975).
Other operating parameters of import for electrolysis
Little research has been performed on the use of electrolysis to generate H+ and OH−. As
such, the importance of a variety of parameters are hard to quantify, and informed guesses
must come from sources exploring similar processes such as the production of hydrogen, or
the use of electrolysis under similar operating conditions.
19
==PAGE:37==
Electrode material is all-important in electrolysis.
An electrode with an undesirable
reduction potential may inadvertently donate electrons, becoming oxidized, reducing the
longevity of the electrode. The ions released through oxidation may also be toxic if, for
example, a copper electrode is used. Oxidation and reduction of electrodes may be useful
for some purposes and will clearly not be so for others. For acid/base reactions, chemically
inert electrodes are likely the most desirable as they do not interfere with the main reaction
that is the splitting of water.
Basic requirements for an electrode is that it should conduct electricity well, corrode
slowly (non-corrosivity is especially important under highly saline conditions), and be cheap
(Wrana, 2010). Boron doped diamond (BDD) is a good example of a very expensive electrode
(Martinez-Huitle and Ferro, 2006), and RuO2 an example of a cheap, corrosive-resistant one
(Mook et al., 2011). The purpose of an electrode must also be taken into account. For pH
adjustment, production of acid at the anode may be enough and the cathode could instead
function as a plating surface for the removal of toxic compounds, such as copper (Spinu et
al., 1998). For the purposes of bacteria removal, a high speciﬁc surface area and biologically
compatible electrode material is an additional must (Wrana, 2010).
Current density and voltage is likely to also be of some importance. Buddhi et al., (2006)
observed a non-linear increase in hydrogen production with an increase in voltage, (4.5 in-
creased production with a 9 volt increase from 3-12 volt). Mook et al., (2011) observed a
linear increase in the removal of TAN, nitrate and TOC with an increase in current density.
The importance of current density was also underscored by Feng et al (2003).
Alkalinity may be of some import as high alkalinity may manifest as unwanted side
reactions - Hasson et al. (2010) reported precipitation of calcium carbonate and magnesium
hydroxide in a high alkalinity solution.
Electrode degradation
Electrodes that are highly resistant may last for a relatively long time - more than a year -
before signiﬁcant degradation impairs function for some applications (Morimoto et al., 1999).
Rabah et al. (1991) reported on the eﬀect that a number of parameters had on the wear of
graphite anodes in brine electrolysis. In particular, corrosion rate should increase linearly
with current density. Corrosion rate should decrease non-linearly with brine molarity and,
somewhat surprisingly, with increasing chloride concentration.
The authors ascribe this
to the adsorption of chloride on the surface of the graphite anode, creating a blanketing
eﬀect preventing further degradation. Especially interesting for the purposes of acid/base
generation is that corrosion will also decrease in a negative parabolic relationship from neutral
pH (Rabah et al., 1991). Graphite anodes may be attractive for the purposes of electrolysis
in sea-water because of the observed pH and chloride eﬀect.

The otherwise unwanted and hazardous production of chlorine gas as a side reaction in the
electrolysis of salt-water RAS may be exploited to inactivate bacteria, reducing or perhaps

even eliminating the need for other kinds of equipment, such as the addition of chemical

electrolysis module must take this domination eﬀect into account.

As the reaction shows, a lower pH will move ammonia towards ammonium, which is
far less toxic to ﬁsh. Because of the possibility of pH swings in RAS, ammonia is conven-
tionally removed through nitriﬁcation in a bioﬁlter, because it is acutely toxic at fairly low
concentrations. Electrolysis have however also been used in waste-water treatment for the
removal of total ammonia nitrogen (TAN), and a number of studies have reported on the
percentage TAN removal involved with the process, which is generally very high (Gendel and
Lahav, 2013; Li and Liu, 2009; Mook et al., 2011; Vlysiddes et al., 2002). Key advantages
of the process as compared to conventional removal are high energy eﬃciencies, minimal
sludge generation, rapid start-up, and is simple to operate (Vlyssides et al., 2002; Mook et
al., 2011), while the key downside is the order of a magnitude higher energy requirements
(Vlyssides et al., 2002). The desired end product when treating TAN electrochemically is
the inert gas N2, but this process needs a large amount of chloride ions to proceed (Gendel
and Lahav, 2013; Li and Liu, 2009; Mook et al., 2011), otherwise, nitrate is merely oxidized
back to ammonia. The removal of TAN by electrolysis may only be economically viable
in salt-water RAS for this reason, and in freshwater RAS, indicates the need to place an
acid/base electrolysis generating unit after TAN removing bioﬁlter for optimal eﬃciency.
Evaluation
Electrode choice, current density, voltage, electrolytic concentration/salinity, electrolyte
composition, acid/base generation unit placement in RAS and unit design are all factors
that are likely to inﬂuence energetic and economic eﬃciency of the electrolysis process for
the purposes of pH adjustment.
Dependent on parameters, a design that does multiple
things in stages may be interesting - such as the removal of TAN before pH adjustment.
21
==PAGE:39==
Alkalinity, initial pH and temperature may also be of import. Interaction eﬀects between
above parameters may also play a role but investigating these thoroughly will be beyond the
scope of this thesis. A design that could simultaneously lower pH in a volume of water at
the anode, then carry this volume through a degassing unit, before increasing it again at the
cathode, may be very useful for as a way of improving the eﬃciency of CO2 removal and
alkalinity adjustment in RAS while keeping pH at levels required for ﬁsh physiology.

Two (2), testing the inﬂuence of this adjustment alongside a variety of other parameters to

Materials and methods
The thesis was divided into two main avenues of investigation: (1) the design and testing of

2.1.1
Design of module: an iterative process
The ﬁrst part of the process of the thesis was gaining an understanding of, and then designing,
a viable working model or unit for the practical separation of the half-cell reactions when
water is split. This working model was thought to be useful as part of a system where water
in a RAS would be diverted a) ﬁrst over the anode of the unit, lowering pH, b) degassing,
c) then raising pH of the same water again to normal levels, before d) letting the water
back into the rest of the RAS system. Doing this would have a theoretical twin beneﬁt
of degassing and lowering alkalinity, as well as functioning as a reservoir of carbon dioxide
for non-degassed water. The design of the electrolysis module was an iterative process, and
progressed as understanding of the technology and the reactions taking place grew over time.
The initial attempts at designs will be described in a qualitative manner. Speciﬁcs are not
necessary as no data from these designs were used in the results or conclusions of this thesis.
These descriptions have been included solely to provide evidence for the working process
of the thesis and the author’s capability to adapt and form new conclusions based on prior
experience.
First attempt
Before beginning the process of this thesis, the author had no prior experience with electro-
chemistry or indeed much laboratory experience at all. As such, progress was slow and error-
prone. The initial idea for the electrolysis module came from a cursory reading of Spinu et
al., (1998), and some fundamental working through of basic redox processes from university-
level chemical introduction course-work related notes. As such, initially, and erroneously,
an understanding of the basic processes involved in the splitting of water amounted to an
expectation that the processes taking place at the anode and cathode were invariably the
following.

author to believe that the pH should drop in any volume of water where the main reaction

through the electrolytic cell and back into the volume. Nickel (Ni) electrodes and a porous
membrane separating the electrodes was used at this time. A large amount of some kind of
precipitate was observed as well as bubbling and some electrode degradation, which at least
conﬁrmed that some redox reactions were taking place and that all equipment functioned as
expected. However, no consistent pH changes were observed and, after further studying, the
design was abandoned.
The most important realization at this point was the necessity of changing the setup so
that, theoretically, the half-cell reactions involved could be separated and true pH change
could be achieved.

Final design
The ﬁnal design was made on the basis of the assumed necessity to completely diﬀerentiate

some consultation with DTU Energy. It was hoped that at least initially, the concentration
of H+ to other cations would be so low as to prevent migration across the membrane. A
simple technical diagram of this design is shown in ﬁgure 8.

slowly be pumped over the anode of the electrolytic housing unit, the other over the cathode.

was placed. Logging was to take place with an oxygen probe and a pH meter. This design
proved the concept of the idea as oxygen was shown to evolve at the water in contact with
the anode and pH changed rapidly in the beaker associated with the cathode. But a major
problem was that pH still changed non-signiﬁcantly in the anode beaker. Another big issue
was that precipitates of unknown chemical composition were observed in both volumes of

water, and when prying the housing unit apart after troubleshooting, damage would be

an electrolyte was still used at this point to improve conductivity. The author concluded
that the reactiveness of the Cl−ion was likely the cause of the issues, and a non-reactive

It took some time to realize, but these were problems of water pressure diﬀerences. When
the water entered the housing unit, it would pass through whichever side had the lesser
amount of pressure. The reason for these pressure diﬀerences were the biggest issue that
remained to be solved before consistent results were liable to be achieved. It turned out that
part of the reason was that the peristaltic pumping heads did not have the same number of
internal cylinders attached. The pumping head would rotate and squeeze the plastic tubing
whenever a cylinder rolled past it. One unit had 4 and the other had 6 cylinders. This meant
that the ﬂow of liquid through each pumping head was diﬀerent and resulted in pressure
diﬀerences. Another reason was gas production. Once power is supplied to the systems, gas
bubbles form and this had an observeable eﬀect on the water pressure. The solution was
to change the peristaltic pumping heads so that cylinder number and size were consistent,
and to change plastic tubing elevation so pressure could be regulated by pumping height.
This did the trick. A ﬁnal issue was the lag time associated with measuring pH, but this

This might seem to indicate that a basic premise of the thesis has failed - that of the
necessity of a cation-exchange membrane for the process to function. That is however not

electrolytic unit is so short that the produced OH−and H+ ions are transported away
before they have a chance to diﬀuse over, so that in eﬀect, other ions are used to facilitate
the process. Initially, this works well, but looking at Figures 13 and 12, the rate of change
in pH actually slows quite rapidly after a while. For small scale laboratory demonstrations
such as this, the separation of ﬂows is a viable way to show the feasibility of the technology.
However, the change in pH is happening in a volume of water that is 800 mL over a 30
minutes period. That is, the production of acids and bases are miniscule in this kind of
setup. To scale the process up to make a diﬀerence in the volumes of water used in a RAS,
you would have to have a much larger unit with two pumps sending ﬂows of water over
an anode and a cathode rapidly and hoping that the separation of ﬂows scales to a much
larger volume of water. The pumping costs would be exorbitant and there is no guarantee it
would work. A passive setup with well mixed volumes of water where ions diﬀuse passively
over a cation-exchange membrane is much more likely to work, and is the reason why it was
considered interesting to test in the ﬁrst place.

3.1.1
Overview
This part of the study investigated the CO2 degassing performance of a cascade column at
several alkalinities (0, 4, 6, 8 and 10 TA (mmol/L)) and salinities - 0o/oo, 17.5o/oo and 35o/oo .
The 0 TA trial was labelled the acid reduction trial, as it reduced total alkalinity by the
addition of HCl. Trials were carried out between March and June 2019 at a RAS research
facility located in Hirtshals, Denmark. Trials were carried out using a self-constructed cas-
cade column. Time for a full pass of water through the system was roughly 10 minutes. The
cascade column was inside a small laboratory with an unknown ventilation rate. The water
temperature for all trials was 13.1 ± 3.0 ◦C (maximum deviation). A ﬁrst set of trials were
conducted at salinity 0o/oo using tap-water from the local municipal water source. This wa-
ter used added alkalinity for some trials. Alkalinity was added by the addition of NaHCO3
(Sodium Bicarbonate) (Ciech Soda Deutschland GmbH and Co.) to a side unit that was
stirred and pumped into the cascade column, corresponding to stepwise-increments in total
alkalinity of 2 mmol/L. A secondary set of trials was conducted at 35o/oo and 17.5o/oo by
the use of saline water pumped directly in from the nearby North Sea. Dilution to half
strength seawater was done by dilution using the municipal water source, assumed to have a
salinity of 0. Alkalinity of the water was measured by laboratory assistants at DTU Aqua.
Total alkalinity of the freshwater was determined to be 3.9 mmol/L, and the saltwater 2.52
mmol/L.

Experiment initiation
Initiation of a degassing trial began by the complete emptying of a previous trial.
The
water pump was removed from the straight pipe inlet at the bottom of the sump, and the
water allowed to drain for 40 minutes. The valve would then be turned. The unit was then
ﬁlled with ≈222 L of water by ﬁlling the circular tank three times, and pumping a preset
height (0.47 m) into the cascade column. For alkalinity trials, the sodium bicarbonate would
be added after weighing on a bench scale (Defender 3000 XtremeW (readability 0.02 kg),
Ohaus) to the circular tank. The tank would be stirred using a wooden ladle for 5 minutes
before pumping, to ensure the added sodium bicarbonate would dissolve. After the addition
of water, the water pump would be reattached to the bottom of the sump at the straight
pipe inlet. CO2 would then be dosed into the tank by opening a valve on a pressurized CO2
ﬂask (Air Liquide, Denmark) for ﬁve seconds total. The CO2 ﬂask was connected by plastic
tubing to an airstone that was permanently sunk into the sump in the tank. The cascade
column would then be left alone until CO2 readings became stable, which were designated
as when sequential measurements would only change every 2 minutes. This would occur
within 40-120 minutes. The air pump would then be turned on, and logging preparations
initialized. The valve at the straight pipe inlet would then be turned, and the water pump
turned on and set to power level 5. Carbon dioxide, pH and temperature readings would
then be logged until CO2 concentrations reached a level < 2 mg/L. The process was repeated
x2 so that three replicates would be had. A new process could then be initiated, adjusting
for salinity or alkalinity as necessary, otherwise procedures ran completely similarly.

probably the likely explanation as all other factors indicate that the column should be very
eﬃcient indeed: 1) There is a larger fall height in the study (1.88 m), 2) The surface area
is fairly large (898.4 m2), and 3) the hydraulic loading is very small as well (0.0014 m3 h−1
m−2). Or in other words, the amount of time to degas is large, the amount of area available
for splitting water droplets is large, and the amount of water present that might recombine
into larger droplets is small.

ticeably over time. For the saline trial, 20 percent of the carbon is available for degassing at
experiment start, dropping rapidly close to 0 after 60 minutes. This is even more noticeable
in the alkalinity trial, where the amount available is slightly above 10 percent, dropping to
0 at around 40 minutes. What these two trials show is that, after running the degassing
unit for a very short time, a lot of eﬀort will be expended to degas relatively low amounts of
the total carbon locked in the system. In terms of the carbon dioxide present in the trials,
here expressed in mmol L−1, the concentration of CO2 in the pH reduced trial is also, as
to be expected, much higher at experiment start. Note however as explained earlier, that
the probe becomes unreliable at concentrations above 50 mg/L, and so the actual amount of
CO2 present may have been much higher than what can be shown in ﬁgure 26. Over time,
all three trials near each other in terms of total concentrations of CO2 present - but almost
all the carbon will have been degassed from the pH reduced trial, and very little in the two
others.
If the completely TA reduced water and almost completely CO2 reduced water is rein-
troduced back into a RAS it should act as a reservoir of both. For an equivalent volume of
water, because of the way the carbonate system works, the TA should then halve for the
other body of water and the concentrations of CO2 should mix. Because of the halving of
TA, the concentration of carbon dioxide should also halve, all other things being equal (see
section 1.3) which means that the reintroduction of completely CO2 reduced and alkalinity
reduced water has a profound eﬀect in that the concentration of carbon dioxide remains low.
Had only the carbon dioxide concentration been reduced, and the TA untouched, equilibrium
reactions would increase concentrations back toward a higher level than degassed to
The ﬁnal takeaway is then this: if pH can be suﬃciently lowered so that TA drops to
or near zero, almost all carbon can be found in gas form in the system, and can then be
removed. The now completely degassed water can then be pumped back into the RAS and
function as a reservoir for carbon dioxide and alkalinity. This may be desirable in some
systems where alkalinities are high enough so that dropping the alkalinity does not impact
bioﬁlter activity, as previously discussed.

Suggestions for further research
There are plenty of things that could and should be further investigated to assess the potential
of electrolysis as an acid/degassing unit. The ions present in a RAS and their impact on
eﬃciency and any side-reactions that may occur in saline or freshwater are of great interest.
Investigating it would be a time consuming process but may help identifying what electrodes
are best to use, in order to better catalyze the process and improve eﬃciency. Improving the
longevity of cation-exchange membranes are likely also necessary to prevent costly, regular
replacement. It would also be of interest to see if placement of the electrolysis module has
any eﬀect on the eﬃciency of the process. It may be that pre-bioﬁlter placement is less
eﬃcient because of the potential to remove TAN by the process, (See also section 1.6 for
a more thorough look at TAN and its eﬀects on electrolysis). This was not investigated
by this study due to time constraints. More precisely determining an optimal voltage is
also of interest, to improve eﬃciency. Determining if the ﬂocculation of particles that were
observed in the pre-bioﬁlter trial is a result of a signiﬁcant amount of colloids being removed
from solution, and if the production of oxygen can reduce or eliminate the need for oxygen
injection elsewhere is also something that can help determine if the electrolysis process can
be cost-viable down the road.

unit and its importance for degassing.

5
Conclusion
Electrolysis proved itself to be a technically viable way to produce acids and bases in a RAS,
with a number of potential side-beneﬁts such as the production of oxygen and the removal of
particulates. For the experimental setup in this study, a cation-exchange membrane was not
strictly necessary for the process to occur, but this is unlikely to be the case for a large scale
setup, which would not separate ﬂow of water over anodes and cathodes and would need
a physical barrier to prevent produced OH−1 and H+ from eliminating any pH changes.
All tested parameters except for temperature had statistically signiﬁcant impacts on the
production of acids and bases, and especially voltage seems to be a deciding parameter.
RAS facilities are likely to have more than enough ions present to facilitate the process of
electrolysis, so no dosing with electrolytes are likely to be necessary, neither in saline nor
freshwater. The process of electrolysis is also likely to be a cheaper and more precise way of
controlling pH than chemical dosing with acids, but only in freshwater RAS as production
eﬃciency in saline water was very low.
Because of this, it is unlikely that the process
can be made economically viable in saline water. Alkalinity may be a determining factor
(economically) as well, as water with a high TA translated into higher operating costs. The
use of the technology will also reduce alkalinity and this needs to be taken into account in
a large scale RAS so that bioﬁlter alkalinity need is still being met. The degassing unit
was ineﬃcient, and may have been so for a variety of reasons, such as poor ventilation and
that the unit was likely not air-tight, reducing the eﬃciency of counter-current ﬂowing air in
the unit. It was diﬃcult to properly assess how salinity and alkalinity aﬀected the process
of degassing because of the methodological problems, but saline water was shown to be
less likely to be eﬃciently degassed than freshwater, which is in accordance with previous
research. For all intents and purposes, a degassing unit with an attached electrolysis module
is currently more expensive to operate than not. In the future, this may change with increased
research focused on improving the eﬃciency of the electrolysis module and the potential of
value-added beneﬁts such as the simultaneous production of oxygen.
