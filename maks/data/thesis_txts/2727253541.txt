Abstract
This MSc thesis deals with the development of a representative yaw controller and its
implementation to both bottom-fixed and floating wind turbine simulations. This yaw con-
troller approach includes two operating modes that make use of moving averages of the
misalignment error to determine the commencement and the completion of the yawing
sequence. Two different averaging filters are compared, while a feature to account for
small direction changes that can last for long time periods is developed (second operat-
ing mode). The defined controller is evaluated against simple direction changes, where
the time windows are examined with respect to the response of the yaw sequence.
The yaw controller is then used in bottom-fixed and floating simulations to capture the
response of the IEA 15MW RWT in varying wind speed and direction conditions. Mea-
surements from a met mast are obtained and 1 hour samples are selected based on
high standard deviation of the wind direction, representing a realistic wind input.
The response of the implemented control system showed that it can effectively align
the rotor with the mean wind direction, eliminating the yaw offset. The selection of
the time windows in the averaging filter is therefore considered sufficient with respect
to not triggering a yaw action too early and not overshooting after reaching zero yaw
error. A comparison between the bottom-fixed and floating simulations revealed higher
moments in the tower-base for the floating application. The difference is attributed to the
platform’s motion, which increases the gravitational moment-arm around the tower-base
position. Blade moments are found identical, while yaw-bearing moments showed minor
differences, mainly in the pitch direction, showing that the yaw movement of the rotor is
not inducing higher yaw-loads in the floating case. The floating simulations also revealed
that the tuning of the default pitch controller is insufficient for the floating case, leading
to pitch instability above rated wind speeds. An analysis of different potential settings
resulted in a new tuning that alleviates the instability, although making the controller
slower and introducing uncertainty in the results obtained.

1.1
Theoritical Background
Wind energy, and especially offshore wind energy has been named as one of the most
promising renewable energy sources that can drive the increase of electricity generation,
needed in order to achieve the ambitious target of net-zero CO2 emissions by 2050 [1].
Despite the impact of COVID-19, the global offshore wind industry had its second-best
year ever in 2020 [2]. In Europe, 2.5GW net offshore capacity were installed by the
end of 2022, corresponding to 306 new offshore wind turbines connected to the grid
and a total installed capacity of 30.3GW [3]. The development of the annual installed
capacity, as well as the cumulative installed capacity in Europe is depicted in figure 1.1.
Nevertheless, there is still a lot of ground to cover if carbon neutrality is to be achieved in
the specified time frame. According to [2], more than 2000GW of offshore wind installed
capacity is required by 2050 in the net-zero emissions scenario.
Figure 1.1: Annual and cumulative installed capacity (GW) in Europe [3].
Offshore wind turbines require robust substructures to support them in the challenging
marine environment. These substructures can be broadly categorized into two types:
bottom-fixed and floating. Each type has distinct characteristics and is suitable for spe-
cific offshore conditions. Bottom-fixed substructures is the most common foundation
type for today’s offshore wind turbines and are typically used in shallow water depths up
to around 50 meters. It is indicative that 79% of all wind turbines in Europe connected
to the grid are located in the North Sea [3], known for its shallow waters where the us-
age of bottom-fixed foundations is favorable. In addition, for the majority of wind farms,
either built or under construction, the water depth in the area of installment does not

usually exceed 50m. As a result, it is not a surprise that bottom-fixed substructures own
the largest share of foundation types used. There are three main types of bottom-fixed
substructures, also depicted in figure 1.2:
• Monopile: Monopiles are large steel tubes driven vertically into the seabed. They
are suitable for relatively shallow waters and are the most commonly used sub-
structure for offshore wind turbines. The wind turbine tower is mounted on top of
the monopile.
• Gravity-Based: Gravity-based substructures are large concrete structures that rely
on their own weight to remain stable. They are commonly used in areas with soft
seabeds and can be placed on the seabed by filling them with water or ballast
material.
• Jacket: Jacket substructures consist of a lattice framework of steel tubular mem-
bers that are also anchored to the seabed. They provide excellent stability and
are suitable for deeper waters where monopiles may not be feasible.
Figure 1.2: The most commonly used types of bottom-fixed wind turbine support struc-
tures [4].
The main advantage of bottom-fixed substructures is their stability, which allows for
larger turbines and higher capacity. However, they are limited to shallow and moder-
ate water depths and may face challenges in areas with complex seabed conditions or
deeper waters.
On the other hand, floating substructures offer the privilege of eliminating the depth
constraint, being able to access abundant wind resources in deep offshore areas, and
could therefore contribute the most to meet the carbon neutrality target. The greater
the distance from the shore, the higher is the possibility of using larger areas to mitigate
wake effects, hence yielding better capacity factors. A typical example is the 30MW
Hywind Scotland wind farm, which achieved an average capacity factor of about 65%

during three consecutive months [5], and 56% in the first two operational years [6]. Other
advantages of moving further away from the shore, such as less visual impact and lower
noise limitations just add to the necessity for even more floating offshore wind farms in
deeper waters. Locations found in the Northwest Europe (Ireland, Norway), US Pacific
(California), Japan, South Korea and the Mediterranean Sea are some typical examples
of deep-water regions offering great wind resources, thus opening the way for utilizing
floating substructures more widely. However, they are more complex and expensive to
build compared to bottom-fixed substructures. Even though the operational readiness of
floaters has reached a very high level, the levelized cost of energy (LCoE) still remains a
major bottleneck. Current LCoE of floating offshore wind is much higher than the bottom-
fixed one, although studies suggest that it could drop to the same levels by 2030 [7].
Therefore, there is still much space for cost reduction in areas such as O&M, installation
and assembly, regarding the floating offshore wind. There are several types of floating
substructures, although the most popular can be seen in figure 1.3 and are as follows:
• Spar Buoy: Spar buoys are long cylindrical structures with most of their mass
located below the water surface. They have a large draft to provide stability and
a platform on top to accommodate the wind turbine.
• Semi-Submersible: Semi-submersible platforms have multiple floating hulls con-
nected by columns or trusses. They can adjust their draft to adapt to different
water depths and wave conditions.
• Tension Leg Platform (TLP): TLPs use vertical tensioned tethers to hold the struc-
ture in place. They provide stability by limiting vertical motions and are often used
in areas with moderate water depths.
• Barge: Barge-based substructures involve converting large barges or vessels into
stable platforms for mounting wind turbines.
These barges are ballasted and
equipped with mooring systems to keep them in place. The wind turbine is in-
stalled on top of the barge, which provides stability and buoyancy.
To be able to maximize power production and thus capacity factors, turbines should al-
ways be aligned with the wind direction. Alternatively, not only the wind energy capture
will not be effective, but also the major components of the turbine (blades, drive train
etc) will suffer higher loads, leading to significantly reduced lifetime for the wind turbine.
It is found that over 50% of turbines operate with more than 6 deg of static yaw misalign-
ment [9]. Regarding the energy production, the theoretical percentage of power loss in
respect to the yaw angle error can be seen in figure 1.4. It is apparent that the more the
misalignment angle increases, the less power is extracted from the wind.
Consequently, and as the yaw error must be as low as possible in operating conditions,
the need of reliable yaw control systems is of high importance in the newest offshore
wind turbines, where the rotor sizes have increased. Generally, a reliable control system
would enable higher energy production under harsh environments and could additionally
contribute to bringing down the operational expenditure (OPEX) by reducing the loads

1.2
Motivation
Floating wind turbine systems are much more complex in comparison to conventional
bottom-fixed turbines. Due to the motion of the floating substructure, there is a higher
probability of severe dynamic instabilities occurrence, especially in deep offshore ar-
eas with complex meteorological conditions or, for instance, when a controller is poorly
tuned. Concerning the yaw controller, the risk of yaw over-utilization is apparent in float-
ing wind turbines, where the platform dynamics heavily affect the movement of the sys-
tem. It can be concluded that a well-functioning control system is imperative to achieve
the best performance and safety for a floating wind turbine, and can also reduce the
loads leading to cost reduction in floating wind energy.
Since the yaw controller has been identified as an important part of wind turbine sys-
tems, it is highly important that is incorporated in aero-elastic simulations. High fidelity
software is used in both academia and industry so as to evaluate novel design con-
cepts of wind turbines in as much realistic conditions as possible. The performance of
a turbine can heavily vary when exposed in wind direction that changes in time. Fur-
thermore, there is often the case where the turbine response is studied in load cases
with extreme direction change, such as gusts which may have a relatively long rise time.
Hence, by introducing a suitable yaw controller in such software, capable of accounting
for wind direction changes, and being used along with the other control systems, would
also offer a more realistic wind turbine response.

1.3
Literature Review
Wind turbine loading under yaw misalignment has been identified as a critical issue in the
wind energy sector and drew a lot of attention. Many studies have been done to assess
the turbine’s components loads under yaw offset conditions [11, 12], but also to analyse
the aero-elastic performance of the blades [13]. It is therefore apparent that yaw con-
trollers contribute the most to reducing this type of loading, by turning the rotor-nacelle
assembly into the mean wind direction, whilst also increase the power production, by
making the energy capture more efficient.
However, yaw control in conjunction with loads has not been extensively investigated
in the literature. That is a bit contradictory with the fact that, as mentioned above, yaw
control is an important part of state-of-the-art wind turbines. Several studies have been
made on different yaw control strategies that can be applied to better estimate the yaw
error. A certain part of them investigates how the proposed control schemes could
potentially reduce the structural or damage equivalent fatigue loads on wind turbines or
on optimizing the energy capture, while other focus only on the accuracy improvement
of measuring the wind direction.
In 2000, Ekelund [14] investigated the potential for active attenuation of structural dy-
namic load oscillations by continuous controlling the torque of the yaw servo. The main
Yaw control for floating wind turbines
5
==PAGE:12==
objective was not to design a fully operational controller, but to discover the magni-
tude of the possible improvement, compared to a conventional yaw system. Therefore,
emphasis was not given on control schemes to measure the wind direction effectively,
however the dependence between yaw dynamics and tower motion was acknowledged.
A method for improving yaw alignment using a spinner based LIDAR was developed
by Kragh et al. [15]. The focus of this study was to exploit the advantages of LIDAR
wind speed measurement technology in order to accurately estimate the yaw error. In
2012, Kragh et al. [16] assessed the performance of an operating wind turbine, with
an addition of a correction scheme using existing measurements in its yaw controller.
The improved yaw controller design introduced an error correction on the misalignment
angle based on the rotor speed. Yaw errors decreased significantly by applying this
correction, variations in blade-root bending moments were found smaller and finally,
the power yield below rated wind speed raised 1-5%. Nevertheless, the amount of
available data posed uncertainties in the results, triggering the need of gathering more
experimental data as a future work. One year later, and after including more data in
the analysis, it is shown that with the correction applied, better yaw alignment can be
achieved and the power capture could be significantly raised in the below-rated domain
(could reach up to 16%), but the blade root loads are increased in the same region [17].
Bossanyi et al. [18] presented a method for tuning control algorithms and quantified
their effects on energy capture, control activity and fatigue loading. They used long-
term simulations to illustrate how the trade-off between yaw activity and energy capture
is affected by yaw error thresholds and averaging times. They also used the results
obtained to demonstrate how to take into account these effects when calculating lifetime
fatigue loads. Although studies during that time had been focusing on improving the
yaw alignment to increase the power capture, on an alternative approach, Kragh et al.
[19] investigated the potential of alleviating blade load variations induced by wind shear
through yaw misalignment. It is found that blade loads can be reduced in significantly
high levels, however, at the cost of increasing the load variations on the non-rotating
parts of the turbine.
While typical controllers heavily rely on the wind vane measurements to steer the turbine
into the mean wind direction, using forecasting methods can potentially eliminate the
time gap created between the actual wind direction change and the controller’s action
command. In 2015, Hure et al. [20] proposed a new yaw control system based on
short-term wind predictions, designed to achieve maximisation of power production and
minimisation of structural loads. A comparison between the suggested and the baseline
controller in the aero-elastic tool BLADED [21] showed reduction of damage equivalent
loads and increase of power production. In a similar way, Chen et al. [22] designed
a novel yaw control scheme which was able to utilize LiDAR signals together with a
probabilistic LSTM neural network (Long Short Term Memory), to accurately forecast
wind speed and direction, thus achieving improved power capture without increasing
yaw duty.
There are also some research activities focusing on yaw control systems which do not

==PAGE:13==
require the wind direction measurements in order to decide to act. Farret et al. [23]
proposed a control strategy based on the output generated power, without making any
use of wind direction measurement system. Karakasis et al. [24] used the error between
the rotor speed determined by the tip speed ratio and the real rotor speed commanded
by a power point tracking control system for active yaw control. By implementing such
control schemes, it is intended to avoid inaccuracies by the wind measurement system
which is usually located on top of the nacelle, exposed in the vortex flow downstream
of the blades.
Finally, little attention has been given on investigating extreme loads when using a yaw
controller in conjunction with an aero-elastic code. Hannesdóttir et al. [25] introduced a
yaw controller to be incorporated in aero-servo-elastic simulations in order to investigate
the load implications of extreme coherent gusts with direction change. Extreme loads
were documented, while a comparison between maximum loads observed on the blade
root with and without the yaw controller also took place.
In the present MSc thesis, the yaw controller proposed in [25] is evaluated and updated
with an extra feature to account for small direction changes that can last for a long time.
In addition, it is integrated in the aero-elastic software HAWC2 [26] for simulating the
response of the IEA UMaine reference wind turbine [27], both when is mounted on a
bottom-fixed substructure and on a floating platform. Especially in the floating case,
the substructure’s motion is expected to have implications both in the wind turbine’s
loading and in the ability of the yaw controller to identify the dominant wind direction
with accuracy. The ability of the controller to capture effectively the mean wind direction
is evaluated and specific load channels are analysed. Specific maximum loads are also
assessed for selected load channels.
It must be acknowledged on this point that a similar thesis ran in parallel with this project
by Mathias Valentin Hemmingsen [28]. The scope was that each of the two projects
would provide an input for the wind turbine simulations. In this study, the yaw controller
is mainly the concerning part, while in [28] the wind input was treated. Once those two
were defined, then a similar set of simulations were performed, for which both provided
contributions in the HAWC2 set-up. Similarities are therefore expected in the results
section of both reports, while it is chosen that different simulations are analysed to iden-
tify the main drivers of specific extreme loads captured.
The following text is organised as follows: In section 2, the yaw controller methodology
is presented, along with the wind input used. Information on the software and on the
turbine models used for the scope of this thesis are also documented. In section 3, the
designed yaw controller is evaluated in defined edge cases, while its basic operation
is showcased using two misalignment offsets. In section 4, results are documented
for bottom-fixed and floating simulations. A comparison is drawn to identify if the yaw
controller is able to follow the mean wind direction in both cases (fixed and floating),
while wind turbine loads are also analysed for specific load channels. Finally, in section
5, conclusions and suggestions for future work are listed.

2.1
Wind turbine models
Reference wind turbines that reflect the state-of-the-art of current wind energy technol-
ogy are important for proper evaluation of the innovative methods in wind turbine de-
sign, while also enable the advanced research and educational usage, on the grounds
that are publicly shared. Numerous reference wind turbines have been produced over
the recent years, to capture the evolution in commercial technology. Two of them, the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 10MW and the International Energy Agency
(IEA) 15MW will be used in the present study. Note that to be able to simulate the yaw
movement of the turbine, the tower-top part is not considered fixed to the tower in both
models; a bearing (bearing2) constraint has been placed between them to represent the
yaw-bearing, allowing the rotation of the former body with respect to the latter.
2.1.1
DTU 10MW RWT
The DTU 10MW reference wind turbine (RWT) [29] was designed as part of the Light
Rotor project, in which DTU Wind Energy and Vestas cooperated. The scope of the
project was to optimize the design of the blades to increase the stiffness and overall
performance of the rotor taking into account both aerodynamic, aero-servo-elastic and
structural considerations. As a result, a 10MW reference rotor was designed, however,
in order to understand its performance and its interaction with the other components, the
entire wind turbine system had to be present. Consequently, an entirely new wind turbine
was established. Apart from the blades, the DTU 10MW RWT was developed as a direct
upscale of the NREL 5MW reference turbine [30]. The blade design was based on new
airfoils, while new aero-servo-elastic and structural methods were also developed during
the project in order to establish an advanced design. Certain parameters are included
in table 2.1 and more detailed specification regarding the overall design process and
properties can be found in [29].

2.5
Yaw controller
Commercial controllers designed by wind manufacturers are expected to be quite ad-
vanced, being capable of handling complex conditions by its numerous features. The
yaw controller developed during this thesis instead relies on two main operating modes
(also called yaw modes hereafter). The first one (yaw mode 1) is used to handle large
yaw errors while the second one (yaw mode 2) is designed for small yaw offsets. In both
yaw modes, the yaw error is filtered by the use of moving averages, which can also de-
termine the start and the stop of the yawing sequence. In the following subsections,
two moving average filters are discussed and compared while the control strategies of
the yaw modes are presented. At this point, it must be highlighted that the design of
the yaw controller was chosen to be as much universal as it could be, meaning that its
scope is to handle realistic wind input (turbulent wind with high variation of wind direc-
tion). However, since it is decided that the wind input does not incorporate turbulence
(section 2.4), the differences of the two sliding averages regarding the filtering of the
input signal are expected to be negligible and their performance on that part (filtering)
cannot be properly assessed. On the contrary, the averaging techniques will be evalu-
ated based on their yawing response (their ability to force a yawing sequence, chapter
3), where similarities in their behaviour are expected as well.

2.5.2
Yaw modes
The yaw controller designed during this study includes two identical features to handle
yaw misalignment. The scope of both modes is to trigger the turbine to yaw under
misalignment error with the use of moving averages. While yaw mode 1 has been
developed to serve as a general highly flexible feature, meaning that it could possibly
be the only feature used in this controller implementation, yaw mode 2 supplements
the function of the whole system, making it more efficient. Interaction between modes
is limited; More specifically, yaw mode 2 can be engaged only when the turbine is not
yawing. On the other hand, if a yawing sequence has been started because of mode 2,
mode 1 can intervene and change the yaw demand angle imposed by mode 2, without
stopping the yaw process.

set to 5 degrees in the example above, a given yaw error of 4 degrees is deployed to

action for an error that will not be present for a long time. Situations where the turbine

must not be over-utilized. 2) The stop averaging window must be very small, since an
overshoot after a zero yaw error is highly possible, especially for small misalignment
angles. Using a short time window allows to capture effectively the information of the
yaw error signal, stopping the yawing sequence in the right time. Thresholds are defined
to 2 and 1 degrees for start and stop respectively. In figure 3.2, the start and stop

4
Results & discussion
In this section, the results obtained by the bottom-fixed and floating simulations are
discussed. A comparison is presented to identify if the yaw controller operates similarly
when the turbine is land-based or floating. Specific load channels are analysed and
differences along with their main drivers are highlighted.
4.1
Parameters selected
The yaw controller parameters are the same as used in the evaluation of the system in
section 3 and are listed in table 3.1. The reasoning for their selection can also be found
in the same section. The yaw mechanism is modelled as a second order dynamical
system with a frequency of 5hz and a damping ratio of 0.7. No limits are imposed
for the maximum and minimum yaw angle, allowing a full rotation of the system, while
the maximum yaw speed and acceleration are constrained (0.4 deg/s and 1.5 deg/s2
respectively) [25].
In the beginning, the same default controller tuning is used for the land-based and the
floating simulations. If the onshore setting induces instabilities in the floating case, then
a different tuning will be used to make the analysis reasonable.
The usage of the wind speed bins as well as the wind input are described in detail at
section 2.4. Each simulation in every wind speed bin and for both bottom-fixed and float-
ing substructure is 1 hour long plus the transient time (400 seconds), while a timestep
of 0.01s is used. The statistics calculated and the PSDs constructed hereafter are not
taking into account the transient time. The rotor-nacelle and the wind direction are pre-
sented in the range between -180 and 180 degrees for better visualization. It should
be noted that a forced yawing sequence is taking place during the beginning of each
simulation, so that at the end of the transitional time, an approximately zero yaw error
angle has been reached. The duration selection of the transient period is such that the
turbine has enough time to align with the wind direction.

whether the yaw controller is operating as expected. In addition, statistics for the pitch

are shown to justify that the turbine is performing according to its standards. Regarding
loads, a comparison between land-based and floating will take place in the next section.
To capture the overall performance of the turbine in each wind speed bin, min, max and
mean values along with their standard deviation are depicted in figure 4.1 for the main
operational sensors. This figure is used to confirm that the system is operating properly

At first, a certain observation can be made: The captured mean wind speed of each bin

is applied on the hub height (150m) using a linear interpolation approach (see section

a main driver for selecting the wind samples. Measurements were selected in a way that
they include large direction changes, which are located in the middle of each sample. By
doing so, the turbine has time to settle before any of these significant events take place.
To deal with that, the measurement time series have been moved/shifted accordingly,
leading to lower mean wind speed values. More detailed information regarding the
choice of the wind component samples are found in [28].
A minimum rotor speed has been set as seen in table 2.2, to avoid possible interaction
between 3P and the tower’s natural frequency. Thus, at low wind speeds the turbine
pitches the blades to track maximum power while maintaining the minimum rotor speed.
In addition, it can be seen that in the first bin (3-5m/s), the mean rotational speed is
below the minimum limit (5RPM). The wind speed in that bin was often below the cut-in

simulation. That is also the reason behind the very low amount of power generated
in that bin. Furthermore, it is noticeable that the standard deviation increases in all
channels when approaching the bins close to the rated wind speed. Since the wind
input is based on measurements, wind speed is highly variable inside each bin, often
reaching or exceeding the rated value, even for the 7-9 chunk. Reasonably, the highest
standard deviation is observed between 5-15m/s. A more steady behaviour is noticed
at higher wind speeds (>15m/s), where standard deviation is at lower levels. In those
bins, wind speed is quite above rated most of the time during the 1 hour sample, hence
the response of the turbine is more stable from an operating point of view.

4.3.1
Pitch controller de-tuning
To find the appropriate tuning for the IEA 15MW reference floating wind turbine, different
natural frequencies are used to obtain the proportional and integral gain of the pitch
controller. Gains that are lower than the onshore gains are targeted, since the controller
needs to be slower than the pitch motion. By lowering its natural frequency, it will react
more slow to execute blade pitching, enabling to alleviate the negative damping issue
created in above rated wind speeds. The objective is to eliminate the pitch instability,
or reduce as much as possible the oscillations of the platform’s pitch motion, so that a
load-comparison with the bottom-fixed case can be reasonable.
Table 4.2 lists the gains obtained using HAWC2S/HAWCStab2 for different controller
natural frequencies (cases 2,3). Lower frequencies are used to avoid the interaction
with the pitch natural frequency of the floating platform, which is ωn = 0.036hz, found
by employing decay tests [32]. Case 1 represents the proposed tuning in [35]. The
standard deviation of the pitch motion is only computed for 2000 seconds excluding
transient time, since during that time period the instability is present. It can be concluded
that as the gains are decreasing, the standard deviation of the pitch rotation is also
decreasing. However, as seen in figure 4.5, even the slowest controller (case 3) does
not eliminate the pitch instability, even though the oscillations are significantly lower.
This is also depicted in the standard deviation values, where a significant drop has been
achieved in case 3. However, a combination of gains inside that area (<0.32 for Kp and
<0.009 for KI) seems to possibly offer a solution to the instability problem, although not
ideal.
By manually trying different proportional and integral gains for the pitch controller, one
combination seem to represent the best practice, providing much better results (im-
proving the overall picture in the above rated wind speeds). For that combination, the
platform pitch and its PSD are depicted (figure 4.5) and compared to the ones obtained
with the other settings seen previously. The standard deviation of the pitch motion is

The yaw-bearing pitch moment is the resultant moment of the thrust and gravity compo-
nents. The gravity-arm (horizontal distance between the center of gravity of the rotor-
nacelle assembly and the yaw-bearing) is higher than the thrust-arm (vertical distance
between the point where thrust is applied and yaw-bearing), since in the IEA 15MW
RWT model used, the nacelle is modelled almost 13m long. Consequently, the gravita-
tional moment is dominating the total pitch moment, explaining the negative sign seen in
the time series (gravitational and thrust moments opposite to each other). The absolute
minimum value recorded in the yaw-bearing pitch moment (slightly above 20000kNm)
is captured in the first seconds of the simulation. At that time, wind speed is close to
rated, so thrust gets high values, minimizing the total pitch moment. In a similar way,
the absolute maximum of this load channel (above 50000kNm) is noted between 3000
and 3500 seconds, a period where thrust is at its lowest values. Low thrust results in
low thrust moment component, maximizing the total pitch moment.
One should not also neglect the influence of the platform’s motion in the resulting mo-
ment. Nevertheless, as mentioned previously, floater’s motion is also coupled with the
yaw movement of the rotor. Since the platform’s rotations are presented in the co-
ordinate system of the platform, their precise implications to yaw-moments cannot be
assessed with the current outputs, however, the correlation is acknowledged.

In conclusion, it is shown that the resulting yaw-bearing pitch moment in this simulation
is driven by a combination of parameters, with thrust and total pitch rotation being the
most important among others. Obviously, there are also a lot of other things that pos-
sibly contribute to the resulting yaw-bearing moments and worth taking into account,
since the simulation is on itself very complex (large wind direction change, rotor yawing,
displacements and rotations in other directions etc). Apparently, any conclusion drawn
includes a certain amount of uncertainty and should be considered accordingly.
Additionally, at 2500 seconds, a small instability issue seems to appear, visible in both
moment signals, as well as in rotor speed, thrust and platform roll channels. It has been
seen in section 4.3.1 that the regulation of the controller used for the purpose of these
simulations is not optimum, but decent concerning the available choices. It is therefore
expected that minor instabilities occur in the simulated response of the wind turbine in
other wind speed bins as well.
The maximum yaw-bearing torsion moment is found in the first seconds of the time
series shown. Yaw motion of the rotor-nacelle or of the floating platform may induce
gyroscopic loads, which in turn may result in torsional moments around the yaw-bearing.
In general, it is expected that these moments are mainly related to the yaw movement
of platform and/or nacelle, although it can be seen (figure 4.11) that the turbine is not
yawing when the maximum moment occurs. In addition, even higher maximum moment
is achieved in the bottom-fixed simulation of the same wind speed bin (figure 4.10),
meaning that the platform’s yaw motion cannot be the one to blame for the extreme
torsional moment in the floating case. As a result, further investigation is needed to
reach a solid conclusion.
Another fact to take into consideration is that during the transient time (not shown in
time series) the turbine is forced to yaw, in order to reach a zero yaw-error before the
end of the transitional period. Especially in the torsion case, it seems that events which
preceed the starting time of the captured response lead to the maximum moment at
approximately 500 seconds, posing a certain level of uncertainty in any claim made.

Conclusions
In the present MSc thesis, a representative yaw controller is developed to employ both
bottom-fixed and floating simulations with slow moving large direction changes in the
time domain. For this purpose, measurements of wind components are obtained from a
met mast and filtered to remove high frequent fluctuations and turbulence. The opera-
tion of the yaw controller is firstly evaluated onshore by running simulations with simple
direction change cases, using the DTU 10MW RWT. Then, simulations with both using
a fixed-substructure and a floating platform are employed, to capture the response of
the IEA 15MW RWT in varying wind and yawing conditions.
The evaluation of the moving average filters showed no significant differences in the
response of the yaw controller. The exponential moving average offers an advantage in
terms of memory and frequency response, however, it weights more the recent values of
the input signal, leading in yaw actions slightly earlier compared to the simple one. The
control system responds well in the direction inputs in onshore conditions, while its two
yaw operating modes are capable of functioning without creating issues in the behaviour
of the system, providing a smooth output even when one interacts with the other. Same
can be concluded for the floating case. The implications of the platform’s motion do not
hinder the yaw controller from effectively capture the mean wind direction and align the
rotor-nacelle assembly accordingly. Instant yaw errors between the bottom-fixed and
the floating cases have minor differences, the majority of them occurring when small
direction changes are captured in the wind.
Regarding the loads, higher tower-base loads are resulted in the floating compared
to the bottom-fixed simulations. The difference is attributed in the floating platform’s
motion, where even small rotation angles can contribute to the increase of the total
moments. Similar blade-root loads are observed between the fixed and floating simu-
lations, while little differences are noticed in the yaw-bearing moments. For the latter
case and based on the results obtained, it is concluded that the yaw movement of the
rotor does not lead to increase in moments for the floating application in respect to the
bottom-fixed.
Concerning the extreme values of the yaw-bearing loads in the 13-15m/s wind speed
bin, it is found that the absolute minimum pitch moment is recorded in a time period
where thrust is at high values, opposing by its moment-component to the corresponding
one from gravity, resulting in a low total pitch moment. On the other hand, the abso-
lute maximum is observed when thrust takes low values, while the coupled motion with
the platform is also a parameter to consider in the floating case. The absolute maxi-
mum yaw-bearing torsion moment occurs right after the transient period, meaning that
is probably directly driven by events taking place at that time.
The present study constitutes a solid basis for further future work. First and foremost,

45
==PAGE:52==
a study regarding the time windows of the yaw controller’s moving averages would pro-
vide clear indications for selecting those parameters. It is expected that the larger the
time windows, the less the utilization of the yaw-drive, however, less yaw activation
would induce higher yaw errors. A balance between utilization-yaw error is always de-
sirable, considering the fact that frequent action of the yaw-bearing due to yawing leads
to increase in fatigue loading of that component. In continuous, it is seen that the basic
onshore controller for IEA 15MW RWT cannot be implemented in the corresponding ref-
erence floating wind turbine. The onshore tuning leads to pitch instability in wind speeds
above rated, thus the controller needs to be differently tuned. A properly regulated pitch
controller, optimized for the floating case would reduce the amount of uncertainty in the
response of the system and would allow a more reasonable comparison. Furthermore,
an interesting task would be to introduce regular and irregular waves in the floating sim-
ulations and study the response of the system together with the yaw controller. Waves
would increase the motion of the floating platform and add complexity, but also would
offer more realistic offshore conditions that can happen in larger depths, where float-
ing turbines are used. Lastly, during this study it is found that proper implementation of
both direction change and turbulence on the wind input cannot be achieved with the cur-
rent tools. Hence, the development of such an interface and its coupling to aero-elastic
software could be a really useful tool for future yaw simulations.
