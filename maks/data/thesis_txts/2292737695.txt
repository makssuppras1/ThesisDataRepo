Summary  
In the generation of process design, the question of sustainability in the form of an economical 
and environmental aspect has become of greater importance in recent years as a consequence of 
demands from modern society. With the increasing demands for sustainability in process 
designs, the current methods for development of process design has therefore difficulties with 
meeting the new demands at a satisfactory level, since the methods have not been developed to 
include innovation processes like new and intensified/hybrid unit operations.  
Process designs have traditionally been generated from the method of process synthesis, in 
where a given process design generated on the basis of a desired product and chemical reaction 
are made at the levels of tasks and unit operations, meaning that the designed process system 
alternatives obtained from process synthesis generate alternatives based on the knowledge of 
existing reaction and separation systems, and any application of innovative reaction or 
separation systems are not considered until after a process design based on conventional unit 
operations has been developed.     
In the development of the method of process synthesis, process intensification is applied. 
Process intensification applies the principals of phenomena building blocks  (PBB), which is a 
method, that makes it possible to include intensified or hybrid unit operations that have been, or 
could be developed. The method of process intensification is combined with the method of 
process synthesis, and thereby creating the method process synthesis-intensification, from 
which more sustainable process designs can be generated, since the combined method has a 
wider search window of possible alternatives.  
As a case study the production of dimethyl carbonate is subjected to the method of process 
synthesis-intensification, where the methodology generates process design alternatives that 
include the utilization of hybrid/intensified unit operations in the generation of more sustainable 
process design.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1  Process synthesis 
Process synthesis is a rather new technology within chemical engineering, only a few decades 
old. Process synthesis was not considered as a research area until the late 1960’s where process 
synthesis was proposed as a field of research where a systematic approach could be applied. The 
problem of process synthesis was partitioned in three sub-tasks, which are still considered 
research areas studied in present time. 
 
• 
Reaction path synthesis 
• 
Separation systems synthesis 
• 
Heat exchanger network synthesis 
 
 The sub-tasks are known as reaction path synthesis, which is the study of finding the correct 
chemical reaction to applied in a production process providing the best desired result. A second 
sub-task is the separation systems synthesis, which considers the downstream process of a 
chemical production process, e.g. purification of desired products, the recycle of un-reacted raw 
materials and the purification of wastewater [1]. 
 
Heat exchanger network synthesis 
The process synthesis sub-tasks that have been studied more then any other, is the synthesis of 
heat exchanger network. The heat exchanger network synthesis considers the heat exchangers 
within a chemical process, i.e. the amount of heat exchangers used and their network.  
Experience have showed that saving to be made the most optimal connections between heat 
exchangers is process is applied, with respect to cooling and heating stages of the process 
significant saving can be made on the operating and capital costs [1]. 
 
Reaction path and separations systems synthesis 
The sub-tasks of reaction path synthesis and separation system synthesis have in recent years 
become more desirable areas in the problem of process synthesis, as the consequence of 
innovation being generated in these areas. The innovation can be seen in the development of 
new unit operations that to some extant can overcome process limitations, such as reaction 
conversions limited by equilibrium conversion, and chemical separations made difficult by the 
presence of azeotropes can be overcome by e.g. novel unit operations that performs reactive 
distillation and membrane separation [4]. 
 
Sustainable design 
==PAGE:20==
 
12
In recent years the desire for more sustainable design has also increased the number of factors to 
consider when process synthesis is to be applied. The factors to be considered have recently 
been limited to economical considerations from the chemical industries, but environmental 
considerations generate from modern society, such as the depletion of natural resources and the 
environmental impacts. With these factors the evaluation of chemical processes has extended 
from an economical evaluation to an evaluation based on economics, sustainability and Life 
Cycle Assessment. The analysis of Life Cycle Assessment measures e.g. the release of carbon 
dioxide from the respective unit operations applied in a chemical process, and the impact of the 
chemicals used in the process on the environment [3,5]. 
 
1.2 Process intensification  
In process synthesis a process flowsheet is typically represented at the unit operations level, i.e. 
a flowsheet shown and generated based on the knowledge of existing unit operations and the 
technology they apply. Process synthesis flowsheet generation are not limited to the one level of 
aggregation, but employs the aggregation level of tasks and phenomena as well. The level of 
tasks show process flowsheet in tasks performed, i.e. a reactor at the unit operations level 
performs the reaction in the process, and at the task level the reactor is translated into a reaction 
task. At the level of phenomena, which lies beneath that of tasks, the tasks in the process 
flowsheet are translated into the phenomena that they perform e.g. a reaction is performed by 
the phenomena R. 
 
• 
Scale of Unit Operations  
• 
Scale of Tasks 
• 
Scale of Phenomena 
 
When process synthesis is performed at the level of tasks, the flowsheet are not limited to tasks 
that can be directly translated to known unit operations at the higher level, but can employ tasks 
that are not limited by known unit operations i.e. the possibility for the application of 
novel/intensified unit operations is feasible. At the phenomena level the combination 
possibilities are even greater then that of the tasks level, as the phenomena’s or phenomena 
building blocks as they are referred to, can be combined and manipulated in generating tasks, at 
the task level that are more sustainable, then the process flowsheet obtained a the tasks level, 
which in return is more sustainable then the flowsheet alternatives obtained at the unit 
operations level [3]. 
When understanding the principals of process intensification based on the methodology of 
phenomenas, a comparison can be made with that of a simple molecule in a chemical. When 
generating a molecule atoms are applied and connected in different ways, these generated 
functional groups are then connected with each other, and thereby forming different molecules. 
In Figure 1 the connections between the three levels of flowsheets are showed in relations to 
their corresponding part in the formation of a molecule. Process synthesis at the phenomena 
level, gives the chemical engineers the possibility of generating sustainable process routes, and 
applying novel or intensified unit operations at the same time resulting in more sustainable

design, then previously achieved by process synthesis at the unit operations level, resulting in 
the method of process synthesis-intensification [1,3,4].

1.3 Thesis objective and motivation  
Sustainable process design has been subjected to increasing demands in recent years, originating 
from the desire to generate sustainable processes, from not only a financial point of view, but 
also from environmental aspects, such as the minimization of greenhouse gasses. Analytical 
methods as the life cycle assessment have been developed with the purpose of analysing the 
environmental impacts of chemical processes.  
A method of process intensification based on the methodology of phenomena’s, has been 
developed with the objective of generating sustainable solutions to chemical processes.  
In this master thesis the process synthesis-intensification methodology as developed in [18] has 
been tested. The method is applied to the case study, in which dimethyl carbonate is the product. 
Based on a known production process of the compound, a more sustainable process is desired in 
which the reaction by-product is converted by the addition of a second reactant and reaction 
process to a process raw material.

2 Process Synthesis, Design and Analysis and 
Sustainable design 
 
The process of finding a sustainable design can be divided into three stages, where each stage 
has it own objective, carried out sequentially with the possibility of returning to a previous stage 
if results from a continued stage becomes infeasible. The three stages are [4,6,18]. 
1. Process synthesis 
2. Design  
3. Sustainable design

2.2 Stage 1 – Process Synthesis  
In the process synthesis stage the product to be produced and the used raw materials are given, 
and based on this a chemical process is obtained in where the following information is obtained: 
 
• Reaction conversion 
• Reaction type: forward or equilibrium 
• Reaction enthalpy; endothermic or exothermic 
 
Other general information about the raw materials, product and by-product, if any formed are 
additionally obtained, where some examples are: 
 
• Purchase price of raw materials 
• Value of product(s) 
• Environmental impact 
• Toxic 
• Availability

• Demand of product 
• Annular global production of raw materials/product(s)

indicating the presence of  
• Azeotropes 
• Miscibility gaps 
 
Which have an influence on a given process flowsheet. With these in information in mind a base

2.3 Stage 2 – Design and Analysis 
 
The base case design achieved in stage 1 is analysed with respect to economics, sustainability 
and Life Cycle Assessment, and based on these analyses bottlenecks within the base case design 
achieved in stage 1 are obtained, theses bottlenecks are referred to as process hot-spots. The 
process hot-spots can based on the three analysis types be either one or a combination of the 
following, based on the given base case design being analysed [5,15]: 
  
• 
Limitations reaction conversion i.e. equilibrium conversion 
• 
Loss of Raw material i.e. MVA (Material Value Added) 
• 
High-energy consumption EWC (Energy Waste Consumption), caused by unit 
operations performing high-energy demands. 
• 
Presence of azeotropes  
 
It should be noted that the listed hot-spots above, are not the only hot-spots possible, but mere 
an example of what typical hot-spots are. These hot-spots constitute a list of possible areas 
within, a given base case design where the potential for optimization is beneficial, from either 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
17
an economical or environmental perspective. After identifying process hot-spots within a given 
base case design, new design targets can be determined. These design targets can include the 
application of different unit operation types bases on existing unit operation or 
intensified/hybrid/innovative unit operations. Furthermore the design targets can also beside 
application of different unit operations, consider the hot-spots such as overcoming equilibrium 
limitations in the reaction conversion, reducing loss of material value from un-reacted raw 
material by recycle (or increasing reaction conversion) and proposal of different downstream 
processing routes. Additionally a design target can also consider the reduction of waste 
products, generated in the base case design process as either a by-product or as the release of 
carbon dioxide from either the chemical reaction, the utilities applied in the production process 
or a combination of both.  
The design targets defined for a given base case design in stage 2, is applied in stage 3, in the 
selection of the more sustainable design process among the generated alternatives, which meets 
the constraints given in stage 1 for e.g. the reaction conversion and/or product(s) purity.  
 
2.4 Stage 3 – Sustainable design 
 
The given base case design that have been either generated or obtained from literature in stage 
1, and analysed in stage 2 is in stage 3 transformed from a unit operations scale process to a task 
scale process and finally into a phenomena scale process, and then rebuild from the phenomena 
scale back to the task and unit operation scale and thereby generating process alternatives 
[4,18].  
 
The base case design is firstly transformed from a unit operations scale process flowsheet into a 
task scale process flow sheet, by replacing the existing unit operation in the process with the 
tasks that each unit operation performs, which can be classified into the main tasks of  
 
• 
Reaction tasks  
• 
Separation tasks 
 
 
After the generation of the task scale process flowsheet, the phenomena build blocks (PBB) that 
each tasks performs are obtained, where a PBB in a reaction tasks could be either a R, M, C or 
H indicating the presence of reaction, mixing, cooling or heating respectively. It is noted that 
the PBBs C and H cannot exist in the same reaction task as it is thermodynamically infeasible, 
they can however exist in the same separation task, this will be explained later. In Figure 3 a 
process consisting of a reactor can be seen at the three scales; unit operation, task and 
phenomena [18].

The optimization problem given in the system of equations for the process synthesis problem 
represented mathematically by equations [1] to [8], is a MINLP problem. The MINLP problem 
can be difficult to solve, if a given process model and its constitutive equations are highly non-
linear. In addition the size of the MINLP problem is an issue. When managing the complexity 
of the problem s solution approach, which is efficient and systematic, the problem is 
decomposed into a set of sub-problems that are further solved in a calculation order, which is 
pre-defined, and referred to the decomposition based method [20]. The majority of the sub-
problems solutions require bounded solution equations in the form of a sub-set of equations, and 
the final sub-problem consists of NLP and MINLP, which are solved as a set.  
In the identification of feasible flowsheet alternatives the process model equations are solved 
simultaneously of equations [2+5] and the constraints from equation [6]. Equation [7] is then 
applied for the evaluation of the feasible flowsheet alternatives. The objective function is 
calculated for the remaining alternatives, where the flowsheet alternatives with the best 
objective function results are selected as the most sustainable process designs [12,18].

4 Algorithms and Tools 
In the process synthesis-intensification method application, two types of algorithms are applied, algorithm 
1 (A1) and algorithm 2 (A2). The first applied algorithm in the process synthesis-intensification 
framework is A1, which moves from the highest scale of the unit operations, to the lowest scale of the 
phenomena scale. The main purpose with algorithm A1 is the identification of desirable tasks that can 
replace existing tasks in the base case, and the PBBs connected with the additional desirable tasks. The 
second algorithm A2 moves in the opposite direction of algorithm A1, that is from the lower phenomena 
scale to the highest operational scale. The objective with algorithm A2, is to combined the PBBs obtained 
in algorithm A1 based on the combination rules into basic structures, which can perform one or more 
tasks. The generated tasks are then connected into tasks-based flowsheets, which are further translated 
into unit operational based flowsheets that can include intensified unit operations. In Figure 6 the flow of 
the two algorithms are shown [18].

6 Conclusion and Future work 
 
6.1 Conclusion 
Sustainable process design have been addressed in this thesis with the application of the 
phenomena based methodology of process synthesis-intensification developed by Post Doc 
D.K. Babi at CAPEC, DTU. The methodology generates sustainable flowsheet alternatives at 
three scales starting at the unit operating level to the task level and ending at the lower level of 
phenomena. The principal of generating sustainable flowsheet alternatives with the process 
synthesis-intensification is that is permits the application of innovative and intensified/hybrid 
unit operations, whereas conventional process synthesis is limited to the application of known 
unit operations, resulting in lesser sustainable flowsheet alternatives, then generated from the 
methodology applying innovative unit operations.  
 
The process synthesis-intensification methodology is tested for its feasibility, in the generation 
of a sustainable process design on a known system process. The system process is tested for 
sustainability with respect to the economical factors, such as the operating costs, and the 
environmental impact of e.g. the release of the greenhouse carbon dioxide.  
The methodology is divided into three stages where each stage represents the stages of process 
synthesis, design and analysis and design. Where process synthesis is the task of   finding the 
reaction based on raw materials, and performing a process simulation based on a known or 
generated base case at the task and unit operating scale are performed. Stage 2 is the stage 
where the base case defined and simulated in stage 1 is analysed for process hot-spots, i.e. 
places with the base case where the potential for optimization of the base case process is 
feasible, and based on this a set of design targets are defined. In stage 3 the lower scale of the 
process synthesis-intensification methodology is applied, from which more sustainable process 
alternatives are generated based on the performance criteria set in stage 1 i.e. performance of 
reaction etc., and the design targets defined in stage 2.  
 
The methodology is applied to a case study, in where the production of dimethyl carbonate is 
considered.  A known production process of dimethyl carbonate is applied, in where the process 
by-product of propylene glycol is desired converted to a process raw material, and thereby 
resulting in the minimization of operating costs, with respect to raw materials. A second 
reaction utilizing the greenhouse gas of carbon dioxide is added to the process, and based on 
this second reaction new process alternatives are generated, resulting in feasible process 
alternatives utilizing the application of intensified/hybrid unit operations. Where the   
 
6.2 Future work 
The process synthesis-intensification method developed based on the phenomena-based 
methodology that have been tested in this thesis, have as yet only been developed for the 
application on chemical and biochemical processes that are known, i.e. chemical processes that 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
82
either have an existing production process or a known reaction sequence which have been 
thoroughly analysed.  
 
Future developments of the process synthesis-intensification could include a step or similar that 
considers reactions of chemical products, which has not been thoroughly analysed, or 
mentioned in the literature. This addition of a step in the methodology, which considers the 
application of unknown or new chemical reactions, opens the possibility for the production of 
not previously produced chemical, because the knowledge of a given process has been found to 
be insufficient.  
Another possible modifications to the methodology, which would make the method better suited 
for the industry, is the further development of the procedure in which the generation of by-
products are considered, and any the downstream processes connected with the presence of by-
products. In addition to the by-product present in the system, the modifications should also 
consider the formation of side-reactions caused by cross-reactions between the chemical present 
in the system. This combined modification of the method developed for the application of 
processes with by-products and side-reactions would make the methodology more applicable for 
the chemical industry.
