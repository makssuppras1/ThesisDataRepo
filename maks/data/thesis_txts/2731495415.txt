Abstract 
 
The construction industry is a significant contributor to resources consumption and waste generation, 
necessitating a shift towards a more responsible consumption pattern. Circular Economy (CE) pre-
sents an opportunity to reshape the sector by decoupling economic growth from raw materials con-
sumption. Despite the acknowledged importance of CE, practical implementation of CE practices is 
slow and research gaps persist. A significant gap is constituted by the lack of assessments that 
assess the circularity of construction products. 
 
This research had the purpose to move a first step toward developing a circular maturity assessment 
for construction products. This first phase involved an exploratory study of academic and non-aca-
demic literature to understand the current state of research development in this context, identify 
gaps, and present a collection of strategies, indicators and maturity dimensions categorised and 
organised in a conceptual framework. To achieve the purpose of the thesis, a systematic literature 
review was performed together with a conceptual framework formulation method. The analysis and 
framework resulted from the study, will serve as foundation for future research and developments. 
 
In the introduction, the motivation for the study and the problem formulation are presented. Subse-
quently, the background section provides additional context to the research. Next, the adopted meth-
odology in both systematic literature review and framework conceptualisation is detailed. The results 
of the systematic literature review are then reported together with the proposed conceptual frame-
work. Finally, the results are discussed, leading to the presentation of conclusions.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
 
The construction industry is globally responsible for 40% of all generated waste, 40% of all material 
resource use and 37% of energy-related carbon emissions (United Nations Environment Pro-
gramme, 2023). In Europe, the construction industry accounts for 50% of all raw materials extraction, 
30% of water consumption, 36% of all generated solid waste, 50% of energy use and 40% of all 
greenhouse gas emissions (European Commission, 2021). These statistics highlight construction 
sector’s large contribution to climate change. 
 
Global Trends such as population and wealth growth are driving continuous urbanisation and it is 
estimated that 300 million new houses will need to be globally constructed by 2030 (United Nations 
Environment Programme, 2023). The global consumption of non-renewable materials will double by 
2060, causing the irreversible depletion of natural resources unless significant changes are made 
(United Nations Environment Programme, 2023) (Eberhardt et al., 2022). The construction sector 
must be reshaped in a more sustainable way and Circular Economy (CE) offers this opportunity. 
 
In contrast to the linear economy model, which is based on the take - make - dispose scheme, the 
CE model aim is to decouple economic growth from raw materials consumption (Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, 2016). The adoption of circular strategies, such as refuse, rethink, reduce, reuse, repair, 
refurbish, remanufacture, repurpose, recycle, and recover, serves as a preventive measure against 
the wastage of raw materials. This approach operates by slowing, narrowing, and closing resource 
flows.(Potting et al., 2017). Although the importance of CE in the construction sector is widely rec-
ognised by industry and academia experts, the practical implementation of CE principles, strategies 
and models is in its early stages (Dräger et al., 2022), and research gaps must still be addressed. 
While much attention has been directed towards lowering the operational energy consumption of 
buildings to reduce the environmental impacts, there has been less focus on addressing the lifecycle 
impacts originating of construction products which is a major source of embodied impacts, beyond 
operational energy use (Eberhardt et al., 2022). It is estimated that the adoption of CE strategies 
could reduce the emissions of construction products and materials employed in residential buildings 
by at least 80% (United Nations Environment Programme, 2023). However, environmental impacts 
of different building components vary, the choice of materials and products significantly influences a 
building’s environmental performance (Eberhardt et al., 2022). Therefore, a method to individually 
assess the CE performance of construction products CE performance must be developed. 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
Page 9 
 
Despite the development of frameworks that measure buildings’ or construction projects’ Circular 
Economy performance, such as Level(s) or Tokazhanov et al. (2022) framework, there is currently 
no systematic and consistent approach for rating and comparing construction products in terms of 
circular maturity. Recognising the urgent need to address raw materials extraction emergency, un-
derstanding the distinct impact that each construction product has on the overall performance of a 
building, acknowledging the benefits from implementing CE strategies at the construction product 
level and acknowledging industry needs for a practical decision making tool that guides the selection 
of circular products, the development of a framework that rate construction products based on their 
circular performance becomes necessary. 
 
Until not long ago, products were not the focus of circular assessments. Circular readiness and ma-
turity assessments were developed at global, national, sectorial, organisational level, etc. Only in 
recent years academic and industry experts have started to develop assessments specifically for 
products (Saidani et al., 2017). At this day, only one assessment was developed specifically for 
construction products and the framework is in its early testing stage (Antwi-Afari et al., 2022). 
 
1.2 Problem Formulation 
 
The formulation of the problem and research questions addressed in this thesis stem from the Bridge 
the Gap research project currently being conducted by the Technical University of Denmark, Delft 
Technical University, and Home.Earth. Home.Earth, founded in 2021 in Denmark, is a real estate 
company guided by a vision to develop cities that strike a balance between the three sustainability 
pillars of people, planet, and profit. The company has initiated the Bridge the Gap research project 
in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark and Delft University of Technology with the 
acknowledgment that academically developed innovation and sustainability practices collide with the 
everyday construction practices (Pomponi & Moncaster, 2017). Therefore, the primary objective of the 
project is to bridge this gap by providing practical and ready-to-use tools for the construction industry, 
specifically related to construction products and circular economy. Construction products are defined 
as complex physical elements used in construction and consisting of various materials and compo-
nents. The specific need that motivated this thesis project is related to construction products: 
 
• 
As a result of the fragmentation of the construction industry and inconsistent data reporting, 
products that support circular economy are difficult to identify. Even if such products are 
identified, there is a lack of clearly defined criteria for comparison and selection. Therefore, 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
Page 10 
 
when such products are selected for a construction project, it becomes challenging to argue 
the validity of the choice.  
 
The purpose of this thesis is to explore literature and to develop an understanding of the advance-
ment of methodologies that measure construction products’ circular maturity. The exploration study 
is a first step towards a future development of a construction products’ circular economy maturity 
assessment in the form of an online platform at European level that responds to the industry’s needs 
and brings together various circular construction products assessing them in a standardised way. 
 
The research conducted aims to answer the following questions: 
 
Research Question 1: 
What are the key gaps and limitations in the existing academic and non-academic literature related 
to maturity assessments and circularity indicators within the context of construction products’ circu-
lar economy maturity assessment? 
 
Answering this question brings an understanding of the current state of development of literature, 
both academic and industrial. The focus is on methodologies to measure construction products’ cir-
cular maturity such as maturity frameworks, indicators, maturity dimensions. In this thesis, “circular 
economy maturity” indicates a set of characteristics of a product that can be measured to determine 
how compliant a product is with circular economy principles. The concept of “circular economy ma-
turity” differs from the concept of “circular economy readiness” which refers to a set of characteristics 
or attitudes that enable the adoption of circular economy practices and typically apply to organisa-
tions rather than products (Pigosso & McAloone, 2021). 
 
Research Question 2: 
How can a circular economy maturity assessment framework be conceptualised from literature as 
a base for further developments toward a circular maturity assessment for construction products? 
 
Answering this question involves conceptualising a theoretical framework that collects and synthe-
tises the currently developed strategies, indicators, and maturity dimensions in a coherent way. 
Therefore, the framework will serve as a picture of the current development state of literature. The 
framework, together with the identified gaps and limitations, will set the path for future developments 
by indicating the areas research must focus next. The information included in the framework will 
undergo further developments as highlighted by the gaps identified in literature.

2. Background 
2.1 Circular Economy 
 
The Circular Economy concept is relatively new, and it is continuously evolving. Many definitions of 
the concept exist, and there is not a commonly recognised and adopted definition. The lack of a 
standard definition is due to the complex and multidisciplinary nature of the economic model (Toka-
zhanov et al., 2022). However, the various definitions present some commonalities, with all recog-
nising that circular economy is a model opposed to the linear one of “take - make - use - dispose” 
(Saidani et al., 2017). The concept aims at separating economic growth from the consumption of 
resources and this is achieved through strategies that promote the development of systems that 
slow, close and narrow resources loops (Eberhardt et al., 2022). An example of such strategies are 
the 10-R strategies: refuse, rethink, reduce, reuse, repair, refurbish, remanufacture, repurpose, re-
cycle, and recover (Potting et al., 2017).  
 
Perhaps one of the most commonly used definitions of circular economy is the one formulated by 
the Ellen MacArthur Foundation in 2015: “A circular economy is one that is restorative and regener-
ative by design and aims to keep products, components and materials at their highest utility and 
value at all times, distinguishing between technical and biological cycles” (Ellen MacArthur Founda-
tion, 2015). Circular economy is based on the principles of waste and pollution elimination, products 
and materials circulation and nature regeneration. The principles are driven by design, which is the 
most important stage in product development since most of the choices made at this stage have 
substantial effect on products’ circular performance (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2015). The princi-
ples are well defined in the Butterfly Diagram (Figure 1), which illustrates the various flows of mate-
rials in a circular economy. There are two cycles, the technical one and the biological one. The 
technical cycle on the right side focuses on non-biodegradable materials, whereas the biological 
cycle, presented on the left side, focuses on materials that can biodegrade. In the technical cycle, 
the illustrated loops represent different ways product and material value can be retained in the sys-
tem. The inner loops are the most preferred, and the outer ones are the least preferred. Starting from 
inner loop, the strategies are sharing, maintaining, reusing, redistributing, remanufacturing and lastly 
recycling. Conversely, the concept of nature regeneration is at the core of the biological cycle and 
the loops represents the ways nutrients can return to the soil. The strategies are farming, composting 
and anaerobic digestion, cascades, and extraction of biochemical feedstock (Ellen MacArthur Foun-
dation, 2022).

2.1.1 Circular Economy in the Construction Industry 
 
As could be expected, the construction industry also suffers from a lack of a commonly adopted CE 
definition as well, and CE initiatives in the sector are not developed systematically and with a com-
mon goal. The different focus areas include, for example, design strategies for building disassembly 
or flexibility, use of secondary materials and generally building design and construction strategies. 
The incoherent development and implementation of CE practices in the construction sector has in 
part prevented the adoption of CE on a large scale (Eberhardt et al., 2022). To conciliate definitions 
differences and create a common understanding of CE in the construction sector, Benachio et al. 
(2020) proposed a definition based on literature review, and they defined CE in the construction 
sector as “the use of practices, in all stages of the life cycle of a building, to keep the materials as 
long as possible in a closed loop, to reduce the use of new natural resources in a construction pro-
ject” (Benachio et al., 2020, p. 5). This is the operating definition this thesis will use.  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
Page 13 
 
Although the level of awareness in the construction sector about CE is widespread (Benachio et al., 
2020), many barriers have been identified that contribute to the delayed adoption of CE practices in 
the sector. Beside the fragmented development, another challenge is the difficulty stakeholders have 
in understanding how CE concepts can pragmatically be applied in the construction sector. This is 
due to the complexity of supply chains and the short-term goals defined by companies given the 
project structure of the sector (Benachio et al., 2020). A consequence of the fragmented develop-
ment is the insufficiency of standardized practices and methods, which in turns contributes to the 
slow adoption of CE in the construction industry (Benachio et al., 2020). Other obstacles include a 
lack in legislation, polices and fundings as enablers and incentives (Benachio et al., 2020). 
 
Given the fact that the construction industry has a substantial role in climate change by globally 
consuming 40% of raw materials and generating 37% of total CO2 emissions of which approximately 
11% are generated during the manufacturing of materials and construction products, the construc-
tion sector has a responsibility to move toward CE practices implementation (United Nations Envi-
ronment Programme, 2023). For a successful transition, value chains must be restructured, the col-
laboration among stakeholders strengthened, new business models need to be developed, as well 
as new customer habits (Tokazhanov et al., 2022). Standardised guidelines and metrics are also 
fundamental to improve CE principles adoption (Eberhardt et al., 2022). 
Currently, there is a lack of well-developed indicators, moreover, none of the existing metrics have 
become standard (Eberhardt et al., 2022). The importance of indicators resides in the fact that they 
are widely used and acknowledged for decision making and reporting. Moreover, through indicators 
it is possible to measure progress and in CE, this translates in the possibility to measure how closed 
a loop is and what is the remaining distance to the achievement of a truly closed loop (Saidani et al., 
2017). 
 
2.2 Existing Circular Economy Assessments Applied to Construction Products 
 
The lack of well-defined indicators and of a standardised way for measuring CE advancement afflicts 
the construction sector at many levels, including at the construction product level (Dräger et al., 
2022). Two studies were found in literature in which the authors attempted to choose an existing CE 
assessment methodology for generic products and apply it to construction products. The assess-
ments were not included in this research because they were not specifically developed for construc-
tion products and because of their limitations which are briefly reported in this section. 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
Page 14 
 
 Dräger et al. (2022), in their efforts to measure the circularity of construction products available on 
ÖKOBAUDAT, a German online database that stores environment and climate data regarding con-
struction products, chose the method to use based on a review of the most common existing meth-
ods to measure circular performance of products. In the review they considered, the indicators were 
compared based on circular objectives as defined by the European Environment Agency (EEA) 
which are: reducing input and use of natural resources, increasing the value preservation of prod-
ucts, reducing emission levels, reducing material losses, and increasing the share of renewable and 
recyclable resources. Economic aspects were not considered in the comparison as they are classi-
fied as enabling factors (Dräger et al., 2022). The indicators included in the review were: 
 
• 
The Circular Economy Index: the index calculates the ratio of recycled materials compared 
to the total materials utilised in a product. 
• 
C-Metric: similarly to the previous one, the indicator measures the ratio of recycled materials 
compared to the total materials utilised in a product. 
• 
Reuse Potential Indicator: the indicator takes into consideration the reusability of a product 
and it is a decision-making tool with a focus on waste prevention. 
• 
Remanufacturing Product Profiles: the indicator evaluates the ability of products to be 
remanufactured, but fails to take into consideration recycling and reuse. 
• 
Material Circularity Indicator: the indicator compares material flows from different sources, 
such as primary, sustainably sourced, recycled, or reused materials. 
 
After comparing the indicators against the objectives outlined by the EEA, it was found that most of 
the indicators concentrate only on a specific objective. Specifically, the Circular Economy Index, C-
Metric, and the Reuse Potential Indicator prioritise increasing the share of renewable and recyclable 
resources. On the other hand, the Remanufacturing Product Profiles focuses on reducing the input 
and use of natural resources. The Material Circularity Indicator resulted as the only indicator covering 
all objectives, except for the goal of reducing emissions (Dräger et al., 2022). 
 
Dräger et al. (2022) chose the Material Circularity Indicator to assess construction products circular-
ity and they adapted it to fit the data represented in the database. They concluded that it was possible 
to adapt the indicator formula to the way data is represented in the ÖKOBAUDAT database, but the 
main obstacle was the differentiation of materials flows from different sources since the information 
was not available in the database (Dräger et al., 2022). 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
Page 15 
 
In the development process of their circular assessment of construction products,(Antwi-Afari et al., 
2022) relayed on literature review to choose an integrative indicator as part of their methodology 
(Antwi-Afari et al., 2022). They included tools measuring circular economy of products and their 
conclusions on the tools were: 
 
• 
Circular Economy Toolkit: the tool is easy to understand and use, but it does not reflect 
CE complexity regarding the nutrients flow in the product lifecycle. 
• 
Material Circularity Indicator: the indicator is user-friendly and intuitive but takes circularity 
into account at material level only and does not include other important aspects of circularity 
such as adaptability, connectivity, and optimization of systems. 
• 
Circular Economy Indicator Prototype: this indicator is easy to use as well and, based on 
a questionnaire, it determines materials’ circularity by a single score that does not include 
materials’ sustainability considerations. 
• 
C-Metric: the indicator uses economic values as weights and therefore the subjectivity of the 
assessment is reduced, but at same time the economic estimations do not accurately reflect 
the value retention across products lifecycles resulting from CE practices.  
 
Antwi-Afari et al. (2022) concluded that the tools are not systemic and do not consider CE complexity 
in relation to the many applications CE has along the life cycle of a product. Therefore, the tools 
cannot be utilised on their own, but have to be integrated with other assessments. For the develop-
ment of their assessment, they adopted the Predictive Building Circularity Indicator which develops 
on multiple levels, starting at the material level with the Material Circularity Indicator and culminating 
at the building level. 
 
2.3 Existing Circular Economy Assessments for Generic Products 
 
The lack of a standardised ways to assess circularity affects generic products as well. Only in recent 
times the focus on circular assessments has increased in academia, until not long ago the assess-
ments at product level were developed exclusively by industry. Starting from this observation and 
based on a literature review of the most common circularity assessments Saidani et al. (2017) de-
veloped the requirements for a Product Circularity Measurement Framework (Figure 2). The authors 
present the required features through a hierarchy inspired by Maslow’s pyramid of needs. The re-
quirements are classified in five categories, starting from the bottom: Systemic by Design, Integrated 
and Operational, Adaptive and Flexible, Intuitive User Interface and lastly, Connection to Sustainable 
Development Pillars.  
==PAGE:16==
 
 
Page 16 
 
Figure 2: Hierarchy of requirements for Product Circularity Measurement Framework 
 
(Saidani et al., 2017) 
 
In the first two categories from the bottom, the included requirements are deemed mandatory. The 
third and the fourth categories include features considered additional and desirable, whereas the 
last category includes features seen as ideal.  
The Systemic by Design category emphasizes the importance of developing a holistic framework 
that covers various facets of circular economy, the interplay between macro, meso, micro and nano 
levels of implementation and adopts lifecycle and system thinking. Moreover, the different ways to 
close the loop should not be considered at the same level and accounted for in a single global score. 
Instead, they should be ranked according to the Ellen MacArthur butterfly circular economy model, 
for instance, from the most preferable circularity loop to the least: maintain/prolong, reuse/redistrib-
ute, refurbish/remanufacture, and lastly, recycle. The different circularity loops are also associated 
with different Design for X strategies (Saidani et al., 2017). 
The Integrated and Operational category highlights the importance of integrating the maturity 
framework with the current industrial practices during design and development phases to help deci-
sion-making. Specifically, the framework should be compatible with other tools and software, support 
data construction and provide practical improvement guidance (Saidani et al., 2017).  
==PAGE:17==
 
 
Page 17 
 
The Adaptive and Flexible requirements deal with the importance of creating a framework that is 
modular and flexible enough to allow improvements based on new developments in circular econ-
omy. The framework should also be adaptable to products in different industrial contexts (Saidani et 
al., 2017).  
The Intuitive User Interface category emphasizes the importance of time-efficiency and user-friend-
liness of the framework. The interface should be easy and intuitive for practitioners to use, as well 
as effective and efficient. Data input and results visualisation should be comfortable and pleasant 
(Saidani et al., 2017).  
Finally, the framework should ensure a Connection to Sustainable Development Pillars: eco-
nomic, environmental, and social. Circularity should be a means to achieve more sustainable devel-
opment, therefore it is relevant to analyse if the circular solutions are effectively leading to sustaina-
bility (Saidani et al., 2017). 
The identified requirements were used in this research for further enhance the analysis of the single 
circularity assessment targeting construction products that was found in literature. 
 
3. Research Methodology – Systematic Literature Review 
This study is explorative and qualitative and builds on the Systematic Literature Review process 
proposed by Biolchini et al. (2005).The Conceptual Framework Analysis proposed by Jabareen 
(2009) provided additional guidance for this study’s methodology. The systematic review process, 
as defined by Biolchini et al. (2005), consists of three phases, Planning, Execution, and Result Anal-
ysis. In the Planning phase, the objectives of the research are formulated together with a review 
protocol. Starting from the problem formulation, clear definitions and criteria are derived to determine 
what type of studies and data are relevant to include in the review. Once the selection of the relevant 
studies concludes, the Execution phase follows. Here, relevant data is selected according to the 
formulated criteria and extracted. Finally, in the last phase, the Analysis, results are reported and 
discussed (Biolchini et al., 2005). During the extraction and analysis phases, the Conceptual Frame-
work Analysis was adopted to further define the process.  
 
3.1 Review Planning 
 
Circular economy is a research area in continuous development that, over the years, has gained 
interest from academia and practice, both contributing to the body of research. Therefore, this study 
includes both academic and non-academic contributions through different data collection methods. 
While focusing on academic articles ensures scientific soundness, reviewing maturity frameworks, 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
Page 18 
 
strategies, indicators, and maturity levels developed by industry provides insights into current prac-
tical reality and potentially cover the gaps where industry might be further ahead. 
 
As previously mentioned, the research addressed the following two questions: 
 
Research Question 1: 
What are the key gaps and limitations in the existing academic and non-academic literature related 
to maturity assessments and circularity indicators within the context of construction products’ circu-
lar economy maturity assessment? 
 
Research Question 2: 
How can a circular economy maturity assessment framework be conceptualised from literature to 
assess construction products’ maturity and allow their comparison? 
 
To address these questions, three literature search categories were developed due to the fact that 
there is minimal research on the exact topic of circular economy maturity assessments for construc-
tion products. These three categories are defined as follows and illustrated in Figure 3:  
 
1. Existing circular economy maturity assessments for construction products: 
As a first step, it was relevant to understand the current level of development, if any, of such 
assessments and how they are formulated.  
2. Existing circular economy maturity assessments for the construction sector at any 
level, might it be organisational, building, project, etc. 
The focus in this area was to review parts of these assessments specifically related to con-
struction products and eventually extract circular economy strategies, indicators and maturity 
dimensions specifically related to construction products. 
3. Existing circular economy indicators for construction products 
The aim in this area was to review indicators specifically formulated for the construction sec-
tor and referring to construction products.

Based on the three areas, search strings were defined with keywords and synonyms for each cate-
gory. The final search strings used were:  
 
1. CE Maturity Assessments for Construction Products: “circular economy” AND “construction” 
OR “building” AND “maturity” OR “readiness” AND “product” 
2. CE Maturity for Construction Sector: “circular economy” AND “construction” OR “building”

3. CE Indicators for Construction Products: “circular economy” AND “construction” OR “build-

Although the focus of the research was on maturity, the word “readiness” was also included in the 
search strings, and the differentiation between “maturity” and “readiness” was left to be made during 
data extraction. 
 
Academic articles were sourced through Scopus database due to its wide coverage of peer-reviewed 
documents and ease of access through DTU system. Non-academic literature was sourced through

snowballing and desk research. In order to potentially cover the gaps where industry might be further 
ahead, experts from the construction sector were contacted and sent a survey. The form requests 
were formulated to mirror the three areas of interest. Specifically, experts were asked to provide 
assessments targeting circular economy in the construction sector, documents regarding circular 
economy indicators in the construction sector, and eventually contacts of other experts. Appendix 
8.2 shows the survey including the questions. Desk research was carried out through Google web 
search, starting from well-known sources such as the Ellen MacArthur Foundation, Arup, WBCSD 
and progressing to other sources. 
 
At the end of the data collection process, a total of 182 articles were found on Scopus, with 16, 42 
and 124 articles identified respectively for each area. Additionally, the contacted experts suggested 
a total of 20 non-academic documents and 12 academic articles. In summary, there were 194 aca-
demic articles and 20 non-academic documents. To select the articles to include, initially, it was 
verified that all articles were written in English and published after 2010 to ensure recency of the 
data found. Secondly, inclusion/exclusion criteria were formulated based on which to decide what 
articles to keep as follows: 
 
1. Circular economy maturity assessments for construction products:  
The included articles must simultaneously be related to the construction sector, present cir-
cular economy maturity or readiness assessment frameworks at the construction product 
functional unit and include circular economy indicators. 
2. Circular economy assessments for the construction sector:  
The included articles must simultaneously be related to the construction sector and present 
circular economy maturity or readiness assessment frameworks other than at the construc-
tion product level. 
3. Circular economy indicators for construction products:  
The included articles must simultaneously be related to the construction sector and present 
circular economy indicators at the construction product functional level. 
 
The articles selection process was conducted in two rounds: during the first selection round, articles

The Conceptual Framework Analysis was adopted to define the extraction process from an operative 
standpoint. As defined by Jabareen, (2009), the Conceptual Framework Analysis is a qualitative 
method consisting of eight stages. This method uses Grounded Theory principles, the open coding 
analysis technique and leads to the development of a conceptual framework. A conceptual frame-
work is “a network of interlinked concepts that together provide a comprehensive understanding of 
a phenomenon” (Jabareen, p. 51, 2009). The eight steps are briefly described: 
1. Mapping of Data Sources:
The first step is to define sources that study the phenomenon from different perspectives to
create a holistic map of sources that facilitates data gathering (Jabareen, 2009).
2. Extensive Reading and Categorising of Data:
The aim of the second step is to extensively read the selected studies and to start analytically
coding selected data. (Jabareen, 2009).
3. Identifying and Naming Concepts:
During this step, the selected data is repeatedly reviewed to allow the emergence of con-
cepts, and the codes are constantly refined. The emerging concepts and relative codes need
to be compared with the previous ones and the already existing codes need to be reanalysed.
The second and the third steps are reiterative and should be conducted in parallel (Jabareen,
2009).
4. Deconstructing and Categorising Concepts:
The step involves refining the coded concepts and categorising them based on emerging
common characteristics (Jabareen, 2009).
5. Integrating Concepts:
==PAGE:23==
 
 
Page 23 
 
The aim of this step is to eliminate redundant concepts by integrating them. The number of 
concepts should reduce. The fourth and the fifth steps are carried out together (Jabareen, 
2009). 
6. Synthesis:  
In this step, the concepts and categories are synthesised to form the theoretical framework. 
This step is reiterated until the framework starts making sense (Jabareen, 2009). 
7. Validating the Conceptual Framework:  
The objective of the step is to ensure that the conceptual framework makes sense to other 
researchers as well. At this stage, a review of the framework is necessary (Jabareen, 2009). 
8. Rethinking the Conceptual Framework:  
In the step, the framework is revised based on new insights (Jabareen, 2009).

4.1 Circular Economy Assessment for Construction Products 
 
A single framework targeting construction products’ circular maturity emerged from academic litera-
ture. The framework is of recent development, and it is in its early testing stage. The method inte-
grates Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) with Cradle-to-Cradle (C2C) method and Predictive Building Sys-
temic Circularity Indicator (PBSCI). Its scope is to systematically assess materials, products, and 
systems of a building to ensure their systemic circularity. The concept of Design for Systemic Circu-
larity comprises not only product circular design guidelines but also guidelines across other three 
CE building blocks namely system conditions, reverse cycles and new business models. From a 
product circular design perspective, during the design of building components, different guidelines 
should be adopted such as intelligent dimensioning, open building, customer needs integration and 
other design for X strategies together with considerations on the use of renewable energy, cleaner 
production techniques and water consumption and treatment. System conditions comprise guide-
lines around value chains, consumption processes, stakeholder behaviour, procurement routes, 
sharing platforms, price, policies, and incentives formation. Reverse cycles block regards guidelines 
for developing material passports, take-back systems, online market platforms for reuse and other 
considerations around waste collection and different end-of-life scenarios. Lastly, new business 
models block incorporates guidelines regarding the design of products-as-service, aftersales ser-
vices for repair and maintenance and take back offers (Antwi-Afari et al., 2022). 
 
The authors of the framework chose to integrate LCA, C2C and PBSCI methods with the aim to 
consider both eco-efficient and eco-effective CE principles. Eco-efficiency principles’ goal is to min-
imise the cost in terms of waste, energy usage, and environmental impacts while at the same time 
maximising production value and volume. On the other hand, eco-effectiveness principles deal with 
material flow to ensure the return of nutrients back to the industrial and ecological system. Contrary 
to eco-efficiency, eco-effectiveness focuses on quality, performance, and cascading potential of ma-
terials (Antwi-Afari et al., 2022). 
To systematically cover all CE dimensions, the midpoints and endpoints of LCA and C2C were inte-
grated to measure circularity across environmental, technical, economic, and social dimensions, 
whereas the PBSCI weighting method was integrated to measure the recovery potential of materials 
which is not covered by LCA and C2C (Antwi-Afari et al., 2022). 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
Page 27 
 
In Figure 7, a visual representation of the framework is presented. The first step of the assessment 
is to determine the impact potential of materials by applying the LCA-C2C method. The method 
follows the LCA methodology steps: goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, impact analysis 
and interpretation. Each step was modified to include C2C methodology aspects. Next, the PBSCI 
analysis is carried out which takes into consideration Design for Systematic Circularity criteria and 
guidelines along the four CE building blocks. The first step in PBSCI analysis is determining the 
Material Circularity Indicator (MCI), which the authors have adapted from Ellen MacArthur founda-
tion. The MCI is then used to determine the Product Circular Indicator, which in turns is used to 
determine the System Circularity Indicator and finally the System Circularity is used to calculate the 
PBSCI (Antwi-Afari et al., 2022). 
 
Three papers analyse the framework application on different products and cases: 
 
• 
The first study, starting from a systematic literature review on eco-efficient and eco-effective 
principles, define design guidelines for systemic circularity, develops the LCA-C2C-PBSCI 
method and discuss the application on a residential building in China (Antwi-Afari et al., 
2022). 
• 
The second study further expands on the impact potentials and LCA method through sys-
tematic literature review. The authors clearly define the indicators to adopt during the assess-
ment and test the LCA-C2C-PBSCI method on three case scenarios of modular steel slab 
for a residential building in China (Antwi-Afari, Ng, Chen, et al., 2023). 
• 
The third study, based on a systematic literature review of multicriteria decision-making anal-
ysis methods, combine the LCA-C2C-PBSCI method with the VIKOR method and apply it on 
three case scenarios of modular partition systems for a residential building in China. The 
authors also develop a best practices model to achieve systemic circularity (Antwi-Afari, Ng, 
& Chen, 2023).

An analysis of the framework was conducted based both on the characteristics emerged during the 
coding process and on Saidani et al. (2017) proposed requirements for a product circularity meas-
urement framework (Section 2.3). The LCA-C2C-PBSCI authors’ perspective was included when 
relevant to the analysis. 
 
Framework’s characteristics 
 
The framework’s intended use is to measure products’ circular performance in a systemic way and 
to inform decision making over the selection of a product. It also covers the whole life cycle of prod-
ucts. The method is a combination of quantitative and qualitative procedures that are widely studied 
and recognised. Its structure is based on LCA, C2C and PBSCI structures and the adopted indicators 
range from those specific to the LCA method to those 31 employed in the PBSCI method and devel-
oped by the authors. The assessment takes in input a variety of data such as materials’ amount, 
composition, technical lifetime, embodied carbon, and embodied energy. It also takes into consider-
ation the adopted Design for Disassembly and End-of-Life strategies. Nonetheless, considerations 
of the products at an aggregate building level are integrated in the assessment. The output at the 
end of the framework application, is a numerical score for each of the assessed products that are 
ultimately compared to identify the product that is systemically the most circular. The products must 
ascribe to the same functionality to be comparable, and this is set at the beginning when the func-
tional unit is decided. The overall method does not present maturity levels, exception made for the 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
Page 29 
 
31 indicators necessary for PBSCI calculation. The indicators maturity dimensions range from 2 to 
5 levels and an arbitrary weight is assigned to each level, which is later accounted for in the calcu-
lation of the PCI. 
 
Requirements for a product circularity measurement framework 
 
The framework assesses products’ circularity at a system level, based on systemic circularity guide-
lines and by considering several areas such as circular product design, system conditions, reverse 
cycles and new business models developed for the product. The method is based on both eco-
efficiency and eco-effectiveness CE principles and measures environmental, economic, social im-
pacts potential and recovery potential across the life cycle of products. Although the final score of 
products is mono-dimensional, the process considers CE multi-dimensionality and assigns different 
weights to those dimensions. Moreover, the different ways to close the loop are distinctly considered 
and the weights reflect their hierarchy. In conclusion, the LCA-C2C-PBSCI method is systemic by 
design. 
 
Based on the available data about the framework, it is not possible to deduce if the framework can 
or cannot be integrated with other tools that are in use in the industry as for example those used 
during the design stage. Also, it is not possible to conclude if the framework supports data construc-
tion through for example standardised input datasheets. Moreover, the framework does not provide 
practical and explicit improvement guidance. Consequently, it is unknown if the framework is inte-
grated and operational. 
 
The authors of the framework have expanded the method with others, such as the VIKOR method, 
and are planning to explore the framework combination with Life Cycle Sustainability Assessment 
(LCSA) (Antwi-Afari, Ng, Chen, et al., 2023). The framework was developed in the construction con-
text, but it is based on systematic literature review pertaining generally to CE and not only to CE in 
the construction sector. The framework can be considered adaptive and flexible at the current level 
of development. Since the developed indicators for the PBSCI method are not exhaustive, the au-
thors are planning to develop a set of indicators together with construction sector experts to include 
specificities of the sector in the assessment (Antwi-Afari, Ng, Chen, et al., 2023). Therefore, the 
future development of the methos might lose its generality. 
 
As for its current form, the framework does not present a graphical user interface. Its application 
requires a set of specific capabilities and knowledge both during the method application and the 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
Page 30 
 
results interpretation (Antwi-Afari, Ng, & Chen, 2023), therefore it cannot be adopted in the industry 
by non-experts in circular economy and LCA assessments. Moreover, the processes of the methods, 
particularly the LCA method, require a great amount of time to carry out. The LCA-C2C-PBSCI 
method is manually applied, meaning that the results are subjected to human computational errors 
and the process is not time efficient (Antwi-Afari, Ng, & Chen, 2023). The framework in its current 
form is not user-friendly and time efficient.  
 
The framework takes into consideration the sustainability pillars, especially environmental and eco-
nomic pillars. This is achieved by adopting the LCA and PBSCI method, particularly the LCA covers 
the environmental pillar, while the PBSCI covers economic considerations. The framework’s authors 
are planning on future integrations of the method with LCSA method (Antwi-Afari, Ng, Chen, et al., 
2023), therefore the assessment will take into consideration the social pillar perspective as well.

usability, and recyclability. 
Design for  
services 
The strategy implies that a product manufacturer is obligated to maintain the product condition 
during its lifetime. In the service contract concept, a material or product passport is created, 
and product performance is tracked all the time. Servicing is applied when the material or 
product starts to lose its condition, not when the situation has reached a critical limit. Services 
apply also at the EoL of the product, through take-back-systems for example. 
Design with LCA 
Use of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) during product design, aiming to reduce environmental 
impacts throughout all stages of the product's life cycle. 
Material use  
optimisation 
The strategy involves reducing both the quantity and diversity of materials used. It is important 
to reduce material input, maintaining the functionality of the product without compromising its 
quality and minimise material losses during product manufacturing and build construction. An 
example are prefabricated products that contributes to minimise material lose on construction 
sites and reduce waste by 65-80%. 
Material  
substitution 
Material substitution involves the replacement of traditional materials with alternatives that of-
fer specific advantages. The material substitution is based on criteria such as locality, renewa-
bility, environmental impact, quality, durability, ease of assembly/disassembly, Cradle to Cra-
dle (C2C) certification, purity, maintenance-free properties, and the ability to retain or increase 
value over time. Examples include replacing concrete and metal constructions with wood. 
Non-toxic  
Materials use 
This strategy involves the use of materials and substances that are not toxic to human health 
or the environment. In construction products, prioritizing non-toxic substances means select-
ing materials that are free from hazardous chemicals or pollutants. This approach aims to cre-
ate a safer and healthier built environment for occupants, construction workers, and surround-
ing ecosystems. 
Traceability 
This strategy involves the creation of databases and tools to document materials, components 
and products characteristics, usage history and conditions across their life cycle. This includes 
the implementation of material passports, products labels, etc. Standardisation and central 
registration of these documents in dedicated platforms are essential practices. 
Use of recycled  
materials 
This strategy involves increasing the recycled content of construction products. The proveni-
ence of materials can be from construction and demolition activities or from the manufacturing 
process itself. The aim is to slow and close resource loops by incorporating materials that 
have undergone recycling processes. 
Use of renewable en-
ergy 
This strategy encourages the transition to renewable energy to completely avoid the use of 
fossil fuels. During production, energy efficiency-driven practices should be implemented to 
reduce energy consumption. The use of renewable energy sources, such as solar panels, ge-
othermal, wind, and biomass energy are a priority. 
Use of reused  
materials 
This strategy involves increasing the reused content of construction products. The proveni-
ence of materials, components or products can be from construction and demolition activities 
or from the manufacturing process itself. The aim is to slow and close resource loops. 
Water and wastewater  
efficiency 
The focus is on employing water and wastewater efficient techniques and technologies to min-
imize water consumption. This includes practices such as recycling water and monitoring wa-
ter consume.

4.5 Proposed Conceptual Framework 
 
The Conceptual Framework is presented in two parts to accommodate its dimensions and maintain 
readability. The framework develops horizontally from left to right, by first indicating the products’ life 
cycle stages targeted by the strategies and indicators on the right. The life cycle stages are grouped 
into Procurement & Manufacturing and Use & End-of-Life, serving as the main partitions of the frame-
work for better visualisation. For each group of life cycle stages, four sub-categories indicate the 
main themes at the core of the strategies and indicators on the right. In each sub-category, the 
related strategies, indicators and indicators’ methods are presented. The implementation degree of 
a strategy can be assessed from more than one perspective, so one or more coherent indicators are 
assigned to each strategy. Each indicator identified in literature might present different assessment 
methods or none. When different methods are available, the one structured in levels of maturity is 
shown in the final representation of the framework and this is because the methods complement 
each other. For example, “Amount of secondary materials from recycling” can involve calculating the 
percentage by mass of recycled materials in a product or using ranges of percentages by mass of 
recycled materials, with each range corresponding to a level. Such methods are coherent and com-
plementary, and they can be simultaneously used. The levels of maturity range from 2 to 5 and this 
is due to the way they were identified in literature. When no method is available for an indicator, the 
star (*) symbol is used to indicate its absence. The symbol is generally used throughout the frame-
work to indicate the absence of strategies, indicators or methods. 
 
Across the framework, the “Primary Materials Substitution” sub-category is the only one that does 
not present absences and comprises the largest number of strategies compared to the other sub-
categories. Materials can be substituted using non-toxic materials, sustainably produced renewable 
materials, and secondary materials both from reuse and recycling. In this sub-category, large part of 
the indicators is quantitative and do not present a maturity levels structure. The indicators grouped 
under the “Material substitution” strategy measure the substitution of raw materials, but also the 
quantity of raw materials that are yet to be substituted. There is an overlap between the “Amount of 
secondary materials” indicator, “Amount of secondary materials from recycling” and “Amount of sec-
ondary materials from reuse”, since secondary materials comprise both recycled and reused mate-
rials without distinction. 
 
The indicators in the "Value Chain" sub-category focus on upstream value chains. This involves 
assessing considerations such as the localization of materials, the intentional selection of procure-

ment routes based on sustainability criteria, the engagement of key actors, and the extent collabo-
rations span across markets. The indicators are structured as maturity levels and each of them pre-
sents three maturity levels. Strategies regarding product value chains were not identified in literature.

5. Discussion 
5.1 Circular Economy Assessment Framework 
 
In the examined literature, a single framework was identified, confirming the lack of assessments 
specifically developed for construction products. The framework presents a scientific and holistic 
methodology to measure products systemic circularity. However, when it comes to the specific prob-
lems and needs of the industry that motivated this thesis, the framework cannot be directly adopted. 
Firstly, the LCA-C2C-PBSCI analysis current development stage suggests that it is not readily scal-
able at industry level, but needs further tests and developments. Moreover, it lacks in user-friendli-
ness because it is a complex method, and it is currently manual. The complexity is given by the 
adoption of methods that require expertise both in the application of those and in the interpretation 
of the results. Not only it lacks in terms of user-friendliness, but it is also time-consuming due to its 
complexity. The complexity is further aggravated by considering the products at a building aggre-
gated level in the PBSCI calculation, requiring building data that might not be available at the building 
design stage when a products’ circularity assessment is needed for decision-making purposes. 
Therefore, the framework is not a ready-to-use solution for non-experts in LCA and CE and it does 
not cover the distance between academically developed instruments and their applicability in the 
construction industry. Another critical observation is that the framework is not specifically tailored for 
the construction sector, even though it was developed in the construction context. In order to specif-
ically cater to industry needs, the framework should undergo experts’ evaluation and further adapta-
tions. In conclusion, academics are starting to address the lack of CE assessments for construction 
products, but the development of such tools is in its infancy and far from becoming an industry stand-
ard. 
 
5.2 Circular Economy Indicators and Maturity Dimensions 
 
Although the non-academic articles analysed were significantly less than the academic articles, the 
rate of indicators identified in each cluster of papers is comparable, in fact around five indicators 
were identified per paper in each cluster. The non-academic papers were included in the study with 
the intent to consider potential developments that are different from those in academia and get an 
understanding of industry focus and potential needs. At the same time, it is important to keep into 
account the different amount of papers in each cluster when drawing conclusions. In the occurrences 
analysis of the indicators, the discordance between the indicators mentioned in academic papers 
and those mentioned in non-academic papers is evident and it is confirmed by the characteristics 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
Page 45 
 
analysis. The indicators addressing business performance, production performance or value chain 
performance were identified only in academic literature. 
Not many indicators are mentioned more than 3 times and in general, those mentioned more than 3 
times can be found in both types of articles. The exceptions are “Amount of secondary materials - 
from reuse” indicator which is mentioned 3 times only in non-academic literature, and “Renewable 
electric energy consumption” which is mentioned 4 times only in academic literature. The thematic 
difference between these two indicators is a pattern, in fact the non-academic articles tend to focus 
on materials, whereas the academic articles tend to focus on materials as well, but to consider also 
other themes such as energy or water consumption, design considerations of the products, etc. 
Therefore, there is a common interest from both sides to analyse material flows, but academics are 
also expanding considerations other than those on materials. The academic efforts to define more 
systemic indicators are not yet consolidated though, in fact 68% of the indicators identified in aca-
demic papers occur only one time. 
The most mentioned indicators beside “Renewable electric energy consumption”, which are “Amount 
of secondary materials - from recycling”, “Reuse potential”, “Take-back system”, “Amount of second-
ary materials - from reuse” and “Recycling potential”, have in common the fact that they all refer to 
reuse and recycling, might it be in terms of material input during manufacturing or at the end-of-life 
of products. Particularly, “Amount of secondary materials from recycling” and “Recycling potential” 
indicators are defined in identical ways both in academic and non-academic literature. This confirms 
that indicators targeting materials, reuse and recycling are more developed and that indicators that 
consider more systemic aspects need to be further developed. 
Another important observation is that academic literature has mostly developed qualitative indicators 
structured as maturity levels, whereas non-academic literature has largely developed quantitative 
indicators in the form of mathematical formulas. The latter define in detail the variables to consider 
in the calculation, and the formulas, even if simple, are very well specified. This might stem from a 
need in the industry of very well-defined tools to reduce misunderstandings during their application 
and produce reliable results. Although 60% of the indicators are formulated as maturity levels, they 
were identified in only two recent academic papers pinpointing the recent development of such indi-
cators. Given the fact that most of the indicators measuring business model performance, value 
chain performance and production performance present a maturity levels structure, once again it is 
confirmed that systemic indicators are in their infancy. The maturity levels proposed are based on 
literature review and when data was missing, the authors formulated them. Some of the maturity 
levels are in fact poorly defined, for example the maturity levels in “Water consumption” indicator are 
defined as “no efforts to reduce water consumption”, “low efforts to reduce water consumption”, 
“moderate efforts” and “high efforts”. This type of indicators needs a thorough formulation based on 
==PAGE:46==

an in-depth industry examination of circular cases to identify present possible and future potential 
levels of development.  
 
5.3 Circular Economy Strategies  
 
Once again, the academic articles outnumbered the non-academic articles. With few exceptions, 
there is accordance between the strategies identified in academic literature and those identified in 
non-academic literature. “Water and wastewater efficiency” is mentioned once in academic literature, 
observation that partially aligns with the development of indicators measuring water efficiency, since 
also those are mentioned once. “Design for maintenance” is mentioned only by academic literature 
and once again the observation aligns with the development of indicators in this area, in fact the 
indicators were identified in academic literature. “Design with LCA” and “Non-toxic materials use” 
were identified in both literature typologies, but they are mentioned twice, and this might be due to 
their affinity with sustainability rather than circularity. Compared to the indicators, strategies are more 
uniformly and thoroughly developed in both types of articles. They are also evenly distributed along 
the life cycle stages.  
 
5.4 Conceptual Framework 
 
By analysing the conceptual framework, it can be noticed that there is accordance between the de-
velopment of strategies and indicators regarding recycled and reused material input during the pro-
duction and recycling and reuse potential at the end of products life. In fact, these indicators and 
strategies are repeatedly mentioned in literature. This confirms that materials reuse and recycle 
strategies and indicators are well developed by academics and non-academics. Another observation 
is that strategies focus particularly on “Design for durability” and “Design for Disassembly”, whereas 
the indicators assessing the achievement of such strategies are underdeveloped. The mismatch in 
terms of level of development is observed for other strategies as well, such as “Traceability”, “Design 
for services” and in part for “Material use optimisation” and “Design for maintenance”. This underlines 
the need for better defined and structured indicators that specifically measure the achievement of 
such strategies.  
There is a mismatch also the other way around, for example indicators in the “Value Chain” category 
do not refer to a strategy and the same can be observed for part of the indicators related to the end 
of products life. Although the identified strategies are well developed, there are other strategies that 
still need to be defined. Moreover, these observations pose the question on how systemic the iden-
tified strategies and indicators are, highlighting the need for further investigations in this direction.

The framework confirms that some indicators’ methods need to be formulated and some others fur-

5.5 Limitations and Future Research 
 
The study applies to construction products and was limited to literature developed in the construction 
industry context, therefore it is exclusively valid only in this context. The included literature from non-
academic sources is limited to few papers and it is not possible to draw accurate conclusions on the 
real development state of construction products circular assessments and metrics in the industry. 
Nonetheless, the inclusion of such evidence was meaningful in data analysis by shading light on 
aspects that otherwise would have been ignored such as the commonality of indicators regarding 
material flows. The academic papers included in the research are limited to those found on Scopus, 
moreover some of the articles selected for further evaluation were discarded due to access limita-
tions. There might be other developments in literature that have not been taken into consideration. 
On another level, some of the articles included that were reporting indicators were also mentioning 
further materials to access, but the link was not functioning. Therefore, it is not known if some of 
those indicators that are not presenting a method are better defined in the extra documents.  
 
Many gaps have been identified that can be summarised as follows: 
 
• 
A lack of circular maturity assessments of construction products, particularly systemic, relia-
ble and at the same time ready-to-use by non-experts of CE in the construction industry as 
a decision-making tool. 
• 
A lack of systemic indicators other than those focusing on material reuse and recycling. 
• 
A lack of industry experts involvement in the formulation of indicators and maturity dimen-
sions. 
• 
A lack of thoroughly defined and standardised maturity dimensions that clearly depict realistic 
levels of development. 
• 
Strategies are well defined, but further research is needed to identify or formulate strategies 
at a systemic level by including considerations on value chains, business models and pro-
duction systems in relation to products. 
 
This study was limited to the construction sector with the objective to specifically identify those strat-
egies, indicators and maturity dimensions that for instance apply to the construction sector. None-
==PAGE:48==

theless, given the advancement level of such concepts in the construction sector, it becomes rele-
vant to further explore other sectors for the future development of a circular maturity assessment for 
construction products. Moreover, it is imperative to include industry experts in the development of 
such tools with the aim to understand needs and formulate indicators and maturity levels specifically 
based on industry needs, reality and practices. Although the building level was not considered in this 
thesis, another important thematic is to explore trough further research how the choice of certain 
circular products might influence the decision on CE strategies to implement at a building level and 
vice versa, based on the CE strategies to implement at a building level what CE products can guar-
antee a better achievement of the CE strategies at a building level.   
 
6. Conclusion 
This research aimed at moving a first step toward developing a circular maturity assessment of con-
struction products that will assist practitioners in the choice of circular construction products during 
the building design process. This first step consisted in an exploratory study in academic and non-
academic literature to understand the current level of development of research in this context, identify 
gaps and present a collection of identified strategies, indicators and maturity dimensions organised 
in a conceptual framework that will serve as base for further studies and developments. In order to 
achieve the purpose of the thesis, two research questions were formulated and a specific method-
ology was developed to answer them. The methodology adopted to answer both questions consisted 
in a Systematic Literature Review process and in the Conceptual Framework Analysis method. As-
sessments, indicators, maturity dimensions and strategies targeting circularity in construction prod-
ucts were analysed. 
 
Research Question 1: 
What are the key gaps and limitations in the existing academic and non-academic literature related 
to maturity assessments and circularity indicators within the context of construction products’ circu-
lar economy maturity assessment? 
 
During the systematic literature review, the research highlighted that academics are starting to de-
velop circular assessments for construction products, but the process is in its infancy, in fact only 
one framework was identified which is in the testing phase. Although research is moving towards 
such tools, much is yet to be defined and developed. For instance, such tools must be developed 
together with experts and must respond the industry needs of ready-to-use tools that can be em-
ployed by practitioners that are not experts in CE. This is a gap in research on a more generic level, 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
Page 49 
 
not only regarding the assessment of construction products. The gap particularly hinders the adop-
tion of CE practices in the construction industry.  
Indicators regarding materials flows, reuse and recycling are well developed in literature, but the sole 
focus on materials it is not enough to encompass CE applications at a more systemic level. By fo-
cusing on particular life cycle stages or particular processes without considering the entire system 
of a product comprising for example of value chains, the risk is to increase emissions and promote 
unsustainable practices in the not considered life cycle stages or processes. The indicators identified 
that cover various parts of the system are underdeveloped. They do not holistically cover the entire 
product system, and this is a limitation for the analysed indicators. Moreover, it is not known what 
the boundaries of the product system are. Large part of the indicators is structured as maturity levels. 
From the analysis emerged that they are not thoroughly defined, for instance some definitions are 
vague and none of those indicators was developed with the inclusion of experts. To conclude, indi-
cators need further development to enhance the coverage of product systems and the accuracy of 
maturity dimensions. Compared to the indicators, strategies are better defined in literature. Never-
theless, further analysis is needed to understand to what degree the strategies are systemic, and 
further development. 
 
 
Research Question 2: 
How can a circular economy maturity assessment framework be conceptualised from literature as 
a base for further developments toward a circular maturity assessment for construction products? 
 
 
To answer the second question, a framework was conceptualised to present the concepts identified 
in literature. Strategies, indictors and maturity dimensions underwent a process of codification, cat-
egorisation and conceptualisation that resulted in the final framework (Table 3, Table 4). The frame-
work categorise strategies and indicators based on the life cycle stage they refer to and eight the-
matic sub-categories. From the framework and the analysis, it is possible to identify the strategies 
and indicators that need improvements. Indicators’ methods are summarised in the framework as 
well. The collection of concepts logically organised in the framework will serve as basis for the next 
development stages.

8.1 Systematic Literature Review Protocol 
 
Review planning 
1. Question formulation 
1.1. Question Focus: 
Main objective: identifying, categorizing and integrating CE maturity assessments and indicators that 
are relevant for the construction sector. 
It is expected to find ways to measure CE maturity that can be applied to construction products.  
 
1.2. Question Quality and Amplitude:

egies, indicators, and maturity levels data types can refer to materials and components since these are prod-
