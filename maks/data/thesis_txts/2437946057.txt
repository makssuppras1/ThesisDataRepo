Abstract 
Visual comfort has been investigated excessively over the last decades, but since comfort is a 
subjective assessment, it has been difficult to reach a consensus regarding the threshold limits for 
optimal comfortable daylight.  
  
The aim of this study is to find a potential upper limit to comfortable illuminance levels, with the 
help of subjective user responses in relation to illuminance levels in an office environment. A 
secondary goal is to assess the use of existing daylight metrics for predicting visual comfort. 
 
A landscaped office located in Copenhagen, Denmark has been investigated, and recordings of 
both horizontal and vertically illuminance has been conducted. 23 occupants participated in the 
questionnaire regarding their assessment of the daylight on a daily basis, resulting in 64 responses. 
The duration of the experiment has been 3 weeks, allowing the lighting conditions ranging from 
177 to 12,055 lux.  
 
In this study an experiment for user-subjective assessment of daylight was conducted in order to 
address the aim of the thesis. Along with continuous measurements of illuminance levels, a 
simulation-based study of annual daylight distribution and assessment and evaluation of existing 
glare was incorporated. The subjective responses and the recordings of illuminance were analysed 
using relevant statistical methods.  
None of the investigations showed statistically significant correlations for neither the recordings 
nor for the simulations. The experiment was limited by the number of subjective responses, which 
may contribute to a non-significant correlation, as well as limitations in both measurement 
equipment and lack of extreme daylight conditions due to a limited measurement period.  
 
However, logistic regression analysis, offers a fine potential for developing a solid mathematical 
model for prediction of the percentage of dissatisfied.  
It was concluded, that more participants are needed for further studies, and that the choice of field 
study must be ensured to offer extreme daylight conditions for investigation of an upper threshold 
limit to daylight for human comfort.

1 Introduction 
The use of daylight in office building is a way of reducing the building’s energy consumption, but 
it also provides the occupants of the building with both psychological and physical benefits. Most 
humans spend a considerable amount of time indoor, and according to Amudadottir et al. 
(Amundadottir et al., 2017), the average human living on the northern latitude spends around 2 
hours  outside pr. day, thus there has been found an decrease in the exposure to daylight with an 
increase in the exposure of artificial lights. A daily exposure to natural daylight is said to increase 
health and provide several psychological benefits (Brembilla, Mardaljevic and Hopfe, 2013).  Too 
much illumination can increase the risk of glare, while low levels of daylight increase the need for 
artificial lights, and therefore contributes to a higher energy consumption. So, the right 
combination of daylight for office environments is however a difficult task. 
 
Many studies have evolved around the need for minimizing discomfort for occupants in spaces 
with daylight but is lacking an established consensus in the design industry, of what metric should 
be used, and which design criteria should be upheld. Glare usually arises, when direct sunlight 
enters the room or reflects of interior surfaces.  Additionally, glare can also be the result from sun 
reflectance of exterior surfaces into an occupied room.  Discomfort caused by daylight can have 
consequences for the occupants both on a long- and short term. Modern day workers with 
computer-oriented task, must adjust their eyes to changes in illumination and has recorded eye 
movements in up to 30,000 times a day, reported by Osterhaus (Osterhaus, 2005). Symptoms such 
as headaches and fatigue are a known consequence from the strain of eyes, and muscular pain may 
be added, if occupants try to adjust their posture in order to avoid discomfort.  
 
The term of glare can be divided into two main categorize: Disability glare and discomfort glare. 
Disability glare arises when the visual performance is reduced, while discomfort glare is glare 
which is not necessary noticeable to the occupants at first.   
 
While disability glare now is well understood by researchers, the experience of discomfort glare 
still needs more research. There has been developed numerous methods to predict glare issues 
from daylight via controlled environments and electrically lit laboratories, but these has proven 
not necessarily to be a representation of the experience of occupants in real spaces lit by daylight 
((Heschong Mahone Group, 2012), (Van Den Wymelenberg and Inanici, 2014)). The need for

more investigation of current daylight metrics is needed for finding the ranges of illumination 
within comfortable ranges. The objective of this thesis is to investigate an upper threshold limit to 
illuminance levels with the use of subjective assessment from users of a real office environment.  
For this purpose, an office building had been selected as a case study, where the occupants will 
answer questionnaires about their satisfaction of the daylight level on a daily basis over a one 
working week period. Meanwhile, both vertical and horizontal illuminance levels will be recorded. 
In addition, a detailed simulation study of the annual daylight distribution will be conducted as 
well. Statistical methods will be used in order to determine or reject correlation between the 
variables. 
 
A study performed by Nabil et al. (Nabil and Mardaljevic, 2005) indicates that exposure to high 
illuminances during daytime, can help maintaining the circadian rhythm which is linked with 
beneficial health aspects. Mardaljevic et al. (Mardaljevic et al., 2013) reports that a Japanese study 
conducted in 2004, found that exposure to a bright lightning level (2500 lux) seem to promote 
alertness and mood, especially in the afternoon. The same study also reported, that it seems to 
affect the production of melatonin (a natural hormone which is helps regulate the circadian rhythm 
in humans and animals) and a fall in body temperature, which should improve the quality of sleep. 
The discovery of photo-receptors cells in the eyes with nerve connections to the brain, that uses 
light to control the biochemical processes, have revealed how light influences the body. Especially, 
the circadian rhythm is controlled by these processes, which increases health, well-being and sleep 
quality. Regularly bright light exposure is also recorded to reduce depressive symptoms, especially 
for people working indoors in winter times in high latitudes as reported by Mardaljevic et al. 
(Mardaljevic and Nabil, 2005). Currently however, there is not conclusive evidences that 
correlates daylight exposure with worker productivity, but it is now evident that occupants prefer 
daylight exposure, as well as a view of the outside whilst working in a glare free environment, 
according to Mardaljevic et al. (Mardaljevic and Nabil, 2005).  
 
According to E. Brembilla et al. (Brembilla, Mardaljevic and Hopfe, 2013) glare is a 
uncomfortable sensation  that occurs when the eye is exposed to another luminous condition, than 
the eye is adapted to. The degree of glare can vary from permanent damage of the retina in the eye, 
to a less serious degree of temporary visual reduction. The occurrence of glare can be divided into 
two groups: (1) Retina damage, flash blindness, distracting glare and paralyzing glare are rarely 
experienced, and (2) discomfort glare, veiling glare and disability glare are more commonly

experienced. Whilst both disability and veiling glare are easier to cope with, discomfort glare is a 
more difficult challenge. Although studies show, that discomfort glare can be associated with pupil 
fluctuation and activities in the facial muscles, there has been found no linear magnitude with 
discomfort, according to Hopkinson (Hopkinson, 1972).   
 
1.1 Background on currently used Daylight Metrics 
In design phases of new buildings, advanced tools for predicting the daylight distribution is 
convenient in order to generate estimations of daylight sufficiency and to estimate, if there is need 
for solar shading solutions to avoid glare issues. A commonly used metric for daylight conditions 
is the Daylight Factor (DF), which is the ratio between the horizontal illuminance level inside and 
the illuminance level outside under an overcast sky condition. The DF is still one of the most used 
daylight metrics, caused by its simple application method (Mardaljevic and Nabil, 2005). Since 
the DF does not consider the rapidly change of illuminance under real sky condition, caused by 
either change in the sun position and/or sky condition, the DF does not fully explain a realistic 
condition. Recent studies (Nabil and Mardaljevic, 2006) recommend using climate-based 
modelling to make a realistic condition in the daylight distribution and intensity in buildings. A 
climate-based daylight simulation calculates the illuminance, while taken sky conditions founded 
on annual climate data sets into account, as data sets contains information about the typically 
occurring weather conditions such as wind speed, air temperatures and solar irradiation.  
 
Many daylight metrics using climate data sets, has been suggested such as Useful Daylight 
Illuminance (UDI), Daylight Autonomy (DA), spatial Daylight Autonomy (sDA), etc.  
 
The Daylight Autonomy is defined as the percentage of hours on an annual basis, where a 
minimum threshold of illuminance (defined by the user) on a horizontal plane is met by daylight 
alone, according to Reinhart (Reinhart, Mardaljevic and Rogers, 2006). The DA was later 
redefined as the percentage of occupied hours where the minimum threshold of illuminance level 
was met by daylight.   
 
The Useful Daylight Illuminance (UDI), was introduced by Nabil et. Al (Mardaljevic and Nabil, 
2005). The UDI is defined, as how often in a year the illuminance provided by daylight is within 
a range of 100 - 3000 lux. It is proposed by the authors, that the range between 100 - 3000 lux is 
considered to offer potentially useful illumination for the occupants. Values below 100 lux are 
generally considered to be insufficient as the sole source of illumination, while values above 3000 
==PAGE:13==
Chapter | 1 Introduction 
Page | 4 
 
lux is likely to cause discomfort. The minimum and maximum levels of illuminance, which were 
stated as being 'useful', were determined by a literature survey based on occupant behaviour in 
different daylit spaces. The authors recommend, that illuminance level over 3000 lux should not 
be excluded entirely by design choices, but there should be paid special attention to the occurrence 
for illuminance levels above 3000 lux. However, it should be noticed that the studies of the range 
of useful illumination were founded on, were performed before LCD screens became common. 
Older types of monitor screens, such as old CRT monitors were more prone to cause veiling glare, 
according to the study by Nabil et al. (Mardaljevic and Nabil, 2005).  
 
1.1.1 Upper Limits for Illuminance Level 
The limits for upper illuminance levels is a question for ongoing research. For office spaces it is 
recommended that an illuminance threshold of 500 lux should be obtained in the task area (on a 
horizontal plane) and with a band of 0.5 m around with a illuminance value of 300 lux, with a 
background illumination of 1/3 of the illuminance value of the immediate surrounding area as 
stated in the Danish Standard 12464-1:2011 (BSI, 2011), as large contrast in illumination also can 
cause visual discomfort.  
 
With the construction of new buildings and with extensive renovation, the Danish Building 
Regulation (Trafik-og-Byggestyrelsen, 2018) states that daylight conditions can be considered as 
sufficient, if it can be documented, that there is at least 300 lux in the work space in at least 50 % 
of the hours with sunlight. The regulation also states, that windows should be designed with solar 
shading, so that direct sunlight and glare issues can be avoided, however it is not specified how a 
documentation on avoidance of glare issue should be conducted. 
 
An extensive field study lead by Lisa Heschong (Heschong Mahone Group, 2012) conducted on 
behalf of California Energy Commission, investigated over 61 different locations in California in 
2011. The field study included offices, schools and public libraries, where both expert and 
occupant assessment were weighted. In a thorough choice of study spaces, 77 locations in three 
states in the United States were visited (California, New York and Washington). Out of the 77 
spaces, 61 locations were chosen. Some spaces were excluded from the study sample, as there 
were limited access to either the space or approach to the occupants, or the space were too 
geometrically complex, so a simulation of the light distribution would not be accurate enough for 
==PAGE:14==
Chapter | 1 Introduction 
Page | 5 
 
the study with the available simulation tools. The final study sample consisted of 28 buildings, 
with 61 space locations divided on 22 classrooms, 23 offices and 16 public libraries.  
The initial space sample (performed on 71 spaces) was conducted by ‘experts’ which were ‘trained 
in architectural daylighting techniques, climate data and analysis’, and therefore could provide 
with a thorough assessment of each sample space. The occupant assessment of the spaces should 
provide the study with direct experience over-time assessment. 
 
The research team from the Daylight metrics (Public Interest  Energy Research) report (Heschong 
Mahone Group, 2012) recommends that a threshold of 300 lux should be used for daylight 
sufficiency metric in offices. In the report, it was found, that the metric with most potential in 
predicting both expert and occupant assessment, were the "spatial Daylight Autonomy" 
(sDA300/50%).  
 
The final report from the study conducted by Heschong did not reach any conclusion for an upper 
limit for daylight, as the findings in the study stipulates, that a high illuminance value (e.g. 4000 
lux) had a positive association with the statement "The daylight in this room is never too bright". 
Thus, the given analysis could not detect a threshold, where either occupants or expert team would 
start to complain about the daylight illuminance. With an increase in the illuminance level, the 
correlation between the illuminance level and the subjective responses steadily got lower. The 
research team suspect that inhabited spaces are not the right methodology for defining the upper 
or lower bounds of acceptable space characteristics. Given these findings, the IES Committee1 
voted not to include an upper value for threshold of daylight and suggest that more studies and 
investigations in that area should be carried out. The research team found, that the metric that best 
could predict visual discomfort, were the number of hour’s sunlight could potentially enter the 
space, which were defined as the Annual Sun Exposure (ASE). The ASE is defined as the 
percentage of an area that receives >1000 lux in 250 hours of the defined occupied hours annually.  
 
The study (Heschong Mahone Group, 2012) shows that there is a consensus, that an illuminance 
level of 100-300 lux is satisfying while supported by artificial lights to some extent, depending on 
the visual task. Some studies (Nabil and Mardaljevic, 2006) even indicates, that lower levels of 
illuminance provided by natural daylight instead of artificial light are accepted by the users, which

1.1.2 Discomfort Glare: Illuminance or Luminance Levels 
Although there has not yet been an agreement upon daylight metric, which includes an upper limit 
for illuminance levels, a series of glare metrics has been developed which measure the extreme 
occurrences for glare conditions specified has ‘disturbing’ or ‘intolerable’. There have been many 
studies on glare conditions in buildings, but with a main focus on artificial lighting. Many indices 
are developed to predict and evaluate glare from light fixtures and their position such as Unified 
Glare Rating index (UGR). 
 
The emerge of high dynamic imaging made it possible to capture (or render) a fish-eye image, 
which could record the actual luminance distribution of entire scenes in the captured moment.   
With this at-the-time new method, the glare probability index (DGP) were introduced by J. 
Wienold et al. (Wienold and Christoffersen, 2006). The DGP index is a glare metric, which can be 
used to predict the probability of glare perception. The DGP evaluates the vertical illuminance at 
the eye level against the luminance of the glare sources taking both the solid angle and position 
index into account.

Findings from a study performed by Tuaycharoen et al. (Tuaycharoen and Tregenza, 2007), 
concluded, that discomfort from glare is associated with the interest of view from the glare source 
(window). Two experiments concluded, that a window with an interesting view (e.g. vegetation, 
nature, large variation in colour) is less likely to course discomfort than a window with a less 
interesting view (e.g. urban, concrete building, little variation in colour) with the same mean 
luminance. These finding indicates that the human acceptance and preference cannot be rated by 
a single metric, and that luminance-based metrics seemly should have a higher correlation with 
human response, as luminance is more closely to the human perception of light than illuminance.    
 
A study performed over six months by Van Den Wymelenberg et al. (Van Den Wymelenberg and 
Inanici, 2014) reviewed common lighting design metrics and their ability to explain the variability 
==PAGE:17==

in human subjective response. The authors of this article confirmed (among others) that the vertical 
illuminance level (measured at either top of monitor screen in user's view direction or at user's 
eye-level) are more fitting to the human subjective response, than illuminance level measured on 
desktop. Luminance-based metrics such as DGI2 and DGP were also recorded during the study 
and showed, that commonly used luminance-metrics did not show a stronger predictive ability 
than the illuminance-based metrics. The authors concluded, that further studies of luminance-
based metrics should be performed, as it is more closely related to human perception of brightness 
than illuminance-based metrics are.  
 
The type of visual task, can also have an impact on how well the level of illuminance is perceived. 
Most visual tasks in offices are now consisting of view direction towards a vertical plane 
(computer-oriented tasks), whereas the number of visual task on the horizontal plane (paper-
oriented task) are more rarely performed − in contrast to most office environments in the past. In 
the work by Van Den Wymelenberg et al. (Van Den Wymelenberg and Inanici, 2014) the only 
horizontal measured illuminance on a desktop ranked highest for questions regarding satisfaction 
for the amount of light suited for a paper-oriented task. For all other subjective items, the horizontal 
measurements were outranked by the vertical illuminance metrics, with the vertical illuminance 
measured on top of the monitor in the participants view direction held the best overall correlation 
coefficient.  
 
An experiment performed by Velds (Velds, 2002) showed a high correlation of vertical 
illuminance measured at the approximately the position of the participants’ position in a 1.2 m 
from the window in the test facility. The experiment used a ’pop up’ question, which were 
developed for the purpose of recording the participants acceptance response, while performing 
continuous recordings of the illuminance. The experiment showed a higher percentage of 
dissatisfied for a computer-oriented task, than for a horizontal task. The study was conducted under 
either intermediate or overcast sky conditions. Velds found a percentage of dissatisfied in the 
interval of 20% - 50% with a vertical eye illuminance measured towards the window from the 
participants position of 3700 – 6300 lux, respectively.

1.2 Problem Statement 
Visual comfort is a relatively new field of study and until the recent focus on energy consumption 
and worker productivity it was seen as part of the indoor climate, as a concept that was based on 
intuitive parts of the design process. From the review of current literature, it can be concluded, that 
the findings regarding subjective responses and measurements of daylight metrics is an ongoing 
process, where there is no final evidence, where the ranges of comfort are found in both controlled 
laboratory environments as well as in case studies of real buildings. Moreover, there is currently 
no consensus of whether the existing daylight metrics should include an upper limit and if it should 
be a luminance or illuminance measure. Several studies, has concluded that measurements at eye 
level have a higher correlation with subjective human responses.   
 
The aim of the study is to find a potential upper limit to comfortable illuminance levels, with the 
help of subjective user responses in relation to illuminance levels in an office environment. A 
secondary goal is to assess the use of existing daylight metrics for predicting visual comfort. 
 
The selected office is a typical office building in Copenhagen, which was studied and investigated 
in both an experimental field study and a simulation study. In the field study the illuminance levels 
were measured using lux meters and compared to subjective responses from the occupants of the 
office, which were recorded using a specific designed questionnaire. The simulation study was 
conducted with detailed annual simulations of the daylight distribution, where simulated 
illuminance levels were investigated.

2.1 Selection of Independent Variables 
In this section, the list of independent variables will be explained briefly and why they were 
chosen.  
 
2.1.1 Illuminance 
Both horizontal illuminance and vertical illuminance in two dominant view directions in the 
investigated office will be measured. The vertical illuminance levels (measured at eye-level of an 
observer) has proven to show better correlation with the subjective user assessment, than the 
horizontal measured illuminance. The illuminance – and not luminance is chosen as the main 
independent variable, as it is easier to both measure and simulate than the luminance, which is 
more complex and time consuming. Illuminance as the independent variable is a better fit for the 
aim of this thesis, which is to find a correlation between subjective responses and easy-to-use for 
professionals, as well as for semi-professionals.    
 
2.1.2 Annual Climate-based Daylight Metrics  
Three annual daylight metrics has been chosen as independent variables, which will be used to 
see, if there can be found a correlation between them and the subjective responses from the 
participants of the questionnaire.   
 
2.1.2.1 Spatial Daylight Autonomy  
The spatial daylight autonomy (sDA300/50%) defines how many occupied hours there is 300 lux or 
more in the area of investigation. The sDA300/50% index were chosen by L. Heschong (Heschong 
Mahone Group, 2012), in a major field investigation of daylight metrics used for predicting visual 
comfort on behalf of the California Energy Commission. In addition, the sDA300/50% index is also 
chosen as one way of predicting daylight sufficiency by the Danish Building Code (Trafik-og-
Byggestyrelsen, 2018).  
 
2.1.2.2 Useful Daylight Illuminance 
The useful daylight illuminance (UDI) is a daylight metric that defines the percentage of hours, 
that is considered to be useful. The illuminances considered to be useful, is defined to be in the 
ranges from 100 lux to 3000 lux and were chosen on the basis of a literature study on occupant 
behaviour and comfort. The UDI index was developed by J. Mardeljevic et al. in 2005 (Nabil and 
Mardaljevic, 2005).

2.1.2.3 Annual Sun Exposure  
The annual sun exposure (ASE) were developed under the study of the Daylight Plus Programme 
report by L. Heschong (Heschong Mahone Group, 2012). The newly developed metric showed the 
most promising result in predicting visual discomfort for office space types. The ASE is defined 
as the percentage of an area that receives >1000 lux in 250 hours of the defined occupied hours 
annually.  
 
2.1.2.4 Daylight Glare Probability 
In addition to the other annual variables, the daylight glare probability is chosen. The DGP index 
can be calculated by DIVA for Rhino, where the number of glare sources and their positions 
against the occupant is evaluated. A captured HDR image or a rendered image is needed in order 
to calculate the DGP index. The simplified daylight glare probability (DGPs), evaluating only the 
vertical illuminance is also investigated, using the recorded vertical illuminance levels.  
 
2.2 Simulation Study 
In this section it will be explained how the simulation study is conducted. DIVA for Rhino is 
selected to create detailed annual daylight simulations of the selected office. DIVA for Rhino uses 
Radiance as calculation engine (Niemasz, 2017) for daylight simulations, which consist of a 
backwards ray-trace algorithm that calculates how the light is distributed and reflected from 
surfaces to the defined sensor points (Greg Ward Larson and Rob A. Shakespeare, 1998). Radiance 
uses a stochastic algorithm, which produces a slightly different result each time the same 
calculation is repeated.  For annual daylight analysis, the Design Reference Year (DRY) weather 
file from 2013 will be used.  
 
2.2.1 Glazing and Material Properties  
For a simulation of the daylight distribution in buildings, the material properties are an important 
factor, as they defined how much light there is reflected from the surfaces. Completely similar 
material properties are difficult to determine, thus the material properties assigned to the surfaces 
of the 3D model is a combination of the typical material reflections for Scandinavian offices and 
measured material reflections. For some surfaces, material properties, that do not fit the 'typical' 
material properties, a simplified method is used in order to find the reflection coefficient. The 
‘simplified’ method of determining the reflection coefficient of the materials, is the ratio of 
illuminance measured perpendicular from to the surface (in a distance of approximately 20 cm) 
==PAGE:23==

and the illuminance measured on the surface. This simplified method is concluded to be sufficient 
for this type of input for the 3D model, without considering that some of the material surfaces may 
be more worn out in some areas (e.g. in walking aisles). The light transmittance of modern 
windows can be found between the glass panes, along with other material properties for the 
window.

2.3  Field Measurements 
Field measurements were conducted over a period of one week (five working days), where data 
loggers are recording the illuminance levels in selected locations of the office. Former studies 
investigating prediction of visual comfort related to subjective user-responses had the best 
correlation with vertical illuminance measurement, measured at eye level in the user's view 
direction. Since, the field study must be performed with the least amount of disturbances for the 
occupants in the offices, the data loggers will be mounted on the back of the monitors in the user's 
view direction. Van Den Wymelenberg et al. (Van Den Wymelenberg and Inanici, 2014), proved 
a strong correlation between measurement of illuminance in eye-height and on top of monitors 
(both in the user's view direction). This is confirmed by the findings of an experiment by Velds 
(Velds, 2002), where there was found a high correlation between subjective user responses and 
vertical illuminance measured at the eye position of the participants. As pointed out in the article 
by Van Den Wymelenberg et al., intuitively, luminance distribution should show better correlation 
with subjective response, as the luminary distribution is by definition, how the human eye receives 
light, but the results did not in fact show a better correlation for luminance distribution than for 
the measured illuminance levels. Lux meters measuring the illuminance is not placed on every 
occupants’ computer monitor (due to limitations of measuring equipment) but are placed in 
strategic areas where it is expected to reach high illuminance levels according to daylight 
simulations, created prior to the field measurements.      
 
2.3.1 Measurement Equipment

2.4 Measuring Subjective Experiences of Daylight 
The goal for seeking an upper limit for daylight accepted by users is a subjective measurement. 
Therefore, each occupant of the investigated office is asked to fill out a questionnaire each day of 
the measuring period. The occupants are asked to rate their experience of the daylight conditions 
via different questions, and they have the opportunity to answer both by verbal and a numerical 
scale.   
 
The choice of words for the two questionnaires were carefully selected, as it may have a great 
influence on the results if the chosen words can be interpreted in different ways.  
 
As recommended by the ISO Standard - Acoustics - Assessment of noise annoyance by means of 
social and socio-acoustic surveys (ISO/TS 15666:2003, 2003), the same question is asked in two 
different ways and scales (verbal and numerical) for the participants judgment of the lighting levels

consistency between the two questions. The verbal scale gives clearest and most transparent 
communication, and the numeric scale is needed to provide a check on consistency on the 
respondent's answer on an important issue.  
The word for the most positive/negative attitude towards two asked question is chosen to be 
'særdeles' (Danish), which is translated into 'highly'. 
 
Although, the ISO standard refers to socio-acoustics surveys, it is concluded, that the same 
methodology for design of surveys regarding agreeability of daylight design for office 
environment can be used, as both goals are to investigate occupant’s/participant’s experience of a 
physiological influence. Preferably, the questionnaire would be pre-validated by a selected group 
in a qualitative semantic survey, but this lies beyond the time frame of this thesis. See Appendix

2.5 Tools for various Analysis Methods  
There exist several methods to find correlations between two data collections. To make a 
prediction for the outcome of the dependent variable (in this case, the subjective response rating 
regarding agreeability of light condition in side-lit offices), there have been chosen four different 
statistical methods as analysis tools. These will be reviewed in this section. Calculation for 
statistical dependence is calculated in Excel using some of the built-in functions. 
 
It is a common widespread problem statement in statistics, to determine which test method to be 
used on which dataset. Especially, when trying to find correlation between two datasets, choosing 
between either Pearson’s correlation method or Spearman’s correlation method is a classic 
problem. It is common practice to conduct both analysis methods. If both methods do not find a 
correlation or if both methods prove a correlation, using both methods are not a problem. However, 
if one fails and the other passes, it will require a statistical analysis on an a more advanced level. 
In addition, a correlation can be disproven by both Spearman’s rank correlation and Pearson’s 
correlation, while being proven and be significant on a logistic regression analysis.  
While the Spearman correlation coefficient is calculated on order of ranks, and reflects monotonic 
correlations between datasets, the Pearson’s correlation uses actual data, thus reflecting linear 
correlation between datasets. Consequently, the Spearman’s correlation is wider in its application 
method, while the Pearson’s correlation shows a linear correlation, which can be used as a 
predictive method, where the Spearman’s correlation cannot. Another important difference 
between the two statistical methods, is that the Pearson’s correlation is more suited to use in 
measurements on an interval scale level, while the Spearman’s correlation is more suited to be 
applied to measurements on an ordinal scale. In relation to levels of comfort and illuminance 
levels, the Spearman’s correlation is the first choice of the two analysis methods, but both are 
presented and applied in this thesis. Description on appliances method on both statistical methods 
are described in this section.

2.5.1 Linear Correlation 
Pearson's correlation coefficient (r) describes the strength of a linear association and is 
representing how many standard deviations y is predicted to change, when x changes with one 
standard deviation (Agresti and Franklin, 2013). The correlation coefficient falls between -1 and 
+1. Often, the determination coefficient (R2), which falls between 0 and 1, is used rather than the 
correlation coefficient. The determination coefficient is the squared value of the correlation 
coefficient. In order to have to conclude a correlation between the independent and dependent 
variable, the determination coefficient R2 must be > 0.95.  In liberal arts, a lower determination 
coefficient of (at least) 0.65 is acceptable in order to determine a correlation between the variables. 
Note that the determination coefficient is noted as R2 in this thesis, even though Agresti et. Al 
(Agresti and Franklin, 2013) notates R2 as the squared correlation for a regression with multiple 
explanatory variables.  
 
The significance coefficient (p-value) describes the probability of whether the coefficients in the 
regression is equal to zero, meaning that there is no correlation between the dependent and the 
independent value.  
To decide whether a correlation between two variables is significant, a hypothesis test is 
performed. A null hypothesis is rejected, if the p-value is less than alpha. A very small p-value is 
evidence of a strong indication against the null-hypothesis. Often chosen alpha values are 0.05, 
0.01 or even 0.001, according to L. Christiansen (Brockhoff Per. B; Møller, Jan Kloppenborg; 
Andersen, Elizabeth W.; Bacher, Peder; Christiansen, 2017). In this case, an alpha value of 0.05 
is chosen, as it is the globally chosen default value. If the p-value is larger than alpha, then the 
null-hypothesis cannot be rejected.

more than 100 hours annually, and 27 °C in 25 hours annually, it would meet the requirements for 
the demands of PMV (predicted mean vote) and PPD (predicted percentage of dissatisfied) index.

4.3 Simulations and Subjective Responses 
Subjective responses concerning visual comfort in terms of brightness, predicted by easy and quick 
daylight simulation tools would be extremely useful. Many daylight (and glare) metrics shows 
good potential in predicting occupant agreeability but is either based on highly time-consuming 
methods or based on outdated literature studies.  
 
The Daylight Glare Probability index (DGP) and the simplified Daylight Glare Probability index 
(DGPs) is closely related, as the DGPs index simply neglects the number of glare sources and their 
peak values, thus only considering the vertical illuminance at eye level as input.  
 
In this section, firstly the correlation between the simulated DGP index and the calculated DGPs 
is investigated. If there is good correlation between the two indexes, the DGP will be compared to 
the subjective responses. It is important to notice, that the DGP and DGPs will never be completely 
similar, as it consist of two different equations (one being a simplified version of the other). 
Nevertheless, the two metrics are compared in order to generate a secondary evaluation of the 3D 
models’ accuracy.   
 
In addition to finding an easy and quick way to use simulation daylight metric, the percentage of 
satisfied and dissatisfied in the three areas of Gaihede A/S, is compared to sensor points in the 3D 
model which receives more than 3000 lux (UDI>3000), the spatial daylight autonomy (sDA) and the 
annual sun exposure (ASE).

4.5 General Discussion 
Almost all the regression analysis performed in this study shows no correlation between the 
recorded illuminance values and the collected subjective responses. Few correlations were found, 
but with a very limited response rate (n = 4) and were therefore not considered to prove a 
correlation. The indications of the results from the subjective response and the recorded 
illuminance values could indicate that the human eyes are capable of great adaption to various 
luminance values. And that the ranges of visual comfort cannot be detected by simple performed 
measurements at an actual office environment. Other studies (Garretón, Rodriguez and Pattini, 
2016) and (Tuaycharoen and Tregenza, 2007) indicates also that visual comfort is not only a matter 
of luminance distribution, but a great range of variables (luminous contrast, disturbances in the 
visual environment, general comfort of the body, etc.) that can interfere with the subjective 
opinions. In relation to other studies examining the upper threshold for visual comfort in terms of 
measurable units, found no limits either as their findings were a direct contradiction towards their 
hypothesis.  
 
The logistic regression shows potential for further investigation, as the calculated predication rate 
were found to be 79 % correct. The simple use of a ‘comfortable’ or ‘uncomfortable’ as a response 
to stimulus of the body is also known from Ole Fanger’s prediction of thermal comfort tools 
(predicted mean vote (PMV) and predicted percentage of dissatisfied (PPD)) (Dansk Standard, 
2001), where a mathematical expression including several factors (including clothing level, 
metabolic rate, air speed and temperature) is widely used for international standards and 
recommendations as a prediction tool for thermal comfort. The PMV index predicts a large group’s 
mean value of the thermal sensation votes on a 7-point scale varying from hot to cold. The PPD 
index is a function of the PMV index and predicts the percentage of a large group of people likely 
to feel thermally dissatisfied. Similar to the PMV and PPD index, it could be interesting to 
investigate if such an index could be developed for visual comfort as well. However, it seems that 
visual comfort is a more subjective matter than thermal comfort, as it relies greatly of the user’s 
subjective opinion while thermal comfort relies more on physical parameters such as sweat 
generation for controlling the narrow limits of a comfortable body temperature. In addition, this 
study indicates that the range of visual comfort in terms of illuminances are very wide compared 
to the ranges of thermal comfort, but it should be noted that the investigated field study could not 
be categorized as being exposed to extreme conditions, making it difficult to draw any conclusive 
on occupant behaviour under extreme sunlight exposure in an office environment.   
==PAGE:77==
Chapter | 3 Results and discussions  
Page | 68 
 
In addition, the questionnaire which were answered by the participants holds great influence over 
the outcome (no correlations found). It is possible, that if the questions in the daily questionnaire 
were designed differently, the outcome of the study would different as well. The use of the words 
on the two Likert scales used for Question QB2 and QB3 has thus a huge impact on the respondents’ 
answer. If say, the participants recognized that word has a stronger feeling than it was supposed 
to, it would ‘tip’ the scale. Another scenario is that the participants were in a hurry to finish the 
questionnaire and paid little to no attention towards the visual environment for the time of 
answering while simply chose the level of the two scales reflecting a general opinion. 
 
Furthermore, if the participants of the field study, were handed out a larger questionnaire with 
more comprehensive questions about the comfort of the luminous environment, the outcome could 
have differed from the current. The size of the daily questionnaire handed out to the participants 
were very short, as it were expected a lower response-rate if the questionnaire took more than 
maximum a few minutes to complete. This could be avoided, if the participants were paid to take 
part in the study, as then they would be obligated to fill out the questionnaire regardless of its 
length.  
  
The fact that the data loggers proved to be unreliable during the investigated period, is 
considered not to be a major issue, as it seems to be very clear when the data should be sorted 
out, as they either logged ‘Above scale’ or showed extremely high illuminance values. However, 
some responses had no measurements to be held up against, and where consequently sorted out 
of the regression analysis.  Ideally, a calibration of the lux meters should be performed, as their 
reliability is questionable.

5 Conclusion 
A field study was conducted over three weeks in the spring of 2018 in a shared office space located 
in Valby, Copenhagen.  The occupants of the office participated in a field study for one working 
week each, where they were sent a daily questionnaire regarding their preference towards the 
visual environment at their office space. Meanwhile, recordings of the vertical illuminance in the 
two dominant view directions and measurement of the horizontal illuminance in the centre of the 
room were performed. In addition to the field study, a simulation study was performed in order to 
compare both annual and instantaneous daylight metrics with the subjective ratings from the 
questionnaire.  
 
Findings from the study show no linear correlation, nor did the study find any correlation for a 
monotonic relationship proved by Spearman’s correlation. A logistic regression with a 79.6 % 
prediction rate for the observations shows a more promising result but needs more subjective 
responses under high illuminance values as it is currently based on only 6 observations for 
illuminance values above 3000 lux.  The participant rate for the field study conducted at Gaihede 
A/S only resulted in 64 responses in total, which makes the investigation statistically weak.  
 
In addition to the field study, where the subjective responses were compared with recorded 
illuminance values, a simulation study for both annual daylight metrics (sDA, UDI, ASE) and 
Daylight Glare Probability (DGP) were conducted. The 3D model of the investigated space proved 
to be a reasonable representation, therefore not surprisingly, did the simulation study not show any 
significant correlations for the subjective responses and the simulated DGP index.  
The annual daylight metrics showed not any results either towards finding an upper limit for 
daylight. Two of the areas (Area B and C) in the investigated office, which were similar in interior 
design and with the same orientation, showed that the occupants of one area (Area C) were 
significantly more prone to dissatisfaction than the other, with the same values of annual daylight 
metrics (sDA, UDI and ASE). As discussed earlier, this might be a sign of dissatisfaction caused 
by too low illuminance values, but since the questionnaire did not address this issue, it is only 
speculation.

6 Further Work 
In order to establish statistical dependency for detecting an upper threshold to comfortable 
illuminance values provided by daylight, the analysis require at a larger number of participants. 
Whilst sensitivity in human subjects can be investigated, no conclusive can be drawn on the few 
participants investigated in this thesis. In addition, more offices should be investigated if the 
analysis should be conducted as a field study. The choice of places to investigated should be going 
through a more detailed selecting process than the ones in this thesis. In order to investigate the 
upper threshold of illuminance for visual comfort, the investigated area should be ensured to be 
exposed to more extreme conditions of exposure. As the illuminance levels did not reach a critical 
level (with very few exceptions) under the field study conducted in this thesis, the opportunity to 
examine the participants responses and behaviour under high illuminance levels was not possible. 
Furthermore, the locations of the field study should be investigated for a longer period (ideally 
under several months and across seasonal changes) to capture different daylight conditions.  
 
Another type of study that could be interesting to investigate, is the use of a controlled environment 
instead of a field study with real office participants in their natural setting. If the participants were 
paid to take part in a study, it could allow the investigators to require more responses. However, 
there is also a few limitations to this method, as it would 1) require a more funding and a study 
laboratory to conduct the study and 2) it can be imagined that the participants signify a more 
strongly opinion towards the subject, than if the participant were seated in their natural office 
environment.

Appendix C - Calculation of regression analysis 
In this appendix, examples of the calculation of regression analysis performed in Excel will be

Pearson’s correlation 
For calculation of the Pearson’s correlation, the add-in “Data analysis” for Excel is used. The add-
in provides the user with a series of statistical analysis tools, with one of them being the 
‘Regression’ tab. The regression tab needs an input area for the Y-axis and the X-axis. The 
dependent variable is used for input on the Y-axis, where the independent variables are used as 
input for the X-axis.  
Figure 1 – Output for regression tool from the Data analysis tab in Excel 
Figure 1 shows an example of output from the regression tool in Excel. The red squares indicate 
which parameters from the output is used for accepting or reject the hypothesis of a correlation 
between the dependent and independent variable. In order to conclude a correlation, R2 must be 
above 0.95 and the significance level, p, must be below or equal to alpha (0.05) (Christiansen, 
2017).  An alpha level of 0.05, is commonly used, as it defines the probability (5 %) of concluding 
a correlation, where no correlation is actually found.

Student’s T-test  
The student’s T-test can be used in order to observe, if two observation sets are statistically 
different or if the difference can be explained by coincidence (Agresti and Franklin, 2013).  
The T-test is testing if the null-hypothesis that the means of the two populations are equal.  
In order to perform a Student’s T-test, one must firstly perform a F-test. The F-test is used to test 
if the null-hypothesis (H0) of two variances of two populations are equal. The F-test is calculated 
by the Data Analysis tab in Excel, where the alpha value is set to be alpha = 0.05:  
 
 
Figure 3 - Input for F-test 
If F > F Critical one-tail, then we can reject the null-hypothesis of the two observations is equal.  
As an example, it is investigated if the subjective responses from Area A and Area B is more 
positive (satisfied) towards Question QB3.                  
In Figure 4, the area for variable 1 is thus the subjective responses for the numeric scale for QB3 
from Area A and Area B, while the area for variable 2 is the subjective responses for the numeric 
scale for QB3 from Area C.  
 
Figure 4 - Result for F-test in Excel 
As F > F-critical, then the null-hypothesis of the two observation sets being equal is rejected and 
can then be found to be statistically unequal.

equal. Like the F-test, the T-test is performed in the Data Analysis tab provided by Excel:

The same example for calculation of the F-test in used in the example calculation for the T-test. 
 
Figure 6 - Results for T-test in Excel 
As the t > t-critical two-tailed (or t< - t-critical two-tailed), then the null hypothesis can again be 
rejected. Thus, it can be concluded that the subjective responses for QB3 for Area A and Area B is 
statistically different from the subjective responses for Area C, as the two observations set neither 
are equal nor is their main value.
