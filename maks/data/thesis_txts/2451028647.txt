Abstract
In recent years, optomechanical systems have become an intriguing platform for designing
interesting interactions between the quantum states of light and matter. In this report, we
present a theoretical study of a protocol for transferring a quantum state from an optical
cavity ﬁeld to a mechanical oscillator in a cavity optomechanical system. This may be used for
generating quantum states on the macroscopic scale or for building a memory for quantum states
of light, which is desirable for quantum computing and quantum communication purposes. The
state transfer mechanism is facilitated by driving the cavity with a pulsed coherent input ﬁeld
red-detuned by the mechanical frequency. By numerically simulating the dynamics in a low
temperature sideband-resolved regime using a high mechanical quality factor, we show that
it is possible to transfer amplitude squeezed quantum states onto the mechanics. We analyse
and discuss how the quality of the state transfer depends on pulse lengths and shapes as well
as losses to the environment. We ﬁnally show that the experiment is achievable using current
state-of-the-art technology.

Introduction
Quantum optomechanics describes any system in which an optical ﬁeld is interacting at the quantum
level with a mechanical element. The ﬁeld has become of great interest to the scientiﬁc community as
current state of the art (nano-)fabrication techniques have allowed the optomechanics experiments
to enter the quantum realm. The ﬁeld has a lot of complexity that remains to be fully understood,
and it also has many useful applications and prospects. For instance, quantum optomechanics may
be used to cool down a mechanical harmonic oscillator towards its quantum ground state [1], it can
be used as a resource for entanglement [2], and perhaps most famously, optomechanics was a key
element in the interferometer used by LIGO for the Nobel Prize-winning detection of gravitational
waves in 2015 [3].
One equally exciting feature about quantum optomechanics is its potential utility as a memory of
quantum states of light. In a cavity optomechanical system, under the right circumstances, the
state of the cavity ﬁeld may be transferred onto the mechanical degrees of freedom, where it may
be stored for as long as the thermal environment and the quality factor of the mechanics allows for.
However, while this has been demonstrated experimentally under certain circumstances (see e.g.
[4]), it remains to be fully understood how to make a state transfer with as little loss of information
as possible. So far, theoretical work has mainly been focused on the transfer of coherent states
[5], but of equal interest is the possibility of transfering nonclassical states such as squeezed states,
photon-subtracted squeezed states, photon number states, or cat states. If we can design a quantum
memory that can store any kind of quantum state, it will be tremendously useful for e.g. quantum
computing and quantum communication purposes.
In this report, we will theoretically and through simulations explore how to perform a quantum
state transfer in a cavity optomechanical system using pulsed light. Speciﬁcally, we will investigate
how well a quadrature squeezed state may be transferred from an input ﬁeld onto the mechanics.
We will investigate what parameters are important in order to perform the state transfer with as
high ﬁdelity as possible. This includes the pulse shapes of the light, the optomechanical coupling
constant, and the loss and decoherence rates of the system.

Simulations of quantum state transfers
We will now present simulations based on the formalism and calculations in section 3 of the per-
formance of a state transfer protocol, transferring a cavity input ﬁeld onto the mechanics. The
performance of the state transfer is assessed through the ﬁdelity between the ﬁnal state of the me-
chanics and the initial input state. As previously mentioned, this ﬁdelity depends on the speciﬁc
input state. In our simulations, we will restrict ourselves to a squeezed input state of squeezing
parameter r = 1.
For the simulations, we consider the following state transfer protocol:
1. First, the state to be transferred encoded in the input ﬁeld ˆain is fed into the cavity on
resonance (∆0 = 0). For a squeezed state, we can calculate for how long we need to feed
the cavity given the input parameter and an upper bound on the ﬁdelity, c.f. the analysis in
section 3.1.3.
2. When this ﬁdelity is suﬃciently high, we immediately switch to driving the optomechanics at
red detuning (∆= Ω) using a pulsed coherent input ﬁeld with pulse shape ϵ(t) and containing

A few comments regarding the above state transfer protocol are in order.
First of all, in the
simulations, we start from step 2 and assume that step 1 has already been executed such that the
input state is now in the cavity with unit ﬁdelity. This is of course an approximation, as in theory
it would take an inﬁnite amount of time to achieve. Secondly, this state transfer protocol avoids
the question of eventually transferring the state back to the cavity and reading out the output. In
reality, these steps are of course important, since if they cannot be implemented successfully, the
optomechanical system cannot be used as a quantum memory. These considerations are left for
future work.

coupled, and the outcome is hard to predict.

the coupling between the cavity and the mechanics will not be resembling a beamsplitter coupling,
but a quantum non-demolition (QND) coupling, and thus no state transfer mechanism will occur

better cavities and higher mechanical quality factors (and/or lowering the temperature), or one
must look for other pulse shapes.

so low for this setup, one may be able to perform a good state transfer using longer, weaker pulses.

ideal, the state transfer of a squeezed state simulated in this report deﬁnitely looks to be within
experimental reach.

4.4
Bad cavity regime
All of the results presented in this report have been for κ ≪Ω, called the good cavity regime or
the sideband-resolved regime. However, a considerable amount of eﬀort during the work on this
thesis has also been made to try and realise the state transfer onto the mechanics for κ ≫Ω, called
the bad cavity regime. Unfortunately, this task has proven much more diﬃcult, for a number of
reasons.
Most importantly, if κ is very high, whichever state is present in the cavity will quickly decohere
into a vacuum state. This means that long, slow driving pulses do not work since the initial state
decoheres much faster than the speed of the state transfer mechanism.
Furthermore, when we
are no longer in the sideband-resolved regime, the optomechanical coupling at red-detuning is no
longer approximately a beamsplitter coupling. For these reasons, very short pulses (on the order
of Ω(toff −ton) ≪1) are needed to possibly make the state transfer work. However, despite some
attempts to ﬁnd a suitable parameter regime, this approach has not born any fruit.
Alternatively, instead of coupling the squeezed state into the cavity at ∆0 = 0 before driving the
optomechanical coupling at ∆0 = Ω, one might consider perfoming these two tasks simultaneously
using a two-tone drive scheme. Such schemes have been investigated theoretically in e.g. [5] and
experimentally in e.g. [4], although only for transfering coherent states, which is not the focus of
this thesis. We have also attempted this approach, but more research is needed, since this expands
the parameter space even further.
Finally, it may be the case that the model used in this report to handle coupling of the cavity ﬁeld
to the environment is not valid in the bad cavity regime. Indeed, it has been shown in [16] and
[17] that this model only holds for a high-quality factor cavity. In the general case, the model is
signiﬁcantly more complicated and the methods used to perform the simulations will need to be
revised.
5
Discussion and outlook
The main goal of this project was to ﬁnd a parameter regime in which a state transfer from from
the cavity ﬁeld to the mechanics could be performed. this goal has been achieved: For two kinds
of pulse shapes, we have shown that transferring a squeezed state is possible in theory and seems
attainable in practice as well.
Also interesting is the behavior we have observed of the eﬀective coupling g(t) as a function of time.
It is useful to know that if the ramp time of the driving pulse is too short compared to the time scale
of the mechanics, we cannot expect g(t) to follow the pulse shape, whereas we can expect it when
the ramp time is long. Some rough mathematical arguments were given for the latter statement
in Ref. [14], but it is useful to observe that it also holds in the simulation. It is also signiﬁcant,
as a constant eﬀective coupling is generally both assumed and desired in the literature. Here, it is
shown that under some circumstances g(t) may not be constant, and it may also not be a problem,
as the state transfer can still be carried out. In fact, it may even be desirable, as we show that
longer pulses may lead to lower state transfer ﬁdelities due to losses to the environment.
Some legitimate questions can be asked about the validity of the simulated results. Most critically,
the correctness of the found state covariance matrices hinge on the linearisation approximation

quadrature ﬂuctuations and the ﬁdelity. This will not be easy, as the phase space methods based on
Gaussian quantum information used in this report will no longer be available to us in a nonlinear
model. That being said, we believe that the obtained ﬁdelities in a full nonlinear simulation will be
similar to what we have obtained from the linearisation approximation.
In addition to this, the obtained results call for a huge number of further studies.
Starting with the low-hanging fruit, it will be useful to put some numbers on how well the mechanics
will store the squeezed state over time. Using the formalism and calculations already presented in
the report, this should not be a diﬃcult task. The only reason that this has not already been
done is time constraints. In the same vein, a more rigorous study of the dependence of the state
transfer ﬁdelity on the loss parameters κ, Qm and ¯nin is desirable. As previously mentioned, we
have in general experienced from our simulations that lowering κ and ¯nin and increasing Qm will
signiﬁcantly improve the ﬁdelity, but it would be nice to give more quantitative statements about
this.
Also, it should be properly investigated how much further the ﬁdelity drops when the squeezed
state is transferred back to the cavity mode. Of equal relevance, though a bit harder to answer,
is the question of how the state of the resulting output ﬁeld ˆaout behaves, and how well the initial
input state can be reconstructed using e.g. quantum state tomography. In particular, this question
regarding the behavior of the output ﬁeld is a concern, since the input-output relations (equation
(2.38)) suggests that the squeezing level of the output ﬁeld will be signiﬁcantly less than that of the
input ﬁeld if κ is small. If this is the case, other methods of reading out the state of the mechanics
will need to be considered. For instance, one could employ a pulsed scheme to perform quantum
non-demolition measurements of the mechanical quadratures as was done in Ref. [18]. All of these
questions need to be answered in order to assess the feasibility of building a quantum memory using
optomechanics.
It would also be interesting to further explore how diﬀerent driving pulse shapes may or may not
improve the ﬁdelity. In this report, we have mainly considered approximately constant pulses, but
for instance, exponential pulses might also be viable. One could also try to apply methods from
variational calculus to optimise the pulse shape, although this will probably be very computationally
expensive to do.
And ﬁnally, the methods developed in this report should be used to explore the state transfer ﬁ-
delities for other quantum states than the squeezed state considered in this report, such as photon-
subtracted squeezed states or cat states. While cat states may be diﬃcult to analyse with Gaussian
quantum information, photon-subtracted squeezed states may be within reach of the developed
frameworks, since the Wigner function of a photon-subtracted state is essentially a sum of two
Gaussian Wigner functions. Also, investigating how a two-mode squeezed entangled state couples

6
Conclusion
In this report, we have theoretically investigated a protocol for transferring a quantum state from
an optical cavity ﬁeld to a mechanical oscillator in a standard cavity optomechanical system. The
protocol consists of initally preparing the cavity state using an input ﬁeld on resonance with the
cavity, and then employing a pulsed coherent input ﬁeld red-detuned by the mechanical frequency
Ωto facilitate a state swap. From numerical simulations of the dynamics, we have investigated
quantitatively and qualitatively what and how the parameters of the system aﬀect the state transfer
ﬁdelity F(ˆρm(t), ˆρc(0)) of the mechanical state ˆρm at time t with the initial cavity ﬁeld ˆρc(0), which
we have taken to be an amplitude squeezed state.
The simulations have taken place in the sideband-resolve regime with a high mechanical quality
factor factor and a low temperature environment. The pulse shape of the input ﬁeld was taken to be
approximately constant with smooth heads and tails. It was investigated how the optomechanical
dynamics responded to either a short ramp time of the pulse (tramp < Ω) or a long ramp time
(tramp ≫Ω). For both pulse shapes, we found that the optimal pulse length for maximising the state
transfer ﬁdelity is inversely correlated with the optimal number of photons in the pulse. This makes
sense, since increasing the number of photons increases the eﬀective optomechanical coupling, which
determines the speed of the state transfer mechanism. At optimal photon numbers and optimal
pulse lengths, the pulses with short ramp times yielded signiﬁcantly higher state transfer ﬁdelities
than pulses with long ramp times. On the other hand, the eﬀective optomechanical coupling was
found easier to predict and control using long ramp times. In general, the simulation results suggest
that a short pulse length is preferable in order to minimise losses to the environment during the
state transfer, but if the pulse is too short and intense, the nonlinear dynamics of the system will
enter a chaotic regime and compromise the state transfer.
Most importantly, with either kind of pulse shape, we were able to successfully transfer signiﬁcant
levels of squeezing to the mechanical quadratures. By comparison with recent high-impact papers
on optomechanics, we showed that this should be experimentally feasible with current technology.
If this state stransfer protocal is achieved in the laboratory, it will be a major step forward in the
pursuit of building a quantum memory.
