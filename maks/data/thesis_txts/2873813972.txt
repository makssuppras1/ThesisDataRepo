Abstract
This thesis investigates Native Language Identification from Reading (NLIR) using deep
learning models, such as Transformer and MLP, in comparison to a random forest base-
line, and the log-linear model presented in the research paper titled Native Language
Prediction from Gaze: a Reproducibility Study by Skerath et. al. Six feature sets are eval-
uated: Words in Fixed Context (WFC), Syntactic Clusters (SC), Information Clusters (IC),
their concatenation (SC+IC), and two semantic variants derived from contextual BERT
embeddings—BERT-clusters (BC) and a reduced Thematic Clusters (TC) subset—each
instantiated over cumulative n-gram bundles (uni; uni+bi; uni+bi+tri). In the same evalua-
tion technique utilized by the study, the Transformer encoder operating on WFC achieves
the strongest aggregate results (accuracy 89.51%, macro-F1 87.98%, uni+bi+tri), with
SC+IC close behind; IC, BC, and especially TC trail.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Native Language Identification (NLI) aims to determine a person’s first language based
on their production or processing of a second language. While traditionally applied to writ-
ten texts, where both machine learning and deep learning methods have achieved strong
performance by leveraging syntactic, lexical, and phonological features, the modality of
predicting native language from reading is severely underexplored with only a few pub-
lished studies to date.
A pioneering study by Berzak et al. [1] demonstrated that a log-linear model trained on
hand-crafted eye-tracking features could successfully classify a reader’s native language
based on their gaze behavior while reading English text. This work was later expanded
in a reproducibility study by Skerath et al. [2], that validated the findings using a broader
range of native languages, but with fewer words per participant and a different set of lin-
guistically interrelated language groups. The first study found that when two languages
are typologically and structurally similar—sharing morphosyntactic features, word order,
and linguistic family relationships—the model struggled to distinguish between their re-
spective eye-tracking patterns. However, the second paper did not replicate this finding,
suggesting that the relationship between reading patterns and linguistic similarity is more
complex than initially thought. These conflicting results raise the possibility that the mod-
eling approach itself, the choice of languages or the structure and size of the input data
to these methods may be a limiting factor.
In recent years, deep learning methods have been increasingly applied to text-based NLI,
marking a shift from handcrafted features to end-to-end representation learning. Architec-
tures such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Long Short-Term Memory networks
(LSTMs), and Transformer-based models have demonstrated strong performance by au-
tomatically learning abstract patterns from raw text input. These models are capable of
capturing subtle lexical, syntactic, and stylistic cues that may be missed by traditional
classifiers. However, they typically require large amounts of labeled data and often lack
interpretability, making it difficult to understand which features influence classification de-
cisions. Inspired by the text-based domain, a recent study by Shalileh et al. [3] explored
both machine learning and deep learning methods to infer L1 on the MECO L2 dataset,
along with second language proficiency. By having raw fixation coordinates as inputs
to their methods (rather than aggregated or linguistically contextualized features), they
showed that neural models could learn from low-level, raw gaze signals. However, their
results also indicated that deep models did not consistently outperform simpler classifiers,
highlighting the challenges of applying end-to-end learning on limited and noisy data.
Unlike these end-to-end approaches, the current study employs deep neural networks
on structured, hand-engineered gaze features. This hybrid approach combines the inter-
pretability of handcrafted features with the modeling capacity of neural networks, aiming
to capture subtle, possibly nonlinear reading patterns associated with a speaker’s native
language, while maintaining transparency and control over the input space.

The expected contribution of DL in NLIR can be stated as follows:
• Non-linear pattern discovery: Deep models can capture complex, non-linear re-
lationships between gaze features and native language, which linear models such
as logistic regression may fail to represent.
• Joint modeling of diverse feature types: Unlike feature-specific pipelines, deep
networks can jointly model different types of aggregated gaze features (e.g., syn-
tactic and information-based clusters) and their interactions, potentially revealing
higher-order structure.
• Improved generalization: With appropriate regularization and architecture choices,
deep models may generalize better across participants and languages, especially
in the presence of subtle or overlapping patterns.
• Interpretability through attention mechanisms: When using attention-based mod-
els (e.g., Transformers), the model can highlight which clusters or fixation patterns
are most informative for distinguishing native languages, offering insights into the
cognitive signatures of L1 influence.
1.2
Research questions
By leveraging the MECO L2 dataset [4], this thesis aims to advance NLIR by applying
deep learning techniques to structured gaze data, with the goal of improving classification
performance and capturing patterns of L1 influence.
The project aims to answer the following questions:
• How effectively can deep learning models classify a reader’s native language based
on eye-tracking data, compared to the log-linear model used in the previous studies?
• Which feature sets utilized in this thesis contribute most to the models’ performance
on native language classification?
• Do deep learning models mitigate classification difficulties observed in the prior stud-
ies when distinguishing typologically similar languages?
1.3
Structure of the thesis
To address these questions, the thesis is structured as follows:
1. Applying deep learning models to the MECO L2 dataset in order to predict the
reader’s native language.
2. Benchmarking their performance against the log-linear model tested in the Skerath
et al. [2] reproducibility study, as well as other machine learning methods previously
applied in the text-based domain.
3. Exploring interpretability techniques and experimenting with additional feature sets
to derive new linguistic insights into how native language affects reading behavior.

2
Literature review
In this section, the conceptual and empirical background for Native Language Identifica-
tion from Reading (NLIR) is discussed. First, eye-tracking measures that serve as indices
of cognitive load in reading (e.g., fixations, saccades, regressions, dwell time, pupil size)
are introduced, along with explanations of what each measure captures and why these
signals are informative for L2 processing.
Next, gaze behavior is considered in the context of broader determinants of L2 reading—
textual and discourse properties, visual and task demands, reader-specific cognitive fac-
tors, and cross-linguistic influence—to distinguish L1-driven effects from general sources
of difficulty. Transferring to the area of linguistics, foundational terms (n-grams, POS tags,
syntactic structures, function words) that are used in later analyses and feature engineer-
ing are then defined.
After this, methods and findings from text-based NLI, ranging from classical machine
learning to deep learning, are reviewed as a methodological reference point, and these
are contrasted with the small but growing body of NLIR studies, including the original log-
linear approach and its replication on MECO L2, to highlight current knowledge and areas
of divergence.
Finally, limitations in prior work (e.g., data scope, feature representations, evaluation de-
sign) are discussed, and the modeling principles that motivate the hybrid deep-learning
approach in this thesis are outlined, providing the context for the dataset choices, feature
sets, and architectures introduced in the Methodology.
2.1
Eye-tracking measures as indicators of cognitive load
There are multiple frameworks for categorizing eye-tracking measures, depending on the
type of eye movement and the scale of measurement used. Drawing on these classifica-
tions, Aryadoust et al. (2023) [5] identified a set of core eye-tracking measures that are
especially relevant to research in second language (L2) processing. Their study offers
a systematic synthesis of eye-tracking measures most commonly used in L2 research
and organizes them according to their interpretive value, making it a suitable foundation
for introducing the measures selected in this thesis as indicators of cognitive load during
reading.
• Saccades and fixations: During reading, the eyes move in a sequence of rapid
jumps known as saccades, punctuated by brief pauses called fixations, during which
visual information is acquired and interpreted [6]. Fixations typically last around 200
to 250 milliseconds, though their duration can fluctuate based on the complexity
of the text and the reader’s proficiency. These eye movements reflect cognitive
load, as longer fixations and shorter saccades often indicate increased difficulty
in processing text, especially in a second language (L2). An increase in fixation
duration typically signals higher cognitive effort required to process the information.
• Regressions: Regressions refer to backwards eye-movements, when the reader
returns to previously read words, phrases, or sentences, often implying difficulty in
comprehension of the re-read text. Regressions are associated with higher cognitive
load, as they indicate areas where the reader struggles to understand or remember
the material, requiring them to revisit previously encountered information.

• Dwell time: Dwell time or visit duration refers to the total amount of time the eyes
spend within a particular area of interest (AOI), encompassing all fixations and sac-
cades made during that visit. It provides a broader picture of attentional focus than
individual fixation durations, capturing how long readers engage with a specific word,
phrase, or region before moving on [7, 8]. Longer dwell times typically suggest
greater cognitive effort, as the reader may need more time to process or understand
the text.
• Skipping: Skipping occurs when the eyes move past a word or region without fixat-
ing on it, suggesting that the reader either predicts the word from context or deems
it unimportant for comprehension [9, 10]. Skipping is often an indicator of lower
cognitive load, as it implies that the reader is able to process the material efficiently,
possibly due to familiarity with the text or predictive reading strategies.
• Blinks: While not traditional eye-movements, blinks are increasingly recognized as
informative markers of cognitive activity and processing load. Blink rate can de-
crease during demanding cognitive tasks and increase during moments of reduced
processing, thus offering an indirect window into the temporal distribution of cog-
nitive effort during reading [11]. Reduced blink rates often correlate with higher
cognitive engagement, while increased blink rates may suggest moments of lower
cognitive effort or lapses in attention.
• Pupillometry: Pupil dilation is another physiological marker of cognitive load. Un-
der consistent lighting conditions, changes in pupil size can reflect variations in men-
tal effort, working memory demand, and emotional engagement. Larger pupil diam-
eters during reading are often interpreted as signs of increased cognitive processing
[11, 5]. Pupil dilation can be used to gauge cognitive load, with greater dilation indi-
cating heightened mental effort or increased cognitive demand during reading.
• Gaze patterns: Gaze patterns refer to the sequence and trajectory of fixations and
saccades over time. These patterns reveal how attention is distributed across the
text and can help identify strategies used by readers (e.g., scanning vs. careful
reading). In L2 contexts, gaze patterns often differ from those of native speakers,
reflecting differences in processing strategy or language proficiency [5]. The distri-
bution and trajectory of gaze patterns provide insights into cognitive load, as more
fixations and regressions typically suggest higher cognitive effort in processing the
text.
2.2
Influences on L2 Reading
From a cognitive demand perspective, gaze behavior during reading is shaped not only
by the linguistic complexity of the text but also by a range of external and internal factors.
These include visual presentation, task type, and reader-specific cognitive capacities such
as working memory and language proficiency [12, 13]. Moreover, bilingual and L2 reading
studies have shown that gaze behavior is further modulated by cross-linguistic factors,
with the second language influencing how the first language is processed during reading
[14, 15]. This section groups and discusses these contributing factors to contextualize the
dataset used in this project and to clarify potential influences on eye-movement patterns
that are not necessarily attributable to the reader’s L1 background alone.

2.2.1
Textual and discourse factors
Textual and discourse-level factors refer to features that are intrinsic to the second lan-
guage (L2) text and influence its processing difficulty. One major factor is discourse co-
herence, which refers to the ease with which a reader can integrate new information into a
coherent mental model. When coherence is low, the reader must expend additional cog-
nitive resources to resolve referential ambiguity or infer unstated relationships between
ideas, often resulting in longer fixations and increased rereading [16].
Another key textual property is word frequency and predictability. Low-frequency words,
or words that are unexpected in context, are generally harder to process, leading to longer
fixation durations and a greater likelihood of regressions. These effects are magnified in
L2 reading due to limited lexical access and reduced automaticity in word recognition [6,
17, 18].
2.2.2
Visual and task context factors
Visual and task-related factors pertain to external aspects of the reading environment
that influence how the reader engages with the text. The typography of the text—such as
font size, spacing, and contrast—can affect visual word recognition, while the layout and
organization of information guide attention and influence the sequencing of fixations.
Task demands, such as whether the reader is skimming, scanning for specific information,
or reading for deep comprehension, also shape gaze behavior. Different tasks evoke dif-
ferent strategies, altering the number, duration, and distribution of fixations and saccades
[19, 20].
2.2.3
Reader-specific cognitive factors
Reader-specific cognitive factors include individual mental capacities that modulate read-
ing efficiency, regardless of the linguistic content of the text. Vocabulary size, working
memory capacity, and overall L2 reading proficiency are among the most robust predic-
tors of reading behavior. Readers with greater lexical knowledge and stronger working
memory are better able to integrate information quickly and effectively, resulting in shorter
fixation durations and fewer regressions [21, 22].
Additionally, internal states such as fatigue and fluctuations in attentional focus influence
eye movement behavior. When readers are fatigued or inattentive, their processing be-
comes slower and less consistent, often manifesting as longer fixations, increased vari-
ability in gaze patterns, and greater difficulty maintaining coherence across a text [23].
2.2.4
Cross-linguistic influence
A reader’s native language (L1) significantly influences cognitive processes involved in
second language reading. This phenomenon, known as cross-linguistic influence, arises
from differences in linguistic properties between the L1 and L2, which shape the reader’s
expectations and processing strategies [24, 25].
Core linguistic properties that mediate this influence include:
• Syntax: Differences in word order or sentence structure can disrupt parsing and
lead to reanalysis.
• Phonology: Variation in sound systems affects subvocalization and lexical access.
• Morphology: Disparities in word formation patterns influence recognition and pro-
cessing time.
• Semantics: Differences in meaning construction and idiomatic usage may cause
interpretive mismatches.

• Orthography: Divergent writing systems or spelling conventions affect visual pro-
cessing of words.
These differences establish processing expectations that are deeply rooted in the L1 and
can interfere with fluent L2 reading. For instance, when the syntactic or orthographic
systems of the L2 differ significantly from the reader’s L1, readers tend to exhibit longer
fixations, more regressions, and increased rereading [26, 27]. Such effects are particu-
larly pronounced when the L1 and L2 differ in canonical word order—for example, Subject-
Verb-Object (SVO) versus Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) languages—which increases pars-
ing complexity and cognitive load during sentence comprehension [28, 29].
In addition, lexical transparency plays a critical role. When the L1 and L2 share cognates
or morphological patterns, readers benefit from faster word recognition and shorter fixation
durations, reducing processing effort [30]. Conversely, significant lexical and phonological
differences increase interference and result in slower processing and reduced compre-
hension efficiency [31, 32].
Furthermore, semantic differences between languages can also contribute to processing
difficulty in L2 reading. Words and expressions that lack direct equivalents in the reader’s
L1—or whose meanings are structured differently—can cause interpretive mismatches
or hesitation. This is particularly evident with idiomatic expressions, culturally specific
references, and polysemous words (words with multiple meanings), which often require
deeper inferencing and contextual integration.
Research has shown that L2 readers are slower to process idioms and metaphors when
these expressions do not exist in their L1 or map differently onto L1 semantic structures
[33, 34].
Even familiar-looking words (cognates) can introduce semantic interference
when they are ”false friends”—i.e., similar in form but different in meaning—resulting in
delayed word recognition and increased fixations [30, 31]. Jiang [32] also highlights how
the mental representation of L2 vocabulary often lacks the full semantic depth and au-
tomaticity found in L1, particularly for abstract or low-frequency words. These semantic
mismatches are reflected in gaze behavior, where readers tend to show longer fixations
or more regressions when encountering semantically opaque or ambiguous items.
Such effects underscore the importance of considering L1-to-L2 semantic mapping in the
study of cross-linguistic influence on eye movement behavior, particularly when designing
features for gaze-based native language identification.
2.3
Foundational terms
Before diving deeper into NLI, it is important to establish some key definitions of often
referenced terms and concepts.
• n-grams: Sequences of n consecutive items (typically words or characters) from a
given text or speech sample. Commonly used in natural language processing for
tasks such as language modeling and text classification [35].
Example: For the sentence “She is reading a book”, the bigrams (n = 2) are: [She
is], [is reading], [reading a], [a book].
• POS tags: Part-of-Speech tags indicate the syntactic category of each word in a
sentence, such as noun (NN), verb (VB), or adjective (JJ). POS tagging is funda-
mental for parsing and syntactic analysis [36].
Example: In the sentence “The dog barked loudly”, the POS tags are: [The/DT,
dog/NN, barked/VBD, loudly/RB].

• Syntactic structures: The hierarchical organization of words into phrases and
clauses governed by grammatical rules. Syntactic structures are often represented
using parse trees or phrase structure rules [37].
Example: In “The student who studied linguistics passed the exam”, the clause “who
studied linguistics” is a relative clause modifying “student”.
• Function words: Grammatical words that express relationships between content
words and contribute to the syntactic structure of a sentence. They include deter-
miners, prepositions, conjunctions, and pronouns [38].
Example: In “She walked to the store”, the words “she”, “to”, and “the” are function
words.
2.4
Methods for text-based Native Language Identification
(NLI)
NLI literature is dominated by text-based methods, and since text is central to both writing
and reading, text-based NLI serves as a natural point of reference for gaze-based NLI. Al-
though the modalities is different: text production versus reading behavior, both tasks aim
to convert complex linguistic regularities into stable, comparable inputs for classification.
2.4.1
Machine learning methods
A wide array of machine learning models have been applied to text-based Native Lan-
guage Identification (NLI). Early approaches such as support vector machines and log-
linear classifiers demonstrated strong performance using features like n-grams, function
words, and syntactic tags. In this section, a few notable examples are mentioned where
the nature of the task (supervised learning) and the structure of the input data (tabular,
aggregated features) are similar to the task at hand, providing background to the models
selected in the Methodology section.
Support Vector Machines (SVMs)
Support Vector Machines (SVMs) have played a foundational role in early NLI research,
particularly in the influential work by Koppel et al. [39], who applied SVMs to English es-
says written by non-native speakers. Their study focused on determining a writer’s native
language (L1) based solely on linguistic traces in their English (L2) texts. They constructed
a dataset composed of essays from speakers of five different L1 backgrounds—Arabic,
Chinese, French, Japanese, and Spanish—and extracted two main types of features:
function word frequencies and character-level n-grams. Function words, such as the, of,
and to, were chosen for their syntactic regularity and resistance to topical variation (i.e.,
changes in vocabulary and content based on the subject matter or theme of the text),
while character n-grams captured morphological and orthographic tendencies reflective
of L1 influence. Each essay was transformed into a high-dimensional sparse vector en-
coding the frequency or TF-IDF weight of these features. Using this representation, SVMs
were trained to classify native languages using both one-vs-all and pairwise (one-vs-one)
strategies. The results demonstrated that SVMs significantly outperformed simpler mod-
els like decision trees, even when classifying relatively short texts. Their success was
attributed to the SVM’s robustness in handling sparse, high-dimensional data, and its
ability to discover subtle stylistic patterns introduced by cross-linguistic interference. This
study provided some of the earliest large-scale empirical evidence that native language
leaves detectable footprints in second language writing.

Ensemble Models
Ensemble models have become a prominent strategy in NLI, particularly in shared-task
competitions such as the NLI Shared Task 2013. These approaches integrate predictions
from multiple base learners to enhance classification performance by capturing comple-
mentary information from diverse feature sets. Ensemble models typically employ meth-
ods such as bagging or boosting to aggregate outputs from varied classifiers.
Malmasi and Dras [40] introduced an ensemble-based architecture for multilingual NLI,
demonstrating that systems combining multiple learners consistently outperformed stan-
dalone classifiers. Their method incorporated linear SVMs trained on character n-grams,
POS n-grams, and function word distributions, with ensemble fusion achieved through
majority voting and meta-classification. The ensemble’s success was attributed to its ro-
bustness against noise and its ability to generalize across domains.
Their findings highlighted:
• the complementarity of syntactic and character-level features,
• the improved domain robustness of ensemble models,
• and the superior generalization of stacked ensembles compared to individual mod-
els.
These results have made ensemble methods a best-practice paradigm in NLI research
and influential in gaze-based adaptations.
Logistic Regression
Logistic regression is a frequently used baseline in NLI due to its simplicity, efficiency, and
interpretability. While often outperformed by more complex models like SVMs or neural
networks, it offers the benefit of directly interpretable coefficients, providing linguistic in-
sight into feature relevance.
The work by Brooke and Hirst [41] is often cited as a precursor to hybrid modeling ap-
proaches that combine structured linguistic analysis with predictive modeling. They ap-
plied L1-penalized logistic regression to hand-crafted syntactic and lexical features, in-
cluding function word frequency and part-of-speech tag patterns. While their model was
linear, their emphasis on interpretability and feature selection laid the groundwork for later
efforts to combine linguistically meaningful inputs with more complex architectures. The
methodological motivation behind their work—capturing subtle stylistic traces of L1 influ-
ence in L2 writing—parallels the goals of many deep learning models that retain human-
curated features for transparency and control. Their findings continue to be relevant in
contexts where model interpretability is a priority, as in cognitively grounded domains like
gaze-based NLI.
String Kernel Methods
String kernel methods offer a unique alternative to traditional feature engineering by op-
erating directly on text as sequences of substrings. These models avoid explicit linguistic
annotations, instead comparing texts based on shared character sequences, making them
particularly adept at capturing morphological and orthographic signals.
Tsur and Rappoport [42] first demonstrated the utility of string kernels for NLI by clas-
sifying essays based solely on substring similarities. Their results showed that even in
the absence of syntactic or lexical features, native language influences could be detected
through orthographic tendencies in L2 writing.
While computationally intensive, especially on large datasets, string kernel approaches

remain influential for their language-agnostic design and sensitivity to subtle stylistic mark-
ers that more traditional models may overlook.
2.4.2
Deep learning methods
Although deep learning has become increasingly prominent in text-based NLI, most stud-
ies adopt end-to-end encoders over raw lexical or character sequences. By contrast,
a smaller line of work applies neural models directly to engineered, linguistically inter-
pretable inputs.
Within the TOEFL11 ecosystem, for example, the CEMI system trained simple feed-
forward networks on distinct crafted feature spaces (TF–IDF word/character/POS n-grams
and speech i-vectors), then fused the hidden-layer outputs with a neural meta-classifier—
showing that MLPs operating on structured inputs can rival stronger kernel/SVM ensem-
bles [43, 44, 45]. In a related vein, Steinbakken and Gambäck combined an attention-
based text encoder with stacked meta-learning over traditional base classifiers (including
FFNNs) trained on TF–IDF n-grams and function-word features, demonstrating competi-
tive gains without relying solely on raw end-to-end representations [46].
Although techniques for text-based NLI were not designed with gaze data in mind, they
offer valuable guidance not only for modelling approaches but also for structuring input
data from a linguistic perspective (e.g., POS tags and n-grams). This influence is evident

2.5.1
Deep learning methods
A more recent study by Shalileh et al. [3] explored the potential of both traditional machine
learning and deep learning models to predict native language and second-language pro-
ficiency from gaze data, also using the MECO L2 dataset. For native language prediction
using only eye-tracking data, their best-performing model (a 2D convolutional neural net-
work trained on scatter plot images of fixations) achieved a macro F1 score of 0.323 and
a ROC-AUC of 0.755. In their approach, the dataset was structured at the level of individ-
ual fixations, incorporating raw gaze coordinates and durations, resulting in a substantially
large dataset for deep learning tasks (approximately 600-800 thousand rows). While this
structure enabled the use of deep neural architectures and resulted in promising overall
performance metrics, the data was modeled under the assumption of Independent and
Identically Distributed (IID) samples. This introduced the possibility of participant overlap
across training and test splits, raising concerns about generalizability. Moreover, per-
formance was primarily reported in terms of overall F1 scores across models, without a
breakdown by language class, making it difficult to assess how well the models performed
across different L1s.
Beyond CNNs, Shalileh et al. evaluated a shallow multi-layer perceptron (MLP) trained
directly on gaze features (Fix_X, Fix_Y, Fix_Duration). To control overfitting with limited
data, they restricted the network to a single hidden layer, fixed batch size = 8, and tuned
hyperparameters via Bayesian optimization; final scores were reported with a 10-fold
cross-validation following a separate 5-fold HPO stage. While MLPs were not the top
performer on gaze-only L1 classification (the 2D-CNN on fixation scatter-images reached
F1 = 0.323, ROC-AUC = 0.755), MLPs became highly competitive once non-gaze tabu-
lar variables were added (eye-fixation + demographics), trailing only Random Forest and
illustrating that tabular, low-dimensional inputs with non-linear interactions are a good fit
for MLPs.

2.6.2
Random Forest
A Random Forest is an ensemble machine learning method that combines many individual
decision trees to make more accurate and robust predictions. It can be used for both
classification and regression tasks, and is particularly effective for structured, tabular data
such as eye-tracking features.
Each decision tree is a recursive structure that splits the input space based on feature
values. At each node, the algorithm selects a feature xj and threshold t that best splits the
data into two groups, typically using a purity metric such as Gini impurity or information
gain. For classification, the goal is to create leaf nodes that are as homogeneous as
possible with respect to the target classes.
A Random Forest grows T such trees independently, each trained on a different bootstrap
sample of the dataset—that is, a random sample with replacement from the training data.
This introduces diversity among the trees, which reduces overfitting and variance when
their predictions are combined.
Additional randomness is introduced by limiting the set of features considered at each
split. Rather than evaluating all d features, the algorithm randomly selects a subset of
size m ≪d at each node, and chooses the best split among them. This encourages trees
to explore different patterns and prevents dominant features from appearing in every tree.
Once all trees are trained, predictions are made by aggregating their outputs. In classifi-
cation, this is done by majority vote:
ˆy = mode{h1(x), h2(x), . . . , hT (x)}
where ht(x) is the class predicted by tree t. In regression, the output is the average of
the predictions from all trees.
Unlike neural networks, Random Forests do not use gradient descent or epochs. They
are trained greedily in a single pass by growing full trees based on data splits. This
makes them fast to train, with few hyperparameters, and highly robust to overfitting due to
ensemble averaging. It is a powerful, interpretable method for tabular classification tasks.

3.3.2
Semantic Clusters with BERT-embeddings
In this section, the influence of a word’s semantic content on the ability to identify a
reader’s native language is explored. A novel feature set is introduced, which clusters
words based on their contextualized BERT embeddings, thereby capturing their meaning
within sentence-level context.
Previous research combining transformer models (including BERT and XLNet) with eye-
tracking data has largely examined whether model attention correlates with human gaze
patterns or can predict fixation durations, often focusing on reading comprehension or
cognitive modeling rather than native language identification [49, 50]. By contrast, the ap-
proach presented here uses semantic information derived from BERT embeddings along-
side eye-tracking features to predict the native language of the reader.
This method is conceptually aligned with how Syntactic Clusters are constructed in the
reproductibility study, which grouped gaze features by syntactic categories such as part-
of-speech tags or dependency roles, such as UD tags. Extending this idea, semantic
clustering groups words with similar meanings (or similar context) and averages the as-
sociated gaze data within each cluster. However, this introduces challenges related to
cluster granularity: selecting too few clusters may obscure important distinctions, while
too many may fragment meaningful categories and introduce noise into the model.
Compared to the reproductibility study, in our case, punctuation does not introduce major
complications, as BERT separates punctuation into distinct tokens. Each token receives
its own 768-dimensional embedding, which are subsequently merged, yielding a single
vector representation per interest area. The 597 word text sequence was separated back
to sentences, and each interest area is embedded in context of the containing sentence.

PCA
To improve the quality and robustness of clustering, Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
is applied to the contextualized BERT embeddings prior to clustering. BERT produces
768-dimensional embeddings, which are known to contain substantial redundancy due to
high inter-dimensional correlations. Clustering algorithms such as k-means, which rely
on distance metrics in Euclidean space, tend to perform poorly in such high-dimensional
settings due to the curse of dimensionality — where distances between points become
less informative and clustering boundaries less stable. PCA mitigates this issue by pro-
jecting the embeddings into a lower-dimensional space (typically 50–100 components)
that preserves the majority of the original variance while discarding noise and redundant
information. This dimensionality reduction not only improves computational efficiency but
also enhances the interpretability and semantic coherence of the resulting clusters.
In this setting, 100 components was proven to be too little to capture at least 90% ex-
plained variance, therefore 200 components were selected, retaining 91.64% of the ex-
plained variance.
Determining the number of clusters
To determine the optimal number of clusters, three evaluation methods were applied. The
Elbow Method identifies the point where adding more clusters yields diminishing returns in
reducing within-cluster variance. The Silhouette Score measures how well each point fits
within its assigned cluster compared to neighboring clusters, with higher values indicating
better-defined groups. The Davies–Bouldin Index evaluates the ratio of within-cluster
compactness to between-cluster separation, where lower scores reflect higher clustering
quality.

3.3.3
Feature matrices
All feature files used in this work are stored as pickled pandas.DataFrames with a consis-
tent structure: rows correspond to individual participants, identified by a unique uniform_id
(e.g., ee_17), and columns form a two-level MultiIndex.
The first level encodes the fixation metric (TF, FP, FF), the n-gram size (uni, bi, tri),
and the feature family label, while the second level specifies the particular unit within that
block, such as an interest area, linguistic tag, length bin, or cluster ID. All values are
floating-point and represent pre-normalized gaze measures, so the matrices can be di-
rectly combined, subsetted by n-gram type, and fed into downstream processing without
additional preprocessing. This uniform organization was created to ensure that feature
sets from different linguistic or structural families can be seamlessly aligned on partici-
pants and concatenated into a single matrix for analysis.

3.5
Models
3.5.1
Linear logistic regression
A multinomial logistic regression classifier was implemented in scikit-learn to map fixed-
length feature vectors to language labels. Prior to fitting, features were standardised per
column with zero mean and unit variance (StandardScaler). Labels were extracted from
participant identifiers and encoded as integer class indices via LabelEncoder.
The estimator was configured as an ℓ2-regularised logistic model with the LBFGS opti-
miser and a fixed inverse regularisation strength, corresponding in scikit-learn to
LogisticRegression(penalty='l2', solver='lbfgs', C=1.0, multi_class='auto',
max_iter=1000, random_state=42). With multi_class='auto', the objective is multi-
nomial cross-entropy for multi-class problems and reduces to the binary log-loss in two-
class subsets; class probabilities are obtained from the model’s calibrated sigmoid/soft-
max outputs. The maximum iteration cap was raised to ensure convergence on high-
dimensional cumulative n-gram representations.
For each feature set (WFC, SC, IC, SC + IC, BC, TC), separate and cumulative n-gram se-
lections (uni; uni+bi; uni+bi+tri) were formed by boolean masking over the column index,
columns were sorted, and missing values were zero-filled. Model evaluation used the
study’s standard protocol (participant-stratified 10-fold cross-validation and a 10% held-
out test where applicable), reporting fold-level accuracy and macro-F1 and their sum-
maries.
3.5.2
Random Forest
Random Forest was included as a strong tabular baseline because prior work in native-
language identification and gaze-based classification commonly reports tree ensembles
as competitive on engineered feature sets, including reproducibility studies on MECO-
style data and text-based NLI where non-linear models over handcrafted predictors match
or surpass linear baselines.
Methodologically, the architecture bags many decision trees with random feature sub-
sampling, which aligns well with eye-movement signals summarized into features: it cap-
tures non-linear effects (e.g., thresholding in fixation durations), high-order interactions
across gaze metrics (e.g., first-pass × regressions), and is robust to outliers and hetero-
geneous noise. The model’s invariance to monotonic scaling and relative tolerance to
multicollinearity reduce the preprocessing demands while still offering diagnostic value
through feature-importance analyses (e.g., permutation or impurity-based).
The Random Forest classifier is instantiated with a fixed hyperparameter set: 300 trees,
unrestricted depth, minimum split size of 2, minimum leaf size of 1, all CPU cores enabled,
and a fixed random seed (42).
In scikit-learn this corresponds to n_estimators=300, max_depth=None,
min_samples_split=2, min_samples_leaf=1, n_jobs=-1, random_state=42; the default
split criterion (Gini impurity) is used.
Inputs are prepared per feature set and n-gram mode. For WFC, SC, IC, BC, and TC,
a single pickled DataFrame is loaded; for SC+IC, the SC and IC matrices are aligned
on participant IDs (reindex on the SC index) and concatenated column-wise to form the
joint representation. N-gram subsets are selected via boolean masks over the column
index, supporting separate (“uni”, “bi”, “tri”) and cumulative (“uni+bi”, “uni+bi+tri”) config-
urations. These masks target the n-gram tag encoded in the first level of the MultiIndex
(e.g., matches for _uni_, _bi_, _tri_). After masking, columns are sorted and missing
values are zero-filled before model fitting.

3.5.3
MLP
MLP was included based on evidence that simple feed-forward networks trained on struc-
tured, linguistically informed inputs can match competitive baselines in Native Language
Identification: in text-based NLI, the TOEFL11 “CEMI” line used MLPs over TF-IDF word/char-
acter/POS n-grams and achieved results on par with strong kernel/SVM systems, and
similar feed-forward components improved stacked/ensemble setups (e.g., with attention
encoders), indicating that shallow neural networks are effective when inputs are engi-
neered rather than raw sequences. In gaze-based NLIR specifically, prior work by Shalieh
et al. on MECO-L2 also evaluated a shallow MLP trained directly on low-level fixation
features, showing that MLPs are a reasonable neural baseline for eye-movement sig-
nals even when data are limited, thereby motivating their use on structured features as
also persued here. Architecturally, MLPs offer smooth non-linear function approximation
with modest capacity, which is well-suited to modeling graded relationships between fixa-
tion measures (FF/FP/TF) and native-language classes without the data-hungry require-
ments of deep sequence models. Finally, MLPs are a natural fit to the thesis’s fixed-size,
tabular inputs: all feature sets materialize as participant-by-feature matrices (MultiIndex
columns), so an MLP can ingest WFC/SC/IC (and BC/TC) directly, and it scales cleanly
as cumulative n-gram bundles expand the feature space (uni →uni+bi →uni+bi+tri) with
standardization—precisely the data organization described for the WFC/SC/IC definitions
and the unified feature-matrix format.
The MLP classifier was implemented with scikit-learn’s MLPClassifier and wrapped
in a Pipeline with StandardScaler. Inspired by the the work presented by Shalileh et.
al [3],this network comprises a single hidden layer of 100 units with ReLU activations,
optimised with Adam (learning_rate_init = 0.001).
An ℓ2 penalty of 10−4 is applied (alpha = 1e−4), the maximum number of iterations is set
to 1000, early_stopping is disabled to avoid validation leakage, and the random seed
is fixed to 42. In scikit-learn terms: MLPClassifier(hidden_layer_sizes=(100,),
activation='relu', solver='adam', alpha=1e-4, learning_rate_init=0.001,
max_iter=1000, early_stopping=False, random_state=42).
Standardisation (StandardScaler) is fitted on the training fold only within the pipeline.
3.5.4
Transformer
Transformer was included based on literature showing that attention-based architectures
excel at capturing long-range and cross-feature dependencies across diverse NLP tasks
and, more recently, in structured/tabular settings where relationships among engineered
predictors drive performance. Additionally, the expectation is that self-attention can dis-
cover interaction patterns among gaze-derived predictors that linear or purely feed-forward
models may miss. Architecturally, multi-head self-attention provides a data-driven mech-
anism to weight and combine heterogeneous eye-movement signals (e.g., first-pass, re-
gressions, total fixation time) and their context, enabling the model to emphasize infor-
mative feature groups while down-weighting noisy or redundant ones—an advantageous
property for eye-tracking data that are noisy and exhibit non-linear thresholds. Layer nor-
malization and residual connections could further help stabilize optimization on smaller
datasets, while the model’s capacity can be tightly controlled (shallow depth, modest
width, dropout) to mitigate overfitting. Finally, the architecture fits the thesis’s fixed-size
input structure as well: all feature sets (WFC, SC, IC, and BERT-cluster variants) can be
implemented as participant-by-feature matrices, and the cumulative n-gram bundles (uni
→uni+bi →uni+bi+tri) can be represented as ordered “slots” or grouped feature tokens
with positional (or group) encodings. In this form, the expectation is that self-attention can
learn dependencies both within and across feature groups and n-gram levels without man-

Cross-Entropy Loss
In multi-class classification (such as predicting one of several na-
tive languages), cross-entropy loss compares the predicted class probabilities (preds)
with the true labels (yb) and penalizes incorrect predictions more heavily when the model
is confident about them.
Mathematically, the cross-entropy loss is defined as:
L = −
∑
i
yi log(ˆyi)
Where:
• yi is the true label, represented as a one-hot encoded vector.
• ˆyi is the predicted probability for class i, typically obtained from the softmax output
of the model.
In PyTorch, the function nn.CrossEntropyLoss() is commonly used for this purpose. It
automatically performs the following:
1. Applies a LogSoftmax to the model’s raw output logits.
2. Calculates the negative log-likelihood loss between the result and the true class
indices.
This loss serves as the core feedback signal during training, guiding the model to adjust
its parameters to better align predictions with actual labels.
Epochs and Gradient Descent
Training a neural network involves minimizing the loss
function L(θ), where θ represents the model parameters (weights and biases).
At each epoch, the model parameters are updated in the direction that is expected to
reduce the loss function.
θ(t+1) = θ(t) −η · ∇θL(θ)
Where:
• θ(t) are the parameters at step t
• ∇θL(θ) is the gradient of the loss with respect to θ
• η is the learning rate
As training progresses:
• t increases with each batch update
• A complete pass through all batches of the training data, which is referred to as one
epoch.
In general, using more epochs provides the model with additional opportunities to adjust
the parameters θ and reduce the loss function L. However, training for too many epochs
can lead to overfitting, where the model learns to memorize the training data rather than
generalize to unseen examples.

In contrast, SC, IC, BC, and TC frequently display rising or oscillatory loss trajectories
across epochs, with several folds failing to establish a stable minimum; this suggests

increases. A couple of reasons are possible for this: (i) p -> n—thousands of n-gram

signal so added n-grams increase dimensionality more than information; and (iii) high
model capacity: a Transformer with relatively large hidden width and multiple heads ap-
plied to small tabular inputs. As the most immediate remedy, the model’s hidden dimen-
sion has been reduced to 64 to better match capacity to data scale while keeping the rest
of the architecture unchanged Across feature sets, moving from unigrams →uni+bi →
uni+bi+tri tends to elevate the loss valley and/or intensify late-epoch degradation, indi-
cating that cumulative n-grams are not uniformly beneficial for this Transformer without
additional regularization or capacity control.
The final Transformer encoder was implemented in a lightweight form in PyTorch to clas-
sify participants from fixed-length feature vectors. Each input vector is first linearly pro-
jected from the original feature dimensionality (din) to a model space (dmodel) via a learn-
able embedding layer. The projected representation is then processed by a
TransformerEncoder comprising L=2 identical encoder layers with H=4 attention heads
and dropout p=0.3; implementation uses batch_first=True. The encoder output is passed
through a 1D adaptive average pooling layer (AdaptiveAvgPool1d(1)) to obtain a fixed-
size summary, which is fed to a two-layer classifier (Linear dmodel →dmodel/2, ReLU,
Dropout, Linear dmodel/2 →C) producing logits over C language classes. Hyperparam-
eters are: dmodel = 64, L = 2, H = 4, dropout 0.3, batch size 16, and 100 training epochs
with early stopping and patience of 15 epochs, and optimisation uses Adam with learning
rate 10−4 and cross-entropy loss.

Evaluation pipeline
4.2.1
Cross-validation over all 2–7 combinations
To enable direct comparability with the Reproducibility Study, the same evaluation method
was adopted as follows: for each K-language combination (K = 2, . . . , 7), six feature
sets (WFC, SC, IC, SC+IC, BC, TC) and three cumulative n-gram modes (uni; uni+bi;
uni+bi+tri) were considered. For every triplet ⟨language subset, feature set, n-gram mode⟩,
the dataset was restricted to the selected languages, the corresponding feature matrix
was constructed (with cumulative modes formed by column concatenation), and 10-fold
stratified cross-validation was performed. In each fold, models were trained on 90% and
validated on 10% of the data, with participant identities held out between train and vali-
dation splits. Accuracy and macro-F1 were computed per fold and averaged, yielding a
single mean score per triplet.
After evaluation of all triplets for a given K, mean ± standard deviation across language
subsets (i.e., across triplets sharing K but differing in language composition) were re-
ported for accuracy and macro-F1, together with the best subset by mean macro-F1 (and
its corresponding accuracy). Because K = 7 admits only a single language subset (all
seven languages), the standard deviation across subsets is necessarily 0.00, even though
the reported mean for that case reflects averaging across folds within the cross-validation.

5
Discussion
In this section, the discussion of the results are presented specifically in the context of the
research questions.
”How effectively can deep learning models classify a reader’s native language based on
eye-tracking data, compared to the log-linear model used in the previous studies?”
Across the weighted 2–7 regime (Table 4.1), the Transformer achieves the best aggregate
scores on most feature sets: WFC uni+bi+tri: 89.51% accuracy and 87.98% macro-F1—
surpassing the log-linear model and MLP; only on BC/TC does it lag. Log-linear remains
competitive (e.g., WFC uni+bi+tri: 84.12% / 82.20% macro-F1), while MLP trails on the
same settings.
Thus, deep learning—particularly the Transformer—provides a clear top-line gain in the
pooled setting. In the 7-language only regime (Table 4.2), the advantage narrows: MLP
often yields the highest macro-F1 (e.g., WFC uni+bi: 66.19%; SC uni+bi: 66.19%) with
the Transformer close or best on select cells (e.g., WFC uni+bi+tri: 65.71% macro-F1;
BC uni+bi: 65.24%). The log-linear model is still strong on some rows (e.g., WFC uni
accuracy 78.57%). Overall, deep models do not uniformly dominate in the stricter 7-class
setting.
”Which feature sets utilized in this thesis contribute most to the models’ performance on
native language classification?”
Consistently, WFC is the most effective representation across models and setups (highest
or near-highest scores in Tables 4.1–4.2), with SC and SC+IC close behind; IC is weaker,
and BC/TC are the least effective overall (Transformer is particularly sensitive to BC/TC in
the weighted setting). These conclusions align with the model-output plots, where WFC
and SC curves dominate and BC/TC trail. WFC is the only feature set that preserves a
semi-sequential setup, and since deep networks thrive on sequential data, this fact could
be a potential explanation behind the performance results.
”Do deep learning models mitigate classification difficulties observed in the prior studies
when distinguishing typologically similar languages?” The model-output plots show a con-
sistent decline in both accuracy and macro-F1 as the number of languages per combina-
tion increases (K=2→7) for all model families, indicating growing confusion when classes
are more interrelated. The Transformer lifts the overall level in the pooled regime but
follows the same downward trend; it does not remove the difficulty. In the 7-language
setting, no model clearly overcomes this challenge. Hence, deep models improve aggre-
gate scores but do not demonstrably mitigate the similarity-related degradation observed
in prior work. (A definitive claim would require per-pair confusion analyses, which are
outside the reported results.)

5.1
Limitations and Future work
Due to time and compute constraints, exhaustive hyperparameter optimization was not
performed for every configuration in the Transformer. The evaluation space is combinatorial—
six feature sets (WFC, SC, IC, SC+IC, BC, TC) × three cumulative n-gram bundles (uni;
uni+bi; uni+bi+tri) × all language combinations × 10-fold CV—so a full search (e.g., over
learning rate, optimizer, weight decay, dropout, depth/width, scheduling, and dimension-
ality reduction) would be prohibitively expensive. Instead, a single, fixed configuration
per model family was used, with early stopping to curb the overfitting seen in Figure A.3
.This choice yields conservative (lower-bound) estimates of absolute performance, but
preserves comparability across conditions because every condition received the same
training budget and selection criterion.
Moreover, the feature sets differ substantially in dimensionality and signal structure; a
“one-size-fits-all” tuner would either overfit to some representations or penalize others.
Tailoring separate optimizers per feature set (or per feature-set × n-gram triplet) would in-
crease granularity to the point of compromising cross-condition comparability and vastly
inflate runtime. A principled remedy would be nested cross-validation with Bayesian or
bandit-style search (e.g., Optuna/BOHB/ASHA) and possibly multi-fidelity early-stopping;
however, this was outside the project’s resource envelope. Future work should explore
such adaptive tuning (and dimensionality control for high-order n-grams) to close the gap
between the conservative baselines reported here and the true capacity of each repre-
sentation.
Beyond optimization, future work could leverage richer data and alternative model fami-
lies. MECO L2 provides now provides raw fixation coordinates as well that enable end-
to-end gaze encoders (e.g., temporal attention/CNN over fixation sequences) rather than
only engineered features. The reproducibility of the study could be also tested by in-
corporating the expanded language coverage in MECO Wave 2 would support stronger
generalization studies across L1s.
On the representation side, instead of BERT, future experiments could try other embed-
ding models and compute embeddings at the text-item level (rather than sentence level)
to match the context of the text-items
Finally, exploring additional deep learning architectures alongside the Transformer (e.g.,
lightweight CNN/RNN/temporal models and tabular transformers) would clarify accuracy–
efficiency trade-offs in this high-dimensional, small-n setting.

5.2
Conclusion
Evaluation across classic and deep models under a unified pipeline shows consistent
gains from deep learning in pooled language subsets. A lightweight Transformer delivers
the strongest overall performance in those settings. In the full seven-language configura-
tion, a compact multilayer perceptron attains the highest macro-F1, with the Transformer
close behind. Performance declines as the number of languages increases, reflecting
greater confusion among typologically similar languages.
Feature comparisons yield a stable ranking. Words in Fixed Context (WFC) provides
the most reliable signal across experiments. Syntactic Clusters (SC) and the combined
SC+Information Clusters (SC+IC) form a strong second tier, while Information Clusters
(IC) performs below these alternatives. Two semantic feature sets are added to the bench-
mark and integrated into the same pipeline:
• BERT Clusters (BC): contextual embeddings for tokens in the shared text are re-
duced in dimensionality and partitioned into k semantic clusters via k-means; gaze
measures are then aggregated within clusters and across cluster n-grams, produc-
ing compact, semantics-aware gaze representations.
• Thematic Clusters (TC): a deliberately small, interpretable subset of BC that em-
phasizes broad themes and reduces sparsity for lightweight modeling.
Across models and class settings, BC and TC trail WFC and SC on average. BC nev-
ertheless reaches competitive accuracy in the seven-language case when paired with
higher-capacity models, indicating practical value as a complementary representation.
TC provides a transparent, low-complexity variant suited to applications that favor inter-
pretability and compactness.
Key figures summarize the empirical picture: in pooled evaluations over 2–7 language
subsets, a Transformer with WFC and cumulative n-grams attains ≈89.5% accuracy and
≈88.0% macro-F1; in the fixed seven-language setting, the best macro-F1 is ≈66.2%
(compact MLP with WFC or SC), with the best Transformer at ≈65.7% macro-F1 using
WFC, and peak accuracies around ≈71.4% across several strong model–feature pairs.
Overall, eye-movement patterns contain usable signals for native-language identification,
and deep models help extract those signals. Effective representation remains central to
this task: WFC paired with an appropriate model yields the most dependable outcomes,
with SC/SC+IC close behind. The added BC and TC feature sets broaden the NLIR
toolkit by injecting semantic structure into gaze-based representations and which could
serve reusable baselines for future, semantic focused research in pursuit of NLIR.
