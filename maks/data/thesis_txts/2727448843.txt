Abstract
In high-precision industrial manufacturing systems like machine tools, friction is a crucial factor con-
tributing to performance degradation and contour inaccuracies. It imposes limitations on the accuracy of
positioning and tracking systems, potentially resulting in instabilities. To achieve precise axis position-
ing, it is essential to effectively compensate for the frictional torques that arise from the drive motor and
axis dynamics. These torques play a critical role in minimizing errors in low-velocity control, ultimately
ensuring optimal performance.
In this project, different friction models, such as the Coulomb model, the Integrated and viscous
model, the Stribeck model, the Dahl model, the LuGre model, and the Leuven model, will be studied in
order to choose the model that best describes the properties of the friction not only in terms of Coulomb
and viscous components but the phenomenon’s hard nonlinearities and dynamic behavior.
Different methodologies will be explored and developed, in a mathematical way, to parameterize
the unknown parameters of the model and to observe the unknown states of the model, so that the
characteristics and properties of the friction to which the servomotor is subjected are known at all times.
Different experiments will be carried out with the Siemens 1FT7042-5AF70 drive motor in order to
know the range of values in which it works and to know the real values of the friction. In this way, through
simulations with real range values, we will study and analyze the different methodologies implemented to
parameterize the parameters of the described model while observing the unknown states of the system.
As we will see in this project, what we are trying to achieve in this project is complicated.

1.1
Motivation, Goals and Scope of the Project
Friction is one of the main sources of performance degradation and contour distortion in high-precision
industrial manufacturing systems, such as machine tools. It limits the precision of positioning and tracking
systems, which can lead to instabilities. Low-velocity control error, which is essential for accurate axis
positioning, requires sufficient compensation of the frictional torques that develop in the drive motor
and axis dynamics. In control applications, it is useful to have simple models that capture the essential
properties of friction. An example is the Coulomb friction model without memory, where the friction
force depends on the direction of velocity and exhibits linear viscous friction. However, describing friction
in terms of only Coulomb and viscous components does not capture the phenomenon’s hard nonlinearities
and dynamic behavior.
In this project, we will explore different methods for online parameter and state estimation of friction
based on analytical friction models, i.e.
estimate these parameters that define the characteristics of
the friction system while receiving the current system data (angular velocity of the motor, and torque
motor applied to the system), with the aim of predicting these behaviors and having the opportunity to
compensate for them so that the system is not affected.
As we will see below, what we are trying to achieve in this project is very complicated. We will find
ourselves with a system that depends not only on known states, but also on unknown states, so in order
to estimate the multiple parameters that define the dynamic friction of the model, we will have to observe
at the same time these unknown states of the system in order to have the opportunity to achieve the

main objective of this project, which is to parameterize the model. And all this will be done at the same
time as the current data is received from the motor system, real-time parametrization.
Thus, although, the main objective of this project is to parameterize the dynamic friction based on
an analytical model, the goals of the project were defined as follows:

1.2
Literature review
The literature study of the relevant topics to the thesis is divided into two categories, the various analytical
motor friction models in the literature and the parametrization of a model.
Referring to the study of the different analytical models of friction, at first instance, Coulomb dis-
covered that the friction force is opposite to the direction of velocity but independent of its magnitude.
Later, extensive experiments to understand the macroscopic properties of friction were conducted by
Bowden et al [1]. Discovering the velocity dependence in friction in ball bearings, Stribeck [2] found that
friction decreases as the velocity increases in certain regimes. This described phenomenon is known as the
Stribeck effect. Friction models developed by the physics and mathematics community include models in
which friction depends on velocity and a state variable known as average bristle deflection, which can be
seen in Rice et al [3] and, Johanastrom et al [4], which are the articles we based on.
On the other hand, with the help of the work carried out by F. Al-Bender and J. Swevers [6] and S. R.
Armstrong and Q. Chen [8] we have been able to understand the properties and characteristics that we

Before starting to describe the existing friction models studied in this project, we will define the
concepts we want these models to capture, i.e.
Stick-Slip motion and stiction, which are the hard
nonlinearities, and, micro bristle elastoplasticity and hysteresis, which are the properties that characterize
the dynamic behavior; apart from the Coulombus and the viscous component.
Stiction is a mechanical phenomenon that arises when two surfaces in contact exhibit adhesive forces
or surface roughness, resulting in resistance to motion. It specifically refers to the static friction that
must be overcome to initiate movement or bring an object to a complete stop. When an object is at
rest, the static friction force is higher but decreases upon initiating motion, transitioning into kinetic
friction [6].
Stick-slip motion, characterized by a cyclic interplay between sticking (static friction) and slipping
(kinetic friction), manifests when two surfaces in contact undergo relative motion. During contact, the
surfaces adhere to one another due to the resisting force of static friction, leading to a state of sticking.
As the applied force surpasses the static friction threshold, the surfaces overcome this resistance, resulting
in slipping and the onset of kinetic friction [6]. This process repeats itself, causing the object to move in
a jerky or discontinuous manner as we can see in Figure 2.2, where the setup is a block connected to a
spring whose free end is driven with a constant velocity v0.
Figure 2.2: Stick-slip behavior. (a) The desired position (dotted line), the actual position (solid line),
and the actual velocity (dashed line) as a function of time. (b) The applied force to the mass (dotted
line) and the friction force (solid line) as a function of time. Source F. Al-Bender and J. Swevers [6]

In Figure 2.2(a) the stick-slip phases are visible, the object sticks until the friction force equals the
breakaway force, the block then begins to slide, and the increasing velocity results in a decreasing friction
force. At the same time, the applied spring force decreases because the driving velocity v0 is smaller than
the velocity of the block. The block accelerates until the friction force equals the spring force. From that
moment on, the block decelerates, resulting in a higher friction force, which maintains this process until
the block sticks again.
Hysteresis is a notable phenomenon in friction that arises from the relationship between the frictional
force and the previous history of relative motion between two surfaces. Unlike the simple dependence on
instantaneous velocity or displacement, hysteresis accounts for the influence of past motion. This effect
becomes evident in systems where energy dissipation occurs during sliding or rubbing, resulting in a delay
or memory effect in the frictional force response. Consequently, the frictional forces experienced for the
same relative motion may vary depending on whether the motion is increasing or decreasing. Hysteresis
in friction is often linked to intricate material behaviors, such as viscoelasticity or nonlinear deformation.
Its presence can significantly impact the performance and stability of systems reliant on precise control
or motion [6].
When we are talking about capturing the micro bristle elastoplasticity we are referring to the be-
havior of the micro bristle-like structures or asperities on the contacting surfaces that undergo elastoplastic
deformation during sliding or rubbing. These micro bristles represent small irregularities or protrusions
on the surface that interact with each other upon contact.
Therefore, after explaining the main concepts we want to capture in our analytical friction models,
based on the articles Johanastrom et al [4], Y. F. Liu et al [5] and F. Al-Bender and J. Swevers [6], the
different friction models studied in this project are being described in the following sections.

2.4
Dahl model
In 1968 Dahl modeled friction as a function of the relative displacement of two contact surfaces, and it
is thus called the Dahl model. The model is based on the fact that friction force is dependent on the
micro-motion, which later on became called pre-sliding behavior, that is when the external force is not
large enough to overcome the static friction, the asperities on two surfaces will experience deformation
that results in the pre-sliding regime. When the external force is sufficiently large, the ”spring” is broken
and the current bristle is overcome, leading to a relative sliding between two contact surfaces. The Dahl
model successfully describes the so-called break-away phenomenon [5], [8].

4.1
Stribeck function
Regarding the friction applied by the friction component base, friction develops between the inner cylin-
der, where the steel shaft is housed, and the outer bearing of the component. On the other hand, a
ring is in charge of regulating the friction between these two surfaces, so that if it is turned clockwise,
the friction increases, and vice versa. Thus, this friction component base is what determines the static
friction Ts and the Coulomb friction Tc of the system. In this project, at all times we will work in the
same angular position of this ring, so that we can consider the value of Tc and Ts constant since the wear
of this component will not be very great during the time we will use it, in fact, it will be almost nil.
So, if this friction ring is not moved, we can determine its corresponding values such as Tc and TS,
and so does the Stribeck velocity ωS. The most optimal approach would be to calculate these values
using a spring torque meter, as was done in Papageorgiou D. [14]. Since this project focuses on capturing
the hard nonlinearities and their dynamic phenomenon, we will conduct an experiment to obtain the real
values.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The main objective of this project was to develop a methodology to estimate the parameters describing
the phenomenon’s hard nonlinearities and dynamic behavior of the friction only knowing the motor torque
Tm applied to the servo motor and the actual angular velocity of the motor wm. Thus in this project, after
a literature study, the Leuven model was chosen as the model describing the friction, since it captures
the desired properties, hysteresis friction, stick-slip motion, stiction, and micro bristle elastoplasticity.
Different methodologies for estimating these values have been discussed. First of all, it has been found
that the difficulty of this project comes from the fact that the parameters have to be estimated at the
same time that the implicit state of the Leuven friction model describing the presliding regime has to be
observed. If somehow the dynamic state z could be known, the parameterization of the model and the
identification of the dynamic friction would simplify the system.
Thus, different methodologies have been practiced to estimate this state z while parameterizing the
model: linear parametrization, looking for the dynamics of the state z and of the parameter describing
the hysteresis friction a through the Lyaunov function, and nonlinear parametrization. Through the
Nonlinear parametrization we have seen that we could parameterize the friction model in question in
specific circumstances, but we cannot guarantee that it will always find the real values of the system,
and therefore this methodology cannot be taken for granted. But we have observed that the only way
we can guarantee that the parameterization is correct in any case, as long as we take into account the
persistence of Excitation, is to know the parameter a. We have also seen how knowing the parameter a
does not require much time and much effort, so we consider this methodology as good.
We have also seen that the presliding regime is a very complicated regime to parameterize since the

6.1
Future work
Once the conclusions of the work have been drawn, we can affirm that there are aspects that could
complement this work:
• Real system testing: All the methodologies undertaken in this project have been carried out by
simulation, so this master’s thesis could be extended by testing the models described as correct with
real system data. Always bearing in mind that we have to have good sensors capable of capturing
the properties of the presliding regime.
• Due to lack of time we have not been able to implement more online parameterization methodolo-
gies, we could study the techniques of double adaptive observer and the NEA-based param-
eter identification technique and see if with these techniques the friction model can be better
parameterized.
