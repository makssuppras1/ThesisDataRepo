Abstract 
Sugars derived from lignocellulosic biomass has showed great potential as feedstocks for the produc-
tion of valuable platform molecules. Research has shown that glucose can be cracked into glycolal-
dehyde with high selectivity through a hydrous pyrolysis reaction at temperatures in the range of 500-
550°C. However, the high temperature requirement challenges the economic privilege of the process. 
The retro-aldol condensation reaction has been identified as the main reaction for obtaining good 
yields of glycolaldehyde from glucose.  
The purpose of the project was to improve the efficiency of hydrous cracking of glucose for glycolal-
dehyde production through the development of heterogenous catalysts active for the retro-aldol con-
densation reaction. This purpose could be followed by either achieving a more desirable process tem-
perature or increasing the glycolaldehyde yield at common process temperatures. Using a gas chro-
matograph equipped with a flame ionization detector, allowed for rapid testing at multiple operating 
conditions and with several different bed materials. 
The repeatability of the experiments was not found to be very good. Pooled standard deviations for 
each experiment, showed large uncertainties, which made it difficult to determine if any trends were 
significant, as all significant differences were covered by the uncertainties. It was discovered that a 
component in the gas chromatograph was defect, which was identified as a likely cause for the poor 
repeatability.  
The influence of varying the glucose concentration was tested at 250-600°C without any catalyst. 
This showed that there was not an effect to changing the glucose concentration. Determination of 
decomposition factors showed that for experiments without catalysts glycolaldehyde decomposed 
significantly at temperatures above 300°C before reaching the FID. 
WO3 and MoO3 supported on SiO2 was synthesized using incipient wetness impregnation and their 
influence on the cracking was tested. The synthesized WO3 and MoO3 catalysts was tested at temper-
atures between 250-450°C. The highest yield of glycolaldehyde was 24.9 wt.% and was obtained at 
400°C using 4 wt.% WO3-SiO2. TEM images indicated that crystal structures present at 4 wt.% WO3 
might have an influence.  
It was not found that there was a noteworthy difference in the performance of the synthesized WO3 
and MoO3 catalysts. Compared to tests without catalysts, they were not found to improve the gly-
colaldehyde yields. Neither was they found capable of lowering the operating temperature. The re-
sults implied that the catalysts might not favour the retro-aldol condensation reaction of glucose to 
glycolaldehyde. However other causes for the low yields were also proposed. One was the discovery 
of significant amounts of unidentified compounds which possible limited the yields by oligomeriza-
tion of glycolaldehyde.  
It was deemed necessary to continue the experiments to try to limit the uncertainties and identify the 
unknown compounds to investigate their influence on the glycolaldehyde yields.

Background 
The search for new platform molecules has intensified during the years, in order to find replacement 
for petroleum-based platform molecules. As lignocellulosic biomass is readily available and has the 
ability to capture the same amount of CO2 as they release, attention has been turned towards ways to 
utilize these as efficient feedstocks in the chemical industry.  
Sugars, monosaccharides derived from lignocellulosic biomass, has showed great potential as feed-
stock for the production of various valuable platform molecules. However, there are still a need for 
research in order to optimize the processes so the products can be produced in large scale, while still 
being commercial feasible. A key place to optimize is the yields associated with the reactions, to 
make them as efficient as possible, so the following separation processes becomes as simple as pos-
sible. Glycolaldehyde is one of the important platform molecules that could be used in production of 
many materials such as polymers. It has been shown that glucose can be cracked into glycolaldehyde 
with high selectivity through a hydrous pyrolysis reaction. However, pyrolysis of aqueous glucose 
into glycolaldehyde is a highly endothermic reaction, which normally takes place at temperatures in 
the range of 500-550 °C. The high temperature requirement may challenge the economic privilege of 
the process. Therefore, working on catalysts with potential to operate at lower temperature and 
thereby increase the overall efficiency of the process is one of the promising research areas. 
Purpose 
The purpose of the project is to improve the efficiency of hydrous pyrolysis of glucose for glycolal-
dehyde production. This purpose could be followed by either achieving more desirable operating 
parameters, i.e., lower process temperature, or increasing the GA yield at common process tempera-
tures. It has been confirmed that using a proper type of bed material in the process could affect the 
GA yield through changing evaporation and heating rate of the feedstock in the fluidized bed reactor. 
However, most of the research in this area are limited to physical properties of bed materials such as

1 Introduction 
The use of petroleum along with other fossil fuels increased dramatically up through the 20th century 
and has not slowed down in the following years. As fossil fuels are a non-renewable resource, the 
continuously growing demand is reducing the resources at a rate that suggest the reserves will last 
roughly 40 more years, before they are depleted [1]. Presently about 53% of the crude oil is being 
used for transportation related uses, and around 13% is used in the chemical industry both for energy 
and feedstocks for petrochemicals [2].  
A lot of the produced petrochemicals are used as platform molecules i.e., intermediates used to pro-
duce a variety of other value-added chemicals. The main platform molecules produced from petro-
leum are ethylene, propene and benzene [3]. Consequently, this has caused a growing importance and 
interest in research into renewable substitutes for fossil fuels for the production of platform mole-
cules. As lignocellulosic biomass is readily available and has the ability to capture the same amount 
of CO2 as they release, attention has been turned towards ways to utilize these as efficient feedstocks 
in the chemical industry.  
However, it is a challenge that it is not necessarily possible nor effective to produce the known plat-
form molecules when using lignocellulosic biomass. Therefore, it is necessary to investigate how to 
utilize different molecules as platform molecules. Sugars, monosaccharides derived from lignocellu-
losic biomass, has shown great potential as a feedstock for the production of various valuable platform 
molecules. Still, there is a need for research to optimize the processes so the products can be produced 
at large scale. A key place to optimize is the yields associated with the reactions. This is to make them 
as efficient as possible, so the following separation processes becomes as simple as possible.  
Glycolaldehyde is an important platform molecule which could be used in the production of many 
materials such as polymers. Glycolaldehyde can also be further catalytically hydrogenated into mono-
ethylene glycol (MEG) [4]. Ethylene glycol (EG) is a valuable platform molecule produced from 
petrochemicals today, so using a renewable feedstock for its production is of great interest.  
It has been shown that glucose can be cracked into glycolaldehyde with high selectivity through a 
hydrous pyrolysis reaction. However, cracking of aqueous glucose into glycolaldehyde is an endo-
thermic reaction, which normally needs temperatures as high as 500-550°C to achieve high yields of 
glycolaldehyde. The high temperature requirement may challenge the economic advantage of the 
process. Therefore, development of catalysts with potential to operate at lower temperature and 
thereby increase the overall efficiency of the process is an interesting research area. 
The aim of this project is to develop heterogeneous catalysts for hydrous cracking of glucose into 
glycolaldehyde. This is done by using incipient wetness impregnation to synthesize different prom-
ising metals, on suitable support materials, and testing their effect on the cracking of glucose at a 
range of temperatures. Additionally, the synthesized catalysts are characterized by N2-physisorption 
to determine the BET surface area as well as X-ray fluorescence and transmission electron micros-
copy. This is to aid the discussion of the catalysts performance by elucidating their properties. Finally,

the presented results will be used to discuss how to proceed with future work in the investigation of 
heterogeneous catalysts for hydrous cracking of glucose to glycolaldehyde.

2 Literature survey 
2.1 Platform molecules from lignocellulosic biomass 
Platform molecules are a crucial part of the chemical industry. This is as the name entails, because 
they serve as a platform for the production of a broad range of other chemicals, it being commodity 
or specialty. Additionally, they can be produced efficiently in large quantities. Ethylene and benzene 
are important platform molecules in the petroleum-based chemical industry. Ethylene is produced by 
steam cracking of hydrocarbons such as naphtha and ethane. For every million tons (Mt) ethylene 
produced, 1-1.6 Mt CO2 is formed. This makes it one of the processes in the petrochemistry that emits 
the most greenhouse gasses. A solution to this problem is to produce ethylene using lignocellulosic 
biomass as a feedstock. As lignocellulosic biomass contains a variety of functional groups it seems 
inefficient to deconstruct it into ethylene, to then build back the functionalities again. Instead it could 
be beneficial to search for replacement platform molecules for ethylene in its various uses, which are 
outlined in Figure 1 [2]. 
 
Figure 1: Applications and market share of ethylene and ethylene oxide. The green fields illustrate that alternative renewable routes 
are available. Purple fields illustrates that no alternative routes are available [2]. 
Figure 1 shows that approximately 32Mt ethylene per year is used for production of ethylene oxide. 
Ethylene oxide is an important C2 platform molecule in the chemical industry, which among other 
things is a result of it strained oxirane ring making it very reactive. However, several problematic 
aspects have made a search for a replacement for ethylene oxide very attractive. Apart from the prob-
lem related to it being produced from ethylene, it is also difficult to transport as well as dangerous to 
handle [2]. Figure 1 further illustrates that 72% of the produced ethylene oxide is used to produce 
ethylene glycols such as monoethylene glycol (MEG). This demonstrates that ethylene glycol is an 
important molecule with many industrial applications. As an example, ethylene glycol is used for 
making polyester fibre and polyesters such as poly(ethylene terephthalate) (PET) and poly(ethylene 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
4 
succinate) (PES) [2]. Therefore, developing new ways for producing MEG using lignocellulosic bi-
omass as the feedstock could alleviate the mentioned challenges related to the conventional produc-
tion methods.  
An alternative to using ethylene oxide as a feedstock to produce MEG, is the one-step conversion of 
cellulose, or its building block, glucose, into MEG, which has been researched extensively. These are 
liquid-phase processes which occur in presence of high hydrogen pressure and at long reaction times 
[5–7]. These processes involve a complex series of subsequent and competitive reactions, which gen-
erally makes it challenging to obtain high yields of MEG [2]. Consequently, it has been investigated 
whether a two-step process would be more beneficial. A possible two-step process is to first convert 
glucose to glycolaldehyde through a fast pyrolysis process and produce MEG from glycolaldehyde 
through a hydrogenation reaction as illustrated by Figure 2.  
 
Figure 2: Two-step process for MEG production. Adapted from Schandel et. al [8]. 
This is a promising route as glycolaldehyde is already an intermediate in the process of converting 
biomass into MEG. Additionally, when using lignocellulosic biomass as the feedstock MEG appears 
as an important intermediate in many synthesis routes for C2 chemistry. Therefore, MEG could be 
envisioned as an alternative for ethylene oxide as a C2 platform molecule. However, MEG is not as 
reactive as ethylene oxide, due to the stability from the alcohol groups. Instead glycolaldehyde could 
be more beneficial to use, as its reactivity resembles that of ethylene oxide, due to its reactive alde-
hyde group. Furthermore, the final hydrogenation step from glycolaldehyde to MEG could be 
avoided, hence limiting the amount of reaction steps. Thus, glycolaldehyde may be able to substitute 
ethylene oxide in a broader range of both commodity and specialty chemicals [2].  
2.2 Glucose 
Glucose (C6H12O6) is the most common monosaccharide and is vital for most living organisms. As it 
has a chiral atom it can exist as both D-glucose and L-glucose. D-glucose, also denoted as Dextrose, 
is the commercially important one of the two enantiomers, as it is almost exclusively D-glucose which 
occurs naturally, and L-glucose must be produced synthetically [9]. 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
5 
For the case of solid D-glucose, more than 99% of the molecules exits as a six-membered cyclic 
hemiacetal structure. In the beginning it was believed to have an open polyhydroxy aldehyde struc-
ture, but now it is instead considered to exists in three crystalline forms: anhydrous a-D-glucose 
(C6H12O6), anhydrous b-D-glucose (C6H12O6) and a-D-glucose monohydrate (C6H12O6·H2O). More-
over, an additional form believed to be a hydrated form of b-D-glucose has also been reported [9].  
In aqueous form at room temperature D-glucose occurs as a mixture containing approximately 36% 
a-D-glucose and 64% b-D-glucose. Almost all of it is observed to take the six-membered cyclic 
hemiacetal structure, but small amounts of the five-membered ring structure are also observed. Using 
the chain aldehyde structure, it mutarotates between these anomeric forms creating an equilibrium as 
shown in Figure 3 [9,10].

2.2.1 Production of Glucose 
Starch is the most important feedstock for glucose production, as it essentially is the only used in 
commercial production. Cellulose from lignocellulosic biomass is another natural source for glucose. 
Attempts at utilizing cellulose has been made, as it unlike starch is inedible, and the usage conse-
quently does not directly interfere with food supply [9,11]. The complexity of lignocellulosic biomass 
poses a problem in relation to its use as a feedstock in commercial production and is consequently a 
focus for much research [4].

2.2.1.1 
Glucose from starch 
Starches are polymers, which consist of anhydroglucopyranose units linked by a-bonds. There are 
two primary types of polymers, which must be characterised. Amylose which for all intents and pur-
poses is a linear molecule, and amylopectin which is highly branched and about 1000 times larger 
than amylose. The anhydroglucopyranose units in amylose are linked by a-1,4-bonds, which is also 
the predominant bond type in amylopectin, but there is also a considerable amount of a-1,6-bonds in 
amylopectin, which causes the branching. The proportion of bonds and the structure differs, depend-
ing on the source of starch [9]. 
 
Figure 4: Schematic representation showing the difference between the two main structures of starch [12]. 
Starch is broken down into D-glucose by breaking the links between the anhydroglucopyranose units 
catalytically. This is called hydrolysis of starch, and the catalysts can either be acids or enzymes. The 
choice of catalyst depends on the desired end product [9]. 
The dextrose content in a starch can be described using the dextrose equivalent (DE) value. The DE 
value can be regarded as a way to indicate how effective the starch hydrolysis has been. It does not 
express how much dextrose, that is present in a syrup, but instead it is a way to measure the amount 
of reducing sugars which are present in a glucose syrup. It is the aldehyde group in dextrose, which 
gives it the characteristic of being a reducing sugar, which is located at the C1 position, as shown in 
Figure 5 [12]. 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
7 
 
Figure 5: Skeleton structure of a dextrose molecule, highlighting the reducing site [12]. 
When two dextrose molecules join, one molecule will join its C1 molecule to the other ones C4. 
Consequently, one of the C1 positions will no longer be available, thus removing one of the reducing 
sites, as illustrated in Figure 6. Essentially as the number of dextrose molecules joined together in-
creases, the reducing power of the molecule diminishes. Consequently, dextrose has a DE value of 
100, maltose has a DE value of 50, and starch has a DE value of essentially 0 [12]. 
 
Figure 6: Structure of a maltose molecule, showing the reducing site and where the two dextrose units link together [12]. 
2.2.1.2 
Glucose from lignocellulosic biomass 
As mentioned, lignocellulosic biomass is another possible source for glucose production. It is a re-
newable source and is thus an attractive alternative to feedstocks which are based on fossil resources. 
However, as lignocellulosic biomass has a different and more complex structure compared to fossil-
based feedstocks it is more difficult to process and separate into its functional components. This 
means that it is oftentimes more expensive to process lignocellulosic biomass, and it thus requires 
selective and effective catalytic processes, to be commercial feasible [4,13].   
Lignocellulosic biomass is primarily composed of the three polymers: cellulose, hemicellulose, and 
lignin and their structures can be seen in Figure 7. Other molecules as e.g., minerals, proteins and oils 
are also present, but in much smaller amounts. Approximately 35-50% is cellulose, 20-35% hemicel-
lulose and 10-25% is lignin. Lignocellulosic biomass has a three-dimensional non-uniform structure, 
where cellulose is captured in a hemicellulose-lignin matrix. This structure combined with the crys-
tallinity of cellulose and the hydrophobic nature of lignin, results in lignocellulosic biomass’ high 
resistance against degradation, and consequently also the difficulties in separation of the desired com-
ponents [4,13,14]. 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
8 
 
Figure 7: The structure of the three main components in lignocellulosic biomass; cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Adapted from 
Li et al. [15]. 
Lignin is an amorphous polymer, consisting of methoxylated phenylpropane structures, e.g., coniferyl 
alcohol, sinapyl alcohol, and coumaryl alcohol as illustrated in Figure 8. This provides the rigid struc-
ture for plants and a hydrophobic system for the transportation of water. As lignin surrounds cellulose 
and hemicellulose in the lignocellulosic biomass structure, it is necessary to permeate or remove the 
lignin. There are several ways of doing this, depending on the desired outcome [13]. 
 
Figure 8: Structure of the three major lignin monomers [16]. 
Hemicellulose is also a polymer with an amorphous and random structure, which primarily consists 
of five different sugar monomers (Figure 9). These sugars are xylose, glucose, mannose, arabinose, 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
9 
and galactose with xylose being the most abundant one. These monomers create various heteropoly-
mers such as xylan, galactomannan, glucuronoxylan, arabinoxylan, glucomannan and xyloglucan, 
which is what makes hemicellulose structure. It is most ideal to remove hemicellulose during pre-
treatment as this makes the process of recovering glucose from cellulose proceed more effectively 
[4,13]. 
 
Figure 9: The structure of the five main sugars in hemicellulose [16]. 
Cellulose is a homopolymer and is the most interesting component in relation to glucose production, 
as the repeating unit is cellobiose, which is a disaccharide consisting of two glucose molecules linked 
together by b-1, 4-glycosidic bonds (Figure 10). Cellulose has both amorphous and crystalline zones, 
with the amorphous being more active, which results in the thermal decomposition of cellulose being 
quite complex. To obtain high yields of glucose from cellulose (above 90% of the theoretical maxi-
mum yields) the mentioned pre-treatment of the lignocellulosic biomass is necessary. Subsequently 
the cellulose should undergo enzymatic hydrolysis. The cellulose can also undergo non-enzymatic 
hydrolysis, but this happens at higher temperatures using mineral acid solutions such as H2SO4. How-
ever, these conditions are more severe than for enzymatic hydrolysis, and thus favours undesired 
degradation products such as hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF) and levulinic acid [4,14].

2.3 Cracking of sugars 
Cracking defines the process where a molecule is split into smaller molecules by breaking the bonds 
between them. Depending on the molecule and the desired products this is done using various tem-
peratures and catalysts. However, this process is highly endothermic and need relatively high tem-
peratures. Cracking is a term originating from the petrochemical world. For biomass the process is 
most often termed as pyrolysis, but essentially the two things relate to processes where carbohydrates 
are broken down into their smaller constituents utilizing high temperature processes in absence of 
oxygen. Hydrous cracking of sugars refers to processes where an aqueous solution of the sugar is 
used to support the desired reactions. Retro-aldol condensation is the main reaction for cracking C-C 
bonds for glycolaldehyde production which is discussed in the following.  
2.3.1 Retro-aldol condensation 
When using sugars as a feedstock it is necessary to understand the reactions that they can partake in, 
to understand how to reach the desired end product. Retro-aldol, isomerization, hydrogenation, oxi-
dation, and dehydration reactions are some of the main reactions glucose will perform. However, the 
retro-aldol reaction is the central reaction in the cracking of sugars to glycolaldehyde. The retro-aldol 
reaction is the reverse of the aldol reaction (also described as the aldol-condensation). An aldol reac-
tion describes the process where two molecules containing carbonyl groups react by creating a car-
bon-carbon bond and form a b-hydroxy carbonyl compound, also denoted as an aldol. Consequently, 
the retro-aldol reaction results in two carbonyl compounds by breaking of a carbon-carbon bond. The 
retro-aldol reaction in saccharides occur through the creation of intramolecular hydrogen bond link-
ages, and the steps are shown in Figure 11 for a general aldose [17,18]. 
 
Figure 11: Depiction of the steps in a retro-aldol reaction occurring in a general aldose [18]. 
From Figure 11 it is noted that before glucose can be cracked to smaller molecules it needs to undergo 
ring opening. This is illustrated in Figure 12Figure 13 for a hypothetic sugar ring.

The need for ring-opening before the retro-aldol reaction can occur, limits which sugars can be used 
as a feedstock for glycolaldehyde production. The structure of cellulose only allows for ring-opening 
at the reducing end, which hinders the retro-aldol reactions. 
2.3.2 Glycolaldehyde from sugars 
Glycolaldehyde (hydroxyacetaldehyde) with a molecular weight of 60 g/mol is a C2-molcule and the 
smallest reducing sugar that has both alcohol and aldehyde functionality. It exists only as a monomer 
in its gaseous states and as a dimer in liquid or solid state [19]. Glycolaldehyde shows great potential 
as a platform molecule, as it can be transformed into other valuable molecules, of which a number of 
them is shown in Figure 13. 
 
Figure 13: Schematic illustration of the potential uses  for glycolaldehyde as a platform molecule [8].

Glycolaldehyde has been identified as a product from the cracking of sugars in various research. 
However, the challenge is to find a way that provides sufficient yields and is scalable, so it is feasible 
for a commercial process. Furthermore, the cracking of sugars to glycolaldehyde also yields other 
products such as formaldehyde, glyoxal, acetol and pyruvaldehyde. Glyoxal, acetol and pyruvalde-
hyde, are also seen as valuable products as they can be further hydrogenated to give their respective 
glycols, ethylene and propylene glycol [8]. Formaldehyde on the other hand is an undesired product, 
as it known to be a hazardous product, as well as it poses issues in the subsequent downstream pro-

In order to investigate how glucose could be most selectively cracked to obtain glycolaldehyde, the 
effects of temperature and pressure on the retro-aldol reaction were evaluated [17]. The experiments 
showed that the retro-aldol reaction became dominant with increasing temperatures, and that the 
isomerization and dehydration reactions were suppressed. The dehydration reactions were influenced 
the most by increasing temperatures, and the selectivity of dehydration of glucose to 1,6-anhydro-b-
D-glucose (1,6-AHG) and 5-hydroxymethyl-2-furfural (5-HMF) respectively decreased from a com-
bined selectivity of 18.9% at subcritical conditions to 0.3 at supercritical conditions.   
The highest selectivity of glycolaldehyde was achieved at 64.2% at 450°C, 35 MPa and with a resi-
dence time of 0.25 s. Sasaki et al. [17] correlated this with the low water density obtained under 
supercritical conditions. This is due to the low water density making the formation of intramolecular 
hydrogen bonds between saccharides more favourable than those between water and saccharide. As 
the retro-aldol reaction of saccharides occur via the formation of intramolecular hydrogen bonds, this 
favours the conversion to glycolaldehyde. They further propose that a higher yield of glycolaldehyde 
could be obtained if a lower water density is reached by increasing the temperature and lowering the 
pressure further. However, providing supercritical water conditions requires large quantities of en-
ergy and the required pressure make it essential to use a high-pressure apparatus, which subsequently 
increases both the capital and operating costs.

solution of 40-70% glucose into a reactor with a fluidized bed of sand operating between 520-560°C. 
The residence time of the vapor product should preferably be 0.5-2s in the reactor.  In Table 2 the 
results from pyrolysis of different sugars show, that the glycolaldehyde yield is considerably higher,

other things result in suppression of dehydration reactions, promotes ring opening and fragmentation

temperature was increased from 475°C. Figure 14 also shows that besides glycolaldehyde, glyoxal,

Lastly, Schandel et al. [8] tested the effect of using different sugars as feedstock for the conversion 
of glycolaldehyde. Glucose gave the highest yields at 73% whereas other sugars such as fructose and 
sucrose resulted in significantly lower amounts of glycolaldehyde, and instead gave higher amounts 
of the other products. This revealed that a C-C bond cleavage of glucose through the retro-aldol con-
densation reaction could efficiently lead to glycolaldehyde production, which will be elaborated in 
section 2.3.2.1. Xylose, a C5 monosaccharide, also gave a relatively high yield at 66% but increased 
the formation of formaldehyde to 15%. The results are consistent with both those of Majerski et al. 
[18] and Kostetskyy et al. [20]. 
All the tested monosaccharides glucose, fructose, and xylose showed high amounts of recovered car-
bon in the condensed products, whereas the disaccharide sucrose gave a lower carbon recovery. 
Schandel et al. [8] suggest that this is due to the sugars needing to perform ring-opening in order to 
participate in the retro-aldol reactions necessary to yield glycolaldehyde. As sucrose is a non-reducing 
sugar, ring-opening cannot occur before hydrolysis of the glycosidic bond.

The research mentioned above have all obtained yields of glycolaldehyde above 50%, but only when 
highly refined sugars were used as a feedstock. However, it is more desirable to use a crude sugar as 
a feedstock, as sugars with high purity is energy intensive and costly to obtain. The challenge is that 
the yields of glycolaldehyde when crude sugar is used as feed, is lower than for high purity sugars.  
Osmundsen et al. [25] patented a process where the feedstock could consist of crude saccharide ma-
terial, which was comprised as a combination of mono- and/or disaccharides. Their solution was to 
remove the salts from the crude carbohydrate e.g., using an ion exchange procedure. By doing that 
they were able to obtain yields of glycolaldehyde from crude saccharides at the same level as those 
obtained using high purity sugars.  
2.3.2.1 
Reaction pathway for glycolaldehyde from glucose  
As a part of their research both Schandel et al. [8] and Sasaki et al. [17] presented reaction schemes 
for the conversion of sugars into glycolaldehyde as seen in Figure 19 and Figure 20. The reaction 
scheme proposed by Schandel et al. [8] seen in Figure 19 describes the decomposition of monosac-
charides, and it supports their experimental findings of the yields of glycolaldehyde and other oxy-
genates when using different monosaccharide feedstocks. Arrows leaving boxes denotes retro-aldol 
reactions and double arrows describes isomerization reactions.  
From the reaction scheme it is seen that after glucose has undergone ring-opening, it is converted to 
both 1,2-ethenediol and the C4-intermediate erythrose trough a retro-aldol reaction. 1,2-ethenediol is 
an intermediate product however, as it tautomerizes spontaneously to glycolaldehyde. Erythrose is 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
20 
also an intermediate as it is broken down into a glycolaldehyde molecule and 1,2-ethenediol by an-
other retro-aldol reaction. So, in theory one glucose molecule could be converted to three glycolalde-
hyde molecules. However, not all glucose molecules behave so ideally. Some are subject to isomeri-
zation reactions and are converted to fructose. From Figure 19, it is noted that fructose will give 
glyceraldehyde and prop-1-ene-1,2,3-triol when doing a retro-aldol reaction. These can furthermore 
undergo both dehydration and isomerization and end up as a mix of formaldehyde, pyruvaldehyde 
and glycolaldehyde. Additionally, erythrose can isomerize to erythrulose, which also hinders the con-
version to glycolaldehyde [8]. Therefore, isomerization of glucose and dehydration reactions are un-
wanted reactions that decrease the yield of the target molecule, glycolaldehyde. Manipulating of op-
erating conditions and using a proper catalyst could increase the selectivity of glycolaldehyde.  
The reaction scheme also explains the results presented in Figure 18. Using glucose as a feed gave 
the highest yields of glycolaldehyde, which indicates that many of the glucose molecules followed 
the desired reaction pathway. The reaction pathway for fructose results in the lowest amount of gly-
colaldehyde molecules, which is consistent with the feeds consisting of fructose giving the lowest 
yields of glycolaldehyde. The reaction pathway for fructose also explains the increased amounts of 
side products. When xylose was used as a feedstock, the glycolaldehyde yield was decent, but there 
was an increased amount of formaldehyde. This is a result of the retro-aldol reaction of xylose yield-
ing one 1,2-ethenediol and glyceraldehyde. Glyceraldehyde will give formaldehyde and another 1,2-
ethenediol molecule. Consequently, one xylose molecule can maximum yield two glycolaldehyde 
molecules and will also give one formaldehyde molecule, even if it decomposes ideally and does not 
isomerize to xylulose.

2.3.2.2 
Effect of operating parameters 
The results presented above from Sasaki et al. [17], Majerski et al. [18], Kostetskyy et al. [20] and 
Schandel et al. [8] gives a general indication on the effect of operating parameters on the glycolalde-
hyde yield from cracking of glucose. One of the key parameters they investigate is the temperature. 
Schandel et al. [8] illustrates the effect over a wider range of temperatures, which is presented in 
Figure 16 (Right). Their results indicates that the yield of glycolaldehyde increases with temperature 
until it reaches a maximum after which the yield decreases if the temperature is increased further.   
The results presented by Kostetskyy et al. [20]  shows that the glycolaldehyde yields are highest at 
475°C and decreases when the temperature is increased further. They do not investigate temperatures 
lower than 475°C, so in theory there could be a lower temperature at which the yield was higher. 
Majerski et al. [18] showed that when the temperature was increased from 528°C to 573°C the gly-
colaldehyde yield decreased slightly (Table 2), however they do not report the yields at lower tem-
peratures either. Since the optimum temperature for Schandel et al. [8], Kostetskyy et al. [20] and 
Majerski et al. [18] are all between 475-528°C it would indicate that the optimal temperature for non-
catalytic cracking lies in that range. However, the difference in temperature demonstrates that other 
operating parameters also plays a significant role. 
Sasaki et al. [17] showed that the pressure also has an influence, but their conditions are very different 
from Schandel et al. [8], Kostetskyy et al. [20] and Majerski et al. [18], so it is not possible to compare 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
22 
their results. Furthermore, the costs of the necessary high-pressure apparatus should be balanced with 
the possible gain in yield the change in pressure provides.  
Schandel et al. [8] also investigated if the sugar concentration in the feed solution influenced the 
glycolaldehyde yield, presented in Figure 17. The results presented by Majerski et al. [18] showed a 
yield of 55.4 wt.% when using a feed solution containing 34 wt.% glucose at 528°C. Schandel et al. 
[8] on the other hand presented a yield of 74 wt.% at almost the same temperature at 525°C, but their 
feed solution contained only 20 wt.% glucose. This comparison highlights that there might also be an 
optimal concentration of the feed solution. However, the results in Figure 17 does not indicate that 
the sugar concentration alone would have that large of an effect on the glycolaldehyde yield, suggest-
ing that something else caused the difference. This could likely be the bed material as Majerski et al. 
[18] uses sand whereas Schandel et al. [8] uses glass beads.  
Kostetskyy et al. [20] obtained a yield of 72.8 wt.% glycolaldehyde at 475°C using 20 wt.% glucose, 
which is almost identical to what Schandel et al. [8] reports, but at a lower temperature. This further 
suggests that the applied bed material is likely quite important. Although Schandel et al. [8] and 
Kostetskyy et al. [20] both use glass beads, it is not the same type or size. Kostetskyy et al. [20] uses 
acid washed glass beads consisting of only silicon oxide between 212-300µm, whereas Schandel et 
al. [8] uses glass beads consisting of silicon oxide, sodium oxide and calcium oxide between 90-
150µm. From only these two instances it is not possible to identify with certainty if it is the size or 
the type of glass beads, that are the determining factor, but it further underlines what importance the 
bed material can have. 
2.4 Heterogeneous catalysts for cracking of glucose 
Most research dealing with the catalytic cracking of glucose are focused on the conversion of glucose 
to ethylene glycol. This is a one-step, liquid-phase process where glucose is first converted to gly-
colaldehyde, which then undergo in-situ hydrogenation and form ethylene glycol. However, as gly-
colaldehyde can be utilized for several valuable reactions, focus on the two-step process is increasing. 
The research on the one-step catalytic cracking of glucose to ethylene glycol, is also very beneficial 
for the two-step process, as catalysts for the conversion of glucose to glycolaldehyde is an important 
part of the that research. 
A group from the Dalian University of Technology’s (DUT) Institute of Chemical Physics [11,26–
30] has performed multiple studies on the one-step liquid phase conversion of cellulose and glucose 
to ethylene glycol. The research was carried out using stainless-steel autoclaves pressurised with H2. 
The experiments were either carried out as a batch process [26–28,30] where cellulose and glucose 
was mixed with water in the autoclave or as a semicontinuous process [11,29]  where an aqueous 
solution was fed continuously to the vessel. For both processes the catalysts were added to the auto-
clave at the beginning along with water and stirred. 
The group at DUT found that using bimetallic catalysts combining tungsten, active for retro-aldol 
condensation reaction,  and a transition metal, active for hydrogenation reaction, gave high yields of 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
23 
ethylene glycol [27]. Furthermore, they discovered that regardless of the composition of the initial 
tungsten compound soluble tungsten bronze (HxWO3) was formed during the reaction as a result of 
the high water temperature and the pressurized H2 [11,30]. At reaction conditions the HxWO3 is dis-
solved in the liquid, but can precipitate upon cooling [30].  They suggest that the dissolved HxWO3 
will function as a homogenous catalyst and is the catalytically active species responsible for the se-
lective cleaving of C-C bonds in cellulose-derived sugars, such as glucose, to produce glycolaldehyde 
[26]. 
The group at DUT [11,26–30] achieved promising results in their search for an effective way to con-
vert biomass into ethylene glycol in a one-step process, but along with the ethylene glycol other 
polyols were also formed. These polyols have boiling points close to each other or form an azeotrope 
with water, which makes separation processes very difficult. This makes the enhancement of catalysts 
and a detailed understanding of the reactions occurring crucial in order to improve the selectivity of 
the desired products [11]. Their studies show that compounds containing tungsten are the key element 
assisting the retro-aldol reaction necessary for glucose conversion to glycolaldehyde.  
Collins et al. [31] has recently patented a process that aims to perform cracking of sugars to glycolal-
dehyde in presence of bed materials with catalytic effect in a fluidized bed reactor. By utilizing cata-
lysts, they report glycolaldehyde yields of up to 85% from a feed of 20 wt.% aqueous glucose at 
525°C. They tested both bed materials without any catalytic effect, and various catalytically active 
materials.  
Their tests with non-catalytical active bed materials (untreated glass beads) were carried out in a 
fluidized bed reactor with the glass beads constituting 6% of the total volume of the bed. They per-
formed glucose cracking at both 525°C and 550°C using a feed solution of 20 wt.% glucose. The 
results can be seen in Table 3 and Table 4. In general, there was not a significant difference on the 
glycolaldehyde yield at 525°C and 550°C. The formation of undesired products was however more 
significant. The formaldehyde yields were almost double at 550° than at 525°C and both carbon di-
oxide and especially carbon monoxide were formed in higher amounts.

lower temperatures, while keeping the produced amount of glycolaldehyde high. 
2.5 Conclusions from literature 
Glycolaldehyde has been identified as a valuable platform molecule as it can be used to produce a 
wide range of molecules such as methyl vinyl glycolate (MVG), glycolic acid, ethylene glycol, etha-
nol amine and ethylenediamine. Glycolaldehyde can be produced by cracking aqueous solutions of 
sugars. The retro-aldol condensation reaction is the main reaction for cracking C-C bonds which is 
needed to produce glycolaldehyde from sugars. However, challenges with obtaining sufficient yields, 
to scale the processes complicates the prospects of using glycolaldehyde as a platform molecule  [8].  
Sasaki et al. [17] presented good glycolaldehyde selectivity under supercritical water conditions 
(450°C and 35 MPa). Around the same time Majerski et al. [18] reported up to 70% glycolaldehyde 
yields from the fast pyrolysis of sugars between 520-560°C. More recently Kostetskyy et al. [20] and 
Schandel et al. [8] both presented high yields of glycolaldehyde of 72.8 wt.% at 475°C and 74 wt.% 
at 525°C respectively.  
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
27 
Sasaki et al. [17] and Schandel et al. [8] both also presented reaction networks detailing the pathway 
for the production of glycolaldehyde from glucose. Both showed that glycolaldehyde can be formed 
with 100% atom efficiency from glucose, as glucose undergo retro-aldol condensation to form 
erythrose and glycolaldehyde, with erythrose undergoing further retro-aldol condensation to form 
two glycolaldehyde molecules.  
Majerski et al. [18], Kostetskyy et al. [20] and Schandel et al. [8] all use non-catalytical bed materials 
and require high temperatures to achieve significant glycolaldehyde yields. Furthermore, they all il-
lustrate that temperature is a key parameter to achieve high glycolaldehyde yields from glucose. Re-
sults from Schandel et al. [8] indicates that the sugar concentration in the feed, does not have a large 
effect. Instead, the difference in optimal temperature for Kostetskyy et al. [20] and Schandel et al. [8] 
point towards the bed material having a great significance as all other conditions are similar including 
the achieved yield.  
A group from the Institute of Chemical Physics at DUT [11,26–30] highlighted, through multiple 
studies of the one-step liquid phase conversion of cellulose and glucose to ethylene glycol, that cata-
lyst containing tungsten was active for the retro-aldol condensation of glucose to glycolaldehyde. 
Recently, Collins et al. [31] patented a process for the cracking of glucose to glycolaldehyde using 
catalytical active bed materials. They tested multiple supported metal oxides and presented the high-
est yields for WO3 and MoO3 coated glass beads. Their results do not indicate the effect of using 
catalysts at lower temperatures as they only present results tested in the same range as with non-
catalytical bed materials. As the temperature has been identified as a key parameter, it is important to 
investigate if employing catalysts could give high glycolaldehyde yields at lower temperatures.

3.1 Description of setup 
A simple schematic of the experimental setup used for the hydrous cracking of glucose and the sub-
sequent identification and quantification of the main reaction products is shown in Figure 21. The 
setup consists of an Agilent 8890 GC System, which is a gas chromatograph (GC) equipped with a 
flame ionization detector (FID).  
 
Figure 21: Schematic representation of experimental setup containing an Agilent 8890 GC System.  
Gas chromatography is a separation technique, used to determine the various compounds in a sample. 
The principle behind the method is that the compounds are distributed between two phases denoted 
as the stationary and mobile phase. The stationary phase is placed inside the column and the mobile 
phase is an inert carrier gas that moves the products through the GC column. The compounds in the 
sample are partitioned between the two phases based on their volatility or polarity, or a combination 
of the two. The principle behind the separation is that the less volatile compounds interact more with 
the stationary phase, making them move slower through the column than the more volatile com-
pounds (illustrated in Figure 22). The same principle applies to the polarity, where if the stationary 
phase is polar, polar compounds will interact more strongly with it.

After being separated in the GC column the compounds reach the FID, where the carrier gas and any 
constituents is mixed with hydrogen. The mixture is ignited by a flame, which forms ions, that are 
detected by an electrode resulting in a signal. The size of the signal is proportional to the amount of 
the compound which is present in the carrier gas [32,33]. 
In the Agilent 8890 GC System a glass liner is placed before the GC column which serves as the 
reaction zone for the cracking of glucose. The setup is used to measure the yield of glycolaldehyde 
and other products at different operating conditions with inert material or catalyst present in the glass 
liner.  
3.1.1 Experimental procedure 
First the glass liner was prepared by adding 20 mg quartz wool. On top of the quartz wool approxi-
mately 1.5 mg bed material was added. The amount of bed material used was pre-determined based 
on volume and should correspond to a height of maximum 1 mm in the liner. The combined height 
of quartz wool and bed material should be placed 3 cm from the top of the glass liner. The tip of the 
syringe goes 3 cm into the glass liner, when making an injection, and to ensure good dispersion of 
the droplet from the injection, it was necessary that the bed material was placed exactly where the tip 
reached. Furthermore, the injection needed to occur in the isothermal zone. The temperature profile 
in the liner was measured and is displayed in Figure 23. From the temperature profile it can be seen 
that 3 cm from the top of the liner was inside the isothermal zone.  
 
Figure 23: Temperature profile inside glass liner. 
When the glass liner has been prepared, it was inserted into the inlet port in the GC. The temperature 
used for the cracking was set for each injection. Each injection took approximately 30 minutes. It was 
possible to program the setup to carry out multiple injections in a sequence. This made it possible to 
carry out a series of injections at varying conditions quite quickly.

At the beginning of each sequence, ethanol was injected at 180°C three times. This was to clean the 
quartz wool and embedded catalyst, so there was no possibility for volatile impurities to influence 
the peaks detected by the FID. It was also necessary to take out the needle and clean it periodically, 
as the glucose solution could dry and leave deposits of glucose on the needle and making the next 
injections more difficult.  
For each injection the syringe extracted 0.1µm of the aqueous solution of glucose from a small vial 
and injected it as a single pulse into the glass liner. The glucose solution was adsorbed by the bed 
material; while the water was vaporized simultaneously, and the reactions took place. The reaction 
products were carried through the GC column, using helium as inert carrier gas. It was necessary to 
consider whether the chosen carrier gas was in fact inert to the process, so that the products did not 
react further after leaving the glass liner. As helium did not have a reducing or oxidizing ability, it 
was assumed to be inert to the reaction. 
The products from the cracking were then transported to the FID to quantify the products. A gas 
chromatogram was created after each injection. Different compounds were identified by their reten-
tion time and quantified by the size of the signal, also denoted as the peak area.

3.4 Characterization of catalysts 
This section introduces the analysis methods used to characterize the synthesized catalysts. Each 
method is presented in a separate sub-section.  
3.4.1 BET surface area 
The Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) method was used to determine the specific surface area of the 
synthesized catalysts. This method is based on physisorption of N2.  Physisorption describes the phys-
ical adsorption of a gas (the adsorptive) on a solid surface (the adsorbent). Additionally the adsorbed 
phase is termed the adsorbate which is distinct from the adsorptive [34,35]. The main principle behind 
physisorption is that the volume of gas adsorbed can be related to the surface area of the solid. This 
is done by measuring the amount of gas adsorbed at a range of relative pressures, until the saturation 
pressure is reached [36].  
The BET isotherm is an extension of the Langmuir equation that describes physisorption in multi-
layers. The underlying assumption behind the BET model is that physisorption in the first layer hap-
pens on a uniform surface without adjacent physisorbed species interacting with each other. Addi-
tionally, it is assumed that the second and following layers have the same characteristics as the bulk 
gas phase. Despite the limiting assumptions the BET method is widely used with great success and 
reproducibility, and therefore accepted as a simple and useful method to determine the surface area 
of powders [34,35].

Before carrying out a physisorption analysis, the solid samples need to be degassed. This is a process

change [37]. Afterwards the physisorption analysis is carried out at the boiling point of the adsorptive, 
for N2 physisorption by cooling the sample in liquid nitrogen [36]. 
The physisorption experiments was carried out on a Quantachrome Nova Touch LX2 instrument. The 
samples were degassed at 350°C for 4 hours in a 38 torr vacuum. After degassing a 6-point BET 
measurement was performed using nitrogen at approximately 77 K [34,35,37]. The saturation pres-
sure was measured in the procedure, as it depends on the day-to-day air pressure in the laboratory, 
which influences the boiling point of the liquid nitrogen held in an open Dewar.   
3.4.2 X-ray fluorescence  
X-ray fluorescence (XRF) can be used to measure the constituents in a sample both qualitatively and 
quantitively, by causing the elements in a sample to emit X-rays. In this project an energy dispersive 
system (EDXRF) was used to determine whether the SiO2 contained any impurities and if the amount 
of metal oxides loaded on the SiO2 was consistent with the determined weight percent. 
A common way to model an atom is with the positively charged nucleus surrounded by negatively 
charged electrons moving within defined areas termed shells or orbitals. The individual shells are 
labelled with the letters K, L, M… etc. with the innermost shell labelled the K-shell, followed by the 
L-shell, and so forth as depicted in Figure 26. The K shell can contain two electrons, the L shell has 
three sub-levels (orbitals) and can contain up to 8 electrons and the M shell has five sub-levels and 
can hold up to 18 electrons [38,39]. 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
 
39 
 
 
Figure 26: Schematic illustration of an atomic model and the generation of characteristic X-ray from XRF. 
The electrons are bonded to the atom with a distinct bonding energy. The energy depends on the shell 
and orbital and the element. Irradiating the sample with photons of sufficient energy, typically X-
rays, can eject a photo electron from K or L shell of the atom according to the photoelectric effect. 
This will produce an electron vacancy in the inner shells, which puts the atom in an exited state with 
higher energy. As this makes the atom unstable it will want to fill the vacancy, by transferring an 
electron from an outer shell, also denoted as a transition. A transition creates a surplus of energy as 
outer shells have a higher energy than inner shells. The difference in energy is dependent on the shell 
that the photo electron is expelled from, and the origin (shell and orbital) of the transferred electron. 
The surplus in energy is emitted as an X-ray photon, called X-ray fluorescence as seen in Figure 26. 
As each element has characteristic shell energy levels, as well as each orbital has, each element will 
produce characteristic transition lines in the XRF spectra, making it possible to identify an element 
in a sample. The intensity of the emitted radiation will make it possible to quantify how much of an 
element is present. The theoretical emission lines for the K, L and M shells are illustrated in Figure 
27 [38,39], showing the sub-energy levels of each shell according to the orbitals.

The X-ray tube accelerate electrons in an electrical field, and when they hit the anode material, in this

voltage set on the instrument determines the maximum energy of the photons directed at the sample. 
The intensity is measured as counts per second (cps) [38,39].  
Apart from X-ray fluorescence, scattered radiation from the X-ray tube will also hit the detector. This 
results in the background noise, which complicate the detection of low peaks, and consequently also 
makes it challenging to determine low concentrations. It is also necessary to be aware of sum peaks, 
which results when two photons arrive at the detector simultaneously. This can interfere with other 
peaks, or be mistaken for different elements and lead to wrong conclusions [38,39]. 
Adding a filter between the X-ray tube and the sample, will make it possible to eliminate background 
scatter and increase the signal-to-noise, as well as interfering lines from the X-ray source. The filter 
will absorb most of the radiation from the source before it hits the sample. By reducing the back-
ground scatter, the detection limit can also be increased. It is necessary to increase the voltage in the 
tube, when adding a filter, to ensure sufficient energy is applied to excite the heavier elements. Add-
ing a filter also lowers the count rates, which can eliminate or reduce the sum peaks [38,39].  
The XRF experiments was carried out on a Bruker S2 Puma instrument. The X-ray source was com-
posed of a silver anode, and each sample was measured three times, once with a Cu filter at 50 keV, 
once with an Al filter at 40 keV and once without a filter at 20 keV. After the measurements, the 
software fitted the measured peaks and calculated the distribution of each element in the samples. It 
was necessary to go through the fits manually, to evaluate if the software had fitted the peaks cor-
rectly.

Out of the three injections carried out at each temperature it was common that one deviated from the

sen to include all measurements in the calculation of the average yield. This was based on the evalu-
ation that three injections at each temperature for each sample, was not enough to determine if devi-
ating measurements was outliers, or if it was the method which fluctuated.

wt.% glucose solution. The same is seen at 500°C, but not at 450°C, so the trend was not clear. 
Furthermore, it was not consistent which of the 5 or 20 wt.% that performed the second best in these 
intervals. It seemed unlikely that one weight percentage of glucose would show to be better than the 
other, if the rest of the concentrations did not perform consistently better or worse than each other. 
Additionally, all differences were covered by the error bars suggesting that the deviations were not 
statistically significant.

5 Conclusion 
The purpose of this project was to develop heterogeneous catalyst for the hydrous cracking of glucose 
to glycolaldehyde with the aim of either increasing the glycolaldehyde yield or lower the operating 
temperatures. The cracking experiments were carried out using a GC equipped with an FID. A syringe 
injected the glucose solution into a glass liner located in the inlet port of the GC. The glass liner was 
prepared with 20 mg quartz wool and 1.5 mg catalyst. The glass liner served as the reaction zone, and 
the cracking products were led to the FID ensuring subsequent identification and quantification.  
WO3 and MoO3 catalysts supported on SiO2 was synthesized using incipient wetness impregnation 
and was characterized using BET and XRF. The BET results showed that all surface areas of the 
synthesized catalyst were close to that of the SiO2 used as support. The WO3 lowered the surface area 
a little, whereas the MoO3 increased it. Selected samples of the WO3 catalysts were also analysed 
using TEM. The images showed that 1 wt. % WO3 only displayed crystal structures matching those 
of SiO2 whereas 4 wt.% WO3 also displayed crystal structures matching those of WO3.  
A repeatability study showed that the results from experiments with identical operating conditions 
fluctuated. Pooled standard deviations determined for each unique experiment further revealed that 
there were large uncertainties associated with the results. This made the repeatability of the experi-
ments poor. Service on the GC showed that a component in the inlet port denoted as the ALEX, could 
not be tightened properly, which was likely to cause the fluctuations. Bubbles forming in the syringe 
as well of movement of quartz wool and bed material due to pressure changes, was also highlighted 
as possible causes for the deviations. 
Experiments testing the influence of the glucose concentration between 250-600°C without any cat-
alyst, showed that changing the concentration did not have a significant effect on glycolaldehyde and 
pyruvaldehyde yields. The subsequent experiments were therefore carried out using a 5 wt.% glucose 
solution. Determination of decomposition factors showed that for experiments without catalyst gly-
colaldehyde decomposed significantly at temperatures above 300°C before reaching the FID. The 
intrinsic yields also revealed a temperature trend with an optimum at 550°C. 
The synthesized WO3 and MoO3 catalysts was tested at temperatures between 250-450°C and com-
pared to SiO2 as well as pure WO3 and MoO3. The highest yield of glycolaldehyde was 24.9 wt.% 
and was obtained at 400°C using 4 wt.% WO3-SiO2. The results generally showed that there was not 
a noteworthy difference in the performance of the synthesized WO3 and MoO3 catalysts.  Addition-
ally, there was not a significant improvement in the yields compared to the cracking of glucose with-
out catalyst. Furthermore, there was not anything that suggested the catalysts could be used to lower 
the operating temperature either. This indicated that the catalysts might not favour the retro-aldol 
condensation reaction of glucose to glycolaldehyde. 
It was not possible to investigate if the low yields, was a result of decomposition, as there were several 
unidentified peaks which showed up when employing the catalytic bed materials. The intensities of 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
 
63 
the peaks further pointed towards oligomerization of the products as a possible explanation of the low 
yields.  
The uncertainties related to the experiments generally made it difficult to determine if any trends 
were significant, as all possible differences were covered by the uncertainties. Therefore, it was 
deemed necessary to continue the experiments to try to limit the deviations and increase the repeata-
bility.

6 Future work 
There is a lot of potential for future work within this topic. Most pressing would be to address the 
repeatability issues related to the experiments. As long as there are large uncertainties connected to 
the results, it makes it near impossible to conclude whether a potential catalyst in fact has an effect 
or if they make no difference. Firstly, it would be suggested to investigate whether changing the 
ALEX, had any impact, as this was a very likely cause for the fluctuations.  
If fluctuations still occur after the change, it would be suggested to investigate in depth the potential 
causes for the fluctuations, their effect, and possible ways to remedy them. Additionally, regardless 
of the effect of changing the ALEX, it would be suggested to make many repeats with each catalyst 
at each temperature. Besides hopefully lowering the uncertainties, additional repeats will make it 
possible to determine if a measurement is a statistical outlier, and possibly caused by e.g., a bubble 
formed in the syringe.  
Further characterization of the synthesized catalysts is also recommended. This is to see if it would 
be possible to link their performance to specific properties. This would include TEM images of pref-
erably all the synthesized catalysts as well as pure SiO2, WO3 and MoO3 to see how crystal and or 
amorphous structures varies with load of the metal oxide. The lattice constant derived from TEM 
images should be supported with ones obtained from XRD analysis to provide a more accurate anal-
ysis and specify the orientation of the crystal planes. Additionally, ammonia temperature-pro-
grammed desorption (NH3-TPD) could be used to analyse the surface acidity of the synthesized cat-
alysts.  
Since there were several unidentified peaks, which were formed in significant amounts, it is suggested 
to investigate what compounds caused these peaks. This will help aid the investigation on whether 
the yields of glycolaldehyde, when employing the WO3 and MoO3 supported catalysts, could be af-
fected by oligomerization and decomposition before it reaches the FID. It would further be good to 
test the amount of catalysts’ effect on the glycolaldehyde yields.  
Since the 4 and 8 wt.% WO3 showed the best performance for the tungsten catalysts, it would also be 
interesting to see if a higher optimum could be achieved. Therefore, it could be useful to synthesize 
a 6 wt.% WO3 catalyst and see how it compares.
