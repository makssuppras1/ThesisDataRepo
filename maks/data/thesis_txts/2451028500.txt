Summary
The purpose of this study was to construct a thorough characterization of contamina-
tion in clayey tills using geostatistical modelling. Traditional methods of contaminant
characterization provide qualified estimates of contaminant mass, however quantifying
the level of uncertainty to the estimate is extremely difficult. Geostatistics employ the
spatial variation of available measurements to predict concentrations in unsampled ar-
eas, which subsequently are used to estimate contaminant mass and a contaminated soil
volume as well as the associated uncertainties. The need to quantify and reduce the
uncertainties, while minimizing the investigation costs therefore strongly encourages the
use of geostatistical methods.
An active industrial sites at Industrivej 2, Lynge located in the central part of the
Capital Region of Denmark with identified contamination of trichloroethylene (TCE),
have been reviewed an used as a case study for the geostatistical modelling.
Motivated by the need for an effective way to estimate contaminant mass and volume,
while quantifying the associated uncertainty, the application of semi-quantitative data
to the geostatistic method in a bivariate and multivariate approach was appraised. Semi-
quantitative data, such as PID and MIP were applied in order to potentially strengthen
the certainty of the estimate.
Four different geostatistical approaches were carried out; monovariate using only
soil sample analyses, two bivariate approaches applying PID or MIP and a multivariate
approach including all three types of data
Geostatistical modelling proved to be a successful tool for quantifying contaminant
mass and contaminated soil volume as well as visualizing the most probable location of
contamination. The monovariate approach yielded a contaminant mass of 12.1 kg TCE
with a 90% confidence interval of 8.0-19.7 kg with a contaminated soil volume of 12,723
m3 (10,065-15,351 m3. Manual calculation of contaminant mass conducted by NIRAS
yielded a total of 9.1 kg with a mass interval of 7-16 kg based on a sensitivity analysis.
All four approaches showed astonishing resemblance to the NIRAS estimate, how-
ever the application of PID and/or MIP as semi-quantitative data did not evidently
improve the certainty of the estimate in this study as expected. Different aspects of the
application was appraised and a future sampling strategy was proposed.
Finally, the overall performance of the geostatistical method and utilized software was
evaluated by assessing, inter alia, the reproducibility, sensitive parameters and features.

Sammenfatning
Formålet med dette studie var at konstruere en grundig og detaljeret karakterisering
a forureningsmasse i moræneler ved brug af geostatistisk modellering.
Traditionelle
metoder for forureningsestimering giver kvalificerede estimater, dog er estimaternes
usikkerheder særdeles komplekse at kvatificere. Geostatistik benytter den rumlige vari-
ation af tilgængelige målinger til at estimere forureningskoncentrationer steder hvor der
ikke er udført målinger, hvilke efterfølgende anvendes til at estimere forureningsmasse
og forurenet jordvolume, samt de tilknyttede usikkerheder. Behovet for kvantificering
og reduktion af estimaternes usikkerheder, samt at minimere undersøgelsesomkostninger
opfordrer kraftigt anvendelsen af geostatistiske metoder.
En aktiv industrigrund på Industrivej 2, Lynge lokaliseret i centralt i Region Hoved-
staden med identificeret forurening af trichloroethylene (TCE), er blevet anvendt som
case study for den geostatistiske modellering.
Motiveret af behovet for en effektiv måde at estimere forureningsmasse og -volume,
samt kvantificering af den tilknyttede usikkerhed, blev tilføjelsen af semi-kvantitativ
data til den geostatistiske model i en bivariat og multivariat tilgang vurderet. Semi-
kvantitativ data, såsom PID og MIP blev tilføjet for potentielt at kunne styrke sikker-
heden på estimatet.
Fire forskellige geostatistiske tilgange blev udført; monovariat hvor kun jordprøve-
analyser for TCE blev anvendt, to bivariate tilgange hvor PID eller MIP blev inkluderet
og en multivariat tilgang hvor alle tre datatyper blev anvendt.
Geostatistisk modellering viste sig at være et vellykket værktøj til kvantificering af
forureningsmasse og forurenet jordvolumen, samt til visualisering af den mest sandsyn-
lige placering af forurening. Den monovariate tilgang gav en forureningsmasse på 12.1
kg TCE med et 90% konfidensinterval på 8.0-19.7 kg med et forurenet jordvolume på
12,723 m3 (10,065-15,351 m3. Manuel beregning af forureningsmasse udført af NIRAS
gav i alt 9,1 kg med et masse-interval på 7-16 kg baseret på en følsomhedsanalyse.
Alle fire tilgange viste forbløffende lighed med NIRAS estimatet, dog viste tilføjelsen
af PID og/eller MIP som semi-kvantitativ data ikke med tydelighed at forbedre sikker-
heden på estimatet i dette studie som forventet. Forskellige aspekter af tilføjelsen af
de semi-kvantitative data blev vurderet og en fremtidig prøveudtagningsstrategi blev
foreslået.
Afsluttende blev den samlede ydeevne af den geostatistiske metode og brugervenlighe-
den af det anvendte software evalueret ved at vurdere blandt andet reproducerbarhed,
følsomme parametre og funktioner.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Problem definition
The water quality of water bodies, such as groundwater, is of great concern for ecosys-
tems, environment, human health and livelihoods. Groundwater provides almost 50%
of all drinking water worldwide, including Denmark where all drinking water originates
from groundwater resources (Smith et al., 2016; Miljøstyrelsen, 2017). The European
Commission have made water protection one of its priorities and have issued the EU
Water Framework Directive (WFD), in which achieving a good water quality status for
all water bodies is the key objective (European Commission, 2000). The WFD requires
all member states to preserve, protect and improve surface water and groundwater by
implementing the measures necessary to limit and potentially reverse the contamination
(European Commission, 2000). In Denmark, more than 37,000 sites have been identi-
fied as contaminated or potentially contaminated. Five regions are responsible for the
prevention, risk assessment and remediation of contamination of these sites (Danske
Regioner, 2019). The Capital Region of Denmark has defined an overall goal to reduce
contamination and conduct risk assessments in the regional areas where 80% of drinking
water is abstracted, by the end of 2024 (The Capital Region of Denmark, 2018).
Chlorinated solvents are chemical compounds which are frequently found in contam-
inated soil and groundwater at industrial areas worldwide. As they are volatile, toxic
and/or carcinogenic, they pose a serious risk to the quality of the groundwater as well as
human health (Lu et al., 2011; McCarty, 2010; The Capital Region of Denmark, 2018).
Chlorinated solvents are dense non-aqueous phase liquids (DNAPLs) that might migrate
downward into the subsurface by gravity, if present in this separate phase, and since
they are denser than water, into the saturated zone until they reach an impermeable
barrier such as clay (Lu et al., 2011; Fjordbøge et al., 2017; McCarty, 2010).
How-
ever, heterogeneities such as fractures and embedded sand bodies affects the transport
of water and contaminants due to higher hydraulic conductivity, potentially creating
preferential flow paths and thereby increasing the complexity of predicting the spatial
distribution of contaminants (Fjordbøge et al., 2017; Kessler et al., 2012; Nilsson et al.,
2001; Jørgensen et al., 1998). The upper subsurface in eastern Denmark predominantly
consist of clayey till deriving from glaciations and inter-glaciations of the Quaternary,
thus heterogeneities as the ones mentioned, occur frequently in the Danish subsurface
(Houmark-Nielsen et al., 2005; Kessler et al., 2013; Pedersen et al., 2011).
Remediation of sites with complex geology and contamination can be especially dif-
ficult and costly. Hence extended knowledge about the contaminated area, the contam-
inant source, mass and spatial distribution is required in order to accomplish the goals
==PAGE:20==

of risk assessment and remediation such as the ones defined by the Capital Region of
Denmark. It is essential to obtain reliable estimates of contaminant mass and spatial
distribution in the subsurface for a successful and not the least cost-saving remedia-
tion. Inaccurate estimates can lead to misguided time frames for source depletion and
remediation time, inefficient remedial actions and failure to reach remediation goals.
Obtaining reliable estimates in cases where embedded and potentially interconnected
sand bodies of individual shapes and hydraulic properties are present within a clayey
till matrix, compose an intricate challenge. The common approach to solve this kind of
challenges, is to divide the subsurface into numerous relevant zones based on multiple
various factors, such as chemical analyses of soil samples, water samples (etc.), horizontal
and vertical delineation and contouring the source area using samples and detectors,
geological interpretations, special areas of interest (etc.). A total mass estimate of the
zone can then be calculated based on the computed volume of the relevant subzones,
weighted averages of different samples within the subzones and finally applying bulk
density, lithological fractions and other applicable parameters (NIRAS, 2018a). The
approach is time-consuming and requires a number of expert decisions and assumptions,
which potentially affects the reproducibility of the result. Despite being a reasonable and
qualified estimate, assessing a level of certainty or quantified confidence to the estimate
is extremely difficult, hence not commonly estimated. Furthermore, applying additional
samples to the data set, can alter the structure and geometry of the known source zone
and thereby entail recalculations of the mass estimate.
In this project, geostatistics will be applied to a known contaminated industrial site
in order to quantify the mass of the chlorinated solvent trichloroethylene (TCE) and
the volume of soil contaminated by TCE. Geostatistics have become a popular and suc-
cessful technique for numerous environmental purposes in the recent decades, including
mapping of contaminant levels, delineating contaminated areas, as well as quantify-
ing contaminant mass and volume of contaminated soil with the associated uncertainty
(D’Or et al., 2009; Carlon et al., 2001; Henshaw et al., 2004; Largueche and Cheniti, 2016;
Einax and Soldt, 1998b). Geostatistics can be used to predict the distribution of contam-
inants in unsampled areas by analyzing the spatial variation of available experimental
data. Predictions of contaminant distribution are made on the basis of kriging inter-
polation and stochastic simulations by employing variograms reflecting the tendency of
increasing spatial variation at increasing distance between measurements. The predicted
values are subsequently used to compute the mass of the contaminant and volume of
contaminated soil. Geostatistics is a technique that, by incorporating the spatial vari-
ation of the measured contaminant to the analysis, gives a close approximation of the
true spatial distribution, while quantifying the associated uncertainty of the estimate.
Increased popularity of the geostatistical methods have encouraged a focus for de-
veloping geostatistical software and vice versa. For this project, I will use the software
Kartotrak developed by French independent software vendor Geovariances.
The intensity of environmental investigations and sampling campaigns at different
contaminated sites can vary, but common to the majority of the sites, is that the avail-
able quantitative data, such as soil samples, are sparse. However, at many of these
sites, a great amount of semi-quantitative data is available. The semi-quantitative data

can consist of screening measures, as for example PID (Photoionization Detector) and
MIP (Membrane Interface Probe) data, which qualitatively indicates the presence of
potential contaminant compounds. These types of data are generally used for detecting

of semi-quantitative data which applies further and potentially valuable information of
the contamination.
Geostatistics have been used for characterizing contamination by a variety of contam-

and bi-/multivariate approaches (Largueche and Cheniti, 2016; Einax and Soldt, 1998b,a;
D’Or et al., 2009; Donati and de Fouquet, 2001). However, the implementation of semi-
quantitative data such as PID and MIP has to my knowledge has not previously been
studied.
The project studies the site Industrivej 2, Lynge located in the central part of the
Capital Region of Denmark, and is a thoroughly investigated site with various available
data. The site is an active industrial site located in an area of drinking water abstraction
(NIRAS, 2018a). Furthermore, the site is an example of clayey till with several sand
bodies of varying extent observed.
1.2
Aim
The overall aim of this study is to construct a thorough characterization of contamina-
tion in clayey tills using geostatistical calculations of spatial variation and uncertainty
focusing on Industrivej 2 as a case study.
The specific aims of the study is as follows:
• Quantify mass and associated uncertainty of contaminant in clayey till by geosta-
tistical modeling of soil sample analyses.
• Assess the location including the associated uncertainty of contaminant volume
within the clayey till.
• Appraise semi-quantitative data of PID and MIP to increase data density and
knowledge of the contamination and subsequently assess the outcome in a compar-
ative analysis.
• Assess and evaluate the performance of the geostatistical method and application
of the Kartotrak software.

2.1
Environmental investigations
Environmental investigations by NIRAS, Rambøll, Geo and DTU Environment have
been conducted at Industrivej 2 by request from the Capital Region of Denmark. The
necessity for the investigation was due to Industrivej being located in a drinking water
abstraction area prioritized by the Capital Region of Denmark and that contamination
of pore-air, soil and groundwater have been found at the site in previous investigations.
The investigations includes characterization and delineation of chlorinated solvents
by reevaluation of the geological and hydrogeological setting and the effect on spatial
distribution of contaminants.

Site description
2.2.1
Industrial history
Industrivej 2 is an industrial site situated in Vassingrød Industrial area in the central
part of the Capital Region of Denmark (Fig. 2.1). The Danish company Arbi-skum A/S
has been operating at the site since 1962 with production and processing of customized
and advanced foam materials for both the public and private sector (Arbi-skum A/S,
2019). The storage and factory buildings at the site was constructed in 1965 after two
fire incidents burned down the original buildings.

2.2.2
Source of contamination
Arbi-skum A/S has used several chemical compounds in their production and processing
of foam solutions, such as glue, thinners and oil-based paints, while chlorinated solvents
appears to only have been used for cleaning purposes (NIRAS, 2018a). Chlorinated
solvents identified at the site includes TCE, cis-DCE, 1,1,1-TCA, 1,1-DCA and VC.
However, trichloroetylene (TCE) is the dominant contaminant and is therefore the main
focus of the investigations and mass estimation.
Several areas of the site are or have been potential sources of contamination. Outdoor
storage of chemicals and production waste is a substantial source for contamination.
The storage areas have generally been located on unprotected soil and chemicals have

at the northern property line (Nordskel) (Fig. 2.2). The spatial distribution of TCE
indicates contamination by numerous smaller spills as opposed to one or few greater spills.
Furthermore, the three hotspots are considered as one coherent source area (NIRAS,
2018a).

CHAPTER 3
Geostatistics
3.1
Geostatistical approach
Geostatistics is a way to statistically describe spatially distributed variables in 2D and/or
3D, such as contamination in the subsurface. By applying geostatistical calculations and
modeling to a contaminated site, it is possible to predict the value of the given variable
at places where no measurements have been made and additionally quantify both mass
and spatial distribution of the variable. Furthermore, geostatistics assign estimates of
errors to the calculations and predictions, applying a level of confidence to the estimates
(Webster and Oliver, 2007).
Geostatistics encompasses a wide range of statistical tools. Three basic components
of geostatistics will be presented in order to give a general overview of geostatistics
utilized in this study (Bohling, 2005):
• The variogram: Characterization of spatial continuity and correlation.
• Kriging: Interpolation between measured data points by employing the variogram
to generate estimates at unmeasured locations.
• Stochastic simulation: Generation of multiple equiprobable images of the variable
by employing the properties of the applied data set including the variogram.
3.2

continuity within the data set and thus describes the tendency that two adjacent points
have similar values contrary to two samples located further apart (Armstrong, 1998;
Isaaks and Srivastava, 1989).

Plotting the variance against the lag, enables for visualizing the calculated dissimi-

lag and averaged to one point of the experimental variogram plotted against the lag.
The variance for the next multiples of the lag is calculated and plotted, correspondingly.

the given lags.

The variogram is fitted by three components: the sill, range and nugget (Fig. 3.4).
The sill is the limiting value of the variance and when the sill is reached, the variogram
plateaus indicating that the correlation between samples is no longer present (Armstrong,
1998). The distance at which the variogram reaches the sill value is called the range.
The range indicates the maximal distance at which the pairs in the data set are spatially
correlated, beyond this point, an increase in lag will no longer cause a corresponding
increase in the variogram (Isaaks and Srivastava, 1989). The range can vary for different
directions reflecting anisotropy of the data e.g. horizontal and vertical directions. When
fitting the variogram, the behaviour near the origin at smaller lag distances is especially
important, as this is related to the continuity of the variable (Isaaks and Srivastava,
1989). A discontinuity observed at the origin (interception at γ(h) > 0) is called nugget
and indicates erratic short scale behaviour. The nugget effect can also be caused by
factors such as micro-structures, measurement errors or errors in location (Armstrong,
1998).

3.4
Stochastic simulation
In order to obtain the uncertainty associated with the kriging estimates, the variogram
model is simulated using the Monte Carlo method. The Monte Carlo algorithm generates
a number of realizations with equal probability while complying with the geostatistical
characteristics of the available data and variogram model (Abzalov, 2016). Fluctuations
and dissimilarities between each simulated realization is used to quantify the uncertainty
of the variogram model, thus applying a level of confidence to the estimate.

4.2
Zone of interest
Defining the zone of interest for geostatistical modelling entails covering as many data
points and boreholes as allowed. However, if the defined zone covers large areas where no
sampling has been conducted with relative great distance to the nearest boreholes, a level
of bias is introduced. These unsampled areas can for each Monte Carlo realization still
comply with the fitted variogram and thus create a large contaminated area without any
basis for it or any sampling points to support the prediction of contamination. Initially,
the whole area of Industrivej 2 (Fig. 4.1) was modelled, resulting in an unsupported mass
estimate of 30+ kg TCE at the site. The unsampled areas need more information to
conduct relevant geostatistical modelling, and thus the area was considered unreliable
and the zone was narrowed. The narrowed zone is illustrated in Figure 4.1. It was
outlined to cover the most sampling dense area, as a greater number of data points
supporting the geostatistical modelling result in a more confident mass estimate. The
limit and extent of the zone corresponded to the zone defined by NIRAS (Fig. 2.2).

4.3.2
PID
4.3.2.1
Functionality
A photoionization detector (PID) is an instrument used to investigate the presence of
volatile organic compounds in pore-air. PID measurements for soil samples are con-
ducted by collecting soil into an diffusion tight RILSAN® bag. A sampling needle is
inserted into the headspace of the bag and the air is drawn into the PID instrument,
where an UV lamp act as a high-energy photon source. When passing the lamp, the
organic vapors are photo-ionized and the ejected electrons are detected as a current
and the gas concentrations are calculated based on standard calibration gas (RAE Sys-
tems, 2013). PID is a non-specific screening method, which means that both chlorinated
solvents, petrol and oil components can affect the readings of the PID instrument.
4.3.2.2
Evaluation of correlation
A preliminary evaluation of the correlation and comparability between TCE concentra-
tions of accredited soil sample analysis and associated PID measurements was carried
out prior to modelling in Kartotrak. This evalution was conducted in order to assess
whether or not the PID measurements were suitable as semi-quantitative data for TCE
at the site. Each measurement was sorted into categories of data measured in clay and
sand bodies as well as saturated and unsaturated zones according to available data such
as transects, lithological descriptions, campaigns and data from groundwater sampling.
The following will summarize the outcome of the evaluation, for full evaluation see
Appendix C.1. The clay matrix and the embedded sand bodies showed a similar linear
trend between TCE concentrations and PID measurements for the NIRAS data, thus
enabling the use of the PID measurements from different lithologies within the clayey till
without correction. However, data from NIRAS and Geo displayed two distinct trends
(Fig. 4.2). The observed difference in trend can be caused by different instruments or
calibration standards, which is also indicated by the difference in detection limits (DL).
The DL in NIRAS is not directly reported, however the lowest values measured are 0.1
ppm, whereas the DL is given as < 5 ppm by Geo. NIRAS uses a MiniRAE2000 instru-
ment with a 10.6 eV lamp and 100 ppm isobutylene as calibration gas to measure PID
on site, whilst Geo sends the RILSAN® bags to the Eurofins lab where PID is measured
on a Phocheck1000 instrument, though with the same type of lamp and calibration gas.

ments, DMR uses a MiniRae3000 instrument similar to the one used by NIRAS. The

4.3.3
MIP
4.3.3.1
Functionality
The MiHpt probe is a combined Membrane Interface Probe (MIP) and Hydraulic Pro-
filing Tool (HPT) probe, which is used for vertical profiling. The probe conducts con-
tinuous simultaneous measurements through the subsurface and thereby examine the
contamination as well as geologic and hydrogeological settings. This study will utilize
the MIP measurements and the HPT data will be disregarded. MIP data is generally
more sensitive to chlorinated compounds than PID.
While the probe is pushed down into the subsurface, the surrounding soil is heated
to 120◦C and the present contaminants will diffuse through the membrane of the probe
and carried on an inert gas to the surface where it is detected (NIRAS, 2018b).
As a result of the continuous measuring down through the subsurface, the MIP data
is of remarkable high resolution with 65-70 data points per meter giving approximately
1000 data points per MIP probing.
4.3.3.2
Evaluation of correlation
In order to conduct bivariate modelling, Kartotrak requires the primary variable (TCE)
and the secondary/additional variable (MIP) to share common data locations to some
extent. Thus, the MIP data was subjected to a preliminary evaluation and processing
in order to coincide with measurements of TCE in soil.
Contrary to PID measurements, which are sampled alongside specific soil samples (of
which some are chemically analyzed), MIP measurements are generally collected with
much higher resolution and not simultaneously or at exact same coordinates as the soil
sample.
A total amount of 18 MIP probings containing 15,857 MIP measurements in total
were carried out at the site.
Considering that the soil samples were collected with much lower resolution, the
MIP data was processed by averaging the MIP data overlapped by a soil sample. This
was done for all MIP data with a distance of less than 2 meter to the nearest soil
sample. The results from the evaluation are reported in Appendix D. Based on the
evaluation, 7 out of 8 MIP probings were selected for the cross-correlation in Kartotrak.
The MIP data of the 8 probings which did not overlap with soil sample analyses and the
remaining 11 MIP probings were averaged over every 4 cm to represent a sample length
and resolution more similar to that of the soil samples. To summarize; 7 processed
MIP probings coinciding with soil samples will be the basis for cross-correlation and
variogram analysis in Kartotrak, whereas the remaining averaged MIP probings will be
included when simulating the model.
A total amount of 18 MIP probings totalling 5,213 MIP measurements were uploaded
to Kartotrak.

4.4
Data regularization
Spatial analysis should be applied to data sets with constant sample volume/length.
However, data sets containing heterogeneous sampling lengths occurs frequently in prac-
tice. At Industrivej 2, sample lengths are at approx. 1 cm (soil samples in borehole F04),
4 cm (averaged MIP-data, core samples and corresponding PID) and 10 cm (regular soil
samples and corresponding PID).
Kartotrak allows the user to apply regularization, which is the most common method
to get samples of equal size from an irregular sampling. The regularization algorithm
is simply a weighted average of the original property, the weights being the length of
the analyzed samples that fall inside the new regularized samples (Geovariances, 2018).
However, regularization can affect the real data values, despite having the same sample
length as the regularized length (Kasmaeeyazdi et al., 2018). Thus, applying additional
data (PID and MIP) to the initial data set changes the geometries of the boreholes and
thus have an impact on the experimental variograms.
A regularization of 4 cm was chosen due to the majority of the soil samples having this
length. The effect of different regularization lengths are addressed further in Appendix
E and discussed later on.

4.5.3
Variogram fitting
When the data is normal score transformed and the variogram cloud has been inspected,
the experimental variogram is ready to be defined and subsequently fitted. The two
variograms, horizontal and vertical, are defined by determining a lag for both individually.
The lag should be selected, when the experimental variogram shows a relatively smooth
curvature rather than an oscillating curve, which is typically seen in the smaller lags.
Furthermore a maximum distance is applied. A value large enough to show when the
experimental variogram starts to deviate from trend of the variogram is selected. For
the horizontal variogram a slicing height is selected, this parameter is defined in order
to not take into account pairs of points within the same borehole (Geovariances, 2018).
The model is fitted by choosing the appropriate mathematical models, ranges and
sills. All the experimental variograms in Kartotrak must be fitted using the same com-
bination of basic structures, thus the selected model and sill is consistent, however the
user is allowed to select individual ranges for the horizontal and vertical direction.
When working with variograms of one variable, the variogram is referred to as a
”simple” variogram (Fig.
4.4(a-d)), whereas working with multiple variables in the
bi- or multivariate approach, the variogram of multiple variables is referred to as a
”cross-variogram” (Fig. 4.4(e-f)). When fitting the variograms in the bi- or multivari-
ate approach, it is worth noticing that the same variogram defining parameters (lag,
max. distance) and basic parameters of the variogram model are applied to each simple
variogram along with the cross-variogram simultaneously.
Due to each variogram being fitted simultaneously, the simple variogram containing
the quantitative data was prioritized in this study.
The cross-variogram have a separate fitting parameter called the correlation coef-
ficient. It reflects the granted correlation between the two considered variables at a
given distance (Geovariances, 2018). The dotted lines on the cross-variogram show the
envelope of maximal correlation allowed from the simple variograms.

4.6.1
Determining model parameters
Kartotrak is a numerical model, which simulates equally possible spatial distributions
of the contaminant TCE by employing the spatial structure defined by the variogram
model. Numerical models and notably the simulation time are sensitive to the different
model parameters such as the enabled search criteria of number of neighbors, maximum
horizontal and vertical distance, in addition to and especially the resolution defined by
the grid and cell size.
The smaller grid size, the higher resolution of the modelled output is obtained. How-
ever, working with a high resolution leads to long simulation time, and it is consequently
important to balance the model parameters to obtain an appropriate resolution with a
reasonable simulation time. Applying secondary variables for bivariate or multivariate
modelling entail an further increment in simulation time, as this will include a greater
number of available data points.
The simulation time is also dependent of the PC used for the geostatistical modelling
as a PC capable for complex 3D computation is required. In this study, the geostatistical
modelling was conducted on a PC with Windows 10 Enterprise, an Intel® Core™i5
processor, 16 GB RAM and a 64-bit operating system.
Kartotrak allows presimulations of the defined parameters which gives an insight
in both the resolution and simulation time to be expected before running the final
simulation.
In order to compare the results from the different simulation approaches, the model
parameters were the same for all simulation. The model parameters were determined
based on the presimulations of the multivariate approach as it comprised the largest
total number of data points, and thereby the longest expected simulation time.

m and a vertical distance of 7 m.
These distances were chosen on the basis of the
variogram analysis, the values tend to stretch the capabilities of the variograms slightly,
but were considered the best model parameters for the overall variogram fit in order to
simultaneously simulate as much of the defined model space in the clayey till (21,284 m3)
as possible. A consequence of a maximum horizontal distance of 25 m, is that the line of
boreholes at the southwestern property line (Fig. 4.1) does not influence the estimates in
the zone of interest. The data does get included in the variogram to analyze the spatial
variation at the site. No TCE has been detected in any of the analyzed soil samples
and all PID measurement are below zero for these boreholes. Zero-values are considered
important as they too increase the area of certainty, thus it is unfortunate that they are
located out of reach for the zone of interest.

thresholds of 0.01, 0.50 and 1.00 mg/kg(dm). Four maps were chosen as outputs; kriging,

4.7
Geostatistical contaminant volume &
mass estimation
Based on the simulation of the 100 Monte Carlo realizations, Kartotrak calculates four
types of volume and mass estimates:
• In situ estimate of contaminated soil volume
• In situ mass estimate of TCE
• Remediation estimates of contaminated volume
• Remediation mass estimate of TCE
4.7.1
In situ estimates
The in situ estimates of contaminant mass and soil volume are the global estimates
which simultaneously integrate the uncertainty of the calculated quantity.
The in situ volume is computed by calculating the total amount of grid nodes where
the simulated value exceeds a specified threshold for every simulated grid and ultimately
the total volume for that specific realization. The frequency at which a certain volume
occurs for the total amount of realizations are plotted in a risk curve (Fig. 4.7). As
an example, consider Figure 4.7, the risk curve indicates that in approx. 65% of the
realizations, a volume of 12 m3 exceeded the defined threshold, yielding a 65% risk of
there being 12 m3 of contaminated soil.
Figure 4.7: The frequency of computed volumes for each realization is expressed by
a risk curve. The risk curve indicates the risk level (or inverse; the confi-
dence) of different computed volumes From Geovariances (2018): Fig. 23.1-1.
==PAGE:55==
4.7 Geostatistical contaminant volume & mass estimation
37
The in situ mass is computed by calculating the simulated values which are greater
or equal to the threshold multiplied by the volume, by summing these results the total
mass is obtained. The mass estimates are expressed in a risk curve as well. The mass
risk curve represent the potential mass present in the model domain. As an example,
if considering the 50th percentile mass is 5 kg TCE of a given concentration threshold,
then there will be 50% probability of retrieving 5 kg TCE if the whole zone volume is
excavated.
The in situ estimates disregard the specific location of each grid node when com-
puting volume and mass, hence neglecting to compute whether or not the specific grid
nodes show a reoccurring pattern of exceeding the specified threshold. Thus, the in situ
estimates cannot be visualized.
4.7.2
Remediation estimates
The remediation estimates of contaminant mass and soil volume represents the quantity
(volume or mass) exceeding the specified threshold with a certain probability. Thus the
uncertainty is integrated in both quantity and location thereof, in order to avoid leaving
behind soil with an unacceptable risk of being contaminated.
Kartotrak calculates the remediation volume by computing the number of time the
simulated value is greater than the defined threshold for each block of the grid. The
value is then normalized to the total number of realizations, this gives a probability for
the block to exceed the defined threshold. Figure 4.8 illustrates the computation process.
Two grid nodes are highlighted (×), in 95 realizations of the total 100, the upper grid
node exceeded the defined threshold, whereas the lower grid node only exceeded in 29
realizations. This is then used to compute the probability map. The probability map
in the example (Fig. 4.8) indicate that in the red area, the risk of contamination by
a grade higher than the threshold is between 75-100%. In the orange area the risk of
contamination by a grade higher than the threshold is between 50-75% and so forth.
The remediation risk curve represents the stepwise probability calculation for the
total amount of grid nodes. The risk curve in Figure 4.8 indicate e.g. that by remove
21 m3, there is a 25% risk of leaving behind soil contaminated by a grade higher than
the threshold.
The remediation mass is computed by multiplying the calculated remediation vol-
umes with the kriging values. The kriging values are the 50th percentile concentration
for each grid node, hence the statistically most likely, however not necessarily the correct
value, thereby introducing a level of bias. Thus, this quantity will not be reported in
this study.

5.2
Remediation volumes
A focal point, when working at contaminated sites, is not simply to estimate global mass
and volume, but entails estimating the volume of soil to be remediated. The global (in
situ) volume is calculated based on the summarized amount of grid nodes exceeding the
given threshold of each equally probable Monte Carlo realizations. Thus, the calculation
of global voilume and mass does not take into account the specific location of each grid
nodes in the model space. Consequently, remediating the site will most likely require
a larger volume in order to assure removing unacceptable levels of contamination with
a given degree of certainty. This quantity is calculated as the remediation volume by
Kartotrak. The remediation volume indicates the volume of soil to be remediated to
ensure removing potentially contaminated soil with a certain confidence.

5.2.2
Volume analysis - obtainable conf. intervals
There are two general aspects of remediation; high effort to remove all contamination
(exceeding an appropriately determined threshold) while keeping expenses low. High
confidence in the estimated volumes is, as discussed, associated with great remediation
volumes or more sampling in previously unsampled volumes and thus also associated with
high costs. It is therefore important to find a balance of appropriate risk at an acceptable
financial input. Hence, you would want to find an acceptable level of confidence, while
diminishing the risk of leaving contaminated soil behind as well as spending money on
remediating uncontaminated soil.
Remediating the soil with a confidence level of 70-90% entails remediation of a soil
volume much larger than actually estimated to be contaminated considering the global
estimates, whereas remediating at a 50-60% confidence level might not be sufficient
according to the global estimates. By comparing and evaluating the remediation volume
based on the global estimates, it is possible to identify the obtainable confidence levels
allowed by each approach.
The 50th percentile of the global volume reflects the statistically most likely con-
taminated volume, however the 90% confidence interval reflects a best and worst case
scenario of a contaminated soil volume at the site.
By remediating a volume corresponding to the lower global volume of the 90% con-
fidence interval, you would risk a potential false negative error of improperly reporting
lower contamination, than actually there, thus potentially leaving behind contamination.
For the higher and more conservative global volume of the confidence interval you would
risk a potential false positive error of incorrectly reporting a higher degree of contami-
nation, when in reality it might be at a lower degree, thus potentially remediating clean
uncontaminated soil.
Taking the monovariate approach with a threshold of 0.01 mg/kg(dm) as an example,
the conservative global volume of the 90% confidence interval is 15,351 m3 (Table 5.1)
which means remediating the soil at a 90% confidence level (21,220 m3 (Table 5.2)) will
result in remediating approx. 5,900 m3 excessive and potentially clean soil (false positive
error). Whereas, at a 50% confidence level (approx. 13,513 m3 (Table 5.2)) there is a
risk of leaving behind approx. 1,800 m3 of potentially contaminated soil (false negative
error).
By conducting a volume analysis for both ends of the 90% confidence interval, you are
able to find the obtainable confidence levels for remediating the contaminated volumes

5.3.2
Variance indicator
5.3.3
Relative certainty and uncertainty
The visualized contaminated volume (at a given threshold) is considered to be comprised
by two sections; a relatively certain volume close to measured high values and a relatively
uncertain volume further away from measured values. The only true certainty of present
TCE is provided by the soil samples in the exact location of where the samples were
measured. The uncertainty increases with increasing distance between the point estimate
and sample locations. Thus, this implies that for the previously visualized volumes (e.g.
Fig. 5.5), a higher confidence level covers a larger uncertain volume.
The ”Variance Indicator” (VI) is a feature in Kartotrak, which allows you to split
the volume into the relatively certain and relatively uncertain sections based on the
variogram (Geovariances, 2018).
The closer a given point is to an actual sampling
point, the less uncertainty and hence smaller variance is obtained for the estimate of
that given point. The VI is another and more practical way of visualizing the realistic
volumes. The VI is defined between 0 and 1, where at a given location, the closer the
variance indicator is to 0 the more certain the estimation is. Selecting a proper value
for the VI is arbitrary, however with a focus on choosing a low value to indicate high
certainty. A VI < 0.20, hence relatively high certainty, was chosen for this study and
the corresponding VI-volumes are seen in Figure 5.8 and 5.9. The VI-volumes indicate
the volume within the zone volume which are considered as relatively certain and are
independent of the estimated concentrations, mass and contaminated volume within.

only coincide with the two measures for the few points processed in order to establish
a correlation as required by Kartotrak. Hence, the MIP data apply further information
without significantly overruling/impacting the variance of the soil samples.

6.1

6.1.1
Contaminant mass and volume estimation
The use of geostatistical modelling proved to be a successful tool for quantifying contam-
ination with the estimates from all approaches overall showing astonishing resemblance
to the mass estimate and mass interval estimated by NIRAS. Even for the monovariate
approach a resembling estimate is achieved, which contrary to the NIRAS estimate solely
bases the estimates on soil samples, thus do not include fugacity, ratio calculations or
other applicable measures.
The estimated total contaminant mass for the monovariate approach was 12.1 kg with
a 90% confidence interval of 8.0-19.7 kg based on simulated concentrations above 0.01
mg/kg(dm). The estimate is roughly double the mass estimate calculated by NIRAS
at 6.5 kg (within the clayey till, total 9.1 kg for clayey till and the deep sandy aquifer).
However, the mass estimate and corresponding 90% confidence interval are validated
as they show a good compliance with the mass interval of 7-16 kg from the sensitivity
analysis conducted by NIRAS. The estimated contaminated volume was 12,723 m3 with
a 90% confidence interval of 10,065-15,351 m3.
The contaminant mass and volume estimated by the bivariate and multivariate ap-
proaches did not evidently improve the final estimate. The largest difference compared
to the monovariate approach was obtained by using the multivariate approach where
the contaminant mass estimate was 9.5 kg (6.8-14.0 kg) and the contaminated volume
9,374 m3 (7,815-11,054 m3). Based on the based on the number of applied data, the
estimate obtained by the multivariate approach could be considered the better estimate
from the four approaches. However, the bi- and multivariate approach all showed a
reduced relatively certain volume, thus the monovariate approach would be considered
to produce the most reliable estimates, this is addressed further in Section 6.1.2.2.
The fact that by geostatistic modelling it was possible to reproduce resembling mass
estimates of manual calculations conducted by NIRAS, signifies the benefits of using the
geostatistical method for this purpose.

6 Discussion
6.1.2
Application of semi-quantitative data
Extensive literature, both books and articles, are available for geostatistical modeling
of environmental issues such as soil contamination, including bivariate and multivariate
modelling (Largueche and Cheniti, 2016; Einax and Soldt, 1998b,a; D’Or et al., 2009;
Donati and de Fouquet, 2001; Carlon et al., 2001; Henshaw et al., 2004; Webster and
Oliver, 2007). However, the implemented data is solely quantitative. I have not been able
to find literature regarding the application of screening measures and semi-quantitative
data of PID and MIP to the geostatistical modeling. Both measures are in practice
utilized as qualitative screening tools for different purposes, in particular locating and
delineating contaminant zones and screening in order to select samples for chemical
analysis.
This study examined the use of these semi-quantitative data for geostatistical model-
ing, to assess if these qualitative and semi-quantitative data could be of effective use for
quantitative purposes, such as quantifying contaminant mass and volume. The results
in the previous chapter illustrated that the data indeed was applicable for the quantifica-
tion of contaminant mass and volume, by yielding mass estimates resembling the mass
estimate and mass interval determined by NIRAS at the site.
6.1.2.1
Correlation
The correlation between PID and TCE in soil samples is expected and inevitable as the
PID is measured from the same point of which the soil is sampled for chemical analysis.
The linear relationship between PID and soil TCE yielded a correlation coefficient (ρ) of
0.89, however the cross-variogram only allowed a correlation coefficient of 0.60 in order
to not overestimate the correlation.
Contrarily, a similar direct correlation between MIP and soil TCE is not achievable,
since it is not possible to take soil samples simultaneous with the MIP probings. Thus,
the soil samples are sampled as closely to the MIP probings as possible or physically
allowed. The consequence hereof is that the correlation will never be totally unbiased,
especially at sites with highly heterogeneous soils as clayey tills. This was evident from
the correlation coefficient which was 0.50, which was also a fit for the cross-variogram.
6.1.2.2
Practicality of model approach
The multivariate approach could be considered to yield the best estimate at the site, due
to being calculated based the largest data set. However, the result only differ slightly
from the results obtained by the other approaches. It is therefore relevant to discuss
whether applying PID and/or MIP for a bivariate or multivariate approach instead of
the more simple monovariate approach is worthwhile.
Applying an additional measure to include in the co-kriging of the geostatistical mod-
eling, and especially an amount as great as the available MIP measurements, leads to
an increase in simulation time. Both the bivariate approach using MIP and particularly
==PAGE:81==
6.1 Geostatistics for contaminant characterization
63
the multivariate approach required over-night simulations, whereas the monovariate ap-
proach and bivariate approach using PID had a simulation time of approximately an
hour at most.
Additionally, coinciding sample location for soil samples and PID, subsequently
makes it easier for preparation of input files for Kartotrak which are quickly prepared.
Whereas a more time-consuming preliminary data processing was performed on the MIP
data. A total of 15,857 data points of MIP was available at the site, which was first
averaged over the length of soil samples with a distance of <2 m at the same depth
and second for MIP with a distance >2m to the nearest borehole, the data points were
averaged over 4 cm to resemble the general length of the soil samples, ultimately result-
ing in a total amount of 5,213 MIP measurements applied to the model. Hence, the
data-processing of the MIP require further time, and the averaging additionally leads to
a smoothing of the MIP data, potentially introducing a level of bias. The soil samples
and PID are more or less uploaded to Kartotrak as raw data, and only require data
processing in the form of excluding PID measurements in points where oil compounds
have been identified and corrections if needed.
The application of PID and MIP had an effect on the remediation volumes, especially
at the lower threshold but less significant at the higher threshold, which is considered to
be a more realistic remediation criterion. Furthermore, the Variance Indicator featured
a larger relatively certain volume for the monovariate approach compared to any of the
other approaches. Hence, the application of PID and MIP did not improve the certainty
of the TCE estimates as expected in this study.
However, it would be relevant to
reevaluate the relatively certain volume obtained using identical variogram parameters
if possible, to see the direct effect of the application of semi-quantitative data unaffected
by different variograms.
Focusing on the relatively insignificant difference in mass estimates and remediation
volumes at the higher threshold as well as the time consumption and processing/ma-
nipulation of data in the specific case at Industrivej 2, it is more feasible to use the
monovariate approach. Overall, this means that the work behind the application of PID
and MIP is not compensated by what is gained by the application. The independent
value of the semi-quantitative data will be addressed in Section 6.2.3.1.
6.1.2.3
Complex contamination
The contaminant source at Industrivej 2 is moderately simple, since sample analyses have
shown TCE to be the predominant contaminant at the site, with few other compounds
like hydrocarbons and other chlorinated solvents e.g. higher concentrations of cis-1,2-
Dichloroethylene measured at trace levels.
Since both PID and MIP are non-specific detectors for delineating source areas of
contaminant it is questionable if it would be feasible to apply the data to geostatistical
bi- or multivariate approaches at sites with a more complex contamination of different
contaminant compounds.

6 Discussion
6.1.3
Recommendation for types of data
As previously mentioned, the only true certainty of TCE present at a contaminated site
is provided by the soil samples in the exact location of where the samples were measured.
Thus, the ideal sample strategy would consist of a detailed 3D grid containing contami-
nant concentrations from chemical analyses of soil samples. However, for Industrivej 2
and many other investigated contaminates sites, the soil samples are analyzed in order
to get adequate knowledge of the concentration levels and extent of contaminants at the
sites. The samples are often only sent to laboratories if screening measures such as PID
or MIP indicate presence of relatively high levels of contaminant. This is typically done
due the the price differences between PID measurements and chemical analysis of soil
samples.
However, the prices for chemical analysis of soil samples have in the recent years
reduced. The prices for the most common chemical analyses of soil samples (such as
chlorinated solvents, hydrocarbons, etc.) is between 75-100 kr. DKK, whereas PID is
between 5-30 kr. DKK (Per Loll, DMR A/S, 2019). From my experience working with
geostatistical modeling, a quantitative measure of the primary variable is worth more
than the semi-quantitative measure of any secondary variable. The purpose of increasing
the certainty of the estimates computed using geostatistics, justifies sending more soil
samples for chemical analyses, if the necessary boreholes are being conducted.
Bivariate or multivariate approaches in cases with a high horizontal density of PID
(predominantly without corresponding soil samples analyses) could potentially result in a
stronger prediction of mass and contaminated volume, however the strongest prediction
will always be based on the chemical analyses of soil samples. At Industrivej 2, 731
PID data points have been measured in 27 boreholes, however only 137 soil samples
were sent to the laboratory for analysis based on the PID screenings.
Focusing on
PID measurements within and in near vicinity of the defined zone, 217 samples could
have been sent to chemical analysis. Furthermore, since expenses have already been
made for man-hours and drilling equipment for the sampling campaign, it simply just
further justifies the relevance and reason for sending the samples to the lab to get
more substantial and quantitative data. Hence, it is recommended for future sampling
campaigns at any investigation site to send as many soil samples as possible for chemical
analysis.
However, if the number of boreholes are still sparse at the investigation site, but with
available MIP data, then it is recommended to conduct bivariate or multivariate geo-
statistical modelling provided that a certain number of MIP are closely located (within
approximately 0.5 m) to boreholes with analyzed soil samples. Between 5-10 MIP prob-
ings closely located to different boreholes are, based on this study, adequate in order to
establish correlation (if there is one) in Kartotrak. If soil samples are sampled in the
full depth of the borehole, then a bivariate approach of soil samples and MIP should be
sufficient, hence excluding PID to reduce simulation time.

6.2.1
Visualization of contaminated volume
The graphical visualization of the contaminant mass and volume estimates, can be diffi-
cult to comprehend. Intuitively, you are interested in visualizing, and thereby locating,
the exact extent of the contaminated volume containing the estimated mass. However,
the statistical aspect of the estimation makes this unattainable. Instead it the possible
to visualize a remediation volume by a level of probability or confidence, that the vi-
sualized volume contains the estimated contamination. The visualized volumes can be
quantified, however mass contained within this volume is not quantified.
The Variance Indicator filter can be applied to indicate the certain and uncertain
parts of the visualized volume.
This feature can be utilized for difference purposes,
one being locating the certain areas for a focused remediation campaign, another being
targeting areas where additional samples can be applied to increase the relative cer-
tainty of the estimate. The volume of the certain or uncertain volume is not calculated
automatically, but require manual calculations.
Overall, the thoroughness of the visual characterization of contamination and de-
pends on the desired level of confidence of the remediation and thereby what risk level
can be accepted. Obviously, a generally high confidence level is desired, thus either a
great volume is to be remediated or additional samples in uncertain areas needs to be
acquired.
6.2.2
Reproducibility
The robustness of the global estimates was analyzed by comparing the estimates resulting
from separate variogram fittings conducted by different individuals (Appendix H).
The global estimates were considered to be robust and hence reproducible, as the
sample standard deviation of the resulting mass estimates were approx 0.45 kg and 1.20
kg for the monovariate approach and bivariate approach using MIP, respectively. The
sample standard deviation for the estimated contaminated soil volume was more scat-
tered however with a general value of approx. 230 m3. This sample standard deviation
is considered acceptable as the global estimates of contaminated soil volume is in the
1,000s and 10,000s.
Despite the test size only consisting of variogram fits conducted by three individuals,
the analysis reflects an important aspect of using geostatistical software for contami-
nant estimation, as two considerably large data sets relatively quickly were fitted and
simulated, which ultimately resulted in relatively identical global estimates.

6 Discussion
6.2.3
Sensitive settings
6.2.3.1
Delineation and location of zone of interest
Despite the global estimates are considered reproducible, they can be sensitive to certain
settings and features, when using geostatistics. The delineation of the zone of interest
was found to affects the estimates. In this study, the geostatistical model was initially
applied to the entire clayey till formation beneath the property, however this resulted
in an estimated mass of 30+ kg based primarily on a large unsampled and therefore
uncertain area. The zone was therefore narrowed to cover a more densely sampled area
(focusing primarily on available soil sample analyses) of the property.
Changing the zone of interest while using exactly the same input data would result
in differences in the estimates. However, the differences would be expected to be within
the same order of magnitude, especially if the zone of interest is covering the densely
sampled area of the site. Thus, the resulting estimates are relatively sensitive to the
deterministic aspect of delineating the zone of interest based on available soil samples
analyses. The qualitative value of the MIP for delineation purposes prior to applying
geostatistics, is beneficial and important, as the narrowed zone was based on the source
zone outlined by NIRAS using MIP in particular.
Locating the zone of interest in an area without any or only limited samples will still
produce an estimate, provided that available data points outside the zone, are within
the horizontal reach defined by the model. The estimates resulting from an interest zone
of such are highly uncertain and unreliable.
6.2.3.2
Data regularization
Another feature that can affect the estimates are data regularization. The data reg-
ularization is applied to data sets containing samples of irregular lengths, to obtain
homogeneous sample lengths.
A sensitivity analysis was performed on the different approaches and data sets (Ap-
pendix E). The analysis compared results from two scenarios. Firstly, estimates obtained
from a monovariate approach of two different data sets with the same soil sample anal-
yses in both, were analyzed. Secondly, estimates obtained by the same geostatistical
appraoch and data set using three different regularization lengths were analyzed.
In the first analysis it was discovered that the regularization step applies to all
the input samples and not just those corresponding to the variable estimated by the
monovariate approach.
This means that by applying the semi-quantitative data at
locations without the primary variable (both horizontally and vertically), the geometry
and number of regularized samples is altered, consequently altering the experimental
variograms and thereby the obtained estimates. This ultimately means that you will not
be able to reproduce the result from any approach, if additional data is applied. Thus,
it is recommended that the input data only contain the variables which are utilized for
the modelling purpose. It would then avoid affecting the regularization.
==PAGE:85==
6.2 Performance of the geostatistical method used in Kartotrak
67
The second analysis showed an effect on the different regularization length. Regu-
larizing the samples from long to short sample was shown in pre-simulations to result
in a model dominated by numerical artifacts as well as increasing the simulation time
tremendously in addition to leading to reduced short-scale variability. Regularizing the
samples from short to long caused loss of data.
In order to avoid altering the raw data excessively, it is recommended to use the most
frequent sample length of the data set. However, in the case where semi-quantitative
data with a high discretization such as the raw MIP data, the most frequent sample
length would be the MIP sample length which in this case is approx. 1.5 cm. Thus,
significantly affecting the lengths of all available soil sample analyses and thereby also
affecting the estimates of the primary variable of the soil sample analyses. Thus, the
regularized length should be based on the most frequent sample length of the primary
variable, in this case TCE.
The data regularization ultimately means that the potential geostatistical value of
the highly discretized MIP data is less than expected. The required data regularization
should also be considered in the sampling strategy. Samples taken with relatively wider
distribution both horizontally and vertically will help support a generally larger area,
than for example a few core samples with a high vertical level of detail.
6.2.4
Geological heterogeneity
Kartotrak only allows a simple geology of the subsurface. Firstly, the geological layers are
required to be consecutive, hence the order of geological boundaries between formations
of different lithology must be consistent throughout the domain.
This prevents the
modeling of a 3D image of the heterogeneities identified in the clayey till matrix. Thus,
only allowing a generalized conceptual model of the subsurface.
Secondly, the algorithm of Kartotrak always consider a the model domain as an
isotropic media. Isotropy entails that the spatial variability is identical in all directions.
However, at a site of varying geology with fractures and sand bodies potentially creating
preferential pathways for the contaminant migration, the spatial variability is expected to
be more complex and anisotropic. For the manual mass calculations, NIRAS attempted
to compensate for how the different lithologies would affect the concentration levels, by
introducing assumed lithological fractions as well as specifying from which lithology the
utilized samples originated. These were static fractions, however an eligible effort to
account for different lithologies.
If it would have been possible to take the heterogeneity of the clayey till into account
it might improve the geostatistical estimate.

6 Discussion
6.3
Kartotrak evaluation
6.3.1
Experience
Kartotrak can be very difficult to get acquainted with for the non-geostatistician. Geo-
variances provides a guide for the user, which is a general run-through of the different
features of the software and of the geostatistics behind these features and includes tuto-
rials of both 2D and 3D examples. However, the guide does not provide any instructions
of which considerations are behind choosing the different parameters for a proper var-
iogram fitting or model simulations nor how they generally affect the simulation and
outcome.
Geovariances offers fundamental and advanced training programs, which seemingly
provide a more comprehensive and necessary understanding. I was unfortunately not
able to attend these training programs and in my opinion it is questionable if a 2 days
training program is sufficient to get fully acquainted with the software. I instead got
acquainted with the software by a trial-and-error approach repeating numerous and
various varigram fittings and simulation attempts, with the help from Per Loll, DMR
and email correspondence with the support team at Geovariances.
This approach is undoubtedly more time-consuming, than an intense training pro-
gram with the developers of the software. However, Kartotrak have a relatively simple
and user-friendly interface, which means that after a number of trials you get more and
more acquainted with the features and options. From there it is only the parameters
such as data regularization, fitting and model parameters, visualization etc. that require
refinement and time. Thus, it is initially easy to produce results and estimates using the
software, however an in-depth understanding of the results and critical approach to the
different parameters require much greater effort.
6.3.2
Application of data and model process
Data uploaded to Kartotrak generally demand very simple pre-processing. The uploaded
input file, e.g. an Excel or CSV file, is required to contain two input sheets; samples
and tops, and an optional third; contacts.
The ”samples” input requires columns containing the names of the different boreholes
or probings, the measures of interest (e.g. soil samples, PID and MIP) and the depth
of the sample expressed as two values of ”depth from” and ”depth to” and optionally a
column containing a lithological description of the sample. Often samples are given as
one point in depth, and hence require processing, e.g. for a normal soil sample, 5 cm is
subtracted from the specific depth to give ”depth from” and added to give ”depth to”,
thus covering 10 cm, corresponding to the actual samples taken in reality. This can be
modified for core samples, MIP and so on. The ”tops” input simply contains the x-,
y- and z- coordinates of the different boreholes and probings. The optional ”contacts”
input contains information of the depth of geological boundaries in the subsurface, which
enables surface interpolation of the different geological layers.
==PAGE:87==
6.3 Kartotrak evaluation
69
Data is easily uploaded to Kartotrak and ready to be modelled. When the user have
become acquainted with the software, fitting the variograms is possible within minutes
and the final time spent on the model depend on the desired model resolution and
simulation time. This is a key and prime element of working with Kartotrak, oppose to
calculating mass estimates using the common approach as NIRAS does.
Furthermore, if additional data is sampled, it can cause increased and supplementary
time for updating the mass estimate obtained by the common approach, as many of
the underlying calculations needs manual recalculations, whereas Kartotrak allows for
thousands of new data points to be added to the system. New variograms are easily
constructed and fitted by the user. Thousands of additional data points will have an
effect on the simulation time, however depending on the desired resolution, the general
time increment is insignificant compared to the time required for manual recalculations.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This study investigated the geostatistical method for quantifying contaminant mass and
volume and appraised the application of semi-quantitative data (PID and MIP) in order
to potentially enhance the certainty of the estimate.
• Geostatistical modelling was found to successfully quantify contaminant mass and
contaminated soil volume with the associated uncertainty expressed as a 90% con-
fidence interval at the study site.
– The monovariate approach using solely soil sample analyses yielded a TCE
mass estimate of 12.1 kg (8.0-19.7 kg) and a contaminated soil volume of
12,723 m3 (10,065-15,351 m3), with the largest difference obtained by the
multivariate approach yielding 11.7 kg TCE (8.0-17.0 kg) and 10,163 m3
(8,341-11,248 m3) contaminated soil.
– All four approaches showed an astonishing resemblance to the mass estimate
of 6.5 kg TCE (in the clayey till unit, 9.1 kg in total) with a mass interval
of 7-16 kg conducted by NIRAS based manual calculations and sensitivity
analyses.
• Assessment of the location of the contaminated volume indicated that in order to
obtain a high confidence level in the estimated remediation volume new additional
quantitative measurements should be applied to increase the certainty of the es-
timate, due to large parts of the visualized volume being considered as relatively
uncertain.
• The application of semi-quantitative data for a geostatistical bivariate or multi-
variate approach did not convincingly enhance the estimate and certainty thereof.
– Bi- and multivariate approaches only showed a slight difference to the mono-
variate approach and it was thereby concluded that the work behind the
application (primarily pre-processing) is not compensated by what is gained
by the application.
– The bi- and multivariate approaches were fitted with individual variogram
parameters, leading to less than the full zone volume being simulated, which
ultimately impacted the estimates as well as reducing the relatively certain
volume.
• The geostatistical method was found to yield reproducible estimates based on var-
iogram fittings conducted by different individuals, although being sensitive to the
==PAGE:90==
72
7 Conclusion
delineation of interest zone and required data regularization. Finally, it requires
great effort to get acquainted with the geostatistical software Kartotrak with nu-
merous trial-and-error simulations to refine the model, however the software is
generally user-friendly and can easily be updated with thousands of data points
without significantly increasing modelling or simulation time.
For further research and future work a few recommendations and ideas are mentioned.
Since the robustness analysis showed that the different variograms yielded repro-
ducible estimates, it would be interesting to reevaluate the variography of the bi- and
multivariate approach in order to possibly conduct a fully in-depth investigation of the
effect of semi-quantitative data on the relatively certain volume compared to the mono-
variate approach.
For future work, the application of PID could be reappraised as pseudo-concentrations.
Converting the PID measurement to pseudo-concentrations of TCE based on the corre-
lation found in the TCE/PID-plots prior to uploading the data to the software, would
enable the PID to be implemented in the monovariate approach alongside the actual soil
samples analyses.

A common approach when estimating contaminant mass at industrial sites is shown
in Equation A.1 and is based on defining the volume of contaminated soil, specifying
the concentration of contaminant within this volume, determine density parameter and
optionally fractions of lithology, which NIRAS have done.

NIRAS have defined three zones in the source area Pelle’s rum, Hal 3 and the northern
property line (Nordskel), each zone has then been subdivided into 3-4 subzones based
on water samples of TCE measured in the shallow aquifer. These TCE concentrations
have been contoured both horizontally and vertically (Fig. A.1 and A.2).
The mass estimates have been calculated in an elaborate Excel document, here de-

global fraction of clay and sand (75% and 25% respectively), approximation of con-

have been conducted for the unsaturated part of the clayey till and for the TCE plume

using fugacity calculations, the horizontal and vertical delineation of contaminated vol-

mass estimate as conducted by NIRAS.

C.1
Data correction
Data with coinciding measurements of TCE in soil and PID measurements was selected
in order to evaluate the correlation between the two variables. Thereafter, the data from
NIRAS and Geo was sorted into simplified lithologies; Backfill, Clay, Sand bodies and
Sand, where Sand refers to the sand matrix of the deep aquifer, Clay refers to the clay
matrix of the clayey till and Sand bodies refers to the sand bodies embedded within the
clayey till. It must be noted that the detailed lithology of the sand bodies demonstrated
a high variation covering grain sizes from silt to gravel, which was disregarded in the
following evaluation due to sand being the dominant lithology in the sand bodies. The
evaluation only focuses on data measured in clay and sand bodies, as a consequence of
limited data in backfill and in the deep aquifer.
Firstly, the PID and soil data measured in clay and sand bodies was compared
and the data in the two lithologies showed a nearly identical linear trend (Fig. C.1).
Measurements in clay show a better fit, than in sand bodies where a few outliers were
observed, which was to be expected as a result of the actual lithological variations. The
overall linearity between PID measurements and TCE concentration confirms integrat-
ing the semi-quantitative PID variable for multivariate modelling and co-kriging with
TCE concentrations in Kartotrak. Furthermore, the identical trends of clay and sand
bodies indicates that the data generally can be utilized from different lithologies without
requiring correction.

APPENDIX E
Effect of data regularization
When working with spatial analysis it is recommended to apply data sets with a constant
sample length. Regularization is a common method to obtain equal samples lengths from
an irregular sampling. The method is applicable in Kartotrak, in which using a sample
length corresponding to the most frequently used samples length, is recommended. For
Industrivej 2, 4 cm is the most frequent sampling length, thus the reported results are
computed using this length. However, changing the geometry of the uploaded boreholes,
e.g.
applying PID data to a soil sample borehole at a depth where no soil sample
is collected, can affect the experimental variogram and thereby the final result. This
appendix illustrates the effect of data regularization.
Same regularization length - different data sets
The same regularization length (4 cm) was applied to the data set only containing soil
samples [A] and to the data set containing the soil samples + PID [B]. The same soil
samples are present in both data sets, however applying PID data to the soil samples,
have changed the geometry for some boreholes, e.g. BA3 contains soil samples from
7.95-12.55 m.g.s. (4.6 m), whereas PID covers the borehole from 0.45-14.05 m.g.s. (13.6
m). A monovariate model of soil samples was simulated for both data sets, hence not
implementing the available PID data.
The effect on the experimental variogram is clearly seen in Figure E.1. The variogram
structure (lag, max. distance) are the same for the two simulations. The experimental
variograms obtained for data set [B] does not follow the same relative smooth increase
as data set [A], indicating that a modification of the model fit is required for [B]. The
global estimates of the two variables are listed in Table E.1. The regularization seems to
have a relevant effect on the mass estimation and to a lesser extent the volume estimate.
Nevertheless, the estimate of both increase from [A] to [B] by 13% in mass and 3% in
volume.

An obvious effect is the number of supports for each variogram point. For 1 cm
reg.
length, the variogram is supported by 1,000s to 100,000s of pairs, whereas for
the 10 cm reg. length, the points are ”only” supported by pairs in the 10s to 1,000s.
Furthermore, the fitting parameters used for reg. length 4 cm, can be applied to the reg.
length of 1 cm, whereas reg. length of 10 cm require a new fit of the prioritized TCE
in soil samples, subsequently resulting in a worse fit for the MIP variogram (especially
the vertical variogram). The correlation coefficient was also reduced from 0.50 to 0.40
for the 10 cm reg. length. The presimulations showed highly different images for the
three scenarios (Fig. E.4). The presimulation for reg. length 1 cm indicate that the
simulation only covers approx. half of the model space, due to the immense amount
of regularized data points with a relatively low defined neighborhood (250). First at
approx. 1000 neighbors, the full model space was pre-simulated, however increasing the
pre-simulation time tremendously. A rough estimation of the full simulation is 2-3 days
for what seems to be a simulated volume dominated by numerical artifacts. On the
contrary, the presimulations of reg. length 10 cm, show a much more smoothed krigning
and variance indicator maps, even compared to reg. length 4 cm.
Regularizing the samples from a short samples length to a long, causes loss of data
and information, since data points are averaged over longer intervals. However, regular-
izing to a short length requires splitting a large sample into equal variable pieces, which
leads to inaccurate reduced short-scale variability (Kasmaeeyazdi et al., 2018). Despite
the variance indicator maps having relative legends, the lower variance value are sim-
ilar, and thus clearly indicate the reduced short-scale variability in Figure E.4 (larger
light-blue circles around measured data). The results are reported in Table E.2.
