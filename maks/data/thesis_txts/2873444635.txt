Abstract 
Early-stage startups operate under high uncertainty, often lacking revenue, customers, or proven 
business models. Traditional valuation methods become inapplicable, forcing venture capitalists 
to rely on alternative signals when making investment decisions. Among these signals, founder 
characteristics have emerged as critical indicators of startup potential and investment risk. 
This thesis investigates how founder team characteristics influence valuation outcomes in pre-
seed and seed funding rounds. Through a mixed-methods approach, this study analyzes a 
manually constructed dataset of 100 early-stage pitch decks, examining relationships between 
team size, gender composition, industry experience, educational background, and the amount of 
capital raised. The quantitative analysis is complemented by a qualitative interview with a 
Danish venture capital partner, applying statistical tests and thematic analysis to uncover patterns 
in founder-based investment decisions. 
The findings reveal that educational background emerges as the most statistically significant 
predictor of funding success, with university-educated founders consistently raising more capital 
than those without formal degrees. Team size shows notable effects, with medium-sized teams of 
three to four founders outperforming both solo founders and larger teams. Industry experience 
demonstrates directional relationships with higher funding amounts, while gender composition 
does not produce significant differences due to limited representation of all-female teams. The 
qualitative analysis reveals that investor evaluation extends beyond quantifiable credentials to 
include interpersonal dynamics, coachability, and psychological resilience under pressure. 
This research contributes to the growing academic literature on venture capital decision-making 
by providing empirical evidence of how founder characteristics correlate with funding outcomes. 
The study offers practical insights for entrepreneurs emphasizing the importance of building 
educated, appropriately sized teams while developing communication skills and team cohesion. 
For investors, the findings underscore the value of systematic assessment processes that extend 
beyond traditional financial metrics to include behavioral and interpersonal factors. 
Keywords: Venture capital; Founder characteristics; Early-stage valuation; Startup funding; 
Team dynamics; Investment decisions.

1. Introduction 
Technological innovation has fundamentally transformed the global economy, reshaping how 
people communicate, access services, and engage in commerce. At the core of many of these 
developments are technology companies that were once early-stage startups. A significant share 
of these ventures has been supported by venture capital (VC) firms, particularly in the United 
States, which has historically dominated the global VC landscape. Notably, approximately 50% 
of companies listed on NASDAQ have received VC funding at some point in their growth 
trajectory (Gompers et al., 2020). 
Venture capital plays a central role in enabling these startups to scale. The model revolves 
around risk-taking: VC firms invest early in high-growth-potential companies with the 
expectation that a few will deliver outsized returns. This model has contributed to the emergence 
of so-called “unicorns” which is privately held companies valued at over 1 billion USD. In 2016, 
there were an estimated 229 unicorns globally, and by 2025, that number had risen to over 1,260 
(CB Insights, 2025). Companies such as Meta (formerly Facebook), SpaceX, Stripe, and Revolut 
have all demonstrated how VC-backed startups can reshape industries and deliver substantial 
returns to early investors. 
Despite their outsized influence, most early-stage startups operate under high uncertainty. They 
often lack revenue, customers, or a product-market fit. According to Kaplan and Strömberg 
(2003), nearly 39% of startups are still pre-revenue at the time of their first funding round, and 
others place this figure even higher at 69% (Wong, 2002). As such, traditional valuation models 
like Discounted Cash Flow (DCF), the Cost-to-Duplicate method, or Market Multiples become 
less applicable because they rely on historical financial performance or asset-backed projections 
(Dittmann, Maug, & Kemper, 2004; Festel et al., 2013). 
Given the limited use of conventional metrics in this context, venture capitalists often rely on 
other indicators, including qualitative assessments of the founding team. Characteristics such as 
the number of founders, gender composition, academic background, and prior industry 
experience have emerged as soft signals that may influence valuation decisions. This reflects a 
==PAGE:6==
 
6
broader shift in early-stage investing from strictly financial analysis to more heuristic and 
subjective forms of evaluation (Hisrich & Jankowicz, 1990; Gompers et al., 2020). 
This thesis explores how specific founder characteristics correlate with valuation outcomes in 
early-stage funding rounds, typically classified as pre-seed or seed. The empirical part of the 
study is based on a dataset of 100 manually extracted pitch decks, supported by an expert 
interview with a Danish venture capital partner. The aim is to shed light on the less tangible 
elements of valuation in venture capital decision-making and contribute to the growing academic 
interest in founder-based investment criteria. 
1.1 Problem Statement 
Despite the central role venture capital plays in financing early-stage startups, the mechanisms 
by which investors assign value to these ventures remain partially opaque, particularly when 
tangible financial data is absent. In the pre-seed and seed stages, conventional valuation models 
such as Discounted Cash Flow or Market Multiples are difficult to apply due to a lack of 
revenue, customers, or historical performance metrics. As a result, venture capitalists often rely 
on alternative signals, including subjective assessments of the founding team. 
While founder-related traits such as academic background, industry experience, and gender 
composition are often mentioned as influential factors in investment decisions, there is limited 
empirical research that systematically examines their relationship to valuation outcomes in early-
stage rounds. Most existing studies either focus on later-stage firms or analyze investor behavior 
through broad surveys rather than concrete funding data. 
This thesis seeks to address this gap by analyzing the link between founder characteristics and 
funding outcomes, using a manually constructed dataset of 100 early-stage pitch decks, 
complemented by a qualitative interview with a Danish venture capital partner. The goal is to 
better understand whether and how team-level traits influence investor valuation decisions at the 
earliest and most uncertain stages of the startup lifecycle.

1.2 Research Question 
This thesis investigates the following overarching research question: 
What role do founder team characteristics play in determining the valuation of early-stage 
startups during their first funding round (pre-seed or seed)? 
To address this central question, the study is guided by the following sub-questions: 
i. 
How does the number of founders influence early-stage startup valuations? 
 
ii. 
Do teams with gender diversity receive different valuations compared to single-gender 
teams? 
 
iii. 
To what extent does prior industry experience among founders affect investor valuation 
decisions? 
 
iv. 
How does the founders’ academic background (degree level) impact the perceived value 
of the startup? 
 
v. 
How do qualitative insights from a Danish VC align with or differ from the quantitative 
patterns observed in the pitch deck data? 
1.3 Scope and limitations 
This thesis explores how founder team characteristics relate to the amount of capital raised by 
early-stage startups during their first funding rounds. The analysis is based on 100 manually 
collected pitch decks, complemented by a qualitative interview with a Danish venture capital 
firm. While the dataset is unique and offers valuable insight into pre-seed and seed-stage 
funding, several limitations influence the generalizability and interpretability of the findings. 
First, pitch decks are not widely accessible to the public. Venture capital is a field where 
confidentiality is deeply embedded, particularly in relation to investment materials and deal 
==PAGE:8==
 
8
structure. When pitch decks are published or leaked, they often exclude key financial or team 
data. This lack of standardization limits researchers' ability to build consistent datasets. Prior 
work has noted that reliable and comprehensive access to such material is rare, especially in the 
early stages of funding (Block, Fisch, & Vismara, 2019). 
Second, the dataset used in this thesis is global in origin and was not constructed with regional 
segmentation in mind. While many pitch decks originate from the United States, there is no 
country-level tagging that allows for systematic comparison across markets. Although it would 
be relevant to study the Danish venture ecosystem specifically, the volume of available data from 
Denmark is insufficient. As a result, this thesis investigates global investment patterns rather 
than country-specific differences, consistent with how most pitch deck databases are structured 
(Gompers et al., 2020). 
Third, although funding dates are included, the dataset is not evenly distributed across time 
periods. Startup valuations are highly sensitive to market cycles, such as post-pandemic 
investment surges or economic downturns. Because this study does not control for 
macroeconomic timing, the observed differences in funding amounts may partly reflect broader 
investment climate fluctuations rather than team characteristics alone (Block, Sandner, & 
Spiegel, 2015). 
Fourth of the 100 pitch decks collected, only 77 contained the complete set of variables required 
for statistical analysis. The remaining cases were excluded due to missing data or anomalous 
values, such as investment amounts exceeding 10 million USD while being labeled as pre-seed. 
These were treated as outliers and not included in the analysis. 
Fifth, many variables in the dataset were manually coded. These include gender composition, 
education level, and prior industry experience of the founders. This coding was based primarily 
on publicly available information such as LinkedIn profiles and signaling in the desks. Although 
coding guidelines were defined in advance and applied consistently, some level of subjectivity is 
inevitable in manual data extraction (Franke et al., 2008). 
Finally, the qualitative component of the thesis consists of a single in-depth interview with a 
Danish venture capital investor. While the conversation adds valuable expert insight and 
==PAGE:9==
 
9
supports interpretation of the quantitative results, it cannot be assumed to represent the full 
spectrum of views held across the Danish VC scene neither the global one.  
In summary, while this thesis offers a rare glimpse into how founder characteristics may shape 
investment decisions in early-stage ventures, the conclusions should be interpreted with 
appropriate caution. The findings are best understood as exploratory and contribute to a growing 
body of research that seeks to clarify how subjective signals substitute for traditional metrics in 
environments marked by high uncertainty.

2. Literature Review 
 
This chapter presents the conceptual and empirical foundations relevant to understanding how 
founder characteristics influence early-stage startup valuation decisions. The purpose: first, to 
place the research within existing venture capital literature, and second, to identify the main 
theoretical perspectives, empirical themes, and research gaps that motivate and guide this study.  
 
It starts by exploring how venture capitalists make investment decisions in environments marked 
by uncertainty, limited financial metrics, and information asymmetry. These conditions are 
typical in pre-seed and seed-stage ventures, where conventional valuation tools such as 
discounted cash flow or market multiple methods cannot easily be applied. In such cases, 
investors often rely on qualitative indicators, personal judgment, and impressions of the founding 
team. The review then turns to specific founder characteristics that may influence perceived 
startup value. These include team size, gender composition, industry experience, and educational 
background. Each of these variables is examined in terms of how they are used as signals of 
quality or credibility in early-stage investment decisions. 
Finally, the chapter highlights gaps in the existing literature, particularly the lack of structured 
data that combines founder traits with real-world funding outcomes. This thesis seeks to address 
that gap by synthesizing insights from prior studies and applying them to a unique dataset of 
pitch decks, supported by qualitative input from a Danish venture capital investor. 
 
2.1 VC Decision-Making and Early-Stage Valuation Challenges 
The valuation of early-stage startups is one of the most complex and ambiguous tasks faced by 
venture capitalists (VCs). Unlike established firms, early-stage ventures typically operate without 
revenue, financial history, or tangible assets. This makes conventional valuation models such as 
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) or Market Multiples largely inapplicable (Keeley, Punjabi, & 
Turki, 1996). The absence of hard financial data amplifies uncertainty and forces investors to 
consider alternative evaluation methods based on expectations, signals, and assumptions. 
==PAGE:13==
 
13
In this context, VCs often rely on non-financial cues or “soft” factors to infer the potential of 
startups in pre-seed and seed stages. Among these, founder characteristics such as prior 
experience, team size, and educational background are used as proxies for future performance 
(Miloud, Aspelund, & Cabrol, 2012). These characteristics are processed heuristically, often 
through pattern recognition and intuitive judgment based on prior investing experience (Huang & 
Pearce, 2015). 
Another challenge arises from valuation inflation. Gornall and Strebulaev (2020) showed that 
headline startup valuations frequently exceed their real, risk-adjusted values. Such distortions 
often reflect aggressive term sheets, competitive investor environments, or strategic signaling. 
Relatedly, Hsu (2004) demonstrated that entrepreneurs are willing to accept lower valuations in 
exchange for affiliations with high-reputation VCs, suggesting that valuation is shaped not just 
by business fundamentals but also by brand association and perceived strategic benefit. 
The business plan and pitch presentation itself also play a substantial role in shaping investor 
judgment. Kirsch, Goldfarb, and Gera (2009) found that VCs often respond more to the 
legitimacy and narrative structure of the business plan than to its financial content. This 
observation supports earlier findings by Wells (1974), who noted that many venture decisions are 
made with limited quantitative data and are heavily influenced by interpersonal dynamics during 
the investment process. 
The prevalence of information asymmetry between founders and investors is a recurring theme 
in this literature. Founders typically possess more domain-specific knowledge and operational 
insight, while investors must rely on external cues, networks, and impressions to form a decision. 
As Bergh et al. (2019) note, “Information asymmetry is perhaps the most fundamental challenge 
in investment decisions, as one party possesses more or better information than the other” (p. 
123). This imbalance contributes to investor dependence on heuristic-based assessments and 
personal trust. 
Taken together, these insights suggest that early-stage valuation is influenced by a combination 
of intuitive judgment, signal interpretation, and perceived team credibility. In the absence of 
==PAGE:14==
 
14
reliable financial data, investor attention shifts to who the founders are, how they present 
themselves, and whether they appear capable of delivering on their vision. 
2.1.1 Founder Characteristics in Investment Outcomes 
Many studies highlight how the characteristics of startup founders play an important role in 
early-stage funding decisions. Since traditional valuation models rely on financial metrics that do 
not exist in most pre-seed or seed-stage companies, investors often assess the founders 
themselves to estimate future potential. This section explores four such founder-related variables: 
team size, gender composition, industry experience, and educational background. 
Team Size 
Team size has been linked to startup resilience, cognitive diversity, and the ability to handle 
uncertainty. McCarthy et al. (2023) analyzed over 21,000 startups and found that teams with 
three or more founders had more than twice the chance of success compared to solo founders. 
These teams also exhibited greater personality diversity, which contributed to improved 
adaptability and performance. Visintin and Pittino (2014) arrived at a similar conclusion in their 
study of university-based spin-offs. They showed that start-ups with larger founding teams had 
higher early-stage growth, partly due to the mix of managerial, technical, and commercial skills. 
Franke et al. (2008) also investigated how team characteristics affect investor evaluations. Based 
on evaluations from real venture capitalists, they found that team size was a positive factor, 
particularly when combined with prior experience and complementary skills. These findings 
suggest that investors often view team structure as a reflection of readiness and reliability. 
Gender 
Gender dynamics play a significant role in how founding teams are perceived. Kanze et al. 
(2018) showed that women and men are often treated differently during investor pitches, with 
women more frequently receiving questions focused on risk and loss prevention. This difference 
in framing affected how investors evaluated the startup, often to the disadvantage of female 
==PAGE:15==
 
15
founders. Brush et al. (2019) further confirmed that women entrepreneurs face barriers in venture 
funding, even when their ventures are equally or more prepared than male-led ones. 
More recent findings by McCarthy et al. (2023) add nuance to the discussion. The study found 
that mixed-gender teams were more likely to succeed than all-male or all-female teams. The 
presence of diverse perspectives appeared to improve strategic decision-making and investor 
trust. Although gender diversity remains underrepresented in many startup ecosystems, the 
evidence suggests that investors who value it may be aligning with real performance outcomes. 
Industry Experience 
Prior experience in a relevant industry is one of the strongest signals of competence and 
execution potential. Colombo and Grilli (2005) studied over 500 technology-based firms and 
concluded that founders with sector-specific knowledge built faster-growing businesses. 
Similarly, Chandler and Jansen (1992) found that founders who rated themselves as competent in 
their chosen field were more likely to lead ventures that performed well and attracted external 
resources. 
Sapienza and Grimm (1997) used data from the U.S. railroad industry to show that founders with 
operational experience made better strategic choices. Hsu (2004) also demonstrated that 
experienced entrepreneurs were able to negotiate better investment terms, suggesting that VCs 
view experience to reduce uncertainty and increase trust. While prior experience is not a 
guarantee of success, it helps investors form more confident expectations about the founder’s 
ability to execute. 
Education 
Education can function as both a signal and a form of human capital. Roure and Maidique (1986) 
found that educational background was positively associated with a startup’s ability to attract 
early funding. Backes-Gellner and Werner (2007) showed that not just the level but also the type 
of education influenced how investors perceived a founder’s credibility. Founders with technical 
or scientific backgrounds were often seen as more prepared to solve complex problems. 
==PAGE:16==
 
16
However, education on its own may not always predict success. McCarthy et al. (2023) found 
that while higher education levels were associated with favorable traits like openness and 
intellect, these traits were only valuable when combined with others such as determination and 
energy. This supports the idea that education contributes meaningfully to how investors evaluate 
founders, but it is part of a larger profile rather than a stand-alone criterion. 
2.1.2 Limitations of Traditional Valuation at Pre-Seed 
Valuing early-stage startups presents well-documented challenges. Pre-seed ventures often lack 
revenue, profits, and tangible assets. They may not yet have launched a product or even formed a 
fully operational team. As a result, most traditional valuation models fall short when applied to 
startups at this stage. 
The Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) method is one of the most widely taught and applied valuation 
models. It relies on forecasting future cash flows and discounting them to present value. 
However, the model depends on several types of input data: historical financials, detailed 
revenue projections, a defensible discount rate, and a clear estimate of terminal value. In a pre-
seed startup, none of these inputs can be estimated with confidence. Kulatilaka and Marcus 
(1992) argue that DCF becomes ineffective when applied to projects under deep uncertainty and 
with no financial track record. 
Other valuation models, such as the Market Multiples method, also face limitations. This model 
compares the target company to similar publicly traded firms, using financial metrics like 
revenue or earnings to derive a valuation multiple. In pre-seed startups, the lack of comparable 
companies and absence of financial results make this approach unreliable. As Holthausen and 
Zmijewski (2012) warn, using inappropriate peers or inconsistent metrics can easily lead to 
misleading conclusions. 
A third method, the Cost to Duplicate approach, estimates what it would cost to build the same 
company from scratch. While this might be useful for asset-heavy businesses, it fails to capture 
intangible value like brand, team capability, and network effects. Akkaya (2020) and Montani et 
al. (2020) both emphasize that early-stage startups typically rely on intellectual property, founder 
vision, and scalability, none of which are reflected in a cost-based estimate.

Empirical studies confirm this disconnect. Miloud, Aspelund, and Cabrol (2012) conducted 
interviews with venture capitalists and found that valuations at early stages depend more on 
founder quality, market opportunity, and scalability potential than on hard financial metrics. 
Wessendorf et al. (2019) also found that investors tend to use heuristics and subjective scoring 
==PAGE:18==
 
18
tools in place of formal models. These include scorecard methods, risk-factor summations, and 
comparability adjustments based on narrative and perceived credibility. 
In conclusion, while DCF, Market Multiples, and Cost to Duplicate have value in later-stage 
analysis, they are ill-suited for evaluating companies that are still conceptual, rapidly evolving, 
or built around founder ambition.  
2.1.3 Summary and Theoretical Framework 
This literature review has emphasized that early-stage startup valuation is shaped less by formal 
models and more by heuristics and qualitative judgment. In environments where financial 
metrics are scarce or unreliable, investors turn to founder characteristics as key decision-making 
inputs. 
Key findings include: 
• 
Traditional valuation methods such as Discounted Cash Flow, Market Multiples, and 
Cost to Duplicate are not practical at the pre-seed stage due to data limitations. 
• 
Investors assess founder traits such as team size, gender composition, industry 
experience, and education as proxies for startup quality. 
• 
These traits serve both as signals of credibility and as indicators of human capital. 
To guide the empirical analysis, this thesis draws on two main theoretical perspectives: 
Signal Theory 
In situations of info 
rmation asymmetry, investors rely on observable signals to reduce 
uncertainty. Founder characteristics such as experience, education, and presentation quality are 
interpreted as indicators of future potential and reliability. (Spence, 1973). 
 
Human Capital Theory 
Founders’ education, skills, and domain knowledge are seen as assets that increase the likelihood 
of venture success. This theory explains why certain backgrounds may be favored in funding 
decisions, even when financial data is limited. (Becker, 1964). 
==PAGE:19==
 
19
Together, these theories explain why and how non-financial founder attributes can influence 
investment behavior. The framework provides the basis for investigating whether measurable 
founder traits are associated with higher funding amounts in pre-seed and seed-stage ventures. 
3. Methodology  
To produce research that is both academically rigorous and practically valuable to entrepreneurs 
and investors, this thesis adopts a mixed-methods approach grounded in real-world investment 
cases. While pitch decks have become a standard medium through which startups communicate 
with investors, they remain an underutilized resource in academic research. These documents 
offer a unique window into how founders frame their ventures and how they are evaluated by 
potential investors. The original intention of this research was to analyze a larger dataset to 
strengthen the statistical robustness of the findings. However, due to the scarcity of publicly 
available pitch decks that also disclose funding amounts and founder details, the final sample 
was limited to 100 cases. This constraint reflects both the private nature of early-stage 
dealmaking and the challenges of working with real-world data within the timeframe of a 
master’s thesis. 
 
The study uses a manually collected dataset of pre-seed and seed-stage pitch decks from startups 
that successfully secured funding. It examines founder-level variables including team size, 
gender composition, industry experience, and educational background. To complement the 
structured data, the study incorporates qualitative insight from a one-on-one interview with a 
Danish venture capital investor. Finally, an extensive literature review informs both the research 
design and the interpretation of results. By integrating literature, structured data, and expert 
insight, this methodology aims to uncover meaningful patterns in how founder characteristics 
relate to funding outcomes. The goal is not to develop a predictive model, but to offer a clearer 
understanding of the qualitative signals that matter most in early-stage investment decisions. 
3.1 Research Strategy and Rationale 
This thesis applies a mixed-methods research strategy to explore how founder characteristics 
influence early-stage startup funding outcomes. The research question is approached through the 
==PAGE:20==
 
20
integration of three data sources: quantitative pitch deck data, qualitative insights from a venture 
capital professional, and an extensive literature review. 
The rationale for this strategy is threefold. First, the quantitative component provides structured 
data on 100 pre-seed and seed-stage pitch decks. These documents are selected because they 
reflect real-time founder-investor communication and typically include details such as funding 
amount, founder team composition, and company profile. Structured datasets at this stage are 
rare due to the confidentiality of funding processes, making pitch decks a valuable primary 
source (Gornall & Strebulaev, 2020; Kirsch, Goldfarb, & Gera, 2009). 
Second, a semi-structured expert interview with a Danish venture capital investor supplements 
the data with practical insight into how investor decisions are made. The interview allows for 
richer interpretation of the quantitative findings, especially regarding aspects such as heuristics, 
personal bias, and real-world trade-offs that cannot be measured through numeric data alone 
(Huang & Pearce, 2015; Bergh et al., 2019). 
Third, an in-depth literature review supports both the design and the interpretation of the study. 
The literature helps frame the conceptual logic behind valuation decisions and supports the 
construction of the theoretical framework based on human capital and signal theory. The 
combined use of empirical data, expert perspectives, and theoretical grounding enhances the 
validity and practical relevance of the study (Creswell, 2014). 
A mixed-methods design is well-suited to contexts characterized by uncertainty, limited financial 
metrics, and information asymmetry as is common in early-stage startup environments. This 
strategy allows for triangulation between data types, helping to balance generalizability with 
contextual depth. 
3.2 Data Source and Manual Extraction 
The primary data used in this study consists of 100 pitch decks from early-stage startups that 
successfully raised funding at the pre-seed or seed level. These pitch decks were accessed 
through the online platform AngelMatch.io, which aggregates investor-facing documents from 
==PAGE:21==
 
21
startups across different sectors and geographical markets. Each selected pitch deck reported the 
amount of capital raised, which allowed for inclusion in the quantitative analysis. 
Due to the scarcity of publicly available pitch decks that also disclose specific funding figures 
and team composition, the collection process was conducted manually. Each pitch deck was 
reviewed individually to extract a consistent set of variables related to founder characteristics 
and funding outcomes. The variables recorded were: 
Team size: Total number of identified founders. 
Gender composition: Categorized as all-men, mixed-gender, or all-women teams. 
Industry experience: Coded as binary, indicating whether at least one founder had prior 
experience in the startup’s target industry. 
Education level: Highest degree attained among team members, coded as: 
0 = No degree 
1 = Bachelor’s or equivalent 
2 = Master’s, MBA, or equivalent 
3 = PhD or equivalent 
Funding year: Year the round was publicly raised, based on the platform or supporting sources. 
Amount raised: Reported capital raised in US dollars. 
When information was missing or unclear in the pitch decks, publicly accessible sources such as 
LinkedIn profiles or company websites were consulted to validate or complete the dataset. If 
essential variables such as gender or education background could not be determined reliably, the 
entry was excluded from relevant statistical analyses to maintain internal consistency and data 
integrity. 
To ensure data accuracy, the extraction process was conducted manually and reviewed in two 
separate passes. A structured Google Sheets spreadsheet was used with data validation for 
dropdown fields and standardized entry formats. Outlier values, especially regarding amount 
raised, were flagged during data preparation for further examination in the analysis phase. 
==PAGE:22==

Initial efforts were made to automate this extraction process to reduce time and improve

3.5 Statistical tests (Descriptives, T-tests, ANOVA, Time Series) 
To examine how founder characteristics influence early-stage funding outcomes, a structured set 
of quantitative methods was applied. These statistical techniques serve both to describe the data 
and to test for meaningful differences across founder traits. 
 
Descriptive Statistics 
Descriptive analysis was used to summarize the central tendencies and distributional properties 
of key variables. This included metrics such as mean, median, standard deviation, and range for 
variables like amount raised, team size, gender composition, education level, and industry 
experience. These statistics help establish the baseline characteristics of the sample and allow for 
the identification of potential outliers (Field, 2013). 
Independent Samples T-tests 
T-tests were applied to compare the means between two independent groups. Examples include 
assessing differences in average funding between startups with or without industry experience, or 
between all-male and mixed-gender teams. The test assumes that the distributions are 
approximately normal and that the variances between groups are equal (Cohen, 1988). These 
assumptions were evaluated prior to analysis. 
One-way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) 
For variables with more than two categories, such as education level or team size, one-way 
ANOVA was used to assess whether mean funding levels differed significantly across groups. 
==PAGE:25==
 
25
While useful, ANOVA results should be interpreted with care due to the relatively small sample 
size of 77. Limited data may reduce the test’s power and make group comparisons less stable. As 
Maxwell and Delaney (2004) note, small group sizes can result in unstable or misleading 
estimates and should be interpreted as indicative rather than definitive. 
Time Series Analysis 
In addition to cross-sectional comparisons, time series plots were generated to visualize the 
relationship between funding year and amount raised. This allows for the detection of potential 
temporal trends or irregularities in funding behavior. Although the primary focus of this thesis is 
not macroeconomic analysis, plotting funding activity across years helps identify whether certain 
founder traits are more common or more successful during funding periods. Given the limited 
number of data points per year, the time series analysis is exploratory and used primarily for 
pattern recognition rather than predictive modeling. 
All statistical analyses were conducted using Python’s scipy.stats, statsmodels, 
and matplotlib packages. Extreme outliers (e.g., values over 10 million USD) were removed in 
accordance with best practices for skewed data distributions. The combined approach balances 
rigor with practicality, aligning with the mixed-methods design of the thesis.

3.7 Limitations and Data Quality Concerns 
While the dataset assembled for this thesis offers a foundation for examining founder 
characteristics and early-stage funding, several limitations affect the precision and 
generalizability of the analysis. 
The manual nature of the data extraction process inevitably introduces subjectivity. Although the 
coding framework was consistently applied, certain variables such as industry experience and 
educational background required researcher judgment based on publicly available profiles, 
particularly LinkedIn. In cases where founder information was incomplete or inconsistently 
reported, assumptions had to be made, potentially affecting data reliability. 
Moreover, the variable used to represent funding success, amount raised, is only a proxy for 
valuation. This distinction is important, as early-stage funding rounds often involve non-obvious 
equity structures, investor syndication, and conditional capital deployment. Without access to 
exact equity stakes or valuation caps, it is not possible to fully account for the financial context 
==PAGE:27==
 
27
behind the capital raised. The dataset, although filtered and cleaned, is skewed toward more 
recent funding years. This temporal clustering may reflect shifts in investor behavior or 
macroeconomic conditions not controlled for in the analysis. While time-series analysis was 
applied to explore this, the relatively limited number of data points constrains its explanatory 
power. 
There are also concerns related to categorical abstraction. Variables such as gender composition 
and educational attainment are reduced to simplified forms. For example, teams are grouped as 
all-male, all-female, or mixed, and education is categorized into general levels. These necessary 
simplifications for statistical modeling mask more granular differences in team composition or 
academic specialization. Geographical variation further complicates generalization. The dataset 
includes global startups, many of which are based in the United States, while qualitative insights 
are drawn from a Danish venture capital investor. The potential misalignment in regional 
investment norms, stage definitions, and capital environments limits the degree to which patterns 
can be interpreted across contexts. 
Finally, the sample size of 77 valid entries after outlier removal and cleaning poses statistical 
limitations. It restricts the use of more robust regression models and reduces the power of 
inferential tests. While exploratory findings are presented with care, the results are best 
interpreted as suggestive rather than conclusive.

4. Quantitative Results 
This chapter presents the findings from the quantitative analysis conducted on a manually 
extracted dataset of 100 early-stage pitch decks. The objective is to evaluate whether specific 
founder characteristics correlate with the amount of capital raised during pre-seed or seed 
funding rounds. The analysis follows the structure of the research questions outlined earlier and 
includes descriptive statistics, group comparisons using t-tests and ANOVA, and time-series 
visualizations. Given the absence of valuation data, the amount raised is used as a proxy for 
investor perception of startup value. Although this approach has limitations, it reflects a tangible 
commitment from investors and allows for structured analysis based on available data. The 
statistical tests employed aim not to establish causality, but to identify observable trends that 
may guide future research or decision-making in early-stage venture capital. 
In addition to the numerical results, visual representations of distributions, group means, and 
temporal patterns are included to enhance interpretability. The analysis also respects sample size 
constraints and acknowledges areas where the data may be too sparse to draw meaningful 
conclusions. 
4.1 Dataset overview 
The dataset used in this analysis includes from 91 to 77 complete observations. These entries 
were selected based on the availability of key variables: amount raised, founder gender 
composition, team size, industry experience, education level, and funding year. Startups with 
missing or insufficient data were excluded to ensure consistent and interpretable results. 
Additionally, six extreme outliers with reported funding amounts above 10 million USD were 
removed, as they likely represented later-stage rounds misclassified as pre-seed. 
Founder characteristics were manually extracted and categorized according to a coding 
framework developed for this thesis. Team size ranges from one to six members. Gender 
composition was categorized into three groups: all-male, mixed-gender, and all-female teams. 
Industry experience and education levels were coded as binary or ordinal variables, respectively, 
based on publicly available biographical information. These simplifications were necessary to 
ensure consistency across the sample but may obscure finer-grained variations.

4.2.5 Summary of Comparative Analysis 
The comparative analysis explores whether observable differences exist in funding outcomes 
across key founder characteristics, using group mean comparisons and visual inspection of 
distributions. While not inferential in nature, this analysis serves as a foundational layer for the 
more rigorous statistical testing that follows. The trends uncovered provide preliminary evidence 
for how characteristics such as team size, gender composition, industry experience, and 
educational background may shape early-stage fundraising outcomes. 
Across the dataset, team size appears to be positively associated with capital raised, up to an 
optimal point. Startups with three or four founders raised the highest average and median 
amounts, whereas solo founders consistently raised the least. This pattern reflects established 
research suggesting that team-based ventures often enjoy greater legitimacy and functional 
bandwidth in the eyes of investors (Franke et al., 2008; McCarthy et al., 2023). Larger teams 
beyond four members did not show further increases, which may indicate diminishing returns 
due to coordination costs or diluted roles. 
In terms of gender composition, mixed-gender teams raised the most capital on average, 
followed by all-male teams, with all-female teams recording the lowest mean. However, the 
extremely small sample size of all-female teams (N = 3) prevents meaningful comparison and 
introduces substantial uncertainty. These patterns mirror findings in the literature suggesting that 
mixed teams can benefit from complementary perspectives and social network reach (Brush et 
al., 2019) but also reflect the persistent underrepresentation of female founders in venture-backed 
ecosystems (Kanze et al., 2018). 
The presence of industry experience among founders is similarly linked to higher funding 
outcomes. Teams with at least one experienced founder raised more on average and at the 
median level than those without. This supports the widely held assumption that prior domain 
exposure enhances perceived execution risk, provides access to relevant networks, and boosts 
investor confidence (Colombo & Grilli, 2005; Hsu, 2004). The relatively small difference in 
spread suggests a modest, yet consistent, advantage.

investor evaluation. Startups without any formal academic credentials raised the least, and while

4.6 Conclusion of quantitative analysis 
The quantitative analysis reveals several consistent patterns that inform our understanding of 
how founder characteristics may relate to early-stage funding outcomes. Across comparative 
statistics, t-tests, ANOVA, and time-series analysis, educational emerged as the most robust and 
statistically significant predictor of capital raised. Teams with at least one formally educated 
founder, particularly at the bachelor’s or master’s level, consistently outperformed those without, 
offering empirical support for human capital and signaling theories. Team size also showed a 
notable effect, with medium-sized teams (three to four founders) raising more than smaller or 
larger teams, suggesting an optimal range for founding team. Industry experience showed a weak 
but directional relationship with higher funding, while gender composition, although 
descriptively interesting, did not produce statistically significant differences due to limited data, 
especially among all-female teams. 
Temporal analysis underscored how broader market conditions, particularly during and after the 
COVID-19 pandemic, may have shaped access to capital independently of founder traits. These 
patterns reinforce the need to view startup fundraising within both firm-specific and 
macroeconomic contexts. 
While these findings offer exploratory insights, they are bounded by several limitations: small 
subgroup sizes, cross-sectional design, and the absence of multivariate controls. As such, the 
results should be viewed as indicative rather than conclusive. To supplement this analysis and 
explore how venture capitalists interpret founder traits in practice, the following chapter presents 
qualitative insights drawn from investor interviews. These perspectives offer a valuable

founding teams, particularly in areas that extend beyond formal qualifications or pitch deck 
content. While themes such as academic background, industry experience, and team composition 
were addressed directly, the interview also revealed underlying preferences and caution points 
that inform the investment process.

5.1.1 Team Dynamics and Evaluation Beyond the Surface 
 
The investor emphasized that evaluation of founders begins at the very first interaction. An HR 
specialist is involved from the initial pitch to assess team dynamics and personality fit, with a 
strong focus on how founders behave under stress or in disagreement. “We conduct a 
personality-based evaluation to identify outliers,” the investor explained, adding that 
“coachability is key.” 
Rather than applying fixed rules regarding founder structures, such as avoiding solo founders or 
couples, the firm focuses on identifying behavioral red flags. Founders must be able to work 
together under pressure, show alignment on vision, and demonstrate openness to input. “It’s 
about understanding how they act when the money is running low, or if they disagree on 
strategy,” the investor noted. Communication skills are especially important: “If they can’t 
explain it to us, they won’t be able to explain it to the market.” 
Importantly, the investor pointed out that storytelling alone is not enough. “You have to be 
careful not to overrate storytelling or drive if it isn’t backed by substance,” they said, adding that 
quieter, more technical founders are often undervalued. This signals a balanced approach that 
values both soft skills and demonstrated competence. 
5.1.2 Background Signals: Education, Experience, and Role Clarity 
The investor did not view academic credentials as a strict requirement but acknowledged that 
“most founders we back have long academic training” due to the technical nature of the firm’s 
deep tech focus. Education functions more as an indirect filter than a direct selection criterion. 
“We don’t invest based on degree alone.” 
Industry experience, however, was described as “very important.” Founders who have worked 
extensively in their target sector are perceived as more credible, better able to navigate 
complexity, and more capable of delivering on execution. “If someone has worked 30 years in an 
industry and launches a startup, it’s valuable,” the investor noted. At the same time, they 
acknowledged that academic founders often need commercial counterparts to round out the team. 
==PAGE:46==
 
46
The firm does not express a clear preference for a specific team size, but large teams raise 
concerns about long-term cohesion. “If you have six founders, half might drop out,” the investor 
said, explaining that this creates problems in cap tables and incentives. What matters is not the 
number of founders, but whether “each person has a clear role, not just a title.” 
5.1.3 Diversity and Stage Consistency 
Gender composition is not used as a formal selection filter, although the firm tracks it. “We’ve 
invested in many female founders, but not by design,” the investor stated. Instead, diversity in 
age, nationality, and educational background is viewed as more relevant for shaping team 
performance and perspective. 
Finally, the interview confirmed that team assessment criteria remain stable across funding 
stages. From pre-seed to seed, the emphasis continues to be on team cohesion, execution ability, 
and founder resilience. The difference lies in exposure and track record. “At seed stage, they’ve 
often worked together longer,” the investor said, which reduces the perceived risk of founder 
conflict or dropout. 
5.1.4 Investor Perspective on Team Dynamics 
Team dynamics emerged as one of the most critical yet least quantifiable factors in the investor’s 
evaluation process. While variables such as education or industry experience can be assessed 
through resumes and credentials, interpersonal qualities such as coachability, cohesion, and 
communication ability require more direct interaction. The investor highlighted that their firm 
involves an HR specialist from the first pitch meeting to assess these aspects, describing the 
approach as personality-based and focused on identifying potential interpersonal risks. “We 
conduct a personality-based evaluation to identify outliers,” they explained, noting that 
coachability is often a deciding factor in whether to proceed with a team. 
This emphasis on relational and behavioral dynamics aligns with previous research on venture 
capital decision-making, which suggests that investors often rely on intuitive assessments of 
founder-team fit, particularly at the early stages where product and market signals are limited 
(Hisrich & Jankowicz, 1990; Franke et al., 2008). In the investor’s words, “If they can’t explain 
==PAGE:47==
 
47
it to us, they can’t sell it to the market.” This reflects the importance of not only internal 
alignment but also external communication. Investors must be convinced that founders can 
translate vision into persuasion across stakeholders. 
Another major concern is how teams perform under pressure. The investor described specific 
scenarios, such as disagreements on strategy or the threat of running out of capital, as crucial 
tests of founder resilience. These situations reveal how well the team functions beyond the pitch. 
Literature has similarly noted that early-stage teams must exhibit psychological robustness and 
adaptability, particularly under the stress of uncertain outcomes and iterative business 
development (Sapienza & Grimm, 1997). 
Recent research also supports this emphasis, showing that startup success is positively associated 
with personality traits such as openness and emotional stability, and that team diversity in 
personality can improve outcomes when paired with strong interpersonal compatibility 
(Schneider et al., 2023). This provides empirical backing for the investor’s attention to 
coachability and psychological alignment as core signals of team viability. 
The interview also pointed to a nuanced understanding of founder roles. While the firm does not 
impose a preferred number of founders, it does assess whether each member adds meaningful 
value. “If you have six founders, half might drop out,” the investor noted, identifying potential 
issues with cap table stability and incentive alignment also known as Dead Equity. This 
observation is consistent with findings that team size alone does not predict success unless role 
clarity and commitment are evident (Visintin & Pittino, 2014; Franke et al., 2008). 
Interestingly, the investor also acknowledged that some traits may be overvalued. They 
cautioned against placing too much emphasis on charisma or storytelling if it is not supported by 
substance. “You have to be careful not to overrate storytelling and drive,” they said, adding that 
technically strong but introverted founders are often overlooked. This echoes prior concerns in 
the literature that investor heuristics can lead to biases in favor of more extroverted or polished 
presenters, even when quieter founders may offer stronger domain expertise (Kanze et al., 2018). 
In sum, the investor’s perspective underscores that team dynamics play a central role in early-
stage investment decisions, even if they are difficult to measure systematically. Factors such as 
==PAGE:48==
 
48
coachability, interpersonal fit, stress response, and role complementarity are treated as early 
indicators of a team’s long-term viability. These aspects, while not directly observable in pitch 
decks, are actively assessed during founder meetings and often carry decisive weight. This may 
help explain why certain founder traits did not show strong statistical correlations with funding 
in the quantitative analysis. Some of the most valued characteristics exist outside structured data 
and instead reside in the relational, contextual, and behavioral domain. 
5.2 Triangulation with Quantitative patterns 
This section compares the statistical findings from the pitch deck dataset with the qualitative 
insights offered by the investor interview. Rather than repeating the same variable-by-variable 
structure from earlier chapters, the analysis focuses on how investor logic complements, 
challenges, or clarifies patterns in the data. This synthesis helps illuminate where founder 
characteristics matter in ways that are not easily observable through quantitative methods alone. 
One of the clearest points of agreement between the two approaches is on education. In the 
statistical analysis, teams without university-educated founders raised significantly less funding. 
The investor echoed this trend, noting that “most founders we back have long academic 
training,” especially in deep-tech sectors. However, they clarified that degrees are not used as 
strict filters. Instead, formal education often functions as a signal of depth, cognitive skill, and 
the capacity to navigate complicated business environments. This is consistent with human 
capital theory and entrepreneurial signaling research, both of which suggest that academic 
credentials contribute to perceived competence, particularly under uncertainty (Becker, 1964; 
Backes-Gellner & Werner, 2007). 
Another area of convergence is team structure. The data revealed that teams of three or four 
founders raised more than both solo founders and larger teams, with the difference between 
small and medium teams being statistically significant. While the investor did not commit to a 
specific number, they noted a tendency to avoid investing in teams that are too large, citing risks 
related to misaligned incentives and future dilution. “If you have six founders, half might drop 
out,” they explained, referring to the risk of creating dead equity. These are structural concerns 
that are not directly visible in pitch decks but play a significant role in funding decisions. The 
==PAGE:49==
 
49
literature also supports the idea that team size interacts with other dimensions such as role clarity 
and cohesion, which are critical for early-stage execution (Franke et al., 2008; Visintin & Pittino, 
2014). 
The relationship between industry experience and funding was more ambiguous. Statistically, 
prior experience showed only marginal significance (p = 0.0547). In contrast, the investor 
described it as “very important,” particularly for understanding market dynamics and reducing 
operational risk. The gap may be due to the variable’s lack of granularity in the dataset, which 
did not differentiate between years of experience, seniority, or sector relevance. Previous 
research suggests that experience in related industries is a strong predictor of early venture 
performance, especially when combined with commercial skill (Colombo & Grilli, 2005). The 
investor’s assessment suggests that experience is not just about familiarity but about 
demonstrated mastery and execution capacity. 
Gender composition presented another case where both data and practice aligned, but in a less 
conclusive way. The statistical results showed no significant differences in funding outcomes 
based on gender makeup, although the number of all-female teams in the dataset was too low (n 
= 3) to support meaningful analysis. The investor emphasized that gender is tracked internally 
but not used as a criterion for selection. Instead, they value broader dimensions of diversity such 
as discipline, background, and nationality. This resonates with research by Brush et al. (2019), 
who argue that single-axis gender measures fail to capture the full scope of diversity’s impact in 
startup teams. What is notable here is not a contradiction, but rather a shared acknowledgment of 
measurement limitations and structural underrepresentation. 
Perhaps the most important finding from this comparison is the absence of team dynamics in the 
quantitative data. The investor placed strong emphasis on factors such as coachability, 
interpersonal chemistry, and psychological resilience. These traits are assessed from the first 
meeting. “Coachability is key,” they stated. “We conduct a personality-based evaluation to 
identify outliers.” These aspects are not accessible through pitch materials and are often 
evaluated through structured conversations or behavioral screening. Literature supports this 
qualitative emphasis. Franke et al. (2008) and Hisrich and Jankowicz (1990) both highlight that 
investor decisions are often shaped by subjective assessments of founder behavior, especially at 
==PAGE:50==
 
50
early stages. More recently, McCarthy et al. (2023) showed that founder personality traits, 
particularly emotional stability and openness, are positively correlated with startup performance, 
especially when team dynamics are diverse but cohesive. 
Altogether, the triangulation reveals a mixed picture. Variables such as education and team 
structure show alignment between what the data suggests and how investors interpret it. Other 
factors, like industry experience, are underrepresented statistically but valued heavily in practice. 
Most critically, some of the investor’s most decisive criteria, including psychological alignment 
and interpersonal fit, are completely absent from pitch deck data. These gaps help explain why 
certain statistically average teams still raise significant funding, while seemingly qualified teams 
may be filtered out early. For early-stage investors, what matters most is often what cannot be 
quantified. 
5.3 Contextual Reflections (e.g. Danish VC scene) 
The statistical dataset used in this thesis is global in scope, with a substantial share of companies 
based in the United States and other major startup ecosystems. As such, the patterns identified 
through pitch deck analysis reflect international investment norms rather than any one national 
context. In contrast, the qualitative component (a semi-structured interview with a Danish 
investor) reflects local practices and cultural assumptions shaped by the Danish startup 
environment. This introduces an important limitation. The investor insights cannot be used to 
explain or predict patterns in the dataset directly, but they offer a valuable lens through which to 
interpret the limitations of purely quantitative screening and to reflect on how investor behavior 
is shaped by context. 
The Danish venture ecosystem is relatively small and primarily driven by local networks and 
investor-founder familiarity. An analysis of 880 publicly disclosed venture transactions between 
November 2014 and June 2025 reveals that early-stage funding remains modest in size. For the 
92 recorded pre-seed rounds, the median investment amount was DKK 3.73 million, with a range 
from DKK 0.5 million to DKK 5.89 million. Seed rounds were more common (n = 271) and had 
a median investment of DKK 12 million, with the largest reaching DKK 24.5 million. These 
funding patterns reflect a conservative investment environment in which Danish startups

typically raise smaller amounts than those seen in larger markets such as the United States or 
Germany. (EIFO, 2025) 
This cultural tendency aligns with broader research on Scandinavian management practices. 
Andersen and Pedersen (2023) note that decision-making in Danish and Norwegian professional 
settings is often guided by trust, transparency, and dialogue rather than rigid performance 
metrics. In the context of venture capital, this implies a strong preference for founder 
compatibility and long-term alignment, even when financial signals are weak. This may help 
explain why the interviewee emphasized team cohesion and personal alignment over more easily 
quantifiable attributes such as education level or market traction. 
Diversity emerged as a shared concern in both the global dataset and the broader European 
venture ecosystem. Although the dataset used in this thesis included only three all-female 
founding teams, which was too few for reliable statistical testing, the investor confirmed that this 
underrepresentation reflects their own experience. While the Danish early-stage landscape lacks 
detailed public funding breakdowns by gender, insights from the DACH region (Germany, 
Austria, and Switzerland) provide a useful point of comparison. The DACH ecosystem is larger 
and more mature than Denmark’s, which has enabled more extensive data collection, but the two 
regions share structural similarities in founder demographics and capital access patterns. In 2023, 
female-only founding teams in the DACH region raised just €0.13 billion in venture capital, 
compared to €8.67 billion raised by male-only teams and €0.62 billion by mixed-gender teams 
(see Figure 10). These disparities highlight persistent gender imbalances in European venture 
funding and reinforce the investor’s observation that all-female teams are rarely present in early-
stage pipelines. Although gender composition did not produce statistically significant results in 
the quantitative analysis, the limited representation itself signals a broader asymmetry in how 
opportunity is distributed and perceived.

6. Discussion 
This chapter interprets the results of the thesis in relation to existing research and practical 
venture capital decision-making. The study set out to examine whether observable founder team 
characteristics are associated with valuation outcomes at the pre-seed and seed stages. While the 
statistical analysis revealed patterns of significance for education and team size, other variables 
such as gender composition and industry experience showed more limited or inconclusive 
results. For reminder, the thesis asked the research question: 
What role do founder team characteristics play in determining the valuation of early-stage 
startups during their first funding round (pre-seed or seed)? 
These findings suggest that certain founder attributes may influence investor perception, 
especially when tangible performance data is limited. Education and team structure appear to act 
as reliable signals, while other factors may depend more heavily on context, investor preference, 
or additional qualitative inputs. This reflects broader literature on early-stage investing, which 
often emphasizes heuristic-driven evaluation when standard financial metrics are unavailable. 
The discussion that follows will synthesize these patterns, contrast them with existing studies, 
and integrate insights from the qualitative interview. It will also address key limitations in 
methodology, such as dataset size, variable granularity, and generalization across regions. 
Together, these elements provide a basis for evaluating how founder characteristics are used in 
practice and what role they may realistically play in shaping early-stage investment outcomes.

6.1 Interpretation of quantitative findings 
The analysis shows that certain founder traits may carry measurable weight in early-stage 
investment decisions, while others appear context-dependent or inconclusive. These results 
reflect both the interpretive nature of early-stage investing and the practical constraints of the 
available data. 
Education emerged as the most consistent signal of investor preference. Founding teams 
with at least a bachelor’s degree raised significantly more capital than those without any formal 
academic background. This supports the notion that education acts as a threshold signal of 
competence, particularly in the absence of hard financial metrics. The finding aligns with 
research on human capital theory, where formal education enhances perceived capability and 
legitimacy (Becker, 1964; Unger et al., 2011). However, no additional fundraising benefit was 
observed for teams with advanced degrees beyond the bachelor level. This plateau effect 
supports arguments made by Colombo and Grilli (2005), who suggest that marginal increases in 
academic capital may not translate into marginal gains in investor interest, especially at the pre-
seed stage. 
Team size also played a significant role, with three- to four-person teams raising more on 
average than solo founders or very small teams. This supports prior work emphasizing the 
advantages of diverse cognitive input and distributed workload within a team structure (Klotz et 
al., 2014). However, there was no observed funding premium for teams larger than four, which is 
consistent with findings from Franke et al. (2008) that suggest coordination costs, unclear roles, 
or reduced commitment can dilute the perceived strength of a large founding team. These 
dynamics are not easily captured in pitch decks, but investors may use team size as a rough 
proxy for capacity, complementarity, and cohesion. 
Gender composition showed no significant effect in the analysis, but the result requires 
careful interpretation. The dataset included only three all-female teams, making any statistical 
conclusion unreliable. Rather than reflecting a lack of gender-based differences, this finding 
more accurately points to an underlying issue of representation. Female founders remain a 
minority in early-stage venture transactions, both in this sample and across Europe. Public data 
==PAGE:55==
 
55
from EIFO (2025) confirms that all-female teams continue to receive a disproportionately small 
share of funding. The absence of statistical insight, in this case, is a product of structural 
imbalance, not investor neutrality. 
Industry experience showed marginal significance, with experienced founders raising slightly 
more on average, though the result did not meet standard confidence thresholds. This mirrors 
previous findings that highlight the conditional nature of experience effects. Dimov and 
Shepherd (2005) argue that industry-specific knowledge may boost investor confidence in 
certain contexts, but its relevance is shaped by other variables such as market familiarity and the 
perceived novelty of the business. In this study, the lack of granularity on experience quality, 
relevance, or depth limits the strength of any conclusion drawn from this variable. 
The inclusion of the EIFO dataset helps contextualize these findings within the Danish venture 
landscape. While the international pitch deck sample includes many ventures from high-capital 
ecosystems like the United States, EIFO’s record of 880 Danish transactions reveals much lower 
typical round sizes. The median Danish pre-seed investment is approximately DKK 3.73 million, 
with seed rounds averaging DKK 12 million. These benchmarks are important when interpreting 
funding outcomes. A five-million-kroner round might appear modest from a global perspective 
but would be considered substantial in Denmark. This difference underscores the need to 
interpret valuation signals relative to local funding norms, not just founder characteristics in 
isolation. 
In summary, the patterns suggest that investor perception is shaped by a mix of clear 
signals and contextual cues. Education and team size appear to influence valuation outcomes 
more consistently, while other factors such as gender or experience require either more granular 
data or more diversified samples to yield meaningful conclusions. These findings support the 
view that early-stage investment decisions combine both quantitative indicators and broader 
interpretive judgments, which are further explored through the qualitative lens in the next 
section.

6.2 Triangulating with investor perspectives 
The quantitative results from the pitch deck dataset point to patterns that are consistent with 
literature on human capital and team composition. Yet early-stage investing often hinges on 
unspoken assessments and interpersonal dynamics that do not appear in a dataset. To better 
understand how these signals are interpreted in real-life decisions, this section draws on insights 
from a Danish venture capital partner. Rather than validating or contradicting the statistical 
outcomes, the interview reveals what data cannot easily capture: how signals are interpreted, 
when uncertainty is high, and what drives conviction without numbers. 
i) Formal signals open doors, but do not drive decisions 
Formal credentials, such as education and industry experience, appear to serve as initial filters. 
Teams without academic background raised significantly less in the dataset, and the investor 
acknowledged that education often serves as a basic proxy for capacity. However, once that 
threshold is passed, other criteria take precedence. The investor stressed that “having a PhD 
doesn’t mean much if the team can’t explain what they want to do.” This nuance helps explain 
why there were no strong differences between bachelor’s, master’s, and PhD-holding teams in 
the quantitative results. It is not the level of the credential but what the founder does with it that 
matters. This interpretation aligns with research by Colombo and Grilli (2005) and Kotha and 
George (2012), both of whom suggest that investors care more about applied problem-solving 
ability than academic prestige. Education is not ignored, but it rarely secures an investment on its 
own. 
ii) Founding teams as trust configurations 
While team size showed statistical significance, the underlying driver appears to be alignment 
and role clarity, not headcount alone. The investor reflected on situations where “if you have 
six founders, half might drop out,” raising concerns about cap table instability and incentive 
misalignment. These dynamics correspond to what practitioner’s call “Dead Equity” - when 
early co-founders remain on the cap table despite no longer contributing value. The dataset’s 
finding that mid-sized teams (three to four members) raised more may reflect an optimal tradeoff 
between diversity and cohesion.The conversation also showed that investors are less concerned 
==PAGE:57==
 
57
with structure and more concerned with interpersonal function. Role distribution, 
complementary skillsets, and clarity of ownership matter more than whether a team has two or 
five members. These are hard to infer from pitch materials alone but are often explored through 
due diligence conversations and founder interviews. 
iii) Interpersonal dynamics beyond the Pitch Deck 
The investor made repeated reference to soft signals like coachability, responsiveness, and 
chemistry. These traits were not measured in the pitch deck dataset yet were described as 
essential in deciding whether to proceed. In the investor’s words, “the real question is how they 
respond when challenged.” This reinforces findings from Chen et al. (2009), who argue that 
delivery style and founder affect significantly influence first impressions. It also reflects the 
reality that early-stage investing often happens before traction, product-market fit, or clear 
commercial signals exist. 
Importantly, the lack of emphasis on gender composition in the interview may say less about 
gender neutrality and more about the structural underrepresentation of female founders. As the 
investor noted, “we rarely see all-female teams.” The dataset echoed this, with only three all-
female teams recorded. This silence is not analytical; it is structural. It suggests that diversity 
issues often arise not during evaluation, but much earlier, in the visibility and inclusion of 
founders within the pipeline. 
6.3 Contribution to the understanding of Venture Capital  
This thesis contributes to a more grounded understanding of how early-stage investors assess 
founding teams by combining limited statistical patterns from pitch decks with qualitative 
insights from an active venture capital partner. While the sample size is relatively small and the 
quantitative findings are not definitive, the study still adds value by connecting observable 
founder characteristics with funding outcomes across real cases. Unlike survey-based studies that 
rely on self-reported preferences or hypothetical scenarios, this approach uses actual pitch 
materials to examine how traits like team size, educational background, and industry experience 
may relate to startup valuations. 
==PAGE:58==
 
58
The quantitative findings confirm that founder traits can be associated with differences in 
startup valuations, particularly in relation to academic background and team size. Startups with 
no formal education represented raised significantly less on average, suggesting that education 
serves as a basic filter. Similarly, founding teams with three to four members raised more than 
smaller or larger teams, which may reflect investor preferences for functional team dynamics and 
stable cap tables. These patterns add empirical depth to existing literature on human capital and 
team composition (Colombo & Grilli, 2005; Klotz et al., 2014). 
By supplementing this with qualitative insights, the thesis clarifies how these signals are 
interpreted in practice. For example, while academic background may serve as a filter, the 
interview indicated that communication skills and coachability are far more influential during 
actual evaluation. Similarly, team size becomes relevant only when it creates incentive 
misalignment or undermines clarity of ownership. These qualitative findings support existing 
research suggesting that many founder-related signals operate in context and cannot be fully 
captured through binary or categorical variables (Franke et al., 2008; Chen et al., 2009). 
The thesis also adds practical relevance by focusing on the pre-seed and seed stages, where 
financial and product-based signals are often weak or missing. This contrasts with most 
quantitative venture research, which tends to focus on later rounds where measurable traction 
exists. By isolating the earliest stages, the thesis highlights how team-level signals function in 
conditions of high uncertainty. 
Finally, the findings offer value to both founders and early-stage investors. Founders can use this 
analysis to understand how they may be perceived based on observable traits and where 
interpersonal communication and alignment matter most. Investors may find that the integration 
of pitch materials with softer, qualitative evaluation remains central to sound decision-making at 
the early stage.

6.4 Limitations and future directions for research 
 
1. Sample size and dataset composition 
The thesis is based on 100 early-stage pitch decks, with between 91 and 77 used for each 
statistical test depending on available data. This sample size enabled an exploratory 
quantitative approach but imposes clear limitations. In subgroup comparisons, such as 
gender composition or academic background, the number of observations was often too 
small to yield reliable significance testing. For instance, there were only three all-female 
teams in the dataset, preventing meaningful statistical inference in that category. 
The pitch decks are sourced globally, with a majority likely representing startups based in the 
United States. These cases operate in fundraising environments that differ significantly from 
regional ecosystems like Denmark, particularly in terms of round sizes, investor expectations, 
and startup maturity. The sample lacks geographic identifiers, making it difficult to account for 
regional funding norms in the statistical analysis. Time-series analysis used a larger set of 
entries, as year data was more consistently available than characteristic-level data. However, the 
lack of consistent data points across all years and the absence of temporal pairing with other 
variables limited the interpretive strength of the findings. 
2. Treatment of outliers 
Valuation outliers above $10 million were excluded from all quantitative tests. This threshold 
was based on the typical round sizes observed in EIFO’s national venture database, where pre-
seed and seed rounds in Denmark usually range between DKK 2 to 5 million. Although such 
rounds may be larger in markets like the United States, including extreme values would have 
distorted means and compromised comparability across founder subgroups. Nonetheless, 
excluding high-valuation outliers removes ventures that may still provide valid insights into 
founder signaling and investor behavior. 
 
 
==PAGE:60==
 
60
3. Assumptions in variable coding 
Founder traits were coded manually based on publicly available pitch materials. Variables such 
as education, team size, and industry experience were reduced to categories that could be 
consistently identified across entries. For example, educational attainment was coded by degree 
level, without accounting for institution prestige, field of study, or relevance to the startup’s 
domain. Similarly, industry experience was treated as a binary yes-or-no variable, regardless of 
depth, recency, or specificity. 
This simplified structure enabled structured analysis but excluded more complex behavioral and 
interpersonal signals. Other research, such as the study by Stoeckli et al. (2023), has explored 
founder personality traits using psychological models, offering a different lens for evaluating 
early-stage potential. In contrast, this thesis focused on attributes that could be derived directly 
from pitch decks and translated into a tangible dataset. 
4. Ecosystem mismatch and triangulation scope 
A qualitative interview was conducted with a partner at a Danish venture capital firm. While it 
provided strong insights into team assessment and investor thinking, it represents only one 
perspective within a small and relationship-driven ecosystem. The Danish venture scene differs 
structurally from the international dataset. Formal institutional investors such as EIFO rarely 
engage at the pre-seed level, meaning early-stage decisions are often made by angel investors or 
accelerators. This reduces the extent to which investor behavior in Denmark can be meaningfully 
compared to patterns observed in the dataset. 
The triangulation between quantitative and qualitative findings added interpretive depth but was 
limited in scale. The integration of one local interview with a globally sourced dataset can 
highlight possible alignments or disconnects, but it does not permit general conclusions about 
regional or international investor norms. 
5. Missed opportunities and late data inclusion 
==PAGE:61==
 
61
A complete dataset of Danish venture transactions from EIFO was identified late in the research 
process. Although not included in the statistical analysis, it was used descriptively to benchmark 
average round sizes in Denmark. Earlier access could have allowed for local comparison of 
funding patterns by team composition or sector. The dataset’s scope, however, is limited in terms 
of detailed founder-level variables, and its focus is skewed toward seed-stage and later 
investments where EIFO plays a role. 
6. Future research 
Future studies should aim for greater statistical power by using larger datasets. A sample of 
1,000 to 5,000 pitch decks would enable more robust tests across founder subgroups and 
facilitate multivariate modeling. This scale could be achieved through partnerships with large 
international venture firms, such as Sequoia Capital or Andreessen Horowitz, or with 
institutional accelerators that regularly handle pitch material at scale. While access to such data 
remains difficult, it would significantly strengthen the empirical foundation of early-stage team 
evaluation research. 
Another promising direction involves the integration of behavioral or psychological data, either 
through founder interviews or the use of AI-based personality analysis. Combining such insights 
with observable variables from pitch materials could clarify how visible signals correlate with 
less tangible but equally important founder attributes. 
Finally, further triangulation across multiple investor interviews and regional comparisons would 
offer a richer view of how decision-making varies by context. Studies could segment ecosystems 
by geography, investor type, or investment stage to examine how founder characteristics are 
interpreted under different risk conditions.

7. Conclusion 
 
This thesis examined how founder characteristics may influence early-stage startup valuations 
during the first funding round. Using a dataset of 100 pitch decks and a supporting investor 
interview, it tested whether traits like team size, gender composition, academic background, and 
industry experience correlate with funding outcomes. 
The findings suggest that academic background was the most consistent factor. Startups with at 
least one founder holding a formal degree tended to raise more than those without. While this 
supports the idea that education can serve as a positive signal to investors, the results for team 
size, industry experience, and gender composition were weaker and less consistent. In 
particular, the dataset included too few all-female teams to support meaningful conclusions about 
gender effects. 
All variables were based on what could be directly observed in the pitch decks. This limits the 
scope of the analysis, as many important factors, such as founder mindset, communication skills, 
and team chemistry, are not visible in these materials. This also distinguishes the thesis from 
studies that use personality tests or in-depth interviews to assess founder traits. 
The interview with a Danish investor helped contextualize the findings. While not statistically 
generalizable, the investor’s views aligned with some patterns in the data. However, the 
comparison is limited because the pitch decks represent a global sample, while the investor 
operates in a smaller and more relationship-driven local market. 
This thesis contributes a simple but replicable method for extracting founder data from pitch 
decks. While the sample size is too small for strong claims, the approach shows potential. Future 
studies could expand on this by collecting more pitch decks, partnering with VC firms or 
accelerators, or combining this method with founder interviews and outcome tracking. 
In short, this study offers an early look at what investors may pick up on when reviewing startup 
teams, and how visible characteristics could shape early funding. While limited in scope, it opens 
the door for future research and provides early-stage founders with insight into how they might 
present themselves more effectively.

8.2 Appendix B – Interview with Danish VC 
Dato: 20/6-2025 
Deltager: VC’ens navn udeladt af fortrolighedshensyn 
Formål: At indsamle kvalitative indsigter til specialet om hvilke founder-karakteristika der har 
betydning for VC-investeringer i pre-seed/seed-stadiet. 
 
------------------------------------------------------------ 
Interview – Dansk version 
 
Hvordan vurderer I founding teams i pre-seed og seed-stadiet? 
Allerede ved første pitch har vi en HR-medarbejder med, som vurderer teamdynamik og 
individniveau. Vi gennemfører en personlighedsbaseret vurdering for at identificere eventuelle 
outliers. Det handler især om at forstå, hvordan founders reagerer under pres, fx når pengene er 
ved at løbe tør, eller hvis de er uenige om strategi. Coachability er helt centralt. Vi investerer ikke 
nødvendigvis ikke i ægtepar eller single-founders, men vi har ikke faste regler imod det. 
 
Hvordan vægter I founders’ baggrund i forhold til fx produkt og marked? 
Vi investerer primært i deep tech, hvor det er essentielt, at founderne har en dyb teknologisk 
forståelse. Det kan være postdocs eller PhD’er, der har arbejdet i årevis med en teknologi. Vi er 
skeptiske over for innovationspersoner, som tager ejerskab i teknologier, de ikke forstår. Vi vil 
have nogen, der kan løse svære problemer – og som har vist, at de kan. 
 
Har I en præference for antallet af founders? 
Nej, vi har ikke en præference. Men det har konsekvenser. Har du seks founders, er der stor 
sandsynlighed for, at halvdelen falder fra, hvilket skaber problemer i cap table og 
incitamentsstrukturen. Vi ser på, om hver person har en klar rolle, ikke bare titler for titlernes 
skyld. 
 
Spiller kønssammensætning en rolle? 
Vi måler på det, men det er ikke en investeringsparameter. Vi har investeret i flere kvindelige 
founders, men det er ikke med vilje, vi vælger ud fra individ og team. Diversitet i alder, 
nationalitet og uddannelsesbaggrund er vigtige, men køn alene er ikke et kriterie for os. 
 
 
==PAGE:72==
 
72
Hvor vigtigt er brancheerfaring? 
Meget vigtigt. Vi ser gerne founders, der kommer fra branchen og forstår dynamikkerne. Har 
man fx arbejdet i industrien i 30 år og starter en virksomhed, så er det enormt værdifuldt. Mange 
deep tech-founders kommer dog fra forskning og skal suppleres med kommerciel forståelse. 
 
Vægter I uddannelsesniveau højt? 
Nej, det er ikke et formelt krav. Men det følger ofte med, fordi de fleste arbejder med tung 
teknologi. Derfor har de som regel lange uddannelser. Men vi vælger ikke ud fra graden alene. 
 
Hvor vigtig er kemi og evnen til at formidle visionen? 
Kemi og evnen til at kommunikere visionen er altafgørende. De skal kunne overbevise andre, 
investorer, medarbejdere, kunder. Hvis man ikke kan formidle det til os, så kan man heller ikke 
til markedet. Det handler om at kunne se ild i øjnene. 
 
Ændrer jeres vurderingskriterier sig fra pre-seed til seed? 
Ikke i forhold til teamet. Vores vurdering forbliver den samme. Teamet skal stadig kunne 
fungere, modstå pres og eksekvere. Men til seed har de typisk arbejdet sammen lidt længere, 
hvilket reducerer risikoen for founder break-ups. 
 
Er der founder-egenskaber, der bliver overvurderet eller undervurderet? 
Ja. Man skal passe på med at overvurdere fx storytelling og drive, hvis det ikke følges op af 
substans. Omvendt er den lidt stille, teknisk stærke founder måske undervurderet. Det er vores 
job som investorer at give dem den nødvendige støtte. 
 
Hvor stor en ejerandel går I typisk efter? 
Det deler vi ikke offentligt. Men vi går efter en fair andel fra starten, så vi undgår problemer i 
fremtidige runder. Det er kritisk for fondens samlede afkast, og derfor er det noget, vi eksekverer 
benhårdt på.

Interview – English Version 
 
How do you assess founding teams at the pre-seed and seed stage? 
Already at the first pitch, we include an HR specialist to assess team dynamics and individuals. 
We conduct a personality-based evaluation to identify outliers. It’s especially about 
understanding how founders act under pressure, for example, when funds are low or there's 
strategic disagreement. Coachability is key. We don’t avoid couples or solo founders, but we 
don’t have fixed rules either. 
 
How important is the founder’s background compared to product or market? 
We primarily invest in deep tech, where it’s essential that founders have deep technological 
expertise. This often means postdocs or PhDs who’ve spent years on the technology. We're 
skeptical of innovation professionals who claim ownership of technologies they don’t fully 
understand. 
 
Do you have a preferred number of founders? 
No preference, but it has consequences. If you have six founders, half might drop out, affecting 
cap table and incentives. Everyone should have a clear role, not just a title. 
 
Does gender composition matter? 
We track it, but it’s not an investment criterion. We’ve invested in many female founders, but not 
by design, we select based on the team and individual. Diversity in age, nationality, and 
educational background matters, but gender alone is not a filter. 
 
How important is prior industry experience? 
Very important. We value founders with insider knowledge of industry dynamics. If someone’s 
worked 30 years in an industry and launches a startup, it’s valuable. Many deep tech founders 
come from academia and need commercial support. 
 
Do you value academic background? 
It’s not a formal requirement. But in deep tech, founders often have long academic training. Still, 
we don’t invest based on degree alone. 
 
==PAGE:74==
 
74
How important is team chemistry and vision communication? 
Crucial. Founders must convince others, investors, employees, customers. If they can’t explain it 
to us, they can’t sell it to the market. We look for passion and clarity. 
 
Do your criteria change from pre-seed to seed? 
Not for the team. The same requirements apply; the team must execute and endure. At seed stage, 
we expect they’ve worked together longer, lowering the risk of founder breakups. 
 
Are any founder traits overrated or underrated? 
Yes. Traits like storytelling and drive can be overrated if not backed by substance. Quiet but 
technically strong founders are sometimes overlooked; we try to support them. 
 
What equity share do you typically seek? 
We don’t disclose exact targets, but we aim for a fair stake from the beginning to avoid problems 
in later rounds. It’s critical for the fund’s long-term returns.
