Abstract
One of the main contributors to deterioration of sandstone monuments is damage caused by
salt. Electro-desalination (ED) is a novel method for removing salt from porous materials
to prevent this. Its eﬃciency has recently been tested in the laboratory scale on controlled
samples, and it was therefore of interest to investigate the method on monuments inﬁltrated
naturally with salt, and this was a major task in the present work. Since this technique would
be applied to fragile monuments in practice, it was important to test if ED results in any
further damage to the treated material. Finally, methods for non-destructive testing (NDT)
and preventive measures were identiﬁed to present ED as a complete method to protect
against salt deterioration.
ED is based on applying an electric DC ﬁeld to an object and the ions from contaminating
salts are transported by electromigration into poultice at the electrodes. The electrode process
creates acid at the anode and alkali at the cathode and the poultice is used to neutralise these
eﬀects, as especially acidiﬁcation would cause damage to the object of interest.
In this project ED was executed on two ornamental stones taken from the Eremitage in
Dyrehaven which have been infected with salt naturally. The major salt was CaSO4. Parts
of the stones were kept as controls and comparisons were made with two ED treated stones for
pH, conductivity, salt content, porosity, density, capillary suction and compressive strength.
The pH increased at the cathode of both ED experiments as expected from previous ﬁndings
in literature, and the conductivity generally followed the salt content. Total sulfate removal
was achieved in the second experiment (97 %) but there was still conductivity in the stone,
which might be due to the hydrogen and hydroxide created by electrolysis. The ﬁrst stone
had a removal of over 40 % after 5 weeks of treatment, where the experiment was terminated
as the electrical potential increased rapidly, probably due to a lack of salt around the cathode.
The ﬁrst stone treated with ED was used for investigation of the material properties and in
comparison to the control stone no diﬀerence was found. The compressive strength seemed
to increase after ED treatment, but it is discussed that further studies are necessary, as
the treated stone were not fully desalinated. It is also discussed that the tests for material
properties should be repeated with samples that have been completely desalinated. Finally,
it was found that desalination by ED on actual sandstone monuments is possible even for
protruding parts and that there seems not to be any further damage from the treatment.
The test methods used in the present investigations for salt content are destructive, meaning
that it is necessary to drill or cut samples from the carved stone. This is not an option in
real life on historic monuments. Thus, a case study was carried out to investigate the cur-
rent methods for evaluating salt content and preventive measures against salt penetration,
and a study visit to Historic Environment Scotland was made. Three NDT methods for de-
tecting moisture were found and since salt aﬀects the moisture, these methods can also be
used to investigate the salt content. The methods currently in use are: Thermal camera,
Microwave moisture meter and Ground penetrating radar (GPR). The thermal camera de-
tects the energy emitted from a material. Microwave moisture meter uses transmitted and
reﬂected microwaves. GPR uses the reﬂection of electromagnetic pulses to investigate the
inner structure of a material. One NTD method dedicated to salt detection was also men-
tioned: X-ray ﬂuorescence (XRF), where x-ray’s of diﬀerent energy distribution is directed
at the area of interest and the returning rays indicates which minerals and salts are present.
All four methods strength and weaknesses are listed in the case study chapter. Using NTD
methods could make it easier to know when to use ED, instead of after damage has already
started. The methods could also be used for evaluation of when the ED treatment is ﬁnished.

1
Introduction
Cultural heritage in the form of architecture, sculptures and monuments are important ele-
ments in the built landscape of the modern world. These are a connection to the past, which
anchor our historical identity and remind us of our place in time and space. Despite long
levity these monuments will eventually deteriorate and their value will be lost.
One of the main contributors to this deterioration process in stone monuments is contaminat-
ing salt in the material. The salts crystallises and exerts pressure on the material from the
inside. This results in cracks, scaling and eventually to total destruction of the object. [1]
To counter this, salt in the material can be removed by the method Electro-desalination (ED)
that so far has been successful in the lab and has also shown promise in the ﬁeld. [2] Further
ﬁeld testing is still needed, and this study is an important step in this direction, as it includes
experiments on actual carved monumental stones, which were infected with salt naturally over
the past 200 years where they have been placed at the Hermitage in Jægersborg Dyrehave,
before their removal due to salt damage. They will be tested in the lab to investigate ED
on irregularly shaped stone and stones with natural infection of salt, as well as possible side
eﬀects on stone properties from the method.
The stones for the investigation are carved in sandstone, a common construction material for
monuments throughout Europe as well as other parts of the world. It is a porous material
that during the last centuries has been readily accessible and relatively easy to cut, compared
to for example granite.
The buildings and monuments of the 18th and 19th century are
now in a state of deterioration which leads to whole building stones taken out and replaced,
and ornamental details having to be re-cut and replaced. This disturbs the uniformity and
authenticity of the monument in the best cases, and in the worst cases it leads to a faster
deterioration of the original stone, if the wrong kind of material or methods are used in the
restoration. [3]
Removing the salt before the deterioration has reached a critical state would result in fewer
invasive restoration methods and keep the monuments original appearance. This could be
achieved using ED.
ED is a non-invasive treatment and should not aﬀect the stone after it has been executed, but
this remains to be ensured. [4] This study therefore includes tests on the sandstone before
and after ED on porosity, density, pore structure and compressive strength.
Finally, for this method to be a viable treatment for stone deterioration, it is important to
consider the entire circle of the deterioration process. How can a critical level of salt be found
before it reaches a level where stone decay accelerates? When is a desalination concluded?
After removing the salt, can it be ensured that no new salt comes into the material again?
This will also be discussed in this report.
1.1
Statement of intent
1) Desalination by ED of actual carved sandstone monuments, which has been naturally
contaminated with salt.
2) Investigating if ED is a safe method for preventing deterioration by strength testing and
investigation of density, porosity and pore structure.
3) Describe non-destructive methods for evaluating salt content and preventive measures
against salt penetration.

Theory
2.1
Salt deterioration
The mechanisms for deterioration of porous building materials is mainly crystallisation pres-
sure or hydration pressure, but as this ﬁeld needs further investigation to see if other factors
might also be contributing [1]. Salt crystallisation is often described as the most important
factor, which can be divided into two types, eﬄorescence and sub-eﬄorescence. The ﬁrst
is crystallisation on the surface of the material and the second is crystallisation inside the
material causing tearing in the pores of the material and scaling of the surface. [5]
The rate of deterioration from salt is dependent on the presence of water, the type of salts
and the porosity and interior surface of the material. [1]
Salts enter and move within porous materials when dissolved in water as liquid or vapour. [1]
For buildings, the main contributors of salt are groundwater entering through capillary rise
or rainwater being inﬁltrated into the material, either from the wind pressure when raining
or from pooling around ornaments or joints due to defective drainage [6]. The salt originates
from both the natural environment and human activity, for example: groundwater, marine
aerosols, farming and deicing salts. Some of the most common salts in building materials are
sodium combined with chloride, nitrite and sulphate. [2]
The Austrian Standards Institute have produced an evaluation of the damage of salt ions,
presented in Table 1 [7]. These values are used as limit values for the salt content in this
project, translated and recalculated from mass percentage to mg salt per kg stone.
This
standard is used as there are no Danish or common European standard for salt content in
masonry.

2.2
Current method for salt removal
Presently, the most used method for removing salt from monuments is poulticing. By applying
a wet poultice to the object, the salts closest to the surface dissolve and travel as ions into the
poultice by diﬀusion and advection. Diﬀusion is the result of molecules in a liquid striving
towards equilibrium, which in a porous material is restricted by the pore structure. Salt in
the material can be removed by diﬀusion by applying liquid with a lower salt concentration,
usually done in a poultice. This method is most eﬀective when there is a large concentration
diﬀerence in between the material and applied liquid and as the salt concentration is lowered
in the material the remaining salt will take longer to remove.
Advection is mass being
transported by a moving medium, such as dissolved ions in a moisture ﬂow. With the right
pore size distribution in the porous material and poultice used, capillary moisture ﬂow can be
established between the two. This requires smaller pores in the poultice than in the porous
material, in order for a pressure gradient to develop within the pore structure towards the
poultice.
Advection is the more eﬃcient transport mechanism of the two, but it will be

counteracted by diﬀusion as soon as the ions start to accumulate towards the poultice, and a
full removal is not possible by advection alone.[8]
The poulticing can be divided into two parts; ﬁrst water from the poultice penetrates the
material and dissolves the salt, then the salt ions are transported into the poultice by diﬀusion
or advection. For the wetting phase the poultice should have larger pores than the porous
material in order for the water to be absorbed. In the desalination phase the pores of the
poultice need to be smaller if advection is to be the main mechanism, which is the more
eﬃcient one. This means that if the same poultice is used for both wetting and desalination,
it needs a large spread in pore size distribution. [8]
The main challenges for poulticing is changes in the poultice porosity and pore size during
drying and achieving consistent contact with the material to be desalinated. This is because
shrinkage and cracking of the clay can lead to detachment of the poultice, but it can be
remedied by exchanging the poultice regularly. [8]
In a review [9] it was discussed that poulticing is unpredictable, as the movement of the
salt is dependent on the drying conditions and initial salt distribution of the object, both
factors which are hard to control outside of a lab. The importance of understanding the
transport mechanisms were also highlighted, as was tailoring of the application for each object
as diﬀerent material characteristics, initial salt content and drying conditions will inﬂuence the
outcome of the treatment. It is stated in [8] that ”there is no single ideal poulticing method
for extracting salts”, and that ”in practice one can never achieve complete desalination of a
nonmoveable object”.
2.3
Electro-desalinaton
The method has been developed as a new treatment for salt removal, as an alternative to
poulticing. By applying an electric ﬁeld to the object to be desalinated, the dissolved salt ions
within moist pores will be transported to the electrodes by electromigration. Charged species
will move in a solution towards the opposite pole, gathering towards it. [10] An illustration
of this with the example salt of sodium sulfate is shown in Figure 1, where calcium as the
positive ion migrates to the cathode and sulfate as the negative ion migrate to the anode.

As was seen in [11] and [5] acidiﬁcation of the stone can lead to severe damage or increased
porosity. Thus, the acid must be neutralised. In [12] it was proposed and in [13] it was
successfully shown that calcite rich clay poultices prevent acidiﬁcation at the anode. The
clay poultice also gathers salt for easy removal as well as ensuring good conductivity between
electrodes and stone.
An alkaline environment have not yet been found to damage masonry, however, a collision of
acid and alkali fronts can hinder the desalination. [14] By the use of a poulticing clay that
neutralises the acidiﬁcation, the possibility for pH collisions is prevented. [5]
One of the main advantages for ED is that in contrast to poulticing, the damaged area does
not need to be disturbed further by having a poultice placed on it as the electric ﬁeld is
applied over it instead. It has been found that the electrodes can be placed in diﬀerent setups
and still be eﬀective even if they are not directly opposite each other. [15] Also, several studies
have shown possibility for successful treatment on protruding and irregularly shaped objects
in the lab. [16] [17] [18]
ED has been shown eﬀective on many porous materials such as bricks, natural stones and
tiles. In the case of stone, both low and high porosity materials such as granite and sandstone
have had successful results. [19] There have been a few pilot scale experiments on walls with
successful results, however, full desalination on a pilot scale is yet to be executed. [2]
2.4
Side eﬀects of ED
In [11] ED was executed without any protective measurements against the pH changes that are
expected from the electrode processes. It was found that the stone materials were damaged
near the anode, where the acidic environment can break down both calcareous stones and
mortars. It is therefore necessary to use some form of buﬀering to prevent this.
Apart from acidiﬁcation, there is also an increase in pH by the cathode. This is not necessarily
bad for sandstone, and it has been investigated if there have been a diﬀerence in compressive
strength from ED [5], which did not indicate any decrease. This needs to be conﬁrmed further
as well as taking into account that the longer ED is taking place, the higher the pH gets at
the cathode, as this is not buﬀered by the clay as the acidiﬁcation by the clay at the anode.
Therefore, if the treatment is of long duration, a very high pH can be reached. This partially
means that the pH is altered in one part of the stone, but it also means that the pH is very
diﬀerent in diﬀerent sides of the object. It could be that this pH gradient is of inﬂuence for
the stones concerning porosity, density or capillary structure.
Conductivity in stone is dependent on the presence of moisture and charged ions.
It is
therefore ﬁrstly important to have a certain level of water content within the stone. So far,
successful desalination has taken place in as low water content as 1,3 % [20], but if the stone
is completely dry no ions can move and there will be no conductivity or desalination. The

The clay introduces a possible new challenge if one of the salts to be removed is sulfate.

concerns towards it is dependent on whether or not it disturbs the desalination process.
Finally, it has not yet been proven that ED does not disrupt the chemical bonding or in other
ways lead to further deterioration of the object, this study will look further into that.
2.5
Material properties as inﬂuential factors
Porosity describes the amount of void space in a material, and therefore how much moisture
it can absorb. A higher porosity indicates more space available for water and with that more
salt. It also makes the material more fragile. [22] Porosity is found by identifying the bulk
density ρb, which is the density including all pores and solid density ρs, which is the material
without any pores. These values can be determined by the Archimedes buoyancy method,
which will be further explained in Section 5 and the porosity of the material can then be
calculated using Equation 3. By comparing both porosity and densities with standard values
for a chosen material, the validity of the measurements can be ensured. [23]
p = 1 −ρb
ρs
(3)
Apart from the porosity, the pore structure can also describe the material. This can be divided
into pore distribution and form. This means small or big pores, amount of each of these and
if the pores are going all the way through the material, only partly so, or are closed oﬀ. These
properties inﬂuence how much and how fast water can be absorbed by pore suction. [22]
For a porous material the most important strength property is compressive strength, which is
diminished with moisture present. The most common way to test this is by applying pressure,
mainly static short term testing. The compressive strength, fc is the force used to brake the
sample divided with the area that the pressure was asserted on. It is optimal to have samples
with the height being twice as large as the diameter or depth of the sample. [22]

3.1
Poultices
Poultices are used to ensure good conductivity between the electrodes and the stones. They
also work to collect the dissolved ions. [24] It was found early on when developing ED that
it is also necessary to use a poulticing clay to prevent acidiﬁcation from damaging the stone
at the anode [11] and other studies have found the same thing [5]. Which clay to use was
investigated and the one currently in use (also in this project) was found to be the most
successful. [13]
3.2
Diﬀerent salts
There have been several studies in the lab investigating ED of single salts [25] [26] [15] [17]
[19] as well as a few with mixed salts [16] [18]. They have all shown positive results and
there is no indication of which salts present are inﬂuencing the success of ED. It has been
discovered however that sulphate is the slower of the negative ions to be desalinated [26] [5]
and there are indications that it would be more damaging than the other ones [25]. Studies
of ED on sandstone naturally contaminated with salt have also been executed, but without
knowing the original salt content value, it has however been concluded that it is possible to
remove salt from stones in an uncontrolled environment [20] [16].
3.3
Diﬀerent stones
Several diﬀerent kinds of sandstone have been tested for ED, including: Cotta, Posta, Nexø,
Obernkirchen and Gotland sandstone. [13] [20] [5] There have also been a comparison between
the porous sandstone and granite. [19] So far all of the tested materials have shown any
success with ED and it seems that a higher interconnection between the pores in the stone
being desalinated improves the speed of the desalination process. [19]
Diﬀerent shapes and setups have also been tested. A square block with electrodes on opposite
sides have been shown most eﬀective, but successful results have been shown for placing the
electrodes parallel as well. [15] It has been concluded that a bigger surface area covered with
electrode units result in a higher eﬃciency for desalination. [27] Successful investigations have
been made of ED on sandstone with irregular shape where protruding areas are in no contact
with electrodes and are not in the direct DC ﬁeld. [17] [18] One have indicated that it is
possible to desalinate monuments that have been naturally contaminated with salt. [16]
3.4
Matrix changes to stone
Two studies have been executed to investigate the damaging eﬀects of ED to sandstone. The
ﬁrst one investigated the porosity before and after ED and did not see any change. [5] The
second one compared visual damage as well as compresive strength after the stones were put
through two drying/wetting cycles. No formation of new salts or dissolution of the carbonate
phase were seen and the measured strength was not decreased. However, there have been a
call for more samples to be able to conclude that no damage have been done. [4]

3.5
Pilot scale experiments
There have been a few pilot scale experiments of ED. The ﬁrst was executed on a brick wall
with a high sulphate content and after 4 months of treatment there was still sulphate present.
Some had been removed, as had the lower contents of nitrite and chloride. [28] The second
pilot scale experiment was executed on both brick and limestone masonry. Electrode units for
ED was developed and tested, which were drilled into place. They contained a metallic mesh
as an electrode that was placed in a case with a loose bottom, in order to ensure continuous
pressure between clay and masonry. The experiments were executed for 4-8 months and a
reduction for chlorides was found but full desalination was not achieved. [29] The latest pilot
scale was an ED test of 25 m2 on a historic limestone wall infected naturally with sodium
chloride. Using 72 electrode units with 50 cm in between each for about a year, 6,3 kg sodium
chloride was removed. However, full desalination was not achieved and further development
of the method and the application was advised. [30]
3.6
Summation of previous research
It has been established that a poulticing clay is necessary to prevent acidiﬁcation in order
to avoid damage of the stone and that calcium clay has been found to be the most suitable
poultice. The salts that are investigated is most often containing chloride, nitrate or sulphate.
It has been found that there are diﬀerent removal rates for these, with sulphate being the
slowest one to remove, and which also seems to be the most damaging one. Placement of
electrodes and the area covered in clay has an inﬂuence on the eﬃciency of the method.
Electrodes opposite of each other is most eﬀective, as is a larger surface area for the clay. The
speed of the desalination is also dependent on the pore structure of the stone, with a higher
interconnection in between pores improving the transport.
It has been shown that there is potential for removing salt from irregular shaped objects
with uneven salt distribution. So far there have been successful experiments in the lab of
irregularly shaped stone with uniform salt content. Several pilot scale tests have been carried
out with success, but there is yet to be one that achieves full desalination.
One experiment have been made where no new salts were forming, there was no dissolution
of carbonate phase in the sandstone and there was no decrease in measured strength. Further
studies will have to repeat the success before it can be conﬁrmed that there is no damage to
the desalinated stone.
3.7
Knowledge gap
The next step is to successfully desalinate an irregularly shaped object with inhomogeneous
salt distribution while investigating that no damage have been done to the stone during the
process.
Eventual damaging eﬀects could come from the increased pH at the cathode or the gradient
of pH within the stone after desalination or formation of gypsum when calcium from the clay
bonds with the sulphate in the stone. These or other unknown eﬀects could have an eﬀect on
the material matrix or strength and should therefore be further investigated.
A full desalination made on pilot scale for a naturally contaminated object in-situ is yet to
be achieved and should be a priority in further studies.

4.2
Method
The installation of the electrodes to the stones were done by attaching a layer of clay between
the electrodes and the stone, such that a good electrical connection was made.
Without
this clay connection it is harder to obtain a transfer of current between electrodes and the
stone. At the same time environmental alterations such as pH changes is avoided in the stone
material. The clay was changed once a week for the ﬁrst experiment and more optimised for
the second, relying on the resistance measurements for when the clay needs changing, which
would be how it should be done in a real setting. Water content was measured on the used
clay for controlling a stable homogeneous behaviour of the clay connection. The water content
of the clay needs to be above 15 % in order for the ED to function optimal [31]. The clay
used was found to be the most eﬀective to buﬀer the acid at the anode [13], which is created
through electrolysis, previously explained in Chapter 2.
The clay is made by mixing the following recipe in a blender until the consistency is right.
• 600 g CaCO3
• 300 g Kaolin
• 640 g Distilled water
Due to the carvings on the stone, it would be diﬃcult to make forms that would be useful for
the experiment. Instead, the clay was be shaped in cling ﬁlm and the proper size had to be
estimated. Since the stones are larger than in previous studies [15] [17], it would be suitable
if there is more clay and a larger surface area of the electrodes. For this to work, the clay has
to have the right consistency, as it can not be too wet, but the water content should still be
enough to ensure high conductivity. The electrodes are placed directly into the clay, and the
setup is shown in Figure 4.
The current is set to 10 mA, and will be maintained at this level throughout the experiments.

4.3
Measurements to ensure eﬀect and continuity of the treatment
To control continuity throughout the object of interest, water content before and after the
treatment was measured. An initial damage assessment was made through pH measurements,
to be certain that the object has not become acidic at the anode during the treatment.
Conductivity was measured to see the pattern of the current, as well as give an indication
of the method’s eﬃciency.
The real test for eﬃciency is the salt content measurements,
which shows how much of each salt ion is left in the stone after the treatment. How these
measurements are made is described in the following section.
4.3.1
Preparing samples
Since the stones were already salt damaged by uncontrolled natural causes, it is not possible
to know the exact salt content of the stone before performing the experiments. However, the
stones are symmetrical around a vertical axis, and it is assumed that the salt content will be
similar throughout the stone around this axis. Therefore, the ﬁrst stone is cut along the axis,
one half kept as a control and ED is executed on the other half.
The salt content was investigated by drilling three holes close to each other, to the depths 1,
2, 3,5 and 5 cm into the stone, in three diﬀerent places, as shown in Figure 5. The dust from
drilling is then used as samples where pH, conductivity and salt content is investigated. The
same method is applied to ER1V after ED for easy comparison.
During the execution of the ﬁrst experiment (ER1), it was found that the drilling method
for investigating salt content was not optimal, as the sand given by this drilling were not
suﬃcient for full 10 g samples for further testing which are the standard used in the lab
to ensure stable measurements.
Therefore it was decided that the next stone should be
investigated in a diﬀerent manner.
The second stone (ER2) was cut in three pieces by saw. Two halves, with a small section from
the middle kept as a control. This gives a full proﬁle of the salt content of the stone, and still
keeping most of the stone for testing. The control, ER2C, was segmented by hammer and
chisel into 16 pieces seen on Figure 6, one of them being the horn. In analysing ER2V, another
section was cut and segmented in the same way as ER2C when the experiment ﬁnished.

Measurements to investigate side eﬀects and damage
Apart from investigating the success of the ED treatment it was also of interest to investigate
side eﬀects or damage done by the treatment to the stone in the present work. Therefore
XRD, porosity, density, pore suction and compressive strength tests were made on samples

4.4.1
Preparing samples
The same grounded stone samples as in previous test were used for the XRD tests, to inves-
tigate if there was gypsum forming in the stone as a side eﬀect of the ED treatment.
To ensure that ED does not damage the stone on which it is used, the material properties
of ER1 was investigated before and after treatment; porosity, density, capillary suction and
compressive strength. If non of these characteristics changed by use of ED on already fragile
monuments, it will indicate that ED is a viable method for salt removal.
The porosity, density and pore suction have been measured on the same stone segments.
They were cut by saw around the drilling holes of ER1, of the dimensions 55x22x22 mm. The
placement of each individual segment is not known. The compressive strength were measured

placement within the stone.

XRD

ER1. The sand from before was grounded to a ﬁne dust, and then thoroughly packed in
moulds for the XRD apparatus. The moulds were placed in the centre of the machine and
settings are changed depending on which kind of material is investigated. A day later the
results are given as peaks on a graph which has to be analysed. There is a minimum 1 %
content that can be detected by the XRD, which means that small amounts of a substance
are not identiﬁed. [34]

4.4.3
Porosity and density
Porosity and density were measured using a scale that can measure under and over water.
The samples were dried, weighted and then put in a desiccator for 3 hours. Distilled water
is passed into the desiccator until the water level was 3 cm over the samples and was then
left for an hour. After this the tap was opened and the samples were left in the desiccator
overnight. Each sample was then weighted under water and then dabbed with a cloth and
weighted again over water. Using the density of water, the volumes of the samples and the
pores can be calculated, and from there the density and porosity. [35]
4.4.4
Capillary suction
One of the main moisture transports in porous materials are capillary suction, especially
important when looking at groundwater rise. This property in the material was calculated,
ﬁrst by using dry samples, measuring the length, depth and width and weight. The samples
were then placed in a tray with distilled water, ﬁlled to 5 mm up on the samples.
The
weight was noted at increasing section of time, after 1 min, 2 min, 4 min, 8 min and so on,
up to 6 hours later. The room and water temperature was noted to ensure continuity and
the absorbed water was calculated as kg/m2. The curve obtained by plotting the absorbed
water over time indicates the pore structure of the sample. Small pores in the capillary range
transport water deeper into the material then large pores. [36]
It is suggested that in each circle, only measure 3 samples at the time, as it might take up
to 20 s to weigh the samples. New circles can then be started with 5 min intervals up to 3
times, after this it has to be further ensured beforehand that new measurements does not
bump into the previous circles.
4.4.5
Compressive strength
Compressive strength is used as a measurement as to how durable a material is. In order to
investigate if any damage was done to the stone by ED, compressive tests were carried out
on stone segments from both the control and treated stone of ER1.
The compression test consisted of applying pressure to air dried segments of the stones.
Pressure was applied to the breaking point, and the ﬁnal force before break is the compressive
strength of the sample. The results are averaged and compared.

Salt content of clay
Using the molar concentrations the content of water extractable sulphate and calium in the
clay poultices can also be found. Knowing the weight of the poultice, these values can be
added up and a total accumulated amount of ions in the poultice clay is found. This is shown
in Figure 32 for sulphate and Figure 33 for calcium.
Both sulphate and calcium is from the anode since virtually no calcium nor sulphate was
found in the cathode.
This is to be expected for the sulphate, as the negatively charges
ion travels to the positive electrode. However, the calcium is positively charged and should
be found by the cathode. That it is found in the anode is probably due to the electrolysis
that is happening at the anode releases positively changed hydrogen ions that dissolves the
carbonate in the clay, as it is designed to do. This is also the reason for ﬁnding more calcium
than sulphate ions.

Case study on non-destructive methods
In order to oﬀer electro-desalination as a complete solution to salt deterioration it must be
investigated how to evaluate the salt content without damaging the object in question, as
well as identify measures to avoid new salt from penetrating the material.
Grundejernes
Investeringfond (GI) have done the most recent and extensive study on these subjects in
Denmark [38], with a focus on masonry, but the principles are transferable to sandstone.
To obtain more information on current methods internationallym a visit was arranged with
Historic Environment Scotland (HES) as a part of the current work. HES is working in a
country where the capital is on the UNESCO world heritage list with its unique sandstone
buildings, and this organisation is on the forefront of sandstone monuments and how to care
for them. They are experts within conservation science and work on historic sandstone objects
regularly. The iconic Edinburgh Castle is one of many monuments in their care.
6.1
Evaluating salt content
In this study the salt content is determined by the use of IC and ICP. These methods does
however require drilled or cut samples of the object to be ground into sand before the testing
31
==PAGE:37==
L. Andersson
Development and test of electro-desalination of sandstone monuments
can commence. It is therefore of interest to look into alternative non-destructive methods for
evaluating salt content.
GI have listed the current methods used in Denmark for evaluating water content, as their
main focus have been the moisture and not salt. It is noted that salt in the moisture can
heavily inﬂuence the measurements and ultimately give completely wrong results [38].
The methods in the GI report includes drying/weighting (of a cut sample from the material
as used in the present investigation). This is the reference of all other methods. A material
sample could also be obtained from drilling, but there is a loss of moisture from the drilling
process and thus the result is not fully correct. Other methods reported follow: Troxler is a
radioactive moisture density gauge, which is aﬀected by salt and requires drilling an access
hose into the tested material ([39]). HF sensors are microwave moisture meters, which in
the last few years have become available as both portable and cost eﬀective ([40]). Gypsum
or wooden bits built into the material is invasive, takes a long time and require the right
moisture content to be eﬀective. RF/temp is also a very slow measurement, and require a
specialist to do the measuring. These methods all have their strength and limits, but so far
dying/weighting is still the most accurate of the current methods. [38]
While only one of the methods mentioned in GI are entirely non-destructive (HF-sensor), HES
are using and investigating possibilities of several non-destructive techniques for moisture and
salt detection. The main ones currently in use are Thermal camera, Microwave moisture
meter, Ground penetrating radar (GPR) and X-ray ﬂuorescence (XRF). [3]
Especially the thermal camera is useful in HES’s current work. Just when HES were trying
it out at the castle courtyard of Edinburgh Castle, heat pipes were found that no one knew
was there. When they were researching the Argyle tower in the castle they found pooling
of moisture in the ceiling which is believed to be the cause of eﬄorescence of salts in the
ceiling.
However, they have yet to ﬁnd the source of the moisture.
Knowing where the
pooling is located is still a very useful tool for understanding and correcting salt and moisture
problems. The other techniques are still being implemented into the restoration activities and
using a combination of these methods could lead to a more precise picture of the moisture
and salt in the masonry. However, no combination have yet been researched. [3]
6.1.1
Thermal camera
The camera uses infrared thermography to sense the radiated, reﬂected and transmitted
energy and converts this into temperature and displays it as thermal images. The values are
aﬀected by the surrounding temperatures, but it is still possible a correct interpretation of
the measured materials moisture condition. It is as easy to use as a photographic camera
and is entirely non-destructive.
Its main use for historic buildings is to detect moisture,
failings in the construction and plumbing conditions. It might not provide a precise moisture
measurement yet, but so far it is a user-friendly detection of moisture and deterioration within
masonry. [41]
6.1.2
Microwave moisture meter
This technique which includes the HF-sensor uses microwaves to be transmitted and reﬂected
through the material. The measurements are dependent on the material’s permittivity, the
ability to resist an electric ﬁeld, and are therefore strongly aﬀected by moisture as it has a high
permittivity. The measurements can be carried out without contact and with a low power
32
==PAGE:38==
L. Andersson
Development and test of electro-desalination of sandstone monuments
microwave, which makes it a non-destructive method for moisture content on all materials.
Since this method is dependent on diﬀerent materials properties, it is also sensitive to the
presence of salt. [42]
6.1.3
Ground penetrating radar (GPR)
GPR is a high resolution radar that can be used for condition surveys of amongst natural stone
and masonry. It uses electromagnetic pulses into the material and measures the variation
that is reﬂected within the material.
It is dependent on the material’s conductivity and
dielectric permittivity, which has to be calibrated when doing measurements. Its main use
is to investigate cracks but can also be used to ﬁnd water or salt when the electro-magnetic
parameters change within the material. [43]
6.1.4
X-ray ﬂuorescence (XRF)
A method for determining the composition of a material, including salts, is XRF. It consists of
an X-ray generator and detector, to emit and receive X-rays with diﬀerent energy distribution.
The sensitivity on the generator can be enhanced using diﬀerent ﬁlters and the detector has
an ampliﬁer that makes detection easier. The method can be used in the open air and no
special preparation of the sample is necessary. [44]

values to see if it would be around or over the critical values for action.
6.2
Preventing salt penetration
Winkler (1988) [6] states: ”Preventing the access of moisture is the most natural but most
diﬃcult way to preserve stone”.
If no moisture can inﬁltrate into the stone, neither can the salt, and therefore the most natu-
ral way to prevent salt deterioration would be to prevent moisture entering the structure. GI
lists solutions for preventing moisture from the ground in form of membranes and remedia-
tion. There are physical and chemical membranes, and the remediation are electrochemical,
impulses or ventilation. The remediation are not widely tried in Denmark. The membranes
both require invasive actions such as sawing or drilling, and is therefore not recommended
for use on historical monuments. The physical membrane consists of a stainless steel plate
or similar, being placed in a grove that have been sawed into the base of the building. The
grove is then ﬁlled up with mortar similar to the original material. The chemical membrane
is put in place by drilling holes into the material, then inserted with either a chemical that
ﬁlls up the pores, minimising the pore diameters or with chemicals that makes the surfaces of
the pores hydrophobic. These eﬀects can also be combined. The chemical is more moisture
reducing that actual barrier, and this method is greatly aﬀected by the salt content in the
material. [38]
All the mentioned methods have reservations and none of them are recommended as the
primary solution to groundwater rise. Instead, it is recommended to remove eventual reasons
for moisture pooling, such as broken drains, ﬁlled up gutters. If the problem is on the inside
consider condensing water and installing ventilation or increase the heat. A drainage can be
installed around the monument to divert water pooling around the base. [38] This was indeed
done at the Scottish National War Memorial at Edinburgh Castle recently by HES, with great
success. [3] In conclusion, GI found that there are no non-invasive treatments and moisture
barriers should therefore only be installed if it is entirely necessary and no other solutions
can be found. [38] This sentiment is echoed in HES and as they try to prevent using invasive
treatments which could lead to further damage later.
[3] That there are no non-invasive
methods for preventing salt penetration makes it all the more important to develop ED as a
non-destructive method remove salt before it does any damage.

7
Discussion
7.1
Desalination by ED of carved sandstone monuments, which has been
naturally contaminated with salt
ED was successfully executed on a carves sandstone, the challenges and results are discussed
further here.
ED is dependent on electrical conductivity and continuity throughout the stone, i.e. moist
pores with ions. The voltage was quite stable (reﬂecting the resistivity as the current was
maintained constant) for ER2V which also indicates that the water content was suﬃcient
throughout the treatment. For ER1V the DC electricity was disrupted, but based on previous
studies [20] the water content of the stones is high enough for this not to be the problem.
Instead the DC electricity was probably disrupted for ER1V due to the removal of ions at the
cathode, eﬀectively isolating the electrode from the anode. This could have been overcome
by moving the cathode closer to the anode, out of the desalinated area. However, without
a non-destructive method for investigating the salt content in the material, this would have
been hard to detect outside of a lab setting and without discontinuing the experiment.
Having the knowledge that an electrode can become isolated can be used, if similar problems
arise in further studies. Then the experiment can be paused and one or more of the identiﬁed
NDT methods (identiﬁed in the case study) can be used to investigate the areas for remaining
salt content. If the area around one or both electrodes becomes desalinated, the NDT can
be used to investigate where to place the electrodes for further desalination. The NTD could
also be used before ED to analyse the area of interest in order to design the best solution for
eﬀective removal of the salts.
There was complete removal of sulphate in ER2V without the expected increase in electric
potential as seen in previous studies [15] [17]. This is probably due to the electrolysis reaction
that is taking place as long as water is present in the system and creates hydroxide, which is
highly conductive. Hydrogen is also created in this process but is neutralised in the poultice
and releasing calcium ions for electromigration. The previous studies [15] [17] were executed
in closed systems without external water that is needed in case the stone dries out. In real
life situations water is usually always present, which could lead to the same phenomenon that
the potential will stay low despite full desalination. Another method for investigating the
extent of desalination in real life situations will therefore be needed. Again NTD could be
used during treatment to investigate the situation and when to discontinue desalination.
The higher salt content in the stone at the anode of ER1V correlated with the conductivity
and is gathered by the anode as is expected from previous studies where full desalination was
not achieved [15] [17]. The initial thought for why the electric potential increased was that
there might have been gypsum forming and clogging the pores as suggested in the simulation
study [21]. However, using the XRD did not show any gypsum as in Ottosen’s studies [4]. In
ER2V there also seems to have been a full desalination without interruption from gypsum,
suggesting that gypsum formation is not an issue.
7.2
Investigating if ED is a safe method for preventing salt deterioration
No further damage have been detected on the stones treated with ED, the relevant tests to
support this is discussed below.
Damage by acidiﬁcation during treatment have been avoided by the use of poulticing clay

L. Andersson

as none of the treated stones have showed a pH below 7 [11]. The pH was increased at the
cathode for both experiments, although not as much as expected from previous studies [15]
[17]. This is possibly due to the hydroxide, that is responsible for the increase, bonding with
calcium that was released from the poultice clay migrating towards the cathode. This would
also explain why no free calcium ions were detected within ER2V, or in the cathode clay for
either of the experiments.
It has previously been indicated that there is no damage to sandstone by ED treatment [5]
[4]. The current work supports this. The porosity, density and pore structure tests showed no
matrix change. From the results of the compressive strength tests it seemed that the strength
of the treated stone is not diminished, but actually increased. If it as previously discussed
is because calcium and hydroxide precipitates within the stone, this is highly interesting for
future developments. All this will, however, have to be studied further.
7.3
Describe non-destructive methods for evaluating salt content and pre-
ventive measures against salt penetration
Several times in this discussion the use of NDT have been mentioned as a solution to a
problem, and it is very possible that the currently available NDT could solve the relevant
problems. Indeed, it is stated in [45] that it is vital to have a precise damage diagnosis for
conservation of monuments. This can be achieved by better understanding of the building
materials and the processes of stone decay and NTD methods are the key to achieve this for
cultural heritage. The
Four non-destructive testing methods were found in the case study: Thermal camera, Mi-
crowave moisture meter, GPR and XRF. The ﬁrst three measure moisture, and are all aﬀected
by salt content if it is present within the material. The last one investigates the molecular
components of the material, including salt if it is present. By using a combination of these
methods a clear picture of a material’s moisture and salt content could be mapped. Doing
this would present and opportunity to execute ED before damage has happened. It would also
produce information that could lead to understanding where the moisture and salt is coming
from, possibly preventing new damage before there is need for desalination. The NDT can
also be used prior to an ED action, to place the electrodes in the most optimal way.
The method for investigating the material composition can indeed detect salt, but would have
the same challenges as the XRD with detecting components below a certain concentration.
It has been stated by some sources that the techniques need further development [38] [3],
but it has also been argued that with more understanding of the techniques and better
communication between practitioners the usability of these methods will increase [46].
No non invasive treatments against salt inﬁltration were identiﬁed in the case study. There are
invasive methods to prevent moisture entering a structure, including physical and chemical
barriers. None of these are therefore recommended for use on historical monuments if it is
possible to avoid it.
7.4
Further studies
Based on the results in this study, it was indicated that the compressive strength of the treated
stone increased during ED, without larger changes in density, porosity or pore structure. If
there is an increase in strength changes in these parameters must be expected. Further studies
should therefore again include techniques for characterisation of the stone, which have a higher

sensitivity than the used.
A study of which minerals are in place before and after desalination would also be interest-
ing, to see if there is calcium hydroxide, and if so, to investigate if that would improve the
compressive strength without aﬀecting the other material properties.
It would also be interesting to take samples from the same stone, half as control and half as
treated and fully desalinated, and then to use these samples for material properties testing,
then strength test and ﬁnally pH, conductivity and salt content. Ultra sound tests could also
be used to investigate any cracks in the material before crushing it in the strength test and
onward. This would give a more clear picture of each individual sample’s composition and
to possibly see a correlation between certain material properties and the salt content, before
and after treatment.
In order to see if any of the current NDT could be used together with ED it would be
interesting to do a study in the same manner as [17]. Samples from the same stone could be
taken at bi-weekly intervals to follow the development of the treatment and NTD could be
used before and after on each sample to investigate how accurate each method is. Based on
this it could be evaluated how to best use each of the NDT together with ED.
8
Conclusion
Two carved sandstone ornaments from the Hermitage in Jægersborg Dyrehave that were
naturally contaminated with salt have been treated with ED. One stone was treated for 5
weeks and the other for 14 weeks. Sulphate was removed with a success rate of 44% and 97
% respectively. Protruding parts showed a sulphate reduction of up to 98 % in the longer
experiment, showing the potential of ED to desalinate sculptures and ornaments.
Testing of the same stone before and after ED treatment show no diﬀerence in density, porosity
or pore structure. The compressive strength was found higher for the ED treated samples than
for the control, but further studies are necessary. These results indicate that no damage is done
to the treated stone from ED. Further research of matrix changes and strength development
after full desalination is required.
Four NTD were found in current use at HES: Thermal camera imaging, Microwave moisture
meter, GPR and XRF. They are able to evaluate structure’s moisture content, and can
indicate the salt content. By using the techniques together a more precise picture of the
situation could be established. Current methods used to prevent salt penetration are invasive
which makes it all the more important to develop ED as a non-destructive method to prevent
salt damage.
