Abstract
With quantum mechanics having an ever so important role in the technological ad-
vancement of society, quantum imaging represents a unique tool in characterizing
nanoscale objects. Within the subeld of quantum imaging, quantum metrology is,
among others, used to detect phase changes with an incredible sensitivity depending
on the type of experiment carried out. In this thesis the basis is laid for quantum
phase estimation, and a set-up is built for the purpose of estimation the phase shift
though a glass plate with varying thickness on the nanoscale.
The set-up is a Sagnac-type Mach-Zehnder interferometer, and the set-up is a unique
contribution as no references of Sagnac-type Mach-Zehnder interferometers are cur-
rently found in the eld of phase sensing. The characterization of the built homodyne
detector showed it was working properly, and measurements were obtained for the
sample glass plate.
The results did not exactly match the expected results, however a discussion on the
sources of errors and ways to optimize the experiment is included. Conclusions on
the experiment are that the set-up was sensitive to measure relative phase shifts on
the 100 nm scale. The experiment was conducted with displaced coherent states,
but improvements can be made with squeezed vacuum states, as suggested, which
is where the future of the project lies.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Before the project as a whole is introduced, a simple overview of the advances in
quantum metrology is in its place. Then the structure of the thesis is presented with
the content of the various chapters.
1.1
Overview
For science to advance it is important to develop lasting and correct theories of how
nature works. In order to understand nature, experiments must be conducted and
the results interpreted. But as science progresses to investigate the smallest parts
of nature it has proven increasingly dicult to both see the samples and measure
the experimental data.
When the optical telescope was invented around the 17th century [1], lenses have been
used to see items both far away and up close. After the invention of the telescope,
many types of optical devices have been created. Amongst the most famous types of
microscopes are three types; the optical microscopes, which use photons to interact
with the sample, the electron microscopes such as the scanning electron microscope
or tunnelling electron microscope, which use electrons to interact with the sample,
and the scanning probe microscopes, which use a probe to interact with the sample,
such as the atomic force microscope or the scanning tunnelling microscopes.
As the magnication of the optical microscopes increased, it became harder and
harder to improve the resolution. The resolution becomes diraction limited at large
magnications. A recent study has shown that the current resolution for visible light
can be improved to just below 100 nm resolution for a technique named High Index
Resolution Enhancement by Scattering. It can theoretically be improved to a 72 nm
resolution for a GaP HIRES lens [2].
With this limit slowly being reached as scientists nd new methods in the eld of
optical microscopy, there is another interesting quantity measurable for a sample,
which is the thickness, or similarly, the phase shift of the light passing through the
sample. Atomic force microscopy is a good method of measuring the thickness of
the sample, however it is time consuming and not suitable in every situation [3].
This is where quantum optics has proved to be a useful theory in describing the
phase change of light through the sample. By using N unentangled probe photons
it is possible to reach the standard quantum limit where the variance of the phase

1.2
Problem Statement and Structure of the Thesis
The overall goal of the master project is to experimentally show that the standard
quantum limit of phase sensing can be reached with coherent states. A scheme is
proposed on how squeezed vacuum states can beat the standard quantum limit.
The project also aims at giving a basic understanding of the electronics used in the
experiment, as well as the optimal approach of optimizing and testing the set-up.
Before the experiments are discussed, the thesis will lay a mathematical basis for
the understanding of the physical processes at hand in Section 2. The section on
the theory goes through the basic theory of a harmonic oscillator. The introduction
of phase space via the quantum equivalent operators of the classical position and
momentum gives a fundamental understanding of coherent states and squeezed vac-
uum states.
From the basic experimental set-up the phase sensitivity is calculated from the sig-
nal to noise ratio in homodyne interferometry. The ultimate achievable lower limit
for the variance of the phase shift is derived from the quantum sher information
and the Cramér-Rao bound.
The variance of the phase shift is also derived for
squeezed vacuum states. Final remarks of the theory section are on optimizing the
Cramér-Rao inequality.
The experimental set-up, a Sagnac-type Mach-Zehnder interferometer, is explained
and discussed in Section 3. The set-up may very well be new in the eld of phase
sensing using homodyne detection, as no references of this experimental set-up with
homodyne detection have been found. Some of the electronic components needed
in the set-up are introduced, and their basic function as well as importance for the
set-up are included. The sample, upon which the phase shift is measured, is also
discussed, as well as the characterization thereof, such as to get a reference once the
experiment is running. In Section 4 the homodyne detector is characterized, and the
measurement results of the sample is discussed in detail. The results are compared,
using a one way ANOVA for the standard deviations of the voltage measurements
and the detected relative phase is compared to the calculated relative phase.
Sources of errors are included and an optimization of the set-up is proposed for
future experiments as well as the conclusion on the results and overall achievement
in the project. The outlook of the thesis focus on the projects future context.

a mathematical and physical foundation of tools, in order to work with light. This
chapter gives an introduction to how light can be modelled as a harmonic oscillator,

information is introduced in relation to the variance of the phase shift φ, and by
choosing the right type of experiment and approximation, it is shown how the vari-
ance of the phase shift φ has an ultimate lower bound.
The phase, phase shift, and relative phase shift φ will be used interchangeably
throughout the thesis.

best achievable phase sensitivity with coherent states scales as ∆φ ∝N −1/2 [6, 19].

3.2.2
The Servo Controller
When the two beams from the two arms of Figure 3.1 combine at PBS2, it is not
possible to get all the intensity of the two beams transmitted to Part 2 of the set-up.
Some of the intensity gets transmitted to the PD1. The objective of the PD1, the
servo controller and the PS is to create destructive interference at PD1, such that
the two beams interfere constructively when transmitted onwards to Parts 2 and 3.
Therefore one of the output signals of the PD1 is the error signal, and this signal
is fed to the servo controller.
The servo controller is a PI-controller.
The P is
for 'Proportional' and basically amplies the error signal (e(t)). The I stands for
'Integral' and is the term in the PI-controller, which sums up the instantaneous error
over time. This gives the oset, which was supposed to be corrected previously.
However there are trade-os when choosing the parameter values for the proportional
and integral terms. Choosing a too large integral gain Ki will decrease the rise
time of the signal and eliminate the steady state error, but it will result in a large
overshooting relative to the signal desired and the settling time increases. The same
applies for the proportional gain Kp, but here the eect on the settling time is minor
and the steady state error just decreases. These two terms add up and the resulting
signal is sent through a high voltage amplier (HV), and nally fed to the PS [35].

5.1
Discussion and Sources of Errors
The results of Section 4.5 were at rst glance expected, to some degree. The mea-
sured voltages appear to be going through the 0 V reference, when no signal pho-
tons are present. The 150 nm groove reference is constant with power applied to
the EOM, and the 300 nm groove and full width measurements have opposite signs
in the measured voltage. In addition, they are the measurements with the largest
measured voltages, also as expected. However all the measurements with the inter-
mediate groove depths are not expected. The 50 and 110 nm groove should have
positive measured voltages. The 110 nm groove is however closer to the reference
groove at 150 nm than the 50 nm groove. The opposite is the case for the 200 and
250 nm grooves. They both have negative measured voltages as expected, but the
250 nm groove measured voltage is closer to the reference groove than the 200 nm
groove.
A factor in the measurements was the fact that the sample stage was removed each
time the grooves were changed. When placing the sample stage back in the set-
up the sample may have had an angle of the normal to the surface of the sample,
which was dierent from zero. Hence by changing the angle of the stage relative to
the signal beam, the signal beam sees a larger thickness of the sample, than what
is actually intended. By overestimating this error, the angle of the normal to the
surface of the sample could be as much as 5◦o, which would mean a factor of 1.087
larger thickness than what was really intended to measure. To solve this problem,
an automated XZ-stage could be built, and the sample holder should be designed
such as only to hold to the sample at 90◦angles.
Another similar, but most important factor, is that that the angle between measure-
ments most likely is dierent. For instance, if the angle of the normal to the surface
of the sample for the full width is 0◦, then the full width is measured. But then
taking out the sample, adjusting it to the 50 nm groove and putting it back in where
the normal has an angle of 1◦, then the eective change in thickness is 8.73 µm and
not 50 nm as what was wanted. This incredible extra thickness of 8.73 µm corre-

5.2
Conclusion and Outlook
Though the project was not completed in its entirety, that is the experiment was
not conducted with squeezed light as was the original goal of the project, results
were still obtained using coherent states. The phase shifts detected were however
not as expected. This was due to a critical mistake in the measuring process. Had
there been more time, the measurements would be re-made, and the sample stage
would be left in the set-up, such that the angle to the normal of the sample plane
with respect to the beam path is xed.
Overall the project is considered a success. Results were obtained, and despite the
fact that they did not match the expected results entirely, the conclusion is that the
set-up used in the project was still able to detect a phase shift φ as the measured
voltage. Starting the project with no experimental knowledge, the end results as
well as the explanations of the components and their function during the thesis,
gives a good understanding of the underlying physics.
Had the timespan of the project allowed it, the various non-critical sources of errors
would be traced back and eliminated, such that the measurements could be even
more precise, and thus getting even closer to the expected results.
The future of the project rests with C. Schäfermeier, who will use the set-up to get
results with a squeezed vacuum state as the probing state.
