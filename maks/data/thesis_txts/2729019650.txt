Summary  
 
 
Many oil reservoirs have experienced reservoir souring as a result of the infusion of 
seawater for secondary recovery purposes. The growth of sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) is the 
principal cause of high H2S concentrations in reservoir waters. The onset of SRB activity at the 
interface between seawater and formation water appears as a key component behind reservoir 
souring. This is consistent with the tendency of oil reservoir systems to generate hydrogen sulfide 
under different temperature and concentration regimes, as well as the general presence of SRB 
throughout the formation. The numerous heterogeneities inherent in reservoirs, however, make 
exact definition of mixing zones difficult. 
 
A bio-chemical model coupled with reservoir flow and transport models were used in this 
investigation to predict H2S production at field scale. The models were used to study how grid 
resolution affects temperature profiles and species concentrations. Furthermore, the study focused 
on explaining the underlying dynamics of microbial activity inside subsurface reservoirs, with a 
particular emphasis on determining the influence of heterogeneity on microbial-induced reservoir 
souring. Notably, the study demonstrated the advantage of fine-grid models in modeling microbial 
development over coarser resolutions. 
 
Following that, an investigation of the upscaling of heterogeneous reservoirs was 
conducted, along with an examination of the consequences for microbial behavior. Computational 
results show that higher levels of upscaling result in smoother permeability distributions, 
substantially changing the geological architecture in comparison to the heterogeneous model. Such 
geological changes cause adjustments in temperature profiles and species concentrations, resulting 
in different onsets for reservoir souring. 
 
The novelty of this study lies in its investigation into how the interpretation of complete 
mixing assumption can yield disparate results, as well as its in-depth exploration of the pivotal role 
of heterogeneity in reshaping microbial behavior and the resulting phenomenon of reservoir 
souring. 
Keywords: Full-Mixing, Temperature dependency, Growth Rate, Heterogeneity. Upscaling

Chapter 1: Introduction 
 
1.1 Background  
 
The primary energy source around the globe for an exceptionally long time has been fossil 
fuels, including coal, oil, and natural gas. There are several reasons for their appeal. First, fossil 
fuels are widely available and plentiful, making them quite easy to obtain and use. Second, these 
energy sources may supply a lot of energy per unit of volume or mass due to their high energy 
density. Furthermore, fossil fuels are often less costly compared to other energy sources. Their 
position as important energy sources are further enhanced by their well-established extraction, 
transportation, and distribution infrastructure. Oil reservoir production, management, and 
maintenance are essential given the significance of oil reservoirs in the context of the current, 
growing energy demand. Utilizing clean technology in all aspects of production, administration, 
and maintenance might help achieve high energy output with a small carbon impact [1]. 
One of the most popular, economical, and effective methods for boosting production and 
extracting more oil from mature oil reservoirs is water flooding. However, seawater typically 
differs from formation water in terms of chemical composition, salinity, ionic strength, pH, and 
temperature [2]. In addition, seawater has a far higher concentration of sulfate ions than does 
formation water. Additionally, the formation water typically holds sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB). 
As a result, during water flooding, the interaction between the seawater that is injected and the 
formation water encourages the in-situ microbial processes that turn sulfate into hydrogen sulfide 
(𝐻2𝑆), which rise with further SRB growth [2]. 
==PAGE:11==
P a g e  | 10 
 
 
Figure 1:Hydorgen Sulfide generation in the reservoir, “mixing zone” concept [3] 
 
Reservoirs that formerly had extremely low or no observable levels of hydrogen sulfide 
begin to produce increasingly of this gas over time. This process is known as reservoir souring. 
Sulphate-reducing bacteria (SRB) can reduce sulfate in injection water, which can result in the 
production of hydrogen sulfide in the reservoir near injection wells [4]. The following production 
of crude oil and related gas also includes the production of hydrogen sulfide. Salinity, pH, 
temperature, pressure, sulfate content (as electron acceptors), organic molecules, hydrogen 
(electron doners), carbon as energy sources, and inorganic nutrients all have an impact on this 
microbial activity (SRB growth and consequent increase in 𝐻2𝑆) as illustrated in Figure (2) [4].  
The souring of oil reservoirs due to microbial activity is a widespread problem in the oil 
industry. As the volume of seawater injection activities increases, reservoir souring becomes more 
of a concern. For Example, BP's experience has revealed that 70% of seawater-flood reservoirs 
have deteriorated [5]. Reservoir souring is still a problem in these oil fields and has been labeled 
as one of the most important ones. However, it might be controlled by understanding the 
fundamental systems and figuring out the governing and controlling factors.[5] 
==PAGE:12==

Figure 2: Factors affecting microbial activity [6] 
 
  
Modeling approaches have shown their ability in assessing the causes of reservoir souring 
and the critical parameters to show effective treatment or control methods, in addition to 
experimental and field considerations. The ability of a souring model to renew the favorable 
environment needed for 𝐻2𝑆 formation and transport in the reservoir figures out how exact its 
predictions are. Underground reservoir characteristics and circumstances including porosity, 
permeability, production/injection flow rate, pressure, temperature, pH, and salinity can have an 
impact on the formation of 𝐻2𝑆 that is caused by bacterial activity. Therefore, the souring model 
might be improved by concurrently including the 𝐻2𝑆 production and transport behavior of the 
created 𝐻2𝑆 in porous medium. In the past 20 years, several souring models have been created. 
For the analysis of 𝐻2𝑆 production and transit, Ligthelm et al. (1991) supplied a 1-D analytical 
model [1]. In various modeling studies, the mixing model of seawater and formation has been 
considered [2],[3] .By resolving the material balance equation, which considers reactions, 
diffusion, and adsorption, Sunde et al. (1993) created the biofilm model [4]. 
The heterogeneity of a reservoir can play a significant role in microbial reservoir souring. 
Heterogeneity refers to the variation in physical and chemical properties of the reservoir, variations 
in sulfate concentration, temperature, and the presence of minerals and organic matter can create 
conditions that are conducive to the growth of SRB and the production of 𝐻2𝑆 [5]. Reservoir 
heterogeneity has long been acknowledged as a significant determinant of reservoir efficiency. The 
petroleum literature is replete with theory and field studies that investigate the impact of 
==PAGE:13==
P a g e  | 12 
 
heterogeneity and describe methods for assessing and mitigating its impact[12].Earlier studies 
confirmed that at either a homogeneous or heterogeneous reservoir, the SRB expanded and 
produced 𝐻2𝑆 at the low temperature region. However, because the flow behavior of 𝐻2𝑆 varied 
across the two reservoirs, the behavior of 𝐻2𝑆 produced from each reservoir behaved significantly 
differently. Therefore, permeability heterogeneity plays a role in the reservoir souring [5]. 
A finely gridded model is employed to reflect the influence of heterogeneity on reservoir 
souring. These models are used to correctly capture even minor variations. As a result, they require 
a large number of grid blocks, which considerably increases the computing effort [13]. One 
technique to addressing this issue is upscaling, which involves turning precise geological models 
into coarser reservoir models [14]. This decreases the number of grids blocks and allows for more 
efficient flow simulation in a fair amount of time. However, it is crucial to highlight that the coarse 
reservoir models do not fully duplicate the dynamic behavior of the fine geological models because 
upscaling leads to information loss.

Despite simulation studies conducted in this area, unanimous conclusions and robust 
modeling efforts are still lacking for the role of heterogeneity in microbial activities in reservoir 
souring.

1.2 Literature review 
 
  
The most pertinent simulation investigations over the previous years are summarized in 
this section. The goal is to get a deeper understanding of the origin of reservoir souring and the 
importance of modelling approached to assess the reservoir souring in hydrocarbon reservoirs.  
1.2.1 Reservoir Souring: 
      
Sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) are type of microorganisms that could respire sulfate 
(𝑆𝑂4
2−) in the absence of oxygen, using it as an electron acceptor during anaerobic respiration, 
producing hydrogen sulfide as one of the reaction products. They obtain energy by oxidizing 
organic compounds while reducing sulfate to hydrogen sulfide (𝐻2𝑆). While some SRBs are 
naturally occurring in subsurface reservoirs, others could be injected into the reservoir during 
drilling or water injection activities. However, the precise knowledge of their origin, whether they 
have been present in the reservoir from its deposition or have been added later from other sources, 
is still difficult to find [6], [7]. 
  
SRB are anaerobic organisms that get carbon from organic components (synthesis part), 
and energy (energy part) from the conversion of sulfate ions to hydrogen sulfide. A synthesis part 
and an energy component are both present in every bacterially mediated process, this is known as 
anaerobic respiration. Three oxidation half reactions are needed for the entire microbial-mediated 
process, one for the electron donor (𝑅𝑑), and the other two for the electron acceptor and synthesis 
((𝑅𝑎), (𝑅𝑠)) respectively [8].

The water source may be important in souring since several of these components can be 
found in the water used for secondary recovery. In practice, the water source selected for secondary 
recovery operations is typically based on availability and cost. For instance, seawater is the 
simplest and least expensive supply of water for activities involving offshore floods. Injection of 
seawater which holds high concentrations of sulfate ions (2700 mg/L) compared to the formation 
water (0.1-1900 mg/L) [18] creates a more conducive environment for souring phenomena or in-
situ microbial responses [10]. SRB are common occupants in such habitats, as proved by a 
microbiological analysis of many samples retrieved from wellheads in some oil fields [6] .It has 
been proven that temperature, pH, and salinity are factors that affect the rate of microbial 
development in the oil reservoirs. Furthermore, the concentration of hydrogen sulfide continues to 
grow when these variables approach an "optimum growth value" at a given point [6]. 
      
The SRB growth rate of the community of microorganisms is significantly influenced by 
temperature. According to research by P. Hosseininoosheri [19], the beginning time of reservoir 
souring first decreases with temperature rise, reaches a minimal value within the ideal temperature 
range, and then increases with temperature rise (beyond the ideal temperature). In other words, the 
microbial growth process that leads to the creation of hydrogen sulfide was seen to be the quickest 
when the temperature reached its best level as shown in Figure (4). An efficient waterflood process 
must consider temperature to lessen the risk of reservoir souring [19]– [21]. 
==PAGE:16==

 SRB activities may be naturally hampered by elevated temperatures (>100°C) in 
reservoirs. Low- and high-temperature reservoirs (<45°C and 45–80°C, respectively) can easily 
experience souring, though .While certain SRB communities (thermophilic bacteria) are found to 
develop in a medium with a temperature as high as 80 °C, other SRB communities (mesophilic 
bacteria) have demonstrated their capacity for growth in the low-temperature range of 20 °C to 40 
°C [21] [20]. Nevertheless, in terms of temperature, Cord-Ruwisch et al. state that most SRB kinds 
grow best in the range of 30 to 60 C [22]. 
      
Although the temperature of the oil reservoirs is often high, the temperature decrease that 
occurs during the cold seawater flooding will create the ideal conditions for SRB development 
(particularly near the injector) [23] .As a result, the production of 𝐻2S from intense bacterial 
growth and activity, likely caused by the creation of a biofilm, frequently occurs close to the 
injection well and decreases as the distance from the injection site to the production well along the 
waterfront grows. According to Vance and Mckinley [24], [25] , the produced 𝐻2S along the flood 
front as it moves from the injection to the production well depends on the rock permeability and 
fracture, scavenging, partitioning, and water movement (flow velocity and resident duration).

Additionally, the movement of other chemicals (e.g., sulfate ion) or microbial species (e.g., SRB) 
along with generated 𝐻2S also happens and their extent of participation in the subsequent reactions 
depends on the dispersion, diffusion, partitioning into different in-situ phases (oleic, aqueous, and 
gaseous phase), and adsorption on the rock and minerals [10], [26]. 
     
Studies explained that the SRB grew and generated 𝐻2𝑆 in the low temperature zone at 
either a homogenous or heterogeneous reservoir. 𝐻2𝑆 Production from a homogeneous or 
heterogeneous reservoir showed noticeably different behavior. This is a result of the two reservoirs 
different 𝐻2𝑆 flow behaviors and different temperature distributions. Several factors can affect 
how the temperature is distributed in homogeneous and heterogeneous reservoirs. The temperature 
distribution is even and stable across a homogenous reservoir and the same in all directions 
resulting in a balance distribution. On the other hand, in a heterogeneous reservoir, the temperature 
distribution shows differences and contrasts between various system components or locations due 
to variations in fluid flow, permeability, geological features, or heat sources [11].  
Yuichi Sugai examined the role of permeability heterogeneity on the flow behavior of 𝐻2𝑆 
.The results indicated that the heterogeneous reservoir's start date was delayed by almost six 
hundred days compare to the time when 𝐻2𝑆 production started in the homogeneous reservoir as 
shown in the Figure (6). This is because the lowest permeability zone was placed in the shortest 
path between the injection well and production well in the heterogenous model as their locations 
were chosen to avoid them from being connected by high permeable channels as illustrate in the 
Figure (5) [11]

Besides the temperature distribution, mixing zone where seawater meets formation water 
was proven to play a paramount role in reservoir souring. This is consistent with the oil reservoir 
association's ability to create hydrogen sulfide over a wide range of temperatures and 
concentrations, as well as the existence of SRB throughout the formation. SRB gets all necessary 
nutrients for development in the mixing zone, including sulfate from injection water, organic acids 
from formation water, and dissolved organic compounds [27].The development and size of the 
formed mixing zone heavily relies on the component’s aqueous phase dispersion and diffusion,

which also has an impact on the 𝐻2𝑆  production. It is crucial to consider the impact of diffusion 
and dispersion since the degree of mixing defines the region at which microbial activity responses 
take place. However, due to reservoir heterogeneities, obtaining a well-defined mixing zone is 
challenging. [28]. 
 
1.2.2 Simulation review: 
 
      
Numerous studies have been conducted in recent years due to the significance of the 
modeling technique to evaluate the reservoir souring in hydrocarbon reservoirs [6] .Along with its 
forward movement, water injection into an oil reservoir creates a gradient in the primary 
environmental factors. Temperature, sulfate, and electron donor concentrations, as well as other 
environmental factors, are the key environmental factors. This offers a wide variety of ecological 
niches that might be suitable for sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB). Additionally, assessing those 
crucial elements will aid in understanding the kinetic development of SRB, improving the accuracy 
of the souring initiation forecast [19]. 
The commencement of reservoir souring has been predicted mathematically in several 
ways during the past 20 years [19]. A good souring model must consider how hydrogen sulfides 
are produced and transported in an oil reservoir. A souring prediction model that is more effective 
and exact results from this connection [27] .The development of more precise modeling and 
simulation tools and approaches may also benefit from a thorough understanding of microbial 
sources and their characteristics as well as the 𝐻2𝑆 production criteria [28]. 
Although results from small-scale research may not necessarily transfer to field 
circumstances. Except that the fact that laboratory studies are simpler to conduct, supply 
comparable parallel control cultures, and are less expensive than field investigations [10] .Thus, 
simulations are being created and improved based on data from laboratory and field investigations 
[27][9]. 
The mixing zone, the biofilm, and thermal viability are the three fundamental models for

Chapter 3: Grid Size Effect 
 
 
As has been mentioned in section 2.1.3, the presumption of full mixing inside each grid 
block is inaccurate for field size simulation. The reason is that the precise temperature distribution 
and species concentration are not accurately reflected in field scale reservoir models because of 
the size of the grid blocks. Thus, microorganism growth is frequently overestimated in coarse 
models compared to the refined model. In this chapter, the effects of grid size of the numerical 
model on the developed profiles of temperature and species concentrations are investigated. 
Additionally, the mean growth rate will be calculated mathematically for two models “Coarse and 
Refined” to confirm the effect of temperature distribution and species concentration on microbial 
activities.  
3.1 Full Mixing 
 
For the purposes of field-scale simulation, full mixing in each grid block refers to the 
presumption that all fluids inside a given grid block are fully mixed, resulting in homogeneous 
composition and characteristics across the whole block. In other words, it is presuming that inside 
a single grid block, there are no concentration gradients or differences in temperature distribution 
[36]. By treating each grid block as a well-mixed, homogenous volume, this assumption 
streamlines the modeling and simulation process. As there is no longer a need to take into 
consideration spatial differences in fluid composition, fluid flow and transport processes inside the 
block may be more easily calculated [37]. 
Certain numerical reservoir modeling approaches, in which the reservoir volume is 
discretized into a grid of cells or blocks, frequently make use of the idea of complete mixing inside 
a grid block. According to this, regardless of the flow pathways or the existence of heterogeneities, 
fluids mix instantly and thoroughly within each grid block. As previously indicated, however, 
due to variables like heterogeneity, changes in fluid characteristics, residence time restrictions, and 
scale effects, it is improbable that full mixing would occur within each grid block at the field size. 
The assumption of complete mixing is less accurate because of these variables, which also produce 
spatial variations and concentration gradients[36].

P a g e  | 27 
 
To outline the problem of full mixing, an investigation of the effect of grid size on 
temperature distribution and the concentration of species will be studied.

investigate the impact of heterogeneity and describe methods for assessing and mitigating its 
impact[12].Consider an enormous rate of movement through a permeable material using 
chemically inert, miscible fluids with equal mobility and density. The characteristic of the 
medium that causes the flood front, the border between the displacing and displaced fluids, to 
distort and expand as the displacement progresses is known as heterogeneity [38]. 
  
Most heterogeneity measures focus on permeability variation in a reservoir, while variation 
in other characteristics is frequently overlooked. Two reasons are typically used to justify this 
practice. First, permeability is a characteristic that, by definition, influences movement. As a 
result, it should be included in any measure of variety. Second, because differences in 
permeability are usually much greater than variations in other properties, such changes can 
easily outweigh the impact of variations in other properties [38]. 
 
In the chapter, the effect of heterogeneity of the reservoir on the temperature profile, 
concentration species and finally the profile of the produced hydrogen sulfide are investigated. 
A comparison of the produced hydrogen sulfide by different models will be studied.

Chapter 5 : Upscaling 
 
 
It is computationally costly and perhaps impracticable to simulate reservoir flow dynamics 
at the smallest scale. As a result, upscaling a number of fine-scale grid blocks into a smaller number 
of coarse-scale grids has become essential to reservoir simulation for the majority of reservoirs 
[39]. This is due to the fact that as the number of grid blocks rises, the quantity of flow equations 
also increases, greatly increasing the amount of time needed to solve flow issues [39]. Upscaling 
is the process of lowering a massive portion of a geological model's fine-scale grid blocks to a 
smaller portion of coarse-scale grid blocks while maintaining the model's underlying geological 
features [40].  
 
Despite the fact that parallel computing may be used to distribute the workload, there are 
still many grid blocks that provide difficult computational problems. As a result, upscaling serves 
as a link between the simulation scale and the reservoir scale. It is necessary to maintain a balance 
between the two because when the upscaling ratio is increased, the accuracy of the numerical 
simulation decreases.[40] 
 
The impact of scaling up on microbial activity and hydrogen sulfide production will be 
examined in this chapter. Examine the effects of increasing the habitat temperature of the 
microorganisms on coarser meshes as well. 
 
5.1 
Upscaled Models 
 
 
To reproduce the flow findings of the complex fine-scale model with the coarse model 
while reducing the number of grid blocks, upscaled rock characteristics must be calculated. It is 
fair to assume that the porosity and pressure values on the coarse scale represent the arithmetic 
average of the fine-scale values. However, the permeability values that make up the flow response 
of the model are a complicated function, and to discover upscaled values of them, a flow-based 
average was utilized to replicate the response of the fine-scale model. 
 
Recalling section 4.1.4, the base case permeability field was created on a 700 x 400 grid 
with a mean value of 2.3 mD and correlation length of 100 and 25 in the X and Y directions,

respectively. The permeability field of the base case was then upscaled using flow-based upscaling 
to various degrees of upscaling ranging from (173x 81) to (7x1). Figure (22) and Figure (23)

The base case permeability field's small-scale features are somewhat retained up to a (34x20) grid, 
as seen in Figure (22-E). These structures are lost when scaling is done at higher levels. It is evident

upscaling causes the permeability fields to become smoother.

Chapter 6 : Conclusion 
 
 
A systematic three-step examination of the impact of heterogeneity on microbial 
activity in reservoir souring was carried out. Initially, the study looked at the ways that various 
grid sizes affected temperature profiles, species concentrations, and mean growth rates inside 
the numerical model. 
Following that, the study looked at the reservoir's heterogeneity, notably its impact on 
temperature profiles, species concentrations, and the subsequent production of hydrogen 
sulfide. This round of research also included a comparison of hydrogen sulfide production 
across several models, coarse, fine, and extra fine, with different degrees of heterogeneities. 
Finally, the researchers looked at the effects of upscaling on microbial activity and 
hydrogen sulfide production breakthrough time. Furthermore, the study looked at the impacts 
of raising the habitat temperature for microorganisms when using coarser meshes. 
 
• It is necessary to understand the temperature and nutrient distribution in the 
reservoir for predicting effective growth rate of microorganisms. 
• Fine-gride models offer superior resolution and provide a better depiction of mixing 
effect. 
• The grid size has a huge impact on the apparent growth rate of microorganisms. 
• The generation of hydrogen sulfide in a reservoir by sulfate reducing bacteria 
(SRB) depends on factors such as nutrients availability, reservoir and injection 
temperatures, and sulfate concentration in seawater. 
• The breakthrough of hydrogen sulfide tends to occur earlier in heterogeneous cases 
than in the other models due to presence of high-flow channels. 
• The degree of reservoir sourness is impacted by the flow distribution and reactions 
caused by reservoir heterogeneity. 
• The coarser the model, the less geological features captured, resulting in a different 
hydrogen sulfide production onset. 
• Numerical dispersion is considerable in coarse-grid models. 
•  Maximum growth temperature of the microorganisms has a discernible impact on 
the starting point of hydrogen sulfide production in coarser models.

6.1 Recommendation for future work 
 
 
 Upscaling the transport of species is shown to be as important as upscaling growth rates 
of microorganisms. Therefore, improving the species transport model for coarse-grid models or 
reducing numerical dispersion will be helpful.  Implementing a more accurate model for biofilm 
and biomass transport will improve the model. Finally, it is highly advised that the bio- chemical 
model for hydrogen sulfide prediction be compared to other tools such as Soursim, BPOPE, and 
UTCHEM, as well as verified against field data.
