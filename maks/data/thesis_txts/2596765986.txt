ABSTRACT
The development of the Danish pig meat industry has led to the production of
large quantities of swine manure (SM) which constitute a signiﬁcant environmental
burden in terms of Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions. One of the most prominent
technologies that has been used for the treatment of liquid manure is anaerobic di-
gestion as it enables the harvest of the organic load while it only produces a small
amount of excess sludge. However, several process-related issues have been reported
when SM is used as the sole substrate, mainly due to its high water and ammonia
content. For resolving these problems, co-digestion of SM with carbon-rich biomasses
has been promoted.
Previous studies have demonstrated that Aqueous Ammonia Soaking (AAS) pre-
treatment has a positive impact on the methane (CH4) yield of certain lignocellulosic
biomasses [1, 2, 3, 4, 5].
In this thesis, the performance of continuous anaerobic
co-digestion of SM with additional raw and AAS-treated carbon-rich biomasses was
studied. The biomasses that were tested included wheat straw (WS), chicken ma-
nure (CM) and seaweed (SW), while the pretreatment was conducted under mild
conditions (15% w/w NH3 solution, 4 days, S:L = 50 g/L, ambient temperature).
A co-digestion TS-based ratio of 50% SM and 50% additional biomass was tested
throughout the experiments.
Overall, the experiments showed that AAS had a positive eﬀect on WS, leading to
an increase in the CH4 yield(+23.79%) and productivity of the digester(+23.41%).
Additionally, the introduction of AAS-treated WS led to a more eﬃcient removal of
the organic fraction. On the contrary, the pretreatment had a negative impact on
CM as the Biogas Production Rate(BPR) and the yield of the digester plummeted
(-18.97% and -23.62% respectively). Finally, a deﬁnite conclusion for the case of SW
could not be drawn since the co-digestion of SM with the untreated SW did not
proceed due to time limitations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Concept of the thesis
Over the previous decade, the global primary energy consumption has increased
sharply leading to the emission of signiﬁcant amounts of GHG. In Denmark, one of the
sectors that greatly contributes to the country’s emissions is agriculture, accounting
for 22% of the total emissions [12]. Speciﬁcally, in terms of CH4 and nitrous oxide
(N2O) emissions, animal manure management contributes a notable amount (26.5%
and 13% respectively in 2017) [12], a fact that can partly be attributed to the large
pig population found in Denmark (>12.7 million) which is one of the biggest in the
EU [6].

The large amount of liquid manure produced by the pig meat industry creates
severe environmental problems aﬀecting air, soil and water quality [13]. This is the
case as only a small fraction of the organic matter can be fully metabolized by the
animals with the rest of it being disposed for further degradation without being
biologically stable. This fact results in the production of N2O through the process of
nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation [13] as well as in the emission of signiﬁcant amounts

One of the best ways for treating liquid manure is anaerobic digestion.
How-

1.2
Research objectives
Previous studies have shown that AAS is a promising biomass pretreatment as it
leads to a higher CH4 yield while it simultaneously removes ammonia which is one of
the main inhibitors of the process [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. In this context, the present thesis is
aimed on evaluating the performance of continuous anaerobic co-digestion of SM with
the carbon-rich biomasses of WS, CM and SW, when the latter have been treated
with AAS. In order of better demonstrating the eﬀectiveness of the treatment, several
indicators were being closely monitored.
1.3
Structure of the thesis
The thesis consists of 6 chapters. After the introductory part (Chapter 1), a liter-
ature review is made aimed on unfolding the various aspects of the process in detail.
Speciﬁcally, the principles of anaerobic digestion are brieﬂy mentioned, followed by
the process stability factors and the inhibition mechanism of ammonia. Additionally,
a short discussion of the various biomass pretreatments and the crucial parameters of
AAS are included. Subsequently, Chapter 3 contains the experimental setup and the
operation scheme of the digesters as well as a concise description of the various ana-
lytical methods used. In Chapter 4, the experimental results from the digesters are
presented and thoroughly discussed while the conclusions of the study are included

Chapter 2
Literature review
2.1
Principles of anaerobic digestion
Anaerobic digestion is one of the most prominent waste treatment technologies
as it enables the recovery of energy in the form of biogas while it only produces a
small amount of by-products in the form of sludge. Additionally, it enables high
organic removal rates with a relatively low energy input [16]. During the process,
the organic material is converted, in the absence of oxygen, to a mixture of CH4 and
carbon dioxide (CO2), which account for the most reduced and the most oxidized
states of carbon respectively [17]. Alongside these, small amounts of other substances
are present including oxygen, hydrogen sulﬁde (H2S), ammonia, water vapor and
hydrogen (H2)[18].
The process removes a number of environmental hazards as the volume of the waste
is greatly reduced while it also leads to a signiﬁcant inactivation of the pathogens that
are present in the inﬂuent [19]. Furthermore, the nutrients that were previously bound
are released, thus opening the way for further exploiting the nitrogen, phosphorus and
potassium content [20].
Although anaerobic digestion is a complex biological process with several micro-
bial communities operating under a symbiotic relationship, it can be narrowed down
to four steps, namely hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and methanogenesis, in-
volving at least three diﬀerent groups of microorganisms [21](Figure 2.1).

2.1.1
Hydrolysis
Hydrolysis is the process where the complex organic polymers are disintegrated by
extracellular enzymes which are either excreted into the bulk liquid and then absorbed
into the substrate, or they are directly produced when microorganisms are attached
to the cell surface [7]. The main groups of bacteria taking part in the conversion
are strict and facultative anaerobes including the species Clostridium, Bacteroides
and Staphylococcus, although several other species have also been reported to be
present[22]. The hydrolytic enzymes that enable the initiation of the process include
lipase, protease, cellulase, cellobiase, xylanase and amylase [20].
The main biopolymers disintegrating in this step are the proteins, carbohydrates
and lipids of the waste resulting in the production of amino acids, monosaccharides
and long chain fatty acids (LCFAs) [23]. The cell membrane is permeable to these
products thus enabling the acidogenic microorganisms to use them as substrate.

•Hydrolysis of carbohydrates: The two main carbohydrates found in the
biomass are cellulose and hemicellulose (HC), both of which are integral parts of lig-
nocellulose. The degradation of these compounds leads to the production of smaller
units including glucose, xylose, mannose, arabinose, galactose and sugar acids (D-
glucuronic and D-galacturonic acids) [24].
•Hydrolysis of proteins: Proteins consist of amino acids bound together through
a peptide or an amide bond which when being hydrolyzed by extracellular proteolytic
bacteria, release polypeptides and amino acids [25]. However, the extent of the hy-
drolysis is largely dictated by the various parameters that inﬂuence the process such
as their structure and solubility [26].
•Hydrolysis of lipids: Lipids make a integral part of several substrates used in
anaerobic digestion and their hydrolysis leads to the release of glycerol and LCFAs.
The rate of hydrolysis may signiﬁcantly diﬀer depending on various factors such as
the length of the chain, the pH and the surface tension [27].
Hydrolysis is considered to be the rate limiting step of the entire process in cases
where the substrate is not easily accessible to the enzymes. This is common when
a high concentration of solids is present as the accessible surface area is severely
limited[28].
2.1.2
Acidogenesis
Hydrolysis is followed by the process of acidogenesis during which the hydrolyzed
products are further converted into simple organic acids and alcohols. Speciﬁcally,
monosaccharides are fermented according to the Embden-Doudoroﬀ-Parnas (EMP)
pathway or the Entner-Doudoroﬀ(ED) pathway to pyruvate and then proceed through
Acetyl-CoA to form the ﬁnal products (alcohols and VFAs) [27]. On the other hand,
amino acids degrade into VFAs in pairs following the Stickland reaction, although
6
==PAGE:18==
July 27, 2020
degradation of a single unit may also occur in cases where hydrogenotrophic bacteria
are present [29]. The fermenting bacteria of this stage are capable of operating at
low pH, high temperatures and high organic rates [22], while the reactions taking
place are considered to proceed faster in comparison to the other stages of anaerobic
digestion[30].
2.1.3
Acetogenesis
During this stage, the higher VFAs and the various intermediates that were previ-
ously derived, are converted to acetate and H2 [31]. Speciﬁcally, H2 concentration is
a very important parameter as it is capable of inhibiting the whole process in cases
where high partial pressures occur [21]. In this context, the syntrophic relationship
between the H2-producing acetogens and the hydrogenotrophic methanogens, also
known as “hydrogen interspecies transfer”, is very important for the stability of the
process[32]. Finally, it should be noted that lipids follow a diﬀerent path in which
glycerol is converted to acetate through acidogenesis while LCFAs degrade to acetate
following the β-oxidation pathway [33].
2.1.4
Methanogenesis
Methanogenesis is the ﬁnal stage of anaerobic digestion during which the accessible
intermediates (mainly acetate and H2) are converted to biogas. This conversion takes
place along two pathways, namely acetoclastic and hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis.
The ﬁrst one accounts for 2
3 of the total CH4 production while the latter accounts for
the remaining 1
3 [31].

The methanogenic microorganisms that perform this conversion belong to the
group of obligate anaerobic archaea and demonstrate an acute sensitivity to the pres-
ence of oxygen [34]. The methanogens have a signiﬁcantly lower growth rate com-

pared to the other microbial consortia that are present, thus making this step the

2.2
Process stability factors
The anaerobic digestion process can be aﬀected by a variety of factors including
substrate availability, electron acceptors and donors, temperature, pH, moisture and
chemical concentration among others [17].
2.2.1
Temperature
Temperature is one of the most important environmental factors as it has a high
impact on the microbial populations operating in the digesters and subsequently to
the rate of digestion and the removal of the organic load. There are three main tem-
perature ranges in which anaerobic digestion may take place, namely psychrophilic
(below 20 oC), mesophilic (25-40 oC) and thermophilic (45-60 oC) range [17]. How-
ever, all the industrial applications lie in the mesophilic and thermophilic regions as
the growth rate and the diversity of the microorganisms is higher [36]. Addition-
ally, the percentage of CH4 in the biogas mixture is severely compromised at low
temperatures resulting to the production of signiﬁcant quantities of CO2 [37].
2.2.2
pH
Another critical factor of the process is the pH. This is the case as the various mi-
crobial communities taking part in the biological conversion have a relatively narrow
region of optimum pH, which ranges from 6.5-8.5 [20]. Speciﬁcally, the microbes that
are involved in the hydrolysis and in the formation of acids tend to operate eﬃciently
around 5.2-6.3 [36], while the methanogens, which are more susceptible to instability,
require neutral pH [27, 38].
2.2.3
Nutrients
The presence of nutrients and vitamins in the digesters is crucial for the process as
the microbial consortia have speciﬁc needs which enable their optimal growth. The
8
==PAGE:20==
July 27, 2020
most essential nutrient after carbon, is nitrogen as the production of metabolic en-
zymes depends on its availability. However, the presence of excessive concentration
of nitrogen may result in the formation of ammonia, leading to inhibition.[36] Sub-
sequently, the nutrient ratio of carbon to nitrogen (C:N) should be in a range that
enables the microorganisms to grow steadily without compromising the CH4 yield.

2.2.4
Hydraulic Retention Time(HRT)
Although the substrates that are usually digested contain various individual com-
pounds whose disintegration rates are functions of multiple factors, a rough estimate
for the HRT can be obtained by measuring the volume added to the digester while
simultaneously considering the organic load of the feedstock. In this context, the
introduction of a low HRT could potentially lead to the washout of microorganisms,
thus imposing severe inhibition [36].
2.3
Inhibition
Inhibition is a complex phenomenon during which the activity of the microbial
groups is suspended. There is a wide variety of factors that may lead to the appear-
ance of inhibitory eﬀects including the presence of ammonia, H2S, LCFAs, heavy
metals, salts etc. Most of these compounds have a direct eﬀect on the methanogens,
thus resulting to the accumulation of VFAs. Consequently, the concentration of VFAs
provides a very important indicator for the stability of the process.

during the degradation of the nitrogenous compounds that are present in the sub-

inorganic ammonia nitrogen that are present in anaerobic digesters are ammonium

2.4
Swine manure as feedstock
SM is one of the most abundant substrates utilized in anaerobic digestion. However,
its composition may vary signiﬁcantly depending on the diﬀerent practises followed
in diﬀerent regions. Generally, its water content ranges between 91-99% [55] while
most of its dry matter is made of organic compounds (68-80%) [56]. Additionally, it
has a high protein content which when being degraded, releases signiﬁcant amounts
of ammonia that are capable of causing partial inhibition. These two main factors,
11
==PAGE:23==
July 27, 2020
coupled with the slow biodegradation of the organic fraction, lead to low productivity
and ultimately make SM mono-digestion economically non-feasible [57, 58, 59, 1].
Nevertheless, since there is an increasing need for treating manure and considering
its high buﬀering capacity and the wide variety of nutrients it contains[60], several
approaches have been suggested for resolving these problems.
2.4.1
Swine manure co-digestion
One of the most common practises for boosting biogas production in plants oper-
ating with liquid manure is the introduction of carbon-rich biomasses. However, the
addition of easily biodegradable biomasses such as food waste, slaughterhouse waste
and energy crops may result in a signiﬁcant increase in costs due to their scarcity [1].
In this context, a major shift towards alternative residues that are widely available
and do not compete with food and feed has been taken.
The utilization of carbon-rich matter may lead to a signiﬁcant increase in biogas
production due to the alteration of the C:N ratio. Furthermore, the partial inhibition
eﬀects caused by the high ammonia concentrations are largely eliminated. Neverthe-
less, there are certain bottlenecks in the process, most notably when lignocellulosic
biomasses are introduced, as the presence of lignin severely limits the microbial abil-
ity to access the fermentable sugars [61]. In order to address this problem, several
pretreatments have been developed.
2.5
Biomass pretreatments
The main goal of biomass pretreatment is to make the substrate more accessible
to the enzymes. This can be achieved by reducing the cellulose crystallinity and by
increasing the porosity of the material. To this end, several methods have been pro-
posed, mainly focused on facilitating the hydrolysis of the main lignocellulosic com-
ponents. There are four main pretreatment categories, namely mechanical, chemical,
physicochemical and biological, while a number of combinations between them has
also been tested.
The most common approach for addressing digestibility issues is the introduction
12
==PAGE:24==
July 27, 2020
of a mechanical pretreatment step (grinding or milling) to reduce the size of the
material and enable its faster degradation by the hydrolytic enzymes. The choice
of the technique depends mainly on the moisture content of the biomass as high
moisture can result in the expansion of the surface area [62]. Despite the reliability
of the process, there are certain disadvantages mainly relating to the electricity cost
as well as the resulting improvement in productivity [11].
Another method that is employed for removing the natural barriers of lignocellu-
lose, is chemical pretreatment. Although there are several sub-divisions, four main
categories exist and can be classiﬁed into acid, alkaline, oxidative and solvent-based
treatments depending on the chemical reagent that is being used [9]. During these
treatments, there is an alteration of the physical and chemical characteristics of the
biomass resulting in the solubilization of HC and the removal of lignin. The main
disadvantages of chemical pretreatments are the high cost of chemical agents and
the formation of inhibitory substances such as furfural, 5-hydroxymethyl furfurals,
aldehydes and phenolic acids [9].
On the other hand, physicochemical pretreatments are comprised of a variety of
combinations between physical and chemical methods. They were developed with
the aim of achieving higher biogas yields without introducing severe inhibition to the
microorganisms. During this kind of treatment, the hydrogen bonds are broken by the
supply of heat, leading to the solubilization of HC and lignin. An important advantage
of this process is the possibility of recovering heat and using it for maintaining the
temperature of the digester, thus resulting in a signiﬁcant reduction of costs [11].
Finally, various biological pretreatments have also been tested by utilizing diﬀerent
microbial consortia, fungi, bacteria and enzymes. These treatments provide a low-cost
alternative to the aforementioned energy demanding methods while the generation
of toxic compounds is non-existent. Nevertheless, their industrial application faces
signiﬁcant challenges as they currently demonstrate a very low hydrolysis rate [10]
while the development of eﬃcient microbes is still in preliminary stage [9].

2.5.1
Ammonia pretreatment
The alkaline pretreatments are generally preferred as they lead to an eﬃcient re-
moval of lignin and in the solubilization of HC. Additionally, they can take place
under milder conditions over a relatively short period. Among the several reagents
that are utilized in these treatments, ammonia holds several advantages as it is safe
to use while it can also be recovered easily due to its high volatility, thus making the
process more environmentally friendly [63].
There are several ammonia pretreatments that have been tested so far, including
ammonia recycled percolation (ARP), low moisture anhydrous ammonia (LMAA),
ammonia ﬁber expansion/explosion (AFEX) and aqueous ammonia soaking (AAS).
Most of these methods operate under high temperatures and pressures and their
applicability on industrial scale faces several limitations.
ARP method is based on the recirculation of aqueous ammonia in a packed bed
ﬂow-through reactor, known as percolation reactor. The process requires long reten-
tion times at temperatures ranging from 150 to 210 oC [64], while the pressure varies
14
==PAGE:26==
July 27, 2020
between 2.2-2.8 MPa to prevent the vaporization of ammonia [65]. Although, exper-
imental results have demonstrated that a signiﬁcant fraction of lignin was removed
(70-90%), the level of HC removal was also high [66].
LMAA was developed as an environmentally friendly alternative as it signiﬁcantly
reduces the use of water and chemicals while it does not require a washing step[67].
The treatment takes place in distinct phases where the anhydrous ammonia and
the biomass are initially placed under ambient conditions while a pretreatment step
follows at higher temperatures (40-150 oC) [64] for 48-144 h [67].
One of the ﬁrst ammonia pretreatments that were developed is AFEX. During this
treatment, liquid anhydrous or highly concentrated ammonia (>70%) is used to treat
the biomass under relatively mild conditions, with the temperatures ranging from
60-120 oC[64] and the pressures varying between 1.7-2.1 MPa over a short incubation
duration (5-60 min) [68].
Finally, another method that was developed for enhancing the digestibility of
lignocellulosic materials is AAS, the main goal of which was to address the issues
related to HC losses occurring during ARP [64]. In this case, lignocellulosic biomass is
soaked in ammonia solution for a certain period (hours to days) under mild conditions
while afterwards, the chemical is removed by distillation or washing. Several solutions
have been tested so far with their concentrations ranging from 1-32% w/w [4] resulting
in a partial removal of lignin while HC is highly retained (>80%) [69, 70, 71, 72, 73].
2.5.2
Ammonia mechanism
Ammonia acts on lignocellulosic materials through several ways, the main of which
being ammonolysis which results in the breakage of biomass recalcitrance. During this
process, a degradation of the ether and ester side chains of the lignin-carbohydrate
complex takes place as well as a cleavage of the ether bonds of lignin [63, 64]. This
process results in the disruption of the lignin structure and in the swelling of cellulose,
thus leading to an increase of the available surface area and in the decrease of crys-
tallinity [74, 75]. Furthermore, a partial solubilization of HC occurs to various degrees
depending on the pretreatment conditions and the characteristics of the biomass[69].
On the contrary, cellulose is almost entirely retained [69, 70, 72, 73, 76].

2.5.3
AAS parameters
There are several parameters that need to be adjusted in order to achieve optimal
eﬃciency for the process without seriously compromising its economic viability. More-
over, on industrial scale applications, the environmental impact of the use of chemicals
should also be considered. To this end, a series of experiments have been conducted
to assess the conditions under which the diﬀerent materials could be treated.
The parameters that have the most signiﬁcant impact on the process are: the
temperature under which the biomass is treated, the concentration of the ammonia
solution, the duration of the treatment and the solid to liquid ratio (S:L) [77].
Temperature has a profound eﬀect on the treatment as it could lead to a faster
degradation of the material, although its increase would also result in higher costs.
Several values have been reported in the literature with most studies focusing on
mild temperatures ranging up to 75 oC [65]. Another crucial factor of the process is
the concentration of the ammonia solution. In general, higher concentrations could
reduce the need for heat supply considerably, although their use could also severely
compromise the process in terms of costs as the evaporation becomes increasingly
diﬃcult to be implemented eﬃciently. A parameter that may resolve these issues
is the elongation of the incubation period, the implementation of which could allow
the application of lower temperatures and concentrations. However, the downside of
this method is the need for a higher capital investment due to the increased spatial
requirements. Finally, another factor that should be noted is the S:L ratio. A high
ratio may substantially decrease the need for chemicals and water, thus lowering
the variable costs of the industry. Considering all the factors, it is clear that the
achievement of an optimal process eﬃciency is a diﬃcult task in which the various
parameters and their interactions should be assessed thoroughly.

Materials and methods
3.1
Substrate origin and inoculum
The amount of SM that was used over the course of the experiments was obtained
from the biogas plant located in Solrød and it was subsequently stored in freezers
at -18 oC to prevent it from degrading. The additional biomasses that were used
included WS, CM and SW. These biomasses were obtained from the KU experimental
park in Taastrup, Madsen Bioenergi in Spøttrup and from the Solrød biogas plant
respectively. All of them were dried and milled to 1 mm in order to prevent clogging
issues from appearing.
To initiate the process, eﬄuent and inoculum were collected from the lab-scale
CSTR digesters already operating with SM. Consequently, the biomass obtained was
already acclimatized to the feedstock.
3.2
AAS treatment
For the AAS treatment to take place, 600 ml of 15% w/w ammonia solution was
prepared and biomass was added to achieve a S:L ratio of 50 g TS/L. Subsequently,
the distillation ﬂasks were secured with paraﬁlm tape and were placed under a fume
cupboard. After a period of 4 days, water was added to the ﬂasks in order to facilitate
the removal of ammonia and the distillation process was initiated using a Rotavapor
R-300 evaporator made by Buchi. The process was conducted under a pressure of 130
mbar while the temperature gradually increased from 20 to 80 oC over the course of
several minutes. The resulting solutions contained only a very small concentration of
ammonia (<1 g/L). Afterwards, the pretreated biomasses were stored in the freezer
and SM was only added prior to their use.

3.3
Feeding medium preparation
The amount of SM that was required for the experiment was thawed in batches
and maintained at 4 oC to prevent any possible degradation from occurring. In order
to prepare the feeding medium in both digesters, a TS-based ratio of 50% SM to 50%
additional biomass was followed throughout the experiments by properly diluting the
biomass with tap water. This procedure was followed once or twice per week and the
ﬂasks produced were then sealed and stored in the refrigerator.
3.4
Experimental process
3.4.1
Experimental set-up
The continuous experiments were conducted by utilizing two identical conﬁgura-
tions, in each one of which, a CSTR reactor of 3 L working volume was set in op-
eration. For ensuring mesophilic conditions (37oC) in both digesters, hot water was
circulated in the void space between the reactor’s cylinders. The temperature of the
water bath was checked through a temperature controller while a mechanical stirrer
was attached to each digester to enforce an adequate mix of the reactor’s content. For
the mixing of the input, magnetic stirrers were used with the same mixing schedule as
that of the digesters. Two controllers were introduced to ensure intermittent 10-min
stirring every 3 hours.
The feeding of the digesters was conducted through two separate peristaltic pumps
which were adjusted to serve their corresponding reactor, thus ensuring a suﬃcient
HRT in both systems. The peristaltic pumps were set in motion every day for 1 min
while a 15-min stirring circle was initiated prior to the feed followed by a 15-min
mixing circle after the completion of the feed.
Biogas was constantly being removed through a tube adjusted slightly above the
level of the liquor. Similarly, the liquid eﬄuent was also removed when it reached a
certain level, through the tube and into a glass vessel. This system allowed the level
of the liquor inside the reactors to be maintained.
The eﬄuent ﬂasks were carefully positioned with their tubings facing upwards,

July 27, 2020
thus allowing the biogas to be separated while the eﬄuents were being removed twice
per week before reaching the height of the tube.
The production of biogas in both digesters was tracked by connecting them to
MilliGascounter devices made by Ritter. Speciﬁcally, the ﬁrst digester was connected
to a measuring device packed with hydrochloric acid (HCl) 1.8% while the second
one was linked to a measuring device operating with silox. The volumetric measuring
chambers of the devices were 3.13 ml and 3.26 ml respectively.

Chapter 5
Summary and conclusions
The main goal of the present thesis was the evaluation of continuous anaerobic
co-digestion of SM with the carbon-rich biomasses of WS, CM and SW and the eﬀect
of AAS pretreatment on the eﬃciency of the process. In order of achieving this goal,
two systems, each one consisting of a CSTR reactor, were operated under mesophilic
conditions for a duration of 157 days. The monitoring of the process was thorough,
thus enabling the drawing of useful conclusions regarding the eﬀect of AAS in the
CH4 yield and in the productivity of the digesters.
BMP tests that have been conducted previously with raw and pretreated WS
captured an increase of 21.8% in the CH4 yield after 17 days (40 ml inoculum, 1 g TS
WS) under similar conditions to the ones applied in this study (16.5 % NH3 solution ,
4 days duration, S:L= 50 g/L) [5]. In the present thesis, the average OLR of digester
A during phase II (1.04 ± 0.11 g VS/L/d) was signiﬁcantly higher in comparison
to the OLR values of the rest of the experiment (Table 4.3, Table 4.4). This fact
resulted in diﬃculties in assessing the eﬀect of AAS pretreatment. In this context,
the introduction of AAS-treated WS on day 76 (digester B) led to a substantially
higher increase in the yield of digester B (23.79% compared to 11.84%) and a higher
increase in BPR (23.41% compared to 0.61%).
On the other hand, a comparison between the digesters on phase III revealed
that AAS of CM had a negative impact on the process, leading to a decrease in the
productivity levels (-18.97%) and in the CH4 yield (-23.62%). Finally, the eﬀect of
AAS on SW could not be properly assessed since phase IV(SM + SW) did not take
place in digester A due to time limitations. However, continuous experiments that
have been conducted previously under thermophilic conditions (55oC) with a diﬀerent
co-digestion ratio (0.80 cattle manure:0.20 SW) reported a productivity of 277 ± 22.6
ml CH4/L/d and a CH4 yield of 123.85 ± 10.1 ml CH4/g VS, although in this case,
the OLR was signiﬁcantly higher (2.50 g VS/L/d) [80].

Chapter 6
Future perspectives
The results of this study demonstrated the eﬀect of AAS in the carbon-rich biomasses
of WS, CM and SW and their impact on the eﬃciency of SM-based anaerobic diges-
tion. To begin with, the addition of AAS-treated WS led to a signiﬁcant increase in
the BPR and CH4 yield while it also stabilized the process, thus demonstrating the
potential usefulness of this solution. However, additional research is still required for
investigating the limitations in terms of OLR and HRT. To this end, an increase in
the dry matter of the inﬂuent should also be considered and it could potentially pro-
vide more accurate results for industrial applications. In this study, the introduction
of higher concentrations of dry matter was not possible due to the clogging of the
inﬂuent tubings. Lastly, another interesting prospect that could be investigated is
the continuous anaerobic co-digestion of SM with optimally-treated WS.
On the other hand, the co-digestion of SM with CM showed that the AAS pre-
treatment has a negative impact on the energy output of the latter. This observation
needs to be further investigated as the compositional changes that occur during the
treatment have not been suﬃciently documented yet. Furthermore, the application
of AAS under milder conditions should also be considered.
Finally, since an indication for the eﬀect of AAS on SW cannot be drawn without
investigating the continuous anaerobic co-digestion of SM and raw SW under similar
operating conditions, this experimental process should be continued. In conjunction
to this, modelling tools and BMP tests could be employed to obtain and validate the
optimal pretreatment conditions and the optimal co-digestion ratio.
