Abstract
Targeted and controlled drug delivery remains a key challenge in pharmaceutical research.
StimuliôÙresponsive systems offer a promising approach by enabling drug release in reôÙ
sponse to specific internal or external cues. This thesis investigates the development
of a piezoelectric and conductive bacterial cellulose (BC)ôÙbased drug delivery platform
designed for onôÙdemand release triggered by mechanical stimulation.
BC membranes were synthesized via microbial fermentation and functionalized with piezoôÙ
electric fillers, such as barium titanate (BTO) and a sodium bismuth titanateãbarium tiôÙ
tanate system (BBO), as well as conductive polymers (polypyrrole) to create composite
films. These materials were characterized using SEM, FTIR, and XRD to assess morpholôÙ
ogy, chemical composition, and crystallinity. Electromechanical testing confirmed voltage
and current generation under mechanical loading, while additional analyses evaluated
mechanical strength, swelling behavior, drug loading capacity, and in vitro drug release
under both passive and ultrasoundôÙtriggered conditions.
The composites exhibited good structural integrity and reproducible electromechanical
performance. Among all tested formulations, BCôÙBBO 10% and BC with polypyrrole and
BBO 10% showed the highest electromechanical response. Despite this, no significant
difference was observed between passive and ultrasoundôÙtriggered drug release, indicatôÙ
ing that mechanical responsiveness did not translate into enhanced release under the
current conditions. Further optimization is needed to improve stimulusôÙcontrolled delivery
and fully harness the potential of these multifunctional materials for responsive therapeutic
applications.

Introduction
Targeted and controlled drug delivery remains a critical challenge in modern medicine. In
recent years, significant progress has been made to address this issue. Some of the latest
advancements focus on optimizing formulations to boost the efficacy of active compounds,
while others emphasize innovative delivery routes to enhance precision and reduce side
effects.
Among these innovations, much attention has been directed toward improving existing
methods of administration. Currently, oral and parenteral routes are the most commonly
used in clinical settings [1, 2]. Although each of these methods provides specific advanôÙ
tages, they are accompanied by inherent limitations that compromise their overall theraôÙ
peutic efficacy. These challenges have driven growing interest in the development of more
advanced drug delivery systems. In particular, conventional approaches are frequently
associated with the following issues [1, 3, 4]:
ãÂ Low Bioavailability ã Many orally administered drugs have poor solubility or are exôÙ
tensively metabolized by the liver, leading to reduced absorption and suboptimal
therapeutic effects.
ãÂ NonôÙSpecific Drug Distribution ã Traditional delivery methods lack precision in tarôÙ
geting diseased tissues, leading to widespread drug distribution throughout the body.
This nonôÙspecificity can reduce treatment efficiency and contribute to unnecessary
drug exposure in healthy tissues.
ãÂ Systemic Side Effects and Toxicity ã Due to the nonôÙtargeted nature of most convenôÙ
tional drugs, high doses are often required to achieve therapeutic effects, increasing
the risk of systemic toxicity and adverse reactions. This is particularly concerning
in treatments like chemotherapy, where cytotoxic drugs affect both cancerous and
healthy cells.
ãÂ Frequent Dosing and Poor Patient Compliance ã Many conventional treatments reôÙ
quire frequent dosing due to short drug halfôÙlives, leading to reduced adherence,
especially in chronic diseases that require longôÙterm management.
ãÂ Inability to Respond to Physiological Changes ã Traditional drug delivery systems
cannot adapt to dynamic biological conditions, making it difficult to optimize drug
release in response to disease progression or patientôÙspecific factors.
To address these limitations, one of the most promising advances in drug administration
is the development of stimuliôÙresponsive delivery systems. These systems aim to take
advantage of the environmental changes induced by diseases in the body, releasing the
drug when needed [5]. They can be triggered by various stimuli, such as shifts in pH,
changes in enzyme expression, redox conditions, temperature changes, among others.
Despite their promise, stimuliôÙresponsive drug delivery systems face several challenges
that limit their potential for clinical application. A key challenge lies in the complexity of
designing systems capable of reliably responding to specific stimuli within the human body.
Furthermore, concerns about biocompatibility, biodegradability, and nonôÙtoxicity must be
carefully addressed before proposing the design of any drug delivery system. Lastly, their
personalized and intricate nature makes largeôÙscale production costly and effortôÙintensive
[6].

2.1
Overview of Drug Delivery Systems
Drug delivery systems can be defined as ãtechnological systems that formulate and store
drug molecules into suitable forms like tablets or solutions for administrationã [7]. These
systems have been a fundamental aspect of medicine for centuries, evolving from rudiôÙ
mentary herbal remedies to sophisticated pharmaceutical formulations.
Traditional drug administration methods, such as oral delivery, injections, and topical apôÙ
plications, have long been the standard for treating various diseases. From these, oral
administration is the most common and convenient method, allowing for easy patient comôÙ
pliance. However, its effectiveness is often limited by factors such as poor solubility, low
bioavailability, and degradation in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract before reaching systemic
circulation [1]. On the other hand, injectable drug delivery offers a more direct approach,
bypassing the GI tract and ensuring faster absorption into the bloodstream. Despite its
advantages, injections can be painful, require medical supervision, and may lead to sysôÙ
temic toxicity due to nonôÙspecific drug distribution [3]. Topical and transdermal delivery
methods, such as creams and patches, provide localized treatment but struggle with penôÙ
etration barriers that limit drug absorption [8].
Given these challenges, there has been a growing need for advanced drug delivery strateôÙ
gies that enhance targeting, minimize side effects, and offer controlled and stimuliôÙresponôÙ
sive release mechanisms [9]. These innovations not only improve therapeutic outcomes
but also contribute to more effective and patientôÙfriendly treatments.
To contextualize the development of such systems, including emerging stimuliôÙresponsive
and piezoelectricôÙbased carriers, it is important to first examine the specific drawbacks of
traditional delivery routes. The following subsections provide a detailed overview of these
methods and the challenges that modern systems are designed to overcome.
2.1.1
Oral Drug Delivery
Oral administration continues to dominate pharmaceutical delivery, with approximately
60 % of approved smallôÙmolecule drugs and nearly 90% of all humanôÙuse formulations
relying on this route [10]. Moreover, around 84% of topôÙselling pharmaceutical products
are administered orally, reflecting its continued preference in clinical practice [11]. Its value
lies in several key advantages, including the potential for selfôÙadministration, high patient
compliance, and its nonôÙinvasive, painless nature compared to other delivery methods.
Oral Drug Absorption: Anatomical Sites and Formulation Approaches
Most orally administered drugs are designed to exert systemic effects after absorption
into the bloodstream. Absorption can occur at various sites along the gastrointestinal
tract, including the buccal cavity, stomach, small intestine, colon, and rectumëƒeach with
distinct anatomical and physiological properties that influence drug uptake [12]. FormulaôÙ
tion approaches are often employed to target specific regions and enhance the efficiency
of absorption.
In the case of the buccal cavity, this site is favorable for drug absorption due to its rich
blood supply and lymphatic drainage, allowing for rapid onset and bypassing firstôÙpass
liver metabolism. However, absorption is limited by factors such as small surface area,
short transit time, poor solubility of highly lipophilic drugs, and low permeability of polar
compounds [13]. Additionally, absorption varies by location within the oral cavity, with the

sublingual area generally providing better uptake than the cheeks or gums [4]. Therefore,
buccal drug formulations should ensure prolonged retention in the mouth to maximize
absorption [14].
Regarding the stomach, this organ plays a relatively minor role in drug absorption due
to its limited surface area and short transit time, especially when drugs are taken on an
empty stomach. Its acidic environment can influence drug solubility and stability, for exôÙ
ample: basic drugs tend to dissolve more in low pH, while acidôÙlabile and ionizable drugs
may degrade or become less absorbable [4]. Moreover, the rate of gastric emptying is
a crucial determinant of the speed at which a medication arrives in the small intestine,
where absorption mainly occurs. This rate is affected by factors such as whether the
drug is consumed with or without food, the mealãs consistency and composition, and the
presence of specific drugs or conditions [15].
Of all the sites involved at the oral route administration, the small intestine is the major
site of absorption for most orally administered drugs. The main reason to this is the large
surface area generated by the folds of Kerckring, that are covered with small fingerôÙlike
structures called villi. Each villus is made up of many cells that have even smaller projecôÙ
tions on their surface called microvilli. Thanks to these structures the absorptive surface
area of the small intestine increases 600 times that of a cylinder with the same diameter
with a plane surface. [16]
Nevertheless, absorption varies along the small intestine, with the duodenum and jejunum
having greater permeability to waterôÙsoluble molecules and small ions than the ileum,
thanks to larger aqueous pores and more active transport systems [4]. Additionally, pH
levels and bacterial populations differ along the segments, influencing drug solubility and
absorption [16]. However, not all of a drug dose is absorbedëƒ some is lost due to degradaôÙ
tion, metabolism by gut enzymes, or excretion. Even after absorption, drugs may undergo
metabolism in the liver before reaching systemic circulation, this process is known as firstôÙ
pass metabolism. The extent of this varies between drugs and can significantly impact
oral bioavailability, with some drugs unsuitable for oral use due to extensive firstôÙpass
metabolism [4].
Beyond these general factors, absorption is also affected at the cellular level by specific
transport mechanisms and metabolic activity within the intestinal lining. One important
system is the PôÙglycoprotein efflux transporter, which is located on the apical surface of
intestinal epithelial cells. This efflux transporter works by pumping certain drugs back into
the intestinal lumen, which can reduce their overall absorption [17]. When multiple drugs
that rely on this transporter are taken together, they may compete for access, leading to
potential drug interactions [18]. Similarly, metabolic enzymes present in the small intestine
can break down drugs before they are absorbed. The activity of these enzymes can be
increased or decreased by other drugs, which may also lead to interactions and changes
in the extent of absorption [4].
The amount of time a drug spends in the small intestine, also known as the intestinal
transit time, is another important factor in determining how much of the drug is absorbed.
On average, this transit time ranges from 6 to 8 hours but can vary depending on the
presence of food, certain medical conditions, other medications, and the physical charôÙ
acteristics of the dosage form Drugs taken without food usually pass through the small
intestine more quickly. Conditions like constipation or intestinal blockage tend to slow
transit, while diarrhea or irritable bowel syndrome can speed it up. When using extendedôÙ
release formulations, it is important that the drug either fully releases within this time frame
or is absorbable in the large intestine to ensure effectiveness. [4]

Most drugs from standard dosage forms such as tablets, capsules, suspensions, or soluôÙ
tions are absorbed in the small intestine. Drugs in solution are immediately available for
absorption once they reach the right location, while suspensions must dissolve first. Solid
forms like tablets and capsules must disintegrate and then dissolve before absorption can
occur [19]. Generally, drugs in solution are absorbed faster than those in suspensions,
and both are absorbed more quickly than capsules or tablets [20]. ModifiedôÙrelease formuôÙ
lations are designed to release the drug more slowly, maintaining therapeutic levels over
a longer period [21]. In all cases, the design of the dosage form and the time it remains
in the gastrointestinal tract play a critical role in determining how much and how quickly
the drug is absorbed.
Finally, the large intestine plays a supporting role in oral drug absorption. Although it
follows the small intestine in the gastrointestinal tract and spans about 130 cm in length,
its absorption capacity is limited due to the absence of villi and microvilli, resulting in a
significantly smaller surface area [22]. The pH in this region is approximately 6.5, and
while the intestinal wall exhibits low metabolic activity, the lumen is rich in aerobic and
anaerobic bacteria capable of degrading drugs [4].
Drug absorption in this region primarily occurs through passive diffusion and paracellular
transport. The long transit time (averaging around 14 hours) helps compensate for the
reduced surface area, with absorption mainly occurring in the cecum and ascending colon,
where the content is more hydrated. As water is absorbed moving through the colon, the
contents become thicker, reducing drug solubility and contact with the mucosa. [4]
To deliver drugs effectively to the colon, especially for treating local conditions or achieving
systemic effects, specialized formulations are needed. These often involve coatings that
resist stomach and small intestine conditions, releasing the drug in the ascending colon.
Strategies include acidôÙresistant coatings, timeôÙcontrolled release (around 4 hours postôÙ
gastric emptying), and the use of prodrugs or polymers activated by colonic bacteria [4,
23]. However, absorption is typically delayed and influenced by the variability in transit
times through the upper GI tract.
Physicochemical Limitations of Conventional Oral Drug Delivery
Although a number of formulation strategies have been created to improve drug absorpôÙ
tion at targeted sites within the GI tract, conventional oral delivery systems still encounter
considerable limitations that impact their overall efficacy.
Biological barriers such as anatomical, physiological, and biochemical factors play a cruôÙ
cial role in drug absorption, as discussed in the previous section. Variations in pH, conôÙ
tinuous enzymatic activity, the presence of over 400 species of aerobic and anaerobic
microorganisms, and the complex structure of the epithelial bilayer combined with the
mucus layer all limit the amount of drug absorbed along the gastrointestinal tract [12, 24].
Nevertheless, these biological factors are not the only limitation oral drug delivery faces.

treatmentãs absorption and effectiveness. The Biopharmaceutical Classification System
(BCS) categorizes drugs based on their solubility, permeability, and dissolution characôÙ
teristics in the intestinal environment to better understand and address these limitations
[25]. This system aids in determining if a drugãs absorption is constrained by its solubility
or permeability and assists in creating more effective formulations.

Limitations of Conventional Parenteral Delivery
Even with these benefits, parenteral administration has a number of major drawbacks.
Due to its invasive nature, patients often experience pain, discomfort, and anxietyëƒ facôÙ
tors that can diminish adherence to treatment, particularly in the long term [42]. Local
tissue irritation, inflammation, or fibrosis may also occur as a result of repeated injections
[43]. Additionally, because it involves a physical breach of the skin barrier, parenteral
administration has an increased risk of infection. If aseptic conditions are not rigorously
upheld, there is a risk of localized or systemic infections. In particular, the administration
of IVs is linked to further complications like thrombophlebitis, embolism, and extravasation
[4, 44].
Another significant constraint concerns its practicality. Since parenteral drug administraôÙ
tion usually requires trained healthcare personnel, it is often less convenient and unsuitôÙ
able for selfôÙadministration [45]. This requirement restricts treatment options and raises
healthcare costs. Additionally, the production and distribution of parenteral formulations
are usually costlier because they require sterile manufacturing environments, specialized
packaging, and often cold chain storage [46]. The logistical demands present challenges,
particularly in environments with limited resources.
Moreover, after a drug has been administered through a parenteral route (particularly an
intravenous one) it becomes irreversible, as the drug cannot be taken out of systemic
circulation [4]. This increases the risk of serious side effects in case of incorrect dosing,
making careful titration and close observation necessary. The physicochemical properties
of the drug, such as poor solubility, also impose limitations on formulation development.

2.1.3
Other Routes of Drug Delivery
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has acknowledged more than a hundred
methods for administering drugs, beyond oral and parenteral routes, with commonly used
alternatives including ophthalmic, auricular, nasal, and vaginal routes [4]. Route selection
is contingent upon aspects like the properties of the drug, type of formulation, individual
patient requirements, and the ailment being addressed.
In the case of ophthalmic delivery, due to the minimal systemic distribution of drugs to
the eye, local ocular drug delivery is generally necessary for treating eye conditions [51].
Drugs are most often administered either topically to the surface of the eye or via injection
into the vitreous body. To prevent irritation, infection, or immune reactions, these products
must be sterile, nonôÙimmunogenic, pyrogenôÙfree, and isotonic [4]. Traditional formulations
encompass solutions, suspensions, and ointments.
Tear production is a significant hurdle in ocular drug delivery, as it dilutes and swiftly reôÙ

necessitates frequent reapplication. Methods such as raising viscosity, modifying dose
volume, and employing inserts or contact lenses can aid in extending drug retention. [52]
The cornea is composed of layers: a lipophilic epithelium and endothelium, along with a
hydrophilic stroma. For effective penetration, drugs should possess balanced hydrophilic
and lipophilic properties [53]. Since unionized forms penetrate better, it is crucial to buffer
the pH in order to enhance absorption [54]. Moreover, the binding of proteins in tears also
restricts the availability of drugs, particularly during inflammation that elevates the protein
content of tears [55].

the glucan chains spontaneously come together to form nanoribbons, which then arrange
into microfibrils and strips, creating a highly porous threeôÙdimensional network. It is also
important to mention that bacterial cellulose (BC) can be produced by different bacterial
genera such as Agrobacterium spp., Acetobacter spp., and Azotobacter, just to name a
few [165].
At laboratory conditions, BC can be generated through two primary fermentation techôÙ
niques: static and agitated fermentation. The selected method influences the physical
and mechanical characteristics of the resulting BC, thereby determining its potential apôÙ
plications. [173]
During static fermentation, BC develops as a thick layer at the interface between air and
liquid. This arrangement leads to an uneven distribution of oxygen and nutrients within
the BC layer: the lower side, which is exposed to the liquid medium, receives less oxygen,
while the upper side has a limited supply of nutrients. This method usually necessitates a
longer cultivation duration, a greater surface area, and additional manual work, resulting
in lower productivity efficiency. Nevertheless, this technique produces highôÙquality BC
that is particularly wellôÙsuited for medical applications. [173]
In contrast, agitated fermentation generates BC as floating fibers or pellets that are disôÙ
persed throughout the liquid medium. The advantage of this method is that it produces
faster and yields more within a shorter time frame. Nevertheless, the agitationôÙinduced

mechanical stress can cause mutations in the bacterial strains, resulting in a cessation
of BC production. These mutant strains frequently exhibit a faster growth rate than the
original bacteria that produce BC, which leads to a decrease in the overall yield. Even
with this difficulty, for the purpose of producing BC on an industrial scale, where speed
and volume take precedence for commercial use, agitated fermentation is regarded as

In both fermentation styles, the selection and formulation of culture media are crucial facôÙ

In addition to yield and cost, the culture medium affects the microstructure and mechanical

demonstrated that various carbon sources, including crude glycerol or sodium alginate,
can modify the crystallinity, fiber diameter, and porosity of BC. For example, glycerol typiôÙ

devices, or a greater surface area to accommodate high drug loading.
2.4.3
BC Composites and Hybrid Systems
While the choice of culture media plays a pivotal role in modulating the yield and structural
properties of BC, further functional enhancement can be achieved through the formation
of composites and hybrid systems.
There are two main strategies to obtain said composites. One of them corresponds to
in situ modification, which enables the addition of additives directly during the bacterial
fermentation process [175]. The structure and properties of BC can be customized in
real time by modifying growth conditions and incorporating reinforcing agents, eliminating
the disadvantages associated with postôÙsynthesis treatments [176]. One example of this
technique is the in situ synthesis of a BC/carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) composite [177].
Researchers added CMC directly into the culture medium during synthesis to influence
the nanostructure of the cellulose as it formed. This approach aimed to enhance the mateôÙ
rialãs water absorption and rehydration properties, making it more suitable for biomedical
applications like wound dressings.
On the other hand, ex situ modification refers to the one made after the byosinthesis of the
cellulose film is done. This method usually entails soaking BC in solutions with plasticizers,
polymers, or other substances [175]. This approach utilizes the naturally porous, threeôÙ
dimensional structure of BC, enabling added materials to infiltrate the matrix, coat the
fibers, or occupy the spaces between them, often resulting in reduced porosity. A major
advantage of ex situ modification is that it allows the incorporation of various reinforcement
materials without disrupting the native structure of bacterial cellulose. This method retains
the basic architecture of BC and avoids compatibility issues with antimicrobial agents,

An interesting example of these ex situ modification is the addition of conductive polyôÙ
mers, such as polypyrrole (ppy). Although BC has an intrinsic piezoelectric property, its
low electrical conductivity restricts its responsiveness to external stimuli. This limitation
is addressed by integrating Ppy into the BC matrix, which introduces high conductivity
and facilitates signalôÙdriven functions like electrically controlled drug release or sensing.
The BC/Ppy composite produced retains the mechanical integrity and biocompatibility of
BC while acquiring Ppyãs electrochemical responsiveness, making it particularly useful
in applications that use external electrical signals to trigger or regulate functionality. For

Building on the versatility of BCôÙbased composites, recent research has explored the
functional enhancement of BC through the incorporation of responsive components [180].
These hybrid systems enhance mechanical and structural properties while also facilitating
dynamic behaviors in response to external stimuli. With these, BC can be converted
into a smart platform for controlled drug release by utilizing materials such as conductive
polymers, pHôÙsensitive agents, or nanostructures. This paves the way for inventive drug
delivery systems that react selectively to physiological or external cues, positioning BC
as a prime candidate for stimuliôÙresponsive biomedical uses.

Proposal
Conventional systems for delivering drugs to the intestine frequently have disadvantages,
including inadequate localization, a lack of precise control over release timing, and diôÙ
minished therapeutic efficacy as a result of physiological variability. To address these
limitations, this proposal explores the development of a stimuliôÙresponsive drug delivery
platform utilizing a composite of piezoelectric materials and bacterial cellulose, specifically
designed for ultrasoundôÙtriggered release in the intestinal environment.
As highlighted in the literature review, piezoelectric materials can transform mechanical
energy, like that provided by external ultrasound, into localized electrical stimuli, which can
be utilized to induce drug release as needed. Bacterial cellulose provides a biocompatible
and structurally adaptable matrix that supports drug loading and diffusion, as well as inteôÙ
gration with functional materials. This method seeks to enhance delivery and therapeutic
outcomes specific to the site by allowing for precise release that is externally controlled
and facilitated by nonôÙinvasive ultrasound stimulation.
The design will utilize bacterial cellulose films that are incubated with piezoelectric ceramôÙ
ics like barium titanate and bismuthôÙbased oxides, recognized for their strong electromeôÙ
chanical coupling and biocompatibility. At the same time, a conductive form of cellulose
will be produced by adding polypyrrole, a conductive polymer that improves electrical reôÙ
sponsiveness and facilitates better integration with electronic stimulation strategies. AddiôÙ
tionally, this conductive cellulose will be combined with piezoelectric ceramics to produce
multifunctional hybrid films that can sense and actuate under ultrasound.

dielectric constant, weakening the electromechanical coupling effect.

5% BBO composite, but even our best sample does not reach the maximum voltage reôÙ

study differs from our fabrication process, which may influence the dispersion, alignment,
and overall microstructure of the composites, thereby affecting their electromechanical
performance. In comparison to their study, our bacterial BBO 10% composite produced a
maximum mean voltage around 5 V at 20 N. This indicates a good piezoelectric response,
though lower than the optimized BTO/BC systems by the previous study, highlighting the
crucial role of filler type, concentration, and fabrication method in determining the elecôÙ

bacterial cellulose matrices. This outcome highlights the importance of aligning ultrasound

prioritize the use of frequencies known to induce cavitation and matrix disruption, and
consider equipment flexibility as a key factor in experimental design.

Conclusion and Future Work
In conclusion, although the development and characterization of bacterial cellulose comôÙ
posites incorporating piezoelectric and conductive materials were successfully achieved,
their application as a platform for drug release under ultrasound stimulation did not meet
expectations. Nonetheless, the electromechanical performance under mechanical loadôÙ
ing and the structural integrity confirmed by chemical and morphological analyses provide
a solid foundation for future exploration.
This thesis successfully demonstrated the development of bacterial cellulose composites
functionalized with piezoelectric ceramics and conductive polymers. Moreover, initial culôÙ
ture optimization revealed that a 14ôÙday incubation produced films with higher mechanical
integrity and handling qualities compared to shorter periods. Additionally, the integration
of piezoelectric ceramic particles (BTO and BBO) and conductive polypyrrole into the BC
matrix was achieved without compromising film flexibility or structural stability. Regarding
ceramic concentration, higher concentrations (10%) resulted in denser and more uniform
particle loading, with BTO showing superior dispersion compared to BBO, likely due to
differences in surface chemistry and interfacial bonding.
The SEM morphological analysis showed that critical point drying effectively preserved
the porous nanofibrous architecture of BC, which was maintained upon composite formaôÙ
tion. FTIR and XRD characterization confirmed strong interactions between BC and the
incorporated materials, with no formation of unwanted secondary phases. These findings
indicate successful composite formation while preserving the crystalline identity of the
fillers and the semiôÙcrystalline nature of BC.
In the case of electromechanical properties, it was demonstrated that increasing force
from 5 N to 20 N consistently enhanced voltage and current outputs across all samples,
confirming the expected piezoelectric response. Composites containing 10% BBO exhibôÙ
ited the highest voltage generation, while those combining polypyrrole and BBO achieved
superior current outputs.
Tests for swelling and recovery revealed different ways the material absorbed water. NaôÙ
tive BC and polypyrroleôÙmodified samples showed excellent initial swelling but limited reôÙ
hydration capacity postôÙdrying. On the other hand, BBOôÙcontaining composites exhibited
reduced swelling but improved water recovery, indicative of enhanced structural stability.
The PPYôÙBBO composites demonstrated the highest recovery percentages, suggesting
a synergistic effect that preserves porous structure and promotes reversible hydration.
The passive drug loading and release test showed important findings about composite
structure, drug properties, and loading protocols. Paracetamol loading faced significant
challenges, including interference during quantification and low recovery in release studôÙ
ies. This can suggest limited drug incorporation and potential premature loss during comôÙ
posite formation. These results emphasize the need to refine loading methods for small
molecule drugs to improve incorporation efficiency and accurate drug release.
In contrast, FD4, a larger macromolecular compound, was incorporated efficiently across
all composite formulations, with loading percentages consistently around 70ã90%. The
majority of the drug remained inside the composite after washing, showing strong physiôÙ
cal entrapment by the nanofibrillar structure of bacterial cellulose. Despite this effective
StimuliôÙResponsive Piezoelectric Carrier as an OnôÙDemand Drug Delivery Payload
59
==PAGE:68==
loading, the release of FD4 was less than 10% of the initially loaded amount, highlighting
the compositeãs ability to retain macromolecules tightly.
Attempts to stimulate drug release using highôÙfrequency ultrasound (10 MHz) did not yield
the anticipated improvement in the drugãs release. Despite the application of two 1ôÙminute
ultrasound pulses at distinct time points, cumulative FD4 release under ultrasound was
actually lower than under passive conditions. This demonstrates the limitations of employôÙ
ing highôÙfrequency ultrasound for drug release from BCôÙbased composites. Usually, the
10 MHz frequency is primarily employed for imaging purposes and offers shallow peneôÙ
tration with minimal cavitation effects, which are critical for disrupting polymeric matrices
and enhancing drug diffusion.
This failure to improve release with ultrasound shows the importance of aligning the physôÙ
ical and mechanical characteristics of the drug delivery system with the appropriate stimôÙ
ulus parameters. Typically, cavitation requires much lower frequencies (20ã40 kHz) that
were not tested in this project. Without this mechanical disruption, the dense BC netôÙ
work remains intact and continues to strongly retain the drug molecules, limiting diffusion
despite external stimulation attempts.
These findings show that, while BC composites provide excellent drug retention and bioôÙ
compatibility, their dense structure may present challenges for stimuliôÙresponsive release.
This can be overcome in future research if coupled with suitable triggering mechanisms.
Future work should employ ultrasound frequencies known to induce cavitation and matrix
disruption and evaluate alternative stimuli or composite modifications that enhance matrix
responsiveness. Additionally, experimental design must account for equipment limitations
to ensure that the chosen stimulus parameters are compatible with the physical properties
of the delivery platform.
