atmospheric icing
Author
Wiktor Wojciechowski
Supervisors
Holger Koss, PhD
Allan Larsen, PhD
This thesis attempts to identify the impact of ice on the stability of bridge stay cables.
Static force measurements of an ice-accreted cable cover section were used to predict
instability using theoretical models.
Several icing conditions were tested by varying
the air temperature, cable inclination and icing direction. Critical wind directions were
determined and compared to the case of dry inclined cable galloping. The instability
criteria were subsequently used to deﬁne the minimum levels of structural damping
necessary to prevent excitation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Ice-wind induced vibrations have been a well known issue in relation to power lines,
with research dating back almost a century (Figure 1.1). The most common type of
conductor instability is the high amplitude, self-excited galloping. In fact, in the last
decades, it was so frequent that the associated societal costs justiﬁed the creation of
event databases made by trained observers along with special task forces focused on
analysing the phenomenon [2].
(a) Example of build–up of low density ice ac-
cretion on conductors (Figure 5.7 from [35])
(b) Close up shot of ice glaze on power lines
with electricity pylons in the background after
a sleet storm. Slovenia. Europe [32]
Figure 1.1: Examples of ice accretion on power lines
Yet when it comes to bridge cables, ice-wind induced vibrations are a relatively uncharted
research area compared to other instability sources such as dry inclined cable galloping
or rain-wind induced vibrations.
The issue of ice generated vibrations is of course most relevant in colder climate countries.
For instance in March 2001 large amplitude vibrations (reaching 2m) were observed
on the vertical hangers of the Great Belt Bridge in Denmark.
A thin ice layer was
subsequently observed on the cables, leading to the presumption that it was the cause
of galloping.[14]
1
==PAGE:20==
2
Chapter 1 Introduction
However icing of bridge cables can also occur in regions where the climate wouldn’t seem
to allow it. An example would be the cable-stayed Dubrovnik Bridge in Croatia.
Opened in 2002, the bridge has a 244m main span supported by 10 pairs of stays, the
longest being 226.7m long (Figure 1.2). Savor et al. [31] made a case study of the bridge
after it has been hit by two major snowstorms (March 20051 and May 2006). During
the ﬁrst event the stays vibrated for over two hours with amplitudes of over 2.5m, so
big that the HDPE protective pipes got damaged as they smashed against light posts.
On the second occasion the stays vibrated for over 6 hours, this time with a TV crew
recording how the newly repaired sheathing breaks down again along with some high-
strength bolts at the pylon anchorage.
Figure 1.2: Dubrovnik Bridge: view [29](left), snow deposits on windward side (right)
and HDPE sheathing damage (bottom) [31]
The study pointed out the incredibly low structural damping which for the ﬁrst two
modes of the 5 longest stays resulted in a Scruton number between Sc = 1 ÷ 2.
After the second storm vibration countermeasures were installed. It was decided to keep
the smooth HDPE sheathing as the installation of a more aerodynamically stable surface
treatment would prove ”diﬃcult, impractical and cost-prohibitive”.
Instead external
dampers were mounted at the lower cable-stay anchorages. The retroﬁt was estimated
to have raised the Scruton number for the aforementioned stays to Sc > 20.

Thesis objective
The main purpose of this thesis is to predict instability regions for an inclined iced
bridge cable section. The analysis should be based on experimental data from static
force measurements of an iced cable. These measurements, being part of this thesis,
should cover multiple icing scenarios with the cable exposed to a wide range of angles
of attack and wind velocities. Instability should be predicted using existing models e.g.
the Den Hartog Criterion for galloping.
Icing of the test section shall be done with a spray bar in a refrigerated facility, attempt-
ing to simulate in-cloud icing conditions. The tests shall consider icing from diﬀerent
directions. Moreover two types of ice accretion should be considered, namely wet and
dry.
Due to a limited time-frame it has been decided that the experiments should be done
for only the most realistic weather conditions. These should be assessed by analysing
weather station data from the Copenhagen area, hence reﬂecting the conditions at the
Øresund Bridge linking Denmark and Sweden.
1.2
Thesis structure
This report is structured in the following way.
In the beginning, Chapter 2, a theoretical introduction is given to explain the basics of
cylinder aerodynamics, icing of bridge cables and the instability criteria used.
Afterwards, in Chapter 3, an analysis of weather data is shown with the attention on
wind speed and wind direction. This knowledge will help plan the experimental part.
Chapter 4 contains information about the experimental setup. This includes the avail-
able facilities, the method of gathering data and the test model with the employed
geometrical deﬁnitions.
Chapters 5 and 6 present the results of the experimental work and describe how they
were obtained. The shape of these two chapters is slightly diﬀerent from the rest of the
thesis in the sense that in addition to facts they also aim to convey the story behind the
results and as such paint a greater picture of the thesis.
Work is concluded in Chapter 7

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter aims to provide the theoretical background necessary to discuss bridge ca-
ble instability. First an introduction is made to the basic concepts used when describing
cylinder aerodynamics. These include ﬂow regimes, vortex shedding, the parameters de-
scribing them as well as force coeﬃcients and the eﬀects of roughness. Afterwards icing
of bridge cables is brieﬂy presented. Here various forms of ice accretion are described
along with the variables aﬀecting ice growth. Finally, the last section covers the insta-
bility of cables. The instability criteria, used in this project to predict cable vibrations,
are introduced.

Cylinder aerodynamics
2.1.1
Basic cylinder aerodynamics
The Reynolds number (Re) is a non dimensional measure expressing the ratio between
the inertia and viscous forces in a ﬂuid. At low Re numbers the viscous forces dominate
while at high Re numbers the inertia forces are stronger.
Re = U · D

The Reynolds number is very often used to deﬁne diﬀerent ﬂow regimes, these are shown
in Figure 2.1. Lower Re numbers are important when considering the hydrodynamics
around structures. In aerodynamics, due to the low viscosity of air, only regimes from
the subcritical (turbulent wake) and beyond are relevant.

An important phenomena when discussing cylinder aerodynamics is vortex shedding.
It occurs at Re > 40 when the vortices forming on both sides get exposed to small
disturbances.
The mechanism of this periodical action can be describes as follows.
Vortex A, forming on one side of the cylinder, grows large creating a pressure diﬀerence
which drags the smaller vortex B from the opposite side across the wake. When the two
meet vortex B cuts the supply to vortex A resulting in the latter being shed oﬀas a
free vortex. At that point vortex B begins to grow large and the process repeats itself.
Examples of this mesmerizing pattern also known as the von K´arm´an vortex street are
shown in Figure 2.2.

In-cloud icing
The in-cloud icing mechanism is similar to that of precipitation icing. The diﬀerence
lies in the size of the supercooled droplets which for in-cloud icing is < 0.5 mm(glaze)
and ≈0.01 mm(rime). As the name suggests, droplets of this size are found in clouds as
well as in fog.
In-cloud icing can be further subdivided into two types, rime and glaze:
Rime
Is a result of dry ice growth (Figure 2.7a), characterized by the lack of a
liquid water layer on the surface. This means that all of the supercooled
droplets hitting the surface freeze on impact. This in turn results in the
formation of air pockets within the structure. As a consequence rime has
a lower density than pure, bubble free ice. Moreover, depending on the
rate of freezing, the air pockets will vary in size. If the rate of freezing is
very fast more air will be trapped and soft rime will form. Conversely if
the rate of freezing is slower the droplets will be more packed and so called
hard rime will form. Rime can be easily distinguished by a characteristic
’feather’ structure.
Glaze
Is a result of wet ice growth (Figure 2.7b). When the water ﬂux is higher
than the freezing rate, droplets do not have the necessary time to freeze
before the next one lands and a thin ﬁlm of liquid water will form on
the surface. Since the air and surface still have subzero temperatures,
freezing will take place below the liquid layer. As such glaze has a high
density, is solid and clear.

Temperature
Temperature, covering both air and surface temperature governs the freezing rate. Nat-
urally for liquid water to freeze the temperature has to be below 0◦C.
If a droplet does not contain an ice nucleus it can become supercooled whereby it will
freeze on impact with a surface. In case of supercooled droplets the surface does not
necessarily have to have a subzero temperature. The droplet will freeze even if the sur-
face temperature is slightly above 0◦C.
It is known that a supercooled droplet can remain liquid down to around −40◦C, at
which point it will freeze spontaneously despite the lack of an ice nucleus.
Wind speed
Wind speed, in conjunction with other parameters, governs the type of ice accretion as
shown in Figure 2.8. All else equal, a higher wind speed will cause a more wet accretion
as the more frequent impacts will mean a reduced time for freezing.
Wind speed also has an inﬂuence on the shape of the ice, this has been exempliﬁed in
a study by Lozowski et al. [19] results are shown in Figure 2.9. Higher wind speed will
tend to blow oﬀthe water particles before they have time to freeze on the surface. This
eﬀect becomes more pronounced as the LWC increases.

2.3
Cable instability
2.3.1
Overview
Several types of cable instabilities exist, some of them as distinguished by the Federal
Highway Administration [3] include:
• Vortex excitation of an isolated cable or groups of cables
Occur when the vortex shedding frequency matches one of the eigen-frequencies of
the cable. This phenomenon is very case dependent and occurs only at a particular
wind velocity. This velocity,UV S, can be derived from UV S = ωnD/St where ωn is
the frequency of the nth natural mode of the cable.
• Rain/wind-induced vibrations of cables
Can occur as a result of a water rivulet forming on the cable which creates a section
asymmetry.
• Wake galloping of groups of cables
Occur e.g. when the wind direction places one of the cables in the wake of another.
This has an implication on the drag and lift forces. Another source of a wake can
be a structural component e.g. the pylon.
• Galloping of single cables inclined to the wind
For cables inclined to the wind i.e. when the wind velocity has a component along
the span, an instability with the same characteristics as galloping has been ob-
served.
• Galloping of cables with ice accumulations
As mentioned before an asymmetry in the cable cross section caused by ice accre-
tion can lead to galloping.
This project focuses on the last type from the list however all of the above represent the
most critical types which according to the report should be carefully considered by the
designer. A commonly applied parameter for portraying a cables resilience to vibrations
is the ratio of structural mass to ﬂuid mass called the Scruton number (Sc):
Sc = mζ
ρD2
(2.7)
in which m is the mass of cable per unit length, ζ is the damping as a ratio of critical, ρ is
the air density and D is the cable diameter. An increase in the Scruton number will limit
the vibrations. As an example the Sc value considered to eliminate rain/wind-induced
vibrations of low damped cables is Sc > 10. [3]

2.3.2
Instability criteria
Due to the complexity of testing and measuring ﬂuctuating forces on a moving object
in ﬂuid, researchers and engineers have often resorted to predicting dynamic motional
instabilities by applying statically derived drag, lift and moment coeﬃcients to theo-
retical models. This is often referred to as the ”quasi-steady” approach or assumption.
During the last century a number of mathematical models have been proposed using
this approach and over the last decade, negative aerodynamic damping, as a driving
force for vibration, has received renewed attention [13]. Two of these models, namely
the Den Hartog Criterion [11] and the model presented by Macdonald and Larose [21],
are utilized in this work to predict the galloping instability and estimate the associated
aerodynamic damping.
In basic terms the aforementioned aerodynamic damping, ζa, should be considered to-
gether with the structural damping ratio of the cable. If the sum becomes negative then
the cable will undergo excitation. For perspective the inherent cable damping ratio can
range anywhere from 0.0005 to 0.01. An accurate value is diﬃcult to predict however a
realistic estimate for cables of inservice bridges is in the range from 0.001 to 0.005. [3]
Galloping and the Den Hartog Criterion
The phenomenon of galloping was initially observed on power lines. These were noted
to oscillate with great amplitudes and at very low frequencies.
The instability was
observed in cold climate countries and in most cases the cables were covered with sleet.
Furthermore the vibrations were known to continue for long periods, sometimes even
24h, indicating that galloping is a self-exciting vibration.
The mechanism of galloping can be explained with the following example. A galloping
cable is in a downward stroke. No external ﬂow is present, yet as the cable moves through
air it experiences resistance or a ﬂow in the direction opposite to the motion with the
same speed v. Add an external ﬂow with velocity V coming from the side and now
the ﬂow experienced by the cable is a resultant of the two, coming at an angle α where
tan α = v/V . The ﬂow will impose a drag and lift force on the cable and consequently
a force component ,F, with the same orientation as the motion v (Eq. 2.8). In case of
a cylindrical section and perpendicular ﬂow that force will act in the opposite direction
to the motion, hereby damping it. However if the section is irregular it can happen that
the resultant force will act in the same direction as the motion, amplifying it. The cable
will move until the elastic forces engage at which point the motion will change direction
and the process will repeat.

2.3.3
Inﬂuence of turbulence
Symes and Macdonald [34] tried to evaluate the inﬂuence of turbulence on dry inclined
cable galloping. The authors used, among others, ESDU provisions for turbulence cor-
rection and wind tunnel data from tests conducted at the National Research Council
of Canada to estimate the impact of turbulence on the minimum structural damping
necessary to prevent galloping. The ESDU turbulence correction states that ”oncoming
wind turbulence shifts the Reynolds number at which ﬂow transitions occur, but it does
not change the corresponding value of the drag coeﬃcient”. In lack of more information
the authors had assumed that the same applies for the lift coeﬃcient. The conclusion
was that, for dry inclined cables, small-scale turbulence is very beneﬁcial as it can reduce
the damping requirement by up to 87%.
Van Dyke et al. [35] have analysed research conducted over the last few decades re-
garding the eﬀects of turbulence on galloping of bluﬀbodies, in relation to iced power
transmission lines. They came to a rather similar conclusion that galloping behaviour
in turbulent ﬂow might be expected to be similar to that in smooth ﬂow, but shifted as
shown in Figure 2.11. As turbulence intensity in the oncoming ﬂow is increased from
i = 0 to 12%, a soft galloping proﬁle becomes weaker and eventually stable while a hard
galloping proﬁle becomes soft.

On the other hand, impact of ice was also investigated by Chadha and Jaster [8] who
attempted to experimentally measure the inﬂuence of turbulence on the galloping insta-
bility of iced conductors. The test program included static and dynamic tests of hori-
zontal models. Four models were used, ice was simulated using foamed polyurethane.
Turbulence intensity varied between 1-18%. The authors didn’t, however, expand their
tests across a range of velocities as ”the variation [of force coeﬃcients with velocity] was,
in general, found to be small”. Instead wind velocities of 7.7 and 9.2 m/s were used.
According to the measurements, turbulence had a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the magnitude
of force coeﬃcients (especially lift). The authors concluded that turbulence of the air
ﬂow can signiﬁcantly increase the galloping instability as well as the range of unstable
angles of attack, for conductor models.
Clearly more information about the eﬀects of turbulence would be beneﬁcial. Especially
for inclined irregular sections. At the time being, however, the ﬁndings of Symes and
Macdonald [34] seem to be the most ﬁtting and up to date research available.

Weather data analysis
This chapter covers the analysis of weather data in relation to bridge cable icing. The
reason for this ’digression’ from the main subject is ironically the limited time-frame
of this thesis. This analysis was done as one of the steps undertaken to streamline the
experimental work.
Previous experiments on inclined iced bridge cables were done by C. Demartino as part
of a Ph.D. project[10]. In these experiments the inclined cable was iced for 1 hour from
one of 5 diﬀerent directions. After each icing, force measurements i.e. the most time
consuming part of the experiment, were done in 10 degree steps over a full rotation of
the section (β = 0 : 10 : 360deg). Although scientiﬁcally valid, in real life terms that
would imply that the wind direction changes dramatically after being constant during
the ice accretion.
In order to allow time for a wider variety of icing scenarios it has been decided that if
the wind direction can be proven to be steady, both during and after an icing event,
then the number of force measurements for each icing scenario could be reduced to cover
a narrower range of only most valid ones.
3.1
Weather data acquisition
Ideally this analysis should be performed on a data sample measured directly at one
of the major cable-stayed bridges.
Unfortunately, at the time of writing this thesis,
data from the weather stations at the Øresund Bridge was unavailable. Instead it was
possible to use data measured directly on DTU campus in Lyngby. At 30km in a straight
line, the campus is relatively close to the Øresund Bridge. It is thus assumed that the
weather conditions are similar.

The spray bar is undoubtedly the most crucial element for creating in-cloud conditions

intervals from the center. Each nozzle is fed with air and water lines and the air/water
mixture is forced through an air cap, with 5 holes, hereby producing in-cloud conditions.
Furthermore the water lines are wrapped around with heating wires to prevent them
from freezing. The bar also has streamlining elements to limit the eﬀect it imposes on
the ﬂow.
The bar is suspended from two aluminum travellers attached to each end. One of which
moves along a rail on the ceiling, the other along a rail on the ﬂoor. The bar is ﬁxed in
place with a rope/pulley system, this makes it possible to rotate construction as needed.

4.2.3
Sampling frequency and the Nyquist criterion
The measuring hardware provides a continuous signal which is later discretized in Lab-
View via periodic sampling. The sampling frequency fs determines the rate at which
discrete values are extracted from the continuous signal.
Initially one of the concepts of this project was to analyze the power spectral density
(psd) of the measured time histories. This process requires a high accuracy of the sam-
ple. According to the Nyquist criterion, the sampling frequency should be at least twice
the maximum frequency of the signal in order to prevent aliasing, fNyquist = fs/2. A
way to ensure that aliasing does not happen is to use oversampling. Meaning choose
the sampling frequency so that the Nyquist frequency is much higher than the largest
expected natural frequency of the sample and later use a digital ﬁlter to eliminate the
noise.
Although, in the end, psd analysis was dropped from the project, the data was measured
in a way to make an analysis possible. As such oversampling was still used to determine
the sampling frequency, which was set to fs = 2048 Hz. The length of each time history
was 30 seconds.

The purpose of these experiments was to create a database of time histories of forces
acting on an inclined iced bridge cable section. In essence the procedure was composed of
two steps. First the cable section was iced from one of several directions. Second, forces
acting on the cable section were measured under a range of wind speeds and angles
of attack. Each individual icing together with the consequent force measurements is
further referred to as an icing scenario, IS.

iced section the inclination can have an impact on the shape of the ice. In particular
the angle of the feathers in rime and the eﬀect of gravity on the overﬂow in glaze.

Inclination θ
The ﬁrst tested inclination was chosen as θ1 = 32◦, which is within the angle range for

and was hoped to pronounce the eﬀect of gravity on the accretion.
Unfortunately, due to the availability of the CWT and the actual time each test proved

previously planned three.
To choose the second inclination for testing it was acknowledged that the cables most
vulnerable to large amplitude vibrations are the longest stays. A typical span to height
ratio of large cable stayed-bridges is l/h = 5÷7 which sets the inclination of the longest
stays between θ ≈16 ÷ 22 ◦.
The second inclination has therefore been chosen as
θ2 = 20◦.

Rotation β
As for the rotation at which the cable was iced, further referred to as β0, it has been

β0 = {0, 30, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180}

availability of the CWT, that was not entirely possible. Hence values of β0 for test series
C and D had to be limited to cases where potential instability was already observed in
test series A and B.

of 10 degrees. As such seven rotations were always tested for each icing scenario namely
βforce = β0 + {−30, −20, −10, 0, +10, +20, +30}
(5.2)
Since symmetry is assumed over β0 −β180, for the cases of β0 = 0 and β0 = 180
the investigated range was limited to four angles, namely βforce = {0, 10, 20, 30} and

5.1.4
Temperature
The goal was to evaluate the impact of both wet and dry ice. As a rule of thumb it has
been accepted that at a wind speed of 10 m/s wet ice accretion occurs at temperatures
within -3 to 0 ◦C while rime accretion is ensured at temperatures below 5 ◦C.
Initially the temperature for obtaining glaze was chosen as −3 ◦C. For test IS:A4 that
worked ﬁne, however at other icing directions a mixture of hard and soft rime was
observed. Therefore in the remaining tests the temperature was increased to −1 ◦C.
The temperature for rime accretion was chosen as −7 ◦C which proved to consistently
form rime.

5.2.2

The ice accretion process is of course paramount for the whole endeavor. Steps were
therefore taken to ensure that the spray is as uniform as possible. Before each icing

6.5.1
Instability areas
The instability areas found for the iced cable should ﬁrst and foremost be compared to
the case of a dry cable. This was investigated previously both through static tests and
using a dynamic setup, Macdonald and Larose [21] used past measurements to calculate
the aerodynamic damping parameter. They indicated three instability regions. First at
φ = 60◦(further referred to as D1), second at 75◦< φ < 90◦(D2) and third for ﬂow
parallel to the cylinder (D3). These results are shown in Figure 6.15. In principle these
are also the critical cases found for the iced cable in this thesis. Any other instability
areas discovered were much smaller in magnitude with Za > −15 × 103.
Figure 6.15: Minimum aerodynamic damping parameter, Za, for any α. Dry cable[21]
These three instability regions coincide both for the dry and iced cable. Therefore the
general mechanism is clearly the same, meaning instability occurs in the critical Re
range. It might, however, be worth looking at the diﬀerences between them.
For the dry cable the unstable Re range is very narrow, 3 × 105 < Re < 3.5 × 105 for
D2 while D1 only occurred around Re = 3.5 × 105. Moreover the aerodynamic damping
parameter does change in magnitude with varying cable-wind angle but the critical Re
band is nearly constant across all φ values.
For the iced cable the instability regions occur at similar cable-wind angles but the
velocity range is much wider and shifts position as φ varies. This eﬀect was most clearly
visible in cases of β0 = 30, 150◦(Figure 6.16).

Chapter 7
Conclusions
This thesis attempted to determine the inﬂuence of icing on the stability of inclined
bridge cables. At the time of writing the amount of experimental data for this subject
was limited. The aim was therefore to sweep across a variety of scenarios to distinguish
the critical cases. An array of tests was done using both wet and dry ice, diﬀerent cable
inclinations and various icing directions. Instability was evaluated based on static force
measurements using two quasi-steady models, the Den Hartog criterion and the uniﬁed
approach presented by Macdonald and Larose [21].
The critical cases were found to be when the cable was skewed towards the wind during
ice accretion, β0 = 30, 60, 150◦, and afterward the wind direction changed by approx-
imately ∆β ≈20◦towards being perpendicular to the cable. Such conﬁgurations cor-
respond to the areas of instability already identiﬁed for a dry inclined cable. However,
in this case, the Reynolds range of these areas was broader and seemed to shift with
change in the cable-wind angle φ.
Other instability regions found were local, much smaller in magnitude and case depen-
dent without a clear trend of occurrence.
The Den Hartog criterion estimated the lowest values of the nondimensional damping
parameter to be in the order of Za ≈−22×103. The uniﬁed approach of Macdonald and
Larose produced lower values of the parameter with the minimum at Za ≈−47 × 103.
These results, although on par with other research, vastly overestimate the structural
damping necessary to prevent instability and suggest that additional eﬀects, such as
turbulence, need to be considered.

Chapter 7 Conclusions
In conclusion, after testing numerous icing scenarios, it seems that the presence of ice
does not create additional instability regions of signiﬁcant magnitude as compared to the
case of a dry inclined cable. However it does seem to have an eﬀect on the velocity range
in which the cable is susceptible to instability. Since this velocity range also appears
to shift with varying cable-wind angle it can be reasoned that ice accretion eﬀectively
makes the inclined cable prone to instability across a much wider range of wind speeds.
Of course the above conclusions apply to a focal, but still rather narrow range of angles
of attack with the wind direction within ∆β = ±30◦from the icing direction. It might
be possible that ice creates instability for wind directions beyond that range.
7.1
Future work
Research presented in this thesis attempted to cover a wide range of icing scenarios
in hopes to indicate which might be more crucial than others. Experimental data for
ice-wind induced vibration of inclined bridge cables is still rather scarce and further
research is essential to get a comprehensive understanding of the subject.
First of all, more tests are necessary to extend the test database along with more de-
tailed tests of the IS which show the highest potential for instability, preferably with the
inclusion of cable rotation.
Furthermore, at the time being, the tests already performed consider only a ”smooth”
cable section. With the ongoing ingress of various surface alterations to bridge cables
such as the helical ﬁllet or dimples, future work should also evaluate how these additions
aﬀect ice-wind induced instability.
Dynamic tests should also be made in order to verify the results from theoretical models.
These tests should include diﬀerent mass, frequency, damping and geometrical position
of the cable.
Future work should also develop a deeper understanding of the inﬂuence of turbulence.
Experience from existing structures shows that the theoretical models used here consis-
tently overestimate the structural damping necessary to prevent cable vibration. Studies
indicate that turbulence has a beneﬁcial eﬀect on cable stability. However a reliable
quantitative model and more experimental data are needed to assess exactly how ﬂow
irregularities impact cable instability.
Finally, variations of mean aerodynamic coeﬃcients are important for section models yet
might not be that signiﬁcant when averaged over the full length of a cable. Hence the
aforementioned aeroelastic model would ideally encompass also the spanwise changes in
aerodynamic behaviour to provide a reliable framework for bridge cable design.
