Abstract
Quantum information, which can be carried by light, can be discrete or continuous due to the
particle-wave duality. Discrete variables describe systems with discrete eigenspectra and orthotog-
onal eigenstates such as the polarisation of a photon. Conversely, continuous variables (CV) are
observables with continuous eigenspectra, for example the quadratures of the electromagnetic field.
This work illuminates two aspects in optical quantum information processing, namely measurement-
based quantum computation (MBQC) and qauntum error correction with continuous variables.
Following literature, we describe the generation of the two-dimensional CV cluster states re-
quired for one-way quantum computation and show a scheme of the computation. In experimental
realisations, squeezed vacuum states are used to approximate the ideal position and momentum
eigenstates from which the cluster is assembled.
The experimental cluster states are therefore
approximations to an ideal system, and the effect on the computation is summarized in this work.
Optical states are subject to interactions with their environment and suffer from decoherence.
The purpose of quantum error correction is to mitigate this effect. Errors can be divided into
Gaussian and non-Gaussian categories, and this work focuses on the latter.
Following in the
footsteps of early CV error correction protocols, we analyse a repetition code based on a tritter-
encoding scheme between a coherent input state and two squeezed vacuum ancillary states with a
stochastic error model. The squeezing of the ancillary states plays an important role in the quality
of the error correction scheme, which we examine in detail. The tritter error correction scheme
could, in principle, be implemented in the MBQC framework.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Quantum information, and in particular quantum computing, is on its way to become a member of
the most impactful technologies of the 21st century. By harnessing abstract quantum phenomena
such as superposition and entanglement, quantum computing promises to solve hard computational
problems in record time. This has the potential to unlock the door to many smaller paradigm shifts
in various areas such as molecular dynamics, quantum chemistry, drug discovery, optimization, and
machine learning [1]. Currently, there is a global effort to build the world’s first quantum computer
that can solve a real world problem.
In theory, there are many viable physical systems that can be used to generate qubits (the
information carriers) and different computational models for processing them. The method studied
in this thesis is measurement-based quantum computation (MBQC), which utilises a large entangled
resource state, a cluster state, where measurements of each element of the cluster propagate the
computation along the resource state through teleportation. This method is also called one-way
quantum computation because the resource state is partially destroyed after measurement.
In quantum optics, photons are used as the information carriers. Linear optical elements such
as beamsplitters and phase modulators are used to process the optical states and non-linear optical
elements generate non-classical light, which together can create the cluster states required for
MBQC. Quantum information can be stored in discrete and continuous variables; discrete variables
have a finite dimensional Hilbert space, while the Hilbert space for continuous variables is infinite.
Qubits are an example of discretisation of quantum information. In this thesis we move away from
discrete information and focus on continuous variables (CV), specifically the information stored in
the quadratures of the light field.
A critical disadvantage and technological hurdle in quantum information processing is overcom-
ing the sensitivity of quantum systems that interact with their environment and undergo decoher-
ence over time. Quantum error correction mitigates this effect and in its simplest form redundantly
encodes the information on a larger subset of information carriers. By measuring the ancillary
carriers, one can in principle identify the error and correct it. Ideally, the quantum error correction
must be fault tolerant over the duration of the computation/experiment.
In this thesis, we provide an overview of how MBQC is carried out with continuous variables
and lay out the groundwork for how simple repetition codes can be analysed in CV. The vision
is to show how experimentally feasible error correction algorithms can be run on a cluster state
for MBQC with current technology and to provide a detailed analytical description of both the
computation and the code.
This thesis is structured as follows: first, we introduce the concept and notation of bosonic
systems using continuous variables, give an overview of Gaussian states in quantum optics, and

discuss unitary transformations in linear optics through symplectic transforms. After these prelim-
inary considerations, the thesis moves in two directions: the first discusses CV cluster states up to
two dimensions and explains how one can implement quantum gates. The second part, which was
the main focus of this work, analyses a CV error correction scheme using a tritter proposed by van
Loock [2] that can correct quadrature displacements.

Chapter 2
Theory: Continuous variables in
quantum optics
The purpose of this chapter is to combine the many sources of literature on continuous variables
related to quantum optics and create a solid theoretical standpoint from which this thesis bases its
ideas and calculations on. We use elements from review papers on Gaussian quantum information
[3] in CV [4] as well as books on quantum mechanics [5] and quantum optics [6] to give a concise
introduction to the topic.
The structure of this chapter is as follows; our outset is to briefly quantize the electromag-
netic field and introduce the dimensionless quadrature operators, which are very important for the
characterisation of optical states. Next, we introduce the notation for multimode bosonic systems,
i.e. the continuous variables central to this thesis. We move on to the general phase space rep-
resentation of states using the Wigner function, specifying how Gaussian states are represented,
discussing the effect of noise, stating how projective measurements affect the states, and defining
the quantum fidelity. Lastly, we show how the evolution of Gaussian unitaries in quantum optics
can be represented using symplectic matrices and phase space displacements.

2.4.2
Effect of noisy transmission channels
In practice, optical states undergo decoherence when they inevitably interact with their environ-
ment. This can be modelled as transmission through a noisy channel [8], where the most relevant
sources of noise in a CV lab are loss and phase noise. Briefly speaking, loss is the loss of excitations,
i.e. loss of energy, which shows up as a loss in displacement amplitude, and as an increase in the
variance of the squeezed quadrature, shifting the state closer to vacuum. Secondly, phase noise is
the random change of the state’s phase, which can be modelled by a Gaussian distribution of the
phase with zero mean and variance σ2
ϕ. In Figure 2.2, the effect of loss and phase noise is illustrated.
A coherent state and displaced squeezed vacuum state shift back towards vacuum. The Gaussian
phase noise gives the coherent state a ”banana”-like smear. When ϕ0 = 0 or π and σϕ ≈0 the
phase noise can be modelled as small ˆp-displacements. Similary, when ϕ0 = ±π/2, small phase
fluctuations can be modelled as small ˆx-displacements.

Chapter 3
Theory: Measurement-based quantum
computation using cluster states
In continuous-variable cluster state quantum computation, large, entangled multimode Gaussian
states are used as a resource to implement quantum gates by performing homodyne measurements
on the individual qumodes. Depending on the basis settings of the quadrature measurements, the
act of measuring applies a unitary ˆU on the state and propagates the state through the cluster via
teleportation [12] [13] [3]. With our mathematical toolbox from Chapter 2 in hand we move to give
an overview of another mathematical toolbox specific to cluster states via graphical calculus [14].
After introducing the notation, we explain how to generate cluster states in multiple dimensions
and how a computation is performed on a 2D cluster state, as was proposed by Larsen [15]. We
end this chapter by presenting a method for two-mode gate implementation and discuss possible
ways to solve for a desired gate matrix.

index. The computation is propagated by performing the CV equivalent to a Bell measurement on

a symplectic operation, and by tuning the computational angles one can, in principle, implement
a specific gate. However, finite squeezing distorts the gate, and increases the output quadrature
variance with each computational step. A second gate operation can be implemented in the next
wire segment, where the input state is now the output state from before, and measuring the new
computational modes can further change the state, hence the computation propagates down the
wire.

Chapter 4
Theory: Quantum error correction
4.1
Quantum error correction with continuous variables
Imperfections in the physical implementation of CV quantum computation will lead to errors that
can propagate during the computation. In general, the most common sources of error in optical
states were loss and phase noise as mentioned in Section 2.4.2. In measurement-based quantum
computation with linear optics, imperfectly squeezed vacuum states are used to create the entangle-
ment required for teleportation. This finite squeezing induces distortions in the quantum gates and
increases the quadrature variance in each computational step, as we saw in Section 3.3. An error
correction protocol can help mitigate these errors, but it can also induce its own errors. Universal
CV quantum computing must be fault tolerant, that is, error rates must be below a fault tolerant
threshold for the duration of the computation. These errors may be split into two categories; Gaus-
sian errors and stochastic (non-Gaussian) errors. In literature, there are many protocols suggested
to deal with these errors in CV ranging in complexity.
4.1.1
Gaussian errors
A Gaussian error transforms a Gaussian state into another Gaussian state. An example of this is
the effect of finite squeezing of the cluster state nodes, which increases the variance of the squeezed
quadrature after a computational step. According to the no-go theorem [18], one cannot correct
Gaussian errors on Gaussian states with Gaussian operations only. Non-Gaussian operations (uni-
taries that are more than quadratic in ˆp and ˆq) such as photon counting can be used to correct
for noise on Gaussian states [19]. One can also circumvent the no-go theorem by encoding qubit
states into non-Gaussian CV states (e.g. GKP states) as proposed by Gottesman et al. [20] and use
Gaussian operations to perform the error correction. Larsen et al. [21] proposed a fault-tolerant
measurement-based quantum computation architecture which utilises a 3D cluster state (by inter-
fering the 2D cluster with itself) and topological qubit error correction as well as an underlying
GKP error correction to handle the Gaussian noise induced by finite squeezing.
Gaussian errors are the main source of error in measurement-based quantum computation, but
as listed above, there are many different proposals to correct such errors. In this project, we will
focus on non-Gaussian errors.

4.1.2
Non-Gaussian errors
Stochastic errors are randomly occurring errors that transform a Gaussian state into a non-Gaussian
state. For example, an initial Gaussian state is transformed into a statistical mixture of Gaussian
states via:

→(1 −perror) |ψ⟩⟨ψ| + perror |ψerror⟩⟨ψerror| ,
(4.1)
where perror is the error probability and the error state |ψerror⟩could be a displaced Gaussian as
illustrated in Figure 4.1. Loss and phase noise, in the limit of small phase fluctuations, can be
decomposed into the Weyl displacement operators in phase space.

Historically, we find inspiration from discrete variable error correction codes. Lloyd et al. [22]
and Braunstein et al. [23] were among the first to generalise Shor’s discrete qubit redundancy codes
to an error correction protocol in CV. They proposed a ”triple-triple”-modular (nine-modular) re-
dundancy code to correct for both displacement and phase errors occurring on a single mode. This
protocol was demonstrated experimentally by Aoki et al. [24]. Niset et al. [25] proposed a four-
mode CV quantum error correction code to recover a coherent state undergoing erasure, which
is also a stochastic error of the form (4.1). Soon after, this was experimentally demonstrated by
Lassen et al. [26].
The codes mentioned above rely on linear optics and squeezed ancillary states.
The linear
optical circuit visualised in Figure 4.1 (described by a n × n unitary ˆU) encodes m wave packets
onto n −m ancillary zero-position/momentum eigenstates. In practice, the ancillary modes are
approximated by highly squeezed vacuum states, and finite squeezing is a limitation of the error
codes. A random phase-space displacement error can occur on any one of the n modes. In order to
recover the error syndrome, the decoding linear optical circuit is run (which is the inverse unitary
ˆU−1) and the position of the ancillary modes is measured revealing if and where the error has oc-
curred. Suitable feed-forwarding operations based on the error syndrome reverses the phase-space
displacement, restoring the original state. Walker et. al. [27] proposed such a five wave-packet code
that can correct arbitrary position shifts and momentum kicks on a single mode. Specifically, they
proposed a unitary matrix representing the encoded circuit which consists of beamsplitters and
phase delays. Although we have not been able to show it yet, we believes that a beamsplitter can
be implemented in a cluster state computation with the appropriate projection. It could therefore

two ancillary squeezed vacuum states and a tritter from van Loock [2].

While there are many interesting proposals for quantum error correction codes in CV, as sum-
marised in Chapter 4, this thesis focuses on a simple triple-modular repetition code that corrects
stochastic x- or p-displacements as described in van Loock [2]. A signal state, in our case a coherent
state, is encoded on two ancillary x or p squeezed vacuum states using a tritter (two beamsplitters).
A stochastically occuring x or p displacement error can occur in any channel, and is, ideally, iden-
tified and corrected by decoding (inverting the tritter) and measuring the x or p quadrature of the
ancillary states. Each type of error has a unique measurement syndrome, which is used to identify
in which channel the error occurred in and determine its size. We can thereby apply feedforwarding
(displacement) operations to correct the error-induced displacement incurred on the signal mode
by the circuit.
In this chapter, we describe the protocol for an x-error in detail and evaluate the quality of
this code by looking at the fidelity. The p-error protocol is implemented by using p-squeezed states
and measuring the p quadrature, so the results obtained for the x-error will be equivalent. In the
analysis, we especially consider the effect of the finite squeezing of the ancillae during syndrome
extraction and feedforwarding, as there is a low, but finite probability of the measurements being
cast in the wrong syndrome, which goes beyond what is considered in van Loock [2].

stochastically with probability γ in each mode. The effect of the error is to displace the state
by an unknown magnitude δx in the x-quadrature. In principle, we do not know the sign of the
displacement, which becomes important during syndrome extraction.

a statistical mixture of the signal state Win(x1, p1) and the error state, which is the input state
translated by δx in x1, Win(x1 −δx, p1), multiplied by their respective probabilities,

5.3
Finite squeezing
In the previous section we considered the fidelity of the corrected state in the limit of infinite
squeezing and found that our circuit could successfully recover the input state up to a certain value
of the error probability pc for all error sizes. In practice, one always has a finite level of squeezing,
which greatly affects our ability to do the correction. In the following, we analyse the effect of
squeezing on the fidelity in Eq. (5.47). We resort to a numerical approach because of two reasons:
i) this equation has 72 terms, and (more importantly) ii) the probabilities ηj in Eq. (5.27) consist
of evaluating the error function erf(x) which is not trivial. There exist many approximate methods
to evaluate this function, and in this thesis, we simply use scipy.special.erf(x) in Python.
In this Section we outline the code used to calculate the fidelity, then we discuss the measurement
binning and how we found an optimal value for the cutoff ±xc. Finally, we present numerical results
for the fidelity and show the critical squeezing required for fault tolerant error correction.

5.4
Generalisation to Gaussian error distribution model
The error model considered here reflects the single shot nature of the experiment we are analysing.
In a single shot, a state can be displaced by a single, unknown value of δx with probability p, or
it can remain unperturbed with probability 1 −p. It is slightly counter-intuitive that we follow
the transformation of the Wigner function, which is a quasi-probability distribution in phase space,
giving us the statistics over many shots of the experiment. In our analysis we consider every possible
outcome of this singular displacement error and demonstrate how well our error correction corrects
this one error value. In the next shot of the experiment, however, we expect the error size to be
different. In order for our analysis to be complete, we must also consider the distribution of the
displacement error. In the general case, where we still consider only x errors, the (un-normalised)

Discussion and Conclusion
6.1
Discussion
The discussion is divided into two main parts: firstly, we discuss our analysis of the tritter CV error
correction scheme. Secondly, we describe the idea behind this work, namely how the repetition
code scheme could be implemented in a cluster state computation, and we discuss the expected
limitations.
6.1.1
Tritter error correction scheme
The repetition code considered in this thesis requires a good estimation of the error parameters,
namely the size of the displacement and the error probability in order to select a method of binning
that will meaningfully increase the fidelity after running the tritter circuit relative to the fidelity of
the direct transmission. As a comment on the binning optimisation method in Section 5.3.1, one
could simply use the half-way point, xc =
δx
2
√
6, between the two distributions, as this overcomes
the issue of the binning becoming too large for small displacements. This way, the results in Figure
5.8 are expected to improve for small displacements. A more serious limitation is that the error
correction protocol is not viable for small displacement errors at realistic squeezing levels, which
are the most experimentally relevant for phase noise errors.
One could correct arbitrary phase-space displacements by running a x-encoding circuit with
x-squeezed ancilla followed by a p-encoding circuit with p-squeezed ancilla and measuring the x
and p quadratures, respectively. Characterising this could be a natural extension of the present
work, although we predict that the corrected fidelity will be strongly affected from the mirrored
variance in both quadratures.
Furthermore, a simulation of a Gaussian displacement error model in Section 5.4 with this code
is expected to give similar results where the corrected fidelity in Eq. (5.47) has an additional vari-
ance term, which is the variance of the Gaussian displacement.
Lastly, there is an overall issue with using coherent states as information carriers, as we have
done in this work, due to their non-orthogonality. The overlap is proportional to e−|α−β|2/2 where
|α| and |β| are the amplitudes of the coherent states. As their absolute difference becomes smaller,
|α −β| →0, the states become indistinguishable, and their fidelity becomes unity. We therefore
obtain a very small gain in fidelity even if the state is fully corrected to the original state. It is
therefore unsurprising that we obtain good results for larger displacement errors by considering
the fidelity - the results for small displacement errors would appear insignificant in comparison.
51
==PAGE:56==
The use of displaced, squeezed vacuum states as information carrier are expected to give far better
results.
6.1.2
Implementation of repetition code using cluster state computation
The initial idea for this thesis was to investigate the possibility of implementing a CV error correc-
tion code in the 2D cluster state computation scheme introduced in Section 3.3. The triple-modular
tritter code was selected due to its simplicity and existing theoretical description and was anal-
ysed in detail in Chapter 5. The desired simplicity is twofold: firstly, the encoding and decoding
operations must be between neighboring modes, as currently two-mode operations can only be
implemented between neighboring wires of the double bilayer square lattice computation scheme.
Secondly, the first CV error correction codes were extensions of qubit repetition codes, so this adds
a logical element to our introduction to CV error correction.
The implementation of the tritter code in the cluster computation proved to be outside of our
scope, as we did not manage to find the measurement angles needed to implement the beamsplitter
unitaries. As briefly discussed in Section 3.3.1, the gate symplectic has many variables, 16 equations
must be satisfied by 10 variables to equate the gate symplectic with a desired symplectic, and a
solution is not guaranteed. Therefore, a sophisticated method needs to be developed where a two-
mode symplectic can be decomposed into the projective measurement angles. Our suggestion is to
start with an Euler decomposition of the symplectic matrix. Another suggestion is to reformulate
the problem into graph-notation, and follow the transformation one rotation at a time.
It might seem that programming a beamsplitter unitary on a cluster state is an overcomplication,
when one could think of using a real beamsplitter. This might be a viable solution for a tritter.
However the decomposition of a system of many interferometers into a single unitary which, in
principle, could be implemented in a cluster state computation in few computational steps could
have many useful applications, such as Gaussian Boson sampling, where photons are sampled from
random probability distribution.
Assuming that the measurement basis for a tritter (and the inverted tritter) is known, the
CV error scheme could run on the double bilayer square lattice where the input mode is along
the middle wire, and the ancilla are along the neighboring wires. The tritter and inverted tritter
symplectic are implemented in n computational steps for a total of 2n steps for the entire circuit.
Each computational step adds noise to the input mode and the ancilla from the gate noise matrix.
The level of noise depends on squeezing of the cluster state and the measurement basis. This extra
noise will affect the quality of the error correction, and could be partially alleviated by appropriate
binning settings. However, the output state will also increase in variance. This has the potential
to constrain the setup to very unrealistic squeezing requirements.
6.2
Conclusion
In this work, we gave a large theoretical overview within continuous variables in quantum optics,
described multi-dimensional CV cluster states as well as how a cluster state computation could be
performed, and lastly, evaluated the performance of an early CV repetition code for a stochastic
error model both analytically and numerically. Despite, or in fact thanks to the restrictive nature
of the tritter repetition code, the stage has been set for the analysis of CV error correction codes,
and CV cluster state computation in general.
