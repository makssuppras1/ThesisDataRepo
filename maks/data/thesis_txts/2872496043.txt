Abstract
Automated multi-organ segmentation in preclinical computed tomography (CT) imaging re-
mains challenging due to species-specific anatomical variations and protocol differences across
studies. Yet, it is essential for reproducible analysis in small animal research, particularly for
quantifying functional companion imaging, such as Single-Photon Emission Computed Tomogra-
phy (SPECT) and positron emission tomography (PET). This thesis establishes a deep learning
framework capable of segmenting seven organs in non-contrast CT scans across both mice and
rats, leveraging advanced convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures to achieve near-
human reliability.
The Rodent Multi-Organ Segmentation (RMOS) dataset, comprising 327 in-house scans from
93 animals across 11 studies (2014–2024), was iteratively expanded and corrected using model-
assisted annotation, improving annotation consistency while significantly reducing manual work-
load. Our mixed-species segmentation approach was powered by two top-performing architec-
tures: nnU-Net ResEnc M and U-Mamba Bot. Both of which achieved exceptional generalization
with estimated generalization Dice similarity coefficients (DSCs) of 0.929 ± 0.067 and 0.929 ±
0.064, respectively. Both models showed robustness in five fold cross-validation, yielding low
failure rates (3.5% and 2.7%) based on a success threshold of average DSC > 0.857. Subsequent
statistical analysis revealed no significant performance difference between the two architectures
(p = 0.93).
The five models from cross-validation was leveraged to construct two ensemble models. Critically,
our best model performance reached the estimated inter-annotator agreement level ( ˆ
DSCIAA of
0.865) with an average agreement DSC of 0.877 towards the three annotators demonstrating the
reliability of automated segmentations for downstream applications.
PET intensity validation on five PET/CT scans confirmed the practical utility of predicted
segmentations for dosimetric applications, with relative errors remaining below 12% for most
organs in both mean and maximum intensity measurements. Organs with higher segmentation
accuracy (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys) demonstrated correspondingly lower PET quantification
errors (1.2–9.1%), while more challenging organs (spleen, stomach) showed higher relative errors
of up to 19.3% for mean intensity. Notably, some observed discrepancies were attributed to man-
ual annotation inaccuracies rather than model failures, highlighting the potential for automated
segmentation to exceed human annotation quality in certain cases.
This work sets a new benchmark for scalable, automated segmentation in preclinical imaging,
enabling high-throughput, standardized analysis while reducing manual annotation time from
approximately 43 minutes to mere seconds per scan. By addressing challenges in dataset hetero-
geneity and annotation quality, this framework lays the groundwork for reproducible, automated
dosimetric assessment in preclinical radionuclide therapy (RNT) research.

Introduction
Radionuclide therapy (RNT) is an increasingly important modality in cancer treatment, offer-
ing a targeted approach to deliver ionizing radiation directly to malignant cells. By coupling
radioactive isotopes with tumor-seeking molecules, RNT enables selective irradiation of tumors
while minimizing damage to surrounding healthy tissue [1]. This therapeutic strategy has shown
clinical success in treating various cancers, including neuroendocrine tumors and prostate can-
cer [2,3].
This trend emphasizes the significance of quantitative imaging and dosimetry in both clinical
and preclinical research to enhance understanding of radiopharmaceutical behavior in the body.
In particular, rodent models remain a cornerstone for developing and assessing new radiother-
apeutics, as they provide a controlled environment for studying biodistribution, efficacy, and
toxicity [4].
2.1
Semantic segmentation in radio nuclide therapy
RNT relies on accurate organ-level dosimetry to evaluate therapeutic efficacy and toxicity in
preclinical studies. Precise quantification of radiotracer biodistribution through Positron Emis-
sion Tomography (PET)/Computed Tomography (CT) imaging is essential for optimizing treat-
ment regimens and ensuring successful clinical translation [5]. However, manual segmentation
of anatomical structures remains labor-intensive and suffers from substantial inter- and intra-
annotator variability, particularly across different research centers and imaging protocols [6,7].
These limitations significantly constrain study throughput and compromise the reproducibility
required for rigorous preclinical evaluation and regulatory compliance in RNT development [8].
Regulatory agencies increasingly demand standardized, transparent methodologies for radio-
pharmaceutical approval, making manual segmentation practices a potential bottleneck in ther-
apeutic development.
Automated multi-organ segmentation using deep learning offers a transformative solution by
providing reproducible, high-throughput analysis while eliminating observer-dependent variabil-
ity [9, 10]. Such automation enables scalable dosimetric assessments and standardized quan-
titative workflows essential for the successful translation of novel radionuclide therapies from
preclinical research to clinical practice.
The advantages of deep-learning-driven segmentation are further illustrated in Figure 1, which
illustrates traditional and Artificial Intelligence (AI)-enhanced workflows in preclinical imaging
studies.
The process from image acquisition to quantitative analysis can be streamlined by
integrating automatic segmentation of PET/CT scans into the workflow using server-side deep
learning models. This automation reduces the manual workload and encourages standardized,
reproducible data processing across different laboratories.

Materials and Methods
This chapter outlines the materials, computational resources, and methodological approaches
employed in this study. We detail the hardware platforms utilized, the deep learning framework
(nnUNetv2) adopted for model development, and the processes involved in dataset creation and
annotation. Furthermore, we describe the evaluation metrics, loss functions, and baseline mod-
els, as well as the preliminary studies and validation procedures that underpin our experiments.
This comprehensive overview ensures the reproducibility and transparency of our thesis work-
flow.
3.1
Hardware
The computational experiments, model training, and inference during this project were con-
ducted on two distinct hardware platforms: a local workstation and a high performance com-
puting (HPC) system. The detailed specifications for each system are provided in Tables 22
and 23 in Appendix B.
These hardware specifications are crucial since the capabilities of Central Processing Units
(CPUs), Graphics Processing Units (GPUs), and available memory can significantly affect model
training times, experimental throughput, and even the feasibility of running certain models or
datasets. For reproducibility and fair comparison of results, it is important to specify the hard-
ware environment used for both development and large-scale experiments.
3.2
Dataset creation
The success of deep learning models for medical image segmentation critically depends on the
quality and characteristics of the underlying dataset [11]. This section details the process of
assembling and preparing the dataset used in this project, including the origin and properties of
the CT images, key image acquisition parameters, and the measures taken to ensure data quality
and consistency. We further describe the annotation workflow, strategies for iterative dataset
expansion, and the construction of the Rodent Multi-Organ Segmentation (RMOS) dataset.
Finally, we outline the approach used to partition the dataset for model training and evaluation,
ensuring robust and less biased performance assessment.
3.2.1
CT-images
All CT and PET images utilized for model training and testing were acquired using the Inveon
PET/CT scanner (Siemens Medical Solutions). This study exclusively employs native (non-
contrast-enhanced) CT scans to minimize inter-dataset variability, enable broader application,

3.2.5
Iterative Dataset expansion
After the initial scans were fully manually annotated, they were used to create a dataset for
training an initial deep-learning model as described in Section 3.4. This model was then used to
predict annotations for the remaining dataset. Although its predictions were not perfect, they
significantly reduced the time required for annotation, as annotators only needed to correct errors
or refine boundaries rather than starting the annotation from scratch. As more annotations were
corrected from the predictions and added to the training dataset, the model was re-trained on
the expanded dataset, leading to improved model performance. The new model re-predicted
the remaining dataset, which was again manually corrected. These steps were repeated until
the desired dataset size was reached.
This iterative process allowed the dataset to expand
increasingly faster due to the enhanced performance of the model with each cycle.

3.3
Semantic segmentation model
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) produce segmentations by learning hierarchical features
through successive convolutional layers that capture local patterns, which are then combined
through skip connections and upsampling to generate pixel-wise classifications at the original
image resolution [24]. Skip connections, also known as shortcut connections, are architectural
elements that allow information to bypass one or more layers by directly connecting the input of a
layer (or block of layers) to its output [25]. These connections address the degradation problem in
deep networks by enabling the learning of residual mappings, where the network learns to refine
features rather than reconstruct them entirely from scratch, making optimization significantly
easier for very deep architectures.
Among deep learning architectures for medical image segmentation, U-Net variations are pre-
dominant [26]. The 3D U-Net extends the conventional 2D architecture by incorporating a third
dimension in kernels and strides, enabling the model to leverage volumetric context crucial for
medical imaging applications [27]. While 3D models impose greater computational demands
than their 2D counterparts, they provide superior contextual understanding for voxel-based
medical images [28].
Additional architectural innovations, including residual learning implementations (U-Res-Net)
and adversarial training approaches (MOS-GAN), demonstrate continued evolution in medical
image segmentation methodologies, with detailed descriptions provided in Appendix D.
3.3.1
nnU-Net framework
The nnU-Net framework represents a significant advancement in U-Net adaptability, automati-
cally determining optimal architectural parameters including dimensionality (2D, 3D, or cascade
models), kernel sizes, and network depth based on input image geometry [29, 30]. This self-
configuring approach typically yields robust performance across diverse medical imaging tasks,
though its generality may limit specificity for particular applications.
Isensee et al. specifically advocate for using the nnU-Net ResEnc model (M, L, and XL) as a
robust baseline, highlighting its systematic design and the importance of standardized evaluation
protocols when comparing novel architectures [31].
The nnU-Net framework has been extensively validated in numerous international medical im-
age segmentation competitions, consistently demonstrating robust and competitive performance
across a wide range of tasks and datasets [32–34]. This proven track record makes nnU-Net par-
ticularly well-suited for our project, where we are working with a proprietary dataset (See section
3.2). Employing a thoroughly validated architecture and framework such as nnU-Net ensures

3.3.2
Batch generation and preprocessing
Training is performed using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent, which is the nnU-Net default
configuration [30], where model parameters are updated after evaluating the loss on small,
randomly sampled subsets of the training data known as mini-batches. An epoch is defined as
one complete pass through a fixed number of mini-batches; in this context, one epoch corresponds
to 250 mini-batches [30].
Image Rescaling
All images are resampled to a common target spacing before training to ensure dataset consis-
tency. By default, nnU-Net sets the target spacing as the median of the voxel spacings per axis;
for highly anisotropic data (anisotropy ratio > 3), the lowest-resolution axis is set to the tenth
percentile to minimize interpolation artifacts and information loss [30]. As seen in Table 2, all
images are isotropic. The target spacing becomes the median spacing of the dataset: 0.209992
mm.
This rescaling procedure is also applied during inference, ensuring that input images are pro-
cessed with the same target spacing as used during training. This consistency between training
and inference helps maintain model performance and reduces the risk of artifacts or discrepancies
arising from differences in image resolution.
Data Augmentation and Transformations
To enhance model generalization and robustness, a range of data augmentation techniques are
applied during training. These include random spatial and intensity transformations, as well
as label processing steps, which collectively increase the diversity of the training data and help
mitigate overfitting [35]. The specific augmentation strategies implemented in this study are
summarized below and described in further detail in Appendix D.
• Spatial augmentations: Random rotations, scaling, and optional mirroring to increase
spatial variability. Optionally, data can be converted to 2D for specific augmentations.
• Intensity augmentations: Addition of Gaussian noise and blur, random changes in
brightness, contrast, gamma, and simulation of low-resolution images to improve robust-
ness.
• Label processing: Cleaning and reformatting of segmentation labels, including conver-
sion to regions or one-hot format, and downsampling for deep supervision. (See Section
3.6.1)
Overall, these transformations are designed to increase the diversity of the training data and
mitigate overfitting.

3.5
Evaluation of segmentation performance
This section aims to outline metrics and methods used to evaluate annotation quality, segmen-
tation model performance, and usage for dosimetry.
3.5.1
Metrics
The performance of the segmentation models was evaluated using several widely adopted metrics,
including Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC), Surface Dice Score (SDSC), Hausdorff Distance
(HD95), and Average Symmetric Surface Distance (ASSD). To assess inter-annotator agreement
(IAA), both DSC and Fleiss’ kappa were employed. These metrics differ in their handling of
overlap, boundary accuracy, and annotation consistency: DSC quantifies the degree of overlap
between segmentations and ground truth, while SDSC and surface-based distances focus on
boundary agreement; Fleiss’ kappa measures the consistency among multiple annotators. For
full mathematical definitions and further details on these metrics, see Appendix C.1.
3.5.2
Statistical testing
Statistical testing is used to verify the significance of performance differences. Two methods
are used for paired and unpaired non-parametric testing of a held-out test set. These are the
Wilcoxon signed rank test and the Mann-Whitney U test.
For a more impressive claim about the models ability to generalize an approach based on Be-
navoli et al. [22, 38] is applied to the baseline, and the best candidate architecture to make a
pairwise comparison. This approach uses validation results from a cross-validation to perform a
correlated t-test and to calculate the estimated 95%-confidence interval for the average difference
in performance.

Wilcoxon signed rank test and the Mann-Whitney U test
To assess whether differences in segmentation performance, quantified by the DSC, are statisti-
cally significant, non-parametric hypothesis tests are employed. This is necessary because DSC
values are bounded in the range [0, 1] and are typically concentrated toward the upper end of
the scale, leading to skewed distributions that violate the normality assumptions required for
parametric tests such as the paired or unpaired t-test.
The choice between the Wilcoxon signed-rank test and the Mann-Whitney U test depends
on the structure of the data and the hypothesis being tested:
• The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is appropriate for paired comparisons, where two seg-
mentation models are evaluated on the same set of test samples. In this case, each DSC
value from one model has a natural counterpart from the other model on the same image,
allowing a direct comparison of paired differences. The test evaluates whether the median
of these differences is significantly different from zero under the null hypothesis that both
models perform equally.
• The Mann-Whitney U test, in contrast, is used for unpaired comparisons, where the
DSC scores come from two independent groups. For example, it is suitable when evaluating
whether a single model performs significantly better on mice compared to rats. In this
case, the DSC scores from each species are treated as independent samples, and the test
assesses whether one group tends to yield systematically higher values than the other.
By using non-parametric tests, we avoid assumptions of normality or homoscedasticity, making
the analysis robust to the distributional properties of the DSC scores [39].
Correlated t-test
For model evaluation and comparison, we use E = 1 −DSCpan organ as the loss function, where
the mean Dice coefficient is averaged across all organs for each sample, providing a robust metric
for multi-organ segmentation performance. Model comparison is performed using a correlated
t-test for cross-validation as outlined by Benavoli et al. [22,38], which accounts for dependencies
introduced by repeated splits. We employ (J = 5) randomized cross-validation splits (See Section
3.2.7 and Section 3.8), with each validation set containing more than 30 test samples, thereby
satisfying the requirements of the Central Limit Theorem.
For each split, both models are
trained and evaluated, and the per-split difference in loss is computed. These paired differences
are then analyzed with the correlated t-test to estimate the mean performance gap, construct a
(1 −α) confidence interval, and compute a p-value for the null hypothesis of equal performance.

The segmentation models were trained using several established loss functions, including cross-
entropy (CE), Dice, focal, and Focal Tversky losses, as well as compound loss combinations (see
Appendix C.2 for full definitions and equations). These loss functions differ in how they handle
class imbalance, anatomical structure, and segmentation uncertainty.
CE loss is widely used for multi-class classification and is computed independently for each
voxel, without considering spatial relationships or shapes. This voxel-wise approach means that
anatomical context is not explicitly modeled, and as a result, CE loss may struggle with highly
imbalanced classes, as is common in medical imaging [40].
Dice loss directly optimizes for the overlap between predicted and ground-truth regions, making
it more robust to class imbalance and better at capturing contiguous anatomical structures [41].
Focal loss extends cross-entropy by down-weighting well-classified examples and focusing learn-
ing on difficult or ambiguous voxels, but like CE loss, it is also computed voxel-wise and does
not explicitly account for spatial context [42].
Focal Tversky loss further generalizes Dice and Tversky metrics, introducing tunable param-
eters to balance false positives and false negatives, as well as a focusing parameter to emphasize
hard-to-segment regions. This loss function is region-based and can be tuned to better capture
challenging anatomical boundaries [43].
Compound losses combine two or more of these functions, leveraging their complementary
strengths to improve model robustness and stability during training.

3.6.1
Deep supervision
Deep supervision is employed in all model trainings to facilitate more effective gradient flow
and to encourage feature learning at multiple scales during training. In the deep supervision
approach, auxiliary outputs are generated at different network depths, with each output su-
pervised by a corresponding downsampled ground truth segmentation mask. The total loss is
computed as the weighted sum of the losses at all resolutions, L = w1 × L1 + w2 × L2 + . . .,
where the weights (w) are halved with each decrease in resolution (i.e., w2 = 1
2w1, w3 = 1
4w1,
etc.) and normalized to sum to one. The deep supervision output scales are determined during
preprocessing [30].

nnU-Net loss
The nnunetv2-repository employs a compound loss consisting of a soft Dice loss (See Equation

3.7.2
Optimal training length experiment
Purpose: This experiment evaluates the impact of training duration on segmentation perfor-
mance by comparing models trained for 1000 and 3000 epochs.
Experimental setup: The experiment utilizes the ResEnc M nnU-Net architecture, as de-
scribed in Section 3.4, and is trained on the RMOS dataset (see Section 3.2.6). As nnU-Net
applies an adaptive learning rate scheduler [30] two separate models are trained on the same
data and under identical conditions, differing only in the number of training epochs.
Evaluation: Model performance is assessed on the dedicated test set (see Section 3.2.8) using
the mean DSC across all organ classes. The two models, trained for 1000 and 3000 epochs,
respectively, are compared using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test to determine the statistical
significance of performance differences. Bootstrapping is used to estimate the 95% confidence
interval for the median paired difference in DSC.

3.8
Cross-validation
To validate the results from the Model architecture selection, a cross-validation procedure is
employed. In this approach, the training dataset is partitioned into five splits as described in
Section 3.2.7. This partitioning forms the basis for K-fold cross-validation, a widely used method
to more accurately estimate a model’s performance while also enabling statistically sound model
comparisons, as discussed in Section 3.5.2.
In K-fold cross-validation, the entire dataset is divided into K equal-sized subsets (folds). For
each iteration, K −1 subsets are used for training while the remaining subset serves as the
validation set, with each unique subset taking a turn as the validation set across K iterations.
This ensures that every data point is used for both training and validation, and the results from
each fold can be averaged to provide a robust estimate of the model’s generalization performance.
While applying cross-validation to every training session would be ideal, practical limitations
such as time and computational resources restricted its use in this project.
3.8.1
nnU-Net ResEnc M vs. U-Mamba Bot
Based on the initial architecture evaluation, two models were selected for a comprehensive cross-
validation analysis. nnU-Net ResEnc M was chosen as the baseline due to its consistent perfor-
mance across both test sets. U-Mamba Bot demonstrated superior performance, achieving the
best results on the STAPLE test set while maintaining computational efficiency with lightweight
architecture and inference times comparable to ResEnc M (Table 7). These two architectures
received full cross-validation treatment to enable robust performance comparison and ensemble
generation (see Section 3.8.2), with detailed results and analysis presented in Section 4 and 5.4.
3.8.2
Ensembling
Ensembling is a technique in machine learning where the predictions of multiple models are
combined to produce a single, typically more robust, output. In the context of K-fold cross-
validation, an ensemble can be constructed by combining the predictions from the K models
trained on each fold. This ensures that the unique strengths of each fold are leveraged when
making predictions on new data. To evaluate the performance of the ensemble, a test set of
unseen data should be used. For this reason, the test set described in Section 3.2.8 is kept
entirely separate from the cross-validation process [50].

5
Discussion
This discussion starts by placing our findings within the larger objective of enhancing preclinical
RNT dosimetry via reliable, automated organ segmentation. Our research shows that the nnU-
Net ResEnc M and U-Mamba Bot deep learning models both achieve high and similar accuracy
in segmenting multiple organs across various rodent CT datasets, with a final cross-validation
pan-organ DSC of around 0.86 on our consensus STAPLE test set. Notably, the mixed-species
approach for segmentation in both mice and rats enables more efficient workflows without a
significant decline in performance compared to models built for individual species.
By integrating deep learning-based segmentation into preclinical PET/CT analysis, we establish
a foundational pipeline for robust quantification of PET signal intensities and organ volumes
within anatomically defined regions of interest (see Figure 26). Automatic segmentation yields
ROIs that closely match manual annotations, as evidenced by consistent agreement in mean and
maximum PET intensity values, supporting the method’s suitability for high-throughput and
standardized dosimetric calculations essential to preclinical RNT research.
One of the main benefits of eliminating manual annotation is the removal of the time bottle-
neck associated with it. As shown in the results, manual annotation required an average of
approximately 43 minutes per scan (see Table 14). Our automated solution reduces this time
to just minutes or even seconds per scan (Table 15), depending on the resolution, allowing for
rapid, high-throughput, and standardized assessment of radiotracer biodistribution. Addition-
ally, automation enables the processing of larger datasets and eliminates the reliance on external
annotators.
Overall, these improvements lay the groundwork for more efficient and reliable dosimetric cal-
culations, directly supporting translational research and preclinical development in radionuclide
therapy.

What was achieved
This thesis involved the development of a comprehensive computational framework extending
well beyond the core segmentation models to support the entire preclinical radionuclide therapy
dosimetry pipeline. The contributions encompass data processing, model development, exper-
imental infrastructure, and analysis tools that collectively enable automated, high-throughput
assessment of radiotracer biodistribution.
5.1.1
Data processing and standardization framework
A critical foundation for this work involved establishing robust data processing pipelines to
handle the heterogeneous nature of preclinical imaging data. We developed tools for converting
proprietary data formats from the Siemens Inveon Multimodality System to standardized NIfTI
formats while preserving the precise spatial alignment between PET and CT modalities essential
for accurate dosimetric calculations. Additionally, we implemented resampling algorithms to
ensure consistent voxel spacing and registration between PET and CT acquisitions.
To address the variable Hounsfield Unit scaling observed across different studies and scanner
configurations (as discussed in Section 5.4.1), we developed tools for HU normalization based
on anatomical landmarks. These tools enable rescaling based on liver and air ROIs, providing
consistent tissue contrast across datasets and improving model generalization.

Cross-validation
The five-fold cross-validation (Section 3.8, Table 7) demonstrated that nnU-Net ResEnc M and
U-Mamba Bot achieve nearly identical performance, both in mean DSC (0.929) and general-
ization error (Table 8). No significant statistical difference was found between the two models

U-Mamba Bot showed a marginally lower failure rate (2.7% vs. 3.5%), hinting at slightly im-
proved robustness for the most difficult cases (Figure 11). Analysis of inter-fold disagreement
(Figure 12) revealed that segmentation variability is correlated to CT quality, with higher dis-
agreement in low-contrast regions.
Table 9 presents the quantitative performance of both ensemble models across the large test set
(N = 68) and the STAPLE test set (N = 10). On the large test set, both ensembles achieved
remarkably high performance, with the nnU-Net ResEnc M ensemble obtaining a mean DSC
of 0.916 and the U-Mamba Bot ensemble achieving 0.915. The nnU-Net ResEnc M ensemble
demonstrated superior performance in five out of seven organs (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys, and
stomach), while the U-Mamba Bot ensemble excelled in bladder and spleen segmentation.
Notably, the performance difference between the two test sets reflects the impact of annotation
methodology consistency. On the large test set (N = 68), which was annotated using the same
approach as the training data, both ensembles achieved higher mean DSC scores of 0.916 and
0.915.
In contrast, on the smaller STAPLE test set (N = 10), which employed a different
consensus-based annotation methodology, both ensembles achieved identical but lower mean
DSC scores of 0.860.
This pattern suggests that models perform optimally when evaluated
on data annotated using methodologies consistent with their training data, highlighting the
influence of annotation protocol alignment on apparent model performance. While the STAPLE
test set has proven to be valuable for internal assessments and provides a better estimate of
generalization performance, it is reasonable to compare scores between studies using the large
test set. This is based on the assumption that other studies employ the same annotation method
for both their training and test sets.
The performance distribution across organs was more balanced between the two ensembles on
the STAPLE test set, with each model achieving the highest scores in three and four organ
classes, out of seven. Both ensembles maintained consistently high performance across most
organs, with the spleen remaining the most challenging organ to segment (DSC ≈0.80 on the
large test set and DSC ≈0.66 on the STAPLE test set) across both evaluations.
Qualitative examples (Figures 13 and 14) confirm that remaining errors are mainly due to chal-
lenging anatomy or imaging artifacts, rather than model limitations. Overall, cross-validation
confirms the reliability and robustness of both models for multi-organ CT segmentation, with the
ensemble approach providing optimal performance when annotation consistency is maintained.

5.3
PET intensity
As discussed in Section 5.5, manual annotation and correction may vary due to factors such as
poor contrast and unclear boundaries between organs. These uncertainties can result in ROIs
that include unwanted organs.
Consequently, this can lead to PET readings from adjacent
organs that do not align with the true values for the specified ROI. This, in turn, introduces
misinformation into future calculations and analyses. When calculating the mean, the differences
may be small in many instances. However, if the specified ROI is expected to emit no signal,
even a small amount of signal can significantly affect the mean value. In contrast, maximum
values can be greatly influenced if an adjacent organ emits a stronger signal, thus being more
sensitive to annotation uncertainties than the mean value in general.
Highlighted with a in the Bland-Altman plots in Figures 15 and 16 is a case where the ground
truth annotation of the stomach included a small portion of the liver. The scan is displayed
in Figure 27. The tracer used in this particular scan was 89Zr-based; thus, the liver exhibited
a signal, whereas the stomach showed a relatively low signal. The outlier marked with (a) in
the Bland-Altman plots (Figures 15, 16) is attributed to the inclusion of the liver in the ground
truth annotation of the stomach, emphasizing the gain from using methods such as including
functional images when annotating, described in Section 5.5.1.

5.3.1
Mask Erosion for Signal Spillover Correction
Signal spillover between adjacent organs in PET imaging poses a significant challenge to dosi-
metric quantification, particularly for maximum uptake values, which are highly sensitive to
contamination from neighboring high-intensity regions (see Figure 27). To address this, we im-
plemented a mask erosion-based strategy to refine ROI boundaries. This method systematically
reduces the mask dimensions of organs near high-intensity regions, such as the liver, to minimize
the inclusion of spillover signals.
The optimal erosion radius for each organ was determined by evaluating the relative stability of
mean and maximum PET signal intensities across varying erosion levels, as shown in Appendix
Figure 33.
Relative stability in these metrics was used as an indicator of reduced spillover
effects. This approach was particularly effective for organs such as the lung, kidney, and stomach,
enabling more consistent and reliable intensity measurements.
While promising, mask erosion has inherent limitations, such as the risk of excluding legiti-
mate high-uptake regions near organ boundaries. These trade-offs highlight the need for more
advanced methods to balance spillover correction with anatomical fidelity.

5.3.2
Bayesian Boundary Refinement for Signal Spillover Correction
To overcome the limitations of mask erosion, we propose a Bayesian boundary refinement ap-
proach to further address signal spillover while preserving anatomical integrity. This method
involves dilating adjacent organ ROIs and identifying overlap regions for refinement. A Bayesian
classifier is trained on PET signal characteristics from the original ROI, excluding contested
boundary regions to prevent contamination.
The classifier then assigns voxels within these
regions based on their signal profiles, ensuring a more accurate boundary delineation.
This approach is adaptive to organ-specific signal characteristics and avoids the uniform geo-
metric transformations inherent in mask erosion. By targeting only the overlapping regions, it
preserves organ volume and minimizes the exclusion of legitimate high-uptake areas. Subsequent
post-processing steps, such as smoothing and morphological adjustments, may be required to
ensure even boundaries. Empirical evaluation against ground truth dosimetric values will be
essential to optimize the method for practical application.

This chapter critically examines the results presented in the previous sections. The findings
are analyzed in relation to the study’s objectives and the broader context of existing litera-

The section begins with an assessment of the dataset characteristics.
It analyzes the
comparative analysis of mixed versus specific-species approaches, an exploration of the trade-off

5.4.1
Dataset
Figure 17 in Section 4.3.1 illustrates the HU distribution across all the used datasets.
The
distribution varies, particularly in mouse studies. Such variation may be influenced by differences
in the materials of the scanner beds or variations in scanner calibration. Different types of beds
can produce different HU intensities depending on the materials used. While this variation can
appear concerning, it may enhance the robustness of deep learning models.
In CT imaging, soft tissues typically fall within the range of approximately 50 to 100 HU, which
represents the most common tissue type in preclinical CT scans [15]. The raw images from
the Betacell study exhibited voxel intensities substantially outside this expected range. This
suggests that a rescaling of the data may be necessary to align it more closely with typical
expectations. The rescaling is explained in Section 3.2.3.
5.4.2
Mixed vs. specific species
The results from the mixed model study (Section 3.7.1) show that species-specific models achieve
marginally higher performance compared to the mixed model when evaluated on their respective
test sets. The differences are limited to the third and fourth decimal places and are either non-
significant or only weakly significant based on the Wilcoxon signed-rank test, as illustrated in
Figure 18.
This minor edge in performance for the specific model must be weighed against the substantial
practical benefit of deploying a single, unified model for both species. The mixed model demon-
strates consistent performance between species, with similar failure rates (approximately 8%)
observed for both rats and mice, similar to those of the species-specific models. Moreover, the

DSC distribution illustrated in Figure 19 exhibits the majority of observations tending towards
higher Dice scores (DSC ≈1) resulting in a strong left skew. This shift towards higher DSC
indicates a robust and stable behavior of the segmentation model.
Considering the small trade-off in accuracy and the maintained failure rate, the decision was
made to continue with the mixed model for future experiments. This choice simplifies deploy-

networks.
The observed generalization capability of the mixed model across two rodent species, mice and
rats, suggests that further incorporating species diversity into the dataset could be a promising
strategy for improving model robustness. Including more species in the training data may help
build a more generalized model capable of handling a wider range of anatomical variability.
There are similar challenges found in clinical imaging in achieving generalization across diverse
populations, including variations in body type, gender, and age. Studies have shown that models
trained on homogeneous datasets may underperform for underrepresented subgroups [55]. In
contrast, using broader training data enhances model reliability [56]. The promising cross-species
generalization observed in this study supports the idea that biological variability in training data
can be a strength rather than a limitation.
5.4.3
Training Length - A tradeoff between time and performance
The results presented in Section 4.5.2 demonstrate that the training length has a measurable
impact on model performance. Increasing the number of epochs from 1000 to 3000 results in a
small but statistically significant improvement in segmentation accuracy, as illustrated in Figure
20 and Table 18. Specifically, the mean Dice score increases from 0.928 to 0.931. Although
an absolute increase of 0.003 is modest, it is consistent in 6 out of 7 organs, with the largest
gain observed for the spleen, which aligns with its greater variability noted in the IAA analysis
(Table 12).
However, this performance gain comes at a substantial cost: training for 3000 epochs requires
approximately three times the computational time and resources compared to 1000 epochs, due
to the linear scaling of training time.
Therefore, it is essential to consider whether such a
marginal improvement in segmentation accuracy justifies the additional investment, especially
in scenarios with limited computational budgets or time constraints.
Subsequently, we decided to train our models for 1000 epochs during our initial experiments.
However, for the final cross-validation, we extended the training duration to 2000 epochs. This
adjustment aims to achieve a balance between performance gains and training time, ensuring
efficient use of resources while maintaining robust model performance.

We evaluated using both test sets: the large, computer-aided, and human-corrected set (Section

The initial focus on the large test set provided insight into model robustness and generalization
across a relatively broad and diverse sample. However, to ensure a less biased evaluation and
minimize potential annotation artifacts, we prioritized the consensus dataset for final model
selection.

achieved the highest pan-organ mean DSC (0.851 ± 0.070). SAMed was again statistically out-

ResEnc L, as confirmed by Wilcoxon signed-rank tests. In this dataset, only nnU-Net ResEnc
L and U-Mamba Enc had fully missed organ predictions for the bladder.
In conclusion, both the U-Mamba Bot and the nnU-Net ResEnc M are among the best performers
in terms of DSC and successfully identify all organs in the consensus test set. Additionally, they
are more lightweight than MedNeXt, allowing for local deployment (see Appendix B, Figure 31).
Considering this, these models were chosen for further development.
5.5
Annotation quality
As evidenced by the results shown in Table 12, the variance between annotations is large for
MOS and other annotation tasks in CT imaging [57]. It is conceivable that the segmentation
model might reach a level of quality where it is outperforming the manual annotations used as
ground truth. This would negatively influence the perceived model performance. This underlines
the need for manual inspection of annotation results.
To deal with error-prone annotation quality, we propose paths to take:
• Employ alternative evaluation metrics: When reference annotations are subject to
significant noise or error, metrics beyond the DSC may provide a more accurate assessment
of model performance. See Section 5.6.2 for a discussion of suitable alternatives.
• Improve annotation quality: Enhancing the quality of manual annotations—albeit
at the cost of increased annotation time—can lead to more reliable model training and
evaluation. Strategies for improving annotation fidelity are discussed in Section 5.5.1.
5.5.1
Strategies for increasing annotation quality
Table 12 reveals pronounced inter-annotator variability, particularly for spleen (DSC: 0.58-0.68)
and stomach (DSC: 0.72-0.76), compared to other organs. These organs present unique chal-
lenges due to their variable morphology, boundary ambiguity, and contrast inconsistencies in
CT imaging [58]. Increasing annotation quality and quantity for the spleen and stomach would
therefore likely result in a substantial improvement in overall pan-organ DSC.

CT Quality
Improving CT image quality represents a fundamental approach to enhancing annotation accu-
racy and consistency. Higher quality CT images provide better soft tissue contrast and clearer
organ boundaries, directly facilitating more precise manual delineation.
Prevention of motion artifacts through improved animal positioning, anesthesia protocols, and
respiratory gating can substantially improve image clarity [59, 60].
As demonstrated in our
worst-performing case (Figure 14), motion blur and imaging artifacts significantly compromise
both automated segmentation performance and manual annotation quality. The presence of
imaging artifacts and motion blur in this example resulted in substantially reduced segmentation
accuracy (DSCpan organ = 0.788), illustrating how image quality directly impacts both human
annotators and deep learning models.
CT Contrast agents
Schoppe et al. [58] showed an inter-annotator DSC of 0.39 for spleens but managed to increase
the DSC to 0.82 when injecting the subjects with a CT contrast agent. This agent increased
the contrast of both the liver and spleen.
Although the use of contrast agents can mitigate challenges associated with the annotation of
organs such as the spleen and stomach (particularly by enhancing the boundary delineation
between the stomach and liver), their utility is limited. Specifically, contrast agents are not fea-
sible for all study protocols due to clinical or logistical constraints, and they cannot retroactively
improve the annotation quality of historical imaging datasets.
Consensus mechanisms
When multiple annotations are available for a single scan, consensus mechanisms can be em-
ployed to reduce inter-annotator variability. In this study, we applied the STAPLE algorithm [16]
to data annotated by three independent annotators to generate a probabilistic consensus seg-
mentation. Further details are provided in Section 3.2.9.
While STAPLE is a widely used method for generating consensus segmentations, alternative
approaches may more effectively leverage the complementary information present across multiple
annotations, as suggested by recent work [61].
A notable limitation of consensus-based methods is the increased annotation burden, as the time
required scales linearly with the number of annotators involved.
Figure 28 demonstrates representative outcomes of consensus segmentation. In panel (a), the
consensus annotation for the spleen exhibits a more rounded contour and reduced volume com-
pared to the individual manual annotations, exemplifying a situation in which the STAPLE
algorithm may insufficiently capture the true anatomical boundaries.
In contrast, panel (b)
presents a case where the heart is markedly overestimated by one annotator; here, the STAPLE
algorithm attenuates the influence of this outlier, resulting in a consensus segmentation that
more accurately reflects the majority of annotations. These observations suggest that exclusive
reliance on a single manual annotation may yield a biased assessment of model performance,
thereby emphasizing the potential advantages of consensus-based approaches. Collectively, these
examples indicate that the effectiveness of consensus algorithms such as STAPLE is context-
dependent, varying with the anatomical region and the degree of inter-annotator variability.

Using functional imaging (PET/SPECT) as a guideline for organ segmentation
The inclusion of functional imaging, such as PET or SPECT, can provide valuable comple-
mentary information to aid in the annotation of anatomically challenging organs, such as the
stomach and spleen. As illustrated in Figure 29, the structural information from the CT scan
alone makes it difficult to distinguish the spleen from surrounding soft tissues due to the inherent
low soft tissue contrast. However, the fused PET/CT image (Figure 29c), acquired using the
64Cu-DOTATATE radiotracer, enhances the visibility of specific organs based on their functional
uptake patterns. This improved organ-specific signal can significantly assist in the delineation
of boundaries, particularly for the spleen and stomach, by providing a functional contrast that
complements the anatomical information in the CT image. Such multimodal imaging approaches
can be especially beneficial for generating high-quality reference annotations and may help mit-
igate inter-annotator variability in complex anatomical regions, as demonstrated by Dhalival et
al. [19] who used 64Cu-tracer to assist the manual annotation.
It should be noted, however, that the utility of functional imaging for annotation is highly
dependent on the choice of radiotracer and the imaging time point. Different radiotracers exhibit
distinct biodistribution and uptake kinetics, which may alter the appearance and distinctness
of organs across scans [20, 21].
Similarly, the timing of image acquisition relative to tracer
administration can introduce further variability in organ uptake patterns [19]. Consequently,
reliance on functional imaging for annotation may not always be appropriate, as these factors
can produce inconsistent visualization of target organs across different studies or protocols.

5.6
Metrics - When to use what?
Choosing the right evaluation metric is crucial for accurately assessing the performance of med-
ical image segmentation models. Different metrics capture different aspects of segmentation
quality, and their suitability depends on the specific task, the characteristics of the structures
being segmented, and the quality of the annotations.
5.6.1
Dice Similarity Coefficient
DSC serves as the gold standard for medical image segmentation evaluation [62], yet its effective-
ness depends critically on task characteristics and annotation quality. While DSC performs well
for large, well-defined structures with high-quality reference annotations, it exhibits significant
limitations for small structures relative to pixel size, where minor boundary disagreements can
drastically affect scores [63]. This limitation is particularly apparent in our results for the spleen
class, which consistently achieved the lowest DSC scores across all evaluated models, reflecting
both its relatively small size and the inherent difficulty in achieving precise boundary delineation
for this organ.
Additionally, DSC values are highly dependent on image resolution and pixel sizes, meaning
identical prediction quality can yield different scores across spatial resolutions.
The boundary sensitivity of DSC becomes problematic when expert annotators disagree, as
visually similar annotations can produce surprisingly low DSC scores (e.g., 0.7 for nearly identical
masks) [64].
5.6.2
Error-prone reference annotations
Medical image segmentation faces inherent challenges from annotation variability, as domain ex-
perts frequently disagree on object definitions and boundary delineations. The SDSC addresses
these limitations through its hybrid design combining boundary-based and counting-based ap-

proaches [63]. By incorporating a tolerance threshold τ that captures known uncertainties and
acceptable boundary deviations, SDSC provides robustness against annotation variability while
maintaining interpretability similar to classical DSC. The critical hyperparameter τ should be
determined through inter-rater agreement studies rather than arbitrary selection.
However, our experimental results reveal that the theoretical advantages of SDSC may not al-
ways translate to meaningful practical differences. While U-Mamba Bot achieved a marginally
higher mean SDSC (0.835) compared to nnU-Net ResEnc M (0.833), this difference is negligible
considering the standard deviations. More notably, for the spleen class where annotation uncer-
tainty would be expected to most benefit from SDSC’s tolerance mechanism, nnU-Net ResEnc
M outperformed U-Mamba Bot (0.735 vs 0.725 SDSC). This suggests that while SDSC indi-
cated U-Mamba Bot as a marginally better performer than nnU-Net ResEnc M, the difference
remains negligible, demonstrating that both models are equally capable performers for organ
segmentation tasks.
5.6.3
Other success criteria
Task-specific validation criteria often provide more clinically relevant assessments than tradi-
tional overlap metrics. Volume-based metrics such as Absolute and Relative Volume Error focus
purely on volumetric accuracy while ignoring spatial location, making them suitable for applica-
tions where object volume is paramount. For linear or tube-like structures, connectivity metrics
like Centerline Dice (clDice) measure topological agreement through the harmonic mean of
Topology Precision and Sensitivity, addressing cases where network topology supersedes precise
boundary delineation [63]. In PET/CT segmentation tasks, domain-specific validation against
PET uptake measurements using professional software as the golden standard can provide a
clinically meaningful performance assessment.
Our proposed optimal approach combines multiple complementary metrics: DSC for general
overlap assessment, SDSC for boundary quality under uncertain annotations, and task-specific
measures for clinical relevance.
5.7
External data and generalization
Despite recent advances in semantic segmentation, a persistent challenge remains: models tend
to perform well on data similar to their training distribution but often struggle when confronted
with out-of-distribution (OOD) samples [65, 66]. As noted by Gao et al., “these models often
falter when faced with distributional shifts[...]” highlighting the need for robust generalization in
real-world applications [66]. To rigorously assess the generalizability of our segmentation models,
we evaluated their performance on external data from the independent study by Rosenhain et
al. [18]. This dataset differs from the RMOS in aspects: a different imaging system (Tomoscope
Duo, CT Imaging GmbH, Erlangen, Germany) and a different imaging bed were used, which
clamps the mouse in position, potentially affecting organ placement. See Figure 30
This qualitative OOD evaluation presented in Section 5.7.1 provides insight into the robustness
of our mixed-species approach beyond the confines of the training data.
To the best of our knowledge, there are currently no publicly available rat CT datasets. Conse-
quently, it is not possible to evaluate model generalization on truly OOD data for rat CT.

5.9
Limitations
Several limitations warrant consideration in this study. Our evaluation on out-of-distribution
data from Rosenhain et al. [18] was constrained to qualitative assessment due to differing an-
notation conventions, precluding quantitative validation of generalization performance across
different imaging systems and protocols.
Annotation quality represents a fundamental constraint, as evidenced by substantial inter-
annotator variability (IAA DSC: 0.58-0.76 for challenging organs such as spleen and stomach).
While consensus-based approaches like STAPLE help address this issue, the absence of veteri-
nary expert involvement in initial annotation protocols may have introduced systematic errors
that propagate through model training and evaluation.
Our models occasionally demonstrate conservative boundary estimation, particularly for liver
and spleen extremities where segmentation may exclude legitimate high-uptake regions near
organ peripheries. This tendency, while reducing false positives, could impact dosimetric calcu-
lations requiring precise volume measurements.
PET signal spillover between adjacent organs continues to challenge accurate quantification,
despite our erosion-based mitigation strategies. The proposed Bayesian boundary refinement
approach requires empirical validation against professional PET analysis software to establish
clinical utility.
The current training paradigm represents a static approach that cannot leverage new data as it
becomes available. Implementation of active learning strategies could enable continuous model
improvement and adaptation to evolving experimental protocols, though this capability was not
explored in the present work.
These limitations highlight opportunities for future development, particularly in establishing
robust validation frameworks for out-of-distribution data and developing adaptive learning ap-
proaches for sustained model performance improvement.

6
Conclusions
This thesis work establishes automated semantic segmentation using deep learning as a robust
alternative to manual annotation in preclinical PET/CT imaging. Our models represent the
first to achieve high-accuracy multi-organ segmentation across both mice and rats, demonstrat-
ing exceptional generalization capabilities across diverse experimental protocols and scanner
configurations while maintaining consistent performance across species.
The automation achieved represents a transformative improvement in workflow efficiency, reduc-
ing processing time by approximately a factor of 200 for mice and 35 for rats while simultaneously
eliminating inter-annotator variability. This advancement directly addresses the primary bottle-
neck in quantitative radionuclide therapy dosimetry and enables high-throughput, standardized
analysis essential for translational research.
Our comprehensive architectural evaluation revealed that while model selection influences per-
formance, training data quality and annotation consistency represent the primary determinants
of segmentation accuracy. The near-identical performance of nnU-Net ResEnc M and U-Mamba
Bot (both achieving 0.86 pan-organ DSC on the consensus STAPLE test dataset and 0.915-
0.916 on the larger test dataset) demonstrates that investment in high-quality, representative
datasets yields greater returns than architectural refinements alone. This finding has important
implications for resource allocation in future medical segmentation development efforts.
Critical validation of PET signal quantification confirms that automated segmentations produce
organ-level intensity measurements suitable for dosimetric analysis, with Bland-Altman analysis
demonstrating strong agreement with manual annotations. The implementation of mask ero-
sion strategies and proposed Bayesian boundary refinement methods addresses signal spillover
challenges, further enhancing quantitative accuracy for dosimetric applications.
Cross-validation analysis confirmed model robustness, with both architectures demonstrating
low failure rates (2.7 −3.5%) and consistent performance across folds.
Evaluation on out-
of-distribution data from independent studies validated generalization capabilities, though it
highlighted the ongoing need for post-processing refinements to address scanner-specific artifacts.
The scalable computational framework developed extends beyond core segmentation to encom-
pass the entire preclinical dosimetry pipeline, from data standardization through quantitative
analysis. This comprehensive approach establishes a foundation for standardized, automated
assessment that can accelerate preclinical radionuclide therapy development.
While limitations remain, including annotation quality challenges and PET spillover effects, our
proposed solutions through consensus-based annotation strategies and advanced post-processing
methods provide clear pathways for continued improvement. The demonstrated clinical utility
and robust performance across diverse datasets position deep learning-based segmentation as an
essential tool for future translational studies in precision radiotherapy.

C.1.3
Surface Dice score:
The Surface Dice score [71] is a variant of the standard Dice coefficient that evaluates the
similarity of predicted and ground-truth surfaces, rather than volumes. The metric is particu-
larly sensitive to boundary alignment, making it useful for assessing segmentation accuracy at
anatomical borders. Given a tolerance parameter τ, it measures the fraction of surface points
on the prediction that are within τ millimeters of the ground-truth surface, and vice versa. The
Surface Dice score is defined as:
Surface Diceτ(P, G) = |SP ∩BG(τ)| + |SG ∩BP(τ)|
|SP| + |SG|
where SP and SG are the sets of surface points for the prediction P and ground truth G,
respectively. BG(τ) denotes the set of points within a distance τ of SG, and BP(τ) denotes the
set of points within a distance τ of SP. The numerator counts the number of prediction surface
points within τ of the ground truth surface and vice versa. A higher Surface Dice score indicates
better boundary agreement within the specified tolerance. [63]

The SAMed model, proposed by Zhang and Liu in 2023 [45], is based on the original Segment
Anything Model (SAM) [44] and has been modified to address the specific challenges of medical
imaging.
SAM is a segmentation model built on a dataset of over one billion segmentation
masks across over 11 million pictures. The architecture of SAMed is designed using a Vision
Transformer framework. It processes the entire slice by breaking it down into patches, allowing
for global attention to be achieved.
Through iterative steps, the model gradually extracts
semantic information. Finally, this information is passed to a ”mask decoder” that produces the
output masks in the desired format.
Additionally, the architecture employs a method known as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on
top of the SAM image encoder. This approach significantly reduces the number of parameters
needed to update the network weights, while having little to no loss of performance [46]. As a
result, this ensures faster training when fine-tuning the model for specific data.
While Vision Transformers require more data for optimal performance, they may offer greater
potential due to their ability to capture global context, though this also demands increased
computational power.
C.3.2
U-Mamba
The U-Mamba architecture shares similarities with the Residual Encoder nnU-Net M (see Figure
7), which serves as our initial model, as described in Section 3.4.1. However, the U-Mamba
architecture distinguishes itself by utilizing U-Mamba blocks instead of residual blocks (Figure
6). Additionally, U-Mamba only uses two blocks at each level, instead of the increasing nature of
Residual Encoder nnU-Net M. In this project, we use two variations of the U-Mamba architecture
based on the placement of the U-Mamba blocks: U-Mamba Enc and U-Mamba Bot.
The U-Mamba Enc model replaces all residual blocks in the encoder, including the bottleneck
layer, with U-Mamba blocks. In contrast, the U-Mamba Bot model only replaces the residual
block in the bottleneck layer with a U-Mamba block.
A U-Mamba block operates by using two standard residual blocks. The output of these blocks
is then flattened into a sequence and fed into a Mamba block, proposed in [47]. This Mamba
block is an expansion of the State Space Model (SSM), which is designed to manage sequences
and is valued for its linear computational complexity per time step, as well as its efficiency in
handling long sequences. The expansion on SSM involves adjusting the parameters based on
the input to emphasize important information and employs a new scanning method that is more
efficient in terms of hardware usage.
By integrating the Mamba blocks into the U-Mamba architecture, the model is equipped to
capture long-range dependencies more effectively than traditional convolutional or transformer-
based designs. This hybrid approach allows U-Mamba to combine the strengths of local feature
extraction from convolutional layers with the Mamba block’s efficient modeling of global con-
text, potentially leading to improved segmentation performance in scenarios where long-range
interactions within the image are important. [48]
C.3.3
MedNeXt
MedNeXt is an architecture built on the U-Net framework, similar to many other architectures.
It features custom blocks in both the encoder and decoder. It replaces traditional convolutional
layers with three distinct blocks: MedNeXt Block, MedNeXt Up Block, and MedNeXt Down

Block. In the encoder, all layers incorporate a MedNeXt Block and a MedNeXt Down Block,
while in the decoder, each layer includes a MedNeXt Block and a MedNeXt Up Block.
All blocks are designed around three key concepts: 3D convolution, inverse bottleneck, and
residual connection. The 3D convolution provides depth-wise context, ensuring understanding
of spatial features.
The inverse bottleneck involves first expanding the number of channels,
utilizes an activation layer, followed by a compression of the channels. This technique helps
retain more information across different resolutions. Residual connections, much like the inverse
bottleneck, are crucial in preserving contextual information between layers by incorporating the
block input into the output.
Each of these three concepts is configured differently across the blocks. The Down Block reduces
the resolution while increasing the number of channels, the Up Block increases the resolution
while reducing the number of channels, and the MedNeXt Block maintains both the resolution
and the number of channels [49].

Abstract
Organ segmentation is a rapidly developing field within deep learning. It can be challenging to grasp com-
mon conventions, state-of-the-art (SOTA), and newly emerged methods for improving performance. This
paper aims to highlight both common and emerging architectures for the semantic segmentation of organs.
Key architectures discussed include 3D U-Net, 2.5D U-Net, nnU-Net, UResNet, MedNeXt, and SegMamba.
The review also investigates strategies beyond model architecture to enhance semantic segmentation per-
formance, with a focus on semi-supervised learning (SSL) and transfer learning (TL). Data augmentation
techniques, including geometric transformations and noise application, are highlighted for improving model
generalization, though their efficacy depends on careful evaluation. Ensembling approaches, which aggregate
predictions from diverse models, demonstrate improved accuracy but increase computational demands. SSL
leverages unlabeled data to improve performance, as exemplified by the Π-model and pseudo-labeling tech-
niques. Meanwhile, TL utilizes pre-trained model weights to improve efficiency, particularly when training
data is limited. These strategies, combined with large, high-quality datasets, offer significant potential for
advancing performance in preclinical CT imaging.

2
Introduction
With the emergence of 3D semantic segmentation using deep learning methods, organ segmentation is one of
the use cases receiving a lot of attention from the medical community with over 2000 papers on the subject
being released in journals in 2024. [1]
Previously multi atlas segmentation was considered state-of-the-art (SOTA). This technique uses other seg-
mented images (the atlases) as templates and finds the best-fitting segmentation mask by applying affine
transformations to the atlases to fit to the new image. Deep learning-based techniques are now favored over
atlas-based techniques due to their increased speed and comparable to better performance. [2]
Many different claims regarding segmentation models being SOTA can be found within the literature. This
report aims to collect some of the most cited model architectures and evaluate the claimed performance.
Further, it aims to investigate avenues for improving the performance beyond model architecture such as
data augmentation, ensembling, semi-supervised learning (SSL) and transfer learning (TL).
While 3D semantic segmentation can be applied to many different imaging modalities such as magnetic
resonance imaging (MRI), computed tomography (CT), and 3D-confocal microscopy this report will focus
on CT imaging.
Within the field of organ segmentation convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are most commonly used but
promising new technologies are emerging such as transformer models, Mamba and Generative adversarial
network (GAN) models. Adaptations of the 3D-U-Net are one of the most commonly used models as they
compare on par with many novel models and are commonly used as a benchmark. [3]
3
Models
This section aims to provide a brief introduction to the most commonly used deep learning models for
segmentation in medical images.
3.1
U-net and U-Net variations
U-net and its variations are among the most widely used architectures for segmenting medical images. In
segmenting medical images, typically voxel-based 3D images, the conventional 2D model [4] can evaluate
each slice but lacks depth context. Therefore, 2.5D or 3D models are often preferred. A 3D U-Net functions
similarly to a 2D U-Net but incorporates a third dimension for the kernel and stride. [5] While 3D models
introduce additional computational demands, 2.5D approaches require similar resources to 2D U-Nets by
working slice-based but consider adjacent slices to enhance contextual understanding. [6] If computational
budget is a consideration working in 2.5D could ease the training, especially on high-resolution images. Due
to the lack of sufficient comparisons between 2.5D and 3D, it is not possible to declare one as better than
the other regarding segmentation performance.
To enhance the user-friendliness and adaptability of the U-Net for various data shapes, Isensee et al. developed
the nnU-Net. This framework automatically determines key parameters, such as whether to utilize 2D, 3D
or U-Net Cascade models, an architecture proposed in the same paper. Additionally, the nnU-Net decides
on the kernel size, the number of pooling layers, and the number of convolutional layers, all based on the
geometry of the input images. [7]
This model is attractive because of its adaptability, which often leads to strong performance. However, this
same adaptability can also be a drawback as the model is quite general. Due to this, it may lack the specificity
needed for particular cases.
Models building on U-Net architecture are numerous, of which a few will be highlighted to emphasize the
potential opportunities. UResNet, proposed by R. Guerrero et al. [8], combines a conventional U-Net with
another technique known as residual learning introduced by Kaiming He et al. in 2016. [9] In short, residual
learning incorporates both the input and output of a neural network module using a skip connection. This can
be understood as the model learning from the difference between the current state (input) and the output
generated by the neural network module.

In a recent study conducted in 2024, Dangguo Shao and colleagues proposed a model for segmenting organs
in chest CT images called MOS-GAN. [10] This model is based on a technique called Generative Adversarial
Networks (GANs), which consists of two networks: a generator and a discriminator. During training, the
model alternates between these two networks. The generator’s objective is to create data that resembles
real data, while the discriminator’s task is to differentiate between real data and the data produced by the
generator. This strategy encourages the two networks to reinforce each other. In this study, the generator
utilizes a residual 2.5D U-Net++, while the discriminator is a traditional Convolutional Neural Network
(CNN). The proposed model outperformed 3D U-Net++ as a baseline model in ablation experiments, as
measured by the Dice score. The largest increase in dice score was for the Esophagus (0.661 to 0.767), while
the smallest increase was for the right lung (0.958 to 0.971). This could indicate that including a discriminator
component enhances overall performance.
3.2
Other models
While U-Net-based architectures are widely accepted as the gold standard in medical image segmentation,
alternative models are occasionally used to push the boundaries of this field. Among these, Mamba and
MedNeXt offer new approaches to organ segmentation.
MedNeXt, while inspired by the macro-structure of a 3D U-Net, distinguishes itself by integrating various
advanced techniques such as transformers, kernel scaling, and residual inverted bottlenecks. This complex
combination builds upon the previously proposed ConvNeXt model [11], tailoring it specifically for medical
image segmentation. MedNeXt outperforms seven baseline models, including nnU-Net, UNETR, TransUNet,
TransBTS, nnFormer, SwinUNETR, and 3D-UX-Net. The evaluation was conducted across four datasets:
BTCV, AMOS22, KiTS19, and BraTS21. The mean Dice scores for MedNeXt and nnU-Net (best-performing
baseline) averaged over all datasets are as follows: nnU-Net achieved a score of 88.39, while MedNeXt attained
a score of 89.22. [12]
SegMamba is a variation of the State Space Model called Mamba, which utilizes an encoder-decoder structure
as its backbone. This structure is well-known for its effectiveness in medical image segmentation. What makes
SegMamba unique is its integration of various methods, including attention mechanisms that emphasize the
most relevant parts of an image. Additionally, it employs multi-scale processing, which combines outputs at
different scales to capture contextual information at both local and global levels. Furthermore, SegMamba
includes a feature-level uncertainty estimation that filters features to enhance robustness. [13]
4
Strategies for boosting performance
In this section strategies beyond architecture design are discussed. Mainly SSL and TL are discussed. These
are two approaches for using data that is either unannotated or not strictly related to the problem at hand.
4.1
Data augmentation
In computer vision and semantic segmentation, data augmentation is often used to increase performance of
the model. Data augmentation can lead to more robust models that are able to generalize better to new
models. Data augmentation for images can be random rotations, flips, mirroring, adding noise, changing
hues and exposure, and applying filters such as Gaussian blur. [14] The different strategies are summarized
in Figure 2.
Image augmentation in semantic segmentation tasks can both increase model performance and degrade model
performance. Using methods such as cross-validation can be used to evaluate augmentations before deciding
which to use. [14]

4.2
Ensembling
Ensembling is the method of having multiple models (often referred to as students) make a prediction from
one observation. The final prediction (in classification tasks) is often found by majority vote although other
methods such as weighted classifiers (AdaBoost) and Bayesian inference are also frequently used. [15]
The introduction of an ensembling method can often increase the performance of any classifier, but it will
also increase the number of models needing to be trained as well as the memory requirements of the system.
Naive approaches to ensembles increase the computational cost linearly with the number of models. Due to
this ensembling might in some cases not be applicable to very large models.
An ensemble can include several of the same models trained on the same dataset, several models trained on
different subsets of the dataset, and different model architectures trained on the same data. Generally, it is
the consensus that larger diversity in the ensembled models increases the performance of the ensemble. [16]
4.3
Semi-supervised learning
The manual annotation of data used to train a segmentation model is labor-costly. This is the very motivation
for automating the process. Using only fully annotated data is referred to as fully supervised learning (FSL).
Often the amount of available unannotated data will far exceed the amount of annotated data.
The idea of SSL is that by using the fully annotated data while leveraging the structural information retained
in unannotated data it should lead to a better model performance. Especially if the training dataset of fully
annotated data is small.
A notable example is the Π-model where a second term is added to the loss function for the model to learn
from unannotated data. [17] The model is trained with dropout layers and data augmentations such as
adding noise (see Section 4.1). This in turn makes each prediction of the same data point xi a stochastic
variable. For each observation, xi, two predictions are made giving zi and ˜zi. The squared loss between the
two predictions are then added to the loss function. This encourages the network to confidently recognize

Pseudo-labeling is an SSL technique commonly applied in segmentation tasks, where model predictions on
unlabeled data are treated as approximate labels to augment the training set. This approach can be effective
but is limited by the potential noise and errors in the pseudo-labels, which can degrade performance if
not managed carefully, such as through confidence thresholding. While pseudo-labeling is straightforward
and widely used, more advanced semi-supervised methods, including consistency regularization and teacher-
student models, often achieve better performance by leveraging additional mechanisms to improve robustness
and generalization. [18,19]
4.4
Transfer learning
Transfer learning (TL) is done by reusing model weights from a model trained on different, often unrelated,
training data.
Large public datasets are used such as ImageNet.
Sometimes all model weights will be
pretrained, but in computer vision segmentation tasks it is quite common to only let the encoder weights
be pretrained. [20] This allows for using classification datasets such as ImageNet for the pretraining, as the
encoder can look the same for a convolutional classifier and a fully convolutional segmentation model. The
encoder then learns how to do feature extraction on large datasets. [21]
While the suitability of transfer learning depends on the specific task and dataset, it is often observed to
outperform random weight initialization in a wide range of scenarios. This is particularly true in cases where
pre-trained models provide a strong prior that aligns well with the target task.
The paper Efficacy of Transfer Learning Over Semantic Segmentation did semantic segmentation on the
Oxford-IIIT Pet dataset with a number of Fully Convolutional Neural Networks. They saw the models with
encoders pretrained on ImageNet always outperformed the randomly initialised counterpart. Further, they
tested the effect of freezing the encoder weights. This severely decreased the performance of each of the
models. [20] It should be noted that the Oxford-IIIT Pet dataset is quite large and in cases where the dataset
is smaller freezing the encoder weights could prevent overfitting to the training data.

5
Discussion and conclusions
This section seeks to summarize the principal findings discussed in the preceding sections and provide rec-
ommendations regarding the appropriate contexts for the implementation of the various strategies.
5.1
Recommendations for semantic segmentation tasks in CT
Model architecture: Given the wide variety of architectures, datasets, and baseline models, it’s challenging
to determine which model is superior. However, more complex models like UResNet, MOS-GAN, SegMamba,
and MedNeXt tend to outperform simpler baseline models, which are usually basic variations of the U-Net
architecture. Many combinations of methods provide a high potential for performance, but finding such
a model can be tedious due to the numerous combinations available.
To find the best-fitting model, a
comparative analysis of the desired models could be conducted to standardize the scoring metric along
the models used for the specific case. Suggestions for new models could include combining the principles
mentioned in Section 3, such as employing a GAN structure with a more advanced network as either a
generator or a discriminator, or similar approaches.
When applying semantic segmentation in a work environment, it is important to prioritize considerations
related to computational budget and runtime. Depending on the available hardware, a simpler and lighter
model may be more beneficial for overall turnover. However, if achieving the best possible performance is
crucial, a heavier and more complex model might be a better fit for the situation, with the sacrifice of runtime.

Semi-supervised learning on the abundant amount of unlabeled data: Due to the large amounts
of unlabeled data/data labeled for different purposes it would make a lot of sense to include the unsuper-
vised learning aspect in the training. While it does not yield the best results the Π-model could easily be
implemented in most training scripts. It is a lightweight solution as it only requires having one model in
memory.
Having a cluster of models making pseudolabels for other networks is also a possibility. In DLUNet: Semi-
supervised Learning based Dual-Light UNet for Multi-organ Segmentation [24] two light identical U-Nets
with different initialization are used to generate pseudolabels for the other network. They saw an increase in
performance when compared to the equivalent networks without SSL.
From the experiments of Oliver et al. [18] the VAT & EM combination grants the best results. While their
findings might not be transferable to an image segmentation problem it should be considered as an option. ´
Transfer learning with analogous imaging data: A good way to improve performance in an organ
segmentation task with limited data would be to pretrain on a similar dataset. Rosenhein et al. [25] could be
used for training all model weights in the segmentation model in a preclinical setting. For training a human
multi-organ segmentation model the Multi-organ Abdominal CT Reference Standard Segmentations dataset
could be used [26]. This dataset contains 90 abdominal CT images with segmentations for the following
organs: the spleen, left kidney, gallbladder, esophagus, liver, stomach, pancreas and duodenum. For an even
larger dataset the AMOS dataset can be used. [27] This dataset contains 500 abdominal CTs with 15 labeled
organs.
MedMNIST+ v3 [28] has a collection of images called OrganMNIST3D with 1,742 samples of 64x64x64 CT
images of organs with corresponding classes. The dataset has 11 individual classes. The encoder weights
for a segmentation model could be pretrained on such a classification task. The intention by using such a
dataset is to teach the encoder how to feature extract information relevant to the task of organ classifica-
tion/segmentation rather than a less specific task.
Karimi et al. [29] showed that using transfer learning across centers, modalities and subject ages leads to small
but significant increases in the performance of their segmentation model. While the returns were diminishing
the less related the pretraining data was to the segmentation task this could still indicate the possibility for
a cross-species transfer learning scheme. In cases of mammals where data is less abundant pretraining on
human or mouse data might lead to a performance boost.
The Superiority of Large Datasets: While the preceding sections focus on strategies to enhance model
learning without increasing the data volume, it is important to note that one of the most effective ways
to improve model performance is by incorporating additional high-quality labeled data. When substantial
amounts of labeled data are available, the introduction of SSL techniques may even adversely affect segmen-
tation quality, as observed by Oliver et al. (2019) [18].

Risk analysis
The risk of delay at each phase is classified on a scale from 0 (no risk) to 5 (high risk). See Table
25.
The individual risks are estimates and will be elaborated below.
Phase 0: Planning and Setup
The data overview is done by accessing the office drive and searching for old studies. This is
not risky, as only a handful of technical issues could delay the process.
Phase 1: Transfer Learning Experiment and Initial Model
A user-friendly architecture, nnUnet [29], is utilized for the initial training. Due to nnUnet’s
adaptability and abundant features, the pretraining and fine-tuning do not seem risky.
Phase 2: Annotation Snowballing
This phase is highly dependent on the results of the initial model, phase 1.
A good initial
prediction will yield more annotations quickly, leading to a broader dataset which likely enhances
the model further. However, a bad initial prediction will result in more time-consuming manual
annotation in the start of the phase. Furthermore, we do not know if the acquired data amount
is sufficient to achieve a good-performing model.
Phase 3: Explorative Phase
As the initial model and dataset expansion are done using nnUnet [29], changing to more cus-
tomizable architectures could propose challenges during migration.
Furthermore many deep
learning techniques require a larger computational budget than that of our work station. The
ease of exploration might depend on the access to more powerful computers. Again, it is not
possible to know if we have enough data to efficiently train a SSL model.
Phase 4: Model Evaluation and Inter-annotator Differences
This phase heavily relies on whether we can get our annotations done externally as this would
greatly increase the validity of the tests.
Phase 5: User Interface and User Experience
This phase possess low risk, as it does not affect the data or performance of the predictions.
The risk lies in the development of the user interface as our limited experience could result in
unforeseen challenges.
