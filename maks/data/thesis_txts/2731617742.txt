Project Formulation
Society is dependent on plastic materials to support the growing population. The most recent projection
of the world’s annual plastics production indicates that in 2050, 20% of the annual oil production should
be used to prepare plastic materials. This underlines the need to ﬁnd alternative sources of raw materials
for preparation of the polymers we need. As part of the EU project UPLIFT, we are working with the
use of 4-hydroxyphenylacetic acid (HPA) as a bio-based monomer that can be produced by fermentation
from waste feedstock. This monomer has the advantage that it is aromatic and contains both an alcohol
(phenol) and a carboxylic acid, which makes it an A-B monomer. Phenols are complicated to polymerize,
and are therefore often derivatized before polymerization in order to suppress the challenges of sidere-
actions through radical coupling and oxidation and this project therefore aims to identify and evaluate
diﬀerent pathways for polymerization of derivatized HPA.
The objective of this project is to investigate methods for polymerization of HPA or 4-hydroxybenzoic
acid (HBA) derivatives. Depending on synthetic possibilities, a secondary objective will be to correlate
the eﬀect of employing a homologous series of diols for the polymerization and to investigate the result-
ing hydrolytic stability of the polymers. The project should be initiated with a literature study into
the synthetic strategies applied for polymerization of HPA and HBA as well as their derivatives. This
could with advantage be supplemented with naturally occurring phenolic compounds such as e.g. vanillin
derivatives[1].
Experimentally, the focus in the project will be on preparation of derivatives and on evaluating their eﬃ-
ciency in polymerizations. Initially the target should be on higher molar mass polymers to achieve stable
properties (Tg, tensile strength/E-modulus). Depending on the success of the polymerizations, lower
molar mass polymers can be exploited for preparation of blockcopolymers with PLA to make superior
additives for PLA. All the prepared polymers should be fully characterized using classical techniques such
as IR, 1H-NMR, SEC, DSC and TGA. After preparation the diﬀerent homologues should be evaluated
using at least chemical hydrolysis to determine their recyclability by solvolysis processes. This could also
be supplemented by a biological degradation evaluation in the context of the UPLIFT project, however,
this is most likely not feasible to complete within the time frame of the project.

Abstract
The ambition of my thesis was to make a polymer based on HPA , as part of ’project UPLIFT’. The
reason being that HPA can be made from biodegraded plastic. Project uplift takes a new look at the
entire plastic chain. Recycled plastic is broken down with the help of bacteria, which created, among
other things, HPA. Uplift was started to increase the recyclability of plastics, since recycled plastic today
is very often burned, or thrown in a landﬁll.
Before this project, it was already known that polymers could be made from analogous to HPA, and that
low molecular weight polymers can be made from pure HPA. It was also known that the phenol in HPA
was most likely the problem, when trying to increase the molecular weight.
However, beforehand it was not known beforehand if the phenol could be worked around, to create high
molecular weight polymers. A high molecular weight polymer was desired, because it was theorised that
it would increase the Tg of the polymer. It was also unknown how well a blockcoplymer of HPA with
diﬀerent polymers would perform.
In this project I have made multiple diﬀerent polymers, of both HPA and HPA, with an ethylene glycol
added to the phenol group. This gave p(HPA) and and p(HEPA), respectively. The molecular weight was
obtained by multiple diﬀerent conﬂicting methods, which revealed weights of up to potentially 67 kg/mol.
Getting high molecular weights is very important, when creating polymers, because the properties of the
polymers are dependent on the molecular weight.
Furthermore, it is shown in this project that it is possible to blockcopolymerise p(HEPA) with PLA.
This increases the thermal degradation point (Td) of the PLA, and reduces the hydrolytic stability of the
polymer. The reduced hydrolytic stability of the polymer is a clear advantage for recycling the polymer.
It reduces the degradation time considerably, especially in a commercial setting. Furthermore, it allows
for the seperation of the two polymers, in as little as 2 hours. The experiments in this project have
thus increased the knowlegede about synthesising p(HEPA). The next could be looking at mechanical
properties of the synthesised polymers.

Introduction
The amount of plastic used in the world is growing. Since the 1970s, the usage of plastic has grown
faster than any other material [2, p 15]. If the growth trend continues, 19% of the world’s fossil fuels will
be used for plastic in 2040. This does not necessarily by itself constitute a problem. If we managed to
recycle plastic eﬃciently, and did not increase the overall consumption in fossil fuels, we would be less
challenged. However, that is not the scenario we presently have. Today, 98% of single use plastic is made
from fossil fuels, not from recycling [2, pp 99-100].
This is an alarming statistic, for multiple reasons. Primarily because, it is a major source of pollution
and devastating for the environment. Moreover, it makes all of the waste sorting seem pointless. Why are
we spending so much time and resources sorting waste, when it is not being recycled suﬃciently anyway?
A fair question. One might point out, however, that the statistic only refers to single use plastic. After
all, we are in the process of banning still more single use plastics, from straws to plastic bags, so maybe
it is not so alarming that single use plastic is not being reused.
Sadly, the low recycling rate does not only apply for single use plastic. This means that out of all plastic
being produced, only 7% of it is being recycled [2, pp 100-101]. Out of those 7%, 98% is downcycled.
The remaining 2% is recycled in a closed loop, i.e., it is recycled to plastic of the same quality. Which
means that out of all plastic, only 0.14% is recycled into plastic of equal quality.
On the bright side, bio-sourced and bio-based plastics are on the rise. In 2018, 2.11 metric tons of bio-
plastics were produced. This sounds like a lot, and it is in fact 4 times as much as the amount of plastic
being recycled in a closed loop. Unfortunately, it only accounts for 0.6% of all plastic, because so little
plastic is actually recycled in closed loops. However, it is important to distinguish between bio-based
plastics, and recycled plastics. Both are positive from an environmental point of view, but they have
very diﬀerent characteristics. Bio-based plastic come from renewable feedstock, but it is usually harder
to recycle than conventionally produced plastic.
To make matters worse, competition is intense on the feedstock of bio-based plastics. All industries want
to brand themselves as green, and there is only so much cheap feedstock available [3]. So far, the market
for renewable energy has been much larger than the market for bio-based plastics. This means, there has
been less research into bio-based plastics than there otherwise could have been - the economic incentive
just has not been there until now.
This thesis is being made as part of the EU project UPLIFT, and is looking at using 4-hydroxypheny-
lacetic acid (HPA) as a bio-based monomer. This is done because, HPA can be created from fermentation
of waste plastic, and is therefore potentially in less competition with renewable energy. HPA has both
an alcohol group and an acid group, which makes it an A-B monomer that should be able to polymerise
with itself. However, phenols are complicated, and therefore often derivatised before polymerisation.
The literature study for this project is looking at the potential polymerisation of HPA, as well as similar
compounds such as 4-hydroxybenzoic acid (HBA) and Vanillin. Most of the possible polymers created
from this are polyesters, with a couple of exceptions. This includes both direct polymerisations, and
polymers of HPA derivatives.
Experimentally, the project will look at synthesising derivatives of HPA, and polymerising these. The
project focuses on p(HPA) derivatives, to avoid the phenol in HPA as much as possible. Depending on
the weight of the synthesised polymers, it might be used for blockcopolymers with PLA, which hopefully
enhances the properties of PLA. All prepared polymers will be characterized using IR, 1H-NMR, SEC,
DSC and TGA. After the analysis, the degradation of the diﬀerent polymers will be studied, with the
eventual goal of determining their recyclability by chemical hydrolysis. Chemical hydrolysis is interesting,
since it potentially enables a full LCA of the synthesised polymer. It could be relevant and to supplement
with a study on biological degradation in the context of the UPLIFT project, however, already at the
outset, it was not considered feasible to complete within the time frame of the project.

2.1
Green chemistry
One of the important elements of project UPLIFT is to upcycle polymers, and thereby reduce the en-
vironmental impact. This has often been called green chemistry. A driving force in green chemistry is
the strive to reduce the amount of petroleum based chemicals in use, for a multitude of reasons, as just
described. Petroleum chemistry is not sustainable in the long run, and harmful for the environment.
Green chemistry is – at the moment – potentially more expensive, but it is healthier for the environment,
and everyone involved.
Green chemistry was originally deﬁned by Paul Anastas and John Warner in their 1998 book ”Green
Chemistry: Theory and Practice” [4]. They deﬁne 12 principles of green chemistry. These principles are:
1. Prevention
2. Atom Economy
3. Less Hazardous Chemical Syntheses
4. Designing Safer Chemicals
5. Safer Solvents and Auxiliaries
6. Design for Energy Eﬃciency
7. Use of Renewable Feedstocks
8. Reduce Derivatives
9. Catalysis
10. Design for Degradation
11. Real-time analysis for Pollution Prevention
12. Inherently Safer Chemistry for Accident Prevention
These principles can be used to guide the focus in green chemistry. In the following, I unfold the principles
that will be most important for the thesis.
The best, of course, would be to follow principle 1; to eliminate harmful chemistry all together. Thus,
principle 1 can be seen as an overarching principle.
The most optimal scenario would be, if we can
completely prevent the harmful processes that are found in conventional chemistry, and the remaining
principles attempt to guide us in that direction.
Atom economy is also worth explaining. The purpose of atom economy is to minimise the waste amount
created in a reaction. The more of our starting materials we can use in the reaction itself, the greener
the reaction. In polymer chemistry, ’wasted’ atoms will usually be in the form of halides or protecting
groups. There is also the solvent from the reaction, which will go to waste, if it is not reused. However,
it is possible to run reactions completely without solvent. This is either done by using one of the starting
materials as solvent, or by melting the polymer and running the reaction as a melt. However, if one
starting material is in large excess, then the end result is the same as using a solvent. Either the solvent
gets reused, or it gets wasted.
Use of renewable feedstock is a subject under continuous research. One of the most common compounds
to look at, is vanillin, since it can be used as a model for reactions with lignin. Vanillin shares many
similarities with HPA, and some of the chemistry is therefore predicted to be similar. Vanillin has been
studied considerably more than HPA (13 564 vs 1 355 articles on DTU ﬁndit, for vanillin vs 4-hydrox-
yphenylacetic acid). In this context, both vanillin and HPA are renewable feedstock, with vanillin mainly
coming from lignin, and HPA coming from fermentation oﬀwaste plastic. Vanillin chemistry is explored
further in section 2.5.

The reduce derivatives step is really just an eﬀort to reduce the amount of steps in the synthesis. If a
reaction can be done in one step with 75% yield, it’s more eﬃcient than a 90% protecting step, a 90%
reaction step and a 90% deprotecting step. So, eliminating additional steps, especially protection groups
can greatly increase yield, and therefore reduce the environmental impact.
In polymer chemistry, it is rarely a question of whether to use a catalyst, but which catalyst to use.
Choosing the right catalyst does not only mean a diﬀerence for the environment, it can also change the
product of the reaction, and determine if the reaction runs at all.

2.2
Polyester
HPA has both an acid group and an alcohol, so when synthesising a polymer from HPA, the immedi-
ate thought would be a polyester. The most common commercial polyester ispolyethylene terephthalate
(PET). This makes PET a natural molecule to compare with, when making a polymer from HPA. PET
is a widely used chemical, with a lot of interesting properties.
PET has a glass transition temperature (Tg) of between 69◦C and 85◦C [6]. The glass transition temper-
ature is the temperature at which the material transforms from a relatively hard state, into a viscous or
rubbery state. The variation in Tg depends on the crystallinity of the PET. More crystalline PET has a
higher Tg, but is also less useful as a material, due to reduced ﬂexibility. Therefore, if we could raise the
Tg from 70◦of amorphous PET to a higher temperature, it would be very useful. If it could be raised
higher than 100◦C, it would be even better. However, any increase in Tg would be beneﬁcial.
PET is used mainly for textiles and plastic bottles. In both of these applications, the material regularly
comes into contact with water. Therefore, if we could raise the Tg to a temperature, where boiling water
no longer ruins the material, the usefulness of the polymer would increase drastically. It would enable
more methods for sanitising the material, and it would enable the use of polyester for hot liquids, such
as tea or coﬀee.
Sadly, PET has some serious disadvantages. The main problem is that it is synthesised from crude oil,
or natural gas [7]. Crude oil is a chemically viable feedstock, since it has fewer impurities than bio-based
feedstock, but it is not sustainable for the environment.

There has also been a couple of published studies on the polymerisation of pure HPA. These papers use
electrochemistry to create the polymer, instead of a more conventional polycondensation.
2.3.1
Electro polymerisation of HPA
Polymerisation with electrochemistry, or electroplating has been done by Silva et al. [5], Ferreira et al.
[11] and Matos et al. [12]. Electroplating actually works very well to make polymers, but it is challenged
by a large problem; the polymer grafts onto a graphite electrode. This can be useful for immobilising
bio molecules in the polymer. Immobilising these molecules allows for functionalised surfaces, with high
customisability. However, outside of this speciﬁc purpose, it is not very useful for the polymer to be
grafted to a graphite surface. The polymer cannot be separated from the graphite electrode, and thus
cannot be used outside of this limited purpose.
Aside from electropolymerisation there has not been done a lot of work to polymerise HPA by itself. It
is possible to make co- or terpolymers with HPA, which is being explored further in section 2.4.1. One
group has also been able to decarboxylate HPA. Decarboxylation of HPA would create p-cresol, which
is then polymerisable. Harrison et al. [13] has done this decarboxylation, but they have done it using
bacteria, which is outside the scope of this project.
Due to the limited amount of research into polymerising pure HPA, I have looked at analogues to HPA,
such as HBA and vanillin, which have been polymerised. This is done to study additional ways HPA
might be polymerised.

the melting point and the crystalline point. Prasad and Pillai [18] measured a melting point for one of
their LCPs at 260◦C, and when they cooled it back down, it began solidifying at 218◦. LCPs can have
diﬀerent types of liquid crystalline structures and in this case, it was a semi ordered structure called a
schlieren nematic texture.

was a melt polycondensation/transesteriﬁcation, where the monomers were heated with PET for 5 h, at
280◦C, with excess acetic acid. This gave polymers of undetermined weight, with Tg of between 70◦C and
85◦C. This shows that polymers, that have LCP properties, does not always have higher Tg temperatures.

2.5
Vanillin
Vanillin is both chemically close to HPA, and it is used as a bio-based monomer. Vanillin can be cre-
ated directly from lignin [20], and since a considerable portion of wood is made up of lignin, there is
an abundance of lignin. This has prompted much research into the chemistry of lignin, since it is a
renewable organic aromatic compound, which, one would argue, has to be useful for something. Some
of that research has been into polymerisation. The trouble is that the phenol group has a tendency to
oxidise. When the phenol group oxidises, it stops the polymerisation at low Mw, and potentially creates
unwanted side products [8].
Vanillin exists both in an aldehyde form as vanillin, and an acid form as vanillic acid. If we want to make
a polyester, we are interested in keeping the acid group, instead of working on an aldehyde. On the other
hand, the reactions of vanillin might be close to a protected acid.
One of the ways to create a polymer from vanillin is to functionalise the phenol group, to prevent it from
oxidising. Mankar et al. [1] have used ethylene carbonate and vanillin to create a nice AB monomer. They
ﬁrst reacted pentaerythritol with vanillin to create ’Spiro-bisphenol’, which they then further reacted with
ethylene carbonate to create ’Spiro-diol V’, see Scheme 5. Afterwards, they made a polymerisation with
either dibutyltin(IV) oxide (DBTO) or titanium(IV) butoxide (TBT) as catalyst.
The polymerisation reactions were run at relatively mild conditions. For the etheriﬁcation reaction specif-
ically, they did not use any toxic compounds, and they did not even use a halogen, as is usual with a
Williamson reaction. They carried out the reaction at 160◦C in dimethylformamide (DMF), and managed
to add the diol selectively to the phenol group, with a 90% yield after 2 hours. The speciﬁcity is crucial,
because if the reaction is not speciﬁc, we would just create a polyethylene glycol.
Importantly, the reaction does not seem to depend on the pentaerythritol, since it has also been reported
to react with phenols that are attached to lignin groups instead [21].

2.7
Environmental impact
It is also very interesting to look at the environmental impact of this polymer, and the possible degrada-
tion. Knowledge of degradation enables a full LCA, when looking at a slightly longer time frame than this
thesis. If we’re extending the polymer with PLA, it is also important to know the degradation patterns of
PLA. There is no study on degradation of p(HPA), but there has been studies on PLA, and HBA. PLA
is characterised by the fact that it degrades in water, if the Mw is low enough. When the Mw increases,
the harsher the conditions need to be for degradation [32].
At very large molecular weights, PLA degrades faster internally than on the surface.
This has the
unfortunate side-eﬀect that the perceived quality of the polymer can be higher than the actual quality,
and then sudden degradation occurs. It should also be noted that the article started the degradation at
a certain pH, and then just measured it afterwards without interfering with the system. This resulted
in the pH settling at 3.4, after 100 minutes of degradation, in all cases. Thus, the degradation might be
sped up considerably by continuous addition of base, instead of just monitoring the system.

Synthesis plan
The synthesis of HPA polymers will for this project be performed on commercially bought HPA. It is
used as a substitute for HPA produced by fermentation, in order to ensure that the procedure works. The
overall plan is illustrated in Scheme 13. The plan is divided into 4 primary reactions, and an optional
reaction. The ﬁrst reaction (A) is a protection of the acid group on HPA. This is necessary, because
otherwise the acid group will react in a similar fashion to the phenol group. The protection is being done
analogously to [10], which should give methyl 2-(4-hydroxyphenyl)acetate (Me-HPA).
In the second reaction (B) Me-HPA, is reacted with ethylene carbonate, analogously to [1], which should
give a glycol spacer on the phenol group.

4.2.1
Upscaling and puriﬁcation
If we want to create the polymer outside of the laboratory, we need to upscale the polymer. This was done
on a 50g scale. Upscaling from mg scale is usually associated with a higher yield of monomer, because the
losses that are associated with spatulas, and liquid left in the ﬂaks get smaller percentage-wise. However,
when the monomer was upscaled, this was not observed.
There is a couple of potential explanations for this. The ﬁrst explanation is user error. A large amount
of monomer got stuck in MgSO4, which was used as a drying agent. If the MgSO4 had been rinsed
thoroughly with a solvent, such as dry dichloromethane, which could subsequently have been removed,
the yield would most likely increase.
Another possible explanation is that a decent amount of the monomer is stuck in the aqueous phase.
During the work up of the monomer, it is washed with water to remove sulphuric acid. The monomer
is slightly water soluble, and therefore a signiﬁcant amount of monomer might have transferred to the
aqueous phase. This can be salvaged by washing the aqueous phase with dichloromethane, which would
extract most of the product back into the organic phase.
Neither of these options were attempted during the process, because the focus of the project was on the
creation of a polymer, not on the optimisation of the creation of the monomer.

The ﬁrst option of the end group boiling oﬀcan be disproven by the IR spectrum. In the IR spectrum we
would see an acid peak, akin to the one in Figure 4, if we indeed had HEPA. We could technically have
a situation where the end group boiled oﬀ, and an ethylene carbonate attached instead. In that case the
integral of the C6 and C7 peaks would be considerably higher. Furthermore, a spectrum of HEPA should
have three sharp peaks, instead of three broad peaks. Therefore the best explanation is that the com-
pound has oligomerised. To ﬁnd the length of the oligomer, we look at the relative integrals. This can be
done in two diﬀerent ways. We can either say that we believe that the end group does not boil oﬀ, and is
present at a slightly higher ppm than C9, and thus the integral of the C9 peak can be measured against the
integral of the aromatic peak. Alternatively, we can integrate the entire 3.7-4.6 ppm range, since we know
that will integrate to 9 in the pure monomer, and then compare that value to the integral of the aromatics.
There is advantages with using both methods. The advantage of integrating a speciﬁc peak is that we
don’t accidentally include something in the integral that we should not include. There might be a signal
from the terminal alcohol that is too weak to give a distinct peak, but still contributes to the integral. On
the other hand, the integration of a speciﬁc peak only works if there is a conﬁrmation of the end group
being present. If we integrate the entire area, we are certain to ﬁnd the end group. Furthermore, we can
also ﬁnd a potential glycol end group, which is the most likely alternative end group, if the methyl has
boiled oﬀ. We would not be able to ﬁnd an acid end group in this area, but we have already established
that we don’t have an acid in this compound.
Integrating the peak at 3.75 ppm (C12+C9) gives an integral of 2.58. Comparing this to the integral of
the aromatic peak means we have an oligomer with 6 repeating units, which corresponds to a molecular
weight of 1100 g/mol. If we instead integrate the entire area, we get an integral of 6.99, which corresponds
to an oligomer with between 3 and 6 repeating units, or a molecular weight of 566 g/mol-1350 g/mol. If
the only thing in the area is the methyl end group, we have an oligomer with 3 repeating units. If we
have an additional glycol group, and two terminal alcohols, we have an oligomer with 6 repeating units,
terminated by a glycol, and thus a weight of 1350 g/mol. The possible variants can be seen in Figure
reﬃg:Vari. Determining exactly which of these oligomers is formed requires spending a lot more time on
NMR spectra. This is done in Section 4.4.1.

is extracted from DMF, it is done by crashing out the product using very dilute NaOH. This gives a
ﬁne white crystalline powder, which is then isolated by ﬁltration, and washed with water, to remove any

version that can be seen in Scheme 15b. In this version no solvent was added, which has two advantages.

solvent, which allows a larger range of temperatures to be used. The other advantage is that it is not
necessary to remove the solvent after creating the monomer. This is a double-edged sword however. It
removes a step, but that cleaning step might also help to remove impurities and side products. How-
ever, it has a considerably higher yield, and the NMR looks identical to the cleaned monomer, so it is
deemed the best option. The NMR spectra of polymers made with both versions can be found in ﬁgure 10.
This version is slightly diﬀerent than the one-pot precursor mentioned in the synthesis plan. This devia-
tion comes from adding the K2CO3 in one step, and then the SnCl2 in a step after. The change has been
made because of sublimation of the monomer. If too high temperature or too low pressure, is present the
monomer has a tendency to sublimate, which makes the yield very unpredictable. To help combat this,
the etheriﬁcation is made ﬁrst, which makes an oligomer of the product. This oligomer sublimates less,
which should raise the predictability of the reaction.

Chemical changes. The compound can be slightly changed in multiple ways. If the reaction between
EC and the phenol does not go to completion, the integral ratio will be wrong. In Figure 18, the ratio
is 4:5.5, which is obviously wrong, but if the reaction goes almost to completion, it can be more subtle
than that.
Furthermore, the methanol end group can boil oﬀ. The end group goes oﬀwhen the polymerisation is
happening, as part of the polycondensation, but it can all go oﬀ, without extending the chain. A good
example of this is the top spectrum in Figure 10. Here the result is an almost pure monomer, but the
integral of the 3.8 ppm peak, which corresponds both to 9 and 12, is 2.07. This means that 98% of the
methanol has boiled oﬀ, not that we have a polymer with n=43. If we had a polymer with n=43, the
rest of the spectrum would not have any sharp peaks remaining.
Chemical impurities. The main impurity in the NMR spectra is from ethylene carbonate. It shows up
around 4.2 ppm, which is in the middle of our non-aromatic area. A good example is the top spectrum in
Figure 10. There is a peak that integrates to 0.34, which most likely corresponds to remaining starting
material. The oligomerisation is ran with excess EC, which is then removed at high temperatures, under
vacuum. If the removal is not completely successful, it will show up in the spectrum, and change the
integral. This is very visible in the bottom spectrum in Figure 10, where the integral ratio is 4:9.95, but
the aromatic area shows up as a polymer.
Resolution. The resolution of the machine is only so good. If we had inﬁnite resolution, we would
be able to separate every single peak, and from that be able to assign all peaks, and in that manner
tell the molecular weight. Since the spectra are run on an 80 MHz machine, that is not the case. A
lot of the peaks melt together, which in some cases make it hard to get the exact integrals. We know
the exact position of the 12, in the polymer, from HSQC, but we cannot reliably get the integral of the
3.90-3.95ppm with no overlap from neighbouring signals.
Post processing. Most of the NMR spectra are taken in a non deuterated TFA. This is mostly due to
the price of TFA, but it has some drawbacks. The ﬁrst drawback is intensity. If there is overlap with the
sample, it becomes impossible to see the sample. This is luckily not the case for TFA, but it does make
it impossible to take 1H-NMR spectra in HFIP.
The second reason is phase shifting, which is slightly harder to see. When the spectrum is run, the NMR
is manually phase shifted, to ensure as good a baseline as possible. Since this phase shifting is manual, it
can deviate slightly between spectra. A slight deviation is usually not a problem, but just as the amount
of scans, it can lead to the diﬀerence in an integral ratio of 4:6.15 in Figure 16, and 4:6.04 in Figure B.9.
These problems shows that for p(HEPA) 80 MHz 1H-NMR, is useful on a relative scale, but cannot tell

The molecular weight is nice to have from a theoretical point of view, nevertheless, the usefulness is
determined through mechanical testing. In this project, DSC, TGA, tensile tests, and hydrolysis has
been run on the samples. The DSC and TGA data are very useful, and can help us discard polymers

Liquid crystalline properties were expected from the literature review, but there was no indication of this

also very useful when comparing to the data in Section 4.8.1.
4.9.1
Hydrolytic degradation
Hydrolytic degradation is an important way to degrade polymers. It helps us understand if and how the
polymer degrades. This is both in terms of degradation time, and what the resulting molecules look like.
With this information it is possible to analyse the full life of the polymer, and truly create a circular
system.
Hydrolytic degradation of samples was tested on p(HEPA)-185, and compared to a commercial sample of
PET. The PET sample was taken from a water bottle. Each sample was fully submerged in liquid, and
the weight of the sample was measured after a given time interval. The solution was a 0.4wt% NaOH
(0.1M), in either pure water, or 50/50 water/THF.
This gave some very promising results, see Figure 26. The commercial sample swelled after half an hour,
and stayed at a close to constant weight for the remaining time period. The p(HEPA) samples also
swelled after half an hour, but then started to degrade. After 70 h, the p(HEPA) in THF was powder,
with the remaining weight being caught by a ﬁlter paper.

5
Conclusion
The purpose of this project is to investigate diﬀerent methods to properly synthesise a polymer from
HPA. The three goals for the project was to create a high Mw polymer, to create a polymer with a high
Tg, and to create a viable blockcopolymer with PLA.
One of the starting assumptions was that the phenol would be a problem in the polymerisation of the
Polymer. In regard to this, the project has clearly shown that the phenol is a very important part of the
polymer. If the phenol is subjected to the wrong conditions, it will oxidise, potentially irreversibly. For
the polymer, this means that the polymerisation reaction stops. If the phenol is oxidised it is impossible
to polycondensate further, which limits the molecular weight.
The importance of the phenol was further revealed by the colour of the polymer. Having a higher con-
version rate in the reaction with the phenol, directly correlates to having a lighter colour. The colour
is important both for the visual presentation of the polymer, and it’s an indication for how oxidised the
phenol is. The project then showed that the phenol can be derivatised in multiple diﬀerent ways, that
allow for polymerisation. This includes both polymerisation to p(HPA) and a version, where an ethylene
glycol was reacted with the phenol before polymerisation, which created p(HEPA).
The p(HEPA) was optimised for molecular weight. First an upper limit for the temperature was fond The
optimal method turned out to be a slow temperature ramp, balancing polymerisation time and thermal
stability of the polymer. If the temperature is increased too much too quickly the polymer degrades,
and if the temperature is not raised, the reaction proceeds very slowly. The reaction still takes 4 days of
polymerisation time. The ﬁnal polymer reached weights of at least 67 kg/mol. This weight was calculated
using DOSY spectroscopy in HFIP. HFIP was used, because DOSY spectroscopy showed a signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent molecular weight using TFA as solvent, vs using HFIP. This goes against conventional wisdom
of polyester being stable in TFA.
All created polymers were fully characterised with the help of multiple diﬀerent kinds of NMR spec-
troscopy, as well as IR, DSC and TGA. This has allowed for determining the exact composition of the
polymer. It has also made it possible to systemically determine the best method for creating the polymer,
since it allowed for continuous reaction monitoring.
The second goal of creating a polymer with a high Tg was was not achieved during this project. It might
be possible to push p(HEPA) to a high Tg, but even when the polymer cross-linked the Tg did not exceed
the desired value. The tensile strength of p(HEPA) was not investigated in this project. This was due
to the brittle nature of the polymer. If higher molecular weights were achieved, it should be possible to
measure more mechanical properties of the synthesised p(HEPA).
Fianally, it has been shown in the project that it is possible to create a viable blockcopolymer with PLA.
The ideal polymerisation temperature of PLA coincides with the melting point of p(HEPA), which makes
for a very simple synthesis of the blockcopolymer.
The p(HEPA)-Block-p(LA) shows better thermal
stability than virgin PLA, but a lower glass transition temperature. That makes p(HEPA) an excellent
polymer to block polymerise with PLA.
Both the synthesised p(HEPA) aswell as the blockcopolymer has been shown to degrade fast, in a weakly
alkaline THF/Water mixture. For the blockcopolymer the degradation gives a nice separation of the two
diﬀerent polymers. The PLA stays as a very ﬁne powder, which slowly degrades. The p(HEPA) instead
degrades, and dissolves. The degradation product is not well deﬁned, but it does contain HPA as a major
product.
All in all the thesis has shown that it is possible to polymerise HPA, to high molecular weights. This is
done by creating a temperature ramp, which controls both time and temperature of the polymerisation.
It has also been presented that those polymers can be blockcopolymerised with PLA. In the future it
would be interesting to look at longer polymerisation times, and to do more mechanical testing on both
p(HEPA) and p(HEPA)-Block-p(LA). This includes measuring the weight of p(HEPA)-Block-p(LA) with
DOSY, but also strain testing, and potential further upscaling.

General materials and methods
4-Hydroxyphenylacetic acid (HPA, >99.0%), Ethylene carbonate, (EC, 98%), methanol (General labo-

peak as reference. Solvent suppression was used on samples with DCM. For samples without DCM, the
CH2 group in HPA was set to a value of 3.8ppm. For the 400 MHz spectra, TFA-d and HFIP were used
as solvents, and the residual solvent peak was used as a reference. The molecular weight of the polymers
was determined by various methods, see section 4.8. NMR samples were analysed in MestReNova 14.2.0.

and in 1H-NMR, the hydrogen on the C or O have been reported. NMR has been reported on the form:

6.7
Hydrolytic degradation
A weighted amount of sample was submerged in solvent. The solvent was either 0.1M NaOH in water,
or 0.1M NaOH in 50/50 water/THF. The weight of the sample was measured by removing it from the
liquid with pliers, and drying it oﬀwith a paper towel. The pH was measured with pH strips, by wetting
them with water, and subsequently adding a drop of the solution to the strip. This was done instead
of using analytical equipment, since it was not a pure aqueous solution. The pH was stable even at 120
hours, for pure p(HEPA) reactions, so a buﬀer solution was not used for any of the experiments. Stirring
was added in two diﬀerent ways. The ﬁrst way was through rotation, where the vial was placed on top
of a magnet. This gave a gentle rotation of the vial. The second way was with a magnet in the vial. The
magnet was spun at 600 rpm, which gave a more vigorous mixing of the vial.
In the experiments where 0.1M NaOH in 50/50 water/THF was heated to 50◦C, a phase separation was
observed This phase separation was only observed at elevated temperatures, and was very stable towards
rotation. For the full dataset see Tables E.1-E.5.
