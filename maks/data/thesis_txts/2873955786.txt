Abstract
The microperforated membrane is a class of absorbing materials used for noise control
in room acoustics, where the absorption arises in the sub­millimeter perforations that are
characteristic of the material. Their design is flexible and varied, as they do not rely on
specific materials or manufacturing processes. In this theses, a microperforated polyester
textile is characterised, and the perforations are modelled with 2 fundamentally different
approaches: direct measurement of the macroscopic flow resistance vs. prediction of the
perforation impedance based on detailed modelling of the geometry. An image segmen­
tation approach is used to estimate the geometric parameters. The vibration of the textile
is also modelled, accounting for size and shape, tension and damping. Good correspon­
dance with impedance tube and reverberation chamber measurements is obtained for
models using flow resistance. The geometrical approach, the widely used Maa’s model,
which assumes uniform cylindrical perforations, has good correspondance with measure­
ments at low frequency, though it overestimates the inertia of the air mass in the perfora­
tions, leading to discrepancies at high frequencies.

1
Introduction
Microperforated membranes (MPM), interchangably reffered to as microperforated plates
(MPP), are a type of absorber characterised by having perforations in the sub­millimeter
range. The narrow perforations give rise to high viscous losses, allowing for good ab­
sorption to be obtained without additional porous backing, as is typically used with macro­
perforated plates.
Because the absorption arises from the geometry of the perforations, MPPs are very
flexible in their design. By adjusting the many parameters of the perforation geometry,
they can be tuned to specific applications. Furthermore, they can be manufactured from
a wide range of materials: in a famous example, absorbers made of transparant acrylic
with laser­cut perforations were installed in the Deutscher Bundestag[1]. They could also
be made from metal for use in industrial settings. Often, the thickness of MPPs are less
than a millimeter, as it should be comparable to the diameter of the holes. Along with the
flexibility in material choice and manufacturing processes of the perforations, this means
that MPPs can be manufactured sustainably in terms of energy and raw material resource
use. They could even be made from agricultural waste.
In this thesis, a textile­type microperforated membrane which is made from knitted polyester
fibres is examined. It is characterised in terms of the material parameters: surface den­
sity, thickness, airflow resistance as well as perforation radius and perforation ratio. An
image segmentation approach is used to measure the perforation radius and ratio.
Two groups of analytical impedance models are used to predict the impedance and ab­
sorption of the material. One group uses the airflow resistance to model the impedance
due to the perforations. The other group of models predict the impedance of the perfo­
rations based on the geometrical paramters (thickness and perforation radius and ratio).
Within the groups, the models differ in how they predict the impedance of the solid part of
the material (the ’membrane’ or ’frame’). Either they assume a rigid frame, a simple mass
inertia, or a tensioned membrane of finite size and circular shape.
The model predictions are compared to impedance tube measurements of multiple sam­
ples with different characteristics. The effect of porous material in the backing cavity, and
its position in the cavity, is also investigated by simulation and impedance tube measure­
ments.
Finally the models are extended to oblique incidence under the assumptions of local re­
action of the membrane and a bulk reacting cavity, and the predicted random incidence
absorption is compared to reverberation chamber measurements.

When the cavity reactance, which oscillates between −∞and +∞as according to the
cotangent function, is negative and comparable to the inertia of the MPP, they cancel out
and the reactance becomes small, causing a peak in the absorption. However when it is

in the absorption. For the permeable layer, both the negative and positive phase of the
cavity diminish the absorption, as there is no inertia to counteract it.

controlled by the cavity. In the region where the cotangent function crosses 0, the reac­
tance becomes small and the surface impedance is dominated by the resistive part of the
MPP, r, and the absorption peaks. Thus the resonance frequency f0 at which the ab­
sorption peak occurs controlled by the depth the cavity, while the higher frequency limit of
absorption is limited by the MPPs inertia. Note that fu of the surface impedance is slightly
lower than fc which was found for the slab impedance of the MPP, because the cavity
reactance is positive for about 200 < f < 800. Then it adds together with the inertia of
the MPP, so that the reactance surpasses r at a lower frequency. If the cavity reactance
was negative near fc, then fu could be at a higher frequency. So the cavity might slightly
alter the upper break frequency, but it will be close to fc.

2.6
Flexible membrane
While Maa’s model discussed in section 2.5 assumes the plate/membrane to be rigid, in
reality it is often be flexible. An early example is seen in a study by Lee & Swenson, where
additional resonances not explained by Maa’s model were measured, and they could
confirm that they were due to structural vibrations, as they were attenuated by adding ribs
to stiffen the structure [18]. Bravo et al. showed the mode shapes by combining laser
vibrometer measurements with impedance tube[19]. Kang & Fuchs proposed a model
that combines Maa’s model with the impedance of an infinite membrane absorber[20]. A
model proposed by Li et al. builds on Maa’s model in a different way, taking account of
modal resonances of a finite circular membrane[21].

4.1
Impedance tube measurement setup
The normal incidence absorption coefficient was measured with the two­microphone tech­
nique, as descibed in section 2.7 and ISO 10534­2[27], with an AED 1000 ­ Acoustitube®
Type 2 impedance tube, which has a diameter of 100 mm. The measurement setup is
pictured in fig. 4.1.
The Type 2 tube is reported to have a frequency range of 50 Hz to 2000 Hz[35]. A mi­
crophone spacing of 75 mm was used, and the frequency limits due to the spacing are,
as according to eqs. (2.107) and (2.108), 68.6 Hz for the lower limit and 2058 Hz for the
upper limit. Thus the measurement range is 68.6 Hz to 2000 Hz.
Figure 4.1: Impedance tube setup.
An inner rim was 3D­printed in order to mount the textile in the impedance tube with a
clamped boundary. The principle is illustrated in fig. 4.2a, where the outer rim is the
sample holder of the impedance tube, and the inner rim is the printed part.
The design of the inner rim is seen in fig. 4.2b. It was designed to a cylindrical shape with
a length of 50 mm and an outer diameter of 99.5 mm, leaving a clearance of 0.5 mm for
the membrane to be squeezed in. The length of the cylinder gives strength to the part,
and it also increases the impedance that any leakage might see. The cross­section of the
rim is tapered so that it is 1 mm thick at the ends and 5 mm at the center. This is meant to
give the part strength while minimising contact with the face of the sample and minimising
reflections from the changing of the cross­section along the cylinder.
The installation of the sample in the sample holder of the impedance tube is pictured in
fig. 4.3. The region of the sample to be sandwiched between the inner rim and the sample
holder was cut into flaps to avoid excess material bunching up. These flaps could then
be pulled on to apply tension when the sample was installed in the sample holder. The fit
was very snug, so the tension was able to hold throughout the measurements, and there
has likely been no leakage through the clearance between the rims. There was still some
bunching up of the material causing creases to appear along the boundary, which, along
with uneven distribution of tension, might contribute to mode splitting, as we will see in
section 4.2.

Sample A was installed in the impedance tube in the configuration seen in fig. 4.5, with
an air gap of 0.2 m. The measurement coherence is reported in fig. 4.6. The coherence
is good at unity in most of the range, but with lesser dips at about 125 Hz, corresponding
to the first membrane resonance frequency where the real part of the impedance is very
close to 0, and at 857 Hz corresponding to the first cavity resonance.
In fig. 4.7 we see the real and imaginary part of the measured surface impedance. To
avoid the graphs getting too crowded, the models using Maa’s MPP model to represent
the perforations (models of Maa, Kang & Fuchs’, Li et al.) are grouped in graphs (a) and
(b), while the models representing the perforations as permeability given by the measured
flow resistance (rigid permeable layer, limp/infinite permeable membrane, finite tensioned
permeable membrane) are grouped in (c) and (d). The absorption for the MPP models is
seen in fig. 4.8 and fig. 4.9.
As in fig. 3.10, there is good correspondance between the pairs of analogous models
(Maa & Rigid perm, Kang & Limp perm, Li & Finite perm.). At higher frequencies, the
MPP models match the measured peak amplitude of the periodic cavity resonances better,
while the permeability models match the overall shape (bandwidth) better. The differences
are not dramatic, as the measurement range is below the MPP corner frequency, which
was estimated to 2.3 kHz in section 3.4.2.
The predicted cavity resonances are clearly seen in the measurement, with absorption
minima at λ = 2D, D →f = c0/2D, c0/D = 857, 1715 Hz. However, in the absorption
plots we can see that the measurement does not go all the way to 0 as the models do. In
the real part of the surface impedance, we also see large peaks at these frequencies. This
is the result of damping in the cavity, and, similarly to how damping was included in the
membrane models, could be modelled by using a complex wavenumber in the expression
for the cavity impedance.
For the models including tension, the tension and loss factor has been fitted by trial and
error to match the strong first and second resonances visible in the measurement (theo­
retical membrane resonance frequencies are referred to as fn in figure legends).
Because of the different implementations of the loss factor in the tensioned membrane
models as discussed in section 2.6.2, the loss factor has different values. The loss factor is
scaled between the two models so that the damping is equivalent at the second membrane
resonance mode. Since the damping is proportional to frequency in Li’s model, we see
that the modal resonances are less damped below the 2nd resonance, and more damped

the membrane modes have the potential to increase low frequency absorption.
The membrane behaviour clearly has a big impact on the absorption, and thus the models
assuming a rigid MPP/frame greatly overestimate the low frequency absorption. In other
words, the movement of the membrane diminishes the low frequency absorption.

absorption, is a bit higher. This is due to resistive losses in the membrane, which were
neglected but could be included as a parallel resistance to the membrane inertia as men­
tioned in section 2.6.1.

5.1
Position of porous
Let us consider the position of the porous material again in a room acoustics context.
Often, absorbers are installed in ceilings, and so it may be favorable in terms of montage
to rest the porous material directly on the membrane, rather than fixing it to the ceiling
above. As we saw in fig. 4.20, when the porous material and the membrane are touching,
it affects the impedance. Let us consider some simple approaches to including that in the
model. We will not consider any modal coupling, as that is unlikely to be of importance
when the samples are large and the effect of tension is diminished. So let us assume that
there are no vibrations due to tension or bending stiffness.
One consequence of the contact could be that the porous layer blocked the movement of
membrane. Then we would simply have to use one of the rigid models for the membrane
layer. However, the porous material in question is very light, and it has a soft, fuzzy
boundary, so this is unlikely.
Another very simple approach would be to simply add the surface density of the mem­
brane to the surface density of the membrane. The porous material could also add some
frictional losses between the two layers.
The predicted random incidence of these different models are seen in fig. 5.3 for a sample
of 1 m radius. The base model used is the finite tensioned permeable membrane, which
at this size is equivalent to the limp permeable membrane. For the blocked movement,
the rigid permeable layer is used.
Above the first cavity resonance, there is no significant difference between the different
predictions, including when the porous is at the back of the cavity. At low frequencies, all
the predictions favour having the porous right by the membrane, and in fact it is beneficial
if it is impeding its movement, wether by blocking or adding to its mass.

The correspondance between measurement and the permeable membrane predictions
are very good, though there are some minor discrepancies. In particular in the 200 Hz
and 250 Hz 1/3rd octave bands, the measured absorption is about 0.15 higher. This is
likely due to the finite size of the measurement samples, which gives rise to diffraction at
the edges, causing more sound energy to be radiated into the absorber and leading to
an overestimation of the absorption coefficient, especially at low frequencies. This could
be accounted for by considering the radiation impedance of the finite absorber as shown
by Thomasson [36]. There is also a discrepancy in the assumption of random incidence
used for the calculation, and the actual distribution of incidence angles in a room, such
as in the reverberation chamber. As we saw in fig. 5.1, the absorption is very dependant
on incidence angle. A weighting function could be applied to Paris’ formula to change
the distribution of the incidence angles, as was done by Jeong [37]. Preferably, including
these discrepancies in the simulation requires knowledge about the energy density distri­
bution in the measurement room and the position of the samples in this field, as well as
the perimeter of the samples. However, Pieren got decent correspondence between cal­
culation and measurement by using an approximation of a weighting function simulated
by Jeong, and using the radiation impedance of a 3x4 m rectangular plate as given by
Thomasson [32].
As we saw for the impedance tube measurements, the permeable membrane models are
more accurate than the MPP based models, which fall off at high frequencies. Here it is
more apparant than in the impedance tube results, as the measurement range extends
beyond the MPP corner frequency. This indicates that, for the many reasons discussed
in section 3.3, the assumptions about the geometry made in Maa’s model are not valid for
the textile examined in this thesis. Specifically the inertia of the vibrating air columns in
the perforations, as well as the frequency dependant resistance predicted by Maa’s model
does not reflect the measurements well.

5.3
Reflections
Across the many different measurement configurations, the permeable membrane mod­
els proved very accurate in their predictions. For small samples as are measured in the
impedance tube, including the effects of size, tension and damping, the latter two could
be fitted to match the measurements very well. While these parameters are not intrin­
sic to the material (the damping may depend both on intrinsic properties and effects at
the boundary), identifying the resulting resonances informs us that those resonances are
indeed not intrinsic to the material. Then we can use the limp membrane model to under­
stand the intrinsic behaviour of the material and be confident in the results. The tensioned
model could also be used in an inverse characterisation approach, by fitting all the model
parameters with no prior material parameters, so that the intrinsic parameters could be
obtained in spite of the tension.
We also found that the membrane resonances can improve the absorption at normal inci­
dence. This effect is less pronounced at random incidence, and besides, that prediction
relied on the assumption of local reaction. It is likely that near­grazing incidence could
introduce surface waves or cavity modes that could impact the absorption. Nevertheless,
imagine the textile installed in a duct, at the end of a hallway or in a high ceiling, where
the room geometry may favour normal incidence. Then the tension and size may be used
to tune the behaviour of the absorber. It would also be easy to change the model to
represent rectangular membranes for this purpose.
While the MPP approach to modelling permeability didn’t give as good predictions, even
with the applied fitting of the parameters, it should not be dismissed. It gave good predic­
tions at low frequencies where the effect of inertia, as well as end corrections is small. If
only the real part of the low frequency approximation of the MPP impedance were used
to represent the impedance in the full frequency range, the model would be equal to the
simple flow resistance model. And it has the huge advantage that you don’t need to
measure the flow resistance (provided you can accurately estimate the perforation geom­
etry), and thus it is very useful ie. for design purposes, as has been also demonstrated
by Pieren[32]. Besides, the MPP models matched the absorption peaks better in some
measurements, indicating that some inertia is present in the perforations, though not as
much as predicted, since the holes in the samples did not match the assumption of cylin­
drical shape well. Perhaps by changing the end corrections a good estimate of the inertia
could be obtained.

6
Conclusions
Several samples of a microperforated textile were characterised in terms of surface den­
sity, flow resistance, and the geometrical parameters thickness, perforation radius and
perforation ratio. Two groups of analytical impedance models were compared: those that
used the measured flow resistance directly and those that used Maa’s model to estimate
the impedance of the perforations from the geometrical parameters.
Using the measured flow resistance, very good correspondance between predictions and
measurements were obtained.
The models using the geometrical parameters overestimated the impedance, and thus
underestimated the absorption, at higher frequencies. This was a consequence of the
assumptions on the geometry not matching well with the structure of the textile, leading
to a largely overstimated inertia of the air mass in the perforations.
On top of this, image segmentation approach used to estimate the geometrical parameters
was prone to misestimation because of the structure of the textile. The impedance model
was very sensitive to errors in the estimation of the because of the very small size holes.
This was remedied by fitting the geometrical parameters to match the predicted resistance
to the measured flow resistance. This however negates the advantage of the model of
not having to measure the flow resistance.
Maa’s model may nevertheless be useful in the design of new materials. For room acous­
tics modelling, measuring the flow resistance is more solid, and results in a model that is
both simpler and proved more accurate for the textiles measured here.
For both model groups, the inertia of the membrane had significant influence on the ab­
sorption. The effect of size and tension and damping was accurately modelled for both
model groups, but was shown to be insignificant for larger samples, although it is useful
to model the tension in order to understand the results of impedance tube measurements,
and may also have design applications.
