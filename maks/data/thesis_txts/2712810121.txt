Abstract
This thesis demonstrates steps toward a final, commercial design of a fiber-based FOPA
device for application for an OPC or WC device, using a polarization-diversity loop scheme
with a circulator. CE spectra of several different PM-HNLFs have been shown and examined,
but only a 94 m PM-HNLF was implemented in the loop scheme. The relevant fiber and
laser parameters that are paramount to achieving a high CE are identified and quantified in
the aforementioned fiber with a relatively good fit to the relevant theory - both analytical
and numerical. It was found that the largest CE values could be achieved by applying phase
modulation (pump dithering) to the pump field and by going to the dual-pump configuration
FOPA, where one RF tone of the pump dithering improved the CE by 10 dB and going to the
dual-pump scheme improved it by 8 dB. It was also experimentally shown that by using two
different pump separation values in the dual-pump scheme, 5 nm and 9 nm, it was possible to
convert the signal to any idler in the range without needing to consider second-order FWM
or other newly generated, irrelevant fields in the non-degenerate FWM scheme.
A proposal on how a potential WC can be designed and characterized has also been shown,
where the 94 m PM-HNLF had a symmetric square with a range of 40 nm and a 54 m
PM-HNLF had three squares with ranges of 11 nm, 15 nm, and 16 nm, both fibers nearly
completely across the C-band.
Finally, the loop scheme proved to achieve a polarization-dependence of 0.5 dB, before the
SoP of the pump begins to drift, with a maximum CE value of up to -18 dB. It is proposed
that a feedback loop for the pump polarization can be implemented using an oscilloscope
that continuously realigns the PC of the pump, and that pump dithering and a dual-pump
scheme should be implemented to improve the CE.

1. Introduction
In contemporary optical communication systems, there is a constantly exponentially increas-
ing demand for more information and higher information rates, and with an ever-growing
demand for shorter delay times. With this demand, we are approaching the limit of what
is possible within the linear regime. The linear regime is where one can increase the launch
power and expect a linear increase in the possible information that can be transmitted. This
regime is from powers of 0 W up until some point where this expectation of linear increase
does not hold any further. When this happens we venture into the so-called nonlinear regime,
where the launch power into the communication medium, i.e. an optical fiber, becomes so
great in magnitude that nonlinearities arise which introduce large noise terms, hence the
nonlinear regime. The linear regime can be extended by going from a wavelength division
multiplexing (WDM) system to a single-channel one, which can be seen in figure 1.1. Here,
it can also be seen that modern technologies1 can push the nonlinear regime toward higher
launch powers, although not towards infinity. For single-channel systems, the achievable in-
formation per channel is higher, which means that some penalties emerge when multichannel
systems are used. These systems are introduced to achieve an overall higher capacity. In
consequence, a better understanding of this nonlinear limit needs to be understood and pre-
vented. This should be done in such a way that WDM systems can perform even better and
even higher information rates can be achieved by breaking the nonlinear limit [1].
New methods and technologies, such as higher modulation formats, WDM, time-division
multiplexing (TDM), and space division multiplexing (SDM) are improving the overall band-
width and capacity of modern systems [2]. This gain however comes at a cost since spectral
interference between the symbols will take place. A common thing for all of these methods
is that new hardware needs to be deployed to be able to utilize all of the above-mentioned
techniques to their fullest [3, 4]. Because of this, additional methods also need to be de-
ployed to extend and optimize already built systems in a search for better connectivity for
everybody. The pros of this are not only better connection for already built systems, but
this will also reduce costs and materials. One method for realizing this is better control of
the nonlinear effects in the fiber instead of simply just avoiding them by keeping the launch
power low. This can be fulfilled in multiple ways, but in this thesis, we will consider only
one: the optical phase conjugator (OPC). The main focus of this thesis will, however, be on
the application of a wavelength converter (WC), but the physics behind both of these appli-
cations is the four-wave mixing (FWM) process as long as a symmetric pump configuration is

To make the most out of the use of an OPC system, the OPC must be placed symmetrically

implementation. In this thesis, however, a practical investigation is only carried out for the
OPC as a component, and how it will work inside a system will not be investigated.

As for an applicable WC, all communication links today use WDM systems more and more,
and with the undergoing research into how they can be utilized even further, it is natural
to consider how the wavelengths in such a system can be effectively converted.
This is
carried out as a vision with fiber-to-the-home and the use of WDM systems very close to the

Besides this, an efficient way
of changing channels needs to be found, such that future engineers can save transceivers
to decrease the cost and energy per bit, and simultaneously increase the speed. Here, an
all-optical wavelength converter could be a strong contender to remove these with clever
engineering [11].
Modern transceivers are both expensive and slow compared to an all-
optical solution because they use electronic processing so that the incoming signal is received
and then retransmitted at the desired wavelength. The aim here is that an all-optical WC
can utilize nonlinear optical processes to change the signal wavelength and partially replace
electronic conversion devices.
Again to implement this, a polarization-independent device needs to be designed with as
broad a bandwidth as possible and with as much flexibility as possible.
All of this will be investigated in this thesis with the main focus of designing, implementing,
and characterizing the process of FWM so that it can be used in a modern communication
system. This will include characterizing different types of fibers and their OPC and WC
capabilities in both single-pump and dual-pump fiber optical parametric amplifier (FOPA)
configurations, and how the conversion efficiency can be increased and therein improved.
Furthermore, the experimental data will be compared to analytical and numerical solutions
so that a best-case scenario with our current lab equipment can be estimated. Finally, the
polarization-independency requirement will be examined using a loop scheme configuration
with a circulator.

2. Theory
To be able to design, implement and characterize an experimental setup that is to be used
for wavelength conversion or optical phase conjugation, some basic theory behind the science
involved in the FWM process needs to be understood. The phenomena which pave the way
for FWM include linear and nonlinear optics and general waveguide optics among others.
Especially, theory regarding the Kerr effect and dispersion seems to be relevant as will be
explored in this section of the thesis. Another topic of interest is birefringence, which in-
troduces a polarization-dependency in the optical fibers that will be used throughout the
experiments. This will pave the way for the understanding of polarization-maintaining (PM)
fibers.
This section will also explore the various possible fiber optical parametric amplifier (FOPA)
setups that can be utilized to create four-wave mixing - some more convenient than others.
One major focus here will be on identifying the relevant fiber and laser parameters regarding
the FWM process.
Additionally, the creation of polarization-independent schemes where
the polarization-diversity loop scheme will be presented. Finally, a validation of analytical
solutions of the FOPA configurations using numerical simulations is undertaken.

2.2. Linear effects
Linear effects in optical fibers are effects that are independent of the intensity of the light
which propagates through the medium. This is in stark contrast to the so-called nonlinear
effects which will be discussed in section 2.3. In short, these nonlinear effects are related to
situations where the propagation of an electromagnetic field depends on the electromagnetic
field itself [14].
Linear optics can in general be contributed to the linear induced polarization which holds
when the applied electric field, i.e. the light wave, is rather weak. The induced polarization
can be described as

i.e. the induced polarization will oscillate at the same frequency as the incoming field. This
means that if there are only linear effects in an optical fiber, no new frequencies will be cre-
ated. This is again in direct opposition to nonlinear effects. Therefore, in the linear regime,
we will expect the incoming light wave to consist of the same frequency components as the
outgoing light.
A variety of different linear effects of interest exist. In this thesis, the effects that will be
explored are fiber losses, birefringence, and dispersion.

2.2.2. Birefringence
By the fundamentals of light propagation in fibers, we know there exist two states of polar-
ization that are orthogonal to each other for a perfect, cylindrical fiber. Birefringence refers
to the phenomenon where the polarization of light determines the experienced refractive in-
dex. Ideally, a standard single-mode fiber should not experience birefringence due to silica
being an isotropic material. However, due to manufacturing problems, practical fibers will
be imperfect, e.g. since there are limitations in creating a fiber with a constant radius. The
symmetry in the fibers is therefore broken and the two orthogonal modes will thereby be
able to exchange power. This imperfection is what will lead to birefringence in the SSMF,
meaning that the two polarizations will propagate differently. This means that there will be
a fast and a slow axis in the fibers meaning nx ╠Ė= ny, where x and y correspond to the two
axes. These changes in the directional refractive index can happen across the entire fiber,
and the two axes can switch between the slow and the fast axis continuously. All of this
means that the state-of-polarization (SoP) quickly becomes random and will shift between
linear, circular, and elliptical throughout the fiber. The fact that different polarizations of
light experience different group velocities in an SSMF is referred to as polarization-mode
dispersion (PMD).
To prevent this, a polarization-maintaining fiber (PMF) can be used. This type of fiber has in
some way been changed so that the two polarization modes experience significantly different
refractive indices, and hereby a natural strong birefringence is introduced into the fiber, which
is much stronger than the natural birefringence that comes from these imperfections. This
means that the refractive index in one direction, orthogonal to the direction of propagation

2.2.3. Dispersion
From the very basics of waveguide optics, propagating fields can be seen as different rays
bouncing at different angles, and as a result, they will travel different distances in the waveg-
uide to reach the same propagating length.
Due to the fact that the rays are traveling
different distances, we can say they have distinct group velocities since their effective speeds
are different from each other.
This kind of delay of light in a waveguide is called inter-
modal dispersion and can be minimized by using a graded-index fiber as an example. This
intermodal dispersion, group velocity dispersion (GVD), or simply just dispersion has two
contributions: waveguide dispersion, and material dispersion. Waveguide dispersion can be
controlled by the size of the fiber or index profile or by engineering the index to the profile.
The other contribution is material dispersion, which is inherent to the used material of the
fiber, i.e. silica glass for a standard fiber.

2.3.3. Brillouin Scattering
Another third-order nonlinear effect is the so-called Brillouin Scattering (BS), which in con-
trast to the three previously mentioned effects is an inelastic process. This means that the
incoming optical field will transfer energy to the material (in this case silica glass) so that the
original optical field loses some of its amplitude, and fields at other frequencies are created.

on the spectrum will be determined by the Brillouin gain spectrum. These acoustic phonons

an electric field is applied [8]. Quantum mechanically, this can also be seen as a pump photon
being annihilated and creating an acoustic phonon and a so-called Stokes photon, the mean-

2.4. FOPA Configurations
A fiber optical parametric amplifier (FOPA) is the name of the device which utilizes FWM
to amplify an incoming signal. As explained in section 2.3.2, this also has the consequence
that a conjugated idler is generated, which makes a FOPA suitable as both an OPC and
wavelength converter. Since this nonlinear process depends harshly on the polarization be-
tween the signal and the pump(s), it is natural to distinguish between two groups of FOPAs:
a polarization-dependent FOPA and a polarization-independent FOPA. By starting out by

describing the fundamental physics behind the polarization-dependent process, we can be-

our FOPA device. However, this type of FOPA would not be suitable for implementation
in a modern optical communication network. This is because the polarization of a signal
propagating through a long fiber link will experience random variations, which complicates
the FWM process since the polarization-dependent FOPA requires a very specific SoP [8].
This is further complicated by the fact that modern communication systems use Polarization
Division Multiplexing (PDM) to increase the maximum capacity. From this, the importance
of the same state-of-polarization between the signal and the pump(s) has been emphasized.

SSMFs, since the cross-section is not perfectly circular at all positions along the z-direction
of the fiber [8]. Additionally, a simple polarization controller (PC) cannot be used to align the

FOPA, e.g. orthogonally polarized pumps [22] and the angled pump scheme [23]. However,

arms that enter the HNLF in each direction. We know from the analytical solutions that if we
are in the undepleted pump regime, the CE does not depend on the signal power. Therefore,
by simply aligning the pump polarization so it is split evenly in each arm of the PBS, the
split signal in each direction will experience the same conversion efficiency independently of
the incoming SoP. The main reason that it is convenient to use a loop scheme is that it
is the easiest way to obtain optical path length matching, which is very important in the
polarization-diversity scheme [8].

each direction to be polarized. By observing figure 2.17a, one may be convinced that this
kind of setup will only work if each oppositely propagating field is polarized orthogonally
to the other. For example, if the PBS splits an incoming field into the two orthogonal axes
of the HNLF, that would mean that the polarization which was reflected in the PBS must

rods to achieve this. This can however be detrimental to the optical path length matching
since a changed refractive index also changes the optical path length. Therefore, the cir-

with the circulator is the main focus, since all experiments, which are not just part of some
characterization, have been undertaken with polarization-maintaining fibers.
Of course, this setup has the advantage of making the output CE polarization-independent.
However, there are some major disadvantages as well. First of all, since the pump power
is essentially split into the two arms of the PBS, the pump power needs to be twice as
large in magnitude to achieve the same CE spectra as in the polarization-independent cases.
Moreover, there is some natural attenuation in the circulator that is not necessarily trivial.
Additionally, this loss may also be wavelength-dependent further complicating the system.
Furthermore, since the pump field needs to be aligned perfectly so that there is the same
pump power in both directions of the PM-HNLF, a slight drift in the pump SoP may yield
the FOPA very unstable. Therefore, a good stabilization of the pump polarization is needed.
Finally, the loop scheme with a circulator is extremely susceptible to noise terms due to SBS
backscattering [24]. This is because the SoP of all propagating fields is linearly aligned, which
yields the highest possible CE. Unfortunately, as has been learned in section 2.3.3, SBS only
happens in the backward direction, meaning that if the backscattered power becomes large
enough, this SBS stokes field, which is created at 11 GHz from the pump frequency, will
begin to interfere with the oppositely propagating pump through additional FWM which can
end up completely distorting the pump, which can be directly transferred to the signal and
idler fields as seen in equations (2.4.5) and (2.4.6). Therefore, working to avoid any SBS
that can be created in the PM-HNLF is paramount when the loop scheme is implemented.
Fortunately, it is possible to suppress this SBS so that its effects are lessened significantly.
2.5. Suppressing Stimulated Brillouin Scattering
From section 2.4 and more precisely figure 2.7 it is obvious that high pump powers are pre-
ferred. These higher pump powers are however limited in real experiments by SBS, which
has been discussed in section 2.3.3. This effect can be limited by several techniques [25, 26]
==PAGE:44==
Theory
Suppressing Stimulated Brillouin Scattering
Page 36 of 122
which can all help to achieve a higher threshold of around 5 dB or more, but also come with
implications such as variations in the ZDW. A more consistent way of achieving higher SBS
thresholds is modulation of the light. This can either be amplitude modulation, frequency
modulation, or phase modulation. All of these have been shown to increase the threshold ex-
perimentally [27]. Here, frequency modulation performed the best, but the working principle
here is to change between two frequencies which will not work for FWM, since the placement
of the pump is very important for the energy conservation and phase-matching. Then, phase
modulation works by widening the pump spectrally, which is also often referred to as pump
dithering. This means that the total power of the pump is the same, but the peak power
of the pump has been lowered. This technique has been improved upon in earlier research
[28], where it is shown that with correct phase modulation, the pump can be divided into
three equally large power peaks by using an external signal generator together with a phase
modulator. This means that the peak power is lowered by a factor of 5 dB, and through
equation (2.3.13) it follows that the SBS threshold can similarly be increased by 5 dB. This
can then be even further extended by applying a new phase modulation again creating 3
times more peaks resulting in another improvement of 5 dB in the SBS threshold. One can
continue to add additional phase modulation frequencies and it has been shown with 4 mod-
ulations, which achieves 34 = 81 new main frequencies, and an SBS threshold improvement
of 20 dB theoretically, although only 18 dB experimentally [26]. From there, each phase
modulation needs to be modulated at a frequency 3 to 3.5 times the previous modulation
frequency. The limit here is however in the width of the resulting light. Other literature
has also shown that small fringes generated by phase modulation can arise around 11 GHz
from the pump frequency, which coincides with the Stokes shift of SBS in silica. Any light
generated here will work as seeding for the SBS in the loop scheme configuration, which will
then result in lowering the SBS threshold [8]. This was however not a problem in [29], where
a pump with a width of 25 GHz was used. Here, 5 phase modulations were used to achieve a
20 dB increase of the SBST, resulting in a 70 dB signal gain in total. This could be achieved
since the polarization-independent loop scheme was not used, and therefore seeding of the
opposite signal could not happen, since no signal or pump was propagating in the opposite
direction.
One major point has been skipped thus far. Before one can simply triple the frequency of
the previous modulation frequency as earlier mentioned, one needs to find the first frequency
which one should modulate with. It has been found that this frequency needs to be twice
the frequency of the Brillouin gain bandwidth [25, 26]. The gain bandwidth of SSMF is in
the range of 10-30 MHz [17] and normally around 20 MHz in a standard HNLF [30]. This
means that the first modulation frequency should be set to a minimum of 60 MHz to ensure
that the modulation frequency is at least twice the Brillouin gain bandwidth.

When optical couplers are shown in the setups, one very important thing should be noted.
When a coupler is not a simple 3 dB coupler, the upper arm that goes into the coupler will
experience the smallest loss in the upper output arm. For example, in the case of a 10 dB
coupler when the optical power propagates into the upper input arm, 90% of the optical power
will exit through the upper output arm, whereas 10% will exit through the lower output arm.
This is because a loss of 10 dB corresponds to a loss of 90% of the power. Therefore, when
an optical coupler is included in a setup sketch, the upper two arms will together experience
the lowest loss, and the lower two arms will also experience low loss.
Additionally, when polarization-maintaining fibers are used in an experimental setup, they
will always be highlighted as blue fibers, whereas SSMFs that do not have any polarization-
maintaining properties will always simply be shown as orange fibers.
In every section, different types of power meters (PM) and tune-able laser sources (TLS) will
be used. It will always be emphasized when the setup is described exactly which laser and
which power meter is used since they will have different properties that define them, e.g. one
power meter may have a different power range it can measure compared to another. The
utilized optical amplifiers are always erbium-doped fiber amplifiers (EDFA), and there are
up to two of these in the setups. These two EDFAs are from the same product line and have
the same properties. Importantly, the amplification will in our case always end up being
saturated and the power output will always be 31.5 dBm.
Throughout this experimental journey, three different types of polarization controlling devices
are used. The first is a simple polarization controller (PC), which consists of two quarter-wave
plates at each end and a half-wave plate in the middle. The two remaining devices are a linear
polarizer and a polarization beam splitter (PBS). These can be seen in figures 3.1 and 3.2,
respectively. The polarizer has a very simple function of only letting light pass through it if
it is in a certain SoP. In our case, the fibers have been designed in such a manner that only
light that is aligned in the slow axis of the PM-HNLFs will pass, while the rest will be filtered
out. The PBS however consists of two input arms labeled T and R, whereas the two output
arms are labeled 1 and 2, which again can be seen in figure 3.2. The PBS works by splitting
the incoming light into the two principal axes of the PM fibers, which lets each polarization
be guided independently. In the case of our specific PBS, when light is guided into the T
input, all outgoing light from the two outputs (both 1 and 2) will be aligned in the slow axis
of the PMF, and oppositely, if the R input arm is chosen both outputs will have light in the
fast axis of the PMF. This is probably achieved by rotating the fiber inside the PBS in such
a way that light that should have come out in the fast axis will be aligned in the slow axis
instead. Using this PBS, one can choose the desired polarization axis and then use a PC to
decide how much power there should be in each output arm of the PBS percentage-wise.

3.2. SBS characterization
The first set of measurements that were carried out concerned the SBS characteristics. This
was done in an attempt to gain some knowledge about what the most optimal input power
levels are for each fiber since the output power of the fiber will be limited by this SBS as
explained in section 2.3.3. This optimization should also maximize the obtainable CE in
the upcoming sections.
Besides this, if we would like to send information through these
HNLFs utilizing the FWM phenomenon, the SBS will limit the bit error rate (BER) of the
modulation system drastically [37].
3.2.1. Setup and method
These measurements were carried out in two different ways, the nature of which can be
understood in figures 3.1 and 3.2. In both of these figures, 3 distinct power meters can be
observed. PM1 measures the backscattered power from the SBS, PM2 measures the input
power into the PM-HNLF where the loss of the PBS is taken into account for the setup
shown in figure 3.2. The last power meter, PM3, simply measures the output power from the
PM-HNLF. The polarizer and the PBS both have the function in these two setups to ensure
that the desired polarization enters the PM-HNLF. In figure 3.1, a polarizer is inserted on
the backside of the PM-HNLF. By having this here, the polarization can be optimized with
the PC that is placed just after the TLS so that the correct SoP can be reached at the PM-
HNLF, which in this case will ensure that all incoming light will lie in the slow axis of the
fiber since that is how the polarizer is designed. The optimization is simply done by aligning
the PC so that the power into PM3 is maximized. The reasoning behind placing the polarizer
behind the PM-HNLF is to protect it from being exposed to higher powers than necessary.
When SBS is at its highest, where close to all light is reflected, the power at the input of the
PM-HNLF is effectively 3 dB more than what is actually measured as the input power due
to this backscattered effect, which then could result in damaging of the fibers if one is not
careful. So by placing it here, unnecessary exposure can be avoided. For the PBS however,
this is not a possibility, since placing it behind the PM-HNLF would defeat its purpose of
aligning the light in either the fast or slow axis.

3.2.2. Results
When measuring the SBS characteristics of any fiber, a threshold needs to be determined.
There are many ways of doing this. Some define it as when backscattered power equals the
output power or when the backscattered power is at 1% of the input power [8, 38]. A more
fancy way of determining the threshold is to find the input power where the amount of SBS
backscattering equals the power of the backscattered light due to Rayleigh scattering. This
graphically means that the SBST is when the backscattered power does not rise linearly with
the input power any longer [28]. We define the SBST as first mentioned as to when the
backscattered light is equal to 1% of the input light as was explained in section 2.3.3. This
SBST line is shown in all plots as a black dash-dotted line, and at the exact point where the
backscattered power crosses the threshold line, the SBST is determined by considering what
the input power is here.

3.3. Method for Calculating Conversion Efficiency
When calculating the experimental conversion efficiency, two major adjustments are made
so that it is possible to compare the experiments to the analytical and numerical solutions.
This is important when characterizing the fiber parameters since it may prove difficult to
compare the experiments to the theory due to the potential large noise terms that arise
because of practical limitations in the experimental devices. First of all, when calculating
the conversion efficiency, the experimental definition is changed compared to the theoretical
definition. As described in section 2.4.1, the theoretical CE is defined as the magnitude of
the output idler divided by the input signal power. However, by assuming that the signal
gain in the FOPA is close to 0 dB due to the short length of the fiber, which neither leaves
much room for a large loss term nor gain term, the output signal power is approximately
equal to the input signal power. This definition will then simply be the output idler power
divided by the output signal power with the pump turned on in both cases. It is also common
to define the experimental CE as the power of the output idler divided by the output signal
power with the pump turned off when measuring the signal power [8]. This also uses the
low-loss approximation. A quick way to test if the gain is around 0 dB is to measure the
CE using these two different experimental methods and compare them to each other. If the
signal power is approximately the same in both scenarios, the calculated CE values should
also not differ meaning the signal gain is very low. It will be shown later that this is precisely
the case. However, in this thesis, the first experimental definition will be used, which can be
summarized in an equation as

3.4. Characterization of Fibers
Now, since one of the main aims of this thesis is to characterize the FWM process for
application in wavelength conversion and OPC, the conversion efficiency spectra for the
fibers need to be measured and understood. As has been analyzed in section 2.4, there are
both the single-pump and dual-pump configurations, and there are many fiber and laser
parameters which decide the shape and magnitude of the CE at a specific pump wavelength
(and center wavelength in the dual-pump case). It is therefore of utmost importance that
the fiber parameters are characterized, since the laser parameters, e.g. the pump power and
center wavelength, can be directly changed by using an optical amplifier and a tunable laser
source. In the following experiments, three different fibers will be used and characterized:
one 54 m PM-HNLF, one 94 m PM-HNLF, and one 497 m PM-HNLF SPINE. These are
chosen due to their polarization-maintaining properties and their tabulated ZDW, which is
located in the vicinity of the C-band. Additionally, the 508 m standard HNLF SPINE has
been removed since the SBS measurement yielded less than satisfactory results.
3.4.1. Setup and Method
In the following experiments, the FWM process itself for the single-pump case will finally
be undertaken and observed. Some initial graphs from the OSA have already been shown
in figure 3.6, where the noise correction approach was introduced, and it is now the aim to
analyze the CE spectra themselves using the setup in figure 3.9. First, there are two TLSs
at the beginning of the setup, where a CoBrite laser is used as the pump, and the Net Test
laser is used as the signal laser. Both arms consist of a PC, which is used to align the signal
and pump so that their polarizations are exactly parallel to maximize the FWM process
since this nonlinear process is very polarization-sensitive as discussed in section 2.3.2. The
pump is then also guided through an EDFA, which amplifies the pump power, and then into
a VOA that lets us control the pump input power, and a 1 nm BPF which extinguishes
a large part of the ASE noise which is a consequence of the EDFA. The pump and signal
are then combined using a 3 dB coupler, where the first output arm ends in a power meter
(PM), and the second output arm goes towards the PM-HNLF. The PM is used so that the
input power into the PM-HNLF is known and can be controlled using the aforementioned
VOA. Before being guided into the PM-HNLF, the combined signal and pump go through
a polarizer, which ensures that both signal and pump only exist in the slow axis of the
PM-HNLF, where the PCs from before are used to maximize the powers in the slow axis.
After the PM-HNLF, when the FWM process has occurred, the output of the fiber goes
into a 20 dB coupler, where the high output of the coupler goes into a VOA, and then the
optical spectrum analyzer (OSA). The VOA is placed here since the input power into the
OSA may not exceed 10 dBm. The low output of the 20 dB coupler goes into a PM, where
the maximum power of which may not exceed 3 dBm, which is why this PM is placed in the
low output power arm. This PM is used as a tool to align the polarization of the pump and
signal lasers into the slow axis. Since a polarizer is added to the setup, the PCs are simply

3.5. Wavelength Conversion Possibilities
The general shapes and properties of the CE spectra regarding the mentioned three different
fibers have now been characterized and examined in the previous section. However, it has
also been pointed out several times that it is not necessarily the CE spectra alone that are
important for applications in OPCs and WCs. It is also very desirable to achieve extensive
knowledge regarding which signal wavelengths can be converted into which range of idler
wavelengths since a wavelength converter will only be attractive if one is able to convert
from and to all needed wavelengths. The conversion efficiency is also extremely important
in this regard since a low CE will yield very weak idler powers, which can be difficult to
utilize in an optical communications system. Since an OPC does not necessarily need to
achieve wavelengths in very large ranges, this section will focus on the application of FWM
in all-optical wavelength converters using these three fiber types: 54 m PM-HNLF, 94 m
PM-HNLF, and 497 m PM-HNLF SPINE.
3.5.1. Method
In the following experiments and data analysis, the same setup as in the previous section
is used, which can be observed in figure 3.9. The overall dynamics of the system have not
changed, and the explanation of what happens to the signal and pump throughout the setup
is explained in section 3.4.1. The results shown in the previous section are also used here so
that it is possible to achieve a good overview of the wavelength conversion ranges that can
be utilized with these specific fibers. To do this, two things are done using surface plots,
where three axes are used: the signal wavelength, the idler wavelength, and the conversion
efficiency.
First, by simply using the already shown results these surface plots are created for each
individual fiber. After this, a threshold is implemented so that only conversion efficiencies
above some specific value are considered. In the following, a CE threshold of -20 dB is chosen.
This number is chosen somewhat arbitrarily since this corresponds to an efficiency of 1%,
which definitely is not very optimal. However, the method of choosing a CE threshold can
be applied to any HNLF where a FWM process has happened. The reason for choosing this
rather low efficiency of -20 dB is that the maximum CE for both the 54 m and 94 m fibers
are around -18 dB, so to showcase this method in the best way, a threshold that is below
this number must be chosen. However, in future work where a dual-pump system or SBS
suppression will be implemented, these CE values will be significantly higher, and a higher CE
threshold may be chosen. Consequently, by applying this threshold, we can achieve a good
estimation of which wavelength ranges can be utilized in a potential wavelength converter.
Secondly, some new measurements are also undertaken. Earlier, the wavelength sweeps were
only done for half the CE spectrum. For example, in the earlier figures showing the CE
spectra, only negative wavelength differences are shown, since theory dictates that the CE
spectrum is symmetric around the pump frequency. However, for better visualization, it may

3.6. Dual-pump Characterization
From the single-pump experiments that are shown in sections 3.4 and 3.5, we have seen that
we are limited to CE values in the order of -16 to -20 dBm range due to the choice of fiber
after we have chosen to go away from the 497 m PM-HNLF SPINE. Now, from the analysis in
section 2.4 and the knowledge that two distinct pumps will be limited individually in regards
to the SBST, i.e. each pump may have a power value equal to the SBST, we can move on
to the dual-pump configuration. From this, we should be able to introduce more overall
power into the system. This results in achieving a higher CE with two pumps of the same
power that was used for the single-pump experiments. Besides the addition of the extra input
power that can be inserted into the fiber, the two pumps also ensure that we can transfer
signal wavelengths to idler wavelengths where we could not before. This is because the two
pumps are now separated so that signals and idlers can be placed between and outside the
two pumps.
In this section, two different kinds of dependencies will be investigated: the dependency of
pump separation, Ōłå╬╗p, and the dependency of the center wavelength, ╬╗c. By undertaking
experiments where the pump separation is varied, it should become clear that it is possible
to convert all signal wavelengths in a specific range without some specific wavelengths being
unreachable, due to the pumps being positioned on top of the signal or idler. Then by sweep-
ing the center wavelength, as was done for the single-pump configuration, the possibilities of
a wavelength converter would also have been shown for the dual-pump scheme. Before this,
a general description of the fields generated in this dual-pump scheme will be made.
3.6.1. Dual pump definitions
When considering the dual-pump configuration and all of the fields that a subsequently
created as mentioned in figure 2.4, a common understanding regarding the terminology of
the created fields is needed. Therefore, figure 3.18 has been created. Here, the two pumps
and their idlers to first and second order are shown. All these four fields are created as a
result of just these two pumps coming in. Then there is the signal which comes into the fiber
and interacts with the pumps to create the idler where the wavelength of the idler obeys
energy conservation. Lastly, there are the signal idlers and the 2nd order idler, which are all
also a product of having two pumps and can be understood by observing figure 2.4 again.
When considering the second-order idler, there should be another one positioned on the other
side of the idler and it should be of the same size. The signal idlers can vary a lot in power
compared to each other depending on the power symmetry of the two pumps where if both
pumps were the exact same power, the two signal idlers should also be comparable.

3.6.3. ╬╗C sweep
Multiple pump separation values have been tested in the previous section and it has been
shown that the differences in the resulting CE spectra are very small. Even so small, pump
separation dependence can be assumed to be negligible in this pump wavelength range. Now,
for clarity, we will examine how various center wavelengths will affect the CE spectra in a
similar manner to how it was done for the single-pump configuration. Before we sweep the
center wavelength, we have to select the pump separation values that we would like. For us
to find the optimal pump separation values we need to create some criteria. Specifically here,
we would like to be able to use the whole wavelength range using only two different pump
separations. First, we want a configuration that should be able to convert all possible signals
to any idler wavelength without the pump or the pump idlers being a problem. Secondly, due
to this, the chosen pump separations should not be divisible by each other. For example, if
one were to choose 5 nm and 15 nm, where 15 is divisible by 5, the second-order pump idler
of the 5 nm pump separation and the two pumps of the 15 nm pump separation would be
right on top of each other, leaving us with at least two spectral positions where we would not
be able to convert any signal to idler. Third, we want to choose the pump separation values,
so we can have more than two measurements at a certain center wavelength for comparison
reasons. Since we are limited in the pump wavelength range, we cannot use the largest pump
separation values in this section because it would give us too few possible center wavelengths.
As a result of these criteria, we have chosen pump separations of 5 nm and 9 nm.

9 nm separation sweep we used the Yenista laser, which meant we could turn the power back

increase the pump power limit, which at this moment is limited by the SBST. This value

3.7.1. Setup
Before considering the new CE plots, an understanding of how to implement this pump
dithering procedure is needed. First, we need to find the right RF tone to modulate the
pump with. From section 2.5, it is known that the minimum frequency should be at least

for this was not found, but it could be attributed to uncertainty in the equipment. The
pump spectra, both modulated and non-modulated, can then be seen and analyzed on the

3.8. Comparison with analytical and numerical model
To see how all the measurements from the previous sections fit the theory explained in
section 2.4, a comparison is carried out. Here, both the analytical and numerical approaches
can be used to fit the fiber parameters to the measurements. The analytical model calculates
much more quickly than the numerical counterpart since the analytical model just consists of a
few equations that need to be calculated, whereas the numerical model has to calculate many
steps throughout the fiber. So from a speed perspective, the analytical model is superior.
However, the numerical model is superior if one actually wants to compute more realistic
values since some of the actual fiber parameters could vary over the range of the fiber, which
the analytical equations are not able to include. Additionally, the numerical approach is also
able to predict pump depletion. Due to these behaviors, a grid search for the fiber parameters
is much simpler and faster to carry out using the analytical model.
This grid search is
executed by searching for the zero-dispersion wavelength (╬╗ZDW ), the dispersion slope (S),
and the dispersion curvature (╬▓4) by attempting to find the best fit and the smallest error
function, while the nonlinear coefficient,╬│, was found manually. ╬│ can be manually found due
to the physics behind the maximum value of the CE. The maximum CE value itself depends
on three parameters: the fiber length, the nonlinear coefficient, and the pump power. Since
the fiber length and pump power are known beforehand due to choosing a specific fiber, the
only remaining parameter that can change the amplitude of the CE is ╬│. Therefore, very
few manual calculations need to be carried out to find the optimal ╬│ value, since it is just a
matter of fitting the maximum CE value.
The grid search is executed by calculating the conversion efficiency analytically and then
comparing it to the measurements.
This then creates a big 3D matrix since there are 3
parameters that are investigated. The combination of parameters, where the minimum dif-
ference between experiments and the analytical solutions, is seen as the optimal fit as a result
of the grid search. For the 94 m PM-HNLF, the result can be seen in figure 3.34. Here, the left
half of a single-pump CE measurement is shown with a pump wavelength of 1543 nm. This is
then compared with the result of the grid search using the analytical model. The values that
were found are a zero-dispersion wavelength of 1541.9 nm, a slope of 0.0142 ps/(nm2km), a
╬▓4-value of ŌłÆ1 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ5 ps4/km, and a nonlinear coefficient (╬│) of 8.5 (Wkm)ŌłÆ1. Here, it can be
seen that the found fit actually seems to fit very well with the experimental measurement.
But it is generally found that all measurements at lower pump wavelengths seem to create
a rather insufficient fit and, oppositely, measurements at pump wavelengths above 1543 nm
seem to have a somewhat good fit, although not as ideal as hoped. The reason that these
fits at pump wavelengths below 1543 nm are deemed bad is that these measurements are
predicted to have these ŌĆØwingsŌĆØ that are also shown and described in section 2.4. These
wings were, however, never really measured and therefore this will always be a problem when
trying to fit using the analytical model since the grid search is not able to fit these wings.

analyze this fitting or examine any fancy algorithms, we chose to always create the dispersion
profile so that the slope value always either increases or decreases monotonously, i.e. the slope
value goes from high to low values or vice versa. While fitting, the fiber was split into 20
segments. This is a good compromise between achieving relatively smooth transitions and
keeping a low simulation time. This being said, we always gradually swept from one value of
the slope to another certain value, which is, of course, not always necessarily the case in a
real fiber, where the dispersion slope does not need to only increase or decrease throughout
the fiber and value might even stay at a certain value for a longer amount of time. However,
it is assumed that this approach is sufficient.

4. Discussion & Outlook
In this part of the thesis, a final discussion of the results found in chapter 3 will be under-
taken in an attempt to summarize the different discussion points, that were made during
each experiment, into a bigger perspective in regards to the application into a modern op-
tical communications system. This discussion will also include an outlook on what future
experiments could be done to come closer to a realization of the all-optical fiber-based WC
and OPC.
One of the major challenges of creating a FOPA for this application is that the fiber needs
to be designed well in order for the FWM process to happen optimally in a fiber. Here,
only PM-HNLFs have been looked into, but it is entirely possible to utilize other types of
fiber designs to optimize the outcoming CE spectra for practical use. Other literature has
found use in standard HNLF to minimize the SBS reflection and to achieve higher CE [8],
and even photonic crystal fiber (PCF) based FOPAs have been looked into since they can be
designed to exhibit high gain and broad bandwidth in the C-band [39]. Therefore, it could
prove interesting to examine many other types of fibers to realize the most optimal FOPA.
Additionally, another interesting future task could lie in improving the OSNR of the gener-
ated idler that results from the FWM process. It was a recurring problem throughout the
experiments that the idler simply was too low in power in relation to the ASE noise from
the EDFA, which forced us to create some solution where it was attempted to correct for
this noise floor. Of course, it would have been easier and probably better to simply use a
better and more narrow BPF, and possibly with more cavities so that the ASE noise could
be pushed further down, but this was not entirely possible due to equipment limitations.
Another solution to the limited OSNR could also be solved by simply increasing the signal
power since the idler power should increase linearly with the signal assuming the pump is
not depleted. Using a signal TLS which was stable at higher output powers while also having
the possibility of being swept quickly could be advantageous in this regard.
Achieving a higher input power of the pump into the PM-HNLF also has the possibility of
increasing the idler power enough so that OSNR becomes a lesser problem. As shown, this
can be even further optimized by adding additional phase modulations and by using the
dual-pump scheme. However, one may then run into the scheme where signal gain becomes
prominent, which means the experimental CE definition needs to be reconsidered, and it was
also shown during the numerical simulations that higher pump power may also deplete the
pump. Therefore, one needs to be careful here.
As for the loop scheme, several problems were identified, e.g. the drifting of the pump SoP
and the wavelength- and polarization-dependent losses of the different components in the sys-
==PAGE:110==
Discussion & Outlook
Page 102 of 122
tem. However, during the experiments the drifting of the pump polarization was identified as
the largest challenge by far, since an unfortunate pump optimization will yield unstable idler
outputs, effectively rendering the FOPA useless in a communication system. Due to time
constraints, a solution to this was never found. However, a major ambition of ours was to
replace the PC after the pump TLS in figure 3.38 with an electronic polarization controller,
which would then be connected to the oscilloscope in the same setup. In our experiments, the
oscilloscope was there to ensure stability of the idler. However, it could also have been used
with a computer program that could continuously maintain the optimal pump polarization
in a feedback loop. A proposed way of doing this would be to use the data from the oscil-
loscope to create a histogram of the idler power and then utilize some sort of optimization
algorithm which could then ensure that the width of the histogram would always be at its
smallest. Succeeding in doing this would immediately solve the pump polarization problem
since the system would be able to correct the drifting on its own. This would probably have
been our next step, had time allowed it. When this has been done, the wavelength- and
polarization-dependence of the components could be looked into so that the CE spectra in
the loop scheme would also be as smooth as in the previous measurements regarding the
polarization-dependent single- and dual-pump configurations.
In theory, we should be able to minimize polarization-dependent loss to basically 0 by op-
timizing the pump polarization at a very high level. This was however not shown, and this
feedback loop could maybe also give this theory another try.
After having created a working loop scheme configuration using CW signals, the next natural
step will be to begin using simple modulation schemes to examine how the system reacts to
modulated signal pulses.
As an easy beginning, simple on-off keying (OOK) modulation
can be used, which is simply a two-level modulation format where the pulses can represent
a 0 or 1 bit. Here, it would be the aim to find relevant parameters that can describe the
performance of such a communication system, e.g. the bit error rate (BER) and the signal-to-
noise ratio (SNR) of the received signal. Depending on how this would go, one could choose
to modulate with more advanced modulation formats where phase modulation also plays a
role, e.g. quadrature phase-shift keying (QPSK) or 16-quadrature amplitude modulation (16-
QAM). For these phase-sensitive modulation formats, however, a dual-pump system needs to
be implemented, since a phase-modulated pump could result in too much phase noise. For
this examination, an investigation in multi-signal processing would be a natural extension.
In this project, all measurements have been carried out using one single signal at a time, and
multi-signal processing has not been shown.
Furthermore, to improve the bandwidth of the CE spectra, two different PM-HNLFs with
similar, but distinct, dispersion properties could be combined. This could result in wider CE
spectra but the order of the fibers has a large effect, and when it comes to the loop scheme
this will add an extra layer of complexity, which we have not investigated.
Finally, when the communication capabilities of the FOPA system have been rigorously
examined, a first prototype could be created which seeks to combine all the devices and
components, that have been introduced thus far, into one compact device which is preferably
==PAGE:111==

portable.
The time aspects regarding this are definitely long, but it seems achievable if
everything goes according to plan.

Conclusion

5. Conclusion
In the process of designing, implementing, and characterizing a functional, polarization-
independent FOPA device using FWM for OPCs and WCs application, many important
parameters and considerations have been found and identified in this thesis. Several fibers
were at some point examined in this thesis, but the 94 m PM-HNLF is the fiber that has been
examined in every single section of the thesis and had the greatest focus. The relevant fiber
parameters have been found to be the dispersion slope S, the dispersion curvature ╬▓4, the
zero-dispersion wavelength, ╬╗ZDW , the nonlinear coefficient, ╬│, and the length of the utilized
fiber, L. Here, only PM-HNLFs have been examined in regards to the outcoming CE spectra.
In an attempt of quantifying these parameters in the 94 m PM-HNLF, the following values
were found: ╬│ = 8.5 (Wkm)ŌłÆ1, S = 0.006 to 0.018 ps/(nm2km), ╬╗zdw = 1542.7 nm, and ╬▓4
= ŌłÆ1 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ5 ps4/km. The found CE spectra using these values ended up fitting very well at a
pump wavelength for 1543.5 nm, which is around the optimum for this fiber. However, when
the wavelength was deviated from this optimum, it was seen in figure 3.37 that the numerical
model consistently over-estimated the bandwidth of the CE spectrum - although the shape
always seemed to fit quite nicely.
The reason for this deviation has not been identified,
but it is proposed that further investigation using some sort of algorithm might find more
appropriate values.
As to the laser parameters, two of these were of great importance in the undepleted pump
regime: the pump power, Pp, and the above-mentioned pump wavelength ╬╗p (or center wave-
length in the dual-pump case ╬╗c). It was generally found that the pump wavelength should
lie a little above the ZDW to achieve the most optimal conversion spectrum. Additionally,
it has been observed that the pump power should be as large as possible, as this will yield
the greatest CE values. However, one quickly runs into problems like pump depletion, as
shown in the numerical solutions, and SBS backscattering, where the SBST was readily iden-
tified at 22.5 dBm for the 94 m PM-HNLF. Since the SBST limits the magnitude of the CE
spectra, pump dithering was used to increase the SBST by 5 dB which in the single-pump
configuration raised the CE by 10 dB. Only one RF tone was used here, but this can easily
be increased to further improve the power of the outcoming idler due to FWM.
Another way of improving the CE of the FOPA device is to go towards a dual-pump con-
figuration, where the CE should theoretically be quadrupled (increased by 6 dB) if one uses
two pumps with the same pump power as in the single-pump case, where an increase of 8
dB was seen experimentally since the pump powers could still be increased by 0.5 dB each
before SBS proved too large a problem. Additionally, here, it was shown that by choosing
two different pump separation values, 5 nm and 9 nm, the whole spectral range could be used
==PAGE:113==
Conclusion
Page 105 of 122
between these two separation values which was seen in figure 3.29.
Furthermore, a method of designing and characterizing a wavelength converter has been
proposed, where a square-symmetric conversion band has been visualized for two PM-HNLFs,
where the 94 m fiber proved more promising since it had a range of 40 nm, which could span
over the entire C-band. Moreover, the 54 m PM-HNLF had three squares with ranges of 11
nm, 15 nm, and 16 nm. However, this has only been tested in the single-pump case and with
no pump dithering, so a lot of improvement can be done, but the method itself can be useful
for future work.
Additionally, the polarization-independent loop scheme was examined where several prob-
lems were identified. First, the SoP of the pump easily began to drift which means that
the polarization-dependence of the FOPA device oftentimes went above values of 1-3 dB in
a time span of 500 seconds which is detrimental since this cannot be implemented into a
communications system. However, at the beginning of each measurement, this polarization-
dependence was only at around 0.5 dB. This means that if the pump polarization can be
controlled continuously, a low polarization-dependence can be found. Specifically, we pro-
pose a feedback loop using an oscilloscope that measured the outcoming idler.
Finally, wavelength-dependency in the system also implemented distortions in our CE spec-
trum, which could be fixed by implementing a better PBS and circulator where the wavelength-
dependency is lower. Additionally, it was experimentally shown that SBS was proved to be
a far larger problem in the loop scheme due to sensitivity to reflections which could then
induce FWM with the oppositely propagating pump.
Before implementing this in an optical communications system, these problems need to be
solved and preferably in combination with additional RF tones during pump dithering and
with the dual-pump configuration, so that the maximum CE can be achieved with no addi-
tional phase noise.
