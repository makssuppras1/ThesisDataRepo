ABSTRACT 
ABSTRACT 
This thesis presents an inductive, empirically based research on the Danish non-profit 
voluntary space association Copenhagen Suborbitals. The purpose of the study was to execute a 
qualitative constructivist grounded theory study to discover and explain the behavior and operation 
of the case subject. Based on this, grounded theory methodology was found highly adequate, as it 
allowed an investigation without predetermined hypotheses, specific research questions, and a 
theoretical framework. The central question was: What are they actually doing? Therefore, prior to 
the study, the researcher formed no specific expectations or demands, and thus, the research could 
develop in either way. The primary data collection involved observations, open interviews, and 
conversations. Observations of meetings and conducted work at the organizational location enabled 
the study of participants who acted in their natural environment, while interviews and conversations 
enabled more concentrated and direct information. Secondary data was also collected, which 
primarily consisted of an extensive research of web-blog posts from the organization. 
The presentation structure is chronological and mimics the research development. Initially, 
a historical research, based on secondary data, presents the subject and serves as a preliminary 
analysis. Hereafter, the central analysis, which is based on primary research data, presents explicative 
findings and interpretive theories of the case subject. As the theories are grounded in research data, it 
only applies to the subject and is not universal. Therefore, subsequently to the analysis, the study is 
contextualized with literature to identify the academic influence.  
The study showed that the subject had successfully integrated the concepts of consensus and 
initiative, and achieved an organizational form that, at one level promoted member unity and 
collective steering, and at another level encouraged individual initiative. These findings were 
consequently put in the context of Mintzberg’s organizational configurations, where adequacy was 
found with the missionary and innovative forms. However, the language and categorization of 
Mintzberg’s model suggest that his theories do not consider hobby-associations. Moreover, the study 
discovered two distinctive operational processes: (1) direction, established a flexible organizational 
heading, and (2) navigation, allowed liberated groups to develop accordingly, but ensured that 
evaluation and coordination was done in collectivity. The processes of direction and navigation were 
congruent with well-known methods of trial-and-error and parallel trails. Consequently, these 
findings unexpectedly emerged to support contemporary claims about project management, which 
should adopt more integrating practices to better cope with contingencies.

DANISH ABSTRACT 
DANISH ABSTRACT 
Denne kandidatopgave præsenterer en induktiv, empirisk baseret undersøgelse af den danske 
amatør raketforening, Copenhagen Suborbitals, som er baseret på frivilligt arbejdende medlemmer. 
Formålet var, at udføre en kvalitativ konstruktivistisk grounded theory-undersøgelse, for derved at 
opdage og forklare emnets interne adfærd og handling. Grounded theory var yderst passende som 
metodegrundlag, da metodologien tillader en undersøgelse uden målrettede hypoteser, specifikke 
spørgsmål og teoretisk ramme. Det centrale spørgsmål var: Hvad gør de faktisk? Forinden 
undersøgelsen, blev ingen specifikke forventninger eller undersøgelseskrav defineret, hvilket betød 
at studiet kunne udvikle sig i adskillige retninger. Den primære dataindsamling bestod af 
observationer, åbne interviews og samtaler. Observationer af møder og i værksted, tillod studie af 
medlemmerne i deres naturlige miljø, mens interview og samtaler bidrog med mere koncentreret og 
direkte information. Sekundærdata blev hovedsageligt indsamlet igennem udførlig gennemgang af 
foreningens web-blog.  
Præsentationen af opgaven er kronologisk, og følger den faktiske udvikling i undersøgelsen. 
Begyndelsesvis er en historisk undersøgelse, baseret på sekundærdata, hvilket præsenterer emnet og 
fungerer som en indledende analyse.  Herefter følger den centrale analyse, som introducerer de 
understøttende og forklarende teorier, baseret på primærdata. Eftersom teorierne er begrundet i data 
om emnet, er de også kun anvendelige i forbindelse med emnet, og er ikke universale teorier. 
Efterfølgende analysen, bliver studiet sat i kontekst med litteratur og forhenværende teori, primært 
for at hæve det akademiske niveau.  
Studiet viste, at emnet med succes havde integreret initiativ og konsensus, og derved opnået 
en organisatorisk form, hvor de på den ene side fremhævede forening og kollektiv styring, og på den 
anden side opfordrede til individuelt initiativ. De organisatoriske former blev sat i forbindelse med 
Mintzbergs konfigurationer, hvor især formerne missionær og innovativ var passende. Dog antyder 
Mintzbergs sprog, at hans teorier ikke er passende for hobby-foreninger. Ydermere identificerede 
analysen to karakteristiske operationelle processer; (1) direction, som var en proces der grundlagde 
en fleksibel organisatorisk retning; (2) navigation, der tillod frigjorte grupper at udvikle i selvkontrol, 
men samtidig opretholdt en kollektiv vurdering og koordinering. Disse to processer var yderst 
sammenlignelige med de kendte metoder: trial-and-error og parallel trails, og i udledning af dette, 
udviklede resultaterne sig til at støtte nutidig litteraturs påstande om, at projektledelse har brug for 
mere integreret praksis, for bedre at kunne omgås med uvidenhed og kompleksitet.

INTRODUCTION 
1 INTRODUCTION 
“The probability of success is difficult to estimate; but if we never search, 
the chance of success is zero.” Cocconi and Morrison, 1959. 
On July 20th 1969, Neil Armstrong took a famous step; a step that defined a giant leap for 
mankind, and set a milestone in the human aspiration to explore and understand our existence. 
Furthermore, he confirmed the American success in their competition with the Russians, in what is 
commonly known as the space race. The famous step by Armstrong was also the climax of 
uncountable interconnected projects performed, executed, and managed by the American NASA (the 
National Aeronautics & Space Association). 
Originally, project management trails back to the development of nuclear missiles in the 
1950s. Back then, project management was a practice of coping with contingent and complex 
propositions, and early projects accepted risk and unforeseeable development as a part of the chance 
for success. In the early 1960s, the newly formed NASA, along with other significant institutions, 
adopted the discipline of project management in its operation. However, NASA facilitated a more 
analytical and process oriented approach than the original projects. Since then, the discipline has been 
extensively discussed, and the discipline as we know it today has been subject to continuous 
development and standardization. In recent years, the emphasis has especially become devoted to 
optimization of certain constraints: Scope, cost, and time; and some contemporary researchers claim 
that practice inadequately neglects the original methods. 
The present thesis investigates the case subject Copenhagen Suborbitals (CS). CS is a 
volunteer based non-profit association aspiring to send a human into space in a homemade rocket – 
The world’s only amateur space program; instantly the case raises questions of how and why. By 
developing comprehensive rocket technology in a hobby-association, CS challenges the common 
notion that rocket science is entitled the brightest minds on the planet.  
As the case subject was perceived as special and potentially interesting in many different 
directions, the study approach was chosen as inductive and empirical, and the methodology as 
grounded theory. With grounded theory there were neither hypotheses nor extant theory to be tested

2 THE STORY – PART I 
This chapter introduces the first six years of Copenhagen Suborbitals. This is presented here 
to provide the reader with useful background information, which is important to the present study. 
As it also is an interesting story, this chapter will hopefully further inspire your interest and 
motivation, as it did mine. 
The Origin 
In 2008, two men met in the hull of a submarine. They both had a passion for outer space, 
and shared dreams of making rockets that could take them to the moon. The submarine they met in 
was called UC3 Nautilus, the largest amateur submarine ever built [1]. It was also temporarily the 
home of one the men, Peter Madsen. Peter followed ideas without worrying about limits, and nobody 
could ever tell him to give up his dreams. He was once a student of mechanical engineering, but did 
not finish the education; he also started a machinery operator education, which he did not finish either. 
Madsen, generally known as an inventor and entrepreneur, called himself an artist engineer; a person 
that uses engineering science and mathematics to please and inspire other people [2]. The interest in 
space adventures was for Madsen, mostly on the rocket part, which he had experimented with since 
the age of ten in his father’s garage [3].With Madsen, it was always full speed ahead, as he would 
rather make, test, and redo, than stand still and get bored. He once wrote, “Exactly the one who has 
made mistakes, should be listened to” [4]. Furthermore, Madsen had a temper like an unstable rocket, 
and had problems with authorities and rules; conflicts tended to stick around him. 
The other man in the submarine was Kristian von Bengtson. Bengtson had a degree from 
The School of Architecture in Copenhagen, yet his career quickly took a turn towards space design, 
and in 2006, he acquired a master degree from The International Space University in France [1]. 
When he met with Madsen, he already had a fine carrier in space design and was earlier employed by 
the American space organization, NASA. Bengtson’s interest in this adventure was on space design 
and functionality, which meant development of a capsule and everything in touch with the astronaut.

2008 - The becoming 
The two men, depicted in Picture 1, agreed to work together on their dream; a mission most 
people would have discarded immediately because it seemed impossible. They wanted to prove that 
rocket science was not only entitled NASA and other governmental organizations [1]. The vision was 
to launch a manned homemade rocket into space and return it safely. As opposed to NASA, and 
general perception of rocket science, Madsen and Bengtson wanted to use relatively cheap materials 
and simple technology. When NASA built spaceships they used extremely light and strong but 
expensive materials, like carbon fiber and titanium. For NASA, minimizing weight made sense 
because their vehicles were launched several times, and thus they optimized costs on fuel savings. 
For instance, NASA Orbiter Columbia, which executed 28 space missions in its career [5]. However, 
Madsen and Bengtson did not care about the weight of the material, since their plan was to launch 
one disposable rocket into space, making the long-term fuel savings irrelevant [6]. They wanted to 
create a homemade rocket with enough power to lift an aluminum rocket, with one of them sitting 
inside it.

2009 – Growth 
January 2nd 2009, Madsen wrote a blog post at the website of Ingeniøren (The Engineer), a 
magazine for Danish engineers and people interested in engineering science [9]. The blog was named 
Rumfart på den anden måde (Alternative Space Travel), and became a great outlet for CS, and 
especially Peter Madsen. At this time, the project accelerated and the interest in the project was 
growing, mainly in engineering societies. In February that year, Madsen and Bengtson officially 
founded a company for the project, and named it Copenhagen Suborbitals [1]. The logo is depicted 
in Picture 3. The name referred to the city of origin and sub-orbital spaceflight: Intersecting the 
atmospheric border, without having an orbital trajectory and breaking the orbital velocity [10].

The interest in the blog exploded, and Madsen and Bengtson discovered that the blog 
constituted an important source of knowledge and resources. A commentary function facilitated a 
two-way communication between fans and CS, and people from the entire country commented, gave 
advice, and offered materials [12]. The blog was entertaining and had a personal and inspiring tone; 
it continuously used the Latin expression: Ad Astra, meaning to the stars, and often, Madsen wrote 
about childhood dreams, and believing in the unbelievable [11]. 
Later in 2009, CS started to reveal plans about the astronaut capsule. Bengtson had based 
the design on a previous design from the 1950s, where the astronaut was half sitting half standing. A 
terrifying detail was that the capsule had limited air supply and the astronaut would not be able to 
open it or get out himself. Furthermore, after landing in the ocean, he would be facing down until 
rescued. Regardless, this was basically Madsen’s dream coming true, and unsurprisingly, he 
volunteered to be the first astronaut if they ever got that far [13]. In April, CS got permission to fire 
from the sea, and immediately started the construction of a floating launch platform [14]. The 
initiative for the platform and the construction of it was decided within a moment. Quickly, CS had 
a rig with two almost 14 meter long steel hulls, one huge steel deck, and a 12 meter tall launch tower 
in the middle [15]. The platform was named Sputnik after the Russian satellite. Picture 4 shows a 
sketch and a photo of Sputnik.

The association was growing fast, and had around 16 active members within six months after 
its birth [15]. While blogging regularly about technical details, CS began to address the fact that 
funding a space rocket project, even though low tech and non-profit, was expensive. Therefore, the 
founders initiated a funding possibility on the website, where fans could make private donations [6]. 
The mission required many side projects with various different technologies and developments, and 
CS started to take the shape of a small space program. Madsen and Bengtson were the only ones 
engaged fulltime, while the rest were volunteers. The founders never profited on the association, and 
earned their living by talking at seminars and conferences [16] [17]. Around June, CS revealed plans 
of moving to a larger workshop at Refshaleøen [18]. However, it was too expensive, and Kristian von 
Bengtson informed the blog readers that, unless they raised money promptly, the association was in 
danger of shutting down. He asked blog readers for donations, and reminded them that it was yet a 
non-profit, open source program [19]. CS seemingly evolved faster than it could raise funds [20].

2010 – Being a space program 
At the beginning of 2010, several companies were involved as minor sponsors, and more 
than 500 people had made a one-time donation [21]. Bengtson expressed how it had occurred to him 
that people were genuinely interested in Copenhagen Suborbitals, and he was moved that fans spent 
their money to support it. The program was now situated in an old hangar, and had full pressure on 
development of technology. Thus, every time the association received funds, it was instantly used on 
more development. Consequently, although CS was in growth, it never had good economy [22].  
 
Picture 5. HEAT 1X on Sputnik in the dock [81]. 
August 31st 2010, CS and its members were on their way to the Danish island Bornholm, to 
execute the first official rocket launch [23]. The rocket was transported on the launch platform as 
seen in Picture 5. CS was far from the technology that could go suborbital; however, this was the first 
test in the line of development. A successful launch would be nothing less than pioneering in amateur 
rocket science, with their rocket, HEAT 1X, being approximately 500 times bigger than most amateur 
rockets [35]. During these days, the blog was frequently updated to include fans and sponsors.

As resources were always used instantly, the economic situation was recurring and at the 
end of September, CS was in deep economic trouble once again. The founders informed fans that

memberships, would raise money for the program [26] [27]. It had good support among fans, and 
within one week there were 130 members who gave a monthly donation to CS.

2011 – Challenges 
After being saved by fans – again, CS initiated further comprehensive technical 
development. In a blog post, Madsen wrote that he was testing and experimenting many different 
things, new engines, new fuel etc. [28]. A bifurcation started within the organization, as some 
members thought CS should stick to known technology, while other members contended CS should 
keep experimenting with new technology. Madsen essentially wanted to change the entire engine 
technology [29] [30]. This made some blog followers and fans frustrated as they thought CS 
constantly changed the technology, when they should have been more consistent [31] [32]. Madsen 
did not care. He came off extremely passionate and uncontrollable, and he wanted to test and develop 
in numerous different directions [33]. Despite this, Madsen stated that decisions were made in consent 
of all CS members, and not only by Bengtson and himself [34]. Picture 7 shows a photo of founders 
and members after a test.  
 
Picture 7. Members of Copenhagen Suborbitals after a static motor test [83]. 
CS was always in economic deficit, and Madsen and Bengtson were in constant search of 
money [35]. Once again, they informed their fans that CS was in danger of bankruptcy, and soon, 
donations had multiplied the capital with more than four factors [36]. Thus, the association evidently 
had a strong fan base and managed to raise money whenever needed; however, internally there were 
indications of disputes. Thirty-nine days before the re-scheduled attempt to launch HEAT 1X,

3 METHODOLOGY 
3.1 Background 
Copenhagen Suborbitals was immediately identified as a special arrangement. It was a non-
profit and volunteer based organization, yet, it developed technology commonly subjective to highly 
professional organizations. Rather than forcefully applying extant theory that would only somewhat 
fit the case study, it was chosen to perform an inductive exploratory research. The proposition offered 
a unique opportunity to explore and discover how such an organization operated, without being tied 
by hypotheses and research questions. Accordingly, the research methodology was grounded theory, 
which allowed me to investigate CS and use the raw data to conceptualize and ground theories in the 
findings. Grounded theory methodology was perceived as unconventional in the community of 
engineering management at DTU; hence, it offered an opportunity to contribute with diversity.  
3.2 No Hypothesis 
When performing a grounded theory research, there is no preset hypothesis (Glaser & 
Strauss, 1967, p. 164). By avoiding the application of hypotheses, data was unconstrained and enabled 
to evolve naturally. 
3.3 Purpose of Research 
The purpose was to perform a fulfilling grounded theory investigation of CS, and by 
induction discover internal elements and processes interesting and relevant to engineering 
management. No – or very few, grounded theory studies has been executed at DTU Management, 
thus the thesis could offer a fresh perspective in this community. Furthermore, the purpose was to 
analyze the obtained data with use of grounded theory techniques, and consequently to develop 
theories that explicated and predicted the internal behavior. Without testing hypotheses and with no 
ingoing theory, the research could potentially discover elements that might have been neglected in 
traditional research methods. According to Suddaby (2006), there is a general misconception that 
grounded theorists can develop their own findings while ignoring literature. To avoid this mistake, 
theoretical concepts and models grounded in data of the subject, were discussed and contextualized 
==PAGE:27==
 
17 
METHODOLOGY 
in relation to extant literature. However, the literature review was delayed until after the analysis, to 
keep the contribution pure. According to Charmaz (2006, p. 165), “delaying the review encourages 
you to articulate your ideas.”  
Conclusively, the objectives of the thesis were to: 
1. Execute a grounded theory study that fulfilled the methodological rules and criteria. 
2. Discover the actual elements of the subject through research data. 
3. Through analysis of data produce a deeper and abstract explication of the subject. 
4. Delay any literature review until the analysis was done. 
5. Contextualize the analysis with extant literature and theory. 
3.4 Research Philosophy 
The philosophical foundation for the research was constructivism. “A constructivist 
approach places priority on the phenomena of study and sees both data and analysis as created from 
shared experiences and relationships with participants” (Charmaz, 2006, p. 130). Therefore, data was 
not perceived as factual reality but as interpretations of reality, and thus both the research participants 
and I, as researcher, have been significant elements in the generation of data. Furthermore, this 
implied that everything experienced in connection to the research was data, since interpretations of 
reality was influenced by personal impressions and relationships. Prior to the research I, as a student, 
had been educated in project management and organizational theory, which created a risk of biasing 
the data with extant knowledge. According to Charmaz (2006), constructivists are aware that pre-
assumptions may affect the research, and they attempt to define the extent hereof. Consequently, 
constructivist grounded theorists are placed in a reality, where they have to balance empirical findings 
with their extant knowledge (Suddaby, 2006).  
In order to claim the outcome to be theoretical, theory was defined. Positivist theory seeks 
causes, favors deterministic explanations, and emphasizes generality and universality; interpretive 
theory calls for the imaginative understanding of the studied phenomenon (Charmaz, 2006, p. 126). 
The contribution of this research could not offer generic deterministic theory of project management, 
as it was only based on one case subject. However, it proposed a subjective conceptualized theoretical 
==PAGE:28==
 
18 
METHODOLOGY 
explication of the premises investigated in Copenhagen Suborbitals. Thus, the theoretical outcome of 
this research was defined as being interpretive rather than positivistic.  
3.5 Research Method 
This section presents grounded theory and the literature used for methodological research. 
Furthermore, the methodological fundamental techniques are presented, and finally a critic view on 
grounded theory. 
3.5.1 
Grounded theory methodology. 
Grounded theory was introduced by Glaser & Strauss (1967). Their claim was that a theoretic 
statement generated by research data, was more likely to predict and explain the subject, rather than 
a theory attained by traditional deduction (Glaser & Strauss, 1967). The core philosophy of grounded 
theory is pragmatic, as it was founded to help researchers understand complex situations, in a practical 
manner (Suddaby, 2006). This methodology was highly adequate in the present study, as it allowed 
a thorough examination of Copenhagen Suborbitals, without becoming biased by preset hypotheses.  
“Grounded theory methods foster seeing your data in fresh ways and exploring your ideas about the 
data through early analytic writing” (Charmaz, 2006, p. 2). By accepting grounded theory I, as a 
researcher, positioned myself with an opportunity to contribute with something unique and special to 
the engineering management community. It is important to understand that grounded theory only was 
a method for data collection and development of analysis; hence, it did not offer further 
contextualization.  
There are no rules of how grounded theory work should be presented (Suddaby, 2006). 
However, it felt logic to present the findings in the natural, and same chronological manner as the 
research was performed. This also prevented “methodological slurring” (Suddaby, 2006, p. 637), 
where grounded researchers claim to have pure findings while their presentation indicates otherwise. 
Consequently, to stay true to the methodology, the structure of the report was aimed at presenting the 
research similar to its chronological development.

3.5.2 
Methodological literature review. 
The methodology was unknown to me prior to the beginning of the study; thus the following 
literature was used to acquire knowledge. George Allan’s article, The Use of the Grounded Theory 
Methodology in Investigating Practitioners' Integration of COTS Components in Information Systems 
(2007), and Georgieva and Allan’s article, Best Practices in Project Management Through a 
Grounded Theory Lens (2008), examined whether the method was viable for researchers, and 
provided with general information of the research fundamentals. Joyce Rothschild-Whitt’s article, 
The Collectivist Organization: An Alternative to Rational-Bureaucratic Models (1979), and the work 
by Lalonde, Bourgault and Findeli, An empirical investigation of the project situation: PM practice 
as an inquiry process (2010), offered direct insight to how grounded theory was used in research. The 
article, From The Editors: What Grouded Theory Is Not (2006) by Roy Suddaby, emphasized 
confusion about grounded theory, and discussed common misconceptions and important elements. 
This aided me in avoiding unwanted situations of methodological mistakes. The main source for 
grounded theory was the book by Professor of Sociology, Kathy Charmaz; Constructing Grounded 
Theory from 2006. According to Charmaz, “My version of grounded theory returns to the classical 
statements of the past century and reexamines them through a methodological lens of the present 
century” (2006, p. xi).  
3.5.3 
Central techniques. 
The central methodological techniques were important to the development of data, and were 
followed to rightfully claim that the grounded theory study fulfilled the requirements of the 
methodology. This relates to thesis objective 1, in section 3.31. 
 Open coding was a technique used to break down the data. According to Charmaz (2006), coding 
had two different phases; initial- and focused coding. Initial coding allowed detailed investigation 
of the data, and was the first step in the research after data collection. This process broke down 
the data in lines and words. Focused coding was a process where the initial findings were

compared and organized in a larger perspective. Thus, it allowed coding in sections and 
paragraphs rather than lines, and it gathered categories of data connected to the same finding. 
 Theoretical coding was the most sophisticated process of coding. It made connections and 
comparison between focused codes and allowed for a deeper analytical level. These codes 
conceptualized the relations of the substantive codes, and moved the analysis in a theoretical 
direction (Charmaz, 2006). Thus, the theoretical coding transformed data and ideas into an 
analytically generated theoretical explication, being the contribution of the research. 
 Memoing was a continuous process of free-writing reflective notes on codes, concepts and 
categories. “Memo-writing constitutes a crucial method in grounded theory because it prompts 
you to analyze your data and codes early in the research process” (Charmaz, 2006, p. 72). This 
method enabled deeper understanding, by early and liberated analytical writing in all directions 
(Allan, 2007; Charmaz, 2006). Memoing developed initial codes to focused codes, and by 
applying more data, focused codes to theoretical categories. 
 Theoretical sampling, was a process of evaluating the categories and the data behind them, and 
then focusing the further research on a few selected areas. Eventually, data reached a higher level 
of abstraction (Suddaby, 2006). 
3.5.4 
Method critique. 
Georgieva and Allan (2008) described grounded theory as still being regarded with 
skepticism by many academics. According to Charmaz (2006), grounded theory has been 
misunderstood, and many researchers have stated to execute grounded theory, but not followed the 
rules and guidelines of it. There is a misconception that grounded theory develops universally 
applicable theory, which does not need further testing or contextualizing. Hence, many researchers 
claim their work to be the reality, when it de facto only explains the studied environment. In order to 
apply grounded theory in other contexts than its origin, it has to be tested and proved first (Allan, 
2007). 
There is a bifurcation in the tradition of grounded theory, which has divided researchers; 
objectivists and constructivists.  Objectivist grounded theory resides in the positivist tradition where 
data is real, and it does not consider the production of it (Charmaz, 2006). As all data is coded and 
==PAGE:31==
 
21 
METHODOLOGY 
memoed by the researcher, it is difficult to acknowledge the positivist view that the interpretation of 
data and analysis is insignificant, which makes this type of grounded theory questionable to some. 
Constructivist grounded theory prioritize the problem of study, and it understands the data and 
outcome as generated from experiences and relationships between the researcher and participants 
(Charmaz, 2006). As this philosophical form embraces interpretivism, it acknowledges that the 
researcher will have personal influence on the data and analyses. Thus, the outcome of constructivist 
grounded theory will vary with the researcher, which makes this type questionable as well. 
Conclusively, researchers are divided because their theory is either grounded in real data or 
socially constructed data. In order to research with grounded theory, the researcher has to follow the 
rules and be aware of methodological pitfalls. An objectivist grounded theorists has to prove the 
personal influence to be insignificant and the data to be factual; a constructivist grounded theorist has 
to acknowledge the personal influence and be aware of it effects on the data (Charmaz, 2006).

3.9 Limitations 
The research was limited due to several identified factors of methodology, data, and time. 
These are presented in the following. 
3.9.1 
Grounded theory. 
There was a philosophical bifurcation in the methodology that parted researchers. These are 
described in section 3.5.4, under method critique. Nonetheless, the limitation here was to accept either 
philosophy, possibly narrowing the acceptance of my research. Personally, I acknowledge the logic 
of both views. 
3.9.2 
Data. 
Data was only collected from one case study, thus the generic appliance and the discussion 
was naturally less reliable in universal context. A generically stronger contribution would have been 
based on more organizations, and developed a theory grounded in more data.  
Several factors were observed to possibly compromise the data. First of all, data was 
interpreted by me and there was risk of influence by stereotyping or appliance of preconceived 
==PAGE:39==
 
29 
METHODOLOGY 
knowledge. Second, there was a risk that sources were acting abnormally in my presence. These are 
further discussed in section 8.3.  
Only interviews were recorded, thus conversations were not. After conversations an audio 
review was made to summarize the points, as it was still fresh in memory. However, important 
information might have been lost. 
The members of CS were busy people working full time during the day and in CS in their 
free time. A few times it was experienced that participants simply did not have time for my research. 
This was found as a limitation of primary sources.  
Another limitation connected here, was the fact that all data was collected in Danish, yet the 
thesis written in English. This caused linguistic interpretation of all data, and a risk of points getting 
lost in translation. 
3.9.3 
Time. 
Qualitative research methods require time, and especially with grounded theory, as I could 
not scope my investigation and ask determined questions. I had to let the data come to me. Time was

5.1 Structure 
CS never had an official structure, and according to KS they, “have always talked more about 
structure, than actually having it” (PS #7). In the past, the members were always put in the 
background, were less informed, and often excluded from decisions (PS #5). Other data was 
supportive of that statement, indicating that the founders led the organization similar to a dictatorship. 
Things started to change when the board was democratically elected to handle the internal issues and 
conflicts of CS. There were five board members; each elected to have different focus areas. However, 
it was observed that these areas were not strictly fulfilled (PS #8). The elected board members were 
not granted authoritative power, did not have specific rights, were not owners of the project, and were 
not in charge of the technology. Their roles were to ensure that all the problematic issues in CS were 
handled, and as well as to ensure that CS could continue to pursue their vision: “To launch a human 
being into space, and bring him safely back to earth” [78]. KS stated that in the period of conflicts 
the association had been going through, what he called, “management of crisis,” (PS #7)  
5.1.1 
State of crisis. 
When the board initiated, the key emphasis was to completely decentralize power structures 
and remove hierarchy, acquiring member equality in an informal organizational structure. About 
formal structure, KS said, “We do not need it.” (PS #7). 
5.1.1.1 
The structural modifications. 
At this time, CS recognized all members to be equally important to the mission, and by 
rejecting hierarchy and emphasizing unity, the board created an egalitarian model where consensus 
was the key stone. Figure 1 depicts a model of the informal structure that demonstrates the anti-
hierarchy. According to sources, no one had executive power or ownership in the association. Thus, 
all members were placed at the same level in the collective group. Within the collective group there 
were a few individual groups. In the model, the motor group and the capsule group are the original 
two departments created by the founders; the other two sections are the newly created board, and a 
few minor groups, depicted by one called others. According to KS, the division of groups in the crisis 
==PAGE:45==

state was very vague, “Earlier we did not really have specific groups. We had people and tasks” (PS 
#11). 
To enable a distinction between collective- and individual level activities, they are defined 
as respectively micro and macro,  also illustrated in the model. I must emphasize that these two 
notions were not given by sources; it is a pragmatic definition of observed structural areas. The model 
demonstrates the following findings: (1) the micro groups were highly interconnected and 
overlapping; (2) the association only had few groups; and (3) the structural form allowed members to 
be present in several groups at a time (except the board, which required election).

Notice, how the board is a part of the collective on equal level with the remaining groups, 
which demonstrates that CS rejected the notion of hierarchy. The board dealt with issues that did not

stakeholders, and practical issues. The board was not executive; however, its responsibility areas 
could be of steering character, i.e. economic decisions connected to the terms of direction, analyzed 
in section 5.2.2.1. In conclusion, a noteworthy finding here was that the board was equally important

CS rejected hierarchy, but still had a chairman. His role was found to be an unusual figure,

This statement was supported by CM, who said that KS had never abused his position, but only

questions to start a discussion – or to finish it. At an observation, he continuously asked, “So what do 
we do with this?” or, “What is the next step then?” (PS #4) 
5.1.1.2 
Benefits of the structural change.  
In the present section, the structural advantages are presented as: Unity, communication, 
enthusiasm, knowledge sharing, and effectivity. These benefits were established by introducing 
consensus, structural equality, decentralization of power structures, and an information obligation. 
5.1.1.2.1 Unity. 
One of the main functions of the structure was to decrease the internal conflicts immediately. 
By decentralizing power, the members were equalized and put on the same level. Without hierarchy, 
members were encouraged to work together, and made decisions about the association in consensus. 
With informal structure, CS had achieved a state where members were equal and involved in decision-
making and important processes; “Now we actually feel like we are involved in the project” (PS #3). 
Discussions were often extensive, and indicated that the subject and atmosphere was inspiring. MW 
stated that currently the members were driven by being part of the project, rather than just helping the 
founders. CM supported this quote, and told that in the past, the members were saying “why are we 
even in this?” (PS #5).  Evidently, the changes had the desired effect as, “conflicts have vigorously 
been reduced” (PS #7).

5.1.1.2.2 Communication. 
KS stated that improved communication was an important factor for creating unity. Evidence 
showed that leading up to the crisis, operations were constantly initiated without other members being 
informed. KS further stated that before the decentralization, “to be informed, one would have to be 
present in the exact situation” (PS #7). According to MW, “they (the founders) did not tell the 
members what was going to happen, but still delegated all kinds of tasks in the workshop” (PS #3). 
The communication was always an issue in the past, and MW explained that often members were 
unaware of CS’ direction. “Sometimes we did not figure out what we were doing, before Peter 
(Madsen) wrote it in his blog. The internal communication was that bad” (PS #3). 
According to KS, the board had introduced an obligation to be informative. In the structural 
model depicted in Figure 1, information could travel easily between groups and members, and in the 
collective of the organization.  KS continuously emphasized the importance of individual groups 
being informative to the collective group, and told that, “One of my functions is to remind members 
to inform the others” (PS #7). This statement further evidenced his role as a chairman; he was not an 
executive, but more a social figure. The information obligation was a substantial part of creating a 
shared foundation and healthy communication.  
Along with the information obligation, the board initiated Monday meetings – a weekly 
collective gathering of all groups and all members. At this meeting, current ideas, processes, and 
developments were presented to the collective at macro level. According to KS, the Monday meeting 
was a central initiative in the change. He stated that, “We wanted to find a way where people heard 
about ideas early in the process” (PS #7). The meetings enabled internal communication, and 
observations confirmed them as very informative; although, it did require members to be present.  
The internal information system was based on meeting summaries and emails. According to 
MW, more than one hundred emails were often sent and received daily in CS, and often he did not 
have time to read all of them. KS expressed that it was impossible to stay informed through emails, 
and that it was simply too much for volunteer members. This was supported by observations, where 
members seemed surprised and uninformed about ongoing activity. During the research, the members 
==PAGE:48==

agreed that they wanted to establish an online server for sharing of documents. According to KS, it 
would reduce the number of daily emails and delimit confusion. 
5.1.1.2.3 Enthusiasm. 
Data indicated that members associated pressure with hierarchy. According to MW, the 
decentralization was an important factor behind the member enthusiasm, because an authoritative top 
steering would entail unwanted pressure on the members. He said that, “It would ruin the joy about 
it, if it was pressured through” (PS #3). Several sources supported the claim that pressure was not the 
way to encourage volunteers. KS asked, “Does one come here to work for others?” (PS #7). He did 
not believe so, which supported the effect of decentralizing power, and operating without a steering 
group.  
Moreover, members were volunteers and had jobs and families. The fact that they took their 
time to be in the workshop, indicated that they were thriving in the environment. Observations showed 
that the social atmosphere in CS was prospering, and at any given moment, there was laughter and 
passionate conversations about rocket engines and science. The social environment was very 
important to CM, who claimed to be, “a part of something very special, with some very special 
people” (PS #5).  
5.1.1.2.4 Knowledge sharing. 
The model in Figure 1, illustrates that groups had intersecting boundaries, and that members 
were enabled to be present in several groups simultaneously. They were constantly developing their 
skills, and there was a high level of knowledge sharing and learning. MW told that since he started in 
CS, he had learned how to weld, operate big machines, make electronic receivers, and how a rocket 
engine is put together.

5.1.1.2.5 Effectivity. 
Sources indicated that in the past, decisions were hasty, which often led to useless creations 
that had to be discarded. The structural change created unity and consensus seeking, and members 
were observed to discuss decisions and processes until agreement. This led to substantial decisions, 
which ensured an effective operation; meaning that CS was doing the right things, and made few 
mistakes. 
5.1.1.3 
Issues of the crisis state. 
The egalitarian structure entailed equality and consensus, and was found as a beneficial 
initiative in many ways. The analysis disclosed positive results on: Unity, communication, 
enthusiasm, knowledge sharing, and effectivity. On the other hand, the following issues were also 
found: 
a) Overlapping groups caused chaos and unspecific focus areas. Many operations and technologies 
were not specified to a group, but was situated somewhere in between. For instance, the capsule 
group could be working on the same activity as the motor group, without a specification of where 
the task actually resided. Unspecific group focus caused complicated consensus seeking. 
According to KS, when many people had responsibility of an area, the process tended to be lost 
between them; “With consensus alone, we get nowhere” (PS #11).  
b) CS had approximately 40-50 members during the state of crisis operation, yet it only had a few 
individual groups. Therefore, in most situations many members were present, and took part in the 
consensus seeking. This was defined as shared space, which caused a very complicated and slow 
consensus process.  
c) Because CS was a voluntary association, members could not be controlled. Thus, members acted 
out of interest, and were occupied with many different activities simultaneously. According to 
KS, there was a tendency for some tasks to be compromised. He said, “Some members are 
involved in too many things, and never finish their activities” (PS #7). Conclusively, uncontrolled 
members were unproductive for CS.

40

Conclusively, evidence indicated that many processes in CS were dominated by complicated 
consensus seeking, due to unspecific focus and shared space. Consensus was the key stone in the 
structural development, and was found to have several benefits. Nevertheless, “With consensus, we 
sit and stare at each other, and nothing happens” KS expressed (PS #11).  
5.1.2 
State of normal operation. 
At the time of the research, the association was in transition to a more normal state of 
operation. KS said, “We need an organizational form that does not require administrative roles, 
because we do not have that” (PS #11). It is important to remember that the board and the chairperson 
were elected to make sure that the organization was well functioning. They were expected to 
implement changes. Therefore, when KS introduced modifications, he was simply playing his role in 
the structural model and he was not executive. When asked whether the board in reality could execute 
a plan without consent, KS said, “We could try, but we would not succeed” (PS #11). Decisions 
required consent; if the collective did not agree, it did not go through.  
5.1.2.1 
Clarification of group area. 
KS had a wish to specify the focus area of the micro groups. “The members are the reason 
for the success. Their level is incredibly high” (PS #7). He believed that enabling members to step 
forward within their respective area, would increase the productivity. KS claimed that the change was 
already visible in CS. Observations during meetings supported this claim, where it was evidenced 
that members were divided into several different areas. Figure 2 depicts a model of the structure with 
increased group clarification.

5.1.2.2 
Member distribution. 
In section 0, it was found that members were allowed to be in many different groups 
simultaneously. According to KS, the board wanted to assess this in order to increase productivity. 
He stated that, “resource allocation was initiated to make sure that skills were not lost due to the 
extent of the structure” (PS #7). He told that he had written a proposal of member distribution, and 
sent it to all members, with an option to protest. However, neither KS, nor any other part of 
Copenhagen Suborbitals had authority to move members around, and decide what groups they could 
join. Consequently, the initiative was unsuccessful. MW stated that members were liberated to join

groups out of interest, and that there were no rules about it (PS #3). It was a premise of CS, as a 
voluntary association, that members could not be arranged. Doing so required an executive act, which 
would challenge the rejection of hierarchy, and possibly reverse the improvement of unity and 
decreased conflicts.  
5.1.2.3 
Benefits of the group clarification. 
Clarifying group areas had several positive effects. First, it enabled individual groups to 
concentrate on specific tasks, which simplified consensus seeking because members had specific 
areas to agree upon. With simple consensus, groups were much more likely to take quick and effective 
decisions, than with complicated consensus. Second, with the emergence of groups, members were 
more scattered across the association, and in individual groups there were suddenly less members 
present. A reduced amount of members, further simplified consensus, but more importantly, members 
attained individual space, rather than shared space. Consequently, this encouraged members to take 
initiative to start activities and make progress. Two examples supported the preceding claims:  
a) The GPS group was in charge of a small technological area, and its responsibilities were specific. 
It had few, specialized members and clear tasks. Members in this group were in consent about 
their developments, and they were observed as very active. This group was evidenced as having 
a good balance of initiative and consensus, and was the most active group during data collection. 
b) On the other hand, the motor group was in charge of an extensive and complex area, and actually 
consisted of several different technologies. It had high variety in tasks, and required many 
different skills. Thus, many members were encouraged to join in. The direction was often unclear 
and members spent time on seeking consensus instead of developing rockets. Furthermore, 
initiative was observed as very low. The reason hereof, was that the motor group still had a too 
unspecific focus, and relatively many members. Consequently, it had a more complicated 
consensus process than the GPS group, and as members had little individual space, they took little 
initiative. The motor group was relatively unproductive and slow, and it was dominated by 
consensus seeking.

Consequently, initiative and consensus were enabled by situational factors. Unspecific focus 
created complicated consensus; specific focus created simple consensus. Shared space was dominated 
by consensus, while individual space enabled individual initiative. By clarifying focus area some 
groups were enabled to be more productive, and in larger perspective CS optimized the operation by 
transitioning towards normal state.  
5.1.3 
Additional findings. 
Initiative and consensus were enabled by situational factors. However, this section analyze 
the underlying motivating roots of these two elements. Additionally, a hierarchy of presence is

5.1.3.1 
The roots of consensus. 
a) Vision. 
Copenhagen Suborbitals had a strong and dreaming vision. KS stated it as: “Proving that we with 
simple resources, can do what earlier required a governmental budget” (PS #11).  However, the 
vision was not observed as the daily motivation in the workshop, but more as a strong underlying 
foundation of motivation reminding members why they were doing it. The vision was a realization 
of many members’ childhood dreams, and they wished to accomplish it. Thus, vision was a strong 
motivation of why they wanted to consent and succeed.  
b) Equality. 
By decentralizing the structure and rejecting the notion of hierarchy, the members were put on 
the same organizational level. Equality among members created member unity and increased the 
enthusiasm towards the mission. Due to the history of conflicts, the equality was important to the 
members, and it was observed how they honored it at meetings, by showing mutual respect to one 
another. Everybody had a chance to be part of consensus, and everybody wanted to.  
c) Social atmosphere. 
Observations showed that the social atmosphere was good, and according to sources, it had never 
been better. At meetings, members were laughing and joking, and it was observed how they 
==PAGE:54==
 
44

enjoyed each other’s company. The good social atmosphere was evidenced to contribute to the 
consensus seeking, as they all wanted to be part of the environment.  
5.1.3.2 
The roots of initiative. 
d) Seniority and character. 
Formally, there was no difference between novel and long-time members. However, novel 
members were observed as more hesitant than other members. During discussions they tended to 
retire, not because they were repressed, but because they were in a new environment. According 
to CM, this was normal people behavior, “I believe it is normal that new people stand back a little 
in the beginning” (PS #5). Indirectly, it was implied that seniority members were more active than 
novel members; thus, unofficially they took more initiative.  
CM stated that dominant characters had significantly been reduced in CS. In the past, Peter 
Madsen’s explosive personality had dominated the association, and according to CM this behavior 
was now unwanted by all members. This was supported in observations, where members 
sometimes seemed afraid to speak in the collective group. CM believed this was the consequence 
of the dictator-like years with the founders. In his words: “No one dares to step forward” (PS #5). 
Even though dominant personalities were unwanted, it was still observed that character 
differences enforced some members to take initiative and be active, while others were more 
reserved and discreet. 
A significant discovery with high initiative, was that it linked directly to individualism, hasty 
decisions and hierarchy. All these were, according to the data, a reason for conflicts and crisis. 
Conclusively, too much initiative suggested individualism and conflicts.  
e) Inspiration. 
Inspiration was defined as members’ drive to do specific tasks. It was observed how inspiring 
tasks encouraged members to take initiative. According to PM, “It has to be fun” before he wanted 
to do it (PS #4). According to MW, it was inspiring to have responsibility of important areas. 
Hence, inspiration was a root of initiative.

The inspiration was a special element, as members were volunteers and could not be pressured to 
doing tasks they did not want to do. According to KS, “we have problems getting some things 
done” (PS #7). Some tasks did not inspire initiative; for instance, insulating the meeting area was 
obviously very little inspiring. This task was on CS’ meeting agenda during the entire research, 
without being initiated. The collective group talked about it, and one joked that if they were lucky, 
they might not freeze next winter. KS answered that if anybody was feeling cold, they should take

the association down, yet there was no apparent solution to it. This, is elaborated in section 5.2.3.3. 
f) Personal pride. 
According to several sources, the association had about ten new member applications each week. 
Being involved with CS was a privilege, and members were observed to be proud of their role. 
Thus, being able to contribute to the operation, and be purposeful, was an important element in 
their personal motivation, and a source of individual initiative.  
g) Special skills. 
Some activities in CS required special abilities, and could only be completed by few members. In 
some situations, an activity simply required a definite member to take initiative.  
5.1.3.1 Hierarchy of presence. 
Copenhagen Suborbitals was a voluntary association based on leisure. Although the 
following might seem obvious, it was still an important element: If members wanted to take initiative, 
or participate in consensus, they had to be present in the situation. This detail implied an underlying 
relative hierarchy of presence, illustrated in Figure 3.  
==PAGE:56==

Figure 3. Hierarchy of presence. Defines influence in relation to participation. 
The model illustrates that participation enabled influence. Following is an example. The PR 
group identified the opportunity and necessity, for a redesign of logo and website. The group 
presented the first draft to the collective during a Monday meeting. The collective discussed it and 
agreed on some modifications. Following, the PR group were to make a new proposal and present it 
again later. Importantly, the collective agreement and decision was made only among those present 
at the Monday meeting. Even though a meeting summary and pictures were shared with the remaining 
members, they were not included in the process. As a result, non-present members did not have 
influence, and in theory, the collective group did not make the decision, as it was not fully represented.  
Furthermore, an observed tendency indicated that 10-15 members were more present than 
the remaining 20-30 members. Thus approximately one third of the association had more underlying 
power than the rest. 
5.1.4 
Sub-conclusion on structure. 
In the state of crisis, CS denied hierarchical structure and emphasized equality and 
consensus. All groups, including the board, was situated at the same structural level in an egalitarian 
model. The chairman was not an executive authority, but a broker of peace that knew how to ask the 
right questions to encourage the other members. This created organizational unity and an environment 
of consensus, with increased communication, good social atmosphere, and effective decisions. 
However, CS was evidenced to be unproductive due to inefficiency. Unspecific focus areas and too 
==PAGE:57==
 
47

many members in each group, caused complicated consensus and little individual initiative, which 
caused slow processes and long-drawn discussions.  
To increase productivity the association was in transition towards a state of normal 
operation. By clarifying technological focus area and forming more groups, CS enabled more specific 
and concentrated tasks, and less members present in groups. This evidently caused simple consensus, 
enabling members to take the right decisions with less long-drawn discussions. Furthermore, groups 
had increased individual space, which encouraged members to take individual initiative. The benefits 
were not observed equivalent in all groups, as some groups were relatively unspecific and overfilled. 
These groups were dominated by complicated consensus, and had low initiative.  
On the good side, consensus fostered unity and effective decisions. However, when many 
people had to consent, the efficiency was extremely low. Initiative fostered activity and increased 
efficiency. However, too much initiative were linked to individualism, and conflicts. Both initiative 
and consensus had underlying motivators, called roots. The roots of consensus were the vision, 
equality, and social environment. The roots of initiative was seniority and character, inspiration, 
personal pride, and special skills.   
In order to be part of consensus or to take individual initiative, members had to be present. 
This created an underlying hierarchy of presence, indicating that approximately one third of the 
association was in relative charge because they were present.

5.2 Steering 
Where the first part of the analysis investigates the structural developments and the effects 
hereof, the second part emphasizes internal operational processes. The term steering was never

undetailed and unfixed, and the most important aspect was to create mutual expectations. This process 
was enabled by increased communication and member equality, presented in section 5.1.1.2. With 
mutual expectations CS limited hasty conclusions, and increased the substance of decisions.

Micro navigation. 
In navigation, groups were absolutely self-controlled, and only had to respect the direction.

5.2.4 
Sub-conclusion on Steering. 
This part of the analysis concerned steering in CS, a problematic discipline throughout time. 
Steering had two significant aspects: Direction and navigation. Direction was the process of making 
a flexible organizational heading, with the terms of respecting the budget and having progress. It was 
divided in two levels; macro- and micro direction. Macro direction was a flexible collectively created 
base plan, built on shared expectations and broad communication. This process was subject to shared 
space, and required complicated consensus; thus, it was slow and long-drawn. Micro direction was a 
more detailed and focused plan, made by the individual groups. Here, the focus was more specific, 
consensus was simpler, and the individual space and initiative higher. Consequently, micro direction

Navigation was concerned with trying to stay on the direction. Groups had self-control and 
were liberated to navigate within the direction, as long as they were informative about their operation. 
At macro level, collective evaluation and coordination, ensured that the groups pursued the same 
overall direction. If coordination revealed deviations in micro developments, it was discussed and 
coordinated; if deviations required an adjustment of direction, CS could go back to the iteration 
process. 
Finally, it was found that unity had a significant cost connected to steering. Nothing could 
be forced, and members could not be arranged. Thus, some tasks were not completed unless members 
decided to take initiative. This especially involved practical and uninspiring tasks.

5.3.1 
Brief recap. 
The state of crisis introduced an egalitarian organizational form where equality, unity, and 
consensus was fundamental. Thus, CS avoided quick and ineffective decisions, and critically reduced 
conflicts. However, it was evidenced that consensus was complicated, and CS lacked individual 
initiative; too much shared space, too many members, and too broad focus areas. Accordingly, by 
clarifying areas and introducing more groups, the board enabled increased specific focus and 
scattering of members, enabling individual space. This created simple consensus and individual 
initiative.  
On the good side, consensus fostered unity and effective substantial decisions; initiative 
fostered activity and efficiency. However, when many people had to consent, it was too complicated 
and the efficiency was extremely low; when initiative became dominating it inferred individualism, 
and were connected to hasty decisions and conflicts. Both consensus and initiative originated from

underlying motivators, called roots. The roots of consensus were the vision, equality and the social 
environment. The roots of initiative was seniority and character, inspiration, personal pride, and 
special skills.   
The steering analysis was divided in direction and navigation. Macro direction was a 
collective activity that required consensus to establish shared expectations; micro direction was a 
group activity with specific focus and simple consensus, enabling a specific plan of micro 
development. The process of making direction was initially steered by a few ingoing terms, but then

7.1 Organizational Perspective 
Organizational theory is a widely studied field with attention from both academics and 
practitioners. According to Aldrich (as sited in, Aldrich & Ruef, 1999, p. 4, 2nd Ed.) “Organizations 
are goal-directed, boundary-maintaining, and socially constructed systems of human activity.” Thus, 
organizations are shaped from forces that rely upon deterministic design, internal coordination,   
expectations, people behavior, and enactment (Poole & Van de Ven, 1989; Sutcliffe & Weick, 2005). 
Baecker (2006) further emphasize the social system in organizations and state that it “consists of 
people who forgo certain interest in the interests of the organization and who accept rules of behavior 
that would surprise anyone observing such behavior outside the organization” (p. 113).  
Mintzberg presents, in one of his first books: The Structuring of Organizations (1979), a 
study concerning the organizational design. His concepts has been excessively discussed and tested 
throughout time, thus perceived as a well-tested and appreciated theory. His work has developed 
somewhat, and was recently rewritten for the book, The Strategy Process: concepts, contexts, cases 
(Lampel, Mintzberg, & Ghosal, 2014). 
All organizations have internal variables tending to pull in a certain direction, forming a 
distinct organizational configuration (Mintzberg, 2014). The basic configurations suggested in 
Mintzberg’s (1980) firsts publications are: Simple (also known as entrepreneurial organization), 
machine, professional, divisionalized (also known as diversified), and adhocracy (also known as 
innovative). Later, Mintzberg (2014) added the missionary and political organizations. His theory 
==PAGE:73==
 
63 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
extends the research by Hage (1965), who only offers a distinction between organic and mechanical 
organizations. Both Hage’s and Mintzberg’s models are positivistic, where the organization is either 
or. Yet, Mintzberg’s framework offers a far more detailed distinction than Hage’s. Theory confining 
organizational structure has, by a number of academics, been argued to be finite, superficial and 
collective (Brown & Iverson, 2004; Dess, Newport & Rasheed, 1993; Greenwood & Hinings, 1988; 
Greenwood, Hinings & Ranson, 1980; Poole & Van de Ven. 1989), and both Mintzberg and Hage 
acknowledge their theories as archetypes that are ideal and extreme (Hage, 1965; Mintzberg, 2014). 
Poole and Van de Ven (1989) argue that organizations are too complex for simple theories to offer 
an adequate description. Therefore, in practice, an organization is likely to mirror itself in more than 
one of the archetypes (Greenwood & Hinings, 1988).  
The different internal pulls within an organization is, according to Mintzberg (1980; 2014), 
affecting the internal organizational parts. He further claims that an organization consist of six 
different internal parts: 
1) the operating core is the base of the organization;  
2) the strategic apex is the top executives;  
3) the middle line creates a hierarchy and connection between the core and the top;  
4) the technostructure is placed outside the operating hierarchy, and controls and administrates the 
jobs and tasks of the core, according to the strategy;  
5) the support staff is also placed outside the main operation, and consists of internal services;  
6) and the ideology is the underlying organizational culture.  
Mintzberg (2014) further established some important elements that distinct the individual 
organization. (1) The coordinating mechanism defines the internal arrangements of the organizational 
parts, described above. (2) The design parameters, describes the internal formalization of jobs and 
behavior, training activities, groupings, planning and control, and power (de)centralization. Hinings 
and Greenwood (1988), support these two distinctions, claiming that organizational configurations 
consist of structures and processes.  
==PAGE:74==

Hage (1965) argues that in order for characteristic factors to apply to research, they have to 
be distinctive, differentiating between similar and different organizations, with similar or different 
goals. Thus, he claims that the factors were abstract and unbounded by contingency. However, 
Mintzberg diversify his research by adding a situational factor into the equation, where the last 
element is the factor of (3) contingency, tied to the study of contingency theory. The structural form 
and internal processes have to fit the context; thus, it adds variables of culture, environment 
technology, size, age or task (Mintzberg, 2014; Drazin & Van de Ven, 1985). Adding contingency,

affect the same outcome (Drazin & Van de Ven, 1985).  
7.2 Innovation Perspective 
Innovation is commonly perceived as a good thing, and Kimberly (1981) identifies the 
reason hereof, claiming that innovation implies new ideas that are helpful and profitable, while ideas 
that are not profitable are rather called mistakes or failures. The term innovation is an expression that 
describes a new thing; an object, an idea or practice (Slappendel, 1996). However, many theorists 
argue that innovation is far from that simple, contending that innovation is present so long as it is new 
or original to the individuals involved (Aiken & Hage, 1971; Slappendel, 1996; Van de Ven, 1986; 
Van de Ven, Polley, Garud, Venkataraman, 1999; Zaltman, Duncan & Holbek, 1973). 
While the definition of innovation is debated amongst some scholars, others focus on the 
innovation process and what characterizes it. Van de Ven (1986) identifies some important factors of 
an innovation process: Ideas, people, and transactions. He argues that ideas proliferate with 
individuals who work within their own context in the organization. For an idea to grow into an 
innovation in the institution, it has to be subject of a collective process (Van de Ven, 1986). During 
this process, ideas are shared, and new ideas proliferate as they are taken into the context of other 
individuals. Van de Ven (1986) argues that among people in the innovative process, there is ongoing 
transaction. “Transactions are ‘deals’ or exchanges which tie people together within an institutional 
framework” (Van de Ven, 1986, p. 597). According to John R. Commons, the originator of the 
==PAGE:75==
 
65 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
concept, transactions have three stages: negotiations, agreements, and administration (as cited in, Van 
de Ven, 1986). In environments with high uncertainty and complexity, these stages are connected to 
parallel trail, and trial-and-error processes (Lenfle & Loch, 2010; Loch, Solt & Bailey, 2008; Pich, 
Loch & De Meyer, 2002; Van de Ven, 1986; Van de Ven, Polley, Garud, & Venkataraman, 1999). 
Van de Ven (1986) suggests a part-whole relationship of transactions among individuals 
(the parts), and collective (the whole) innovation. This relationship can be complicated and create i.e. 
lack of communication, misunderstanding, uncertainties, ambiguities and low productivity - issues 
that has to be attended to (Hackman, 1983; Lenfle & Loch, 2010; Van de Ven, 1986). Morgan (1983) 
and Ramirez (1984) present a holographic model that integrates the innovative processes as ongoing 
iterations between the participants. This is linked to concurrency, simultaneous progress of research 
and development, which is a significant trade of adaptive and innovative organizations (Lenfle & 
Loch, 2010). The idea of a hologram is based on self-organizing groups that “think globally while 
acting locally” (Van de Ven, 1986, p. 600). This model challenges innovation put into organizational 
structures and strict models of project management.  
Van de Ven (1986) further highlight the importance of establishing an organizational culture 
and leadership style that emphasize the innovative institution. This is supported by Hackman (1983, 
p. 40) claiming that: “An unsupportive organizational context can easily undermine the positive 
features of even a well-designed team.” The importance of leadership is supported by Senge (1990), 
who claims that the leadership in learning organizations differ from the old-fashioned view that a 
leader is a strong heroic front figure who makes all the decisions. Based on the holographic approach 
by Morgan and Ramirez (1983; 1984), Van de Ven (1986) makes three distinctions of the innovative 
institution. (1) Negative feedback, where the management emphasize experimenting by avoiding the 
wrong goals, rather than reaching the only right goal. This is connected to the trial-and-error process. 
With this mindset, management has to emphasize the relevance of ongoing development rather than 
forward planning (Van de Ven, 1986). (2) Double-loop learning, as opposed to single-loop learning, 
enables the institution to, not only redirect their current path, but to revise the underlying strategic 
norms (Argyris & Schön, 1983). According to Aiken and Hage (1971), the ability to adapt is 
fundamental to innovative organic institutions. However, Van de Ven (1986) also argues that 
==PAGE:76==
 
66 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
innovative processes, like double-loop learning, can lead to internal conflicts and defensive behavior, 
because it tends to place doubt towards the strategic choices. (3) Preserving uncertainty instead of 
reducing it, to attain turbulence and creativity. According to Lenfle and Loch (2010) elimination of 
uncertainty leads to exclusion of trial-and-error processes, less ability to adapt, and eventually less 
innovation.  
Consequently, innovation is identified and connected with decentralized organic 
organizations, where bureaucracy and rigid structure is low (Aiken & Hage, 1971; Mintzberg, 2014;

7.3 Project Management Perspective 
This field has been in extensive development since its origin in the mid-1950s. It is subject 
to massive standardization and is defined as “The application of knowledge, skills, tools, and 
techniques to project activities to meet the project requirements” (Project Management Institute, 
2008, p. 4), in order to meet the constraints of scope, time, and cost (Hällgren & Söderholm, 2010; 
Lenfle & Loch 2010). PMI’s guide gathers general beliefs and practice in project management, and 
creates a standardized form widely agreed as “good practice” in modern projects (Project 
Management Institute, 2008, p. 4). The ISO 21500 developed by PMI (2012), presents standardized 
methods like: Developing a project charter, managing stakeholders, defining scope, estimating 
resources, controlling cost, controlling risks, and developing budget. The ISO 21500 is a typical 
project management standard that seeks to estimate and assess all elements in the project operation. 
Standards suggest a solution to unknowns and complexity in projects, by introducing risk 
management methods, like identifying the critical path (CPM), where the project activities, duration, 
and dependencies are mapped (Project Management Institute, 2008). According to Morris (2013, p. 
10), PMI first introduced risk as cost and schedule, and later factors of technical, market, finance, 
social, and institutional character. Risk management is the application of “probabilities times the 
impact” (Pich, Loch, & De Meyer, 2002, p. 1009), trying to foresee the probability that uncertain 
events will occur, and identify their effect on the operation.  
==PAGE:77==
 
67 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
There seems to be a bifurcation in the field, on one side there is numerous project 
management standards that has a positivistic approach to contingency; on the other side researches 
claim that contingencies cannot be foreseen or predicted, especially those of social character 
(Ghoshal, 2005; Morris, 2013). “There is no scientific basis on which to form any calculable 
probability whatever. We simply do not know” (Keynes, 1937, p. 211). McFarlan (1981) argues that 
in uncertain and ambiguous projects, tight planning and control only gives the manger an idea of 
having control, when in reality they do not. This is supported by Morris (2013) and Söderlund (2002), 
highlighting that standards present unpredictable variables in a positivistic universal theory. Standard 
planning measures are only successfully applicable when an adequate level of information is present 
(Pich, Loch, & De Meyer, 2002). The factors of contingency in projects are divided in three variables: 
Uncertainty, ambiguity and complexity. These are also called “unknown unknowns” by several 
authors (i.e. by Loch et al., 2008, p. 29). Uncertainty represents elements we cannot predict because 
they are out of our control, like weather or people behavior; ambiguity is when little knowledge about 
the development is present, thus it becomes unpredictable; complexity is when numerous interacting 
parts exhibit unpredictable behavior (Pich et al., 2002; Loch et al. 2008).  
There are existing methods suited for contingency management and complex development; 
trial-and-error learning and parallel trails (Lenfle & Loch, 2010; Pich et al., 2002; Sommer & Loch, 
2004). Both methods originated in complex and uncertain projects in the USA around the 1950s - 
before the project management institutionalization by NASA and others around the 1960s (Lenfle & 
Loch, 2010). The trial-and-error learning method is associated with innovation and implies 
experimentation of technology and iterative development of knowledge (Mintzberg, 2014). Thus, 
trial-and-error is closely connected to innovative theory, and the appliance of iterations is comparative 
to single- and double-loop learning; “Projects are first and foremost a way to explore and learn” 
(Lenfle, 2008). According to Lenfle and Loch (2010), this method allows project to become strategy 
tools, rather than just strategy fulfillers, as ongoing development allow managers to change and adapt 
the underlying norms and not only the project development. Developing parallel trials involves the 
development of several design proposals, in order to increase the likelihood to develop at least one 
successful design (Lenfle & Loch, 2010). Neither of the methods are found in the standards, and they 
==PAGE:78==
 
68 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
are conflicting many of the standardized beliefs concerned with scope, cost and time. Consequently, 
many projects fail while planning and budgeting in environments that do not allow it (Lenfle & Loch, 
2010; Pich et al., 2002).  
Lenfle and Loch (2010) claim that modern practice has lost its roots, and that project 
management standards favor cost/performance relationship instead of the optimal solution. 
Furthermore, they accuse the standards of being obsessed with “execution” and “uncertainty 
elimination and control” (p. 48). Morris (2013) agrees with this by stating that project management 
has to focus on delivering the adequate output, not solely on constraints of scope, time, and cost. 
Contemporary standards use a phased approach of controlled sequential stages; thus, projects are 
managed on calculations rather than possibilities, causing free innovation and new ideas to be limited 
(Aldrich & Ruef, 1999; Lenfle & Loch, 2010). Kreiner (1992) supports this, and argues that projects 
in organizations should be releasing creative forces instead of planning, and increase people 
participation instead of control. The result hereof, is that the art of project management has placed 
itself in a niche of top-down perspectives, where innovation and strategy formation are under-
prioritized (Blomquist, Hällgren, Nilsson & Söderholm, 2010; Cicmil, Williams, Thomas, & 
Hodgson, 2006; Hällgren & Söderholm, 2010, Lenfle & Loch, 2010; Pich et al., 2002). Ghoshal 
(2005) argues that excessive use of assumptions and partial empirical analysis has created imperfect 
positivistic theories that are destroying good practice. Organizations that avoid project management 
standards and use iteration and parallel methods, are often seen as unprofessional and poorly managed 
because their techniques are situated “outside” of best practice (Lenfle & Loch, 2010, p. 47). 
Organizations that use trial-and-error methods in their projects, are often novel, or in a complex and 
dynamic environment (Aiken & Hage, 1971; Lenfle & Loch, 2010; Van de Ven 1986). 
However, research in the last decade has shown that trial-and-error and parallel trial are 
viable methods of managing novelty, especially when “unforeseeable uncertainty” is high (Loch, 
Solt, & Bailey, 2008, p. 33). When managing technology and innovation with high risk, management 
should acknowledge the uncertainty and expect situational variables; thus, methods should differ from 
standardized measures (Lenfle, 2008; Lenfle & Loch, 2010; Loch et al., 2008; McFarlan, 1981; 
Mintzberg, 2014; Pich et al., 2002; Van de Ven, 1986). However, in some cases trial-and-error and

8.1 The Hobby-Organization 
The present study revealed that CS had gone through several stages of internal structure. 
Now, before initiating a discussion about this it is important to further accentuate that CS was a non-
profit and voluntary organization based on personal interests and social bonds. Regardless of personal 
sacrifice of the members, CS is defined as being a hobby-organization – and I mean that in the least 
offensive manner. The association was a sanctuary for the members whom wanted to have fun; after 
all it was voluntary.  
Many researchers argue that organizations are socially constructed systems, which is also 
supported in this study. The members had socially constructed a reality in which they could identify 
themselves and their purpose. Thus, the self-perception in CS was that all members were valuable 
because if not, they could not be members. Collective settlement is a basic element in hobby-
organizations because the purpose is not dependent on market pull or technology push, and basically 
it comes down to having fun and being entertained. Accordingly, the members are liberated to

The history of CS exposed that the founders controlled the operation for many years. The

that combines the organization. The board had initiated an information obligation and weekly 
meetings; something they never had before. KS, the chairman in CS, stated that no one wanted to 
work for others, which became the base of their egalitarian structure and unity, which evidently 
reduced the conflicts and increased enthusiasm. The crisis structure succeeded in reducing conflicts, 
arguably because the ideology was very powerful - the members wanted to consent and proceed.

this united form was extremely low. The desire for unity transcended the importance of production, 
which corresponds to Mintzberg’s description of the missionary configuration, as it does not 
emphasize any form of production.

discussion about alternative methods of project management. Sources stated that CS did practice 
project management, which actually helped me to think: OK, then what are the alternatives? As a 
consequence hereof, the data collection and analysis emphasized the data and the research displayed 
the alternative methods: Direction and navigation. The aspect that I did not recognize project 
management, yet the grounded theory still supported contemporary literature of project management, 
suggests the analysis to be pure. Fortunately, the alternative methods were highly contextual to 
literature; after all, grounded theorists do need good luck sometimes (Suddaby, 2006). This turn of 
event contextualized the contribution and enabled an interesting discussion about the practice of 
project management. Conclusively, by delaying literature and maintaining a receptive position, I 
claim that influence from preexisting knowledge was limited.  
In section 3.6.1 it was described how interview questions were open-ended and non-
judgmental.  However, asking neutral questions in an interview did not guarantee that the interview 
actually was neutral (Charmaz, 2006). Being a student from DTU Management, there was a risk of 
my origin alone influencing the research participants, causing them to act unnatural and answer in

CONCLUSIVE REMARKS 
9 CONCLUSIVE REMARKS 
The present study has, through an untraditional research method, offered a fresh perspective 
and a deeper theoretic explication of a case subject and in more traditional manner contextualized this 
with the use of substantial and contemporary literature. Grounded theory method constituted an 
inductive and unbiased research technique, by which the purpose was to collect unique data and 
analyze it in order to produce deep explication and abstract models to predict the subject.  
The findings suggested that the subject had successfully integrated the concepts of consensus 
and initiative, and achieved an organizational form that at one level promoted unity and collective 
executive steering, and at another level encouraged individual initiative and productivity. Data 
application suggested that dominance by either concept could be critical, and thus a balance was 
preferred. The balance of the concepts was consistent and reappearing throughout the research.  
An operational analysis discovered the process of direction and navigation. Direction was a 
flexible course at both collective and individual level that identified activities, yet allowed for ongoing 
adjustment. Navigation was an operational process of following the direction. Here, groups were self-
controlled within their respective areas, and everything was evaluated and coordinated in collectivity. 
As there was no hypothesis to be tested, and the literature review was consciously delayed until after 
the analysis, context was unknown prior to the discussion chapter. Unexpectedly, during the literature 
review, the analysis about operational processes emerged to support contemporary claims about 
project management. While the field of project management is subject to massive standardization, 
some researchers claim that we should adopt more integrating practices, to better cope with 
contingencies. The methods of direction and navigation were specifically congruent to trial-and-error 
and parallel trails, and were highly comparable to double-loop learning theory.  
The discussion led to a contextualization with Mintzberg’s organizational configurations. The 
study showed correspondence with the missionary and innovative organizations. However, 
Mintzberg’s use of language did not comply with the present study. This is arguably due to hobby-
organizations not being defined in Mintzberg’s literature. In terms of project management methods, 
the study supported contemporary claims that alternative methods are viable in managing contingent

contribution was affirmed, as a concurrent development of a conference paper has received interest 
from academic communities.

When starting this thesis journey, I had no clear idea where to go, or where it would end up.

10.2 Methodology 
Executing grounded theory was foremost an exciting experience, and I believe it was an 
important part of the study. Due to the methodology the data and analysis evolved as presented, and 
arguably, if a theoretical frame had been developed before the data collection and analysis, the study 
would have been quite different and not been able to discuss the same topics. 
In the initial phase, some time was spent on methodological research, which in reflection 
was a good choice. The analytical phase was tough as it had massive data pointing in different 
directions. The important thing here was to stay patient, and let the data evolve naturally through 
numerous iterations of coding and comparing. I recommend using the methodology to future students; 
yet warn that it can be a slow and time consuming process, since the data cannot be forced. I further 
recommend future students, who want to do grounded theory research, to begin with a good 
methodological preparation, and use the techniques of the methodology. Moreover, it is a method that 
requires hard work and with hard work comes a little luck.
