Summary
The wind energy sector is a key element in the green transition towards a decar-
bonised future. Indeed, in recent years, the number of installed wind turbines has
increased and yet with it, so has the amount of material that needs to be treated at
its end-of-life. Sustainable waste management is therefore of crucial importance.
Today, about 90% of the total wind turbine mass can be recycled, with the ex-
ception being for the blades which are made up of materials very diﬀicult to handle.
More specifically, their structure consists of reinforcement fibres impregnated within
a thermoset matrix, with the latter itself exhibiting a complex reticular structure.
To make matters worse, there has been a lack of attention in the design and waste
management phase of the mentioned product over the past decades. This report sets
out to examine existing and in-development solutions for wind turbine blade disposal,
and to provide for each scenario a comprehensive picture of the advantages, disadvan-
tages and possible future improvements. To this end, the study is based on adopting
a circular approach, i.e. keeping products and materials in use, and on the use of
a robust science-based tool, i.e. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). The assessed waste
handling routes are namely, landfill, incineration, use in cement production, pyrolysis
and finally mechanical recycling, all of which have been modelled using Environmen-
tal Assessment System for Environmental Technologies (EASETECH).
The main findings of this work can be summarized as follows: Landfill is the
least sustainable option due the high volumes of lost material. However, it exhibits
a good environmental profile. The same can be said for incineration though some
energy recovery can also be achieved and thus could represent a short-medium term
option. The better long-term options are techniques that result in the recovery of the
glass fibres and their potential recycling. Of these techniques, the Cement Kiln route
appears to be the most sustainable treatment for glass fibre WTBs, which it could
also potentially provide significant CO2 savings. The use of mechanical recyclates in
short fibre applications represents another viable route, though it provides a lower
benefit. Finally, pyrolysis may look promising but in practise currently has the largest
environmental footprint. This may change in the future with suﬀicient research. As
a final note, fossil fuel and electricity consumption were found to be the main causes
of the processes’ environmental impact and thus the use of renewable energy sources
should be encouraged.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Background
Global Warming, Climate Change and the depletion of Natural Resources are just
a few of the burning topics at the forefront of social discourse in recent years and
increasingly central to the environmental objectives of various nations. In order to
achieve an adequate level of development whilst addressing these environmental is-
sues, it is of utmost importance to find solutions based on a sustainable consumption
and production model. First of all, a change is needed in the current linear economic
system, based on take-make-waste consumption model which will have to evolve to-
wards a system relying on a more sustainable and eﬀicient management of products,
i.e. the circular economy system. This model focuses on two important aspects that
were previously neglected: limiting the emission of greenhouse gases and the waste
of valuable resources throughout the entire product’s life cycle. In this regard, one of
the key principles strongly encouraged by the circular system is the transition from
carbon-fossil based sources to the use of renewable energy sources, of which wind
power is one of the main drivers. Indeed, due to its incredible environmental benefits,
this technology has developed enormously in recent decades, both in terms of scale
and number of installed units. However, while wind turbines are a major ally in the
green transition, they fail to fully address one other crucial issue hindering sustainable
development: resource depletion. In particular, if on one hand the composite materi-
als, which wind turbine blades (WTB) are made of, have played a crucial role in the
development of the wind power sector, then on the other, they represent the largest
challenge for the industry. The structure of these materials consists of glass or carbon
reinforcing fibres in a thermoset matrix, and this improves the performance of tur-
bines by allowing for lighter and longer blades with an optimised aerodynamic shape.
However, today end of life (EoL) composite materials are primarily sent to landfill or
in some cases incinerated for energy recovery and there are only a few other options
under consideration, especially for Glass Fibre Reinforce Plastics (GFRP). The cur-
rent methods of WTB waste management is certainly not the optimal solution, and
it is therefore important to investigate new waste management strategies that allow
material reintegration into the value chain.

1 Introduction
2
Research objective and questions
The purpose of this project is to evaluate the environmental load of current and in-
development End-of-Life solutions for Glass Fibre wind turbine blades. The assessed
waste handling scenarios are namely, landfill, incineration, the cement kiln route, py-
rolysis and finally mechanical recycling. To reach the goal of this study a comparative
life cycle assessment (LCA) of the mentioned processes is performed. Great attention
has been given to the selection of data sources. However, due to the lack of avail-
able information in the literature, assumptions had to be made, hence influencing the

2.1
An overview of composite material
”The first generation of wind turbines are now starting to come to the end of their
operational life”. This is now a recurring phrase in many wind energy articles. Indeed,
according to the European wind association WindEurope, 22GW of installed capacity
will approach the end of their design life in the next few years which corresponds to
approximately 10% of the total installed capacity in Europe today [8]. This implies
that a large amount of material will need to be treated.
Today, around 85-90% of the total mass of wind turbines can be recycled [9] this
is because their main components; concrete, steel and other metals have established
recycling practices and they are treated accordingly. The missing gap in WT recycling
rate is due to the complex composite structure of wind turbine blades. Generally
speaking, Polymer Composites (PC) are materials that consist of reinforcement fibres
impregnated within a polymer matrix, which explains why they are often referred
to as fibre-reinforced polymer (FRP) composites. The resins used in the polymer
compounds are usually either thermoplastics, thermosets, or bio-based resins while
natural fibres, glass or carbon fibres are the main component of the reinforcing agent.
In wind turbine applications, the fibrous fraction is mixed together with a ther-
moset matrix whose polymers crosslink when they cure impeding the mobility of
macromolecules [10]. If on one hand this mechanism confers excellent mechanical
strength and resistance to high temperatures and corrosion, on the other these bonds
are irreversible and when heated up the polymers cannot untangle, remaining in a
permanent solid state. Consequently, they do not melt, but only burn [11] [10], mak-
ing it very diﬀicult to recover the composite material. Unlike thermoset polymers,
thermoplastics have a lower degree of cross-linking [10] and thus can be recycled much
more easily. When they cure, the polymers contained in thermoplastics tangle so they
can be repeatedly remelted back into a liquid form and reprocessed again. Figures
2.1 and 2.2 graphically illustrate the difference between thermoset and thermoplastic
bonds respectively.

2.2 Moving Forwards: Circular Economy

2.2
Moving Forwards: Circular Economy
The major reason for the large quantity of produced waste, is the linear economy sys-
tem which has characterized the production processes of the past century. This model
is built on the ”take, make, use, dispose” industrial scheme [12]: once consumption
ends, the product cycle also ends and all its parts become unexploited waste. Re-
source availability is considered unlimited and all the life stages rely on an extensive
use of energy from non-renewable sources. In line with this, the value of a product
is determined by its selling price, which often does not take into account both the
environmental costs and the quality of the product itself in terms of durability and
usefulness over time. The adverse consequences arising from this modus vivendi are
observed, for example, in the diﬀiculties in managing and disposing waste as well as
in the high environmental damages caused by manufacturing products [12].
This way of thinking has of course been applied to wind turbines as well with
these giant structures always having been planned to be as cost-effective as possible
[13] without any sustainability considerations made for the design and the end-of-life
use. This explains why currently installed wind turbines are not designed for reuse
or recycle, though huge efforts are being made to try to minimize the EoL waste and
reduce the value losses.
Measures that have been implemented in recent years, thus encouraging a decrease
in resource consumption and fossil energy per unit of production, have only succeeded
in delaying the crisis of the economic system and are not enough to solve the waste
issues and the problem of limited resources. A trend reversal is necessary.
To tackle the adverse effects of the linear production scheme, a counter-model
initially centred on the ’doing more with less’ principle was proposed but it was soon
replaced by the more sustainable vision of ’doing more with what you already have’;
a system known as the circular economy (CE).
According to the Ellen MacArthur Foundation, circular economy ”is a generic term
to define an economy designed to be able to regenerate itself. All activities, starting
from extraction and production, are organized so that someone’s waste becomes a
resource for someone else” [14].
In other words, the economic system is planned so that materials are kept in subse-
quent production cycles for as long as possible so that the waste across the production
chain is minimized. The circular economy calls for a redefinition of the industrial sys-
tem that is regenerative and reconstitutive, within its purposes and means. The aim
is to ’close the circle’, by optimising the use of resources and raw materials throughout
the entire life cycle: from production to consumption and recovery [14].
This strategy implies a shift from the ’cradle-to-grave’ waste management, typical
of the linear economy, to ’cradle-to-cradle’ approach which implies a reduction in
waste production through eco-innovation and the use of new business models.

2.2.1
Waste Regulatory Framework
One of the primary principles underpinning the effective transition to a circular econ-
omy is the waste hierarchy first introduced in 2008 with Directive 2008/98/EC. How-
ever, concepts relating to the importance of waste minimization as well as environmen-
tal and human health protection were already presented in 1975 with the Directive
75/442 EEC and, hence have ironically coexisted with the linear economy thinking
for more than 40 years. The key concepts of the founding directive have been pro-
gressively updated culminating in the waste hierarchy concept, establishing waste
management criteria [15].
Directive 2008/98/EC defines the five steps of the waste hierarchy as:
Prevention refers to measures taken before a substance, material or product has
become waste, that reduce (a) the quantity of waste, including through the re-use of
products or the extension of the life span of products; (b) the adverse impacts of the
generated waste on the environment and human health; or (c) the content of harmful
substances in materials and products;
Preparing for re-use means checking, cleaning or repairing operations, by which
products or components of products that have become waste can ultimately be re-
used without any further pre-processing;
Recycling which refers to any recovery operation by which waste materials are re-
processed into products, materials or substances, whether for the original or another
purpose;
Recovery which refers to any operation that involves, among others, preparing for
re-use, recycling and backfilling. However, it does not include energy recovery and
the reprocessing into materials that are to be used as fuels or other means to generate
energy; (Directive (EU) 2018/851)
Disposal is any operation whose primary purpose is not recovery (or any of the pre-
ceding steps), even if the operation may result in some degree of material reclamation.
This waste hierarchy can be visually represented as a pyramid, presenting the op-
tions as an order of priority with prevention being the preferred solution and disposal
being the last resort (see Figure 2.4). Referring to the linear economy approach based
on a lack of attention to prevention, and disposal abuse, the waste pyramid of the
linear economy can be approximated as shown in Figure 2.3.

good, or simply kick the can down the road as it were; an issue that is sometimes

implementation of measures that only shift the impact to another product chain [16].

been identified in the inclusion of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) within the circular

Life Cycle Assessment
Life Cycle Assessment is a robust and science-based tool to quantify a product’s
potential impact from cradle to grave. Hence, its application to CE propositions can
ensure a transition towards an effectively circular economy.
At an international level, the LCA methodology is regulated by the ISO 14040/44
standards, which define the LCA study as an iterative process comprising of four
main steps [17].

2.2.3
Redefining Waste
As mentioned above, the waste hierarchy represents a fundamental element in the
transition to a circular economy and is increasingly used by decision makers in waste
management. However, Directive 2008/98/EC has little clarity and is imprecise in the
terminology it introduces, which also affect the implementation of the pyramid. To
begin with, in the current formulation ”waste” is defined as ”any substance or object
which the holder discards or intends or is required to discard”. However, the author
argues that any subjective assessment of the nature of materials to be classified as
waste or not is unacceptable, since waste is not what is no longer of any use to the
holder on the basis of their personal choice but, rather, what is qualified as such on
the basis of objective data that define the conduct of the holder, or an obligation
to which the holder is still required to fulfil, i.e. disposing such material. In this
paper, therefore, we will refer to the term ”waste” as ”material or product whose
life cannot be extended anymore and hence specific processing is required in order to
extract further value from it”. On the other hand, the term ”non-waste” will be used
to refer to the product or its parts that do not need to be processed but rather, at
most, need to be maintained with operations of checking, cleaning or repairing. As a
consequence of these definitions, prevention and reuse are considered to be measured
that extend the life of a product or its parts (non-waste), while measures such as
recycling, recovery and disposal are considered as waste management activities. As
it will be described in more details over the next chapters, decommissioned WTBs is

2.3
Wind Turbine Blades
Material Composition
Wind turbine blades are built up like a composite beam structure where two main
parts; the load carrying beam connected to the root and the aerodynamic outer shell,
can be identified. The root is the final part of the blade and consists of unidirec-
tional fiber laminate connected to the hub of the turbine through either root inserts
or metallic T-bolts [18]. The proportion of metallic components, including the steel
bolts, the blade tip cap and the copper lightning conductor, varies approximately
between 2% and 8% [1].
The material composition of the aerodynamic part varies depending on the blade
type, the year of production and the blade manufacturers, however, the optimal struc-
ture must always ensure a balance between parameters like aerodynamic performance,
lifetime, loads and mass. This specific composition is based on a mixed and complex
material structure to ensure lighter and longer blades, necessary to maximize wind
turbines’ performance (Fig. 2.5).
Referring to Figure 2.5, consider first the surface structure, i.e. shells and shear
webs, which defines the aerodynamic shape of the blade.
These components are
built as a sandwich structure made up of axial reinforcement layer laminates with
a core material, usually balsa wood, polyethylene terephthalate (PET) or Polyvinyl
Chloride (PVC). The main load carrying part, the spar cap, is made instead of unidi-
rectional layers of reinforcement in the blade length direction [19]. The outer surfaces
in the aerodynamic section are protected from wear and tear using gel coats, usually
polyurethanes [18].
In general, the reinforcing fibres (glass, carbon or hybrid combination) constitute
around 60-70% of the WTB weight, with the matrix, effectively contributing the rest
[9]. As previously explained, the latter is a thermoset, typically epoxy, vinylester or
polyester resin [18].

Waste Management Technologies - Overview
Today, there are three possible ways in which to deal with WTB waste: landfill, energy
and/or material recovery and recycling.
However, not all have yet been applied
at an industrial scale. As previously mentioned, according to the waste hierarchy
principles landfill is considered the least preferred option. As the goal is to recover
value from retired blades, in the case of the landfill scenario, all the materials are
lost and they cannot be reinserted back into the material loop resulting in potential
side effects on the environment and on the economy. The second most common route
is the Incineration of blades with or without energy recovery. Waste that undergoes
thermal treatment is used as fuel for the process, whereas energy in the form of
heat and electricity is produced by the subsequent combustion. However, the latter
reaction results in the release of fine pollutants considered very harmful to human
health [16]. Higher in the waste hierarchy there are treatment processes which allow
both material and energy recovery from wind blades of which the cement kiln-route
is the most representative example [20]. Finally, the recycling of composite materials
is the most desirable alternative. There are a number of possible techniques namely
thermal, chemical and mechanical recycling [21]. Thermal and chemical recycling
take advantage of the difference in the phase properties between the matrices and
the fibres to separate the two materials, one using heat and the latter a solvent or
electricity [21]. For wind turbine blades, the most promising techniques for thermal

high voltage pulse fragmentation and solvolysis are the main examples of chemical
recycling. Lastly, mechanical recycling implies a drastic size-reduction of the materials

Common to all of the recycling processes mentioned above is the reuse of the
final product in lower composite applications which has been identified as one of the

Pre-treatment operations
The pre-treatment activities include all the steps preceding the entry of the blade to
the waste management plants, refer to Figure 3.3. Once WTBs are removed from

farm site (selected option) or by sorting it from the other materials during the waste
treatment processes through a magnetic separator. Once the sectioning stage is com-

Non-Ferrous Metal Sorting
Metal sorting is the collection of metal residues from waste, in this case Aluminium
and Copper, and is a recursive activity in all the assessed scenarios except for Scenario
I - Landfill. As aluminium and copper are non-ferrous, they cannot be extracted
using magnets as is the case for ferrous metals, and consequently a different process
is necessary, such as the Eddy current separator, which is the most commonly used
technology on the market. This technology relies on the induction of Eddy currents
in nonmagnetic metal objects in response to an electromagnetic field [24].
Subsequently, in order to separate aluminium from copper, two possibilities exist:
the water gravity separator and the cyclones, with both exploiting the difference in
density between the two metals. However where the water gravity separator works
by allowing the copper to sink and the aluminium to float within a water system,
in the cyclones, selected option in this work, the separation is accomplished in the
centrifugal force field within it [24]. However, all these technologies have a medium-
to-low sorting eﬀiciency, with the loss rate of materials depending on parameters such
as volumes, weight, contamination [7] [24].

Currently, the second most common route for managing end-of-life blades in Den-

At the cement plant, blades are ground down in a crossflow mill, with copper and

The main two steps in cement clinker production are the preheating stage and the
combustion of the materials in a rotary kiln. The preheating phase takes place in a
tower of heat exchangers, usually composed of 4 to 5 stages of cyclones that permit
a gradual temperature transition from 100 to 900 [29]. This progressive heating in
the cyclone tower of the incoming material is driven by hot gases leaving the rotary
kiln. In this phase, a fuel, usually coal, is also necessary to bring the cement kiln
temperature above 850°C. However, the organic portion present in the blades as well
as the SRF (if present in the mixture, not in this study), act as a fuel, replacing a
significant portion of the otherwise fossil fuel [1]. Before entering the last cyclone, the
fine powder passes through the pre-calcination stage, which allows the limestone to be
completely decarbonised [29]. The next step takes place in the rotary kiln fuelled by
coal where the material reaches the clinker formation temperature of 1450 °C [23]. A
schematic representation of the steps examined in the assessment is presented in Fig-

belts (CB). To date, this technology is only in use for glass reinforced composites and
the only cement plants able to process wind turbine blades are located in Germany [1].

Scenario IV - Pyrolysis
Scenario IV represents waste management via pyrolysis: a thermal degradation of
organic material occurring in a oxygen-free atmosphere.
The steps of the entire
process are schematically illustrated in Figure 3.7. To begin with, the blade sections
are placed inside an electric furnace where nitrogen gas is inserted to create an inert
atmosphere [30]. In this work it is assumed that the process lasts around one hour
using a nitrogen rate of 1 l/min [30].
In the case of epoxy resins, temperatures
typically range between 300 °C and at least 500-550°C [31], as below 400°C polymer
matrix do not decompose satisfactorily [16].
When the maximum temperature of
the process is reached, the composite material undergoes thermal cracking; breaking
down into a solid component and a volatile fraction which is partly condensable. The
respective proportions depend on the condition and parameters set for the process
in the reactor, as well as on the waste input composition [24]. The volatile products
are hot gases which constantly routed by the nitrogen flow into a condenser where
they separate into a liquid part and an incondensable gaseous fraction. The latter
consist of a mixture of hydrogen, methane, carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide
among others, whilst the liquid is mainly made up of tar, oil and water containing a
complex range of hydrocarbons [24]. Meanwhile, the solid fraction is left behind in the
reactor and consists of char and glass fibres. Due to the treatment being carried out
in the absence of oxygen, aluminium and copper do not oxidize and can thus be easily
recovered from pyrolysis char [7]. The remaining char residues can be used together
with pyrolysis oil and gas, as a fuel in the waste to energy plants [7] where bottom
ashes from the combustion can be applied during road construction whereas fly ashes
are typically dumped in landfill. With regards to the glass fibres, post pyrolysis they
are cleaned of the carbon residues through oxidation in an electric furnace.

Scenario V - Mechanical recycling
Mechanical recycling consists of a series of size reduction activities which lead to a
shredded blades as final product. After the pre-treatment operations (refer to Section
3.3.1), the 1m blade section undergoes two size reduction steps. The first occurs in a
slow cutting or crushing mill where the WTB dimension is reduced to 50-100 mm [32];
the second, carried out after the non-ferrous metal recovery (refer to Section 3.3.1),
reduces the blade chunks to 10-50mm in size with a high-speed mill or hammer mill
[32].
Finally, the material is sorted into different size categories by cyclones and sieves.
Three outputs can thus be identified: powdered fraction, coarse fraction (matrix
reach) and the fibre reach powder. Some studies point out that up to 40% of the
input material of WTB mechanical recycling, becomes waste of the process [19] [20]
and most likely landfilled. Being a very straightforward mechanism wherein the ma-
terial is directly transferred from one machine to the other, it is reasonable to assume
that most of the waste belongs to the post-sieving stage. Indeed, while there are
options for reuse as reinforcement and filler for the course product and the recycled
fibres [19], no application has been found (yet) for the finely powdered recyclate and
it is assumed by the author that it is sent to landfill. The transportation of materials
along the process happens through conveyor belts.

3.4.4
Data quality
The evaluation of the quality of data has been qualitatively described in detail in the
above sections, whereas a quantitative assessment is carried out in the present sec-
tion in order to provide an overview for each scenario. To quantify the overall quality
of the processes of this study, a pedigree matrix developed by Weidema & Wesnæs
(1996) is used which consists of quality indicator values assigned with regard to five
indicator categories namely; reliability, completeness, temporal correlation, geograph-
ical correlation and technological correlation. It is desirable that these indicators are
satisfied to as great a degree as possible.
Consider the following definitions [3]: the Reliability indicator refers to the means
by which the data is obtained and verified, i.e. the sources and methods; the Com-
pleteness indicator refers to how representative the sample is and if it includes all the
necessary attributes; Temporal correlation refers to the time correspondence between
the year of study and the year of the acquired data; Geographical correlation refers
to the correspondence between the defined area for the study and the acquired data;
and lastly, Technical correlation refers to all the parameters of correlation other than
temporal and geographical. With regards to the indicator scores, the values are as-
signed on a semi-quantitative scale ranging between 1 and 5, where 1 indicates that
the required process is based on complete and reliable data, and 5 denoting lack of
information and compliance with the considered scenario. The average value for the
indicator categories is then estimated for each case. A detailed description of the
indicator scores for each category is provided in Figure 1 in the Appendix.
An overview of the overall data quality of the V47 material composition, the ac-
tivities common to all scenarios, and the scenarios themselves is presented in Table
3.12. Overall, the indicator categories show the lowest score with regards to the tech-
nical correlation and the completeness indicator while the geographical and temporal
correlations are the most satisfactory. Regarding the scenarios, the quality of data
for Pyrolysis and the Cement kiln route is, in general, very low while for landfill and
incinerator said quality is relatively high.

CHAPTER 4
Impact Assessment
This chapter presents the potential environmental impacts from the treatment of 1
ton of end-of-life V47 wind turbine blade. The findings of the analysis are expressed
in Person Equivalent, PE, so that comparisons across the different impact categories
can be performed. Also note that numerically-negative values indicates environmen-
tal benefits or avoided environmental impacts, with increasingly negative numbers
indicating a more beneficial effect. Positive numbers in a category means damage
to the environment. Normalised results are presented as a total impact for individ-
ual scenarios in Section 4.1.1 and disaggregated for individual processes within said
scenarios in Section 4.1.2.
Characterized potential impacts are then presented in
Appendix 5.1. Finally, considerations from a circular economy perspective are made
for each scenario in Section 4.2 in order to provide a comprehensive picture of the
sustainability of the processes.
Table 4.1 lists the impact categories used to communicate the results by the se-
lected method in EASETECH, i.e. ILCD recommended 2013 Prosuit Global Normal-
isation Reference without Long Term. In addition, for each category, an overview of
what they describe is provided, as well as the recommendation level according to their
quality: Level I which is recommended and satisfactory, Level II which though rec-
ommended indicates that some improvements are needed, and lastly Level III which
again, though recommended, must be applied only with caution.
Note that Human Toxicity impact categories are associated with a high level of
uncertainty (Level II/III) as is the case for Freshwater Eco-toxicity impact category
which also presented not reliable scores in the final potential impacts. Hence, the
decision of the author not to include Freshwater Eco-toxicity in the discussion of the
results. Moreover, Ozone Depletion potential and Ionising Radiation are not consid-
ered to be significant issues for this study as none of the process-related activities
is associated with the consumption and emission of ozone depleting substances, e.g.
CFC, HCFC, halon (Ozone Depletion) or the release of radionuclides (Ionising ra-
diation); the latter two categories will not considered in the environmental impact
assessment discussion.

4.2 Circularity Potential
48
4.2
Circularity Potential
The present section pictures the circularity of each process taking into account the
scenario set-up previously described in Chapter 3.3.
Scenario I - Landfill
From a circular economy perspective, landfilling wind turbine blades translates into
the loss of valuable resources: only steel is treated for material substitution while
none of the remaining components is reintroduced into the material circle or used for
energy recovery. Other important issues to consider is that WTB in landfills take up
a lot of space and in addition they do not degrade [25], i.e. they remain present un-
derground far into the future. This concern is accentuated by the incoming composite
waste in the next few years. However, the landfill route for wind turbine blades might
represent a potential for digging them up in the future when appropriate recycling
technologies will be available; hence, WTB storage in landfills might be adopted as a
short term solution for this product.
Scenario II - Incinerator
The processing of wind turbines by incineration presents major problems, as the
inorganic component of GFRC only decomposes above 1000°C [16]; a temperature
infeasible for a regular waste incinerator [37]. Consequently, approximately 60% of
the composite materials (the inorganic component), is left behind as bottom ash and
fly ash [27], which are either sent to landfill or, as assumed in this project, downcy-
cled as a substitute for road construction material, as in the case of bottom ashes;
fly ashes can be used as a backfilling material in German salt mines. Note that these
materials can not be re-inserted into the material loop again. Moreover, although in-
cineration allows for energy recovery in the form of heat and electricity, the calorific
value (LHV) of GFRP waste is rather low (around 10 MJ/kg, [38]) and the amount
of energy that can be recovered is thus limited. However, the incineration route can
reduce the final landfill volume by up to 90%, mitigating at least the size problem of
WTBs in dumping sites [39].
Scenario III - Cement Kiln route
As introduced earlier in Chapter 3.3.2, due to the similar composition based on cal-
cium oxide, silicon oxide and aluminium oxide, the cement industry can potentially
substitute part of its raw materials with wind turbine blades. Additionally, some of
the heat required during clinker production can be partially replaced by the organic
compounds of WTBs. For the above mentioned reasons, co-processing of composite
==PAGE:64==
4.2 Circularity Potential
49
materials complies with the European Waste Framework Directive 2008/98/EC and
hence considers both recycling and energy recovery.
Another significant advantage of this process is that no material is sent to landfill
and there is no waste throughout the process. On the other hand, the main drawback
of this treatment route is that the composite materials, once turned into cement, can-
not be utilized for their original or similar purposes. However, it could be possible
to use the resulting cement in wind turbine foundations. Such an application would
be the closest to the wind industry that a WTB, treated as per this scenario, could
potentially be re-used for.
As for the Incinerator scenario, due to the low calorific value of GFRPs (about 10
MJ/kg, [38]) only a low amount of energy can be recovered mainly when compared
to many other waste sources, e.g. tyres (LHV of 33-35 MJ/kg, [40]). Thus, in the
future, the cement industry may very well discontinue the processing of wind turbine
blades in favour of other wastes. However, if this does not transpire, then the already
existing cement plant in Denmark, located in Aalborg, may do well to consider ac-
quiring the necessary machinery to also process WTBs and not just raw materials, so
as to create a market for Denmark.
Scenario IV - Pyrolysis
During the thermal treatment of composite materials via pyrolysis, the fibrous frac-
tion is separated from the resin by heating the material in an inert atmosphere so
that the organic component of the wind turbine blades does not burn, but can be
recovered in the form of fuel (oil and syngas). Furthermore, the treatment carried
out in the absence of oxygen allows for the easy recovery of the ferrous residues, as
they do not show signs of oxidation.
Today, glass fibres can only be repurposed in low value applications, like for example,
as filler in short fibre products (i.e. noise insulation panels [9]) or they might be used
as correction for the clinker composition. In the worst case, however, they are land-
filled. The cement route is the option considered in this work. In this hypothetical
case, the recovered glass fibres are intended for material substitution, however, as no
energy recovery is possible due to the absence of organic compounds, this product
might very well be rejected a priori by the cement plant.
One of the main obstacles preventing the use of glass fibres in applications with
higher value is in identifying the combination of parameters that maximises the qual-
ity of the final products: though high temperatures correspond to a dense organic
portion, the clean fibres are damaged and have low mechanical properties compared
to what they originally had; conversely, at lower temperatures, the fibres retain their
structural strength but have a higher roughness due to the higher presence of epoxy
on their surface.

Scenario V - Mechanical recycling
Mechanical recycling implies the conversion of the composite components into a pow-
dered fraction, a fibrous fraction (rich in fibres) and coarse fraction (rich in matrix),
which can be reused as filler or reinforcement in new applications. However recycled
materials undergo downcycling, mainly due to the loss of mechanical properties of the
composite products, as once GFRP are chopped up, all the fibers lose their stiffness
and strength and can only be repurposed in industrial sectors other than the original
one [2]. Figure 4.9 shows a comparison between the surface morphology of ground
fibres (on the left side of the figure) and virgin fibre (on the right). The compro-
mised quality of the fibres and their random orientation, is clearly evident, as is the
contamination by the presence of epoxy resin; contributing to an increase in surface
roughness.
Figure 4.9: Surface morphology of grinded GFs (left side) and virgin GF (right side)
[2].
Moreover, it is estimated that up to 40% of the material becomes waste of the pro-
cess [9]. This amount like corresponds to the powdered fraction due to its extremely
deteriorated condition compared to the other two fractions whose mechanical proper-
ties are already low to begin with. With regards to the fibrous fraction, applications
include its use as short reinforcing fiber to concrete [19] or for acoustic insulating
purposes [41]. Coarse recyclates, having a lower aspect ratio, are mainly utilized as
fillers, e.g. as asphalt additives.

4.3
Conclusions regarding the overall sustainability of
the scenarios
This section of the report presents an overall picture of the sustainability of the
assessed scenarios based on the LCA findings and the circularity potential of the pro-
cesses; final thoughts regarding possible future implementations of the waste treat-
ments are also part of the discussion.
Consider first the loads on the environment. The impact assessment carried out in
previous sections indicates that the use of decommissioned wind turbines as raw ma-
terials in the cement industry results in overall savings across all impacts categories,
with avoided CO2 emissions of around 911.35kgCO2-eq. A good environmental per-
formance is also observed for the landfill and the mechanical recycling scenarios, with
associated CO2 emissions of 61.82kgCO2-eq and 81.84kgCO2-eq respectively. Regard-
ing the pyrolysis route; its technological state is such that significant loads on the
environment with a carbon footprint about 50 times higher than for the landfill case
(i.e. 3347.47kgCO2-eq) are produced. Finally from an environmental perspective, the
incinerator route represents an intermediate situation among all considered scenarios,
with CO2 emissions of about 782.99kgCO2-eq.
Remember that due to the lack of data available in literature, the results are asso-
ciated with significant uncertainty, mainly with regards to the cement kiln and the
pyrolysis scenarios; indeed, a data quality score of 3.4 on a scale of 1 to 5 characterises
either of these two treatment processes. Thus, further studies of the processes are
needed in order to provide more solid considerations thereof.
The low environmental costs of landfilling WTBs, and the established use of this
treatment, mean that further improvements are not necessary in the overall setup.
However, a more resource-eﬀicient approach is urgently needed and hence other waste
handling alternatives must be considered. With regard to incinerators, being well-
established technologies in Denmark, no further improvements can be identified at
this time, in part because the major reason for the low degree of recovery is primarily
driven by the incombustible properties of GF. Additionally, as the final product is
in the form of ashes, it would appear to be diﬀicult to find a better purpose for this
product. Despite the relatively low environmental costs of this scenario, it is reason-
able to assume that the incinerator route is unlikely to have a long-term future as a
WTB treatment strategy. Hence, it is clear that recycling technologies represent a
valid alternative to landfilling and incineration, as they recover value from the WTB
composite material. However there are weaknesses in the overall performance of the
processes that need to be pointed out. Regarding mechanical recycling, during the
process the waste generated is still very high and the fibres have reduced mechanical
properties compared to the virgin product. Hence, the mechanical recycling industry
should aim at improving the milling techniques in order to try to keep the fibres as
long as possible, whilst at the same time exploring new areas of application with

potentially higher value. On the other hand, no waste is produced throughout the
cement kiln route even though the WTB materials completely lose their original prop-
erties.
Pyrolysis seems to be the most promising WTB handling technology from a circular
perspective, as it is the only one aiming at effectively recycling both the composite
matrix and reinforcement for use in high-value applications. Current studies are look-
ing into using syngases as an energy source for the process itself. However, a low
quality product is produced, making further development necessary before this pro-
cess can be employed to recycle composite materials. Indeed, pyrolysis’ application
at an industrial scale may only be available at some point in the future.
From the above considerations, the Cement Kiln route appears to be the most
sustainable treatment for glass fibre WTBs, as it could potentially provide significant

4.4
Sensitivity Analysis - Scenario Alternative
Given the wide array of input variables and assumptions that underpin such complex
studies, a sensitivity analysis is important to carry out. Such an evaluation assesses
the degree to which the results are dependant on the inputs, and are especially im-
portant when the data or scenario is highly uncertain.
To begin with, it is first crucial to identify the parameters in each scenario that
may significantly affect the overall results, by means of a contribution analysis. Such
a study has already been performed in previous sections (refer to Chapter 4.1.2), and
the results show that, for all the scenarios considered, the main contributors to the
environmental impact loads are strongly associated with the consumption of both
diesel and electricity. Consequently, the primary objective of the present sensitivity
study is to assess the impact of changing the assumed way in which the electricity
utilised in all routes, is produced.
In the baseline scenarios, the current electricity mix for Denmark, as well as for
Germany with regards to the cement kiln route, have been used. On the other hand,
in this sensitivity analysis an alternative scenario is considered whereby the electric-
ity used is entirely generated by wind turbine technology; a case that is perfectly
reasonable in both countries, given the current trends and governmental energy poli-
cies. Note that the general set-up of each scenario, as well as the location, remains
unchanged, and the wind-generated electricity is applied only to the activities carried
out at the waste treatment plants.
The potential impacts resulting from the analysis of this alternative scenario are
presented normalised in Table 4.3 where they are compared with the previously ob-
tained results for the baseline energy mix.
Overall, it can be observed that this change in the source of electricity production
has a relatively small effect across all impact categories. For Scenario I (Landfill)
the values remain practically unchanged; whilst higher savings are instead observed
in Scenario III (cement kiln route) and Scenario V (Mechanical recycling) where the
overall results are improved.
In the case of incinerating WTB waste (Scenario II), most impact categories ac-
tually show reduced overall savings.
This can largely be explained by the lower
environmental impact of the electricity offset by the process.
Last but not least, significant changes can only be observed in the pyrolysis sce-
nario, as this treatment is very energy intensive and is mostly based on electricity
consumption.
Despite this significant change to the underlying scenario setup, it should be noted
that the use of wind-generated electricity, does not affect the ranking of the scenarios
according to their environmental results in most impact categories.

whereby selected main contributor parameters (discussed in Chapter 4.1.2) are tested

CHAPTER 5
Overall Conclusions
In this study, the potential environmental loads of the decommissioned V47 wind tur-
bine blade are quantified using the Life Cycle Assessment tool. Five waste handling
strategies have been considered: landfill, incinerator, cement kiln route, pyrolysis and
finally mechanical recycling. This is the first study to comparatively assess the envi-
ronmental costs and the circularity potential of said processes.
The main purpose of this work is to provide an answer to the following research
question:
What is today the most sustainable solution to decommission waste from Glass Fiber
Wind Turbine Blades?
The findings of this work are based on data availability and current knowledge:
• Landfill-based scenario and Incineration are currently the most common dis-
posal practices and exhibit a good environmental performance. However, most
of the value of the treated materials can not be recovered and therefore the
future application of said processes is highly discouraged.
• The Cement Kiln route seems to be the most sustainable waste treatment solu-
tion for glass fibre WTBs, in particular potentially providing significant savings
for the Climate Change impact category. Throughout this process no waste is
generated, and among all scenarios it is the one with the lowest overall environ-
mental impacts. However, the cement manufacturing process results in a near
total degradation of the glass fibre properties (down-cycling).
• The use of mechanical recyclates in short fibre applications appears to be the
second most beneficial route due to its environmental profile. Even though this
is in effect, down-cycling, such an application may still present a short-medium
term solution for handling WTB waste.
• Pyrolysis seems to be the most promising option from a circular economy per-
spective, as amongst all the processes considered, it results in the least fibre
degradation and a product that is very similar to its pre-use shape. On the
other hand, this treatment also has the largest negative effect on the environ-
ment across all impact categories. Thus, at the present time, pyrolysis is simply
==PAGE:72==
5.1 Future work and Recommendations
57
a case of burden shifting, though with significant investment and research, it
may one day be an environmentally viable solution.
• Transportation can be considered one of the minor contributor to the environ-
mental load in every scenario; in general, it is responsible of about 1% of the
total potential impacts.
• In general, the environmental impacts result mainly from electricity consump-
tion and fossil fuel consumption (especially that of diesel) during the sectioning
activity. In both cases, it is thus highly recommended to focus on encouraging
the use of renewable energy sources.
• Metal sorting and recycling benefits the environment and should thus be con-
tinued. Further attention should also be given to increasing the metal sorting
eﬀiciency, as this provides the greatest environmental benefits.
• The above conclusions bring to light that environmental protection might affect
the priority order of what constitutes the most sustainable waste treatment
option according to the waste hierarchy. Hence, it is important to observe the
waste management scheme not as a steady conventional principle, but as an
approach that should be examined on a case-by-case basis through the use of
the LCA tool.
5.1
Future work and Recommendations
Before exploring other fields of application or expanding the system boundaries of
this work, it is important to acquire more specific and reliable data concerning the
assessed processes, as the results will then be characterised by lower uncertainty;
additional studies on the topic must have the absolute priority. Moreover, future
works in order to provide a more comprehensive and solid answer to the research
question should include an evaluation of non-impact indicators such as the water and
energy consumption, as well as an analysis of the mechanical properties of the recycled
product. As a further recommendation, when selecting the waste treatment technique,
as well as assessing their sustainability, the social aspect must be considered as it
might represent an obstacle for development and execution of the assessed treatment
scenario. Furthermore, other relevant parameters such as technical feasibility and the
economic viability should be also considered as potential deviating factors from the
suggested waste hierarchy scheme. Finally, these suggested areas of focus in future
studies may very well lead to significantly different conclusions than the ones obtained
with the presented set up and consequently, the reader is advised to view the results
of this work with caution and not as a final opinion on the matter. Nevertheless, the
evaluation and conclusions drawn presented here are the result of the assumptions
taken and data available at the time of writing, and should be a suitable guide for
future activities.
