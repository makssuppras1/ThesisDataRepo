Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The report accounts for 35 ECTS credits. This thesis is

Executive summary 
In accordance with the imminent regulation, Danish municipalities are implementing mandatory 
separate collection of textiles with the aim of favoring reuse and recycling practices. Yet, mainly due 
to a lack of data, there is currently limited knowledge on the magnitude of the environmental effects 
associated with an increased amount of separately collected textiles. In the context of textile 
circularity, a lot of pressure is put on recycling practices in the effort of making the clothing industry 
more sustainable. However, within the textile sector, recycling faces several challenges, rising from 
multiple aspects of various nature. 
 
The overall aim of this project is to address some of those aspects that, if remain overlooked, can 
increase the risk of focusing too much on recycling being the only pathway to make the textile 
industry more sustainable. The project applies three different methods, each covering a specific 
purpose. A field study is conducted to gather primary data on specific textile characteristics. A 
material flow analysis (MFA) is applied with the aim of tracking potential flows of separately 
collected textiles, as well as estimating the propagation of uncertainties across these flows. A life 
cycle assessment (LCA) is conducted to evaluate how specific factors potentially influence the 
environmental benefits of reuse and recycling. Specific collection methods, pre-processing 
techniques, and replacement rates are among the focus areas of this project. To embrace the holistic 
nature of this research, the analysis is expanded to five impact categories. 
 
The main outcome of the study is that collection and pre-processing practices indirectly influence the 
overall environmental assessment of reusing and recycling clothes. Collection methods mainly affect 
the share and the quality of reusable textiles. The specific technology applied to remove accessories 
from garments has an effect on both the share of material suitable for fiber-to-fiber recycling and the 
amount of virgin fibers that needs to be added to the recycled product. All these aspects contribute to 
the overall reduction of the replacement rate of recycling, which is seen to be a very sensitive 
parameter in the model. 
 
Lack of data highly challenges any kind of environmental assessment within the textile industry, 
which should always be interpreted by keeping uncertainty considerations in mind.

1. Introduction 
As a basic human need, clothing is a topic of interest for virtually every individual. Global textile 
production and consumption have risen drastically in recent years, and technological development 
and globalization highly contributed to this trend. In many industrialized countries the advent of fast 
fashion impacted the lifetime of a garment, which now is disposed of when it does not meet the 
requirement set by the following fashion trend, even if it is still good enough from a functional point 
of view (Piribauer & Bartl, 2019). While this helped the growth of the textile industry, it fueled 
overconsumption patterns and the related excessive use of energy and resources. This, paired with a 
resource-inefficient management strategy to manage the waste generated, led to a concerning 
accumulation of textile material, leading to environmental harm in the EU and in third countries 
through, among other factors, excessive levels of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, water 
consumption, pollution, and land use (Circular Economy Perspectives in the EU Textile Sector, 
2023).  
 
In recent years, growing attention has been given to a more responsible use of resources, in general 
and within the textile industry. Emphasis has been put on textile circularity, which finds increasing 
of reuse and recycling practices to be a key pathway towards reducing the impacts of the sector by 
exploiting the value of textile products and the resources they contain (Watson et al., 2018). 
Consequently, handling and disposal of used textiles have been the focus of an increasing amount of 
research, not only within academia, but also at legislation and regulation level. According to the 
European hierarchy of waste, the most preferable option to handle waste is prevention, followed in 
the order by preparing for re-use, recycling, other recovery (e.g. energy recovery) and, ultimately, 
disposal (OJ L312/3, 2008). However, when it comes to textiles, the present scenario is far from 
following the desired order in terms of treatment options. 
 
As a matter of fact, most recent numbers show that around 78% of the post-consumer textiles 
(comprising clothing, footwear, home textiles and technical textiles) is currently not separately 
collected and ends up in mixed household waste, destined to incineration or landfilling (Circular 
Economy Perspectives in the EU Textile Sector, 2023). Of the remaining 22%, around 52% is 
recycled (around 32% within the EU and around 20% outside the EU) and around 46% is reused 
(around 8% within the EU and around 38% outside the EU), while the rest is exported (Circular 
Economy Perspectives in the EU Textile Sector, 2023). This means that only roughly one fifth of

European used textiles are either reused or recycled, while the majority is incinerated or landfilled.

In the effort of reverting this trend, Directive 2018/851 of the European Parliament (OJ L150/109, 
2018) requires separate collection of the textile fraction across European countries from 2025. In 
Denmark, this regulation has entered into force as of 1st July 2023. As a consequence, post-consumer 
garments available to either reuse or recycling are expected to significantly increase in the near future. 
However, it is uncertain what environmental effects this increase in the collected material would have 
in the overall assessment (Damgaard et al., 2019).  
 
While separate collection is a necessary step towards a more circular industry, various factors might 
affect how much of the material collected can in fact be reprocessed into new one, and to what extent 
the recycled product avoids the production of new textiles, i.e., what is their replacement rate. The 
collection of clothes does not automatically translate to reuse or recycling, as reuse is limited by 
quality of collected garments and the availability of a market with a matching demand (Roos et al., 
2019; Watson et al., 2016). 
 
To reach the goal of a circular economy, non-reusable textiles should be recycled in such a way that 
new fibers with similar properties to the virgin material can be produced. This is known as closed-
loop recycling, or fiber-to-fiber recycling. This is opposed to the concept of open-loop recycling, 
where textiles are reprocessed into products that serve a different purpose other than the original.

Nonetheless, fiber-to-fiber recycling is currently much less mature in the textile than in other sectors, 
and the exception rather than the norm (Roos et al., 2019). Lack of incentives and information, 
absence of policies and regulations as well as more technical issues related to the variety of fibers and 
chemicals involved in the production of garments are cited among the main challenges with textile 
recycling (Hole & Hole, 2020).  
 
In the context of textile recycling, several processes are necessary along the value chain. Once the 
textiles are collected, a sorting step is required to ensure a correct identification of the material, to in 
turn select the most suitable treatment option. Solutions are available to automate this process, which 
is essential to handle textiles in big quantities. However, current technologies are challenged, for 
example, by the heterogeneous composition of clothes, or the presence of other materials, which 
reduces the yield or adds extra purification steps, increasing both the environmental and economic 
cost (Roos et al., 2019).  
 
All the challenges mentioned above need to be transparently documented, to avoid the risk of relying 
too much on recycling as a way to make the textile industry sustainable (Roos et al., 2019). As fibers 
represent only a part of the environmental impact of the textile sector, a heavy focus on fiber recycling 
only can result in overlooking potential challenges that may arise in different phases of the life cycle 
of textiles (Ljungkvist & Elander, 2016; Sandin et al., 2019). Keeping this in mind, attention should 
be paid to what considerations can make recycling more or less beneficial, by addressing it as part of 
a bigger solution rather than the only one (Roos et al., 2019).   
 
A holistic approach is thus needed to correctly identify potential limitations and tradeoffs, to avoid 
the risk of burden shifting, or to pay too much attention to a limited section of the problem. A correct 
evaluation needs to be based on specific data and context. However, within the textile industry, the 
availability of primary data is scarce, and considerable research is needed (Muthu, 2020a). 
 
1.1. Project scope and research questions 
The overall aim of this project is to address some of those aspects that, if remain overlooked, all 
contribute to increasing the risk of focusing too much on recycling being the main way to make the 
textile industry more sustainable.  The hypothesis to be tested herein is then threefold: 1) to bring 
attention to direct challenges and environmental impacts connected to collection and sorting life cycle 
==PAGE:16==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis

stages, 2) to uncover the role that such phases indirectly play towards the recycling rate, alongside 
the sensitivity of results on the recycling rate itself, and 3) assess the limitations in terms of quality 
and availability of primary data. From here, the following research questions are formulated: 
 
i. 
What are the factors that influence potential environmental benefits associated with the 
management of post-consumer textiles, when the collection and sorting phases are included 
in the assessment? 
ii. 
What is the impact of the replacement rate in the overall assessment, and how do collection 
and sorting influence its quantification? 
iii. 
How does the availability, the quality, and the uncertainty of input data affect the reliability 
and the scalability of the assessment? 
 
By filling gaps in the general understanding of the challenges posed by the collection and sorting 
phase, this report will hopefully provide a more nuanced understanding of their direct and indirect 
involvement in the environmental impacts related to recycling of textile waste. 
 
In the present section, a general introduction to the project has been given, and the research aims of 
the project have been outlined. Section 2 presents an analysis of past literature dealing with topics 
deemed relevant in the context of treatment of post-consumer textile, highlighting key concepts and 
literature gaps. The materials and methods adopted to meet such goals are described in Section 3. 
Results are analyzed in section 4 and discussed in section 5, while section 6 draws the overall 
conclusions.

2. Terminology and analysis of past literature 
In this chapter, some of the aspects that play a role towards a more sustainable textile value chain are 
discussed and presented in relation to the current literature dealing with the topic. Relevant 
terminology is presented in section 2.1, while section 2.2 highlights key themes and main related 
literature gaps. 
2.1. Relevant terminology  
To avoid misinterpretations, it is important to start by having clear in mind some basic definitions 
related to the management of textile waste. An overview of the terminology adopted in this thesis is 
presented in Table 1. 
Table 1 Relevant definitions in the context of textile recycling. 
Terminology 
Definition 
Post-consumer textile 
In this report, clothes that the current owner does not use anymore, and wants 
to get rid of. Any kind of textiles other than clothes is not considered here. 
Finding 
An item added to the garment during the design/production phase, which was 
not part of the textile fabric in the first place. Button, zipper, patches, are 
examples of findings. The term can be interchanged with wording such as

2.2. Key concepts and literature gaps 
Various factors influence the quality and the quantity of disposed textiles. This section presents an 
outline of how the literature reviewed for this project deals, and potentially fails to deal, with those 
aspects that might play a role in the overall environmental impacts of textile reusing and recycling. 
 
2.2.1. Key concepts 
Relevance of collection phase. Among the studies reviewed for this thesis, many point out the 
significance of the collection phase within the management of used textiles. Together with the need 
for proper sorting, separate collection of textile and municipal waste is seen as key towards an 
efficient textile waste management strategy, as it represents the very first step towards recycling 
(Piribauer & Bartl, 2019). The following considerations are found to be the most relevant to keep in 
mind: 
- 
Textiles must be kept dry in order to avoid deterioration due to e.g. mold and rot (Piribauer & 
Bartl, 2019; Roos et al., 2019). Wet textiles are currently sent directly to incineration, thus a 
set-up that ensures textiles are protected from water potentially leads to a higher share of 
material suitable for reuse or recycling (Personal Conversation (UFF - Humana), 2023).  
- 
The presence of other types of waste (e.g. residual waste) can soil textiles that could have 
otherwise been reused or recycled, as well as compromising the quality of any second-hand 
products that could still be manufactured from them (Nørup et al., 2018).  
- 
The fraction of clothes that could be classified as reusable varies according to the method 
textiles had been collected. For example, shop collection gives a higher quality textile and a 
much smaller percentage of waste (Watson et al., 2016).  
 
Relevance and limitations of sorting phase. Sorting holds a pivotal role towards the development of 
collection systems and recycling technologies (Nørup et al., 2018). In the context of textile recycling, 
sorting is found to be a crucial step towards closing the material loop, because a correct identification 
of feedstock material is essential to select the proper recycling processes (Karell & Niinimäki, 2019). 
Nonetheless, the sorting phase is associated with many challenges: 
- 
The identification of textile materials is challenged mainly by the complex structure of textile 
polymers (Damayanti et al., 2021). Current automatic sorting technologies pose limitations 
when it comes to accurately distinguishing between colors, material blends and textile surfaces. 
==PAGE:19==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis 
7 
 
For instance, in cases where spinning is needed, the presence of fibers of different materials 
and colors results in low quality yarn (Piribauer & Bartl, 2019).  
- 
The presence of materials other than textiles, represented by accessories attached to garments, 
also challenges the process of automatic sorting (Damayanti et al., 2021). 
- 
Automatic sorting poses some limitations when specific fractions need to be identified (Karell 
& Niinimäki, 2019) 
- 
Manual sorting is still needed to distinguish what can be reused from what is instead only 
suitable for recycling (Eppinger, 2022; Karell & Niinimäki, 2019; Nørup et al., 2018). 
However, particularly for lower quality clothing and household textiles, marketing approaches 
vary among sorting centers (Nørup et al., 2018).  
- 
The amount of sorted materials can vary significantly when different sorting methods are 
applied, even when they had been sorted from the same sample (Nørup et al., 2018).  
 
Pre-processing step and challenges of mechanical recycling. Fiber-to-fiber recycling of textile is 
much less mature than in many other sectors for a number of reasons:  
- 
There is a general lack of ways to deal with the great variety of materials in terms of fibers, 
blends, dyes and finishes, which limits the effectiveness of mechanical recycling of textile 
(Roos et al., 2019). 
- 
 Fabrics are commonly mixed with other materials, such buttons, zippers or other decorations 
(Damayanti et al., 2021; Ljungkvist & Elander, 2016). Current mechanical recycling 
technologies cannot process parts of the clothes with accessories (Spathas, 2017), and the 
material needs to be cleaned beforehand.  
- 
Current recycling strategies have a big problem with multi-material textile waste (Piribauer & 
Bartl, 2019).   
 
Relevance of and factors influencing the replacement rate of second-hand textiles. The replacement 
rate, as well as what material is substituted, has a large influence on the environmental benefits of 
recycling (Roos et al., 2019). This holds true not only within the textile sector, but for material 
recycling in general, and examples can be found in the plastic (Aryan et al., 2019; Lazarevic et al., 
2010; Schwarz et al., 2021), and paper (Villanueva et al., 2004)industries. In the context of textile 
research, publications have found that results are influenced considerably in cases where different 
==PAGE:20==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis 
8 
 
replacement rates were adopted (Sandin & Peters, 2018). With this in mind,  it is relevant to evaluate 
some of many factors that are seen to be affecting the replacement rate: 
- 
The term recycling is, in most cases, used to indicate what is actually downcycling, where 
textile is reprocessed into materials of lower quality, thus without avoiding the production of 
a comparable virgin material (Trzepacz et al., 2023). About 95% of the recovered fibers are in 
fact not re-spun into yarn, but are directly processed into nonwovens, as if the lengths of the 
fibers after the disintegration are too short, re-spinning into yarns is not possible (Gulich, 2006; 
Piribauer & Bartl, 2019).  
- 
The recycled material often needs to be blended with virgin raw material in order to increase 
the quality of the final product (Eppinger, 2022; Karell & Niinimäki, 2019; Personal 
Conversation with Industry Expert, 2023). This means that, in such cases, the replacement rate 
is reduced, as new material is still necessary for the garment to reach a performance that is 
comparable with one that is entirely made of virgin fibers.  
- 
The replacement rate inevitably depends on the availability of a market with a matching 
demand (Roos et al., 2019). European sorters are very dependent on the global market for sales 
of separated collected used textile, although recently the global market has shown signs of a 
stabilizing demand (Trzepacz et al., 2023) 
- 
The acquisition of a second-hand piece of clothing not necessarily displaces the purchase of a 
new one (Nørup et al., 2019b). The geographical area is seen to play an important role in this 
regard, as major differences are found in the purchase habits of the population depending on 
the country (Nørup et al., 2019b).  
 
2.2.2. Literature gaps 
Collection and sorting phases. Despite their role played in the field of textile recycling, very little 
data is publicly available on sorting facilities (Nørup et al., 2019a). In fact, both collection and sorting 
life cycle stages are often excluded from the system boundaries from LCAs found in literature. In an 
analysis of 64 studies, Santos & Campos (2021) found that only 7 focused on collection, sorting, and 
reinsertion in the production chain. Among these, 3 were applied to reuse and 4 to recycling processes. 
Sandin & Peters (2018) also bring attention to the matter, highlighting that about half of the 41 
publications analyzed in their work fully or partly excluded collection and/or sorting phases.

Replacement rate=1 In the context of textile recycling, most studies use a value of 1 without 
justification (Roos et al., 2019; Trzepacz et al., 2023). This means that such research is carried out on 
the basis that one recycled garment fully substitutes one virgin item of a similar material, hence fully 
absorbing the environmental credits of avoiding the production of that specific new material. This is 
however an issue as it affects the reliability of the evidence that studies brought to substantiate the 
environmental advantages of textile reuse and recycling rate (Sandin & Peters, 2018). 
 
Impact categories. Alongside potentially relevant life-cycle stages, additional uncertainty around the 
environmental consequences of textile recycling is also due to limiting the scope to few, specific 
impact categories (Roos et al., 2019), and categories such as land use or water consumption and 
contamination have seldom been considered (Sandin & Peters, 2018).

3.2. Material flow analysis (MFA) 
Material flow analysis (MFA) is utilized to estimate post-consumer textile flows according to 
different collection methods and pre-processing criteria, and to evaluate the propagation of the 
uncertainties of the input data. The MFA is carried out through the software STAN2, version 2.6.801, 
developed by the Technical University of Vienna. Alongside enabling the tracking of material flows, 
the software helps in the process of estimating how uncertainties propagate throughout the model. 
This is relevant especially in the context of textile recycling, characterized by a lack of primary data. 
Understanding and tracking how uncertainties spread across related flows is essential in case any 
decision is to be made at a specific point in a complex value chain, which would always need to be 
referred to the upstream uncertainty.

is in turn believed to lead to different outcomes depending on the 3) product category, as it will be

3.3. Life cycle assessment (LCA) 
Life cycle assessment (LCA) is applied to evaluate the environmental impacts associated to the 
management of post-consumer textiles, to test the relevance that the recycling rate has on these 
impacts, and to investigate how collection and sorting practices influence these two aspects. First, the 
scoping of the analysis is described through the definition of the functional unit adopted, the scenarios 
evaluated, and the system boundaries considered (section 3.3.1). Then, an overview of the data 
collection process is given together with the main assumptions made to evaluate the environmental 
impacts (section 3.3.2). Lastly, sections 3.3.3 lays the foundation of the sensitivity analysis. 
  
3.3.1. Goal & scope 
Functional unit and reference flow 
The assessment is made on the basis of the following functional unit: 
 
“End-of-life management of 1 kg of post-consumer clothes of specific product type average fiber 
composition separately collected in Denmark in 2022.” 
 
The type of garment is specified to track any eventual differences in fiber composition and presence 
of recycling disruptors. This allows for a more tailored assessment, while still maintaining the 
analysis on a broad level without narrowing the scope to a specific clothing item. A unitarian 
reference flow is chosen to make it easier to apply directly values in relative terms, and to see how 
they vary according to the specific scenario.  
 
System boundaries and definition of scenarios 
The assessment is structured as a gate to grave LCA and includes life cycle stages between the 
collection of post-consumer textile waste to its end-of-life treatment (Figure 8). Raw material 
extraction, manufacturing, and use phases are excluded from the system boundaries. Activities related 
to what is necessary for the storage and packaging of the collected material, e.g., production and 
treatment of plastic bags or metal wires, are omitted. The life cycle of accessories is also not included, 
and the assessment is limited to their indirect impact on textile recycling. For both garment types, 
two collection strategies and two sorting methods are studied, for a total of eight different scenarios, 
as defined for the MFA (see section 3.2).

The assessment of the environmental impacts associated with post-consumer textiles is carried out

handled through system expansion.

Recycling process. Mechanical fiber-to-fiber is chosen as the close-loop recycling option for both 
product categories. Different open loop recycling practices are instead considered in the assessment, 
with top items assumed to be reprocessed into insulation material, and bottom articles into industrial 
wipes. This choice is made to reflect the different recycling options according to the fiber 
composition. Recycling is assumed to happen at a facility in Spain, regardless of the specific method. 
This is used as a generic representation of a Southern European country, where recycling is assumed 
to take place, but it should be noticed that textiles could also be exported to eastern Asia for 
mechanical recycling (Watson et al., 2016). The portion of material available for mechanical fiber-
to-fiber recycling depends on the method adopted to remove the accessories, as fiber length is 
preserved in a better way if a guillotine cutter is used compared to a shredder. A value of 80% is 
adopted for the guillotine scenario, while Eppinger (2022) suggests a figure of 5% for the shredding 
scenario. 
 
Recycling energy requirements and replacement rates. Closed-loop recycling is modelled at the fiber 
level. This entails that any further processing (e.g., spinning) needed to transform the recycled fiber 
into recycled yarn is not included in the system boundaries. This choice allows us to keep the 
assessment more manageable, maintaining it at a generic level without going into detailed modelling 
of specific products. The electricity necessary to reprocess textile fragments into recycled fibers is 
retrieved from Spathas (2017), and sense-checked with the value estimated by Esteve-Turrillas & de 
la Guardia (2017). Energy requirements for reprocessing textiles into industrial wipes are based on 
Schmidt et al. (2016), where one of the modelled scenarios describes the manufacturing of industrial 
wipes from used mixed textiles as a substitution for new cellulose-based ones. These two processes 
have different inputs in terms of materials, electricity and heat, and a distinction is kept between the 
energy consumed in the recycling step and that saved by avoiding the production of new industrial 
wipers. Downcycled into insulation material is modelled following the process proposed by 
Youhanan (2013), where cotton from used denim trousers replaces conventional insulation material 
for a share of 80%. The substituted material is modelled as polystyrene foam slab (Youhanan, 2013).

Virgin fiber needs. The share of virgin fibers needed in the fiber-to-fiber recycling process is assumed 
to change depending on the method used to clear the garment from accessories. In particular, 
guillotine cutters are seen to preserve the fiber length better than shredding machines, hence they 
affect the quality of the output material to a lesser extent. Typically, between 20% and 70% of 
mechanically recycled fibers are used depending on the original quality of the textiles, based on 
conversation with an industry expert. The highest (70%) and the lowest (20%) values are used to 
represent, respectively, the consequent need for virgin fibers for material processed with guillotine 
cutters (30%) and shredding machines (80%). 
 
Energy mix. Data on electricity and heat is based on information made presented by the International 
Energy Agency (IEA)7 on the countries and regions of interest, namely Denmark, Sweden, and 
Europe. For each of the three areas, the most recent, accessible figures on electricity and heat 
generation by source are considered (2021 for Denmark and Sweden, 2020 for Europe). The values 
are then adjusted for the energy mix to be representative of the two distinct cases of energy consumed 
and energy substituted. The former is used to model the energy requirements of any of the processes 
included in a specific system boundary, the latter represents electricity and heat of which generation 
is displaced by the energy obtained from incinerating textile waste. In both cases, further adaptation 
was also necessary as it was not possible to find a corresponding Ecoinvent process for specific energy 
sources, such as geothermal or solar thermal. 
 
Transport distances. Transport between facilities is based on average distances calculated between 
Denmark, Sweden, and Spain. Textile is assumed to be transported from the from the pre-sorting and 
recycling facility to a final marked either in Denmark, Europe, or Asia/Africa.

3.3.3. Perturbation analysis and scenario analysis 
A perturbation analysis is carried out to quantify the effect of changes in input parameters on the 
model results, whereas a scenario analysis is used to evaluate choices and model uncertainties, and to 
test different possibilities as well as their impact on the results. 
Perturbation analysis 
The perturbation analysis is performed in EASETECH applying the same impact method used for the 
life cycle impact assessment, i.e., EF 3.0. A default value of 1.1 is applied, meaning that the sensitivity 
of the model to every single parameter is assessed by evaluating the effects that a 10% increase of 
each parameter value provokes. The outcome is then translated into a contribution analysis by 
weighing the sensitivity of a single parameter to the cumulated sensitivity of the model, to better 
visualize the relative influence of each input data.  
 
Scenario analysis 
Recycling rate = 1. In order to test the effect of different modelling choices, two sets of alternative 
scenarios are evaluated. One of the main focuses of the project is to evaluate the influence of the 
recycling replacement rate in the overall environmental impact assessment. From the baseline 
scenario, however, it is not possible to appreciate this parameter in a straightforward way, since the 
amount of textile material that is available for recycling is influenced by various factors in the value 
chain between the bin and the recycling facility. In order to simulate a recycling rate equal to 1 in a 
context where neither collection nor sorting phases are considered, an alternative the following 
changes are applied: 
- 
The fraction of wet textiles collected is set = 0;  
- 
The efficiency of the automatic sorting facility is set = 100%; 
- 
The share of material lost in association with the removal of accessories is set = 0; 
- 
The share of material available for fiber-to-fiber recycling is set = 100%; 
- 
The need for virgin fiber is set = 0. 
 
Including upstream impacts. Another focus point is to evaluate to what extent textile circularity can 
contribute towards a more sustainable textile industry. An additional scenario is thus modelled to 
include upstream impacts related to the production of textiles, to assess to what extent different 
treatment options can counter benefit its burdens.

5. Discussion 
In following sections,  it is discussed how the flows of textile material as well as the overall 
environmental performance associated with different treatment options vary depending on collection 
and pre-processing methods (section 5.1). Such differences are then discussed through a perturbation 
analysis, to spot what are some of the main factors that cause them (section 5.2) and put in relation 
with the quality and uncertainty of the associated input data (section 5.3). Alternative scenarios are 
evaluated in 5.4, while sections 5.5 and 5.6 present some of the main limitations of the study and 
potential for future research, respectively. 
 
5.1. Collection, sorting and treatment options. 
The material flow analysis revealed how changing the collection strategy affects the fate of the 
collected material, with drop-off schemes leading to a significant higher share of reusable (70% vs 
40%) and a much lower fraction of waste (1% vs 11%) compared to door-to-door pickup solutions. 
The share of clothes resalable in Denmark is also higher in the first case (10% vs 5%). These outcomes 
suggest that specific collection strategies need to be evaluated to meet specific requirements, since 
bring-bank strategies are found to both maximize the share of high-quality clothes for domestic reuse, 
as well limiting the fraction potentially lost to incineration.  
 
A guillotine cutter leads to higher loss of textile material compared to a shredding machine, as they 
break the clothes into fragments of different sizes. However, a shredder affects the quality of the 
output fibers to a great extent, it being a much harsher treatment option. This is seen to influence both 
the share of textile suitable for fiber-to-fiber recycling purposes as well as the need for virgin material. 
Especially in a curbside collection scenario, where the flow of recyclable increases, it might become 
relevant to select the technique based on both the type of garment to be treated, and in association 
with the specific recycling option that will be employed.  
 
A larger share of textile material is lost by bottom products compared to top garments in both 
scenarios considered. This is to be related to the higher amount of accessories present on average in 
the first category (5) compared to the other (<1). Nonetheless, it should be kept in mind that these 
values are based on the assumption that each accessory counts as 1, as no information related to the 
size, nor the mass of the finding was registered in the survey.  
==PAGE:57==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis 
45 
 
Direct impacts associated with the life cycle stages of collection and sorting are found to be negligible 
when compared to the treatment options. However, the way clothes are collected and sorted plays a 
major role in the overall environmental impacts of managing post-consumer textiles. When evaluating 
the performance of scenarios sharing the same product type, variations are observed within the same 
impact category, which means that collection and pre-sorting practices alone have an indirect 
influence on the overall outcome of the assessment and should be taken into consideration in the 
implementation of strategies for handling post-consumer textiles. Tradeoffs between impact 
categories are also observed, as the relative performance of each scenario varies to the extent that the 
one performing worst towards one indicator can be that performing best in another. This implies that 
there is no set-up that can be applied alone with the aim to maximize environmental benefits in 
absolute terms, and the assessment should be rather made keeping a specific impact category in mind. 
A general tendency, although, can be traced, as among the best performing scenarios, four out of five 
rely on drop-off collection, while all the worst performing ones are based on door-to-door pick-up.  
 
The analysis at the process level reveals that there is no single treatment option that leads to the best 
case for all impact indicators across all scenarios, underlining again the relevance of directing the 
assessment to specific categories. Tradeoffs are found within all the five considered impact categories 
between recycling and reusing depending on the collection method and the pre-sorting technique. As 
a general tendency, reusing practices perform best in association with bring-banks solutions, while 
recycling can bring environmental savings especially in association with door-to-door collection. This 
can be directly linked to the material flow analysis results, as the share of textiles available for reusing 
is significantly higher compared to door-to-door strategies, which favor the stream of recyclables. 
Hence, the collection scheme in particular needs to be taken into account if, following the hierarchy 
of waste, reusing practices are to be encouraged. All the considerations above are based on differences 
in outcome based on the overall assessment. Reasons behind such differences are discussed further 
at the parameter level.

Post-consumer textiles fibers composition 
The model is found to be sensitive to the share of cotton (cotton_share) across almost all scenarios 
and impact categories. This can largely be explained by the heavy environmental impacts associated 
with the cultivation of this fiber, which the survey results showed to be representing the highest share 
in the composition of both bottoms (92%) and tops (63%). On the other hand, only around 5% of an 
average garment is polyester, and it is found to significantly contribute to the impacts in terms of 
ozone depletion. This shows how the composition of the garment indeed plays a role towards its 
environmental performance, although it should be reminded that upstream impacts associated with 
the fiber production are not included, and that these savings do not come free from upstream burdens 
in reality. This is further discussed in the scenario analysis. 
 
Reusable share 
The share of reusable garments is found to have a high influence across most impact categories, for 
both product types and collection scenarios. This can be related to the process contribution results, 
where reusing practices are found to be leading to the highest savings for all impact indicators. 
Connecting this with the outcome of the MFA, where a higher share of reusable clothes is associated 
==PAGE:60==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis 
48 
 
with bring-bank solutions, it becomes apparent the role of the collection strategy becomes apparent. 
In the context of the European hierarchy of waste, where material reuse should generally be preferred 
to material recycling, drop-off containers seem to be the best fit.  
 
The contribution analysis shows that, for most impact categories, recycling practices associated with 
door-to-door collection have also the potential to bring significant environmental benefits. This is 
also mainly related to considerations that can be drawn upon textiles flows. Having less feedstock 
available for reuse indirectly increases the amount of textile material sent to recycling, which 
represents around 30% in drop-off collection schemes and roughly 50% in curbside scenarios. Despite 
more feedstock available for recycling in association with curbside schemes, the savings connected 
with recycling practices almost never exceeds the ones brought by reusing. With the implementation 
of mandatory separate collection of textiles, the feedstock potentially available for reusing and 
recycling practices is most probably going to increase significantly. This is likely to reduce the portion 
of textiles lost to incineration and landfilling in the future. Nonetheless, evaluations with regards to 
the quality of material should be done when discussing implications of different collection schemes 
in terms of post-consumer textile reuse and recycling. An increased curbside collection might result 
in lowering the quality of the material collected but this might influence more the pre-sorting step 
rather than the recycling step. This should be kept in mind to establish a strategy at the municipality 
level.  
 
Reuse replacement rate  
The reuse replacement rate is found to significantly impact the results for all impact categories 
considered and across all scenarios evaluated, for both top and bottom products. This can be directly 
linked to the avoided production of virgin fibers, in case used clothes replace new items. In this 
perspective, the value assigned to the replacement rate is a key factor to keep in mind when evaluating 
the environmental benefits of reusing, since assuming a factor 1 arbitrarily can lead to large 
overestimations. An important aspect to consider is whether the acquisition of a second-hand piece 
of clothing displaces the purchase of a new one, and the geographical area is seen to play an important 
role in this regard. When taking into consideration a high-income country, second-hand clothing is 
found to be acquired as an alternative to a new item less than 50% of the time, the most likely reason 
being a high purchasing power (Nørup et al., 2019b). This implies that, in most cases, second-hand 
garments are bought on top of, and not as an alternative to new items. Replacement rates are also 
shown to be relatively low in African countries such as Angola, Malawi and Mozambique, where 
==PAGE:61==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis 
49 
 
clothes can be exported to. In these instances, the purchase of used clothes is not likely to replace the 
acquisition of new items for the opposite reason i.e., second-hand clothing may be the only 
economically viable option (Nørup et al., 2019b). This means that, with a big portion of the African 
population unable to afford new clothing, unless a cheap, second-hand option is available, no other 
purchase would be made (Nørup et al., 2019b; Piribauer & Bartl, 2019). In both instances, no 
production of new item would be replaced. 
 
Pre-processing step 
In scenarios involving guillotine cutter machinery, the share of textiles suitable for fiber-to-fiber 
recycling (ftf_share_guillotine) is found to be sensitive in both collection options and for both product 
types. However, this parameter is not found to be significantly contributing to the overall assessment 
if a shredder is applied. This indicates that the cleaning step can indirectly influence the 
environmental performance of textile recycling by directing a higher amount of textile to closed-loop 
recycling practices. 
 
The share of virgin fibers needed (virgin_fiber_need)  to reprocess the feedstock into high quality 
material is also found to be a sensitive parameter, although only in association with curbside 
collection scenarios.  
 
It should be noted how the material loss is not found to be affecting the results considerably, meaning 
that the implications of the cleaning step are mainly qualitative.  
 
Incineration. The efficiency of the incineration process to generate electricity is a parameter found 
to be sensitive especially in terms of ionizing radiation. This can be explained since this impact 
category is mainly associated with electricity. When investigating the process causing the most 
savings, this was found to be incineration of textile regarded as waste in the pre-sorting step. The 
values have a higher influence in drop-off collection scenarios compared to curbside ones, which can 
be explained by the differences in the composition and amount of the waste fraction. Bring-banks 
lead to less amount of wet textiles, but a higher share of impurities, which are modelled as shoes and 
leather.

estimation of the shares of different fibers, it should be kept in mind that no information was

Reusable shares  
The share of reusable clothes is found to be a sensitive parameter from the contibution analysis. 
However, it is essential to keep in mind that, while these values are highly reliable, since they are 
obtained on recent primary data, they cannot be considered representative, as they are based only on 
one organization. Nonetheless, this still leaves room for further discussion. If different collection 
schemes have such an influence on the amount of reusable textiles also according to a wider range of 
companies and organizatoin, then it is confirmed that the collection phase is a key element to factor 
in to follow the prioritization suggested by the European hierarchy of waste. On the other hand, in 
case that this situation is the exception rather than the norm, and it is only valid for the one 
organization that the data refer to, it could be argued that this difference is due to how the material is 
pre-sorted, proving how necessary basic criteria and guidelines are, as claimed by Nørup et al. (2018).  
Considerations around the fraction of reusable clothes should also be made in terms of uncertainty 
propagation. Since this distinction is done at the pre-sorting phase, thus very early in the value chain 
of textile recycling, and since it influences all other treatment flows, a high uncertainty in the input 
data point here propagates to a large extent everywhere downstream.  
 
Reuse replacement rate 
The reuse replacement rate results to be a highly contributing parameter in the model, which also 
presents a high input value uncertainty. In addition, this parameter absorbs the uncertainty related to 
both the reusable shares and the fiber composition. Therefore, in the perspective of uncertainty 
propagation, the reuse replacement rate is believed to be an important factor to investigate. 
 
Final point of sale and transport distances 
From the contribution analysis, only the replacement rate associated with exported clothes is found 
to be influencing the results significantly, but not with regards to clothes sold in Denmark. This is 
mainly related to the current flows of textile material, as the share of garments that can be reused 
==PAGE:64==
Valentina Rossi, s202644 
DTU Environment 
Master thesis

domestically is deemed to be much lower than the share of items sold internationally. The current 
market for second-hand clothes highly relies on international and intercontinental exports. This 
should be a key discussion point, as the burdens associated with the management of textiles discarded 
in Northern Europe claiming sustainable practices fall mainly on other regions. Countries outside the 
Nordics are thus expected to wear reusable clothing that has been discarded by consumers in higher 
income countries.  In addition, ample environmental benefits associated with reusing practices are 
evident for direct reuse scenarios, i.e., when no or few additional processes are needed (Muthu, 
2020b). However, long transportation distances might counterbalance the environmental savings 
related to the avoided production of virgin fibers (Muthu, 2020b). In the present overview, distances 
between facilities and to the final point of sale are not found to be affecting the results significantly. 
However, this is believed to be associated with choices made in terms of processes, since 
transportation is modelled assuming the highest vehicle emission standard (EUR6). Keeping in mind 
that second-hand clothes collected in Denmark are mostly exported internationally, this is believed 
to be a too optimistic assumption, since the vehicle class might very a lot across countries. Since 
clothes potentially need to travel long distances to reach the final point of sale, a more thorough 
modelling of the transport process should be done case by case. 
 
The interpretation of the uncertainty at the data point level should be integrated with how this 
uncertainty propagates alongside the model, considering the results from the MFA. The uncertainty 
of values associated with material flows of process happening early in the value chain will influence 
the interpretation of all the processes downstream. With this in mind, as the error spreads to the flows 
present in later steps, the potentially high quality of a data point placed towards the end of the value 
chain will be affected by the previous uncertainty.

in the model. The data retrieved from the field study are used to calculate the different average 
composition of tops and bottoms. However, the product category is modelled as fractions which are 
kept separate throughout the model, as if one piece of clothing was merely an aggregation of clearly 
distinguishable fibers instead of a blend of various materials. In this way, challenges related to the 
multi-fiber nature of most items, especially of top category, are not accounted for either in the sorting, 
nor in the recycling step. This most likely leads to an overestimation with regards to both the amount 
of textile correctly detected in the automatic sorting process and the fraction of material available for 
high quality fiber-to-fiber recycling.

for a more comprehensive assessment.

5.6. Future research 
Handling of uncertainty 
In a context of low data availability, an assessment based on ranges rather than punctual data would 
help limiting issues related with the high uncertainty of data. Software such as OpenLCA can help in 
performing range LCAs, as well as to evaluate what are the breakeven points between different 
treatment options in combination with various collection schemes and cleaning processes. Deploying 
a Monte Carlo analysis could also help evaluating the effect of uncertain data points. 
 
Transport distances 
The final point of sale is also believed to be an area worth investigating. In the present study, transport 
distances are not found to be contributing to the results to a great extent, although reasons are partly 
discussed. Given the currently high share of clothes sold internationally, it would be worth 
investigating more in detail to what extent traceability issues can hide some of the additional burdens 
related to the transportation of second-hand clothes. More information relative to the real final 
destination of exported items would allow for a more detail modeling of replacement rate, type of 
vehicle, and shipment options, providing a more complete overview of the environmental impacts 
associated with textile reusing and recycling. 
 
Use phase 
In the present study, the use phase is excluded from the system boundaries. For a more realistic 
estimation of the replacement rate, considerations in terms of the durability of a garment would also 
need to be considered. Here, it is assumed that a second-hand item has the same lifetime as a new 
one. However, especially with regards to recycled textiles, the pre- and re-processing steps can affect

6. Conclusions 
This thesis was conducted with the main goal of evaluating some of the factors which potentially 
limit the environmental benefits associated with reuse and recycling of post-consumer garments. 
Another aim of the project was to evaluate whether including collection and cleaning practices into 
the system boundaries would affect the overall assessment. To achieve this purpose, a life cycle 
assessment, LCA was performed, in combination with an MFA and a questionnaire survey.  
 
It was found that collection phase and pre/processing  step have a negligible direct impact compared 
to the various treatment options. However, changes in collection strategy and/or cleaning methods 
comported changes in the overall environmental assessment, which underlined an influence of an 
indirect nature. The MFA showed that collection plays a relevant role in determining the reusable, 
recyclable, and waste flows of the collected textile. The cleaning step associated with the removal of 
accessories prior recycling also indirectly affects the results, as it influences the fraction of textile 
material suitable for closed/loop recycling. It also affects the share of virgin fiber that needs to be 
mixed with the recycled material in order to obtain a product of good-enough quality. 
 
All these factors contribute to the overall assessment of the environmental impacts associated with 
textile circularity. Thus, excluding from the system boundaries collection and cleaning processes a 
priori may result in a wrongful estimation of the environmental benefits related to reuse and recycling 
of textiles. For the same reason arbitrarily assuming the replacement rate equal to 1 potentially leads 
to large overestimations across all impact categories. The replacement rate absorbs much of the 
environmental benefits of circular practices, and the assessment can lead to significant overestimation 
if specific factors influencing this value are not considered. Major tradeoffs are identified overall 
across impact categories within the same scenario, as well as across scenarios in relation to a single 
impact indicator. When making any environmental claim related to a practice being more sustainable 
than another, it should be clear in which perspective sustainability is evaluated. As there does not 
seem to be any absolute best practice, no one-size-fits-all kind of solution should be declared without 
contextualization. 
Finally, the lack of primary data limits the modelling of textile-to-textile recycling, and  a correct 
interpretation should be always done by keeping in mind uncertainty considerations. In the 
perspective of the implementation of mandatory separate collection, all these considerations should 
be taken into account.

SIPTex facility and question flow 
SIPTex (Swedish Innovation Platform for Textile sorting) is a big scale facility for automatic sorting8 
located in Malmö, Sweden. The project took off in 2015, and it has been carried out in three 
consecutive phases, namely 1) initiation (June 2015 – January 2016), 2) collaborative project (June 
2016 – August 2018), and 3) implementation (May 2019 – October 2022), all successfully completed. 
The first step focused on the construction of the textile sorting facility, as well as on gathering the 
technical equipment and identifying potential customers. Afterwards, the potential to automatically 
sort textile and followingly produce high-quality recycled products was investigated, and a detailed 
business plan for an industrial-scale facility was developed based on the practical sorting results. In 
the third and last step, it was possible to test and evaluate the sorting of textile waste on a full scale, 
which provided additional knowledge and new insights about the industry’s challenges and 
opportunities. Figure 26 shows a side view of the machinery at SIPTex.

Textile waste is sorted by fiber type and color through near-infrared and visual spectroscopy 
(NIR/VIS). The first detects the fiber type, while the second senses the color. The machinery sorts 
the waste in three consecutive steps, and in each of them both near-infrared and visual spectroscopy 
are employed. This means that it is possible to sort three material categories during one cycle. Both 
unsorted input and sorted output textiles are grouped into bales, typically weighing between 300-350 
kg, which are then tied with metal wires (Figure 27).  
   
    
Figure 27 Baled textiles (left) and piled textiles ready to be loaded onto the conveyor belt (right). Pictures taken during the visit at

The process starts when mixed, loose textiles are fed into the plant. A drum is present inside the feeder 
to separate the units, which then slowly move on to the conveyor belt, so that they are distributed as 
evenly as possible. Three conveyor belts carry the feedstock through the three scanners. When the 
fabric reaches one of the sensors, it is illuminated with near-infrared light, which is reflected in 
different ways depending on the material. This makes it possible for the machinery to detect the type 
of fiber. The feedstock is scanned from above, and as this is done multiple times, it is possible to 
detect also what lies underneath. After each scanning, compressed air blows the desired fraction into 
a separate belt. The selected textile goes through a purification step, where the same type of sensor 
sorts out what may be wrong. At the end of this step, the sorted textile goes on into the baler, where 
it is packed and made ready for material recycling. The full process takes approximately 7.5 minutes 
and is entirely automated, with only two semi-manual steps in a complete sorting cycle. The first is 
needed to load the clothes at the top of the first conveyor belt, the second to take the finished bales 
away from the machinery. Both are done with the aid of a forklift truck. A schematic summary of the 
automated sorting process is shown in Figure 28.
