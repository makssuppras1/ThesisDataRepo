Abstract
In this report we document our efforts in applying pre-training to a large amount of
unlabelled data in order to increase the performance of downstream tasks in which
there is only a limited amount of labelled data. Specifically we used the recently
proposed XLNet model, which introduced a new pre-training framework named per-
mutation language modelling.
We present and explain the underlying theory leading up to the attention mechanism,
the Transformer and ultimately the XLNet model. We explain the alterations made
to the original model in order to be able to process and train on proteins.
The
experiments performed for both the pre-training and the finetuning are presented as
well as their results.
We found that pre-training under this framework had a significant impact on the
performance of the model on the task of subcellular localisation. By pre-training the
model on unlabelled data the model increased its accuracy by approximately 10%
compared to no pre-training.
The best accuracy achieved was 73.53% which is a
slight increase of the baseline of 72.89% using raw protein sequences. This suggests
that the XLNet model is applicable on protein prediction tasks.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Many subfields within biology, such as genetics, immunology and enzymology are
concerned with the well-being of humans. Much of the research within these fields
builds on the workings of proteins. Among many of its many vital roles, proteins are
a crucial part of cellular biology and living organisms. For this reason, a full under-
standing of proteins and their functionalities would significantly push the boundaries
of research in biology and medicine. However, dealing with proteins has proven a
challenge not only because of the vast amount of different proteins, but also because
of the great diversity of amino acid sequences that make up proteins. Bioinformatics
is a field of research that combines biology, computer science, information engineering,
mathematics and statistics, and has become increasingly important and useful when
dealing with biomolecular sequences [19]. This is mainly due to the advancements
made in computational hardware and computer science, as well as the increasing
amount of protein data that has become available [8, 5].
A particular subfield of computer science that has proven to be extremely useful in
recent years is deep learning. It has made significant advancements in areas such
as computer vision, speech recognition and natural language processing (NLP) [44,
37, 11, 47]. In particular, recurrent neural networks, and more recently, Transformer
networks have shown great promise when dealing with data of sequential nature.
These models have been shown to be highly applicable in the field of bioinformatics
as well [42, 2]. However, a common problem with deep learning models is that they
often require a lot of labelled data in order to learn meaningful information about the
problem at hand. Despite there being millions of protein sequences available, only a
fraction of them are labelled. Therefore a lot of information is potentially lost by not
considering these unlabelled sequences. This poses the question of the possibility of
extracting information from the large amount of unlabelled protein sequences in order
to improve the performances on downstream tasks such as subcellular localisation and
secondary structure prediction.
In this thesis, a new model is proposed which aims to utilise unlabelled protein se-
quences to improve performance on downstream tasks. Taking inspiration from the
state-of-the-art within NLP, we propose to use XLNet [47].
XLNet introduces a
specific way of pre-training the network, namely permutation language modelling,
such that the parameters of the model are able to capture underlying structures of

the domain of interest. Of particular interest in the new pre-training framework is
modelling deep bidirectional dependencies within the sequences. XLNet has shown
great improvement on various downstream tasks in NLP. Our hope is to show that
this model architecture as well as the pre-training framework can improve the perfor-
mance on downstream tasks in bioinformatics.
The goal of the thesis is to show that pre-training a model on a large amount of
unlabelled protein sequence data can help improve the accuracy of models when
performing downstream tasks on proteins. Specifically using the ideas proposed in
XLNet. One such downstream task in bioinformatics is predicting the subcellular
localisation of proteins. The function of a protein depends on where it is located
within a cell and mislocalisation of proteins can lead to a wide range of diseases [23].
Being a vital task, and already having been extensively studied [13, 48] it serves
perfectly as a task to test the effect of pre-training.
In the following section (1.1), the motivation for the thesis is presented. This is fol-
lowed by a formal problem statement (1.2) and related work to the thesis (1.3). In

considerations taken and tools used to complete the thesis. In chapter 4, the imple-
mentation details are explained and in chapter 5, the datasets and the experiments
conducted as well as their results are presented. In chapter 6, the thesis is concluded
which includes a small section about possible future work.
1.1
Motivation
Responsible: Frederik W. Andersen (s144440)
The field of deep learning is evolving rapidly with the increasing computational power
available and the innovative model architectures being invented. Deep learning meth-
ods have also achieved state-of-the-art performance in a wide range of scientific fields.
In light of the modern model architectures dealing with data of sequential nature
within deep learning, it has become applicable in the field of bioinformatics. Specifi-
cally it has shown a lot of promise in protein prediction tasks, reaching state-of-the-art
in such tasks as subcellular localisation and secondary-structure prediction [2, 18].
Using unlabelled data has already shown promising results in the field of NLP. In
most of these results, a language model is used to improve performance. Language
models are models that are trained to predict words given a previous context of words.
However, the common approach has been to implement a model which is pre-trained
on unlabelled data using a unsupervised training objective, and then using this model
to produce input embeddings to task-specific model architectures. Newer approaches
have shown that it is possible to finetune the pre-trained model on downstream tasks,
with minimal task-specific architectural changes, often achieving state-of-the-art.

1.2 Problem Statement
3
Research in NLP has also shown that modelling deep bidirectional context improves
performance in many cases [11].
XLNet proposes a new pre-training framework,
namely permutation language modelling, which combines the best of auto-regressive
language modelling and denoising auto-encoding and uses a Transformer-XL network
architecture. At the time of release they pushed the state-of-the-art of multiple tasks
in NLP.
1.2
Problem Statement
Responsible: Magnus Nolsøe (s144471)
It has been shown that deep learning-based language modelling applied to amino acid
sequences can to some degree predict amino acids given their context, and improve
downstream tasks such as subcellular localisation. With the capability of modelling
bidirectional contexts, denoising autoencoding based pre-training like BERT achieves
better performance than pre-training approaches based on autoregressive language
modelling.
However, relying on corrupting the input with masks, BERT neglects
dependency between the masked positions and suffers from a pretrain-finetune dis-
crepancy. Standard language models has the caveat of not considering bidirectional
information when encoding sequences. XLNet combines denoising autoencoding with
autoregressive language modelling and doing so achieves state of the art in the tasks
it is applied to.
The goal of this project is to explore the effect on downstream
protein prediction tasks by pre-training a model on a large unlabelled dataset of pro-
teins. Specifically using XLNet and the pre-training objective they propose [47], in
an attempt to improve understanding of protein sequences and hopefully improve the
above-mentioned subcellular localisation task.
1.3
Related Work
Responsible: Frederik W. Andersen (s144440)
Experimentally subcellular localisation of proteins have been determined by fluores-
cent microscopy. Being a rather time consuming and laborious task, a lot of research
has been done in predicting the subcellular localisation of a protein using computer
models [13, 48]. Traditionally, these models have used non-linear classifiers such as
support vector machines.
However, newer models have focused on the sequential
structure of the amino acids sequences by using sequential models such as recurrent
neural networks, out-performing current state-of-the-art algorithms[2].
In order to improve the performance on protein prediction tasks, many methods
of extracting features from the raw protein sequences have been proposed.
One
widely used representation of proteins are protein profiles which are position specific
scoring matrices (PSSMs). These protein profiles are acquired by multiple sequence
alignments against a query protein, where the amino acid residues are scored at
==PAGE:16==
4
1 Introduction
each position based on the sequence alignment. The profiles are then used as input
features to a computer model. The benefit of using protein profiles is that they encode
evolutionary information and therefore often lead to increased performance. However,
a downside is that these profiles are usually computationally demanding to produce.
Another method of representing a protein sequence that has been proposed is SeqVec
[21]. This method draws inspiration from NLP, using a bidirectional language model
proposed by ELMo. The aim is to train a model that is capable of producing dense
embedding vectors based on the amino acid sequence. These embeddings are trained
to capture biophysical properties of the protein, and are used as input to protein
prediction tasks.
Research within NLP, especially using deep learning, has lead to a wide range of
new model architectures. One of the newest models introduced is the Transformer
[44]. This model architecture uses self-attention [4] and completely removes recurrent
connections seen in RNNs, meaning they, to a larger extent, can be trained in parallel.
Many of the new state-of-the-art models in NLP are based on the Transformer [11,
47, 37].

2.1
Bioinformatics
Responsible: Magnus Nolsøe (s144471)
2.1.1
Proteins
Proteins are polymers of amino acids. Amino acids polymerize when they form pep-
tide bonds between one another. This peptide bond formation is an example of a
dehydration reaction, because the two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom are
lost during the formation, making a water molecule. If two amino acids combine,
it results in a dipeptide. If between 3 and 10 amino acids come together, it results
in a oligopeptide. If more than 10 amino acids combine, it is called a polypeptide.
Proteins are large polypeptides, often between 300-1000 amino acids, that are folded
in such a way that they have some biological function. Proteins have very specific
shapes from which their function is derived. Their structure follows a very specific hi-
erarchy, namely primary, secondary, tertiary and quaternary protein structure. The
primary protein structure is the sequence of the amino acids, often with each amino
acid written in abbreviated form for simplicity. The shape the protein takes depends
on the primary structure as it is the identity of all the side chains and the sequence
they are found in that determines how the protein will fold and thus the function of
the protein. It is this data, the sequence of amino acids, that will be used to represent
proteins in the thesis. A lot of protein data is available but only a limited amount of
it is labelled hence finding a way to gain knowledge from the unlabelled protein data
would vastly increase the data which could be used to train a model.

2.1.2
Amino Acids
Amino acids are monomers that combine over long chains to make proteins. There
are 20 amino acids and an additional two amino acids which are modified variations
only found in certain organisms. All amino acids share the same basic structure with
an amine group, NH2, a carboxyl group, COOH, a hydrogen atom, H, and then a
variable side chain, R, that is unique for every amino acid. The side chain determines
the characteristic of the amino acids. Such characteristic could be whether an amino
acid is acidic, like glutamate, basic, like lysine or hydrophobic, like leucine. The 20
amino acids, as well as their three and one letter abbreviations can be seen in figure

2.1.3
Subcellular Localisation
Proteins found in eukaryotic cells, which are cells that have a nucleus enclosed within
membranes, can be found at one or more functionally distinct compartments within
the cell, namely organelles. The function of a protein in a cell depends very much
on its location within the organelles. Moreover, subcellular localisation is critical for
target drug testing, and mislocalisation of proteins leads to a wide range of diseases
[23]. Therefore predicting subcellular localisation of proteins is an essential task in
bioinformatics. In addition to this, traditional methods of determining the subcellular
localisation, such as fluorescent microscopy, fluorescent tagging with bio-markers, are
laborious, time consuming and costly.
These methods are therefore infeasible to
apply on large databases of proteins. This motivates the usage of computer model
techniques such as machine learning. Research into this area has been widely carried
out, and computational models have been developed which have proven to attain a
level of precision exceeding that of high-throughput laboratory approaches [38].
The amino acid sequence of proteins contains information as to the where the protein
is destined. This information is called the sorting signal (also known as target se-
quence or signal peptide). In table 2.1 is a list of the most common sorting signals in
eukaryotic cells. The problem of subcellular locailisation therefore consists in finding
these sorting signals and recognising them.

2 Methods
Machine learning is a subfield in computer science in which learning algorithms learn
from data to fit some parameters of a model in order to perform some task optimally
according to some metric. To understand what this means we first have to define
what it means to ’learn’. Almost all computer models within machine learning are
built from adjustable parameters, that can be tweaked so as to better perform some
given task. These parameters are adjusted by being exposed to data, and according
to some optimisation algorithm the parameters are fitted so the model is able to
capture the data. The act of adjusting the parameters in this way is called learning,
and running multiple iterations of learning steps is called training. A very common
method of optimising the parameters of a model is gradient descent, which is the
main optimisation method in almost all cases in deep learning.
Learning algorithms can be roughly categorised into two learning frameworks, super-
vised learning and unsupervised learning. There is no exact distinction between these
two frameworks and they are often overlapping [15].
Supervised learning:
Each data point is a pair of an input value x, consisting of
a set of features, and one or more target variables or labels t. The aim of supervised
learning is then to predict the target given the input. This is often written as p(t|x),
meaning that supervised learning often consists in fitting a conditional probability
distribution. The target variables could be whether an email is spam or not, if an
input image depicts a cat or a dog, the sentiment of a text or a numeric value. If
the target consists of a set of categories, the learning task is known as a classification
task. If the targets is a continuously varying variable, the learning task is is known
as a regression task.
Unsupervised learning:
The data is unlabelled, i.e.
the data consist in only
the inputs x. The model will be given unlabelled data and attempt to learn some
structure and extract useful information from the data. The objective is often written
as p(x), meaning that unsupervised learning often consists in fitting some probability
distribution of x. As the model does not have access to the true labels of the data,
there is no way for the model to measure its qualitative performance. Instead, the
model will be learning how to create a mapping from given input to particular output
based on what is it learning about the structure of the data.
A popular application of unsupervised learning are clustering algorithms. Clustering
is concerned with classifying unlabelled data into a set of clusters. If we have data in
some dimensional space, we then wish to group the datapoints such that points that
are related are grouped together. An example of clustering can be seen in figure 2.2.
In the first figure (a), we can see a graph of weight against height. In the second figure
(b) we can see the same graph but divided into two clusters. These cluster could be
interpreted as genders, where the green cluster corresponds to female and the red
cluster corresponds to male.
Unsupervised learning is also used by autoencoders,
which will be further explained in section 2.5.

2.2.1
Models
The aim of machine learning is to model real world distributions. Alternatively (and
maybe more to the point) it is to produce a function f, that solves a given real
world task. The distributions to be modelled are often extremely complicated, and
depend on a lot of different factors.
It is therefore extremely difficult to capture
such distributions perfectly with a universal learning algorithm or the absolute best
learning algorithm. What is therefore done instead is to use parameterised functions,
otherwise known as models, denoted as fθ, where θ is the set of adjustable parameters
that control how the function f performs. Most models also have parameters that are
not possible to automatically adjust with the learning algorithm. These are typically
called hyperparameters, and the task of adjusting these parameters lie in the hands
of the developer.
2.2.2
Collecting and preparing data
In machine learning a dataset is commonly represented as a design matrix X where
each row of X is an observation (also called data-point), and the columns are features.
So Xi,j is feature j of observation i.
There are of course datasets that can’t be
represented in this way. For example in the case of time-series, the data is represented
as a sequence x = (x1, x2, ..., xn), where xi is the value measured at timestep i. In the
case of supervised learning each data-point contains a label or target. These labels
are commonly provided in a vector y, where yi is the label (or target) of example Xi.
Labels may consist in more than just a single number.
==PAGE:22==
10
2 Methods
A lot of the work in machine learning consists in collecting and preparing data. The
models almost always require the data to have a specific form, whether it be in a
vector, a single number or have a specific dimensionality.
Some pre-processing is
therefore often performed on the data before it is fed to the model. This could for
example be a principal component analysis (PCA) [25] processing step, in the case
of dimensionality reduction. In the case of natural language processing (NLP), and
indeed also bioinformatics (or more specifically protein sequences), a pre-processing
step consists in encoding the tokens in the sequence. For NLP the tokens can be the
words in a sentence, and for protein sequences could be the amino acids.
An important feature of the data is that it is representative of the real world. By
this it is meant that in order for the models to have any chance of becoming good at
solving a real world problem, the data needs to capture the entirety (or at least close
to the entirety) of the underlying distributions. Given that machine learning models
are trained solely on data, it means that the models will only learn distributions that
are captured by the data.
2.2.3
Evaluating the model
In order to assess the performance of a model, and comparing it to other models,
we need to be able to evaluate it. The way this is usually done is by defining what
is known as a cost function (also called loss, error, objective or criterion function).
These terms may be used interchangeably. The cost function C usually takes as input
the output of the function fθ on a particular observation xi, this can be written as
Ci = C(fθ(xi), yi). The total cost is then a sum of the cost function on each individual
observation over the entire dataset. Using this we can then compare functions with
various parameters θ, and choose the optimal set. Most often the optimal θ is the
one that minimizes the cost.
A problem encountered when training and evaluating models is that how do we know
how good the model is on data it has never seen before? Or rather how does the
model generalise? To measure this we introduce the generalization error Lgen, which
is defined as the expected loss. It can be written as the following:
Lgen = Efθ[C(fθ(x), y)] =
∫
C(fθ(x), y)p(x, y)dxdy
(2.1)

over all possible values for x and y, i.e. all possible observations and corresponding
labels. The best model would then be the one that minimises Lgen, however since
Lgen can not be calculated in practice, we approximate it using a technique known
as K-fold cross-validation. The dataset D is split into K folds, where K −1 folds are
used to trained the model, and the last fold is used as a validation dataset Dvalid, i.e.
to evaluate the model performance on unseen datapoints. This is carried out such
that all K folds have been used as validation datasets, and the losses across all folds

us to measure how the model generalises.
2.2.4
Regularization
Regularization is an integral part of machine learning. Regularization is any modifi-
cation we make to a learning algorithm that is intended to reduce its generalization
error but not its training error [15]. Essentially regularization is a method that allows
us to put preference on the parameter space θ, such that unpreferred solutions are
only chosen if they perform significantly better on the training data. An example of
regularization is what is known as weight decay. Assuming the parameters of a given
model are a vector of weights, i.e. θ = w, then weight decay is a modification to
the cost function such that we minimize both the normal cost function as well as the
squared L2 norm of the weights, defined as the following:
C∗(fw(Xtrain), ytrain) = C(fw(Xtrain), ytrain) + λw⊤w
(2.2)
where fw is the model, Xtrain and ytrain are training data and labels respectively.
The λ factor is known as weight decay rate, and is a hyperparameter. It controls
to which degree weights with lower squared L2 norm are preferred. Minimising C∗
results in a choice of weights that is a tradeoff between fitting training data and being
small. The point is that a preference towards lower weights has been introduced, and
only in the case when a set of higher weights significantly reduce the cost are they
chosen.
Introducing preference towards some functions over others is a general way of control-
ling the model’s capacity2. In the example of weight decay we introduced a preference
towards weights with lower squared L2 norm, however there are a wide variety of other
methods to do so. Together these methods are known as regularization [15].
2.2.4.1
Bias-Variance tradeoff
Regularizing a model is a way of introducing a bias, leading to a constraint on the
model complexity. A high bias may lead to such a low model complexity that it is
unable to capture the data, i.e. the model is underfitting. On the opposite side we
have the notion of variance of the model, i.e. a model is said to have high variance if
there is low constraint on the complexity. Too high variance may on the other hand
lead to overfitting. Given two models, one with high bias and one with high variance,
the way we usually pick is again by using cross-validation. This means that we choose

2.3.5
Gradient Based Learning
An essential issue of deep learning (or machine learning for that matter) is to obtain
the optimal parameters of a given model. By this it is meant that the weights of a
given model need to be tuned correctly, in order for the model to perform optimally on
the task at hand. Linked to this problem is an optimisation task, which in some cases
in machine learning have analytical solution or linear solvers to obtain the optimal
parameters. For example for optimisation of linear regression models there is a closed
form solution for the weights, that always gives the minimum with respect to the
MSE cost function. However artificial neural networks are highly non-linear, as a
result of the activation functions described in 2.3.2, and therefore the cost function
becomes non-convex.
Gradient Descent
Gradient descent [28] is the backbone optimisation method used to train ANNs. The
objective of gradient descent is to minimize (or maximize) a given cost function. It
works by incrementally changing the weights of the model, such that the cost function
is minimized/maximized at each step. The way in which the weights are changed at
each step is based on the gradient of the cost function with respect to the weights. If
we define a cost function C, then the weight updating scheme is done in the following
way:

Optimization algorithms

In section 2.3.5 gradient descent was discussed as an optimisation method for neural
networks. There are in fact a whole variety of different optimisation algorithms, most
of which build on gradient descent. Much research has been done in this area, leading
to better optimisation algorithms, that either reach better optimum points, or reach
the optimum faster. Some of these algorithms are briefly outlined in this section.
2.4.1
Stochastic Gradient Descent
Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is a stochastic approximation of gradient descent.
Gradient descent (GD) works by calculating the gradient of the cost function over
the entire dataset. SGD approximates the gradient by calculating the gradient over
a random subset of data from the entire dataset. These random subsets are often
called mini-batches [39]. The reason why SGD is more applicable than standard GD,
is because in order to make a single update on the model in GD the entire dataset
has to be evaluated. If the dataset is large, this can take a long time to converge. For
SGD we evaluate a subset of points, leading to more update of the parameters of the
model, and in practice a faster convergence.
2.4.1.1
Momentum
Momentum is an addition to SGD which optimises the convergence time. I.e. SGD
with momentum is almost always faster than normal SGD. In short it works by calcu-
lating the gradient over the mini-batch as an exponential moving average. Ultimately
the effect it has is that it reduces oscillation of the gradients in some directions. Specif-
ically the oscillation in directions which do not minimise the cost function are reduced,
such that the convergence is faster in the direction towards the minimum.
2.4.2
RMSProp
RMSProp is a slight variation on SGD with momentum. In SGD with momentum
the gradients are calculated using a moving average over a mini-batch, in a linear
==PAGE:34==
22
2 Methods
fashion, and then the weights are calculated using equation 2.12. With RMSProp the
moving average is calculated using the square of the gradient, and when updating the
weights the gradient is normalized by this squared average.
2.4.3
Adam
An optimisation algorithm which has been shown to work really well on a great variety
of problems is the Adam optimizer [26]. This algorithm is somewhat of a combination
of RMSProp and SGD with momentum. Adam calculates two exponential moving
averages using both the SGD with momentum method and the RMSProp method
and combines these averages when calculating the gradient.
For all the experiments done in this report, the Adam optimiser was used.

2.6.1
Backpropagation Through Time
To train the RNN, an extension of backpropagation called backpropagation through
time is used. Similar to feed forward models, a forward pass is made through the
model. However, for RNNs the forward pass consists of going through time, updating
the cell state based on the input as well as the previous cell state, generating an
output and loss at each time step and eventually summing these losses into the final
total loss. So now, instead of backpropagating the loss through a single model at a
single time step, the loss is backpropagated at each individual time step and then
across time steps. So the backpropagation takes place at the current time step and
all the way back to the very beginning of the sequence, as illustrated in figure 2.13.
==PAGE:40==
28
2 Methods
Figure 2.13: Illustration of the loss backpropagating through each time step to the
very beginning of the sequence.
As seen in the figure, between every time step, when doing the backpropagation, is a
matrix Whh. This means that at every step a matrix multiplication involving Whh
has to be done. In addition to this, each cell state update results from a nonlinear
activation. So when computing the gradient with respect to the initial cell state h0,
the task comprises many matrix multiplications involving the weight matrix Whh as
well as the repeated use of the derivative of the activation function. This can pose
two problems, namely exploding and vanishing gradients.
2.6.2
Exploding and Vanishing Gradients
When considering the matrix multiplication operations, if many of the values are
greater than 1 the gradient can become extremely large making it very difficult to per-
form any optimisation. This problem is known as exploding gradients. One method
to prevent exploding gradients is by clipping the norm of the exploded gradients when
it is too large [33]. By doing this, the gradients are simply rescaled whenever they
exceed a threshold, preventing them from ever exploding.
Another problem arises when the matrix values are too small, such as when there
are a lot of values below 1. In this case, at each step the small values are multiplied
together, resulting in smaller and smaller errors and thus smaller gradients as you
backpropagate through time. Eventually, the gradients become so small that it once
again is difficult to perform any optimisation. This problem is known as vanishing
gradients. A side-effect of this problem is that during training, the model will end
up with a bias to capture short-term memory as you are really only optimising the
model a few time steps back. There are a variety of ways to deal with the problem of
vanishing gradients. One method to alleviate the vanishing gradients problem is to
==PAGE:41==
2.6 Recurrent Neural Networks
29
use a regularisation term that forces the error signal not to vanish as it travels back
in time [33]. Another solution is to use more complex types of recurrent cells that
use gates to control the flow of the information that is passed through the cell. In
particular, Long Short Term Memory networks (LSTMs) [22] and Gated Recurrent
Unit (GRUs) [6] have gained a lot of traction.

Embeddings
Responsible: Magnus Nolsøe (s144471)
A problem that arises within NLP is how to encode words or phrases such that they
can be processed by a computational model. A way to do it is by using a one-hot
encoding of the vocabulary. One-hot encoding lists the words into a 1 × N (where
N is the number of words in the vocabulary) vector with 0’s in all places except for
a single 1, which identifies the word. The problem with this encoding is that N is
usually a very large number, as it depends on the size of the vocabulary of the given
language. Furthermore the expressive strength of one-hot encoded vectors is very
poor. Therefore it is more common to use word embeddings, which are dense vector
representation of the words, meaning they are vectors of real numbers. The dimension
of the embedding space is a configurable parameter, and usually much smaller than
the dimension of the one-hot encoded vectors. Given the embeddings are vectors of
real numbers, they are able to carry a lot more information about the words.
A feature of the word embeddings is that they are able to capture relations between
words in a vocabulary. These relations are normally seen in the euclidean distance
between their embedding vectors. In the embedding space, words that share certain
==PAGE:45==
2.8 Attention
33
feature are usually close together. Embeddings can be trained in the same way as
normal neural network models, and are therefore often trained in conjunction with the
model using them. However it is also possible to utilise pre-trained word embeddings.
Many such embeddings have been created for various languages using ideas such as
GLoVe[34] and the Word2Vec, which are trained using either continuous bag-of-words
(CBOW) or continuous skip-gram [31]. These methods of generating embeddings are
often called context-insensitive, as they are constant and do not depend on the context
the words are used in. Examples of the opposite, i.e context-sensitive embeddings,
are seen in models such as ELMo[35] and BERT[11]. These models produce the word
embeddings based on the context the words occur in.

2.9.1
Embedding layers
Embeddings are learned to turn each input tokens and output tokens into vectors
using an embedding weight matrix. The embedding layer outputs a vector of a pre-
defined dimension no matter the input and can help capture categories in the trans-
formed space. The embedding only takes place at the bottom most encoding/decoding
layer and the weights for the two embedding layers are shared.
2.9.2
Positional Encoding
Due to the removal of the recurrence in the model, to be able to capture the sequential
order of the input additional information about the relative or absolute position of
the tokens in the sequence is needed. To accommodate this, the Transformer adds a
vector, a positional encoding, to the bottom of the encoder and decoder stacks after
the embedding. The embedding and the positional encoding have the same dimension
dmodel so the two can be summed. The positional encoding can either be learned or
fixed. In the original Transformer, it was found that learned and fixed positional
encoding achieved similar results, so a fixed positional encoding was chosen.

different positions of a single sequence in order to compute a representation of the
sequence [44]. The first step in calculating self attention is to create three vectors,
namely a Query vector, a Key vector and a Value vector. This is done by multiplying
the input to the attention layer with three matrices. Multiplying the input with the
Query weight matrix, W Q, the Key weight matrix, W K and the Value weight matrix,
W V creates the Query, Q, Key, K, and Value, V , vectors, respectively. These weight
matrices are not shared between each encoder or between each decoder and they are
trained during the training process.
The second step is to calculate a score which determines how much focus has to be
placed on which parts of the input for the given input at a certain position. This
score is calculated by taking the dot product of Qi and the Kn vector of the word
currently looked at.

2.9.5
Masked Self Attention and Encoder-Decoder Attention
There are two self attention layers in the decoder and they operate a little differently
than the one in the encoder. The first self attention layer is a masked self attention
layer. At the time of decoding, the decoder should only know of the outputs before
the position which is being looked at. This is done by masking the embeddings of
future positions so predictions are only based on earlier positions.
After this attention layer, the decoder has used the information from its previous
predictions.
This is followed by the second attention layer, namely the encoder-
decoder attention layer. Here the key and value vectors are the output of the encoder
stack and the query is the output from the first self attention layer in the decoder.
The intuition behind this additional self attention layer is to use the information
about the previous predictions as queries on the information the encoder found on
the input and use this to predict the next output. Other than the aforementioned
changes, the self attention layers works in the same way.
2.9.6
Layer Normalization and Residuals
In both the encoder and the decoder, residual connections are employed around each
of the sub-layers followed by layer normalization [3]. Layer normalization is applied as
a means of stabilizing the training process. By using residual connections, the model
gains the ability to retain some information from the previous layer. To accommodate
for the residual connections, all sub layers and embedding layers produce outputs of
the same dimension.
2.9.7
Feed Forward Neural Net
Both the encoder and the decoder contains a fully connected FFNN sub layer. The
FFNN consists of two linear layers with a ReLU activation between them. The FFNN
is applied to each position separately and identically. Thus, the task of the FFNN is
essentially to map the input to a different latent space with the same dimensions.
2.9.8
Linear and Softmax Layer
The linear layer is a fully connected neural network that takes the output vector
of floats from the final decoder and turns it into a vector of logits representing the
output space. The softmax layer then takes the logits and turn them into positive

vector, allowing the query vector to easily distinguish inputs based on their different
distances. By doing this, there will never be a problem distinguishing two inputs as
their relative distances to the query vector will never be the same.

2.10
Pre-training
Responsible: Frederik W. Andersen (s144440)
Research within the field of natural language processing (NLP) has shown empirically
that pre-training a model under some unsupervised (or semi-supervised) training
framework, is effective for improving performance on downstream tasks [9]. The basic
idea of pre-training is to define some objective that a model is optimised against. This
objective should be defined in such a way that the model will, hopefully, learn some
underlying structure of the domain of interest. The pre-trained model is then used to
improve performance on downstream tasks. The strength of a model usually depends
on the quality and quantity of the data available. However, the amount of labelled
data is often limited, whereas the amount of unlabelled data is often far greater. The
idea is then to perform pre-training on the large amount of unlabelled data, in the
hope that the model may learn some underlying structures that may be transferred
into the labelled setting.
In general there are two kinds of strategies of applying pre-trained models on down-
stream tasks; feature-based and finetuning-based. In the feature-based approach, such
as ELMo[35], the aim is to extract features, produced by the pre-trained model, from
raw data and use these features as additional input to another model architecture
specifically designed for some downstream task. On the other hand in the finetuning-
based approach, such as OpenAI GPT[36] or BERT[11], a model is first pre-trained
on some data and then afterwards the very same model, with minimal task-specific
additions, is finetuned on downstream tasks. The advantage of the finetuning-based
approach is exactly that it only needs a minimal task-specific architectural addition.
2.10.1

Two of the most prevailing unsupervised (or semi-supervised) pre-training objectives
within NLP have been autoregressive (AR) language modelling and denoising auto-
encoding (AE) [37, 11]. Many downstream tasks in NLP, and in bioinformatics as
well, often require bidirectional context information. Recent research has shown that
modelling deep bidirectional contexts in pre-training has a positive effect on the per-
formance on downstream tasks. However, a problem with conventional AR language
models is that they are intrinsically uni-directional, i.e. they model either a forward
(or backward) factorization order of the sequence. This problem is addressed by some
AE pre-training objectives most notably as seen in BERT [11]. Given a text sequence
x = (x1, ..., xT ), the objective in pre-training of BERT is to corrupt the input by
replacing certain amount of the tokens with the special token <MASK>. It then tries to
reconstruct the original sequence from the corrupted sequence, and therefore this can
be seen as a denoising AE objective. Given that the model is given the full corrupted
sequence as context, it is able to utilise information in both directions. In this way
the pre-training objective directs the model to learn deep bidirectional dependencies.
A downside with BERT is that it relies on masking input tokens with an artificial
token that is not present during finetuning, namely the <MASK> token, leading to a
pretrain-finetune discrepancy. Furthermore, because the target tokens are masked
in the input, BERT is not able to model dependencies within the target tokens,
essentially this is that BERT assumes the target tokens are independent of each
other. XLNet is a generalized autoregressive method that seeks to leverage the best
of both AR language modelling and AE whilst avoiding their limitations [47].
XLNet introduces a novel pre-training objective called permutation language mod-
elling. This pre-training objective is an alteration on the AR language modelling
objective, designed to train the model such that it learns deep bidirectional context.
Moreover, because this is a generalized AR language model, it does not rely on cor-
rupting the data, as is the case with BERT, ultimately eliminating the independence
assumption between target tokens. At its core XLNet uses a Transformer-XL model
architecture with the addition of two-stream self-attention, made in order for it to
work with the permutation language modelling objective.

In the XLNet pre-training we actually do partial prediction, where only a subset of
the tokens in x are predicted, i.e. we predict T ′ tokens where 0 < T ′ < T. Therefore
the task may be defined as:
f : {0, 1}T ×|Σ| →{0, 1}T ′×|Σ|
(4.2)
Subcellular Localization

simply entails a mapping from input to one of the possible classes. Let C denote the

f : {0, 1}T ×|Σ| →C
(4.3)

e : {0, 1}T ×|Σ| →RT ×d
(4.4)
where d (embedding dimension) is a hyperparameter.

4.2.1
Data Loading for Pre-training
The data-loading for pre-training involves pre-processing the data as well as delivering
an iterable dataset during training and evaluation.
Moreover, to correctly apply
attention according to the permutation language model objective, a proper attention
mask is created based on a random factorization order of the protein.
Pre-processing
The pre-processing in pre-training is implemented in the data_utils.py
script, which takes a number of arguments (or flags) specified in appendix A.1.1. The
task ultimately boils down to the creation of inputs, targets and target-masks.
The Transformer-XL processes segments of a pre-defined length at a time. Given
this, the proteins are segmented into sequences of length specified by the seq_len
flag.
These segments are created by striding along the proteins with a stride of
length specified by reuse_len flag. This means that some tokens are reused with
each consecutive segment. For each segment a target array and a target mask are
created. The targets are simply the same tokens shifted one to the right, which comes
from the fact that we are continually predicting the next token. The target mask is
simply an array of 1’s and 0’s that specifies which tokens within the segment we wish
to predict.
The final features of a single Example are then
- input: int array of shape [seq_len], the input sequence.
- is_masked: bool array of shape [seq_len], the target mask, i.e. specifies which
tokens are to be predicted.
- target: int array of shape [seq_len], the target tokens at each position.

The is_real_example boolean value is present as TPUs require all batches to be
the same size. If a batch has fewer examples than the specified batch size it will be
dropped by the TPU, and thus valuable data is lost. For this reason, batches that
consist of fewer examples than the specified batch size are appended with dummy
examples. These examples will indicate that they are not real examples and will not
contribute towards the training or the performance of the model.
Another feature is also stored segment_ids stemming from the original XLNet im-
plementation, which indicate whether there is a split of the input segment. However,
for our purposes this feature is superfluous and essentially has no effect.

There are two pre-training scripts, namely train_tpu.py and train_gpu.py which

going through some extra steps to be able to run on TPUs.
The first step of the pre-training is to retrieve the input functions for the training and
validation data. This involves retrieving the data created during the pre-processing
step, as described in section 4.2.1, as well as converting this data to an iterable dataset.
Among the input flags is a parameter which determines the length of the memory, i.e.
how many hidden steps the model should be able to look back. If this value is more
than 0, the next step is to create a list of tensors, one for each layer, which holds the
previous hidden steps. The next step is to create the model according to the provided
model configuration.

4.4 Training scripts
63
After the computational graph for XLNet is created with given parameters, the train-
ing loop is started. As oppose to the original implementation of the training loop,
which loops through the data for a specified amount of times without any kind of
validation, the loop has been altered to be epoch-based. This means that all the
training data is looked at exactly once and then validated on the validation data.
This not only allows to monitor the performance of the model as the model is being
trained, it also allows to save a checkpoint after each epoch, allowing revisiting previ-
ous checkpoints. Additionally, this also allows implementing a form of regularization
in the shape of early stopping. Given that the models can be very large and run on
expensive hardware, being able to automatically stop early can save a lot of resource
in the shape of computational time and money. The early stopping was implemented
by looking a specified amount of previous validation errors, calculate the gradient of
these points and checking whether or not the gradient is positive. A positive gradient
means that the loss is, on average, rising again and suggests that the model has con-
verged. Once the model has gone through all the epochs or is converged, the results
and a variety of meta data is saved in a json file so it can be stored and accessed at
any point.

Subcellular Localisation
The finetuning of the pre-trained model is done by running the train_calssifier.py
script. As for the pre-training scripts, train_classifier.py takes a large amount
of flags, which determines the finetuning process, as seen in appendix A.2. The first
step of finetuning is to determine which task is being worked on and then select the
appropriate processor. The processor is responsible for handling the problem specific
data, allowing to use the same finetuning script for different tasks. Currently, one
finetuning task is supported which is the task of determining subcellular localisation.
Selecting this task is done by setting the task_name flag to ’subloc’, which in turns
means a SubLocProcessor will be created and handle the data, as described in section
4.2.2. Using the selected processor, the TFRecord files for the validation and training
dataset are created, or loaded if they already exists. Once the data has been loaded,
the model graph is constructed. This is done by reading the model configuration file
for the pre-trained model to be finetuned, creating the computational graph. This is
followed by initialising the parameters in the model to the specified checkpoint. In
addition to this, a dense linear layer is appended to the model. This layer takes the
final output, the last hidden layer from the end of the sequence, which is the <EOP>
token, of the pre-trained model and outputs the logits for the classes of the finetuning
task. These logits are then passed through a softmax function, yielding a prediction
as seen in figure 4.1.

double or categorical. For integer and double types, the range must be given as a

5.3
Pre-training
Responsible: Magnus Nolsøe (s144471)
The purpose of the pre-training task is to achieve as low a perplexity as possible. This
goal comes from the hypothesis of the thesis that a lower perplexity will lead to a
higher accuracy in downstream tasks. As such, the majority of the time spent on this
task has been devoted to tuning the model to yield the smallest perplexity, without
regards to training time and model size.
5.3.1
Experimental Setup
The main hypothesis of this project is that pre-training a model on large unlabelled
datasets improves performance on downstream tasks. Given this a lot of the focus
of the experiments carried out in this project has been doing hyperparameter search,
in order to achieve as low perplexity as possible. Another reason for the high focus
on this is that these kinds of models are highly sensitive to hyperparameters, and
therefore to achieve any good result they have to be tuned. There are a great number
of hyperparameters for XLNet.
For this reason, SigOpt was used in order to do
Bayesian optimisation rather than a grid search.
Two pre-training experiments were carried out on SigOpt.
The first task of the
pre-training was to reduce the amount of hyperparameters having to be optimized
by looking at the ones that made the most impact. This was done by setting up
an experiment on SigOpt in which models were optimized on 15 hyperparameters,
chosen based on the expertise of the group and what had yielded better results for the
original XLNet model. Given that this was an initial test to find the hyperparameters
with the most impact, the ranges of which the hyperparameters could inhibit were
chosen to narrow and the hyperparameters determining the size of the model, and

The purpose of the finetuning task is to take a pre-trained model and use it to help
solve a downstream task. This is done by making minor modifications to the pre-
trained model as to be able to handle a specific problem and then training it on a
different, often smaller, problem specific training set. In the case of this thesis, the
task is subcellular localisation and the pre-trained models are those found during the
pre-training task.
5.4.1
Experimental Setup
The experiments carried out on subcellular localization were mostly made using two
of the best pre-trained models, namely XLProt_mix and XLProt_euk_tt. The first
experiment was to find the best hyperparameters for this task. This was done by
creating a new SigOpt experiment where the hyperparameters searched for were
• dropout
• dropatt
• learning_rate
• lr_layer_decay_rate
==PAGE:90==
78
5 Experiments and Results
• warmup_steps
• weight_decay
The new introduced parameter lr_layer_decay_rate is a parameter that decays
the learning rate down through the layers such that
Top layer:
lr[L] = learning_rate
Low layers:
lr[l −1] = lr[l] · lr_layer_decay_rate
This means that with a low value of the lr_layer_decay_rate, the weights in the
lower layers will be much less affected by the training. If the value of
lr_layer_decay_rate is 1, it has no effect. This parameter is especially interesting
since it has an effect on how much the weights of the pre-trained model are changed.
For the hyperparameter search, the XLProt_mix model was used as the pre-trained
model.
Using the best parameters from SigOpt a further uni-dimension hyperparameter op-
timisation was carried out, where one hyperparameter was changed and the rest were
fixed. The uni-dimensional search was done on both XLProt_mix and XLProt_euk_tt.
During the hyperparameter search the models were validated using 4-fold cross-
validation. The validation error was calculated as a weighted average over the 4 folds
used for validation, where the errors were weighted by the numbers of observations
in the given validation fold with respect to the total number of observations.
The best performing hyperparameters were chosen and the models were then trained
for 100 epochs, generating a checkpoint after each epoch. Each checkpoint was tested
against the test set and the best checkpoint was chosen as the final model. The final
results were compared with the baselines as well as the results reached in the DeepLoc
paper.
To further investigate the effects of pre-training on the downstream task, models
with various degrees of pre-training were finetuned with the same set of hyperpa-
rameters. I.e checkpoints reaching decreasing values of perplexities were finetuned.
This was done for both the models trained on the mixed dataset as well as euk_full
(train+test).

5.5
Discussion
Responsible: Magnus Nolsøe (s144471)
Throughout the project, the goal was to investigate the effect of pre-training a model
and then finetuning it on some downstream task. For this reason, minimising the
perplexity as much as possible was regarded especially important in order to show
that it would increase the performance of the model when performing downstream
tasks. This turned out to be the case, as illustrated by predicting the subcellular local-
isation of the proteins. However, in pursuit of better perplexity the model increased
drastically in size. In addition to this, the final models did not achieve significantly
better results than previous models whom were magnitudes smaller in size. A differ-
ent way to go about this project could have been to first attempt to achieve a close
to state-of-the-art performance for a small, simple Transformer model. After this, it
would have been interesting to see if this performance could have been pushed further
by building more complex models like done in this project, ultimately investigating
XLNet. This was however not the focus of this project but nevertheless an interesting
alternative way to go about the problem at hand.
The final embedding size for the best model ended up at 1024. Usually the embedding
dimension is set to the number of variables needed to explain the words they are
encoding. For the amino acids this could be something like the polarity, molecular-
mass, pH-value etc. However the 1024 embedding dimension is not clearly justified
with respect to this definition, and therefore it would maybe make sense to use smaller
embeddings. It would then be interesting to investigate the embeddings, to see if they
have captured some of these structures of the amino acids.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Many of the tasks in bioinformatics lack labelled data in order to train machine learn-
ing models that generalise sufficiently. Due to the increase of unlabelled protein data
available, in databases such as UniProt [8], the question of extracting knowledge from
this data to increase performance on downstream tasks presents itself. This problem
is also found in the field of natural language processing, where the amount of unla-
belled data is nearly unlimited. However, labelled data still requires manual labour
and is thus harder to come by. Recent research has shown that pre-trained language
models significantly improve performance on downstream tasks in NLP. Furthermore
it has been shown that pre-training models using language modelling on amino acid
sequences also increases performance in some cases [21]. In this project we have inves-
tigated the effect of pre-training, using the recently proposed framework XLNet, on
unlabelled protein sequences, and applying the pre-trained models on the subcellular
localisation task.
The XLNet model was adapted to be able to take protein sequences as input. The
optimal configuration of the model was found by performing Bayesian optimisation
using SigOpt. The model was then pre-trained until convergence on both a dataset
consisting of proteins found either in bacteria or eukaryotic cells as well as a dataset
consisting of proteins exclusively found in eukaryotic cells. The best performing model
on the mixed dataset achieved a perplexity of 6.99 and the best performing model on
the eukaryotic dataset achieved a perplexity of 13.87.
The pre-trained models were modified to be able to perform subcellular localisation by
taking the output of the model and feeding it to a linear layer. This was done in order
to test if pre-training led to an improved understanding of protein sequences and could
lead to an improvement in downstream tasks, such as subcellular localisation. The
model pre-trained on the mixed dataset achieved an accuracy of 0.7353 and Gorodkin
metric of 0.676 and the model pre-trained on the eukaryotic dataset achieved an
accuracy of 0.722 and a Gorodkin metric of 0.66. Additionally, the performance of
the pre-trained models were compared to ones that had not been pre-trained and an
increase of about 10% was observed.
Despite not having achieve state-of-the-art, we do believe we have presented some
interesting results. Based on these results we conclude that the models are able to
==PAGE:98==
86

perform rather well considering they are presented with raw protein sequences rather
than protein profiles. In addition to this, we have shown that pre-training improves
the performance of subcellular localisation when dealing with a large Transformer
model.
All in all we believe we have reached the goal as stated in the problem
statement (section 1.2).
